# DBR Theory And Practice Integration

The distinctive ambition of design-based research is that the intervention is simultaneously a practical product meant to work in real settings and a test of theory about how learning or change happens. The judgment problem is that the two aims pull against each other and agents routinely collapse into one. On the practice side, the work drifts into product development, producing an intervention that works but yields no theoretical insight. On the theory side, the work decorates a practical effort with abstract language that the design never actually instantiated or the data never actually tested. Either failure wastes the effort. A purely practical DBR project ships an artifact but leaves no transferable understanding. A theory-decorated project makes claims the design cannot support, exporting plausible-sounding but untested ideas into the literature. The deeper harm is that theory-practice disconnect misleads both practitioners, who adopt principles never validated, and researchers, who build on theory never genuinely refined.

The agent should use this skill when deciding how an intervention will function as a theory test, constructing or revising a conjecture map, deciding what counts as evidence for or against a theoretical claim, formulating design principles as an output, or judging whether a DBR project genuinely advances theory. The goal is to keep the agent from treating theory as window dressing and from confusing a working intervention with a confirmed theory.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Intervention As Both Product And Theory Test

In DBR the designed artifact is an instrument for theory refinement, not merely a solution to a practical problem.

- State, before enactment, which theoretical claim the design is meant to instantiate and probe.
- Identify the mechanism the theory predicts should produce the intended outcomes.
- Ensure the design features make that mechanism operative and observable.
- Treat both practical success and theoretical insight as required outputs.

When the intervention is treated only as a product, theoretical questions are never asked; when theory is invoked only after the fact, it is description, not test. The dual role must be designed in from the start.

### Build An Explicit Conjecture Map Linking Theory, Features, And Outcomes

A conjecture map makes the theoretical reasoning of the design visible and testable.

- Anchor the map in theoretical conjectures about how learning or change occurs.
- Link each conjecture to specific design features intended to embody it.
- Connect features to mediating processes and to intended observable outcomes.
- Mark which links are well-supported by prior work and which are genuine hypotheses.

Without an explicit map, the connection between theory and design stays implicit in the researcher's head, so neither the team nor later readers can tell which conjectures the evidence bears on. The map is the device that turns a design into a theory test.

### Make Theory Genuinely Falsifiable Through The Design

Theory is only tested if the design could produce evidence against it.

- Define, in advance, what outcomes or processes would count as support for each conjecture.
- Define what would count as disconfirmation, not only confirmation.
- Ensure the measures capture the mediating process the theory predicts, not just final outcomes.
- Avoid retrofitting theory to whatever results appear.

If every plausible result can be interpreted as consistent with the theory, the theory was never at risk and the project makes no theoretical contribution. Falsifiability is what separates theory testing from theory illustration.

### Use Iterative Cycles To Refine Theory, Not Only The Product

Each cycle should feed back into both the intervention and the theoretical understanding.

- After each cycle, ask what the evidence implies for the conjectures, not only for the features.
- Revise conjectures that the data challenge, and record the revision.
- Distinguish refining a conjecture to fit new evidence from merely saving it with ad hoc exceptions.
- Carry refined conjectures forward as the theory input to the next design.

Iteration that only tunes the product produces a better artifact but stagnant theory. Iteration that revises conjectures produces the cumulative theoretical advance that is DBR's signature contribution.

### Formulate Design Principles As A First-Class Output

Design principles are the bridge between a specific intervention and transferable knowledge.

- Express principles at a level abstract enough to guide new designs but concrete enough to act on.
- Ground each principle in the evidence and conjectures that support it.
- Specify the conditions and boundaries under which the principle is expected to hold.
- Distinguish relatively mature principles from provisional conjectures.

Vague principles such as make it engaging carry no theoretical content, while overly specific recipes apply only to the exact artifact. Useful principles name the mechanism, the feature that enacts it, and the reason it should work.

### Distinguish Theoretical Claims From Practical Effectiveness Claims

A working intervention and a confirmed theory are different achievements and must be reported as such.

- Practical claims state that the intervention produced desired outcomes in this setting.
- Theoretical claims state that the underlying mechanism is supported or refined.
- Avoid sliding from it worked to the theory is confirmed when the data support only the former.
- Report separately what was learned about the design and what was learned about the theory.

Conflating the two lets a practically successful but theoretically uninformative project claim theoretical advance, or lets a theoretically rich but practically modest project be dismissed.

### Align Measures With The Theoretical Mechanism, Not Only Outcomes

To test theory, evidence must speak to the mechanism, not just whether the end goal was reached.

- Measure mediating processes the theory predicts, such as the reasoning, interaction, or engagement the design is meant to provoke.
- Use process data, interaction logs, discourse, or observation alongside outcome measures.
- Triangulate outcome gains with evidence that the mechanism actually operated.
- Treat a gain with no evidence of the mechanism as practical success but theoretical silence.

An outcome gain alone cannot tell whether the theory's mechanism caused it or whether some other path did. Mechanism-aligned measurement is what makes the data theoretically informative.

### Acknowledge The Limits And Scope Of Theoretical Contribution

DBR rarely proves theory in the strong sense; it refines and contextualizes it.

- State the scope conditions under which the refined theory is expected to hold.
- Distinguish local theoretical insight from general law.
- Be explicit about the uncertainty remaining after the cycles.
- Resist presenting a context-bound refinement as a universal confirmation.

Honest scoping protects later researchers and practitioners from overextending claims that were established only in particular settings.

## Common Traps

### Treating DBR As Mere Product Development

When the intervention's success becomes the whole goal, theoretical questions are never posed and the project ships an artifact with no transferable understanding. This trap is attractive because a working product is a tangible, fundable result, but it forfeits the theoretical contribution that justifies DBR over ordinary design.

### Theory As Decoration Applied After The Fact

Invoking theory only when writing up, to give the work an academic gloss, means the design never actually tested anything. The trap is that post-hoc theorizing reads plausibly, but it is unfalsifiable description masquerading as empirical contribution.

### Non-Falsifiable Conjectures

If a conjecture is framed so loosely that any outcome can be made consistent with it, the project cannot refine theory because nothing could count against it. The trap is that vague conjectures feel safe, but safety from disconfirmation is safety from meaning.

### Confusing A Working Intervention With A Confirmed Theory

Sliding from the intervention worked to the theory is right overstates what was established, because success can flow from mechanisms other than the one the theory names. The trap conflates practical effectiveness with theoretical support and exports unsupported theory into the literature.

### Measuring Only Outcomes, Not Mechanisms

When evidence captures only final outcomes, a gain cannot be attributed to the theoretical mechanism, leaving the theory untested regardless of practical success. The trap is that outcomes are easier to measure, but without process data the theoretical claim has no empirical anchor.

### Ad Hoc Theory Patching To Save A Conjecture

Revising a conjecture with unprincipled exceptions whenever disconfirming evidence appears protects the theory from ever being wrong and thus from ever being genuinely refined. The trap is that saving the conjecture feels like intellectual care, but it converts theory into an unfalsiable story.

### Overstating Design Principles As Universal Laws

Presenting context-bound principles as general truths invites others to apply them where they do not hold, wasting effort and potentially harming learners. The trap is that strong claims are more publishable, but unscoped principles mislead the field.

### Disconnecting Design Features From Theoretical Conjectures

When features are added for practical reasons with no link to a conjecture, the design becomes an uninterpretable bundle and later outcomes cannot speak to theory. The trap is that pragmatic feature-adding feels like responsive design, but it severs the evidence-theory link the project depends on.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the intervention explicitly designed to instantiate and probe a stated theoretical claim, not only to solve a practical problem?
- [ ] Is there an explicit conjecture map linking theoretical conjectures to design features to mediating processes to outcomes?
- [ ] For each conjecture, are both supporting and disconfirming evidence defined in advance, so the theory is genuinely at risk?
- [ ] Do iterative cycles revise conjectures based on evidence, with revisions recorded, rather than only tuning the product?
- [ ] Are design principles formulated at a useful level of abstraction, grounded in evidence, and scoped with explicit conditions?
- [ ] Are theoretical claims reported separately from practical effectiveness claims, without sliding between them?
- [ ] Do measures capture the mediating mechanism the theory predicts, through process data, not only final outcomes?
- [ ] Are the scope and limits of the theoretical contribution stated honestly, distinguishing local refinement from universal law?
- [ ] Is the project clearly distinguishable from both pure product development and theory-decorated practice?
- [ ] For high-stakes educational interventions, theory claims intended to influence a field, or cases where the theory-practice link is uncertain, has an experienced design-based researcher or learning theorist reviewed the conjecture map and theoretical claims before publication or dissemination?

This skill provides methodological guidance on integrating theory and practice in design-based research. It is not a substitute for experienced DBR mentorship or formal ethics consultation. DBR intervenes in real educational settings, and a project that disconnects theory from design can mislead both practice and research, exporting unvalidated principles and untested theory into the literature. Poorly integrated DBR can legitimize interventions and theoretical claims that were never properly established, wasting educational effort and eroding trust in the method. Where stakes are high or the theoretical contribution is uncertain, consult experienced DBR researchers, learning theorists, and ethics advisors before acting.
