# Experimental Control And Validity Types

Designing an experiment is a series of tradeoffs among four kinds of validity: internal (did the manipulation cause the effect), construct (do the operations represent the intended variables), external (do the findings generalize), and statistical conclusion (is the inference precise and correct). Strengthening one often weakens another. A tightly controlled laboratory study maximizes internal and construct validity at the cost of external validity; a field study does the reverse. Agents often optimize one validity obsessively and ignore the others, then overclaim along the dimension they ignored. The skill is to make the tradeoffs deliberately, name them, and calibrate claims to the design's actual strengths.

Use this skill when designing controls and conditions, choosing within- or between-subjects structures, or balancing validity demands. The goal is to prevent the agent from maximizing one validity while silently sacrificing the others and overclaiming.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Four Validities As Competing Demands

No single design maximizes all four validities. The researcher chooses which to prioritize based on the question and stage of research.

Balance:

- internal validity: isolate the manipulation's effect;
- construct validity: operations represent the intended constructs;
- external validity: findings extend beyond the study setting;
- statistical conclusion validity: inference is precise and unbiased.

State which validities the design prioritizes and which it sacrifices, and calibrate claims accordingly.

### Design Control Conditions To Isolate The Active Ingredient

A treatment-versus-nothing comparison confounds the treatment with attention, expectation, and novelty. Strong control conditions isolate what is actually doing the work.

Choose controls by:

- active or placebo controls that match attention and expectation without the active ingredient;
- waitlist or treatment-as-usual where appropriate, acknowledging their limits;
- dose-response or component designs to identify the effective element;
- dismantling designs to test which parts of a package matter.

A significant treatment-versus-nothing effect may reflect placebo or attention, not the mechanism of interest.

### Match Within- And Between-Subjects Designs To The Question

Within-subjects designs are powerful but vulnerable to carryover, order, and practice effects. Between-subjects designs avoid carryover but need more participants and are vulnerable to individual differences.

Choose by:

- within-subjects when carryover is minimal and each participant can serve as their own control;
- between-subjects when the manipulation is irreversible or contaminating;
- counterbalancing order and assessing carryover in within-subjects designs;
- accounting for correlation in within-subjects analysis.

Ignoring carryover in a within-subjects design manufactures artifacts that look like effects.

### Protect Construct Validity Through Operationalization

The manipulation and measures must represent the intended constructs, or the study tests something other than what it claims.

Protect by:

- justifying that the manipulation operationalizes the intended construct;
- using validated measures where available;
- avoiding mono-operation bias (a single operation of a construct);
- avoiding monomethod bias (a single method for all measures);
- checking for demand characteristics that shift what participants do.

A clean internal-validity result is meaningless if the construct was mis-operationalized.

### Define External Validity Through Sampling, Not Assertion

External validity is not declared; it is earned by sampling from the population and settings to which one wishes to generalize.

Build external validity by:

- sampling participants from the target population, not just convenience;
- varying settings and contexts;
- replicating across populations and conditions;
- testing boundary conditions where the effect should change.

A single convenience sample supports a hypothesis, not a generalization.

### Avoid Confounds By Design, Not By Statistical Adjustment

The cleanest control is structural: randomization, matching, or holding variables constant. Statistical adjustment is weaker and assumption-laden.

Prefer design controls:

- random assignment to balance confounders;
- matching or stratification on key prognostic factors;
- holding context constant where appropriate;
- using covariates only as a supplement, not a substitute.

A confound designed out is removed; a confound adjusted for is only assumed away.

### Assess And Report Implementation Fidelity

An effect cannot be attributed to an intervention that was not delivered as designed. Fidelity checks link the design to what actually happened.

Assess:

- whether the intervention was delivered as specified;
- dose received by participants;
- deviations and their reasons;
- the impact of fidelity variation on results.

Low fidelity turns an efficacy test into a test of an unimplemented intervention.

### Calibrate Claims To The Design's Validity Profile

The strength and scope of claims should match which validities the design supports. Overclaiming along an unsupported validity is the central error.

Calibrate by:

- causal language only when internal validity supports it;
- construct claims only when operationalization supports them;
- generalization only when sampling supports it;
- precision claims only when statistical conclusion validity supports them.

A laboratory causal effect should not be narrated as a universal real-world truth.

### Use Pilot Work To Test The Design Before The Main Study

Piloting reveals whether manipulations work, measures behave, and procedures run, before resources are committed to the main test.

Pilot to:

- verify the manipulation produces the intended state;
- check that measures are reliable and not ceiling or floor bound;
- test procedures, timing, and instructions;
- estimate effect sizes for power.

Pilots are not the main study; their purpose is to de-risk the design.

## Common Traps

### Maximizing One Validity, Ignoring Others

A pristine internal-validity study overclaimed as general is a common and serious error.

### Treatment Versus Nothing

Comparisons without active controls confound the treatment with attention and expectation.

### Ignored Carryover In Within-Subjects Designs

Order and practice effects masquerade as treatment effects.

### Mis-Operationalized Constructs

A clean result on the wrong construct answers no meaningful question.

### External Validity Asserted Not Earned

A convenience sample does not generalize without replication and argument.

### Statistical Adjustment For Design Confounds

Adjustment assumes away what design could have removed.

### Unreported Fidelity Problems

An effect attributed to an unimplemented intervention is misleading.

## Self-Check

- Are the four validities treated as competing, with priorities stated and claims calibrated?
- Do control conditions isolate the active ingredient rather than comparing treatment to nothing?
- Is the within- or between-subjects structure matched to the question, with carryover addressed?
- Is construct validity protected through justified operationalization and multiple operations or methods?
- Is external validity built through sampling and replication, not asserted?
- Are confounds removed by design where possible, with statistical adjustment only supplementary?
- Is implementation fidelity assessed and reported, with deviations and their impact noted?
- Are claims calibrated to the design's validity profile, with unsupported dimensions hedged?
- Was pilot work used to verify manipulations, measures, and procedures before the main study?
- Would a reader know which validities the design supports and which it sacrifices?
