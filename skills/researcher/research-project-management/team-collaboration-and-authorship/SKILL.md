# Team Collaboration And Authorship

Research is increasingly collaborative, and collaboration is governed by implicit expectations that, left unspoken, become sources of conflict. Who does what, who decides, who gets credit, and how disagreements are resolved are questions that teams often avoid until they become disputes. Agents often assume goodwill will carry the team, then discover that authorship disagreements, unequal workloads, or unclear decision rights fracture collaborations that were scientifically sound. Managing collaboration and authorship deliberately, from the start, is what allows talented people to work together productively over the life of a project.

The harm this skill prevents is authorship disputes that poison relationships, unequal contributions that breed resentment, miscommunication that derails work, and the loss of collaborators through unresolved conflict. The agent has freedom in team structure, but the discipline of explicit agreements and fair credit is binding.

## Core Rules

### Establish Roles, Contributions, And Decision Rights Early

Ambiguity about who does what, and who decides what, is the root of most collaboration conflict. Define roles and decision rights at the outset, when goodwill is high, rather than negotiating under pressure later.

Define early:

- who leads each aim or work package;
- who is responsible, consulted, and informed for key decisions;
- what each member's expected contribution is;
- who has authority over data, analysis, and dissemination;
- how the team coordinates and communicates;
- how new members join and what they are told.

### Agree On Authorship Criteria And Order In Advance

Authorship is the currency of academia, and disputes over it are among the most damaging conflicts. Agreeing on authorship criteria, eligibility, and ordering at the project's start, in writing, prevents later disputes. Waiting until a paper is drafted is too late.

Agree on:

- the criteria that earn authorship (substantive contribution to design, data, analysis, or drafting);
- who does not qualify and why (acknowledgment instead);
- the expected order of authors and what it signifies;
- how corresponding and senior or last authorship are assigned;
- how authorship is revisited if contributions change;
- a written authorship agreement all members endorse.

### Follow Recognized Authorship Standards

Ad hoc authorship decisions breed unfairness. Recognized standards, such as those defining authorship as substantial contribution plus drafting or critical revision plus final approval, provide a defensible basis. Apply a recognized standard consistently.

Apply standards:

- require genuine intellectual contribution, not just data collection or funding;
- gift, honorary, or ghost authorship are unethical;
- large consortia should use group authorship with clear contributor lists;
- contributors who do not meet authorship criteria go in acknowledgments;
- document each author's specific contribution.

### Write A Collaboration Agreement For Complex Projects

For multi-institution or long-term collaborations, a written agreement is essential. It specifies roles, data ownership, authorship, intellectual property, publication rights, and dispute resolution. Verbal understandings fail under pressure.

A collaboration agreement covers:

- roles and responsibilities of each party;
- data ownership, sharing, and use rights;
- authorship and publication processes;
- intellectual property and commercialization;
- confidentiality and embargoes;
- dispute resolution mechanisms;
- what happens if a party leaves or the project ends.

### Establish Clear Communication Norms

Teams fail through miscommunication as often as through disagreement. Establish how the team communicates, how often, and through what channels, so that information flows and decisions are visible.

Set norms:

- regular meeting cadence and required attendees;
- shared documentation and version control for outputs;
- response time expectations for emails and requests;
- where decisions are recorded so they are not lost;
- how remote and asynchronous work is coordinated;
- language and accessibility considerations for international teams.

### Manage Credit Beyond Authorship

Not all contribution earns authorship, but all contribution deserves recognition. Acknowledgments, contributorship statements, and credit for non-author contributions maintain morale and fairness. Ensure those who contribute are seen.

Recognize contribution:

- acknowledge data collectors, coordinators, and technical staff;
- use contributorship taxonomies where appropriate;
- credit contributions in presentations, not just papers;
- ensure junior and supporting members are visible;
- recognize effort even when it does not meet authorship thresholds.

### Address Conflict Early And Constructively

Conflict in collaborations is inevitable; unaddressed conflict is destructive. Create mechanisms to surface and resolve disagreements before they escalate, and address issues directly rather than letting them fester.

Manage conflict:

- raise concerns early and privately before they grow;
- focus on the issue and shared goals, not personalities;
- use the collaboration agreement's dispute process;
- involve a neutral third party when needed;
- document agreements reached;
- know when a collaboration cannot be salvaged and plan an exit.

### Onboard New Members Thoroughly

New team members need context to contribute effectively and to integrate into established norms. A structured onboarding transfers institutional knowledge, clarifies expectations, and prevents the newcomer from disrupting established arrangements.

Onboard by:

- sharing the collaboration agreement and authorship plan;
- explaining roles, decision rights, and communication norms;
- providing access to data, documentation, and prior work;
- assigning a mentor or point of contact;
- clarifying what is expected of the new member.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Goodwill Replaces Explicit Agreements

Unspoken expectations become disputes under pressure. Put roles and authorship in writing early.

### Deciding Authorship Only When The Paper Is Drafted

Late authorship negotiations are bitter. Agree on criteria and order at the start.

### Gift, Honorary, Or Ghost Authorship

These practices are unethical and damage credibility. Apply recognized standards.

### No Written Collaboration Agreement

Verbal understandings fail in complex projects. Document roles, data, and IP.

### Poor Communication Norms

Lost decisions and slow responses derail work. Establish clear channels and cadence.

### Unrecognized Non-Author Contributions

Supporting staff who feel invisible disengage. Acknowledge all real contributions.

### Letting Conflict Fester

Unaddressed disagreements escalate. Surface and resolve issues early.

- [ ] Are non-author contributions acknowledged and credited?
- [ ] Is there a mechanism to surface and resolve conflict early?
