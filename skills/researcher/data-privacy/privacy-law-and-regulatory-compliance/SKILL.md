# Privacy Law And Regulatory Compliance

Research that processes personal data is subject to privacy and data protection law. These laws vary by jurisdiction, apply based on who is processed and where, and carry obligations that interact with but go beyond research ethics. Researchers often assume that ethics approval satisfies privacy law, or that the law of the institution's country governs, when in fact the law of the participants' location often applies. The judgment problem is identifying which laws govern, establishing a lawful basis, and meeting obligations that can conflict with convenient research practice.

Use this skill when determining applicable privacy law, assessing lawful basis, handling cross-border transfer, and managing data subject rights. The goal is to keep the agent from assuming ethics approval equals legal compliance and to make the regulatory obligations explicit and met.

This is a high-stakes domain because privacy violations carry legal liability, fines, and harm to participants, and can halt or void research. When the applicable law or its requirements are unclear, the agent should consult the institutional privacy officer, data protection officer, or legal counsel rather than interpreting obligations alone.

## Core Rules

### Identify Which Laws Apply Based On Participants And Processing

Privacy law applies based on who is processed and where the processing occurs, not only where the institution sits. A single study can trigger multiple regimes.

Identify applicability of:

- the law of the jurisdiction where participants are located;
- the law of the jurisdiction where data are processed or stored;
- sector-specific laws, such as health privacy or education records laws;
- international regulations that apply extraterritorially, such as data protection regulations with broad scope;
- institutional and funder requirements that may exceed legal minimums.

A study with participants in several countries may be subject to several laws simultaneously. Map the applicability before assuming a single regime governs.

### Establish A Lawful Basis For Processing Research Data

Most privacy laws require a lawful basis for processing personal data. Research is often permitted under specific bases, but the basis must be identified and documented.

Determine:

- the lawful basis available under each applicable regime, such as consent, public interest research, or legitimate interests;
- whether the research-specific provisions apply and what conditions they impose;
- whether the basis requires ethics approval, public interest balancing, or additional safeguards;
- whether special category data, such as health, genetic, or biometric data, require an additional condition;
- how the basis interacts with consent for research participation, which is not always the same as consent for data processing.

Assuming that research is automatically lawful is a common error. The basis must be identified and the conditions met.

### Recognize That Ethics Approval Does Not Equal Legal Compliance

Ethics review and privacy law overlap but are distinct. Approval satisfies the ethics obligation; it does not automatically satisfy the legal one.

Understand:

- that privacy law may impose obligations ethics review does not, such as data subject rights and breach notification;
- that ethics approval may be a condition for a research lawful basis but is not itself the basis;
- that privacy law may restrict processing that ethics approved;
- that both must be satisfied independently.

Treat ethics approval and privacy compliance as separate streams that must be reconciled. Do not substitute one for the other.

### Handle Special Category And Sensitive Data With Additional Conditions

Most regimes impose extra conditions on sensitive data, such as health, genetic, biometric, racial, sexual, or criminal data. Research often involves these categories.

Address:

- the additional lawful condition required for each special category used;
- the heightened security and minimization expected;
- restrictions on secondary use and sharing;
- whether explicit consent or other specific conditions are required;
- the interaction with biospecimen and genomic data rules.

Sensitive data are not prohibited, but they carry obligations that must be met. Plan for them from the design stage.

### Manage Cross-Border Data Transfer

Moving personal data across borders is restricted under many regimes. Research collaborations often require such transfer.

Manage transfer by:

- identifying whether transfer restrictions apply to the planned data flows;
- using approved transfer mechanisms, such as adequacy decisions, standard contractual clauses, or binding corporate rules;
- assessing whether the destination provides adequate protection;
- documenting the transfer basis and safeguards;
- considering whether data can remain in the country of origin with remote analysis.

Unlawful cross-border transfer can halt research and create liability. The transfer basis must be established before data move.

### Plan For Data Subject Rights

Privacy law often grants individuals rights over their data, such as access, correction, erasure, and objection. Research must plan to honor these where applicable.

Plan for:

- how access requests will be handled without undermining study integrity;
- whether erasure rights apply and how they interact with retention and reproducibility;
- how correction requests will be processed;
- whether research exemptions limit certain rights and under what conditions;
- how requests will be verified and responded to within legal timelines.

Ignoring data subject rights is a violation. Research exemptions exist in some regimes but are conditional and must be invoked correctly.

### Align Retention, Minimization, And Breach Obligations

Privacy law imposes obligations on how long data are kept, how much is collected, and how breaches are handled. Research design must respect these.

Align:

- data minimization, collecting only what the research requires;
- retention limits, keeping data only as long as necessary and as the lawful basis permits;
- purpose limitation, using data only for the purposes specified;
- breach notification, reporting within the timelines the law requires;
- records of processing, documenting what is processed, why, and on what basis.

These obligations shape data management. They are not optional steps added after the research design is fixed.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Institution's Law Governs

The law of the participants' location often applies. A single study can trigger multiple regimes.

### Equating Ethics Approval With Legal Compliance

Ethics and privacy law are distinct. Approval satisfies one obligation, not the other.

### Assuming Research Is Automatically Lawful

Most regimes require an identified lawful basis and conditions. Research is often permitted, but not by default.

### Ignoring Special Category Conditions

Sensitive data carry additional conditions. Processing them without meeting those conditions is unlawful.

### Transferring Data Across Borders Without A Basis

Cross-border transfer is restricted. Unlawful transfer can halt research and create liability.

### Overlooking Data Subject Rights

Individuals have rights over their data. Ignoring access, correction, or erasure requests is a violation.

### Treating Retention And Minimization As Optional

Privacy law limits what is collected and how long it is kept. These shape data management, not follow it.

## Self-Check

- Have the applicable privacy laws been identified based on participant location, processing location, and sector?
- Is a lawful basis for processing identified and documented under each applicable regime, with research-specific conditions met?
- Is ethics approval recognized as distinct from, not equivalent to, legal compliance?
- Are special category and sensitive data handled with the additional conditions each regime requires?
- Is cross-border data transfer managed through approved mechanisms with documented safeguards?
- Are data subject rights planned for, including access, correction, erasure, and applicable research exemptions?
- Are retention, minimization, purpose limitation, breach notification, and records of processing aligned with legal obligations?
- Where applicable law or its requirements are unclear, has the institutional privacy officer, data protection officer, or legal counsel been consulted rather than interpreting obligations alone?
