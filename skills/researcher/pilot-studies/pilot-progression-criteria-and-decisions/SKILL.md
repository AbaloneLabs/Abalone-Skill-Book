# Pilot Progression Criteria And Decisions

A pilot without predefined decision rules becomes a story the team tells after the fact. Whatever the pilot shows, the team can rationalize proceeding, because abandoning or heavily revising a study is psychologically and politically costly. Progression criteria exist to force the difficult decisions to be made in advance, when the team is still honest about what would count as success, and to make the final proceed, revise, or stop judgment traceable to evidence rather than hope. When criteria are missing, vague, or set after seeing the data, the pilot loses its value as a decision gate and the main study proceeds on optimism.

The judgment problem is that predefining thresholds feels restrictive and teams resist committing to a stop rule before they know the answer. Agents often set criteria that are too lenient, too numerous, or unfalsifiable, so that any result can be declared a pass. The harm this skill prevents is a main study launched on the back of a pilot that quietly failed its feasibility tests, burning resources and participant goodwill on a design that cannot deliver. The agent has latitude in what thresholds to set, but the criteria must be specific, measurable, set before data collection, and tied to a real decision.

This skill offers methodological guidance for setting progression criteria and making defensible pilot decisions. It is not a substitute for methodological or statistical consultation, and a pilot whose results are interpreted against shifting or absent criteria can mislead the main study and waste participants' contributions.

## Core Rules

### Set Criteria Before Data Collection, Not After

The defining feature of credible progression criteria is that they exist before the pilot data are seen. Criteria drafted after results are known are rationalizations, not rules, and reviewers can usually tell. Draft and date the criteria during protocol development, before enrollment, and version-control them.

Record the criteria in the protocol and any registration, with the date. If they are revised before data collection for a good reason, document the change and the reason. Any criterion that appears only in the final report, tailored to the observed result, has no evidential value.

### Use A Traffic-Light Structure With Three Outcomes

A binary pass or fail pushes teams toward proceed because stopping feels catastrophic. A three-tier green-amber-red structure creates a middle path, revise, that captures the common reality that a pilot is partly workable and partly broken.

Define three outcomes explicitly:

- green, proceed to the main study essentially as designed;
- amber, proceed only with specified revisions, or run a further iteration;
- red, stop or fundamentally rethink the design.

For each criterion, state which traffic-light color each result range maps to. Avoid criteria that can only turn green.

### Make Each Criterion Specific, Measurable, And Falsifiable

A criterion such as "recruitment acceptable" can be satisfied by any result and therefore tests nothing. Each criterion must name a quantity, a threshold, and a time window, and there must be a result that fails it.

Specify for each criterion:

- the metric, such as recruitment rate per site per month;
- the threshold for each color, with the numeric value;
- the denominator and time window, such as over the first three months;
- the data source and how it will be calculated;
- a result that would trigger red, confirming the criterion can actually fail.

If you cannot write down a result that fails a criterion, the criterion is decorative.

### Cover The Dimensions That Could Kill The Main Study

Progression criteria should target the failures that would actually prevent the main study from answering its question, not the dimensions that are easy to measure. Identify the highest-risk dimensions first and set criteria for those.

Typical high-risk dimensions:

- recruitment rate against the target needed for the main study timeline;
- retention and attrition against the assumed rate;
- eligibility criteria yielding a workable screen-pass rate;
- intervention adherence or completion;
- fidelity to protocol across delivery staff;
- data completeness and data system reliability;
- participant and staff acceptability and burden;
- cost and staff time within the budget envelope;
- absence of serious safety signals.

Rank by risk to the main study and ensure the riskiest dimensions each have a criterion with a real red threshold.

### Predefine How Conflicting Criteria Are Resolved

Pilots often produce mixed signals, strong recruitment but poor retention, or good fidelity but excessive burden. Without a rule, teams cherry-pick the green criteria and ignore the red. Decide in advance how to combine criteria into an overall decision.

Options include:

- any single red criterion stops or triggers major revision;
- a weighted or majority rule across criteria;
- separate mandatory gates, such as safety and data completeness, that override all others;
- a documented decision matrix mapping combinations of results to traffic-light outcomes.

State the combination rule before data collection. The rule should make stopping or revising a real possibility, not a theoretical one.

### Separate Feasibility Evidence From Efficacy Claims

Progression criteria should be about whether the study can be run well, not whether the intervention worked. A smooth pilot with promising outcomes is still a feasibility pass, not proof of effect. Conflating the two lets an easy-to-run pilot generate efficacy momentum that carries a weak intervention into a costly main study.

Word criteria in terms of process, such as recruitment, retention, fidelity, and data quality. If outcome data influence the decision, do so only through pre-specified, clearly labeled non-confirmatory inputs, and never let a promising pilot effect override failed feasibility criteria.

### Record The Decision And Its Reasoning

A pilot that ends without a recorded decision has wasted its purpose. Convene the team against the predefined criteria, reach an explicit decision, and write down the reasoning so future reviewers and funders can audit it.

Record:

- the result of each criterion against its threshold and the resulting color;
- the overall decision, proceed, revise, iterate, or stop;
- the specific revisions or conditions attached to an amber outcome;
- the reasoning where judgment was exercised beyond the rules;
- who made the decision and when.

An undocumented decision is indistinguishable from no decision.

### Build In A Genuine Option To Stop

If the criteria cannot realistically produce a red outcome, they are not criteria. The team must be willing, in advance, to stop or fundamentally redesign if the evidence warrants it. This is the hardest part and the most important.

Pressure-test the criteria before launch by asking, for each one, what result would make you stop. If the honest answer is "nothing," the criterion is not protecting the main study. Strengthen it, or acknowledge that the pilot is illustrative rather than decisive and say so plainly.

### Align Criteria With Ethics And Participant Protection

Progression criteria are not only about feasibility; they also protect participants in the main study from a design that is unsafe, futile, or unable to answer its question. Safety signals and excessive burden should be mandatory gates.

Include criteria that can halt progression for safety reasons, unacceptable burden, or consent failures, and define who has authority to trigger a stop independently of the team, such as a data monitoring committee. A pilot that exposed harm but was declared green has failed ethically, not just methodologically.

## Common Traps

### Criteria Set After Seeing The Data

Drafting or adjusting thresholds once results are known turns rules into justifications. The trap is "refining" criteria to match what happened. Date and lock criteria before enrollment; later changes have no evidential weight.

### Criteria That Can Only Pass

A criterion with no realistic failing result tests nothing and exists to reassure. The trap is setting lenient thresholds so the team never has to stop. For each criterion, confirm a result exists that would trigger red.

### Too Many Criteria Diluting The Decision

A long list of low-stakes criteria lets teams point to the green ones and ignore the red. The trap is mistaking volume for rigor. Focus on the few dimensions that could kill the main study and give them real teeth.

### Cherry-Picking Green Criteria Under Mixed Results

When signals conflict, teams favor the favorable ones. The trap is declaring overall success by ignoring retention or burden failures. Predefine a combination rule that makes mixed results resolve to amber or red, not selective green.

### Letting A Promising Pilot Effect Override Failed Feasibility

A large observed effect is tempting and can sweep recruitment or fidelity problems under the rug. The trap is letting efficacy momentum carry a non-feasible design forward. Feasibility criteria govern progression; outcome data are non-confirmatory.

### Vague Or Unmeasurable Thresholds

"Acceptable retention" can mean anything and therefore means nothing. The trap is leaving thresholds qualitative to preserve flexibility. Quantify every threshold with a metric, denominator, and window.

### No Recorded Decision Or Reasoning

A pilot that ends in a vague "looks good, let's proceed" has no audit trail. The trap is treating the decision as obvious. Record each criterion's result, the overall decision, the conditions, and the reasoning.

### No Genuine Willingness To Stop

If stopping was never really on the table, the criteria are theater. The trap is designing criteria that protect the project rather than the science. Pressure-test each criterion for a realistic failing result before launch.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are progression criteria drafted, dated, and version-controlled before pilot data collection, with any pre-collection changes documented with reasons?
- [ ] Does each criterion use a green-amber-red structure with a real revise or stop outcome, not only a pass?
- [ ] Is every criterion specific, measurable, and falsifiable, naming a metric, threshold, denominator, time window, data source, and a result that would trigger red?
- [ ] Do the criteria cover the highest-risk dimensions, such as recruitment, retention, fidelity, data completeness, burden, cost, and safety, each with a genuine red threshold?
- [ ] Is there a predefined rule for combining conflicting criteria into an overall decision, such that mixed results can resolve to amber or red rather than selective green?
- [ ] Are feasibility criteria kept distinct from efficacy claims, with any outcome influence pre-specified and clearly labeled non-confirmatory?
- [ ] Are safety, burden, and consent failures treated as mandatory gates that can halt progression independently of other criteria?
- [ ] Has each criterion been pressure-tested by stating a result that would make the team stop, confirming the pilot can actually fail?
- [ ] Is the final decision, proceed, revise, iterate, or stop, recorded with each criterion's result, the conditions attached, the reasoning, the decision-makers, and the date?
- [ ] For high-stakes pilots, costly main studies, or cases where criteria conflict or stopping is contentious, has a methodologist or independent reviewer assessed the criteria and the decision before scaling to the main study?
