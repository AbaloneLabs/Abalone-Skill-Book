# Responding To Reviewer Comments

Peer review comments are not commands; they are expert feedback that the author must triage, address, or respectfully contest. A revision is judged not only on the changed manuscript but on the response letter, which demonstrates whether the authors understood the criticism and engaged with it seriously. Agents often treat the response as a formality, agreeing to everything superficially or rebutting defensively, both of which annoy editors and weaken the paper.

The harm this skill prevents is a desk rejection after revision, a damaged relationship with editors, and a paper that remains flawed because criticism was deflected rather than absorbed. The agent has freedom in how to implement changes, but the discipline of engaging each point honestly is mandatory.

## Core Rules

### Read All Comments Before Responding To Any

Resist the urge to answer the first comment immediately. Read every reviewer and the editor's letter in full to understand the full scope of requested changes, spot overlapping concerns, and identify the highest-stakes issues. Some comments reframe others, and a coherent revision plan requires the whole picture.

Map the comments:

- list every distinct point from each reviewer;
- note where reviewers disagree with each other;
- identify the editor's specific requirements versus optional suggestions;
- flag points that require new analysis or data;
- prioritize structural or methodological demands over wording tweaks.

### Triage Each Comment Into Accept, Partial, Or Rebut

Not every comment warrants equal effort. Classify each point and plan a response type.

For each comment decide:

- accept fully and revise accordingly;
- accept the spirit but implement differently, with justification;
- rebut with evidence if the comment is factually wrong or out of scope;
- request editor guidance when reviewers contradict each other.

A rebuttal is legitimate when grounded in evidence or scope, not when it is defensive. Reviewers can be wrong, but disagreement must be argued respectfully and backed by citations or data.

### Make The Response Letter Easy To Audit

Editors and reviewers should be able to see exactly what changed and why without hunting. A well-structured response letter turns a frustrating review into a clear conversation.

Structure the letter:

- thank the editor and reviewers briefly and sincerely;
- summarize the major changes in an opening overview;
- address comments point by point, labeling each with reviewer and number;
- quote the original comment, then give your response;
- specify the exact location of changes (page and line);
- use formatting (bold, color) to distinguish old from new text where helpful.

Do not make reviewers guess whether their concern was addressed.

### Distinguish Substantive From Cosmetic Requests

Some comments request fundamental changes to design, analysis, or framing; others ask for clearer wording. Allocate effort proportional to substance.

Substantive requests often involve:

- additional analyses or sensitivity checks;
- new controls or covariates;
- re-framing of claims to match evidence;
- addressing a confound or alternative explanation;
- reporting previously omitted results.

Cosmetic requests involve clarity, grammar, citation additions, or figure labels. Address both, but never let a quick wording fix substitute for a demanded analytical change.

### Provide Evidence For Rebuttals, Not Just Opinion

When you decline a change, the reason must be stronger than preference. Editors side with evidence and scope arguments, not with assertions of authority.

Strong rebuttals cite:

- prior literature that contradicts the reviewer's premise;
- a methodological reason the requested analysis is inappropriate;
- scope boundaries stated in the original submission;
- new analysis you ran that shows the change does not alter conclusions;
- feasibility or ethical constraints with explanation.

Weak rebuttals say "we prefer our approach" or "this is beyond our scope" without justification.

### Revise The Manuscript And The Response Together

The response letter and the manuscript must stay synchronized. A point addressed in the letter must appear in the manuscript, and a manuscript change should be explained in the letter.

Synchronize:

- every promised change is actually in the revised manuscript;
- line numbers in the letter match the revised version;
- new text referenced in the letter is clearly marked;
- the response does not claim a change that reviewers cannot find;
- supplementary materials are updated alongside the main text.

### Handle Reviewer Disagreement Through The Editor

When reviewers give conflicting advice, do not simply pick the easier one. Acknowledge the conflict, explain your reasoning, and let the editor arbitrate. Propose a solution that respects both concerns where possible, and justify why you chose one direction.

### Maintain A Professional, Non-Defensive Tone

Reviewers volunteered their time. Hostility, sarcasm, or condescension in the response letter can sink an otherwise acceptable revision, because editors weigh author conduct. Disagree on the merits, thank reviewers for catching real problems, and never imply the reviewer is incompetent even when frustrated.

## Common Traps

### Agreeing To Everything Superficially

Saying yes to every comment without genuinely implementing the change produces a paper that looks revised but is not. Reviewers notice.

### Rebutting Defensively Without Evidence

Dismissing criticism as wrong without citations or data signals the authors did not engage. Rebuttals need support.

### Ignoring A Comment Hoping It Will Not Matter

Every comment deserves a response, even a brief one explaining why no change was made. Silence reads as evasion.

### Making Reviewers Hunt For Changes

Vague statements like "we revised the discussion" without location force reviewers to re-read the whole paper. Point to exact lines.

### Over-Revising Beyond What Was Asked

Adding unrequested major changes can introduce new problems and confuse reviewers. Keep changes focused unless the editor invited broader revision.

### Treating The Editor As A Rubber Stamp

The editor makes the final decision. Address the editor's specific concerns prominently, since the editor may not share every reviewer's view.

### Losing Patience After Multiple Rounds

Fatigue leads to curt responses and sloppy revisions in later rounds. Maintain rigor and courtesy throughout, because later rounds are often decisive.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have you read all reviewer and editor comments in full before drafting responses?
- [ ] Is every distinct comment classified as accept, partial, or rebut with a plan?
- [ ] Does the response letter quote each comment and point to exact revised locations?
- [ ] Are substantive requests (analysis, design, claims) distinguished from cosmetic ones?
- [ ] Is every rebuttal backed by evidence, citations, or scope justification rather than opinion?
- [ ] Do the response letter and revised manuscript stay synchronized with matching line numbers?
- [ ] Are conflicting reviewer comments acknowledged and routed through the editor?
- [ ] Is the tone professional and non-defensive throughout?
- [ ] Are new analyses or data requested by reviewers actually included?
- [ ] Does the opening overview summarize the major changes for the editor?
