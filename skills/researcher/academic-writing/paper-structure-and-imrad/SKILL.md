# Paper Structure And IMRaD

A research paper is an argument supported by evidence, not a chronological log of work performed. The IMRaD structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion) exists to let a reader audit that argument efficiently: what was asked, how it was answered, what was found, and what it means. Agents often treat sections as containers to fill rather than as load-bearing parts of a logical chain, which produces papers where the introduction promises more than the methods can deliver, the results report work unrelated to the question, and the discussion overreaches the evidence.

The harm this skill prevents is a paper that reads as competent prose but fails peer review because its internal logic is broken. The agent has substantial freedom in phrasing and emphasis, but the structural contract between sections must hold.

## Core Rules

### Make The Introduction End With The Gap And The Question

The introduction should narrow from context to contribution. A strong introduction moves through the importance of the problem, what is already known, where knowledge stops, and the specific question or objective this paper addresses.

Clarify in the introduction:

- the problem and why it matters;
- the relevant prior work, not a exhaustive history;
- the specific gap, tension, or unanswered question;
- the aim, objective, or hypothesis stated explicitly;
- a brief preview of the approach and what is new;
- the contribution the paper claims to make.

Do not bury the research question. A reader should be able to state the paper's question after reading the introduction.

### Let Methods Enable Replication And Audit

Methods must be detailed enough that a competent peer could repeat the study or judge whether the design can support the claims. Vague methods are the most common reason for rejection.

Specify in methods:

- design type and rationale;
- participants, sample, or corpus and how they were obtained;
- inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- variables, measures, instruments, and their sources;
- procedures and timeline;
- analysis plan, software, and parameters;
- assumptions and how they were checked;
- preregistration or protocol reference if applicable.

Report what was actually done, not an idealized protocol. Deviations from the plan should be transparent, not hidden.

### Report Results Without Interpretation

Results should present findings neutrally. The discussion is where meaning is made. Mixing interpretation into results blurs what was observed versus what was inferred.

In results:

- answer the research question directly with the primary finding first;
- report effect sizes and confidence intervals, not just p-values;
- describe negative and null results, not only supportive ones;
- use tables and figures that stand alone with captions;
- avoid restating numbers already in tables;
- keep speculative language out.

The reader should be able to extract the raw findings without the author's spin.

### Build The Discussion As A Controlled Climb

The discussion should return to the question, interpret findings in light of prior work, acknowledge limits, and state implications. It should not introduce new results.

Structure the discussion:

- summarize the key finding without repeating numbers verbatim;
- compare with prior evidence and explain agreement or conflict;
- offer mechanisms or explanations, clearly labeled as such;
- state limitations honestly, including design, sample, and measurement;
- describe theoretical and practical implications;
- suggest future work that follows from the findings, not generic calls for more research.

Each claim in the discussion must trace back to evidence in the results or cited literature.

### Keep The Abstract As A Faithful Compressed Map

The abstract is often the only part read. It must accurately compress the whole paper, not oversell it.

Include in the abstract:

- background and gap in one or two sentences;
- the question or objective;
- key methods in brief;
- primary results with magnitude;
- main conclusion and implication.

Do not include claims in the abstract that the body does not support. Match the abstract to the final version of the paper, not an early draft.

### Align Title, Abstract, And Body

The title sets an expectation the whole paper must meet. A title that overstates scope or novelty creates a credibility gap before the reader reaches the methods.

Check alignment:

- the title names the phenomenon, population, and design where possible;
- the abstract delivers on the title;
- each section serves the promise made by the title and introduction;
- keywords reflect the actual content.

If the title and body diverge, change one of them rather than hoping reviewers will not notice.

### Front-load The Logic, Back-Load The Detail

Readers skim. Place the most important sentence of each paragraph first, then support it. Avoid burying the key finding in the middle of a dense paragraph.

Apply this to:

- topic sentences of paragraphs;
- the first sentence of the results section;
- the opening of the discussion;
- figure captions, which should state the takeaway, not just describe content.

A reader scanning topic sentences should reconstruct the paper's argument.

### Respect Discipline Conventions While Serving Logic

Fields vary. Some use IMRaD strictly; others allow merged results and discussion, separate limitations sections, or structured abstracts. Follow the target venue's format, but never let convention override logical integrity. A merged Results and Discussion section still needs a clear boundary between fact and interpretation.

## Common Traps

### Writing The Paper In Chronological Order

Recounting "first we did this, then we did that" produces a diary, not an argument. Organize by logical contribution, not by the order work happened.

### Overstating In The Introduction To Seem Important

A grand framing that the methods cannot support invites rejection. Calibrate the claim to the evidence the paper actually delivers.

### Hiding Limitations To Appear Stronger

Reviewers find weaknesses regardless. Stating limitations proactively demonstrates judgment and often prevents harsher criticism. Hidden limits read as either naïveté or dishonesty.

### Duplicating Numbers Across Text, Tables, And Figures

Restating every table value in prose wastes space and risks inconsistency. Reference tables for detail and highlight only the key numbers in text.

### Letting The Discussion Drift Into New Analysis

Introducing a post hoc subgroup or a new dataset in the discussion breaks the structure. Move new analysis to results or save it for a future paper.

### Treating The Abstract As Marketing

Inflated abstracts that promise more than the paper delivers erode trust and can be flagged as misleading. Keep claims proportional.

### Ignoring The Reader's Need To Skim

Dense unstructured paragraphs with buried findings make the paper hard to review and hard to cite. Structure aids discovery.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does the introduction end with an explicit research question, aim, or hypothesis?
- [ ] Could a competent peer replicate the study from the methods section alone?
- [ ] Are results reported neutrally without premature interpretation?
- [ ] Does every discussion claim trace back to evidence in results or cited work?
- [ ] Are limitations stated honestly and specifically?
- [ ] Does the abstract accurately compress the final paper without overstating?
- [ ] Do title, abstract, and body align in scope and claims?
- [ ] Are key findings front-loaded in paragraphs, sections, and captions?
- [ ] Are negative or null results reported, not only supportive ones?
- [ ] Does the structure follow the target venue's conventions without sacrificing logic?
