# Literature Citation And Referencing

Citation is not decoration. Each reference is a link in an evidentiary chain: it credits prior work, locates the contribution within a field, and lets readers verify claims. Agents often treat referencing as a formatting chore and miss its substantive role, producing papers that overcite irrelevant work, undercite foundational work, cite without reading the source, or misattribute ideas. Poor citation practice can trigger plagiarism findings, retraction, or loss of credibility even when the underlying research is sound.

The harm this skill prevents is both ethical and intellectual: stolen credit, unverifiable claims, and a literature that cannot trace its own development. The agent has freedom in which sources to use, but the integrity rules around attribution and accuracy are strict.

## Core Rules

### Cite The Source You Actually Used

Track whether you read the primary source or a secondary summary. Citing a claim through a paper you did not read misattributes it and can propagate errors. If you only saw a result described in a review, cite both or cite the review honestly as your source.

For each citation:

- confirm you read the cited work or know its content;
- verify the claim actually appears where the citation points;
- prefer the primary source for specific findings;
- use a secondary source only when the primary is inaccessible, and label it as such.

### Cite To Support Claims, Not To Pad

Every citation should do work. A claim of fact, a methodological choice, a theoretical position, or a comparison to prior work earns a citation. A generic "many studies have shown" with a long citation string often signals the author has not engaged with the individual studies.

Evaluate each reference:

- does it directly support the sentence it attaches to?
- is it the best available source for that claim?
- is it current enough, or has the field moved?
- is it foundational, representative, or a counterexample?

Remove citations that exist only to appease potential reviewers unless they genuinely fit.

### Represent Cited Work Accurately

Misrepresenting a source, even favorably, is a form of academic dishonesty. A citation that distorts what the original authors found or argued undermines the entire paper's credibility.

When summarizing cited work:

- distinguish what authors claimed from what they demonstrated;
- preserve their caveats and limitations;
- do not inflate a small finding into a landmark;
- do not cite a study to criticize a position it did not hold.

### Cover Foundational, Recent, And Diverse Work

A balanced reference set shows command of the field. Foundational work establishes lineage; recent work shows currency; diverse work avoids an insular citation network that excludes relevant voices.

Aim to include:

- seminal papers that defined the question or method;
- recent reviews or empirical work showing current state;
- work from different research groups and geographies;
- relevant work in adjacent fields where appropriate;
- dissenting or competing perspectives, not only confirming ones.

### Follow The Target Style Consistently

Citation styles (APA, MLA, Chicago, Vancouver, Harvard, IEEE) differ in order, punctuation, and what elements are required. Inconsistency signals carelessness and slows review.

Manage style by:

- identifying the venue's required style before drafting;
- using a citation manager to enforce consistency;
- checking in-text citations match the reference list one-to-one;
- ensuring DOIs, volume, issue, pages, and years are complete;
- formatting retractions and preprints per current guidance.

### Manage References Reproducibly

Reference lists decay. A DOI that resolves, a citation manager file, or a supplementary bibliography makes the work auditable over time.

Practice:

- store references in a manager like Zotero, EndNote, or Mendeley;
- attach PDFs and notes to each entry;
- keep a versioned bibliography file for the project;
- include DOIs or stable URLs;
- archive the final reference list with the manuscript.

### Avoid Plagiarism And Self-Plagiarism

Plagiarism is presenting another's words, ideas, data, or structure without attribution. Self-plagiarism is reusing your own prior text without citation or permission, which can still violate copyright and publication ethics.

Guard against:

- verbatim copying without quotation marks and citation;
- paraphrasing that follows the original structure too closely;
- reusing methods text verbatim across papers without attribution;
- republishing figures or data without permission or credit;
- salami-slicing one study into many papers without cross-citation.

When reusing your own prior wording, cite it and check the publisher's policy on prior publication.

### Verify References Before Submission

Reference errors are common and embarrassing. A wrong author, year, or volume makes the citation useless and signals sloppiness.

Before submission:

- cross-check every in-text citation against the reference list;
- confirm each entry has complete metadata;
- verify DOIs resolve;
- check that no cited paper has been retracted;
- confirm spelling of author names and journal titles.

## Common Traps

### Citing Without Reading The Source

Relying on how others described a paper propagates distortion. Read the original for any load-bearing citation.

### Citation Cartels And Coercive Citation

Citing reviewers' or friends' work purely to gain favor, or being pressured to cite irrelevant work, corrupts the record. Cite only what is genuinely relevant.

### Inflating Citation Strings To Seem Thorough

Long lists of tangential references do not strengthen a claim; they obscure it. Curate to the most relevant sources.

### Ignoring Retracted Or Superseded Work

Citing a retracted paper as current evidence misleads readers. Check the status of key citations, especially older ones.

### Over-Reliance On Review Articles

Reviews are useful entry points, but citing only reviews means the paper rests on summaries, not primary evidence. Trace to primary studies for specific claims.

### Losing In-Text And Reference List Synchronization

Adding or deleting text often leaves orphan citations or dangling references. Reconcile the two lists in a final pass.

### Treating Citation As A Formatting Afterthought

Reference work done at the end under time pressure produces errors and omissions. Maintain the bibliography throughout drafting.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does every citation directly support the claim in the sentence it attaches to?
- [ ] Have you read or reliably know the content of each cited source?
- [ ] Is cited work represented accurately, with its caveats preserved?
- [ ] Does the reference set include foundational, recent, and diverse sources?
- [ ] Is the citation style applied consistently and matching the venue requirement?
- [ ] Do in-text citations and the reference list correspond exactly?
- [ ] Are DOIs included and verified to resolve?
- [ ] Is there any verbatim or near-verbatim reuse of others' or your own text without attribution?
- [ ] Have you checked that no cited work has been retracted?
- [ ] Is the bibliography stored in a citation manager or reproducible format?
