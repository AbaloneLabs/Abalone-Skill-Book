# Sequential And Concurrent Design

The timing of qualitative and quantitative strands is a defining design decision in mixed-methods research, and it is too often made by convenience rather than logic. In a concurrent design, both strands run at the same time and are integrated at the interpretation stage; in a sequential design, one strand informs the other, so the second strand depends on the results of the first. These are not interchangeable. An explanatory sequential design uses qualitative data to explain quantitative results, so the qualitative sample must be drawn from the quantitative findings; an exploratory sequential design uses qualitative data to build an instrument or theory that the quantitative strand then tests, so the qualitative must come first and shape the quantitative. Agents frequently pick a design because it sounds balanced, then run the strands in parallel and call it integration. The judgment problem is to choose the timing whose logic matches the research question, and to recognize that timing constrains sampling, integration, and the entire inferential structure of the study.

Use this skill when choosing between sequential and concurrent mixed-methods designs. The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing timing by convenience, from breaking the dependency logic that defines sequential designs, and from claiming a kind of integration the timing cannot deliver. The agent has freedom in strand priority and order, but the timing-integration-claim alignment must be deliberate.

## Core Rules

### Match The Timing To The Research Question

Sequential and concurrent designs answer structurally different questions. The timing must follow the question, not the calendar.

Match by:

- concurrent (convergent) when the question compares or confirms two views of the same phenomenon at once;
- explanatory sequential when the question asks why quantitative results came out as they did;
- exploratory sequential when the question needs qualitative insight to build a measure or theory to then test;
- embedded when one strand supports the other within a larger design.

A design whose timing does not match the question produces strands that cannot speak to each other and an integration that cannot occur.

### Respect The Dependency Logic Of Sequential Designs

In sequential designs, the second strand depends on the first. Breaking that dependency destroys the design's rationale.

Respect by:

- explanatory sequential: using quantitative results to drive qualitative sampling and questions;
- exploratory sequential: using qualitative findings to build the quantitative instrument or model;
- letting the first strand's results genuinely shape the second, not just following a preset plan;
- documenting how first-strand results informed second-strand decisions.

A sequential design that runs the second strand to a predetermined script, ignoring the first strand's results, is two parallel studies mislabeled as sequential.

### Decide Priority Deliberately And Transparently

The relative weight of the strands is a design decision with analytic consequences. It must be stated, not hidden.

Decide by:

- equal status versus one strand dominant;
- how priority affects integration, sampling, and claims;
- the rationale for the chosen priority;
- consistency between stated priority and the resources and reporting given to each strand.

A study claiming equal status that devotes ten pages to quantitative results and one paragraph to qualitative has not matched its claim to its design.

### Plan The Point Of Integration Around The Timing

Integration happens at a specific point, and the possible points depend on the timing. Concurrent designs integrate at interpretation; sequential designs integrate at the connection between strands.

Plan by:

- concurrent: integration at the interpretation or data level, merging results;
- explanatory sequential: integration when qualitative explains quantitative results;
- exploratory sequential: integration when qualitative findings shape the quantitative instrument;
- embedded: integration where the nested strand supports the primary.

If the timing and the integration point are mismatched, the strands will not connect and the study will report two separate sets of findings.

### Align Sampling Across Strands

In mixed-methods, the samples of the two strands must relate to each other in a way the timing supports.

Align by:

- concurrent: same or parallel samples for comparison;
- explanatory sequential: qualitative sample drawn from quantitative participants to explain their results;
- exploratory sequential: qualitative sample that can inform the quantitative instrument for the target population;
- embedded: nested sample that supports the primary strand.

A survey of one population and interviews with an unrelated group produce strands that cannot integrate because they do not address the same phenomenon.

### Manage The Tradeoff Between Depth And Breadth Realistically

Mixed methods promise both depth and breadth, but resources force tradeoffs that the timing can worsen.

Manage by:

- realistic allocation of effort to each strand given the timing;
- not letting one strand become tokenistic;
- acknowledging where one strand is exploratory or limited;
- the risk that sequential designs stretch timelines and risk attrition between strands.

A study that attempts full rigor in both strands with insufficient resources often produces two weak strands. The allocation must be realistic and acknowledged.

### Handle Paradigm Tension Explicitly

Qualitative and quantitative strands often rest on different paradigm assumptions, and the timing does not resolve this.

Handle by:

- the pragmatic stance that justifies combining paradigms;
- acknowledging the tension rather than hiding it;
- ensuring claims respect the assumptions of each strand;
- transparency about the philosophical stance of the study.

Pretending paradigm differences do not exist hides real tensions that affect how the strands can be integrated and interpreted.

### Anticipate Timing Risks And Plan Contingencies

Sequential designs carry risks that concurrent designs do not, especially when the second strand depends on the first.

Anticipate by:

- what happens if the first strand's results are inconclusive or surprising;
- timeline risk if the second strand cannot start until the first is analyzed;
- attrition between strands in longitudinal sequential designs;
- contingency plans for when the planned dependency does not materialize.

A sequential design with no contingency for a null or surprising first strand can stall the entire study.

## Common Traps

### Timing Chosen By Convenience

Picking concurrent or sequential because of the calendar rather than the question produces strands that cannot integrate.

### Broken Dependency Logic

Running the second strand to a preset script, ignoring the first strand's results, destroys the sequential rationale.

### Mismatched Priority And Reporting

Claiming equal status while giving one strand tokenistic attention contradicts the stated design.

### Integration Point Mismatched To Timing

Planning integration at a point the timing cannot support guarantees separate, non-integrated findings.

### Unrelated Samples Across Strands

Surveying one population and interviewing another produces strands that cannot address the same phenomenon.

### Tokenistic Secondary Strand

Including a brief secondary strand to claim mixed methods, without a real analytic role, wastes the combination.

### No Contingency For Sequential Risks

A sequential design with no plan for null or surprising first-strand results can stall the whole study.

## Self-Check

- Is the timing (concurrent, explanatory sequential, exploratory sequential, embedded) matched to the research question?
- In sequential designs, does the second strand genuinely depend on and respond to the first strand's results?
- Is strand priority decided deliberately and reported transparently, consistent with the resources given to each?
- Is the point of integration planned at a location the timing can actually support?
- Are the samples of the two strands related in a way the timing licenses?
- Is the depth-versus-breadth tradeoff managed realistically without a tokenistic strand?
- Are paradigm differences between strands acknowledged and handled rather than hidden?
- Are contingencies planned for when sequential dependencies do not materialize?
- Is the timeline realistic for the chosen timing, including attrition risk between strands?
- Does the timing support the kind of integration and inference the study claims?
