# Systematic Review Protocol And Eligibility

A systematic review is defined by decisions made before evidence is seen. The protocol is what makes the review systematic rather than a literature essay with extra steps. Once results are visible, every loosening of a criterion, addition of an outcome, or redefinition of a subgroup can be tuned toward a preferred conclusion, and the reader has no way to detect it. Agents often reach for systematic-review language because it carries authority, then implement only the parts that are easy, leaving the discipline behind. A review without a fixed protocol is not systematic; it is a narrative review wearing a costume.

Use this skill when drafting a protocol, defining the question and eligibility, selecting outcomes, registering the review, or deciding what must be fixed in advance to keep the synthesis honest. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a review whose methods were retrofitted to its results.

## Core Rules

### Frame The Question With A Structured Tool

A systematic review question must be specific enough to govern eligibility and analysis. Unstructured questions drift under pressure from the data.

Use a question framework matched to the review type:

- PICO or PECO for intervention or exposure questions (population, intervention, comparator, outcome);
- PCC for scoping reviews (population, concept, context);
- SPIDER or PICo for qualitative questions;
- SPICE or ECLIPSE for policy or evaluation questions.

Define each element concretely. Vague population or outcome definitions let the reviewer re-scope after seeing results.

### Specify Eligibility Criteria Operationalized Enough To Apply

Criteria must be precise enough that two screeners applying them reach the same decision. "Relevant studies" is not an eligibility criterion.

Operationalize:

- population characteristics, including subgroups to be analyzed separately;
- intervention or exposure definitions and comparators;
- outcome list with primary versus secondary status and timing;
- study designs eligible and excluded, with rationale;
- publication status, language, and date limits with justification;
- minimum sample or data requirements.

Pre-specify how to handle studies that partially match, multi-arm studies, and overlapping samples.

### Pre-Specify Outcomes And Their Priority

Which outcomes are primary, secondary, and exploratory changes the interpretation of the review. Selecting outcomes after seeing data inflates the chance of a notable finding.

For each outcome define:

- primary versus secondary status;
- the construct and how it is measured;
- time points of interest;
- how multiple measures of the same outcome will be handled;
- whether outcomes are patient-important or surrogate.

Do not promote a secondary outcome to primary because its result was more favorable.

### Register Or Time-Stamp The Protocol

A protocol that exists only in the reviewer's head cannot constrain anything. Registration makes the prior plan verifiable.

Register where appropriate:

- PROSPERO for health systematic reviews;
- OSF or institutional registries for other fields;
- an a-priori published protocol.

Record the version and date. Any deviation from the registered plan must be documented with a rationale, not silently absorbed.

### Plan For Deviations Transparently

Real reviews deviate from protocol for legitimate reasons: a database changed its interface, an outcome was inconsistently reported, a subgroup was unanalyzable. Transparency, not perfection, is the standard.

For each deviation document:

- what changed;
- when it changed relative to seeing data;
- why it changed;
- the impact on conclusions.

Silent deviation converts a systematic review into a post-hoc narrative. Documented deviation preserves credibility.

### Define The Synthesis Method In Advance

Whether the review will pool quantitatively, synthesize narratively, or do both should be decided before results are known, because the choice interacts with heterogeneity and outcome availability.

Pre-specify:

- whether meta-analysis is anticipated and under what conditions;
- the narrative synthesis framework if meta-analysis is not possible;
- how heterogeneity will be assessed and interpreted;
- the unit of analysis and handling of multi-arm studies;
- subgroup and sensitivity analyses planned a priori.

Do not decide to meta-analyze only the favorable subset after seeing the forest plot.

### Plan Risk-Of-Bias And Certainty Assessment

Quality appraisal is part of the protocol, not an afterthought added when results look weak.

Pre-specify:

- the risk-of-bias tool matched to the design (RoB 2, ROBINS-I, QUADAS, JBI tools);
- who assesses and how disagreements resolve;
- how risk of bias feeds into synthesis and conclusions;
- the certainty-of-evidence approach (GRADE or equivalent);
- whether and how to downgrade for inconsistency, indirectness, or imprecision.

### Plan Subgroup And Sensitivity Analyses A Priori

Subgroup analyses are a major source of false positives when chosen post hoc. Pre-specify which subgroups matter and why.

Specify:

- which subgroups are hypothesized, with a rationale;
- whether subgroup analysis is exploratory or confirmatory;
- sensitivity analyses for risk of bias, missing data, and outliers;
- the threshold for treating a subgroup finding as reliable.

A subgroup result found after seeing data should be labeled exploratory, not conclusive.

### Address Reporting And Publication Bias In The Plan

The protocol should anticipate that the available literature is a biased sample of conducted research.

Plan to:

- search grey literature and registries for unpublished studies;
- assess small-study effects and funnel plot asymmetry where possible;
- consider the direction of likely publication bias;
- interpret pooled effects in light of possible suppression of nulls.

## Common Traps

### Calling A Selective Review Systematic

If the protocol, comprehensive search, and dual screening are absent, the review is not systematic. The label imposes obligations the method must meet.

### Vague Eligibility That Drifts

"Studies about X" lets the reviewer include agreeable studies and exclude inconvenient ones with no auditable rule.

### Outcome Selection After Seeing Results

Promoting a favorable secondary outcome to primary, or adding an outcome that showed an effect, is a form of p-hacking at the review level.

### Silent Protocol Deviations

Changing criteria, outcomes, or analyses without recording the change erases the protection the protocol was meant to provide.

### Post-Hoc Subgroup Fishing

Finding a subgroup effect after seeing the data and presenting it as confirmatory inflates false positives and misleads readers.

### Skipping Risk-Of-Bias In The Protocol

Treating quality appraisal as optional or adding it only to weaken disliked studies is inconsistent and undermines the synthesis.

### Ignoring Publication Bias In Planning

A plan that does not address suppressed nulls will over-credit a literature dominated by positive findings.

## Self-Check

- Is the question framed with a structured tool and each element defined concretely?
- Are eligibility criteria operationalized enough for two screeners to apply consistently?
- Are primary and secondary outcomes pre-specified with timing and measurement detail?
- Is the protocol registered or time-stamped, with version and date recorded?
- Are any deviations from protocol documented with timing, reason, and impact?
- Is the synthesis method (meta-analysis, narrative, or both) planned before results are seen?
- Are risk-of-bias and certainty-of-evidence tools selected and integrated into the plan?
- Are subgroup and sensitivity analyses pre-specified and labeled exploratory where appropriate?
- Does the protocol address publication and reporting bias?
- Could a reader verify that the methods preceded, rather than followed, the results?
