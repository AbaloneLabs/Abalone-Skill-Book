# Survey Methodology And Questionnaire Design

A survey looks deceptively simple: write questions, collect answers, report percentages. This simplicity is the source of most survey failure. Badly worded items measure something other than the construct, biased samples produce answers that do not represent the target population, low response rates magnify nonresponse bias, and the chosen mode shapes who answers and how. Researchers often treat a survey as a quick method and discover too late that the responses cannot support the claim.

Use this skill when designing a questionnaire, choosing a mode and sample, writing and ordering items, pretesting, and interpreting results. The goal is to keep the agent from writing leading or ambiguous questions, from ignoring coverage and nonresponse bias, and from reporting population estimates from convenience samples. The agent has latitude in topic and instrument, but must defend sampling, question quality, and bias adjustment for the specific inference.

## Core Rules

### Define The Target Population And The Sampling Frame

A survey estimates something about a defined population. Without a clear population and a frame that reaches it, the answers cannot be generalized.

Specify:

- the target population, with boundaries in time, place, and membership;
- the sampling frame, such as a list, registry, or platform;
- coverage error, who is missing from the frame;
- the sampling design, including probability versus non-probability methods;
- the planned sample size and its rationale;
- expected response rate and nonresponse;
- whether weights or adjustments will be needed.

A sample drawn from a frame that excludes part of the population produces systematically biased estimates, regardless of size.

### Write Items That Measure The Intended Construct

Question wording is where surveys gain or lose validity. Small changes in phrasing produce large changes in answers.

For each item, check:

- whether it measures the construct or a related but different one;
- clarity and absence of ambiguity;
- neutrality and absence of leading or loaded language;
- appropriateness of the assumed knowledge;
- cultural and linguistic appropriateness;
- sensitivity of the topic and respondent comfort;
- single versus double-barreled structure;
- avoidance of assumptions in the question;
- availability of a "don't know" or refusal option where appropriate.

Every item is a measurement instrument. Treat wording as a design decision, not a draft.

### Choose Response Scales Deliberately

The response format shapes the data. Categories, scale points, labels, and order all influence answers.

Decide:

- number of scale points and whether a midpoint is included;
- labels for each point versus only the endpoints;
- balanced versus unbalanced scales;
- whether a neutral or "not applicable" option is offered;
- numeric versus verbal scales;
- order of categories and risk of order effects;
- whether the scale can be meaningfully compared across respondents;
- use of forced-choice versus allow-skip.

A seven-point scale is not inherently better than a five-point scale; the choice depends on the construct, the population, and the analysis.

### Order Items To Avoid Contamination

Earlier questions change how later questions are interpreted and answered. Order effects are real and often large.

Plan:

- easy, non-threatening items early to build engagement;
- sensitive items later once rapport is established;
- outcome questions before explanatory questions to avoid priming;
- randomize or rotate where order effects are expected;
- separate related items that could cue each other;
- place demographic items where they do the least harm, often at the end;
- test order effects in pretesting.

The questionnaire is a conversation. Its flow shapes the answers as much as the wording.

### Select A Mode That Fits The Population And Content

Mode affects coverage, response, data quality, and the kinds of bias that operate.

Consider:

- web, mail, telephone, face-to-face, or mixed mode;
- coverage of the population by the mode;
- literacy, technology access, and disability;
- sensitivity of content and mode privacy;
- interviewer effects versus self-administration;
- cost, speed, and feasibility;
- mode effects on scale use and social desirability;
- consistency of measurement across modes if mixed.

A web-only survey of a population with limited internet access has coverage bias no amount of weighting can fully fix.

### Estimate And Adjust For Nonresponse

Nonresponse is inevitable. The danger is systematic nonresponse correlated with the variables of interest.

Plan for:

- tracking response rates overall and by subgroup;
- follow-up of nonrespondents;
- analysis of nonresponse patterns;
- post-stratification or weighting to known totals;
- propensity or model-based adjustments;
- sensitivity analysis for nonresponse bias;
- reporting of response rates and adjustment methods.

A high response rate does not eliminate nonresponse bias, and a low rate does not guarantee it. The pattern of nonresponse matters more than the rate.

### Pretest Cognitively And Behaviorally

A questionnaire that has not been pretested is a draft. Cognitive and field pretesting expose problems the designer cannot see.

Pretest for:

- comprehension of items and instructions;
- recall and the difficulty of answering;
- sensitivity and respondent burden;
- the meaning respondents attach to scale points;
- navigation and technical issues;
- timing and fatigue;
- social desirability and acquiescence;
- whether items discriminate among respondents.

Use think-alouds, cognitive interviews, pilot administration, and behavior coding. Revise based on evidence, not preference.

### Align Survey Quality To The Inference

Not every survey needs probability sampling and exhaustive adjustment. The required rigor depends on the claim.

Match quality to claim:

- descriptive population estimates demand probability sampling and bias adjustment;
- exploratory or internal comparisons tolerate weaker sampling if labeled;
- longitudinal comparisons demand consistent measurement over waves;
- cross-cultural comparisons demand measurement invariance;
- high-stakes decisions demand stronger modes and higher response efforts.

Do not over-engineer a quick internal poll, and do not under-engineer a population estimate dressed up as a survey.

## Common Traps

### Convenience Samples Reported As Population Estimates

Social media or classroom samples cannot support claims about a broader population regardless of size.

### Leading Or Ambiguous Question Wording

Small phrasing changes swing answers. Wording is a measurement decision, not a stylistic one.

### Ignoring Coverage Error

If the sampling frame excludes part of the population, the estimates are biased before anyone even refuses.

### Treating Response Rate As The Only Quality Indicator

A high response rate with systematic nonresponse still biases estimates. The pattern matters as much as the level.

### Skipping Pretesting

A questionnaire fielded without cognitive or pilot testing is a guess about how respondents will interpret it.

### Mode Mismatched To Population

A web survey of a population with limited internet access produces coverage bias that weighting cannot fully repair.

### Comparing Scales Across Groups Without Invariance Evidence

Mean differences across groups whose members use the scale differently are not real differences.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the target population defined, and does the sampling frame cover it without major gaps?
- [ ] Does each item measure the intended construct with clear, neutral, and culturally appropriate wording?
- [ ] Is the response scale chosen deliberately, with attention to points, labels, balance, and order effects?
- [ ] Is item order planned to avoid contamination, priming, and fatigue?
- [ ] Is the survey mode matched to the population's coverage, literacy, privacy, and content needs?
- [ ] Are response rates tracked by subgroup, and is nonresponse bias addressed with appropriate adjustments?
- [ ] Has the questionnaire been pretested cognitively and behaviorally, with revisions based on evidence?
- [ ] Is survey quality aligned to the strength of the intended inference?
- [ ] Are measurement invariance and mode effects considered for comparisons across groups or waves?
- [ ] Are limitations of sampling, coverage, nonresponse, and measurement reported honestly?
