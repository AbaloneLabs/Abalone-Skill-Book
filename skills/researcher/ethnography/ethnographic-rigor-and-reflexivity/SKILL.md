# Ethnographic Rigor And Reflexivity

Ethnographic rigor cannot be borrowed from quantitative methods. There is no sample size that guarantees credibility and no blinding that removes bias. Instead, ethnography builds trustworthiness through prolonged engagement, triangulation, attention to disconfirming evidence, and above all reflexivity, the disciplined examination of how the researcher's position, assumptions, and relationships shape what is seen and claimed. Agents often treat reflexivity as a confessional aside (a paragraph acknowledging the researcher's background) rather than an analytic practice that runs through the study. Equally common is the opposite failure: treating ethnography as purely subjective and therefore exempt from rigor, which abandons the obligation to produce defensible knowledge. The judgment problem is to establish credibility without pretending to objectivity, and to use reflexivity as a tool of analysis rather than a disclaimer.

Use this skill when establishing the trustworthiness of ethnographic work or managing the researcher's position in the field. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating reflexivity as decoration and from confusing the absence of quantitative controls with the absence of rigor.

## Core Rules

### Build Credibility Through Prolonged Engagement And Triangulation

Ethnographic credibility comes from depth of engagement and convergence across sources, not from sample size.

Build by:

- sustained time in the setting to learn routine practice;
- triangulation across observations, interviews, and documents;
- triangulation across times, events, and participants;
- seeking convergent and divergent evidence.

A finding that holds across multiple sources, times, and participants is more credible than one observed once. Triangulation tests findings rather than assuming them.

### Use Reflexivity As Analytic Practice, Not Confession

Reflexivity is the ongoing examination of how the researcher shapes the data. It is analysis, not autobiography.

Use by:

- reflexive memos throughout fieldwork and analysis;
- examining how the researcher's position affects access and trust;
- tracking how expectations steer observation and coding;
- using reflexivity to revise, not just to disclose.

A reflexive paragraph in the methods section that is never acted upon is decoration. Reflexivity that changes how data are collected and interpreted is rigor.

### Make Positionality And Its Consequences Explicit

The researcher's social position (gender, race, status, outsider or insider) shapes what members will show and tell.

Make explicit by:

- how position affects access to different members and activities;
- how members' perception of the researcher shapes disclosure;
- what the position makes easier and harder to see;
- how position shifted over the fieldwork.

An insider may see things an outsider cannot but may take them for granted; an outsider may see the obvious but miss subtlety. Neither position is neutral, and both have consequences that must be examined.

### Manage Power Asymmetries In The Field

Ethnography often involves power differences between researcher and participants, and among participants themselves.

Manage by:

- recognizing the researcher's power to represent and to publish;
- attending to how participants' relative power shapes whose voice dominates;
- avoiding extractive research that takes without giving back;
- reciprocity appropriate to the setting and relationship.

Power asymmetry does not invalidate ethnography, but ignoring it produces findings that reflect the powerful and research that exploits the vulnerable.

### Seek And Handle Disconfirming Evidence

Credible ethnography actively tests its emerging claims against evidence that might refute them.

Seek by:

- searching for cases that contradict the emerging pattern;
- attending to data that do not fit the developing analysis;
- revising claims in light of disconfirming evidence;
- reporting the boundaries of where claims hold.

A claim that survives an active search for disconfirmation is far more trustworthy than one asserted from confirming instances alone.

### Use Member Checking Where Appropriate And Honestly

Member checking asks participants to respond to emerging findings. It can strengthen credibility but is not a validity stamp.

Use by:

- sharing interpretations, not just raw data, with participants;
- treating disagreement as data, not as invalidation;
- recognizing that members may not agree with analytic framings;
- not using member agreement as proof of objectivity.

Members may disagree with an analysis because it makes visible what they take for granted, or because it is wrong. Disagreement is analytically informative, not disqualifying.

### Maintain An Audit Trail Of Decisions

Trustworthiness requires that others can follow how the researcher moved from fieldwork to findings.

Maintain by:

- a record of fieldwork decisions and access negotiations;
- memos tracing the development of themes and interpretations;
- decision rules for inclusion, exclusion, and emphasis;
- the chain of evidence from raw data to final claims.

An audit trail does not make ethnography objective, but it makes the interpretive process inspectable, which is what credibility requires.

### Acknowledge The Temporal And Situational Limits

Ethnographic findings are tied to a time, place, and set of conditions. They are not timeless universals.

Acknowledge by:

- the historical moment of the fieldwork;
- the specific conditions that shaped the setting;
- how findings might differ at another time or in another place;
- transferability rather than generalizability as the appropriate claim.

A setting studied during a crisis differs from the same setting in calm. Findings must be tied to their conditions and transferred with care.

## Common Traps

### Reflexivity As Confessional Aside

A one-time acknowledgment of position that never shapes data collection or analysis is decoration, not rigor.

### Ignoring Power Asymmetries

Failing to examine how researcher and participant power shapes findings produces accounts that reflect the powerful and exploit the vulnerable.

### No Search For Disconfirming Evidence

Asserting claims from confirming instances without testing them against contradiction produces fragile findings.

### Member Checking As Validity Stamp

Treating member agreement as proof of objectivity misuses the technique and ignores that disagreement is analytically informative.

### Generalizing Beyond The Setting

Extending ethnographic findings to other times, places, or populations without attention to transferability overstates the contribution.

### No Audit Trail

Findings presented without a traceable chain of evidence cannot be inspected or challenged, undermining credibility.

### Confusing Subjectivity With Exemption From Rigor

Treating ethnography as purely subjective and therefore beyond quality control abandons the obligation to produce defensible knowledge.

## Self-Check

- Is credibility built through prolonged engagement and triangulation across sources, times, and participants?
- Is reflexivity used as ongoing analytic practice that shapes data collection and interpretation?
- Is the researcher's positionality made explicit, with its consequences for access and insight examined?
- Are power asymmetries between researcher and participants, and among participants, acknowledged and managed?
- Is disconfirming evidence actively sought, with claims revised in light of it?
- Is member checking used honestly, with disagreement treated as data rather than invalidation?
- Is an audit trail maintained, tracing decisions and the chain of evidence from data to findings?
- Are the temporal and situational limits of the findings acknowledged?
- Are transferability claims bounded to similar settings rather than generalized universally?
- Is rigor established through trustworthiness rather than by importing inappropriate quantitative criteria?
