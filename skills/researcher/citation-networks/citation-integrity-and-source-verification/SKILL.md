# Citation Integrity And Source Verification

Citations are the load-bearing structure of a research argument, and they fail more often than authors admit. A claim attributed to a source the source does not make, a secondary citation presented as primary, a quotation stripped of its context, or a retracted paper cited as current all transfer false authority into an argument. Agents tend to trust citations as they find them, copying reference lists from other papers and inheriting their errors. Citation integrity is the discipline of treating every reference as a claim to be checked, not a fact to be trusted.

Use this skill when verifying that sources support their attached claims, auditing a reference list, handling retractions or corrections, or deciding whether to cite a primary or secondary source. The goal is to prevent the agent from propagating citation errors and from lending authority to sources that do not earn it.

## Core Rules

### Verify Each Citation Supports Its Specific Claim

A citation next to a sentence asserts that the source supports that sentence. This must be checked at the level of the specific claim, not the general topic.

For each load-bearing citation verify:

- the source actually states or supports the specific claim;
- the relevant page, section, table, or figure is cited, not just the whole work;
- the claim is not stronger than what the source found;
- the source is being represented accurately, not paraphrased into reversal.

A source that is topically relevant but does not make the attached claim is a citation error.

### Read Primary Sources For Central Claims

For claims that anchor an argument, a secondary citation is insufficient. The original source must be read.

Cite primary when:

- the claim is central to the argument;
- a precise result, definition, or quotation is involved;
- the secondary source may have simplified or distorted the original;
- the original is accessible.

Secondary citations are acceptable for background or when the primary is unavailable, but should be labeled as such, not presented as direct reading.

### Distinguish And Label Secondary Citations

Citing a source you have not read, via a source that cited it, is a secondary citation. Presenting it as primary is misleading.

Handle by:

- using an "as cited in" construction where the style permits;
- noting in working notes that the original was not consulted;
- flagging the risk that the intermediary misrepresented the original;
- seeking the primary before publication for central claims.

Silent secondary citations hide that the author has not verified the foundation.

### Check For Context Stripping And Cherry-Picking

A quotation or finding removed from its context can reverse meaning. A single supportive sentence may sit inside a paper that reaches the opposite conclusion.

Check that:

- quotations include necessary qualifications;
- a cited finding is not contradicted elsewhere in the same source;
- the cited result is not exploratory being presented as confirmatory;
- the source's own limitations are not hidden by the citation.

### Handle Retractions, Corrections, And Expressions Of Concern

Citing a retracted paper as if it were valid propagates discredited work. The status of central sources must be checked.

Check and handle:

- whether the paper has been retracted, corrected, or flagged;
- if retracted, do not cite it as evidence; cite it only to discuss the retraction if relevant;
- if corrected, cite the corrected version and note the change;
- if under expression of concern, flag the uncertainty.

Retraction databases and publisher notices should be checked for foundational citations.

### Detect Inherited Citation Errors

Citations copied from other papers inherit their errors, including misattributions that can persist for decades. Do not assume a frequently repeated citation is correct.

Guard by:

- checking suspicious or surprising attributions against the original;
- being wary of citations to sources you cannot locate;
- noting when many papers cite a claim via the same intermediary;
- verifying foundational or counterintuitive claims directly.

An error repeated across many papers is still an error.

### Watch For Citation Misconduct Patterns

Some citation patterns signal dishonesty rather than carelessness, and an audit should be alert to them.

Watch for:

- excessive self-citation unrelated to relevance;
- citation cartels or mutual citation clusters;
- citations to the author's own prior work to inflate metrics;
- citations inserted at the request of editors or reviewers for coercive reasons;
- citations to sources that cannot plausibly support the claim.

### Maintain A Verifiable Reference Trail

Every citation should be traceable to where in the source the claim is supported. A reference list without this trace cannot be audited.

Maintain for each citation:

- the exact location (page, section, timestamp, table);
- a note or quotation of the supporting text;
- whether the source was primary or secondary;
- the version or edition consulted;
- the date accessed for online or changing sources.

This trail is what makes a reference list defensible rather than aspirational.

### Cite The Version That Was Actually Used

Sources have versions: preprints, published articles, corrected versions, editions, and updated datasets. Citing the wrong version can attach a claim to a text that no longer contains it.

Cite by:

- matching the citation to the version read;
- noting preprint versus published where results differ;
- updating citations when a corrected version appears;
- recording access dates for sources that change.

## Common Traps

### Trusting Citations As Found

Inheriting citations from other papers propagates their errors, including long-lived misattributions.

### Topical But Unsupported Citations

A source on the right topic that does not make the attached claim is still a citation error.

### Silent Secondary Citations

Citing an unread primary via an intermediary without labeling it hides that the foundation was not verified.

### Context-Stripped Quotations

A supportive sentence excised from a source reaching the opposite conclusion reverses meaning.

### Citing Retracted Work As Current

A retracted paper cited as evidence propagates discredited findings.

### Cherry-Picked Findings

Citing a result while hiding that the same source found the opposite elsewhere misleads.

### Unverifiable Reference Lists

Citations without location, version, or supporting text cannot be audited and erode trust.

## Self-Check

- Does each load-bearing citation support the specific claim, at the cited location, without overstatement?
- Are central claims cited to primary sources that were actually read?
- Are secondary citations labeled rather than presented as primary reading?
- Are quotations and findings checked against their original context for reversal or qualification?
- Have foundational citations been checked against retraction, correction, and expression-of-concern records?
- Are inherited or frequently repeated attributions verified against originals rather than trusted?
- Are misconduct patterns (excessive self-citation, cartels, coercive citations) considered in the audit?
- Does each citation have a verifiable trail of location, supporting text, version, and access date?
- Is the cited version the one actually consulted, with preprint and corrected versions distinguished?
- Would a reader checking the references find the claims supported, or only topically proximate?
