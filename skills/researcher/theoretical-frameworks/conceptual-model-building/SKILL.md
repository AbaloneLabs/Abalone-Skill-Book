# Conceptual Model Building

A conceptual model is the bridge between abstract theory and concrete study. It names the constructs, specifies how they relate, and shows what the study will actually examine. Agents often produce models that are either too vague to test (boxes and arrows with no direction or mechanism) or too rigid (importing a theory's full apparatus without adapting it to the phenomenon). A good model is precise enough to generate hypotheses or interpretive claims, but honest about what it assumes and what it leaves open. The model is where the study's logic becomes visible, and where its hidden assumptions become falsifiable.

Use this skill when constructing a conceptual model, defining constructs and relationships, drawing a model diagram, or translating theory into testable propositions. The goal is to prevent the agent from presenting a diagram that looks rigorous but carries no real logic.

## Core Rules

### Define Each Construct Concretely

A model is only as clear as its constructs. Vague constructs let the model mean anything and therefore nothing.

For each construct define:

- a clear conceptual definition;
- what it is and is not;
- how it differs from neighboring constructs;
- its level of analysis (individual, group, organization);
- how it will be operationalized or evidenced.

Constructs that overlap heavily or are defined circularly make the model untestable and the findings uninterpretable.

### Specify The Form And Direction Of Relationships

An arrow between two boxes asserts a relationship, but the form and direction matter. "A affects B" is underspecified.

Specify for each relationship:

- the direction (which causes or precedes which);
- the form (linear, curvilinear, threshold, feedback);
- the valence (positive, negative, conditional);
- the proposed mechanism linking them;
- whether the relationship is direct, mediated, or moderated.

Arrows without mechanisms are guesses; arrows with stated mechanisms are propositions.

### Distinguish Mediators, Moderators, And Confounders

These roles are conceptually distinct and conflating them mis-specifies the model and the analysis.

Clarify:

- a mediator explains how or why A affects B (the mechanism);
- a moderator changes the strength or direction of A's effect on B (a boundary condition);
- a confounder is a common cause of both that creates a spurious association;
- a control is included to reduce error or rule out alternatives.

Putting a moderator in a mediator's place, or vice versa, tests the wrong question.

### Make The Model's Scope Conditions Explicit

A model claims to apply under certain conditions. Stating them prevents overgeneralization and clarifies where the model should break.

State:

- the populations and settings the model targets;
- the time frame or conditions assumed;
- the contextual factors treated as fixed;
- where the model is expected not to hold.

A model presented as universally applicable usually has unstated, narrow scope.

### Derive Hypotheses Or Propositions From The Model

The model should generate specific claims that the study can test or interpret. If it does not, it is a picture, not a framework.

Derive by:

- translating each key relationship into a hypothesis (for quantitative work) or proposition (for qualitative);
- stating the expected direction and form;
- grounding each in the theory or prior evidence the model draws on;
- ensuring the hypotheses are falsifiable or the propositions are investigable.

A model that generates no specific claims cannot be confirmed or disconfirmed.

### Justify Each Path With Theory Or Evidence

Every arrow should have a rationale. Models assembled from intuition or convenience do not survive scrutiny.

Justify by:

- citing the theory or mechanism supporting each path;
- referencing prior empirical evidence where available;
- noting where a path is exploratory rather than grounded;
- being explicit when a relationship is hypothesized for the first time.

Unjustified paths read as a fishing expedition dressed as a model.

### Keep The Model Parsimonious Enough To Test

A model with dozens of constructs and paths is hard to test and hard to interpret. Parsimony is a virtue, not a limitation.

Balance by:

- including only constructs central to the question;
- treating peripheral factors as controls or boundary conditions;
- resisting the urge to diagram every conceivable influence;
- justifying each included element against the cost of complexity.

An untestable model, however complete, cannot contribute evidence.

### Show How The Model Connects To Methods

A conceptual model must be translatable into the study's design. A model that cannot be operationalized is aspirational.

Connect by:

- mapping each construct to a measure, observation, or coding category;
- mapping each relationship to an analysis or interpretive strategy;
- identifying where the data will confirm, refine, or challenge the model;
- noting where the model will be revised based on findings.

### Represent Feedback And Dynamics Where Relevant

Many phenomena are dynamic, with feedback loops, reciprocal causation, or change over time. Static models can misrepresent them.

Represent dynamics by:

- including reciprocal paths where causation is bidirectional;
- specifying time lags where effects unfold over time;
- noting feedback loops that create equilibrium or escalation;
- distinguishing cross-sectional from longitudinal implications.

A cross-sectional model of an inherently dynamic process is a known limitation, not an oversight to hide.

## Common Traps

### Boxes And Arrows Without Mechanisms

A diagram with no stated mechanisms or directions looks rigorous but carries no logic.

### Conflating Mediators, Moderators, And Confounders

Misassigning these roles tests the wrong question and misreads results.

### Unjustified Paths

Arrows added by intuition or convenience read as fishing dressed as theory.

### Overstuffed Models

Including every conceivable influence makes the model untestable and uninterpretable.

### Hidden Scope Conditions

Presenting a context-bound model as universal invites overgeneralization.

### Models That Generate No Testable Claims

A picture that yields no hypotheses or propositions is not a framework.

### Static Models Of Dynamic Phenomena

Ignoring feedback, reciprocity, and time misrepresents processes that unfold.

## Self-Check

- Does each construct have a clear, non-circular definition at a stated level of analysis?
- Are relationships specified by direction, form, valence, and mechanism, not just by arrows?
- Are mediators, moderators, confounders, and controls distinguished and correctly placed?
- Are the model's scope conditions (population, setting, time, context) stated explicitly?
- Does the model generate specific, falsifiable hypotheses or investigable propositions?
- Is each path justified by theory or prior evidence, with exploratory paths labeled?
- Is the model parsimonious enough to be tested or interpreted within the study?
- Does the model connect clearly to measures, observations, and analytic strategies?
- Are feedback, reciprocity, and time represented where the phenomenon is dynamic?
- Could a reader predict what the study will find or examine from the model alone?
