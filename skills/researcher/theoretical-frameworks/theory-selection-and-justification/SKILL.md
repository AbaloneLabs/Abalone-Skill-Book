# Theory Selection And Justification

A theoretical framework is not a citation garnish. It is the set of assumptions that determines what counts as a phenomenon, which variables matter, how they relate, and what a finding means. Choosing a theory is choosing the lens through which the entire study is interpreted. Agents often bolt a fashionable theory onto a study after the design is fixed, or cite a theory without using it, treating the framework section as a ritual rather than a load-bearing structure. A theory that does not actually guide the question, hypotheses, or interpretation is decoration, and its presence can mislead readers about what the study really assumes.

Use this skill when selecting, comparing, or justifying a theoretical framework, integrating theories, or deciding whether a study is genuinely theory-driven. The goal is to prevent the agent from using theory as ornamentation and from letting unexamined assumptions do the real interpretive work.

## Core Rules

### Let The Question Drive The Theory, Not The Reverse

The research question should determine which theory is relevant. Selecting a theory first and bending the question to fit it produces a study that serves the theory rather than the phenomenon.

Align by:

- identifying what kind of explanation the question demands (mechanism, structure, meaning, process);
- surveying theories that address that explanatory target;
- checking whether the theory's core constructs map onto the phenomenon of interest;
- rejecting theories whose central assumptions do not fit the context.

A theory that requires distorting the question to fit is the wrong theory.

### Make The Theory's Core Claims Explicit

A framework cannot guide a study if its assumptions are vague. The central claims and constructs must be stated concretely enough to generate predictions.

Specify:

- the theory's core constructs and their definitions;
- the proposed relationships among constructs;
- the scope conditions under which the theory claims to apply;
- the mechanisms it invokes;
- what the theory predicts and what it cannot explain.

Vague appeals to "social constructivism" or "systems theory" without stated mechanisms generate no testable claims.

### Justify Fit Between Theory And Phenomenon

A theory is appropriate only if its assumptions hold for the phenomenon and context studied. Fit must be argued, not assumed.

Argue fit by:

- checking the theory's scope conditions against the study context;
- verifying the theory's constructs are meaningful in that population or setting;
- considering whether the theory was developed in a very different domain;
- identifying where the phenomenon may exceed the theory's boundaries.

A theory built for organizations applied to individuals, or vice versa, may mis-specify the relevant constructs.

### Use The Theory To Generate Specific Predictions

A theory earns its place by generating hypotheses or interpretive frames that structure the study. If removing the theory changes nothing, it is not doing work.

Use the theory to:

- derive specific, falsifiable hypotheses where appropriate;
- define which variables or themes are in scope;
- predict the direction and form of relationships;
- guide the interpretation of expected and unexpected results.

A study whose hypotheses could have been written without the theory has not used it.

### Handle Competing Theories Deliberately

Most phenomena have multiple candidate theories. Pretending there is only one, or silently picking the convenient one, weakens the work.

When theories compete:

- name the main alternatives;
- explain what each predicts and where they diverge;
- design the study to distinguish between them where possible;
- justify the chosen theory over alternatives on fit, evidence, or scope.

A study that can adjudicate between rival theories is more valuable than one that assumes one.

### Be Cautious With Theory Integration

Combining theories is sometimes necessary but often done badly. Merged theories can contain contradictory assumptions that silently invalidate the synthesis.

When integrating:

- check that the theories' core assumptions are compatible;
- identify the integrating logic that binds them;
- avoid cherry-picking constructs while discarding the mechanisms that make them coherent;
- state explicitly what the integrated framework predicts that neither parent does.

An incoherent mash-up is worse than a single, honestly limited theory.

### Acknowledge When A Study Is A-Theoretical Or Descriptive

Not every study needs a grand theory. Descriptive, exploratory, or applied work can be valuable without one, and forcing a theory onto it is dishonest.

If a-theoretical:

- state that the study is exploratory or descriptive;
- avoid implying theoretical guidance that is absent;
- clarify what kind of contribution is offered instead (mapping, measurement, application);
- resist the pressure to cite a theory for legitimacy.

Honest description is better than a theory used as a costume.

### Make Metatheoretical Assumptions Visible

Every study rests on assumptions about the nature of reality, knowledge, and values, whether or not they are named. Hidden assumptions shape interpretation invisibly.

Surface assumptions about:

- ontology (what kind of entity the phenomenon is);
- epistemology (what counts as knowledge of it);
- the level of analysis (individual, relational, structural);
- the stance on causality and agency.

Naming these lets the reader judge whether the framework and methods are coherent.

### Match The Theory To The Methodological Approach

A theory's assumptions should be compatible with the methods used to study it. Mismatches produce incoherent designs.

Check compatibility:

- a theory of individual cognition fits experimental or cognitive methods;
- a theory of social construction fits interpretive or discourse methods;
- a structural theory fits network or multilevel methods;
- forcing a structural theory into individual-level data mis-specifies the test.

## Common Traps

### Theory As Citation Ornament

Citing a theory without using it to generate predictions or guide interpretation is decoration that misleads readers.

### Bending The Question To A Favorite Theory

Selecting a theory first and distorting the question to fit serves the theory, not the phenomenon.

### Vague Frameworks Without Mechanisms

Abstract appeals to broad schools generate no testable or interpretable claims.

### Ignoring Competing Theories

Silently choosing one theory among several avoids the contribution of adjudicating between them.

### Incoherent Theory Mash-Ups

Merging theories with contradictory assumptions produces a framework that explains nothing coherently.

### Forcing Theory Onto Descriptive Work

Implying theoretical guidance in an exploratory study is less honest than naming it as descriptive.

### Hidden Metatheoretical Assumptions

Unstated ontological and epistemological commitments shape interpretation invisibly and evade scrutiny.

## Self-Check

- Does the research question drive the theory choice, rather than the theory distorting the question?
- Are the theory's core constructs, relationships, mechanisms, and scope conditions stated explicitly?
- Is the fit between the theory's assumptions and the study's phenomenon and context argued?
- Does the theory generate specific predictions or interpretive frames that structure the study?
- Are competing theories named, and is the chosen one justified over alternatives?
- If theories are integrated, are their assumptions checked for compatibility and the integration logic stated?
- Is an a-theoretical or descriptive study honestly labeled rather than dressed in a theory?
- Are the metatheoretical assumptions (ontology, epistemology, level, causality) made visible?
- Is the theory compatible with the methodological approach used to study it?
- Would removing the theory change the study; if not, is it actually being used?
