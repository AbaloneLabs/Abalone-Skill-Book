# Theory Testing And Refinement

Testing a theory is not the same as finding a significant result. A theory makes coordinated predictions, and a genuine test examines whether the pattern of evidence fits those predictions, including the predictions that should not hold if the theory is wrong. Agents often treat a single confirmed hypothesis as support for an entire theory, ignore the predictions that failed, or retrofit a theory to whatever they found. Theory refinement is equally hazardous: revising a theory after every result can make it unfalsifiable, a moving target that explains everything and predicts nothing. The discipline is to test theories severely, report disconfirming evidence honestly, and refine only with justification.

Use this skill when designing a theory test, interpreting results against a framework, or revising a theory in light of evidence. The goal is to prevent the agent from offering weak confirmation and from turning theories into untestable stories.

## Core Rules

### Test Multiple Coordinated Predictions, Not One Hypothesis

A theory is a system of related claims. Confirming one hypothesis is weak evidence for the theory; the stronger test is whether the whole pattern of predictions holds.

Design tests that:

- examine several derived hypotheses simultaneously;
- include predictions that are distinctive to the theory (what only it would predict);
- check relationships that should be absent if the theory is wrong;
- look for the proposed mechanism, not just the outcome association.

A theory survives when its distinctive predictions hold and its competitors' fail, not when one correlation is significant.

### Include Strong Inference And Rival Hypotheses

A result consistent with a theory is often consistent with several theories. Strong inference designs the study to distinguish among them.

Apply strong inference by:

- identifying rival explanations for the predicted effect;
- deriving what each rival would predict differently;
- measuring or manipulating conditions that separate the theories;
- reporting which theory the pattern of results favors.

A study that cannot tell the theories apart provides only weak support for any of them.

### Take Disconfirmation Seriously

The most informative tests are those that could falsify the theory. Protecting a theory from disconfirmation makes the test worthless.

Take disconfirmation seriously by:

- specifying in advance what result would count against the theory;
- reporting failed predictions, not only confirmed ones;
- resisting the urge to explain away every null with a post-hoc qualifier;
- treating a failed distinctive prediction as a real challenge to the theory.

A theory that explains any possible result explains nothing.

### Distinguish Confirming The Theory From Confirming A Hypothesis

A confirmed hypothesis is evidence, but it does not validate the whole theory. The inferential leap must be proportionate.

Calibrate by:

- claiming support for specific propositions, not wholesale validation;
- noting which parts of the theory were and were not tested;
- acknowledging that auxiliary assumptions may carry the result;
- avoiding "the theory is proven" language.

Theories are supported cumulatively across many studies, not established by one.

### Avoid Ad Hoc Rescues Of Falsified Predictions

When a prediction fails, it is tempting to add a post-hoc modifier that explains the failure. Occasional refinement is legitimate; routine rescue makes the theory unfalsifiable.

Guard by:

- pre-specifying the conditions under which the theory should hold;
- treating post-hoc modifiers as hypotheses for future testing, not as fixes;
- counting how many rescues a theory has accumulated;
- preferring revision that narrows and clarifies scope over expansion that absorbs anomalies.

A theory that grows a new clause after every disconfirmation has stopped being predictive.

### Refine Theory With Justification, Not Convenience

Refinement is legitimate when evidence accumulates that the theory's scope or mechanism is wrong. But revision should be principled, not cosmetic.

Justify refinement by:

- showing the accumulated evidence that demands revision;
- stating precisely what changes and why;
- deriving new, distinctive predictions from the revised theory;
- ensuring the revision increases, not decreases, falsifiability.

Refinement that makes the theory vaguer or more flexible is regression, not progress.

### Test Boundary Conditions And Scope

A theory's claims hold within scope conditions. Testing where the theory breaks is as informative as testing where it holds.

Test boundaries by:

- studying populations or contexts at the edge of the theory's scope;
- examining moderators that should attenuate or reverse the effect;
- looking for contexts where the mechanism should not operate;
- reporting where the theory fails as a contribution to its refinement.

### Separate Theory Contribution From Empirical Contribution

A study can add empirical knowledge without advancing theory, and vice versa. Conflating them overclaims the contribution.

Clarify:

- empirical contribution: new data about a phenomenon;
- theoretical contribution: new or revised understanding of why or how;
- a study may offer one, both, or neither;
- claim theory contribution only when the framework itself is moved.

Labeling a descriptive finding as a theoretical advance overclaims and invites rejection.

### Use Replication To Build Theoretical Confidence

Single studies are noisy. Confidence in a theory builds through independent replication, especially of its distinctive predictions.

Value:

- direct replications of key tests;
- conceptual replications with different operationalizations;
- replications across populations and contexts;
- meta-analytic synthesis of the cumulative evidence.

A theory resting on one lab or one dataset is fragile regardless of the p-values.

## Common Traps

### One Confirmed Hypothesis Treated As Theory Validation

A single significant result is weak evidence; theories are systems of coordinated claims.

### Ignoring Rival Explanations

A result consistent with several theories supports none of them strongly.

### Explaining Away Every Null

Post-hoc qualifiers that rescue every disconfirmation make the theory unfalsifiable.

### Wholesale Validation Claims

"The theory is proven" overstates what any single study can establish.

### Cosmetic Refinement

Revisions that make the theory vaguer or more flexible reduce its predictive power.

### Untested Boundary Conditions

Claiming universality without testing where the theory breaks overgeneralizes.

### Conflating Empirical With Theoretical Contribution

New data is not the same as new understanding; claiming both overstates the work.

## Self-Check

- Does the test examine multiple coordinated predictions, including ones distinctive to the theory?
- Are rival hypotheses identified, and does the design distinguish among them?
- Is there a pre-specified result that would count against the theory?
- Are failed predictions reported and engaged, not hidden or explained away?
- Is the claim proportionate, supporting specific propositions rather than wholesale validation?
- Are post-hoc modifiers treated as future hypotheses, not as immediate rescues?
- Is any theory refinement justified by accumulated evidence and made more falsifiable?
- Are boundary conditions and scope tested, with failures reported as informative?
- Is the contribution honestly labeled as empirical, theoretical, or both?
- Does confidence in the theory rest on replication and synthesis, not a single study?
