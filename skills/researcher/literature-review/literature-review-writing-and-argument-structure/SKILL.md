# Literature Review Writing And Argument Structure

A literature review is a written argument, and writing decisions are inferential decisions. How sources are introduced, whose voice dominates a sentence, how transitions between clusters of evidence are handled, and how claims are hedged all change what the reader believes the evidence shows. Agents often treat the prose as packaging around the "real" content, but in a review the prose is where claims are made, softened, overstated, or smuggled past scrutiny. A review can be well-researched and still mislead if the writing grants authority it has not earned.

Use this skill when drafting or revising a literature review, when deciding how to attribute claims, when sequencing sections, or when tightening an argument so that every paragraph advances the case. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing prose that is fluent but inferentially loose.

## Core Rules

### Lead The Argument, Do Not Follow The Citations

The reviewer's voice should organize the section. If the structure is dictated by the order sources were found, or by listing authors chronologically without purpose, the argument has no spine.

Build the argument by:

- stating the point of each section or paragraph first;
- using sources as evidence for that point, not as the point itself;
- sequencing sections so each builds on the previous;
- ending sections with the implication, not the last citation.

The reader should be able to outline the review's logic from the topic sentences alone.

### Control Citation Voice And Attribution

Citation verbs and sentence structure silently assign certainty. "Smith shows that X" claims more than "Smith suggests that X"; "X causes Y (Smith, 2020)" hides that only Smith, in one sample, found this.

Control voice by:

- matching the verb to the strength of the evidence (show, demonstrate, suggest, argue, claim, speculate);
- keeping the original study's scope visible (sample, design, context);
- avoiding synthesis claims disguised as single-source facts;
- distinguishing the reviewer's analysis from reported findings.

Do not let a confident verb do the work that the underlying study's design did not do.

### Use Tense To Signal Evidence Status

Tense conventions carry meaning in academic writing. Misuse can make a single finding sound like established fact or make settled knowledge sound provisional.

Generally:

- past tense for what a specific study did or found;
- present tense for established, widely replicated knowledge;
- present perfect for the trajectory of a research stream;
- conditional or hedged forms for speculative claims.

A sentence like "X causes Y" in the present tense implies a settled general law; reserve it for genuinely established relationships.

### Make Transitions Carry Inferential Weight

Transitions between paragraphs and sections are where synthesis lives or dies. "Also," "in addition," and "furthermore" merely list; "by contrast," "building on this," and "despite this" relate.

Use transitions to signal:

- extension or support;
- contradiction or tension;
- refinement or qualification;
- shift in level of analysis;
- movement from evidence to gap.

A review whose transitions are all additive is a list, not an argument.

### Hedge And Claim In Proportion To Evidence

Overclaiming is the most common writing failure in reviews. The prose should calibrate certainty to the actual strength, directness, and replication of the evidence.

Calibrate by:

- using qualifiers (may, tends to, in this sample, under these conditions);
- naming the population or context the claim applies to;
- noting when evidence is preliminary, single-study, or contested;
- avoiding universal statements from limited data.

Underclaiming is also a failure: settled findings should not be hedged into invisibility. Match the hedge to the evidence.

### Integrate, Do Not Drop, Quotations

Dropped quotations interrupt the argument and shift voice to the cited author without the reviewer's control. Integrate quotations into the reviewer's sentence structure.

When quoting:

- introduce or follow the quotation with the reviewer's framing;
- quote only what is load-bearing, not whole sentences for decoration;
- ensure the quotation is not cherry-picked to reverse the source's meaning;
- paraphrase when the idea, not the wording, is what matters.

### Sequence From Established To Contested To Gap

A defensible review structure moves the reader from what is known, through where it is contested, to what remains unknown. This sequence earns the gap statement.

A typical spine:

- establish the phenomenon and why it matters;
- summarize settled findings;
- present the live debate or tension;
- diagnose the source of disagreement;
- identify the specific gap;
- state the contribution the new work makes.

Reversing this, for example leading with the gap before establishing the field, weakens the justification.

### Handle Reviewer Stance And Conflict Of Interest

When the reviewer has a stake in a debate, the writing should acknowledge it rather than hide behind apparent neutrality.

Disclose:

- the reviewer's prior position or related work;
- theoretical or methodological allegiance;
- funding or institutional interests where relevant;
- how the reviewer handled sources that disagree with the preferred view.

Stance acknowledged is more credible than stance disguised as objectivity.

### Revise For Coherence At The Section Level

A first draft often has all the right sources in the wrong order. Revision should test whether the argument holds at the outline level before line editing.

Revise by:

- reading only the topic sentences in sequence;
- checking that each paragraph has one main point;
- cutting paragraphs that do not advance the argument;
- merging fragmented evidence on the same point;
- ensuring the gap statement is supported by what precedes it.

## Common Traps

### Author-Driven Structure

Organizing by author or chronologically, with no conceptual logic, produces a timeline rather than an argument.

### Overclaiming Through Confident Verbs

"Proves," "demonstrates," and "establishes" grant certainty the evidence does not have, especially for single or preliminary studies.

### Additive Transitions

"And," "also," and "furthermore" throughout signal listing, not synthesis. The review reads as a concatenated abstract set.

### Dropped Or Cherry-Picked Quotations

Quotations introduced without framing, or selected to reverse a source's meaning, distort the evidence and erode trust.

### Hedging Settled Findings Into Nothing

Excessive hedging of well-replicated results makes the field look emptier than it is and weakens the basis for the gap.

### Hiding Reviewer Stance

Pretending neutrality while advancing a preferred position is less credible than openly acknowledging and managing the stake.

### Ending Sections On A Citation

A paragraph that ends with a source rather than an implication leaves the reader without the point of the evidence.

## Self-Check

- Does each section and paragraph lead with the reviewer's point, using sources as evidence?
- Do citation verbs match the actual strength and scope of each study?
- Is tense used to distinguish specific findings from established knowledge?
- Do transitions relate ideas rather than merely add them?
- Are claims hedged in proportion to the evidence's strength, directness, and replication?
- Are quotations integrated and framed rather than dropped or cherry-picked?
- Does the structure move from established, to contested, to gap, to contribution?
- Is the reviewer's stance or conflict of interest acknowledged where relevant?
- Does reading only the topic sentences reveal a coherent argument?
- Does every paragraph advance the case rather than repeat a neighboring point?
