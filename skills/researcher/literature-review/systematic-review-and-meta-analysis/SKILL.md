# Systematic Review And Meta-Analysis

A systematic review is not a thorough literature summary. It is a structured method designed to minimize bias in locating, selecting, appraising, and synthesizing evidence. A meta-analysis is the statistical pooling that may sit on top of such a review. Researchers often call a narrative summary "systematic," pool heterogeneous studies into a single number, or apply a risk-of-bias tool without acting on its findings. The result is a synthesis that looks authoritative but inherits and amplifies the biases of its source studies.

Use this skill when planning a systematic review or meta-analysis, when writing the protocol, when searching and screening, when extracting and appraising, when pooling, and when grading the certainty of the conclusion. The goal is to keep the agent from producing a biased review disguised as exhaustive, from pooling incompatible studies, and from stating certainty the evidence does not support. The agent has latitude in scope and methods, but the protocol, transparency, and bias handling are non-negotiable.

## Core Rules

### Start With A Registered Protocol Before Searching

A systematic review without a protocol is a narrative review with extra steps. The protocol fixes the question, eligibility, search, screening, extraction, and synthesis plan before results can shape them.

Specify in the protocol:

- the review question in a structured form such as PICO or SPIDER;
- inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- databases and other sources to be searched;
- the draft search strategy;
- screening process and roles, including dual review;
- data items to extract;
- risk-of-bias tool to use;
- synthesis method, including any meta-analysis plan;
- heterogeneity and subgroup analysis plan;
- certainty-of-evidence approach.

Register the protocol, for example on PROSPERO, or publish it before starting screening.

### Define The Question And Eligibility With Precision

Vague eligibility produces a grab-bag of studies that cannot answer one question. Define the question so that screening decisions are reproducible.

Define:

- population, including subgroups of interest;
- phenomenon, intervention, exposure, or experience;
- comparators;
- outcomes, primary and secondary, with timing;
- study designs eligible;
- publication status and language filters and their justification;
- date restrictions and their justification.

Each criterion should be testable. Two independent screeners should reach the same decision on most records.

### Search Comprehensively And Report Reproducibly

A biased search produces a biased review. A reproducible search lets others check and update the work.

Build the search to cover:

- multiple relevant databases;
- gray literature, trials registers, and dissertations where relevant;
- hand-searching of key journals or reference lists;
- contact with authors for unpublished data;
- documented search strings for each database;
- date of search and number of results per source;
- removal of duplicates with method noted.

Report the full search strategy in an appendix, not a paraphrase. A search that cannot be re-run is not systematic.

### Screen And Extract With Dual Review And Transparency

Selection and extraction are where bias and error quietly enter. Independent dual review with reconciliation reduces both.

Operationalize:

- title and abstract screening against criteria;
- full-text screening with reasons for exclusion recorded;
- at least two independent reviewers per stage;
- disagreement resolution by discussion or third reviewer;
- extraction of data into a piloted form;
- extraction of effect estimates, confidence intervals, and context;
- contact with study authors for missing information;
- a flow diagram showing records in and out at each stage.

A single reviewer deciding alone, even with a second checking, is weaker than true independent dual review.

### Appraise Risk Of Bias And Use The Appraisal

A risk-of-bias tool that is filled in and ignored is decoration. The appraisal should shape synthesis and interpretation.

Choose a tool matched to the design:

- Cochrane RoB 2 for randomized trials;
- ROBINS-I for non-randomized studies;
- QUADAS-2 for diagnostic accuracy;
- appropriate tools for qualitative, animal, or other designs.

Then:

- judge each domain with supporting evidence;
- produce an overall risk-of-bias judgment;
- explore the effect of restricting to low-risk studies;
- downplay or exclude studies at critical risk;
- incorporate risk of bias into the certainty-of-evidence rating.

The point is not to score studies; it is to weight the conclusions by how trustworthy they are.

### Decide Whether Meta-Analysis Is Appropriate At All

Pooling is not mandatory. If studies differ in population, intervention, comparator, outcome, or design, a single pooled estimate can be actively misleading.

Before pooling, judge:

- conceptual similarity of the studies;
- statistical heterogeneity;
- direction and magnitude consistency;
- outcome measurement comparability;
- unit-of-analysis issues;
- dependence among effect sizes within studies.

If heterogeneity is large and unexplained, a narrative synthesis or a stratified presentation is more honest than a single number.

### Handle Heterogeneity, Subgroups, And Publication Bias Honestly

A meta-analysis is a model of variation, not a device to erase it.

Address:

- quantify heterogeneity with statistics such as tau-squared or I-squared;
- explore prespecified subgroups, not data-mined ones;
- use random-effects models when true effects plausibly differ;
- test for and adjust against small-study effects and publication bias, with appropriate caveats;
- avoid over-interpreting a single subgroup split;
- report prediction intervals where helpful;
- resist the temptation to explain all heterogeneity post hoc.

A forest plot with a tidy diamond can conceal profound disagreement among studies.

### Grade The Certainty Of Evidence Separately By Outcome

The certainty of a body of evidence is not the average of its p-values. It is a judgment about how much confidence the synthesis warrants.

Apply a framework such as GRADE and rate certainty down for:

- risk of bias across studies;
- inconsistency of results;
- indirectness of population, intervention, or outcome;
- imprecision of estimates;
- publication bias.

Rate up in rare cases for large effects, dose-response, or confounding that would minimize the effect. Report a separate certainty rating per outcome, not a single grade for the whole review.

## Common Traps

### Calling A Narrative Summary Systematic

Without a protocol, reproducible search, dual screening, and bias appraisal, a review is narrative regardless of how many papers it cites.

### Pooling Incompatible Studies

A single diamond pooled from studies of different populations, doses, or outcomes hides disagreement behind false precision.

### Filling In Risk Of Bias Without Acting On It

A risk-of-bias table that does not influence synthesis, sensitivity analysis, or certainty rating is decorative.

### Searching One Database And Stopping

Single-database searches miss studies and bias the pool. Comprehensiveness is part of the method, not a nicety.

### Explaining Heterogeneity Post Hoc

Inventing subgroup stories after seeing the forest plot produces false confidence and unfalsifiable narratives.

### Ignoring Publication Bias

Small-study effects and unpublished null results distort the pool. Test for them and interpret cautiously.

### Overstating Certainty

A significant pooled estimate from biased, indirect, or imprecise studies does not justify a strong recommendation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is a structured, registered protocol in place before searching begins?
- [ ] Are the review question and eligibility criteria precise enough for reproducible screening?
- [ ] Is the search comprehensive, reproducible, and fully reported across databases and gray literature?
- [ ] Are screening and extraction performed with independent dual review and recorded reasons for exclusion?
- [ ] Is risk of bias appraised with a design-appropriate tool and used to shape synthesis and certainty?
- [ ] Is the decision to pool, or not to pool, justified by conceptual and statistical compatibility?
- [ ] Are heterogeneity, prespecified subgroups, and small-study or publication bias addressed honestly?
- [ ] Is the certainty of evidence graded separately per outcome using a transparent framework?
- [ ] Are limitations of the review, including search, language, and publication biases, reported?
- [ ] Are conclusions calibrated to certainty, not to the significance of a pooled estimate?
