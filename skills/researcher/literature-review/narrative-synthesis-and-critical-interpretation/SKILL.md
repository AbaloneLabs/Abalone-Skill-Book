# Narrative Synthesis And Critical Interpretation

A narrative synthesis is not a paragraph-per-paper summary. It is an argument that organizes a body of evidence around concepts, debates, mechanisms, and gaps so that a reader understands what is known, where it is contested, and why it matters. Agents often default to the "one paragraph per source" structure because it is easy to produce, but that structure signals no synthesis has occurred. The harder and more valuable work is deciding how studies relate, where they conflict, and which findings deserve more weight.

Use this skill when moving from a collected set of sources to a written synthesis, when reconciling studies that disagree, when deciding how much weight to give each study, or when constructing the interpretive spine of a review. The goal is to prevent the agent from presenting an annotated bibliography disguised as a literature review.

## Core Rules

### Organize By Concept, Not By Source

The unit of a synthesis is the idea, debate, or finding, not the article. A reader should learn how the field thinks about a problem, not merely that many people have written about it.

Choose an organizing logic such as:

- by construct or variable;
- by theoretical perspective;
- by methodological approach;
- by population or context;
- by time period or evolution of thought;
- by level of analysis;
- by agreement and disagreement clusters.

Introduce each cluster with the conceptual claim, then bring in studies as evidence for or against it. A source may appear under several clusters if it speaks to several ideas.

### Make The Logic Of Comparison Explicit

When studies are compared, the reader needs to know on what dimensions they are being compared and why those dimensions matter.

State for each comparison:

- the dimension of comparison (sample, measure, context, design, time);
- whether the studies are directly comparable on that dimension;
- what a difference on that dimension would mean;
- whether apparent disagreement is real or an artifact of method.

Do not assert that "studies conflict" without diagnosing whether the conflict is substantive, methodological, or contextual.

### Diagnose The Source Of Disagreement

Disagreement is one of the most informative signals in a review. It usually points to a moderator, a boundary condition, or a methodological artifact rather than to random noise.

Investigate whether disagreement reflects:

- different populations or settings;
- different operationalizations of the same construct;
- different designs with different biases;
- different time periods or historical conditions;
- different analytical choices or covariates;
- publication or reporting differences;
- genuine theoretical divergence.

Resolve or frame the disagreement rather than listing it and moving on. "Findings are mixed" is a stopping point, not a conclusion.

### Weight Studies Proportionally

Not every study deserves equal space or authority. A small exploratory study, a large preregistered experiment, a retrospective chart review, and a meta-analysis carry different evidentiary weight.

Weight by:

- design strength relative to the claim;
- sample relevance and size;
- measurement quality;
- risk of bias;
- directness to the review question;
- replication and consistency;
- independence from other cited studies.

Give more interpretive space to stronger, more direct, more replicated evidence. A weak study should not anchor a paragraph that a stronger study only decorates.

### Separate Result, Interpretation, And Current Claim

Three layers are often conflated: what a study found, what its authors concluded, and what the current reviewer claims it means. Conflating them transfers the authors' spin into the review's argument.

For each key source, keep distinct:

- the reported result (with design and sample);
- the authors' interpretation and its limits;
- the current reviewer's use of the study and any reframing.

If the reviewer disagrees with the original authors, say so and explain why. Silent reinterpretation misleads readers about the source's stance.

### Show The Shape Of The Evidence

A good synthesis conveys the overall pattern: is the evidence convergent, divided, sparse, outdated, or dominated by one research group? The reader should leave with a map, not a list.

Convey shape through:

- statements of where evidence is consistent;
- statements of where it is contested or absent;
- identification of dominant versus minority positions;
- noting when evidence is thin, old, or geographically concentrated;
- flagging when one lab or dataset drives most of a finding.

### Build Toward The Gap Or Contribution

A synthesis exists to justify what comes next. The argument should culminate in what remains unknown, contested, or untested, and why that matters.

Ensure the synthesis:

- establishes what is already settled;
- identifies the live tension or missing piece;
- explains why the gap is consequential, not merely unfilled;
- sets up the specific contribution the new work will make.

A synthesis that ends with "more research is needed" has not earned its conclusion.

### Maintain Transparency About Synthesis Method

Narrative does not mean unstructured. The reader should understand how the synthesis was produced.

Disclose:

- how studies were grouped;
- whether quality appraisal informed weighting;
- whether any framework guided the synthesis;
- the reviewer's perspective or stance where relevant;
- limitations of the narrative approach itself.

## Common Traps

### The Annotated-Bibliography Structure

One paragraph per study, in sequence, with no conceptual links, is not synthesis. It signals the reviewer has read but not integrated.

### Asserting "Mixed Findings" And Stopping

"Mixed findings" is a symptom to diagnose, not a conclusion. It usually hides a moderator or methodological difference.

### Giving Equal Weight To Unequal Studies

Treating a small pilot and a large preregistered trial as equally authoritative distorts the field's actual evidentiary balance.

### Conflating Author Conclusion With Study Result

Bold author conclusions can outrun the data. Report the result and assess the conclusion, do not import it wholesale.

### Erasing Minority Or Uncomfortable Findings

A clean story built by omitting contradictory studies is advocacy. The synthesis is more credible when it engages the hardest counterexample.

### Over-Interpreting A Single Study

One striking study, especially the reviewer's own preferred one, should not anchor an entire section unless its quality and replication justify it.

### Failing To Show Why The Gap Matters

A gap without consequence reads as filler. The synthesis must explain what decision, theory, or harm the missing evidence leaves unaddressed.

## Self-Check

- Is the section organized by concept or debate rather than one paragraph per source?
- Are comparisons made on explicit, relevant dimensions?
- Is each instance of disagreement diagnosed for its source rather than labeled "mixed"?
- Are studies weighted by design, quality, directness, and replication rather than treated equally?
- Are study results kept distinct from author conclusions and from the reviewer's own claims?
- Does the synthesis convey the overall shape and strength of the evidence?
- Does the argument culminate in a consequential gap, not just "more research is needed"?
- Is the synthesis method, grouping logic, and reviewer stance transparent?
- Are minority or contradictory findings engaged rather than omitted?
- Would a reader leave with a map of the field rather than a list of citations?
