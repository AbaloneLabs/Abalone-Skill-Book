# Systematic Review And Meta-Analysis Method

A systematic review is not a literature summary; it is a structured method for identifying, evaluating, and synthesizing all relevant evidence on a question, with the goal of producing a more reliable answer than any single study can provide. Done rigorously, systematic reviews and meta-analyses are among the most powerful tools in evidence-based practice, pooling evidence to reveal effects that individual studies are underpowered to detect. Done poorly, they produce misleading syntheses that inherit and amplify the biases of their included studies, that pool heterogeneous studies into meaningless averages, and that present subjective narrative conclusions dressed in the authority of systematic method. The difference between a rigorous and a sloppy systematic review is not effort; it is methodological discipline at every stage, from protocol to search to bias assessment to synthesis. The judgment problem is conducting systematic reviews with the rigor that gives their conclusions credibility, recognizing where the evidence does not support pooling, and being honest about the limits of what a synthesis can conclude.

Use this skill when conducting a systematic review or meta-analysis, developing a protocol, designing searches, assessing study bias, synthesizing evidence, or interpreting pooled estimates. The goal is to prevent the agent from conducting reviews without protocols, from inadequate searches that miss evidence, from pooling heterogeneous studies inappropriately, from ignoring publication bias, and from overclaiming what the synthesis shows.

## Core Rules

### Develop And Register A Protocol Before Conducting The Review

A systematic review protocol specifies the question, inclusion criteria, search strategy, bias assessment methods, and synthesis plan before the review begins. Pre-registration prevents the selective decisions, in inclusion, analysis, and reporting, that undermine a review's credibility when made after seeing the studies. The protocol is what makes a review systematic rather than narrative.

Develop a protocol by:

- specifying the research question using frameworks like PICO, population, intervention, comparison, outcome;
- defining inclusion and exclusion criteria before searching;
- specifying the search strategy, databases, terms, and limits;
- planning bias assessment and synthesis methods in advance;
- registering the protocol, through PROSPERO or similar, before conducting the review.

A review whose methods were determined after seeing the studies has methods shaped by the studies, and its credibility is compromised.

### Conduct A Comprehensive, Reproducible Search

A systematic review is only as complete as its search. A search that misses relevant studies produces a biased evidence base, and a search that cannot be reproduced cannot be verified. Comprehensive, documented searching across multiple databases and gray literature is essential.

Search comprehensively by:

- searching multiple relevant databases, not just one;
- using search terms developed with an information specialist where possible;
- documenting the full search strategy so it can be reproduced;
- searching gray literature, conference proceedings, and trial registries to reduce publication bias;
- tracking the number of records identified, screened, and included through a flow diagram.

A review that searched one database with an undocumented strategy may have missed half the relevant evidence, and neither the authors nor readers would know.

### Use Independent Dual Screening And Extraction

Single-person screening and data extraction introduce error and bias, as one person's judgments and mistakes go unchecked. Independent dual screening, where two reviewers screen and extract independently and resolve disagreements, reduces error and provides a check on subjective judgments.

Use dual processes by:

- having two reviewers independently screen titles and abstracts for inclusion;
- having two reviewers independently screen full texts and extract data;
- resolving disagreements through discussion or third-reviewer adjudication;
- reporting inter-rater agreement to demonstrate reliability;
- not relying on single-reviewer screening for a review claiming systematic rigor.

A single reviewer who misjudges inclusion criteria excludes studies silently, and the bias is invisible in the final review.

### Assess Risk Of Bias In Each Included Study

The credibility of a synthesis depends on the credibility of its included studies, and studies vary enormously in their risk of bias. Assessing risk of bias in each study, using validated tools, is essential to interpreting the synthesis and to deciding whether pooling is appropriate. A synthesis of biased studies produces a precise but wrong pooled estimate.

Assess bias by:

- using validated risk-of-bias tools appropriate to the study design;
- assessing each study for selection, performance, detection, attrition, and reporting bias;
- using the bias assessments in interpreting the synthesis, not just reporting them;
- conducting sensitivity analyses excluding high-risk studies to test robustness;
- recognizing that a pooled estimate of biased studies is not more credible than the studies.

A meta-analysis of studies with high risk of bias produces a precise number that is precisely wrong, and the precision lends false confidence.

### Decide Whether Pooling Is Appropriate Based On Heterogeneity

Meta-analysis pools effect estimates across studies, but pooling is appropriate only when the studies are similar enough that a pooled estimate is meaningful. Excessive heterogeneity, in populations, interventions, outcomes, or methods, means the pooled average does not represent any real effect and may mislead. Assess heterogeneity and decide whether to pool.

Decide on pooling by:

- assessing clinical, methodological, and statistical heterogeneity before pooling;
- using statistical measures of heterogeneity, such as I-squared, alongside judgment;
- exploring heterogeneity through subgroup analysis where it exists;
- not pooling studies that are too different to yield a meaningful average;
- reporting a narrative synthesis where pooling is inappropriate.

A pooled estimate from heterogeneous studies is an average of different effects that applies to no real population, and presenting it as a finding is misleading.

### Address Publication Bias And Reporting Bias

Publication bias, the tendency for studies with significant findings to be published and null findings to remain unpublished, distorts the evidence base available for synthesis. A review that ignores publication bias may synthesize a biased subset of the evidence, producing inflated effect estimates. Address publication bias explicitly.

Address by:

- searching for unpublished studies through trial registries and gray literature;
- using funnel plots and statistical tests to assess publication bias where enough studies exist;
- recognizing the direction of likely bias, typically toward inflated effects;
- interpreting findings in light of probable publication bias;
- being honest where publication bias cannot be ruled out and may inflate findings.

A review that finds large effects from published studies may be finding the effects of publication bias, not the intervention, and ignoring this possibility overstates the evidence.

### Synthesize Evidence Appropriate To The Data

Synthesis methods must fit the evidence. Quantitative synthesis, meta-analysis, is appropriate when studies are similar enough to pool. Narrative synthesis is appropriate when they are not. Network meta-analysis can compare multiple interventions but requires strong assumptions. Choosing the wrong synthesis method produces misleading results.

Synthesize appropriately by:

- using meta-analysis only when studies are similar enough to pool meaningfully;
- using narrative synthesis where heterogeneity or diverse designs preclude pooling;
- using network meta-analysis only when its assumptions are met;
- pre-specifying subgroup and sensitivity analyses rather than conducting them post hoc;
- recognizing the limits of each method and not overreaching.

A meta-analysis conducted where narrative synthesis was appropriate produces a precise number that misrepresents the evidence; a narrative synthesis where pooling was possible leaves information on the table.

### Interpret Pooled Estimates With Appropriate Humility

A pooled effect estimate, with its confidence interval, carries an authority that can exceed what the evidence supports. Pooled estimates are sensitive to the included studies, their biases, publication bias, and heterogeneity, and they should be interpreted with attention to these factors, not as definitive answers.

Interpret with humility by:

- examining whether the pooled estimate is robust to excluding high-risk studies;
- considering the confidence interval's width and clinical meaning, not just the point estimate;
- assessing the certainty of the evidence using frameworks like GRADE;
- recognizing that a precise pooled estimate of biased studies is not strong evidence;
- stating the limits of what the synthesis can conclude.

A pooled estimate is a synthesis of the available evidence, not a revelation of truth, and its credibility depends on the evidence and method, not on the precision of the number.

## Common Traps

### No Protocol, Methods Determined Post Hoc

A review without a pre-specified protocol has methods shaped by the studies. Develop and register a protocol.

### Inadequate Or Undocumented Search

Missing studies bias the review. Search comprehensively across databases and gray literature, and document the strategy.

### Single-Person Screening And Extraction

Single reviewers introduce unchecked error and bias. Use independent dual processes.

### Pooling Without Assessing Risk Of Bias

A synthesis of biased studies is precisely wrong. Assess bias and use it in interpretation.

### Pooling Heterogeneous Studies

A pooled average of different effects misleads. Assess heterogeneity and do not pool where inappropriate.

### Ignoring Publication Bias

Published evidence is a biased subset. Search for unpublished studies and assess publication bias.

### Wrong Synthesis Method For The Evidence

Meta-analysis where narrative is appropriate, or vice versa. Match method to the evidence.

### Overconfident Interpretation Of Pooled Estimates

Pooled estimates inherit the biases and limits of included studies. Interpret with humility and certainty assessment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A protocol specifying question, criteria, search, and methods was developed and registered before the review.
- [ ] The search was comprehensive across multiple databases and gray literature, and is fully documented and reproducible.
- [ ] Screening and data extraction used independent dual processes with disagreement resolution.
- [ ] Risk of bias was assessed in each included study using validated tools and used in interpretation.
- [ ] Heterogeneity was assessed, and pooling was conducted only where studies were similar enough to pool meaningfully.
- [ ] Publication and reporting bias were addressed through search and assessment.
- [ ] The synthesis method fits the evidence, with narrative synthesis where pooling is inappropriate.
- [ ] Pooled estimates are interpreted with attention to bias, heterogeneity, and certainty, not as definitive answers.
- [ ] Subgroup and sensitivity analyses were pre-specified, not conducted post hoc.
- [ ] The review's conclusions reflect the strength and limits of the synthesized evidence.
