# Literature Review Scoping And Search Strategy

A literature review's credibility is decided before any source is read. If the search is narrow, biased toward one database, tuned to find only agreeable results, or impossible to reproduce, then every later claim built on that review inherits a hidden flaw. Agents frequently treat searching as a mechanical step and concentrate their effort on writing, but a review whose retrieval cannot be audited is not evidence; it is a curated opinion.

Use this skill when defining the boundaries of a review, building search strings, deciding what counts as in or out, or justifying why certain bodies of literature were searched and others were not. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a review that looks thorough but rests on an untraceable, selective, or convenience-based retrieval.

## Core Rules

### Define The Review Type And Its Retrieval Obligation

Different review types impose different retrieval obligations, and the obligation determines how rigorous the search must be. A narrative background review may justify purposive sampling, but a systematic or scoping review demands demonstrable comprehensiveness.

Clarify before searching:

- review type (narrative, scoping, systematic, rapid, umbrella, living);
- whether comprehensiveness is required or purposive selection is acceptable;
- whether the protocol is registered or published;
- the decision the review is meant to inform;
- the audience's tolerance for selective coverage.

Do not borrow the language of systematic review (PRISMA, exhaustive search) for a review that is actually selective. The retrieval method must match the label.

### Make The Search Reproducible By Design

A reader or reviewer should be able to rerun the search and reach a comparable set of records. Reproducibility is not a paragraph added at the end; it is a property of how the search is constructed.

Document for every database:

- exact database and platform (publisher and interface matter);
- full search string with Boolean operators and field tags;
- date the search was run;
- date range applied;
- any filters or limits active in the interface;
- number of records returned;
- database version or update if relevant.

Avoid relying on "similar articles" recommendations, personalized feeds, or browser history as the retrieval method. These are non-reproducible and invisible to a reader.

### Balance Sensitivity And Specificity Deliberately

Every search trades off sensitivity (catching everything relevant) against specificity (keeping results manageable). A search tuned only for precision will miss key studies; a search tuned only for recall will drown screening in noise.

Tune by:

- testing the search against a set of known key studies (a benchmark seed set);
- checking whether index terms and free-text variants are both covered;
- including synonyms, spelling variants, acronyms, and regional terminology;
- using truncation and wildcards carefully and noting their behavior per database;
- reviewing a sample of excluded records to confirm they are genuinely irrelevant.

Record the tradeoff decision. If the team chose precision for feasibility, say so and explain the risk of missed studies.

### Decide Inclusion And Exclusion Criteria Before Screening

Criteria defined after screening begins can silently bend the review toward a preferred conclusion. Define eligibility first, then screen against it.

Specify:

- population, phenomenon, or setting of interest;
- types of study eligible or excluded by design;
- publication status rules (e.g., peer-reviewed only, grey literature included);
- language and date restrictions and the justification for each;
- whether conference abstracts, theses, preprints, or protocols count;
- handling of duplicate or overlapping publications.

Pre-register or timestamp the criteria when possible. Any change after screening starts should be documented with a reason.

### Search Multiple And Appropriate Sources

No single database covers a field. Relying on one source produces a distorted map of the literature.

Include, as appropriate to the field:

- at least two subject-relevant bibliographic databases;
- one multidisciplinary database;
- grey literature (reports, theses, government documents);
- trial or study registries where relevant;
- hand-searching of key journals;
- citation chasing forward and backward from seed studies;
- expert contact for unpublished or ongoing work.

Justify omissions. Excluding grey literature, non-English sources, or conference work should be a conscious decision, not an accident.

### Handle Database Syntax And Indexing Differences

Identical concepts are indexed differently across databases. Copying one search string into another platform produces silent misses.

For each database:

- translate subject headings (e.g., MeSH, Emtree, PsycInfo thesaurus terms);
- adjust field tags and proximity operators to that platform's syntax;
- verify truncation symbols and wildcards behave as expected;
- re-test the translated search against the seed set.

Treat each database search as a separate, validated object, not a translation footnote.

### Plan The Screening Workflow

Retrieved records must be deduplicated, screened, and decisions logged. A sloppy screening process hides how the final set was reached.

Plan:

- deduplication method and threshold;
- title and abstract screening rules;
- full-text retrieval and second screening;
- single versus dual screening and conflict resolution;
- a record of every exclusion reason at full text;
- use of screening tools and their version.

Keep a flow of records from identification to inclusion. PRISMA-style reporting helps even for non-systematic reviews.

### Anticipate And Disclose Retrieval Limits

Every search has limits. Naming them is more credible than hiding them.

Disclose:

- paywall and access constraints;
- language limits and resulting blind spots;
- index lag in databases;
- reliance on abstracts when full text was unavailable;
- known under-indexed regions, journals, or languages;
- any time pressure that forced a rapid-review compromise.

## Common Traps

### Searching One Database And Calling It Comprehensive

A single database reflects that platform's coverage, indexing priorities, and biases. Comprehensive claims require multiple, complementary sources.

### Letting The Search Find Only Agreeable Studies

If the search terms are tuned to the preferred hypothesis, the review becomes confirmation in disguise. Test the search against studies that contradict the expected finding.

### Trusting Algorithmic Recommendations As Retrieval

"Recommended for you" feeds and relevance rankings are personalized and non-reproducible. They are aids, not a documented search strategy.

### Defining Criteria To Exclude Inconvenient Studies

Post hoc exclusion of studies that complicate the story is a form of cherry-picking. Eligibility must precede and govern screening.

### Ignoring Grey Literature And Non-English Sources

Published, English-language, peer-reviewed work over-represents certain regions and positive findings. Excluding the rest biases the map of evidence.

### Failing To Record Numbers At Each Stage

Without a record flow, no one can tell whether studies were lost to access, screening, or error. Missing counts make the review unauditable.

### Treating Citation Chasing As Optional

Backward and forward citation tracing often recovers studies that keyword searches miss, especially for conceptually labeled phenomena. Skipping it leaves predictable gaps.

## Self-Check

- Is the review type named, and does the retrieval rigor match that label?
- Could a reader rerun each database search from the documented string and filters?
- Was the search tested against a seed set of known relevant and known contradictory studies?
- Are inclusion and exclusion criteria defined before screening, with any later changes documented?
- Does the search span multiple databases plus grey literature or citation chasing where appropriate?
- Were database-specific syntax, subject headings, and field tags translated and verified per platform?
- Is there a documented flow of records from identification through screening to inclusion?
- Are access, language, index lag, and time-pressure limits explicitly disclosed?
- Were screening decisions and exclusion reasons logged at full text?
- Is the sensitivity-versus-specificity tradeoff acknowledged rather than hidden?
