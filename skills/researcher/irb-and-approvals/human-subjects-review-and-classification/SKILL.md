# Human Subjects Review And Classification

Whether a project needs ethics review, and what level, is a determination that researchers are poorly placed to make for themselves. The temptation to self-certify a project as exempt is strong, especially when review seems slow, but the determination usually belongs to the authorized body, not the investigator. Misclassification can void a project, expose participants to unreviewed risk, and create integrity problems that surface late. The judgment problem is assessing reviewability honestly, classifying risk correctly, and preparing a submission that reviewers can evaluate efficiently.

Use this skill when deciding whether a project needs human subjects review, classifying the level of review, preparing a protocol, and navigating the boundary between research and non-research. The goal is to keep the agent from self-certifying exemption, from underclassifying risk, and from submitting protocols too thin to protect participants or to pass review.

This is a high-stakes domain because errors harm participants and can invalidate entire projects. When classification is uncertain, the agent must defer to the authorized body and consult the IRB or ethics office rather than deciding alone.

## Core Rules

### Determine Reviewability Before Assuming Exemption

Under frameworks such as the Common Rule, whether an activity is human subjects research, and whether it is exempt, are determinations that often belong to the IRB. Self-certifying exemption to avoid review is a recurring and serious error.

Assess:

- whether the activity meets the regulatory definition of research, meaning a systematic investigation designed to develop or contribute to generalizable knowledge;
- whether it involves human subjects through intervention, interaction, or identifiable private information;
- whether identifiable biospecimens are involved, which can trigger specific rules;
- whether the activity is quality improvement, program evaluation, or surveillance that may or may not count as research depending on intent and design;
- whether the institution or funder requires review regardless of federal definitions.

When the activity is ambiguous, submit for a determination rather than self-certifying. Document the outcome.

### Classify The Level Of Review By Risk, Not By Convenience

Review levels calibrate scrutiny to risk. Classifying down to obtain lighter review can expose participants and invalidate the project.

Classify considering:

- exempt review for minimal-risk categories defined by regulation, where the determination usually belongs to the IRB;
- expedited review for minimal-risk studies falling into specified categories;
- full-board review for greater-than-minimal-risk studies, vulnerable populations, certain interventions, or sensitive topics;
- whether the study crosses into categories that force full review regardless of other features;
- whether prisoners, pregnant people, neonates, children, or decisionally impaired adults are involved, each carrying specific protections;
- whether deception, sensitive questioning, or biospecimen collection raises risk.

Risk is judged from the participant's standpoint. What feels minimal to the researcher may be significant to a participant facing stigma, legal exposure, or distress.

### Prepare A Protocol Reviewers Can Actually Evaluate

A vague protocol forces review cycles and signals weak risk thinking. Reviewers need enough detail to judge whether risks are minimized and justified.

Include:

- background, question, and design;
- population, sample size rationale, and recruitment;
- inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- procedures, interventions, and data sources;
- variables and measures;
- analysis plan;
- risk identification and minimization;
- data handling, security, retention, and destruction;
- consent process and materials;
- compensation and conflict management;
- data and safety monitoring if applicable;
- plans for adverse events and deviations.

Write for a reviewer who is methodologically literate but outside the subfield. Specificity protects participants and speeds review.

### Treat Recruitment And Consent As Reviewable Artifacts

Recruitment scripts and consent documents are how participants understand and enter the study. Reviewers scrutinize them.

Ensure they cover:

- purpose and procedures in plain language;
- expected duration and burden;
- reasonably foreseeable risks and discomforts;
- potential benefits, with honest acknowledgment if none;
- alternatives to participation;
- confidentiality and its limits;
- mandatory reporting obligations;
- compensation and what happens if the study ends early;
- voluntary participation and the right to withdraw;
- contacts for questions and rights;
- data sharing, future use, and biospecimen handling;
- avoidance of therapeutic misconception when research is framed as treatment.

Coercive recruitment wording, especially with students, employees, patients, or dependent communities, is a frequent cause of review delays and participant harm.

### Identify Vulnerable Populations And Their Specific Protections

Certain populations require additional protections beyond standard review. Failing to identify them underprotects participants.

Recognize when the study involves:

- prisoners or justice-involved populations, with strict limits on permissible research;
- pregnant people, fetuses, and neonates, with specific risk standards;
- children, requiring assent and parental or guardian permission and limits on acceptable risk;
- decisionally impaired adults, requiring surrogate consent and capacity assessment;
- economically or educationally disadvantaged persons, where coercion and undue influence are concerns;
- communities vulnerable to group harm from individual participation.

When a vulnerable population is involved, the review level and protections escalate. Plan for the additional requirements from the start.

### Coordinate Review With Registration, Data, And Funding

Ethics review interacts with study registration, data sharing mandates, funding conditions, and publication requirements. Treating them as separate creates gaps.

Align:

- study registration timing with approval;
- data sharing plans with consent language and privacy constraints;
- funder reporting with continuing review;
- publication of the protocol with the final approved version;
- conflict of interest disclosure with review submission;
- authorship agreements with contributor roles.

A consistent, dated record across these obligations protects participants and the team.

### Plan For Multi-Site And International Requirements

Studies crossing institutions, borders, or communities often need more than one review, and requirements may conflict.

Check:

- single-IRB versus local review requirements;
- international ethics requirements and local law;
- community advisory boards or tribal review;
- permissions from clinics, schools, employers, or platforms;
- whether consent processes are culturally and linguistically appropriate;
- whether data can lawfully leave the country of collection.

Build the review timeline around the slowest and most demanding body, not the fastest.

## Common Traps

### Self-Certifying Exemption To Avoid Review

The exemption determination usually belongs to the IRB. Getting it wrong can void the project and harm participants.

### Classifying Down To Lighten Review

Underclassifying risk to obtain expedited or exempt review exposes participants and can invalidate the work.

### Submitting A Vague Protocol To Save Time

A thin protocol produces more review cycles, not fewer, and signals weak risk thinking.

### Missing Vulnerable Populations

Failing to recognize prisoners, children, pregnant people, or decisionally impaired adults underprotects them and violates specific protections.

### Treating Approval As The End Of Ethics

Approval starts an ongoing duty to monitor harm, report problems, and amend when circumstances change.

### Ignoring Multi-Site And International Requirements

Assuming the home IRB's approval satisfies all sites or countries leads to blocked data collection and legal exposure.

### Decoupling Ethics From Registration And Data Obligations

Separating ethics review from registration, data sharing, and funding creates contradictions that surface late.

## Self-Check

- Has reviewability been assessed honestly, with exemption determined by the authorized body rather than self-certified?
- Is the level of review classified by risk from the participant's standpoint, with full-board triggers recognized?
- Does the protocol contain enough detail for a reviewer to evaluate risks, benefits, consent, and data protection?
- Do recruitment and consent materials cover procedures, risks, benefits, alternatives, confidentiality, withdrawal, and contacts in plain language?
- Are vulnerable populations identified, with their specific protections planned from the start?
- Is ethics review coordinated with registration, data sharing, funder, and publication obligations?
- Are multi-site, international, and community review requirements identified and sequenced around the slowest body?
- Where classification is uncertain, has the IRB or ethics office been consulted and their determination deferred to rather than deciding alone?
