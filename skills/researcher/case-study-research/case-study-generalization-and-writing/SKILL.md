# Case Study Generalization And Writing

The most misunderstood aspect of case study research is generalization. A case study does not generalize to a population; it generalizes analytically, from the case to theory, to a phenomenon, or to a broader theoretical proposition. Agents often either dismiss case studies as incapable of generalization (and therefore of limited value) or, conversely, treat a single case as if it licensed population-level claims. Both errors misread the design. The judgment problem is to frame the case's contribution in terms of analytical generalization, to specify the theory or proposition to which the case speaks, and to write the report in a way that conveys both the particularity of the case and its theoretical or practical implications. Case study writing is also distinctive: it must present enough detail for the reader to judge transferability to their own context while advancing an analytic argument, not merely narrating events.

Use this skill when generalizing from a case study or writing the case study report. The goal is to prevent the agent from overclaiming transferability, from underclaiming the case's theoretical contribution, and from writing reports that are chronicles rather than analyses.

## Core Rules

### Generalize Analytically, Not Statistically

Case studies generalize to theory and theoretical propositions, not to populations. This distinction is foundational.

Generalize by:

- analytical generalization: from the case to a theory or phenomenon;
- specifying the theory or proposition the case informs;
- not extrapolating to a population or prevalence;
- the case as a test, illustration, or extension of theory.

A case study of one hospital does not show what most hospitals do; it may show how a mechanism operates, test whether a theory holds, or reveal an unexpected pattern that refines theory.

### Specify What The Case Is A Case Of And What It Tests

Analytical generalization requires a clear statement of the theoretical target.

Specify by:

- the theory, proposition, or phenomenon the case addresses;
- whether the case is meant to confirm, extend, or challenge theory;
- the level of generality claimed;
- the relationship between the case findings and the theory.

Without a specified theoretical target, the case's contribution is vague and the generalization unjustified. The reader must know what the case is claimed to inform.

### Use Replication Logic Across Multiple Cases

When multiple cases are used, generalization follows replication, not aggregation.

Use by:

- literal replication: cases predicted to yield similar results;
- theoretical replication: cases predicted to yield contrasting results for predictable reasons;
- each case analyzed as a whole before cross-case synthesis;
- cross-case patterns tied back to theory.

Multiple cases do not add up to a sample; they replicate or contrast findings in ways that test and refine the theoretical proposition.

### Provide Thick Description To Support Transferability

The reader, not the researcher, judges whether findings transfer to their own context. This requires thick description.

Provide by:

- rich description of the case and its context;
- the conditions under which the phenomenon occurred;
- the features that seem consequential;
- enough detail for readers to assess similarity to their own setting.

Transferability is the reader's judgment, enabled by the researcher's description. Thin description prevents the reader from making that judgment.

### Distinguish Description From Analysis In The Report

Case study reports must convey the case and advance an argument. Both are needed; conflating them weakens both.

Distinguish by:

- description that establishes the case and its context;
- analysis that connects the case to theory and proposition;
- explicit movement between the particular and the general;
- an argument that organizes the report.

A report that is all description is a chronicle; one that is all analysis loses the case that grounds it. The report must do both.

### Present Alternative Explanations And Rival Interpretations

A credible case study considers and addresses rival explanations for its findings.

Present by:

- plausible alternative explanations for the observed patterns;
- evidence bearing on each rival;
- why the preferred explanation is better supported;
- honest acknowledgment of ambiguity.

A case study that presents only the favored interpretation is advocacy. Considering and addressing rivals is central to analytic credibility.

### Match Claims To Evidence And To The Design

Claims must be proportionate to the evidence and to what the design can support.

Match by:

- claims about the case itself stated with evidential support;
- claims about the theory or phenomenon stated as analytical generalization;
- no population or prevalence claims from case data;
- hedging where evidence is thin or the case is singular.

Overclaiming invites skepticism; underclaiming undersells the contribution. Proportion is the discipline.

### Write For Both Academic And Practical Audiences As Appropriate

Case studies often speak to both theory and practice. The report should serve its intended audiences.

Write by:

- clear theoretical implications for academic readers;
- practical implications for readers in comparable settings;
- accessible rendering of the case without losing rigor;
- implications tied to the evidence and design.

A report that is only theoretical misses the practical contribution case studies often make; one that is only practical misses the theoretical contribution.

## Common Traps

### Statistical Generalization From A Case

Extrapolating from one or a few cases to a population misuses the design and overstates the findings.

### Vague Theoretical Target

Generalizing without specifying the theory or proposition the case informs leaves the contribution unclear and unjustified.

### Thin Description

Insufficient detail prevents readers from judging transferability to their own contexts.

### Chronicle Without Analysis

A report that narrates the case without advancing an analytic argument is a chronicle, not a case study contribution.

### Unaddressed Rival Explanations

Presenting only the favored interpretation without considering alternatives reads as advocacy, not analysis.

### Overclaiming From A Single Case

Extending claims beyond what the evidence and design support invites deserved skepticism.

### Audience Mismatch

Writing only for theory or only for practice misses the dual contribution case studies can make.

## Self-Check

- Is generalization framed as analytical, to theory or proposition, rather than statistical to a population?
- Is the theoretical target, the theory or phenomenon the case informs, clearly specified?
- For multiple cases, is replication logic used rather than aggregation?
- Is thick description provided so readers can judge transferability to their own contexts?
- Are description and analysis both present, with explicit movement between the particular and the general?
- Are alternative explanations and rival interpretations presented and addressed?
- Are claims matched to the evidence and to what the design can support?
- Are both theoretical and practical implications drawn where appropriate?
- Is the case's contribution framed without overclaiming transferability?
- Does the report advance an argument rather than merely narrate the case?
