# Case Selection And Bounding

A case study's value rises or falls on how the case is selected and bounded. The case is not self-evident; it is a constructed unit defined by the researcher, and the decisions that define it determine what the study can and cannot claim. Agents often pick a case because it is available, fail to define its boundaries, and then make claims that the unbounded case cannot support. The judgment problem is that case selection in case study research is purposive and theoretical, not representative, and that bounding the case, what is inside and outside, what counts as the case and its context, is an analytic act that must be explicit. A well-selected and well-bounded case can illuminate a phenomenon, test a theory, or reveal a mechanism; a poorly selected or unbounded case produces findings of unclear relevance and invites the charge of cherry-picking.

Use this skill when designing a case study, selecting cases, or defining case boundaries. The goal is to prevent the agent from selecting cases by convenience, leaving boundaries vague, and making claims the case selection cannot support.

## Core Rules

### Define What The Case Is A Case Of

Before selecting a case, define the phenomenon it is meant to illuminate. A case without a clear phenomenon is unbounded.

Define by:

- the phenomenon the case is meant to illuminate;
- the theoretical or practical question the case addresses;
- why a case study, rather than another design, fits the question;
- the relationship between the case and the phenomenon.

A study of "a school" is not a case of anything in particular until the researcher specifies what about schools, or what phenomenon, the case is meant to reveal.

### Choose Between Intrinsic And Instrumental Cases Deliberately

Cases serve different purposes. An intrinsic case is studied for its own sake; an instrumental case is studied to understand a broader issue.

Choose by:

- intrinsic: the case itself is the interest, unique and worthy of study;
- instrumental: the case is a vehicle to understand something else;
- collective: multiple instrumental cases to illuminate a phenomenon;
- the implications of each for selection and claims.

An intrinsic case does not generalize; an instrumental case supports analytical generalization to theory or the phenomenon. Confusing the two produces mismatched claims.

### Bound The Case Explicitly

A case must have boundaries. Without them, the study has no limits and the findings have no defined scope.

Bound by:

- spatial boundaries: where does the case begin and end?
- temporal boundaries: what period does the study cover?
- the relationship between the case and its context;
- what is treated as inside the case and what as context.

A case study of an organization without temporal or spatial bounds sprawls into a study of everything related to the organization, and its findings become unbounded.

### Select Cases Purposively Against The Research Question

Case selection is purposive and theoretical, not random or representative. The logic must be explicit.

Select by:

- typical cases to illustrate a phenomenon;
- critical or revelatory cases to test or extend theory;
- maximum variation cases to explore range;
- most or least likely cases to test theoretical predictions.

Selecting a case because it is available, then justifying it post hoc as typical or critical, is cherry-picking. The selection logic must precede and drive the choice.

### Justify Single Versus Multiple Case Design

The number of cases is a design decision with analytic consequences.

Justify by:

- single case: depth, uniqueness, or revelatory power;
- multiple cases: replication logic, cross-case comparison;
- the tradeoff between depth and breadth;
- what each additional case adds analytically.

A single case allows depth but is vulnerable to the charge of idiosyncrasy; multiple cases allow replication but reduce depth per case. The choice must fit the question.

### Use Replication Logic For Multiple Case Designs

Multiple case studies follow replication, not sampling, logic. Each case is selected to predict similar or contrasting results.

Use by:

- literal replication: cases expected to produce similar findings;
- theoretical replication: cases expected to produce contrasting findings for predictable reasons;
- selecting cases to test and extend the emerging analysis;
- treating each case as a whole before cross-case comparison.

Treating multiple cases as a sample for statistical generalization misuses the design. The logic is replication of findings across cases, not aggregation to a population.

### Anticipate The Charge Of Cherry-Picking

Case selection is vulnerable to the accusation that favorable cases were chosen. The defense is transparency.

Anticipate by:

- stating the selection criteria before the choice where possible;
- justifying why this case and not others;
- acknowledging what the case cannot represent;
- reporting selection constraints and alternatives considered.

A selection justified only after the fact, or only by convenience, invites skepticism. A theoretically justified selection, with alternatives acknowledged, is defensible.

### Define The Relationship Between Case And Context

The case is embedded in a context. How case and context relate is a core analytic decision.

Define by:

- what is treated as the case and what as context;
- whether context is a backdrop or part of the phenomenon;
- how contextual features shape the case;
- the limits context places on transferability.

A case study that ignores context treats the case as if it existed in a vacuum; one that dissolves the case into context has no bounded unit to analyze.

## Common Traps

### Case Selected By Convenience

Choosing an available case without theoretical justification invites the charge of cherry-picking and limits the findings' relevance.

### Unbounded Case

A case without spatial, temporal, or contextual bounds produces sprawling findings of unclear scope.

### Intrinsic Case Treated As Generalizable

Extending findings from an intrinsic case to other settings misuses the design and overstates the contribution.

### Multiple Cases As A Sample

Treating multiple cases as a representative sample rather than following replication logic misuses the design.

### Vague Relationship Between Case And Context

Failing to distinguish case from context leaves the unit of analysis unclear and findings hard to interpret.

### Post Hoc Justification Of Selection

Justifying a convenient case as typical or critical after the fact undermines the credibility of the selection.

### Phenomenon Left Undefined

A case study without a clear sense of what the case is a case of produces findings of uncertain relevance.

## Self-Check

- Is the phenomenon the case is meant to illuminate clearly defined?
- Is the choice between intrinsic, instrumental, and collective case design deliberate and justified?
- Are the case boundaries, spatial, temporal, and contextual, explicitly stated?
- Is case selection purposive and tied to the research question, with the logic stated in advance?
- Is the choice of single versus multiple cases justified by the analytic goal?
- For multiple cases, is replication logic used rather than sampling logic?
- Is the selection defended against the charge of cherry-picking, with alternatives acknowledged?
- Is the relationship between case and context defined, with context's role specified?
- Are the limits the selection places on transferability acknowledged?
- Would a critic be able to follow and evaluate the selection logic?
