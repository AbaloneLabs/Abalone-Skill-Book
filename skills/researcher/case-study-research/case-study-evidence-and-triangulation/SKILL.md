# Case Study Evidence And Triangulation

Case study research relies on multiple sources of evidence converging on an understanding of the case. Unlike a single-method study, the case study's strength is the triangulation of evidence: documents, interviews, observation, and artifacts each contribute a partial view, and their convergence, or reasoned divergence, builds the case for the findings. Agents often rely on a single source (most often interviews) and call it a case study, or gather multiple sources but never integrate them, leaving the evidence in parallel rather than triangulated. The judgment problem is to select evidence sources that bear on the research question, maintain a case database that preserves each source separately and in detail, build a chain of evidence linking data to claims, and handle divergence among sources as analytic data rather than as noise to be reconciled away.

Use this skill when collecting or integrating case study evidence. The goal is to prevent the agent from single-source case studies, from un-integrated parallel data, and from claims that outrun the convergent evidence.

## Core Rules

### Use Multiple Sources Of Evidence By Design

The defining strength of case study research is the convergence of evidence from multiple sources. Single-source designs forfeit this.

Use by:

- documents, interviews, direct observation, participant-observation, and artifacts;
- selecting sources that bear on different facets of the question;
- sources that offer independent corroboration;
- sources with different weaknesses, so weaknesses do not align.

A study based only on interviews is an interview study, not a case study, regardless of how it is labeled. Multiple sources are what make triangulation possible.

### Maintain A Formal Case Database

Case study evidence must be preserved in a organized database that separates raw data from interpretation.

Maintain by:

- raw data organized by source and chronology;
- each source's data kept separate and traceable;
- notes, memos, and interpretations distinguished from evidence;
- the database complete enough for others to inspect.

A case study whose evidence exists only in the researcher's narrative cannot be audited. A formal database is the basis of trustworthiness and replicability.

### Build A Chain Of Evidence Linking Data To Claims

Each claim should be traceable through a chain from the research question to the evidence.

Build by:

- explicit links from research questions to data collected;
- links from data to patterns identified;
- links from patterns to conclusions;
- a reader able to follow the chain in both directions.

A conclusion that cannot be traced back to specific evidence is an assertion. The chain of evidence is what allows readers to evaluate and challenge the findings.

### Triangulate By Convergence And Divergence

Triangulation tests whether sources agree. Both convergence and divergence are analytically valuable.

Triangulate by:

- convergence strengthening confidence in a finding;
- divergence treated as data about the phenomenon;
- examining why sources differ;
- not forcing agreement by privileging one source.

When documents and interviews disagree, the divergence may reveal strategic reporting, recall error, or contested reality, all of which are findings. Smoothing divergence away hides the most interesting evidence.

### Treat Each Source With Attention To Its Limits

Each evidence source has characteristic strengths and weaknesses. These must be understood, not assumed away.

Treat by:

- documents: official, stable, but possibly self-serving or incomplete;
- interviews: rich, but subject to recall and presentation bias;
- observation: direct, but limited to what is visible and when;
- artifacts: concrete, but requiring interpretation of meaning and use.

Triangulation works because sources have different weaknesses. Assuming any source is transparent misuses it.

### Establish The Authenticity And Quality Of Evidence

Evidence must be authentic and of sufficient quality to support claims.

Establish by:

- verifying the authenticity and provenance of documents;
- assessing the position and knowledge of interviewees;
- corroborating key facts across independent sources;
- noting where evidence is second-hand or uncertain.

A case study built on unverified or low-quality evidence is vulnerable. Authenticity checks are part of rigor, not optional diligence.

### Integrate Evidence Into A Coherent Case Narrative

The case study's final product integrates evidence into a coherent account of the case. Integration is analysis.

Integrate by:

- weaving evidence sources into a coherent narrative;
- showing how each source contributes to the account;
- acknowledging where evidence is thin or conflicting;
- not letting one source dominate without justification.

A case study that lists findings by source, never integrating them, has not completed the analysis. The integration is where the case becomes intelligible.

### Match Evidence To The Level Of Claim

Claims must be proportionate to the evidence. Overclaiming is the most common threat.

Match by:

- claims supported by convergent evidence stated with confidence;
- claims based on a single source hedged appropriately;
- distinguishing what the case establishes from what it suggests;
- acknowledging what the evidence cannot address.

A sweeping claim from thin or single-source evidence invites deserved skepticism. Matching claim to evidence is central to credibility.

## Common Traps

### Single-Source Case Study

Relying on one source, usually interviews, forfeits the triangulation that defines case study research.

### Parallel Data Never Integrated

Gathering multiple sources but never triangulating them leaves evidence in silos and analysis incomplete.

### No Formal Database

Evidence existing only in the narrative cannot be audited and undermines trustworthiness.

### Broken Chain Of Evidence

Conclusions that cannot be traced back to specific data are assertions, not findings.

### Smoothing Over Divergence

Reconciling conflicting sources by privileging one hides the divergence that is often the most analytically valuable finding.

### Assuming Source Transparency

Treating any source as a transparent window ignores its characteristic biases and weaknesses.

### Overclaiming From Thin Evidence

Extending claims beyond what the convergent evidence supports overstates the contribution and invites skepticism.

## Self-Check

- Are multiple sources of evidence used by design, selected to bear on different facets of the question?
- Is a formal case database maintained, separating raw evidence from interpretation?
- Is a chain of evidence built, linking research questions to data to conclusions, traceable in both directions?
- Is triangulation used for both convergence and divergence, with divergence treated as data?
- Is each source understood in terms of its characteristic strengths and weaknesses?
- Is the authenticity and quality of evidence established, with key facts corroborated?
- Are evidence sources integrated into a coherent case narrative, not left in parallel?
- Are claims matched to the strength of the evidence, with single-source claims hedged?
- Are conflicting sources examined for what they reveal rather than smoothed away?
- Could a reader follow the evidence and challenge the conclusions?
