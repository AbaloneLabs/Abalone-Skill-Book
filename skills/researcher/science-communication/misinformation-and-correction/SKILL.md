# Misinformation And Correction

Correcting misinformation is harder than it appears. Naive corrections often fail or even strengthen the false belief, because repeating a myth to debunk it can reinforce it, and confrontational corrections provoke defensiveness. Agents frequently assume that presenting facts will change minds, then find that their careful debunking backfires or gets ignored. Effective correction is a skill rooted in understanding how people process information, identity, and trust, not merely in having the right facts.

The harm this skill prevents is the amplification of the very misinformation being countered, the alienation of audiences through condescension, and the erosion of the scientist's credibility as a trusted source. The agent has freedom in tone and strategy, but the evidence-based principles of effective correction are binding.

## Core Rules

### Decide Whether Correction Is Worthwhile

Not every piece of misinformation deserves a response. Engaging can amplify the falsehood, lend it legitimacy, or waste effort on an audience that will not shift. Assess whether correction serves a purpose before acting.

Assess:

- is the misinformation reaching a large or influential audience;
- would silence allow the false claim to become entrenched;
- is there a realistic chance the correction will land;
- are you the right messenger for this audience;
- will engaging amplify the falsehood more than it helps.

Sometimes the best response is no response; sometimes it is essential.

### Lead With The Truth, Not The Myth

The "continued influence effect" means that repeating a myth, even to debunk it, reinforces familiarity with it. Effective corrections present the accurate fact first and prominently, with the myth mentioned only briefly if at all.

Structure corrections:

- state the accurate information clearly and first;
- warn that misinformation exists if necessary, but do not dwell;
- keep the myth reference brief and clearly labeled as false;
- repeat the accurate version more than the false one;
- make the truth the memorable takeaway.

### Avoid The Backfire Effect Through Respectful Framing

Confrontational, mocking, or condescending corrections trigger defensiveness, especially when the belief is tied to identity or group affiliation. People process information through the lens of who they trust and who they feel respects them.

Respectful correction:

- avoid belittling those who hold the misconception;
- acknowledge the understandable reasons for the confusion;
- frame correction as shared pursuit of accuracy;
- use "we" and inclusive language;
- separate the idea from the person holding it.

### Provide A Coherent Alternative Explanation

People hold onto a false belief partly because it explains something. A correction that merely negates leaves a gap. Providing a compelling, accurate alternative explanation helps the true account replace the false one.

Build the alternative:

- explain why the misconception seemed plausible;
- offer the accurate mechanism or account;
- make the true explanation as coherent and memorable as the myth;
- use concrete examples and evidence;
- address the underlying concern the myth spoke to.

### Use Trusted Messengers And Appropriate Channels

Who delivers a correction matters as much as what is said. Audiences accept information from sources they trust and reject even accurate information from perceived opponents. Match the messenger to the audience.

Consider messenger:

- are you a trusted source for this specific audience;
- would a community insider or peer be more effective;
- does the channel reach the people who hold the misconception;
- is a private or public correction more effective here;
- can institutions or coalitions amplify trusted voices.

### Distinguish Honest Confusion From Bad-Faith Propaganda

Some misinformation spreads through genuine misunderstanding; some is deliberately propagated by actors with incentives to deceive. The response differs. Honest confusion responds to evidence and respectful engagement; bad-faith propaganda often seeks to provoke, exhaust, or launder legitimacy.

Differentiate response:

- for honest confusion: educate, provide resources, engage patiently;
- for bad-faith actors: consider whether engagement legitimizes them;
- avoid being drawn into endless debate designed to exhaust;
- document and expose coordinated deception where appropriate;
- protect your time and mental health.

### Prebunk Where Possible

It is easier to prevent misinformation from taking root than to correct it once believed. Prebunking inoculates audiences by warning them about manipulation techniques and false claims before they encounter them.

Prebunk by:

- explaining common misleading tactics in the domain;
- warning about specific false claims likely to circulate;
- teaching audiences how to evaluate sources;
- building media literacy proactively;
- establishing trust before controversy erupts.

### Maintain Credibility Through Accuracy And Humility

Your authority as a corrector rests on your own accuracy. Overstating certainty, dismissing legitimate uncertainty, or being caught in error destroys the trust needed for correction to work.

Maintain credibility:

- correct only what you can support with evidence;
- acknowledge genuine scientific uncertainty;
- admit when you do not know;
- update your own positions when evidence changes;
- avoid claiming more authority than the evidence grants.

## Common Traps

### Repeating The Myth Prominently To Debunk It

Dwelling on the falsehood reinforces familiarity. Lead with and emphasize the truth.

### Condescending Or Mocking Tone

Belittling those who hold a misconception triggers defensiveness and entrenches the belief. Engage respectfully.

### Negating Without Offering An Alternative

Saying "that is wrong" without explaining why leaves a gap. Provide a coherent accurate explanation.

### Engaging Every Falsehood And Getting Exhausted

Not all misinformation warrants response. Choose battles strategically to protect effectiveness and wellbeing.

### Assuming Facts Alone Change Minds

Information processing is shaped by identity, trust, and emotion, not just evidence. Address those dimensions.

### Being The Wrong Messenger For The Audience

Audiences reject accurate information from distrusted sources. Match messenger to audience.

### Overstating Your Own Certainty

Claiming more than the evidence supports undermines the credibility needed to correct others. Stay accurate and humble.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Have you assessed whether correction is worthwhile or would amplify the falsehood?
- [ ] Does the correction lead with and emphasize the accurate information, not the myth?
- [ ] Is the tone respectful, avoiding condescension toward those holding the misconception?
- [ ] Does the correction provide a coherent alternative explanation, not just negation?
- [ ] Is the messenger appropriate and trusted for the target audience?
- [ ] Have you distinguished honest confusion from bad-faith propaganda in choosing a response?
- [ ] Are prebunking strategies used where prevention is possible?
- [ ] Is your own correction accurate, with genuine uncertainty acknowledged?
- [ ] Have you addressed the underlying concern or identity the misinformation spoke to?
- [ ] Are you protecting your time and credibility rather than engaging exhaustively?
