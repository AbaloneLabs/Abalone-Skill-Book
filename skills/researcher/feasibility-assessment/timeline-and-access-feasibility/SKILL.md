# Timeline And Access Feasibility

Timeline and access feasibility asks whether the study can actually reach its participants, sites, and data within the time available, given the lead times and access barriers that govern real research. It is distinct from scientific merit and from the separate question of whether the study is affordable. A study can be well funded and well designed and still be infeasible because the approvals take longer than the funding window, the target population is inaccessible in the planned season, the single recruiting site has already committed its patients to a competing trial, or the data source requires an access agreement that will not be signed in time.

Agents tend to build timelines as if the only work is the data collection itself, and to treat access as something that will be granted because it was requested. Both assumptions fail routinely. Approval timelines are the most common cause of slipped starts, and access is the most common cause of stalled recruitment. The harm of an infeasible timeline is concrete. A study that starts six months late into a two-year grant has already lost a quarter of its time and may not complete follow-up before the money ends. A study that cannot reach its population recruits slowly, enrolls past the season when the condition is present, and either abandons participants mid-study or produces data too sparse to answer the question. Either way, participant time and trust, and scarce funds, are wasted.

This skill covers the schedule, lead-time, and access dimension of feasibility. It is planning guidance, not a substitute for institutional, grants office, or IRB consultation. Launching a study on an infeasible timeline wastes research funds, exposes participants to procedures for a study that may not finish, and erodes the community trust on which future recruitment depends. For high-stakes, multi-site, clinical, or time-sensitive cases, confirm timeline and access judgments with a methodologist, the regulatory or sponsored-programs office, and the IRB before committing.

## Core Rules

### Separate Timeline Feasibility From Scientific And Budget Feasibility

Be explicit that this assessment answers whether the study can be done in the time and with the access available, not whether it is worth doing or affordable. The three dimensions interact, but each must be tested on its own. A study that is affordable and important but cannot be completed in the funding window is timeline-infeasible and must be redesigned, extended, or not started. State the timeline feasibility question in operational terms, such as whether approvals, recruitment, and follow-up can all complete before the grant ends.

### Map The Approval And Regulatory Lead Times First

Approvals are usually the longest and most underestimated component of a timeline. Before any optimistic schedule is drawn, map every gate that must open before enrollment. This typically includes IRB or research ethics committee review, scientific review, data safety monitoring setup, institutional biosafety or radiation committee review, data use agreements and material transfer agreements, site-specific approvals for each recruiting site, clinical trial registration, and any funder or sponsor sign-off. Obtain a realistic estimate for each from the relevant office, not a guess, because review cycles and revisions commonly add weeks or months. If a study needs approval at multiple sites, model the slowest site plus revisions, not the fastest. Build the timeline from these lead times backward from the desired start, not forward from an optimistic wish.

### Test Site And Investigator Access Realistically

Access to sites and investigators is a feasibility constraint as hard as money. For each potential site, confirm whether it is genuinely available, whether the principal investigator has capacity and interest, whether the site has the staff and rooms to run the protocol, and critically whether the site has already committed its eligible patients to a competing study. Patient pools at a single clinic are finite, and overlapping studies cannibalize each other. Do not count a site as available on the strength of a verbal expression of interest. A written commitment, a budget, and a confirmed recruitment projection are required before a site enters the timeline. Where access depends on a gatekeeper such as a department head, health system, or community leader, secure that access in writing and budget the time to obtain it.

### Test Participant Access And Recruitment Realistically

Participant access is where most timelines collapse. Estimate the eligible population and the realistic fraction that can be reached, will consent, and will complete. Ground the estimate in local data or published experience at similar settings, not in hope. Account for the referral pathway, whether participants self-present, are referred by clinicians, or must be found through outreach, and the friction at each step. If the population is rare, geographically dispersed, holds distrust toward research, or is sought by competing studies, recruitment will be slower than the headline prevalence suggests. Convert the eligible pool into an expected enrollment per month and check whether that rate can deliver the sample within the recruitment window. If not, the timeline is infeasible as designed.

### Account For Seasonal, Cyclical, And Historical Constraints

Many studies are constrained by calendars that are invisible in a generic Gantt chart. Seasonal conditions such as respiratory illness, malaria, or allergy appear only part of the year, so a study that finishes recruitment just after the season ends waits a full year. Academic calendars govern student and school-based research. Holidays, exam periods, harvest cycles, and religious observances close recruitment channels. Historical context matters too. A population that was recently over-researched, or that experienced a breach of trust, or that is the subject of active political attention, may be inaccessible regardless of the protocol. Map these constraints onto the timeline and confirm that the recruitment window overlaps the period when participants are actually present and reachable.

### Account For Data Source And Record Access Constraints

When the study depends on existing data or records, the access agreement is often the longest pole in the timeline. Data use agreements, privacy review, honest broker arrangements, linkage approvals across registries, and extracts from electronic health records each have their own queues and revision cycles. A study that assumes the data will be available on day one commonly discovers it is available six months in, after multiple revisions to the data use agreement. Identify every external data source, the agreement required for each, the expected turnaround, and whether the data are complete enough for the planned analysis. Build the extraction, linkage, and cleaning time into the schedule.

### Build Milestones With Float And A Realistic Critical Path

Construct the timeline as a critical path, not a list of parallel hopes. Identify the longest chain of dependent tasks, where each cannot start until the previous finishes, and confirm that this chain fits within the funding window with float to spare. Float is the slack that absorbs the inevitable slips. A timeline with zero float is infeasible by construction, because any single delay breaks the end date. Place explicit milestones at approval, first site activated, first participant enrolled, 50 percent recruitment, last enrollment, and last follow-up, and attach dates that reflect realistic lead times and recruitment rates. Where milestones are sequential across sites, model the slowest site as the driver.

### Plan Contingencies For Schedule And Access Failures

Feasibility is not the absence of delay. It is having a credible response to the delays that will occur. For each high-impact schedule risk, define a contingency. If IRB approval slips by two review cycles, can recruitment start at an already-approved site, or is the season lost. If the lead site withdraws, are backup sites identified and budgeted. If recruitment runs at half the projected rate, is there funding and time to extend, or does the sample shrink with a documented power analysis, or does the study stop. A study with no contingency for its critical-path risks is not feasible, it is merely lucky so far.

### Treat Timeline Infeasibility As An Ethical Issue

A study that cannot finish within its real timeline is an ethical problem, not just a logistical one. Participants consent on the understanding that their contribution will help produce an answer. If follow-up will be cut short when the grant ends, or if recruitment will drag on so long that the question is no longer relevant, the consent is partly false and the participant exposure to inconvenience or risk is wasted. Slow, stalled studies also tie up sites and participants that could have served better-designed research. Assess timeline feasibility before enrollment, and treat a study that cannot realistically finish as one that should be redesigned, resourced for extension, or not started.

## Common Traps

### Drawing The Timeline From Data Collection Alone

Building the schedule as if enrollment and follow-up are the only work ignores the approvals, agreements, and setup that precede them. The result is a study that starts months late. Always build the timeline backward from the regulatory and access lead times.

### Assuming Approval Takes One Cycle

Modeling IRB or multi-site approval as a single quick pass ignores revisions, stipulations, and full-board resubmissions that add weeks or months per cycle. Use the office's realistic estimate and model at least one revision, especially for sensitive or multi-site studies.

### Counting A Site As Available On Verbal Interest

A clinic that expressed interest is not a recruiting site. Competing studies, lack of staff or rooms, or a withdrawn investigator commonly remove sites that were assumed available. Require a written commitment and a recruitment projection before a site enters the timeline.

### Using Headline Prevalence As The Recruitment Rate

Dividing the population by the recruitment window assumes everyone eligible is reached, consents, and completes. Real funnels lose most of the pool at each step. Convert the eligible pool into a realistic monthly enrollment using local or published data.

### Ignoring Seasonal And Calendar Windows

A study that recruits just outside the season for its condition waits a year. Holidays, academic calendars, and over-researched periods close channels that look open on a generic chart. Map the real calendar onto the recruitment window.

### Assuming Data Access Will Be Granted Quickly

Data use agreements, linkage, and registry extracts routinely take months and several revisions. Assuming the data arrive on day one guarantees a stalled analysis phase. Identify each external source and model its real turnaround.

### Zero-Float Critical Paths

A timeline where every task is back-to-back with no slack breaks on the first delay. Build float into the critical path and treat a zero-float plan as infeasible.

### No Stopping Or Redesign Rule For Stalled Recruitment

Continuing to recruit at a fraction of the projected rate, burning time and funds without a decision point, turns a slow study into a failed one with no clean exit. Define in advance the recruitment rate below which the study is redesigned, extended, or stopped.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the timeline feasibility question stated separately from scientific merit and budget feasibility, with operational criteria?
- [ ] Are all approval and regulatory lead times (IRB, scientific, biosafety, data use, site, registration) mapped with realistic estimates from the relevant offices, including revisions?
- [ ] Is each site confirmed with written commitment, staff and room capacity, and a check for competing studies, rather than verbal interest?
- [ ] Is participant recruitment modeled as a realistic monthly enrollment from local or published funnel data, not headline prevalence?
- [ ] Are seasonal, academic, holiday, and historical trust constraints mapped onto the recruitment window?
- [ ] Are external data sources identified with their access agreements, turnaround, and completeness checked?
- [ ] Does the timeline have a critical path with float, and explicit milestones for approval, first site, first and last enrollment, and last follow-up?
- [ ] Is there a concrete contingency for each high-impact schedule or access risk, including backup sites and an extension or stopping rule?
- [ ] Is a study that cannot realistically finish within its timeline treated as one to redesign, resource for extension, or not start, for ethical reasons?
- [ ] For high-stakes, multi-site, clinical, or time-sensitive cases, does the output recommend confirming timeline and access feasibility with a methodologist, the regulatory or sponsored-programs office, and the IRB before committing?
