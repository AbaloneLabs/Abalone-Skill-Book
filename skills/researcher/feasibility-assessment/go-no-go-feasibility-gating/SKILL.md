# Go/No-Go Feasibility Gating

Go/no-go feasibility gating is the structured decision process that converts feasibility evidence into an explicit proceed, revise, or stop judgment before a study commits participants and funds. It is the point where the separate assessments of resources, budget, timeline, and access must be integrated and a decision rendered, rather than left to drift. Without gating, feasibility analysis becomes a document that everyone reads and no one acts on, and the study launches by default because no one was forced to say no.

Agents tend to treat the feasibility decision as a single subjective judgment made at the end, when it should be a set of predefined, measurable criteria agreed before the evidence is in. The most damaging blind spot is the absence of a stopping rule. A study that misses its recruitment target, or whose cost-per-participant is climbing, continues by inertia because there is no predefined threshold that triggers a revise-or-stop decision, and because stopping feels like failure. The result is money and participant time consumed by a study that was infeasible from the point the first milestone was missed. A second blind spot is conflating the feasibility decision with the efficacy decision, so that promising early data overrides a missed feasibility threshold and a study that cannot answer its question keeps running.

This skill covers the decision and gating dimension of feasibility. It is planning guidance, not a substitute for institutional, methodologist, or IRB consultation. Proceeding with an infeasible study wastes research funds, exposes participants to procedures and follow-up that will not yield usable knowledge, and damages trust in the institution and the research enterprise. Stopping or revising a study that fails its gates is not failure; it is the responsible use of scarce resources. For high-stakes, clinical, multi-site, or uncertain cases, confirm the gating criteria and the decision with a methodologist, the data safety monitoring board where relevant, and the IRB before committing and at each gate.

## Core Rules

### Define The Gate Decision As Proceed, Revise, Or Stop

Frame the feasibility decision as three explicit outcomes, not a binary go. Proceed means the study meets its feasibility criteria and can enroll as designed. Revise means the study is conditionally feasible but the design must change, such as a smaller sample with a documented power analysis, fewer sites, a longer timeline, additional funding, or a simplified protocol, after which it is re-gated. Stop means the study is infeasible and should not proceed in any form, and the honest answer is to not start. Forcing a three-way choice prevents the default of launching a marginal study unchanged because revise and stop felt uncomfortable.

### Set Predefined, Measurable Go And No-Go Criteria Before The Evidence

The core of gating is that the criteria are written before the evidence is gathered, not after. For each feasibility dimension, define a threshold that separates proceed from revise from stop. Recruitment might require reaching a minimum enrollment rate by a fixed date. Retention might require keeping a minimum fraction of enrolled participants through the primary outcome. Cost might require the running cost-per-participant to stay below a ceiling. Access might require a minimum number of sites activated and producing by a milestone. Timeline might require first-patient-in by a date that preserves the season and the funding window. Predefining the criteria prevents moving the goalposts when the numbers disappoint, which is the mechanism by which infeasible studies keep running.

### Use Traffic-Light Thresholds, Not Single Lines

A single pass-or-fail line is too brittle for feasibility, because evidence is noisy and dimensions interact. Use traffic-light thresholds. Green means the dimension is on track and supports proceed. Amber means the dimension is borderline and triggers a mandatory revision or a re-gate at the next checkpoint, not silent continuation. Red means the dimension has failed its threshold and triggers a stop-or-fundamentally-revise decision. Define the thresholds numerically where possible, such as enrollment above, within, or below a band around the target rate, so that the color is determined by the data rather than by impression.

### Integrate The Dimensions Into A Composite Decision

The dimensions are not independent votes. A study can be green on budget and red on recruitment, and the recruitment failure can make the budget green irrelevant because the sample will never answer the question. Integrate the dimensions by asking which are on the critical path and which failures are recoverable. A red on a recoverable dimension with a credible contingency may permit a revise. A red on an unrecoverable dimension, such as a population that is genuinely inaccessible, should drive a stop regardless of how green the other dimensions are. Make the integration explicit and recorded, not implicit.

### Stage The Gates Across The Study Life Cycle

Feasibility gating is not a single event at launch. Stage gates across the life cycle. A pre-launch gate confirms that approvals, funding, sites, and access are in place before the first participant. An early-implementation gate, typically after the first participants or the first sites activate, tests whether real-world recruitment, retention, and cost match the projections. A mid-study gate tests whether the trends hold once the study is fully ramped. Each gate re-applies the predefined criteria and forces a proceed, revise, or stop. Staging catches the infeasibility that only becomes visible once execution begins, which is most of it.

### Distinguish Feasibility Gates From Efficacy And Futility Analyses

Keep the feasibility decision separate from the question of whether the intervention works. A feasibility gate is about whether the study machinery can run, measured by recruitment, retention, protocol fidelity, and cost. An efficacy or futility analysis is about the treatment effect and is governed by its own statistical plan and usually by a data safety monitoring board. Conflating them is dangerous. Promising early outcome data must not override a failed feasibility threshold, because a study that cannot recruit or retain cannot produce a valid answer regardless of how the early data look. Conversely, a study that meets every feasibility gate but shows futility under its statistical plan stops for futility, not for infeasibility. Keep the two decision streams separate and documented.

### Define The Stopping And Revision Rules And Who Decides

For each red and amber outcome, define in advance what happens and who decides. Specify the stopping rule, the conditions under which the study is halted, and the revision options, such as extending the timeline, adding sites, reducing scope, or securing more funding. Name the decision-maker, which may be the steering committee, the sponsor, the data safety monitoring board, or the IRB, and define the quorum and the escalation path. A gate with no named decision-maker and no defined response is not a gate; it is a status report. Predefine the response so that a red threshold produces action, not a meeting that defers the decision.

### Record The Decision And Its Justification

Every gate produces a recorded decision with its evidence and justification, dated and signed by the decision-maker. Record which dimensions were green, amber, or red, the underlying numbers, the criteria applied, the resulting proceed, revise, or stop, and the rationale where judgment was exercised. Recording serves three purposes. It forces the decision to be explicit rather than drifting. It creates accountability for moving the goalposts, because any deviation from the predefined criteria is visible. And it provides the audit trail that funders, the IRB, and future reviewers expect, and that protects participants and the institution if the study is later questioned.

### Treat The No-Go Decision As A Responsible Outcome

A decision to revise or stop is not a failure of the investigators. It is the success of a feasibility process that prevented wasted participant exposure and wasted funds. Frame gating so that stopping an infeasible study is a legitimate, respected outcome, and so that the absence of a stopping rule is the real failure. Continuing an infeasible study out of reluctance to stop is the outcome that harms participants, wastes resources, and damages trust. Build the culture and the documentation so that a well-justified no-go is recorded as sound research practice.

## Common Traps

### No Predefined Criteria, So The Goalposts Move

When thresholds are set after the evidence arrives, disappointing numbers get reinterpreted as acceptable and the study continues. This is the core mechanism of infeasible studies that keep running. Define the criteria before the evidence and write them down.

### Binary Go Only, No Revise Or Stop

Forcing only a proceed-or-not choice pushes marginal studies into a binary launch, because stopping feels drastic. The revise option is where most conditional feasibility should land. Always allow a three-way decision.

### Conflating Feasibility With Efficacy

Letting promising early outcome data override a missed recruitment or retention threshold keeps a study alive that cannot answer its question. Keep feasibility gates and efficacy or futility analyses in separate decision streams.

### Single Pass/Fail Lines Instead Of Bands

A single threshold treats noise as failure and invites goalpost-moving. Traffic-light bands make the amber zone a mandatory revision trigger rather than a judgment call, which is more honest and more defensible.

### Amber That Silently Becomes Green

An amber threshold is meant to trigger action, but in practice it often becomes silent continuation with a note to watch it. Define amber as a mandatory re-gate or revision at the next checkpoint so that borderline dimensions cannot drift into green without a decision.

### No Named Decision-Maker Or Response

A gate that reports status without naming who decides and what happens next is not a gate. Predefine the decision-maker, the quorum, and the stopping or revision rule for each red and amber outcome.

### One-Time Gate At Launch Only

Most infeasibility appears only once execution begins, so a single launch gate misses it. Stage gates across the life cycle, especially an early-implementation gate after the first participants and sites activate.

### Reluctance To Stop As Failure

Treating a no-go as a personal or institutional failure creates pressure to continue infeasible studies. Reframe a well-justified stop as responsible stewardship of participants and funds, and the absence of a stopping rule as the real failure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the gate decision framed as three explicit outcomes, proceed, revise, or stop, rather than a binary go?
- [ ] Are predefined, measurable go and no-go criteria written for each feasibility dimension before the evidence is gathered?
- [ ] Are traffic-light thresholds, green, amber, and red, defined numerically so the color is set by data rather than impression?
- [ ] Is the composite decision integrated across dimensions, with critical-path and recoverability reasoning made explicit?
- [ ] Are gates staged across the life cycle, including a pre-launch gate and an early-implementation gate after the first participants and sites?
- [ ] Are feasibility gates kept separate from efficacy and futility analyses, with no override of a failed feasibility threshold by promising outcome data?
- [ ] Is a stopping rule and a set of revision options defined for each red and amber outcome, with a named decision-maker and escalation path?
- [ ] Is every gate decision recorded with its evidence, criteria, outcome, rationale, date, and sign-off, creating an auditable trail?
- [ ] Is a no-go or revise decision treated as a responsible outcome, with the absence of a stopping rule identified as the real failure?
- [ ] For high-stakes, clinical, multi-site, or uncertain cases, does the output recommend confirming the gating criteria and the decision with a methodologist, the data safety monitoring board where relevant, and the IRB?
