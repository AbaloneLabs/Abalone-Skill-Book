# Resource And Budget Feasibility

Feasibility is the question of whether a study can actually be done with the resources, time, access, and money available. It is distinct from whether the study is scientifically worthwhile or whether the intervention works. A study that is elegant in design but infeasible in resources is a study that will fail partway through, and the failure is rarely clean. Recruitment stalls, the budget runs out before follow-up is complete, a single expensive assay consumes the lab budget, the data system cannot handle the volume, and the team is left with partial data, abandoned participants, wasted funds, and a damaged institutional reputation.

Agents tend to treat budgeting as a back-office task of listing line items, when it is in fact a design constraint that should reshape the study. The most common and damaging blind spot is optimism bias applied to cost and resource estimates. Costs are estimated at best case, attrition is ignored, indirect costs and overhead are forgotten, equipment is assumed to be free because it already exists, and the cost of failure and rework is never budgeted. The result is a study that looks affordable on paper and collapses in execution. A second blind spot is conflating feasibility with efficacy, treating a small pilot as if it were testing whether the intervention works rather than whether the study machinery can run.

This skill covers the resource and budget dimension of feasibility. It is planning guidance, not a substitute for institutional, grant office, or IRB consultation. Launching an infeasible study wastes scarce research funds, consumes participant time and trust without producing usable results, can expose participants to risk for no scientific benefit, and can damage the relationship between the institution and the community. For high-stakes, clinical, or uncertain cases, confirm resource and budget judgments with a methodologist, the sponsored-programs or grants office, and the IRB before committing.

## Core Rules

### Separate Feasibility From Efficacy And Scientific Value

Before costing anything, be explicit about what question the feasibility assessment answers. Resource and budget feasibility asks whether the study can be run with the available inputs. It does not ask whether the intervention is effective, whether the hypothesis is correct, or whether the effect size is large enough to matter. A pilot whose purpose is to test feasibility should not be sized or budgeted as if it were testing efficacy. Mixing the two inflates the sample, the cost, and the risk, and it produces a study that satisfies neither goal. State the feasibility question in operational terms, such as whether the team can recruit and retain 80 participants, deliver the protocol with fidelity, and capture the primary outcome at the planned rate.

### Build A Bottom-Up Cost Model Around Cost-Per-Participant

The most reliable budget structure is bottom-up and participant-driven. Estimate the fully loaded cost of recruiting, consenting, treating or interviewing, assessing, and following a single participant, then multiply by the target sample and add fixed costs. The cost-per-participant must include every touchpoint. Recruitment advertising, screening and eligibility verification, consent time, each study visit or contact, the staff time per visit, the materials or drugs consumed, the assays or tests run, data capture, and the incentive or reimbursement paid to the participant. Many budgets omit screening costs for people who screen fail, but those costs are real and can dominate when eligibility is narrow. Build the model so that if the sample changes, the budget recalculates.

### Include The Full Personnel And Capacity Picture

Personnel is usually the largest cost and the most underestimated constraint. List every role and the fraction of full-time effort required, not just the principal investigator. Study coordinators, research nurses, recruiters, data managers, statisticians, lab technicians, interpreters, and administrative support all carry real time. Check whether each role has the capacity for the planned effort given their other commitments. A coordinator who is nominally assigned at 20 percent but is already fully loaded on three other studies is not actually available. Capacity is a feasibility constraint as real as money. Also account for training time, onboarding, and the learning curve before a site reaches full throughput.

### Cost Equipment, Materials, And Facilities Realistically

Equipment and materials are often assumed free because they exist, but existing equipment has opportunity cost, maintenance cost, calibration cost, and limited throughput. For each piece of equipment, decide whether it is purchased, leased, or shared, and cost the consumables, calibration, and downtime. Facilities include clinic rooms, lab benches, interview spaces, and secure storage for biospecimens or data. If the study needs a room two days a week and the room is shared, the contention is a feasibility risk. For interventions requiring drugs, devices, or specialized kits, cost procurement, storage (including cold chain), dispensing, and disposal or return. Never assume a donated or discounted resource will remain available for the full study without a written agreement.

### Size And Cost The Data Infrastructure

Data systems are routinely under-budgeted and are a frequent cause of feasibility failure. Cost the electronic data capture platform, licensing per user or per study, database build and validation time, the programmer or data manager who builds the forms and edit checks, and the secure storage and backup. If the study collects sensitive data, add the cost of encryption, access controls, audit logging, and possibly a separate secure environment. If data come from multiple sites or instruments, add integration and reconciliation effort. Budget for the data flow at the end too, including lock, archive, and the retention period required by the funder or regulator. A study that can collect data but cannot afford to clean, store, and retain it is not feasible.

### Add Overhead, Indirect Costs, And Contingency

Direct participant-level costs are only part of the budget. Add institutional overhead or facilities and administrative costs at the negotiated rate, which can be a large fraction of direct costs and is non-negotiable for most funders. Add costs that are easy to forget, including publication fees, translation and interpretation, participant travel reimbursement, monitoring visits, audit support, and regulatory filings. Then add contingency. A flat contingency of 15 to 25 percent is a reasonable starting point, but the right number depends on the volatility of the estimates. Recruitment that depends on an uncertain referral stream, or a cost dominated by a single reagent whose price fluctuates, warrants more contingency. Contingency is not optional optimism. It is the explicit acknowledgment that estimates are uncertain.

### Rank Resource Risks And Plan Contingencies For Each

Not every resource risk is equal. Rank them by likelihood and impact. A single expensive assay that is 40 percent of the budget and depends on one supplier is a higher-impact risk than a modest travel cost. For each high-ranked risk, define a concrete contingency, not a vague hope. If the assay price rises, is there a validated alternative, or does the sample size shrink, or does the study stop. If a key staff member leaves, is there cross-training. If the funder cuts the budget, which arm or outcome is dropped first. Feasibility is not the absence of risk. It is having a credible plan for the risks that matter.

### Reconcile The Budget Against The Funding Envelope Explicitly

The feasibility decision is the comparison of the bottom-up cost against the funding envelope. State both numbers and the gap explicitly. If the cost exceeds the envelope, the study is not feasible as designed, and the design must change before participants are enrolled. Do not paper over the gap by trimming contingency, dropping overhead, or assuming in-kind contributions that are not confirmed in writing. Reducing the sample to fit the budget may destroy statistical power, which converts a funded but underpowered study into an unethical use of participants. The honest options are to secure more funding, reduce scope deliberately, simplify the protocol, lengthen the timeline, or decide not to proceed.

### Treat Resource Infeasibility As An Ethical Issue

A study that cannot be completed with the resources committed is not merely a management problem. It is an ethical one. Participants consent to contribute to knowledge that the study, if completed, would produce. If the study will run out of money before follow-up, the consent is partly false, the participant time and any exposure to risk are wasted, and the community trust on which future research depends is eroded. Resource feasibility should therefore be assessed before, not after, IRB submission, and a study judged infeasible should be redesigned or not started rather than launched on a hope.

## Common Traps

### Optimism Bias On Cost And Recruitment

Estimating costs and recruitment at best case is the single most common feasibility failure. Costs come in low, recruitment comes in high, and attrition is assumed zero. The study then runs out of money or participants halfway through. Always model a realistic and a pessimistic scenario, and base the go decision on whether the study survives the pessimistic case.

### Forgetting Screening And Attrition Costs

Budgets often cost only enrolled participants. But every screen failure consumed staff time, advertising, and possibly a clinic visit, and every dropout consumed the cost of enrollment plus partial follow-up. When eligibility is narrow or attrition is high, these costs can exceed the cost of completed participants. Cost the funnel, not just the endpoint.

### Omitting Overhead And Indirect Costs

Skipping the negotiated indirect cost rate because it makes the budget look too large does not make the cost disappear. The institution will still charge it, and the gap will appear mid-study. Always include indirect costs at the real rate.

### Assuming Existing Equipment And Staff Are Free

Equipment that already exists still has maintenance, calibration, consumable, and opportunity costs, and staff who already exist are usually already committed. Treating existing resources as zero-cost hides the true cost and the true capacity constraint. Cost them at their real loaded value.

### Conflating Feasibility With Pilot Efficacy

Designing a pilot to detect a treatment effect inflates the sample and the budget and muddies the purpose. A feasibility pilot should be sized to estimate recruitment, retention, and procedural success rates, not to prove efficacy. Keep the questions separate so the budget matches the actual goal.

### Single-Supplier Or Single-Person Dependency

A budget dominated by one reagent supplier, one assay lab, or one indispensable coordinator is fragile. If that node fails, the study cannot run, yet the contingency plan is often absent. Identify single points of failure in the resource model and define a fallback for each.

### Under-Budgeting Data And Regulatory Work

The cost of building, validating, cleaning, storing, and archiving the database, plus regulatory and monitoring support, is consistently underestimated. A study that can collect data but cannot afford to manage it produces unusable data. Budget the full data and regulatory lifecycle.

### Trimming Contingency To Fit The Envelope

When the bottom-up cost exceeds the funding envelope, the temptation is to delete contingency to make the numbers match. This removes exactly the buffer that uncertainty will consume, guaranteeing a mid-study shortfall. If the gap is real, change the design or the funding, not the contingency.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the feasibility question stated separately from efficacy and scientific value, with operational success criteria?
- [ ] Does the budget use a bottom-up cost-per-participant model that includes recruitment, screening, each visit, materials, assays, data capture, and incentives?
- [ ] Are personnel listed by role and effort fraction, with capacity checked against existing commitments and training time included?
- [ ] Are equipment, materials, and facilities costed for purchase or lease, consumables, calibration, maintenance, and throughput contention?
- [ ] Is the data infrastructure fully costed, including build, validation, secure storage, integration, and retention archive?
- [ ] Are indirect costs, overhead, and forgotten line items such as publication, translation, monitoring, and regulatory filings included?
- [ ] Is a contingency of roughly 15 to 25 percent added, sized to the volatility of the largest estimates?
- [ ] Are resource risks ranked by likelihood and impact, with a concrete contingency defined for each high-ranked risk?
- [ ] Is the bottom-up cost explicitly reconciled against the funding envelope, with the gap stated and the design changed rather than the contingency trimmed?
- [ ] For high-stakes, clinical, or uncertain cases, does the output recommend confirming resource and budget feasibility with a methodologist, the grants or sponsored-programs office, and the IRB before committing?
