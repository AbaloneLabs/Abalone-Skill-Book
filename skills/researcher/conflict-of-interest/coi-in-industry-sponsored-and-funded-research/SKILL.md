# COI In Industry-Sponsored And Funded Research

Industry funding is a powerful engine of research, especially in medicine, pharmaceuticals, devices, and agriculture. It is also one of the most consistent sources of bias in the published record. The judgment problem is not whether to accept industry funding but how to preserve research integrity when the sponsor has a direct financial stake in the outcome. Researchers often underestimate sponsor influence because it operates through contracts, data access, and editorial control rather than overt pressure, and they often accept contract terms that quietly compromise independence. The skill covers identifying sponsor-driven risks, negotiating protective terms, and preserving the independence of design, analysis, and publication.

Use this skill when negotiating research agreements, conducting industry-sponsored studies, or assessing the integrity risks of funding. The goal is to keep the agent from accepting terms that compromise independence and to ensure that sponsor influence is recognized, limited, and disclosed.

This is a high-stakes domain because biased industry-sponsored research can distort clinical practice, regulatory decisions, and public health. When contract terms, data access, or publication rights are uncertain, the agent should consult the institution's research contracts office, legal counsel, and the ethics or conflict of interest committee rather than relying on the sponsor's assurances.

## Core Rules

### Secure Publication Rights And Editorial Independence Before Starting

The single most important protection in industry-sponsored research is the right to publish regardless of outcome. Terms agreed before the study starts determine what can be published later.

Negotiate for:

- the right to publish the results regardless of whether they favor the sponsor;
- the right to control the content and conclusions of publications;
- protection against sponsor veto or unapproved changes to findings;
- a defined, time-limited review period for the sponsor that cannot become a veto;
- publication of both positive and negative results.

Accepting a study without secured publication rights risks suppressing unfavorable findings. This protection is non-negotiable for integrity.

### Control Data Access, Analysis, And Interpretation

Sponsor influence often operates through control of data and analysis. Who holds the data, who runs the analysis, and who interprets it determines the integrity of the findings.

Secure:

- full access to all primary data, not just summary tables provided by the sponsor;
- independent ability to verify the analysis and reproduce results;
- control over the analysis plan, ideally pre-specified and registered;
- transparency about who conducted the analysis and their relationship to the sponsor;
- the right to conduct and publish secondary analyses.

Blind reliance on sponsor-provided summaries or analyses leaves the researcher unable to verify or defend the findings. Insist on access to the underlying data.

### Recognize The Forms Sponsor Influence Takes

Sponsor influence is rarely overt pressure. It operates through subtle channels that researchers may not recognize as bias.

Recognize:

- outcome selection that favors the sponsor's product;
- analysis choices, including subgroup and post hoc analyses, that shift results;
- selective reporting of favorable outcomes and suppression of unfavorable ones;
- framing of conclusions that overstates benefits or understates harms;
- ghostwriting or sponsor-controlled drafting of manuscripts;
- timing of publication or data release to serve commercial interests.

Each of these can bias the record without the researcher feeling pressured. Recognize them and resist them.

### Negotiate Research Agreements That Protect Integrity

The research agreement is where integrity protections are encoded or lost. Researchers often focus on budget and timeline and neglect the integrity clauses.

Negotiate:

- publication rights and editorial independence;
- data access and analysis control;
- intellectual property and data ownership terms;
- confidentiality terms that do not suppress results indefinitely;
- the role of the sponsor in review and comment;
- dispute resolution that does not give the sponsor a veto.

Involve the institution's contracts office and legal counsel. Do not rely on the sponsor's draft as the default.

### Disclose Funding And Sponsor Role Fully

Full disclosure of industry funding and the sponsor's role allows readers to assess the risk of bias. Partial or vague disclosure hides the relevance.

Disclose:

- the funding source and amount, current and recent;
- the sponsor's role in design, analysis, reporting, and publication;
- the financial relationships of all authors with the sponsor;
- the contractual constraints on publication, if any;
- whether the sponsor reviewed or approved the manuscript.

Vague statements such as "the sponsor had no role" should be accurate and specific. If the sponsor did have a role, say so.

### Design For Independence In Multisite And Sponsored Trials

In large sponsored trials, independence is protected by structure, not just goodwill. Governance arrangements determine integrity.

Use:

- independent steering committees with authority over design and analysis;
- independent data monitoring committees for safety and efficacy decisions;
- independent statistical analysis centers not controlled by the sponsor;
- pre-specified, registered analysis plans that limit discretion;
- transparent governance documented in the protocol.

Structural independence protects integrity even when the sponsor's interest is strong. It also protects the sponsor's credibility.

### Resist Ghostwriting And Sponsor-Controlled Drafting

Ghostwriting, in which the sponsor or a commercial writer drafts the manuscript and the academic authors lend their names, is a serious integrity violation. It hides the sponsor's role in shaping the message.

Insist that:

- named authors draft or substantively control the manuscript;
- all contributors, including sponsor employees and writers, are disclosed;
- no contributor is hidden or listed inaccurately;
- the conclusions reflect the authors' independent judgment.

Ghostwriting distorts the record by presenting sponsor-shaped messages as independent academic work. Refuse to participate in it.

## Common Traps

### Accepting A Study Without Secured Publication Rights

The most common and damaging error. Without publication rights, unfavorable findings can be suppressed.

### Relying On Sponsor-Provided Summaries Without Primary Data Access

Summary tables can hide unfavorable patterns. Insist on access to primary data for verification.

### Treating The Sponsor's Draft Agreement As The Default

The sponsor's draft protects the sponsor. Negotiate integrity protections with institutional support.

### Vague Disclosure That Hides The Sponsor's Role

"The sponsor had no role" is often inaccurate. Disclose the sponsor's actual role specifically.

### Underestimating Subtle Sponsor Influence

Outcome selection, framing, and selective reporting bias the record without overt pressure. Recognize and resist these channels.

### Participating In Ghostwriting

Lending a name to a sponsor-drafted manuscript is a serious violation. Insist on genuine authorial control.

### Assuming Structural Governance Is Unnecessary

Goodwill does not protect integrity in large trials. Structural independence through committees and pre-specified plans does.

## Self-Check

- Are publication rights and editorial independence secured in the research agreement before the study starts?
- Is there full access to primary data and the ability to independently verify and reproduce the analysis?
- Are the forms of sponsor influence, from outcome selection to framing, recognized and resisted?
- Does the research agreement protect data access, analysis control, and publication rights, negotiated with institutional support?
- Is funding and the sponsor's role disclosed fully and specifically, not vaguely?
- Are independent steering and data monitoring committees used in large sponsored trials?
- Is ghostwriting refused, with named authors maintaining genuine control over the manuscript?
- Where contract terms, data access, or publication rights are uncertain, has the institution's research contracts office, legal counsel, and the conflict of interest committee been consulted rather than relying on the sponsor's assurances?
