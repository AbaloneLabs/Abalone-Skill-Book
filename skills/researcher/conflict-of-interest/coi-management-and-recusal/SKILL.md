# COI Management And Recusal

Identifying and disclosing a conflict of interest is only the first step. The harder judgment is deciding what to do about it. Some conflicts are managed adequately by disclosure; others require restriction of roles, independent oversight, or complete recusal from decisions. Researchers often under-manage their own conflicts because management feels like an admission of wrongdoing or an inconvenience, and they often over-rely on disclosure when the conflict actually threatens the integrity of a decision. The judgment problem is matching the management response to the severity of the conflict and the vulnerability of the decision.

Use this skill when designing conflict management plans, deciding on recusal, restricting roles, and implementing oversight. The goal is to keep the agent from treating disclosure as sufficient and to ensure that substantial conflicts are actively managed.

This is a high-stakes domain because unmanaged conflicts can bias analysis, interpretation, peer review, and resource allocation, distorting the record and eroding trust. When the appropriate management is uncertain or the conflict is substantial, the agent should consult the institutional conflict of interest committee, the editor, or the review body rather than deciding alone.

## Core Rules

### Match Management To Conflict Severity And Decision Vulnerability

The right management depends on how much the conflict could influence the work and how vulnerable the specific decision is to bias.

Assess:

- the magnitude and proximity of the secondary interest;
- the discretion the conflicted person has over the decision;
- the consequences if the decision is biased;
- whether the conflict is ongoing or resolved;
- the availability of independent alternatives.

A small, indirect, resolved conflict over a low-stakes decision may need only disclosure. A large, direct, ongoing conflict over analysis, interpretation, or review requires management or recusal. Calibrate the response to the risk.

### Restrict Roles When The Conflict Touches A Vulnerable Decision

When a conflicted person controls a decision vulnerable to bias, restricting their role protects integrity without removing their contribution entirely.

Consider restricting the conflicted person from:

- primary data analysis and interpretation;
- writing the sections most affected by the conflict;
- selecting outcomes, stopping rules, or analysis decisions;
- peer review or editorial decisions on related work;
- allocation of resources, opportunities, or supervision.

Restriction targets the vulnerable decisions, not the person. The goal is to keep the conflicted interest from shaping the parts of the work most susceptible to its influence.

### Design Independent Oversight For Substantial Conflicts

When a conflict is substantial but the conflicted person's expertise is essential, independent oversight can protect integrity while preserving contribution.

Independent oversight includes:

- an independent statistician or analyst conducting or verifying analysis;
- a non-conflicted committee reviewing key decisions;
- an independent data monitoring committee for trials;
- dual review of analysis and interpretation by non-conflicted parties;
- transparent, pre-specified analysis plans that limit discretion.

Oversight reduces the discretion that a conflict could exploit. It is most valuable when expertise is irreplaceable but the conflict is real.

### Recuse When The Conflict Cannot Be Managed

Some conflicts are too substantial or too central to manage. Recusal is the appropriate response when management cannot protect integrity.

Recuse when:

- the conflict is large, direct, and ongoing;
- the conflicted person controls a decision central to the outcome;
- management options are impractical or insufficient;
- the appearance of bias would undermine credibility regardless of actual bias;
- the decision involves competitors, close collaborators, or personal relationships.

Recusal removes the conflict from the decision entirely. It is the strongest response and should be used when nothing less suffices.

### Manage Conflicts At The Team And Institutional Level

Conflicts are not only individual. A team, a department, or an institution can have conflicts that require collective management.

Address:

- the conflicts of each team member relative to their role;
- the institution's financial or reputational stake in the outcome;
- the funding source's influence over design, analysis, or reporting;
- the composition of oversight bodies to ensure independence;
- the contractual terms that might constrain publication or analysis.

Team and institutional conflicts can be harder to see because they are shared. Make them explicit and manage them collectively.

### Protect Integrity In Peer Review And Editorial Decisions

Gatekeeping roles carry special responsibility because they control what enters the record. Conflicts in review and editing must be managed strictly.

In review and editorial roles:

- decline to review work by close collaborators, recent co-authors, or direct competitors;
- disclose any relationship that could appear to bias judgment;
- recuse from editorial decisions where the conflict is substantial;
- do not use unpublished material seen in review to advantage;
- ensure that management of editorial conflicts is documented.

The power to suppress or advance others' work is great, and the obligation to manage conflicts in these roles is correspondingly strict.

### Document Management Decisions And Their Rationale

Management decisions should be documented so they can be reviewed and updated. Undocumented management is hard to verify and easy to let slip.

Document:

- the conflict and its assessed severity;
- the management plan and who approved it;
- the roles restricted and the oversight put in place;
- the recusals and their scope;
- updates when circumstances change.

Documentation makes management accountable and transparent. It also helps distinguish genuine management from cosmetic gestures.

## Common Traps

### Treating Disclosure As Sufficient Management

Disclosure informs readers; it does not remove the conflict. Substantial conflicts need active management or recusal.

### Under-managing One's Own Conflicts

Researchers tend to under-manage their own conflicts because management feels like an accusation or an inconvenience. The result is biased decisions that disclosure merely labels.

### Over-restricting And Losing Essential Expertise

Recusal or restriction can remove essential expertise. Where the conflict can be managed with oversight, prefer that over recusal that weakens the work.

### Ignoring Team And Institutional Conflicts

Shared conflicts are easy to miss because no individual owns them. Failing to manage them leaves institutional bias in place.

### Managing The Person Instead Of The Decision

Restriction should target vulnerable decisions, not exclude the person entirely. Over-broad restriction wastes expertise; under-targeted restriction leaves bias.

### Weak Oversight That Preserves Only Appearance

Oversight that is not genuinely independent, or that lacks authority, protects appearance but not integrity. Ensure oversight has real independence and real authority.

### Failing To Document Management

Undocumented management is hard to verify and easy to abandon. Document the plan, the rationale, and the updates.

## Self-Check

- Is the management response matched to the severity of the conflict and the vulnerability of the decision?
- Are roles restricted at the vulnerable decisions, preserving contribution while protecting integrity?
- Is independent oversight designed with genuine independence and authority where the conflict is substantial?
- Is recusal used when the conflict cannot be managed, including in editorial and review roles?
- Are team and institutional conflicts identified and managed, not only individual ones?
- Are peer review and editorial conflicts managed strictly, including recusal where substantial?
- Are management decisions documented with rationale and updated as circumstances change?
- Where the appropriate management is uncertain or the conflict is substantial, has the institutional conflict of interest committee, the editor, or the review body been consulted rather than deciding alone?
