# COI Identification And Disclosure

A conflict of interest exists when a researcher's secondary interests could unduly influence their primary obligation to the integrity of research. Conflicts are common and not inherently wrong; the wrong lies in failing to identify, disclose, and manage them. Researchers often recognize financial conflicts but miss personal, intellectual, and institutional ones, and they often treat disclosure as the complete response when the conflict requires management or recusal. The judgment problem is identifying the full range of conflicts, disclosing them honestly, and deciding when disclosure is insufficient.

Use this skill when identifying conflicts, preparing disclosure statements, assessing conflict severity, and deciding on management. The goal is to keep the agent from treating conflicts as rare and embarrassing and to make identification, disclosure, and management routine and honest.

This is a high-stakes domain because undisclosed or unmanaged conflicts distort the record, erode trust, and can cause real-world harm when biased findings influence practice or policy. When the severity or management of a conflict is uncertain, the agent should consult the institutional conflict of interest office and the editor or review body rather than deciding alone.

## Core Rules

### Recognize The Full Range Of Conflict Types

Conflicts are not only financial. Limiting attention to money misses the personal, intellectual, and institutional interests that can bias research.

Identify:

- financial conflicts, including employment, consulting, equity, patents, royalties, speaker fees, and gifts;
- personal conflicts, including relationships, family ties, friendships, and enmities with participants, authors, or competitors;
- intellectual conflicts, including strong commitment to a theory or prior published position that the current work could confirm or refute;
- institutional conflicts, including the institution's own financial or reputational stake in the outcome;
- career conflicts, including dependence on the outcome for promotion, funding, or reputation.

Each type can bias design, analysis, interpretation, or reporting. Cast the net wide when identifying conflicts.

### Disclose Proactively And Completely

Disclosure is the baseline obligation. Partial or reactive disclosure, waiting to be asked, defeats its purpose.

Disclose:

- all relevant financial relationships over the relevant threshold, current and recent;
- personal and professional relationships that could bias judgment;
- intellectual commitments that could bias interpretation;
- institutional interests where applicable;
- the disclosure at the right stages, including ethics submission, registration, manuscript submission, and presentation.

Disclosure should err toward completeness. Omitting a relationship because it seems minor is a judgment that belongs to the reader or reviewer, not the researcher.

### Match Disclosure To The Audience And Stage

Different audiences need different disclosure detail. A manuscript disclosure, an ethics submission, and a public presentation each have their own requirements.

Tailor disclosure to:

- journal and funder requirements, which specify what and how to disclose;
- ethics review, which assesses conflicts as part of risk and management;
- public presentations, where audiences need to judge credibility;
- participants, who may need to know of conflicts that affect them;
- collaborators and co-authors, who need to know of conflicts that affect the work.

A disclosure adequate for one stage may be inadequate for another. Meet the requirement of each.

### Distinguish Disclosure From Management

Disclosure informs readers; it does not by itself remove the conflict or its effect. Many conflicts require management beyond disclosure.

Recognize when management is needed:

- when the conflict is substantial enough that disclosure alone does not protect integrity;
- when the conflicted person controls a decision vulnerable to bias, such as analysis, interpretation, or peer review;
- when the conflict could affect participant safety or rights;
- when the conflict could affect the allocation of resources or opportunities.

Management options include independent analysis, oversight by non-conflicted parties, restriction of roles, or recusal. Disclosure without management leaves the bias in place, merely labeled.

### Assess Severity By Influence And Stakes

Not all conflicts require the same response. Severity depends on how much the conflict could influence the work and what is at stake.

Assess:

- the magnitude of the secondary interest;
- the proximity of the interest to the specific decision or task;
- the discretion the conflicted person has over vulnerable decisions;
- the consequences of bias for participants, the field, and the public;
- whether the conflict is ongoing or resolved.

A large, direct, ongoing conflict over high-stakes decisions requires strong management or recusal. A small, indirect, resolved conflict may require only disclosure. Calibrate the response to the severity.

### Address Conflicts In Peer Review And Editorial Roles

Conflicts in reviewing and editing are especially dangerous because they shape what enters the record. Reviewers and editors must identify and manage their own.

In review and editorial roles:

- decline to review work where you have a conflict;
- disclose any relationship that could appear to bias judgment;
- avoid reviewing work by close collaborators, recent co-authors, or competitors where possible;
- do not use the unpublished material to advantage;
- recuse from decisions where the conflict is substantial.

The obligation is stricter in gatekeeping roles because the power to suppress or advance others' work is great.

### Document And Update Disclosures Over Time

Conflicts change. A disclosure made once can become outdated as relationships and interests evolve.

Maintain:

- current, dated disclosure records;
- updates when new conflicts arise or old ones resolve;
- consistency across the disclosures made to different bodies;
- a record of management decisions and their basis.

Stale disclosures are a common problem. Treat disclosure as a living record, not a one-time statement.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing Only Financial Conflicts

Personal, intellectual, and institutional conflicts also bias research. Limiting attention to money misses the majority.

### Treating Disclosure As The Complete Response

Disclosure labels the conflict; it does not remove it. Substantial conflicts need management or recusal.

### Disclosing Reactively Or Minimally

Waiting to be asked, or disclosing only what cannot be hidden, defeats the purpose of disclosure.

### Assuming A Conflict Implies Misconduct

Conflicts are common and not inherently wrong. The wrong is failing to identify, disclose, and manage them.

### Ignoring Conflicts In Review And Editorial Roles

Gatekeeping conflicts are especially dangerous. Reviewers and editors must manage their own strictly.

### Letting Disclosures Go Stale

Conflicts change over time. A disclosure made once becomes outdated and misleading.

### Underestimating Intellectual Conflicts

Strong commitment to a theory or prior position can bias interpretation as much as money can. Intellectual conflicts are real and often invisible.

## Self-Check

- Are financial, personal, intellectual, institutional, and career conflicts all considered, not only financial ones?
- Is disclosure proactive and complete, erring toward including rather than excluding relationships?
- Is disclosure tailored to the requirements of each audience and stage, from ethics to publication to presentation?
- Is disclosure distinguished from management, with management or recusal applied where the conflict is substantial?
- Is severity assessed by the conflict's potential influence and the stakes, calibrating the response accordingly?
- Are conflicts in peer review and editorial roles identified and managed, including recusal where substantial?
- Are disclosures documented and updated over time as relationships and interests change?
- Where the severity or management of a conflict is uncertain, has the institutional conflict of interest office and the editor or review body been consulted rather than deciding alone?
