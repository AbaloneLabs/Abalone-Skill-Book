# Multi-Site And Consortium Research

A multi-site study can answer questions no single site can, by pooling populations, increasing power, testing generalizability, or capturing rare events. But adding sites multiplies complexity. Each site brings its own ethics review, data systems, staff, language, regulations, and culture. When that complexity is handled casually, three harms follow. Sites implement the protocol differently and the pooled results become uninterpretable. Governance disputes stall the project or leave some partners excluded from credit. And participants at some sites receive different protections, so that consent and safety become uneven across the study.

The agent should use this skill when designing a multi-site study, joining or forming a consortium, harmonizing protocols, writing a data sharing agreement, or planning analysis across heterogeneous sites. The goal is to keep the agent from treating a multi-site study as a bigger single-site study, when in fact it is a coordination and governance problem as much as a scientific one.

## Core Rules

### Decide The Scientific Rationale For Multiple Sites

Multiple sites impose cost and complexity, so the rationale must be explicit. Common reasons include the following.

- Rare outcomes requiring pooled sample size.
- Diversity of populations for generalizability.
- Variation in exposure or context to test mechanisms.
- Access to specialized expertise or equipment at each site.
- Replication of an effect across settings.

State which rationale applies, because it determines design choices. A study pooling for power needs strict harmonization. A study testing context effects needs preserved site variation. Confusing the two produces a dataset that answers neither question well.

### Harmonize The Protocol Without Erasing Necessary Variation

Harmonization means every site implements the same core protocol so that data can be combined meaningfully. Decide what must be identical and what may vary.

Typically identical across sites.

- Inclusion and exclusion criteria.
- Primary outcome definition and measurement.
- Randomization or assignment procedure.
- Key covariates and timepoints.
- Analysis plan for the pooled dataset.

Typically allowed to vary.

- Recruitment channels reflecting local context.
- Language and cultural adaptation of instruments.
- Staffing structure.
- Local data capture tools, if they map to the common schema.

Document each harmonization decision and the reason. A protocol that claims uniformity while sites quietly adapt produces hidden heterogeneity that later distorts analysis.

### Establish Governance Before Data Collection

Governance disputes are the most common reason consortia fail. Agree on governance before anyone collects data, while all parties still have incentive to cooperate.

Governance should cover the following.

- Leadership and decision-making structure.
- Roles and responsibilities of each partner.
- Authorship and credit rules, including order and contribution tracking.
- Data ownership, access, and sharing rules.
- Publication approval and dissemination process.
- Dispute resolution mechanism.
- Process for adding or removing partners.
- Intellectual property and commercialization rights.

Write governance into a consortium agreement or memorandum of understanding signed by all institutions. Verbal understandings do not survive the first disagreement.

### Coordinate Ethics Review Across Jurisdictions

Each site typically needs approval from its own ethics board, and requirements differ by country and institution. Plan for this early, because ethics delays are a leading cause of timeline slippage.

Approaches include the following.

- A single central ethics review with local reliance, where allowed.
- Mutual recognition agreements between boards.
- Parallel submission to multiple boards with coordinated responses.
- A master protocol with site-specific addenda for local requirements.

Track approval status at each site and do not begin data collection at any site before its approval is in place. Starting early at some sites and late at others introduces temporal bias and can invalidate pooling.

### Standardize Data Capture And Definitions

Pooled analysis requires data that map to a common schema. Standardize the following.

- Variable definitions and units.
- Codebooks and value labels.
- Missing data codes.
- Date and time formats.
- Coding of free text where possible.
- Metadata describing each site's deviations.

Use a shared data capture platform where feasible, with validation rules that prevent inconsistent entry. Where sites use local systems, define a transformation pipeline and test it on pilot data before full collection. A schema mismatch discovered at analysis time can delay results by months.

### Plan For Site-Level Analysis And Pooled Analysis

Multi-site data are clustered. Ignoring clustering in analysis produces false precision. Plan analysis at two levels.

- Site-level estimates to check consistency and heterogeneity.
- Pooled analysis that accounts for clustering, such as multilevel models or fixed effects for site.

Pre-specify how heterogeneity will be measured and reported, such as the variation in site effects and whether any site departs meaningfully. A pooled estimate that hides large site differences can be misleading even when statistically significant.

### Manage Communication And Quality Across Sites

Distributed teams drift without active communication. Establish regular touchpoints.

- Periodic all-site calls for protocol questions and deviations.
- A shared issue log for problems and resolutions.
- Training and certification for staff performing key procedures.
- Central monitoring of data quality, completeness, and protocol adherence.
- Site visits or audits where feasible.

Document protocol deviations at each site and report them in the final paper. Undisclosed site-level deviations are a transparency failure and can invalidate pooled results.

### Plan For Equitable Credit And Benefit

Consortia often include partners with unequal resources, located in different countries or institutions. Plan credit and benefit sharing to avoid extracting data or samples from lower-resourced partners while publishing from better-resourced ones.

Equity considerations include the following.

- Authorship that reflects contribution, not just affiliation prestige.
- Local capacity building, training, and infrastructure investment.
- Return of results to participating communities and institutions.
- Intellectual property rules that do not strip partners of future benefit.
- Inclusion of local researchers in analysis and writing, not only data collection.

Extractive partnerships damage trust and make future collaboration harder.

## Common Traps

### Treating Multi-Site As A Bigger Single-Site Study

The coordination, governance, and harmonization work is the hard part. Ignoring it produces uninterpretable pooled data and fractured teams.

### Allowing Silent Protocol Adaptation

When sites adapt the protocol without recording it, the pooled dataset hides heterogeneity. Require and document every adaptation.

### Starting Data Collection Before Governance Is Signed

Once data exist, partners lose incentive to agree on rules they dislike. Finalize governance before collection begins.

### Ignoring Clustering In Analysis

Pooling clustered data without accounting for site produces artificially narrow confidence intervals and inflated significance.

### Centralizing Analysis And Authorship

When the lead site analyzes and writes while partners only collect data, credit and capacity concentrate unfairly. Include partners in analysis and writing.

### Underestimating Ethics Review Timelines

Multi-jurisdiction ethics review can take many months. Plan for it or miss enrollment windows.

### Hiding Site-Level Heterogeneity

A clean pooled estimate that masks large site differences misleads readers. Report heterogeneity honestly.

### Failing To Return Results To Sites And Communities

Partners and participants who never see results feel used. Plan dissemination to all sites and communities, not only to journals.

## Self-Check

- Is the scientific rationale for multiple sites explicit, and does the design match that rationale?
- Is the protocol harmonized with documented decisions about what must be identical and what may vary?
- Is governance, including authorship, data access, disputes, and intellectual property, agreed and signed before data collection?
- Is ethics review coordinated across jurisdictions, with no site starting before its approval?
- Is data capture standardized to a common schema with tested transformation pipelines?
- Does the analysis plan account for clustering and report site-level heterogeneity?
- Are communication, training, monitoring, and deviation tracking established across sites?
- Are credit, capacity building, and return of results planned equitably across partners?
- Are protocol deviations at each site documented and reported in the final paper?
- For complex consortia spanning institutions or countries, has a research governance advisor, legal counsel, or experienced consortium coordinator reviewed the agreements before launch?
