# Team Roles And Responsibilities

A research team is not just a collection of skilled people. It is a system of divided work that has to fit back together into a coherent study. When roles are vague, three harms follow. Tasks fall through the gaps because everyone assumed someone else owned them. Decisions stall or conflict because no one has clear authority. And credit and accountability become impossible to assign, which feeds authorship disputes and integrity problems later. Most research team failures are not failures of skill; they are failures of role clarity.

The agent should use this skill when forming a team, onboarding a new member, planning a project, reorganizing after someone leaves, or untangling a team that has stopped functioning. The goal is to keep the agent from assuming that competent people will naturally sort out who does what, when in fact unspoken role assumptions are the most common source of research team breakdown.

## Core Rules

### Map The Work Before Assigning The People

Start from the work, not from the people available. List every major task the project requires, then assign owners. Assigning people first and hoping the work distributes itself produces gaps and overlaps.

Typical research project workstreams include the following.

- Study design and protocol writing.
- Ethics and regulatory approvals.
- Participant recruitment and consent.
- Data collection and capture.
- Data management and curation.
- Statistical or qualitative analysis.
- Software and tooling.
- Writing and revision.
- Project management and coordination.
- Budget and reporting to funders.
- Dissemination and communication.

For each workstream, name a single accountable owner. Shared ownership with no lead is a recipe for inaction.

### Distinguish Accountability From Contribution

A common confusion is treating everyone who contributes as accountable. Accountability means the person answers for the outcome and resolves problems. Contribution means the person does part of the work. A task can have many contributors but should have one accountable owner.

Use a simple model such as the following.

- Accountable, the one owner who answers for the task.
- Responsible, those who do the work.
- Consulted, those whose input is required before decisions.
- Informed, those who must be told of outcomes.

This separation prevents the two failure modes of either no owner or too many owners. Write it down so the team can see it.

### Define Decision Rights Explicitly

Roles define who does work; decision rights define who decides when there is a conflict. Many teams leave decision rights implicit, which forces every disagreement into a power struggle or into paralysis.

For each major decision type, identify who decides.

- Scientific and design decisions.
- Budget and resource allocation.
- Personnel and hiring.
- Authorship and credit.
- Publication timing and outlet.
- Data sharing and access.
- Response to adverse events or integrity concerns.

A decision that has no owner either stalls or gets relitigated. A decision with a named owner gets made and the team moves on, even if not everyone agrees.

### Clarify Roles At Onboarding

New members arrive with assumptions from their previous teams, which often do not match this team's norms. Onboard explicitly.

- Describe the project goals and current status.
- Explain the role structure and the new member's place in it.
- Introduce decision rights relevant to their work.
- Review communication norms and tools.
- Set expectations for availability, response time, and meeting participation.
- Clarify how performance and contribution will be recognized.

A new member who infers the norms will often infer wrong, and the mismatch surfaces only when something goes wrong.

### Plan For Departures And Transitions

Research teams have high turnover, especially with students, postdocs, and fixed-term staff. Plan roles to survive departures.

- Document key procedures so knowledge is not locked in one person.
- Use version control and shared storage so work is not lost with a laptop.
- Assign backup owners for critical tasks.
- Plan handover timelines when departures are known.
- Update role maps when membership changes.

A team where one person is the sole keeper of a critical pipeline is fragile. The departure does not have to be hostile; a graduation or a new job is enough to break the project.

### Align Roles With Authorship And Credit

Role assignments should connect to how credit will be allocated. If someone owns analysis, that contribution should appear in the authorship plan and contribution statement. If someone owns a support task that does not qualify for authorship, they should know that and be acknowledged instead.

Surprise at authorship time usually traces to role-credit mismatches that were never discussed. Connect roles to credit early and revisit as roles evolve.

### Establish Communication Norms That Match The Work

Roles need communication channels to function. Match the channels to the decisions.

- Synchronous meetings for decisions needing discussion.
- Asynchronous channels for status and questions.
- Shared documents for work in progress.
- Issue trackers for tasks and blockers.
- Recorded decisions for items that will be referenced later.

Norms should specify response-time expectations, where different types of information live, and how decisions are recorded. A team that relies on hallway conversations and private messages excludes remote or junior members and loses its institutional memory.

### Revisit Roles At Milestones

Roles set at the start drift as the project evolves. The person who owned recruitment now needs to own analysis. The student who assisted now leads a sub-study. Revisit the role map at milestones such as end of data collection, start of analysis, drafting, and submission.

Updating roles is not a sign of poor planning; it is how a real project stays aligned. Failing to update roles leaves the team working from a stale map.

## Common Traps

### Assigning Tasks To The Most Available Person

Routing work to whoever is free, rather than to the accountable owner, fragments responsibility and ensures no one tracks the whole task.

### Shared Ownership With No Lead

Saying a task is owned by the team means no one owns it. Name a single accountable owner for each task.

### Leaving Decision Rights Implicit

When authority is unclear, decisions either stall or escalate into conflict. Name the decider for each decision type.

### Assuming New Members Will Absorb Norms

Norms absorbed by osmosis are absorbed inconsistently. Onboard explicitly to avoid mismatches.

### Letting One Person Become Indispensable

A single keeper of critical knowledge is a project risk. Document and share so departures do not break the work.

### Disconnecting Roles From Credit

When roles and authorship are discussed separately, mismatches emerge late and contentiously. Connect them from the start.

### Relying On Informal Communication Channels

Hallway and private-message decisions exclude part of the team and vanish from the record. Use shared, recorded channels for decisions.

### Never Updating The Role Map

A role map set at kickoff and never revisited becomes fiction. Realign roles at milestones.

## Self-Check

- Has the project work been mapped to workstreams, each with a single accountable owner?
- Is accountability distinguished from contribution, with a clear owner for each task?
- Are decision rights named for scientific, budget, personnel, authorship, and integrity decisions?
- Are new members onboarded with explicit role, decision-rights, and norms explanations?
- Is the team resilient to departures, with documentation, backups, and handover plans?
- Do role assignments connect clearly to authorship and contribution credit?
- Are communication norms matched to decision types, with recorded decisions?
- Is the role map revisited at milestones such as data collection end, analysis start, and submission?
- Are support roles that do not qualify for authorship acknowledged and recognized?
- For teams managing sensitive data, safety-critical work, or large budgets, has an experienced project lead or research administrator reviewed the role structure?
