# Responding To Reviewer Comments

A revision is not a capitulation. Reviewers raise concerns that range from essential corrections to mistaken preferences, and the response must distinguish between them. Researchers often respond by making every requested change, which can distort the paper, or by defending every point, which alienates reviewers and editors. The judgment problem is deciding which requests to accept, which to push back on, and how to communicate the reasoning so that the revision genuinely improves the paper while preserving its integrity.

Use this skill when responding to reviewer comments, drafting a response letter, deciding how to handle conflicting reviewer requests, and defending decisions to the editor. The goal is to keep the agent from treating revision as either total compliance or total defense, and to make each response a reasoned, traceable decision.

This is a high-stakes domain because mishandled revisions lead to rejection of otherwise sound work, and capitulation to mistaken requests can weaken the record. When reviewer requests conflict with the study's design or ethics, the agent should consult the editor and co-authors rather than silently complying or silently refusing.

## Core Rules

### Read The Decision Letter As A Whole Before Reacting

The first read of reviews is emotional. Decisions made in that state are often poor. Read the full set of reviews and the editor's letter, then set it aside before responding.

On careful reading identify:

- the editor's actual decision and what it hinges on;
- which reviewer comments are essential versus preferential;
- where reviewers agree and where they conflict;
- which comments reflect misunderstandings the paper must address;
- which comments request changes beyond the study's scope or evidence.

Mapping the full landscape before drafting prevents overreacting to the harshest comment or underreacting to a quiet but essential one.

### Distinguish Essential From Preferential Requests

Not every reviewer request must be honored. The response should reflect a judgment about which matter.

Categorize requests:

- essential, addressing a real flaw in design, analysis, or interpretation that must be fixed;
- clarifying, where the reviewer misunderstood something the paper can explain better;
- preferential, reflecting the reviewer's taste rather than a flaw;
- out of scope, requesting work beyond what the study can or should do;
- mistaken, where the reviewer is factually or methodologically wrong.

Essential and clarifying requests should generally be accepted. Preferential requests may be accepted if they improve the paper without distorting it. Out-of-scope and mistaken requests warrant respectful pushback with reasoning.

### Address Each Comment Explicitly In The Response Letter

Reviewers and editors need to see that every comment was considered. A response that addresses some comments and ignores others signals carelessness or evasion.

For each comment:

- quote or paraphrase the comment so the mapping is clear;
- state the action taken in response;
- point to the exact location of the change in the manuscript;
- explain the reasoning where the response is partial or a pushback;
- remain professional even when the comment is unfair.

A point-by-point response that is complete, specific, and courteous is the core artifact of revision. Its thoroughness signals seriousness to the editor.

### Make Pushback Reasoned And Respectful

Declining a request is legitimate when the request is wrong or out of scope, but the refusal must be justified, not asserted.

When pushing back:

- acknowledge the concern behind the request;
- explain why the requested change is not appropriate, with evidence or reasoning;
- offer an alternative that addresses the underlying concern if possible;
- cite methodological standards, the study design, or prior literature where relevant;
- avoid defensiveness or dismissiveness.

Reviewers and editors respond to reasoning. A respectful, evidence-based refusal is more persuasive than compliance that weakens the paper, and more effective than a hostile defense.

### Reconcile Conflicting Reviewer Requests

When reviewers disagree, the author cannot satisfy both by default. The conflict must be resolved deliberately, often with the editor's guidance.

For conflicts:

- identify the substance of the disagreement;
- assess which position better serves the paper's integrity and the field's standards;
- explain the resolution and the reasoning to both reviewers;
- consider whether the conflict reveals an ambiguity the paper should address;
- escalate to the editor if the conflict cannot be resolved to both reviewers' satisfaction.

Silently choosing one reviewer over the other without explanation invites rejection. Surface the conflict and the resolution explicitly.

### Revise The Manuscript And The Claims Together

Changes in response to reviews can affect the paper's claims. The revision must keep claims, results, and conclusions consistent.

Check after revision:

- whether new analyses change the results or effect sizes;
- whether the abstract, results, and conclusions still align;
- whether limitations need updating to reflect changes;
- whether any claim now exceeds or understates the revised evidence;
- whether figures and tables match the revised text.

A revision that changes the analysis but not the conclusions, or vice versa, is internally inconsistent and invites further rejection.

### Keep The Editor Informed Of Substantive Judgment Calls

The editor mediates between authors and reviewers and makes the final decision. Substantive judgment calls should be visible to the editor.

Communicate to the editor:

- where you accepted essential requests and how;
- where you pushed back and why;
- where reviewers conflicted and how you resolved it;
- any changes that alter the paper's scope or claims;
- any constraints, such as ethics or data limits, that bound what could be done.

The editor should not have to infer your reasoning from the manuscript. Make the revision logic explicit in the response letter's opening and throughout.

## Common Traps

### Capitulating To Every Request Regardless Of Merit

Total compliance can distort the paper, weaken the claims, or introduce inconsistency. Judgment, not blanket acceptance, is required.

### Defending Every Point And Alienating Reviewers

Hostile or dismissive responses damage credibility with the editor. Reasoned pushback is effective; blanket defense is not.

### Ignoring Comments That Seem Minor

Overlooking comments signals carelessness. Even minor comments deserve an explicit, brief response.

### Silently Choosing One Reviewer In A Conflict

Resolving a conflict without explanation invites rejection. Surface the conflict and the reasoning.

### Changing The Analysis But Not The Conclusions

Revisions that affect results must propagate through abstract, conclusions, and limitations. Inconsistency is a red flag for editors.

### Reacting To The Harshest Comment Disproportionately

The loudest criticism is not always the most important. Map all comments before deciding what matters.

### Hiding Judgment Calls From The Editor

The editor needs to see where you complied, pushed back, and reconciled conflicts. Hiding judgment calls forces inference and weakens your case.

## Self-Check

- Has the full decision letter and all reviews been read and mapped before drafting any response?
- Are reviewer requests categorized as essential, clarifying, preferential, out of scope, or mistaken, with responses matched accordingly?
- Does the response letter address every comment explicitly, with actions, manuscript locations, and reasoning?
- Is pushback reasoned, respectful, and supported by evidence or standards rather than asserted?
- Are conflicting reviewer requests reconciled deliberately, with the resolution and reasoning made visible?
- Do revisions to analysis propagate consistently through results, abstract, conclusions, and limitations?
- Are substantive judgment calls communicated clearly to the editor in the response letter?
- Where reviewer requests conflict with the study's design or ethics, have the editor and co-authors been consulted rather than silently complying or refusing?
