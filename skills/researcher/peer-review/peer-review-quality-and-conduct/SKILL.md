# Peer Review Quality And Conduct

Peer review is a judgment about whether a manuscript merits publication, and like all judgments it can be done well or poorly. A weak review wastes the authors' and editor's time, either by being vague, by pushing personal preferences, or by missing serious flaws. A harmful review can delay or suppress sound work or allow flawed work through. The judgment problem is reviewing in a way that is rigorous, fair, constructive, and honest about what the review can and cannot establish.

Use this skill when performing peer review, drafting review comments, deciding on a recommendation, and navigating confidentiality and bias obligations. The goal is to keep the agent from treating review as an opportunity to impose preferences or as a perfunctory hurdle, and to make the review a genuine service to authors, editors, and the field.

This is a high-stakes domain because review outcomes shape the published record and authors' careers. When a manuscript falls outside the reviewer's expertise or raises integrity concerns, the agent should disclose the limit or alert the editor rather than reviewing beyond competence or staying silent.

## Core Rules

### Review Against The Manuscript's Own Claims And Standards

A review judges whether the manuscript does what it sets out to do, to the standard the field expects. It is not an opportunity to require the authors to write a different paper.

Assess:

- whether the research question is clear and worth asking;
- whether the design can answer that question;
- whether the methods are appropriate and adequately described;
- whether the results support the conclusions;
- whether the claims are matched to the evidence;
- whether the limitations are acknowledged.

Hold the manuscript to its own aims and to the field's standards, not to the reviewer's preferred agenda. Suggesting a different study is only useful when the chosen study cannot answer its question.

### Separate Essential Flaws From Preferences

A useful review distinguishes problems that must be fixed from improvements that would be nice. Conflating them burdens authors and obscures priorities.

Distinguish:

- fatal flaws in design, analysis, or interpretation that undermine the conclusions;
- important gaps that weaken but do not invalidate the work;
- clarifications needed to evaluate the manuscript;
- stylistic or preferential suggestions that are optional.

Mark the distinction for the editor and authors. A review that lists everything as equally important helps no one prioritize, and a review that treats preferences as requirements oversteps.

### Be Specific And Actionable

Vague comments cannot be addressed. "The methods are unclear" tells the authors nothing; specifying which step, why it is unclear, and what is needed makes revision possible.

For each comment:

- locate the specific passage or issue;
- state the concern precisely;
- explain why it matters for the conclusions;
- suggest a concrete fix where possible;
- distinguish requests from suggestions.

Specific, actionable comments respect the authors' effort and make the revision efficient. Vagueness signals that the review was not done carefully.

### Evaluate Methods And Evidence, Not Just The Conclusion

A review that focuses on whether the reviewer agrees with the conclusion misses the point. The question is whether the methods and evidence support whatever conclusion the authors draw.

Examine:

- the soundness of the design for the question;
- the appropriateness and correctness of the analysis;
- the adequacy of the sample and power;
- the handling of bias, confounding, and missing data;
- the reporting of effect sizes and uncertainty;
- the match between evidence and claims.

A conclusion the reviewer likes, supported by weak evidence, should not pass. A conclusion the reviewer dislikes, supported by strong evidence, should not fail on that basis alone.

### Recognize And Manage Bias

Reviewers bring preferences, commitments, and sometimes conflicts. Unrecognized bias distorts reviews and harms authors.

Guard against:

- preference for conclusions that align with the reviewer's own work;
- harsher scrutiny of methods that produce disliked conclusions;
- bias against authors, institutions, or approaches;
- demands to cite the reviewer's own work excessively;
- severity inflation or deflation based on competitiveness.

Disclose conflicts of interest to the editor. If the bias cannot be managed, decline the review. Self-citation requests should be limited to genuinely relevant work.

### Respect Confidentiality And The Reviewer's Role

Peer review relies on confidentiality. Misusing privileged material or usurping the author's role breaches the trust the system depends on.

Maintain:

- confidentiality of the manuscript and its contents;
- no use of the ideas or data for personal advantage;
- no sharing of the manuscript without the editor's permission;
- delegation only with the editor's knowledge and only to qualified colleagues;
- timely completion or prompt decline.

A reviewer who uses an unpublished manuscript to advance their own work commits a serious breach. The role is to evaluate, not to appropriate.

### Calibrate The Recommendation To The Evidence

The recommendation should follow from the review, not precede it. Overstated recommendations mislead the editor.

Calibrate:

- reject when fatal flaws cannot be remedied or the work is fundamentally out of scope;
- major revision when serious but fixable problems exist;
- minor revision when the work is largely sound and needs clarification;
- accept when the work is sound and well-presented.

A recommendation of major revision should be supported by specific essential concerns; a recommendation of accept should reflect genuine evaluation, not courtesy. The editor weighs multiple reviews, so each must be honest.

## Common Traps

### Requiring The Authors To Write A Different Paper

Reviewing against a personal agenda rather than the manuscript's aims oversteps and burdens authors with unrequested work.

### Listing Preferences As Requirements

Treating optional improvements as mandatory distorts the paper and obscures real flaws. Separate preferences from essential concerns.

### Vague Comments That Cannot Be Addressed

"The analysis is weak" without specifics wastes everyone's time. Actionable comments require precision.

### Judging By Agreement With The Conclusion

Reviewing whether one likes the result, rather than whether the evidence supports it, lets weak agreed work pass and strong disagreed work fail.

### Excessive Self-Citation Demands

Requiring citation of the reviewer's own work beyond genuine relevance is self-serving and erodes trust in the review.

### Breaching Confidentiality

Using unpublished material for personal advantage is a serious integrity breach that undermines the review system.

### Recommendation Disconnected From The Review

A harsh or lenient recommendation not supported by the specific comments misleads the editor and the authors.

## Self-Check

- Is the manuscript reviewed against its own aims and the field's standards rather than a personal agenda?
- Are essential flaws, important gaps, clarifications, and preferential suggestions clearly distinguished?
- Are comments specific, located, and actionable rather than vague?
- Does the review evaluate methods and evidence rather than agreement with the conclusion?
- Are biases, conflicts of interest, and self-citation demands recognized and managed, with recusal where needed?
- Are confidentiality and the reviewer's role respected, with no misuse of privileged material?
- Is the recommendation calibrated to the specific evidence in the review?
- Where the manuscript falls outside the reviewer's expertise or raises integrity concerns, has the limit been disclosed or the editor alerted rather than reviewing beyond competence or staying silent?
