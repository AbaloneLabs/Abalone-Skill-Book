# Manuscript Preparation And Submission Readiness

A manuscript is not ready to submit just because the analysis is done. Submission readiness is a separate judgment that covers fit with the target journal, completeness of reporting, compliance with ethics and data obligations, and the strength of the contribution relative to the claims made. Researchers often submit prematurely because they are eager or because a deadline looms, and the result is desk rejection or review outcomes that could have been avoided. The judgment problem is deciding what must be true before a manuscript leaves the team, and checking it systematically rather than hoping.

Use this skill when preparing a manuscript for submission, when checking readiness against journal and ethical requirements, when selecting a target journal, and when verifying that reporting and data obligations are met. The goal is to keep the agent from treating submission as the end of writing and to make readiness a deliberate, checkable state.

This is a high-stakes domain because premature or non-compliant submission wastes reviewer effort, delays publication, and can create integrity problems if obligations were overlooked. When readiness is uncertain, the agent should consult co-authors, the institutional office, or the journal editor rather than submitting on assumption.

## Core Rules

### Verify Fit With The Target Journal Before Formatting

Submitting to the wrong journal wastes time. Fit should be assessed before formatting effort is invested.

Assess fit:

- the journal's scope, aims, and typical subject matter;
- the article types it accepts and whether the manuscript matches one;
- the methodological and theoretical traditions the journal favors;
- the audience the journal reaches and whether the contribution speaks to it;
- recent issues to confirm the journal publishes work like this;
- the journal's standing, indexing, and whether it serves the intended readership.

A manuscript outside scope is desk-rejected regardless of quality. Confirm fit before anything else.

### Confirm The Contribution Is Stated And Defensible

The manuscript must state what it contributes and the evidence must support that contribution. Vague or overclaimed contributions trigger rejection.

Clarify:

- the specific gap, question, or problem addressed;
- what the study adds that was not known before;
- the type of contribution (empirical, methodological, theoretical, applied);
- the strength of evidence relative to the claim;
- the limitations that bound the contribution.

A contribution buried in the methods or inflated in the abstract misleads readers and reviewers. State it honestly and match the claim to the evidence.

### Check Reporting Completeness Against The Relevant Standard

Most fields have reporting standards. Ignoring them signals carelessness and invites rejection or revision.

Verify against the appropriate standard:

- the relevant reporting guideline for the study type;
- that all required sections, items, and details are present;
- that methods are detailed enough to evaluate and replicate;
- that results report the required statistics, effect sizes, and uncertainty;
- that limitations and generalizability are addressed;
- that the abstract matches the body and the conclusions match the results.

Reporting gaps are a leading cause of review failure. Use the standard as a checklist before submission.

### Satisfy Ethics, Registration, And Consent Obligations

Submission requires evidence that ethical obligations were met. Missing or incomplete ethics documentation can block publication.

Confirm:

- ethics approval was obtained and the reference is cited;
- the approved protocol matches what was done, with deviations explained;
- informed consent was obtained where required and is documented;
- trial or study registration is current and matches the manuscript;
- conflicts of interest are disclosed;
- human or animal subjects protections are described;
- any required data safety monitoring is reported.

Do not assume approval is implied. Cite the approval, the registry, and the consent process explicitly.

### Prepare Data, Code, And Materials Availability

Many journals require data and materials availability. Incomplete or misleading availability statements create problems at and after acceptance.

Prepare:

- a truthful data availability statement;
- deposition of data and code in a repository with persistent identifiers where possible;
- documentation sufficient for a reader to understand and reuse the materials;
- explanation where data cannot be shared, with the access path described;
- compliance with the journal's specific data policy.

Claiming availability that does not exist, or omitting constraints, is an integrity problem. State availability truthfully.

### Coordinate Authorship, Order, And Contributions

Authorship disputes are common and damaging. They should be resolved before submission.

Confirm:

- that all authors meet the criteria for authorship;
- that contributors who do not qualify are acknowledged instead;
- that author order is agreed and documented;
- that contributions are described, often through a contributions statement;
- that no ghost or guest authors are included;
- that all authors have approved the submitted version.

A submitted manuscript with unresolved authorship can be withdrawn or corrected painfully. Resolve it first.

### Complete A Pre-Submission Internal Review

The team should review the manuscript as a reviewer would before it leaves. Self-review catches problems that familiarity hides.

Run an internal review that:

- reads the manuscript cold, as a stranger would;
- checks the logic from question to conclusion;
- tests whether the claims exceed the evidence;
- looks for inconsistencies between abstract, results, and conclusions;
- verifies figures, tables, and statistics;
- simulates reviewer objections and addresses them.

A manuscript that has only been read by its authors in their usual frame is not ready. An internal review approximates external scrutiny.

## Common Traps

### Submitting To A Journal Without Confirming Scope Fit

A manuscript outside scope is desk-rejected regardless of quality. Fit checks come first.

### Burying Or Inflating The Contribution

A contribution hidden in the methods or overstated in the abstract misleads and triggers rejection. State it honestly.

### Ignoring The Reporting Standard

Reporting gaps signal carelessness and invite rejection or revision. The standard is a checklist, not optional.

### Assuming Ethics Approval Is Implied

Approval must be cited and the protocol matched. Missing ethics documentation can block publication late.

### Misstating Data Availability

Claiming openness that does not exist, or hiding constraints, is an integrity problem. Availability must be truthful.

### Leaving Authorship Unresolved

Authorship disputes after submission are damaging and hard to fix. Resolve order, criteria, and contributions first.

### Skipping Internal Review

Familiarity hides problems. Without a cold internal review, the manuscript reaches reviewers with avoidable flaws.

## Self-Check

- Has the target journal's scope, article types, and audience been confirmed as a fit before formatting?
- Is the contribution stated specifically and matched in strength to the evidence?
- Does the manuscript satisfy the relevant reporting standard, with all required items present?
- Are ethics approval, registration, consent, and conflict disclosures cited and consistent with what was done?
- Is the data and materials availability statement truthful, with deposition and documentation where possible?
- Are authorship criteria, order, contributions, and approval resolved and documented?
- Has a pre-submission internal review simulated reviewer objections and addressed avoidable flaws?
- Where readiness is uncertain, have co-authors, the institutional office, or the journal editor been consulted rather than submitting on assumption?
