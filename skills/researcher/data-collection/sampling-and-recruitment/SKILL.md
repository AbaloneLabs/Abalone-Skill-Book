# Sampling And Recruitment

Sampling determines what a study can claim, often more than any other design decision. A study can be methodologically sophisticated and still produce useless or misleading findings if the sample does not match the population the study wants to talk about. Agents frequently treat sampling as a logistical detail, choose a convenience sample because it is available, then generalize as if they had a representative one. Or they compute a sample size from a rule of thumb without connecting it to the effect they expect to detect. The judgment problem is to align the sampling strategy with the intended inference, to build a sampling frame that actually covers the target population, and to anticipate the recruitment failures, nonresponse, and attrition that erode representativeness. A sample that is large but biased is worse than a small but well-justified one, because its size lends false confidence to wrong conclusions.

Use this skill when planning sampling and recruitment. The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing a sample by convenience, from mismatching sampling strategy to claim type, and from ignoring the recruitment and nonresponse dynamics that determine who actually ends up in the data. The agent has freedom in strategy, but the link between sample, population, and inference must be explicit and defensible.

## Core Rules

### Align The Sampling Strategy With The Intended Inference

Different inferences require different sampling strategies. The strategy must match the claim.

Align by:

- probability sampling for statistical generalization to a defined population;
- purposive sampling for in-depth, information-rich qualitative insight;
- theoretical sampling for developing categories in grounded theory;
- representative or random sampling for prevalence or population-level claims.

Using convenience sampling and then generalizing to a population is the most common and damaging sampling error. The strategy must license the inference the study wants to make.

### Define The Target Population And Sampling Frame Precisely

Generalization requires a clear definition of who the study is about and a frame that actually covers them.

Define by:

- the target population: who the study aims to generalize to;
- the sampling frame: the actual list or mechanism from which sample is drawn;
- inclusion and exclusion criteria and their justification;
- the gap between frame and population, known as coverage error.

A sampling frame that systematically misses part of the population (those without phones, without addresses, outside institutions) produces coverage bias no sample size can fix.

### Justify Sample Size Through Logic, Not Rule Of Thumb

Sample size should be derived from the study's logic: the effect to detect, the precision desired, or the saturation sought.

Justify by:

- power analysis for the expected effect size and alpha for quantitative work;
- precision or margin of error for estimation studies;
- saturation or information power for qualitative work;
- the tradeoff between size and the quality of measurement and engagement.

A sample size pulled from a generic rule ("at least 30," "at least 10 interviews") without connection to the study's logic is unjustified and often wrong in either direction.

### Plan Recruitment To Reach The Target Population

Recruitment is where representativeness is won or lost. Who responds shapes the sample more than the sampling plan.

Plan by:

- recruitment channels that reach the target population, not just the accessible;
- incentives calibrated to encourage participation without coercing;
- multiple channels to reduce dependence on a single skewed route;
- tracking who is reached, who responds, and who is missed.

Recruitment through a single online panel or a single gatekeeper reaches a skewed subset. The recruitment plan must address coverage of the intended population.

### Anticipate And Address Nonresponse

Nonresponse is inevitable. Ignoring it produces nonresponse bias that undermines generalization.

Address by:

- tracking response rates overall and within key subgroups;
- comparing respondents to nonrespondents on available frame variables;
- follow-up of nonrespondents to reduce and understand nonresponse;
- weighting or sensitivity analysis where nonresponse is substantial.

A high response rate does not eliminate bias if nonrespondents differ systematically. The pattern of nonresponse must be analyzed, not just the rate.

### Plan For Attrition In Longitudinal Designs

In longitudinal studies, attrition erodes the sample over time and can bias findings if those who leave differ from those who stay.

Plan by:

- tracking attrition and its correlates at each wave;
- retention strategies: contact updates, incentives, relationship maintenance;
- analysis of attrition bias and its likely direction;
- methods such as weighting or multiple imputation where appropriate.

Differential attrition, where those with worse outcomes drop out, can make an intervention look effective when it is not. Attrition must be planned for and analyzed.

### Recruit Hard-To-Reach And Vulnerable Groups Ethically

Some populations are both hard to reach and vulnerable. Recruitment must balance access with protection.

Recruit by:

- trusted intermediaries and culturally appropriate channels;
- avoiding coercion through excessive incentives or power imbalances;
- extra protections for consent, confidentiality, and safety;
- acknowledgment of who remains unreached and why.

Hard-to-reach groups are often precisely those whose exclusion biases research. Recruitment must reach them without exploiting them, and must report who could not be reached.

### Document The Sampling And Recruitment Process Transparently

The reader must be able to assess how the sample was obtained and what it implies for the findings.

Document by:

- the target population, frame, and sampling strategy;
- recruitment methods and response and attrition rates;
- analysis of nonresponse and attrition bias;
- the implications of sampling limitations for the claims.

A methods section that reports only the final sample size hides the process that determines credibility. Transparency lets readers judge the inferential limits.

## Common Traps

### Convenience Sample Generalized As Representative

Drawing an easy sample and generalizing to a population is the most damaging and common sampling error.

### Frame That Misses The Population

A sampling frame that systematically excludes part of the population produces coverage bias no size can fix.

### Rule-Of-Thumb Sample Size

A size from a generic rule, unconnected to effect, precision, or saturation, is unjustified and often wrong.

### Single Skewed Recruitment Channel

Recruiting through one panel or gatekeeper reaches a skewed subset and undermines representativeness.

### Ignoring Nonresponse Bias

Reporting only the response rate, without analyzing who did not respond, hides systematic bias.

### Unanalyzed Attrition

Ignoring differential attrition in longitudinal designs can manufacture or hide effects.

### Overclaiming From A Skewed Sample

Asserting population-level conclusions from a sample shaped by access and nonresponse overstates the findings.

## Self-Check

- Is the sampling strategy aligned with the intended inference (probability, purposive, theoretical)?
- Are the target population, sampling frame, and inclusion criteria defined precisely, with coverage gaps noted?
- Is the sample size justified by power, precision, or saturation rather than a rule of thumb?
- Does the recruitment plan reach the target population through multiple, non-skewed channels?
- Is nonresponse tracked and analyzed for bias, not just reported as a rate?
- In longitudinal designs, is attrition tracked, mitigated, and analyzed for differential effects?
- Are hard-to-reach and vulnerable groups recruited ethically, with protections and acknowledged limits?
- Is the sampling and recruitment process documented transparently, including response and attrition?
- Are the implications of sampling limitations stated for the study's claims?
- Does the sample actually support the generalization or transferability the study asserts?
