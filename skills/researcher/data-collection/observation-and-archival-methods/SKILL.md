# Observation And Archival Methods

Observation and archival methods study behavior and records as they naturally occur, rather than as they are reported in response to a researcher's questions. Their great strength is the avoidance of reactivity: people act differently when they know they are being studied, and self-report is filtered through memory, social desirability, and the interview interaction. Their great demand is discipline. Observation that is unstructured becomes impressionistic tourism; archival research that ignores how records were produced treats documents as transparent windows when they are artifacts shaped by the bureaucracies and biases that generated them. The judgment problem is to choose between structured and unstructured observation deliberately, to build coding schemes that capture behavior reliably, and to read archives critically as products of their own production rather than as neutral facts. An observational or archival study that ignores how the data came to exist produces findings that reflect the data-generation process more than the phenomenon.

Use this skill when planning observation or archival research. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating observation as mere watching, from reading archives naively, and from ignoring the reactivity or bias that observational and documentary data still carry. The agent has freedom in focus and source, but the recording, coding, and critical reading of sources must be deliberate.

## Core Rules

### Choose Structured Or Unstructured Observation Deliberately

Observation ranges from fully structured coding to open ethnographic fieldwork. The choice must match the question.

Choose by:

- structured observation with a predefined coding scheme for comparable, quantified behavior;
- semi-structured observation with focal categories and room for context;
- unstructured observation for exploratory, context-rich understanding;
- the tradeoff between comparability and contextual depth.

Structured observation yields reliable counts but may miss meaning; unstructured observation yields rich meaning but is hard to compare across observers or settings. The choice must fit the claim.

### Build A Coding Scheme That Captures Behavior Reliably

For structured observation, the coding scheme is the instrument. Its quality sets the ceiling of the data.

Build by:

- clear, mutually exclusive, and exhaustive categories;
- operational definitions of each behavior so observers code consistently;
- a sampling plan: what, when, and how often to observe;
- pilot testing and refinement of the scheme before full use.

A vague coding scheme produces observer drift and unreliable data. The scheme must be explicit enough that trained observers reach the same codes.

### Establish And Monitor Interobserver Reliability

Observational data are only as trustworthy as the agreement among those who record them.

Establish by:

- training observers to a common standard;
- calculating reliability statistics such as agreement or kappa;
- periodic rechecking to detect and correct observer drift;
- documenting reliability for the reader.

Without reliability evidence, observational counts are the unsubstantiated judgments of whoever happened to watch. Reliability must be built in and reported.

### Record Observation Systematically And Timely

Observational data degrade quickly in memory. Recording discipline determines data quality.

Record by:

- notes or coding during or immediately after observation;
- separation of observation from interpretation in the record;
- audio, video, or time-sampling where ethically possible and useful;
- systematic organization for retrieval and analysis.

Notes reconstructed long after the event, or that mix observation with inference, lose the detail and trustworthiness that observation is meant to provide.

### Read Archives As Products Of Their Production

Documents and records are not neutral facts. They were produced by people, bureaucracies, and systems with purposes and biases.

Read by:

- who produced the record and why;
- what the recording rules and categories were;
- what was excluded, standardized, or systematically distorted;
- how the record's survival shapes what the archive contains.

A police record, a medical chart, and a corporate memo each reflect the institution that made them. Treating them as transparent windows hides the production process that shapes the data.

### Triangulate Archival Sources Critically

Archival findings are stronger when multiple, independent records converge, but independence must be genuine.

Triangulate by:

- seeking records from different producers on the same event;
- checking whether sources are truly independent or circularly derived;
- interpreting divergence as information about production, not just error;
- weighting sources by their likely accuracy and bias.

Two reports that both draw on the same underlying record are not independent corroboration. Their agreement proves little about the event.

### Handle Access, Preservation, And Selection Bias In Archives

Archives are not complete records of the past. What survives shapes what can be found.

Handle by:

- recognizing selection bias in what was kept, discarded, or lost;
- access restrictions and how they shape the reachable evidence;
- preservation gaps that systematically exclude some groups or events;
- acknowledging what the archive cannot show.

The archive is a sample shaped by survival and power. Conclusions drawn only from what survived may misrepresent the whole, especially for marginalized groups whose records were less often kept.

### Address Ethics And Privacy In Naturally Occurring Data

Observation and archives can capture people who never consented to be studied. Ethics cannot be an afterthought.

Address by:

- consent and notification for direct observation where feasible;
- anonymization of identifiable individuals in records and observations;
- special care with sensitive or legally protected data;
- the ethical limits of studying publicly available but private behavior.

Just because behavior is observable or a record exists does not mean studying it is unproblematic. The ethical analysis must match the sensitivity and identifiability of the data.

## Common Traps

### Observation As Mere Watching

Unstructured observation without recording discipline becomes impressionistic and cannot support credible claims.

### Vague Or Drifting Coding Schemes

A coding scheme without operational definitions or reliability checks produces data that reflect the observer, not the behavior.

### Naive Reading Of Archives

Treating documents as transparent facts ignores the production process and biases that shaped them.

### Circular Triangulation

Sources that both derive from the same origin provide false corroboration and prove little.

### Ignoring Archive Selection Bias

Drawing conclusions only from what survived misrepresents the whole, especially for the marginalized.

### Reactivity Assumed Away

Believing observation is always unobtrusive ignores how awareness of being watched changes behavior.

### Ethics Treated As Afterthought

Studying observable behavior or existing records without consent or anonymization can harm people who never agreed to participate.

## Self-Check

- Is the choice between structured, semi-structured, and unstructured observation matched to the research question?
- For structured observation, is the coding scheme explicit, mutually exclusive, and pilot-tested?
- Is interobserver reliability established, monitored for drift, and reported?
- Is observation recorded systematically and timely, with observation separated from interpretation?
- Are archives read critically as products of their production, with recording rules and biases examined?
- Are archival sources triangulated with genuine independence, and divergence interpreted?
- Is selection and survival bias in the archive acknowledged, including for marginalized groups?
- Are access restrictions and their effect on reachable evidence reported?
- Are ethics, consent, and anonymization handled for naturally occurring and documentary data?
- Does the study account for reactivity and production bias rather than treating data as neutral?
