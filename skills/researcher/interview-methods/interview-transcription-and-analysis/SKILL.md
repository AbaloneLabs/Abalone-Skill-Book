# Interview Transcription And Analysis

Transcription is the bridge between the recorded interview and the analyzed data, and it is routinely treated as a clerical task. It is not. Every transcription decision, what to include, how to render speech, how much detail to preserve, encodes analytic assumptions that shape what can later be found. Agents often send recordings to a transcription service, receive clean text, and begin coding, unaware that "clean" text has already stripped the prosody, pauses, overlaps, and repairs that may carry meaning. The same applies to analysis: the move from transcript to theme is where participant meaning is either preserved or overwritten by the researcher's categories. The judgment problem is to transcribe at a level of detail that fits the analytic goal, and to analyze in a way that stays answerable to what participants actually said rather than what the researcher expected them to say.

Use this skill when transcribing interviews or analyzing interview data. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing transcripts too thin for the analysis and from imposing themes that overwrite participant meaning.

## Core Rules

### Match Transcription Detail To The Analytic Goal

Different analyses require different levels of transcription detail. The level is a design decision.

Match by:

- clean verbatim for thematic analysis of content;
- detailed transcription with pauses, emphasis, and overlap for discourse or conversation analysis;
- noting non-verbal and contextual features where relevant;
- the relationship between what is captured and what can later be claimed.

A clean transcript cannot support claims about hesitation, emphasis, or interactional sequencing, because those features were stripped at transcription. Match the record to the intended analysis.

### Preserve Participant Language And Meaning

Participants' own words carry meaning that paraphrase destroys. Transcription and analysis must respect this.

Preserve by:

- faithful rendering of participants' words, including dialect and idiom;
- minimal "cleaning up" of disfluencies unless analytically justified;
- indicating paraphrase, ellipsis, and translation choices;
- retaining the terms participants use for their own experiences.

Editing participant speech into smooth prose may read better but erases the very features that carry meaning, and it imposes the researcher's register on the participant's voice.

### Document Transcription Decisions

Transcription involves choices, and those choices should be transparent to readers.

Document by:

- the transcription conventions used and why;
- what was included and excluded;
- how non-verbal elements, overlaps, and pauses were handled;
- who transcribed and what quality checks were applied.

Undocumented transcription decisions hide analytic assumptions and prevent readers from judging the fit between data and claims.

### Code Iteratively And Stay Close To The Data

Interview analysis develops through cycles of coding that stay close to what participants said.

Code by:

- first-cycle codes close to participant language;
- second-cycle codes that organize and synthesize;
- revisiting transcripts as patterns emerge;
- resisting premature abstraction into researcher categories.

Coding that leaps quickly to abstract themes risks imposing the researcher's frame and losing the participant's meaning, which is the point of the interview.

### Preserve The Context Of Quotations

A quotation removed from its context can be made to support almost anything. Context must travel with the quote.

Preserve by:

- the question or topic that elicited the response;
- the surrounding conversation;
- the participant's broader stance;
- enough context for the reader to judge the quotation's meaning.

A vivid quote presented without context may be unrepresentative or misread. Context is what makes a quotation evidence rather than decoration.

### Attend To Both Content And Interaction

Interviews are not just repositories of participant content; they are interactions. What is co-produced matters.

Attend by:

- how the interviewer's questions shaped the response;
- moments of hesitation, hedging, or contradiction;
- what participants resisted or declined to answer;
- the interactional context of disclosure.

Treating the transcript as a transparent window onto participant views ignores that the interview is a joint production, and that the interaction itself is data.

### Manage Data Volume Without Losing Depth

Interview studies generate large volumes of text. Managing this volume without losing depth is a real challenge.

Manage by:

- systematic organization and coding from the start;
- summaries that preserve key participant language;
- memos that track emerging patterns across participants;
- avoiding reduction to counts that lose the texture of accounts.

Reducing rich transcripts to frequency counts of codes discards the depth that interviews are conducted to capture. Volume must be managed through structure, not compression.

### Triangulate Across Participants And With Other Data

Interview accounts are partial. Triangulation tests and enriches them.

Triangulate by:

- comparing accounts across participants with different positions;
- seeking convergent and divergent perspectives;
- comparing interview accounts with observation or documents where possible;
- treating divergence as data about the phenomenon.

When participants give conflicting accounts, the conflict often reveals the contested or situated nature of the phenomenon, which is itself a finding.

## Common Traps

### Clean Transcription For Analysis Needing Detail

Stripping pauses, emphasis, and overlap at transcription prevents later claims that depend on those features.

### Editing Participant Speech Into Smooth Prose

Cleaning up disfluencies and dialect imposes the researcher's register and erases meaning-carrying features.

### Undocumented Transcription Choices

Hiding transcription decisions prevents readers from judging the fit between data and claims.

### Premature Abstraction In Coding

Leaping to abstract themes quickly imposes the researcher's frame and overwrites participant meaning.

### Quotations Without Context

Presenting vivid quotes in isolation can make them support claims the broader data do not warrant.

### Treating Interviews As Transparent Content

Ignoring the interactional co-production of the interview misses that how something was said is often as important as what.

### Reducing Transcripts To Code Counts

Compressing rich accounts into frequencies discards the depth interviews are conducted to capture.

## Self-Check

- Is the transcription detail matched to the analytic goal, with features relevant to the claims preserved?
- Is participant language, including dialect and idiom, preserved rather than smoothed away?
- Are transcription conventions and decisions documented transparently?
- Is coding iterative, staying close to participant language before abstracting?
- Do quotations retain enough context for readers to judge their meaning and representativeness?
- Is the interactional nature of the interview acknowledged, with attention to how questions shaped responses?
- Is data volume managed through structure and memos without compression to mere counts?
- Are accounts triangulated across participants and with other data, with divergence treated as data?
- Are contradictions and hesitations in participant accounts examined rather than smoothed over?
- Does the analysis preserve participant meaning rather than overwriting it with researcher categories?
