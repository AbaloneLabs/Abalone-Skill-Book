# Interview Conduct And Rapport

A well-designed interview guide fails in the hands of an interviewer who cannot conduct. Conduct is the real-time work of building rapport, listening actively, probing without leading, managing the flow of the conversation, and handling whatever the participant brings, including emotion, silence, digression, and reluctance. Agents often treat the interview as a reading of the guide, asking each question in order and recording the answer. This produces thin, reactive data. The judgment problem is that the richest material emerges from the interaction between interviewer and participant, and that interaction depends on the interviewer's skill in making the participant comfortable enough to think aloud, follow up on what is significant rather than what is scripted, and hold the line between empathy and leading. Conduct is where the interview's potential is realized or lost.

Use this skill when conducting interviews. The goal is to prevent the agent from mechanical guide-reading, from over- or under-probing, and from mishandling the relational and emotional dynamics that determine data quality.

## Core Rules

### Build Rapport Before And Throughout The Interview

Rapport is the condition for disclosure. It is built before the first research question and maintained throughout.

Build by:

- a warm, respectful opening that explains the study clearly;
- time for the participant to ask questions and settle;
- genuine interest signaled through attention and body language;
- ongoing attentiveness rather than a one-time warm-up.

Participants who feel respected and heard will share more; those who feel processed will give guarded, minimal answers. Rapport is not a technique to turn on; it is sustained attention.

### Listen Actively And Signal That The Participant Is Heard

Active listening encourages elaboration and signals that the participant's account matters. It is the core interviewing skill.

Listen by:

- verbal and non-verbal acknowledgments without interrupting;
- reflecting back key words and ideas;
- tolerating silence to let participants think and elaborate;
- following the participant's thread rather than rushing to the next question.

The richest material often comes after a pause or a reflected word. An interviewer focused on the next scripted question misses what the participant is actually offering.

### Probe In Real Time Without Leading

Probing is where depth is generated, but probes must follow the participant, not steer them.

Probe by:

- generic probes: "tell me more," "what was that like?";
- echoing the participant's own words to invite elaboration;
- asking for examples, contrast, and detail;
- avoiding probes that imply a desired answer.

"Tell me more about that disagreement" opens; "Were you angry about how they treated you?" leads. The line between probing and leading is the line between depth and contamination.

### Follow The Participant's Frame, Not Just The Guide

The guide structures the interview, but the participant's account may reveal more important threads. Skilled interviewers follow both.

Follow by:

- departing from the guide when the participant raises something significant;
- returning to the guide when the thread is exhausted;
- noting unanticipated themes for later analysis;
- treating the guide as a flexible structure, not a script.

Mechanically working through the guide regardless of what the participant offers produces complete but shallow interviews. Judgment about when to follow and when to return is central.

### Handle Sensitive Content With Care And Respect

Interviews may surface sensitive, painful, or distressing material. How this is handled affects both data quality and participant welfare.

Handle by:

- signaling in advance that sensitive topics may arise;
- letting the participant set the pace on sensitive material;
- not pushing past clear discomfort;
- knowing the referral and support resources available.

Pushing for detail on painful material may yield data but at an ethical cost that can also damage disclosure and trust. The participant's welfare takes precedence over data.

### Manage Power And Its Effect On Disclosure

The interviewer-participant relationship is rarely equal. Power affects what participants will say.

Manage by:

- recognizing status differences that inhibit candid responses;
- avoiding a coercive or overly formal posture;
- giving participants control over pace and topics where possible;
- attending to how recording and setting affect disclosure.

Participants may give socially desirable answers to appear competent, compliant, or agreeable. Managing power reduces, though it does not eliminate, this tendency.

### Use Silence Deliberately

Silence is a tool, not a failure. Participants often elaborate after a pause.

Use by:

- tolerating pauses after a question or answer;
- not rushing to fill silence with the next question;
- recognizing that silence may signal thought or emotion;
- using silence to invite the participant to continue.

An interviewer who fills every silence cuts off the reflection and elaboration that produce the deepest material.

### Track Time And Coverage Without Rushing

The interview must cover the key topics, but rushing destroys depth.

Track by:

- awareness of time and remaining priority topics;
- gentle redirection when a thread is exhausted;
- prioritizing depth on the most important topics if time runs short;
- not letting coverage become a substitute for depth.

An interview that covers every question superficially is less useful than one that goes deep on the most analytically important topics.

## Common Traps

### Mechanical Guide-Reading

Reading questions in order without listening or following the participant produces complete but shallow data.

### Over-Probing Or Leading

Pushing for a specific answer or probing with leading questions contaminates the data with the interviewer's frame.

### Rushing Past Silence

Filling every pause cuts off the reflection and elaboration that yield the richest material.

### Mishandling Sensitive Content

Pushing past discomfort for detail may yield data but at an ethical cost and at the expense of trust and disclosure.

### Ignoring Power Dynamics

Failing to recognize how status and recording affect responses produces socially desirable rather than candid data.

### Prioritizing Coverage Over Depth

Treating completion of the guide as the goal produces superficial coverage of all topics at the expense of depth on any.

### Inattentive Or Distracted Conduct

Signaling disinterest or distraction through body language or interruptions shuts down disclosure.

## Self-Check

- Is rapport built before the interview and maintained throughout through genuine attentiveness?
- Is active listening practiced, with acknowledgment, reflection, and tolerance of silence?
- Are probes used to generate depth without leading the participant?
- Does the interviewer follow significant participant threads while using the guide as a flexible structure?
- Is sensitive content handled with care, with the participant's welfare prioritized over data?
- Are power dynamics recognized and managed to reduce socially desirable responding?
- Is silence used deliberately to invite elaboration rather than rushed past?
- Is time managed to ensure depth on priority topics without sacrificing coverage entirely?
- Does the interviewer signal genuine interest and respect throughout?
- Are unanticipated themes noted for later analysis rather than dismissed as off-topic?
