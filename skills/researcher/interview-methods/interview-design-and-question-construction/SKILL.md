# Interview Design And Question Construction

An interview is not a conversation, and it is not a survey read aloud. It is a structured method for eliciting accounts that participants would not otherwise produce, in a form that can answer the research question. The quality of what participants say is largely determined by the quality of what the interviewer asks and how. Agents often draft a list of topics, call it an interview guide, and assume that good questions will emerge in the moment. This underestimates how much question wording, sequencing, and format shape responses. Leading questions produce leading answers; abstract questions produce abstract answers; questions that assume the participant's frame produce answers in the researcher's frame rather than the participant's. The judgment problem is to align the interview format with the research question and to construct questions that open up the participant's perspective rather than confirm the researcher's.

Use this skill when designing an interview study or constructing an interview guide. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing guides that elicit socially desirable, leading, or shallow responses, and from mismatching the interview format to the analytic goal.

## Core Rules

### Match The Interview Format To The Research Question

Structured, semi-structured, and unstructured interviews serve different purposes. The format is a design decision tied to the question.

Match by:

- structured: when comparability across participants is the goal;
- semi-structured: when depth with some comparability is needed;
- unstructured: when the participant's frame and narrative are the focus;
- the relationship between format and the kind of claim intended.

A structured interview forces the researcher's frame; an unstructured interview yields rich but less comparable data. The choice must fit the question.

### Write Questions That Open, Not Close, The Participant's Perspective

Question wording determines whether participants describe their own frame or the researcher's. Most weak interviews fail here.

Write by:

- open questions that ask how and what, not yes or no;
- avoiding leading questions that telegraph the desired answer;
- avoiding questions that assume the researcher's categories;
- using the participant's own terms once introduced.

"Tell me about a time when..." opens experience; "Don't you think that..." closes it. The difference determines whether the data reflect the participant or the interviewer.

### Sequence Questions To Build Trust And Depth

The order of questions shapes what participants will share. Early questions set the tone for the whole interview.

Sequence by:

- opening with non-threatening, easy-to-answer questions;
- moving from concrete to abstract;
- placing sensitive questions after rapport is built;
- closing with reflection and opportunity to add.

Asking sensitive questions before trust is established shuts down disclosure; asking abstract questions before concrete grounding produces vague answers.

### Build Probes Into The Guide, Not Just Questions

Probes are where depth is generated. A guide of questions without planned probes yields surface responses.

Build by:

- generic probes: "can you tell me more about that?";
- specific probes tied to likely short answers;
- probes for examples, contrast, and detail;
- comfort with silence to let participants elaborate.

The richest data often come from the follow-up, not the initial question. A guide that lists only main questions misses the depth the method can deliver.

### Avoid Leading, Double-Barreled, And Jargon-Laden Questions

Specific question pathologies corrupt interview data. Each must be actively avoided.

Avoid by:

- leading questions that imply the answer;
- double-barreled questions that ask two things at once;
- jargon or academic terms participants do not use;
- assumptions about the participant's experience or views.

A question like "How stressful did you find the new policy?" assumes stress and a view of the policy. A better question asks what the change was like.

### Pilot The Guide And Revise Based On Comprehension

A guide that makes sense to the researcher may not make sense to participants. Piloting tests comprehension and flow.

Pilot by:

- testing with people similar to the target participants;
- checking whether questions are understood as intended;
- observing where participants hesitate or ask for clarification;
- revising wording, order, and probes accordingly.

A guide piloted only on colleagues tests collegial comprehension, not participant comprehension, and may fail in the field.

### Plan For The Interviewer-Participant Relationship

The relationship shapes disclosure. It must be considered in design, not left to chance.

Plan by:

- how the researcher's position affects what participants will share;
- managing power asymmetries that inhibit candid responses;
- establishing rapport without over-familiarity;
- the effect of recording on disclosure.

Participants disclose differently to researchers they trust, fear, or identify with. The relationship is part of the method, not background.

### Ensure The Guide Can Actually Answer The Research Question

A guide that flows well but does not connect to the research question produces pleasant but useless data.

Ensure by:

- mapping each question to a research question or sub-question;
- identifying which questions address which analytic goal;
- removing questions that do not serve the analysis;
- checking coverage of all dimensions the analysis requires.

A guide that collects interesting but unfocused stories may be engaging but will not support the intended analysis.

## Common Traps

### Format Chosen By Convenience

Defaulting to semi-structured interviews without considering whether structured or unstructured formats fit the question better misuses the method.

### Leading Questions

Questions that telegraph the desired answer produce confirmatory rather than authentic data.

### Abstract Questions Without Grounding

Asking about abstract concepts before concrete experience yields vague, ungrounded responses.

### Guide Without Probes

A list of main questions without planned probes yields surface responses and misses the depth interviews can deliver.

### Jargon And Researcher Categories

Using academic terms or imposing the researcher's frame produces answers in the researcher's language, not the participant's.

### No Pilot With Target Participants

A guide tested only on colleagues may fail comprehension with the actual participant group.

### Guide Disconnected From The Research Question

A flowing guide that does not map to the research question produces engaging but analytically useless data.

## Self-Check

- Is the interview format (structured, semi-structured, unstructured) matched to the research question and intended claims?
- Are questions open, non-leading, and free of the researcher's imposed categories?
- Is the sequence designed to build trust, move from concrete to abstract, and place sensitive topics appropriately?
- Are probes built into the guide to generate depth beyond initial answers?
- Are leading, double-barreled, and jargon-laden questions actively avoided?
- Has the guide been piloted with participants similar to the target group and revised?
- Is the interviewer-participant relationship, including power and position, considered in the design?
- Does each question map to a research question or analytic goal, with no orphan questions?
- Are sensitive topics handled with attention to timing and rapport?
- Will the data the guide elicits actually support the intended analysis?
