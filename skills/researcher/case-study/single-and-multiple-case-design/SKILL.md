# Single And Multiple Case Design

A case study examines a contemporary phenomenon in depth within its real-world context, especially when the boundary between phenomenon and context is not clear. The central design decision is whether one case is enough or several are needed, and what each additional case is meant to do. Agents often default to a single case for convenience, or pile on cases without a replication logic, treating them as a small sample survey. Both errors waste the method's distinctive strength. A single case can be powerful when it is critical, revelatory, or unique, but it cannot do what replication across cases does. Multiple cases, when chosen with replication logic, allow the researcher to test whether a finding replicates literally or contrasts theoretically; without that logic, they are just several unrelated case descriptions. The judgment problem is to choose the number and type of cases so that the design can support the kind of generalization the question demands.

Use this skill when designing a case study and deciding between single and multiple cases. The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing the number of cases by convenience, from lacking replication logic across multiple cases, and from claiming a kind of generalization the design cannot deliver. The agent has freedom in case focus and number, but the replication logic and claim type must be matched.

## Core Rules

### Define What Counts As The Case Before Choosing Its Number

A case is not a unit of data collection; it is the bounded phenomenon-context unit the study is about. Confusion here invalidates all later design decisions.

Define by:

- the case as a person, group, organization, event, action, decision, or program;
- the phenomenon of interest within the case;
- the context that surrounds and is intertwined with the phenomenon;
- the unit of analysis, so that data collected at one level are not analyzed at another.

A study that calls each interviewee "a case" when the real case is the organization confuses units and cannot structure replication. The case must be defined before its number is decided.

### Justify A Single Case By Its Type, Not By Convenience

A single case is defensible when the case itself justifies singularity. Convenience is not a justification.

Justify by:

- the critical case that permits logical generalization because it tests a clearly formulated theory;
- the unique or extreme case rarely available for study;
- the revelatory case that exposes a phenomenon previously inaccessible;
- the longitudinal or representative case serving a specific descriptive purpose.

A single case without a stated type is a weak design dressed as a case study. The type explains why one case can carry the analytic weight.

### Use Replication Logic For Multiple Cases

Multiple cases are not a sample drawn for statistical generalization. Each case is chosen to replicate, extend, or contrast the findings of others.

Use by:

- literal replication: cases expected to produce the same result;
- theoretical replication: cases expected to produce contrasting results for predictable reasons;
- a case selection table showing what each case is predicted to replicate or contrast;
- revising predictions as cases are analyzed, then explaining confirmations and disconfirmations.

Without replication logic, multiple cases produce parallel descriptions with no cumulative analytic value, and the reader cannot see why each case was included.

### Decide Between Holistic And Embedded Units

A case can have one unit of analysis (holistic) or multiple embedded subunits (embedded). This decision shapes data collection and analysis.

Decide by:

- holistic single-case: one unit, one case;
- embedded single-case: multiple subunits within one case;
- holistic multiple-case: one unit across several cases;
- embedded multiple-case: multiple subunits across several cases.

An embedded design that ignores its subunits, or a holistic design that secretly has subunits, loses analytic structure. The choice must be deliberate and consistently applied.

### Match The Number Of Cases To The Claim And Resources

More cases are not automatically better. The right number depends on the replication logic and on what the study can do well.

Match by:

- the complexity of the theory being developed or tested;
- the literal and theoretical replication needed to support the claim;
- the depth each case requires to be credible;
- the realistic limit on resources, access, and analytic capacity.

A study with too many cases does each shallowly; a study with too few cannot replicate. The number must be justified against the replication logic, not inflated for appearance.

### Preserve The Phenomenon-Context Link

Case study inference depends on the phenomenon being understood within its context. Stripping context to isolate variables abandons the method's strength.

Preserve by:

- describing the context richly, not as background noise;
- analyzing how context enables, constrains, or shapes the phenomenon;
- resisting the urge to control away the contextual features that define the case;
- recognizing that context is part of the finding, not a confound to remove.

A case study that removes context to imitate an experiment has chosen the wrong method for its question.

### Plan For Analytic, Not Statistical, Generalization

Case studies generalize to theory and to broader theoretical propositions, not to populations. The claim type must match.

Plan by:

- analytic generalization from case findings to a theoretical proposition;
- thick description that lets readers judge transferability to their own contexts;
- avoiding language implying the cases represent a population;
- clear statement of the theoretical contribution the cases support.

A case study that claims its findings apply to "most organizations" misuses the method. It can claim that the findings refine, challenge, or extend a theory that others may then apply.

### Build A Case Study Database And Chain Of Evidence

Case study credibility rests on the traceability from research question to evidence to conclusion.

Build by:

- a case study database separate from the final report;
- organized raw data, coded data, and analytic notes per case;
- a chain of evidence linking each finding back to specific data;
- explicit handling of how cross-case conclusions were reached.

Without a database and chain of evidence, case study findings appear as unsupported assertions and cannot be audited.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Case Number By Convenience

Picking one case because it is easy, or several because they are available, lacks the replication logic that justifies the design.

### Multiple Cases Without Replication Logic

Including several cases with no prediction about what each will replicate or contrast produces parallel descriptions with no cumulative value.

### Confusing Units Of Analysis

Calling data units "cases" when the real case is a higher-level entity mixes levels and invalidates the design structure.

### Single Case With No Stated Type

A single case not justified as critical, unique, revelatory, or representative is a weak design presented as a case study.

### Ignoring Embedded Subunits

An embedded design whose subunits are never analyzed, or a holistic design with hidden subunits, loses analytic structure.

### Stripping Context To Imitate Experiments

Removing the contextual features that define the case abandons the method's strength and produces an unconvincing hybrid.

### Claiming Statistical Generalization

Asserting that case findings represent a population misuses the method and overstates what the design can support.

## Self-Check

- Is the case defined as a bounded phenomenon-context unit before its number is decided?
- If single-case, is it justified as critical, unique, revelatory, longitudinal, or representative rather than convenient?
- If multiple-case, is there explicit replication logic predicting literal or theoretical replication across cases?
- Is the choice between holistic and embedded units deliberate and consistently applied?
- Is the number of cases matched to the replication logic and to realistic depth, not inflated for appearance?
- Is the phenomenon-context link preserved and analyzed rather than stripped away?
- Is the intended generalization analytic (to theory), not statistical (to populations)?
- Is there a case study database with organized data per case and a chain of evidence to findings?
- Are cross-case conclusions shown to follow from the within-case evidence?
- Does the design support the specific claim type the research question demands?
