# Case Selection And Boundaries

Case selection is the most consequential decision in a case study, because the cases chosen determine what the study can possibly find and what it can claim. Unlike statistical sampling, case selection is theory-driven: cases are chosen for the analytic work they can do, not for representativeness of a population. Agents often select cases because they are accessible, or fail to bound them, producing studies that sprawl across phenomena, levels, and time periods with no clear focus. An unbounded case generates data about everything and findings about nothing in particular. The judgment problem is to choose cases whose characteristics serve the theoretical purpose, to bound each case sharply so the phenomenon and context are distinguishable, and to acknowledge how access constraints shape the cases actually studied. Case selection that ignores theory produces a study whose cases cannot support the claim, no matter how rich the data.

Use this skill when selecting cases and defining their boundaries. The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing cases by convenience, from leaving cases unbounded, and from ignoring how gatekeepers and access shape the sample. The agent has freedom in case type, but the selection logic and boundaries must be explicit and defensible.

## Core Rules

### Select Cases For Their Theoretical Purpose

Cases are chosen for the analytic work they can do, not for representativeness. The selection logic must match the theoretical aim.

Select by:

- the typical case that illustrates a phenomenon in everyday form;
- the critical case that decisively tests or exemplifies a theory;
- the extreme or unique case that reveals a phenomenon vividly;
- the deviant or outlier case that exposes boundaries and hidden assumptions;
- the most-similar or most-different case for comparative logic.

Each case type licenses a different kind of claim. Selecting without reference to type leaves the reader unable to judge why these cases matter for the theory.

### Bound The Case Sharply Before Data Collection

An unbounded case sprawls. The researcher must decide what is in and what is out before the data overwhelm the design.

Bound by:

- the phenomenon under study, distinct from its context;
- the spatial and organizational limits of the case;
- the temporal boundaries: start, end, and period studied;
- the level of analysis and the units within it.

A case study of "innovation in a hospital" that drifts into the whole health system, several years, and multiple units has no bounded case and cannot structure analysis or replication.

### Distinguish Phenomenon From Context Deliberately

Case study inference depends on the relationship between phenomenon and context. The boundary between them is a design decision.

Distinguish by:

- what is the phenomenon of analytic interest;
- what is the context that surrounds and shapes it;
- where the boundary is drawn and why;
- how context will be described and analyzed without becoming the phenomenon.

The boundary is not always obvious. Drawing it deliberately lets the researcher analyze how context shapes the phenomenon rather than conflating the two.

### Justify The Selection Against Alternative Cases

Credible case selection anticipates the question of why these cases and not others.

Justify by:

- what other cases were considered and why rejected;
- what the chosen cases allow that alternatives would not;
- the tradeoff between information richness and transferability;
- the limits the selection imposes on the claim.

A selection that cannot answer "why not other cases?" is hard to defend. The justification should make the analytic value of the chosen cases visible.

### Acknowledge And Analyze Access Constraints

Access is never neutral. Who lets the researcher in shapes which cases are even possible.

Acknowledge by:

- how gatekeepers and sponsors shaped which cases were available;
- whether the accessible cases are typical or skewed in important ways;
- what kinds of cases were excluded by access constraints;
- how access routes bias the perspectives encountered within each case.

The cases studied are a function of both theory and feasibility. Hiding the access constraints hides a major source of selection bias.

### Define The Unit Of Analysis Consistently

The unit of analysis must be the same across the design, or comparison and generalization break down.

Define by:

- whether the case is an individual, group, organization, event, decision, or program;
- consistent application of that unit across data collection and analysis;
- clear handling of nested units when the design is embedded;
- avoiding drift from one unit to another as the study proceeds.

A study that begins treating the organization as the case and ends treating departments as cases has silently changed its unit and cannot sustain its claims.

### Plan For Disconfirming And Boundary Cases

A strong case selection includes cases likely to challenge the emerging theory, not only those likely to confirm it.

Plan by:

- deliberately seeking cases where the theory might not hold;
- examining boundary cases that test the limits of categories;
- treating disconfirming evidence as analytic material, not noise;
- revising the theory to account for variation rather than discarding awkward cases.

A selection of only confirming cases produces a theory that looks tidy but fails at its edges, where real phenomena usually reveal their conditions.

### Document The Selection Logic And Its Limits

The reader must be able to assess how cases were chosen and what that implies for the findings.

Document by:

- the selection criteria and case type for each case;
- how access constraints shaped the final set;
- what kinds of cases are missing and why;
- the implications of the selection for the scope of claims.

Transparent selection logic lets readers judge transferability and prevents overclaiming from a convenient or skewed set of cases.

## Common Traps

### Convenience Selection

Choosing cases because they are accessible rather than for their theoretical purpose undermines the analytic value of the study.

### Unbounded Cases

Cases without spatial, temporal, or phenomenological boundaries generate sprawling data and vague, unfocused findings.

### Conflating Phenomenon And Context

Failing to distinguish what is under study from its surrounding context muddies analysis and weakens inference.

### Ignoring Access Bias

Hiding how gatekeepers shaped which cases were possible conceals a major source of selection bias.

### Drifting Units Of Analysis

Silently shifting the unit from organization to department to individual breaks the consistency comparison requires.

### Only Confirming Cases

Selecting only cases likely to support the theory produces a brittle account that fails at its boundaries.

### Inflating Claims From Skewed Cases

Asserting broad transferability from a set shaped by access constraints overstates what the cases can support.

## Self-Check

- Are cases selected for a stated theoretical purpose (typical, critical, extreme, deviant, comparative) rather than convenience?
- Is each case bounded sharply in phenomenon, space, time, and level before data collection?
- Is the boundary between phenomenon and context drawn deliberately and analyzed?
- Can the selection answer why these cases and not others were chosen?
- Are access and gatekeeper constraints acknowledged and analyzed as sources of bias?
- Is the unit of analysis defined consistently and applied without drift across the study?
- Are disconfirming and boundary cases deliberately sought and used to refine the theory?
- Is the selection logic documented with its limits and implications for claims?
- Are the implications of missing case types acknowledged rather than hidden?
- Do the selected cases actually support the analytic generalization the study intends?
