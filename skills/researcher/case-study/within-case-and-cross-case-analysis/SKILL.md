# Within-Case And Cross-Case Analysis

Case study analysis proceeds in two stages that must be kept distinct yet connected: first a rigorous within-case analysis that establishes what happened and why in each case on its own terms, then a cross-case analysis that compares cases to develop, refine, or test theoretical propositions. Agents frequently collapse these stages, leaping to cross-case patterns before the within-case work is done, or treating cross-case comparison as averaging across cases rather than analytic replication. Both errors destroy the method's inferential strength. A cross-case pattern built on shallow within-case analysis is a castle on sand; a set of rich within-case narratives with no cross-case synthesis leaves the theoretical contribution undeveloped. The judgment problem is to do each case justice in its own context, then compare cases in a way that respects their distinctiveness while extracting transferable theoretical insight.

Use this skill when analyzing case study data. The goal is to prevent the agent from skipping within-case rigor, from forcing cross-case equivalence that erases context, and from asserting cross-case patterns without showing how they emerged from the cases. The agent has freedom in analytic technique, but the two-stage logic and the link between evidence and proposition must be sustained.

## Core Rules

### Complete Within-Case Analysis Before Cross-Case Comparison

Each case must be understood on its own terms before it is compared. Premature comparison imports a frame that distorts every case.

Complete by:

- writing a full within-case description for each case;
- establishing the sequence of events and the causal logic internal to the case;
- identifying the case's own categories and conditions;
- confirming the within-case account is credible before moving on.

A within-case analysis that exists only as a codebook entry is not a within-case analysis. The case must be rendered as a coherent, contextually grounded account first.

### Write Rich Within-Case Descriptions

The within-case description is the foundation. Its quality sets the ceiling for everything that follows.

Write by:

- a chronological or thematic narrative that captures the case in context;
- direct evidence: quotes, documents, observations, not only the researcher's summary;
- the conditions, actions, and consequences that constitute the case's logic;
- enough detail that a reader could judge the credibility of the account.

Thin within-case descriptions force the cross-case analysis to rely on the researcher's assertions rather than on traceable evidence.

### Use Pattern Matching To Test Propositions

Pattern matching compares an empirically observed pattern with a predicted one. It is a core analytic technique for case-based inference.

Use by:

- deriving a predicted pattern from theory or rival explanations before analysis;
- comparing the case's pattern against the prediction;
- considering rival explanations that could also produce the pattern;
- treating match and mismatch as evidence about the proposition, not proof.

Pattern matching is strongest when rival explanations are considered and ruled out, not when a single pattern is confirmed in isolation.

### Build Explanations, Not Just Descriptions

Case study analysis aims to explain how and why, not only to describe what. Explanation building moves toward theoretical contribution.

Build by:

- iterative drafting of an explanation for each case;
- revising the explanation as new evidence is encountered;
- testing the explanation against negative or disconfirming evidence;
- connecting the case-level explanation to broader theory.

A purely descriptive cross-case summary lists similarities and differences but offers no explanatory account and therefore limited theoretical value.

### Compare Cases Without Forcing Equivalence

Cross-case analysis must respect that cases differ in context and history. Forcing equivalence erases the very nuance cases provide.

Compare by:

- identifying dimensions for comparison derived from the cases, not imposed;
- looking for literal replication (similar outcomes) and theoretical replication (contrasting outcomes for predictable reasons);
- accounting for differences in context rather than treating them as noise;
- preserving each case's distinctiveness within the comparison.

Treating cases as interchangeable units to be averaged abandons the case study logic for a weak imitation of statistical analysis.

### Develop And Refine Theoretical Propositions

The payoff of cross-case analysis is theoretical: propositions that travel beyond the cases while remaining grounded in them.

Develop by:

- stating propositions about relationships among conditions, actions, and outcomes;
- refining propositions as each new case confirms, extends, or challenges them;
- specifying the conditions under which a proposition holds;
- distinguishing what is case-specific from what is transferable.

Propositions that are too vague to be tested or refined add no theoretical value; propositions that ignore the cases' specifics are untethered assertion.

### Handle Convergence And Divergence Explicitly

Cases will both converge and diverge. Both are analytic material, not problems to explain away.

Handle by:

- examining why cases converge on a pattern;
- examining why cases diverge and what that reveals about conditions;
- using divergence to refine the conditions and boundaries of propositions;
- resisting the urge to report only the convergent, tidy findings.

Divergence often reveals the conditions under which a proposition holds, which is more theoretically informative than uniform confirmation.

### Maintain A Chain Of Evidence To Propositions

Every cross-case proposition must trace back through within-case evidence. The reader must be able to follow the logic.

Maintain by:

- linking each proposition to the within-case evidence that supports it;
- showing how cross-case patterns emerged from the comparisons;
- documenting the analytic decisions that moved from data to proposition;
- making the analysis auditable through memos and a case database.

Propositions presented without a visible chain of evidence read as the researcher's opinion and cannot be assessed for credibility.

## Common Traps

### Skipping Within-Case Rigor

Leaping to cross-case patterns before each case is understood on its own terms distorts every case and undermines the comparison.

### Thin Within-Case Descriptions

Cursory case narratives force cross-case claims to rest on assertion rather than traceable evidence.

### Averaging Across Cases

Treating cases as interchangeable units to be averaged abandons case logic for weak statistical imitation.

### Forcing Cross-Case Equivalence

Erasing contextual differences to find common patterns destroys the nuance cases are meant to provide.

### Confirming A Single Pattern

Pattern matching without rival explanations produces apparent confirmation that rules out nothing.

### Reporting Only Convergence

Highlighting convergent findings and hiding divergence misses the conditions that make propositions informative.

### Untethered Propositions

Theoretical claims that do not trace back to within-case evidence read as opinion and cannot be audited.

## Self-Check

- Is a full within-case analysis completed for each case before cross-case comparison begins?
- Are within-case descriptions rich, chronological or thematic, and grounded in direct evidence?
- Is pattern matching used with rival explanations considered and ruled out?
- Does the analysis build explanations of how and why, not just descriptions of what?
- Are cases compared on dimensions derived from the cases rather than imposed externally?
- Are theoretical propositions developed, refined, and bounded by conditions across cases?
- Are convergence and divergence both examined and used to refine the propositions?
- Is there a visible chain of evidence from within-case data to each cross-case proposition?
- Are case distinctiveness and context preserved within the cross-case comparison?
- Does the analysis deliver transferable theoretical insight rather than only case summaries?
