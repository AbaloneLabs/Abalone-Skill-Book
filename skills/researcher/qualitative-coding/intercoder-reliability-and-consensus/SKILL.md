# Intercoder Reliability And Consensus

When more than one person codes qualitative data, the agreement among them becomes a core piece of evidence for the credibility of the analysis. Yet intercoder reliability is widely mishandled. Agents often calculate a single agreement statistic at the end, report a number, and treat the task as complete, when reliability is actually a process that must be built, monitored, and maintained throughout coding. Others conflate two distinct approaches: a reliability approach, which treats coding as a measurement task and quantifies agreement, and a consensus approach, which treats disagreement as productive and resolves it through discussion. These are not interchangeable, and they license different claims. The judgment problem is to choose the right approach for the analysis, to build reliability or consensus into the coding process rather than appending it, and to handle disagreement as analytic material rather than as a nuisance to be averaged away. A study reporting a kappa with no account of how disagreement was handled provides a number without the understanding that gives it meaning.

Use this skill when establishing or managing intercoder agreement. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating reliability as an afterthought statistic, from conflating reliability and consensus approaches, and from hiding disagreement that could refine the analysis. The agent has freedom in approach, but the process and its handling of disagreement must be deliberate and transparent.

## Core Rules

### Choose Between Reliability And Consensus Approaches Deliberately

These approaches rest on different assumptions and license different claims. The choice must be explicit.

Choose by:

- reliability approach: coding as measurement, quantified agreement, suited to structured and deductive coding;
- consensus approach: coding as interpretation, disagreement as productive, suited to inductive and interpretive work;
- the match between the approach and the coding style and research question;
- consistency between the approach and the claims made about rigor.

A reliability statistic on deeply interpretive coding lends false precision; a consensus process on highly structured coding may be unnecessary. The approach must fit the work.

### Build Reliability Into The Process From The Start

Reliability is not computed at the end; it is built through training, calibration, and iterative codebook refinement.

Build by:

- joint coding of a calibration subset before independent coding;
- discussing disagreements to refine code definitions;
- iterating until coders reach a working standard;
- documenting the calibration process and its revisions.

A codebook fielded without calibration produces low agreement that no end-of-study statistic can rescue. The codebook itself must be tested and improved through the reliability process.

### Use An Appropriate Reliability Statistic

Different statistics capture different things, and each has known limitations.

Use by:

- percent agreement as a simple but liberal measure that ignores chance;
- Cohen's kappa for agreement between two coders corrected for chance;
- Krippendorff's alpha for two or more coders and various data types;
- reporting the statistic, its value, and its interpretation against accepted thresholds.

Reporting "agreement was high" without a statistic, or a statistic without its value and threshold, gives the reader no way to judge reliability. The statistic must be named, reported, and interpreted.

### Recognize The Limits Of Reliability Statistics

A high reliability statistic does not guarantee valid or meaningful coding. It guarantees consistency, not correctness.

Recognize by:

- coders can reliably agree on the wrong coding;
- reliability says nothing about whether codes capture the construct;
- chance correction has its own interpretive pitfalls;
- reliability is necessary but not sufficient for credibility.

Two coders can consistently apply a poorly defined code. Reliability must be paired with a defensible codebook and validity evidence, not treated as the whole rigor argument.

### Treat Disagreement As Analytic Material

Disagreement between coders often reveals ambiguity in the data or the codebook. It should be examined, not averaged away.

Treat by:

- analyzing patterns of disagreement to find problematic codes;
- revising code definitions where disagreement is systematic;
- distinguishing genuine ambiguity in the data from coder error;
- using disagreement to deepen rather than dilute the analysis.

Disagreement that is simply overridden or split the difference loses the insight that the data are ambiguous in a meaningful way. The analysis of disagreement can refine the codebook and the findings.

### Establish And Follow A Disagreement Resolution Protocol

Whether reliability- or consensus-based, there must be a defined way to handle disagreement.

Establish by:

- for reliability: independent coding with a rule for final code assignment;
- for consensus: structured discussion, with a tie-breaker or escalation rule;
- documentation of how disagreements were resolved;
- consistency in applying the protocol across the dataset.

Ad hoc resolution produces inconsistent data and undermines both approaches. The protocol must be defined before coding and applied consistently.

### Monitor And Prevent Coder Drift

Over a long coding effort, coders drift from the original standard. Reliability must be maintained, not just established.

Monitor by:

- periodic double-coding of a sample throughout the effort;
- recalibration when drift is detected;
- tracking reliability over time, not just once;
- retraining or codebook revision when agreement declines.

Reliability measured only at the start or end hides drift in between. Ongoing monitoring keeps the coding consistent across the whole dataset.

### Document The Reliability Or Consensus Process Transparently

The reader must see how agreement was established and maintained, not just that it was.

Document by:

- the approach chosen and why;
- the calibration and training process;
- the statistic used, its value, and the threshold applied;
- how disagreement was handled and how drift was monitored.

A methods section that reports only a final kappa hides the process that determines whether the coding is trustworthy. Transparency lets readers judge the credibility of the analysis.

## Common Traps

### Reliability As Afterthought Statistic

Computing agreement only at the end, without calibration, produces a number untethered from a trustworthy process.

### Conflating Reliability And Consensus

Mixing the two approaches without recognizing their different assumptions produces an incoherent rigor argument.

### Statistic Without Value Or Threshold

Reporting that agreement was high, without the statistic, its value, or the threshold, gives no basis for judgment.

### Reliability Mistaken For Validity

Treating high agreement as proof of meaningful coding confuses consistency with correctness.

### Averaging Away Disagreement

Resolving disagreement by splitting the difference loses the insight that the data are genuinely ambiguous.

### Undetected Coder Drift

Measuring reliability once allows drift to erode consistency across the dataset unnoticed.

### Undocumented Process

Reporting a result without the calibration, resolution, and monitoring process prevents readers from assessing credibility.

## Self-Check

- Is the choice between reliability and consensus approaches deliberate and matched to the coding style?
- Is reliability built in through calibration and iterative codebook refinement before independent coding?
- Is an appropriate statistic used, with its value and threshold reported and interpreted?
- Are the limits of reliability, especially the distinction from validity, recognized?
- Is disagreement analyzed as material that can refine the codebook and the findings?
- Is there a defined, consistently applied protocol for resolving disagreement?
- Is coder drift monitored throughout, with recalibration when detected?
- Is the reliability or consensus process documented transparently, including training and monitoring?
- Are the claims about rigor consistent with the approach actually used?
- Can a reader judge whether the coding process supports the analysis's credibility?
