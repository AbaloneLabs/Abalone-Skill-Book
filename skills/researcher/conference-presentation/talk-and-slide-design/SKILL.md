# Talk And Slide Design

A research talk is not a paper read aloud. The medium is different: a live audience cannot rewind, skim, or pause, so the talk must be designed for one-time comprehension under time pressure. Agents often make slides that mirror manuscript sections, cram text, or assume the audience will read along, producing talks that run over time, lose listeners in detail, and bury the contribution. A good talk distills the work to a clear narrative that a listener can follow and remember.

The harm this skill prevents is a wasted presentation opportunity: a strong study that audiences dismiss because the talk was incomprehensible, overtime, or forgettable. The agent has freedom in narrative style and visual design, but the constraints of time, legibility, and cognitive load are binding.

## Core Rules

### Design For The Ear, Not The Page

Listeners process speech linearly and cannot re-read. Sentences must be shorter, structure more explicit, and transitions more signaled than in writing.

Adapt for oral delivery:

- state the roadmap early so listeners can orient;
- use signposting ("first," "the key finding," "this matters because");
- repeat the main message at strategic points;
- pause after dense ideas to let them land;
- avoid long parenthetical asides that break the thread.

### Lead With The Contribution, Not The Literature

Audiences decide quickly whether to keep listening. A talk that spends ten minutes on background before reaching the question loses attention. Open with the problem, the gap, or a motivating example, then state what you did and found within the first few minutes.

A strong opening includes:

- the problem and why it matters;
- the specific question or gap;
- a one-sentence preview of the approach;
- the headline finding or contribution.

### Build A Narrative Arc With A Clear Through-Line

A talk needs a story, not a list. Each slide should advance the argument, and the audience should always sense where they are in that arc.

Structure the arc:

- motivation and question;
- approach and why it can answer the question;
- key results, primary first;
- interpretation and implications;
- limitations and future directions;
- takeaway the audience should remember.

Cut anything that does not serve the through-line, however interesting.

### Make Slides Legible From The Back Row

Slides fail when text is too small, too dense, or low contrast. A common error is fitting a manuscript paragraph onto one slide.

Legibility rules:

- use large fonts (roughly 24-point minimum for body, larger for titles);
- limit text to a few bullets or a single clear visual per slide;
- ensure high contrast between text and background;
- avoid reading slides verbatim;
- prefer one idea or one figure per slide.

### Use Visuals That Communicate In Seconds

A slide visual should convey its point faster than words could. Complex tables and dense figures that work in a paper fail in a talk.

Adapt visuals for talks:

- simplify figures to their essential elements;
- enlarge labels, axes, and legends;
- use one main chart per slide with a clear takeaway;
- animate builds only if they aid understanding, not for decoration;
- avoid raw software output or unformatted tables.

### Rehearse Against The Clock

Time limits at conferences are enforced, often ruthlessly. A talk that runs over is cut off, sometimes before the conclusion. Rehearse with a timer repeatedly until the talk fits comfortably within the limit with margin.

Rehearsal discipline:

- time the full talk including any planned pauses;
- leave a buffer for technical setup and questions;
- identify which slides to skip if running long;
- practice transitions between slides and sections;
- rehearse aloud, not just by reading notes.

### Prepare For Technical Failure

Projectors, clickers, adapters, and file formats fail. A speaker who cannot start because of technical issues loses the audience and the time slot.

Prepare contingencies:

- bring the talk on a USB in a standard format (PDF as fallback);
- carry your own adapters and clicker;
- have a version that works without internet;
- know how to advance slides without a clicker;
- have a plan if the video does not play.

### Tailor Depth To The Audience And Venue

A keynote, a specialist conference talk, and a department seminar demand different depth. Gauge the audience's background and calibrate technical detail accordingly. A specialist crowd may want methodological depth; a general audience needs the implication emphasized over the method.

## Common Traps

### Putting The Paper On Slides

Copying manuscript text or full tables onto slides produces an unreadable, boring talk. Redesign for the oral medium.

### Running Over Time

Overtime talks get cut before the conclusion, the most important part. Rehearse to fit with margin.

### Reading Slides Verbatim

When the speaker reads what the audience can read, the talk adds no value. Use slides as support, speak to the points.

### Too Much Background, Too Little Result

Spending most of the talk on literature leaves no time for the contribution. Front-load the finding.

### Over-Animating Slides

Excessive transitions and builds distract and slow the talk. Use animation only when it reveals the argument.

### Assuming The Audience Knows Your Notation

Unexplained symbols and jargon exclude listeners. Define terms briefly when first used.

### No Clear Takeaway

If the audience cannot state the main message after the talk, the talk failed regardless of its content. End with a memorable summary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the talk designed for oral delivery with short sentences and clear signposting?
- [ ] Does the talk state the question and headline finding within the first few minutes?
- [ ] Does the narrative arc have a clear through-line from motivation to takeaway?
- [ ] Are slides legible from the back row with large fonts and minimal text?
- [ ] Do slide visuals communicate their point within seconds?
- [ ] Has the talk been rehearsed against the clock with time margin?
- [ ] Are contingencies prepared for technical failure (formats, adapters, offline version)?
- [ ] Is technical depth calibrated to the audience and venue?
- [ ] Can the audience state the main takeaway after the talk?
- [ ] Are there identified slides to skip if running long?
