# Poster Design

A research poster must compete for attention in a crowded room and communicate a complete study in the time a passerby spends standing in front of it, often under a minute. Unlike a talk, there is no narrator guiding the reader; unlike a paper, there is no long-form text to explain. Agents frequently treat posters as miniature papers, packing them with dense text and complex figures that no one will read, which defeats the entire purpose of the format. A strong poster is a visual abstract that invites engagement and delivers its message instantly.

The harm this skill prevents is a poster that gathers no visitors, a study that goes unseen at a conference, and missed networking opportunities because the poster was illegible or incomprehensible. The agent has freedom in layout and visual style, but the constraints of legibility, hierarchy, and distillation are mandatory.

## Core Rules

### Design For The Three-Second Test

A viewer walking the aisle decides in seconds whether to stop. The poster's title, key visual, and headline finding must be graspable almost instantly. If a passerby cannot tell what the poster is about and why it matters in a glance, they will move on.

Achieve instant comprehension:

- write a short, specific title that names the topic and contribution;
- place a striking, self-explanatory central visual;
- state the main result prominently, not buried in a results block;
- use a clear visual hierarchy so the eye knows where to start;
- keep the overall layout uncluttered.

### Distill Ruthlessly To The Essential

A poster is not a paper. Every sentence must earn its place. Most viewers will not read paragraphs.

Cut to essentials:

- one or two sentences of background and gap;
- a concise methods summary, often as a simple diagram;
- two to four key results, each as a clear figure with a takeaway caption;
- a brief interpretation and implication;
- contact and acknowledgment details.

If a block of text exceeds a few lines, it is likely too long for the medium.

### Make Text Legible At Reading Distance

Posters are read from roughly one to two meters away. Text that looks fine on a screen is often unreadable when printed at full size.

Legibility standards:

- title: very large, visible from across the room;
- section headers: large and bold;
- body text: roughly 24 to 28 point minimum at full print size;
- high contrast between text and background;
- avoid thin or decorative fonts;
- limit text density per block.

### Use Visuals As The Primary Communication

Posters are a visual medium. Figures, diagrams, and icons communicate faster and more memorably than text. Where a process, design, or result can be shown rather than described, show it.

Visual-first principles:

- use a methods flowchart instead of a methods paragraph;
- let result figures carry the findings with caption takeaways;
- use icons or simple graphics to convey concepts;
- ensure every visual is large enough to read;
- align and group visuals to guide the eye through the story.

### Create A Clear Reading Path

Without a narrator, the reader must infer the order. A poster with no clear flow confuses viewers who do not know where to look next.

Guide the reader:

- use columns, numbers, or arrows to indicate sequence;
- place sections in logical order (intro to conclusion, left to right or top to bottom);
- align elements on a grid for visual calm;
- use consistent styling across sections;
- avoid scattered elements that break the path.

### Prepare A Verbal Pitch And Handout

A poster session is a conversation. When someone stops, the presenter should deliver a concise one-to-two-minute pitch, then engage questions. Standing silently or reading the poster aloud both fail.

Prepare:

- a short spoken summary of the problem, approach, and finding;
- a one-page handout or QR code linking to the paper and contact;
- answers to likely questions about methods and limitations;
- a way to capture interested contacts.

### Ensure Accessibility

Posters should be readable by all attendees, including those with visual impairments or who are not native speakers of the conference language.

Accessibility checks:

- use color combinations accessible to colorblind viewers;
- maintain high contrast;
- do not rely on color alone to convey meaning;
- avoid overly complex jargon given the international audience;
- provide a digital version accessible via QR code with larger text or screen reader support.

### Proofread And Verify Print Specifications

A poster with typos, misaligned elements, or wrong dimensions undermines credibility. Print specifications vary by conference and printer.

Before printing:

- check required dimensions and orientation;
- verify resolution of all images (300 dpi at print size);
- proofread all text multiple times;
- confirm color mode (CMYK for print);
- print a small-scale proof to catch layout errors;
- check that no text is cut off at margins.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Poster As A Miniature Paper

Packing manuscript text onto a poster ensures no one reads it. Distill to visuals and headlines.

### Tiny Text And Dense Paragraphs

Text that is unreadable at distance defeats the format. Use large fonts and minimal prose.

### No Clear Headline Finding

Burying the main result forces viewers to hunt. State the takeaway prominently.

### Cluttered Or Directionless Layout

Without a clear reading path, viewers do not know where to start. Use columns, numbers, and alignment to guide them.

### Low-Resolution Images

Blurry figures signal carelessness. Use vector graphics or high-resolution images.

### No Verbal Pitch Prepared

A silent presenter or one who reads the poster wastes the interaction. Rehearse a concise spoken summary.

### Ignoring Print Specifications

Wrong dimensions or color mode cause printing failures or cropped content. Verify requirements and proof.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Can a passerby grasp the topic and headline finding within three seconds?
- [ ] Has all content been distilled to the essential, with no long paragraphs?
- [ ] Is all text legible at one to two meters reading distance?
- [ ] Do visuals carry the primary communication rather than text?
- [ ] Is there a clear reading path through the poster sections?
- [ ] Is a concise verbal pitch prepared for when viewers stop?
- [ ] Is the poster accessible (contrast, colorblind-safe, digital fallback)?
- [ ] Are all images high resolution at print size?
- [ ] Have dimensions, orientation, and color mode been verified against requirements?
- [ ] Has the poster been proofread and a small-scale proof checked?
