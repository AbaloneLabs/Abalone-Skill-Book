# Action Research Reflection And Praxis

Action research is not only a method of producing change; it is a method of learning through change, and that learning depends on reflection. Two ideas carry most of the weight here. Reflection is the disciplined looking-back at what an action did and what it revealed. Reflexivity is the harder discipline of turning the lens on the researcher's own assumptions, position, and influence within the setting. Praxis is the integration of the two, the ongoing effort to connect theory and practice so that each revises the other. The judgment problem is that all three are easy to name and easy to skip. Agents frequently substitute description for reflection, treat reflexivity as a confessional paragraph rather than an analytic practice, or invoke praxis as a label without doing the work of connecting abstract ideas to concrete action. When reflection and reflexivity are absent, action research collapses into unreflective intervention. The researcher imposes a change shaped by unexamined assumptions, fails to notice how their own presence altered the setting, and reports findings that reproduce the researcher's worldview rather than revealing the situation. In settings of real consequence, this does not merely weaken the research; it can harm people by acting on them from a position the researcher never examined.

The agent should use this skill when writing the reflective components of an action research effort, examining how the researcher's position shapes the work, integrating theory with observed practice, or judging whether reflection has been done substantively rather than ritually. The goal is to keep the agent from treating reflection as decoration and from mistaking self-disclosure for the analytic work that reflexivity requires.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Reflection, Reflexivity, And Praxis

These three are related but not interchangeable, and conflating them weakens all three.

- Reflection looks outward at the action and its effects, asking what happened and what it means.
- Reflexivity looks inward at the researcher, asking how position, assumptions, and presence shaped what was seen and done.
- Praxis is the integration, the recursive movement between theory and practice where each revises the other.

A report that contains reflection but no reflexivity has examined the action without examining the actor. One that claims praxis without showing the theory-practice connection is using the term as a label.

### Make Reflection Analytic, Not Merely Descriptive

Describing what happened is not reflecting on it. Reflection must do interpretive work.

- Move beyond recounting events to interpreting what they reveal about the change and the context.
- Connect specific observations to the change hypothesis and to emerging theory.
- Generate alternative explanations rather than settling on the first plausible one.
- Use reflection to revise the understanding, not only to document the process.

Reflection that reads as a chronological log has captured activity but not learning. The test is whether a reader could tell what was understood differently afterward.

### Practice Reflexivity As Analysis, Not Confession

Reflexivity is not a personal disclosure appended to satisfy a requirement. It is an analytic examination of how the researcher shaped the work.

- Examine how your position, discipline, identity, and institutional backing influenced what you noticed and what you ignored.
- Consider how your presence changed the setting and the behavior of participants.
- Trace how your assumptions shaped the problem definition, the intervention, and the interpretation.
- Treat reflexivity as a source of insight about the findings, not as an apology.

A reflexive statement that lists the researcher's background without connecting it to any analytic consequence is confession, not reflexivity. The connection to the findings is what makes it analytic.

### Examine Positionality And Power In The Research Relationship

The researcher is never a neutral observer in action research, because the researcher is also an actor intervening in the setting.

- Identify the power you hold through expertise, institutional affiliation, resources, or social position.
- Consider how that power affected what participants felt able to say, refuse, or challenge.
- Recognize that stakeholders may align with the researcher's views out of dependence, not agreement.
- Reflect on whose knowledge counts as authoritative in the process and why.

Unexamined positionality lets the researcher's framing pass as the objective view of the setting, when it is one situated perspective among several.

### Connect Theory And Practice Recursively

Praxis is the movement in both directions, theory informing practice and practice revising theory.

- Use theory to inform the design and interpretation of action, not as decoration cited at the start.
- Let the surprises of practice challenge and refine the theory rather than only illustrating it.
- Make the connection explicit, showing how a concept shaped a decision or how an observation revised a concept.
- Avoid theory that is invoked but never tested against the reality of the action.

When theory and practice run in parallel without touching, the work has description and abstraction but no praxis. The integration is the substance.

### Use Critical Self-Examination To Surface Hidden Assumptions

The assumptions most in need of examination are the ones the researcher does not realize they hold.

- Question the taken-for-granted framing of the problem and the change.
- Ask whose interests the framing serves and whose it overlooks.
- Surface assumptions about what counts as improvement, progress, or success, and who defines those.
- Remain alert to assumptions imported from the researcher's discipline or culture.

Critical self-examination is uncomfortable because it questions the ground the work stands on, but that discomfort is where the most important biases are exposed.

### Keep Reflection Connected To Action And Consequences

Reflection that floats free of action becomes abstract contemplation. It must reconnect to what is done.

- Ensure reflection feeds back into the next cycle's plan and action.
- Consider the consequences of findings and actions for the people affected.
- Reflect on ethical implications as they emerge, not only at the design stage.
- Avoid reflection that is sophisticated in form but disconnected from any change in practice.

Reflection earns its place by altering subsequent action. Reflection that changes nothing is, in practice, no reflection at all.

### Document Reflection So It Can Be Scrutinized

Reflection that lives only in the researcher's head cannot be examined, challenged, or trusted.

- Keep a reflective record across the work, not only at the end.
- Include the questions, doubts, and revisions, not only the conclusions.
- Make the record specific enough that a reader can follow the reasoning.
- Use the record as an audit trail for how understanding evolved.

Undocumented reflection is invisible, and invisible reflection cannot be held accountable. The record is what makes the reflective process trustworthy.

## Common Traps

### Description Mistaken For Reflection

Recounting what happened in detail, without interpreting it, produces a process log rather than learning. The trap is that description feels like reflection because it is about the work, but it does no interpretive work and changes no understanding.

### Reflexivity As Confessional Add-On

Listing the researcher's background and biases in a separate section, with no connection to the findings, treats reflexivity as a disclosure requirement rather than analysis. The trap is that it satisfies an expectation while leaving the actual influence on the work unexamined.

### Praxis As Label Without Integration

Invoking praxis or theory-practice integration as a keyword, while theory and practice never actually touch, uses the term rhetorically. The trap is that the label signals sophistication while the substance, the recursive connection, is absent.

### Treating The Researcher As Neutral

Assuming the researcher observes without affecting the setting ignores the interventionist nature of action research. The trap is that neutrality feels like objectivity, but in an intervention the researcher is always an actor whose presence and power shape what occurs.

### Reflection That Changes Nothing

Sophisticated reflection that never feeds back into action becomes an intellectual exercise detached from the work. The trap is that the reflection can be impressive in itself, but if it revises no plan and alters no action it has not done what reflection is for.

### One-Sided Reflection That Confirms The Researcher

Reflection that consistently concludes the researcher was right and the intervention succeeded is a sign of confirmation bias, not learning. The trap is that flattering conclusions feel like validation, but reflection that never surfaces doubt or surprise has not been honest.

### Ignoring Ethical Consequences In Reflection

Reflecting on outcomes without considering harms or unintended effects on participants narrows reflection to effectiveness. The trap is that an effective intervention can still be ethically problematic, and reflection that omits consequences misses the duties action research carries.

### Over-Personalizing Reflexivity

Turning reflexivity into a focus on the researcher's feelings at the expense of structural and power analysis makes it self-indulgent. The trap is that personal disclosure feels honest, but reflexivity must analyze position and power, not only narrate experience.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are reflection, reflexivity, and praxis distinguished rather than conflated, with each doing its distinct work?
- [ ] Is reflection analytic, moving beyond description to interpret what the action revealed and revise understanding?
- [ ] Is reflexivity practiced as analysis connecting the researcher's position to specific consequences for the findings, not as a confessional add-on?
- [ ] Is positionality and power in the research relationship examined, including how the researcher's presence shaped the setting?
- [ ] Is theory connected to practice recursively, with each revising the other, rather than running in parallel?
- [ ] Does critical self-examination surface hidden assumptions about the problem, the change, and what counts as success?
- [ ] Does reflection feed back into subsequent action and consider ethical consequences, rather than floating free of practice?
- [ ] Is reflection documented specifically enough across the work to serve as a scrutinizable audit trail?
- [ ] Does reflection surface genuine doubts and surprises rather than only confirming the researcher's prior views?
- [ ] For high-stakes interventions, settings with deep power imbalances, or cases where the researcher's position is hard to assess, has an experienced action research facilitator or critical scholar reviewed the reflective and reflexive practice before conclusions are drawn?

This skill provides methodological guidance on reflection, reflexivity, and praxis. It is not a substitute for experienced facilitation or formal ethics consultation. Action research intervenes in real settings with real people, and reflection conducted poorly or superficially can let harmful assumptions and power dynamics go unexamined. Poorly run action research can damage communities, erode trust, and legitimize changes that serve the researcher rather than those affected. Where stakes are high or the situation uncertain, consult experienced facilitators and ethics advisors before acting.
