# Survey Question Design

A survey's quality is determined at the question level long before any analysis. A poorly worded question does not measure what it claims to, and no amount of statistical sophistication can recover a meaningful answer from a meaningless question. Agents often write questions that read naturally but contain hidden flaws: double-barreled items that ask two things at once, leading phrasing that pushes respondents toward an answer, ambiguous terms that different respondents interpret differently, and response formats that constrain or distort what people can say. The discipline is to treat every question as a measurement instrument whose wording, format, and context must be tested, not assumed.

Use this skill when writing or revising survey questions, choosing response formats, or pretesting a questionnaire. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a fluent questionnaire that systematically mis-measures the constructs it claims to capture.

## Core Rules

### Write One Question At A Time

A double-barreled question asks about two things but offers one answer, making the response uninterpretable. If a respondent agrees with one part and not the other, the answer is meaningless.

Avoid by:

- splitting compound questions into separate items;
- checking that each item asks about a single object, action, or attribute;
- watching for "and" or "or" joining two distinct concepts;
- testing whether disagreement with one part forces an overall negative answer.

"How satisfied are you with the price and quality?" cannot be interpreted if price and quality differ.

### Avoid Leading Or Loaded Wording

The phrasing of a question can steer respondents toward a particular answer. Leading questions measure the question's bias, not the respondent's view.

Avoid by:

- neutral phrasing that does not presume the answer;
- balanced response options that include both sides equally;
- avoiding emotionally loaded or prestige-laden terms;
- not framing one position as the default or normative.

"Do you support the harmful policy?" and "Do you support the protective policy?" will yield different answers to the same underlying question.

### Ensure Terms Are Understood Uniformly

If respondents interpret a term differently, their answers are not comparable. Ambiguity manufactures measurement error.

Ensure by:

- defining any technical or ambiguous term;
- avoiding jargon, acronyms, and culturally specific references;
- specifying time frames, quantities, and referents concretely;
- pretesting to reveal divergent interpretations.

"Often," "recently," and "a lot" mean different things to different people; specify or use defined frequency categories.

### Choose Response Formats That Fit The Construct

The response format constrains what respondents can express. A mismatch between the construct and the format distorts measurement.

Match by:

- bipolar scales (e.g., agree-disagree) for evaluative dimensions;
- unipolar scales (e.g., not at all to extremely) for intensity;
- frequency categories for behaviors;
- numeric or open responses for quantities;
- avoiding forced choices when a genuine middle or "don't know" exists.

Forcing a yes-no answer on a nuanced attitude produces measurement error and satisficing.

### Order Questions To Avoid Context Effects

Earlier questions can change how later ones are interpreted or answered. Order effects are a common and invisible source of bias.

Manage by:

- placing sensitive or demanding questions later, after rapport or engagement;
- randomizing or rotating order where context effects are likely;
- avoiding leading context that frames subsequent questions;
- grouping by topic but considering carryover within and across groups.

Asking about satisfaction with life before a specific domain can anchor the domain rating; order matters.

### Provide Clear And Exhaustive Response Options

Respondents must find their answer among the options, or the question fails. Missing options produce missing or misfit data.

Provide by:

- mutually exclusive and exhaustive categories for closed questions;
- an "other" or open option where the universe is not enumerable;
- a genuine "don't know" or "not applicable" where appropriate;
- avoiding overlapping ranges (e.g., "20-30" and "30-40").

Overlapping or incomplete categories force respondents into wrong answers or nonresponse.

### Minimize Cognitive Burden And Satisficing

Hard questions invite shortcuts: choosing the first acceptable option, straight-lining, or acquiescing. Burden degrades data quality.

Minimize by:

- keeping questions short and concrete;
- avoiding complex recall or computation;
- limiting the number of response options;
- breaking long recall tasks into manageable parts;
- using aided recall where appropriate.

A respondent asked to recall a year of behavior will estimate, guess, or quit.

### Address Sensitive Topics To Reduce Social Desirability Bias

On sensitive topics, respondents over-report socially desirable behavior and under-report stigmatized behavior. Direct questions invite distortion.

Address by:

- assuring confidentiality and explaining its basis;
- normalizing the behavior to reduce stigma;
- using indirect or list techniques where appropriate;
- careful, non-judgmental wording;
- placing sensitive items thoughtfully.

A direct "Have you ever cheated?" underestimates; design can recover more honest reporting.

### Pretest And Revise Before Fielding and consider Mode Effects On Responses

Questions that read well to the author often fail with respondents. Pretesting reveals problems before they corrupt the data.

Pretest by:

- cognitive interviewing to see how respondents interpret items;
- pilot fielding to detect item nonresponse and straight-lining;
- expert review for established flaws;
- revising based on evidence, not defensiveness.

A questionnaire fielded without pretesting is a gamble on wording that has not been tested.

The mode of administration (online, phone, in-person, mail) changes how people answer, independent of their true views.

Consider by:

- visual versus aural presentation changing scale use;
- interviewer presence increasing social desirability bias;
- mode differences in nonresponse and coverage;
- consistency of mode across the sample or accounting for mixed modes.

The same question can yield different distributions by mode, complicating comparison.

## Common Traps

### Double-Barreled Questions

Two concepts in one item produce uninterpretable answers.

### Leading Or Loaded Wording

Biased phrasing measures the question's slant, not the respondent's view.

### Ambiguous Terms And Vague Quantifiers

Divergent interpretations make answers non-comparable.

### Format-Construct Mismatch

Forcing a nuanced attitude into a yes-no format distorts measurement.

### Unmanaged Order Effects

Earlier questions anchoring later ones bias responses invisibly.

### Overlapping Or Missing Categories

Bad response options force wrong answers or nonresponse.

### No Pretesting

Fielding untested wording gambles with data quality.

## Self-Check

- Does each item ask about a single object, action, or attribute, with no double barrels?
- Is the wording neutral, balanced, and free of leading or loaded terms?
- Are all terms, time frames, and referents defined so respondents interpret them uniformly?
- Does the response format match the construct, with appropriate bipolar or unipolar scales?
- Is question order managed to avoid context effects, with rotation or randomization where needed?
- Are response options mutually exclusive, exhaustive, and supplemented where the universe is open?
- Is cognitive burden minimized to reduce satisficing, straight-lining, and acquiescence?
- Are sensitive topics handled to reduce social desirability bias?
- Was the questionnaire pretested with cognitive interviews or a pilot before fielding?
- Are mode effects on responses and coverage considered in design and comparison?
