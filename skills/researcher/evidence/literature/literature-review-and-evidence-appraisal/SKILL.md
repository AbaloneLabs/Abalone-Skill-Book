# Literature Review And Evidence Appraisal

A literature review is not a stack of summaries. It is a disciplined account of what is known, how it is known, how strong the evidence is, where studies disagree, and what remains uncertain. A researcher can cite many papers and still mislead if the search is selective, weak studies are treated as strong, context is ignored, or findings are reported without design limitations.

Use this skill before writing a literature review, summarizing a field, appraising evidence, creating a research gap statement, comparing methods, or deciding whether prior studies justify a claim. The goal is to prevent the agent from turning convenient citations into authority.

## Core Rules

### Define The Review Purpose

Different reviews serve different purposes. A narrative background, scoping review, rapid review, systematic review, meta-analysis, theoretical review, state-of-the-field review, and evidence brief require different methods.

Clarify:

- review question;
- audience;
- decision supported;
- breadth versus depth;
- whether comprehensiveness is required;
- whether quality appraisal is required;
- whether synthesis is qualitative, quantitative, or conceptual;
- timeline;
- documentation expectation.

Do not call a selective background section a systematic review. Use the label that matches the method.

### Make Search Strategy Visible

The reader should know how the evidence was found. Even a non-systematic review benefits from transparent search choices.

Document:

- databases searched;
- search terms;
- date searched;
- date range;
- language limits;
- publication type limits;
- inclusion criteria;
- exclusion criteria;
- grey literature sources;
- citation chaining;
- screening process;
- reason important sources were excluded.

Selective searching can create false consensus. If the review is intentionally selective, say so.

### Appraise Study Quality By Design

Evidence quality depends on the design and how well it was implemented. Different designs have different risks.

Check quantitative studies for:

- sampling strategy;
- comparison group;
- randomization or allocation;
- confounding;
- measurement validity;
- missing data;
- statistical power;
- model assumptions;
- selective reporting;
- external validity.

Check qualitative studies for:

- research question fit;
- sampling rationale;
- data collection depth;
- reflexivity;
- context description;
- analytic transparency;
- triangulation or validation;
- evidence for interpretations;
- transferability.

Check reviews for:

- protocol;
- search comprehensiveness;
- duplicate screening;
- risk-of-bias assessment;
- synthesis method;
- publication bias;
- heterogeneity;
- certainty of evidence.

Do not evaluate every study with the same checklist.

### Separate Finding From Interpretation

A paper's result is not the same as the author's interpretation, and neither is the same as what the current researcher may claim.

For each key source, distinguish:

- research question;
- design;
- sample;
- context;
- measure;
- result;
- author interpretation;
- limitations;
- relevance to current question.

Avoid quoting conclusions while ignoring methods. A bold abstract can rest on narrow data.

### Look For Disagreement And Boundary Conditions

Disagreement is informative. It may reflect different populations, measures, interventions, theories, contexts, time periods, or methods.

Ask:

- Which findings are consistent?
- Which findings conflict?
- Are differences methodological?
- Are differences contextual?
- Are effects subgroup-specific?
- Are findings time-dependent?
- Are there publication or reporting biases?
- Are null results visible?
- Are negative cases explained?

A good review does not erase uncertainty to make a clean story.

### Identify The Actual Gap

A research gap is not simply "few studies exist." The gap should explain why more research is needed and what kind of study would add value.

Gap types include:

- population gap;
- context gap;
- method gap;
- measurement gap;
- theory gap;
- mechanism gap;
- implementation gap;
- conflicting evidence;
- outdated evidence;
- missing replication;
- equity or subgroup gap.

Do not claim novelty because the exact combination of words has not appeared before. Show why the missing evidence matters.

### Weight Evidence Proportionally

Not all citations deserve equal weight. A single small exploratory study should not outweigh a body of stronger evidence. A systematic review should not be accepted blindly if its included studies are weak or search is narrow.

Weight by:

- design fit;
- methodological quality;
- sample relevance;
- recency where relevant;
- replication;
- directness;
- precision;
- risk of bias;
- consistency;
- source independence.

Use proportional skepticism. Minor imperfections do not automatically invalidate a study, but major design flaws should limit claims.

### Track Citation Integrity

Citation errors spread easily. Verify that each citation supports the statement attached to it.

Check:

- citation says what the text claims;
- quoted idea is not from a secondary citation unless stated;
- original source is read when central;
- page or section supports claim;
- no cherry-picked sentence;
- no overclaim from abstract;
- no outdated version;
- no retracted or corrected article ignored.

If a source is retracted, corrected, or under serious dispute, handle it explicitly.

### Synthesize, Do Not Catalogue

A literature review should build an argument. Organize around concepts, methods, findings, debates, and gaps, not one paragraph per article.

Synthesis should show:

- main lines of evidence;
- how studies relate;
- where methods differ;
- what is stronger or weaker;
- what remains unknown;
- why the new study follows.

The reader should leave with a map of the field, not a bibliography in prose.

## Common Traps

### Summarizing Only Friendly Sources

If the review only includes sources that support the preferred claim, it is advocacy, not evidence appraisal.

### Treating Peer Review As Proof

Peer review is a filter, not a guarantee. Appraise design, data, analysis, and limitations.

### Confusing Popularity With Validity

Highly cited papers can be wrong, limited, or outdated. Citation count is not quality appraisal.

### Ignoring Context

A finding from one population, setting, country, era, or implementation condition may not transfer.

### Overstating Gaps

"No research exists" is often false. Search before making absence claims and qualify scope.

### Using Review Labels Loosely

Do not call work systematic, scoping, or meta-analytic unless the method matches the label.

### Forgetting Nonpublication Bias

Published literature can miss null, failed, proprietary, local, or negative findings. Consider grey literature where appropriate.

## Self-Check

- Is the review purpose and review type named accurately?
- Is the search strategy, date, source set, and inclusion logic visible?
- Are studies appraised using criteria appropriate to their design?
- Are findings separated from author interpretations and current claims?
- Are disagreements, null findings, and boundary conditions included?
- Is the research gap specific and meaningful rather than only "more research is needed"?
- Is evidence weighted by quality, directness, consistency, and relevance?
- Does each citation support the exact claim attached to it?
- Are retractions, corrections, outdated findings, and secondary citations handled carefully?
- Does the review synthesize the field rather than catalogue papers one by one?
