# Evidence Grading And Certainty Assessment

Not all evidence is equal, and treating it as equal produces conclusions and recommendations that do not reflect the strength of what is actually known. A single observational study with bias does not provide the same certainty as a body of randomized trials; a finding from surrogate outcomes does not provide the same certainty as a finding from patient-important outcomes; a precise estimate from consistent studies does not provide the same certainty as an imprecise estimate from heterogeneous ones. Evidence grading frameworks, such as GRADE, provide structured methods for rating the certainty of a body of evidence, distinguishing what we are confident about from what remains uncertain. Without such grading, conclusions tend to overstate the evidence, presenting all findings as if equally secure, and decision-makers cannot distinguish strong recommendations from tentative ones. The judgment problem is grading evidence certainty systematically, integrating the grade into conclusions, and communicating uncertainty honestly, rather than presenting all evidence as equally definitive or ignoring certainty altogether.

Use this skill when grading evidence certainty, applying GRADE or similar frameworks, integrating certainty into conclusions, communicating uncertainty, or diagnosing overstated conclusions. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating all evidence as equal, from overgrading weak evidence, from undergrading strong evidence, and from presenting conclusions without the certainty context decision-makers need.

## Core Rules

### Grade Certainty, Not Just Summarize Evidence

Evidence summaries that report findings without grading their certainty leave readers unable to distinguish confident from tentative conclusions. Grading certainty, rating how confident we are that the estimate of effect is adequate to support a decision, is what transforms a summary into a usable basis for conclusions and recommendations.

Grade by:

- rating the certainty of the evidence for each outcome, not just reporting the findings;
- using a structured framework, such as GRADE, to make ratings systematic and transparent;
- distinguishing high, moderate, low, and very low certainty explicitly;
- connecting certainty ratings to the strength of conclusions and recommendations.

A finding reported without its certainty rating is a finding whose reliability is unknown, and readers may treat it as more or less certain than it is.

### Use A Structured Framework For Consistency

Structured frameworks, such as GRADE for intervention evidence, provide domains for rating certainty up or down based on the evidence's properties. Using such frameworks makes grading consistent across outcomes, studies, and reviewers, rather than idiosyncratic. The framework's structure prevents the subjective overall impressions that produce unreliable grades.

Use a framework by:

- applying the framework's domains, risk of bias, consistency, directness, precision, publication bias;
- rating each domain explicitly and documenting the basis;
- following the framework's rules for upgrading and downgrading;
- using the framework consistently across outcomes in the review;
- documenting judgments where the framework requires subjective assessment.

Idiosyncratic certainty ratings reflect the rater's overall impression, which is unreliable; framework-based ratings are transparent and reproducible.

### Rate Down For Risk Of Bias

Evidence from studies with high risk of bias provides less certainty than evidence from rigorous studies, because biased studies may produce distorted estimates. Rate certainty down when the body of evidence comes from studies with serious risk of bias.

Rate down by:

- assessing risk of bias in the included studies using validated tools;
- rating down when a substantial proportion of the evidence comes from high-risk studies;
- considering the likely direction of bias, which often inflates effects;
- conducting sensitivity analyses excluding high-risk studies to test robustness;
- rating down more seriously when bias likely affects the estimate materially.

Evidence from biased studies may show large effects, but the certainty that those effects are real is low, and the grade should reflect this.

### Rate Down For Inconsistency

When studies of the same question produce widely different results, the body of evidence is inconsistent, and the certainty of any pooled or representative estimate is reduced. Inconsistency suggests that the effect varies, that some studies are biased, or that the studies differ in ways that matter, all of which reduce confidence in a single estimate.

Rate down by:

- examining the consistency of effects across studies, statistically and visually;
- rating down when studies show effects in different directions or widely varying magnitudes;
- exploring sources of inconsistency through subgroup analysis;
- recognizing that a pooled estimate from inconsistent studies may not represent any real effect;
- rating down more seriously when inconsistency is unexplained.

A pooled estimate from studies that disagree widely has low certainty, because the average may not represent any real population effect.

### Rate Down For Indirectness

Evidence is indirect when the studies do not directly address the question of interest: different populations, different interventions, different comparisons, or surrogate rather than patient-important outcomes. Indirect evidence provides less certainty than direct evidence for the specific question.

Rate down by:

- examining whether the study populations match the population of interest;
- examining whether the interventions and comparisons match those of interest;
- examining whether outcomes are patient-important or surrogate markers;
- rating down when evidence relies on indirectness in population, intervention, comparison, or outcome;
- recognizing that chains of indirectness compound, reducing certainty further.

Evidence from surrogate outcomes, such as biomarker changes rather than clinical events, provides lower certainty about the patient-important outcomes decisions require.

### Rate Down For Imprecision

Evidence is imprecise when the confidence interval around the estimate is wide enough that the estimate is consistent with both benefit and harm, or with materially different decisions. Imprecise evidence provides low certainty about the true effect size and thus about the decision.

Rate down by:

- examining the width of confidence intervals around estimates;
- rating down when the interval crosses decision thresholds, meaning different decisions are consistent with the evidence;
- considering both statistical and decision-imprecision, where the interval includes both clearly worthwhile and clearly trivial effects;
- recognizing that a precise estimate from few events may be spuriously precise;
- distinguishing imprecision from non-significance, which are different concepts.

An estimate with a confidence interval spanning from large benefit to harm is imprecise, regardless of the p-value, and decisions based on it are uncertain.

### Rate Down For Publication Bias

As discussed in the dedicated skill, publication bias inflates the visible evidence's effect estimates. Where publication bias is suspected, the certainty of the evidence is reduced, because the true effect may be smaller than the published evidence suggests.

Rate down by:

- assessing publication bias through funnel plots and search for unpublished evidence;
- rating down when publication bias is likely and its direction would inflate effects;
- recognizing that a suspiciously positive literature may reflect bias more than effect;
- documenting the basis for the publication bias assessment;
- rating down more seriously when bias is likely to materially affect the estimate.

A body of evidence with high risk of publication bias warrants lower certainty, even if the published studies are individually rigorous and consistent.

### Integrate Certainty Into Conclusions And Recommendations

Certainty ratings must inform the conclusions and recommendations drawn from the evidence. High-certainty evidence supports strong conclusions and recommendations; low-certainty evidence supports tentative conclusions and weak recommendations or calls for more research. Disconnecting conclusions from certainty, presenting all findings as equally actionable, misleads decision-makers.

Integrate by:

- matching the strength of conclusions and recommendations to the certainty of the evidence;
- making recommendations conditional on certainty, strong for high certainty, weak or conditional for low;
- explicitly stating when evidence is insufficient for a recommendation;
- communicating that low certainty means the estimate may change with future evidence;
- recognizing that decision-makers need certainty information to weigh evidence against costs and risks.

A strong recommendation based on low-certainty evidence overstates what is known and may lead to decisions that future evidence reverses.

## Common Traps

### Summarizing Evidence Without Grading Certainty

Findings without certainty ratings leave reliability unknown. Grade certainty for each outcome.

### Idiosyncratic Ratings Without Framework

Subjective overall impressions are unreliable. Use a structured framework consistently.

### Ignoring Risk Of Bias In Grading

Biased studies provide lower certainty. Rate down for serious risk of bias.

### Pooling Inconsistent Studies Without Rating Down

Inconsistent evidence has low certainty. Rate down and explore heterogeneity.

### Treating Surrogate Or Indirect Evidence As Direct

Indirectness reduces certainty. Rate down for indirect population, intervention, comparison, or outcome.

### Equating Imprecision With Non-Significance

Imprecision is about confidence interval width and decision thresholds, not p-values. Rate down for wide intervals.

### Ignoring Publication Bias In Certainty

Publication bias inflates effects. Rate down where bias is likely.

### Conclusions Disconnected From Certainty

Strong recommendations on low-certainty evidence overstate what is known. Integrate certainty into conclusions.

- [ ] The framework is applied consistently across outcomes with documented judgments.
- [ ] Certainty is rated down for serious risk of bias in the body of evidence.
- [ ] Certainty is rated down for unexplained inconsistency across studies.
- [ ] Certainty is rated down for indirectness in population, intervention, comparison, or outcome.
- [ ] Certainty is rated down for imprecision, based on confidence interval width and decision thresholds.
- [ ] Certainty is rated down for suspected publication bias.
- [ ] Conclusions and recommendations are matched in strength to the certainty of the evidence.
- [ ] Low-certainty evidence supports conditional conclusions and weak or no recommendations.
