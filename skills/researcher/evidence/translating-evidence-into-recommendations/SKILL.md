# Translating Evidence Into Recommendations

Evidence does not translate itself into recommendations. A body of evidence must be weighed against benefits, harms, burdens, costs, and the values and preferences of those affected, and the resulting recommendation must reflect both the certainty of the evidence and the balance of these factors. Strong evidence of a small benefit may warrant only a weak recommendation; weak evidence of a large benefit may still warrant a strong recommendation when the alternative is worse. Recommendations that ignore certainty, that present evidence-based conclusions as automatically actionable, or that fail to balance benefits against harms, mislead decision-makers and may cause harm. The judgment problem is translating evidence into recommendations that reflect both the certainty of what is known and the balance of factors that determine what should be done.

Use this skill when translating evidence into recommendations, developing guidelines, balancing benefits against harms, deciding recommendation strength, handling weak evidence with required decisions, or diagnosing recommendations disconnected from evidence certainty. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating evidence summaries as recommendations, from ignoring certainty in recommendation strength, and from failing to balance the multiple factors recommendations require.

## Core Rules

### Recommendations Are Not Just Evidence Summaries

Evidence summaries describe what is known; recommendations describe what should be done. A recommendation requires a judgment that the evidence, weighed against benefits, harms, burdens, costs, and values, supports a particular action. Summarizing evidence without this judgment leaves decision-makers to integrate the factors themselves, which may produce inconsistent or poorly reasoned decisions.

Translate by:

- summarizing the evidence for each relevant outcome, benefit and harm;
- grading the certainty of that evidence;
- weighing benefits against harms, burdens, and costs;
- considering the values and preferences of those affected;
- deciding whether to recommend, recommend against, or offer no recommendation;
- deciding the strength, strong or weak, and direction of the recommendation;
- documenting the basis for each judgment.

A recommendation is a judgment, not a finding, and it must be reasoned and documented as such.

### Match Recommendation Strength To Certainty And Balance

The strength of a recommendation reflects both the certainty of the evidence and the balance of benefits against harms and burdens. High certainty and a clear balance of benefits over harms support a strong recommendation; low certainty or a close balance supports a weak or conditional recommendation. Mismatching strength to these factors, strong recommendations on weak evidence or weak recommendations on strong evidence, misleads decision-makers about how confident the recommendation is.

Match by:

- rating the certainty of the evidence for the critical outcomes;
- assessing the balance of benefits versus harms, burdens, and costs;
- rating strength as strong when certainty is high and benefits clearly outweigh harms;
- rating strength as weak when certainty is low or the balance is close;
- documenting the factors that drove the strength decision;
- recognizing that strong recommendations on low-certainty evidence are occasionally appropriate, when benefits clearly outweigh harms despite uncertainty, but require explicit justification.

Strength is a judgment that integrates certainty and balance, not a direct readout of either alone.

### Explicitly Weigh Benefits Against Harms

Recommendations require weighing the desirable outcomes, benefits, against the undesirable outcomes, harms, burdens, and costs. A treatment with strong evidence of benefit may warrant no recommendation or a recommendation against if the harms or burdens are substantial. This weighing must be explicit, not implicit, so that decision-makers can see how the recommendation was reached.

Weigh by:

- listing the desirable and undesirable outcomes;
- estimating the magnitude of each, based on the evidence;
- considering the time horizon over which benefits and harms accrue;
- weighing them against each other, using formal decision analysis where appropriate;
- documenting the weighing and its basis;
- recognizing that weighing involves values and preferences, which may differ across populations.

Implicit weighing, where the recommendation asserts a balance without showing it, is opaque and cannot be scrutinized or adapted to different contexts.

### Incorporate Values And Preferences

Recommendations apply to people whose values and preferences may differ from those assumed by the recommender. A treatment with modest average benefit may be worthwhile for those who highly value the outcome and acceptable to those who tolerate the burden, but not worthwhile for those who place low value on the benefit or high burden on the treatment. Recommendations must consider whose values are being applied and how much they vary.

Incorporate by:

- identifying the population to whom the recommendation applies;
- considering the values and preferences of that population;
- recognizing where values vary substantially across individuals;
- using weak or conditional recommendations where values vary, allowing individual choice;
- using strong recommendations where values are consistent or the balance is overwhelming;
- documenting the values assumptions and their source.

Recommendations that assume uniform values may be inappropriate for populations whose values differ, and conditional recommendations allow individualization where variation is substantial.

### Address Costs And Resource Use

Recommendations often have resource implications, and in many contexts costs and resource use are a legitimate factor in the recommendation. A highly effective intervention may be impractical or inappropriate to recommend if it is unaffordable or if its opportunity cost, what is forgone to fund it, is high. Ignoring costs produces recommendations that cannot be implemented or that displace more valuable care.

Address by:

- considering the costs and resource use of the intervention;
- considering the opportunity cost, what else the resources could fund;
- weighing costs against benefits using cost-effectiveness analysis where appropriate;
- recognizing that cost considerations vary across settings and health systems;
- documenting whether and how costs influenced the recommendation;
- being transparent about value judgments involved in cost considerations.

Cost considerations are context-specific and ethically sensitive, and must be transparent rather than implicit.

### Handle Weak Evidence With Required Decisions

Sometimes evidence is weak but a decision is required, because inaction has harms of its own. In these cases, a recommendation may be necessary despite low certainty, and the challenge is to make it honestly, signaling the uncertainty while still providing guidance. Refusing to recommend because evidence is weak may abandon decision-makers who must act.

Handle by:

- recognizing when a decision is required despite weak evidence;
- making a weak or conditional recommendation based on the best available evidence;
- explicitly stating the uncertainty and the basis for the recommendation;
- recommending research to address the evidence gap;
- setting a timeframe for reviewing the recommendation as evidence accrues;
- distinguishing "insufficient evidence to recommend" from "recommend against".

A weak recommendation in the face of weak evidence is often more useful than no recommendation, because it provides guidance while signaling uncertainty.

### Distinguish Evidence Quality From Recommendation Strength

Evidence quality and recommendation strength are related but distinct. High-quality evidence can support a weak recommendation when the balance of benefits and harms is close; low-quality evidence can support a strong recommendation when benefits clearly outweigh harms despite uncertainty. Conflating the two, assuming strong evidence automatically means strong recommendations, misleads decision-makers about the reasoning.

Distinguish by:

- reporting evidence quality and recommendation strength separately;
- explaining how each contributed to the recommendation;
- recognizing that strong recommendations can rest on low-quality evidence, with justification;
- recognizing that weak recommendations can rest on high-quality evidence, when the balance is close;
- documenting the reasoning that connects evidence to recommendation.

The GRADE separation of evidence quality from recommendation strength is a model for this distinction, and should be applied where possible.

## Common Traps

### Treating Evidence Summaries As Recommendations

Summaries describe what is known; recommendations judge what to do. Make the judgment explicit.

### Strong Recommendations On Weak Evidence Without Justification

Strong recommendations require either high certainty or overwhelming balance. Justify exceptions.

### Ignoring Harms And Burdens In The Balance

Benefits must be weighed against harms, burdens, and costs. Make the weighing explicit.

### Assuming Uniform Values And Preferences

Values vary across individuals and populations. Use conditional recommendations where variation is substantial.

### Ignoring Costs And Opportunity Costs

Costs affect feasibility and appropriateness. Address them transparently.

### Refusing To Recommend When Decisions Are Required

Weak evidence may still require guidance. Make conditional recommendations and flag research needs.

### Conflating Evidence Quality With Recommendation Strength

They are related but distinct. Report and reason about them separately.

### Recommendations Without Documented Reasoning

Recommendations are judgments. Document the basis for scrutiny and adaptation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The recommendation is a judgment weighing evidence against benefits, harms, burdens, costs, and values, not just a summary.
- [ ] Recommendation strength matches both evidence certainty and the balance of benefits against harms.
- [ ] Benefits are explicitly weighed against harms, burdens, and costs, with the weighing documented.
- [ ] Values and preferences of the affected population are considered, with variation addressed through conditional recommendations.
- [ ] Costs and resource use are addressed transparently, including opportunity costs.
- [ ] Where evidence is weak but decisions are required, a conditional recommendation is made with uncertainty flagged.
- [ ] Evidence quality and recommendation strength are reported and reasoned about separately.
- [ ] The basis for the recommendation is documented for scrutiny and adaptation to other contexts.
- [ ] The recommendation distinguishes "insufficient evidence to recommend" from "recommend against".
- [ ] Decision-makers can see how the recommendation was reached and how confident it is.
