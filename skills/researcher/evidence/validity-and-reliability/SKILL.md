# Validity And Reliability

A measurement is only useful if it captures what the researcher thinks it captures, and if it does so consistently enough to trust. Researchers routinely conflate these two properties, report a reliability coefficient as if it proved validity, or treat a validated instrument as automatically valid for a new population, language, or purpose. The result is a literature built on scores that may be stable but wrong, or right in one group and meaningless in another.

Use this skill when selecting, adapting, or developing measures; when interpreting reliability statistics; when defending the inferential use of scores; and when moving an instrument across populations, languages, or settings. The goal is to keep the agent from treating validity as a property of an instrument rather than an argument about evidence, and from confusing consistency with correctness. The agent has freedom to choose instruments, but must justify the validity argument and reliability evidence for the specific intended inference.

## Core Rules

### Treat Validity As An Argument, Not A Stamp

Validity is not a binary attribute an instrument possesses. It is a degree of support for a specific intended interpretation and use of scores. The same instrument can be valid for one purpose and invalid for another.

Build the argument around:

- the construct the score is meant to represent;
- the population and context of intended use;
- the specific inference or decision the score supports;
- the evidence that supports that inference;
- the plausible rival interpretations the evidence rules out;
- the boundaries and known weaknesses.

A "validated scale" imported into a new population restarts the argument, it does not inherit it.

### Build Evidence Across Multiple Validity Types

Different validity questions address different threats. A strong argument assembles converging evidence rather than relying on a single type.

Consider:

- content validity, whether items represent the construct's domain;
- substantive validity, whether response processes match the intended construct;
- structural validity, whether internal structure matches the hypothesized dimensionality;
- convergent and discriminant validity, relationships with related and unrelated measures;
- criterion validity, relationship with an outcome or gold standard;
- consequential validity, whether intended and unintended consequences of score use are fair;
- face validity, a weak but sometimes necessary surface check.

Each type addresses a specific threat; absence of evidence for one type is not proof of invalidity, but it leaves a gap.

### Do Not Equate Reliability With Validity

Reliability is the consistency of measurement; validity is the correctness of inference. A measure can be highly reliable and systematically wrong, like a scale that reads five kilograms heavy every time.

Keep them distinct:

- reliability constrains validity, since a noisy measure cannot be valid for fine distinctions;
- high reliability does not establish validity;
- validity does not require the highest possible reliability if it is sufficient for the decision;
- different reliability estimates answer different consistency questions.

Report reliability as one input to the validity argument, not as a substitute for it.

### Choose The Right Reliability Estimate For The Question

There are many reliability coefficients, and they are not interchangeable. Each answers a specific consistency question.

Match coefficient to question:

- internal consistency, such as Cronbach alpha or McDonald omega, for items measuring one construct at one time;
- test-retest reliability for stability over time;
- alternate or parallel forms reliability for equivalence of different item sets;
- inter-rater reliability for agreement among coders or observers;
- intra-rater reliability for consistency of one rater over occasions;
- intra-class correlation for continuous scores with multiple sources of variance.

A high alpha does not prove temporal stability, and a high test-retest correlation does not prove internal coherence. Use the estimate that matches the inference.

### Interpret Reliability Coefficients In Context

Cut-offs such as "alpha above 0.7 is acceptable" are heuristics, not laws. The required reliability depends on the stakes and the decision.

Consider:

- high-stakes individual decisions demand higher reliability than group means;
- short scales mechanically produce lower alpha;
- alpha is inflated by many similar items even if dimensionality is poor;
- omega is often preferable when items load unevenly or scale length is short;
- reliability is sample-dependent and may differ across populations;
- standard error of measurement often matters more than the coefficient for individual decisions.

Report the estimate with its confidence interval and the sample in which it was obtained.

### Test Measurement Invariance Before Comparing Groups

Scores only mean the same thing across groups if the measurement function is equivalent. Comparing means or relationships across groups without invariance evidence can produce false differences or mask real ones.

Assess, where applicable:

- configural invariance, the same factor structure across groups;
- metric invariance, equivalent factor loadings;
- scalar invariance, equivalent intercepts allowing mean comparison;
- strict invariance, equivalent residual variances;
- differential item functioning for specific items that behave differently across groups;
- longitudinal invariance for measures used to track change over time.

Lack of invariance is not a nuisance; it is a finding about whether comparison is licensed.

### Examine Validity And Reliability When Adapting Instruments

Translation, mode change, item revision, or moving to a new population can alter both reliability and validity. Adaptation is a new measurement problem.

Plan for:

- forward and back translation with reconciliation;
- cognitive interviewing in the new population;
- expert review of content coverage;
- pilot estimation of reliability;
- re-examination of factor structure;
- differential item functioning analysis;
- re-establishing convergent and discriminant evidence;
- documenting what changed and why.

A translated instrument is not automatically equivalent to the original, and a self-report moved online is not automatically equivalent to its paper version.

### Align Measurement Quality To The Inference

Not every study needs a gold-standard instrument. The required evidence depends on the claim.

Match quality to claim:

- exploratory work can tolerate weaker measures if labeled as such;
- individual-level high-stakes decisions demand strong reliability and validity;
- longitudinal change demands sensitivity to change and longitudinal invariance;
- cross-cultural comparison demands measurement invariance;
- mediation and mechanism claims demand measures that isolate the construct;
- screening tools demand sensitivity and specificity against a criterion.

Do not over-engineer a low-stakes descriptive study, and do not under-engineer a high-stakes inferential one.

## Common Traps

### Reporting Alpha As Proof Of Validity

A high alpha proves only that items correlate internally. It says nothing about whether they measure the intended construct.

### Treating A Validated Instrument As Universally Valid

Validation is population-, language-, and purpose-specific. Importing an instrument without re-establishing evidence for the new context can produce meaningless scores.

### Trusting A Single Reliability Coefficient

Alpha, test-retest, and inter-rater coefficients answer different questions. Reporting one and implying the others invites misinference.

### Ignoring Measurement Invariance Across Groups

Comparing means across groups whose measurement functions differ manufactures or masks differences. Invariance is a prerequisite for comparison, not an optional refinement.

### Using Long Scales To Inflate Alpha

Adding redundant items raises alpha without improving measurement. A high alpha built from nearly identical items can hide multidimensionality.

### Confusing Reliability With Stability Of The Construct

Test-retest reliability assumes the construct is stable across occasions. If the construct genuinely changes, low correlation may reflect real change, not poor measurement.

### Applying Generic Cut-Offs Without Considering Stakes

A coefficient acceptable for group-level research may be unacceptable for individual decisions. Cut-offs must be calibrated to the inference and its consequences.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is validity framed as an argument about a specific intended inference rather than a property of the instrument?
- [ ] Is evidence assembled across content, structural, convergent, discriminant, criterion, substantive, and consequential validity as relevant?
- [ ] Are reliability and validity kept distinct, with reliability reported as one input to the validity argument?
- [ ] Is the reliability coefficient matched to the consistency question, such as internal, test-retest, alternate-form, or inter-rater?
- [ ] Are coefficients reported with confidence intervals, the sample, and the stakes in mind?
- [ ] Is measurement invariance tested before comparing groups, time points, or cultures?
- [ ] Are adapted, translated, or mode-changed instruments re-validated for the new context?
- [ ] Is measurement quality aligned to the strength of the intended inference?
- [ ] Are rival interpretations of scores identified and addressed with evidence?
- [ ] Are weaknesses, boundaries, and known limitations of the measure reported honestly?
