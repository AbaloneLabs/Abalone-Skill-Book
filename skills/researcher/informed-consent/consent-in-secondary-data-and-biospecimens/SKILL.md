# Consent In Secondary Data And Biospecimens

Research increasingly uses data and specimens that were collected for other purposes. The consent question becomes whether the original agreement covers the new use, and what to do when it does not. Researchers often assume that because data exist and are accessible, consent has been satisfied, but consent is specific to use, and secondary use can exceed what participants agreed to. The judgment problem is determining what the original consent covered, what broad or future consent legitimately permits, and when waiver or re-consent is required.

Use this skill when using existing data or biospecimens, planning broad consent, deciding on re-consent or waiver, and handling consent for scraped or secondary data. The goal is to keep the agent from treating the existence of data as proof of consent and to make the fit between original consent and new use explicit.

This is a high-stakes domain because secondary use beyond consent violates participant trust and can cause privacy harm, especially with re-identifiable data. When the consent status of secondary data is unclear, the agent should consult the IRB and the data steward rather than proceeding on assumption.

## Core Rules

### Determine What The Original Consent Actually Covered

Consent is specific. Participants agreed to particular uses, and secondary research must fit within those terms.

Examine:

- the original consent form or agreement and what uses it described;
- whether the new use falls within the described scope or exceeds it;
- whether the original consent mentioned future or unspecified research;
- whether limitations on sensitive uses, populations, or commercialization were stated;
- whether the consent was obtained to standards that satisfy current requirements.

Do not assume that broad consent language in an old form covers any conceivable future use. Read the actual terms and judge the fit honestly. Where the new use exceeds the scope, the original consent does not authorize it.

### Use Broad Consent Deliberately And Within Limits

Broad consent asks participants to agree to unspecified future research uses. It is permitted in some frameworks but with constraints and ongoing duties.

When using or seeking broad consent:

- be honest with participants that future uses are not fully specified;
- describe the types of research, data, and sharing as specifically as feasible;
- provide mechanisms for participants to learn about or withdraw from future uses;
- maintain governance that keeps secondary use within the broad consent's bounds;
- recognize that broad consent does not extend to uses fundamentally different from what a participant would expect.

Broad consent is not a blank check. It permits future research within a described envelope, not any use the researcher later finds interesting.

### Seek Waiver Only When The Criteria Are Genuinely Met

When consent for secondary use was not obtained or does not cover the new use, waiver may be possible, but only under strict criteria.

Waiver typically requires:

- that the research could not practicably be carried out without the waiver;
- that the research poses no more than minimal risk;
- that the waiver will not adversely affect participants' rights and welfare;
- that participants will be given pertinent information after the fact when appropriate;
- that the data could not be obtained through a process that includes consent.

Waiver is a determination for the IRB, not the investigator. Self-justifying waiver to avoid the work of re-consent or to access data faster is a serious error.

### Plan Re-Consent When Scope Changes Materially

When secondary use exceeds the original consent and waiver is not appropriate, re-consent may be required. This is common when new risks, linkages, or sharing emerge.

Re-consent when:

- new data linkage or combination materially increases re-identification or sensitivity risk;
- the research moves into areas participants may not have anticipated, such as sensitive traits or controversial topics;
- data will be shared more broadly or with different types of users than disclosed;
- commercial use or intellectual property claims emerge that were not disclosed;
- the participant population or context has changed what the use means.

Re-consent respects participants' continuing authority over their data. Where re-contact is impossible, the path is waiver or non-use, not silent expansion.

### Treat Biospecimens With Heightened Consent Attention

Biospecimens carry genetic, familial, and re-identification risks that ordinary data do not. Consent for biospecimen use requires special care.

Address:

- whether the specimen is identifiable and what genomic or familial information it can yield;
- whether the original consent covered the type of analysis now planned, including whole-genome or multi-omic work;
- whether sharing with biobanks or commercial parties was disclosed;
- whether return of individual findings was addressed and what the plan is;
- whether family members have interests that individual consent does not cover.

Biospecimen consent that omits these dimensions does not authorize later genomic or broad sharing uses. The gap must be addressed through re-consent or waiver, not assumed away.

### Handle Scraped And Platform Data Under Consent And Terms

Data scraped from online platforms are not automatically free to use in research. Consent and terms of service both apply.

Consider:

- whether platform terms permit research use and under what conditions;
- whether users reasonably expected their content to be used in research;
- whether the data are identifiable or re-identifiable despite being public;
- whether the research could embarrass, stigmatize, or harm individuals or communities;
- whether ethical, as distinct from legal, obligations require notification or consent.

Public accessibility is not ethical permission. Re-identification, community harm, and violated expectations all create obligations that mere access does not discharge.

### Maintain Provenance And Consent Records For Secondary Use

Secondary research must be able to show the consent basis for the data it uses. Without provenance and consent records, the basis cannot be defended.

Maintain:

- the source of each dataset or specimen and the terms of access;
- the consent form or agreement under which it was collected;
- any broad consent, waiver, or re-consent that authorizes the current use;
- data use agreements and sharing restrictions;
- the chain of custody from collection to current analysis.

A secondary analysis that cannot produce its consent basis is vulnerable to challenge and may need to halt. The records are part of the research, not an afterthought.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Existing Data Means Consented Data

The existence and accessibility of data do not establish that participants agreed to this use. Consent is specific to use.

### Treating Broad Consent As Unlimited

Broad consent permits future research within a described envelope, not any use. Uses outside the envelope require re-consent or waiver.

### Self-Justifying Waiver To Avoid Work

Waiver requires strict criteria and IRB determination. Invoking it to avoid re-consent or to access data faster is a serious error.

### Silent Expansion Beyond Original Scope

Linking datasets, broadening sharing, or moving into sensitive analysis without checking or updating consent violates the original agreement.

### Underestimating Biospecimen Risks

Biospecimens carry genomic and familial risks. Consent that omits these dimensions does not authorize later broad genomic use.

### Treating Public Data As Ethically Free

Public accessibility is not ethical permission. Re-identification and community harm create obligations beyond legal access.

### Losing The Consent Provenance Record

Without records of source, consent, and agreements, the consent basis for secondary use cannot be defended.

## Self-Check

- Has the original consent been read and the fit between its scope and the new use judged honestly?
- Is broad consent used deliberately, within described limits, with governance and withdrawal mechanisms?
- Is waiver sought only when the strict criteria are genuinely met and determined by the IRB rather than self-justified?
- Is re-consent planned when linkage, sharing, sensitivity, or commercial use materially changes the scope?
- Are biospecimens given heightened consent attention, covering identifiability, genomic analysis, sharing, and return of findings?
- Are scraped and platform data assessed under both terms of service and ethical expectations, recognizing public access is not ethical permission?
- Are provenance and consent records maintained so the consent basis for secondary use can be defended?
- Where the consent status of secondary data is unclear, has the IRB and the data steward been consulted rather than proceeding on assumption?
