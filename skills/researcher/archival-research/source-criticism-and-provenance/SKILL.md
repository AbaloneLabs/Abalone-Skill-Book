# Source Criticism And Provenance

Archival and historical claims rest on the quality of the sources behind them. A document that looks authoritative can be a later copy, a forgery, a biased selection, or a record whose survival itself distorts the past. Source criticism is the discipline of asking how a source came to exist, who made it and why, what it actually shows, and what it cannot show. Researchers who skip this step produce claims that collapse when a single assumption about authorship, dating, or transmission turns out to be wrong.

Use this skill before drawing conclusions from primary documents, when assessing the reliability of a record, when tracing where a document came from, when comparing competing versions of a text, and when deciding whether a source can carry the inferential weight a claim requires. The goal is to keep the agent from treating any surviving document as transparent evidence of what happened, and to make the chain of inference from document to claim explicit and defensible.

This is a high-stakes domain. Errors propagate into published narratives, misattributed authorship, and false historical claims that are difficult to retract. When uncertainty about authenticity, dating, or provenance cannot be resolved, the agent should say so and consult a specialist archivist, paleographer, or subject historian rather than forcing a confident conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Establish Origin And Provenance Before Reading For Content

Before asking what a document says, establish where it came from. Provenance is the documented history of a record's creation, custody, and transfer. A document without a clear provenance is harder to trust and harder to cite.

Determine:

- who created the record and in what institutional or personal capacity;
- when and where it was produced;
- the immediate purpose or function it served (administrative, legal, personal, propagandistic);
- the chain of custody from creation to the present archive;
- whether the archive acquired it through donation, purchase, transfer, or seizure;
- whether gaps or reordering in the chain of custody raise integrity concerns.

A document whose provenance is unknown, broken, or suspicious cannot be read the same way as one whose custody is continuous and documented. Flag provenance gaps explicitly rather than smoothing over them.

### Apply External Criticism To Test Authenticity

External criticism asks whether a document is what it claims to be. Forgery, misattribution, later interpolation, and accidental misdating are real possibilities, especially for high-value or politically charged records.

Check:

- material support (paper, parchment, ink, binding) consistent with the claimed date;
- handwriting or script consistent with the period and region;
- language, vocabulary, and formulae consistent with the era;
- watermarks, seals, signatures, and administrative markings;
- whether the content anachronistically references events or terms from a later period;
- whether independent corroboration exists for the document's existence;
- whether the custodial history explains how it survived.

When authenticity is in doubt, do not treat the document as genuine by default. Note the uncertainty and seek specialist confirmation before building claims on it.

### Apply Internal Criticism To Assess Reliability

Once a document is accepted as authentic, internal criticism asks how reliable its content is. A genuine document can still be inaccurate, biased, incomplete, or self-serving.

Assess:

- the author's proximity to the events described (eyewitness, secondhand, rumor);
- the author's motive and stake in the record;
- the time elapsed between event and recording;
- whether the record was produced under constraint, censorship, or fear of surveillance;
- what the author would have known and what they could not have known;
- internal consistency and contradictions;
- omissions and silences, which are often as significant as what is stated.

Reliability is not binary. A self-serving administrative report may be unreliable about blame but accurate about dates and logistics. Calibrate trust to the specific claim being drawn from the source.

### Recognize The Biases Of Survival And Selection

Archives are not neutral samples of the past. What survives is shaped by deliberate preservation, accidental loss, administrative weeding, political destruction, and the purchasing priorities of collecting institutions.

Consider:

- which voices produced written records and which did not (literacy, power, bureaucracy);
- which records were deemed worth keeping and which were destroyed;
- whether the surviving corpus overrepresents elites, institutions, or literate groups;
- whether colonial, state, or institutional archives systematically excluded certain populations;
- whether digitization has compounded selection bias by privileging already-famous collections;
- whether the archive itself was assembled to support a particular narrative.

A claim built only on the records that happen to survive risks describing the archive rather than the past. Name the survival bias explicitly and avoid generalizing from preserved records to populations who left few or no records.

### Compare Sources Rather Than Relying On A Single Witness

A single source can be wrong, partial, or misleading. Convergence across independent sources strengthens a claim; divergence forces interpretation.

Compare:

- multiple independent accounts of the same event;
- official versus private records of the same transaction;
- records produced by different parties with different interests;
- textual variants when multiple manuscript copies survive;
- material evidence, images, or quantitative records against narrative accounts.

When sources conflict, do not silently pick the convenient one. Explain the conflict, weigh the likely reasons for divergence, and state which reading is better supported and why. When only one source exists, state that the claim rests on a single witness and cannot be independently corroborated.

### Distinguish Primary, Secondary, And Derivative Evidence

Confusing these levels weakens inference. A primary source is a record produced by a participant or eyewitness close in time to the event. A secondary source is an interpretation built on primary sources. A derivative source is a copy, transcription, translation, or summary of an earlier original.

Clarify:

- whether you are reading the original, a contemporary copy, a later transcription, or a translation;
- whether editorial interventions, normalization, or silent corrections have altered the text;
- whether a published edition is based on the best surviving manuscript or a convenient one;
- whether a digital surrogate reproduces the original faithfully or introduces compression, color shifts, or cropping.

Whenever possible, work from the earliest surviving witness and cite the specific manuscript or record, not a derivative reprint. When relying on an edition, know its editorial principles.

### Document Citation At The Level Of The Specific Record

Imprecise citation makes claims unverifiable and undermines the integrity of the record. A folder-level or box-level reference is often insufficient.

Record:

- repository name and location;
- collection or fond name and number;
- series, subseries, box, folder, and item identifiers;
- document date as given and as corrected if needed;
- folio or page reference;
- whether the citation refers to an original, a copy, or a digital surrogate;
- any access restrictions or rights that affect reproduction.

Citation should allow another researcher to retrieve the exact document. If the repository uses a different finding-aid system after reprocessing, note the citation as it stood at the time of use.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Document's Survival As Evidence Of Typicality

Because records survive unevenly, the documents in an archive are rarely representative of what once existed. Treating surviving records as typical overstates their generality and can produce a distorted picture of the past.

### Reading Administrative Records As Neutral Description

Bureaucratic records reflect the categories, interests, and blind spots of the institutions that produced them. A census, police file, or ledger encodes institutional logic, not an objective portrait of the people it describes.

### Trusting Published Editions Without Checking Editorial Choices

Editions normalize spelling, silently emend text, select among variants, and sometimes omit material deemed irrelevant. Reading an edition as if it were the original inherits the editor's judgments invisibly.

### Inferring Intent From A Single Document

A single letter, memo, or report rarely establishes intent on its own. Drafts, revisions, surrounding correspondence, and institutional context can change the reading entirely.

### Ignoring The Author's Stake And Audience

A document written for a superior, a court, a rival, or posterity is shaped by its anticipated reader. Reading it as a candid private record ignores the performance embedded in the writing.

### Collapsing Copy And Original

Treating a transcription, translation, or digital scan as equivalent to the original obscures the layers of mediation. Errors and interventions at each layer can change meaning.

### Overreading Silences

Absence of evidence is not always evidence of absence, but it is also not meaningless. The trap is to either ignore silences entirely or to fill them with confident speculation. Name what is missing and resist inventing its content.

## Self-Check

- Is the provenance of each key source documented, including creator, date, purpose, and chain of custody?
- Has external criticism been applied to test authenticity, with material, script, and content consistency checked?
- Has internal criticism been applied to assess reliability, including author proximity, motive, and omissions?
- Are the biases of archival survival and selection named rather than treated as neutral?
- Are claims built on convergence across independent sources, or is reliance on a single witness stated explicitly?
- Is each source classified correctly as primary, secondary, or derivative, with the specific witness cited?
- Do citations identify the exact record at item, folio, or page level so the claim is verifiable?
- Are conflicts between sources explained and weighed rather than silently resolved in a convenient direction?
- For any unresolved question of authenticity, dating, or provenance, has a specialist archivist, paleographer, or subject historian been consulted rather than forcing a confident conclusion?
