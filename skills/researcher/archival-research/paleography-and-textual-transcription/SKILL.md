# Paleography And Textual Transcription

Transcription is the bridge between a manuscript and any analysis built on it. A transcription that silently regularizes, guesses at illegible words, or imposes modern conventions becomes an invisible filter between the reader and the source. Once a transcription circulates, later researchers treat it as the text, and errors propagate into citation, translation, and interpretation. The judgment problem is how to render a historical document into readable form while preserving the evidence that allows others to check, correct, and reinterpret the reading.

Use this skill when transcribing manuscript or early printed material, when confronting unfamiliar scripts or hands, when deciding between diplomatic and normalized transcription, when handling abbreviations, deletions, insertions, and damage, and when representing uncertainty. The goal is to keep the agent from producing a clean-looking transcription that has silently discarded the very features that make the source evidentiary.

This is a high-stakes domain because transcriptions become the basis for historical claims, editions, and digital corpora that are difficult to retract. When a reading is genuinely uncertain, the agent should mark it as such and consult a specialist paleographer rather than presenting a guess as settled text.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Script, Hand, And Period Before Transcribing

You cannot reliably read what you have not identified. Scripts follow conventions of abbreviation, letterform, and abbreviation that vary by region, period, and document type.

Establish:

- the script family (e.g., a national book hand, a cursive chancery hand, a secretary hand);
- the approximate date range and regional origin;
- the number of hands in the document and where each begins and ends;
- the document type and its conventional formulae, which help predict expected words;
- the language and its historical stage, including spelling variation;
- reference manuals and facsimile corpora for the specific script.

A confident identification of script and context narrows plausible readings dramatically. Guessing letterforms in isolation is unreliable.

### Choose A Transcription Convention And State It

Different research purposes call for different levels of fidelity. The choice must be explicit because it determines what the transcription preserves and what it discards.

Choose between:

- diplomatic transcription, preserving original spelling, punctuation, abbreviations, capitalization, and lineation as exactly as feasible;
- semi-diplomatic transcription, expanding abbreviations (usually marked) but preserving spelling and significant features;
- normalized transcription, regularizing spelling and punctuation for readability, generally unsuitable as primary evidence;
- encoded transcription using a markup standard to record deletions, insertions, and uncertainty explicitly.

State the convention in a headnote. A reader should know, without guessing, whether abbreviations have been expanded, whether line breaks are preserved, and how corrections are represented.

### Handle Abbreviations, Contractions, And Suspensions Consistively

Historical documents use abbreviation systems that are systematic but not self-evident. Inconsistent handling produces a transcription that misrepresents the source.

Apply:

- expansion of abbreviations, usually marked with italics or brackets per the stated convention;
- recognition of suspension marks (superscript letters, macrons, tildes, special signs) common to the script;
- consistency across the document, not case-by-case improvisation;
- a note where an abbreviation is genuinely ambiguous.

Silently expanding or silently ignoring abbreviations both distort the text. The reader must be able to recover what was abbreviated.

### Represent Deletions, Insertions, And Revisions Faithfully

Authors crossed out, inserted, and revised text. These revisions are evidence of thought and process, not noise to be cleaned away.

Record:

- deleted text, usually struck through or bracketed with a deletion marker;
- insertions and their placement (interlinear, marginal, on a separate slip);
- substitutions and the order in which they were made where determinable;
- revisions that change meaning, with enough detail that a reader can reconstruct the layering.

A clean final reading text erases the author's decisions. Preserve the revision sequence so interpretation can engage with the document as a process, not just a product.

### Mark Uncertainty Rather Than Smoothing It

Illegible, damaged, or ambiguous passages are common. The temptation is to produce a plausible word and move on, but an unmarked guess becomes evidence for the next reader.

Use a consistent system to signal:

- entirely illegible text, with an estimate of missing characters or words;
- partially legible text, with the doubtful letters marked;
- conjectural readings, clearly distinguished from certain readings;
- supplied text inferred from context but not visible on the page;
- damage, fading, or obstruction that affects the reading.

A transcription dotted with uncertainty markers is more trustworthy than a clean one, because it shows where confidence ends. Reviewers and future researchers rely on those markers.

### Preserve The Material And Spatial Features That Carry Meaning

Layout is evidence. Line breaks, column structure, marginalia, page breaks, and the physical placement of text can carry meaning that flat transcription destroys.

Decide and document how to handle:

- line and page breaks;
- marginal and interlinear additions;
- columns, tables, and spatial arrangement;
- changes of ink or hand;
- foliation, pagination, and catchwords;
- physical features such as seals, wrappers, or binding that contextualize the text.

Where spatial features matter to the argument, describe them in a note or use an image alongside the transcription. Do not assume a linear text captures what the page shows.

### Cross-Check Transcription Against The Image And The Object

Transcription errors accumulate through fatigue and familiarity. Systematic checking reduces error and catches persistent misreadings.

Build in checks:

- re-read the transcription against a high-quality image after a break;
- have a second reader check difficult passages independently;
- compare against any existing edition or calendar, noting disagreements;
- verify proper names, dates, and formulaic passages against parallel documents;
- keep a log of recurring letterform confusions specific to the hand.

A single unchecked transcription of a difficult hand should not be the sole basis for a strong claim. Independent verification is the safeguard against systematic error.

## Common Traps

### Regularizing Spelling To Make The Text Read Smoothly

Silent modernization of spelling and punctuation produces a readable text that no longer represents the source. Later readers inherit the regularization as if it were original.

### Guessing Illegible Words From Context Without Marking

A contextually plausible guess, unmarked, becomes part of the record. When the guess is wrong, it misleads everyone who trusts the transcription.

### Treating All Hands As If They Were One

Multi-hand documents shift conventions, abbreviation habits, and letterforms. Transcribing all hands with one mental model produces systematic error in the transitions.

### Expanding Abbreviations Inconsistently

Expanding some abbreviations and not others, or expanding the same abbreviation differently in different places, makes the transcription internally incoherent.

### Discarding Revisions To Reach A Clean Reading Text

Crossings-out and insertions show the author thinking. Removing them to produce a tidy text erases evidence that may be central to interpretation.

### Trusting A Single Reading Without Verification

Fatigue and pattern-fixation produce persistent misreadings that the transcriber cannot see in their own work. Lack of independent checking lets these propagate.

### Ignoring Layout And Marginalia

Flattening a spatially complex document into linear text can reverse its meaning, especially for administrative, legal, or annotated manuscripts where placement matters.

## Self-Check

- Has the script, hand or hands, period, and document type been identified before transcription began?
- Is the transcription convention (diplomatic, semi-diplomatic, normalized, encoded) stated explicitly in a headnote?
- Are abbreviations, contractions, and suspensions handled consistently and in a way the reader can recover?
- Are deletions, insertions, and revisions preserved with enough detail to reconstruct the author's process?
- Is uncertainty marked with a consistent system distinguishing illegible, conjectural, and supplied text?
- Are material and spatial features such as line breaks, marginalia, columns, and hand changes preserved or described?
- Has the transcription been cross-checked against a high-quality image and, where possible, by a second reader?
- For genuinely uncertain readings of difficult or damaged hands, has a specialist paleographer been consulted rather than presenting a guess as settled text?
