# Triangulation Design And Source Selection

Triangulation is the strategy of examining a question from more than one vantage point so that the strengths of one source cover the weaknesses of another. Done well, it strengthens confidence in a finding. Done poorly, it produces the appearance of corroboration from sources that are not independent, or it suppresses genuine divergence to reach a tidy conclusion. The judgment problem is selecting vantage points that are genuinely independent and relevant, and designing the comparison so that convergence and divergence both inform rather than comfort.

Use this skill when planning triangulation, when choosing among method, source, investigator, and theory triangulation, when selecting which sources to combine, and when designing how the comparison will be interpreted. The goal is to keep the agent from treating triangulation as the mere accumulation of sources and to make the independence and relevance of each vantage point explicit.

This is a high-stakes domain because triangulation is often invoked to defend findings that the evidence does not actually support. When the independence of sources or the appropriateness of a triangulation design is unclear, the agent should consult a methodologist rather than claiming convergence the design cannot establish.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Type Of Triangulation That Fits The Question

Triangulation is not one technique. The type must match the inferential need.

Distinguish:

- method triangulation, combining different methods (e.g., interviews and observation) to test whether a finding holds across methodological lenses;
- source triangulation, combining different data sources (participants, documents, sites) to test whether a finding holds across sources;
- investigator triangulation, using multiple researchers to reduce dependence on a single analyst's reading;
- theory triangulation, using multiple theoretical frames to illuminate different facets of the same data.

Each type addresses a different threat. Method triangulation tests robustness to method bias; source triangulation tests robustness to source bias; investigator triangulation tests robustness to analyst bias; theory triangulation tests the completeness of interpretation. State which threat the triangulation is meant to address.

### Ensure The Vantage Points Are Genuinely Independent

Triangulation's power comes from independence. Sources that share the same biases provide no corroboration.

Check independence:

- whether data sources share a common origin that would produce consistent error;
- whether methods share assumptions that would produce consistent blindness;
- whether investigators share training or commitments that would produce consistent readings;
- whether theoretical frames are genuinely distinct or merely variants of one perspective;
- whether documents derive from the same institution or author that generated the primary account.

Two interviews with participants from the same tightly knit group, sharing the same institutional story, are not independent corroboration. Name the dependencies and avoid claiming independence that does not exist.

### Select Sources For Both Relevance And Difference

A good triangulation source is both relevant to the question and different enough from the others to constitute a real test.

For each source consider:

- whether it speaks to the same question or construct;
- whether it brings a different perspective, population, or method;
- whether its strengths compensate for another source's weaknesses;
- whether its weaknesses are not shared with the other sources;
- whether it is strong enough that divergence would actually force reinterpretation.

Adding a weak source that cannot challenge the finding adds bulk without triangulation. Adding a strong but redundant source adds comfort without test. Seek relevant difference.

### Design The Comparison Before Interpreting It

How convergence and divergence will be read should be decided before the sources are examined together. Otherwise interpretation bends toward the desired verdict.

Predefine:

- what would count as convergence across the sources;
- what would count as divergence;
- how partial convergence will be treated;
- what each pattern would imply for the finding;
- how conflicts between equally credible sources will be handled.

A comparison designed after seeing the results can always be framed to support the preferred conclusion. Prior design protects the integrity of the inference.

### Treat Divergence As Evidence, Not Noise

Divergent findings are often the most informative product of triangulation. They reveal where the finding is conditional, contextual, or wrong.

When sources diverge:

- investigate the reasons rather than dismissing the outlier;
- determine whether divergence reflects context, method sensitivity, source bias, or genuine complexity;
- consider whether the finding holds only under certain conditions;
- revise the claim to incorporate the conditions rather than forcing a single verdict;
- report the divergence and its interpretation honestly.

Smoothing divergence to claim convergence discards the most valuable signal triangulation produces. A conditional finding that accounts for divergence is stronger than a false universal.

### Weight Sources By Credibility, Not By Convenience

When sources conflict, resolution requires judging which is more credible for the specific point of conflict. Convenience or preference should not drive the weight.

Assess:

- the methodological strength of each source for the specific claim;
- the proximity of each source to the phenomenon;
- the independence and potential bias of each;
- the consistency of each with other independent evidence;
- the consequences of error in each direction.

A weaker source should not override a stronger one because it is more convenient, nor should a stronger source silence a weaker one without examining why they differ. Make the weighting explicit and defensible.

### Report The Triangulation Logic, Not Just The Conclusion

Readers must be able to evaluate the triangulation, not just trust its verdict. The logic is part of the finding.

Report:

- which sources or methods were combined and why;
- how independence was assessed;
- what converged and what diverged;
- how divergence was interpreted;
- how conflicts were weighted and resolved;
- what the triangulation can and cannot establish.

A conclusion that claims triangulation without showing the logic asks readers to take convergence on faith. Show the work.

## Common Traps

### Claiming Convergence From Non-Independent Sources

Sources that share origin, bias, or method produce consistent readings that do not corroborate. Apparent convergence masks shared error.

### Treating Divergence As Failure To Be Suppressed

Divergence often reveals the conditions under which a finding holds. Smoothing it away discards the most informative result.

### Accumulating Sources Without Testing Independence

More sources feel like stronger evidence, but if they are not independent they add comfort, not corroboration.

### Adding A Weak Source For The Appearance Of Triangulation

A source too weak to challenge the finding adds bulk without test. It cannot falsify, so it cannot corroborate.

### Designing The Comparison After Seeing Results

Post-hoc framing of convergence and divergence can always be bent toward the preferred verdict. Prior design protects inference.

### Weighting Sources By Preference Rather Than Credibility

Resolving conflicts in favor of the convenient or expected source is not triangulation. It is confirmation with extra steps.

### Reporting Only The Convergent Conclusion

Omitting the logic, the divergence, and the weighting asks readers to trust convergence they cannot evaluate.

## Self-Check

- Is the type of triangulation (method, source, investigator, theory) matched to the threat the design is meant to address?
- Are the vantage points genuinely independent, with shared biases and dependencies identified rather than ignored?
- Is each source both relevant to the question and different enough to constitute a real test?
- Is the comparison of convergence and divergence designed before the sources are interpreted together?
- Is divergence treated as evidence to investigate rather than noise to suppress?
- Are conflicting sources weighted by credibility for the specific point of conflict, with reasoning made explicit?
- Does the report show the triangulation logic, including sources, independence, convergence, divergence, and weighting?
- Where the independence of sources or the appropriateness of the design is unclear, has a methodologist been consulted rather than claiming convergence the design cannot establish?
