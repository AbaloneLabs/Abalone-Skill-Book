# Interview Methods And Protocol Design

Interviews are the most common qualitative method and also the most frequently misused. Agents often assume that asking people questions and recording their answers is straightforward, then discover too late that the answers reflect the questions asked rather than the participants' actual experience. An interview is a co-constructed interaction: what participants reveal depends on the format, the wording, the order, the rapport, the researcher's demeanor, and the social dynamics of the encounter. The judgment problem is to match the interview format to the research question, to write questions that elicit participants' meaning rather than the researcher's assumptions, and to design the interaction so that depth is possible without leading. A protocol full of leading, double-barreled, or abstract questions produces data that confirm the researcher's frame and miss what participants actually think.

Use this skill when designing an interview study or protocol. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating interviews as simple Q&A, from writing questions that telegraph desired answers, and from choosing a format that cannot deliver the depth or comparability the question requires. The agent has freedom in format and focus, but the alignment of format, question, and analysis must be deliberate.

## Core Rules

### Match The Interview Format To The Research Question

Different interview formats produce different kinds of data and support different claims. The format is a design choice tied to the question.

Match by:

- structured interviews for standardized, comparable data across many participants;
- semi-structured interviews for depth with a consistent core across participants;
- unstructured or ethnographic interviews for exploratory, narrative, emergent understanding;
- the relationship between format and the intended analysis.

A structured interview cannot capture the texture of lived experience; an unstructured interview cannot support systematic comparison. The format must fit what the study claims to find.

### Write Questions That Elicit, Not Lead

The single most common interview failure is questions that telegraph the answer the researcher wants or expects.

Write by:

- open questions that let participants define what matters;
- avoiding leading phrasing such as "How stressful was it?" when stress is the construct;
- avoiding double-barreled questions that ask two things at once;
- concrete, experience-near questions ("Tell me about the last time...") before abstract ones ("What do you think about...").

Participants will often agree with a researcher's framing out of politeness or deference. Questions must be designed so that a participant could give any answer, including one that contradicts the researcher's hypothesis.

### Sequence Questions To Build Trust And Depth

The order of questions shapes what participants are willing to share. Jumping to sensitive topics early shuts down the interview.

Sequence by:

- warm, low-stakes questions that establish rapport and context first;
- core questions in the middle once trust and comfort are established;
- sensitive or emotionally demanding questions later, when rapport is solid;
- a closing that lets participants add what the protocol missed.

A protocol is a guide, not a script in semi-structured work, but its sequencing should respect the relational logic of disclosure.

### Plan Probes And Follow-Ups Deliberately

Depth comes from probes, not from the main questions. A protocol without planned probing produces shallow, first-answer data.

Plan by:

- generic probes ("Can you tell me more about that?") to deepen without leading;
- specific probes tied to anticipated answers, used flexibly;
- silence and minimal encouragers as legitimate probing tools;
- distinguishing probes that elicit from probes that suggest.

The skill of probing is following the participant's lead rather than steering toward the researcher's interest. Probes that steer turn an interview into a leading interrogation.

### Decide Sample Size Through Purpose, Not Convenience

The number of interviews should be justified by the study's purpose and analytic approach, not by what was easy to arrange.

Decide by:

- the concept of information power or saturation relevant to the approach;
- the homogeneity or heterogeneity of the sample and the comparison sought;
- the depth and length of each interview;
- stopping rules tied to whether new information is still emerging.

There is no universal number. A study seeking rich description of a shared experience may need fewer interviews than one comparing across diverse subgroups. The rationale must be stated, not assumed.

### Sample For Information, Not Convenience

Interview sampling is purposive and theory-driven. Convenience sampling relabeled as purposive undermines the entire rationale.

Sample by:

- information-rich cases that illuminate the question;
- maximum variation, typical, critical, or criterion sampling matched to the goal;
- deliberate inclusion of disconfirming or edge cases;
- an explicit account of who is missing and why it matters.

Who is interviewed determines what can be claimed. A sample drawn only from the willing or reachable cannot support claims about those who are not.

### Address Power And Social Desirability In The Interaction

Interviews are asymmetric interactions. Participants shape answers to what they think the researcher wants or what is socially acceptable.

Address by:

- acknowledging the researcher's position and its likely effect on disclosure;
- reducing demand characteristics through neutral, non-evaluative phrasing;
- building rapport and confidentiality to lower social desirability pressure;
- recognizing that some topics will be underreported no matter the design.

Social desirability is not a flaw to eliminate entirely but a force to recognize and report as a limitation shaping the findings.

### Plan Recording, Transcription, And Analysis Together

The interview does not end when the participant leaves. How it is recorded and transcribed shapes what can be analyzed.

Plan by:

- audio or video recording with explicit consent and a fallback for refusal;
- transcription conventions matched to the analysis (verbatim, Jeffersonian, intelligent verbatim);
- who transcribes and how quality is checked;
- how the transcript connects to the planned coding and analysis.

Decisions made at the recording and transcription stage constrain the analysis later. Plan them as part of the design, not as afterthoughts.

## Common Traps

### Treating Interviews As Simple Q&A

Assuming that asking and recording is enough ignores how the interaction shapes the data, producing thin or biased accounts.

### Leading Or Double-Barreled Questions

Questions that telegraph desired answers or ask two things at once generate agreement rather than genuine participant meaning.

### Format Mismatched To The Question

Using a structured format for an experience question, or unstructured for a comparison, produces data that cannot answer the question.

### No Planned Probing

A protocol with only main questions and no probing strategy yields first-answer, shallow data that miss depth and nuance.

### Convenience Sampling Relabeled As Purposive

Calling a convenient sample purposive without an information-rich rationale undermines credibility and the study's claims.

### Ignoring Power And Social Desirability

Failing to recognize the asymmetric interview dynamic produces overreports of desirable behavior and underreports of the sensitive.

### Abstract Questions Before Concrete Ones

Leading with abstract opinion questions before concrete experience produces rehearsed, ungrounded answers.

## Self-Check

- Is the interview format (structured, semi-structured, unstructured) matched to the research question and intended analysis?
- Are questions written to elicit rather than lead, avoiding leading, double-barreled, and abstract-first phrasing?
- Is the question sequence designed to build trust before sensitive topics?
- Is there a deliberate probing strategy that follows the participant rather than steering?
- Is the sample size justified by purpose, saturation, or information power rather than convenience?
- Is sampling purposive and information-driven, with disconfirming cases included?
- Are power asymmetry and social desirability acknowledged and mitigated in the design?
- Are recording, transcription conventions, and analysis planned together from the start?
- Is the protocol treated as a guide that allows flexibility rather than a rigid script?
- Are the researcher's position and its likely effect on disclosure acknowledged?
