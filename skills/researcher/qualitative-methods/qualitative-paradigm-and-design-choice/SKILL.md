# Qualitative Paradigm And Design Choice

Qualitative research is not "non-quantitative research" or research with smaller samples. It is a family of approaches each grounded in assumptions about the nature of reality (ontology), how it can be known (epistemology), and what counts as rigorous knowledge (methodology). Agents often select a qualitative method because the data are text or the sample is small, without considering whether the method's assumptions fit the question. An ethnography, a grounded theory, a phenomenology, and a case study differ not only in technique but in what they treat as a finding and what kind of claim they license. The judgment problem is to align paradigm, question, and method so that the design can actually answer the question asked. Misalignment produces work that is technically competent within a method but answers a different question than intended.

Use this skill when choosing among qualitative approaches, defining a qualitative question, or justifying a design. The goal is to prevent the agent from picking a method by convenience and then forcing the data and claims into a shape the method cannot support.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Research Question, Not The Method

The question determines the method, not the reverse. Different qualitative questions imply different designs.

Match by:

- "what is it like to experience X?" implies phenomenology;
- "how is meaning negotiated in this setting?" implies ethnography;
- "what process explains this phenomenon?" implies grounded theory;
- "how does this case illuminate the phenomenon?" implies case study.

Choosing a method first and bending the question to fit produces a design that cannot deliver the intended insight.

### Make The Paradigm Explicit And Consistent

Paradigm assumptions shape every later decision. Hiding them produces internal contradictions.

Make explicit:

- ontological stance: is reality singular or multiple, external or constructed?
- epistemological stance: what is the relationship between knower and known?
- methodological implications: what counts as evidence and rigor here?
- consistency between paradigm, method, and claims.

A constructivist paradigm licenses claims about how participants construct meaning; a positivist-influenced design licenses different claims. Mixing them without awareness produces incoherent findings.

### Define The Phenomenon And Its Boundaries

A qualitative study must bound what it is studying. Unbounded phenomena produce sprawling, unfocused data.

Bound by:

- the phenomenon itself: what exactly is under study?
- the context or setting in which it occurs;
- the participants whose perspectives are sought;
- the time frame and level of analysis.

A study of "leadership" with no boundaries will generate data about everything and findings about nothing in particular.

### Justify Why Qualitative Methods Fit The Question

Qualitative methods suit questions about meaning, process, context, and phenomena not yet well understood. They are weak for questions about prevalence or causal magnitude.

Justify by:

- the question concerns meaning, experience, or process, not frequency;
- the phenomenon is underexplored and needs conceptual development;
- context is central to understanding, not a nuisance to control;
- the goal is depth and theory development, not generalization to populations.

Using qualitative methods to answer "how many" or "which is larger" wastes the approach and answers the wrong question.

### Align Sampling With The Logic Of The Design

Qualitative sampling is purposive and theory-driven, not representative. But purposive is not arbitrary.

Align by:

- purposive sampling for information-rich cases;
- theoretical sampling in grounded theory to develop categories;
- criterion sampling to define who belongs in the study;
- maximum variation, typical, or critical cases depending on the goal.

Convenience sampling relabeled as purposive undermines the design's rationale and the credibility of findings.

### Choose A Single Coherent Approach, Or Justify A Hybrid

Most qualitative studies follow one approach. Hybrids are possible but require explicit handling.

Choose by:

- the dominant approach that shapes data collection and analysis;
- whether borrowed techniques fit the dominant paradigm;
- the risk of importing assumptions from a conflicting approach;
- clear statement of what is borrowed and why.

A "grounded theory ethnography" that borrows fieldwork from ethnography and coding from grounded theory must reconcile their different assumptions about theory and emic meaning.

### Match The Level And Type Of Claim To The Design

Claims must match what the design can support. Overclaiming is the most common qualitative error.

Match by:

- transferability claims bounded to similar contexts, not statistical generalization;
- theoretical generalization from theory-building designs;
- analytical generalization from case studies to theory, not populations;
- acknowledgment of the partial, situated nature of findings.

A 15-person interview study cannot claim that "most people" do anything; it can claim insight into how meaning is constructed among these participants.

### Plan For Rigor Appropriate To The Paradigm

Qualitative rigor differs from quantitative rigor. Applying checklists from one to the other produces confusion.

Plan by:

- credibility: prolonged engagement, triangulation, member checking where appropriate;
- transferability: thick description of context;
- dependability: audit trail of decisions;
- confirmability: grounding findings in data.

Rigor in qualitative work is about the trustworthiness of the interpretive process, not the size of the sample.

## Common Traps

### Method Chosen By Convenience

Selecting interviews because they are easy, then calling the study ethnography or grounded theory, forces data and claims into a shape the method cannot support.

### Hidden Paradigm Assumptions

Failing to state ontological and epistemological assumptions lets contradictions pass unnoticed and weakens the coherence of findings.

### Unbounded Phenomenon

A study of a broad concept without boundaries generates unfocused data and vague findings.

### Qualitative Methods For Prevalence Questions

Using interviews to answer "how common is X" misuses the approach and produces unreliable prevalence estimates.

### Convenience Sampling Relabeled As Purposive

Calling a convenient sample purposive without an information-rich rationale undermines credibility.

### Unjustified Method Hybrids

Mixing techniques from different approaches without reconciling their assumptions produces incoherent designs.

### Overclaiming Generalizability

Extending findings to populations or universal claims beyond what the design supports overstates the contribution.

## Self-Check

- Is the qualitative method chosen to fit the research question rather than convenience?
- Are ontological and epistemological paradigm assumptions stated and consistent across design decisions?
- Is the phenomenon bounded by context, participants, time, and level of analysis?
- Is there a clear justification for why qualitative methods fit this question?
- Does sampling follow the logic of the design (purposive, theoretical, criterion) rather than convenience?
- If the approach is hybrid, are borrowed techniques reconciled with the dominant paradigm?
- Are claims matched to what the design can support, avoiding statistical generalization from small samples?
- Is a paradigm-appropriate rigor plan in place (credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability)?
- Are transferability claims bounded to similar contexts with thick description?
- Is the relationship between the researcher and participants acknowledged as part of the design?
