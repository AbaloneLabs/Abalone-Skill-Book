# Narrative And Discourse Analysis

Narrative analysis and discourse analysis study meaning that lives in language: the stories people tell and the ways language constructs, positions, and exercises power. These approaches are powerful precisely because they reach what thematic content alone cannot, but that same reach makes them vulnerable to a distinctive failure: the analyst reads into the data more than the data can bear, and presents an interpretation as if it were simply present in the text. Unlike thematic coding, where evidence can be pointed to, narrative and discourse findings rest on theoretical frameworks whose assumptions must be declared and whose claims must be disciplined by close attention to the actual language. The judgment problem is to choose the right approach for the question, to gather data of the right kind, to ground interpretation in a named framework rather than in the analyst's impression, and to hold the interpretation answerable to the data.

Use this skill when conducting narrative or discourse analysis, choosing among these approaches, deciding what data they require, or setting their rigor standards. The goal is to keep the agent from treating these approaches as impressionistic commentary and from producing interpretations that float free of both the data and a defensible framework. The agent has latitude in framework and focus, but the approach, its assumptions, and its standards must be explicit, and the interpretation must be disciplined.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish The Approaches By What They Treat As The Object Of Study

Narrative analysis, discourse analysis, and conversation analysis are often named interchangeably, but they study different objects and answer different questions. Choosing among them is the first and most consequential decision.

Distinguish:

- narrative analysis studies the stories people tell, their structure, sequence, and meaning-making, asking how experience is organized into narrative;
- discourse analysis studies language-in-use and how it constructs social reality, identities, relations, and power, often beyond single stories;
- conversation analysis studies the sequential, turn-by-turn organization of interaction, with strict attention to how actions are produced and recognized in talk;
- critical discourse analysis foregrounds power, ideology, and inequality in how language reproduces or contests social structures.

Each approach licenses different claims and requires different data. Calling work "discourse analysis" while actually doing thematic coding of content abandons the specific analytic purchase these approaches offer.

### Match The Data To The Approach

These approaches make strong demands on their data, and the wrong data make the intended analysis impossible. The decision about what to collect follows from the object of study.

Match data to approach:

- narrative analysis needs stories, elicited through interviews or life-history prompts, or found in documents, diaries, or media;
- discourse analysis can use naturally occurring text or talk, policy documents, media, institutional records, or online interaction;
- conversation analysis requires naturally occurring interaction, audio or video recordings, and is undermined by elicited or recalled talk;
- critical discourse analysis often combines texts with attention to their social and institutional context.

Elicited data, such as interview stories, are appropriate for narrative analysis but problematic for conversation analysis, which depends on interaction that would have occurred without the researcher. State how the data were generated and why they suit the approach.

### Ground Interpretation In A Named Theoretical Framework

The distinctive rigor of these approaches comes from disciplined interpretation under a theoretical framework, not from the analyst's sensitivity alone. An interpretation without a declared framework is an opinion dressed as analysis.

Declare and use:

- the theoretical tradition informing the analysis, such as a narrative theorist's model of story structure or a discourse theorist's account of power;
- the specific analytic concepts being applied, such as positioning, framing, intertextuality, or turn design;
- how the framework guides what counts as evidence and what counts as a finding;
- where the analyst's reading goes beyond what is explicit and on what grounds.

A framework does not guarantee correctness, but it makes the interpretation inspectable and debatable, which is what rigor requires here. Switching frameworks mid-analysis, or invoking them only decoratively, undermines the claim.

### Preserve The Detail Of Language And Sequence

These approaches lose their object of study when language is reduced to summary or topic. The findings depend on close attention to how things are said, ordered, and structured.

Preserve by:

- working from verbatim transcripts that retain hesitations, repairs, overlap, and emphasis where relevant;
- attending to word choice, grammatical form, sequencing, and category labels, not only to content;
- for narrative, preserving the structure of the story, its plot, characters, and temporal organization;
- for conversation, preserving the turn-by-turn sequential context in which an utterance appears;
- avoiding the reduction of rich talk to paraphrased themes before the analysis is done.

Summarizing language into themes too early collapses exactly the phenomena these approaches exist to study.

### Make The Interpretation Answerable To The Data

The central risk in narrative and discourse work is over-reading: claiming meanings, motivations, or power effects that the data do not support. Rigor demands that every interpretive claim be tied to evidence in the language.

Discipline interpretation by:

- showing the specific passages and features on which each claim rests;
- distinguishing what the text does from what the analyst infers about intent or effect;
- considering alternative readings and explaining why the chosen reading is preferred;
- searching for disconfirming instances, passages that complicate or contradict the interpretation;
- acknowledging the limits of what can be claimed from the available data.

An interpretation that cannot be challenged by the data is not analysis; it is projection. The strongest findings are those that survive an active search for counter-evidence.

### Address Positionality And The Researcher's Role

In these approaches the researcher is the interpretive instrument, and their position shapes what they notice and claim. Ignoring this does not produce neutrality; it produces hidden influence.

Address by:

- reflecting on how the analyst's standpoint, discipline, and assumptions shape the reading;
- considering how the researcher's presence shaped the data, especially in elicited narratives;
- distinguishing participant meaning from analyst interpretation;
- being transparent about the inferential leap from text to claim.

Positionality is not a confessional aside but a condition on the interpretation's credibility, and it should be woven into how findings are presented and bounded.

### Set Rigor Standards Appropriate To The Approach

Narrative and discourse analysis are not judged by the criteria of positivist quantitative work, but they are not thereby exempt from rigor. They have their own standards, and applying the wrong ones, or none, weakens the work.

Apply standards such as:

- transparency of the analytic process, so a reader can follow how claims were reached;
- thoroughness of engagement with the data, not selective quotation;
- coherence between the framework, the data, and the claims;
- reflexivity about the researcher's role and assumptions;
- attention to deviant cases and competing readings;
- appropriate generality, claims about these texts and contexts, not universal pronouncements.

Rigor here is about the discipline of interpretation and the traceability of claims, not about sample size or replicability in the quantitative sense.

## Common Traps

### Treating These Approaches As Impressionistic Commentary

Without a named framework and disciplined method, narrative and discourse work becomes the analyst's reactions to the text, presented as findings.

### Using The Wrong Data For The Approach

Elicited interview talk cannot support conversation analysis of naturally occurring interaction; documents may not support claims about interactional sequence. The data must suit the object of study.

### Over-Reading And Projecting Meaning

Claiming motivations, ideologies, or power effects that the data do not evidence turns interpretation into projection and cannot be challenged by the data.

### Reducing Language To Themes Too Early

Summarizing talk and text into topics before the analysis discards the linguistic and sequential detail these approaches exist to examine.

### Invoking Frameworks Decoratively

Naming a theorist without using the framework's concepts to guide the analysis lends false authority and leaves the interpretation undisciplined.

### Ignoring Positionality

Treating the analyst as neutral hides the interpretive work and overstates the objectivity of claims that depend on the researcher's reading.

### Selective Quotation

Choosing vivid passages that support the reading while ignoring passages that complicate it produces a tidy but untested interpretation.

### Overgeneralizing From Specific Texts

Narrative and discourse findings describe particular texts and contexts; extending them to populations or universal claims misuses the approach.

## Self-Check

- Is the approach, narrative, discourse, conversation, or critical discourse analysis, chosen to match the question and clearly distinguished from the others?
- Is the data suited to the approach, with naturally occurring versus elicited data matched to what the analysis requires?
- Is the interpretation grounded in a named theoretical framework whose concepts actually guide the analysis?
- Are the details of language, sequence, and structure preserved, rather than reduced to paraphrased themes too early?
- Is every interpretive claim tied to specific evidence in the data, with alternative readings considered?
- Have disconfirming instances and competing readings been actively sought and addressed?
- Is the researcher's positionality and its influence on the interpretation acknowledged rather than hidden?
- Are rigor standards appropriate to the approach applied, including transparency, thoroughness, coherence, and reflexivity?
- Are claims bounded to the texts and contexts studied, avoiding overgeneralization?
- Is the distinction between what the text does and what the analyst infers about intent or effect made clear throughout?
