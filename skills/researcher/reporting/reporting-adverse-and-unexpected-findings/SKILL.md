# Reporting Adverse And Unexpected Findings

Not every finding is the one the study set out to find. Some are adverse, such as harms to participants, side effects, or unintended consequences. Some are unexpected, such as anomalies that contradict the hypothesis, failed replications, or signals that something is wrong with the data or the design. How these findings are handled determines whether the study remains trustworthy. When adverse or unexpected findings are buried, delayed, or rationalized away, three harms follow. Participants and the public are exposed to preventable risks. The literature keeps a false consensus alive by suppressing disconfirming evidence. And the researchers lose the chance to correct their own work before it harms others.

The agent should use this skill when an adverse event appears, when results contradict the hypothesis, when a replication fails, when data look wrong, or when a sponsor or collaborator pressures the team to soften or omit a finding. The goal is to keep the agent from treating inconvenient findings as public-relations problems instead of as evidence that must be reported.

## Core Rules

### Treat Participant Safety As The First Priority

When a finding involves harm to participants, safety outranks publication, reputation, and sponsor interests. Adverse events, serious adverse events, and unexpected harms must be reported to the relevant oversight bodies within required timeframes.

For clinical and intervention research, know the reporting rules before they are needed.

- The ethics board or institutional review board.
- The data safety monitoring board if one exists.
- The sponsor and regulatory authority.
- Participants currently enrolled, if the finding affects their willingness or safety.

Do not wait for analysis to be complete if the signal is urgent. A preliminary report of harm can prevent further harm while the full picture is assembled.

### Distinguish Adverse Findings From Expected Noise

Not every unexpected number is an adverse finding. Distinguish among the following.

- True adverse events, harms caused or worsened by the study.
- Expected adverse events that were anticipated in the protocol.
- Random variation and noise that requires no action.
- Data errors and anomalies that indicate a problem with measurement or processing.
- Genuine unexpected findings that change the interpretation of the study.

Misclassifying noise as an adverse event causes unnecessary alarm and reporting burden. Misclassifying a real harm as noise endangers participants. Use predefined criteria where they exist, and consult the safety monitoring process when unsure.

### Report Unexpected Findings Even When Inconvenient

A finding that contradicts the hypothesis, fails to replicate prior work, or undermines the sponsor's interest is still a finding. Report it. The scientific record depends on disconfirming evidence as much as confirming evidence.

Suppression can take subtle forms.

- Omitting the outcome from the paper.
- Reclassifying a planned primary outcome as secondary after seeing the result.
- Emphasizing a favorable secondary result over an unfavorable primary one.
- Delaying publication indefinitely.
- Describing a clear null result as inconclusive.

Each of these distorts the record. Report what was planned, what was found, and the discrepancy if any.

### Follow Preregistered Outcomes And Analysis

Preregistration is the strongest defense against quietly dropping adverse or unexpected results. If outcomes and analyses were preregistered, report them as planned regardless of direction. If a deviation is needed, disclose it and explain why.

When a primary outcome is unfavorable and a secondary one is favorable, report both and resist the temptation to promote the secondary outcome to headline status. Outcome switching is a recognizable form of misreporting and undermines credibility.

### Report Failed Replications Honestly

A failure to replicate a prior finding is informative, not embarrassing. Report the replication attempt with full detail of the original method, the deviations, the sample, and the result. A null replication does not prove the original was wrong, because power, context, and implementation matter, but it does contribute evidence that readers need.

Avoid framing a failed replication as confirmation that the original was fraudulent, and avoid framing it as irrelevant. Report what the replication can and cannot say.

### Escalate Data Anomalies Before Explaining Them Away

When data look wrong, such as impossible values, suspicious patterns, or results too clean to be real, investigate before interpreting. Anomalies can indicate data entry errors, software bugs, fabrication, or a genuine unexpected phenomenon.

Steps include the following.

- Verify the raw data against the source.
- Check the processing pipeline for errors.
- Look for patterns across sites, times, or collectors.
- Consult a statistician or methodologist.
- If misconduct is suspected, follow the institutional integrity process.

Do not silently correct or drop anomalous data. Document the investigation, the decision, and its rationale.

### Resist Pressure To Soften Or Omit Findings

Pressure to soften adverse findings can come from sponsors, senior collaborators, or the researchers' own hopes. Recognize the pressure and respond with the evidence.

- Keep records of the finding and the discussions about it.
- Refer to the protocol, preregistration, and analysis plan.
- Involve the ethics board or integrity officer if pressure persists.
- Remember that post-publication discovery of suppression is far worse than pre-publication conflict.

A finding that is uncomfortable now is less damaging than a finding that is exposed later as having been hidden.

### Communicate Uncertainty About Unexpected Findings

Unexpected findings, especially from subgroup or post hoc analyses, are often underpowered and prone to false positives. Report them with appropriate caution.

- Label exploratory findings as exploratory.
- Report effect sizes and confidence intervals.
- State that the finding requires confirmation.
- Avoid implying causal conclusions from unexpected associations.

Honest reporting of an unexpected finding includes honest reporting of its uncertainty.

### Consider Broader Reporting Obligations

Some adverse or unexpected findings carry obligations beyond the paper. These can include the following.

- Updating informed consent for ongoing or future participants.
- Notifying regulatory authorities.
- Informing clinical practice or policy if the finding affects safety.
- Communicating to participants and communities, especially in studies where they have a stake in the results.

Consider who needs to know and when, not only what goes into the manuscript.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Adverse Event Reporting To Finish Analysis

Waiting for a clean dataset before reporting a harm can expose participants to ongoing risk. Report within required timeframes first, analyze later.

### Reclassifying Unfavorable Primary Outcomes As Secondary

Switching outcomes after seeing results is a recognizable form of misreporting. Report planned outcomes as planned.

### Framing A Clear Null As Inconclusive

A well-powered null result is evidence of no effect, not merely absence of evidence. Mislabeling it as inconclusive keeps false hopes alive.

### Treating A Failed Replication As A Failure Of The Replicator

Replication attempts are contributions, not accusations. Report them neutrally and let the evidence speak.

### Silently Dropping Anomalous Data

Removing outliers or suspicious values without documentation hides potential errors or misconduct. Investigate and document.

### Yielding To Sponsor Pressure To Soften Harms

Sponsor interests do not override participant safety or scientific accuracy. Document pressure and escalate if needed.

### Overinterpreting An Unexpected Subgroup Finding

A striking subgroup result from many tested comparisons is likely a false positive. Report it as exploratory and requiring confirmation.

### Assuming Small Studies Cannot Cause Harm

Even small or early-phase studies can produce harms or signals that matter. Apply reporting standards regardless of study size.

## Self-Check

- If the finding involves participant harm, has it been reported to all required oversight bodies within the required timeframe?
- Has the finding been correctly classified as adverse, expected, noise, data error, or genuine unexpected result?
- Are unfavorable primary outcomes reported as planned, without reclassification or outcome switching?
- Are failed replications reported with full method detail and neutral framing?
- Have data anomalies been investigated and documented rather than silently corrected?
- Has pressure to soften or omit findings been recognized, documented, and resisted or escalated?
- Are unexpected and exploratory findings labeled as such with appropriate uncertainty?
- Have broader obligations, such as updating consent or notifying regulators, been considered and acted on?
- Are records of the finding and related discussions preserved for future audit?
- For findings involving participant safety, possible misconduct, or regulatory obligations, has the relevant ethics board, integrity officer, or qualified advisor been consulted before proceeding?
