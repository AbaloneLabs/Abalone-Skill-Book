# Transparency And Disclosure

Transparency is the difference between a study that can be checked and a study that must be taken on faith. A reader who cannot see the methods, data, code, conflicts, funding, and any deviations from the plan cannot evaluate whether to trust the conclusions. When transparency is treated as an afterthought, three harms follow. Errors and biases go undetected because no one can re-examine the work. Biased or sponsored research looks identical to independent research. And the cumulative literature becomes a stack of uncheckable claims rather than a self-correcting body of knowledge.

The agent should use this skill when finalizing a manuscript, writing a methods section, preparing a data or code availability statement, completing a conflict of interest disclosure, or deciding whether and how to report a deviation from a preregistered protocol. The goal is to keep the agent from treating disclosure as a compliance formality and instead make the study auditable by readers.

## Core Rules

### Disclose Enough Method To Allow Reproduction

A methods section should let a competent peer repeat the study. Vague descriptions of what was done are a transparency failure even when they are technically published. For each major method element, include the following.

- Population, sample, and recruitment details.
- Inclusion and exclusion criteria as actually applied.
- Measures and instruments, with versions and validation evidence.
- Procedures and timing.
- Analysis plan, including model choices and software.
- Any changes made after data collection began.

If space limits force abbreviation in the main text, provide full detail in supplements. The standard is whether another researcher could reproduce the result, not whether the section reads smoothly.

### Disclose Data And Code Availability Honestly

A data or code availability statement should describe exactly where the materials are and under what conditions. Avoid statements that promise availability on reasonable request when there is no real intention or mechanism to share.

Options include the following.

- Public repository with a permanent identifier, such as a DOI.
- Restricted access repository with described application process.
- Available in supplementary files.
- Publicly unavailable, with the specific legal, ethical, or contractual reason.

If data cannot be shared because of consent or confidentiality, say so explicitly and explain what can be shared, such as a synthetic or aggregated version. Silence or vague promises are worse than a clear explanation of constraints.

### Disclose Conflicts Of Interest For Every Author

Conflicts of interest are not inherently wrong, but hiding them is. Require each author to declare financial and non-financial relationships relevant to the work.

Cover the following categories.

- Employment and consulting.
- Equity and ownership.
- Patents and royalties.
- Advisory and board roles.
- Speaking and honoraria.
- Grants and contracts from interested parties.
- Personal, professional, or political relationships that could bias interpretation.

Disclose even when uncertain whether a relationship is relevant. Readers can weigh disclosed conflicts; they cannot weigh hidden ones. Update declarations at submission, revision, and publication, because relationships change.

### Disclose Funding Sources And Their Roles

Name every funder and describe the funder's role, if any, in the design, conduct, analysis, reporting, or publication decision. If the funder had no role, state that. If the funder reviewed or approved the manuscript, state that.

Industry funding does not invalidate a study, but undisclosed industry control over analysis or publication does undermine trust. Disclose contractual constraints on publication rights, because these affect whether negative findings could have been suppressed.

### Disclose Deviations From The Preregistered Plan

If the study was preregistered, report every deviation from the registered plan and the reason. Deviations are common and often justified, but undisclosed deviations turn a preregistered study into a post hoc one in disguise.

Common deviations to disclose include the following.

- Changes to inclusion criteria after seeing data.
- Changes to the primary outcome or its measurement.
- Adding, dropping, or swapping covariates.
- Different analysis model than registered.
- Different sample size or stopping rule.
- New subgroup analyses not planned.

For each deviation, state what changed, when, and why. Readers can then judge whether the change was justified or whether it constitutes result-driven modification.

### Disclose Analytic Choices That Affect Results

Many analyses involve choices that change the answer, such as how to handle outliers, how to treat missing data, which covariates to include, and which transformations to apply. Disclose the primary choices and, where feasible, show that results are robust to reasonable alternatives.

Sensitivity analyses demonstrate that a conclusion does not depend on an arbitrary choice. A single analysis with no robustness check leaves readers unable to judge whether the finding is fragile.

### Disclose Prior And Overlapping Publications

Disclose whether the data, methods, or findings have been published elsewhere, including conference proceedings, preprints, and overlapping papers. Duplicate publication distorts the literature and can double-count evidence in reviews.

Disclose preprints, because most journals allow them and readers benefit from knowing the work's history. Failure to disclose overlapping publication can trigger retraction for self-plagiarism or dual submission.

### Make The Evidence Chain Traceable

Transparency is not a single statement; it is a chain. Each claim should trace to a result, each result to an analysis, each analysis to code and data, and each method choice to a documented rationale. Where any link is missing, the chain is broken and the claim becomes harder to trust.

Aim for a reader to be able to follow any conclusion back to its raw evidence without guessing.

## Common Traps

### Promising Data Availability Without A Mechanism

The phrase available on reasonable request is often a hollow promise. If there is no data sharing agreement, repository, or process, the promise is misleading. Use a real mechanism or state the constraint honestly.

### Disclosing Only Financial Conflicts

Non-financial conflicts, such as strong personal, ideological, or career commitments to a result, can bias interpretation as much as money. Intellectual conflicts deserve disclosure too, especially in contested fields.

### Treating Methods Brevity As Elegance

A short methods section may read well but fail to enable reproduction. Brevity at the cost of traceability is a transparency failure.

### Hiding Deviations To Preserve A Clean Narrative

Undisclosed deviations from a preregistration are a serious integrity problem. A clean story built on hidden changes is worse than an honest story with explained deviations.

### Disclosing Funding But Not The Funder's Role

Naming a funder without describing their involvement leaves readers unable to judge independence. The role matters as much as the name.

### Reporting A Single Analysis As If It Were Inevitable

When many defensible analyses exist, reporting only one without justification hides the fragility of the result. Show sensitivity to alternatives.

### Forgetting To Update Disclosures At Revision

Conflicts and funding can change between submission and revision. Stale disclosures are inaccurate disclosures. Refresh them at each stage.

### Assuming Supplements Do Not Need To Be Transparent

Supplementary material is part of the published record. Vague, incomplete, or contradictory supplements undermine the main paper's transparency.

## Self-Check

- Does the methods section provide enough detail for a competent peer to reproduce the study?
- Does the data and code availability statement describe a real mechanism or a clear constraint, not a hollow promise?
- Has every author declared financial and non-financial conflicts, updated at each stage?
- Are all funding sources named, with the funder's role in the work explicitly described?
- Are all deviations from any preregistered plan disclosed with timing and reasons?
- Are key analytic choices disclosed and supported by sensitivity or robustness checks?
- Are prior, overlapping, and preprint publications disclosed?
- Can a reader trace any conclusion back through analysis, code, and data without gaps?
- Are supplements as transparent and complete as the main text?
- For studies with industry sponsorship, contested findings, or policy implications, has a qualified advisor or research integrity officer reviewed the disclosures before submission?
