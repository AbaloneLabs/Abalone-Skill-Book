# Research Writing And Argument Construction

A research paper is an argument, not a container for results. The same data, arranged differently, can make a defensible claim or a misleading one. Researchers often treat writing as the cosmetic stage that follows the real work of analysis, and in doing so they produce papers whose sections do not connect, whose discussion outruns its findings, whose limitations are formulaic confessions, and whose abstract promises more than the methods can deliver. The judgment problem is to construct a written argument in which every section serves the claim, the evidence supports each step, and the conclusion is calibrated to what was actually shown.

Use this skill when drafting or revising a research paper, report, thesis, or brief; when deciding how to structure the argument; when writing limitations, discussion, or abstract; when appraising a manuscript for coherence; or when translating a set of findings into a written claim. The goal is to keep the agent from treating structure as a template to fill and from letting the discussion escape the evidence. The agent has freedom to choose structure and emphasis, but must ensure the written argument is internally coherent and honestly bounded.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Paper As A Single Argument, Not A Sequence Of Sections

A research paper works when its parts form a chain: the question motivates the design, the design produces the findings, the findings support the interpretation, and the interpretation is bounded by the limitations. When sections are written in isolation, the chain breaks. The introduction promises one thing, the methods test another, the results report a third, and the discussion claims a fourth.

To keep the argument whole:

- state the claim the paper will defend, and check that every section serves it;
- ensure the research question in the introduction is the one the results answer;
- ensure the methods can actually produce the evidence the discussion relies on;
- ensure the abstract states only what the paper delivers;
- ensure the conclusion follows from the findings, not from aspiration.

Read the paper as a reader would, start to finish, and ask at each transition whether the next section follows from the one before it.

### Use IMRaD As A Default, But Justify Deviations

The Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion structure is dominant because it maps the logic of empirical inquiry. It is a strong default, but it is not universal, and rigid adherence can obscure arguments that follow a different logic.

Consider deviation when:

- the argument is theoretical and follows a claim-counterclaim rather than empirical structure;
- the work is qualitative and integrates findings and interpretation throughout, as in some ethnographic or narrative formats;
- the study is multi-study and benefits from per-study mini-IMRaD units;
- the audience or venue expects a different convention, such as a policy brief or technical report.

When deviating, do so for a reason connected to the argument, and make the structure serve comprehension. Do not abandon convention merely to seem distinctive.

### Build The Introduction As A Funnel To A Specific Question

The introduction should move from context to gap to question to contribution. Its job is not to summarize everything known, but to establish why this question, why this design, and what the reader will gain.

Construct the funnel:

- establish the problem and why it matters;
- concisely review what is known, focused on what bears on the question;
- identify the specific gap, conflict, or limitation the study addresses;
- state the research question or hypothesis precisely;
- preview the approach and the contribution, without overclaiming.

A common failure is an introduction that reviews a field broadly but never narrows to a researchable question, leaving the reader unsure what the paper is testing.

### Make Methods Reproducible Enough To Be Challenged

The methods section is where the argument's foundation is laid. A reader should be able to understand exactly what was done, judge whether it could answer the question, and identify threats to validity.

Ensure the methods specify:

- the design and its rationale;
- the population, sample, recruitment, and inclusion and exclusion criteria;
- the variables, measures, and their sources or validation evidence;
- the procedures and timing;
- the analysis plan, including primary and secondary outcomes, covariates, and handling of missing data;
- any preregistration and deviations from it;
- software, versions, and code availability.

Vague methods, such as standard statistical analyses were used, prevent readers from evaluating or replicating the work and weaken the credibility of every later claim.

### Report Results As Evidence, Not Interpretation

The results section should present what was found, in an order that follows the research question, with enough detail that the reader can evaluate the evidence independently of the author's interpretation.

Keep results clean by:

- reporting effect sizes and uncertainty, not only significance;
- reporting all planned analyses, including null and negative results;
- labeling exploratory or post hoc analyses as such;
- separating description of findings from claims about their meaning;
- using structure, such as one paragraph per question or hypothesis;
- avoiding selective emphasis that buries inconvenient results in supplements.

When interpretation leaks into results, the reader cannot tell what was found from what the author wants it to mean.

### Bridge Findings To Discussion Without Overleaping

The discussion interprets the findings, but the leap from result to meaning is where overclaiming happens. Each interpretive claim should be tied to a specific finding and bounded by the design's limits.

Bridge carefully:

- open by restating the main finding in plain terms;
- interpret each key result, connecting it to the question and prior evidence;
- offer mechanisms or explanations only where the evidence supports them;
- compare with prior work and explain agreement or disagreement;
- avoid speculating beyond what the design can support;
- keep causal language matched to the design's inferential strength.

A discussion that introduces new results, ignores unfavorable findings, or reaches for broad implications the data cannot support has broken the argument chain.

### Write Limitations That Are Substantive, Not Formulaic

Every study has limitations, and reporting them honestly is part of accurate argument. But formulaic limitations, generic lists that could apply to any study, do not help the reader calibrate trust.

Make limitations substantive:

- name the specific limitation, not the category;
- explain its likely direction and magnitude of effect on the conclusion;
- distinguish limitations that threaten the main claim from those that do not;
- note what was done to mitigate each, where possible;
- acknowledge limits that cannot be resolved and what they imply for the conclusion.

A limitation section that says the sample was small, without stating whether that threatens the main finding and in which direction, is a ritual, not analysis.

### Ensure The Abstract Is A Faithful Compressed Argument

The abstract is often the only part read, and it carries disproportionate weight. It should be a faithful compression of the whole argument, not a stronger version of it.

Check that the abstract:

- states the question, design, main findings, and conclusion;
- reports effect sizes and uncertainty, not only significance;
- matches the strength of the conclusion to the evidence;
- avoids claims absent from the body;
- includes the key limitation where space allows.

An abstract that overstates the findings misleads readers who never read further, and it distorts the literature when it is the basis for citation.

### Align Language With Evidence Across Sections

The words used in each section imply a level of confidence, and inconsistent language across sections signals a broken argument. The introduction should not promise more than the methods can deliver, the results should not imply more than the data show, and the discussion should not claim more than the results support.

Audit verbs and qualifiers:

- reserve causal verbs for designs that support them;
- use tentative language for exploratory or underpowered findings;
- keep the same calibration in abstract, results, and discussion;
- avoid hedging that hides real findings, and avoid confidence that overstates them.

### Revise For Coherence, Not Just Correctness

Revision is where the argument becomes coherent. Read the paper as a whole, checking that the claim is sustained from introduction to conclusion, that transitions carry the logic, and that no section is doing work the others do not support. Cut redundancy that dilutes the argument, and strengthen transitions where the reader could lose the thread.

## Common Traps

### Introduction That Never Reaches A Question

A broad literature review that never narrows to a specific, researchable question leaves the reader unsure what the paper tests and why it exists.

### Methods Too Vague To Evaluate Or Replicate

Generic descriptions of standard analyses prevent readers from judging validity or reproducing the work, undermining every later claim.

### Interpretation Leaking Into Results

Mixing the author's interpretation into the results section prevents the reader from seeing what was found independent of what it is claimed to mean.

### Discussion That Outruns The Findings

Speculating broadly, introducing policy implications, or making causal claims the design cannot support breaks the chain between evidence and conclusion.

### Formulaic Limitations

Generic limitation lists that could apply to any study do not help readers calibrate trust and signal that the author has not analyzed the study's actual weaknesses.

### Abstract Stronger Than The Body

An abstract that overstates findings misleads readers who read only it and distorts the literature when cited as the basis for claims.

### Selective Emphasis Burying Inconvenient Results

Foregrounding favorable findings while moving null or negative results to supplements distorts the message and the evidence base.

### Inconsistent Language Across Sections

Causal verbs in the discussion unsupported by the design, or hedging in results that hides real findings, signal an argument whose parts do not cohere.

## Self-Check

- Does every section serve a single sustained claim, with the introduction's question answered by the results and bounded by the discussion?
- If IMRaD is deviated from, is the deviation justified by the argument's logic rather than by preference or novelty?
- Does the introduction funnel from context to a specific researchable question and preview the contribution without overclaiming?
- Are methods specified in enough detail to be evaluated, challenged, and reproduced, including design, sample, measures, analysis plan, deviations, and code?
- Are results reported as evidence, with effect sizes and uncertainty, all planned analyses including nulls, and exploratory work labeled?
- Does the discussion bridge findings to interpretation without overleaping, keeping causal and implication language matched to the design?
- Are limitations substantive, naming specific threats, their likely direction and magnitude, and their effect on the main claim?
- Is the abstract a faithful compression of the whole argument, matching the conclusion's strength to the evidence?
- Is language calibrated consistently across abstract, results, and discussion, avoiding both overclaiming and unjustified hedging?
- When read as a whole, does the paper sustain a coherent argument from question to conclusion, with transitions that carry the logic?
