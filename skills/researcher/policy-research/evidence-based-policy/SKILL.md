# Evidence-Based Policy

Evidence-based policy is the ideal that public decisions should be informed by the best available research. In practice, the hard part is not the ideal but the translation. Research evidence is usually incomplete, conducted in contexts different from the policy setting, and contested. When the translation is done carelessly, three harms follow. A single study is overinterpreted as proof and drives a policy that fails elsewhere. Evidence is cherry-picked to support a predetermined position. Or uncertainty is used as an excuse to ignore strong evidence that does exist. Evidence-based policy is a discipline of calibrating confidence to what the evidence actually supports, in the context where the policy will apply.

The agent should use this skill when summarizing evidence for a policy decision, weighing study quality, generalizing findings to a new context, or advising decision-makers. The goal is to keep the agent from treating evidence-based as a slogan that justifies any favored conclusion, when it is properly a method of disciplined uncertainty and contextual judgment.

## Core Rules

### Base Confidence On The Body Of Evidence, Not A Single Study

Individual studies are estimates with error and context. Confidence comes from convergence across many studies.

- Prefer systematic reviews and meta-analyses over single studies.
- Look for consistency of direction and magnitude across designs and settings.
- Weight studies by quality and relevance, not by recency or prominence alone.
- Treat a single striking study as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.

A policy built on one study can fail when the finding does not replicate. A body of evidence, even if each piece is imperfect, gives a more reliable base.

### Assess The Quality And Relevance Of Each Source

Not all evidence is equal. Evaluate each source before weighing it.

- Study design, randomized, quasi-experimental, observational, expert opinion.
- Risk of bias, confounding, selection, measurement, publication bias.
- Sample size and power.
- Directness, does the study measure the relevant outcome in the relevant population.
- Replication, has the finding been reproduced independently.

A hierarchy of evidence can guide weighting, but relevance matters as much as design. A well-conducted observational study in the right context can outweigh a randomized trial in the wrong one.

### Account For Context And External Validity

Evidence generated in one setting may not transfer to another. Judge transferability deliberately.

- How similar is the study population to the policy population?
- How similar are the institutions, resources, and culture?
- Was the intervention implemented as it would be in the policy setting?
- What conditions were necessary for the effect, and do they hold here?

An effect that depends on specific implementation capacity, funding, or culture may not replicate where those differ. State the conditions for transfer and whether they are met.

### Separate What Is Known From What Is Assumed

Policy advice often blends evidence with assumptions. Make the boundary explicit.

- What does the evidence directly show?
- What must be assumed to extend it to this decision?
- How sensitive is the conclusion to those assumptions?
- What would change the conclusion if the assumptions were wrong?

Muddling evidence and assumption lets weak links hide behind strong ones. Separating them lets decision-makers see where the confidence actually lies.

### Address Uncertainty Honestly

Policy decisions are made under uncertainty. Honest advice conveys it.

- Report ranges and uncertainty intervals, not false precision.
- Distinguish strong, moderate, weak, and inconclusive evidence.
- State the main sources of uncertainty.
- Offer scenarios that reflect the range of plausible outcomes.

Decision-makers can handle uncertainty; what they cannot handle well is false certainty that collapses later. Calibrated honesty serves them better than reassurance.

### Consider Implementation And Feasibility

Evidence about whether a policy can work is different from evidence about whether it will work in practice.

- What does implementation require in capacity, resources, and time?
- Has the intervention been delivered at scale, or only in pilots?
- What are the political and administrative constraints?
- What supports, training, or infrastructure are needed?

A policy with strong efficacy evidence may fail if implementation is infeasible. Feasibility is part of the evidence base, not a separate concern.

### Weigh Benefits, Harms, Costs, And Distribution

Policy decisions involve tradeoffs across multiple values.

- Who benefits and who bears the cost or harm?
- Are benefits and harms distributed equitably?
- Do the benefits justify the costs, including opportunity costs?
- Are there unintended consequences, such as behavioral responses or displacement?

Evidence of an average benefit is not enough. The decision-relevant question includes distribution, cost, and unintended effects, which the evidence must address.

### Recognize The Limits Of Evidence And The Role Of Values

Evidence informs policy; it does not determine it. Values, priorities, and political feasibility enter every decision.

- Be clear where evidence ends and values begin.
- Do not let values masquerade as evidence, or evidence as values.
- Acknowledge legitimate disagreement about priorities.
- Present the tradeoffs rather than smuggling in a preferred outcome.

Evidence-based policy does not mean technocratic override of democratic deliberation. It means giving decision-makers the best evidence while leaving value judgments to them.

### Guard Against Cherry-Picking And Motivated Reasoning and update As Evidence Evolves

The temptation to select favorable evidence is constant, especially when the advisor has a stake.

- Actively seek disconfirming evidence.
- Report the full body of evidence, including null and negative findings.
- Disclose the advisor's own perspective and potential biases.
- Subject the synthesis to independent review where stakes are high.

Cherry-picking turns evidence-based into evidence-as-rhetoric. Discipline in seeking and reporting the whole base is what keeps the label honest.

Evidence accumulates over time, and yesterday's strong conclusion may weaken.

- Treat the synthesis as provisional.
- Build in mechanisms to update advice as new evidence appears.
- Record the evidence base at the time of the decision for later review.
- Acknowledge when new evidence should change the advice.

A policy advice that never updates is not evidence-based; it is frozen. Provisional, updatable advice is the honest form.

## Common Traps

### Overreading A Single Study

One study is a hypothesis, not a conclusion. Base confidence on the body of evidence.

### Ignoring Quality And Relevance

Not all evidence weighs equally. Assess design, bias, and directness.

### Assuming Transfer Without Justification

Effects depend on context. Judge whether conditions for transfer hold.

### Blending Evidence And Assumption

Muddled boundaries hide weak links. Separate what is known from what is assumed.

### False Certainty

Decision-makers need calibrated uncertainty, not reassurance. Convey ranges and confidence levels.

### Ignoring Implementation

Efficacy without feasibility fails in practice. Address capacity and constraints.

### Reporting Only Average Benefits and letting Values Pose As Evidence

Distribution, harms, and costs matter. Weigh the full tradeoff.

Values are legitimate but should not masquerade as evidence. Keep the boundary clear.

### Cherry-Picking Favorable Evidence and never Updating

Selective citation turns evidence into rhetoric. Report the full base.

Frozen advice is not evidence-based. Build in updates as evidence evolves.

## Self-Check

- Is confidence based on the body of evidence across studies, not a single study?
- Is each source assessed for design quality, risk of bias, directness, and replication?
- Is external validity and transfer to the policy context judged explicitly?
- Is the boundary between what evidence shows and what must be assumed made clear?
- Is uncertainty conveyed honestly with ranges and confidence levels?
- Are implementation, feasibility, and scale addressed alongside efficacy?
- Are benefits, harms, costs, and distributional effects weighed together?
- Is the role of values distinguished from the role of evidence?
- Has disconfirming evidence been actively sought and the full base reported?
- Is the advice provisional, with a mechanism to update as evidence evolves?; for advice driving significant policy or resource decisions, has an independent methodologist or domain expert reviewed the evidence synthesis before it is conveyed to decision-makers?
