# Policy Impact Assessment

A policy impact assessment estimates the consequences of a policy before or after it is implemented, across benefits, harms, costs, and affected groups. It is the bridge between evidence about effects and the decision about whether to adopt, continue, or modify a policy. When impact assessment is done poorly, three harms follow. Benefits are counted and costs ignored, or vice versa, producing a biased case. Indirect and unintended effects are missed because the assessment looked only at the direct target. And distributional consequences are buried under an aggregate that hides who wins and who loses. Impact assessment is a structured exercise in enumerating consequences honestly, and its value depends entirely on the completeness and honesty of that enumeration.

The agent should use this skill when estimating a policy's impact, building a cost-benefit or impact model, identifying stakeholders and effects, or presenting an assessment to decision-makers. The goal is to keep the agent from collapsing a multi-dimensional decision into a single flattering number, when a useful assessment makes the full set of consequences, with their uncertainty and distribution, visible.

## Core Rules

### Define The Policy Option And Its Comparators Explicitly

An impact assessment compares a policy to something. Define both.

- The policy option, including its specific design, scope, and dose.
- The comparator, whether status quo, an alternative policy, or no action.
- The population and geographic scope to which the assessment applies.
- The time horizon over which effects and costs accrue.

An assessment of an undefined policy against an undefined baseline is not informative. Specify both so the comparison is clear and revisable.

### Enumerate All Affected Stakeholders

Impact is distributed across groups. Identify them all.

- Direct beneficiaries and those who bear costs.
- Indirectly affected groups, such as competitors, suppliers, or neighboring communities.
- Future generations for policies with long horizons.
- Vulnerable or marginalized groups who may be overlooked.
- Implementing agencies and their staff.

Missing a stakeholder means missing their impacts. A comprehensive stakeholder map is the foundation of a complete assessment.

### Identify Direct, Indirect, And Unintended Effects

Policies ripple beyond their direct target. Map the full chain.

- Direct effects on the targeted outcome or behavior.
- Indirect effects through markets, institutions, or behavior responses.
- Unintended consequences, such as substitution, gaming, or displacement.
- Long-run effects that differ from short-run effects.
- Effects on outcomes not targeted by the policy.

A narrow focus on the direct target misses the consequences that often determine whether a policy succeeds or fails. Enumerate widely, then assess what can be estimated.

### Estimate Benefits And Harms In Comparable Terms

To compare consequences, express them in comparable terms where possible.

- Monetize benefits and harms where credible methods exist.
- Quantify non-monetary effects in natural units, lives, cases, years.
- Qualitatively describe effects that resist quantification.
- Be explicit about which effects are monetized, quantified, or qualitative only.

Forcing everything into money can distort; leaving everything qualitative can obscure. Use a mix, and be transparent about what is measured how. Never bury an important unquantified effect because it was inconvenient.

### Account For Costs, Including Opportunity And Administrative Costs

Costs extend beyond direct spending.

- Direct fiscal costs to government.
- Compliance costs to affected parties.
- Administrative and enforcement costs.
- Opportunity costs of resources used.
- Transition and adjustment costs.

A policy that achieves its goal at excessive cost may be worse than alternatives. Full cost accounting prevents the error of counting benefits while ignoring what they cost.

### Model Behavioral Responses

People and organizations respond to policy, often in ways that change its impact.

- Anticipated behavioral responses, such as avoidance or substitution.
- General equilibrium effects in affected markets.
- Gaming or strategic responses to incentives.
- Dynamic responses over time as adaptation occurs.

A static assessment that ignores response can badly misestimate impact. Build behavioral response into the model, and acknowledge the uncertainty it introduces.

### Handle Distribution And Equity Explicitly

Aggregate impact hides distribution. Make distribution visible.

- Report effects by stakeholder group, not only in aggregate.
- Identify who bears costs and who receives benefits.
- Assess equity implications, especially for vulnerable groups.
- Consider whether the policy narrows or widens disparities.

A policy with positive aggregate impact that concentrates benefits on the advantaged and harms on the vulnerable may be undesirable on equity grounds. Distribution is a first-class output, not a footnote.

### Express Uncertainty And Sensitivity and distinguish Ex-Ante And Ex-Post Assessment

Impact estimates depend on assumptions and data of varying quality. Convey the uncertainty.

- Provide ranges or scenarios, not single point estimates.
- Identify the assumptions that most affect the conclusion.
- Conduct sensitivity analysis on key parameters.
- Distinguish well-established effects from speculative ones.

A single number conveys false precision. A range with identified drivers lets decision-makers see where the estimate is fragile and where it is robust.

Assessment before and after implementation answer different questions.

- Ex-ante, predicts likely impact to inform the decision.
- Ex-post, measures actual impact to inform continuation or revision.

Ex-ante assessments rely more on assumptions and models; ex-post on observed data. Be clear which is being done, and use ex-post evidence to refine ex-ante methods over time. A policy decision should be revisited when ex-post evidence accumulates.

### Present The Assessment In Decision-Relevant Form and build In Review And Updating

The assessment must be usable by decision-makers.

- Lead with the main conclusion and its confidence.
- Show benefits, harms, costs, and distribution clearly.
- Provide scenarios reflecting key uncertainties.
- State the main assumptions and their effect on the conclusion.
- Distinguish what the assessment can and cannot decide, leaving value judgments to decision-makers.

An assessment that buries the answer in technical detail serves no one. Clarity and completeness together make the assessment useful.

Impact assessments are provisional. Plan to revisit.

- Update the assessment as new evidence and data arrive.
- Record the assumptions and evidence base at the time of the decision.
- Compare ex-post results to ex-ante predictions to learn.
- Adjust the policy based on accumulated evidence.

A one-time assessment becomes stale. Building in review keeps policy tied to evolving evidence.

## Common Traps

### Counting Benefits, Ignoring Costs

A biased case misleads decisions. Account for full costs.

### Missing Stakeholders

Overlooked groups mean overlooked impacts. Map all stakeholders.

### Focusing Only On Direct Effects

Indirect and unintended effects often dominate. Enumerate widely.

### Forcing Everything Into Money

Monetization can distort or omit important effects. Use mixed methods transparently.

### Ignoring Behavioral Response

Static models misestimate impact. Build in response and dynamics.

### Burying Distribution In Aggregates

Aggregates hide who wins and loses. Report distribution explicitly.

### False Precision In Point Estimates and confusing Ex-Ante And Ex-Post

Single numbers hide uncertainty. Provide ranges and sensitivity.

They answer different questions. Be clear which is being done.

### Technical Opacity and one-Time Assessment Without Review

An assessment decision-makers cannot use fails its purpose. Present clearly.

Stale assessments mislead. Plan to update.

## Self-Check

- Are the policy option and its comparator defined explicitly, with scope and horizon?
- Are all affected stakeholders identified, including indirect and vulnerable groups?
- Are direct, indirect, unintended, and long-run effects enumerated?
- Are benefits and harms expressed in comparable terms, with monetized, quantified, and qualitative effects distinguished?
- Are full costs, including compliance, administrative, opportunity, and transition costs, accounted for?
- Are behavioral responses and general equilibrium effects modeled?
- Is distribution across stakeholders reported explicitly, with equity implications assessed?
- Is uncertainty conveyed through ranges, scenarios, and sensitivity analysis?
- Is the assessment clearly labeled as ex-ante or ex-post, with plans to update?
- Is the assessment presented in decision-relevant form, leading with the conclusion and distinguishing evidence from value judgments?; for assessments driving major policy or budget decisions, has an experienced policy analyst or economist reviewed the model, assumptions, and distributional analysis before it informs the decision?
