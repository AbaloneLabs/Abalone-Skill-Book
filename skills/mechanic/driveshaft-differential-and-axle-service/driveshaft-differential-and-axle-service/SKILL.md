# Driveshaft, Differential, and Axle Service

The driveline from the transmission output to the wheels — driveshafts, differentials, axles, and CV joints — is a precision system where a few thousandths of an inch of backlash, a single degree of driveline angle, or a worn bearing turns a quiet car into a vibrating, howling, clicking complaint. The judgment problem is that driveline symptoms (vibration, howl, clunk, click, shudder) overlap across driveshaft, differential, axle, and wheel-bearing causes, and because differential setup (backlash, carrier preload, pinion depth) is exacting work where a wrong setting destroys the gear set in miles. A technician who replaces a ring and pinion for what is a wheel bearing, or who sets up a differential without verifying preload and backlash, hands back a vehicle that howls or vibrates again. This skill covers the disciplined diagnosis and service of driveshafts, differentials, and axles: localizing the noise and vibration, and setting up gears correctly.

## Core Rules

### Localize Driveline Noise and Vibration by Speed, Load, and Condition

Driveline noises and vibrations are localized by when they occur (vehicle speed, engine speed, acceleration or coast, torque on or off) and where they come from, because each component has a characteristic signature. A wheel bearing noise is a growl or rumble that changes with vehicle speed and gets louder on turns (loading the bad bearing) and is unaffected by throttle; a differential howl is a tonal whine that changes with throttle (acceleration howl often indicates pinion bearing or pattern; coast howl indicates the coast side of the pattern) and is present at a specific speed range; a driveshaft vibration is a speed-related shake (not engine-speed-related) that often peaks at a specific vehicle speed; a CV joint click is a rapid clicking on tight turns, especially under acceleration; a clunk on engagement is lash in the differential, the U-joints, or the slip spline.

The disciplined diagnosis uses the conditions to localize the source: a vehicle-speed vibration that does not change with gear or throttle points to the driveshaft, axles, or wheels; an engine-speed vibration that changes with gear points to the engine or torque converter; a howl that changes with throttle points to the differential. The tradeoff is that this localization takes a road test with attention to conditions, but it prevents replacing the wrong component.

### Check Driveline Angle, Phasing, and U-Joint Condition for Vibrations

A driveshaft vibration is most often a U-joint, an angle, or a phasing problem, and the disciplined diagnosis checks all three. The U-joints are checked for play (in all directions, with the driveshaft unloaded), for notchiness or binding when rotated, and for rust seeping from the caps (a sign of failed needle bearings). The driveline angle (the angle between the transmission output and the pinion, and the operating angle of each U-joint) must be within spec — large or unequal angles cause a cyclic vibration, and the pinion angle changes under load (on leaf-spring suspensions, the pinion rotates up under acceleration), so the static angle must account for the dynamic change. The phasing (the orientation of the U-joints at each end of a two-joint shaft) must be in the same plane; an out-of-phase shaft vibrates at a specific speed.

The tradeoff is that measuring angles takes an angle gauge and the vehicle on a level surface, but a wrong angle is a common cause of a vibration that no balancing fixes. The disciplined technician checks U-joints, measures the operating angles, and verifies phasing before balancing or replacing the shaft.

### Set Up Ring and Pinion With Correct Backlash, Preload, and Contact Pattern

Differential gear setup is the most exacting driveline work, because the ring and pinion must mesh with the correct backlash (typically 0.006 to 0.010 inch, per OEM spec), the correct pinion depth (which sets the contact pattern), the correct carrier bearing preload (the rotating drag of the carrier bearings), and the correct pinion bearing preload (set with the crush sleeve or shims and the pinion nut torque). The contact pattern — checked with marking compound, rotating the ring gear in both directions under load — shows where the pinion tooth contacts the ring gear tooth: a correct pattern is centered on the tooth; a heel contact (toward the outside of the ring gear) indicates the pinion is too shallow; a toe contact (toward the inside) indicates the pinion is too deep; a flank contact indicates wrong backlash.

The tradeoff is that setup takes multiple iterations of shims and rechecks, but a wrong setup howls and destroys the gear set. The disciplined technician sets backlash with a dial indicator, sets preloads with an inch-pound torque wrench, checks the contact pattern with marking compound, and iterates until the pattern is correct — never assembles a gear set without verifying the pattern.

### Evaluate Limited-Slip and Locker Function and the Friction Modifier

Limited-slip differentials (clutch-type or cone-type) and lockers (mechanical or electronic) add traction but require specific service: the clutch-type units need friction modifier in the gear oil (without it, the clutches chatter on turns), the correct gear oil specification, and periodic clutch-pack inspection and adjustment; the electronic lockers need electrical diagnosis (the actuator, the control module) when they fail to lock or unlock. The disciplined service verifies the limited-slip function (a chassis-dyno or a low-traction surface test confirms both wheels drive), uses the OEM-specified fluid with the correct friction modifier (or a fluid that already contains it), and diagnoses chatter (clutch chatter on turns is the classic sign of wrong or missing friction modifier). The tradeoff is that the friction modifier is cheap and often curative of chatter, but it is skipped because the fluid "is gear oil."

### Inspect Axle Bearings, Seals, and CV Boots Before They Fail Catastrophically

Axle bearings and seals and CV boots are the components that fail gradually and catastrophically if ignored, and the disciplined inspection catches them before the failure. The axle bearings (on solid axles, the inner axle bearings; on independent rear, the carrier bearings) are checked for play, noise, and leakage (a leaking axle seal wets the brake shoes or pads and causes a brake pull). The CV boots are inspected for tears, cracks, and grease slinging (a torn boot dumps the grease and lets dirt in, destroying the CV joint within thousands of miles — a boot replaced early saves the joint). The tradeoff is that a boot replacement is cheap and a CV joint replacement is expensive, so the disciplined technician flags torn boots for immediate service.

## Common Traps

### Replacing a Differential for a Wheel Bearing Noise — The vehicle has a growl that changes with speed, the technician assumes the differential, and replaces the ring and pinion — when the cause is a wheel bearing. The trap mechanism is that a wheel bearing growl sounds like a differential howl, but it changes on turns (loading the bad bearing) and is unaffected by throttle. The false signal is the growl sounding like the differential; the harm is an expensive, unnecessary gear setup. The disciplined technician localizes the noise by speed, load, and turn before the differential.

### Setting Up a Gear Set Without Verifying the Contact Pattern — The ring and pinion are installed, the backlash is set, but the contact pattern is not checked, and the gear set howls or fails because the pinion depth was wrong. The trap mechanism is that backlash alone does not confirm correct mesh; the contact pattern shows the actual mesh, and skipping it leaves a wrong setup. The false signal is the backlash "being in spec"; the harm is a howling or failing gear set. The disciplined technician checks the contact pattern with marking compound before final assembly.

### Reusing the Old Crush Sleeve or Not Setting Pinion Preload — The pinion is reassembled with the old crush sleeve, the pinion nut is torqued, and the pinion bearing preload is wrong (too loose or too tight), causing bearing failure. The trap mechanism is that the crush sleeve is a one-time-use part that sets the pinion preload, and reusing it gives incorrect preload. The false signal is the nut "being torqued"; the harm is pinion bearing failure. The disciplined technician uses a new crush sleeve and sets the preload with an inch-pound wrench.

### Diagnosing a CV Click as a Wheel Bearing — The vehicle clicks on tight turns, the technician diagnoses a wheel bearing, and the click persists because it was a worn outer CV joint. The trap mechanism is that a CV click on turns sounds like a bearing noise, but it is specific to tight turns under acceleration. The false signal is the click sounding like a bearing; the harm is an unnecessary bearing job. The disciplined technician distinguishes the CV click (turns, acceleration) from a bearing growl (speed, turns).

### Adding the Wrong Fluid or Skipping Friction Modifier in a Limited-Slip — The limited-slip differential is serviced, generic gear oil is installed without friction modifier, and the differential chatters on turns. The trap mechanism is that clutch-type limited-slip units need friction modifier, and without it the clutches grab and chatter. The false signal is "gear oil is gear oil"; the harm is chatter and accelerated clutch wear. The disciplined technician uses the OEM-specified fluid with the correct friction modifier.

## Self-Check

- Did I localize the noise or vibration by vehicle speed, engine speed, throttle (acceleration or coast), and turn, before condemning a component?
- Did I distinguish a wheel bearing growl (speed, turns) from a differential howl (throttle, speed range) from a driveshaft vibration (speed, not throttle) from a CV click (turns, acceleration)?
- For a driveshaft vibration, did I check U-joint play and notchiness, measure the driveline operating angles, and verify phasing before balancing or replacing?
- For a differential gear setup, did I set backlash with a dial indicator, set carrier and pinion preload with an inch-pound wrench, and verify the contact pattern with marking compound?
- Did I use a new crush sleeve (or the correct shim setup) and set the pinion bearing preload correctly?
- For a limited-slip or locker, did I verify function, use the OEM-specified fluid with the correct friction modifier, and diagnose chatter as a friction-modifier or clutch issue?
- Did I inspect the CV boots for tears and grease slinging, and recommend boot replacement before the joint is destroyed?
- Did I check axle bearings for play, noise, and seal leakage, and address a leaking seal before it contaminates the brakes?
- Did I road-test after the repair and confirm the noise, vibration, click, or clunk is resolved under the original complaint conditions?
