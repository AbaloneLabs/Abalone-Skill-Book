# EV Charging and High Voltage Safety

Electric vehicle charging and high-voltage service combine two of the highest-stakes activities in a shop: charging faults (a no-charge, a slow charge, a charge interruption) that strand the customer and require understanding the AC onboard charger, the DC fast charge, the charge port, and the pilot-signal handshake, and high-voltage safety that, if violated, kills. The judgment problem is that charging faults overlap across the vehicle, the charger, the cable, and the grid, and that high-voltage work has zero tolerance for a skipped safety step — a single contact with an exposed HV conductor is lethal, and a single dropped glove or a bypassed lockout is the mechanism of injury. A technician who condemns the onboard charger for a bad charge port, or who opens an HV cover without disabling and verifying the system, hands back either a vehicle that does not charge or a catastrophic injury. This skill covers the disciplined diagnosis of EV charging faults and the non-negotiable safety of high-voltage service.

## Core Rules

### Follow the High-Voltage Disable and Lockout-Tagout Before Any HV Work

The single most important rule in EV service is that no high-voltage work begins until the system is disabled, verified de-energized, and locked out, and the disciplined procedure follows the OEM steps exactly. The disable: turn the ignition off, remove the key, disconnect the 12 V battery (some vehicles require this before the service disconnect), remove the HV service disconnect plug (the main safety disconnect, often under a seat or in a covered compartment), wait the specified time for the HV capacitors to discharge (often 5 to 15 minutes, per OEM), and verify the HV system is de-energized with a calibrated CAT III-rated high-voltage meter at the HV terminals. The lockout-tagout: the service disconnect is locked with a personal lock and tagged with the technician's name, so no one can re-energize the system while the technician is working on it. The tradeoff is that the disable and the wait are mandatory and not optional, and a technician who skips a step risks lethal contact with HV.

### Use the Correct PPE: HV Gloves, Insulated Tools, and Eye Protection

High-voltage service requires specific personal protective equipment, and the disciplined use is non-negotiable. HV gloves (Class 0, rated for 1000 V, inspected for holes before every use by a roll-test or an air test, and within their test date — gloves are recertified every six months), worn with leather outer protectors to prevent mechanical damage, are required for any work near exposed HV conductors. Insulated tools (rated for 1000 V, with the insulation intact and inspected) are used for any tool that could contact an HV conductor. Safety glasses or a face shield protect against arc flash. The tradeoff is that the PPE is cumbersome and slows the work, but a single HV contact is lethal and the PPE is the barrier. The disciplined technician inspects the gloves before every use, never uses expired gloves, and never works without the PPE.

### Diagnose Charging Faults Through the Handshake: Pilot, Proximity, and the Charge Session

EV charging is a handshake between the vehicle and the charger (or the EVSE — the charging station), and the disciplined diagnosis evaluates the handshake. The pilot signal (the control pilot, the PWM signal from the EVSE that announces the available current and the vehicle's readiness) and the proximity signal (the plug-present signal) are the two communication lines, and a fault in either prevents the charge. The disciplined diagnosis reads the charge data on the scan tool (the charge session status, the pilot signal state, the charge current, the onboard charger status), checks the charge port for damage and moisture, tests the EVSE and the cable with a known-good setup (is the fault the vehicle or the charger?), and evaluates the onboard charger (for AC charging) and the DC fast charge interface (for DC charging) separately. The tradeoff is that the handshake is complex and the fault can be anywhere, but isolating the vehicle from the charger is the first step.

### Distinguish AC Onboard Charger Faults From DC Fast Charge Faults

AC charging (Level 1 and Level 2) uses the onboard charger (OBC) to convert AC to DC and charge the battery; DC fast charging (Level 3, CCS or CHAdeMO) bypasses the OBC and feeds DC directly to the battery through a separate interface. The disciplined diagnosis distinguishes the two: if AC charging fails but DC fast charge works, the fault is in the OBC or the AC charge port; if DC fast charge fails but AC works, the fault is in the DC fast charge interface or its contactors and communication; if both fail, the fault is in the shared charge port, the battery management, or the HV system. The tradeoff is that testing both AC and DC charging (when available) localizes the fault to the OBC, the DC interface, or the shared components, and prevents replacing the OBC for a DC fault or vice versa.

### Evaluate HV Isolation and Ground Faults as a Safety and a Charge Fault

HV isolation — the resistance between the HV bus and the vehicle chassis — is monitored continuously by the battery management system, and an isolation fault (a low-resistance path from the HV bus to the chassis, from a damaged insulation, a coolant leak into an HV component, or a failed component) sets a fault, disables the HV system, and prevents charging and driving, as a safety measure. The disciplined diagnosis reads the isolation fault code, identifies the affected subsystem (the battery, the inverter, the motor, the OBC, the compressor, the charger), and isolates the fault by disconnecting HV components one at a time (with the system disabled and the PPE on) and rechecking the isolation. The tradeoff is that an isolation fault is both a safety issue and a charge fault, and the disciplined diagnosis finds the leaking component rather than replacing the battery or the inverter blindly.

### Handle the HV Battery and the Coolant With Specific Procedures

The HV battery and its thermal management (the coolant loop) have specific service procedures, and the disciplined service follows them. The battery coolant (often a specific dielectric or deionized coolant, not generic coolant) is checked for level and condition and serviced with the correct fluid; a coolant leak into the battery or an HV component causes an isolation fault. The battery itself is not opened or serviced beyond the OEM-specified procedures (module replacement on some, full pack replacement on others), and any battery service follows the disable, the PPE, and the fire-safety precautions (a battery fire is a lithium fire that requires specific suppression and can reignite). The tradeoff is that the battery service is specialized and hazardous, and a wrong procedure risks a thermal event. The disciplined technician is OEM-trained for the specific vehicle and follows the procedures.

## Common Traps

### Working on the HV System Without the Disable and the Wait — The technician opens an HV cover or disconnects an HV component without removing the service disconnect and waiting for the capacitor discharge, and contacts live HV. The trap mechanism is that the HV capacitors retain lethal energy after the ignition is off and the battery is disconnected, and the wait is for the capacitors to discharge; skipping it risks lethal contact. The false signal is the ignition "being off"; the harm is a lethal HV injury. The disciplined technician removes the service disconnect, waits the specified time, and verifies de-energization with a calibrated HV meter.

### Using Expired or Damaged HV Gloves — The technician uses HV gloves that are past their six-month test date or have a pinhole, and the gloves do not protect. The trap mechanism is that HV gloves degrade and develop pinholes, and an expired or damaged glove does not provide the rated insulation; a pinhole lets the HV through. The false signal is the gloves "looking fine"; the harm is an HV injury through a failed glove. The disciplined technician inspects the gloves before every use, recertifies every six months, and discards any damaged glove.

### Condemning the Onboard Charger for a Charge Port or EVSE Fault — The vehicle will not AC charge, the technician condemns the OBC, and the cause is a damaged charge port or a faulty EVSE. The trap mechanism is that the AC charge path includes the port, the cable, the EVSE, and the OBC, and a fault anywhere stops the charge; the OBC is the expensive, easy target. The false signal is the no-charge pointing at the OBC; the harm is an unnecessary OBC replacement. The disciplined technician isolates the vehicle from the EVSE and checks the port before the OBC.

### Treating an Isolation Fault as a Battery Failure — The isolation fault sets, the technician condemns the HV battery, and the cause is a leaking coolant into an HV component or a damaged insulation in the inverter or the compressor. The trap mechanism is that an isolation fault is a low-resistance path to the chassis from any HV component, and the battery is one of many possible sources. The false signal is the isolation code pointing at the battery; the harm is an unnecessary, expensive battery replacement. The disciplined technician isolates the leaking component by disconnecting HV subsystems.

### Diagnosing a DC Fast Charge Fault as an Onboard Charger Fault — The DC fast charge fails, the technician condemns the OBC, and the cause is the DC fast charge interface (the contactors, the communication, the CCS or CHAdeMO pins), which is separate from the OBC. The trap mechanism is that DC fast charge bypasses the OBC, and a DC fault is in the DC interface, not the OBC. The false signal is the no-charge pointing at the charger; the harm is an unnecessary OBC replacement. The disciplined technician distinguishes AC (OBC) from DC (fast charge interface) faults by testing both.

## Self-Check

- Did I follow the OEM high-voltage disable procedure (ignition off, 12 V disconnect, service disconnect removed, specified wait for capacitor discharge) and verify de-energization with a calibrated CAT III HV meter before any HV work?
- Did I apply lockout-tagout with a personal lock on the service disconnect?
- Did I inspect the HV gloves (Class 0, within the six-month test date, no pinholes by roll-test) before every use, wear leather protectors, and use insulated rated tools and eye protection?
- For a charging fault, did I evaluate the handshake (pilot, proximity), isolate the vehicle from the EVSE with a known-good setup, and check the charge port for damage and moisture?
- Did I distinguish an AC onboard charger fault from a DC fast charge fault by testing both charge methods?
- For an isolation fault, did I read the affected subsystem and isolate the leaking component by disconnecting HV components (with the system disabled and PPE on) rather than condemning the battery?
- For battery or coolant service, did I use the correct dielectric coolant and follow the OEM battery service procedure and the fire-safety precautions?
- Did I verify the HV system re-energizes correctly after the repair (the contactors close, the system ready, no isolation faults)?
- Did I verify the vehicle charges (AC and DC if applicable) to the expected rate and the charge session completes without interruption?
- Am I OEM-trained and current on the specific vehicle's HV procedures, and did I document the disable, the PPE, and the verification?
