# Collision Damage Assessment and Repair Planning

Collision repair begins long before a panel is pulled or a part is ordered — it begins with an accurate damage assessment that determines whether the vehicle can be safely repaired at all, what must be replaced versus straightened, and in what order the work must proceed. The judgment problem is that visible damage is often the smallest part of the story. The impact that crushed a quarter panel transferred energy through the structure, and the harm that energy did — to crush zones, to suspension mounting points, to wiring harnesses routed behind the sheet metal, to restraint system components — is frequently invisible without measurement. A repair plan built on what the eye can see will miss the damage that returns as a dog-tracking vehicle, a tire-wear complaint, an airbag that fails to deploy, or a structural failure in the next collision. This skill covers the disciplined assessment process that separates a safe, complete repair from a cosmetic patch that puts a weakened vehicle back on the road.

## Core Rules

### Document the Impact and Establish the Damage Path Before Estimating

Before any part is ordered or any panel is pulled, reconstruct how the impact energy traveled through the vehicle. Document the point of impact, the direction of the force, and the components in the direct path of that force. A frontal impact at 30 mph does not stop at the bumper — it loads the rails, the aprons, the radiator support, the strut towers, and potentially the cowl and floor pan. A rear impact loads the rear body panel, the trunk floor, the rear rails, and can drive the fuel tank or suspension mounting points forward.

The disciplined approach is to trace the energy path component by component and ask, for each, whether it could have absorbed load without visible deformation. Crush zones are designed to deform in a controlled way to absorb energy; the fact that a rail "looks straight" does not mean it has not yielded past its design limit. Cautions at this step: do not assume that cosmetic damage (a dented fascia) means only cosmetic underlying damage, and do not assume that a panel that looks undamaged was not loaded. Indirect damage — the damage that appears away from the impact point because energy transferred through the structure — is where the expensive and dangerous misses live.

### Separate Direct Damage, Indirect Damage, and Pre-Existing Damage

A complete damage assessment distinguishes three categories. Direct damage is the visible deformation at and immediately around the point of impact. Indirect damage is the deformation or component failure that resulted from energy transfer — a buckled cowl, a shifted rear axle, a cracked windshield, a deployed seatbelt pretensioner triggered by the impact pulse. Pre-existing damage is corrosion, prior poor repair, or wear that was present before this collision and must not be charged to the insurance claim or hidden from the customer.

The trap is conflating these categories. Charging pre-existing rust to the claim is fraud and will be caught on audit; failing to identify indirect damage leaves the vehicle structurally compromised. The disciplined approach is to inspect the entire vehicle, not just the impact area, and to document with photographs anything that may be pre-existing so there is a record of its condition on arrival. A suspension alignment check and a four-wheel measurement should be part of any structural impact assessment, because a shifted suspension mounting point is indirect damage that the eye will not catch but the alignment machine will.

### Determine Structural Versus Cosmetic Damage and Know the Repair Limits

Not all sheet metal is structural. Outer door skins, quarter panel cosmetics, and fascias can be repaired or replaced without affecting crashworthiness. But frame rails, aprons, torque boxes, rocker panels, pillars (A, B, C), the cowl, the floor pan, and the roof structure are load-bearing, and damage to them is a structural integrity question, not a cosmetic one. Structural damage that exceeds the manufacturer's repair limits must not be pulled back into shape and returned to service — it must be sectioned or replaced per OEM procedure, or the vehicle must be declared a total loss.

Cautions: never straighten a structural part that the OEM says must be replaced. Never section a part at a location the OEM has not approved. Never rely on "it measures within spec" as the sole criterion for a structural repair — a rail that has been yielded and pulled back to dimension has lost work-hardening and crush-zone performance, and it will not protect occupants in the next collision the way an undamaged or properly replaced rail will. When in doubt about whether a structural repair is within limits, escalate to a certified frame specialist or the OEM technical line; guessing on structural limits is how shops hand back vehicles that fail catastrophically in the next impact.

### Sequence the Repair to Avoid Compounding Damage

The order of operations matters. Pulling a frame back to dimension before removing damaged bolt-on components can transfer stress into undamaged panels. Welding a replacement panel before the underlying structure is fully corrected locks the misalignment into the vehicle permanently. The disciplined sequence is: measure the structure, correct the structure to dimension first, then replace structural panels, then cosmetic panels, then mechanical and electrical components, then refinish. Each step depends on the one before it; skipping ahead creates rework and hidden defects.

A specific caution: if the vehicle has deployed airbags or seatbelt pretensioners, the restraint system control module, impact sensors, and possibly the seatbelts themselves must be replaced per OEM procedure — not reset, not cleared, not "it still works." A deployed pretensioner has fired its pyrotechnic charge and cannot be reused; reusing it means the restraint will not function in the next collision. Document every restraint component replaced and keep the old parts for the claim file.

### Write the Estimate Against OEM Procedures, Not Memory

Every repair operation on the estimate should reference the OEM repair procedure for that vehicle: the sectioning locations, the weld types and counts, the adhesives, the corrosion protection requirements, the calibration procedures for ADAS sensors that may have been disturbed. Estimating from memory or from "how we've always done it" produces estimates that do not match what the vehicle actually requires — and when the repair deviates from OEM procedure, the shop assumes liability for the structural performance of the vehicle. If OEM procedures are not available for a specific operation, that is a reason to contact the OEM technical line, not a reason to improvise.

## Common Traps

### The "It Drives Straight" False Reassurance — A vehicle that tracks straight down the road and "feels fine" on a short test drive is assumed to be structurally sound, so the shop skips the four-wheel measurement and releases the vehicle. The trap is that a vehicle can drive acceptably with significant structural misalignment — a rear frame rail shifted by several millimeters may not produce a noticeable pull, but it changes suspension geometry, accelerates tire wear, and critically, alters how the vehicle will absorb energy in the next collision. The false signal is the "normal" driving feel; the harm is a vehicle returned to the customer with compromised crashworthiness that neither the shop nor the customer can perceive until the next impact. Driving straight is never a substitute for measurement on a vehicle that has absorbed collision energy.

### Hidden Damage Behind Undamaged Skin — A side impact crumples the outer rocker panel but the inner structural rocker appears intact because the outer skin hides it, so the estimator writes only the outer panel. The trap is that the inner rocker — the actual load-bearing member — frequently yields or cracks under the same load, and the damage is invisible until the outer panel is removed or the structure is measured. The false signal is the cosmetic appearance of the inner panel through a small inspection hole; the harm is a structural member returned to service cracked or yielded, which will fail under far less load than designed. Any impact that deformed an outer structural panel warrants measurement or inspection of the inner structural member before the estimate is finalized.

### Reusing Deployed Restraint Components to Save Cost — The airbag light is off after clearing codes, the seatbelt retracts and extends, so the shop assumes the restraint system is functional and reuses the deployed pretensioner or a "reset" control module. The trap is that a pyrotechnic pretensioner, once fired, is a single-use device — the charge is spent and the mechanism cannot fire again, regardless of whether the light is on. The false signal is the absence of a code; the harm is a restraint system that will not deploy in the next collision, a potentially fatal failure. OEM procedure is mandatory and non-negotiable here: every component the OEM says to replace after deployment must be replaced, and the module must be replaced or certified-reprogrammed, never merely code-cleared.

### Totaling the Vehicle on Visible Damage Alone — The visible damage estimate reaches 70% of the vehicle's value, so the shop declares it a total loss without completing the structural measurement or teardown that would reveal whether the true damage is higher (confirming the total) or whether indirect damage was missed. The trap is that an incomplete total-loss determination can go either way: it can understate damage and release a repairable-looking but actually totaled vehicle (creating liability), or it can miss repairable indirect damage and short the owner. The disciplined approach is to complete the structural assessment and a partial teardown of suspect areas before the total-loss threshold is applied, so the determination is based on actual damage, not visible damage.

## Self-Check

- Did I reconstruct the impact energy path and inspect every component in that path, or did I estimate only from visible damage at the impact point?
- Did I perform a four-wheel structural measurement on any vehicle that absorbed collision energy, or did I rely on "it drives straight"?
- For every structural component, did I check the OEM repair limit before deciding to straighten rather than replace?
- Did I distinguish direct, indirect, and pre-existing damage, and document pre-existing damage photographically?
- For any airbag or pretensioner deployment, did I list every OEM-required restraint component replacement and plan for module replacement or certified reprogramming — not code clearing?
- Did I sequence the repair plan so structural correction precedes panel replacement and welding?
- Does every line item on my estimate reference an OEM procedure, or did I estimate any operation from memory?
- Did I schedule ADAS sensor calibration for any sensor in or near the impact zone or any structural repair that could shift its aim?
- For structural damage beyond my certification, did I escalate to a certified frame specialist rather than proceeding on my own judgment?
