# Telematics and Connectivity Module Setup

Telematics and connectivity modules are the vehicle's link to cellular networks, GPS satellites, and OEM cloud services, and the judgment problem is that a unit can power up, report no faults, and still be functionally useless because it was never provisioned, has a weak antenna, or lacks an active subscription. These modules drive connected services—remote start from a phone app, stolen-vehicle tracking, automatic crash notification, over-the-air update capability, and Wi-Fi hotspot—and they are tied to the vehicle's identity, the customer's account, and a carrier relationship that the technician cannot see from the bay. The technician must understand that a replacement telematics unit is not just hardware: it carries an embedded SIM or eSIM, a modem identity, and a cryptographic provisioning state that must be matched to the VIN and activated through the OEM backend before any service works. Antenna placement is a precision task because GPS and cellular signals are fragile, and a module mounted with a misplaced, pinched, or wrong-length antenna may pass a self-test yet fail in the field with dropped calls and lost location. Because telematics modules also collect and transmit customer driving and location data, the work carries privacy and consent obligations that a routine mechanical repair does not. Setup that skips provisioning, antenna verification, or subscription activation produces a vehicle that looks repaired but delivers none of its connected features, so every step must be completed and verified against the OEM backend, not just against the scan tool.

## Core Rules

### Understand OEM Provisioning and the VIN-to-Account Binding

A telematics module is provisioned at the factory or at module replacement with the vehicle's identity, and the OEM backend associates that module's modem identity (IMEI, ICCID, SIM) with the VIN and the customer's connected-services account. When you replace a module, the old binding must be retired and the new module must be provisioned to the same VIN through the OEM's dealer or service portal; simply installing and programming the hardware is not enough, because the backend still points at the old unit and the new one has no service entitlement. Follow the OEM provisioning sequence exactly, which typically involves installing the module, programming it to the VIN with the diagnostic tool, then performing a backend activation or "TCU replacement" procedure that registers the new modem identity. Confirm provisioning success by checking module status in the OEM portal and by triggering a test connection from the vehicle, not just by reading a successful code on the scan tool. Be aware that provisioning can take time to propagate across the carrier and OEM systems, and that a module removed from another vehicle may be blacklisted or carry a conflicting identity that the backend will reject.

### Position GPS and Cellular Antennas for Signal Integrity

Antenna placement is the single largest determinant of whether connected services actually work, and it is where most installation failures originate. GPS antennas require a clear view of the sky through glass or a non-metallic surface, mounted horizontally with the correct orientation, away from metallic trim, heated windshield elements, and other RF sources; a GPS antenna placed under a metal dash or behind a metallic defroster grid may never achieve a position fix. Cellular antennas benefit from antenna diversity—two antennas placed with orthogonal polarization or spatial separation—so that the modem can select the stronger signal and survive multipath fading; follow the OEM placement diagram exactly, including the specified spacing and the orientation of the diversity pair. Never cut, splice, extend, or coil the antenna cables to make them fit, because the cable length is part of the tuned antenna system and altering it changes the impedance and ruins performance. Route cables away from high-current wiring, airbag harnesses, and moving parts, and secure them so they do not chafe or pinch. After installation, verify GPS fix time and satellite count, and confirm cellular signal strength, before declaring the job complete.

### Verify Cellular Signal and Network Registration in the Bay

A telematics module that powers up and reports no DTCs can still have no cellular service if the SIM is not activated, the modem has not registered to the network, or the signal in the bay is too weak. After installation and provisioning, use the diagnostic tool to read the modem's registration status, the carrier, the signal strength, and the data connection state; a module stuck in "searching" or "denied" has not registered and will not deliver services. Be aware that a steel-framed shop building attenuates cellular signal, so a weak reading in the bay may be normal while the module works fine outdoors; move the vehicle outside or near a window if registration fails, and re-test. Confirm the module is on the correct carrier and APN for the region, because a misconfigured or wrong-region module may register to a roaming partner with no data entitlement. If the module will not register after provisioning and a strong-signal location, escalate to the OEM connected-services help line with the IMEI and ICCID, because the issue is usually in the backend entitlement, not the hardware.

### Program Replacement Modules to the VIN and Configure Options

Replacement telematics modules require the same disciplined programming as any control unit—stable battery voltage, the correct OEM application, a matched file—but with the added requirement of connectivity configuration. Program the module to the VIN, load the correct firmware and calibration, and configure the options the vehicle is equipped with: emergency call (eCall) region, data call settings, Wi-Fi hotspot, and the connected-services feature set. Some platforms require entering the vehicle's current mileage and region, and entering the wrong values writes incorrect configuration that may disable features or cause regulatory issues. After programming, perform any required setup such as antenna diagnostics, microphone calibration for hands-free calling, and the eCall self-test where mandated. A module that is programmed but not configured will often communicate on the bus and report healthy yet provide no actual connectivity, because the feature flags are still at default. Run the full OEM post-installation procedure and a complete vehicle scan to confirm no new codes in the telematics module or in dependent modules such as the radio or gateway.

### Activate the Connected-Services Subscription With the Customer

Connected features are gated by a subscription or trial entitlement on the OEM backend, and installing hardware does not activate the service. After provisioning the module, confirm the customer's account has the correct services enabled—remote start, stolen-vehicle location, Wi-Fi, vehicle health reports—and that any trial or paid subscription is active and dated correctly. Some services require the customer to complete an enrollment step, accept terms, or pair the vehicle in the OEM mobile app, and the technician should guide the customer through this or document that it is pending. If the vehicle is used or the ownership changed, the prior owner's account may still be linked, and a transfer or reset is required before the new owner can enroll; never leave a customer's vehicle tied to a stranger's account. Verify activation by performing a real test from the app or the OEM portal—a remote lock command, a vehicle location request—rather than assuming the green status indicator means the service works. Document the activation status and any pending customer steps on the repair order.

### Handle Customer Data Privacy and Consent

Telematics modules collect and transmit location, driving behavior, and usage data, and service on these modules touches customer privacy in ways that mechanical work does not. Before replacing a module, understand what data the old unit holds and follow the OEM data-handling procedure, which may include de-registering the old account and confirming data removal; do not ship a customer's data-bearing module to a core return without following the documented wipe or return process. Obtain the customer's consent for any enrollment, app pairing, or data-sharing agreement that the setup requires, and explain in plain terms what data the connected services collect and who can access it. On a used vehicle, ensure the prior owner's account and data access are fully severed before handing the vehicle to a new owner, because residual access is both a privacy breach and a security risk. Be aware of regional privacy regulations that govern vehicle data, and follow the OEM's compliance guidance rather than improvising. Document that consent was obtained and that account transfer or data removal was completed.

### Diagnose and Escalate Provisioning and Activation Failures

When a telematics module is installed correctly but will not provision, register, or activate, the fault is usually in the backend rather than the vehicle, and the resolution requires escalation, not repeated hardware swaps. Gather the diagnostic data first: the module part and serial number, the IMEI and ICCID, the provisioning status reported by the scan tool, the cellular registration state, the signal strength, and any error codes. Confirm the module is the correct part number for the vehicle and region, that it is not a used or blacklisted unit, and that the VIN in the OEM portal matches the vehicle. Attempt the provisioning or activation sequence once more following the OEM steps exactly, and if it still fails, escalate to the OEM connected-services or technical help line with all the gathered data. Do not repeatedly re-flash or replace the module on a hunch, because backend entitlement issues will reproduce on every unit and the churn wastes parts and time. For a module that provisioned but provides no service, escalate with a test-case timestamp so the backend team can trace the failed transaction.

## Common Traps

### Treating a No-Fault Module as a Successful Installation

The most common trap is reading no DTCs from the telematics module and declaring the installation complete, when the module has not been provisioned or activated and provides no actual service. The mechanism is that the module powers up, communicates on the bus, and passes its internal self-test, so the scan tool reports healthy, but the backend still points at the old unit and the new modem has no entitlement. The false signal is the clean scan and the absence of warning lights, which feels like success. The harm is a customer who pays for a connected-services repair and receives none of the features, discovers it days or weeks later when the app does not work, and loses trust in the shop. Always verify provisioning in the OEM portal and perform a real end-to-end service test before returning the vehicle.

### Misplacing or Modifying the Antennas

A second trap is mounting the GPS or cellular antenna in a convenient spot, coiling excess cable, or extending a cable to reach a tidy routing, all of which destroy signal performance. The mechanism is that antenna systems are tuned to a specific cable length and placement, and metal structures, heated grids, and impedance changes from coiling or splicing attenuate or detune the signal; the module may still see a weak signal and report no fault while dropping connections in real use. The false signal is that the module achieved a GPS fix in the bay or showed bars of cellular signal, so the antennas appear to work. The harm is intermittent service—lost location in parking structures, dropped eCall, failed remote commands—that surfaces only in the field and is hard to trace back to the installation. Always follow the OEM antenna placement diagram exactly, use the specified cables unmodified, and verify GPS satellite count and cellular strength after installation.

### Skipping Subscription Activation or Account Transfer

A third trap is installing and provisioning hardware but leaving the connected-services subscription inactive, or leaving a used vehicle tied to the prior owner's account. The mechanism is that connected features are entitlement-gated on the backend, so a healthy, provisioned module still delivers nothing without an active subscription, and a prior owner's residual account blocks the new owner from enrolling and may give a stranger access to the vehicle's location. The false signal is that the module is online and the app shows the vehicle, so the service appears set up. The harm is a customer who cannot use the features they expected, or a privacy and security breach where a prior owner can track or control the vehicle. Always confirm subscription status, complete enrollment or transfer, sever prior accounts on used vehicles, and verify with a live service test.

### Installing a Used or Wrong-Region Module Without Authorization

A fourth trap is installing a salvage or wrong-region telematics module to save cost, without confirming OEM policy and backend compatibility. The mechanism is that telematics modules carry region-specific carrier entitlements, modem bands, and cryptographic identities tied to the original vehicle, and a used or wrong-region unit may be blacklisted, may not register to the local carrier, or may carry a conflicting identity that the backend rejects. The false signal is that the part number looks similar and the connector fits, so the unit appears interchangeable. The harm is a module that will not provision or register, days of diagnosis, a stranded connected-services feature, and possible security concerns if the donor vehicle's data is still linked. Always use a new or OEM-authorized module of the correct part number and region, and confirm OEM policy before considering any used unit.

### Failing to Sever Prior Owner Data on a Used Vehicle

A fifth trap is handing a used vehicle to a new owner while the telematics module is still enrolled to the prior owner's account, leaving the prior owner with ongoing access to the vehicle's location and remote features. The mechanism is that the account binding persists across the sale unless it is explicitly de-registered and the module is reset or re-provisioned, and the new owner cannot enroll until the old account is removed. The false signal is that the module works and the vehicle is delivered, so the transaction appears complete. The harm is a privacy breach and potential stalking or theft vector where a stranger retains control of the vehicle, plus the new owner's inability to use the services. Always perform the OEM account-transfer or factory-reset procedure on used vehicles, confirm the prior account is severed, and document the reset.

### Repeatedly Re-Flashing or Swapping Modules on a Backend Failure

A sixth trap is treating a provisioning or activation failure as a hardware fault and repeatedly re-flashing or replacing the module, when the root cause is in the OEM backend entitlement. The mechanism is that backend issues—stuck entitlement, carrier provisioning error, blacklisted IMEI—reproduce identically on every module, so each swap fails the same way and consumes expensive parts and labor. The false signal is that the failure is consistent and "hardware-like," which feels like a defective module. The harm is wasted modules, long vehicle downtime, and a frustrated customer, when a single escalation with the right data would have resolved the backend issue. Always gather the IMEI, ICCID, provisioning status, and a test-case timestamp, and escalate to the OEM connected-services help line before swapping hardware on a repeatable failure.

## Self-Check

- Did I provision the replacement module to the VIN through the OEM portal or backend procedure, not just install and program the hardware?
- Did I follow the OEM antenna placement diagram exactly, leaving cables uncut, unspliced, and uncoiled, and verify GPS satellite count and cellular signal strength?
- Did I confirm the modem registered to the correct carrier and has a data connection, testing outside or near a window if the bay signal was weak?
- Did I program the module to the VIN and configure all connectivity options and region settings, entering correct mileage and region values?
- Did I confirm the connected-services subscription is active and dated correctly, and guide the customer through any required enrollment or app pairing?
- On a used vehicle, did I sever the prior owner's account and perform the factory reset or transfer so no stranger retains access?
- Did I obtain and document customer consent for data collection and any data-sharing enrollment, and follow the OEM data-handling procedure for the removed module?
- Did I perform a real end-to-end service test—a remote command or location request through the app or portal—rather than relying on a clean scan, and escalate backend failures with IMEI and ICCID rather than swapping hardware?
