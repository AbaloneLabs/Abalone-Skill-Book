# PPE and Personal Safety in the Shop

Personal protective equipment is the last line of defense against shop hazards, and its selection and use require judgment because the hazards vary dramatically by task—eye hazards from grinding, chemical hazards from battery acid, thermal hazards from exhaust, rotational hazards from running engines, and ergonomic hazards from heavy lifting. The judgment problem is that PPE is uncomfortable, slows the work, and is easy to skip for "a quick task," yet the injuries it prevents—eye loss, chemical burns, hearing loss, crush injuries—are permanent and career-ending. The technician must match the PPE to the specific hazard of each task, must understand the limitations of each PPE type (a safety glasses does not protect against a chemical splash; a dust mask does not protect against solvent vapors), and must never treat PPE as optional based on convenience or experience.

## Core Rules

### Eye and Face Protection Selection

Eye protection is required for virtually every shop task because the eye is vulnerable to impact, chemical splash, and radiant energy, and an eye injury is often irreversible. Safety glasses with side shields (ANSI Z87.1 rated) are the baseline for general shop work, protecting against flying particles from grinding, drilling, wire brushing, and compressed air. Safety goggles (sealed, with indirect vents) are required for liquid hazards—battery service, coolant service, brake clean use, and any task with splash risk—because safety glasses do not seal against the face and a splash runs around the lens into the eye. A face shield is required for high-energy or high-volume splash tasks—battery jump-starting (explosion risk), significant acid spills, and angle grinding—worn over safety glasses or goggles, not instead of, because a face shield alone does not seal against the face. Welding requires a welding helmet with the correct shade lens for the process (shade 10 for MIG, darker for higher-amperage processes), and oxy-acetylene cutting requires shade 5 or 6 goggles. Never look at a welding arc without the correct shade, even briefly, because arc flash (UV keratitis) is painful and damaging. Prescription eyeglasses are not safety glasses unless they are ANSI Z87.1 rated; wear safety glasses over or use prescription safety lenses.

### Hand Protection and Chemical Compatibility

Hand protection must match the hazard: mechanical (cuts, abrasion), chemical (solvent, acid, base), thermal (hot components), or electrical (HV work). Mechanic's gloves (synthetic leather, nitrile-palmed) protect against abrasion and minor cuts for general service but offer limited chemical protection and melt onto the skin against hot exhaust. Nitrile gloves protect against oils, fuels, and most solvents for short-duration contact, but they degrade with some solvents (acetone, some brake cleans) and against heat; change them when they soften or tear. Heavy nitrile or neoprene gloves are required for prolonged solvent contact and for battery acid service. Leather or Kevlar welding gloves are required for welding and for handling hot exhaust components, but they are not cut-resistant and they reduce dexterity. Cut-resistant gloves (Kevlar, steel-mesh) are required for handling sharp sheet metal, broken glass, and for some cutting tasks. For high-voltage work on hybrids and EVs, Class 0 rubber insulating gloves with leather protectors are mandatory (see the high-voltage safety skill). Never substitute a glove type for a hazard it is not rated for; a mechanic's glove against battery acid, or a nitrile glove against a hot exhaust, fails and injures.

### Hearing Protection and Noise Exposure

Shop noise—from air tools, grinders, engines running, and compressed air—regularly exceeds the OSHA action level of 85 dBA time-weighted average, and chronic exposure causes permanent, irreversible noise-induced hearing loss that develops slowly and painlessly. Hearing protection (earplugs rated NRR 25-33, or earmuffs) is required for tasks that produce sustained loud noise: grinding, air chisel use, engine run-at-high-RPM, and compressor proximity. The selection depends on fit and preference: foam earplugs, when properly inserted (rolled, inserted, held until expanded), provide the highest NRR; earmuffs are easier to don and remove for intermittent noise. Dual protection (plugs and muffs together) is appropriate for extreme noise like a chassis dyno or a jet-engine-grade air source. The trap is that hearing loss is painless and gradual, so technicians skip protection because "it doesn't seem that loud," but the cumulative damage is permanent. Wear hearing protection for any task that requires raising your voice to be heard at arm's length, which is the practical threshold for hazardous noise.

### Respiratory Protection and Airborne Hazards

Respiratory protection is required for tasks that generate hazardous dust, fumes, or vapors, and the respirator must match the contaminant. A NIOSH-rated N95 or P100 disposable respirator protects against particulate—brake dust (which may contain legacy asbestos fibers in older vehicles), welding fume particulate, and grinding dust—but it does not protect against gases or vapors. A half-face or full-face cartridge respirator with organic vapor cartridges protects against solvent vapors during heavy solvent use, paint spraying, and certain chemical services; the cartridges must be the correct type for the contaminant and must be changed when breakthrough is detected (smell or taste) or per a schedule. A supplied-air respirator is required for confined-space work, isocyanate paint (which a cartridge respirator cannot fully protect against), and some heavy solvent tasks. Brake dust deserves special attention: while modern brake friction materials do not contain asbestos, older vehicles and some aftermarket pads may, and the safest practice is to use a brake clean solvent or a dedicated brake dust collection vacuum rather than compressed air, which aerosolizes the dust for inhalation. Never use compressed air to blow brake dust; use a liquid cleaner or a HEPA vacuum.

### Protective Clothing, Footwear, and Ergonomics

Protective clothing prevents burns, cuts, and chemical contact, and the selection must avoid creating new hazards. Avoid synthetic fabrics (polyester, nylon) for shop wear, because they melt onto the skin in a flash fire or arc event; cotton or flame-resistant clothing is safer. Long sleeves and pants protect against minor burns, cuts, and chemical contact, but they must fit closely enough not to catch on rotating components. Steel-toed or composite-toed boots protect against dropped tools and components; the toe rating must match the weight hazard (a 100-pound transmission dropped on a running shoe crushes toes). Remove rings, watches, and loose jewelry before working, because a ring caught on a battery post or a rotating component causes degloving injuries (the ring removes the finger's skin and flesh) and a metal watch band shorts electrical components and causes burns. Long hair must be tied back, and loose clothing must be secured, because both are caught in rotating components like cooling fans, belts, and drill chucks, pulling the technician into the machinery. Ergonomics—lifting with the legs, using hoists for heavy components, varying posture, and using creepers—prevents the chronic back, knee, and shoulder injuries that end mechanic careers.

### Task-Specific Hazard Assessment

Each task has a specific hazard profile that dictates the PPE combination, and the technician must assess the task before starting rather than defaulting to a single PPE set. Grinding requires safety glasses or goggles, a face shield, hearing protection, and gloves that do not compromise grip. Battery service requires goggles, acid-resistant gloves, and a face shield for jump-starting or significant acid handling. Welding requires a welding helmet, flame-resistant clothing, leather gloves, and hearing protection. Brake service requires a N95/P100 respirator (or liquid cleaning), nitrile gloves, and safety glasses. Exhaust work on a hot system requires heat-resistant gloves and awareness of burn risk. Running-engine diagnosis requires no loose clothing, no jewelry, tied hair, and awareness of rotating components (fans, belts, pulleys) and hot surfaces (exhaust manifolds, catalytic converters). High-voltage work requires Class 0 gloves, insulated tools, and the full disable procedure. Always assess the task, identify the hazards, select the PPE for those hazards, and verify the PPE is in good condition before starting.

## Common Traps

### Skipping Eye Protection for a Quick Task

The most common trap is skipping safety glasses for a "quick" task—checking a fluid, blowing off a part with air, looking under a hood—because the task seems low-risk. The mechanism is that eye injuries occur in fractions of a second from a single particle or a single splash, and the perceived risk does not match the actual risk of tasks like compressed air use (which propels particles at high velocity) or battery inspection (which can produce a splash from a bubbling cell). The false signal is that nothing has happened in similar quick tasks before. The harm is a permanent eye injury—a particle embedded in the cornea, a chemical burn, or vision loss—from a task that took seconds. Always wear safety glasses for every shop task, without exception, regardless of duration.

### Using the Wrong Glove for the Hazard

A second trap is using a glove that is not rated for the specific hazard, such as a mechanic's glove for battery acid or a nitrile glove for a hot exhaust. The mechanism is that each glove material has specific chemical and thermal compatibility, and the wrong material fails—melting, dissolving, or permeating—and the hazard reaches the skin. The false signal is that the glove "feels fine" initially. The harm is a chemical burn from acid through a mechanic's glove, a thermal burn from exhaust through a nitrile glove, or a cut from sheet metal through a glove not rated for cuts. Always match the glove material to the specific hazard—nitrile for oil and solvent, heavy nitrile or neoprene for acid, leather or Kevlar for heat and welding, cut-resistant for sharp metal.

### Wearing Jewelry or Loose Clothing Near Rotating Equipment

A third trap is wearing rings, watches, bracelets, necklaces, or loose clothing while working near running engines, belts, fans, or rotating tools. The mechanism is that a ring caught on a battery post short-circuits and heats to incandescent in seconds, causing a degloving burn; a watch band caught in a belt pulls the hand into the rotating machinery; a loose sleeve caught in a drill chuck or a cooling fan pulls the arm in with force that cannot be resisted. The false signal is that the jewelry or clothing has not caused a problem before. The harm is amputation, degloving, severe burns, or being pulled into machinery. Always remove all jewelry, secure long hair, and wear close-fitting clothing before any work near rotating equipment.

### Using a Dust Mask for Solvent Vapors

A fourth trap is using an N95 or P100 particulate respirator (a "dust mask") for protection against solvent vapors, paint fumes, or exhaust gases. The mechanism is that a particulate respirator filters solid particles but offers no protection against gases or vapors, which pass through the filter material and are inhaled; the technician believes they are protected because they cannot smell the particulate, but the vapor is uncontrolled. The false signal is that the respirator is in place and breathing feels normal. The harm is solvent vapor exposure (neurotoxicity, liver and kidney damage), isocyanate exposure from paint (sensitization and asthma), or carbon monoxide from exhaust (asphyxiation). Always use a cartridge respirator with the correct organic vapor cartridge for solvent and vapor hazards, never a particulate respirator.

### Blowing Brake Dust With Compressed Air

A fifth trap is using compressed air to clean brake dust from brake assemblies, which aerosolizes the dust for inhalation. The mechanism is that brake dust may contain legacy asbestos fibers (from older friction materials), heavy metals, and respirable crystalline silica, and compressed air launches these particles into the breathing zone where they are inhaled deep into the lungs. The false signal is that the dust "is gone" from the brake assembly. The harm is inhalation of hazardous particulate, with risk of asbestosis, mesothelioma (from legacy asbestos), and heavy metal toxicity, developing years after exposure. Always clean brake assemblies with a liquid brake cleaner or a dedicated HEPA brake dust vacuum, never with compressed air.

## Self-Check

- Did I wear ANSI Z87.1 safety glasses for every shop task, and goggles or a face shield for splash and high-energy hazards?
- Did I select gloves matched to the specific hazard—nitrile for solvent, acid-resistant for batteries, heat-resistant for exhaust, cut-resistant for sharp metal?
- Did I wear hearing protection for any task requiring raised voice at arm's length, including grinding, air tools, and engine run?
- Did I use the correct respirator—particulate for dust, organic vapor cartridge for solvents, supplied air for isocyanates?
- Did I avoid synthetic fabrics and wear cotton or flame-resistant clothing, with long sleeves and pants for protection?
- Did I wear steel- or composite-toed boots appropriate to the weight hazard?
- Did I remove all rings, watches, and jewelry and secure long hair and loose clothing before working near rotating equipment?
- Did I clean brake assemblies with liquid or HEPA vacuum rather than compressed air?
- Did I assess the task's specific hazards and select the PPE combination before starting?
- Did I verify all PPE is in good condition—no cracked lenses, no pinholed gloves, no expired respirator cartridges—before use?
