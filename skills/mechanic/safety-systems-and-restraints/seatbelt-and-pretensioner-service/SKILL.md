# Seatbelt and Pretensioner Service

Seatbelts are the primary occupant restraint, and unlike almost every other system on the vehicle, they are designed to be replaced — not repaired — after any event that loaded them. The judgment problem is that a seatbelt that looks fine after a crash may have stretched, a retractor that still locks may have a weakened torsion bar, and a pretensioner that did not deploy may still have been commanded and failed internally. Servicing seatbelts with the same "test and reuse if it works" mindset used for other systems puts occupants at risk in the next crash, because the component's load-bearing capacity has been silently compromised. This skill covers the disciplined inspection, replacement-decision, and service procedures for seatbelts and pretensioners, with emphasis on the safety-critical rules that distinguish this work from ordinary repair.

## Core Rules

### Replace, Never Repair, Any Seatbelt Component That Was Loaded in a Crash

Seatbelts are single-use load-bearing devices. The webbing is designed to stretch in a controlled way to absorb energy; the retractor's torsion bar twists to limit peak force; the anchor pretensioner fires to take up slack. Once any of these has functioned in a crash — even a minor one — the component has permanently deformed or consumed its energy-absorbing capacity and must be replaced, even if it "still works." Webbing that stretched will stretch further and may break in the next event; a torsion bar that twisted is now permanently yielded; a pretensioner that fired is spent. The disciplined technician follows the manufacturer's crash-damage repair guide, which specifies which seatbelt components must be replaced based on the severity and location of impact, and replaces every component the guide lists — not just the ones that "look damaged."

This rule also applies to components that were not directly loaded but are in the impact zone. A front impact may require replacement of front seatbelts, buckles, pretensioners, and the seatbelt height adjuster; a side impact may require replacement of the side seatbelt and the side airbag's related anchors. The guide is authoritative; the technician's visual judgment is not.

### Inspect Every Seatbelt at Every Service for Hidden Damage

Seatbelts fail from causes other than crashes: webbing cuts from being trapped in a door, fraying from rubbing against a sharp seat frame, UV degradation, pet damage, and contamination from chemicals that weaken the fibers. The disciplined technician inspects seatbelt webbing at every opportunity — pulling the full length of the belt out of the retractor and examining both sides for cuts, fraying, burns, discoloration, and stiffness — because a damaged belt may look acceptable in its normal stowed position but fail under load. Any webbing with a cut, burn, or significant fray is a reject; the entire belt assembly (webbing is not serviceable separately from the retractor on most vehicles) must be replaced. Buckle function must be verified: the tongue latches positively, releases cleanly, and the buckle switch (which tells the SRS module whether the belt is fastened) reads correctly in scan data.

### Diagnose Buckle and Retractor Complaints Through the System, Not by Guessing

Common seatbelt complaints — buckle will not latch, retractor will not extend, belt is locked and will not pull out — have specific causes that must be diagnosed before replacement:

- **Buckle will not latch** — foreign debris in the buckle (crumbs, coins), a damaged tongue, or a failed buckle latch mechanism. Clean and inspect before condemning; if the latch mechanism is damaged, replace the buckle assembly.
- **Retractor locked, belt will not pull out** — most retractors lock when the vehicle is on a steep angle or after sudden deceleration; the belt often frees when the vehicle is level or the belt is slowly fed back and gently pulled. A belt that remains locked on level ground may have a vehicle-sensitive locking mechanism that triggered in a minor impact (ALR/ELR), in which case the retractor must be replaced per the crash guide. Do not force a locked belt.
- **Buckle switch fault code** — the SRS module reports the buckle state; a fault code points to the buckle switch, its wiring, or the connector. Diagnose with a resistance check and wiggle test before replacing the buckle.

The disciplined technician diagnoses the specific cause rather than replacing the entire belt assembly for a debris-blocked buckle or a belt that simply needs to be leveled to release.

### Handle Pretensioners as Live Pyrotechnic Devices

Anchor and buckle pretensioners contain pyrotechnic charges that fire during a crash. They are electrically initiated, and improper handling can cause inadvertent deployment — which is dangerous and destroys the component. The disciplined service procedure is: disable the SRS system (disconnect the negative battery cable and wait the specified time, typically 10+ minutes, for the backup capacitors to discharge), verify the system is de-powered, and only then disconnect any pretensioner connector. Never probe a pretensioner connector with an ohmmeter or test light — the probe current can fire the charge. Never carry a pretensioner by its wires, and never point a pretensioner toward yourself or others. Treat every unfired pretensioner as if it could fire. A pretensioner that has deployed (visible damage, scorching, the piston is extended) is spent and must be replaced; a pretensioner that has not deployed but is past its service life or in a vehicle that had a deployment elsewhere must be evaluated per the crash guide.

### Verify Buckle Switch and Occupant Detection Function After Any Seat Service

The seatbelt buckle switch and the occupant classification system (OCS, the weight sensor in the passenger seat) together determine whether and how the airbags deploy for each seating position. After any seat or seatbelt service — replacing a buckle, replacing a seat, servicing the OCS — verify that the buckle switch reads correctly (fastened/unfastened) in SRS live data, and that the OCS classifies the occupant correctly (empty/child/adult) per its calibration procedure. A misadjusted buckle switch or a disturbed OCS can cause the airbag to deploy with the wrong force or to suppress deployment entirely. The disciplined technician performs the post-service verification and the OCS zero-weight calibration if specified, and confirms no SRS codes before returning the vehicle.

### Follow Torque and Anchor Procedures Exactly

Seatbelt anchors — at the buckle, the retractor, and the floor/D-pillar — are torqued to specific values because they carry crash loads. Under-torquing allows the anchor to slip or the bolt to back out under load; over-torquing can yield the bolt or damage the mounting structure. The disciplined technician uses a calibrated torque wrench, torques to the manufacturer's specification (not "good and tight"), and replaces any single-use bolt (many seatbelt anchor bolts are torque-to-yield and must not be reused). The anchor mounting area must be inspected for damage or corrosion; an anchor bolted to a weakened floor structure will pull out in a crash.

## Common Traps

### Reusing a Seatbelt After a Minor Crash Because It "Still Works" — After a low-speed collision, the seatbelt still retracts and latches, so the technician or estimator declares it reusable. The trap mechanism is that the webbing stretched beyond its elastic limit absorbing crash energy, the retractor's torsion bar yielded, or the ALR locking mechanism triggered; the component still appears functional but has lost its load-bearing capacity. The false signal is that the belt operates normally in a hand test. The harm is that in the next crash, the stretched webbing breaks or extends further, the yielded torsion bar offers no force limiting, and the occupant strikes the interior or is ejected — a catastrophic failure traced to a belt that was reused because it "still worked." The disciplined technician replaces every seatbelt component the crash-damage guide specifies, regardless of apparent function.

### Splicing or Sewing Damaged Seatbelt Webbing — A seatbelt has a small cut or fray, and someone splices, sews, or heat-welds the webbing to avoid replacement cost. The trap mechanism is that seatbelt webbing is engineered with specific weave, fiber orientation, and stitching that achieves its rated tensile strength; any field repair introduces a weak point that will fail at a fraction of the rated load. The false signal is that the repaired belt holds under a hand pull. The harm is that in a crash the repair fails, the occupant is unrestrained, and the injury or death is directly attributable to the field repair. The disciplined technician never repairs seatbelt webbing; any cut, burn, or fray requires replacement of the entire belt-and-retractor assembly, because webbing is not serviceable separately.

### Probing a Pretensioner Connector and Inadvertently Deploying It — The technician uses an ohmmeter or test light to check a pretensioner circuit, probing the connector to "see if it's live." The trap mechanism is that pretensioners fire on a small, well-defined current; a meter or test light can deliver enough current to ignite the pyrotechnic charge, deploying the pretensioner violently in the technician's hand or in the vehicle. The false signal is that the probe "should be safe because it's just measuring." The harm is the pretensioner deploys — a pyrotechnic event that can cause injury, destroys the component (now requiring replacement), and may trigger other SRS deployments. The disciplined technician never probes a live pretensioner connector with any instrument, disables the SRS and waits for capacitor discharge before disconnecting, and follows the manufacturer's diagnostic procedure that uses the scan tool, not direct probing.

### Replacing a Buckle for a Debris Blockage or a Locked Belt — The customer reports the buckle will not latch or the belt will not extend, and the technician replaces the buckle or belt assembly. The trap mechanism is that the buckle often fails to latch because of trapped debris (crumbs, coins, pet hair) that blocks the latch mechanism, and a belt that will not extend is often in its normal vehicle-sensitive or ALR lock mode triggered by angle or deceleration — both are conditions that clear with cleaning or leveling, not component failure. The false signal is that the complaint is present, interpreted as a failed component. The harm is that the customer pays for an unnecessary buckle or belt, the real cause (debris or lock mode) is not addressed, and the complaint may recur. The disciplined technician cleans and inspects the buckle, levels the vehicle and feeds the belt to release a lock, and confirms the component is actually failed before replacing it.

### Disturbing the Occupant Classification Sensor During Seat Work and Suppressing Airbag Deployment — The technician removes the passenger seat to access wiring, reinstalls it, and returns the car. The trap mechanism is that the occupant classification sensor (OCS) in the seat cushion or the seat frame must be calibrated to zero weight in its installed position; removing and reinstalling the seat without recalibration leaves the OCS mis-calibrated, so it may classify an adult as a child or as empty. The false signal is that the seat is bolted in and the SRS light is off. The harm is that in a crash the airbag deploys with child-level force (or not at all) for an adult occupant, because the OCS reported the wrong classification — a silent, invisible failure that only manifests in a crash. The disciplined technician performs the OCS zero-weight calibration after any seat removal or sensor disturbance and verifies the classification in scan data with a known weight before returning the vehicle.

## Self-Check

- For any vehicle that has been in a crash, did I consult and follow the manufacturer's crash-damage repair guide for which seatbelt and pretensioner components must be replaced?
- Did I replace — never repair — any seatbelt with webbing cuts, burns, fraying, or chemical contamination?
- Did I inspect the full length of each seatbelt (both sides of the webbing) for hidden damage, not just the stowed portion?
- For a buckle or retractor complaint, did I clean, inspect, and diagnose the specific cause before replacing the assembly?
- Before disconnecting any pretensioner, did I disable the SRS, wait for capacitor discharge, and avoid probing the connector with any meter or test light?
- After any seat or seatbelt service, did I verify the buckle switch reads correctly and the occupant classification sensor classifies correctly in SRS live data?
- If the OCS was disturbed, did I perform the zero-weight calibration and verify classification with a known weight?
- Did I torque all seatbelt anchors to specification with a calibrated wrench, and replace any single-use (torque-to-yield) anchor bolts?
- Did I inspect anchor mounting areas for damage or corrosion that could let the anchor pull out under crash load?
