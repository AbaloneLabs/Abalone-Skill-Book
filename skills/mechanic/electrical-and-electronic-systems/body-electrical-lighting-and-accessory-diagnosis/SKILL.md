# Body Electrical, Lighting, and Accessory Diagnosis

Body electrical systems — power windows, locks, mirrors, seats, wipers, washers, horns, lighting, gauges, and the ever-growing array of comfort and convenience features — are the highest-volume electrical work in the shop, and they are also where technicians waste the most time replacing parts on suspicion because the circuits are unfamiliar and the control strategies have changed. A power window that does not work can be the switch, the motor, the regulator, the module, the wiring, or the anti-pinch software; a headlight that is out can be the bulb, the socket, the wiring, the module, or a CAN bus command that never arrived. The judgment problem is that modern body electrical is no longer simple switch-to-load circuits — it is module-controlled, network-commanded, and often pulse-width-modulated, and the technician who tests it like a 1980s circuit (with a test light and a guess) will misdiagnose it repeatedly. This skill covers the disciplined diagnosis of body electrical and accessory faults: reading the wiring diagram to understand the control strategy, testing the circuit at the correct points, and distinguishing switch, wiring, module, and load faults.

## Core Rules

### Read the Wiring Diagram to Understand the Control Strategy Before Testing

The single most important step in body electrical diagnosis is reading the wiring diagram for the affected circuit before touching a meter, because the control strategy determines what tests are meaningful. A power window on an older vehicle is a simple circuit: battery power through a fuse, through a reversible switch, to the motor, to ground — and the test is straightforward (power at the switch, power at the motor, ground at the motor). A power window on a modern vehicle is module-controlled: the switch is an input to a body control module (BCM), the BCM commands the window motor through a relay or a driver, the BCM may communicate with a door module over the LIN bus or the CAN bus, and the anti-pinch feature requires the motor to report its position. The tests for these two circuits are completely different, and applying the simple-circuit test to the module-controlled circuit produces meaningless results.

The disciplined technician reads the diagram, identifies the power source, the switch (or input), the controlling module (if any), the load (motor, lamp, solenoid), the ground, and the communication path (if any). The diagram reveals whether the circuit is switched-power-to-load (older), module-driven (modern), or network-commanded (newest), and the test strategy follows from the architecture. The trap is the technician who assumes all window circuits, all lighting circuits, all wiper circuits are the same, applies a generic test, and replaces the switch or the motor when the actual fault is in the module, the network command, or a power or ground that is not where the technician assumed it was. Read the diagram first; the test follows from the architecture.

### Test at the Load First, Then Work Backward

For a non-functioning load (a motor that does not run, a lamp that does not light, a solenoid that does not click), the disciplined test sequence starts at the load and works backward toward the power source and the control. With the load connector accessible (back-probed or disconnected), activate the circuit and check for power and ground at the load: if both power and ground are present under load and the load does not function, the load is faulty (the motor, lamp, or solenoid). If power is missing, the fault is upstream (the wiring, the switch, the module driver, the fuse); if ground is missing, the fault is in the ground path (the wiring, the ground connection). This single test — power and ground at the load under activation — narrows the fault to the load or to the supply, and avoids the common trap of replacing the load when the actual fault is in the supply.

The trap is the technician who hears "the window does not work," removes the door panel, replaces the motor, and the window still does not work — because the actual fault was the switch, the wiring in the door hinge, the module driver, or a blown fuse, and the motor was fine. The disciplined technician tests for power and ground at the motor connector (with the connector plugged in and the switch activated, back-probing to test under load) before removing or replacing the motor. If power and ground are both present, the motor is condemned; if either is missing, the motor is good and the fault is upstream.

### Distinguish Switch Faults From Module and Network Faults

In a module-controlled body circuit, the switch is an input to the module, not the direct controller of the load, and a non-functioning load can be caused by the switch (the input never reaches the module), the module (the input arrives but the module does not command the output), the network (the command is sent but does not arrive at the output module), or the output driver and wiring (the command arrives but the driver or the wiring fails). The disciplined diagnosis tests each stage. The switch input is tested at the module connector: with the switch activated, the module should see the input (a voltage change, a resistance change, or a data message, depending on the switch type). If the module sees the input but does not command the output, the module or its programming is suspect; if the module commands the output (verified at the output driver or relay) but the load does not function, the fault is in the output wiring or the load.

For network-commanded loads (where the command travels over the CAN or LIN bus from one module to another), the disciplined technician uses the scan tool to command the output bidirectionally — if the load functions when commanded by the scan tool, the load and its output driver and wiring are good, and the fault is in the input (the switch or its module) or the network command. If the load does not function when commanded by the scan tool, the fault is in the output side (the driver, the wiring, the load). Bidirectional testing is one of the most powerful tools in body electrical diagnosis, because it isolates the output side from the input and command side in seconds. The trap is the technician who replaces the switch for a non-functioning load when the actual fault is the module, the network, or the output driver.

### Evaluate PWM and LIN-Controlled Components Correctly

Modern body electrical uses pulse-width modulation (PWM) for variable-speed loads (blower motors, cooling fans, fuel pumps) and dimmable loads (instrument lighting, some headlights), and the LIN bus for single-wire communication to sub-assemblies (door modules, seat modules, wiper motors). These components cannot be tested with a simple on-off test light or a DC voltmeter, because their control signals are digital or pulsed. A PWM-controlled blower motor receives a duty-cycle command (a square wave whose on-time varies from 0 to 100 percent), and the motor speed depends on the duty cycle; testing this circuit requires a scope (to see the PWM signal) or a scan tool (to command the duty cycle and watch the response). A test light on a PWM line will glow dimly or flicker and tells the technician nothing useful.

A LIN-controlled component (a door module, a smart wiper motor) communicates with its master module over a single wire, and the line carries bidirectional data at about 1–20 kilobits per second. A LIN fault can be a power or ground loss to the slave, a wiring fault on the LIN line, or a failed slave module — and the diagnosis is similar to CAN bus diagnosis (check power and ground to the slave, check the LIN signal with a scope, isolate by disconnecting). The trap is the technician who tests a PWM or LIN circuit with a test light or a DC meter, sees an unexpected reading, and condemns the component — when the actual signal is fine and the test tool is wrong for the circuit. The disciplined technician identifies PWM and LIN circuits from the wiring diagram and uses the correct tool (scope, scan tool) for the signal type.

### Inspect Sockets, Connectors, and Grounds for Body Electrical Faults

A large fraction of body electrical faults are not in the components or the wiring but in the connections: corroded lamp sockets (especially tail light and headlight sockets, which run hot and collect moisture), corroded or spread terminals in connectors (especially in door hinges, under the vehicle, and in areas exposed to water), and corroded or loose ground points (which cause a wide range of intermittent faults — dim lights, slow motors, false sensor readings, module resets). The disciplined technician inspects the sockets and connectors visually (for corrosion, heat damage, melted plastic, spread or pushed-back terminals) and tests the grounds with a voltage drop test under load, because a ground that reads zero ohms with no load can drop voltage under the load of a motor or a lamp.

The trap is the technician who replaces a bulb, a motor, or a module for a fault that is actually in the socket, the connector, or the ground — and the new component fails the same way (or immediately) because the connection fault was never addressed. A headlight socket that is heat-damaged will melt the connector on the new bulb; a corroded ground will cause the new motor to run slow or the new lamp to glow dim. The disciplined technician inspects and repairs the connections as part of the diagnosis, not as an afterthought, and applies dielectric grease to prevent future corrosion in repaired connections.

## Common Traps

### Replacing the Motor Without Testing Power and Ground at the Load — The technician gets a non-functioning power window or wiper, removes the panel, replaces the motor, and the load still does not function because the actual fault was the switch, the wiring, the module, or a fuse. The trap mechanism is that the load (motor) is the most visible and replaceable part, so it is replaced on assumption. The false signal is the motor not running. The harm is an unnecessary motor replacement, the real fault remains, and the customer returns. The disciplined technician tests for power and ground at the motor connector under load before replacing the motor.

### Testing a Module-Controlled Circuit Like a Simple Switch Circuit — The technician diagnoses a non-functioning power window on a modern vehicle by testing the switch and the motor with a test light, assuming a simple switch-to-motor circuit, and gets meaningless results because the circuit is BCM-controlled with a LIN door module. The trap mechanism is that the control strategy has changed but the test approach has not. The false signal is the test results not matching expectations. The harm is a misdiagnosis, unnecessary parts, and wasted time. The disciplined technician reads the wiring diagram to understand the control strategy before testing.

### Using a Test Light on a PWM or LIN Circuit — The technician tests a PWM-controlled blower motor or a LIN-controlled door module with a test light, sees a dim or flickering glow, and condemns the component — when the signal is fine and the test light is the wrong tool. The trap mechanism is that PWM and LIN signals are digital or pulsed, and a test light (designed for DC on-off) cannot interpret them. The false signal is the abnormal test light reading. The harm is condemning a good component and missing the real fault. The disciplined technician identifies PWM and LIN circuits and uses a scope or scan tool.

### Replacing the Bulb Without Inspecting the Socket — The technician replaces a failed headlight or tail light bulb, does not inspect the socket, and the new bulb fails quickly (or immediately) because the socket is corroded or heat-damaged. The trap mechanism is that the socket is part of the circuit, and a corroded or heat-damaged socket causes high resistance that overheats and fails the bulb. The false signal is the bulb being the obviously failed part. The harm is a repeat bulb failure, a customer return, and in some cases a melted connector or harness. The disciplined technician inspects and repairs the socket as part of the bulb replacement.

### Ignoring Corroded Grounds as a Cause of Body Electrical Faults — The technician chases intermittent body electrical faults (dim lights, slow motors, false readings) and replaces components, while the actual cause is a corroded ground point that drops voltage under load. The trap mechanism is that ground resistance causes voltage drops and intermittent faults that look like component failures. The false signal is the symptoms pointing at specific components. The harm is unnecessary component replacements, the faults continue, and the customer loses confidence. The disciplined technician voltage-drop tests the grounds in any body electrical diagnosis.

## Self-Check

- Did I read the wiring diagram to understand the control strategy (simple switch, module-driven, network-commanded, PWM, LIN) before testing?
- For a non-functioning load, did I test for power and ground at the load connector under activation (back-probing) before replacing the load?
- Did I distinguish a switch input fault (the module does not see the input) from a module fault (the input arrives but no output) from an output driver or wiring fault (the output is commanded but the load does not function)?
- Did I use the scan tool's bidirectional command to isolate the output side from the input and command side?
- For PWM and LIN circuits, did I use a scope or a scan tool rather than a test light or DC meter?
- Did I inspect lamp sockets, connectors, and terminals for corrosion, heat damage, and spread or pushed-back pins?
- Did I voltage-drop test the ground points for the affected circuit under load?
- Did I apply dielectric grease to repaired connections to prevent future corrosion?
- Did I check the fuses for the affected circuit, and recognize that a blown fuse indicates an overload that must be found, not just replaced?
- Did I confirm the repair by operating the circuit under all its conditions (all switch positions, all speeds, all modes) and verifying no related warning lights or codes?
