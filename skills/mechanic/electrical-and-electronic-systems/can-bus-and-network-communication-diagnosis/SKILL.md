# CAN Bus and Network Communication Diagnosis

Modern vehicles are networks of dozens of electronic control modules — the engine, transmission, ABS, body, lighting, climate, and infotainment modules — that communicate over a Controller Area Network (CAN) bus and other serial data protocols. When the network fails, the symptoms are unlike any other automotive fault: multiple unrelated warning lights illuminate simultaneously, modules cannot be reached by the scan tool, the vehicle may not start (because the immobilizer or the engine module cannot authenticate over the network), and the traditional component-by-component diagnostic approach fails completely because the fault is in the communication, not in any single component. The judgment problem is that network faults are invisible to ordinary electrical testing — a module that "does not communicate" can be failed, can be unpowered, can have a wiring fault, or can be silenced by a shorted module elsewhere on the bus dragging the whole network down. A technician who replaces the module that "does not communicate" without testing the network will be wrong most of the time, because the module is usually fine and the fault is in the wiring, the power, the ground, or another module. This skill covers the disciplined diagnosis of CAN bus and network faults: reading the network with a scope, measuring termination resistance, isolating shorted modules, and distinguishing module faults from network faults.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish a Network Fault From Individual Module Faults

The first diagnostic step when multiple warning lights are on or when the scan tool cannot communicate with one or more modules is to determine whether the fault is in the network (a bus wiring fault, a shorted module, a power or ground loss to the network) or in individual modules (separate, coincidental faults). The disciplined technician reads all the codes in all the modules (not just the engine module) and looks for the pattern: U-codes (network communication codes) in multiple modules indicate a network fault; isolated faults in a single module indicate a module-specific problem. A scan tool that cannot communicate with any module indicates a total network failure or a scan tool power or connection problem; a scan tool that communicates with some modules but not others indicates a partial network failure (a fault on one bus segment) or a fault in the unreachable module or its wiring.

The disciplined technician maps the vehicle's network architecture (which modules are on which bus — there are often multiple CAN buses: high-speed CAN for powertrain, medium-speed CAN for body, LIN for sub-assemblies, and a gateway module that bridges them) from the wiring diagram, and uses the pattern of reachable and unreachable modules to localize the fault to a bus segment. The trap is the technician who sees multiple warning lights, assumes "the computer is bad," and replaces the BCM or the gateway — when the actual cause is a shorted module on the bus that is dragging the network down, or a chafed wire shorting CAN high or CAN low to ground or power. The disciplined technician tests the network before replacing any module.

### Measure CAN Bus Voltage and Signal Quality With a Scope

The CAN bus consists of two wires — CAN high and CAN low — that carry differential signals, and the health of the bus is visible only on an oscilloscope. The disciplined technician connects a scope to CAN high and CAN low at the diagnostic connector (or at a module connector) and observes the signal. A healthy CAN bus at rest (no communication) shows CAN high at about 2.5 volts and CAN low at about 2.5 volts (the recessive state). During communication, CAN high rises to about 3.5 volts and CAN low drops to about 1.5 volts (the dominant state), producing the characteristic differential signal. A bus that is shorted CAN high to CAN low shows both lines at the same voltage (about 2.5 volts, no differential); a bus shorted CAN high to power shows CAN high at battery voltage; a bus shorted CAN low to ground shows CAN low at zero volts; an open bus shows a distorted or absent signal.

The scope pattern reveals the fault type and points to the cause: a short to power or ground, a short between the lines, or an open. The disciplined technician compares the observed pattern to the known-good pattern and uses the deviation to direct the isolation. The trap is the technician who uses only a scan tool (which can reach some modules and not others) and guesses at the fault, when a scope connected to the bus would reveal the exact nature of the fault in seconds. The scope is the essential tool for network diagnosis, and using it separates the network diagnostician from the parts-swapper.

### Check Termination Resistance to Find Opens and Wiring Faults

The CAN bus requires termination resistors (typically two 120-ohm resistors, one at each end of the bus, often integrated into the engine module and the ABS or gateway module) to prevent signal reflection and ensure reliable communication. The termination resistance is measured at the diagnostic connector with the vehicle off (modules powered down): with both resistors present and the bus intact, the resistance between CAN high and CAN low at the diagnostic connector should be about 60 ohms (the two 120-ohm resistors in parallel). A reading of 120 ohms indicates one resistor is missing (one end module is off the bus — powered down, failed, or its connector is unplugged); a reading of infinity (open) indicates a break in the bus wiring; a reading near zero indicates a short between CAN high and CAN low.

The disciplined technician uses the termination resistance measurement as a quick network health check and as a tool to localize opens. By unplugging modules along the bus and re-measuring, the technician can determine which segment of the bus is open and which modules are still connected. The trap is the technician who measures the resistance, sees an unexpected value, and replaces a module — when the actual cause is a broken wire, a corroded connector, or a module that is simply unplugged or unpowered. The termination resistance is a wiring and connectivity test, and an unexpected value points to the wiring and the connections, not necessarily to a module.

### Isolate Shorted Modules by Disconnecting Along the Bus

A shorted module — one that has failed internally and shorted CAN high to CAN low, or to power, or to ground — silences the entire bus segment it is on, because the short prevents any module on that segment from communicating. The disciplined isolation is to disconnect modules along the bus one at a time (at their connectors), re-checking the bus signal on the scope or the communication on the scan tool after each disconnection. When the shorted module is disconnected, the bus recovers and the remaining modules communicate. The shorted module is the one whose disconnection restores the bus.

The trap is the technician who, faced with a dead bus, starts replacing modules one at a time (the most expensive, or the one with a code) until the bus recovers — without realizing that disconnecting (not replacing) the modules would have found the fault at no cost. The disciplined technician disconnects to isolate, identifies the shorted module by the disconnection that restores the bus, and only then replaces that one module — after confirming the module is shorted (by measuring resistance between its CAN pins, or by checking that the bus recovers only when that module is off the bus). The discipline of disconnecting before replacing saves the customer the cost of unnecessary modules and confirms the fault before the replacement.

### Verify Power and Ground to Non-Communicating Modules

A module that does not communicate may be failed, but it is far more likely to be unpowered or ungrounded — and a module without power or ground cannot communicate, regardless of its internal health. The disciplined technician, before condemning a non-communicating module, checks its power and ground at the module connector with the connector plugged in (back-probing) and the ignition on. A module needs constant battery power (for keep-alive memory), switched ignition power (to operate), and a good ground — and the loss of any one of these causes a no-communication that looks exactly like a failed module. The power and ground are checked under load (with the module connected and drawing current), because a power feed that reads battery voltage with no load can drop to nothing when the module tries to draw current through a corroded fuse or a high-resistance connection.

The trap is the technician who finds a module that does not communicate, assumes the module is bad, and replaces it — when the actual cause is a blown fuse, a corroded power connector, or a broken ground strap. The disciplined technician always verifies power and ground (with a voltage drop test, under load) before condemning a module. A large fraction of "failed modules" are actually modules that are unpowered or ungrounded, and the replacement module will not communicate either, leading to a confused and expensive comeback.

## Common Traps

### Replacing the Module That Does Not Communicate — The technician finds a module that the scan tool cannot reach, assumes the module is failed, and replaces it — but the module was fine and the fault was in the network wiring, the power, the ground, or a shorted module elsewhere on the bus. The trap mechanism is that a non-communicating module looks like a failed module, but the cause is usually external to the module. The false signal is the scan tool's inability to reach the module. The harm is an expensive, unnecessary module replacement that does not fix the fault, and a customer who pays for a part they did not need. The disciplined technician tests the network, the power, and the ground before condemning the module.

### Assuming Multiple Warning Lights Mean a Failed Computer — The technician sees the ABS, check engine, airbag, and other lights all on, assumes "the computer is bad," and replaces the BCM or the gateway — when the actual cause is a network fault (a shorted module, a chafed bus wire) that is preventing the modules from communicating. The trap mechanism is that multiple simultaneous warning lights are the signature of a network fault, not of multiple separate module failures. The false signal is the lights looking like a computer failure. The harm is an unnecessary module replacement, the network fault remains, and the lights stay on. The disciplined technician reads the U-codes and tests the network.

### Diagnosing a Network Fault Without a Scope — The technician faces a network fault, uses only a scan tool (which can reach some modules and not others), and guesses at the cause — replacing modules or wiring on speculation. The trap mechanism is that the scan tool shows which modules are reachable but not why the unreachable ones are unreachable, and the bus signal (the actual electrical behavior) is visible only on a scope. The false signal is the partial communication pattern, which constrains but does not identify the fault. The harm is repeated, speculative replacements that do not fix the fault. The disciplined technician uses a scope to see the bus signal and identify the fault type.

### Ignoring Termination Resistance as a Quick Network Check — The technician faces a flaky or dead network and skips the simple termination resistance measurement at the diagnostic connector, going straight to module replacement or wiring tracing. The trap mechanism is that the termination resistance (60 ohms at the connector, vehicle off) is a five-second test that reveals opens, missing modules, and shorts, and skipping it wastes time on more complex tests. The false signal is the assumption that the resistance must be fine if some modules communicate. The harm is a delayed diagnosis and unnecessary work. The disciplined technician measures termination resistance as the first network test.

### Condemning a Module Without Checking Its Power and Ground — The technician finds a non-communicating module, replaces it, and the new module does not communicate either — because the actual cause was a blown fuse or a corroded ground, and the module was never powered. The trap mechanism is that a module without power or ground cannot communicate, and the no-communication looks identical to a failed module. The false signal is the scan tool's inability to reach the module. The harm is an unnecessary module replacement, the new module does not work either, and the customer has paid for a part that did not fix the problem. The disciplined technician verifies power and ground under load before condemning any module.

## Self-Check

- Did I read the codes in all modules (not just the engine) and look for U-codes indicating a network fault, rather than assuming multiple separate module failures?
- Did I map the vehicle's network architecture (which modules on which bus, the gateway) from the wiring diagram to localize the fault to a bus segment?
- Did I connect a scope to CAN high and CAN low at the diagnostic connector and observe the bus signal, rather than relying only on the scan tool?
- Did I measure the termination resistance (about 60 ohms at the connector, vehicle off) as a quick check for opens, missing modules, and shorts?
- For a dead bus, did I disconnect modules along the bus one at a time to isolate the shorted module, rather than replacing modules on speculation?
- For a non-communicating module, did I verify its power (constant and switched) and ground under load before condemning the module?
- Did I distinguish a shorted module (bus recovers when it is disconnected) from a module that is unpowered (bus is fine, module is silent)?
- Did I check for a chafed or pinched bus wire as a cause of intermittent or total network failure, especially after collision or other recent work?
- Did I confirm the repair by verifying all modules communicate and all U-codes are cleared after a road test and a key cycle?
- Did I document the network architecture, the fault, and the isolation procedure so the next technician can understand the diagnosis if the fault returns?
