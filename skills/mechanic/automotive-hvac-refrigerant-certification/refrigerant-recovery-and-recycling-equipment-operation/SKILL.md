# Refrigerant Recovery and Recycling Equipment Operation

Recovery, recycling, and reclamation are three distinct operations that the law and the equipment treat differently, and conflating them is the root of most equipment-related failures in mobile A/C service. Recovery is the extraction of refrigerant from a vehicle into a storage container; recycling is the on-site cleaning of that recovered refrigerant to a level acceptable for reuse in mobile A/C through oil separation, moisture removal, and filtration; reclamation is the off-site processing of contaminated or aged refrigerant back to the purity standard of new refrigerant, which only an EPA-certified reclaimer may perform. The judgment problem is that the recovery machine looks like a single appliance that does everything, so technicians tend to trust it to produce reusable refrigerant from any input, when in fact its recycled output is only as good as its last filter-drier change, its oil separator function, and the discipline with which the operator prevents cross-contamination. The technician must understand the equipment's internal sequence, must verify a deep vacuum with a micron gauge rather than a manifold gauge, and must keep the records that prove the machine was maintained and the refrigerant was tracked. Because contaminated or moisture-laden refrigerant destroys compressors across an entire customer base and creates EPA recordkeeping liability, any uncertainty about refrigerant purity, equipment maintenance status, or evacuation depth must be resolved by stopping, segregating the suspect material, and escalating to the shop's compliance records before recharging any vehicle. Operating this equipment correctly is the difference between a compliant shop that reuses refrigerant safely and a shop that contaminates its own inventory, damages compressors across its customer base, and cannot document its compliance to an inspector.

## Core Rules

### Understand the Difference Between Recovery, Recycling, and Reclamation

Recovery is the physical transfer of refrigerant out of the vehicle into the machine's internal or an external storage vessel, and it is the only operation that must occur before any sealed-system service. Recycling takes the recovered refrigerant and passes it through the machine's oil separator, filter-drier, and moisture-removal stages to bring it to the SAE standard purity for reuse in mobile A/C, typically J2788 for R-134a and J2843 for R-1234yf, but recycled refrigerant is not equivalent to new or reclaimed refrigerant and may not be sold. Reclamation is the off-site, laboratory-grade restoration of refrigerant to AHRI 700 specification purity, performed only by an EPA-certified reclaimer, and it is required for any refrigerant that is contaminated, mixed, or whose purity cannot be confirmed. The operator must know which of the three is happening at each step, because recharging a vehicle with refrigerant that needed reclamation rather than recycling spreads contamination, and sending off-spec refrigerant for reuse rather than reclamation violates the purity requirements.

### Recover Fully Before Opening the System

Before any component is removed, any line is opened, or any compressor is replaced, the full refrigerant charge must be recovered into the machine until the system pressure reaches the level specified by the equipment manufacturer, typically a vacuum or near-zero reading held for a defined time to confirm no residual refrigerant remains. The recovery is not complete when the gauge reads zero momentarily; it is complete when the system holds a low pressure for the specified dwell, because liquid refrigerant trapped in the oil or in a low point continues to vaporize and re-pressurize the system. The technician must monitor the recovery until the machine indicates completion, and must not rely on the gauge alone, because a cold ambient or a restriction can produce a false completion signal. Recovering incompletely means venting the residual charge to atmosphere when the system is opened, which is a Section 609 violation.

### Separate and Account for Compressor Oil

As refrigerant is recovered, it carries compressor oil with it, and the machine's oil separator captures this oil in a graduated container that must be checked and emptied each cycle. The volume of oil separated is not arbitrary data; it indicates how much oil left the vehicle, and an equal quantity of new, refrigerant-compatible oil must be reinstalled during recharge to maintain the compressor's lubrication charge. Failing to measure and replace separated oil leaves the compressor under-lubricated and shortens its life, while over-adding oil reduces heat-transfer capacity and can cause hydraulic locking. The separated oil is a contaminated waste stream that must be collected and disposed of per hazardous-material rules, not returned to the system or mixed with fresh oil. Record the oil volume removed and the oil volume added on the repair order so the lubrication balance is documented.

### Achieve and Verify a Deep Vacuum

After recovery and repair, the system must be evacuated with a vacuum pump to remove both non-condensable air and moisture before recharge, and the depth of that vacuum must be verified with a micron gauge, not inferred from a manifold gauge reading. A manifold gauge that reads 30 inches of mercury is not proof of a deep vacuum; it only proves the gauge has reached its mechanical limit, while hundreds of microns of moisture can remain in the system. The target is a vacuum below 1000 microns, ideally 500 microns or lower, held stable, with the system isolated from the pump and the micron reading observed to rise no more than a few hundred microns over several minutes, which confirms both that moisture has boiled off and that the system is leak-free. A system that cannot hold vacuum has a leak and must not be recharged until the leak is found. Skipping the micron gauge or shortening the evacuation is the most common cause of premature compressor failure from moisture-driven acid formation.

### Maintain the Filter-Drier and the Machine

The recovery machine's filter-drier is the consumable that removes moisture and acid from the recycled refrigerant, and it has a finite capacity measured by the weight of refrigerant processed or by a moisture indicator that changes color. The filter-drier must be changed on the manufacturer's schedule or when the indicator signals saturation, and the change must be recorded with the date and the refrigerant weight processed; an exhausted filter-drier passes moisture through to the recycled output and contaminates every subsequent recharge. The machine's oil separator, internal seals, and pump also require scheduled service, and a machine that is out of calibration may under-recover, leaving charge in the vehicle, or may fail to reach the vacuum required for proper recycling. Treat the machine as calibrated equipment: log every service, post the maintenance schedule, and remove the machine from service if it is overdue.

### Prevent Cross-Contamination Relentlessly

Cross-contamination is the single most expensive failure in recovery equipment operation, because one contaminated batch can spread through the shop's entire recycled inventory and into multiple customer vehicles. Prevent it by dedicating each machine, each hose set, and each storage cylinder to a single refrigerant, by labeling all equipment permanently, and by never transferring refrigerant between cylinders of different types or between a machine and a cylinder of uncertain provenance. Before recovering from any vehicle, confirm the refrigerant type from the underhood label and, on R-1234yf machines, run the integrated refrigerant identifier to detect hydrocarbon or R-134a contamination introduced elsewhere. Any refrigerant of unknown or mixed composition must be segregated in a clearly marked contaminated cylinder and sent for reclamation or destruction, never recharged. A shop that allows even one cross-use event has compromised its recycled inventory and must flush or replace affected pathways before resuming service.

### Keep Records That an Inspector Can Read

Section 609 and the equipment standards require that the shop maintain records of refrigerant processed, filter-drier and oil changes, equipment certification, and the disposition of any refrigerant sent to a reclaimer, and these records must be retained for at least three years and produced on EPA demand. The records should be contemporaneous, entered at the time of service rather than reconstructed later, and they should reconcile against the cylinder weights on the bench so that an inspector can trace each gram of refrigerant from vehicle to cylinder to reclaimer. Recordkeeping that cannot be reconciled is treated as a violation even when the underlying service was compliant, because the EPA's enforcement model relies on the paper trail. Design the log to capture technician, vehicle, refrigerant type, weights recovered and recharged, oil removed and added, filter-drier changes, and reclaimer shipments, and audit it periodically.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Recycling With Reclamation

The trap is assuming that the recovery machine's recycled output is equivalent to new refrigerant and can be sold, transferred, or used in any vehicle without question. The mechanism is that the machine processes the recovered gas through its filter-drier and produces a clean-looking product that measures correctly on a scale. The false signal is that the recycled refrigerant cools the test vehicle acceptably. The harm is that recycled refrigerant is not certified to AHRI 700 purity, may contain residual moisture or acid below the machine's detection limit, and may not be sold or used outside the specific mobile A/C context for which recycling is defined, so selling or broadly reusing it violates the purity and recordkeeping rules. Reserve recycled refrigerant for in-house mobile A/C recharge, and send anything off-spec or uncertain to a certified reclaimer.

### Declaring Evacuation Complete From a Manifold Gauge

The trap is ending the evacuation when the manifold gauge needle bottoms at 30 inches of mercury, on the belief that this reading indicates a deep vacuum. The mechanism is that the manifold gauge has a mechanical floor and cannot resolve the difference between a few thousand and a few hundred microns, so it reports success while moisture remains. The false signal is a pegged low-side gauge that looks definitive. The harm is residual moisture that reacts with the refrigerant and oil to form acids, destroying the new compressor within months and producing a warranty failure traced back to the incomplete evacuation. Always use a micron gauge connected directly to the system, pull to below 1000 microns, and verify the system holds vacuum when isolated from the pump.

### Ignoring the Filter-Drier Change Interval

The trap is continuing to process refrigerant through a machine whose filter-drier is exhausted because the indicator has not been checked or the interval has not been tracked. The mechanism is that the drier saturates silently, and once it is spent, moisture and acid pass through into the recycled reservoir and into every recharged vehicle. The false signal is that the machine runs and recovers normally with no alarm. The harm is systemic contamination of the shop's recycled inventory and accelerated compressor failures across the customer base, plus the cost of recalling or warrantying those vehicles. Track the refrigerant weight processed, change the drier on schedule or when the indicator changes, and record every change with its date.

### Recharging Without Measuring and Replacing Removed Oil

The trap is recovering the charge, performing the repair, and recharging by weight without accounting for the oil that the separator captured. The mechanism is that the oil volume is small and easy to overlook, and the system cools acceptably on the test drive. The false signal is normal pressures and vent temperature immediately after recharge. The harm is a compressor running below its lubrication charge, which fails prematurely and often after the warranty window, and which the customer attributes to the shop's repair. Measure the separated oil at every cycle, add the equivalent volume of new oil, document both, and dispose of the contaminated oil as hazardous waste.

### Trusting the Recovery Is Done When the Gauge Hits Zero

The trap is disconnecting the recovery machine the moment the low-side gauge reads zero, assuming the system is empty. The mechanism is that liquid refrigerant trapped in the compressor oil or in a low point continues to vaporize, re-pressurizing the system after the machine is removed, and when the system is opened that residual charge vents. The false signal is a momentary zero reading that looks complete. The harm is an incomplete recovery that becomes an illegal venting when the line is cracked, plus the safety risk of a spray of refrigerant and oil. Run the recovery to the machine's completion indicator and confirm the system holds a low pressure for the specified dwell before opening it.

### Mixing Refrigerants Through a Shared Cylinder

The trap is using a recovery or storage cylinder that previously held a different refrigerant, or topping off a cylinder without confirming its prior contents, because the cylinder looks empty or the fitting fits. The mechanism is that residual refrigerant and oil remain in any "empty" cylinder, and adding a different refrigerant creates a mix that is neither pure nor usable. The false signal is that the cylinder accepts the charge and the scale reads correctly. The harm is a contaminated batch that cannot be recharged or sold, that must be sent for costly reclamation or destruction, and that may already have been partially recharged into a vehicle before the contamination is discovered. Dedicate and permanently label each cylinder to one refrigerant, evacuate and verify before first use, and never repurpose a cylinder between types.

## Self-Check

- Did I recover the full charge and confirm the system held a low pressure for the specified dwell before opening any line or component?
- Did I distinguish clearly between recovery, recycling, and reclamation, and send any contaminated, mixed, or off-spec refrigerant to an EPA-certified reclaimer rather than reusing it?
- Did I measure the separated compressor oil, add the equivalent volume of new refrigerant-compatible oil, document both, and dispose of the waste oil properly?
- Did I evacuate with a vacuum pump and verify a deep vacuum below 1000 microns with a micron gauge, then confirm the system held vacuum when isolated?
- Did I change the filter-drier on the manufacturer's schedule or when the moisture indicator signaled, and record the change with the date and refrigerant weight processed?
- Did I dedicate each machine, hose set, and cylinder to a single refrigerant, label them permanently, and run the refrigerant identifier on R-1234yf before recovering?
- Did I segregate any refrigerant of unknown or mixed composition in a clearly marked contaminated cylinder for reclamation rather than recharging it?
- Did I record technician, vehicle, refrigerant type, weights recovered and recharged, oil removed and added, filter-drier changes, and reclaimer shipments, in a log that reconciles against bench cylinder weights and is retained for at least three years?
