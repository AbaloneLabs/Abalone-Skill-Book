# Hybrid Auxiliary Systems and DC-DC Converter Service

Hybrids and electric vehicles do not have an alternator. The 12-volt electrical system — which still runs the lights, the body modules, the starter relay (on hybrids), the infotainment, and all the conventional loads — is powered by a DC-DC converter that steps high-voltage DC from the traction battery down to 12-14 V. The same HV bus also feeds the electric air-conditioning compressor, the electric water pumps, and the PTC cabin heater. The judgment problem is that a failure in any of these auxiliary systems can mimic a high-voltage battery or inverter failure, can strand the vehicle (a dead 12 V system can prevent the HV contactors from closing, so the car will not start even with a full traction battery), and can be misdiagnosed because the symptoms overlap with conventional vehicle faults. This skill covers the disciplined diagnosis of the DC-DC converter and HV-fed auxiliary systems, with emphasis on distinguishing 12 V system faults from HV system faults and the safety rules that govern work on HV-fed components.

## Core Rules

### Understand That the 12 V System Gates the HV System

On most hybrids and EVs, the 12 V battery powers the controllers that close the high-voltage contactors. If the 12 V battery is dead or the DC-DC converter has failed and the 12 V battery has discharged, the vehicle will not "start" — the contactors will not close, the HV bus will not energize, and the vehicle is stranded, even though the traction battery is fully charged. This is the most important concept in hybrid auxiliary diagnosis: a 12 V system failure can present as a no-start that looks like an HV failure. The disciplined technician, facing a no-start or "ready" mode that will not engage, checks the 12 V battery and the DC-DC converter output before assuming an HV battery or inverter fault. A jump-start of the 12 V system (at the designated jump points, never directly at the small 12 V battery in some EVs) often brings the vehicle to "ready" and reveals that the HV system is fine.

### Diagnose the DC-DC Converter as You Would an Alternator, With HV Cautions

The DC-DC converter is the EV equivalent of the alternator: it maintains the 12 V battery and powers the 12 V loads while the vehicle is in "ready" mode. Diagnose it similarly: with the vehicle in ready (HV system active), measure the 12 V bus voltage at the battery and at the converter output; it should be in the regulated range (typically 13-14.5 V, varying with temperature and load as the converter follows a charge curve). A voltage that does not rise above 12 V in ready indicates the converter is not producing output. Then load the 12 V system (lights, blower, heated seats) and confirm the converter holds voltage under load. A converter that produces voltage at idle but sags under load is failing. The HV caution: the converter's input is high-voltage from the traction battery; do not probe the HV input, and follow the disable procedure if the converter must be removed or its HV connector accessed.

The disciplined technician distinguishes a converter fault from a 12 V battery fault (load-test the 12 V battery first), from a converter control or command fault (the converter may be commanded off by the HV controller for a legitimate reason, such as an isolation fault or an HV shutdown — read the codes), and from a wiring fault between the converter and the 12 V battery (voltage-drop the output cable).

### Do Not Condemn the DC-DC Converter for a Fault That Is Commanding It Off

The HV controller can command the DC-DC converter to shut down or derate for reasons that are not converter faults: an isolation fault elsewhere on the HV bus, an HV battery fault, an inverter fault, or an overtemperature of the converter caused by a cooling fault. The converter's DTC may report "output disabled" or "no output," but the cause is the upstream condition. The disciplined technician reads the full HV and converter code set and the live data (converter command status, HV bus status, isolation status) before condemning the converter. Replacing a converter that was correctly shut down by the controller for an isolation fault fixes nothing and the new converter is shut down the same way.

### Diagnose the Electric A/C Compressor Through Its HV Feed and Control Signals

The electric A/C compressor on hybrids and EVs is driven directly by the high-voltage bus (not by an engine belt), and it is controlled by the climate ECU via a serial data line. A no-A/C complaint can be caused by: an HV-side fault (no HV to the compressor, an isolation fault at the compressor), a control-side fault (the climate ECU is not commanding the compressor, a refrigerant-pressure or temperature input is inhibiting command), or a compressor internal fault (motor or inverter within the compressor). The disciplined technician reads the climate and HV codes, checks the command status and the refrigerant-pressure/temperature inputs in live data, and verifies HV presence at the compressor (with the HV safety procedure followed) before condemning the compressor. A compressor that is not commanded on is not failed; a compressor that is commanded and has HV but does not run is failed.

### Service Electric Coolant Pumps and Cooling Loops With the Right Coolant and Air-Bleed Procedures

Hybrids and EVs use electric coolant pumps for the HV battery, the inverter/motor, and often the cabin heater loop. These pumps are commanded by controllers based on temperature, and they can fail electrically (no command, no operation) or mechanically (impeller wear, airlock). A pump that is airlocked runs but does not move coolant, causing the component it serves to overheat. The disciplined technician verifies pump command and operation (often audible, or visible in live data as pump RPM or current), checks for airlocks by following the manufacturer's bleed procedure (some loops self-bleed, others require specific fill and vent steps), and uses only the specified non-conductive coolant in loops that contact HV components (inverter, battery, motor) — never standard conductive green coolant, which can cause isolation faults.

### Recognize PTC and Heat-Pump Heater Faults and Their Effect on Range and Comfort

EVs and many hybrids heat the cabin with a PTC electric heater (a resistive element in the coolant or air path) or, on newer EVs, a heat pump. A PTC heater fault produces no cabin heat and, because the heater draws significant HV power, may set an HV-side code. A heat-pump fault may produce no heat in cold conditions or no A/C in warm conditions. The disciplined technician distinguishes a heater fault (no heat, but the rest of the climate system works) from a coolant-circulation fault (no heat because no coolant flows through the heater core) and from a control fault (the heater is not commanded because a temperature sensor is wrong). PTC and heat-pump components are HV-fed; follow the disable procedure before accessing them.

### Treat the 12 V Battery Itself as a Primary Suspect

The 12 V battery on hybrids and EVs is smaller than a conventional starting battery (because it does not crank an engine, except on some hybrids with a conventional starter), but it is still subject to aging, internal shorts, and parasitic discharge. Many hybrid/EV no-starts and "weird electrical" complaints are simply a degraded 12 V battery. The disciplined technician load-tests or conductance-tests the 12 V battery early in any electrical or no-start diagnosis, because a weak 12 V battery can cause modules to reset, contactors to chatter, and the vehicle to refuse "ready" mode — symptoms that look like complex HV faults but are a $200 battery.

## Common Traps

### Condemning the HV Battery for a No-Start That Is a 12 V System Failure — The vehicle will not enter "ready" mode, the dash shows an HV system warning, and the technician assumes the traction battery has failed. The trap mechanism is that the HV contactors require a healthy 12 V supply to close; a dead or weak 12 V battery (or a failed DC-DC converter that has let it discharge) prevents the contactors from closing, so the HV bus never energizes and the vehicle reports an HV system fault. The false signal is the HV warning and the refusal to enter ready mode. The harm is that the technician quotes a traction battery replacement — the most expensive repair on the vehicle — when the actual cause is a dead 12 V battery or a failed DC-DC converter. The disciplined technician checks and load-tests the 12 V battery and verifies DC-DC converter output before any HV battery diagnosis.

### Condemning the DC-DC Converter That the Controller Has Correctly Shut Down — A DTC reports "DC-DC converter no output," so the technician replaces the converter. The trap mechanism is that the HV controller commands the converter off when an isolation fault, an HV battery fault, or an inverter fault has caused an HV shutdown; the converter reports "no output" because it was told to produce none, not because it has failed. The false signal is the converter DTC. The harm is that a good converter is replaced, the actual HV fault remains, and the new converter is shut down the same way on the next start. The disciplined technician reads the full code set and the converter command status in live data to determine whether the converter is faulty or correctly commanded off.

### Using Standard Conductive Coolant in an HV Cooling Loop and Creating an Isolation Fault — The inverter or battery coolant loop is low, so the technician tops it off with standard green glycol coolant. The trap mechanism is that standard coolant is electrically conductive due to its silicate and ionic-inhibitor package; the HV cooling loops have live HV components (inverter cold plates, battery cooling plates) in contact with the coolant, and conductive coolant creates a leakage path that trips an isolation fault and shuts down the HV system. The false signal is that the coolant level is now correct and the loop "looks fine." The harm is an isolation fault that appears after the top-off, leading to a misdiagnosis of inverter, battery, or HV cable failure. The disciplined technician uses only the specified non-conductive coolant in any loop that contacts HV components.

### Condemning the Electric A/C Compressor That Is Not Being Commanded On — The A/C does not work, the technician assumes the electric compressor has failed, and replaces it. The trap mechanism is that the compressor runs only when the climate ECU commands it, and command depends on refrigerant pressure, evaporator temperature, cabin setpoint, and HV system status; a low refrigerant charge, a faulty pressure sensor, or a climate-control input fault can prevent command, and the compressor is healthy. The false signal is "no A/C, must be the compressor." The harm is an unnecessary compressor replacement (an expensive HV-fed component), while the actual cause — a low charge or a sensor — remains and the new compressor is still not commanded on. The disciplined technician reads the climate codes and live data (compressor command, refrigerant pressure and temperature, HVAC inputs) before condemning the compressor.

### Replacing the Electric Coolant Pump for an Airlock or a Command Fault — The inverter or battery overheats, a pump DTC sets, and the technician replaces the pump. The trap mechanism is that the pump may be running but moving no coolant because the loop is airlocked (common after coolant service or a leak), or the pump may not be commanded on because its controller sees no temperature demand or a fault elsewhere. The false signal is a pump DTC or an overheating component. The harm is an unnecessary pump replacement while the actual cause — an airlock or a missing command — remains, and the new pump also moves no coolant or is not commanded on. The disciplined technician verifies pump command and operation in live data and performs the specified coolant bleed procedure before condemning the pump.

## Self-Check

- For a no-start or refusal to enter "ready" mode, did I check and load-test the 12 V battery and verify DC-DC converter output before diagnosing the HV battery or inverter?
- Did I diagnose the DC-DC converter as I would an alternator (output voltage in ready, under load), while following HV cautions on its input?
- Before condemning the DC-DC converter, did I read the full code set and the converter command status to confirm it is faulty, not correctly shut down by the controller?
- For a no-A/C complaint, did I read the climate codes and live data (compressor command, refrigerant pressure/temperature) before condemning the HV-fed electric compressor?
- For an overheating inverter or battery, did I verify coolant pump command and operation and perform the coolant bleed procedure before condemning the pump?
- Did I use only the manufacturer-specified non-conductive coolant in any loop that contacts HV components, and avoid standard conductive coolant?
- For a no-heat complaint, did I distinguish a PTC/heat-pump fault from a coolant-circulation fault and from a control/sensor fault?
- Did I follow the HV disable-and-verify procedure before accessing any HV-fed auxiliary component (DC-DC converter, compressor, PTC heater, pump)?
