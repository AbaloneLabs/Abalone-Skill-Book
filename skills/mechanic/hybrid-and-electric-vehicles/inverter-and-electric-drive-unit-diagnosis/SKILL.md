# Inverter and Electric Drive Unit Diagnosis

The inverter and electric drive unit (EDU — the traction motor and reduction gearset) are the core power path of every hybrid and electric vehicle, and they are also the most expensive assemblies on the vehicle. A misdiagnosis here costs the customer thousands of dollars and may condemn a repairable component, or worse, returns a vehicle with a latent insulation or overcurrent fault that fails catastrophically. The judgment problem is that inverter and motor faults produce symptoms — no-drive, shudder, reduced power, warning lamps — that overlap with high-voltage battery faults, DC-DC converter faults, and even conventional driveline faults, and the inverter's own self-diagnostics are rich (current, temperature, resolver, insulation) but only useful when read carefully. This skill covers the disciplined diagnosis of inverter and EDU faults, with emphasis on isolating the failed assembly, interpreting the HV system's diagnostic data, and the safety and procedural rules that govern work on these assemblies.

## Core Rules

### Disable and Verify the High-Voltage System Before Any Hands-On Work

Before touching the inverter, motor, or any orange high-voltage cable, disable the HV system per the manufacturer's procedure: remove the HV service disconnect (the "plug" or "key"), wait the specified time for the capacitors in the inverter to discharge (often 10+ minutes), and verify with a CAT III-rated multimeter that the HV bus reads 0 V at the inverter input. This is non-negotiable: the inverter's DC bus capacitors hold lethal charge after the system is "off," and the bus itself is at 200-400 V (or higher) when live. The disciplined technician treats every orange cable and every inverter terminal as potentially lethal until proven de-energized, wears the specified high-voltage gloves (inspected for holes), and never bypasses the interlock. Diagnosis that involves connector access, resistance checks, or insulation testing all require the system disabled and verified dead first.

### Read the Full HV Diagnostic Data Set Before Condemning an Assembly

Inverter and motor faults are encoded in a rich set of data: DTCs for overcurrent, overtemperature, phase imbalance, resolver/rotor-position faults, and isolation faults; live data for inverter temperature, motor temperature, phase current, resolver angle, commanded versus actual torque, and HV bus voltage. Before condemning the inverter or motor, read the full data set and interpret the pattern:

- **Overcurrent or phase imbalance codes** — point to a shorted or open phase in the motor, a failed inverter phase (IGBT), or a poor HV connection at a phase cable.
- **Overtemperature codes** — point to a cooling fault (the inverter is on the same coolant loop as the motor and often the HV battery; low coolant, a failed pump, a clogged cold plate, or air in the loop), not necessarily an internal inverter fault.
- **Resolver/rotor-position faults** — point to the resolver (the motor's position sensor), its wiring, or its air gap; the inverter cannot commutate the motor without valid position data.
- **Isolation faults** — a loss of electrical isolation between the HV bus and chassis, which could be in the inverter, the motor, a cable, the battery, or the compressor/charger; requires systematic isolation testing.

The disciplined technician reads the codes and the live data, forms a hypothesis about which assembly is at fault, and tests that hypothesis before recommending a multi-thousand-dollar assembly replacement.

### Distinguish Inverter and Motor Faults From HV Battery and DC-DC Faults

A no-drive or reduced-power complaint can originate in the HV battery (which cannot deliver current), the DC-DC converter (which fails to keep the 12 V system alive, causing modules to drop out), the inverter (which cannot convert DC to AC), or the motor (which cannot produce torque). The diagnostic data separates them: if the HV bus voltage sags badly under load, the battery is the suspect; if the 12 V bus drops, the DC-DC converter is the suspect; if the HV bus is stable but phase current or torque is abnormal, the inverter or motor is the suspect. The disciplined technician does not condemn the inverter for a no-drive that is actually a battery sag or a DC-DC shutdown — read the bus voltages and the current data under the conditions of the complaint before naming the assembly.

### Diagnose Cooling System Faults Before Condemning the Inverter for Overtemperature

The inverter and motor are liquid-cooled, and the majority of "inverter overtemperature" faults are caused by cooling-system problems, not internal inverter failures. The coolant loop must be checked for: correct level, correct coolant (the specific non-conductive coolant specified — never generic green coolant, which is electrically conductive and can cause isolation faults), pump operation, flow (no airlocks, no clogged cold plates), and thermostat/valve operation. A vehicle that goes into reduced power after a few minutes of driving and logs an inverter overtemperature code often has a dead coolant pump, an airlocked loop, or a degraded coolant — all repairable without replacing the inverter. The disciplined technician diagnoses and repairs the cooling system first, clears the code, and re-tests before condemning the inverter.

### Perform Insulation Resistance Testing to Localize Isolation Faults

An isolation fault (the HV bus is no longer electrically isolated from chassis ground by the specified resistance, typically megohms) is a safety-critical condition that the HV controller detects and responds to by shutting down or limiting the system. The fault could be in the inverter, the motor windings, any HV cable, the battery, the electric A/C compressor, or the onboard charger. The disciplined localization procedure is: disable and verify the HV system, disconnect the suspect assemblies one at a time from the bus, and use a megohmmeter (insulation tester) to measure insulation resistance to chassis for each assembly. The assembly whose disconnection restores isolation (or whose measurement reads low) is the fault. Guessing at the cause — "isolation fault, replace the inverter" — often condemns a good inverter when the fault is a contaminated cable or a compressor.

### Evaluate Resolver and Position-Sensor Data Carefully

The resolver (or rotor position sensor) tells the inverter where the motor's rotor is so it can commutate correctly. A resolver fault produces symptoms that mimic a motor failure: shudder, no-start, reduced power, abnormal noise. The diagnostic data shows resolver angle, resolver excitation, and signal amplitude; a fault may be the resolver itself, its drive circuit in the inverter, the wiring between them, or the air gap (affected by bearing wear in the motor). The disciplined technician checks resolver data and wiring before condemning the motor, because a resolver fault is often repairable (sensor or wiring) while a motor replacement is expensive.

### Inspect Reduction Gearset and Bearings Separately From the Motor

The electric drive unit combines the motor and a reduction gearset (and sometimes a differential) in one housing. A noise complaint attributed to "the motor" may be a bearing, a gearset, or a differential problem. The disciplined technician classifies the noise by its correlation (motor-speed-related, vehicle-speed-related, load-dependent) and, with the HV system disabled, may rotate the motor by hand or perform a controlled no-load motor run (per service procedure) to separate motor noise from gearset noise. Condemning the entire EDU for a bearing or gearset noise that is serviceable separately wastes the customer's money.

## Common Traps

### Condemning the Inverter for an Overtemperature Code Caused by a Cooling Fault — The inverter logs an overtemperature code and the vehicle goes into reduced power, so the technician quotes an inverter replacement. The trap mechanism is that the inverter is reporting a real temperature, but the cause is upstream in the cooling loop — a dead electric coolant pump, an airlocked cold plate, degraded electrically-non-conductive coolant, or a stuck coolant valve — not an internal inverter fault. The false signal is the inverter overtemperature DTC, which names the inverter. The harm is that the customer pays for an inverter that was not failed, the cooling fault remains, and the new inverter overheats the same way on the first hard drive. The disciplined technician diagnoses the cooling loop (level, pump operation, flow, coolant type, air) and re-tests before condemning the inverter.

### Replacing the Motor for a Resolver or Position-Sensor Fault — The vehicle shudders or will not start, a "motor position" or resolver code is set, and the technician replaces the traction motor. The trap mechanism is that the resolver is a separate sensor (with its own wiring and an inverter-side drive circuit) that reports rotor position; a fault in the sensor, its connector, its wiring, or the inverter's resolver circuit produces the same symptoms as a failed motor, but only the motor is the expensive assembly. The false signal is a code that references motor position. The harm is an unnecessary motor replacement at major cost, while the actual fault — a chafed resolver wire, a corroded connector, or a failed sensor — remains and reproduces the symptom with the new motor. The disciplined technician checks resolver data, wiring, and the inverter-side circuit before condemning the motor.

### Treating an Isolation Fault as an Inverter Fault Without Localizing — An isolation-fault DTC sets and the technician replaces the inverter. The trap mechanism is that the isolation fault is a loss of HV-to-chassis resistance somewhere on the bus, and the inverter is only one of many possible locations — the motor windings, any orange cable, the battery, the electric A/C compressor, and the onboard charger are all suspects. The false signal is that the inverter is a prominent HV component and "probably" the cause. The harm is that a good inverter is replaced, the isolation fault remains (because the actual cause is a contaminated cable or a compressor), and the customer pays for a misdiagnosis. The disciplined technician performs systematic insulation-resistance testing, disconnecting assemblies one at a time, to localize the fault before replacing anything.

### Using the Wrong (Electrically Conductive) Coolant and Creating an Isolation Fault — The inverter coolant loop is low, so the technician tops it off with standard automotive (green) glycol coolant. The trap mechanism is that standard coolant contains silicates and ionic inhibitors that make it electrically conductive; the inverter and motor cold plates have live HV components in contact with the coolant path, and conductive coolant creates a leakage path from the HV bus to the chassis, tripping an isolation fault. The false signal is that the coolant "looks fine" and the level is correct. The harm is an isolation fault that appears after the top-off, leading to a diagnosis of inverter or battery failure when the actual cause is the wrong coolant. The disciplined technician uses only the manufacturer-specified non-conductive coolant (often a specific G-series or a dedicated EV coolant) and never mixes standard coolant into the HV cooling loop.

### Condemning the Entire Drive Unit for a Serviceable Bearing or Gearset Noise — A grinding or whining noise comes from the drive unit, and the technician quotes a complete EDU replacement. The trap mechanism is that the EDU houses the motor, a reduction gearset, bearings, and often a differential; a noise from a worn bearing, a pitted gear, or a failing differential is serviceable or replaceable separately on many designs, without replacing the motor. The false signal is that the noise "is from the drive unit," treated as a monolithic failure. The harm is that the customer pays for a complete drive unit when a bearing or gearset service would have resolved the noise at a fraction of the cost. The disciplined technician classifies the noise by correlation and isolates the source within the unit before condemning the whole assembly.

## Self-Check

- Before any hands-on work on the inverter, motor, or HV cables, did I disable the HV system, wait for capacitor discharge, and verify 0 V with a CAT III meter?
- Did I read the full HV diagnostic data set (DTCs plus live data: temperatures, phase current, resolver angle, bus voltage, commanded vs actual torque) before condemning an assembly?
- Did I distinguish inverter/motor faults from HV battery sag and DC-DC converter shutdown by reading bus voltages and current under the complaint conditions?
- For an overtemperature code, did I diagnose the cooling loop (level, pump, flow, coolant type, air) and re-test before condemning the inverter?
- Did I use only the manufacturer-specified non-conductive coolant in the HV cooling loop, and avoid standard conductive coolant?
- For an isolation fault, did I perform systematic insulation-resistance testing to localize the fault before replacing the inverter or any assembly?
- For a resolver or position-sensor code, did I check the sensor, its wiring, and the inverter-side circuit before condemning the motor?
- For a noise complaint, did I classify the noise by correlation and isolate the source within the drive unit (bearing, gearset, differential) before quoting a complete EDU replacement?
