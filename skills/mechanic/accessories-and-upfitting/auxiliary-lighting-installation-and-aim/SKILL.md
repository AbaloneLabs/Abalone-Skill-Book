# Auxiliary Lighting Installation and Aim

Auxiliary lighting — driving lights, fog lights, off-road light bars, work lights — extends a vehicle's visibility beyond what the factory lighting provides, and it is one of the most common and most poorly executed upfits. The judgment problem is that auxiliary lighting has two failure modes that both create real harm: the obvious failure is an unsafe installation (undersized wire, no relay, no fuse) that creates fire risk; the subtle and more common failure is an incorrectly selected, aimed, or legal-status installation that blinds oncoming drivers, interferes with the driver's own vision, or violates lighting regulations. A light bar mounted at windshield height and aimed at the horizon lights up the road brilliantly for the driver and blinds every oncoming driver; fog lights aimed up become glare lights; off-road lights used on the road are illegal. A technician who installs lights that "work" without considering beam pattern, aim, and legal use will hand the customer a setup that is dangerous to others and a liability. This skill covers the installation and aim discipline that makes auxiliary lighting effective and responsible.

## Core Rules

### Select the Light Type and Beam Pattern for the Use Case

Auxiliary lights are not interchangeable; each type is engineered for a specific purpose and a specific beam pattern. Driving lights (often called pencil or spot beam) project a long, narrow, intense beam to extend high-beam range on open road; they are for use with (not instead of) high beams and are illegal to use with oncoming traffic. Fog lights project a wide, low, flat beam with a sharp cutoff to illuminate under fog and rain without reflecting back at the driver; they mount low and aim low. Flood or scene lights project a wide, diffuse pattern for illuminating a work area around a stationary vehicle. Off-road light bars combine patterns for maximum area illumination and are for off-road use only; on-road use is illegal in most jurisdictions because they blind other drivers.

The disciplined approach is to determine the use case (extended high-beam range for rural driving; fog penetration; work area illumination; off-road visibility) and select the light type and beam pattern for that use, not the brightest or most impressive-looking option. Cautions: a customer who wants "more light" may request a light bar when what they need for legal road use is a set of driving lights with a controlled beam; advise on the correct type for the intended and legal use. And mixing beam patterns in a single installation (a spot and a flood on the same switch) is sometimes appropriate (spot down the road, flood to the sides) but must be aimed accordingly; a mismatched installation wastes the lights' capabilities. Match the light to the job.

### Mount Lights in the Correct Position for Their Type

Light position is determined by the light's function and by regulation. Fog lights mount low (typically below the headlights, near bumper height) because their wide, flat beam must project under the fog, and a high-mounted fog light reflects fog back at the driver. Driving lights mount at or near headlight height for a beam that extends down the road. Off-road lights mount where they illuminate the terrain, often high (roof, windshield pillar) for maximum spread, but high-mounted lights used on-road blind oncoming drivers and are illegal. Work lights mount to illuminate the work area (the sides, rear, or bed of a work truck).

The disciplined approach is to mount each light in the position appropriate to its type and the vehicle, following any regulatory position requirements (height limits, lateral position, restrictions on certain mounting locations), and to ensure the mount is structurally sound (the light vibrates and the mount fatigues if flimsy) and does not obstruct required lighting, the driver's view, sensors, or air flow. Cautions: a roof- or windshield-mounted light bar, even if aimed down, creates glare off the hood for the driver and a large frontal area that affects aerodynamics and can catch overhead obstacles; advise on the tradeoffs. And lights must not obstruct or interfere with ADAS sensors (radar in the grille, camera at the windshield) — a light bar in front of the adaptive cruise radar disables the system, and the customer must be informed. Position is a function and regulatory decision, not just a mounting-convenience decision.

### Aim Auxiliary Lights to Their Function and to Avoid Glare

Aiming is where most auxiliary light installations fail the public. Driving lights must be aimed to extend the high-beam pattern without scattering light into oncoming drivers' eyes — typically aimed slightly down and to the right (for left-hand traffic) from level, with the hot spot landing at a controlled distance. Fog lights must be aimed low and flat, with a sharp cutoff below the driver's eye level and oncoming drivers' eye level, because any light above the cutoff becomes glare in fog and to oncoming traffic. Off-road lights, when used off-road, are aimed for terrain coverage; but if they are aimed for on-road use (or used on-road), they blind oncoming drivers regardless of aim because their output is simply too high.

The disciplined approach is to aim each light type to its specification: driving lights to a defined hot-spot position (using a wall at a set distance, per the light manufacturer's or a standard aiming procedure), fog lights to a low, flat cutoff, and work lights to cover the work area. Cautions: do not aim any on-road auxiliary light at or above the horizon — a light aimed level or up scatters into oncoming drivers' eyes and into the driver's own eyes via reflections, and it is the aiming, not just the brightness, that causes glare. And verify the aim at night, loaded (the vehicle's stance changes with load, changing the aim), because a daytime aim on an unloaded vehicle points higher when the vehicle squats under load. Aim is a safety obligation to other drivers, not a preference.

### Wire With Relay, Fuse, and Correct Switching for Legal Operation

Auxiliary lights draw significant current (often 10-20 amps per pair, more for high-output LED bars) and must be wired with a relay (the switch handles only coil current), a fuse at the battery, and correctly sized wire. The switching must also reflect the legal use: driving lights must be wired to operate only with the high beams (so they turn off when the driver switches to low beams for oncoming traffic), and fog lights must be wired to operate only with the low beams (so they are not used with high beams, which defeats their fog purpose). Off-road lights should be switched independently but must not be operable on-road — some jurisdictions require them to be covered on-road.

The disciplined approach is to wire the relay coil trigger from the correct factory beam circuit (high beam for driving lights, low beam for fog lights) so the auxiliary lights operate only in their legal combination, in addition to the driver's on/off switch, and to fuse and relay the power circuit per electrical load management practice. Cautions: wiring driving lights to operate anytime (independent of high beams) means the driver can leave them on with low beams and blind oncoming traffic — the legal and safe setup is high-beam-interlocked. And tapping the trigger from the wrong circuit (a module-controlled beam circuit that may not provide clean voltage) can cause relay chatter or failure; use the correct trigger source, often the headlight switch or relay output, verified with a meter. The wiring must make correct operation easy and incorrect operation difficult.

### Verify Legal Compliance and Inform the Customer

Auxiliary lighting is regulated: the number, type, color, height, and use of auxiliary lights are specified by state and federal regulation, and non-compliant installations are subject to citation and to liability if they contribute to an accident (a light that blinded another driver). Off-road lights used on-road, fog lights used with high beams, white lights facing rearward, flashing or colored lights reserved for emergency vehicles — these are common violations that an upfitter must not install for on-road use without informing the customer of the legal restriction.

The disciplined approach is to know the applicable lighting regulations (state law for the jurisdiction, federal standards for new vehicle equipment), install compliant equipment for the intended on-road use, and clearly inform the customer when equipment is for off-road use only and must be covered or switched off on-road. Cautions: "the customer asked for it" is not a defense for installing illegal on-road lighting; the upfitter shares liability for a non-compliant installation. Document the intended use and the legal restrictions in the work order, and ensure the customer understands them. An installation that blinds other drivers or violates lighting law is a harm to the public and a liability to the shop and the customer, regardless of what was requested.

## Common Traps

### The Windshield Light Bar Aimed at the Horizon — The customer wants maximum light, so a large light bar is mounted at the windshield line and aimed down the road. The trap is that such a light, used on-road, blinds every oncoming driver with output far exceeding any headlight, and the glare reflects off signs and the vehicle's own hood back into the driver's eyes, reducing the driver's vision. The false signal is the brilliant illumination; the harm is a hazard to oncoming traffic (and the driver's own vision) and a near-certain citation if used on-road. Aim and position auxiliary lights to control glare, and inform the customer that high-mounted bars are for off-road use only.

### Fog Lights Aimed Up as Glare Lights — The fog lights are installed and aimed level or slightly up "for more light," and they blind oncoming drivers in all conditions. The trap is that fog lights aimed above their low, flat cutoff become pure glare projectors — they add no useful seeing distance (their wide, short beam is already at maximum useful range when aimed correctly) and they scatter light into oncoming eyes and into the driver's own eyes in fog. The false signal is that the road "looks brighter"; the harm is glare to others and back-scatter to the driver in fog. Aim fog lights low and flat, with a sharp cutoff; aiming them up defeats their purpose and creates a hazard.

### Driving Lights Wired to Operate Anytime — The driving lights are wired through a switch only, independent of the high beams, so the driver can use them with low beams. The trap is that driving lights are extremely bright and are designed to extend high-beam range; using them with low beams (when oncoming traffic is present) blinds oncoming drivers, which is exactly the situation the high-beam interlock exists to prevent. The false signal is the convenience of independent control; the harm is glare to oncoming traffic and a liability if it contributes to an accident. Wire driving lights to operate only with the high beams, so they switch off automatically when the driver dips for traffic.

### Obstructing ADAS Sensors With the Light Mount — The light bar is mounted in the grille, directly in front of the adaptive cruise control radar or the front camera, and the system now faults or disables. The trap is that ADAS sensors have specific fields of view that cannot be obstructed, and a light or bracket in the field blocks or reflects the sensor, causing the system to fail — sometimes silently (the feature is just unavailable) and sometimes with a fault that illuminates a warning. The false signal is that the light "fits"; the harm is a disabled safety system and a customer who does not know their adaptive cruise or automatic braking no longer works. Check sensor locations before mounting any front-mounted accessory, and do not obstruct them.

### Assuming Brighter Is Better Without Regard to Beam Control — The customer selects the highest-output lights available, and the technician installs them without considering beam pattern or aim. The trap is that raw output without beam control scatters light everywhere — into oncoming eyes, into trees, into the driver's eyes via reflection — and adds glare without adding useful seeing distance, because useful distance comes from a controlled hot spot, not from total lumens. The false signal is the lumen rating; the harm is glare, legal non-compliance, and poor actual visibility despite high output. Select lights for beam pattern and control appropriate to the use, and aim them correctly; output is useful only when directed.

## Self-Check

- Did I select the light type and beam pattern (driving, fog, flood, off-road) for the customer's actual use case, not just the brightest option?
- Did I mount each light in the position appropriate to its type and to any regulatory position requirements?
- Did I ensure the mount is structurally sound and does not obstruct required lighting, the driver's view, or ADAS sensors?
- Did I check ADAS sensor locations (radar, camera) before mounting any front accessory?
- Did I aim driving lights to a controlled hot spot slightly down and right, fog lights to a low flat cutoff, and work lights to the work area?
- Did I aim and verify at night, with the vehicle loaded, because stance changes with load?
- Did I wire driving lights to operate only with high beams, and fog lights only with low beams, so they cannot be used in the wrong combination?
- Did I use a relay, a source fuse, and correctly sized wire for the light current?
- Did I verify the installation complies with applicable state and federal lighting regulations for the intended use?
- For off-road-only equipment, did I inform the customer of the on-road legal restriction and document it in the work order?
