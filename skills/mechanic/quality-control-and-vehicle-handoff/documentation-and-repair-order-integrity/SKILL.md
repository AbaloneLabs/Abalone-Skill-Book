# Documentation and Repair Order Integrity

The repair order is the legal, financial, and historical record of the service performed, and its accuracy determines whether the shop gets paid, whether the warranty claim is honored, and whether the shop can defend itself in a dispute. The judgment problem is that documentation is performed under time pressure, the technician would rather be turning wrenches than writing, and the temptation to abbreviate or generalize is constant. Yet a repair order that says "fixed brake noise" without the complaint, the diagnosis, the parts, the labor, and the verification is worthless when the customer returns with a complaint, when the manufacturer audits a warranty claim, or when an attorney subpoenas the records. Documentation must be treated as part of the repair, not as paperwork after the repair, because a repair that is not documented is a repair that cannot be proven to have been performed correctly.

## Core Rules

### The Three C's: Complaint, Cause, Correction

The industry-standard documentation structure is the Three C's: Complaint (the customer's verbatim concern), Cause (the diagnostic finding that explains the complaint), and Correction (the action taken to resolve the cause). The Complaint must be the customer's words, not the advisor's interpretation, because interpretation loses the nuance that drives the diagnosis ("noise over bumps" is different from "noise when braking"). The Cause must be the specific diagnostic finding—the failed component, the measurement, the code with its freeze frame—not a guess or a generalization, because the cause is what justifies the correction and what the warranty claim depends on. The Correction must be the specific action taken—the part replaced, the adjustment made, the calibration performed—with enough detail that another technician could verify the work. The Three C's structure forces the diagnostic discipline that prevents comebacks and that makes the repair order defensible. A repair order that captures all three C's with specificity is a professional document; one that captures only "replaced X" is a liability.

### Recording Diagnostic Findings and Measurements

Diagnostic findings—the measurements, the test results, the observed data—are the evidence that supports the cause, and they must be recorded, not just performed. Record the actual readings: the fuel pressure (e.g., "35 psi at idle, spec 30-40"), the brake rotor thickness (e.g., "26.2 mm, discard spec 24.0 mm"), the wheel speed sensor resistance (e.g., "1.2 kOhms, spec 0.9-1.5"), the scan tool data (e.g., "STFT +25 percent at idle, returns to +2 percent at 2500 RPM"), and the code with its freeze frame. These numbers prove the diagnosis was data-driven, they provide a baseline for future service, and they are essential for warranty claims, which increasingly require diagnostic documentation. A statement like "found bad sensor" is not documentation; "found O2 sensor B1S1 switching at 0.5 Hz, spec 3-5 Hz, voltage stuck at 0.45V, replaced sensor and verified 4 Hz switching at 2500 RPM" is documentation. The discipline of recording findings also improves the diagnosis, because writing the numbers forces the technician to confirm they are real and within spec.

### Parts, Labor, and Pricing Documentation

Every part installed must be documented with the part number, the description, and the source (OEM, aftermarket, remanufactured), because the part identity affects the warranty, the price, and any future failure analysis. Labor operations must be documented with the operation code or description and the time, because the labor time drives the price and the technician's pay, and because the warranty claim requires the correct operation code. Pricing must be transparent and match the estimate, with any variance explained and approved by the customer before the work proceeds (see the estimating skill). Core charges and returns (old parts, batteries, catalytic converters) must be documented because they affect the price and the environmental compliance records. Sublet work—work sent to another shop, such as machine shop work or specialized diagnostics—must be documented with the sublet vendor and the work order. The parts and labor documentation is the basis for the invoice, the warranty claim, and any dispute resolution; an undocumented part or labor operation is a gap that a customer or a manufacturer can exploit.

### Warranty and Manufacturer Claim Documentation

Warranty claims—both the manufacturer's new-vehicle warranty and the shop's or part supplier's repair warranty—require specific documentation that must be captured at the time of the repair, not reconstructed later. For manufacturer warranty, the repair order must include the customer's complaint, the verified concern, the diagnostic steps (often with the specific test and the result), the part number replaced, the labor operation code, and the cause code (the manufacturer's classification of the failure). Many manufacturers require a diagnostic attachment—a photo, a scan tool data screenshot, or a test result—and claims without the required attachment are denied. For shop or part warranty (the warranty on a repair the shop performed), the documentation must support that the failure is covered (within the warranty period and the warranty terms) and that the failure is the same as the original repair. A repair order that lacks the original complaint, the diagnosis, or the parts cannot support a warranty claim, and the shop eats the recheck. Always document as if the claim will be audited, because increasingly it will be.

### Service History and Maintenance Records

The service history—the cumulative record of all maintenance and repair performed on the vehicle—is valuable to the customer (for resale value and for tracking maintenance), to the shop (for diagnosing recurring issues and for identifying related prior repairs), and to the manufacturer (for warranty validation, because some warranty coverage requires proof of maintenance). Every repair order must be retained for the legally required period (often 3-7 years, varying by jurisdiction and by type), and must be retrievable by VIN and by customer. The service history also protects the shop: if a customer claims the shop caused a failure, the history shows whether the failure relates to a prior repair or whether it is unrelated. For maintenance, document the mileage, the date, the fluid or part, and the next service interval, because the maintenance record is the proof the customer maintained the vehicle per the schedule, which preserves the warranty and the resale value. A shop with meticulous service history is a shop that retains customers and defends itself effectively.

### Digital Documentation, Photos, and Telematics

Modern documentation increasingly includes digital evidence: photos of the vehicle at intake (for damage documentation), photos of the failed part (for warranty and customer communication), scan tool screenshots (for diagnostic evidence), and telematics or remote diagnostic data (for vehicles with connected services). Photos should be clear, well-lit, and framed to show the relevant detail, and they should be attached to the repair order, not left on a phone. Scan tool screenshots should capture the relevant PID, the code, and the freeze frame, with a timestamp if possible. Telematics data—vehicle health reports, remote diagnostic sessions—must be saved and attached, because the data may not be retrievable later. Digital documentation is powerful evidence in disputes and warranty claims, and it is increasingly expected by manufacturers and customers. Treat digital evidence with the same discipline as written documentation: capture it at the time, attach it to the repair order, and retain it for the required period.

## Common Traps

### Generalizing the Complaint Instead of Using the Customer's Words

The most common documentation trap is paraphrasing or generalizing the customer's complaint rather than recording it verbatim. The mechanism is that the advisor or technician interprets the complaint into their own words, losing the specificity that drives the diagnosis—"noise over bumps" becomes "suspension noise," and the technician diagnoses the wrong component because the complaint was generalized. The false signal is that the generalization captures the "intent" of the complaint. The harm is a misdiagnosis, a comeback, and a repair order that does not match the customer's actual concern, which weakens the shop's position in a dispute. Always record the customer's complaint in their own words, with their specific conditions (speed, temperature, surface, load).

### Documenting the Cause Without Supporting Measurements

A second trap is documenting the cause ("found bad sensor") without the measurement or test result that supports it. The mechanism is that the technician performed the test but did not write down the reading, relying on memory, and the repair order states a conclusion without evidence. The false signal is that the conclusion is correct, so the evidence seems unnecessary. The harm is a warranty denial (the manufacturer requires the evidence), a dispute loss (the shop cannot prove the diagnosis), and a lost diagnostic baseline for future service. Always record the actual measurement, the spec range, and the test method, not just the conclusion.

### Omitting Labor Operations or Part Numbers

A third trap is omitting a labor operation code or a part number from the repair order, either to save time or because the operation seemed minor. The mechanism is that the operation or part was performed but not billed or not recorded, creating a gap in the documentation. The false signal is that the operation or part was minor and does not matter. The harm is an inaccurate invoice (the customer is under-billed or the technician is under-paid), a warranty denial (the operation or part is required for the claim), and a service history gap that complicates future diagnosis. Always document every operation and every part, regardless of size, with the correct code and number.

### Failing to Capture Diagnostic Evidence for Warranty

A fourth trap is not capturing the diagnostic evidence (photos, scan tool screenshots, test results) that the warranty claim requires, then trying to reconstruct it after the fact. The mechanism is that the technician performs the diagnosis, replaces the part, and submits the claim, but the manufacturer denies it for lack of the required attachment, and by then the vehicle is gone and the evidence cannot be recreated. The false signal is that the claim was submitted on time and correctly coded. The harm is a denied claim that the shop must absorb, a chargeback, and a hit to the shop's warranty compliance score. Always capture and attach the required diagnostic evidence at the time of the repair, per the manufacturer's claim requirements.

### Discarding Old Parts Without Documentation

A fifth trap is discarding the old part—the failed component that was replaced—without documenting it or offering it to the customer, especially for warranty or core-return parts. The mechanism is that the old part goes into the scrap or core bin immediately, and when the customer asks to see it or the warranty requires its return, it is gone. The false signal is that the part was replaced and is no longer needed. The harm is a customer who suspects the part was not actually replaced (a trust failure), a warranty denial that requires the old part, or a lost core value. Always retain the old part until the customer has seen it and the warranty period has passed, document the part number and condition, and offer it to the customer at handoff.

## Self-Check

- Did I record the customer's complaint in their own words with specific conditions, not a generalization?
- Did I document the cause with the specific diagnostic finding, including measurements and test results?
- Did I document the correction with the part number, the labor operation, and the verification?
- Did I record actual readings (pressure, thickness, resistance, scan data) with the spec range for comparison?
- Did I document every part and labor operation, including minor ones, with correct codes and numbers?
- Did I capture and attach the diagnostic evidence (photos, screenshots, test results) required for warranty?
- Did I retain the old part for customer inspection and warranty return until the period has passed?
- Did I record the mileage, date, and next service interval for maintenance operations?
- Did I document any customer-approved variance from the estimate before the work proceeded?
- Did I retain the repair order for the legally required period, retrievable by VIN and customer?
