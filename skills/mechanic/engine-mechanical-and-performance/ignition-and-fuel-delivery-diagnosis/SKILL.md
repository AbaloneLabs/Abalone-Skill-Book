# Ignition and Fuel Delivery Diagnosis

Misfire, no-start, hesitation, and stall are among the most common engine performance complaints, and they are also among the most misdiagnosed because the same symptoms can be caused by the ignition system, the fuel system, or the air delivery system — and the technician who replaces parts across all three systems without isolating the fault will waste the customer's money and often fail to fix the problem. The judgment problem is that the engine needs compression, correct air-fuel mixture, and a properly timed spark to fire each cylinder, and a fault in any of these produces the same symptom: a misfire. The disciplined diagnosis isolates which of the three is missing on the affected cylinder(s), and then narrows within that system to the specific component, using tests that can fail rather than parts-swaps that can only succeed by luck. This skill covers the disciplined approach to ignition and fuel delivery diagnosis: testing spark, fuel pressure, injector operation, and using the scan tool data to pinpoint the system at fault.

## Core Rules

### Isolate the Misfiring Cylinder Before Guessing the System

A misfire code (P0300 series) names the cylinder or cylinders that are misfiring, but it does not name the cause — and the cause can be ignition, fuel, compression, or a mechanical fault in that cylinder. The first step is to confirm which cylinder(s) are misfiring, using the misfire counter on the scan tool (which shows the misfire rate per cylinder) or by the power balance test (shorting out each cylinder in sequence and watching the RPM drop — a cylinder that does not drop RPM when shorted is not contributing). Once the misfiring cylinder is identified, the disciplined technician moves the suspected component to another cylinder to see if the misfire follows: swap the ignition coil from the misfiring cylinder to a known-good cylinder, and if the misfire moves with the coil, the coil is the fault; if the misfire stays, the coil is good and the fault is elsewhere.

The same swap logic applies to fuel injectors (swap and see if the misfire follows) and to spark plugs. This component-swapping is legitimate diagnosis (not parts-swapping) because it is a controlled test with a pass/fail outcome: if the misfire follows the component, the component is proven faulty; if it does not, the component is proven good. The trap is the technician who pulls a P0301 (cylinder one misfire), assumes the coil, and replaces it without confirming — when the cause could be a fouled plug, a clogged injector, a vacuum leak affecting that cylinder's runner, or low compression. Isolate the cylinder, then isolate the system, then isolate the component, in that order.

### Test Spark Quality, Not Just Spark Presence

A spark that jumps a gap in the air at atmospheric pressure may still fail to fire the mixture under the much higher pressure of the combustion chamber, where the resistance to the spark is far greater. The disciplined ignition test evaluates spark quality (the ability of the coil to produce a hot, blue spark that can jump a wide gap under compression), not just spark presence (whether any spark appears at all). An adjustable spark tester (with a gap that can be set to a quarter inch or more) tests whether the coil can produce a spark capable of firing under compression; a weak, yellow, or intermittent spark at a wide gap indicates a coil, a module, or a primary-side fault that a simple in-line spark tester (with a small fixed gap) would miss.

The disciplined technician also evaluates the spark plug itself: the gap (an over-wide gap from wear stresses the coil and causes misfire under load), the condition (fouling from oil, carbon, or fuel indicates a separate engine condition; a cracked insulator causes a misfire that tracks down the side of the plug; a melted or eroded electrode indicates detonation or an over-advanced condition). The plug is the most direct indicator of what is happening in the cylinder, and reading it (heat range, deposits, color, wear pattern) is a diagnostic skill that the scan tool cannot replace. The trap is the technician who replaces the coil because "there is no spark," when the actual cause is a fouled plug that is shorting the spark to ground through the deposits — replacing the coil on a fouled plug produces a temporary fix at best.

### Measure Fuel Pressure and Volume, Not Just Pressure

Fuel delivery requires both adequate pressure and adequate volume (flow), and measuring only pressure misses volume faults that cause lean conditions under load. A fuel pressure gauge connected to the fuel rail Schrader valve (or the appropriate test port) measures the static pressure, which should be within specification (varies widely by system: port-injected systems typically 35–60 psi, direct-injected systems hundreds to thousands of psi, throttle-body systems lower). But a pump that produces correct static pressure may still fail to deliver adequate volume under load — a restricted filter, a weak pump, or a restricted pickup screen reduces flow, and the pressure drops when the injectors demand more fuel than the restricted system can supply.

The disciplined technician performs a fuel volume test (disconnecting the fuel line and measuring the volume delivered in a timed interval, typically a pint in fifteen to thirty seconds, depending on specification) alongside the pressure test, and performs a pressure-drop test (commanding the pump on, watching the pressure rise, then watching how fast it bleeds down after the pump stops — a fast bleed-down indicates a leaking injector, a faulty check valve in the pump, or a faulty pressure regulator). The pressure, the volume, and the hold pattern together characterize the fuel delivery system. The trap is the technician who reads the static pressure, sees it in spec, and concludes the fuel system is good — missing a volume fault that causes a lean misfire or a stall under load.

### Evaluate Injector Operation Electrically and Mechanically

A fuel injector can fail electrically (the coil opens or shorts, and the injector does not open), mechanically (the pintle sticks open or closed, or the nozzle is clogged), or in balance (one injector flows less than the others, causing a lean condition in that cylinder). The disciplined diagnosis tests each mode. Electrical testing uses a noid light (a test lamp that flashes when the injector is pulsed by the PCM, confirming the injector driver and the pulse are present) or a scope (which shows the injector current and voltage waveform, revealing a shorted coil, an open coil, or a driver fault). Resistance measurement with an ohmmeter (comparing all injectors, which should be within a tight range) finds an open or shorted coil.

Mechanical and balance testing uses the scan tool's injector balance function (which commands each injector to pulse a set number of times while watching the short-term fuel trim or the exhaust oxygen response — a cylinder that does not lean out when its injector is disabled is not getting fuel) or a pressure-drop balance test (commanding each injector to pulse while watching the fuel pressure — each injector should produce a similar pressure drop, and one that drops much less is clogged or restricted). The trap is the technician who assumes all misfires are ignition, ignores the fuel injectors, and replaces coils and plugs repeatedly while the real cause is a clogged injector. The disciplined technician tests the injectors electrically and for balance before concluding the fuel system is good.

### Distinguish Air and Vacuum Faults From Ignition and Fuel

A vacuum leak or an air delivery fault causes a lean misfire that looks like an ignition or fuel problem but is neither. An unmetered air leak (a cracked vacuum hose, a leaking intake manifold gasket, a failed PCV, a brake booster leak) allows air to enter the engine without being measured by the MAF sensor, leaning out the mixture and causing a misfire (often most prominent at idle, where the leak is a larger fraction of the total airflow, and improving at higher RPM). The disciplined technician tests for vacuum leaks with a smoke test (the most reliable method, pressurizing the intake and watching for smoke at the leak), with a propane enrichment test (briefly adding propane around the suspected leak and watching for an RPM change), or with a scan tool reading of fuel trim (high positive trim at idle that improves at RPM indicates a vacuum leak).

The trap is the technician who reads a lean code or a misfire, assumes a bad oxygen sensor or MAF sensor or fuel pump, and replaces those — while the actual cause is a vacuum leak that is leaning out the mixture. The disciplined technician checks for vacuum leaks (with smoke or propane) and checks the air delivery (a dirty MAF sensor that underreports airflow, a clogged air filter, a restricted intake) before replacing ignition or fuel components. The pattern of the fault (idle-only versus load-only, single cylinder versus multiple cylinders, lean trim versus rich trim) constrains the system at fault, and the disciplined technician uses that pattern to direct the testing.

## Common Traps

### Replacing the Coil for a Misfire Without Confirming It Follows the Cylinder — The technician pulls a cylinder-specific misfire code, assumes the coil, and replaces it — but the misfire was caused by a fouled plug, a clogged injector, or a vacuum leak, and the misfire persists. The trap mechanism is that the coil is the most common misfire cause and the easiest to replace, so it is replaced on assumption. The false signal is the misfire code naming a cylinder that has a coil. The harm is an unnecessary coil, the real cause remains, and the customer returns with the same misfire. The disciplined technician swaps the coil to another cylinder and confirms the misfire follows before replacing.

### Concluding the Fuel System Is Good Based on Static Pressure — The technician measures fuel pressure at idle, sees it in spec, and declares the fuel system good — but the pump cannot deliver adequate volume under load, causing a lean misfire or stall on the highway. The trap mechanism is that static pressure does not reveal volume or flow limitations that appear only under demand. The false signal is the in-spec pressure reading. The harm is a missed fuel delivery fault, a vehicle that stalls under load, and a customer who is told the fuel system is fine. The disciplined technician performs a volume test and a pressure-drop test alongside the static pressure.

### Ignoring the Spark Plug Condition When Replacing the Coil — The technician replaces a coil for a misfire, does not remove and inspect the plug, and the misfire returns because the plug was oil-fouled or carbon-fouled and was shorting the spark. The trap mechanism is that a fouled plug can defeat a new coil, and the plug condition also indicates an underlying engine condition (oil consumption, rich mixture) that must be addressed. The false signal is the new coil appearing to be the fix. The harm is a repeat misfire, a ruined new coil in some cases, and a missed engine condition. The disciplined technician always inspects and reads the plug when diagnosing a misfire.

### Diagnosing a Lean Code as a Bad Oxygen Sensor — The technician pulls a lean code (P0171), reads "system too lean," sees the oxygen sensor in the code name, and replaces the sensor — but the sensor was correctly reporting a lean condition caused by a vacuum leak, and the new sensor reports the same lean condition. The trap mechanism is that the code names the monitored condition and the sensor that detects it, not the failed component. The false signal is the sensor name in the code. The harm is an unnecessary sensor, the lean condition remains, and the customer returns with the same code. The disciplined technician reads the fuel trim and tests for vacuum leaks before condemning the sensor.

### Assuming All Misfires Are Ignition and Missing Fuel Injector Faults — The technician diagnoses a misfire, swaps coils and plugs, sees no change, and concludes the engine has a mechanical fault — missing that the fuel injector on that cylinder is clogged or electrically failed. The trap mechanism is that ignition is the most common misfire cause, so fuel injectors are overlooked, and the injector fault produces the same symptom as an ignition fault. The false signal is the misfire persisting after coil and plug replacement, which feels like it rules out ignition and points to mechanical. The harm is an unnecessary engine mechanical diagnosis or even engine work, when the real cause is a clogged injector that a balance test would have found. The disciplined technician tests injector operation and balance before concluding the fault is mechanical.

## Self-Check

- Did I isolate the misfiring cylinder using the misfire counter or a power balance test before guessing the system?
- Did I swap the suspected component (coil, injector, plug) to another cylinder to confirm the misfire follows before replacing it?
- Did I test spark quality with an adjustable spark tester (wide gap) rather than just spark presence with a small in-line tester?
- Did I remove and read the spark plug for the misfiring cylinder, checking gap, fouling, cracking, and electrode condition?
- Did I measure fuel pressure, fuel volume, and pressure hold (bleed-down) to fully characterize the fuel delivery system?
- Did I test the fuel injectors electrically (noid light, scope, resistance) and for balance (scan tool balance or pressure-drop test)?
- Did I check for vacuum leaks with a smoke test or propane enrichment before condemning ignition or fuel components?
- Did I read the fuel trim pattern (idle versus RPM, single bank versus both) to constrain the system at fault?
- Did I distinguish a vacuum leak (improves with RPM) from a fuel delivery fault (worsens with load) from a MAF underreport (worsens with load) from an ignition fault (often load-dependent but cylinder-specific)?
- Did I confirm the repair with a road test under the conditions that triggered the misfire, and verify the misfire counter is zero and the fuel trim is back in range?
