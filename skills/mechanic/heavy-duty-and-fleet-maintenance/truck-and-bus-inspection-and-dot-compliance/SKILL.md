# Truck and Bus Inspection and DOT Compliance

Commercial vehicle inspection is not the same as passenger-vehicle inspection, and the stakes are different. A passenger car with a worn brake pad is a maintenance item; a commercial truck or bus with an out-of-service (OOS) condition is a vehicle that cannot legally move, that exposes the operator to fines and the carrier to compliance penalties, and that — if it moves — endangers the public and the driver. The judgment problem is that DOT inspection standards are specific, documented, and unforgiving: a brake chamber with insufficient pushrod travel, a tire below the minimum tread depth, a lighting circuit failure, a suspension component crack — each is a defined OOS condition, and "it still works" is not a defense. A technician who inspects a commercial vehicle to passenger-car standards, or who judges "is it safe enough" by feel rather than by the regulation, will pass vehicles that fail roadside inspection and will miss conditions that cause accidents. This skill covers the inspection discipline that keeps commercial vehicles legal, safe, and on the road.

## Core Rules

### Inspect to the Regulatory Standard, Not to "Looks Okay"

The North American Standard Inspection and the federal annual inspection (49 CFR Part 396) define specific pass/fail criteria for every major system: brakes (pushrod travel, adjustment, chamber size, air leak rates), tires (minimum tread depth by axle position, regrooving rules, matching on duals), wheels and rims (cracks, rim damage, fastener torque), steering (free play limits, component wear), suspension (spring, U-bolt, bushing, and torque arm conditions), lighting (all required lamps functioning), frame and body (cracks, corrosion, securement), and emergency equipment. These are measurable, documented criteria — not subjective judgments.

The disciplined approach is to inspect each item against the specific regulatory criterion, using the required tools (a brake stroke gauge or drum-mounted indicator, a tread depth gauge, a torque wrench), and to document the measurement, not just "pass." Cautions: do not substitute visual judgment for measurement on items that have numeric criteria — a brake that "looks adjusted" can be over the stroke limit, and a tire that "has tread" can be below the minimum depth at a wear bar. And know which conditions are OOS (vehicle cannot move until repaired) versus which are "defects" that must be repaired but do not ground the vehicle — the distinction matters legally and operationally, and getting it wrong either strands a vehicle unnecessarily or releases one illegally.

### Measure Brake Stroke on Every Chamber, Every Inspection

Brake adjustment is the most common OOS condition at roadside inspection, and it is the condition most directly tied to commercial vehicle accident causation. Air brake chambers have a maximum legal pushrod stroke; when adjustment drifts and the stroke exceeds the limit, the brake cannot apply full force, and the vehicle's stopping distance increases — dramatically if multiple brakes are out. The stroke limit depends on chamber type and size, and must be measured with the system at full pressure and the brakes applied, using a stroke gauge or marked pushrod.

The disciplined approach is to measure every brake chamber's stroke at every inspection, compare to the limit for that specific chamber (look up the chamber size from the markings, do not assume), and flag any chamber at or near the limit for adjustment or repair. Cautions: a chamber that is at the limit today will be over it soon — adjust or repair it, do not pass it because it is "barely within." And automatic slack adjusters (ASAs) that are out of adjustment are not "fixed" by manually adjusting them — an ASA that is out of adjustment has a fault (often a worn ASA or a foundation brake problem), and manually cranking it back masks the fault and it will be out again within miles. Diagnose why the ASA is not self-adjusting; do not just crank it.

### Check Tires Against the Specific Axle and Position Requirements

Tire requirements for commercial vehicles are position-specific. Steer axle tires have a minimum tread depth (typically 4/32 inch across the major grooves) that is stricter than drive and trailer positions (typically 2/32 inch). Regrooved tires are permitted on some positions and prohibited on others (never on steer). Tires must be matched within a dual pair (same size, ideally same tread depth and wear pattern) or the larger tire carries the load and the smaller one spins and wears, and the mismatch can cause the dual to overheat and fail. Recap (retread) tires are prohibited on the steer axle of trucks and buses.

The disciplined approach is to measure tread depth at the wear bars on every tire, check for matching on duals, identify regrooves and recaps and verify they are permitted on the position, and inspect every tire for cuts, bulges, separation, and uneven wear that indicates an alignment or load problem. Cautions: a tire at the minimum depth is legal but has minimal wet traction and should be flagged for near-term replacement, not passed as "good." And uneven wear — cupping, feathering, one-side wear — is a diagnostic signal pointing to a suspension, alignment, or inflation problem that will destroy the next tire too; report the wear pattern, not just the depth.

### Verify Steering and Suspension Components Against Wear Limits

Steering and suspension wear on a commercial vehicle affects handling and, in failure, can cause loss of control at highway speed with a heavy load. The inspection criteria include free play in the steering wheel (a maximum before wear is indicated), wear in steering linkages (tie rod ends, drag link, pitman arm — checked for movement and for lubrication), kingpin wear (checked with the axle raised and a pry bar), spring and U-bolt condition (broken leaves, loose U-bolts that let the axle shift), bushing and torque arm wear, and any cracks in steering or suspension mounting.

The disciplined approach is to physically check each steering and suspension joint for play with the appropriate method (a pry bar at the joint, an assistant wiggling the wheel), and to flag any movement beyond the wear limit or any visible crack, broken spring leaf, or loose fastener. Cautions: a hairline crack in a suspension bracket or spring hanger is an OOS condition and a precursor to catastrophic failure — do not pass it because it "looks small." And loose U-bolts are an emergency: they allow the axle to shift under load, changing alignment and, in severe cases, allowing the spring to leave the seat. Re-torque to spec and investigate why they loosened.

### Document the Inspection and Maintain the Inspection Record

Commercial vehicle inspection is a documented, regulated process. The annual inspection (Part 396) requires a specific report, and the driver vehicle inspection report (DVIR) is required daily for each vehicle operated. The technician's inspection findings must be recorded accurately, defects must be reported for repair, and the records must be retained. This documentation is not bureaucracy — it is the legal record that the vehicle was inspected and that defects were identified and addressed, and it is the first thing reviewed after an accident or during a compliance audit.

The disciplined approach is to document every inspection completely: the items inspected, the measurements taken, the defects found, and the repairs performed. Cautions: signing off an inspection that was not actually performed (pencil-whipping) is fraud and creates liability for the technician and the carrier — and after an accident, the false inspection record is evidence of negligence. And defects that are found but not repaired must be communicated to the driver and the carrier, not buried; an unrepaired OOS condition that later causes an accident, with a paper trail showing it was identified, is far worse for everyone than addressing it when found.

## Common Traps

### Passing a Brake Because It "Stops Fine" — The technician road-tests or applies the brakes in the yard, the vehicle stops, and the brake is passed without measuring pushrod stroke. The trap is that a commercial vehicle can stop adequately in a low-speed yard test with brakes that are significantly out of adjustment, because the other brakes compensate — but at highway speed and full load, the under-performing brakes cannot contribute, the stopping distance increases, and the vehicle may be unable to stop in time. The false signal is the adequate low-speed stop; the harm is a vehicle released with an OOS brake condition that fails at the moment it matters most. Measure every chamber's stroke; do not rely on the "it stops" test.

### Manually Adjusting an Automatic Slack Adjuster and Calling It Fixed — The ASA is out of adjustment, so the technician manually cranks it back to spec, the stroke is within limits, and the vehicle is released. The trap is that an ASA that has drifted out of adjustment has a fault — the ASA itself is worn or failing, or there is a foundation brake problem (a worn drum, a sticking cam, a weak spring) that the ASA cannot overcome — and manually adjusting it does not fix the fault. The ASA will be out of adjustment again within miles or days. The false signal is the within-limit stroke after manual adjustment; the harm is a recurring OOS condition and an underlying fault that progresses until a brake fails or the ASA seizes. Diagnose why the ASA is not self-adjusting; do not just crank it.

### Inspecting Tires for Tread Depth Only — The technician measures tread depth, finds it above minimum, and passes the tire. The trap is that a tire can be legal on depth and still be unsafe or near-failure: a bulge indicating casing separation, a cut into the belts, a slow leak from a puncture, a recap that is delaminating, or a dual mismatch that is overheating the pair. The false signal is the passing depth reading; the harm is a tire that passes inspection and blows out on the highway, or a dual pair that overheats and catches fire. Inspect the whole tire — casing, beads, sidewalls, match — not just the groove depth.

### Pencil-Whipping the Inspection — The inspection is due, the vehicle is needed on the road, and the technician signs off the inspection report without fully performing each step. The trap is that the signature is a legal attestation that the inspection was performed to standard, and if an OOS condition that should have been caught causes an accident, the false record is evidence of negligence that implicates the technician and the carrier. The false signal is the "passed" report; the harm is legal liability, regulatory penalties, and potentially a preventable accident that a real inspection would have prevented. Inspect fully and document honestly; if the vehicle cannot be inspected properly in the time available, that is a scheduling problem, not a reason to falsify the record.

### Missing a Hairline Crack Because It "Looks Small" — The technician sees a hairline crack in a spring hanger, suspension bracket, or frame rail and dismisses it as cosmetic or minor. The trap is that a crack in a structural or suspension mounting component is an OOS condition and a failure initiator — under cyclic load, the crack propagates, and what was hairline becomes a complete fracture, often suddenly and at load. The false signal is the small visible size; the harm is a catastrophic suspension or frame failure at highway speed. Any crack in a structural or suspension component is OOS until evaluated and repaired per OEM or qualified engineering guidance; do not pass it on appearance.

## Self-Check

- Did I inspect each item against the specific regulatory criterion (Part 396 / North American Standard), using the required measurement tools, rather than visual judgment?
- Did I measure the pushrod stroke on every brake chamber, at full pressure, and compare to the limit for that specific chamber type and size?
- For any ASA out of adjustment, did I diagnose the cause (worn ASA, foundation brake fault) rather than manually cranking it back?
- Did I measure tread depth at the wear bars on every tire, and check dual matching, regroove/cap permissibility by position, and casing/sidewall condition?
- Did I physically check every steering and suspension joint for play with the appropriate method, and flag any movement beyond the wear limit?
- Did I inspect for cracks in all structural and suspension mounting components, and treat any crack as OOS until evaluated?
- Do I clearly distinguish OOS conditions (vehicle cannot move) from repairable defects, and communicate OOS conditions immediately to the driver and carrier?
- Did I document the inspection completely — items, measurements, defects, repairs — and sign only what was actually performed?
- Did I report defects found but not repaired, rather than burying them, so the carrier and driver are informed?
