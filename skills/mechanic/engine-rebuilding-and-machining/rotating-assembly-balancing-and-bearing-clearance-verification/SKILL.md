# Rotating Assembly Balancing and Bearing Clearance Verification

The rotating assembly — the crankshaft, the connecting rods, the pistons, the flywheel or flexplate, and the harmonic balancer — is the moving heart of the engine, and its balance and bearing clearances determine whether the rebuilt engine is smooth and long-lived or rough and short-lived. The judgment problem is that an assembly can be installed and turn freely and still be wrong, because the bearing clearances can be too tight or too loose, the rods can be out of weight balance, and the crank can be marginally out of spec, and none of these show up until the engine runs and fails. A technician who assembles without measuring the clearances, or who reuses an unbalanced assembly after machining, hands back an engine that knocks, burns oil, or vibrates. This skill covers the disciplined measurement, balancing, and clearance verification of an engine rotating assembly.

## Core Rules

### Measure Every Bearing Clearance, Not Just a Sample

The disciplined assembly measures the bearing clearance at every journal (every main and every rod), because a single tight or loose bearing can fail the engine, and clearances vary with the journal size, the bearing selection, and the bore condition. The disciplined technician uses plastigage (for a quick field check) or, preferably, a dial bore gauge and a micrometer (for a precise measurement of the bore inside diameter minus the journal outside diameter), at every journal with the bearings installed and the caps torqued to spec. The clearance is compared to the OEM spec (typically 0.001-0.002 inch for most engines, with specific values by application), and the bearing selection is adjusted (using oversize or undersize bearings, or selective-fit bearings by color or size code) to bring every journal into spec. The tradeoff is that measuring every journal takes time, but a single unmeasured tight bearing spins and fails the engine.

### Verify Crankshaft Runout, Journal Condition, and Thrust Clearance

The crankshaft's condition is verified before the bearings are selected, because a bent or worn crank cannot be corrected by bearing selection. The disciplined inspection measures the crankshaft runout (the main journals checked with the crank on V-blocks and a dial indicator on the center journal; the runout must be within the OEM spec, typically under 0.002-0.003 inch), inspects every journal for taper, out-of-round, scoring, and wear (beyond the wear limit, the crank must be ground undersize and fitted with undersize bearings), and checks the thrust surface and the thrust bearing clearance (the crank's end play, measured with a dial indicator at the nose or the flange, typically 0.004-0.008 inch). The tradeoff is that these measurements require V-blocks and a dial indicator, but installing a bent or worn crank guarantees a rough or failing engine.

### Balance the Rotating Assembly After Any Machining or Component Change

The disciplined rebuild balances the rotating assembly whenever a component is changed or machined, because the balance is specific to the assembly's weights. The pistons are weighed and matched (the lightest is the reference, and the others are lightened by milling the pin boss to match, within a few grams), the connecting rods are weighed (big end and small end separately, and matched), and the crankshaft is balanced with the bobweights (representing the reciprocating and rotating weights of the rods and pistons) on a balancing machine, which drills or mills the crank's counterweights to bring the assembly into balance. The flywheel or flexplate and the harmonic balancer are balanced separately or as part of the assembly. The tradeoff is that balancing is a machine shop operation and adds cost, but an unbalanced assembly after machining vibrates and shortens the bearing life.

### Check Ring End Gap, Side Clearance, and Back Clearance in the Finished Bore

The piston rings must have the correct end gap and side clearance in the finished bore, because a tight gap butts the ring ends and scuffs the cylinder, and a loose gap loses compression and oil control. The disciplined verification places each ring in the finished bore (squared with a piston), measures the end gap with a feeler gauge (compared to the OEM spec, typically 0.004 inch per inch of bore for a top ring, with specific values by application and forced induction), and checks the ring side clearance in the piston groove (the ring's up-and-down play, typically 0.001-0.002 inch for a top ring). The technician also checks the ring back clearance (the ring must not protrude past the piston's diameter when compressed in the groove). The tradeoff is that these checks take time per cylinder, but a tight ring gap scuffs the cylinder on the first hard acceleration.

### Verify Rod and Main Bolt Condition, Torque, and Stretch

The rod and main bolts are critical fasteners that hold the rotating assembly together under enormous load, and their condition and torque determine whether the bearings survive. The disciplined inspection checks every bolt for stretch (measuring the length against the OEM spec, or using the bolt's indicated stretch method, and replacing any bolt that has stretched beyond the limit), verifies the threads are clean and undamaged, and torques the bolts with the OEM procedure (which may be a torque-plus-angle method that requires the threads and the bolt head lubricated with the specified oil or moly lube, and may require multiple passes). The technician uses new bolts where the OEM specifies (many engines use torque-to-yield rod and main bolts that must not be reused). The tradeoff is that bolt inspection and replacement add cost, but a stretched or reused torque-to-yield bolt loosens and spins a bearing.

## Common Traps

### Measuring Only a Few Bearing Clearances and Missing a Tight Journal — The technician plastigages two or three journals, assumes the rest are similar, and a tight main journal spins its bearing on the first hard drive. The trap mechanism is that clearances vary by journal and bearing selection, and a sample misses a tight journal. The false signal is the crank "turning freely"; the harm is a spun bearing. The disciplined technician measures every journal.

### Reusing a Crankshaft Without Checking Runout — The crank "looks fine," the technician installs it with new bearings, and the engine vibrates or the bearings fail because the crank is bent. The trap mechanism is that runout is not visible and is not measured, and a bent crank ruins the bearings. The false signal is the crank "looking okay"; the harm is a rough or failing engine. The disciplined technician measures the runout on V-blocks.

### Assembling After Machining Without Balancing — The pistons or rods are replaced, the crank is reground, and the engine vibrates because the assembly is out of balance. The trap mechanism is that machining and component changes alter the balance, and the assembly is not rebalanced. The false signal is the components "fitting"; the harm is a vibrating engine and shortened bearing life. The disciplined technician balances the assembly after any change.

### Skipping the Ring End Gap Check in the Finished Bore — The rings are installed out of the box, the engine is run, and a top ring butts its ends and scuffs the cylinder on the first hard acceleration. The trap mechanism is that the ring gap must be verified in the finished bore, and the gap is not checked. The false signal is the rings "fitting in the piston"; the harm is a scuffed cylinder. The disciplined technician checks the ring end gap in the bore.

### Reusing Torque-to-Yield Rod or Main Bolts — The bolts "look fine," the technician reuses them, and a bolt loosens or breaks and spins a bearing. The trap mechanism is that torque-to-yield bolts are one-time-use and have stretched beyond their limit, and reusing them fails. The false signal is the bolts "looking okay"; the harm is a spun bearing. The disciplined technician replaces torque-to-yield bolts and measures reusable bolts for stretch.

## Self-Check

- Did I measure the bearing clearance at every main and rod journal with plastigage or a dial bore gauge?
- Did I verify the crankshaft runout on V-blocks, inspect every journal for taper and wear, and check the thrust clearance?
- Did I balance the rotating assembly after any machining or component change, with matched pistons and rods and a balanced crank?
- Did I check the ring end gap, side clearance, and back clearance in the finished bore for every cylinder?
- Did I inspect every rod and main bolt for stretch, replace torque-to-yield bolts, and torque with the OEM procedure and lubricant?
- Did I verify the rod bearing and main bearing selection brings every journal into the OEM clearance spec?
- Did I confirm the crank turns freely with the pistons and rods installed, with no binding, before torquing the caps?
- Did I document the clearance measurements, the balancing, and the bolt replacement on the repair order?
