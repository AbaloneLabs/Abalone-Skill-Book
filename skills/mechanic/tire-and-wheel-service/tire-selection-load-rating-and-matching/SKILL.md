# Tire Selection, Load Rating, and Matching

Tire selection is a safety-critical judgment that is often treated as a commodity decision — find the right size, pick the cheapest, mount and balance — but the tire is the only component connecting the vehicle to the road, and the wrong tire, the wrong load rating, the wrong speed rating, or a mismatched set can cause handling changes, accelerated wear, anti-lock brake and stability control issues, and in the worst case a catastrophic failure. The judgment problem is that the tire sidewall carries a dense code of size, load index, speed rating, construction, and service description, and a technician who reads only the size and ignores the rest can mount a tire that fits the wheel but is wrong for the vehicle's weight, speed capability, or all-wheel-drive system. This skill covers the disciplined tire selection, load rating, and matching decisions that keep the vehicle safe and the systems functioning as designed.

## Core Rules

### Match the Size, Load Index, and Speed Rating to the Vehicle Specification

The vehicle's tire specification (found on the tire placard on the door jamb, and in the service information) includes the size (e.g., 225/45R17), the load index (a number representing the maximum load capacity, e.g., 91 = 1356 pounds), and the speed rating (a letter representing the maximum speed capability, e.g., H = 130 mph). The disciplined technician matches all three to the placard specification, not just the size. A tire of the correct size but a lower load index is overloaded by the vehicle's weight and may fail; a tire of the correct size but a lower speed rating may overheat and fail at sustained highway speeds (especially on a vehicle capable of those speeds); a tire of a different size changes the rolling diameter, the gear ratio, the speedometer accuracy, and the ABS and stability control calibration.

The trap is the technician who matches the size, sees a cheaper tire with a lower load index or speed rating, and mounts it because "it fits" — overloading the tire or reducing the speed margin. The disciplined technician verifies the load index and speed rating meet or exceed the placard specification, and explains to the customer why a cheaper tire with a lower rating is not an equivalent choice. For plus-sizing (mounting a larger wheel with a lower-profile tire of the same overall diameter), the load index must still meet spec, and the overall diameter should be within a few percent of the original to avoid speedometer, ABS, and stability control issues.

### Match Tires on All-Wheel-Drive and Four-Wheel-Drive Vehicles

On all-wheel-drive and four-wheel-drive vehicles, the tires must be matched in circumference (which derives from size, tread depth, and wear) across all four positions, because the differentials and the transfer case are designed to accommodate only small differences in rotational speed between the front and rear axles. A tire with a different circumference — from mixing tire models with different diameters, from installing new tires on one axle and worn tires on the other, or from an incorrect size — forces the differentials to continuously absorb the speed difference, which overheats the differential clutches, burns out the transfer case, and causes expensive drivetrain failure. The disciplined technician measures the circumference of all four tires (by measuring the loaded rolling circumference or by measuring the tread depth and the diameter) and ensures they are within the manufacturer's tolerance (often as tight as 1/4 inch of circumference, or 2/32 of tread depth).

The trap is the technician who replaces one tire on an all-wheel-drive vehicle (because one was damaged), matches the size, and returns the vehicle — with three worn tires and one new tire of significantly greater circumference, forcing the differentials to absorb the mismatch and damaging the drivetrain. The disciplined technician, when replacing a tire on an all-wheel-drive vehicle, checks the tread depth of the remaining tires and either replaces all four, replaces the damaged tire and shaves the new one to match the worn tread depth (a service some tire specialists offer), or explains to the customer that the remaining tires are too worn to match and all four must be replaced. The cost of four tires is far less than the cost of a transfer case or a center differential.

### Evaluate Puncture Location and Depth Before Repairing a Tire

A tire puncture is repairable only under specific conditions, and repairing a puncture that does not meet those conditions creates a safety hazard — a repair that fails at speed can cause a rapid deflation and a loss of control. The disciplined technician evaluates the puncture location (repairable only in the tread area, the central portion of the tread; not repairable in the shoulder or the sidewall, where flexing will fail the repair), the puncture size (repairable up to about 1/4 inch diameter; larger punctures require replacement), the puncture angle (a straight-in puncture is repairable; an angled puncture may not seal reliably), and the tread depth (a tire worn below the wear indicators, typically 2/32 inch, should be replaced rather than repaired). The repair must be a plug-patch combination (a plug through the puncture and a patch on the inside), not a plug alone (which can leak) or a patch alone (which does not fill the puncture path and can allow water into the carcass).

The trap is the technician who repairs any puncture to save the customer money — plugging a sidewall puncture (which will fail), plugging a puncture in a run-flat tire (which is generally not repairable because the internal damage from run-flat operation is not visible), or plugging a large or angled puncture that will not seal reliably. The disciplined technician follows the tire industry repair standards (TIA guidelines) and refuses to repair a puncture that does not meet them, explaining to the customer that the safety risk of a failed repair exceeds the cost of a tire. A repair done wrong is worse than no repair, because it gives false confidence in a tire that will fail.

### Inspect for Tire Damage Beyond the Puncture

When a tire comes in for a puncture or any service, the disciplined technician inspects the entire tire for additional damage that affects its safety and serviceability. This includes checking the tread depth (a tire at or below the wear indicators must be replaced), the wear pattern (which indicates suspension, alignment, or inflation problems that must be addressed to protect the new tires), the sidewalls for cuts, bulges, or impact damage (a bulge indicates broken cords from an impact, and the tire must be replaced — it can fail without warning), the bead area for damage from mounting, and the inner liner (visible when the tire is dismounted) for signs of run-flat damage, previous repairs, or aging cracks. A tire that has been driven underinflated may have internal damage (the sidewalls flex and overheat, delaminating the cords) that is not visible externally but makes the tire unsafe.

The trap is the technician who repairs the puncture and returns the tire without noticing the sidewall bulge, the inner liner damage, or the tread depth at the wear indicators — sending the customer out on a tire that is unsafe for a different reason. The disciplined technician inspects the whole tire and recommends replacement for any safety-relevant damage, and addresses the wear pattern cause (alignment, inflation, suspension) so the replacement tires do not wear the same way.

### Match Tire Types and Avoid Mixing on an Axle

The friction characteristics of a tire — its compound, tread pattern, and construction — determine its grip, its braking, its handling, and its response to ABS and stability control. Mixing tire types on a vehicle (different models, different tread patterns, different compounds) creates side-to-side or front-to-rear imbalances that cause handling changes, braking imbalance, and unpredictable behavior in emergency maneuvers. The disciplined technician installs tires in matched sets of four (ideal) or at minimum in matched pairs on an axle, using the same model, size, load index, and speed rating. Mixing a performance tire on one side with a touring tire on the other creates a side-to-side grip difference that can cause a pull or a handling change in a turn; mixing an all-season with a winter tire creates a front-to-rear mismatch that affects braking and stability.

The trap is the technician who installs whatever tire is in stock or cheapest on each position, creating a mismatched set that the customer does not notice until an emergency maneuver reveals the imbalance. The disciplined technician matches the tires, explains to the customer why a matched set matters for safety, and if a full set is not possible (budget constraint), installs matched pairs on the rear axle (for stability in wet conditions — the rear tires must not lose grip before the front) and keeps the better pair on the rear regardless of whether the vehicle is front-, rear-, or all-wheel-drive.

## Common Traps

### Matching the Size but Ignoring the Load Index and Speed Rating — The technician matches the tire size, mounts a cheaper tire with a lower load index or speed rating, and returns the vehicle — with a tire that is overloaded or under-rated for the vehicle's capability. The trap mechanism is that the size fits the wheel, so the tire appears correct, but the load and speed capabilities are insufficient. The false signal is the tire fitting and appearing correct. The harm is an overloaded or overheated tire that may fail, and a vehicle that does not meet its design capability. The disciplined technician verifies the load index and speed rating meet or exceed the placard spec.

### Replacing One Tire on an All-Wheel-Drive Vehicle — The technician replaces one damaged tire on an all-wheel-drive vehicle, matches the size, and returns the vehicle — with a circumference mismatch that damages the center differential or the transfer case. The trap mechanism is that the new tire has full tread depth and the worn tires have less, creating a circumference difference that the AWD system cannot tolerate. The false signal is the size matching. The harm is a damaged center differential or transfer case (thousands of dollars) from a single tire replacement (hundreds of dollars). The disciplined technician measures the circumference and replaces or shaves to match.

### Plugging a Sidewall or Out-of-Spec Puncture — The technician plugs a puncture in the shoulder or sidewall, or plugs a large or angled puncture, to save the customer the cost of a tire — and the repair fails at speed, causing a rapid deflation. The trap mechanism is that sidewall and shoulder punctures are in flex zones where the repair cannot hold, and out-of-spec punctures do not seal reliably. The false signal is the plug appearing to hold in the bay. The harm is a tire that fails at speed, a potential loss of control, and a liability for the shop. The disciplined technician repairs only punctures that meet the TIA guidelines and refuses the rest.

### Repairing a Run-Flat Tire Without Inspecting for Internal Damage — The technician repairs a puncture in a run-flat tire that has been driven flat, plugs it, and returns it — but the run-flat operation has internally damaged the tire (delaminated cords from the flexing and heat) that is not visible externally, and the tire fails later. The trap mechanism is that run-flat tires that have been driven flat generally cannot be safely repaired, because the internal damage is not visible and the tire is compromised. The false signal is the tire appearing undamaged externally. The harm is a tire that fails without warning, a potential accident, and a liability. The disciplined technician follows the run-flat manufacturer's repair policy (most prohibit repair after run-flat operation) and replaces the tire.

### Mixing Tire Types on an Axle or Vehicle — The technician installs different model tires on different positions (whatever is in stock or cheapest), creating a mismatched set with different friction characteristics. The trap mechanism is that the tires fit and appear functional, but the friction differences cause handling and braking imbalances that appear only in emergency maneuvers. The false signal is the tires fitting and the vehicle driving normally. The harm is unpredictable handling in an emergency, a braking imbalance, and a vehicle that does not behave as designed. The disciplined technician installs matched sets or matched pairs and explains the safety rationale.

## Self-Check

- Did I match the tire size, load index, and speed rating to the placard specification, not just the size?
- Did I verify the load index and speed rating meet or exceed the original specification?
- For plus-sizing, did I verify the overall diameter is within a few percent of the original and the load index still meets spec?
- On an all-wheel-drive or four-wheel-drive vehicle, did I measure the circumference of all four tires and ensure they are within the manufacturer's tolerance?
- When replacing one tire on an AWD vehicle, did I check the tread depth of the remaining tires and replace all four or shave the new tire to match if needed?
- For a puncture repair, did I verify the location (tread only, not shoulder or sidewall), size (under 1/4 inch), angle, and tread depth meet the repair guidelines?
- Did I use a plug-patch combination repair, not a plug alone or a patch alone?
- Did I inspect the entire tire for sidewall bulges, cuts, inner liner damage, run-flat damage, and tread depth, not just the puncture?
- Did I install matched tires (same model, size, load index, speed rating) in sets of four or pairs on an axle?
- Did I address the cause of any abnormal wear pattern (alignment, inflation, suspension) to protect the new tires?
