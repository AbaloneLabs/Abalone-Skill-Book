# Mobility Device Selection and Progressive Ambulation

The default in many care settings is to default to a wheelchair the moment a client shows any unsteadiness, because a wheelchair feels safe. But immobility is itself a cause of decline — deconditioning, contractures, pressure injuries, pneumonia, and depression all accelerate when a client stops walking. The judgment problem is selecting the device and the ambulation plan that preserves as much independent mobility as the client can safely sustain, while recognizing when a wheelchair is genuinely the right choice. This is not a binary decision, and it changes as the client's condition changes.

## Core Rules

### Match the Device to the Client's Actual Capacity, Not the Setting's Convenience

A walker is appropriate for a client who can bear weight, has adequate upper-body strength, sufficient cognition to remember to use it, and the balance to use it correctly. A cane is appropriate for clients with mild balance deficits who need a cue or minor support. A wheelchair is appropriate when the client cannot safely ambulate the distances required, when ambulation causes unacceptable fall risk or exhaustion, or when the client's condition makes walking therapeutically counterproductive. The selection should follow an objective assessment — ideally by a physical therapist — not a default based on what is available or what reduces staff burden.

### Reassess Regularly; Capacity Is Not Static

A client who needed a wheelchair post-surgery may regain ambulation capacity through rehabilitation. A client who was walking independently may decline due to progressive illness, medication changes, or deconditioning. The mobility plan should be reassessed at regular intervals and after any significant change in condition. The trap is a one-time device assignment that persists for months or years without review, even as the client's condition has changed.

### Plan Progressive Ambulation After Deconditioning

After hospitalization, illness, or a period of immobility, clients are often deconditioned — weaker, less balanced, less endurance than their baseline. A progressive ambulation plan rebuilds capacity gradually: assisted standing, then a few steps with a walker and standby assistance, then increasing distance, then independent ambulation with a device. The plan should be coordinated with physical therapy, should have measurable goals (distance, level of assistance), and should be documented so progress is visible. Pushing a deconditioned client to their previous baseline immediately risks a fall; keeping them in a wheelchair indefinitely guarantees further decline.

### Recognize When Ambulation Is No Longer Safe

There is a point in many progressive conditions (advanced dementia, end-stage Parkinson's, severe cardiopulmonary disease) where ambulation becomes more dangerous than beneficial — the fall risk is unacceptably high, the effort causes distress, and the therapeutic benefit is minimal. Recognizing this point requires honest assessment, not optimism. The transition to wheelchair-primary mobility should be discussed with the family, documented with rationale, and should still include transfer-level mobility and repositioning to prevent the complications of total immobility.

### Consider Cognition in Device Selection

A walker is only effective if the client remembers to use it, uses it correctly, and does not attempt to ambulate without it. Clients with moderate-to-severe dementia may forget the walker, use it incorrectly (pulling it behind them, leaning too far forward), or become agitated by it. In these cases, a device that depends on consistent cognitive engagement may create more risk than it mitigates. The selection must account for whether the client can reliably use the device, and if not, whether environmental modifications (grab bars, lowered bed, staffing) are a better strategy.

### Coordinate With Therapy and Family

Mobility decisions should involve the physical therapist for clinical assessment, the family for goals and preferences, and the care team for implementation feasibility. A device recommended by therapy that staff cannot consistently assist with (due to staffing levels or training) will not be used correctly. A family preference for ambulation that exceeds the client's safe capacity will lead to falls. The decision is collaborative and should be documented with the reasoning of all parties.

## Common Traps

### The Wheelchair Default

When a client is unsteady, the reflexive response is to put them in a wheelchair because it eliminates ambulation fall risk. The trap is that the wheelchair also eliminates the ambulation that maintains strength, balance, bone density, bowel function, and psychological well-being. A client who could walk with a walker and standby assistance is placed in a wheelchair "for safety," and within weeks has lost the capacity to walk at all — a self-fulfilling decline. The wheelchair should be selected when ambulation is genuinely unsafe or non-therapeutic, not as a default response to any unsteadiness.

### Using the Wrong Device Because It Is Available

A walker that is too tall or too short, a wheelchair that does not fit, a cane that is the wrong height — these create their own fall risks and postural problems. The trap is using whatever device is in the supply closet rather than the device that fits the client. An ill-fitting walker forces the client into poor posture, reduces its stability benefit, and may cause back or shoulder pain that further discourages use. Device selection includes fitting.

### The "Use It or Lose It" Fallacy in Reverse

The principle that ambulation preserves capacity is correct, but it can be over-applied. The trap is pushing a client to ambulate when they are acutely ill, in pain, or experiencing an acute decline (a UTI, a medication reaction, a transient ischemic attack) under the rationale that walking is good for them. During acute episodes, the priority is the acute condition, and ambulation should be paused or reduced until the client is stable. Resuming progressive ambulation after the acute episode is appropriate; forcing it during is dangerous.

### Assuming a Device Eliminates Fall Risk

A client with a walker can still fall — they can fall forward over the walker, they can leave the walker behind, they can fall while turning. The trap is reducing supervision or assistance because "they have a walker now," treating the device as a substitute for the human support that was preventing falls. The device changes the level of support needed, but does not eliminate it. The care plan should specify the level of assistance (independent, standby, contact guard, assist-of-one) in addition to the device.

## Self-Check

- Did I select the device based on an assessment of the client's capacity, or based on convenience and availability?
- Does the device fit the client correctly (height, width, weight capacity)?
- Did I account for the client's cognition — can they reliably use the device?
- Is there a progressive ambulation plan for deconditioned clients, with measurable goals?
- Have I reassessed the mobility plan recently, or is it months old?
- Did I recognize when ambulation is no longer safe and transition to wheelchair-primary with documented rationale?
- Did I coordinate with therapy, family, and the care team?
- Did I specify the level of assistance in addition to the device, rather than treating the device as sufficient?
- During an acute illness, did I pause ambulation rather than forcing it?
