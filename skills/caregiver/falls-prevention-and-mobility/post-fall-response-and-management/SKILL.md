# Post-Fall Response and Management

The judgment problem after a fall is that the most natural impulses — to help the person up immediately, to reassure them that they are fine, to move them out of the awkward or exposed position they have landed in — are often the most dangerous. A person who has fallen may have an injury that is worsened by movement, a head injury whose signs emerge over minutes rather than seconds, or a clinical cause (a stroke, a cardiac event, a hypoglycemic episode) that produced the fall and is still in progress. The skilled caregiver's first job after a fall is not to lift the person but to slow down, assess, and protect them from further harm, while escalating appropriately. The second job, often neglected, is to treat the fall as information: a single fall may be an accident, but a pattern of falls is a signal that something has changed and the care plan must change with it.

This is a high-stakes domain. Mishandled movement after a fall can convert a stable fracture into a catastrophic one, turn a minor head injury into an undetected serious one, and delay treatment of a life-threatening cause. The caregiver's role is to assess, keep the person safe and still when indicated, provide comfort and reassurance, summon help, document accurately, and report — not to diagnose the injury, to decide the person is uninjured, or to move a person who may be injured without clinical guidance.

## Core Rules

### Do Not Rush To Lift The Person; Assess First

The instinct to get the person off the floor quickly must be resisted. Your first action is to assess, not to lift.

- Approach calmly, speak to the person, and note their responsiveness and orientation. A person who is unresponsive, confused, or unable to answer simple questions has a possible head injury, stroke, or other acute event and needs emergency escalation.
- Ask where they have pain. Move your eyes, not their body, to look for obvious deformity, swelling, bruising, bleeding, or a limb at an unnatural angle.
- Check for red-flag symptoms (below) before any consideration of moving the person.

Only when the person is alert, oriented, in no significant pain, with no deformity and no red-flag symptoms, and the fall was clearly minor (a slip from a seated position, a stumble onto a carpeted surface), should a cautious, assisted return to a safe position even be considered — and only with the person's full cooperation and a second person if there is any doubt.

### Recognize And Escalate Red-Flag Symptoms Immediately

Certain findings make the fall a medical emergency requiring activation of the emergency plan and summoning emergency services, not a wait-and-see approach.

- Loss of consciousness, even briefly, before or after the fall.
- Any suspicion of head injury, especially if the person is on anticoagulants, in which case even a minor head bump carries a risk of intracranial bleeding.
- New or worsening confusion, slurred speech, facial droop, weakness or numbness on one side, or visual disturbance — possible stroke signs.
- Chest pain, palpitations, severe shortness of breath, or a very slow or very fast pulse — possible cardiac cause.
- Severe pain anywhere, especially in the hip, pelvis, spine, or neck; inability to bear weight; or a limb that looks deformed — possible fracture.
- Bleeding that does not stop with pressure, or a large bruise that is expanding.
- Vomiting, a seizure, or incontinence associated with the fall.

When any red flag is present, keep the person still, summon emergency help, and do not move them unless they are in immediate danger (for example, in a position where they cannot breathe safely).

### Keep The Person Safe, Warm, And Comfortable While Waiting

While you are assessing and while help is coming, your job is to prevent further harm and to reduce distress.

- Keep the person still if injury is possible; do not allow them to try to get up if there is any concern about fracture or head injury.
- Protect them from cold and from exposure: cover with a blanket, preserve modesty, and shield them from onlookers.
- Offer calm reassurance and explanation; a person who has fallen is often frightened and embarrassed as well as hurt. Speak slowly and clearly, especially if hearing or cognition is impaired.
- Do not give food or drink in case they need surgery or have impaired swallowing.
- Stay with them continuously; do not leave to "find help" if you can summon help by phone or call-out, and if you must leave, ensure someone else stays.

### Move The Person Only When It Is Safe And Necessary

Movement after a fall is a clinical decision when any injury is possible, not a comfort or convenience decision.

- If a red-flag symptom is present, do not move the person except to protect their airway or remove them from immediate danger.
- If the person is uninjured and fully cooperative and the fall was minor, an assisted return to a chair may be appropriate, using correct technique and a second person if needed; never lift by pulling on the arms.
- If you are unsure whether the person is injured, the default is to keep them still and wait for a nurse or emergency services to assess. "I think they're fine" is not a clinical clearance.

### Document The Fall Accurately And Completely

Accurate documentation supports clinical follow-up, protects the care recipient, and protects the caregiver. It is not optional or a formality.

- Record the time and location of the fall and what you observed when you found or witnessed it.
- Record what the person was doing at the time and any reported symptoms, in the person's own words where possible.
- Record your assessment findings: responsiveness, orientation, pain and its location, any visible injury, vital signs if taken, and any red-flag symptoms.
- Record what you did: who was called, when help arrived, what instructions were given, and the person's condition over time.
- Record the outcome: whether the person was moved, where they went, and their status at handover.
- Avoid speculation about the cause ("they must have tripped") and avoid blame; describe what you saw and heard, not what you infer.

### Treat Every Fall As Information And Feed It Back Into The Care Plan

A fall is never just an isolated event to be cleaned up and forgotten. It is data about the person's current risk and the adequacy of the current plan.

- Report every fall to the nurse or supervisor, even a minor fall with no apparent injury; minor falls and "near misses" often precede serious ones.
- Participate in the post-fall review: what factors were present (medications, environment, new symptoms, time of day, footwear), what was modifiable, and what should change.
- A pattern of falls, a fall with new symptoms, or a change in the type of fall should trigger a clinical review of causes and a revision of the care plan — not simply more of the same precautions.
- Follow up on delayed effects: a person who seemed uninjured may develop pain, confusion, or a headache hours later, which must be reported and treated as a possible injury.

## Common Traps

### Lifting The Person Immediately To Relieve Embarrassment Or Restore Routine

When a person falls in a communal or visible setting, the overwhelming social pressure is to get them up quickly, into a chair, and back to normal, to spare them embarrassment and to resume the schedule. The mechanism is that the discomfort of a person lying on the floor in public feels urgent and resolvable, so the caregiver acts on that discomfort by lifting, before assessing whether lifting is safe. The false signal is the person's own wish to get up and the relief of standing them upright, which feels like resolution but may be worsening an undiagnosed fracture or missing a developing head injury. The harm is that a stable hip or spinal injury is displaced by the movement, a slow intracranial bleed after a head bump goes unrecognized because the person was walked to a chair, and the underlying cause of the fall is never investigated because the appearance of normalcy was restored. The defense is to make assessment the first act regardless of social pressure, to keep the person still and covered when any injury is possible, and to treat the wish to get up as understandable but not as clearance to move.

### Reassuring The Person They Are Fine Before They Have Been Assessed

In the moment, the caregiver wants to comfort a frightened person, and "you're fine, you're fine" comes easily. The mechanism is that reassurance is a kindness in intent but a clinical pronouncement in effect, and the caregiver who has not yet assessed is in no position to make it. The false signal is the person's relief and the caregiver's wish to believe it, which suppresses the careful observation that should be happening. The harm is twofold: the person may minimize or stop reporting symptoms because they have been told they are fine, hiding a serious injury; and the caregiver's own documentation and reporting may be colored by the premature reassurance, so a serious finding is downgraded. The defense is to comfort without diagnosing — "I'm here, help is coming, let me check you over" — to assess fully before any statement about the person's condition, and to never tell a person they are uninjured when no one has yet determined that.

### Missing A Head Injury Because The Person "Looks Fine" Or Is On Anticoagulants

A person who hits their head may be alert, oriented, and in no pain immediately afterward, which can lull the caregiver into underestimating the risk, especially when the person is on a blood thinner and the danger is an intracranial bleed that develops over hours. The mechanism is that serious head injury often has a lucid interval, so the early presentation looks reassuring and the warning signs emerge later when the caregiver has moved on. The false signal is the person's normal appearance in the first minutes, which is mistaken for the absence of injury. The harm is that a person on anticoagulants who had a minor head bump is put to bed without escalation and develops a life-threatening intracranial hemorrhage that would have been treatable if reported. The defense is to treat any head contact, however minor, as a red flag requiring escalation, to specifically ask about and document anticoagulant use, to monitor for delayed signs (worsening headache, confusion, drowsiness, vomiting, weakness) for hours afterward, and to escalate any such change immediately.

### Assuming A Single Fall Is An Isolated Accident And Not Investigating The Cause

When a person with no prior falls has one fall, it is easy to attribute it to a wet floor or a moment of carelessness and to leave the care plan unchanged. The mechanism is that a single event feels like bad luck rather than a pattern, so the caregiver documents the fall and moves on without asking what underlying factor made this fall possible or likely to recur. The false signal is the absence of a prior pattern, which is read as the absence of underlying risk. The harm is that the first fall was the warning, and the modifiable cause — a new medication causing postural hypotension, an undiagnosed infection causing confusion, a developing cardiac arrhythmia — is never addressed, so the second, more serious fall follows. The defense is to treat every fall, including the first and including falls with no apparent injury, as a signal requiring a review of possible causes, to report every fall so the nurse or clinician can investigate, and to revise the care plan in response rather than assuming recurrence is unlikely.

### Documenting The Fall Vaguely Or With Inferred Causes

Under time pressure, the post-fall documentation may be reduced to "resident found on floor, assisted to chair, no injury," with no detail about what was observed, what the person reported, or what was done. The mechanism is that documentation feels like a chore after a stressful event, so it is completed minimally to satisfy the requirement, and the gaps are filled with inference ("must have tripped") because the caregiver wants a tidy explanation. The false signal is that the form is complete, which is mistaken for the record being adequate. The harm is that the clinical team cannot investigate the cause from a vague record, patterns across multiple falls cannot be detected, delayed injuries cannot be correlated with the event, and the caregiver's own account is unprotected if questions arise later. The defense is to document observations and the person's own words rather than inferences, to record times and specific findings, and to complete the documentation promptly while details are fresh, recognizing that an accurate record is part of the care, not an afterthought to it.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I resist the impulse to lift the person immediately, and assess responsiveness, orientation, pain, and visible injury before any movement?
- [ ] Did I check for and escalate red-flag symptoms — loss of consciousness, possible head injury (especially on anticoagulants), stroke signs, chest pain or palpitations, severe pain or deformity, uncontrolled bleeding, vomiting, seizure, or incontinence?
- [ ] Did I keep the person safe, still, warm, and comforted while waiting, without giving food or drink, and stay with them continuously?
- [ ] Did I move the person only when uninjured, fully cooperative, and with correct technique — and keep them still when any injury was possible?
- [ ] Did I document the time, location, circumstances, observed findings, the person's own words, actions taken, and outcome, without inferring a cause or assigning blame?
- [ ] Did I report the fall to the nurse or supervisor even when it seemed minor, and participate in reviewing the cause and updating the care plan?
- [ ] Did I monitor for delayed effects — headache, confusion, drowsiness, new pain — for hours afterward, and escalate any such change?
- [ ] Did I stay within my scope — assessing, comforting, summoning help, and documenting — rather than diagnosing, declaring the person uninjured, or moving a possibly injured person without clinical guidance?
