# Feeding Tube Site Care And Administration Safety

A feeding tube sustains the nutrition and hydration of a person who cannot eat or drink safely or adequately by mouth, and it is both a medical device and a daily caregiving routine whose correct performance determines whether the person is nourished, hydrated, and free of complications. The judgment problem is that feeding through a tube feels mechanical and simple (connect the bag, let it run), but the details — the position of the person, the verification of tube placement, the rate and tolerance of feeding, the care of the stoma, the administration of medications — each carry specific risks (aspiration, infection, blockage, medication errors, skin breakdown) that a casual approach will trigger. A caregiver who feeds a person lying flat, who does not check residual or placement, who crushes the wrong medication, or who neglects the stoma will cause aspiration pneumonia, tube site infection, tube failure, and suffering. The agent must understand that enteral feeding is a medical procedure with technique that matters, that the stoma is a surgical wound and a chronic opening, and that the margin between nourishment and harm is in the details.

## Core Rules

### Perform Feeding Tube Site Care To Prevent Infection And Breakdown

The tube site (stoma) is a surgical opening through the abdominal wall, and it requires daily care to prevent infection, skin breakdown, and complications. Clean the site daily (and when soiled) with sterile technique and the prescribed solution (often mild soap and water; avoid harsh antiseptics on healed stomas unless directed), gently removing crusting. Inspect the site for signs of infection (redness that spreads, warmth, swelling, pain, pus, odor) and skin breakdown (from leakage, friction, or tension), and report concerns. Keep the tube secured (to prevent pulling and dislodgement) but without pressure on the stoma — use a securement device or tape, and ensure the external bumper or disc is not digging into the skin (it should allow a millimeter or two of play, not be flush and tight, which causes buried bumper syndrome). Rotate the tube gently (as directed) to prevent skin adherence and buried bumper. Manage leakage (digestive fluid around the tube is irritating and breaks down skin) by ensuring the balloon or bumper is correctly positioned, using skin-protective barriers, and reporting persistent leakage. The stoma is the most common site of preventable complication; care for it daily.

### Administer Feedings Safely: Position, Rate, And Tolerance

The technique of feeding determines whether the person is nourished or aspirated. Position: always elevate the head of the bed to at least 30 to 45 degrees (ideally sitting upright) during feeding and for at least 30 to 60 minutes after, because lying flat during feeding causes reflux and aspiration — this is non-negotiable. Verification: for nasogastric tubes, verify placement (by pH testing of aspirate, or as prescribed) before every feeding, because a tube that has moved into the lung or esophagus will deliver formula into the lungs; for PEG and gastrostomy tubes, confirm the external length and that the tube is in position. Rate and method: administer by the prescribed method (bolus, intermittent, or continuous via pump) and rate; bolus feedings (large volumes by gravity over 15 to 30 minutes) are convenient but less tolerated, while pump feedings (slow, continuous) are better tolerated but require equipment. Check gastric residual (the volume in the stomach before feeding) as prescribed (usually for gastric tubes), and hold or reduce the feeding if the residual is high (per the plan), because high residual indicates poor tolerance and aspiration risk. Monitor tolerance: report nausea, vomiting, abdominal distension, cramping, diarrhea, or constipation, which indicate the feeding needs adjustment. Feedings are a medical procedure; follow the plan and observe the response.

### Manage Tube Patency And Prevent Blockage

A blocked tube is a common and serious problem, because it interrupts nutrition and hydration and may require tube replacement. Prevent blockage by flushing the tube with water before and after each feeding and each medication administration (and every four to six hours for continuous feedings), using the prescribed flush volume (often 30 to 60 mL for adults, less for jejunostomy or fluid-restricted patients). Use only liquid medications when possible, and crush only medications confirmed safe to crush (never crush extended-release or enteric-coated medications), dissolving them thoroughly in water and administering each separately (not mixed together, which causes clogging and interactions). Never mix medications with formula. If the tube blocks, attempt to clear it with warm water and gentle flushing (use a push-pull technique with a syringe), and avoid forceful flushing or using sharp objects (which puncture the tube). If it cannot be cleared, contact the medical team (pancreatic enzymes or a declogging device may be used, or the tube may need replacement). Prevention through flushing is far easier than clearing a block.

### Administer Medications Safely Through The Tube

Medication administration through a feeding tube carries specific risks that must be managed. Verify the route: not all medications are appropriate for a feeding tube (especially jejunostomy, which bypasses the stomach's acid and may not absorb certain drugs), and some medications interact with the tube material or formula. Use liquid formulations whenever possible. If crushing, confirm the medication can be crushed (never crush extended-release, enteric-coated, or cytotoxic medications), crush finely, dissolve in water, and administer each medication separately with flushes between. Hold tube feedings around certain medications as directed (some medications, like phenytoin and certain antibiotics, bind to formula and lose effectiveness if given with feeding). Do not mix multiple medications together (which causes precipitation and clogging). Flush before, between, and after. Document what was given and the response. A feeding tube is not a simple alternative route; each medication must be considered for appropriateness and technique.

### Recognize And Respond To Tube Complications

Feeding tubes have a range of complications that must be recognized and addressed. Infection at the site (cellulitis, abscess) shows as spreading redness, warmth, swelling, pain, pus, and fever, and needs medical evaluation. Leakage around the tube (gastric contents or formula) breaks down skin and may indicate tube loosening, balloon deflation, or infection; manage the skin and report persistent leakage. Tube blockage (inability to flush or feed) requires clearing attempts and possible replacement. Tube dislodgement or removal (the tube comes out) is an emergency: for a mature tract (several weeks old), the stoma closes within hours, so replace with a backup tube (if trained) or seek care immediately; for a new tract, the stoma closes in minutes, so seek emergency care. Never try to reinsert a tube that has fallen out unless trained and authorized, because misplacement (into the peritoneum instead of the stomach) is fatal if fed. Buried bumper syndrome (the internal bumper erodes into the stomach wall) shows as leakage, pain, and inability to rotate or advance the tube, and needs surgical correction. Granulation tissue (red, friable tissue around the stoma) is common and may need silver nitrate or treatment. Know the complications and the response for each.

### Support Hydration, Nutrition Goals, And Oral Care

The feeding tube delivers nutrition and hydration, but the whole picture must be managed. Ensure the prescribed formula, volume, and rate meet the person's nutritional and hydration needs (monitored by weight, lab values, and clinical status), and report weight loss, dehydration, or signs of malnutrition. Ensure adequate free water (the water flushes are often the person's main hydration, and they may be under-hydrated if flushes are missed). Monitor for refeeding syndrome (in malnourished persons starting feeding, a dangerous shift of electrolytes — report weakness, confusion, or palpitations when feeding is started or increased). Provide oral care (mouth cleaning, moisturizing, and hygiene) even though the person is not eating by mouth, because oral health deteriorates without eating and causes infection and discomfort. Support any oral intake that is safe and prescribed (some people eat small amounts by mouth in addition to tube feeding, assessed by a speech therapist). Nutrition and hydration are the goal; the tube is the means.

### Coordinate With Nutrition, Speech Therapy, And The Medical Team

Feeding tube care is a team effort: the dietitian manages the formula, volume, and nutritional goals; the speech therapist assesses swallowing and any safe oral intake; the physician (often gastroenterology or the primary care) manages the tube and the underlying condition; and the home infusion or durable medical equipment company supplies the formula and equipment. Keep all appointments, report feeding intolerance, weight changes, tube complications, and supply needs promptly, and ensure the formula and equipment are stocked. Have the key numbers posted. Feeding tube care is not a solo endeavor; the team supports the person's nutrition and the tube's function.

## Common Traps

### Feeding With The Person Lying Flat

The caregiver runs the feeding with the head of the bed down. The mechanism is the convenience or the sleeping person. The false signal is that the feeding is running fine. The harm is that formula refluxes and is aspirated, causing aspiration pneumonia. Always elevate the head during and after feeding.

### Not Verifying Placement For Nasogastric Tubes

The caregiver feeds without confirming the tube is in the stomach. The mechanism is the assumption that the tube has not moved. The false signal is that the tube looks the same. The harm is that formula is delivered into the lung (if the tube migrated), causing aspiration. Verify placement before every feeding.

### Crushing Medications That Should Not Be Crushed

The caregiver crushes all pills for convenience. The mechanism is the route assumption. The false signal is that crushing is standard for tubes. The harm is that extended-release medications dump their full dose at once (overdose) and enteric-coated medications are destroyed or irritate the stomach. Confirm each medication is safe to crush.

### Neglecting The Stoma Site And Causing Infection Or Buried Bumper

The caregiver does not clean or inspect the site daily. The mechanism is the focus on feeding over site care. The false signal is that the site is healed and stable. The harm is that infection, skin breakdown, or buried bumper syndrome develop. Clean and inspect the site daily.

### Trying To Reinsert A Dislodged Tube Without Training

The caregiver reinserts a tube that fell out. The mechanism is the instinct to fix it. The false signal is that the tract is open. The harm is that the tube goes into the peritoneum instead of the stomach, and feeding causes peritonitis and death. Seek trained care or emergency services unless trained.

### Missing Refeeding Syndrome In The Malnourished Person

The caregiver starts full feeding in a malnourished person without monitoring. The mechanism is not knowing the syndrome. The false signal is that feeding is restorative. The harm is that electrolytes shift dangerously, causing heart and neurological problems. Start feeding slowly in malnourished persons and monitor.

## Self-Check

Before treating feeding tube site care and administration safety as complete, verify:

- The tube site was cleaned and inspected daily (and when soiled), with signs of infection, breakdown, leakage, and buried bumper recognized and reported, and the tube secured without pressure.
- Feedings were administered with the head elevated 30 to 45 degrees during and after, placement verified (for nasogastric tubes), the prescribed method and rate followed, residual checked as directed, and tolerance monitored.
- Tube patency was maintained by flushing before and after feedings and medications, using liquid medications or safely crushed medications, and clearing blockages with warm water (not force or sharp objects).
- Medications were administered safely (liquid when possible, only crushable medications crushed, each given separately with flushes, tube feedings held when required).
- Tube complications (infection, leakage, blockage, dislodgement, buried bumper, granulation) were recognized, and the response (including not reinserting without training) was known.
- Hydration, nutrition goals, and oral care were managed, with refeeding syndrome risk recognized in malnourished persons.
- Care was coordinated with the dietitian, speech therapist, physician, and equipment company, with changes reported and supplies stocked.
- Did the work stay within scope, performing trained tube care and feeding while deferring formula, feeding plan, and tube management decisions to clinicians?
