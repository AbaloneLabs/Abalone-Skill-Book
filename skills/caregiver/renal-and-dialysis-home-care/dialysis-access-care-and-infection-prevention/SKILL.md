# Dialysis Access Care And Infection Prevention

The dialysis access — the surgically created connection (fistula or graft) or the central venous catheter through which blood is drawn and returned during dialysis — is the person's lifeline, and it is also their greatest source of danger. The judgment problem is that the access is both essential and vulnerable: a fistula or graft can clot, collapse, bleed, or become infected and end the person's ability to dialyze; a central venous catheter is a direct highway from the outside world to the heart and bloodstream, and catheter infections are a leading cause of hospitalization and death in dialysis patients. Caregivers who do not know how to protect the access, who let blood pressure be taken on the access arm or the arm be used for IVs, who do not recognize the signs of clotting or infection, or who handle a catheter without meticulous sterile technique will cause access loss, bacteremia, and sepsis. The agent must understand that the access is sacred, that protecting it is daily care, and that catheter hygiene is infection prevention at its most literal.

## Core Rules

### Protect The Fistula Or Graft Arm From All Compression And Puncture

An arteriovenous fistula (a surgical connection of an artery to a vein, usually in the forearm or upper arm) or graft (a synthetic tube connecting artery to vein) relies on high blood flow to stay open and functional, and anything that compresses or restricts that flow can clot the access and end its usefulness. The access arm must never be used for blood pressure measurement, blood draws, IV placement, or needle sticks (except by the dialysis staff for treatment). Do not sleep on the access arm, do not wear tight sleeves, watches, or bracelets on it, do not rest heavy objects on it, and do not compress it. Protect it as the lifeline it is; losing it means surgery for a new access and months of a catheter in the meantime.

### Assess The Fistula Or Graft Daily For Flow (Thrill, Pulse, Bruit)

A functioning fistula or graft has a distinct feel and sound that confirms blood flow. Feel for the thrill — a buzzing, vibrating sensation — at the access, which indicates good arterial flow; listen for the bruit — a swooshing sound — with a stethoscope if available. A strong pulsation without the buzzing thrill suggests the access may be clotting at the venous end. Absence of the thrill or bruit, or a change from the person's baseline, suggests clotting and requires urgent contact with the dialysis team or vascular access center, because a clotted access must be declotted quickly or it is lost. Check the access daily, know the person's baseline feel and sound, and report changes the same day.

### Recognize And Respond To Access Bleeding

Bleeding from the fistula or graft needle sites after dialysis is usually controlled with pressure, but prolonged or heavy bleeding is dangerous and can signal a problem with the access. After dialysis, the staff applies pressure until bleeding stops; at home, if a site bleeds, apply firm but not crushing pressure (do not crush the access) with a clean dressing for 10 to 15 minutes, and if bleeding does not stop, continue pressure and seek urgent care. Heavy, pulsatile, or uncontrolled bleeding from the access is a life-threatening emergency: apply firm pressure, call emergency services, and do not let go. Never apply a tourniquet to the access arm. Report any unusual bleeding to the dialysis team.

### Recognize And Report Access Infection Immediately

Fistulas, grafts, and especially catheters can become infected, and access infection can progress to bacteremia and sepsis rapidly. The signs are redness, warmth, swelling, tenderness, or drainage at the access site; fever or chills; and sometimes generalized illness without an obvious source. Any of these warrants urgent contact with the dialysis team or urgent care, because access infections require antibiotics and sometimes access removal. Do not wait and see; an infected access can seed the bloodstream within hours. Catheter exit-site infections are especially common and dangerous and require prompt evaluation.

### Maintain Central Venous Catheter Hygiene With Meticulous Sterile Technique

A tunneled central venous catheter (a permanent or temporary line, usually in the chest or neck, going to a large vein near the heart) is the highest-infection-risk access, and its care demands meticulous technique. The catheter and its exit site must be kept clean and dry, the dressing changed per protocol (by trained staff or trained caregiver with sterile technique), the catheter never opened except by trained staff for dialysis, the site monitored daily for redness, drainage, or tenderness, and the catheter protected from water (no swimming, and careful showering per the team's instruction). Do not use the catheter for anything other than dialysis. Any fever, chills, or exit-site change in a catheter patient is a potential bloodstream infection and requires urgent evaluation.

### Prevent Dialysis Access-Related Bleeding And Bruising Through Technique

Dialysis patients often take anticoagulants and have fragile skin and vessels, and the needle sites can bleed and bruise. Support skin protection (gentle handling, avoid tape trauma, moisturize fragile skin per the team's guidance), ensure adequate hold time after needle removal, and report unusual bruising, bleeding, or a growing lump at the access site (which may be a hematoma or a pseudoaneurysm that needs evaluation). A pulsating lump at the access site is concerning and should be reported.

### Coordinate Access Care With The Dialysis And Vascular Teams

The dialysis center monitors the access at each treatment (flow, pressures, recirculation, appearance), and the vascular access team handles declotting, revisions, and new access creation. Report any home observation (loss of thrill, bleeding, infection signs, swelling, changes in the arm) to the dialysis team promptly, keep all access-monitoring appointments, and ensure the person's full medical team knows the access type and location (so no one uses the access arm for blood pressure or IVs). Access preservation is a team effort, and early reporting saves accesses.

## Common Traps

### Letting Blood Pressure Or IVs Be Done On The Access Arm

A nurse or caregiver takes blood pressure or draws blood from the access arm. The mechanism is not knowing or not checking which arm has the access. The false signal is that any arm works. The harm is that compression or puncture can damage or clot the access, ending its usefulness and requiring surgery. Protect the access arm from all compression and puncture.

### Missing The Loss Of The Thrill That Signals Clotting

The caregiver does not check the access daily or does not recognize the change. The mechanism is not knowing what to feel for. The false signal is that the access is fine unless there is pain. The harm is that a clotted access must be declotted within hours to days or it is lost, requiring a catheter and new surgery. Check the thrill daily and report changes the same day.

### Delaying Evaluation Of An Access Infection

The caregiver notes redness or drainage but waits for the next treatment. The mechanism is deferring to the schedule. The false signal is that it looks minor. The harm is that access infections progress to bloodstream infections and sepsis rapidly, and delay is dangerous. Report access infection signs urgently.

### Handling A Central Catheter Without Sterile Technique

A caregiver opens or manipulates the catheter without sterile precautions. The mechanism is not understanding the infection risk. The false signal is that the catheter is just a tube. The harm is that any contamination enters directly to the bloodstream near the heart, causing bacteremia and sepsis. Only trained staff handle the catheter, with sterile technique.

### Using Crushing Pressure Or A Tourniquet On Access Bleeding

The caregiver applies a tourniquet or crushing pressure to stop access bleeding. The mechanism is the instinct to stop bleeding. The false signal is that maximum pressure is safest. The harm is that crushing pressure can clot or rupture the access, and a tourniquet destroys the flow. Apply firm but controlled pressure and seek help for uncontrolled bleeding.

### Ignoring A Pulsating Lump At The Access Site

The caregiver notes a growing or pulsating lump but does not report it. The mechanism is not recognizing the significance. The false signal is that lumps are normal after needles. The harm is that a pseudoaneurysm or hematoma can rupture and bleed catastrophically. Report any new, growing, or pulsating lump.

## Self-Check

Before treating dialysis access care and infection prevention as complete, verify:

- The fistula or graft arm was protected from all compression and puncture (no blood pressure, no blood draws, no IVs, no tight clothing, no sleeping on it).
- The access was assessed daily for thrill and bruit, with the baseline known and any change (loss of thrill, change to pulsation) reported the same day.
- Access bleeding was managed with firm controlled pressure, and heavy or uncontrolled bleeding was treated as an emergency without tourniquets or crushing pressure.
- Access infection signs (redness, warmth, swelling, tenderness, drainage, fever) were recognized and reported urgently.
- Central venous catheter hygiene was maintained with meticulous sterile technique, water protection, daily site monitoring, and no use except for dialysis.
- Bleeding, bruising, and lumps at the access site were monitored, with pulsating or growing lumps reported.
- Access care was coordinated with the dialysis and vascular teams, with all appointments kept and the access type and location communicated to all providers.
- Did the work stay within scope, protecting and monitoring the access while deferring declotting, revision, infection treatment, and access procedures to the dialysis and vascular teams?
