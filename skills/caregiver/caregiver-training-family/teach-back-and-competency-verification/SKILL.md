# Teach-Back and Competency Verification

Training is not complete when the trainer has finished explaining; it is complete when the learner can perform the task safely and independently. The gap between these two points is where most family caregiver training fails. A family member who nods and says they understand may not; a family member who can repeat the steps verbally may not be able to execute them under stress. Teach-back—asking the learner to explain or demonstrate the task in their own words—and return demonstration are the tools that close this gap. They transform training from a one-way delivery into a verified transfer of competence, and they expose the gaps that would otherwise surface as errors at home.

This skill covers the verification of family caregiver competence: using teach-back and return demonstration, assessing against safety criteria, documenting verification, and deciding when more training or alternative support is needed.

This guidance addresses competency verification judgment. It does not replace clinical protocols, scope-of-practice rules, or institutional policy. Where a family caregiver cannot achieve safe competence, escalate to the clinical team for alternative arrangements.

## Core Rules

### Use teach-back for understanding, return demonstration for skill

Teach-back (the learner explains the task in their own words) verifies understanding; return demonstration (the learner performs the task) verifies skill. Use both, because they test different things. A caregiver who can explain a transfer but cannot execute it has partial competence; a caregiver who can execute but cannot explain warning signs has a different gap. Match the verification method to what must be verified.

### Frame teach-back as testing the trainer, not the learner

The way teach-back is framed shapes whether the learner admits confusion. Introduce it as "I want to make sure I explained this clearly—can you tell me back how you'll do it?" rather than "Show me you understand." The first invites honesty; the second invites performance of understanding that may not exist. A learner who feels tested hides gaps; a learner who feels the trainer is checking their own teaching reveals them.

### Define explicit safety criteria for each task

Before verifying, define what constitutes safe performance for the task: the critical steps that must be done correctly, the safety checks, the contraindications, and the response to problems. Verify against these criteria, not against a general impression of competence. A task performed "mostly right" with one critical safety step omitted is not safe competence.

### Verify under realistic conditions, including stress and interruption

Home care is performed under fatigue, interruption, and time pressure—conditions different from a calm training session. Where feasible, observe performance under conditions closer to reality: with the television on, with a timer, with a question that interrupts. Competence that survives realistic conditions is more reliable than competence demonstrated in ideal conditions.

### Document what was verified, not only that training occurred

Record the task, the method of verification (teach-back, return demonstration), the safety criteria assessed, the result, and any gaps identified and addressed. "Trained family in transfers" is not documentation of competence; "Family caregiver demonstrated independent, safe transfer using slide board with correct body mechanics and verbalized three safety checks" is. Documentation protects the patient and the caregiver and supports later review.

### Decide: competent, needs more training, or needs alternative support

Verification produces one of three conclusions: the caregiver is competent to perform independently; the caregiver needs more training or practice before independence; or the caregiver cannot achieve safe competence and alternative support (professional services, different placement, revised care plan) is needed. Do not declare competence to avoid a difficult conversation; do not declare incompetence without arranging the alternative support the patient needs.

## Common Traps

### Equating nodding or verbal agreement with understanding

A learner who nods and says "I understand" frequently does not. Verify with teach-back rather than asking "Do you understand?", which invites assent regardless of actual understanding.

### Verifying understanding but not skill, or vice versa

Explanation and execution are different competences. Use teach-back for one and return demonstration for the other; do not substitute.

### Framing teach-back as a test of the learner

When the learner feels tested, they perform understanding rather than revealing gaps. Frame teach-back as checking the trainer's explanation.

### Verifying against a general impression rather than explicit criteria

"Looked fine" is not competency verification. Define the critical safety steps and verify each one.

### Declaring competence to avoid difficult conversations

Declaring a caregiver competent because the alternative is uncomfortable leaves the patient at risk. Where competence is not achieved, arrange alternative support.

### Inadequate documentation

"Trained" without detail is not verification documentation. Record what was verified, how, against what criteria, and with what result.

## Self-Check

- Have I used teach-back to verify understanding and return demonstration to verify skill, matching method to what must be verified?
- Did I frame teach-back as checking my own explanation rather than testing the learner?
- Did I define explicit safety criteria for the task before verifying, and assess each criterion?
- Did I observe performance under realistic conditions where feasible, not only ideal conditions?
- Is my documentation specific: task, method, criteria, result, gaps identified and addressed?
- Did I reach a clear conclusion: competent, needs more training, or needs alternative support?
- Where the caregiver needs alternative support, have I escalated to the clinical team and arranged it?
- Have I scheduled reinforcement or re-verification for tasks prone to decay?
