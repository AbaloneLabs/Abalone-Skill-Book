# Recognizing And Responding To Medical Emergencies

The judgment problem in a medical emergency is that the window for effective treatment is short and narrows fast, while the social and emotional cost of "overreacting" feels large and immediate. A stroke treated within the first hour has dramatically better outcomes than one treated three hours later; a heart attack treated early saves heart muscle that dies permanently while a caregiver deliberates. Yet caregivers routinely delay the 911 call — not because they fail to notice the symptom, but because they are afraid of being wrong, afraid of the cost or fuss of an ambulance, afraid of upsetting the client or family, and unsure whether the situation is "really" an emergency. The skilled caregiver's job is to recognize the red flags, to treat time as the critical resource it is, and to act on the side of escalation — because the cost of an unnecessary ambulance is recoverable and the cost of a delayed one is often not.

This is a high-stakes domain. The caregiver is not a clinician and does not diagnose, but is frequently the first and only person present when a stroke, heart attack, severe hypoglycemia, sepsis, or other acute event begins. The caregiver's role is to recognize the warning signs, activate the emergency response immediately, keep the person safe and monitored while help arrives, and communicate clearly to responders and family. Two failures dominate this domain: the delayed 911 call driven by fear of overreacting, and the misattribution of stroke or acute confusion to the person's baseline dementia, UTI, or "a bad day." Both are preventable, and both are common.

## Core Rules

### Treat Time As The Critical Resource, Not The Diagnosis

In an acute medical event, the single most important variable is not whether you are certain of the diagnosis — you will rarely be — but how fast expert help reaches the person. Stroke protocols and cardiac interventions are time-dependent; every minute of delay translates to tissue loss that is often irreversible. This reframes your decision: the question is not "am I sure this is a stroke?" but "is there enough possibility this is time-critical that I should call now?" When the answer is yes, call. An ambulance that turns out to be unnecessary is a minor inconvenience and a small cost; a stroke that receives no treatment for three hours because the caregiver waited to be sure is a permanent disability. Bias toward the call.

### Know And Use FAST For Stroke, And Know Its Limits

Stroke recognition rests on a small set of sudden-onset signs. Use FAST and its extensions consistently, and check them on anyone with sudden neurologic change:

- **F — Face:** Ask the person to smile. Look for drooping on one side, an uneven smile, or numbness on one side of the face.
- **A — Arms:** Ask the person to raise both arms and hold them. Watch for one arm drifting downward or being unable to rise — a sign of one-sided weakness.
- **S — Speech:** Ask the person to repeat a simple sentence. Listen for slurring, wrong words, inability to speak, or inability to understand you.
- **T — Time:** If any of these are present, note the time the symptoms started (this drives treatment eligibility) and call 911 immediately.

Extend beyond the classic four: sudden confusion, sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes, sudden severe headache with no known cause, sudden dizziness or loss of balance, and sudden numbness or weakness in a leg are all stroke signs. The defining feature is **sudden onset** — a change that appeared over minutes, not the gradual decline of dementia. Suddenness is the signal; treat any sudden neurologic change as possible stroke until a clinician says otherwise.

### Recognize Cardiac Red Flags And Atypical Presentation

Cardiac emergencies in older adults do not always present as the classic crushing chest pain. Recognize both typical and atypical presentations:

- Chest pain, pressure, tightness, squeezing, or heaviness, especially spreading to the jaw, neck, shoulder, arm, or back.
- Sudden or severe shortness of breath, with or without chest discomfort.
- Palpitations, a very fast or very slow pulse, or an irregular pulse the person finds distressing.
- Sudden cold sweat, nausea, vomiting, or lightheadedness.
- Unexplained extreme fatigue, especially in women, in people with diabetes, and in older adults — fatigue can be the dominant or only symptom of a heart attack.
- Upper abdominal pain or indigestion-like discomfort that is unusual for the person and accompanied by other signs.

Atypical presentations are the rule rather than the exception in elderly and diabetic clients, who may feel little or no chest pain. Do not be reassured by the absence of classic chest pain; weigh the cluster of signs and the person's baseline, and escalate when the picture fits.

### Decide 911 Versus Family Versus Nurse By Urgency, Not By Comfort

The decision of whom to call is a triage decision based on how time-critical the situation is, not on who is easiest or least disruptive to reach.

- **Call 911 immediately** for any possible stroke, any cardiac red flags, loss of consciousness, severe difficulty breathing, uncontrolled bleeding, suspected hypoglycemia with reduced responsiveness, a seizure, or any sudden severe decline you cannot explain. Do not call the family first to ask permission; do not call the nurse to "run it by them" first. Call 911, then notify others.
- **Call the nurse or supervisor urgently** for acute changes that are concerning but not clearly life-threatening — a fall with possible injury, a sudden change in mental status that is not clearly stroke, new pain, a fever with confusion — and follow their direction, while being ready to escalate to 911 if the person deteriorates.
- **Call the family** to inform and to gather history (medications, baseline, prior episodes), but family notification does not precede the 911 call in a time-critical emergency. Families can also pressure you to wait or to avoid the hospital; do not let that pressure delay emergency care.

When in doubt between 911 and a lower tier, choose 911. The threshold for an ambulance is "this could be time-critical and I am not certain it is not," not "I am certain this is an emergency."

### Do Not Attribute Acute Change To Baseline Dementia, A UTI, Or "A Bad Day"

One of the most dangerous and most common errors is explaining away an acute change as the person's usual state. Sudden confusion, slurred speech, weakness, or unresponsiveness in a person with dementia is a stroke or other acute event until proven otherwise — it is not "just their dementia acting up." The mechanism of harm is that the person already has confusion, slurred speech, or unsteady gait as their baseline, so the new stroke signs blend in and are read as the normal state. The defense is to compare against the person's **own baseline that day and that week**, to treat any **change from baseline** as a red flag regardless of the baseline, and to escalate sudden changes even in a person whose normal is already impaired. Similarly, while a UTI can cause confusion, do not assume confusion is a UTI and delay escalation — a UTI is a clinician's diagnosis, and the same confusion could be stroke, sepsis, hypoxia, or hypoglycemia.

### Keep The Person Safe And Monitored While Help Arrives

Once you have called, your job is to prevent further harm and to preserve information for responders.

- For suspected stroke, keep the person comfortable, note the exact time of symptom onset and the last time they were known well, and do not give food, drink, or medications (aspiration risk and because some treatments depend on a clear intake history).
- For suspected cardiac event, help the person into a comfortable position (usually sitting up if breathless, or semi-reclined), loosen tight clothing, keep them calm, and have any prescribed nitroglycerin available only if their care plan authorizes it and you are within scope — do not administer medications outside your authorization.
- For any reduced responsiveness, monitor breathing continuously and be prepared to support the airway; place in the recovery position if vomiting or if the airway is at risk, unless a spinal injury is possible.
- Gather the medication list, recent vitals if taken, the care plan, and any advance directives, and have them ready to hand to responders.
- Note the time of onset, what you observed, what the person said, and what changed — this timeline is clinically critical, especially the "last known well" time for stroke.

### Document And Report The Emergency And Its Timeline

Accurate, time-stamped documentation is part of the care in an emergency, not paperwork afterward. Record the time you first noticed the change, the last known well time, the exact signs you observed (in the person's and your own words), the time you called 911, who you spoke to, when help arrived, what was done, and the person's status at handover. Record the decision-making: why you escalated, what you ruled in or out. Objective contemporaneous notes protect the person, support the clinical team, and protect the caregiver if questions arise.

## Common Traps

### Delaying The 911 Call From Fear Of Overreacting

The caregiver notices the symptom but hesitates, because calling 911 feels drastic, embarrassing, costly, and likely to upset the client and family — and because "what if it's nothing?" feels like a real question. The mechanism is that the social and emotional cost of an unnecessary ambulance is vivid and immediate, while the cost of a delayed ambulance is invisible and future, so the mind weights the visible cost and waits. The false signal is the person's momentary stability or the wish that the symptom will pass, which is read as evidence the situation is not urgent. The harm is that stroke and cardiac treatments are time-bound, and the minutes spent deliberating are minutes of brain or heart tissue lost permanently; the person who could have walked and talked with early treatment is left disabled or dead. The defense is to reframe the decision around time rather than certainty — to recognize that the recoverable cost of overreacting is tiny compared to the irreversible cost of underreacting — and to set a personal rule that any FAST sign, any cardiac red flag, or any sudden severe decline triggers the call without further deliberation.

### Calling The Family First To Ask Whether To Call 911

In a time-critical emergency, the caregiver reaches for the family member rather than 911, wanting guidance, permission, or reassurance before taking the dramatic step. The mechanism is that the family feels like the right authority and the call feels less drastic than an ambulance, so the caregiver defers the 911 call until the family weighs in. The harm is twofold: the family may not answer immediately, may be hard to reach, or may minimize the situation ("oh, Mom does this"), and the minutes lost are irreversible; and the family may actively pressure the caregiver to wait, avoid the hospital, or "see how she is in an hour," which delays care further. The defense is to call 911 first in any time-critical emergency, then notify the family; to treat family input as historical and informational, not as authorization to delay; and to recognize that no family member's preference overrides the person's need for emergency care.

### Mistaking Stroke For The Person's Dementia Or Baseline Confusion

A client with dementia suddenly becomes more confused, slurs words, or develops one-sided weakness, and the caregiver attributes it to "a bad dementia day," a UTI, fatigue, or agitation — because confusion and unsteadiness are already part of this person's normal. The mechanism is that the new deficit is invisible against the noisy background of the existing impairment, so the change is read as fluctuation rather than as an acute event. The false signal is the person's pre-existing confusion, which provides a ready-made explanation that requires no escalation. The harm is that an acute stroke in a person with dementia goes untreated, and the resulting disability is added permanently to the dementia, when early treatment could have reversed it. The defense is to anchor every assessment to the person's own baseline and to treat any **change from that baseline**, especially sudden change, as a red flag; to specifically check FAST signs rather than rely on a general impression; and to never accept "it's just their dementia" as an explanation for an acute decline.

### Waiting To See If The Symptom Resolves Before Calling

The caregiver observes a symptom — mild weakness, slurred speech, chest heaviness — and decides to watch for a few minutes to see if it passes, treating resolution as confirmation it was minor. The mechanism is that transient symptoms (a TIA, stuttering chest pain) often do partially resolve, so the wait appears vindicated and reinforces the habit. The false signal is the temporary improvement, which is read as evidence the event is over. The harm is that a TIA is a warning of an imminent stroke and stuttering chest pain is a warning of an imminent heart attack, and the "improvement" is the calm before the definitive event; the minutes or hours spent watching are exactly the window in which preventive treatment is most effective. The defense is to treat any FAST sign or cardiac red flag as an emergency even if it resolves, to call 911 for transient neurologic symptoms (they may be a TIA preceding a stroke), and to recognize that resolution is not safety — it is often the most dangerous moment to wait.

### Being Reassured By The Absence Of Classic Symptoms

The client has no chest pain, so the caregiver rules out a heart attack; the client has no facial droop, so the caregiver rules out stroke. The mechanism is that the classic textbook presentation is treated as a checklist, and absence of the most famous sign is read as absence of the condition. The false signal is the missing classic symptom, which feels reassuring. The harm is that atypical presentations are common in older adults, women, and people with diabetes — a heart attack may present as fatigue or nausea alone, a stroke as sudden confusion or visual change alone — and ruling out based on the absence of the headline sign misses the diagnosis entirely. The defense is to weigh the cluster of signs and the change from baseline rather than require the textbook presentation, to know the atypical presentations, and to escalate based on the overall picture of sudden, unexplained decline even when no single classic sign is present.

### Assuming Confusion Means A UTI And Missing Something Worse

Confusion in an older adult is so often attributed to a UTI that the caregiver may jump to that explanation and plan to call the nurse in the morning, rather than consider stroke, sepsis, hypoxia, hypoglycemia, or medication toxicity — all of which can also present as confusion. The mechanism is that "confusion equals UTI" is a familiar, low-urgency script that feels manageable, so it is preferred over the alarming alternatives. The false signal is the plausibility of the UTI explanation, which closes off consideration of more dangerous causes. The harm is that a life-threatening cause of confusion is managed as a minor infection, and the window for treatment of a stroke, sepsis, or hypoglycemia closes while the caregiver waits for a urine culture. The defense is to treat sudden or severe confusion as a red flag requiring urgent clinical assessment of its cause, not as a diagnosis; to check a glucose if hypoglycemia is possible and within scope; and to escalate immediately if the confusion is accompanied by any FAST sign, fever with rapid deterioration, or any other red flag.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I treat time as the critical resource and call 911 immediately for any possible stroke, cardiac event, or sudden severe decline — rather than waiting to be certain?
- [ ] Did I check FAST (face, arms, speech) plus the extended stroke signs (sudden confusion, vision change, severe headache, dizziness, leg weakness), and note the last-known-well time?
- [ ] Did I recognize both classic and atypical cardiac presentations — including fatigue, nausea, shortness of breath, and jaw/back discomfort without chest pain — in elderly and diabetic clients?
- [ ] Did I choose whom to call based on urgency, calling 911 first for time-critical emergencies and notifying the family afterward, rather than seeking family permission before the ambulance?
- [ ] Did I compare the person's signs against their own baseline and treat any sudden change from baseline as a red flag, instead of attributing it to dementia, a UTI, fatigue, or a bad day?
- [ ] Did I avoid waiting to see if symptoms resolved, recognizing that a TIA or stuttering chest pain is a warning that demands escalation, not reassurance?
- [ ] Did I keep the person safe and monitored — comfortable position, no food/drink for suspected stroke, airway protected, medications and advance directives ready for responders?
- [ ] Did I document the timeline — onset, last known well, time of the 911 call, observations, actions, and status at handover — objectively and contemporaneously?
- [ ] Did I stay within my scope — recognizing, escalating, keeping safe, and documenting — rather than diagnosing, administering unauthorized medications, or deciding the person does not need emergency care?
