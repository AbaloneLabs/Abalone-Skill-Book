# Fall Response And Post-Fall Assessment

The judgment problem at a fall is that the body's and the social environment's impulses are almost all wrong. The person on the floor wants to get up; the caregiver wants to help them up; bystanders want the scene restored to normal; and the schedule wants to move on. But the single most dangerous action after a fall is often the lifting itself. A person who has fallen may have a spinal injury that movement will worsen into paralysis, a hip fracture that standing on will displace, or a head injury whose only warning signs emerge over the next hours. The skilled caregiver's first job is to resist the impulse to lift, to perform a deliberate assessment before any movement, and to recognize the situations in which the person must not be moved at all. The second job, equally important and equally neglected, is to investigate why the fall happened — because a fall is rarely random, and the cause (often a medication, an infection, or a new cardiac or neurologic event) is usually more dangerous than the injury it produced.

This is a high-stakes domain. A fall can be the visible tip of a life-threatening underlying event — a stroke, a cardiac arrhythmia, a gastrointestinal bleed causing faintness, a sepsis causing collapse — and focusing only on the bruise or the broken bone misses the cause that will kill the person. The caregiver's role is to assess before moving, to keep the person still when injury is possible, to recognize red flags for emergency escalation, to document the injury and the circumstances precisely, and to ensure the fall triggers an investigation of causes rather than being written off as clumsiness. The caregiver does not diagnose, does not declare the person uninjured, and does not move a possibly injured person without clinical guidance.

## Core Rules

### Assess Before You Move — The First Act Is Observation, Not Lifting

When you reach a person who has fallen, your first action is to slow down and assess, not to lift. Rushing to lift is the most common and most dangerous error.

- Approach calmly, speak to the person, and note their responsiveness and orientation. A person who is unresponsive, confused, or unable to answer simple questions may have a head injury, a stroke, or another acute event that caused the fall and is still in progress.
- Ask where they have pain, and ask them not to move while you assess. Move your eyes, not their body, to look for obvious deformity, swelling, bruising, bleeding, or a limb at an unnatural angle.
- Specifically check the high-injury areas: the head (any contact, bump, or laceration), the neck and spine (pain, tenderness, numbness or tingling), the hips and pelvis (pain, inability to bear weight, leg shortening or rotation), and any limb that hurts or looks wrong.
- Check the basics of airway, breathing, and circulation; note the pulse and breathing rate if you can, and watch for labored breathing.

Only after this assessment do you decide whether movement is even an option. In most cases where any injury is possible, it is not.

### Perform A Spinal And Neurologic Check Before Any Movement

The spinal check is the step most often skipped, because spinal injury is invisible and the person usually feels able to move. Skipping it can convert a stable spinal injury into a permanent paralysis.

- Ask the person to keep still and ask specifically about neck and back pain, and about any numbness, tingling, or weakness in their arms or legs. Spinal injury should be suspected after any fall with a head impact, any fall down stairs, any fall from a height, or any fall in which the person cannot describe what happened.
- Ask the person to gently wiggle their fingers and toes; any inability, asymmetry, or new numbness is a red flag for spinal injury and means do not move them.
- Check for stroke signs (FAST) as part of the neurologic assessment — a fall can be the first visible sign of a stroke, and the person on the floor may have facial droop, slurred speech, or one-sided weakness that explains the fall.
- Check for cardiac red flags — chest pain, palpitations, shortness of breath, irregular pulse — because a fall can be the result of a faint from a cardiac event that is still active.

If there is any suspicion of spinal injury, any new numbness or weakness, or any FAST or cardiac sign, do not move the person — keep them still, summon emergency help, and stabilize the neck in the position found without twisting.

### Know When NOT To Move The Person

There are clear situations in which the person must not be moved except to protect the airway or remove them from immediate danger. Memorize these as hard stops:

- Suspected spinal injury (neck or back pain, numbness, tingling, weakness, or a mechanism suggesting it).
- Any head injury with loss of consciousness, confusion, or a person on anticoagulants (blood thinners) — even a minor head bump on a blood thinner carries a risk of intracranial bleeding.
- Suspected fracture, especially of the hip, pelvis, spine, or a limb that is deformed — standing or moving on a hip fracture can displace it catastrophically.
- Any FAST sign, chest pain, severe shortness of breath, or other red-flag symptom suggesting the fall had a serious medical cause.
- Uncontrolled bleeding, a seizure, vomiting, or incontinence associated with the fall.
- The person is unable to cooperate, is in severe pain, or you are unsure whether they are injured.

In all of these, keep the person still, cover them for warmth and modesty, summon emergency services, and stay with them continuously. "I think they're fine" is not a clinical clearance; when in doubt, keep them still and wait for a clinician.

### Move The Person Only When It Is Safe And Necessary

Movement is appropriate only when the person is alert, fully oriented, in no significant pain, has no deformity and no red-flag symptoms, the spinal and neurologic checks are normal, and the fall was clearly minor (a slip from a seated position, a stumble onto a carpeted surface). Even then:

- Get the person's full cooperation and use correct technique; never lift by pulling on the arms, which can injure shoulders and does not protect the spine.
- Use a second person and proper equipment (a transfer board, a hoist, or a mechanical lift) whenever there is any doubt; do not attempt a solo lift of a heavy or unsteady person.
- If you are unsure whether the person is injured, the default is to keep them still and wait for a nurse or emergency services to assess. The cost of waiting is comfort and time; the cost of a wrong lift is a displaced fracture or a worsened spinal injury.

### Investigate The Cause — A Fall Is A Symptom, Not An Event

A fall is rarely random. It is usually the visible result of an underlying factor, and that factor is often more dangerous than the injury. Treat every fall as a signal to investigate causes.

- **Medications are the most common modifiable cause.** Review the medication list for drugs that cause falls: antihypertensives (postural hypotension, dizziness on standing), sedatives and sleeping pills (drowsiness, unsteadiness), antipsychotics and benzodiazepines (impaired balance), opioids (drowsiness, hypotension), diuretics (rushing to the toilet, dehydration), and blood sugar medications (hypoglycemia). A recent change, addition, or dose increase is a strong clue; a new fall within days of a medication change should be flagged as possibly medication-related and reported for clinical review.
- **Acute illness can present as a fall.** A new infection (urinary tract, respiratory, or other), dehydration, hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia, anemia from a gastrointestinal bleed, a cardiac arrhythmia, or a stroke can all cause a fall as the first sign. A fall with new confusion, fever, low or high glucose, pallor, or shortness of breath points to an acute cause that needs urgent clinical assessment, not just injury care.
- **Environmental factors** — poor lighting, loose rugs, clutter, wet floors, inappropriate footwear, a bed or chair at the wrong height — are common and modifiable and should be noted and addressed.
- **The pattern matters.** A first fall is a warning; a second fall, a fall with new symptoms, or a change in the pattern of falls should trigger a full clinical review of causes and a revision of the care plan, not simply more of the same precautions.

Report every fall, even a minor one with no apparent injury, so the cause can be investigated. The first fall is often the warning before the serious one.

### Document The Injury And The Circumstances Precisely

Accurate documentation is part of the care, not paperwork afterward. It supports clinical follow-up, enables pattern detection across falls, protects the person, and protects the caregiver.

- Record the time and location of the fall, and whether it was witnessed or the person was found on the floor.
- Record what the person was doing at the time and any symptoms they reported, in their own words where possible ("I felt dizzy," "my leg gave way," "I don't remember").
- Record your assessment findings: responsiveness, orientation, pain and its exact location, any visible injury, the spinal and neurologic check results, vital signs if taken, and any red-flag symptoms.
- Record what you did: who was called and when, when help arrived, what instructions were given, whether and how the person was moved, and their condition over time.
- Record the outcome: where the person went, and their status at handover.
- Record the medication list and any recent changes, and the environmental factors present — these feed the cause investigation.
- Avoid speculation about the cause ("must have tripped") and avoid blame; describe what you saw and heard, not what you infer. "Found on floor beside bed, no wet floor, stated 'I felt dizzy then my legs went'" is useful; "must have slipped" is not.

### Monitor For Delayed Effects After The Fall

A person who seems uninjured immediately after a fall can develop serious signs hours later. This is especially true for head injury (slow intracranial bleeding, particularly on anticoagulants) and for occult fractures.

- Monitor for worsening headache, confusion, drowsiness, vomiting, weakness, or visual changes for at least 24 hours after any head contact, and escalate any such change immediately as a possible intracranial bleed.
- Monitor for new or worsening pain, especially in the hip, back, or ribs, that appears hours later as an occult fracture becomes symptomatic.
- Monitor for the underlying cause: a fever developing after the fall may indicate the infection that caused it; continued dizziness or palpitations may indicate the cardiac cause that is still active.

Document the monitoring plan and the observations, and ensure the next shift knows to continue watching.

## Common Traps

### Lifting The Person Before Checking For Spinal Injury

The caregiver reaches the fallen person and, wanting to help and to relieve the awkwardness of the scene, lifts them to a chair before any spinal or neurologic assessment. The mechanism is that lifting feels like the helpful action and the spinal check feels like an unnecessary delay, especially when the person says they feel fine and wants to get up. The false signal is the person's ability and willingness to move, which is read as the absence of spinal injury. The harm is that a stable spinal fracture is displaced by the movement, converting a treatable injury into a permanent paralysis, or that an unrecognized head injury is worsened. The defense is to make the spinal and neurologic check a non-negotiable first step before any movement, to specifically ask about neck and back pain and test finger and toe movement, and to keep the person still whenever spinal injury is possible — recognizing that the minutes spent assessing are trivial compared to the lifetime cost of a worsened spinal injury.

### Moving A Person With A Possible Hip Fracture To Get Them Off The Floor

The person has fallen and has hip pain but can, with effort, be helped to stand and shuffle to a chair, so the caregiver does so to get them off the floor and out of view. The mechanism is that the floor is an undignified and uncomfortable place, and the person's ability to bear some weight is read as evidence the hip is not broken. The false signal is the partial weight-bearing, which is possible with some fractures. The harm is that weight-bearing on a hip fracture can displace it, converting a fracture that could be repaired into one that destroys the hip's blood supply and leads to avascular necrosis and major surgery. The defense is to treat any hip pain, any inability to bear weight, or any leg that is shortened or rotated as a suspected fracture, to keep the person still, and to summon emergency help rather than walking them to a chair.

### Assuming A Person On Anticoagulants Is Fine Because They "Look Fine"

A client on a blood thinner bumps their head in a fall, is alert and oriented minutes later, and the caregiver concludes the head is fine and puts them to bed. The mechanism is that the early presentation of an intracranial bleed is reassuring — the person looks and feels normal — and the absence of immediate symptoms is read as the absence of injury. The false signal is the lucid interval, the period of normal appearance before the bleed declares itself. The harm is that an intracranial hemorrhage on a blood thinner can develop over hours and become fatal, in a person who could have been scanned and treated if the head bump had been escalated. The defense is to treat any head contact in a person on anticoagulants as a red flag requiring escalation regardless of how well the person looks, to specifically document anticoagulant use, and to monitor for delayed signs (headache, confusion, drowsiness, vomiting, weakness) for at least 24 hours, escalating any change immediately.

### Treating The Fall As An Isolated Accident And Missing The Medication Cause

The person falls, is found to have no serious injury, and the incident is closed as a trip or a stumble, without any review of the medication list or the recent changes to it. The mechanism is that an injury-free fall feels resolved once the person is up and comfortable, so the cause is never investigated, and the ready explanation ("they tripped") closes the case. The false signal is the absence of injury, which is read as the absence of a serious underlying problem. The harm is that the fall was caused by a new antihypertensive causing postural hypotension, or a new sedative causing unsteadiness, and because the cause was not investigated the medication continues and the next fall — more serious — follows within weeks. The defense is to treat every fall as a signal to review the medication list and any recent changes, to flag a fall within days of a medication change as possibly medication-related, and to report every fall so the clinician can investigate the cause and adjust the regimen.

### Writing Off The Fall To "Confusion" Or "A Bad Day" And Missing An Acute Illness

An elderly client with dementia falls, and the fall is attributed to their confusion or unsteadiness without considering that the fall may be the first sign of a urinary tract infection, dehydration, hypoglycemia, a cardiac event, or a stroke. The mechanism is that the person's baseline impairment provides a ready explanation that requires no investigation, so the acute cause is hidden behind the chronic condition. The false signal is the plausibility of the baseline explanation. The harm is that a life-threatening acute illness — sepsis from a UTI, a gastrointestinal bleed causing faintness, a stroke — goes untreated because the fall was explained away, and the person deteriorates over the following hours or days. The defense is to treat any fall, especially with new or worsening confusion, as a reason to assess for an acute cause — check temperature, glucose if within scope, skin color and hydration, pulse and breathing — and to escalate any abnormality rather than accept the baseline explanation.

### Documenting The Fall Vaguely With An Inferred Cause

Under time pressure, the post-fall documentation is reduced to "resident found on floor, assisted to chair, no injury," with no detail about the assessment, the person's reported symptoms, the medications, or the circumstances, and the cause is filled in with inference ("must have tripped"). The mechanism is that documentation feels like a chore after a stressful event, so it is completed minimally, and the gaps are filled with a tidy guess. The false signal is that the form is complete, which is mistaken for the record being adequate. The harm is that the clinical team cannot investigate the cause from a vague record, patterns across falls cannot be detected, delayed injuries cannot be correlated with the event, and the caregiver's own account is unprotected if questions arise later. The defense is to document observations and the person's own words rather than inferences, to record times, specific findings, the spinal and neurologic checks, the medication list, and the environmental factors, and to complete the documentation promptly while details are fresh.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I resist the impulse to lift the person immediately, and assess responsiveness, orientation, pain, and visible injury before any movement?
- [ ] Did I perform a spinal and neurologic check — asking about neck and back pain, testing finger and toe movement, and checking for FAST and cardiac signs — before considering any movement?
- [ ] Did I keep the person still and summon emergency help when any red flag was present — suspected spinal injury, head injury (especially on anticoagulants), suspected fracture, FAST signs, chest pain, uncontrolled bleeding, seizure, vomiting, or incontinence?
- [ ] Did I move the person only when uninjured, fully cooperative, with normal spinal and neurologic checks, using correct technique and a second person or equipment when needed?
- [ ] Did I investigate the cause — reviewing the medication list and recent changes, checking for acute illness (fever, glucose, hydration, pulse), and noting environmental factors — rather than writing the fall off as an accident?
- [ ] Did I specifically consider and document anticoagulant use with any head contact, and plan to monitor for delayed intracranial bleed signs for at least 24 hours?
- [ ] Did I document the time, location, circumstances, witnessed or found, the person's own words, assessment findings, spinal and neurologic checks, actions taken, medication list, environmental factors, and outcome — without inferring a cause or assigning blame?
- [ ] Did I report every fall, including minor and unwitnessed ones, so the cause is investigated and the care plan revised?
- [ ] Did I monitor for delayed effects — headache, confusion, drowsiness, new pain, fever — in the hours and day after the fall, and escalate any change?
- [ ] Did I stay within my scope — assessing, keeping safe and still, summoning help, documenting, and reporting — rather than diagnosing, declaring the person uninjured, or moving a possibly injured person without clinical guidance?
