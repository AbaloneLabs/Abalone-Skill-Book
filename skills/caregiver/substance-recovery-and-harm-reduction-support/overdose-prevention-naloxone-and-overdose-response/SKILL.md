# Overdose Prevention, Naloxone, And Overdose Response

Overdose is a leading cause of preventable death, and the home setting — where a person uses substances, returns after treatment, or manages prescription opioids — is the most common site of fatal overdose. The judgment problem is that overdose is rapid, silent, and reversible if recognized and treated within minutes, but the signs are easy to misattribute (to sleep, to intoxication, to a "long night"), and the window for response is narrow. A caregiver who is unprepared, who lacks naloxone, or who hesitates out of fear, legal concern, or the belief that "it's not that serious" will watch a reversible event become a death. The agent must understand that overdose risk is predictable and elevated at specific moments (after a period of abstinence, when tolerance drops; with new or unknown supply; with mixing substances), that naloxone is a safe, lifesaving tool that anyone can use, and that the goal is to keep the person alive and connected to care, not to judge or punish. This is a domain where preparation and a non-judgmental, rapid response are the difference between life and death.

## Core Rules

### Assess Overdose Risk And Recognize High-Risk Moments

Overdose does not happen at random; risk concentrates at predictable moments, and recognizing them enables preparation. The highest-risk moments are: after any period of reduced or stopped use (discharge from detox, jail, or hospital; a break of even days), when tolerance has dropped and the old dose becomes lethal; when using alone (no one to respond); when mixing opioids with alcohol, benzodiazepines, or other depressants (which multiply the risk); when the supply changes (a new dealer, a new batch, fentanyl contamination); and with certain health conditions (lung, liver, kidney disease, older age). Assess these risks with the person (if possible) and those close to them, and intensify support and naloxone access at these moments. Do not assume that a "stable" user is safe; the risk is dynamic.

### Maintain Naloxone Access And An Overdose Response Plan

Naloxone (Narcan) reverses opioid overdose by displacing opioids from the receptors, and it must be immediately accessible, because the window is minutes. Ensure naloxone is in the home, carried by the person if they use alone, and known to those around them (family, roommates, caregivers). Know where it is, check expiration dates, and replace expired doses. Know the formulation (intranasal is most common and easiest; intramuscular injectable is also used) and how to use it — the steps are simple but must be learned in advance, not during a crisis. Have an overdose response plan: who calls emergency services, who gives naloxone, who does rescue breathing, and where the naloxone is kept. Practice the plan. Naloxone causes sudden withdrawal (uncomfortable but not dangerous) and wears off before the opioid, so the person can re-overdose; always call emergency services and stay until help arrives.

### Recognize The Signs Of Opioid Overdose Accurately

The signs of opioid overdose are specific and must be recognized without delay: unresponsiveness (the person does not respond to shouting, shaking, or a sternal rub), slow or absent breathing (fewer than about 10 breaths per minute, shallow, or stopped), snoring or gurgling (the airway partially blocked by a relaxed tongue), pinpoint pupils (tiny, in the case of opioid overdose), and blue or gray lips or fingertips (cyanosis, from low oxygen). The critical distinction from sleep or intoxication is that the person cannot be woken and is not breathing normally. If unsure, treat it as an overdose — the cost of naloxone when not needed is mild withdrawal; the cost of not giving it when needed is death. Do not wait for the person to "sleep it off," and do not assume loud snoring is normal.

### Respond To Overdose Rapidly And Correctly

The overdose response is a sequence that must be executed in minutes. First, stimulate and assess: shout the person's name and do a sternal rub (knuckles rubbed hard on the breastbone); if no response, check for breathing. Second, call emergency services (or have someone call) and get the naloxone. Third, give rescue breathing (if trained) — tilt the head, lift the chin, pinch the nose, and give a breath every five seconds; oxygen is the most immediate need. Fourth, administer naloxone (one spray into one nostril, or one injection into the outer thigh); wait two to three minutes, and if no response, give a second dose. Fifth, continue rescue breathing and stay with the person until help arrives. If the person wakes, they will be in withdrawal (agitated, nauseous, in pain) and may want to use again — explain that the naloxone will wear off and they could re-overdose, and encourage them to stay and accept further care. Never put the person in a cold shower, give other drugs, or leave them alone.

### Support Post-Overdose Recovery And Connection To Care

An overdose is a critical moment for connection to care, because the person has survived a near-death event and may be most open to change. After the immediate emergency, support the person medically (they need monitoring for re-overdose as naloxone wears off), emotionally (overdose is traumatic, and shame and fear are common), and practically (connection to treatment, harm reduction, and support services). Approach without judgment — overdose is a medical event, not a moral failing — and offer warm, concrete options: medication for opioid use disorder (methadone or buprenorphine), treatment programs, peer recovery support, and harm reduction services. Do not force or coerce; offer and support. Understand that recovery is a process with setbacks, and that keeping the person alive and connected is the foundation.

### Practice Harm Reduction To Prevent Overdose And Complications

Harm reduction reduces the harm of substance use without requiring abstinence, and it is evidence-based and lifesaving. Support and connect the person with harm reduction services: syringe service programs (which provide clean supplies, testing for fentanyl and xylazine, naloxone, and referrals), fentanyl test strips (to detect contamination), and education on safer use practices (never use alone, start with a small test dose, avoid mixing, carry naloxone). Support safe disposal of used supplies and access to wound care for injection-related infections. Do not refuse harm reduction because you believe it "enables" use — it prevents death, HIV and hepatitis C, and infections, and it is the bridge to treatment for many. Meet the person where they are.

### Address The Caregiver's Own Safety, Legal Concerns, And Support

The caregiver supporting a person at overdose risk faces stress, fear, and sometimes legal and safety concerns. Know the legal protections: most jurisdictions have Good Samaritan laws protecting those who call for help in an overdose, and naloxone access laws allowing lay use. Know your own safety: if the scene is unsafe (violence, weapons), call emergency services and do not enter. Seek your own support: supporting someone at overdose risk is exhausting and traumatic, and peer support, counseling, and respite are important. Do not carry this alone.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Person Is Just Sleeping Or Intoxicated

The caregiver sees unresponsiveness and snoring and assumes it is sleep. The mechanism is the normalizing of the appearance. The false signal is that snoring means sleeping. The harm is that overdose is missed and the person dies. Unresponsiveness with slow or absent breathing and pinpoint pupils is overdose until proven otherwise; assess and respond.

### Waiting To Call For Help Or Give Naloxone

The caregiver hesitates to call or give naloxone out of fear, legal concern, or denial. The mechanism is the hope that it will resolve on its own. The false signal is that waiting is safer. The harm is that the window closes and the person dies or suffers brain injury from lack of oxygen. Call immediately and give naloxone; the protections and the lifesaving benefit far outweigh the risks.

### Leaving After Naloxone Reverses The Overdose

The caregiver gives naloxone, the person wakes, and the caregiver leaves. The mechanism is the belief that reversal means it's over. The false signal is that waking means safety. The harm is that naloxone wears off (30 to 90 minutes) before the opioid, and the person re-overdoses, often fatally. Stay until emergency help arrives.

### Putting The Person In A Cold Shower Or Walking Them

The caregiver tries physical stimulation (cold shower, walking, slapping) instead of naloxone. The mechanism is folk belief about "waking" the person. The false signal is that stimulation reverses overdose. The harm is that it does not reverse the overdose, wastes time, and causes falls, aspiration, and delay. Give naloxone and rescue breathing.

### Refusing Harm Reduction Because It "Enables" Use

The caregiver withholds naloxone, clean supplies, or harm reduction education. The mechanism is the abstinence-only belief. The false signal is that reducing harm prolongs use. The harm is that the person is left at higher risk of death, infection, and isolation, with no bridge to care. Harm reduction saves lives and connects to treatment; offer it.

### Judging Or Punishing After An Overdose

The caregiver responds with anger, shame, or coercion after an overdose. The mechanism is moral framing of a medical event. The false signal is that shame prevents future use. The harm is that the person is driven away from care and support, increasing risk. Respond with compassion and offer connection.

## Self-Check

Before treating overdose prevention, naloxone, and overdose response as complete, verify:

- Overdose risk was assessed, and high-risk moments (post-abstinence, using alone, mixing, supply change, health conditions) were identified and prepared for.
- Naloxone was maintained in the home and with the person, with expiration checked, the formulation and use learned, and an overdose response plan practiced.
- The signs of opioid overdose (unresponsiveness, slow or absent breathing, snoring/gurgling, pinpoint pupils, cyanosis) were recognized, and the distinction from sleep or intoxication was clear.
- The overdose response sequence (stimulate and assess, call for help, rescue breathing, naloxone administration, staying until help arrives) was known and ready to execute.
- Post-overdose recovery was supported with medical monitoring (re-overdose risk), emotional support without judgment, and connection to treatment, medication, peer recovery, and harm reduction.
- Harm reduction services (syringe services, fentanyl testing, naloxone, safer-use education, wound care) were offered and connected.
- The caregiver's own safety, legal protections (Good Samaritan, naloxone access), and support needs were addressed.
- Did the work stay within scope, preventing and responding to overdose and connecting to care while deferring medical treatment and addiction medicine to clinicians?
