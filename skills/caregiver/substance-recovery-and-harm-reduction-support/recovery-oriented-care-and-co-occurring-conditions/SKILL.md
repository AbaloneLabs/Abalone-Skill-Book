# Recovery-Oriented Care And Co-Occurring Conditions

Sustained recovery from substance use disorder rarely happens in isolation; it unfolds alongside other health conditions — chronic pain, hepatitis C or HIV, liver disease, depression, anxiety, trauma, COPD, heart disease — and these conditions interact with recovery in complex ways. The judgment problem is that addiction care and general medical care are often siloed, so a person in recovery may have their pain undertreated (because clinicians fear enabling addiction), their mental health untreated (because it is attributed to the substance use), or their recovery destabilized by a medical treatment (an opioid prescription after surgery, a medication that interacts with recovery medications). A caregiver who addresses only the recovery or only the medical condition misses the interaction, and the person falls through the gaps between systems. The agent must understand that co-occurring conditions are the rule, not the exception, in recovery, that integrated care across addiction, medical, and behavioral health is the standard, and that the caregiver's role is to hold the whole picture together — advocating for coordinated, non-stigmatizing, recovery-oriented care of the whole person.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Common Co-Occurring Conditions And Their Interactions

Substance use disorder rarely travels alone, and knowing the common companions guides integrated care. Chronic pain is extremely common (and often drove the opioid use), and its undertreatment is a major relapse trigger. Mental health conditions — depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder, ADHD — co-occur in the majority, and each must be treated in its own right, not as a symptom of the substance use. Infectious diseases (hepatitis C, HIV, endocarditis, abscesses) result from injection drug use and need ongoing care. Liver disease (from alcohol or hepatitis) complicates medication choices. Lung disease (from smoking, common in recovery) and heart disease are common. Tobacco use disorder is nearly universal in recovery and is the leading cause of death in this population. Know the person's full condition list, and understand how each interacts with recovery: chronic pain drives craving, mental illness drives relapse, and medical events (surgery, injuries) introduce high-risk medications.

### Advocate For Integrated, Non-Stigmatizing Care

People in recovery face pervasive stigma in the healthcare system — being labeled "drug-seeking," having pain undertreated, being denied appropriate medications, or being treated with suspicion — and this stigma causes real harm (undertreated pain, avoided care, destabilized recovery). The caregiver's advocacy role is critical. Accompany the person to appointments when possible, document the recovery status and the treatment plan (including medication-assisted treatment), and advocate for evidence-based, non-stigmatizing care. Insist that pain be treated (chronic pain in recovery is best managed with non-opioid and multidisciplinary approaches, but acute pain and surgery sometimes require opioids, managed with the addiction team). Insist that mental health conditions be treated. Insist that the person not be denied care because of their history. Build relationships with providers who understand addiction as a medical condition.

### Manage Medications Safely Across Recovery And Other Conditions

Medication management in recovery with co-occurring conditions is complex, because medications can support recovery (methadone, buprenorphine, naltrexone, acamprosate), interact with it (sedatives, opioids, certain pain and psychiatric medications), or be misused. Maintain an accurate, current medication list (including recovery medications, psychiatric medications, and all other prescriptions and over-the-counter drugs), and ensure every provider has it. Flag medications that pose risk (opioids, benzodiazepines, stimulants, sedating medications) and ensure they are prescribed only when necessary, with the addiction team involved. Be aware of liver-toxic medications in liver disease, kidney-toxic medications in kidney disease, and interactions with HIV and hepatitis C medications. Ensure the person takes recovery medications reliably (missed buprenorphine or methadone destabilizes recovery and risks relapse). Use a single pharmacy for interaction checking. The medication picture must be coherent across all conditions.

### Support Chronic Pain Management Without Destabilizing Recovery

Chronic pain is one of the most challenging co-occurring conditions in recovery, because the most familiar pain tools (opioids) are high-risk, and undertreated pain drives relapse. Support a multidisciplinary, non-opioid approach: physical therapy, exercise, and movement; non-opioid medications (acetaminophen, NSAIDs, certain antidepressants and anticonvulsants for nerve pain); interventional treatments (injections, nerve blocks); behavioral approaches (cognitive behavioral therapy for pain, acceptance and commitment therapy, mindfulness); complementary approaches (acupuncture, massage); and self-management (pacing, sleep, stress reduction). For acute pain (surgery, injury), advocate for adequate pain control with the addiction team involved — undertreatment is not recovery-oriented, and a coordinated plan (regional anesthesia, multimodal approaches, short carefully-managed opioids if necessary) is possible. The goal is function and quality of life, with recovery protected.

### Address Mental Health And Trauma As Core Recovery Work

Co-occurring mental health conditions and trauma are not secondary to recovery; they are core drivers, and untreated, they are the most common cause of relapse. Support engagement in mental health care (therapy, psychiatric medication) for depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder, and other conditions, and ensure these are treated as real conditions, not attributed to the substance use or recovery. Trauma is nearly universal in this population (childhood trauma, violence, overdose loss, the trauma of active addiction), and trauma-informed care — which recognizes the prevalence and impact of trauma and avoids re-traumatization — is the standard. Support trauma-specific treatment (EMDR, trauma-focused CBT) when the person is stable. Understand that mental health symptoms in early recovery may be protracted withdrawal or adjustment, but persistent symptoms need treatment. Recovery without mental health care is incomplete.

### Support Health Restoration After Active Addiction

Active addiction takes a heavy physical toll, and recovery is a period of physical restoration that the caregiver can actively support. Support nutrition (malnutrition is common; a balanced diet, vitamins as needed, and addressing specific deficiencies), sleep (sleep architecture is disrupted for months; support sleep hygiene and patience), exercise (which supports mood, craving reduction, and physical recovery), dental care (methamphetamine and other substances cause severe dental disease), and preventive care (vaccinations, cancer screening, smoking cessation). Address hepatitis C (now curable with well-tolerated medications) and HIV (now manageable as chronic conditions). Support smoking cessation, because tobacco is the leading killer in this population, but time it carefully (early recovery may not be the moment). Physical restoration reinforces recovery.

### Coordinate Care Across The Addiction, Medical, And Behavioral Health Systems

The person in recovery with co-occurring conditions navigates multiple systems — addiction treatment, primary care, specialty medical (hepatology, infectious disease, pain), behavioral health, and social services — and these systems rarely coordinate well. The caregiver often holds the picture together. Maintain the care plan and medication list, ensure information flows between providers (with the person's consent), accompany to key appointments, and identify a care coordinator or primary care provider who can anchor the care. Use electronic health records and patient portals where available. Address the social determinants — housing, food, income, transportation, legal issues — that profoundly affect recovery and health, by connecting to social services and community resources. Integrated, coordinated care is the standard, and the caregiver is often the integrator.

## Common Traps

### Siloing Addiction Care From Medical And Mental Health Care

The caregiver or system treats the addiction separately from the medical and mental health conditions. The mechanism is the structural separation of services. The false signal is that each system handles its part. The harm is that interactions are missed, conditions are untreated, and recovery destabilizes. Integrate care across all systems.

### Undertreating Pain Because Of Recovery Status

The clinician or caregiver refuses adequate pain treatment because of the addiction history. The mechanism is the fear of enabling. The false signal is that no opioids is always safer. The harm is that severe pain drives relapse, and the person suffers unnecessarily. Advocate for adequate, multidisciplinary pain care.

### Attributing All Symptoms To The Substance Use Or Recovery

The caregiver or clinician dismisses mental health or physical symptoms as "just the addiction." The mechanism is the single-diagnosis bias. The false signal is that the addiction explains everything. The harm is that co-occurring depression, trauma, infection, or disease goes untreated. Treat each condition in its own right.

### Ignoring Stigma And Its Impact On Care

The caregiver does not recognize or address the stigma the person faces. The mechanism is the invisibility of stigma to those not targeted. The false signal is that care is care. The harm is that the person receives worse care, avoids the system, and outcomes suffer. Advocate for non-stigmatizing, evidence-based care.

### Discontinuing Recovery Medications During Medical Care

A clinician stops methadone or buprenorphine during a hospitalization or medical treatment. The mechanism is not understanding recovery medications. The false signal is that they are optional or dangerous. The harm is that withdrawal, craving, and relapse are triggered. Ensure recovery medications are continued across all care.

### Neglecting Physical Restoration And Preventive Care

The caregiver focuses only on the addiction and ignores nutrition, dental, sleep, and preventive care. The mechanism is the acute-addiction focus. The false signal is that stopping the substance is enough. The harm is that physical health problems accumulate and undermine recovery. Support comprehensive health restoration.

## Self-Check

Before treating recovery-oriented care and co-occurring conditions as complete, verify:

- The common co-occurring conditions (chronic pain, mental health, infectious disease, liver and lung disease, tobacco) and their interactions with recovery were understood and mapped for this person.
- Integrated, non-stigmatizing care was advocated for, with the recovery status documented, pain and mental health treated, and providers engaged who understand addiction as a medical condition.
- Medications were managed safely across all conditions (current list, recovery medications protected, high-risk medications flagged, interactions checked, single pharmacy).
- Chronic pain was supported with a multidisciplinary, non-opioid approach, with acute pain adequately treated and the addiction team involved.
- Mental health and trauma were addressed as core recovery work, with engagement in therapy and psychiatric care and trauma-informed treatment.
- Physical restoration (nutrition, sleep, exercise, dental, preventive care, hepatitis C and HIV treatment, smoking cessation) was supported.
- Care was coordinated across addiction, medical, behavioral health, and social service systems, with a care plan, information flow, and an anchoring provider.
- Did the work stay within scope, supporting integrated recovery and health while deferring medical, psychiatric, and addiction treatment to clinicians?
