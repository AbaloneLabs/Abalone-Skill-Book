# Post-Discharge Follow-Up and Preventing Readmission

The first days and weeks after discharge are the highest-risk period in the care transition. Medication errors, unrecognized complications, service gaps, and inability to follow discharge instructions converge to drive avoidable readmissions. The caregiver's role in this period is not passive presence but active monitoring, reinforcement, coordination, and early intervention—catching problems while they are still solvable at home, before they escalate to an emergency. The risk is treating discharge as the end of the episode, when in fact it is the start of a fragile recovery during which the patient is most vulnerable.

This skill covers the post-discharge judgments: monitoring for deterioration, reinforcing instructions, coordinating services, managing adherence, and deciding when a problem can be managed at home versus when it requires escalation.

This guidance addresses post-discharge support judgment. It does not replace the discharge team's clinical instructions, applicable care standards, or institutional policy. Escalate to the clinical team or emergency services per the discharge plan when red flags appear.

## Core Rules

### Intensify monitoring in the high-risk window

The first 24–72 hours and the first 7–14 days post-discharge carry the highest readmission risk. Intensify monitoring during this window: vital signs where indicated, symptoms of recurrence or complication, medication effects, functional status, and hydration/nutrition. Frequent, structured check-ins catch deterioration early. Document baseline status at discharge so changes are recognizable.

### Reinforce discharge instructions actively, not once

Patients retain a fraction of discharge instructions, especially when unwell, fatigued, or cognitively impaired. Reinforce instructions repeatedly and in multiple forms: verbal, written, and demonstration. Confirm understanding by teach-back (asking the patient or family to explain it back). Focus reinforcement on the highest-stakes items: medications, warning signs, activity restrictions, and whom to call.

### Manage medication adherence proactively

Post-discharge medication regimens are complex, changed, and easily confused. Set up the medication schedule, confirm fills, observe the first doses where possible, and watch for side effects and interactions—especially with high-risk medications like anticoagulants, insulin, and opioids. Identify barriers to adherence (cost, complexity, side effects, cognition) and address them.

### Coordinate the service mosaic

Post-discharge care often involves multiple providers: primary care, specialists, home health, therapy, and durable medical equipment. Coordinate among them: confirm each is aware of the others, share the discharge summary and medication list, and watch for gaps and overlaps. A single missed home health visit or an unfilled equipment need can cascade into a crisis.

### Use a clear, pre-agreed escalation pathway

Decide in advance, with the patient and family, what symptoms trigger a call to the clinical team, what triggers a visit to urgent care, and what triggers an emergency call. A pre-agreed pathway prevents both under-reaction (ignoring a serious sign) and over-reaction (defaulting to the emergency department for a manageable issue). Keep the pathway visible and simple.

### Distinguish manageable problems from emergencies

Not every post-discharge problem requires readmission. Many issues—minor symptom fluctuation, a missed therapy visit, a medication side effect—can be managed at home with clinical guidance. Train the patient and family to recognize the difference, and use the escalation pathway to obtain guidance before defaulting to the emergency department. Conversely, do not delay escalation for true red flags.

### Document monitoring and interventions throughout the post-discharge period

The post-discharge period generates a stream of observations: vital signs, symptoms, medication effects, functional changes, service activations and gaps, and any escalations. Document these, along with the interventions taken and their results. Documentation serves multiple purposes: it creates a baseline against which to recognize change, it provides evidence for the clinical team at follow-up, it supports continuity if the caregiver changes, and it creates a record if the patient deteriorates and the question of readmission arises. A post-discharge period that is experienced but not documented is harder to learn from and harder to communicate.

### Address the social determinants that drive readmission

Readmission is not only a clinical phenomenon; it is driven by social determinants—transportation to follow-up, ability to afford medications, food security, housing stability, social isolation, and health literacy. A patient who cannot get to the follow-up appointment, cannot afford the prescription, or cannot read the discharge instructions will be readmitted regardless of clinical quality. Identify the social barriers early in the post-discharge period and connect the patient to resources (transportation, prescription assistance, meal programs, community support) that address them. Ignoring social determinants while optimizing clinical care leaves the largest readmission drivers untouched.

## Common Traps

### Treating discharge as the end of the episode

The post-discharge period is the start of a fragile recovery, not the conclusion of care. Passive presence during this window misses the deterioration that drives readmission.

### Delivering instructions once

Single-delivery instructions are poorly retained, especially by unwell patients. Reinforce repeatedly and confirm understanding by teach-back.

### Assuming medications are being taken as prescribed

Complex regimens, side effects, cost, and cognitive impairment drive non-adherence. Confirm adherence actively, especially for high-risk medications.

### Losing coordination across multiple providers

With several providers involved, gaps and overlaps are likely. Actively coordinate and share information to prevent services falling through the cracks.

### Defaulting to the emergency department for manageable issues

Some readmissions are avoidable; not every fluctuation is an emergency. Use the pre-agreed pathway to obtain guidance and manage at home where clinically appropriate.

### Delaying escalation for true red flags

The opposite trap—managing at home when a red flag appears—delays necessary care. Train the family to recognize and act on true emergencies immediately.

## Self-Check

- Am I intensifying monitoring in the first 24–72 hours and 7–14 days, with a documented baseline for recognizing change?
- Am I reinforcing discharge instructions repeatedly and confirming understanding by teach-back?
- Am I managing medication adherence proactively, confirming fills, observing early doses, and watching for side effects, especially with high-risk medications?
- Am I coordinating across all post-discharge providers to prevent gaps and overlaps?
- Is there a clear, visible, pre-agreed escalation pathway for what to call, visit, or emergency-triggers?
- Am I distinguishing manageable problems (handled at home with guidance) from true red flags (escalated immediately)?
- Am I documenting monitoring findings, interventions, and escalations throughout the post-discharge period?
