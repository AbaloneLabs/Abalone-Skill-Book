# Discharge Readiness Assessment and Safe Transition

A discharge that moves a patient from a supported clinical environment to an unprepared home is a setup for readmission, injury, and setback. Readiness is not a single checklist item; it is the convergence of clinical stability, environmental preparedness, medication continuity, service activation, and follow-up scheduling. The caregiver is often the person closest to the patient's actual living situation and daily needs, and is therefore well placed to identify the gaps that the discharge team—who may never have seen the home—cannot. The risk is that discharge proceeds on the assumption that "home" is ready when in fact stairs are impassable, medications are unfilled, the home health agency has not started, and the first follow-up appointment is six weeks away.

This skill covers the readiness assessment judgments: what to verify before discharge is finalized, how to identify and close gaps, and how to ensure the transition is safe rather than merely completed.

This guidance addresses transition planning judgment. It does not replace the discharge team's clinical authority, applicable care coordination standards, or institutional policy. Escalate unresolved readiness gaps to the discharge planner and attending clinician before discharge.

## Core Rules

### Assess readiness across all domains, not only clinical stability

A patient who is medically stable may still be unready for discharge if the environment, services, medications, or follow-up are not in place. Assess readiness across: clinical stability, functional capacity, home environment, equipment, medications, community services, caregiver capacity, and follow-up. A gap in any domain can undermine the transition.

### Verify the home environment and equipment before discharge

Confirm that the home can receive the patient: stairs negotiated, bathroom access managed, bed and seating available, and necessary equipment (hospital bed, commode, walker, oxygen, suction) delivered, installed, and tested. Do not assume equipment ordered is equipment delivered. Verify installation and that the patient or family can operate it.

### Ensure medication continuity across the transition

Medication errors are a leading cause of post-discharge harm. Verify that the discharge medication list is reconciled against the pre-admission regimen, that prescriptions are filled or arrangements made, that the patient and family understand each medication (purpose, dose, timing, side effects), and that high-risk medications (anticoagulants, insulin, opioids) have specific education. Confirm how medications will be obtained ongoing.

### Confirm that community services are activated, not merely ordered

Home health, nursing, physical therapy, and personal care services must be not only arranged but activated, with start dates confirmed and the patient enrolled. A service ordered but not yet started leaves a gap in the first days home, when risk is highest. Verify start dates and contact information.

### Secure follow-up appointments before discharge

Confirm that follow-up appointments with the primary care provider, specialists, and therapy are scheduled, with dates, times, and locations provided to the patient and family. Appointments "to be arranged" frequently are not. For high-risk patients, confirm the first follow-up is within the clinically appropriate window.

### Identify red flags and provide a clear escalation pathway

Ensure the patient and family know the warning signs that require urgent attention and whom to call. A discharge without a clear "what to do if" pathway leaves the family to default to the emergency department. Provide written, specific escalation instructions.

### Assess functional capacity, not only clinical stability

A patient may be clinically stable yet unable to function safely at home: too weak to walk to the bathroom, unable to prepare a meal, unable to manage toileting independently. Functional capacity—mobility, activities of daily living, cognition, and stamina—determines whether the home environment will work. Assess functional capacity against the demands of the home setting, and where capacity is insufficient, arrange for the support (home health, personal care, equipment, family assistance) that bridges the gap. Discharging a patient whose functional capacity does not match the home demands sets up a fall, a setback, or a readmission.

### Confirm caregiver capacity and agreement before discharge

A discharge plan that assumes a family caregiver will provide specific support is only safe if that caregiver has agreed, has the capacity, and has been trained. Confirm the designated caregiver's availability, physical and emotional capacity, and training on the tasks they will perform. A plan that lists "family will provide care" without verifying the family member's agreement and capacity is a plan built on an assumption, not evidence.

## Common Traps

### Equating clinical stability with discharge readiness

A medically stable patient may be functionally unable to manage at home, or the home may be unprepared. Readiness is multidomain; clinical stability is necessary but not sufficient.

### Assuming ordered equipment is delivered and working

Equipment orders can be delayed, incomplete, or delivered without setup. Verify delivery, installation, and that the family can operate the equipment.

### Neglecting medication reconciliation and education

Unreconciled medication lists, unfilled prescriptions, and uneducated patients drive post-discharge harm. Reconcile, fill, and educate—especially for high-risk medications.

### Treating service orders as service activation

A service ordered but not started leaves a coverage gap. Confirm start dates and enrollment before discharge.

### Leaving follow-up "to be arranged"

Follow-up appointments that are not scheduled at discharge frequently are not scheduled at all, especially for patients with limited capacity to self-advocate. Confirm appointments before discharge.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed readiness across all domains: clinical, functional, environmental, equipment, medication, services, caregiver capacity, and follow-up?
- Is the home environment prepared, and is necessary equipment delivered, installed, tested, and operable by the family?
- Is the discharge medication list reconciled, filled or arranged, and understood by the patient and family, with high-risk medications specifically addressed?
- Are community services activated with confirmed start dates, not merely ordered?
- Are follow-up appointments scheduled, with dates and locations provided, and the first appointment within the clinically appropriate window?
- Have I provided clear, written red-flag warnings and an escalation pathway for the patient and family?
- Where gaps remain, have I escalated to the discharge planner and attending clinician before discharge?
