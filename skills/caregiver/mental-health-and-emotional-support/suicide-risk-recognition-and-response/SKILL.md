# Suicide Risk Recognition And Response

Suicidal statements are the highest-stakes situation a caregiver will face, and they are also the situation most likely to be mishandled through dismissal. A caregiver is not a clinician and cannot assess suicide risk, but the caregiver is often the first and only person who hears the statement, and the temptation to downgrade it — to decide it was "just venting," "just seeking attention," "out of character," or "not serious" — is strong precisely because the alternative (treating it as real and escalating) is uncomfortable and disruptive. That judgment, when wrong, is final and irreversible. The correct stance is radical conservatism: take every statement about suicide, death, or self-harm seriously, do not leave the person alone, remove access to means, and escalate immediately to a clinician or crisis service. The cost of overreacting is small and temporary; the cost of underreacting is a death that could have been prevented.

This is a high-stakes domain. Suicide is a leading cause of death in older adults and in people with chronic illness, disability, and depression, and the people caregivers serve carry many of the strongest risk factors: social isolation, physical illness, loss, pain, and access to lethal means. The caregiver's job is not to assess or treat but to recognize, stay present, and escalate — and these actions save lives.

## Core Rules

### Take Every Statement About Suicide, Death, Or Self-Harm Seriously

Any statement about wanting to die, killing oneself, being better off dead, being a burden, or having nothing to live for must be treated as a real signal. Do not grade the statement by tone, by whether the person "really means it," by whether it was said calmly or in anger, or by whether the person has said it before. Calmness is not reassurance; many people at genuine risk speak with eerie calm because they have made a decision. A history of similar statements does not make the current one less serious — a prior statement may have been a precursor to a later attempt. Your job is not to assess whether the risk is real; it is to treat it as real and escalate.

### Never Leave The Person Alone

If someone expresses active suicidal thoughts, has a plan, or has taken any action toward self-harm, stay with them. Do not step out to "give them space," do not leave to call for help from another room if you can call while present, and do not assume they will be safe for five minutes. The window of greatest danger is exactly the window when the person is alone with their thoughts and any available means; a few unattended minutes is enough for a fatal act. The single most important action is continuous presence until a clinician or emergency responder takes over. If you must summon help, use a phone while staying with the person, or call out for someone else to come to you.

### Ask Directly If You Are Unsure — Direct Questions Do Not Cause Suicide

A widespread and dangerous myth is that asking about suicide plants the idea. Evidence shows the opposite: direct, calm questions can lower risk and surface a plan that allows correct escalation. It is appropriate and right to ask, "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" and "Do you have a plan, and do you have what you would use?" Asking does not make you responsible for the idea; it gives you the information needed to escalate correctly. If the client expresses death-related thoughts indirectly ("I'd be better off gone," "I'm just a burden"), it is right to ask the direct question rather than assume they only meant they were sad.

### Escalate Immediately To A Clinician Or Crisis Service

This is a clinical emergency and is outside caregiver scope. Contact emergency services, a crisis line (such as a national suicide and crisis lifeline), the client's mental-health clinician, your supervisor, and the family or emergency contact per your protocol. Do not wait for the next shift, do not wait to "see how they are later," do not wait for the supervisor to call back, and do not agree to keep it a secret. Act now. When uncertain whether the situation is urgent enough to escalate, escalate — an unnecessary call costs little; a delay in a real crisis is irreversible.

### Remove Access To Means While You Wait

If you can do so safely and without leaving the person alone, reduce access to obvious means — medications (especially the client's own stockpiles), firearms, sharp objects, ligature points, vehicles, and other means specific to any plan the client described. Removing or securing means is one of the most effective suicide-prevention actions, because most suicide attempts are impulsive and method-specific, and reducing access to the preferred method during a high-risk period saves lives. If you cannot remove something safely, note it for responders and focus on staying with the person. Firearms in particular are so lethal that their presence multiplies risk; if a firearm is present and you cannot secure it, tell responders immediately.

### Do Not Promise Confidentiality You Cannot Keep

A person may ask you to keep their suicidal thoughts secret. You cannot. Say honestly that you care about them and because you care you cannot keep this to yourself, and that you are going to get help. Do not agree to "just this once" or "until tomorrow" or "if I feel better later." Agreeing to secrecy binds you to silence while the person remains at risk, and the delay can be fatal. Trust can be repaired after an honest escalation; a life cannot be repaired after a missed one.

### Document Objectively And Report

Record the exact words the person used, the time, what you observed, what you did, who you contacted, and when. Quote rather than paraphrase, because the specific wording matters to the clinicians who take over. Note any plan, means, or preparatory acts described, and any means you removed or secured. Objective, contemporaneous documentation protects the person, supports the clinical team, and protects the caregiver. Report to your supervisor per agency policy, and ensure the information reaches the clinicians and family through the proper channel.

### Know The Warning Signs That Precede A Crisis

The caregiver, present daily, may notice the signs before any direct statement. Watch for: giving away possessions, putting affairs in order, saying goodbye, a sudden brightening or calm after a period of despair (which can reflect a decision made), withdrawing from contact, stopping food or medication, stockpiling medications, acquiring means, expressing hopelessness or being a burden, and any direct or indirect reference to death or suicide. Treat these as signals to flag and watch closely, and escalate any direct statement immediately.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Statements As Attention-Seeking, Venting, Or Not Serious

The caregiver, frightened and wanting the threat not to be real, seizes on any reason to downgrade it: the person has said this before, they are angry, they are manipulative, they said it casually, they are "just venting." The mechanism is that a familiar, low-drama, or manipulative-sounding statement feels less dangerous than a dramatic one, and the discomfort of escalation biases toward dismissal. The harm is catastrophic and irreversible: many people who die by suicide had previously made statements that were dismissed as attention-seeking, and the label "attention-seeking" itself becomes the reason the real risk was missed. The defense is to treat every statement about death or self-harm as serious regardless of tone, history, or framing, and to recognize that the downside of taking it seriously is small and temporary while the downside of dismissing it is final.

### Leaving The Person Alone To Get Help Or Give Them Space

The caregiver, wanting to act quickly, runs to find a phone, fetches a supervisor, or steps out so the person can "calm down," leaving the person alone for what feels like a brief, harmless absence. The mechanism is that action feels like helping and a brief absence feels safe, so the caregiver leaves to mobilize help or to give space. The harm is that the window of greatest danger is exactly the window when the person is alone with their thoughts and any available means, and a few unattended minutes is enough for a fatal act. The defense is to stay with the person continuously, to call for help while present, and to have someone else retrieve supplies or responders — recognizing that presence is the single most protective action.

### Agreeing To Keep Suicidal Thoughts A Secret

The person begs for confidentiality, and the caregiver, wanting to preserve trust and avoid distress, agrees, believing that keeping the channel open is the compassionate response. The mechanism is that agreement feels compassionate and preserves the relationship in the moment, so the caregiver is bound to silence while the person remains at risk. The harm is that the delay in escalation can be fatal, and the caregiver is now complicit in the risk going unaddressed. The defense is to never agree to keep suicide risk confidential, to explain honestly and kindly that you cannot because you care, and to recognize that honest escalation is the caring response and that trust can be rebuilt later.

### Treating A Calm Demeanor As Evidence Of Low Risk

The person appears calm, composed, even cheerful, and the caregiver reads the calm as resolution and stability, downgrading the risk because the person "seems fine now." The mechanism is that calmness is read as control and safety, so the statement is reinterpreted as less serious than it was. The harm is that some people become calm precisely because they have decided on a plan and feel relief at the decision, and that calmness is the resolve that precedes an attempt — the period of greatest danger mistaken for safety. The defense is to never use demeanor to downgrade risk, to escalate based on the statement and any plan or means rather than on how composed the person appears, and to recognize that a sudden brightening after despair can be a warning, not a recovery.

### Waiting To Escalate Or Waiting For Permission

The caregiver, uncertain whether the situation is serious enough, waits — for the supervisor to call back, for the next shift, to see how the person feels later, or for a family member's opinion — and the window for intervention closes. The mechanism is that escalation feels drastic and the discomfort of an unnecessary call biases toward waiting, and the ambiguity of the situation provides cover for delay. The harm is that a real crisis goes unaddressed during the hours of waiting, and a person who could have been protected is not. The defense is to escalate immediately when there is any statement about suicide or self-harm, to err toward action when uncertain, and to recognize that the cost of an unnecessary escalation is small and the cost of delay is irreversible.

### Failing To Address Means Because The Conversation Is Uncomfortable

The caregiver knows there are firearms or medication stockpiles in the home but does not raise securing them, because the conversation is awkward or feels outside the role, and the most modifiable risk factor goes unaddressed. The mechanism is that the discomfort of the means conversation, and uncertainty about the caregiver's authority, lead to its omission, and the means remain accessible during the highest-risk period. The harm is concrete: a significant proportion of suicide deaths occur with means already in the home, and securing or removing them during high-risk periods prevents deaths. The defense is to raise means directly, to secure or note them for responders, to involve the supervisor and family in securing them, and to recognize that means restriction is one of the few interventions shown to reduce suicide death.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I treat every statement about death, suicide, or self-harm as serious, regardless of tone, history, or how it was framed?
- [ ] Did I stay with the person continuously and avoid leaving them alone, even briefly, to get help?
- [ ] Did I ask directly about suicidal thoughts and any plan or means, knowing that asking does not cause suicide?
- [ ] Did I escalate immediately to emergency services, a crisis line, the clinician, my supervisor, and the emergency contact, rather than waiting?
- [ ] Did I remove or reduce access to means — medications, firearms, sharp objects, ligature points — where I could do so safely without leaving the person?
- [ ] Did I refuse to promise confidentiality, and explain honestly and kindly that I had to get help?
- [ ] Did I avoid being reassured by a calm demeanor, and escalate based on the statement and plan rather than how composed the person appeared?
- [ ] Did I document the person's exact words, the time, my observations, my actions, the means addressed, and my notifications?
- [ ] Did I watch for and flag the warning signs — giving away possessions, putting affairs in order, sudden brightening after despair, withdrawal, stockpiling — even before a direct statement?
- [ ] Did I remember that assessing suicide risk is a clinician's job, and that my job is to take it seriously, stay present, restrict means, and escalate?
