# Initial Intake And Baseline Assessment

The first assessment of a new care client is not a form-filling exercise. It establishes the reference point against which every future change will be judged, the factual basis for the care plan, and the earliest opportunity to surface risks that could injure the client or expose the caregiver. Caregivers tend to under-collect here because intake feels routine, because the client is anxious and wants to please, or because family members answer on the client's behalf. The harm is invisible at first: a missed baseline means a later decline cannot be proven, a wrong assumption becomes a permanent care habit, and a hidden condition goes unmanaged until it causes a fall, a medication interaction, or a hospitalization. The agent has latitude to adapt the intake to the client's communication style and energy, but it must not trade thoroughness for speed or comfort.

## Core Rules

### Establish Baselines Before Making Any Care Decision

Before assisting with personal care, mobility, medication, or meals, capture the client's current functional, cognitive, and emotional starting point. Document what the client can do independently, what they need prompting for, and what requires full assistance. A baseline that records only diagnoses is useless for day-to-day care; you need observable abilities, typical pain levels, usual sleep, normal appetite, and customary mood. Without this, later changes are uninterpretable.

### Separate Client Voice From Proxy Voice

During intake, family members often answer questions directed at the client. This is sometimes helpful, but it can mask the client's actual capacity, wishes, and symptoms. Ask the client directly first, allow time for response, and record separately what the client says versus what a family member reports. Note any conflict between the two, because it often signals capacity questions, denial, or undisclosed tension that will shape care later.

### Map The Full Risk Picture, Not Just The Presenting Need

A client referred for help with bathing may also have fall risk, swallowing difficulty, medication complexity, cognitive impairment, skin breakdown, or social isolation. Run through a structured risk review rather than only investigating the stated reason for care. Missing a risk at intake means it will not appear in the care plan and will only be noticed after harm occurs.

### Confirm Identity, Allergies, And Emergency Contacts Early

Verify the client's full identity, known allergies and drug reactions, current medications, and the people to contact in an emergency. These are safety-critical and must be confirmed against a reliable source, not assumed from a referral note. Record who provided each piece of information and when, because accountability matters if a later error is investigated.

### Respect Consent And Dignity During Information Gathering

Intake involves sensitive questions about toileting, continence, mental health, finances, and relationships. Explain why each question is needed, give the client choice over what to disclose, and conduct the conversation privately. A client who feels interrogated or shamed will withhold information that matters for their safety, and the assessment will be quietly inaccurate.

### Identify Scope Limits And Escalation Triggers

Not every finding belongs in a caregiver's plan. Distinguish what is within caregiving scope from what requires a nurse, physician, therapist, or social worker. Flag assessment findings that need clinical follow-up, such as unexplained weight loss, new confusion, signs of abuse, or unmanaged pain, and route them through the correct channel rather than absorbing them into routine care.

## Common Traps

### Treating Intake As Paperwork Rather Than Clinical Evidence

The trap begins when the caregiver experiences the intake form as an administrative hurdle to complete quickly. Because the form looks like routine paperwork and supervisors reward completion, the caregiver fills in plausible defaults, checks boxes from memory, or copies prior notes. The false signal is that the form is now complete and the file looks thorough, so the task appears done. The harm emerges weeks later when the client declines and no one can tell whether the change is new, because there is no genuine baseline to compare against. Worse, fabricated or assumed baselines become the standard the client is measured by, so real deterioration looks normal and real improvement goes unrecognized.

### Letting An Eager Family Member Override The Client's Account

Family members often arrive ready to help and may answer every question before the client can speak, especially when the client is elderly, slow, or cognitively slowed. The mechanism is well-intentioned efficiency: the family member is fluent and accurate about some facts, so the caregiver accepts all their input as equally reliable. The false signal is a smooth, fast interview that feels collaborative. The harm is that the client's own wishes, pain reports, and functional abilities are overwritten, the care plan reflects the family's assumptions, and the client loses agency over their own care. It also conceals capacity issues and family conflict that would otherwise surface early.

### Collecting Diagnoses Instead Of Function

A caregiver records hypertension, diabetes, and arthritis because those are the documented conditions, and treats the intake as complete. The mechanism is that diagnoses are easy to copy and feel authoritative. The false signal is a medically impressive list that looks comprehensive. The harm is that the care plan lacks the information that actually drives daily care: can the client stand from a chair unaided, reach overhead, cut their food, remember a morning routine, walk to the bathroom at night? Without functional baselines, the plan cannot be tailored and staff cannot detect subtle decline.

### Skipping The Risk Review Because The Referral Reason Seems Narrow

When a client is referred for one need, such as meal preparation, the caregiver may focus only on that area and skip screening for falls, skin, swallowing, mood, and medication risk. The mechanism is scope narrowing driven by the referral label. The false signal is efficiency and staying within the assigned task. The harm is that unassessed risks go unmanaged, and the first sign of a fall risk may be the fall itself. A narrow intake guarantees a narrow plan, and the client is left exposed to risks no one checked for.

### Accepting Secondhand Medication And Allergy Information Without Verification

A family member reports the client's allergies and pills from memory, or an old list is copied forward. The mechanism is trust in convenient sources and avoidance of the effort to confirm. The false signal is that a list exists and looks plausible. The harm is severe: an unrecorded allergy can cause a life-threatening reaction, a missing medication can lead to an uncontrolled condition, and a wrong dose assumption can cause a fall or overdose. Allergy and medication information is safety-critical and must be verified, not inherited.

### Documenting Only Strengths To Keep The Record Positive

Caregivers sometimes record what the client can do and omit what they cannot, hoping to keep the file encouraging or avoid labeling the client. The mechanism is a protective instinct and discomfort with recording limitations. The false signal is a kind, strengths-based note. The harm is that the care plan then overestimates ability, assistance is not scheduled where needed, and the client is set up to fail at tasks they cannot safely do alone, leading to falls, missed medication, or neglect of unmet needs.

## Self-Check

Before treating the intake assessment as complete, verify:

- The record captures functional baselines for mobility, self-care, cognition, mood, appetite, sleep, and pain, not just a list of diagnoses.
- The client's own statements are recorded separately from family or proxy reports, and any disagreements between them are noted.
- A structured risk review has been completed covering falls, skin, swallowing, medication complexity, infection exposure, abuse or neglect indicators, and social isolation.
- Identity, allergies, current medications, and emergency contacts have been confirmed against a reliable source, not assumed.
- The client gave informed consent to the assessment, sensitive questions were asked privately, and the client could decline to answer.
- Findings outside caregiving scope have been flagged and routed to the appropriate clinical or social-work channel.
- The baseline is specific and measurable enough that a future caregiver could detect whether the client has improved, declined, or stayed the same.
