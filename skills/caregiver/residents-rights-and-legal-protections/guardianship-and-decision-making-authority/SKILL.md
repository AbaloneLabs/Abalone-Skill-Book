# Guardianship And Decision-Making Authority

When a client cannot make their own decisions, someone else must, and the question of who that person is — legally, not just socially — is one of the most consequential and most often mishandled determinations in care. The judgment problem is that caregivers tend to assume that the closest or most involved family member is the decision-maker, that being a relative confers authority, or that whoever shows up and seems responsible can direct care. In reality, decision-making authority for an incapacitated adult is conferred by specific legal instruments or court orders, and the absence, presence, or limits of those instruments completely changes who may consent, refuse, or direct care. The recurring failures are taking instructions from a family member who has no legal authority, letting one relative override another who actually holds the authority, missing an existing guardianship order, and accepting a surrogate's decision that clearly contradicts the client's known wishes or interests. The agent has latitude to gather information and communicate, but it must not treat relationship, warmth, involvement, or persistence as substitutes for legal authority, and it must escalate whenever authority is unclear, contested, or absent.

This skill covers the legal framework of surrogate and substitute decision-making: healthcare powers of attorney, court-appointed guardians and conservators, default surrogate consent statutes, and the limits of each. It is distinct from the capacity-and-consent skill, which addresses whether a client can decide for themselves; here the client has been determined to lack capacity, and the question is who may decide for them and within what bounds.

## Core Rules

### Determine Who Holds Legal Authority Before Acting On Anyone's Instructions

Before accepting care instructions, consent, or refusal from anyone other than the client, establish what legal authority that person holds. Decision-making authority for an incapacitated adult comes from one of: a valid durable healthcare power of attorney or proxy designation made by the client while they had capacity; a court-appointed guardian (of the person) or conservator (of the estate); a default surrogate consent statute that lists a priority order of family members when no document or guardian exists; or, in some settings and emergencies, a clinician acting under emergency or best-interests doctrine. Being a spouse, child, sibling, or primary caregiver does not by itself confer authority, and being the most involved or most vocal family member confers none. Ask for and document the basis of authority — the document, the court order, or the statutory priority — and when none can be produced, treat authority as unestablished and escalate.

### Know The Priority Order And Use It When No Document Or Guardian Exists

When a client lacks capacity and has no healthcare power of attorney and no guardian, most jurisdictions have a default surrogate consent statute that sets a priority order, commonly: spouse, adult children, parents, adult siblings, other relatives, and sometimes close friends. The order matters: a higher-priority surrogate decides, and a lower-priority relative does not get to override them. Know the priority order in your jurisdiction and setting, and recognize that it applies only when no higher legal authority (a designated agent or guardian) exists. Two practical cautions: surrogates at the same tier (for example, multiple adult children) may need to agree, and a surrogate's authority is to decide based on the client's known wishes or, if unknown, the client's best interest — not their own. When the priority is unclear or contested, escalate rather than picking the most convenient relative.

### Distinguish The Types Of Authority And Their Limits

Decision-making roles are not interchangeable, and each has defined limits. A healthcare power of attorney or proxy is limited to healthcare decisions and is activated only when the client lacks capacity; it does not grant authority over money or property. A guardian of the person is court-appointed to make personal and healthcare decisions for an incapacitated adult; a conservator or guardian of the estate is appointed to manage finances and property, and may or may not also hold personal/healthcare authority. A power of attorney for finances handles money and is distinct from a healthcare proxy. Do not assume that the person managing the client's money can consent to a procedure, or that the healthcare agent can redirect the client's funds. When someone asserts authority, identify which role they hold and whether it covers the decision at hand.

### Honor The Client's Known Wishes And Best Interest As The Standard For Surrogate Decisions

A surrogate or guardian does not get to decide whatever they want. The legal standard in nearly every jurisdiction is the substituted judgment standard: the surrogate must choose what the client would have chosen based on the client's known values, prior statements, religious beliefs, and expressed wishes. Only when the client's wishes are genuinely unknown does the surrogate fall back on the best-interest standard. This means a surrogate's decision that clearly contradicts what the client is known to have wanted is not a valid exercise of authority, even if the surrogate holds the legal role. When a surrogate directs care that conflicts with the client's documented or well-known wishes, escalate to the nurse, clinician, and where needed the ethics committee, administrator, or adult protective services — do not simply comply.

### Recognize The Signs That Authority Is Being Abused Or Exceeded

A surrogate or guardian who holds legal authority can still misuse it, and the caregiver is often the person best positioned to notice. Warning signs include a decision-maker who refuses to allow the client any say in decisions the client could still participate in; who isolates the client from other family, friends, or staff; whose decisions consistently serve the decision-maker's interests rather than the client's (for example, refusing care that would help, or directing care that benefits the decision-maker financially); who is unusually resistant to the client being assessed for capacity; or who directs care that contradicts the client's documented wishes. Authority that is being abused is not authority to honor. Document the observations factually, escalate to the supervisor and clinician, and where financial or physical abuse is suspected, follow the mandated-reporting process.

### Do Not Let One Family Member Override Another Who Holds Higher Authority

Family conflict over decisions for an incapacitated client is common, and the caregiver must not resolve it by siding with the most insistent party. When relatives disagree, identify who holds the legal authority — the designated agent, the guardian, or the highest-priority statutory surrogate — and route decisions through that person. A lower-priority relative's objection does not override a higher-priority surrogate's decision, but a contested decision that risks the client should be escalated to the clinician, ethics committee, or administrator, and documented. If two parties each claim authority (for example, a healthcare agent and a court-appointed guardian), do not choose between them; escalate to resolve which authority governs.

### Know The Limits Of Your Own Role And Escalate When Authority Is Absent Or Contested

A caregiver does not have the authority to determine legal decision-making status, to adjudicate a family dispute, or to consent on a client's behalf. The caregiver's role is to identify who appears to hold authority, to document the basis, to communicate clearly with that person and the care team, and to escalate whenever authority is unclear, contested, or absent. When no legal decision-maker can be identified and a decision is needed, the nurse, clinician, administrator, risk management, and in some cases the court or adult protective services are the proper channels. Attempting to resolve an authority question alone exposes the client to a decision by someone without legal power and exposes the caregiver to liability.

### Document The Authority Basis For Consequential Decisions

For any consequential decision made by someone other than the client, record who made the decision, their relationship to the client, and the stated basis of their authority (the document, the court order, or the statutory priority). If a family member gave instructions, note which relative and on what claimed basis, and flag for the nurse whether documentation of authority is on file. This protects the client against decisions by unauthorized parties, protects the caregiver against later disputes, and creates the record that allows the team to confirm authority before it is relied upon again.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Closest Or Most Involved Family Member Is The Decision-Maker

A devoted daughter who visits daily and knows the client best gives instructions, and the caregiver follows them assuming she is the decision-maker. The mechanism is that involvement, warmth, and apparent responsibility feel like authority. The false signal is that this person clearly belongs and is helping. The harm is that she may hold no legal authority — a healthcare agent, guardian, or higher-priority surrogate may exist whom she has not mentioned or whom she is overriding — and care directed by an unauthorized person is not validly authorized. The corrective is to establish the basis of authority for anyone directing a consequential decision, and to remember that involvement is not authority.

### Taking A Spouse's Or Adult Child's Word As Consent Without Checking Authority

A spouse or adult child consents to a procedure, a transfer, or a restriction, and the caregiver accepts it because of the relationship. The mechanism is the assumption that close family can consent for an incapacitated adult. The false signal is that family consent is naturally valid. The harm is that for an incapacitated adult, family consent is valid only if they hold a recognized legal role — a healthcare proxy, a guardianship, or qualifying statutory surrogate priority — and absent that, the consent is not legally effective. The corrective is to identify the specific role and document it, and to escalate when the role is unclear.

### Missing An Existing Guardianship Or Healthcare Power Of Attorney

A guardian or healthcare agent exists but the caregiver never asks and the family member present does not mention it, so the caregiver treats the present relative as the decision-maker. The mechanism is that guardianship and proxy documents are not obvious and no one looks for them. The false signal is that the family member present is the natural authority. The harm is that the actual legal decision-maker is bypassed, decisions are made by someone without authority, and the caregiver and facility are exposed when the guardianship or proxy later surfaces. The corrective is to ask explicitly at intake and after transitions whether a guardian or healthcare power of attorney exists, to request the documents, and to record what was found.

### Letting One Relative Override Another Who Holds Higher Authority

Two family members disagree, and the caregiver follows the more insistent or more convenient one, who may actually hold lower priority than the relative they are overriding. The mechanism is that conflict is uncomfortable and the vocal party feels authoritative. The false signal is that the decision got made and the family seems to accept it. The harm is that the legally authorized decision-maker is bypassed, the client's care is directed by someone without authority, and the bypassed relative later contests the care. The corrective is to identify who holds legal authority, route decisions through that person, and escalate contested decisions rather than siding with the loudest voice.

### Accepting A Surrogate's Decision That Contradicts The Client's Known Wishes

A healthcare agent or guardian directs care that conflicts with what the client is documented or well-known to have wanted, and the caregiver complies because the person holds the legal role. The mechanism is that legal authority feels like the end of the inquiry. The false signal is that following the authorized decision-maker is the correct and safe path. The harm is that the surrogate's authority is bounded by the substituted-judgment and best-interest standards, and a decision that violates the client's known wishes exceeds that authority. The corrective is to recognize that authority is not unlimited, to flag the contradiction, and to escalate to the clinician, ethics committee, or administrator rather than silently complying.

### Confusing Financial Authority With Healthcare Authority

The person who manages the client's money under a financial power of attorney gives instructions about medical care, and the caregiver follows them. The mechanism is that the financial role feels like overall authority over the client. The false signal is that the person in charge of the client's affairs can direct all decisions. The harm is that a financial power of attorney or conservator of the estate does not confer healthcare decision-making authority unless a separate healthcare role or personal guardianship also exists, and medical decisions by a financial-only representative are not validly authorized. The corrective is to identify which type of authority the person holds and whether it covers the decision at hand.

### Failing To Escalate When No Legal Decision-Maker Can Be Found

No document, guardian, or clearly qualifying surrogate can be identified, and a decision is needed, so the caregiver accepts instructions from whichever relative is available to keep care moving. The mechanism is that the absence of clear authority feels like a problem to solve quickly rather than escalate. The false signal is that someone has to decide and this person is willing. The harm is that care may be directed by someone with no legal authority, and the caregiver has effectively made an authority determination outside their role. The corrective is to recognize that absent or contested authority is itself an escalation trigger, and to route the situation to the nurse, clinician, administrator, risk management, or court as the setting requires.

## Self-Check

Before treating guardianship and decision-making-authority handling as complete, verify:

- The legal basis of authority was established for anyone directing a consequential decision — a healthcare power of attorney, a court-appointed guardianship or conservatorship, or statutory surrogate priority — not assumed from relationship or involvement.
- When no document or guardian exists, the statutory surrogate priority order was identified and applied, and a higher-priority surrogate was not overridden by a lower-priority relative.
- The type of authority each person holds was distinguished — healthcare proxy vs. guardian of the person vs. conservator of the estate vs. financial power of attorney — and confirmed to cover the decision at hand.
- Any existing guardianship or healthcare power of attorney was actively sought at intake and after transitions, not missed because no one asked.
- A surrogate's or guardian's decision that contradicts the client's documented or known wishes was flagged and escalated, not silently complied with, recognizing that authority is bounded by the substituted-judgment and best-interest standards.
- Family disputes over who decides were resolved by identifying legal authority and escalating contested decisions, not by siding with the most insistent party.
- Warning signs of abuse or overreach by a surrogate or guardian were documented factually and escalated, with mandated reporting followed where financial or other abuse is suspected.
- Any situation with absent, unclear, or contested authority was escalated to the nurse, clinician, administrator, or proper authority rather than resolved by the caregiver alone.
- Consequential decisions by a surrogate were documented with the decision-maker's identity, relationship, and stated basis of authority.
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring legal interpretation of authority, guardianship disputes, and court involvement to the nurse, clinician, administrator, risk management, or legal channel where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
