# Advance Directives And Care Plan Implementation

An advance directive only protects the client if the people at the bedside actually follow it, and the gap between a signed document and what happens in the room is where most directive failures occur. The judgment problem is that caregivers are often the first and only people present when a client deteriorates, yet they tend to treat directives as a nurse-and-doctor matter they need not understand, to defer to whichever family member is present and vocal, or to panic-default to calling 911 and starting CPR because that feels like the safe thing to do. The recurring failures are initiating resuscitation or transfer the client explicitly refused, letting family override a documented directive at the moment of crisis, applying a stale or revoked directive, and failing to translate a vague directive into concrete actions so that every shift interprets it differently. The agent has latitude in how it communicates and supports the client and family, but the legal baseline is fixed: a valid, current directive and the orders that flow from it govern care, and a family member's preference does not override them.

This skill covers the implementation and operational dimension of directives — DNR, POLST/MOLST, living wills, and healthcare power of attorney — as they are carried out in daily and emergency care. It is distinct from the bedside preferences-and-goals skill, which addresses clarifying and honoring a client's wishes and revisiting goals of care; here the focus is recognizing the legal instruments, knowing their hierarchy, translating them into actions, and resolving conflicts at the point of implementation.

## Core Rules

### Know The Instruments, What Each Does, And What It Does Not

Advance-care instruments are not interchangeable, and confusing them produces wrong decisions. A living will (advance directive) is a document in which a person states, in advance, what treatment they want or refuse if they lose capacity, typically in terminal or permanently unconscious states; it takes effect only when the person cannot speak for themselves. A durable healthcare power of attorney (healthcare proxy, healthcare agent) names a person authorized to make healthcare decisions when the client lacks capacity; it grants authority to a person, not a list of instructions. A DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) order is a medical order, signed by a clinician, directing that CPR not be attempted if the client has no pulse and is not breathing; it is narrow and applies only to resuscitation. A POLST or MOLST (Physician/Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) is a set of medical orders, signed by a clinician and ideally the patient, that translates goals into specific orders across resuscitation, medical interventions (comfort-only vs. limited vs. full treatment), and artificial nutrition — and unlike a living will it is immediately actionable by any provider. Know which instruments the client has, what each covers, and that a living will is a statement of wishes while a POLST/DNR is an order you act on.

### Honor The Order Hierarchy: Valid Medical Orders And The Directive Govern Over Family Preference

When a documented directive or medical order exists and is current, it governs care, and a family member's contrary request does not override it. The practical hierarchy is: a current, signed POLST/MOLST or DNR order is acted on immediately; a valid living will states the client's wishes and must be followed when it applies; the named healthcare agent decides on questions the documents do not settle. Family members, even loving and involved ones, do not outrank these instruments; their role is to represent the client's known values, not to substitute their own wishes. If a family member demands action that contradicts a current directive or order, the correct response is to explain that the documented wishes govern, to notify the nurse and clinician, and to document the conflict — not to comply with the family to keep the peace. The painful cases are exactly where family and directive disagree, and resolving them through the proper channel protects the client and the caregiver.

### Translate The Directive Into Concrete, Actionable Care Instructions Before A Crisis

A directive that says "no extraordinary measures" or "comfort care only" is unimplementable at the bedside because every reader fills in different meaning. Before a crisis, work with the nurse and care team to translate the directive into specific, operational instructions: resuscitation status (full code or DNR); hospital transfer (yes, no, or only for comfort); antibiotics (yes, no, or only if treatable); artificial hydration and nutrition (yes, no, time-limited); symptom-focused comfort measures; and who to call and in what order. Write these into the care plan in plain language so any caregiver on any shift knows exactly what to do. Vague directives produce opposite decisions across shifts and almost guarantee that the client receives care they did not want.

### Make The Directive Immediately Accessible And Known To Everyone Who Needs It

A directive locked in a file cabinet or buried in an electronic record it takes five screens to reach is functionally absent at the moment it matters. Know where the current directive, POLST, and DNR are physically kept — often posted by the bed, on the refrigerator, in a wallet card, or flagged in the chart — and confirm that the care team, the client's family contact, and emergency responders can find it. In the home, a POLST form on the refrigerator or bedside is what EMS looks for; in a facility, the DNR must be immediately retrievable by the staff who respond. A directive no one can find in time cannot be honored, and the default when none is produced is usually full treatment.

### Confirm The Directive Is Current And Not Revoked Before Acting On It

A client can revoke or change a directive at any time while they have capacity, and a directive written years ago may no longer reflect current wishes. Before implementing a directive in a consequential moment, confirm it is the current version, that it has not been revoked or superseded, and that the client has not expressed a more recent, different wish. A client who verbally revokes a directive while capable generally revokes it, even without paperwork. Conversely, do not set aside a directive because it is old if it is still the current document and the client has not changed it. When the directive's currency is in doubt, escalate to the nurse and clinician rather than guessing; the safest action depends on the urgency and the document's status.

### Know What To Do In The Moment: DNR, CPR, And 911 Decisions

When a client with a DNR or comfort-only POLST is found without a pulse or not breathing, the directive and order direct the response: do not initiate CPR, do not call 911 for resuscitation (though you may call for comfort, pronouncement, or per the care plan), and follow the documented comfort and notification steps. When there is no DNR and no directive, or the directive cannot be found, the default is to initiate CPR and call emergency services. The emotionally difficult but legally clear point is that initiating CPR on a client with a valid DNR is performing treatment they legally refused, and failing to initiate CPR on a client without a DNR is failing to provide care. Know the client's status before the crisis, and if you arrive at a pulseless client and do not know the status, follow your setting's emergency protocol and escalate immediately.

### Honor Documented Wishes Even When They Conflict With Your Own Judgment

A caregiver may believe a directive asks for too little (under-treatment) or too much (over-treatment) relative to what they would choose. Personal disagreement does not authorize deviation. A client with capacity has the right to refuse any treatment including life-sustaining care, and a directive made with capacity carries that right forward. The caregiver's role is to implement the documented wishes, to provide comfort and dignity throughout, and to raise concerns through the proper channel — not to substitute personal judgment for the client's stated wishes. Documenting your own disagreement with a directive in the care record is inappropriate; raising it with the nurse or supervisor is appropriate.

### Revisit And Update The Directive And Orders After Major Changes

A directive and its orders are living instruments that should be revisited when the client's condition changes meaningfully — a new diagnosis, a hospitalization, a significant decline, a move between settings, or a change in the client's wishes. Flag the need to revisit goals of care and orders to the nurse, clinician, and healthcare agent; do not assume a years-old POLST still fits the current situation. Stale orders can direct care the client would no longer choose, and updated wishes that were never put into orders cannot be acted on by the people at the bedside.

## Common Traps

### Calling 911 Or Starting CPR On A Client With A Valid DNR

The client collapses and the caregiver, in the adrenaline of the moment, calls 911 and begins CPR because that is the default emergency response. The mechanism is that emergency training is automatic and overriding it feels wrong and frightening. The false signal is that acting fast and "doing everything" is the responsible, safe choice. The harm is that the client is subjected to resuscitation they legally refused and explicitly asked to avoid, the directive is violated at the exact moment it was meant to protect them, and the caregiver and facility are exposed to liability. The corrective is to know each client's code status before a crisis, to recognize that a DNR directs a different response, and to follow the documented comfort-and-notification steps rather than the default resuscitation reflex.

### Letting Family Override The Documented Directive At The Moment Of Crisis

A client with a comfort-only directive deteriorates, and a family member present demands that "everything be done" or that the client be sent to the hospital. The mechanism is that grief and fear make the family's demand feel urgent and authoritative, and complying feels compassionate. The false signal is that the family knows best and the directive can be set aside. The harm is that the client's explicitly documented wishes are overridden by someone who never had the authority to override them, the client receives aggressive treatment they refused, and other family members who knew the real wishes are silenced and later resentful. The corrective is to honor the directive, explain to the family that the documented wishes govern, notify the nurse and clinician, and document the conflict — not to comply with the family under pressure.

### Treating The Directive As The Nurse's Or Doctor's Responsibility

The caregiver assumes directives are a clinical matter and never learns what the document says, where it is, or what to do if the client deteriorates. The mechanism is scope-narrowing — the belief that knowing and acting on directives is above the caregiver's role. The false signal is that the clinical staff have it handled. The harm is that the caregiver is often the person present when the client declines, and without knowing the directive they cannot recognize when a proposed action contradicts it, cannot advocate for the client, and may unknowingly initiate care the client refused. A directive the floor staff have not read cannot protect the client.

### Implementing A Stale Or Revoked Directive Without Checking

The directive was written five years ago in different circumstances, or the client verbally changed their mind last month, and the caregiver applies the old document literally. The mechanism is treating the paper as fixed and authoritative regardless of context or more recent statements. The false signal is that following the written instruction is the legally safe path. The harm is that the client may have changed their wishes as their condition changed, and care is directed by a document that no longer speaks for them — or, in the case of a verbal revocation, by a document that is no longer in force. The corrective is to confirm the directive is current and not revoked before relying on it, and to escalate when its currency is in doubt.

### Leaving The Directive Vague And Hoping Each Shift Interprets It Right

The care plan records "comfort care" or "no aggressive measures" without defining what that means operationally. The mechanism is avoidance of the difficult, specific conversations that make a directive usable. The false signal is that a preference has been documented and the client is therefore protected. The harm is that at the critical moment each reader interprets the phrase differently, the same directive produces opposite decisions across shifts, and the client receives inconsistent care that probably does not match their actual wishes. The corrective is to translate the directive into concrete orders and instructions — code status, transfer, antibiotics, fluids, comfort measures — before they are needed.

### Confusing A Living Will With A Medical Order

The caregiver treats a living will that says "no heroic measures" as authority to withhold CPR in the moment, or conversely ignores a POLST because "it's just paperwork." The mechanism is not distinguishing a statement of wishes (living will) from an immediately actionable medical order (POLST/DNR). The false signal is that any advance-care document functions the same way. The harm is that a living will alone may not be enough to direct EMS in the moment, while a POLST or DNR is an order that must be followed — confusing the two leads either to unwanted resuscitation or to wrongly withheld care. The corrective is to know which instruments the client has and what each legally does, and to ensure wishes are backed by medical orders where action is required.

### Documenting Care That Contradicts The Directive, Or Failing To Document The Conflict

A caregiver proceeds with transfer or treatment the directive limits, or faces a family demand that conflicts with the directive, and the care record does not capture what happened or why. The mechanism is that documenting a deviation or a conflict feels like creating trouble. The false signal is that a clean record protects everyone. The harm is that the deviation is invisible and undefended if it is later questioned, and a pattern of quiet non-compliance with the directive goes undetected. The corrective is to document factually what was done, what the directive stated, any conflict with family, who was notified, and the response — so the record shows the directive was honored or that the conflict was properly escalated.

## Self-Check

Before treating advance-directive implementation as complete, verify:

- The instruments the client has (living will, healthcare power of attorney, DNR, POLST/MOLST) are known, along with what each legally does and does not do.
- A current, valid directive or medical order governs care, and no family member's contrary request has been allowed to override it.
- The directive has been translated into concrete, operational care instructions — code status, transfer, antibiotics, artificial hydration and nutrition, comfort measures — not left as vague phrases.
- The directive and orders are immediately accessible to the people who respond in a crisis, including EMS in the home setting.
- The directive's currency has been confirmed and it has not been revoked or superseded, especially after any major change in condition.
- For a client with a DNR or comfort-only POLST, the response to deterioration follows the documented comfort-and-notification steps rather than defaulting to CPR and 911.
- Personal disagreement with the directive has not produced deviation; concerns have been raised through the nurse or clinician, not acted on unilaterally or recorded as opinion in the care note.
- Any conflict between the directive and a family request, or any deviation, has been documented factually with who was notified and the response.
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring clinical interpretation of orders, revocation questions, and ethics disputes to the nurse, prescriber, or ethics channel where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
