# Documenting Suspected Abuse for Mandated Reporting

The quality of your documentation determines whether a report of suspected abuse leads to investigation, intervention, and protection — or is dismissed as vague and unactionable. Adult Protective Services and law enforcement receive thousands of reports; they triage based on specificity, corroboration, and the clarity of the evidence presented. A note that says "client may be abused" is useless. A note that says "at 14:30 on March 4, the client had a 4cm bruise on the left upper arm consistent with a grab mark, stated 'my daughter grabbed me when I wouldn't eat,' and flinched when the daughter entered the room" is actionable. The skill is in documenting what you observe objectively, in sufficient detail, at the time of observation, without contaminating the record with conclusions, assumptions, or leading information. The dominant trap is writing notes that summarize rather than record, or that reflect what you believe rather than what you saw.

## Core Rules

### Document Immediately, Not from Memory

Document observations at the time they occur, or as soon as practicable afterward. Memory degrades quickly, details blur, and a note written three days later is less credible than one written the same day. If you cannot write the full note immediately, record the key facts (time, what you saw, what was said) in a brief form and expand it the same day. Delayed documentation is vulnerable to challenge in legal proceedings.

### Record Observations, Not Conclusions

The distinction is critical. An observation is what you saw, heard, or measured: "The client has a bruise on the right cheek, approximately 3cm in diameter, purple-yellow in color." A conclusion is your interpretation: "The client was hit." Document observations and let the reader draw conclusions. If you include an interpretation, label it clearly as such: "In my observation, the bruise is consistent with the client's report of being struck." Objective observations are admissible; unsupported conclusions are dismissible.

### Use Specific, Measurable Language

Vague: "Client has some bruises." Specific: "Client has three bruises: one 2cm bruise on the left forearm, one 5cm bruise on the right upper arm, and one 3cm bruise on the left shin. The forearm and upper arm bruises are in locations consistent with grab marks; the shin bruise is in a location common to accidental injury." Specificity allows the reader to assess pattern, location, and plausibility. Include size (use measurements, not "small" or "large"), color (which indicates age), location (anatomically precise), and pattern (shape, bilateral symmetry).

### Document the Client's Own Words in Quotes

When the client makes a disclosure, record their exact words in quotation marks, not a paraphrase. "He hits me when I wet the bed" is a disclosure. "Client reports being hit" is a paraphrase that loses the specificity and may be challenged as your interpretation. If the client uses a word you would not choose (a nickname, a colloquialism, an ambiguous term), record it exactly and add context if needed: "Client stated 'Tommy was mean to me.' Tommy is the client's grandson and primary caregiver."

### Document the Context and Setting

Record where the observation occurred, who was present, what preceded it, and what followed. Context determines meaning. A bruise observed during bathing has different significance than one the client points to while crying. A disclosure made when the suspected perpetrator has left the room is different from one made in their presence. Document the circumstances so the reader can assess the reliability and significance of the observation.

### Document Baseline and Changes

Abuse is often a pattern, not a single event. Document the client's baseline condition (skin integrity, mobility, mood, cognitive status) so that changes are recognizable. If the client has a new injury, note whether it was present at the last visit. If the client's behavior has changed (withdrawn, fearful, flinching), note when the change began and what coincided with it. Pattern documentation — repeated injuries, escalating fear, progressive isolation — is what establishes abuse over time.

### Avoid Leading the Client

When you observe a sign of abuse, do not ask leading questions ("Did someone hurt you?" "Did your son do this?"). Leading questions contaminate the disclosure and make it inadmissible. Use open-ended questions: "Tell me about this bruise." "How did this happen?" "What happened next?" Let the client tell their story in their own words. If the client does not disclose, document the observation without pressing — the injury or behavior is reportable based on suspicion regardless of disclosure.

### Document Your Reporting Actions

When you report to Adult Protective Services, document: the date and time of the report, the agency and person you spoke with, the case or report number, the information you provided, and any instructions you received. This creates a record that you fulfilled your mandated reporting duty and provides a reference for follow-up. If you decide not to report (which should be rare and based on clear reasoning), document the reasoning — because the question of why you did not report may arise later.

## Common Traps

### Summarizing Instead of Recording

The caregiver writes "client appears to be abused" at the end of the shift. The trap is substituting a summary for the detailed observations that support it. A summary without supporting observations is a conclusion without evidence — it tells the reader what you believe but not what you saw, and it provides nothing that APS or law enforcement can act on or verify.

### Paraphrasing Disclosures

The client says "she locks me in my room and won't let me out to use the bathroom." The caregiver writes "client reports being confined." The paraphrase loses the specific, visceral detail that makes the disclosure credible and actionable. The exact words carry weight that a paraphrase does not, and paraphrasing introduces the risk that your interpretation has altered the meaning.

### Contaminating the Record with Leading Questions

The caregiver sees a bruise and asks, "Did your daughter hit you?" The client, confused or eager to please, says "yes." The trap is that the leading question has manufactured the disclosure — the client may not have been hit, or may not know what happened, but agreed because the question implied it. The contaminated disclosure is inadmissible and may prevent a real investigation.

### Delaying Documentation Until End of Shift

The caregiver observes a sign of abuse at 10:00 a.m. but waits until the end of the shift at 3:00 p.m. to document it. By then, details have faded — the exact size of the bruise, the exact words the client used, the sequence of events. The trap is that delayed documentation is less accurate and less credible, and in legal proceedings, the gap between observation and documentation invites challenge about what was really observed.

### Documenting Only Physical Signs

The caregiver meticulously documents bruises but ignores behavioral signs — the client's fear, withdrawal, flinching, sudden silence when the caregiver enters, or change in mood. The trap is that behavioral signs are often the earliest and most consistent indicators of abuse, preceding visible injuries. Physical signs are episodic; behavioral signs are continuous, and documenting them establishes the pattern that physical signs alone cannot.

## Self-Check

- Did I document observations immediately, or from memory hours later?
- Did I record what I saw and heard, or what I concluded and assumed?
- Did I use specific, measurable language (size, color, location, pattern)?
- Did I record the client's exact words in quotes, or paraphrase the disclosure?
- Did I document the context (setting, who was present, what preceded)?
- Did I document baseline and changes over time, or only isolated incidents?
- Did I use open-ended questions, or contaminate the disclosure with leading questions?
- Did I document my reporting actions (date, agency, case number, instructions)?
