# Prosthetic Use, Mobility, And Adaptive Daily Living Support

A prosthesis is a sophisticated device that can restore remarkable function, but only if it fits well, is worn and used correctly, and is supported by ongoing skin care, gait training, and device maintenance. The judgment problem is that a prosthesis is not a passive tool the person simply puts on and uses; it is a dynamic interface between a sensitive residual limb and a mechanical device, and the fit changes day to day with swelling, weight change, and activity. A caregiver who does not understand socket fit and sock management, who does not inspect the limb skin every day, or who assumes any discomfort is normal will allow skin breakdown, poor gait, pain, and abandonment of the device. The agent must understand that prosthetic use is a daily practice of fit management, skin protection, functional training, and coordination with the prosthetist and therapy team, and that the device only works when the whole system is cared for.

## Core Rules

### Master Prosthetic Sock And Liner Management For Daily Fit Changes

The residual limb changes volume throughout the day (swelling in the morning, shrinking with activity), and the socket fit must be adjusted to match by adding or removing prosthetic socks (ply-rated wool or cotton socks worn over the liner, inside the socket) or adjusting the liner. The person learns to read the fit: if the limb feels loose or pistons (slides up and down) in the socket, add a sock ply; if it feels tight or constricting, remove a ply. This is a constant, daily adjustment, and getting it wrong causes either a loose fit that rubs and blisters or a tight fit that constricts and pressures the skin. Support the person in managing the socks (carry spares, know the ply system, change as needed through the day), keep the socks and liners clean (laundered per instructions, dried without heat), and inspect the liner for tears and wear. Fit management is the daily work of prosthetic use.

### Inspect The Residual Limb Skin Every Time The Prosthesis Is Removed

The skin inside the socket bears significant pressure and friction, and breakdown happens fast and silently. Every time the prosthesis is removed, inspect the entire limb (using a mirror for hidden surfaces) for redness that does not fade in 30 minutes, blisters, abrasions, calluses, open sores, rashes, and fungal infection. Redness that fades is normal pressure; redness that persists is a pressure area that will break down if not addressed (often by adding or adjusting socks, or by contacting the prosthetist for a socket adjustment). Any broken skin means the prosthesis should not be worn until it heals (or per the team's guidance), because wearing over a wound deepens it. Catch problems early; a small red area managed today prevents a deep wound that takes weeks to heal and sidelines rehabilitation.

### Support Correct Donning And Doffing Technique

Putting the prosthesis on (donning) and taking it off (doffing) requires specific technique that ensures the limb is seated fully and correctly in the socket, with no wrinkles in the liner or socks (which cause instant blisters) and no air gaps (which cause pistoning and breakdown). For liner systems, this means rolling the liner on without wrinkles, pulling the limb fully into the socket using a stocking or pull strap, and confirming full seating. Learn the specific technique for this person's system from the prosthetist and therapist, and support the person in doing it correctly every time. Rushed or incorrect donning is a leading cause of skin breakdown.

### Support Gait And Functional Training, And Recognize Bad Habits

Walking with a prosthesis (or using an arm prosthesis) requires retraining the body to move in a new way, and physical and occupational therapy are essential. Support the person in doing the prescribed exercises and gait drills, attending therapy, and practicing, and recognize the bad habits that develop when a person compensates for pain or weakness: a gait that swings the prosthetic leg out to the side, hops, or takes uneven steps; leaning to one side; or avoiding weight-bearing on the prosthesis. These compensations cause back, hip, and knee pain, wear the device unevenly, and lock in a poor gait that is hard to unlearn. Report persistent gait problems or pain to the therapy team, because they indicate a fit, alignment, or strength problem that needs addressing.

### Maintain The Prosthesis And Recognize When It Needs Professional Attention

The prosthesis is a mechanical device with moving parts, screws, valves, and components that wear, loosen, and break. Perform the daily maintenance the prosthetist prescribes (cleaning the socket and liner, checking screws and components, replacing worn socks and liners), and recognize when the device needs professional attention: a clicking or squeaking sound, a loose component, a valve that sticks, a crack in the socket, or a sudden change in fit that sock adjustment cannot fix. Do not attempt to repair or adjust the device yourself (loosening screws, grinding the socket, modifying components), because amateur adjustments damage the device and the fit and can void warranties. Contact the prosthetist for any mechanical issue or persistent fit problem.

### Prevent Falls In The New And Experienced Prosthesis User

Prosthesis users are at high risk of falls, especially new users, users with bilateral amputations, and users with other balance or medical issues. Prevent falls through: consistent use of the prosthesis only in settings and for distances the person has been trained for, use of a walker or cane as the therapy team recommends, good footwear (a stable shoe on the sound foot and the prosthetic foot, with the same heel height the prosthesis was aligned for — changing heel height changes the alignment and causes gait and skin problems), a clear and well-lit home environment, and a plan for what to do if the person falls while alone (a phone within reach, a personal emergency response system). Report falls to the team, because a fall may indicate a fit, strength, or balance problem to address.

### Support The Full Range Of Adaptive Daily Living, Not Only Walking

Prosthetic rehabilitation is about returning to life, not only walking (or grasping). Support the person in adapting daily activities — dressing, bathing, toileting, cooking, driving, work, recreation, intimacy — some of which will be done with the prosthesis and some with adaptive techniques or assistive devices. Occupational therapy addresses these activities specifically. Encourage the person to return to meaningful roles and activities, support the use of adaptive equipment where it helps (a shower bench, grab bars, a reacher, one-handed devices), and resist the assumption that the prosthesis must replace all lost function; sometimes a mix of prosthetic use and adaptive technique works best. The goal is participation and quality of life, not prosthetic purity.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Socket Fit Changes And Not Adjusting Socks

The caregiver or person wears the same sock ply all day regardless of fit changes. The mechanism is not understanding volume fluctuation. The false signal is that the fit is set in the morning. The harm is that a loose fit pistons and blisters, and a tight fit pressures and breaks down skin. Adjust socks through the day to match the limb.

### Missing Persistent Redness That Signals Pressure

The caregiver notes redness but assumes it is normal. The mechanism is treating all redness as expected. The false signal is that redness from pressure is harmless. The harm is that persistent redness is the precursor to breakdown, and unaddressed it becomes a deep wound. Inspect every time and act on redness that does not fade in 30 minutes.

### Donning With Wrinkles In The Liner Or Socks

The caregiver or person rushes donning and leaves wrinkles. The mechanism is treating donning as a quick step. The false signal is that close enough is fine. The harm is that wrinkles cause instant, focused pressure that blisters and breaks down skin within hours. Don carefully, with no wrinkles.

### Letting Compensatory Gait Habits Become Permanent

The caregiver does not address a developing limp or uneven gait. The mechanism is the assumption that walking is walking. The false signal is that any walking is progress. The harm is that compensations cause joint pain, uneven wear, and a poor gait that is hard to unlearn. Report gait problems to therapy.

### Attempting DIY Repairs On The Prosthesis

The caregiver tightens a loose screw or files the socket. The mechanism is the desire to fix a problem quickly. The false signal is that minor adjustments are safe. The harm is that amateur adjustments damage the device, alter the alignment, void warranties, and cause injury. Contact the prosthetist for any repair.

### Changing Shoe Type And Altering The Prosthetic Alignment

The person switches to a different heel height or shoe style. The mechanism is not knowing that the prosthesis is aligned to a specific heel. The false signal is that any shoe works. The harm is that a different heel height changes the alignment, causing gait problems, skin breakdown, and falls. Use consistent footwear, or consult the prosthetist before a change.

## Self-Check

Before treating prosthetic use, mobility, and adaptive daily living support as complete, verify:

- Prosthetic sock and liner management was supported for daily volume changes, with ply added or removed to match fit, and liners kept clean and intact.
- The residual limb skin was inspected every time the prosthesis was removed, with persistent redness, blisters, abrasions, and breakdown recognized and addressed (sock adjustment, prosthetist contact, resting the limb).
- Correct donning and doffing technique (no wrinkles, full seating) was supported per the person's specific system.
- Gait and functional training were supported, with compensatory habits (swinging gait, leaning, avoiding weight-bearing) recognized and reported to therapy.
- The prosthesis was maintained daily (cleaning, component checks) and professional attention sought for mechanical issues, without DIY repairs.
- Falls were prevented through appropriate device use, assistive devices, consistent footwear, a clear environment, and a fall plan, with falls reported.
- Adaptive daily living (dressing, bathing, cooking, driving, work, recreation, intimacy) was supported with a mix of prosthetic use and adaptive technique, guided by occupational therapy.
- Did the work stay within scope, supporting fit, skin, function, and safety while deferring socket modification, alignment, prescription, and clinical decisions to the prosthetist and therapy team?
