# Communicating With a Person Who Has Dementia

The judgment problem in dementia communication is that ordinary conversational instincts — correcting factual errors, answering the literal question, giving multi-step instructions, reasoning someone out of a false belief — frequently make things worse. Dementia changes what language can do. A person may ask the same question thirty times an hour not to obtain information they have forgotten, but to relieve an anxiety that the information alone cannot fix. They may state a falsehood ("my mother is coming to collect me") that is emotionally true even though it is factually impossible, and correcting it does not restore reality; it produces distress and a breach in the relationship. The skilled caregiver learns to communicate to the person's current emotional and cognitive reality, not to the calendar, and to notice when a sudden change in the ability to communicate is not a dementia symptom but a sign of illness, pain, or delirium that must be escalated.

This is a domain where dignity and safety are both at stake. Poor communication causes avoidable agitation, resistance to care, distress, and sometimes aggression, and it can mask a treatable medical problem. The caregiver's role is to communicate in ways that reduce distress, preserve personhood, support the care plan, and recognize and escalate clinical changes — not to diagnose the type or stage of dementia, to test the person's cognition, or to decide that a communication change is "just the dementia."

## Core Rules

### Match Your Communication To The Person's Current Ability, Not To Their History

Cognitive ability fluctuates across the day, with illness, with fatigue, and across the course of the disease. Communicate with the person in front of you today, at this hour, not with the person you knew last month.

- Reduce complexity: short sentences, one idea at a time, simple choices ("Would you like tea or juice?") rather than open questions ("What would you like to drink?").
- Allow extra time for processing; do not rush, repeat, or rephrase too quickly, which can overwhelm. Pause and wait for a response before trying again.
- Gain attention before speaking: approach from the front, make eye contact, address the person by their preferred name, and reduce background noise and distractions.
- Match your language to the person's level, and adjust within a single interaction if their ability shifts.

### Choose Validation Or Redirection Over Correction For Emotionally Held Beliefs

When a person holds a belief that is false but not dangerous (a deceased parent is alive, they need to go home to children who are now adults, they are waiting for a long-past event), correcting the fact usually causes distress without restoring orientation.

- Consider what the belief expresses emotionally: the wish for safety, for family, for purpose, for home. Respond to the emotion beneath the statement ("You must miss your mother; tell me about her") rather than the fact ("Your mother died twenty years ago").
- Validation does not mean lying or agreeing with a harmful delusion; it means acknowledging the feeling the belief carries.
- Redirect gently to a related, pleasant topic or activity after acknowledging the feeling, rather than arguing the person out of their reality.
- Reserve reality orientation for low-stakes, neutral facts where it does not cause distress (the day of the week, the next meal) and where the person tolerates it; do not force it on emotionally charged beliefs.

### Recognize That Repeated Questions Usually Express An Unmet Need

When a person asks the same question repeatedly, the literal answer rarely satisfies, because the question is serving a different purpose — anxiety, boredom, a need for reassurance, hunger, pain, or the need to use the toilet.

- Before answering the literal question for the tenth time, ask what need the repetition might express: Are they hungry, in pain, uncomfortable, anxious about an upcoming event, or needing the toilet?
- Answer briefly and consistently each time, but also address the underlying need: offer food or drink, a toilet visit, reassurance, or a distracting and engaging activity.
- Avoid showing irritation or saying "I already told you," which increases anxiety and can escalate the repetition.
- A written sign, a clock, or a simple visual schedule can help for some people; for others it is ignored, and that is information too.

### Give Instructions For Care In A Way The Person Can Follow

Personal care requires cooperation, and cooperation requires communication the person can act on.

- One step at a time: "Let's stand up now," then once standing, "Now let's walk to the chair." Avoid chaining ("Stand up, walk over, and sit in the blue chair").
- Use positive, concrete language describing what to do, not what to stop: "Keep your hands on the armrests" rather than "Don't grab me."
- Offer choice and control wherever possible to preserve autonomy: which shirt, which side of the bed, which drink. Choice reduces resistance.
- If the person refuses, do not force; pause, return later, or try a different approach. Refusal is communication, often of fear, pain, or feeling rushed.

### Preserve Personhood And Dignity In Every Interaction

How you speak shapes whether the person feels like a person or a task. Dementia does not remove the capacity to feel respected or humiliated.

- Address the person directly, not over them to a family member, even when you are coordinating care.
- Use the person's preferred name and honor preferences about their history, identity, and routines that you learn from the care plan and from them.
- Do not talk about the person in their presence as though they cannot understand; assume some comprehension remains even when expression is impaired.
- Never mock, mimic, or joke at the person's expense, even affectionately; do not infantilize by using diminutives or a sing-song voice the person would not have accepted before their illness.

### Recognize Communication Changes That Signal A Clinical Problem

A sudden worsening in communication — new confusion, loss of words that were available yesterday, inability to follow instructions that were manageable this morning, or a sharp increase in repetition — is not necessarily dementia progression. It may be delirium, infection, pain, dehydration, a medication effect, or a stroke.

- Know the person's baseline and notice change against it; a change over hours or days is more likely to be an acute medical problem than gradual decline.
- Look for accompanying signs: fever, urinary symptoms, cough, pain behavior, a fall, reduced fluid intake, or a recent medication change.
- Escalate any acute change in communication or cognition to the nurse or clinician promptly, and document the baseline, the change, and any associated symptoms.

## Common Traps

### Correcting A False Belief Because The Truth Feels Like The Right Thing To Do

The caregiver who values honesty will often correct a person's false belief ("Your mother passed away years ago") because telling the truth is a deeply held value and lying feels wrong. The mechanism is that the caregiver applies a general moral rule — do not lie — to a situation where the person can no longer use the truth, so the correction lands as a fresh grief or a confrontation rather than as orientation, and the person relives a loss or feels attacked. The false signal is the moral satisfaction of having told the truth, which feels like integrity but is experienced by the person as cruelty. The harm is that the person becomes distressed, sometimes severely and for hours, the relationship is damaged, and the belief returns anyway because the underlying cognition has not changed, so the truth-telling produced only suffering. The defense is to recognize that dementia changes the purpose of communication from information transfer to emotional connection, to respond to the feeling the belief carries rather than its factual content, and to reserve correction for neutral, non-distressing facts where it serves the person rather than the caregiver's conscience.

### Answering The Literal Repeated Question And Becoming Frustrated When It Continues

When a person asks "When is lunch?" every two minutes, the caregiver answers "At twelve o'clock" each time, growing increasingly irritated that the answer never sticks, until the irritation leaks into tone and the person becomes anxious or agitated. The mechanism is that the caregiver treats the question as a request for information and supplies the information, but because the memory of the answer is not retained, the question recurs unchanged, and the caregiver interprets the recurrence as the person "not listening," when in fact the question was never primarily about the time. The false signal is the surface meaning of the question, which looks like a request for a fact. The harm is that the underlying need — hunger, anxiety, boredom, the need for the toilet — is never met, so the repetition continues and escalates, the caregiver burns out, and the relationship deteriorates into mutual frustration. The defense is to treat persistent repetition as a signal of an unmet need, to look beyond the literal question for hunger, pain, fear, or discomfort, to address that need directly, and to offer engagement or reassurance rather than re-supplying information the person cannot retain.

### Overloading The Person With Choices Or Multi-Step Instructions

In an effort to respect autonomy, a caregiver may offer wide-open choices ("What would you like for lunch?"), or in an effort to be efficient, may chain instructions ("Come with me, sit here, take off your jacket, and we'll get started"). The mechanism is that both patterns exceed the person's reduced processing capacity: the open choice requires generating and comparing options the person can no longer hold in mind, and the chained instruction requires sequencing and holding multiple steps, so the person freezes, gives a random answer, or refuses, which the caregiver reads as non-compliance. The false signal is that the person appeared to understand (they may nod or say yes), which the caregiver takes as evidence the message landed. The harm is that the person fails at a task they could have managed with simpler communication, feels incompetent or frightened, and resists the care that follows, while the caregiver concludes the person is being difficult. The defense is to offer two concrete options at most, to give one instruction at a time and wait for it to be completed before the next, and to recognize freezing or refusal as a sign of overload rather than opposition.

### Talking About The Person In Front Of Them As If They Are Absent

When coordinating with a family member or another caregiver, it is convenient to discuss the person's condition, behavior, or care across them as though they cannot understand, sometimes within arm's length. The mechanism is that the person's impaired expression is mistaken for absent comprehension, so the caregivers speak freely, assuming no harm, when in fact the person often understands more than they can express and absorbs the emotional tone fully. The false signal is the person's silence or apparent inattention, which is read as absence. The harm is that the person hears themselves described as a burden, a problem, or a set of symptoms, feels humiliated or frightened, and may become distressed or withdrawn in ways the caregivers then attribute to the disease rather than to their own conduct. The defense is to include the person in conversations about them whenever possible, to address them directly, and to hold sensitive discussions privately, assuming comprehension remains until there is clear evidence it does not.

### Attributing An Acute Communication Decline To The Dementia

When a person who communicated reasonably well yesterday is suddenly more confused, less able to find words, or unable to follow instructions, the caregiver may record it as "dementia worsening" and adjust expectations downward rather than investigate. The mechanism is that dementia is the most available explanation for any cognitive change in a person who has dementia, so the acute change is folded into the chronic disease and the possibility of a separate, treatable cause is not considered. The false signal is the person's diagnosis, which makes cognitive decline seem expected and unremarkable. The harm is that a delirium caused by a urinary tract infection, dehydration, a medication interaction, an undetected pain, or a stroke goes unrecognized and untreated, sometimes with permanent consequences, because the change was attributed to the disease the person already had. The defense is to know each person's baseline, to treat any change over hours or days as a potential acute problem rather than as progression, to look for and document associated physical signs, and to escalate promptly so a clinician can distinguish delirium from decline.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I match my communication — sentence length, pace, one idea at a time — to the person's ability at this moment, not to their history?
- [ ] For emotionally held false beliefs, did I respond to the feeling and redirect, rather than correct the fact and cause distress?
- [ ] For repeated questions, did I look for and address the underlying need (hunger, pain, anxiety, toilet) rather than re-answering the literal question?
- [ ] Did I give care instructions one step at a time, with concrete positive language and meaningful choices, and treat refusal as communication rather than opposition?
- [ ] Did I preserve the person's dignity — addressing them directly, not discussing them in their presence, not infantilizing or mocking?
- [ ] Did I notice any acute change in communication against the person's baseline, and escalate it as a possible clinical problem rather than attributing it to dementia?
- [ ] Did I document the baseline, the change, and any associated symptoms for the nurse or clinician?
- [ ] Did I stay within my scope — communicating, observing, and reporting — rather than diagnosing the dementia type or stage or testing the person's cognition?
