# Responding To Distress And Behavioral Expressions

The behaviors that accompany dementia — agitation, aggression, repetitive questioning, wandering, refusal of care, calling out, shadowing, sundowning, accusations, sleep disturbance — are the most distressing and exhausting part of dementia care, and the most common reason families seek institutional placement or request sedating medication. The judgment problem is that these behaviors are almost never random symptoms of the disease to be suppressed; they are expressions of unmet need, pain, fear, or overwhelm communicated by a brain that has lost the ability to communicate in words. The recurring failures are labeling the behavior as "the dementia" and reaching for medication without investigating the cause; missing pain, infection, constipation, and other physical triggers; responding to distress with confrontation that escalates it; and treating the behavior as the problem rather than as a signal pointing to the problem. The central discipline is to become a detective rather than a controller: to ask what the person needs and what is hurting them, before deciding how to respond.

Use this skill when a caregiver reports a distressed or distressed-appearing behavior in a person with dementia and needs help understanding, de-escalating, preventing, or deciding whether to escalate for medical or specialist help. This skill focuses on the immediate behavioral response; pair it with the broader dementia-support skill for environment, communication, and planning.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Behavioral Expression As Communication Of Unmet Need

The foundational reframe is that behavior is communication. A person with dementia who cannot say "I am in pain," "I am frightened," "I do not understand what you are doing," "I need the toilet," "I am hungry," or "I am overstimulated" will express that need through behavior — resistance, pacing, calling out, striking out, refusing. Before doing anything else, ask: what is this person trying to tell me? Common unmet needs include pain, hunger or thirst, a full bladder or bowel, fatigue, infection (especially urinary tract), constipation, hunger, thirst, feeling cold or hot, fear, loneliness, boredom, and sensory overload or deprivation. The behavior is the message; suppressing it with medication without reading the message leaves the need unmet and the person suffering silently. Make identifying the unmet need the first move, every time.

### Always Look For A Physical Cause, Especially Pain And Infection

A sudden change in behavior or a new or worsening behavioral expression in a person with dementia is a medical signal until proven otherwise, because the diseased brain often cannot report symptoms in any other way. The single most under-recognized driver is pain — from arthritis, constipation, a urinary tract infection, dental problems, pressure sores, an injury from an unwitnessed fall, or ill-fitting footwear — and untreated pain produces agitation, aggression, and refusal of care that no behavioral approach will fix. Infection, especially urinary tract infection, is the classic cause of acute confusion and agitation in dementia and must be checked. Constipation is another silent driver. The rule is: any new or suddenly worse behavior warrants a search for a physical cause, and assuming the behavior is "just the dementia" without this search is a serious error. Advocate for a clinical assessment when a physical cause is plausible.

### Use The Detect-Approach: Detective, Environment, Communication, Team

A practical framework for any behavioral expression is to work through layers before considering medication. First, be a detective: what happened just before, what is the pattern across days, what time, who was present, what was being attempted? Patterns reveal triggers. Second, address the environment: reduce noise, clutter, and stimulation; check temperature, lighting, and comfort; remove the person from an overwhelming setting. Third, adjust communication: slow down, use a calm tone and low pitch, get to eye level, use the person's name, offer one simple thing at a time, avoid arguing or correcting, and use touch only if it is welcome. Fourth, bring in the team: share what you have observed with the clinician, the family, and other caregivers, because consistent, documented patterns are what allow a real plan to form. Most behavioral expressions improve substantially with this layered approach and never require medication.

### Respond To Acute Agitation Or Aggression With De-Escalation, Not Force

When a person with dementia becomes acutely agitated or aggressive — striking out, shouting, threatening — the immediate goal is safety and de-escalation, never control or punishment. Give the person space; do not crowd or corner them; remove other people and triggers from the area; lower your voice and slow your movements; avoid direct confrontation, demands, or "no"; do not attempt to force care (a bath, a medication, a transfer) that is triggering the response — stop and return later; identify and remove the immediate trigger if possible; and use the person's name and reassuring, simple language. Recognize that aggression in dementia is almost always fear-driven self-defense from a brain that misperceives a threat, not malice. If you are the trigger (the person has become afraid of or angry with you), step out and let another caregiver the person trusts take over. Physical restraint is a last resort for imminent danger, never a routine response, and carries serious risk of injury and death in people with dementia; if it is ever needed, it must be minimal, brief, and followed by review.

### Address Specific Common Expressions Through Their Likely Drivers

Different expressions tend to have characteristic drivers worth knowing. Repetitive questioning usually reflects anxiety, a need for reassurance, or short-term memory loss, and responds to calm, consistent answers, reassurance, and redirecting to a comforting activity rather than irritation. Wandering often reflects an unmet need (hunger, toileting, boredom), a desire to "go home" to a place that no longer exists, pain, or overstimulation, and responds to meeting the need, safe walking outlets, secured exits, and identification in case of elopement. Sundowning — late-day agitation — often reflects fatigue, hunger, low light, and disrupted body clocks, and responds to daytime activity and light, an early and calm evening routine, a snack, and reduced evening stimulation. Refusal of care usually reflects fear, misunderstanding, pain, or feeling rushed, and responds to slowing down, warming the environment, explaining, offering choice, and returning later. Accusations (often of theft or infidelity) reflect the brain confabulating to fill memory gaps and the emotion of loss, and respond to joining the feeling, reassuring, and avoiding defensive argument — never take it personally or try to prove innocence.

### Build Prevention And Routine Into The Day

Most behavioral expressions are preventable through a structured, calm, predictable day that meets needs before they become distress. Build in consistent times for meals, hydration, toileting, rest, and meaningful activity; reduce unstructured, overstimulating time; ensure adequate daytime light and exercise to support sleep; address pain on a schedule rather than waiting for it to be reported; and keep the environment and the caregivers as consistent as possible, because change destabilizes the dementia brain. Document what works and what triggers distress, so the approach becomes data-driven rather than reactive. Prevention is far more effective and humane than managing a crisis, and a good daily structure prevents most crises.

### Reserve Medication For Specific, Documented Indications, Never As First-Line Convenience

Medication for behavioral expressions in dementia — antipsychotics in particular — has a limited role, carries serious risks (a black-box warning for increased mortality in dementia, sedation, falls, stroke, worsening cognition, and movement disorders), and should never be the default response to distress or to caregiver fatigue. When medication is considered, it should be for a specific, documented, severe, and persistent indication (such as distress that endangers the person or others and has not responded to non-pharmacologic approaches), prescribed at the lowest effective dose, with a plan for review and deprescribing, and with informed discussion of the risks. The goal is comfort and safety, not sedation, and sedation that masks unmet need is not treatment. If a caregiver is requesting medication because they are exhausted, the right response is more support and respite for the caregiver, not a sedative for the person.

## Common Traps

### Reaching For Medication Before Investigating The Cause

The caregiver, exhausted by a distressing behavior, asks the doctor for something to calm the person, and an antipsychotic or sedative is prescribed without a search for the cause. The mechanism of harm is that the underlying pain, infection, fear, or unmet need goes untreated, the person is sedated into a quieter but still suffering state, and the medication carries serious risks including increased mortality. The false signal is that the behavior "settled." The harm is untreated suffering, side effects, falls, and accelerated decline, for a behavior that often had a reversible cause. Always search for and treat the cause first; reserve medication for specific, severe, documented indications.

### Missing Pain Or Infection As The Driver

The caregiver attributes new agitation or refusal to "the dementia getting worse" and does not consider that the person is in pain or has an infection. The mechanism of harm is that the physical cause — a urinary tract infection, severe constipation, a fracture from an unwitnessed fall, dental pain — goes untreated, the behavior is managed with sedation or endured, and the underlying condition worsens, sometimes fatally. The false signal is that there is "no obvious reason." The harm is treatable physical illness masquerading as behavioral decline. Treat any sudden change in behavior as a medical signal and advocate for assessment of pain, infection, constipation, and injury.

### Responding To Distress With Confrontation That Escalates It

The caregiver, frustrated or frightened, raises their voice, argues, insists, crowds the person, or tries to force the triggering care, and the agitation becomes aggression. The mechanism of harm is that the diseased brain reads the confrontation as threat and responds with fight-or-flight, escalating exactly the behavior the caregiver wanted to reduce. The false signal is that firmness "should work." The harm is aggression, injury, and a damaged relationship. De-escalation — space, calm, slowing down, stopping the trigger — reduces what confrontation amplifies.

### Taking Accusations Or Rejection Personally

The caregiver is wounded when the person accuses them of theft, fails to recognize them, or pushes them away, and responds with hurt, defensiveness, or withdrawal. The mechanism of harm is that the caregiver's emotional reaction becomes part of the dynamic, the person senses the tension, and the relationship — often a spouse or child — is damaged by a disease the person cannot help. The false signal is that the behavior reflects the person's true feelings. The harm is caregiver burnout, guilt, and the erosion of a bond. The reframe is that the disease is speaking, not the person, and the response is to join the emotion, reassure, and not defend.

### Treating Each Episode In Isolation Rather Than Finding The Pattern

The caregiver manages each behavioral episode as a one-off crisis and never steps back to look for the pattern, so the same triggers keep producing the same distress. The mechanism of harm is that without documentation, the triggers — a particular time, task, person, or environment — are never identified and removed, and the behavior recurs indefinitely. The false signal is that each episode is unpredictable. The harm is preventable distress that persists because no one read the pattern. Keep a simple behavior log: what happened, when, who was there, what came just before, and what helped — the pattern almost always emerges and points to the fix.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat the behavioral expression as communication of unmet need, and ask what the person is trying to tell me, before deciding how to respond?
- Did I search for a physical cause — especially pain, infection, constipation, or injury — before attributing the behavior to the dementia?
- Did I work through the layers — detective work on triggers, environment, communication, and team — before considering medication?
- In acute agitation or aggression, did I use de-escalation (space, calm, slowing, removing the trigger) rather than force, confrontation, or restraint?
- Did I match my response to the specific expression's likely driver, rather than applying a one-size-fits-all approach?
- Did I help build a structured, calm, predictable daily routine that meets needs before they become distress?
- If medication is being considered, is it for a specific, severe, documented indication at the lowest dose with a review plan — not as first-line convenience or a response to caregiver exhaustion?
- Did I document the pattern of episodes so the approach becomes data-driven and preventive rather than purely reactive?
