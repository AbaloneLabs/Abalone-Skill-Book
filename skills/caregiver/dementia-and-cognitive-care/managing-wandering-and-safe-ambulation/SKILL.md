# Managing Wandering and Safe Ambulation

The judgment problem in wandering is that the word itself misleads. "Wandering" sounds purposeless, but a person with dementia who walks about is almost always trying to achieve something — to find a bathroom, to relieve pain or anxiety, to search for a person or a place, to escape an overstimulating environment, to satisfy a lifelong routine of going to work, or simply because sitting still has become unbearable. The danger is twofold and opposite: the caregiver may treat all walking as a problem to be suppressed, restraining a person whose need to move is legitimate and whose stillness would cause worse distress; or the caregiver may treat all walking as harmless exercise, missing the real risks of elopement, getting lost, traffic, weather exposure, and falls. The skilled caregiver reads the walking as communication, makes movement as safe as possible, and puts real safeguards around the situations that could end in serious harm.

This is a high-stakes domain. A person with dementia who leaves a safe area can become lost in familiar surroundings, be injured by traffic or weather, or go missing for hours, and these events can be fatal. The caregiver's role is to understand why the person is moving, provide safe ways to move, reduce the risk of unsafe exit, respond rapidly when a person is missing, and escalate and document — not to diagnose the cause, to lock or restrain without authority, or to assume a person who "always comes back" will always come back.

## Core Rules

### Read Walking As Communication And Look For The Trigger

Before deciding how to respond, ask why the person is moving right now. Wandering is a symptom with many causes, and the right response depends on the cause.

- Unmet physical needs: hunger, thirst, the need to toilet, pain, constipation, or discomfort from clothing or temperature. A person may pace because they need the bathroom and cannot find it or cannot ask.
- Unmet emotional or psychological needs: anxiety, fear, boredom, loneliness, the wish to "go home" or to find a family member, or agitation that rises in the late afternoon ("sundowning").
- Environmental triggers: noise, crowding, an unfamiliar setting, a change of routine, or a closed door that the person is determined to get through.
- Habit and identity: the person may be enacting a lifelong routine — leaving for work, collecting children, walking after dinner — that is meaningful to them.
- Medical triggers: pain, infection, a medication change, or delirium can produce new or worsened restlessness and must be considered and escalated.

Identifying the trigger shapes the response: a person who needs the toilet is helped to the toilet; a person who is bored is engaged; a person in pain is assessed and the pain reported.

### Provide Safe And Meaningful Ways To Move

The need to move is legitimate and suppressing it causes harm. The goal is to make movement safe and purposeful, not to stop it.

- Offer regular, supervised walking at times the person tends to become restless, before the restlessness peaks.
- Provide purposeful activity that uses the body and the attention: folding laundry, setting a table, sorting objects, gardening, or accompanying the caregiver on an errand.
- Create a safe loop or path indoors where the person can walk without encountering hazards or dead ends that frustrate them.
- Ensure basic safety for all walking: safe footwear, adequate lighting, a clear path, and supervision matched to the person's fall risk and cognitive status.

### Put Real Safeguards Around Unsafe Exit And Elopement

Elopement — leaving a safe area without the ability to return safely — is the most dangerous form of walking about, and the safeguards must be proportional to the risk and lawful.

- Know the person's elopement risk from the care plan and from observation; a person who has tried to leave, who talks about going home or to work, or who heads for exits is higher risk.
- Secure or monitor exits in line with the setting's policy and the person's care plan: alarmed doors, coded locks, or door sensors that alert staff when an exit opens. Do not improvise locks or barriers that violate fire safety, regulations, or the person's rights; follow the agreed plan.
- Disguise or de-emphasize exits where appropriate (for example, covering door handles or placing a mural over a door) only as the care plan permits.
- Register the person with any local safe-return or wandering registry, ensure they carry identification, and keep a current photo and description readily available in case they go missing.
- For a person at high risk in a home setting, discuss with the family and the care plan whether additional measures (locks, alarms, supervision levels) are needed and authorized.

### Manage Nighttime Walking Specifically

Walking at night is common and especially risky, because the person may be disoriented, the environment is dark, others are asleep, and the person may leave the home unnoticed.

- Address common contributors: pain, the need to toilet, hunger, daytime underactivity, and reversed day-night rhythms. Daytime activity, sunlight exposure, limited daytime napping, and a calming bedtime routine can help.
- Ensure the nighttime path to the bathroom is lit, clear, and safe, and that the bathroom is easy to find and use.
- Consider whether the person is waking because of a medical issue (pain, urinary frequency, sleep apnea, medication timing) and escalate rather than assuming it is behavioral.
- Use motion sensors or a door alarm so the caregiver is woken if the person gets up or approaches an exit, in line with the care plan.

### Respond Rapidly And Systematically When A Person Is Missing

A person with dementia who is missing is an emergency. The response must be immediate and organized, because the person may hide, may not call for help, and is vulnerable to weather, traffic, and falls.

- Begin an immediate search of the immediate area, including places the person might hide, rest, or be drawn to (a former home, a workplace, a place of worship, a shop they frequent).
- Notify the responsible person, family, and supervisor at once, and contact emergency services without delay — do not wait to see if the person returns, because time and weather are critical.
- Provide a current photo, a description of clothing, the direction last seen, known destinations, and any medical needs.
- After any elopement, however it resolves, participate in a review of why it happened and what safeguards must change; a near-miss is a warning.

### Document the Basis and the Reasoning

Every conclusion should be traceable to its evidence, assumptions, and alternatives considered. Record not only the outcome but the reasoning path: what was checked, what was ruled out, what uncertainty remains, and what would change the conclusion. Documentation that captures the basis allows another practitioner to review, reproduce, or challenge the work, and it prevents confident conclusions from becoming unrepeatable assertions. A decision made without a recorded basis cannot be audited, improved, or safely handed off.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Walking As A Problem To Be Stopped

When a person with dementia paces the corridor repeatedly, the caregiver's first impulse may be to get them to sit down, because the walking looks purposeless and disruptive and the caregiver wants order. The mechanism is that the walking is read as a symptom to be suppressed rather than as a need to be met, so the caregiver redirects to a chair without investigating the cause, and the person, whose need to move or to find something remains unmet, becomes more agitated, gets up again, or resists. The false signal is the appearance of purposelessness, which justifies stopping the behavior. The harm is that a legitimate need — to toilet, to relieve pain, to escape overstimulation, to enact a meaningful routine — goes unmet, the person's distress rises, and the caregiver may escalate to restraint or medication when the underlying cause was addressable. The defense is to treat walking as communication first, to look for and address the trigger before trying to stop the movement, and to provide safe, meaningful ways to move rather than suppressing the need to move altogether.

### Assuming A Person Who Has Always Returned Will Always Return

When a person has left the home or facility before and come back on their own, the caregiver may relax the safeguards, reasoning that the person knows their way and "always comes back." The mechanism is that the prior safe returns lower the perceived risk, so the next exit is treated as routine and the search is delayed, even though dementia means the person's spatial memory and problem-solving are declining, so a route that was manageable last month may not be manageable now. The false signal is the string of past safe returns, which is taken as evidence of continuing competence. The harm is that on the day the person does not return — because they have progressed, because the weather has changed, because they fell, because they took a different turn — the delayed response costs critical time, and a recoverable event becomes a tragedy. The defense is to treat every exit by a person at risk as an emergency regardless of past returns, to maintain the safeguards consistently, and to recognize that past success predicts nothing about the next episode in a progressive disease.

### Relying On Telling The Person Not To Leave

The caregiver may believe that explaining to the person why they should not go out, or reminding them that this is their home, will prevent elopement, and may therefore under-invest in physical safeguards. The mechanism is that the caregiver relies on the person's retained reasoning and memory, both of which are impaired, so the explanation is understood in the moment and forgotten by the time the person reaches the door, or is overridden by a strong drive to leave. The false signal is the person's apparent agreement in conversation, which looks like the message landing. The harm is that the door is unsecured because the caregiver trusts the verbal agreement, and the person walks out an hour later with no memory of the conversation, into traffic or cold weather. The defense is to recognize that verbal agreement does not reliably prevent elopement in dementia, to combine communication with the physical and environmental safeguards in the care plan, and never to substitute a conversation for an alarm, a lock, or supervision.

### Ignoring The Weather And Time Element In A Missing Person

When a person goes missing, the search may focus on the person's likely destinations while under-weighting the lethal variables of temperature, darkness, terrain, and the person's clothing and physical condition. The mechanism is that the search is organized around where the person might be, which feels concrete, while the time and weather exposure, which determine survival, are treated as background rather than as the driver of urgency. The false signal is the familiarity of the neighborhood, which suggests the person is fine. The harm is that a search for a lightly-clothed person with dementia is conducted at walking pace on a cold night, the emergency services are called late because the person "probably just went to the shop," and the person is found hours later with hypothermia or not at all. The defense is to treat time, temperature, weather, terrain, clothing, and the person's medical frailty as the determinants of urgency, to call emergency services immediately rather than after a self-search, and to escalate without waiting for the person to return.

### Attributing New Or Worsened Restlessness To The Dementia

When a person who has been calm begins to pace, try doors, or wander at new times, the caregiver may record it as disease progression and adjust routines rather than investigate a medical cause. The mechanism is that the dementia diagnosis absorbs the new behavior, so the possibility that the restlessness is pain, infection, constipation, dehydration, a medication change, or delirium is not considered, and a treatable cause is missed. The false signal is the chronic diagnosis, which makes the new symptom seem expected. The harm is that a urinary tract infection causing agitation, an undiagnosed pain driving the person to move, or a medication side effect producing restlessness goes untreated, the behavior worsens, and a reversible cause of distress becomes a crisis. The defense is to treat any new or changed pattern of walking about as a possible sign of a medical problem, to look for and document associated symptoms, and to escalate to the nurse or clinician so the cause can be investigated rather than assumed.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I look for the trigger behind the walking — physical need, emotional need, environment, habit, or a medical cause — before trying to stop or redirect it?
- [ ] Did I provide safe and meaningful ways to move, matched to the person's fall risk and cognitive status, rather than suppressing the need to move?
- [ ] Did I follow the care plan's authorized safeguards for exits and elopement (alarms, locks, sensors, identification, registry) and avoid improvising measures that violate safety or rights?
- [ ] Did I address nighttime walking contributors — pain, toileting, daytime activity, day-night rhythm — and ensure a safe, lit path, while escalating possible medical causes?
- [ ] If a person was missing, did I search immediately, notify the responsible people at once, and call emergency services without delay, providing photo, clothing, direction, and destinations?
- [ ] Did I treat every exit by a person at risk as an emergency, regardless of past safe returns, and avoid relying on verbal agreement not to leave?
- [ ] Did I escalate any new or worsened pattern of walking about as a possible medical problem, documenting associated symptoms, rather than attributing it to dementia?
- [ ] Did I stay within my scope — understanding, safeguarding, searching, reporting — and follow the agreed plan rather than independently diagnosing, locking, or restraining?
