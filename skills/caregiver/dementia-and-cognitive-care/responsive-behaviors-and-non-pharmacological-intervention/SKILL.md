# Responsive Behaviors and Non-Pharmacological Intervention

What gets labeled "agitation," "aggression," "wandering," or "sundowning" is rarely random. In dementia, behavior is communication — the client is responding to something: pain, hunger, a soiled brief, an overstimulating environment, an unmet need, a perceived threat, a urinary tract infection. The dominant trap in care settings is to treat the behavior as the problem and reach for medication to suppress it, rather than investigating the trigger. Antipsychotics and sedatives do not fix unmet needs; they sedate the client, increase fall risk, and are associated with increased mortality in elderly dementia clients. The skill is in reading behavior as a signal, systematically identifying the trigger, and addressing the root cause before considering pharmacological intervention.

## Core Rules

### Treat Behavior as Communication, Not as a Problem to Suppress

A client who strikes out during personal care is not "being aggressive" — they are likely communicating fear, a sense of violation, pain, or an inability to understand what is happening. A client who paces is not "wandering" — they may be looking for a bathroom, a door, a person, or relief from boredom. A client who calls out repeatedly is not "agitated" — they may be in pain, frightened, or seeking reassurance. The first response to any behavioral expression is curiosity: what is this person trying to tell me? Suppressing the behavior with medication silences the communication without addressing the need.

### Investigate Triggers Systematically

Use a structured approach to identify what precedes the behavior. The DICE (Describe, Investigate, Create, Evaluate) framework or a simple ABC (Antecedent, Behavior, Consequence) log over one to two weeks often reveals patterns: the behavior happens during bathing (suggesting fear or cold), at shift change (suggesting overstimulation), after meals (suggesting postprandial discomfort), or when a specific staff member is present (suggesting an interaction pattern). Document the time, setting, people present, what happened immediately before, and what the behavior looked like. Patterns emerge that point to the trigger.

### Rule Out Physical Causes First

Before attributing behavior to dementia, rule out the common physical causes: pain (often undertreated because the client cannot report it), urinary tract infection (a classic cause of acute behavioral change in elderly dementia clients), constipation or fecal impaction, medication side effects or interactions, hunger or thirst, a soiled brief, an ill-fitting device, an underlying infection, or a recent fall. Acute behavioral change in a previously stable client is a medical red flag until proven otherwise — it warrants assessment for a new physical cause, not a behavior plan.

### Implement Non-Pharmacological Interventions First

Once a trigger is identified, the intervention should address it directly: pain management for pain, toileting schedules for incontinence-related distress, modified bathing approaches (towels baths, warming the room, bathing at the client's preferred time) for bathing resistance, environmental modification (reducing noise, improving lighting, adding familiar objects) for overstimulation, structured activity and engagement for boredom, and validation and reassurance for fear. Music, touch, familiar photographs, pet therapy, and one-on-one engagement have evidence for reducing distress behaviors. These interventions require staff time and consistency, which is why medication is often substituted — but they address the cause rather than suppressing the symptom.

### Reserve Pharmacological Intervention for Specific Circumstances

Antipsychotic medication should be reserved for situations where the client is in imminent danger to themselves or others, where non-pharmacological interventions have been systematically tried and failed, where the distress is severe and persistent, and where the benefit clearly outweighs the documented risks (increased mortality, sedation, falls, stroke). The decision should involve the physician, the family, and documented evidence of the interventions tried. If medication is started, there should be a plan to reassess and attempt tapering — it should not become a permanent default.

### Adapt the Environment and the Approach

Many distress behaviors are iatrogenic — caused by the care environment and the care approach. A loud, brightly lit, unfamiliar environment with multiple staff rotating through will distress a client who cannot process that input. A task-focused approach that moves quickly through care without explanation or rapport will provoke resistance. The environment and approach should be adapted to the client's capacity: consistent staffing, calm pacing, explanation before touch, respect for personal space, and modification of routines to fit the client's preferences rather than the facility's schedule.

## Common Traps

### The Antipsychotic Default

A client exhibits distress behavior, and within days they are on an antipsychotic. The trap is that the medication appears to work — the client is quieter, easier to care for, less disruptive. But the behavior was suppressed, not resolved; the unmet need remains, the fall risk has increased, and the client is now at elevated mortality risk. The medication becomes a permanent default because no one attempts to taper it. Antipsychotics have a role, but they are overused precisely because they are easier than investigating triggers and implementing consistent non-pharmacological care.

### Labeling Instead of Investigating

"He's a wanderer." "She's a sundowner." "He's combative." Once a label is applied, investigation stops — the behavior is attributed to the client's dementia and the label, rather than treated as a signal to be decoded. The trap is that the label becomes self-perpetuating: the client is assumed to be agitated, so staff approach with anxiety and rigidity, which provokes the very behavior the label predicted. Replacing labels with descriptions ("paces between 3 and 5 pm, calms with a snack and a walk") opens the door to intervention.

### Ignoring Pain Because the Client Cannot Report It

Clients with advanced dementia cannot reliably report pain, and the default assumption is often that they are not in pain. But pain prevalence is high in this population (arthritis, contractures, pressure injuries, recent procedures), and untreated pain is a major cause of distress behavior. The trap is attributing the behavior to dementia when the cause is a painful condition that could be treated. Use observational pain assessment tools (PAINAD, the Abbey Pain Scale) and a low threshold for an analgesic trial — if the behavior improves with pain treatment, pain was the cause.

### Treating All Staff the Same

A client who is calm with one caregiver and distressed with another is telling you something important. The trap is attributing the difference to the client ("she's just variable") rather than to the interaction. The caregiver who moves slowly, explains before touching, and has built rapport may receive cooperation where a task-focused, hurried caregiver receives resistance. The behavioral log should note which staff are present during distress — the pattern often points to the interaction, not the client.

## Self-Check

- Did I investigate the trigger before reaching for intervention, or did I label the behavior and suppress it?
- Did I rule out physical causes (pain, UTI, constipation, medication) before attributing the behavior to dementia?
- Did I keep a behavioral log over time to identify patterns, or did I rely on impressions?
- Did I implement non-pharmacological interventions systematically before considering medication?
- If medication was started, is there a documented plan to reassess and attempt tapering?
- Did I adapt the environment and the care approach, or did I expect the client to adapt to the setting?
- Did I use observational pain assessment, or assume the client was not in pain because they could not report it?
- Did I note which staff are present during distress, to identify interaction patterns?
