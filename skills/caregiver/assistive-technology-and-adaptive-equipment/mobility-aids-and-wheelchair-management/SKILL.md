# Mobility Aids And Wheelchair Management

Mobility aids are prescribed clinical devices, not convenience props. A walker, cane, or wheelchair that is the wrong height, the wrong type, poorly maintained, or used with the wrong technique destabilizes the very person it is meant to protect and becomes a direct cause of falls. The same is true of wheelchair seating: a chair that looks fine can quietly manufacture a pressure injury over the sacrum, ischial tuberosities, or heels because the cushion is wrong, the posture has slumped, or pressure relief is not happening. Caregivers tend to treat these devices as static furniture — set once, used forever — when in fact the person's body, the equipment, and the technique all drift over days and weeks, and the drift is where the harm accumulates.

The judgment problem is that an ill-fitting aid rarely fails dramatically. It fails by degrees: a cane that is two centimeters too tall subtly shifts weight onto the weak side; a wheelchair footrest that is too low gradually swells the calves; a cushion that has lost its inflation silently concentrates pressure on one ischium. By the time a fall or a pressure injury appears, the cause has been present for days. The caregiver's job is to check fit and function proactively every shift, to recognize that a device the person "has always used" may no longer be correct, and to escalate fitting and prescription changes to the therapist rather than improvising adjustments. This skill covers fit checks, wheelchair positioning and pressure relief, equipment maintenance, and the scope boundary between caregiver tasks and therapist/clinician authority.

## Core Rules

### Treat Fit As A Clinical Safety Check, Not A One-Time Setup

A mobility aid is only safe when it fits the person's current body, and bodies change. Weight loss, edema, contractures, new weakness, a change in footwear, or even a different pair of shoes shifts the correct height and the safe use of the device.

- Cane and crutch height: the handgrip should sit at the level of the wrist crease when the person stands upright with arms relaxed, elbow flexed about 20 to 30 degrees when bearing weight. A cane that is too high hitches the shoulder and reduces control; too low forces a stoop that destabilizes balance and strains the back.
- Walker height: the handgrips should align with the wrist crease or the crease of the hip joint when standing inside the frame. A walker set too low is a leading cause of forward-flexed, unstable posture in older adults.
- Check fit with the person wearing the shoes they actually use, because a thick-soled shoe changes the measurement. Do not fit in socks and then send the person out in different footwear.
- Re-check fit after any fall, any noticeable change in posture or gait, any weight change, and whenever the person reports the device "feels off." Do not assume the original fitting still holds.

### Teach And Reinforce Correct Technique, Because Misuse Causes Falls

The right device used the wrong way is still a fall hazard. The caregiver who supervises ambulation is responsible for noticing and correcting technique, not just walking alongside.

- Cane on the strong side, advancing with the weak side: the cane moves forward together with the weaker leg so it shares the load. Using the cane on the weak side, or swinging it wide, removes the support exactly when the weak limb needs it.
- Walker advanced, then stepped into, not pulled along while walking: the person moves the walker, steps into it, and only then takes the next step. Lifting the walker too high, or shuffling it while moving, reduces stability and tires the arms.
- Never pull or steady a person by grabbing the walker, cane, or wheelchair armrest during a wobble; grab the person (via a gait belt) or the device, not both, and never lift weight through equipment that was not designed to be a handle.
- Watch for the device being used as a reacher (leaning on the walker to grab something across the room), which tips it and the person.

### Manage Wheelchair Positioning To Prevent Injury, Not Just For Transport

A wheelchair is a seating system, and poor positioning causes pressure injuries, postural deformity, swallowing and breathing compromise, and unsafe ejection during movement.

- Seat the person fully back in the chair with the pelvis against the backrest; slumping or sliding forward ("sacral sitting") concentrates pressure and shear on the sacrum and is a primary cause of wheelchair pressure injury.
- Feet flat and supported on the footrests at the correct height: footrests too low leave the thighs unsupported and concentrate pressure on the ischial tuberosities; too high flexes the hips and pushes the person forward. The thighs should be roughly horizontal, distributing weight across the seat.
- Use the prescribed cushion (pressure-relieving, gel, air, or foam) and confirm it is the right one for that person and that it is correctly inflated and oriented. An air cushion that has lost pressure, or a foam cushion that is bottomed out, provides no relief.
- Apply posture and positioning supports (lateral trunk supports, pelvic belt, headrest, foot straps) as prescribed; they are not optional accessories. A lap belt or pelvic positioning belt is for posture, distinct from a safety lap belt for transport — know which is which.
- Keep the backrest angle and tilt as prescribed; tilt-in-space and recline redistribute pressure and aid posture but must be set to the assessed position, not left wherever the last person left it.

### Perform Pressure Relief On A Schedule For Every Chairbound Person

No cushion eliminates pressure. A person who cannot independently shift weight in a wheelchair will develop a pressure injury unless pressure relief happens on a schedule.

- Independent relief: teach and prompt the person to lift (push-ups), lean side to side, or tilt back every 15 to 30 minutes. Do not assume they will remember; cognition and fatigue erode adherence.
- Dependent relief: for a person who cannot shift weight themselves, reposition them — lean them side to side, tilt the chair back, or transfer out of the chair — at least every hour, and more often for high-risk skin. Two hours in a static wheelchair position is too long for an at-risk person.
- Combine chair relief with skin checks at the change of shift and after any prolonged sitting; look at the sacrum, ischial tuberosities (seat bones), coccyx, and heels.
- Treat a cushion as a complement to relief, never a substitute for it. The most expensive air cushion will still permit breakdown in an immobile person who is never repositioned.

### Inspect And Maintain Equipment Every Shift

Mobility equipment fails silently. A loose brake, a worn rubber tip, a flat tire, or a torn sling can cause a fall or a collapse with no warning.

- Walkers and canes: check rubber tips (ferrules) are present, intact, and not worn smooth or cracked — a bald tip slips on smooth floors. Check that the frame is rigid, height-adjustment buttons lock securely, and any wheels roll freely and lock.
- Wheelchairs: confirm brakes (wheel locks) engage firmly and hold, tires are inflated or solid casters intact, footrests and armrests lock in place, the sling seat is not sagging or torn, and the cushion is the correct one and properly inflated. Test that the chair does not roll when brakes are applied.
- Battery and powered chairs: confirm the battery is charged, the controller responds, and any tilt/recline functions work; a dead battery mid-transfer or mid-meal strands the person.
- Remove any device that is broken, missing parts, or visibly unsafe from use, label it out of service, and escalate for repair or replacement. Do not "make do" with a broken aid while waiting.

### Know Your Scope: Fit And Prescribe Belong To The Therapist

Caregivers check, maintain, reposition, prompt, and report. They do not prescribe, resize beyond minor adjustment, change cushion type, or alter a seating prescription.

- Routine adjustments within the care plan (e.g., re-seating the person correctly, restoring a cushion to its prescribed setting) are caregiver scope.
- Changing the type of aid, raising or lowering a wheelchair seat-to-floor height, swapping a cushion for a different model, or fitting a new orthotic or brace requires a therapist (PT/OT) assessment. Document the problem and request the review rather than improvising.
- Any skin breakdown, new postural deformity, recurrent falls, or sustained pain related to the device must be escalated the same shift, not logged for the next care conference.

## Common Traps

### Using An Ill-Fitting Aid Because It Is "The Client's Own"

The trap begins because the cane or walker belongs to the person, has been used for months, and was presumably correct once, so the caregiver assumes it remains correct. The mechanism is that ownership and familiarity stand in for fit; nobody re-measures because the device is treated as a fixed possession rather than a clinical tool that must match a changing body. The false signal is that the person is managing with it, which is read as proof it fits. The harm is that gradual height drift — from a bent frame, a slipped adjustment button, new footwear, or the person's own changed posture — quietly destabilizes gait until a fall occurs that is then attributed to the floor or to clumsiness rather than to the two-centimeter error that has been present for weeks. The defense is to check cane, crutch, and walker height against the wrist crease on a schedule and after any change, and to treat "the client's own device" as subject to the same fit checks as loaned equipment.

### Grabbing The Walker Or Wheelchair To Steady A Wobbling Person

When a person stumbles, the instinct is to grab whatever is closest, which is usually the walker frame or the wheelchair armrest. The mechanism is that the equipment feels solid and the caregiver reaches for it as a handle. The false signal is that the grab seems to steady things. The harm is that the device moves, tips, or rolls — a walker skids away, a wheelchair with unlocked brakes slides — and the person, now leaning on something that is no longer where it was, falls harder than if nothing had been grabbed. The defense is to steady the person through a gait belt or by guiding the person's trunk, never by pulling on the mobility device, and to ensure wheelchair brakes are locked whenever the person transfers or is being assisted.

### Assuming The Cushion Makes Repositioning Unnecessary

Once a pressure-relieving cushion is in place, the caregiver reduces or skips weight shifts and repositioning on the belief that the equipment handles it. The mechanism is that an expensive, clinical-looking device is mistaken for a complete solution. The false signal is the cushion's presence and the absence of immediate redness. The harm is that no cushion eliminates pressure; an immobile person on the best cushion available will still develop ischial and sacral breakdown if never repositioned, and the breakdown appears late because the cushion created a false sense of security that suppressed the skin checks that would have caught it early. The defense is to continue scheduled pressure relief and skin checks regardless of the cushion, and to verify the cushion is correctly inflated and positioned, not merely present.

### Leaving Wheelchair Brakes Unlocked During Transfers And Care

The caregiver positions the wheelchair, assists the person to stand, and the chair rolls away mid-transfer because the brakes were not set. The mechanism is that setting brakes is an invisible step in a familiar routine and is easily skipped when the caregiver is focused on the person. The false signal is that the chair did not move on the previous transfer. The harm is that an unlocked chair is a moving platform exactly when the person is most vulnerable — during a partial-weight-bearing stand or a hoist approach — and the resulting fall, shear injury, or dropped transfer is entirely preventable. The defense is to make brake-checking a non-negotiable first step of every wheelchair interaction, to verify both brakes hold under load, and never to begin a transfer until they are confirmed set.

### Failing To Notice Sacral Sitting And Forward Slump

The person is placed in the wheelchair correctly at the start, but over an hour slides forward until the pelvis is at the front of the seat and the spine is slumped — sacral sitting. The mechanism is that gravity, low muscle tone, and an unsuitable seat angle cause gradual migration that no one re-checks because the person was positioned well initially. The false signal is that the person is "still in the chair." The harm is that sacral sitting concentrates pressure and shear on the sacrum and coccyx, the most common site of deep wheelchair pressure injury, and it also compromises breathing, swallowing, and dignity. The defense is to re-check pelvic position at every interaction, to reposition the person fully back against the backrest regularly, and to escalate recurrent sliding for a seating review (tilt, anti-thrust seat, or pelvic belt).

### Continuing To Use Broken Or Worn Equipment Until It Is Replaced

A rubber tip is bald, a brake is loose, a tire is soft, or a sling sags, and the caregiver continues using the device because a replacement has not arrived. The mechanism is that the defect seems minor and the device still "works." The false signal is that no fall has happened yet. The harm is that a worn tip slips on a smooth floor, a soft tire destabilizes the chair, and a sagging sling creates a pressure pocket — each a latent fall or injury cause that activates without warning, often on the day the caregiver is in a hurry. The defense is to inspect equipment every shift, to remove and label unsafe devices out of service immediately rather than continuing to use them, and to escalate repair or replacement the same day.

### Overlooking The Edge Case Of The Asymmetrical Or One-Sided Weakness Client

The textbook assumes a roughly symmetrical user, but the stroke, hemiplegia, or amputation client loads the device and the chair asymmetrically. The mechanism is that standard checks and standard cushions are applied without accounting for the unequal load. The false signal is that the setup "looks balanced." The harm is that the weaker or absent side bears no weight while the strong side is overloaded, producing pressure injury on the loaded ischium and postural collapse toward the weak side, and a cane used on the wrong side removes support from the very limb that needs it. The defense is to always ask how this person's specific asymmetry changes fit, cushioning, and technique, and to confirm the aid is on the correct side and the seating addresses the lean.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I check the fit of the cane, crutch, or walker against the person's wrist crease with their actual footwear, and re-check after any fall, posture change, or weight change?
- [ ] Did I confirm correct technique (cane on the strong side advancing with the weak leg; walker advanced then stepped into) and correct misuse rather than just walking alongside?
- [ ] Did I seat the person fully back in the wheelchair with feet correctly supported, the prescribed cushion in place and inflated, and posture supports applied?
- [ ] Did I perform or prompt pressure relief on a schedule (independent shifts every 15 to 30 minutes; dependent repositioning at least hourly), treating the cushion as a complement and not a substitute?
- [ ] Did I check skin over the sacrum, ischial tuberosities, coccyx, and heels after sitting, and escalate any non-blanchable redness or breakdown the same shift?
- [ ] Did I inspect the equipment every shift — rubber tips, brakes, tires, sling, cushion, battery — and remove and label any broken or unsafe device from use rather than continuing with it?
- [ ] Did I set and confirm wheelchair brakes before every transfer and care interaction?
- [ ] Did I stay within scope, escalating prescription, resizing, cushion changes, and any new deformity or recurrent falls to the therapist or clinician instead of improvising?
