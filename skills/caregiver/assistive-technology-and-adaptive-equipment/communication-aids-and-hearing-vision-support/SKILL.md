# Communication Aids And Hearing-Vision Support

Hearing aids, glasses, and communication devices are the bridges between a person and the world. When they are in place and working, the person can perceive, understand, and be understood; when they are missing, dead, dirty, or broken, the person does not simply lose a convenience — they lose orientation, connection, safety awareness, and the ability to make needs known. The most damaging error a caregiver makes is to treat these devices as optional accessories that the person "should ask for," when the very condition the device addresses (hearing loss, low vision, aphasia, cognitive impairment) is the condition that prevents the person from asking. A hearing aid left in a drawer overnight means a confused, uncomprehending resident the next morning who is then labeled "agitated" or "non-compliant" rather than "unable to hear."

The judgment problem is that a missing or dead sensory aid is invisible to the caregiver who is not looking for it. The person who cannot hear does not always know they cannot hear (especially with dementia); the person whose glasses are lost sees a blurry world and may not connect it to a missing object; the person whose communication board is across the room is left silent and assumed to have nothing to say. The caregiver must treat placement, charging, cleaning, and verification of these devices as a clinical routine as important as medication, must recognize that behavior changes often signal a sensory or communication failure rather than a behavioral problem, and must never assume the person cannot communicate when the device is simply missing. This skill covers daily management of hearing aids, glasses, and augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) devices; troubleshooting; behavioral interpretation; and escalation scope.

## Core Rules

### Establish A Daily Routine For Placement, Charging, And Cleaning

These devices fail predictably when routine is weak. A hearing aid runs out of battery overnight; glasses get lost in bedclothes; a speech device is left unplugged and dies mid-morning. Prevention is procedural, not heroic.

- Hearing aids: insert in the morning after a skin and ear check, remove at night, open the battery door or place on the charger, wipe the earmold with a dry cloth, and store in the same labeled container every time. Check the battery or charge level at the start of each shift. Replace batteries on a predictable schedule, not when the aid stops working.
- Glasses: clean lenses with the correct cloth (not clothing or paper towels, which scratch), confirm they are the correct pair for the correct person (label at intake), and store in the case in the same place when not worn. Lost or damaged glasses are a major, expensive, slow-to-replace harm.
- Communication devices and amplifiers: charge overnight, power on and confirm function at the start of the day, and return to the person after any activity in which they were set aside.
- Label every device and its storage container with the person's name at intake. The single most common loss event is a device placed on a meal tray or bedside table and swept away with the linen or trash.

### Verify The Device Is In Place And Working Before Assuming The Person Can Perceive Or Communicate

Before concluding that a person is confused, uncooperative, deaf, or unable to communicate, confirm the aids are actually on and functioning. This single check prevents the majority of misattributed "behavioral" episodes.

- At every meaningful interaction — morning care, meals, activities, care discussions — confirm hearing aids are in and switched on, glasses are on and clean, and the communication device is within reach and powered.
- A person who was oriented yesterday and is "confused and unresponsive" today may simply be unable to hear or see you. Test by getting in their visual field, facing them, speaking slowly, and checking the aids before assuming cognitive decline.
- A person who is "withdrawn and non-communicative" may have no working means to communicate because the board or device is missing, dead, or out of reach. Restore the device and re-assess before recording a communication deficit.

### Interpret Behavior Changes As Possible Sensory Or Device Failure First

Behavior is communication, and a sudden change in behavior is very often a signal that a sensory or communication aid has failed, not that the person's condition has deteriorated.

- New agitation, withdrawal, refusing meals, startling easily, speaking very loudly, or turning the television volume up sharply are classic signs of hearing aid failure or loss. Check the aids before treating the behavior.
- Squinting, holding objects close, bumping into things, refusing to move, or new anxiety in dim light often signal a vision or glasses problem. Check the glasses.
- A person with aphasia or a speech device who becomes silent, frustrated, or aggressive may be experiencing device failure or may have been separated from their communication aid. Find the device and re-assess.
- Do not chart a permanent cognitive or behavioral change until you have ruled out a missing, dead, or dirty sensory aid as the cause.

### Never Assume The Person Cannot Communicate When The Device Is Missing

This is the highest-stakes error in this domain. A person who cannot speak is not a person with nothing to say. When the communication aid is absent, the silence is the caregiver's failure, not the person's limitation.

- A communication board, picture book, or speech-generating device is the person's voice. Its absence is equivalent to silencing them. Locate it, return it, and offer it before any decision-making conversation, care task, or assessment of their wishes.
- If the device is genuinely missing or broken, do not proceed as if the person has no communication. Use alternative methods immediately: yes/no questions, pointing, gestures, writing, drawing, or a temporary low-tech board. Document the device loss as an incident, not a personal trait.
- Never write in a care plan or handoff that a person "is non-verbal" or "cannot communicate" without recording whether their communication aid was present and working at the time of assessment. A person assessed without their device is a person assessed with a hand tied behind their back.

### Handle Devices Safely To Avoid Loss, Damage, And Cross-Contamination

These devices are expensive, fragile, and frequently lost, and some carry infection-control risk.

- Never wrap a hearing aid in a tissue or napkin — it will be thrown away. This is one of the most common and most preventable losses. Use the labeled container.
- Keep hearing aids away from heat, moisture, and hairspray; remove before showering or hair washing. A wet hearing aid is a dead hearing aid.
- Do not share hearing aids, eyeglass frames, or earbuds between people; in-ear devices are personal and can transmit infection. Clean only with the methods specified for that device.
- Handle speech devices and tablets with clean hands, charge with the correct cable, and protect from drops and spills. A cracked screen or dead battery removes the person's voice for days.

### Escalate Loss, Damage, And Functional Decline Promptly

Caregivers maintain, place, clean, and troubleshoot. They do not reprogram devices, fit hearing aids, change prescriptions, or perform audiology or vision assessments.

- A lost hearing aid or pair of glasses is an incident: document, search, notify the family and supervisor, and arrange replacement. Do not let the person go days without correction because "they seem to manage."
- A device that whistles, distorts, or is rejected as "too loud" or "not working" needs audiology review; do not simply turn it off and leave it out.
- New difficulty hearing even with the aid, or new vision changes even with glasses, may indicate wax impaction, infection, or a medical change — escalate for clinical review.
- Record objective observations (device present/not present, working/not working, person's response) rather than conclusions about the person's abilities.

## Common Traps

### Removing The Hearing Aid Because It Whistles, And Leaving It Out

The trap begins because a hearing aid whistles (feeds back) when loose or poorly seated, and the noise is unpleasant for everyone, so the caregiver removes it "so it stops." The mechanism is that the symptom (whistle) is treated instead of the cause (poor fit, wax, wrong insertion), and the easy fix is removal. The false signal is that the person seems calmer and the room is quieter. The harm is that the person is plunged back into auditory isolation, which in a person with dementia often produces confusion, paranoia, withdrawal, or agitation within hours, and the underlying fit or wax problem is never addressed. The defense is to reseat the aid, check for wax, and escalate persistent whistling to audiology rather than removing the device, and to recognize that a silent, unhearing resident is not an improvement on a whistling aid.

### Assuming A Confused Or Unresponsive Person Has Deteriorated, When The Aid Is Simply Missing Or Dead

The trap begins because a person who was communicative yesterday is now withdrawn, and the caregiver charts a cognitive or behavioral decline. The mechanism is that the change is attributed to the person's disease rather than to their environment, because the device is invisible and its absence is not checked. The false signal is that the decline "makes sense" given the diagnosis. The harm is twofold: the person suffers hours or days of unnecessary confusion and isolation, and the chart records a permanent deterioration that was actually a dead battery or a missing hearing aid, distorting the medical record and potentially triggering inappropriate medication or transfer. The defense is to make "check the aids and glasses" the first response to any sudden behavioral or cognitive change, before any chart entry about decline.

### Wrapping A Hearing Aid Or Glasses In A Tissue Or Leaving Them On A Meal Tray

The trap begins because the device needs a temporary resting place during care or a meal, and a tissue or the meal tray seems convenient. The mechanism is that small devices placed on absorbent or disposable surfaces are indistinguishable from trash once bundled, and tray-clearing and linen routines carry them away. The false signal is that the device was "just put down for a minute." The harm is severe and disproportionate: a hearing aid or pair of glasses costs hundreds to thousands, takes weeks to replace, requires re-fitting, and leaves the person without essential sensory input in the interim — and the loss is almost always preventable with a labeled container and a consistent storage spot. The defense is to never place a device on a tray, in a tissue, or on loose linen, and to always return it to the labeled container or the person.

### Treating A Communication Device As Optional Because The Person "Doesn't Use It Much"

The trap begins because the person has not used the speech device consistently, so the caregiver stops offering it and eventually stops retrieving it. The mechanism is that low observed use is read as low need, when in fact low use often reflects the device being dead, out of reach, poorly set up, or the person never having been given the opportunity and support to learn it. The false signal is the silence itself, which is mistaken for absence of intent. The harm is that the person is progressively denied their means of expression, decisions are made over and around them, and the care plan hardens around the false belief that they have nothing to communicate — a self-fulfilling erasure. The defense is to treat the communication device as the person's voice, to offer and position it consistently, to troubleshoot why use is low rather than abandoning the device, and to never document a communication deficit without confirming the aid was present and functional.

### Failing To Charge Or Maintain Devices Because "It Still Works"

The trap begins because a device still functions in the moment, so the nightly charging or battery-replacement routine is skipped as unnecessary. The mechanism is that maintenance is deferred because the failure has not yet happened, and the routine erodes one shortcut at a time. The false signal is that the device worked this morning. The harm is that the battery dies mid-morning, mid-meal, or mid-conversation, the person loses hearing or voice without warning, and the failure is discovered only when behavior changes — by which point the person has already suffered the isolation and the team is chasing a "behavioral" problem instead of a dead battery. The defense is to make charging and battery checks a fixed part of every shift handover, to treat a low battery as an immediate task rather than a deferred one, and to replace batteries on a schedule before they fail.

### Cleaning Lenses With Clothing Or Paper, Or Submerging A Hearing Aid

The trap begins because the caregiver uses whatever is at hand — a shirt hem, a paper towel, or runs the aid under water — to clean a device quickly. The mechanism is that these methods feel effective and are convenient. The false signal is that the lens or aid looks cleaner. The harm is that paper and clothing scratch coated lenses over time, ruining expensive glasses, and that moisture destroys hearing aid electronics instantly, turning a cleaning attempt into a replacement event. The defense is to use only the specified microfiber cloth and cleaning methods for each device, to keep hearing aids dry, and to treat incorrect cleaning as a form of damage rather than care.

### Overlooking The Edge Case Of The Person Who Cannot Report Device Failure

The textbook assumes the person will say "I can't hear" or "my glasses are blurry," but the person with advanced dementia, aphasia, or cognitive impairment cannot self-report. The mechanism is that the caregiver waits for a complaint that will never come. The false signal is the absence of complaint, read as satisfaction. The harm is that the device fails silently and the person deteriorates silently, with the connection between the two never made because no one checked. The defense is to proactively verify device function for every person who cannot reliably self-report, to treat behavioral change as the complaint, and to never rely on self-report alone for those with communication or cognitive barriers.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I establish and follow a daily routine for inserting, removing, charging, and cleaning each hearing aid, and confirm battery or charge level at the start of every shift?
- [ ] Did I clean glasses with the correct cloth, confirm they are the correct labeled pair, and store them in the case in a consistent place?
- [ ] Did I verify that hearing aids are in and switched on, glasses are clean and on, and the communication device is powered and within reach before any meaningful interaction?
- [ ] Before charting confusion, withdrawal, agitation, or cognitive decline, did I check whether a missing, dead, or dirty sensory aid was the actual cause?
- [ ] Did I locate and restore the communication aid, or use an alternative method, rather than assuming the person has nothing to say when the device is missing?
- [ ] Did I avoid placing devices on trays, in tissues, or on linen, and always return them to the labeled container or the person?
- [ ] Did I document objective observations (device present/absent, working/not working, person's response) rather than conclusions about the person's abilities, and escalate loss, damage, whistling, or new sensory decline for repair or clinical review?
- [ ] For a person who cannot self-report, did I proactively check device function and treat behavioral change as a possible device-failure signal?
