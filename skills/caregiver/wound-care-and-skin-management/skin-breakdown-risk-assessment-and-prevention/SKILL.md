# Skin Breakdown Risk Assessment And Prevention

Skin is the body's largest organ and its most visible barrier, and in clients who are immobile, incontinent, poorly nourished, or with impaired circulation, it breaks down quietly and fast — a pressure injury can begin in hours and take months to heal. The judgment problem is that skin breakdown is almost entirely preventable but only if prevention is treated as an active, scheduled, whole-body intervention rather than as something that happens when there is time. Caregivers tend to notice skin only when it is already damaged, to rely on a single pressure-relieving cushion as if it solved everything, to underestimate how fast moisture and friction injure fragile skin, and to miss the earliest sign of a pressure injury — the redness that does not fade. The agent must assess risk proactively, must inspect at-risk skin every shift, must relieve pressure on a schedule, and must understand that preventing skin breakdown is far easier, faster, and cheaper than healing a wound.

## Core Rules

### Assess Skin Breakdown Risk Proactively, Not After The First Wound Appears

Use a structured risk picture (the standard factors are immobility and limited activity, incontinence and moisture, poor nutrition and hydration, impaired circulation, reduced sensation, older age, and cognitive impairment) to identify which clients are at high risk before any breakdown occurs. High-risk clients need more frequent turning, more aggressive skin protection, better nutrition and hydration support, and daily inspection of every pressure point. Do not wait for a pressure injury to justify prevention; the clients who need the most prevention are those who cannot reposition themselves, feel the pressure, or report the discomfort.

### Inspect At-Risk Skin Every Shift, Especially Over Bony Prominences

Pressure injuries form where bone presses skin against a surface, so inspect the sacrum and tailbone, the heels and ankles, the hips, the elbows, the shoulders and shoulder blades, the back of the head, the ears, and any skin under a device or splint. Look for redness that does not blanch (does not turn pale when pressed and released), purplish or bluish discoloration, blistering, shiny or taut skin, warmth, firmness, or breaks. For clients with darker skin, blanching is harder to see, so look for changes in color, temperature, and firmness compared to surrounding skin. Check under medical devices (oxygen tubing, splints, catheters, compression stockings) where hidden injuries form. The earliest sign is the non-blanching redness, and catching it then — before the skin breaks — is the prevention window.

### Turn And Reposition Immobile Clients On A Schedule, Day And Night

For clients who cannot reposition themselves, establish and follow a turning schedule (commonly every two hours in bed, and repositioning in the chair hourly), including through the night. Relieve pressure completely under bony points, use pillows and wedges to offload heels (so they float free of the bed), avoid positioning directly on the hip, and alternate the side of lying. Turning schedules fail most often at night when staffing is thin and the client is asleep, which is exactly when immobile skin is injured. The schedule is not a suggestion; it is the core prevention intervention, and skipping it is the most common cause of pressure injuries.

### Use Pressure-Relieving Surfaces Correctly And As Part Of A Larger Plan

Pressure-relieving mattresses, overlays, and chair cushions reduce but do not eliminate pressure, and they do not replace turning and repositioning. Use the prescribed surface, ensure it is set up and inflated correctly, do not layer extra padding that defeats the surface's function, and check that sheets are smooth and dry. A cushion or mattress alone, without turning, will not prevent injury. Surfaces are one tool in the prevention plan, not the whole plan.

### Manage Moisture Aggressively, Because Wet Skin Breaks Down Fast

Incontinence, sweat, and wound drainage soften and break down skin (maceration), and moisture combined with friction tears fragile skin within hours. For incontinent clients, use a structured toileting schedule, change soiled briefs and pads promptly, cleanse gently with no-rinse cleansers rather than soap and water, pat rather than rub, apply barrier creams to protect intact skin, and check the skin at every change. Do not let a client sit in a wet or soiled brief, even briefly, because the moisture and the ammonia from urine chemically injure the skin. Moisture-associated skin damage is one of the fastest-developing and most preventable forms of breakdown.

### Reduce Friction And Shear During Transfers And Positioning

Friction (skin rubbing against a surface) and shear (skin sliding one way while the bone beneath slides another, stressing the tissue) injure fragile skin, especially during transfers, sliding up in bed, and repositioning. Use draw sheets and slide boards to move clients rather than dragging them across the bed, lift rather than slide when possible, keep the head of the bed below 30 degrees when possible to reduce shear, and protect skin over bony points from friction with dressings or pads as ordered. Dragging a client up in bed by the underarms or sheet is a common way fragile skin is injured.

### Support Nutrition And Hydration, Because Malnourished Skin Cannot Resist Pressure

Skin integrity depends on adequate protein, calories, fluids, and micronutrients, and a malnourished or dehydrated client breaks down faster and heals slower. Support the prescribed diet and fluid plan, encourage protein intake, report poor intake and weight loss, and recognize that the client who is not eating or drinking well is a high skin-breakdown risk even if they are otherwise stable. Nutrition is not separate from skin care; it is the foundation of it.

## Common Traps

### Noticing Skin Only After It Has Broken Down

The caregiver does not check the skin until a wound is visible or the client complains. The mechanism is that intact skin feels like a non-issue. The false signal is that nothing has been reported. The harm is that by the time a wound is visible, the tissue has already been injured for hours to days, and the easy prevention window is gone. Daily inspection of at-risk skin, especially over bony points, is what catches the non-blanching redness before it becomes a wound.

### Relying On A Cushion Or Mattress To Replace Turning

The caregiver assumes that because the client is on a pressure-relieving surface, turning is less important. The mechanism is that the surface feels like the solution. The false signal is that the surface is in place. The harm is that no surface eliminates pressure entirely, and without scheduled repositioning the client still develops pressure injuries, often at the sacrum and heels. The surface supports turning; it does not replace it.

### Skipping The Night Turning Schedule

The client is asleep, the caregiver is tired, and turning is skipped to let everyone rest. The mechanism is that night feels like a pause in care. The false signal is that the client is comfortable and undisturbed. The harm is that eight hours of unrelieved pressure overnight is more than enough to begin a pressure injury, and immobile clients must be turned through the night. The schedule applies 24 hours.

### Letting An Incontinent Client Sit In A Wet Or Soiled Brief

The caregiver delays a change because the client "just went" or because a change is due soon anyway. The mechanism is convenience and timing. The false signal is that the brief is only mildly damp. The harm is that urine and stool moisture and enzymes macerate and chemically burn the skin within a short time, and the resulting incontinence-associated dermatitis can progress to skin breakdown and infection. Prompt changes and barrier protection are the prevention.

### Dragging The Client Across The Sheet To Reposition

The caregiver pulls the client up in bed by the sheet or underarms, dragging the skin against the bed. The mechanism is that it is faster than using a draw sheet or lift. The false signal is that the move is done. The harm is friction and shear injuries to fragile skin, especially over the sacrum and heels, which then break down. Use a draw sheet, slide board, or lift to move clients, and lift rather than drag.

### Treating Non-Blanching Redness As Insignificant

The caregiver notices a red area, presses it, sees it stay red, and assumes it will resolve on its own. The mechanism is that redness feels minor. The false signal is that the skin is intact. The harm is that non-blanching redness is a Stage 1 pressure injury, meaning the tissue is already injured, and without immediate pressure relief and escalation it will progress to an open wound. Any non-blanching redness over a bony prominence is reported and the pressure relieved immediately.

## Self-Check

Before treating skin breakdown risk assessment and prevention as complete, verify:

- Skin breakdown risk was assessed proactively, and high-risk clients (immobile, incontinent, malnourished, reduced sensation, impaired circulation) received intensified prevention.
- At-risk skin over all bony prominences and under devices was inspected every shift, with non-blanching redness recognized as the earliest injury sign (including in darker skin, where color change, warmth, and firmness are the signals).
- Immobile clients were turned and repositioned on a schedule day and night, with heels offloaded and direct hip pressure avoided.
- Pressure-relieving surfaces were used correctly as part of a larger plan, not as a replacement for turning.
- Moisture from incontinence, sweat, and drainage was managed aggressively with prompt changes, gentle cleansing, and barrier creams, and no client sat in a wet or soiled brief.
- Friction and shear were reduced during transfers and positioning using draw sheets, slide boards, and lifting rather than dragging.
- Nutrition and hydration were supported, recognizing malnourished and dehydrated clients as high skin-breakdown risk.
- Did the response stay within scope, deferring wound staging, treatment, and clinical decisions to the wound care team and prescriber?
