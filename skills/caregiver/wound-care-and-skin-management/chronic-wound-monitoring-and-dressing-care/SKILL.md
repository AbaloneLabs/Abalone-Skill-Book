# Chronic Wound Monitoring And Dressing Care

Chronic wounds — diabetic foot ulcers, venous and arterial leg ulcers, pressure injuries, and slow-healing surgical wounds — are a daily test of disciplined observation, because they heal slowly, change subtly, and can deteriorate into infection, sepsis, or amputation without dramatic warning. The judgment problem is that the wound is hidden under a dressing for most of the day, that early infection and early healing can look similar, and that caregivers tend to focus on the mechanical act of changing the dressing rather than on assessing what the wound is telling them. The recurring failures are peeking only at dressing-change time, assuming more drainage is always bad or always normal, missing the early signs of infection because they are subtle, contaminating the wound with unwashed hands or non-sterile supplies, and failing to recognize that a wound that stops improving is itself a warning. The agent must treat every dressing change as a structured assessment, must compare against the documented baseline, and must escalate the specific changes that signal deterioration.

## Core Rules

### Follow The Written Wound Care Order Exactly, Including Product, Technique, And Frequency

Obtain and follow the prescriber's or wound nurse's written order: the specific cleanser and dressing product, the technique (clean versus sterile, irrigation method), the frequency of changes, whether to use any topical treatments, and the position or offloading required. Wound care products are not interchangeable — a dressing chosen for a wet wound will harm a dry one, and the wrong cleanser can damage new tissue — so do not substitute products based on what is available. If the order is unclear, the product is unavailable, or the wound looks different from what is described, contact the wound care team before improvising, because improvised wound care is a common cause of delayed healing and infection.

### Perform A Structured Assessment At Every Dressing Change And Compare To Baseline

At every change, assess and document the wound systematically: size (length, width, depth, and any tunneling or undermining), the wound bed (the percentage and type of tissue — granulation, slough, eschar, epithelial), the amount, color, consistency, and odor of drainage (exudate), the condition of the surrounding skin (maceration, redness, warmth, induration), and the client's pain. The single most important habit is to compare today's assessment to the documented baseline and to the last few changes, because a wound is judged on trend, not on a single look. A wound that is shrinking, filling with red granulation tissue, and producing less drainage is healing; a wound that has stalled, is growing, is producing more or different drainage, or is showing new tissue breakdown is deteriorating, and the trend is what reveals it.

### Recognize Infection Through Specific Signs, Including In Older And Diabetic Clients

Wound infection signs include increased or new pain, increasing redness around the wound, warmth, swelling, induration (firmness), increased drainage, a change in drainage color to yellow, green, or thick, new or worsening odor, the wound bed looking more sloughy or breaking down, fever, and in deeper infection a spreading redness. Critically, in older adults, diabetics, and immunocompromised clients, infection often presents without fever and with blunted signs, so do not use the absence of fever to rule out infection. Increased pain alone, or a wound that suddenly stops healing, can be the only sign of infection in these clients. Any of these signs requires prompt provider contact, because wound infection can progress to cellulitis, osteomyelitis, and sepsis.

### Use Clean Or Sterile Technique Consistently And Protect The Wound From Contamination

Wash hands thoroughly before and after every dressing change, use the technique (clean or sterile) specified in the order, use only the supplies and solutions intended for wound care, never touch the wound or the inside of the dressing with unwashed or ungloved hands, and never reuse single-use supplies. Do not let the wound touch bedclothes, floors, or non-sterile surfaces. The wound is an open portal to bacteria, and contamination is a direct cause of infection; disciplined technique is the barrier. If sterile supplies are specified and only clean are available, do not proceed — obtain the right supplies or contact the team.

### Manage Drainage And Protect The Surrounding Skin From Maceration

Drainage management is part of healing. The dressing should absorb the amount of drainage present without letting it pool, strike through to the clothes, or sit against the surrounding skin; if drainage is soaking the dressing between changes, a more absorbent product or more frequent changes is needed and the team should be notified. Protect the surrounding skin from maceration (the white, soft, broken-down skin caused by constant moisture), because macerated skin breaks down and enlarges the wound. Use skin barriers or barrier films as ordered, and ensure the dressing is the right size and is secured without tension.

### Recognize The Difference Between Healthy Granulation, Slough, Eschar, And Necrosis

Learn to read the wound bed. Healthy granulation tissue is red, beefy, bumpy, and moist, and it means the wound is filling in and healing. Slough is soft, yellow, stringy, or filmy tissue that may need debridement. Eschar is hard, dry, black or brown dead tissue; on a diabetic foot or arterial wound it may be intentionally left dry and stable (dry gangrene), but on most wounds it indicates necrosis needing debridement. Do not attempt to debride or remove slough or eschar yourself unless specifically trained and ordered; report what you see and let the wound team decide. Misreading the wound bed leads to both false reassurance and harmful intervention.

### Offload Pressure And Address The Underlying Cause, Not Just The Dressing

A pressure injury will not heal while pressure continues, a diabetic foot ulcer will not heal while the client walks on it, and a venous ulcer will not heal without compression and elevation. Follow the prescribed offloading (turning and repositioning schedule, pressure-relieving mattress, offloading boot or cast, heel protectors), compression, or elevation, because dressing changes alone cannot overcome ongoing pressure or poor circulation. If the offloading or compression is not being used correctly or consistently, the wound will not heal and the team needs to know.

## Common Traps

### Looking At The Wound Only At Dressing-Change Time

The caregiver inspects the wound only when the dressing is due and assumes all is well in between. The mechanism is that the dressing change is the defined task. The false signal is that the dressing looks clean and dry from the outside. The harm is that a wound can soak through, separate, or become infected between changes, and a soiled or compromised dressing that waits until the scheduled change allows deterioration. Check the dressing at every visit for strike-through, loosening, soiling, odor, and the client's pain, and inspect immediately if any of these appear, regardless of schedule.

### Confusing Increased Drainage With Infection Or With Normal Healing

The caregiver sees more drainage and either panics that the wound is infected or dismisses it as normal. The mechanism is that drainage feels like a single, obvious signal. The false signal is that any change in drainage is alarming, or conversely that drainage is always expected. The harm is that drainage must be read in context: an increase can be a normal part of debridement and healing, or it can be the first sign of infection, and the distinguishing features are color, odor, consistency, and accompanying signs (pain, redness, fever), not the amount alone. Read drainage as part of the whole assessment.

### Assuming A Normal Temperature Rules Out Wound Infection

The caregiver checks for fever, finds none, and concludes the wound is not infected. The mechanism is that fever is taught as the cardinal sign of infection. The false signal is a normal thermometer reading. The harm is that older adults, diabetics, and immunocompromised clients often mount a blunted or absent fever response to serious wound infection, and relying on temperature misses cellulitis and osteomyelitis until they are advanced. Increased pain, spreading redness, new drainage, or a stalled wound are infection signs even without fever.

### Substituting Wound Products Based On What Is Available

The caregiver runs out of the ordered dressing and substitutes a similar-looking product from the cabinet. The mechanism is that the products look interchangeable and the change is due now. The false signal is that the substitute covers the wound. The harm is that wound products are chosen for specific properties (absorbency, moisture level, adherence, antimicrobial action), and the wrong product can dry out, over-moisturize, adhere to and damage the wound bed, or fail to manage drainage, setting back healing. Use only the ordered product, and if it is unavailable, contact the team for guidance.

### Contaminating The Wound Through Poor Technique

The caregiver changes the dressing in a hurry, with unwashed hands, reusing a supply, or setting the old dressing on the bed. The mechanism is time pressure and the assumption that a quick change is fine. The false signal is that the dressing looks on. The harm is that bacteria introduced during a careless change seed wound infection, which can progress to sepsis, especially in vulnerable clients. Hand hygiene, the specified technique, single-use supplies, and a clean field are not optional refinements; they are the infection barrier.

### Treating A Stalled Wound As Stable Rather Than As A Warning

The wound has not changed in two weeks, and the caregiver concludes it is stable and fine. The mechanism is that no change feels safe. The false signal is that the wound is not getting worse. The harm is that a chronic wound that has stopped shrinking and filling is not stable — it is stalled, and a stalled wound often signals unresolved infection, inadequate offloading, poor circulation, or malnutrition, and will not heal without a change in plan. A wound not actively healing is a wound to report.

## Self-Check

Before treating chronic wound monitoring and dressing care as complete, verify:

- The written wound care order (product, technique, frequency, offloading) was followed exactly, with no product substitution or improvisation.
- A structured assessment (size, wound bed tissue, drainage, surrounding skin, pain) was performed at every change and compared to the documented baseline and trend.
- Infection was assessed through specific signs (pain, redness, warmth, swelling, drainage change, odor, stalled healing) without relying on fever alone, especially in older, diabetic, or immunocompromised clients.
- Clean or sterile technique was used consistently, with hand hygiene, single-use supplies, and protection of the wound from contamination.
- Drainage was managed with the right absorbency and frequency, and the surrounding skin was protected from maceration.
- The wound bed was read correctly (granulation, slough, eschar), and no debridement or tissue removal was attempted without training and order.
- Offloading, compression, or elevation was used as prescribed, recognizing that the dressing alone cannot heal a wound under ongoing pressure or poor circulation.
- Did the response stay within scope, deferring debridement, product changes, and clinical decisions to the wound care team and prescriber?
