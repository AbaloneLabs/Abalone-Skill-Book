# Mechanical Lift and Hoist Operation

The judgment problem in mechanical lift operation is that a hoist feels safer than a manual transfer and therefore invites less vigilance, when in fact a hoist introduces its own failure modes that are less familiar and often more severe. A caregiver who would never attempt a dependent manual lift will nonetheless use a sling with a frayed strap, leave a battery half-charged, choose the wrong sling style for the person's tone and posture, or swing a loaded boom near obstacles — because the machine is doing the lifting and the danger is hidden in the equipment rather than the caregiver's muscles. The result is that the very tool meant to prevent injury can drop a person, trap or pinch skin, tip the lift, or fail mid-transfer when the battery dies or a strap tears. The skilled caregiver treats the hoist as equipment that must be inspected, selected, and operated with discipline on every single use, not as a convenience that removes the need for judgment.

This is a high-stakes domain. A fall from a hoist is a fall from a height with a sling and straps attached, which can entangle, choke, or trap; a wrong sling can compromise breathing or circulation; a tipping lift can crush a caregiver's foot or drop the person onto a hard surface. The caregiver's role is to inspect the equipment before every use, select and fit the correct sling, position and operate the lift within its limits, protect the person's skin and dignity throughout, and stop if anything is wrong — not to use unfamiliar equipment, to override a low-battery or weight-limit warning, or to improvise a sling or attachment.

## Core Rules

### Inspect The Lift And Sling Before Every Single Transfer

Equipment failure is preventable only if the inspection happens every time, not on a schedule. A sling that was intact last week can fail today; a battery that was charged this morning can be depleted by midday.

- Inspect the sling before each use: every strap, loop, seam, and panel. Look for fraying, thinning fabric, torn stitching, holes, stretched or distorted loops, discoloration that may indicate fiber weakening, and chemical or heat damage. Run the straps through your fingers, not just your eyes, because fraying is felt before it is seen. Remove any sling with damage from service immediately and tag it; never set a damaged sling aside "to deal with later" where someone else may use it.
- Inspect the lift: the base spreads and locks correctly, casters roll freely and brakes hold, the mast and boom are undamaged, the actuator (the lifting mechanism) moves smoothly without jerking or grinding, the handset or controls respond, and the emergency stop and emergency-lower functions work.
- Check the battery and charger: the battery is adequately charged for the transfer, the charge indicator is read honestly, and a spare is available for lifts in constant use. A lift that dies mid-transfer leaves a person suspended or stranded.
- Verify the safe working load (SWL) of both the lift and the sling is adequate for the person, and that the sling's SWL is not lower than the lift's. The combination is only as strong as the lower rating.
- Confirm you have been trained and are competent on this specific make and model; controls, loop configurations, and emergency functions differ between lifts.

If any inspection item fails, do not use the equipment. Fetch a replacement, charge the battery, or obtain a different sling. There is no transfer urgent enough to justify using equipment that has not passed inspection.

### Select The Correct Sling For The Person And The Transfer

The sling is the part that touches the person, and the wrong sling is a common cause of failed or unsafe hoist transfers. Sling choice depends on the person's abilities, body shape, tone, skin integrity, and the destination.

- Universal / divided-leg (U-sling) slings: the most common general-purpose sling; appropriate for many dependent transfers, with leg loops that pass under the thighs. Ensure the leg loops are correctly routed (under, not over, the thighs) and not twisted, and that they do not press into the popliteal space or groin.
- Full-body slings with head support: required for a person who cannot hold their head up, who has poor trunk control, or who may become agitated during the lift; the head support prevents the head falling back.
- Hygiene / toileting slings (with a commode aperture): used to access clothing and toileting; only appropriate for a person with adequate sitting balance and trunk control, because these slings provide less support.
- Standing / sit-to-stand slings: used only with sit-to-stand lifts and only for a person who can bear partial weight, follow instructions, and cooperate; they are not a substitute for a full hoist.
- Size matters as much as type: a sling too small concentrates pressure and may not support the thighs; a sling too large lets the person slump or slip. Use the size the assessment specifies for this person, and never substitute a different size because the correct one is in the wash.

Match the sling to the person's tone and contractures: a person with high tone or hip contractures may need a divided-leg sling with the legs positioned carefully to avoid forcing the joints, and may require a specialist sling assessed by a therapist.

### Position The Lift And Attach The Sling Correctly

Most hoist incidents occur during positioning and attachment, when the person is being prepared but not yet supported by the lift.

- Position the lift so the boom is over the person's center of mass, not off to one side; an off-center load swings when lifted and can strike obstacles or tip the base. For a floor lift, the base is typically placed under or alongside the bed/chair with the legs spread and locked.
- Apply the brakes on the lift base only if the manufacturer specifies braking during lifting; some lifts are designed to move freely during the transfer so they can settle under the load, and braking them can cause tipping. Follow the manufacturer's instruction for the specific model.
- Roll or slide the sling under the person using correct technique and a second caregiver for a dependent person; never drag the person to get the sling under them. Cross the leg loops correctly and route them under the thighs without twists.
- Attach the loops to the correct points on the boom, matching loop color or length to achieve the sitting position intended (more reclined for poor head/trunk control, more upright for transfers to a chair). Misattached loops produce an unbalanced or suffocating posture.
- Check that nothing is trapped — fingers, clothing, tubing, catheters, oxygen lines — before lifting, and that the sling is not pinching skin or compressing the neck or abdomen.

### Operate The Lift Smoothly And Within Its Limits

Once the person is suspended, the lift, not the caregiver, bears the weight — but the caregiver controls the movement.

- Lift just enough to clear the surface, then pause and check: is the person sitting correctly in the sling, is the head supported, is breathing unobstructed, is the load balanced and not swinging? Adjust before moving if anything is wrong.
- Move the lift and person together as a unit, slowly, with a second caregiver guiding the person's legs and watching for obstacles. Never push the person by pulling on the boom or sling straps; guide the lift by its frame.
- Keep the boom low during transit to keep the center of gravity down and reduce swing; a person moved at full boom height is unstable and the lift is more likely to tip.
- Never leave a person suspended in a hoist unattended, even briefly. If you must step away, lower them to a safe surface first.
- Lower slowly onto the destination, positioning carefully, and confirm the person is fully supported by the surface before loosening the sling and detaching the loops. A person lowered onto the edge of a chair can slide off.

### Decide When A Hoist Is Required And When Two Caregivers Are Needed

The hoist exists for transfers that cannot be done manually without injury. Under-using it is the most common error, but knowing the staffing requirement is equally important.

- A full hoist is required for any person who cannot bear weight, cannot cooperate, or whose size or unpredictability exceeds what one caregiver can guide safely — regardless of how the care plan was written last month.
- Two caregivers are generally required for a full hoist transfer: one to operate the lift and one to guide the person's body and legs and watch for skin, tubing, and obstacles. Solo hoist use is appropriate only in specific, trained circumstances for a cooperative person; default to two when in doubt.
- A sit-to-stand lift is appropriate only when the person can bear partial weight, can follow instructions, and can cooperate; it is not a substitute for a full hoist for a dependent person.
- If no working hoist is available for a transfer that needs one, do not perform a manual substitute; summon a second caregiver, wait for equipment, or use a different transfer. A dependent person is not lifted manually because the hoist is inconvenient.

## Common Traps

### Using A Sling Without Inspecting It Because It "Looked Fine" Last Time

A caregiver who used a sling earlier in the shift may assume it is still intact and skip the inspection, but sling fabric and stitching fail progressively under repeated loading, and a strap that was sound this morning can begin to tear after the next wash cycle or the next heavy load. The mechanism is that the sling's damage is often invisible to a quick glance — fraying inside a seam, a stretched loop, fiber weakening from bleach or heat — so the absence of obvious damage is read as the absence of any damage, and the sling is loaded with a person's full weight on the strength of a look. The false signal is the sling's familiar, clean appearance, which feels like evidence of integrity but is not. The harm is a strap tearing mid-lift, the person dropping or tipping within the sling, skin sheared or pinched by the failed panel, and the lift tipping as the load shifts suddenly — a fall from height with entanglement. The defense is to inspect every strap, seam, loop, and panel by hand before every transfer, to feel for fraying as well as look for it, and to remove any questionable sling from service immediately, treating the inspection as non-negotiable regardless of how recently the sling was last used.

### Choosing A Sling By Availability Rather Than By The Person's Needs

When the correct sling is in the laundry or in use elsewhere, the temptation is to use whatever sling is on the hoist, rationalizing that "a sling is a sling." The mechanism is that sling type and size profoundly affect the safety and comfort of the transfer — a hygiene sling gives inadequate support to a person with poor trunk control, a too-small sling concentrates pressure and lets legs slip, a too-large sling lets the person slump — so substituting a wrong sling changes the transfer from safe to hazardous even though the lift itself is functioning. The false signal is that the wrong sling "works" in the sense that the person is lifted, which conceals the inadequate support, the pressure concentration, or the unstable posture being produced. The harm ranges from skin breakdown and breathing restriction from a poorly fitted sling, to the person slipping or tipping within the sling during transit, to a transfer that fails because the person cannot be positioned correctly at the destination. The defense is to use only the sling type and size specified in the person's transfer assessment, to treat the correct sling as a precondition of the transfer rather than a convenience, and to delay the transfer until the right sling is available rather than substitute.

### Ignoring The Battery Charge Or Weight-Limit Indicator

A lift with a low-battery warning or a load near its safe working limit will often still operate, which invites the caregiver to proceed because the lift "seems fine." The mechanism is that a depleted battery can fail partway through a lift — leaving the person suspended with no power to lower them safely, or causing a jerky, uncontrolled descent — and a load at the limit stresses the actuator, sling, and attachment points at their maximum, where any weakness becomes catastrophic. The false signal is that the lift moves normally at the start of the transfer, which is mistaken for the lift being safe for the whole transfer, when the failure point is reached only after the load is fully applied or the battery is further drained. The harm is a mid-transfer power loss that strands or drops a suspended person, or an overload failure that drops the load as a strap, loop, or actuator gives way. The defense is to read the charge indicator honestly before starting and to charge or swap the battery whenever it is not confidently adequate, to verify the person's weight is within the lift's and sling's safe working load before lifting, and to never override or disregard a low-battery or overload warning, treating any such indicator as a stop condition.

### Braking A Lift That Is Designed To Move, Or Not Braking One That Is Designed To Lock

Different lifts have different requirements for the base brakes during lifting: some are designed to move and settle under the load so that braking them causes tipping, while others require the brakes locked before lifting. The mechanism is that the caregiver, trained on one model, applies the same brake habit to a different lift, and the wrong choice destabilizes the base at exactly the moment the load is applied. The false signal is that the brakes "feel" set or the lift "feels" stable, which gives confidence that is specific to the wrong model. The harm is a tipped lift with a person suspended, which can drop the person onto the frame or floor and trap or injure the caregiver. The defense is to know and follow the manufacturer's instruction for the specific model in use, to confirm the base configuration (spread and locked, brakes set or deliberately free) before lifting, and to never assume a new lift behaves like a familiar one — read the operating manual or the label on the lift before first use of an unfamiliar model.

### Swinging The Loaded Boom Near Obstacles Or Operating Without A Spotter

Once the person is suspended, the caregiver focuses on the controls and the destination, and the loaded boom swings through an arc that can strike bed rails, furniture, door frames, or IV poles, or the person's legs can catch on the wheelchair being vacated. The mechanism is that the lift's range of motion is larger than the caregiver's attention when operating solo, so obstacles in the swing path are not noticed until impact, and the impact can dislodge the person, tip the lift, or injure the caregiver. The false signal is the smooth start of the movement, which feels controlled but does not account for the full arc. The harm is a person struck against furniture, a sling loop snagged and torn on a bed rail, or a tipped lift — any of which can drop the person mid-transit. The defense is to use two caregivers for a full hoist transfer so one operates the lift and one spots the person's body, legs, and the swing path, to clear the arc of obstacles before lifting, and to move slowly enough to stop if anything enters the path.

### Leaving A Person Suspended Or Unattended During The Transfer

A caregiver who needs to fetch a forgotten item, answer a call, or adjust the destination may step away from a person suspended in the sling "just for a second," assuming the lift holds them safely. The mechanism is that a suspended person is in an unstable, unsupported posture dependent entirely on the equipment and the caregiver's presence; if they become agitated, the sling shifts, the battery fails, or the lift is bumped, there is no one to intervene, and the person cannot self-rescue. The false signal is the apparent stillness and stability of the suspended person, which feels like safety but is a state of total dependence. The harm is an unattended person who slips in the sling, is struck by the swinging boom, or whose breathing is compromised by a shifted sling, with no one present to lower them or respond. The defense is to never leave a person suspended in a hoist unattended for any reason, to prepare everything needed before lifting so stepping away is unnecessary, and if you must leave, to lower the person fully onto a safe surface and detach only after they are supported.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I inspect the lift and the sling — every strap, seam, loop, panel, casters, brakes, actuator, handset, emergency stop and lower, battery charge, and safe working load — before this transfer, by hand as well as by eye?
- [ ] Did I select the correct sling type and size for this person's abilities, body shape, tone, skin, and destination, as specified in the transfer assessment, rather than the sling that was available?
- [ ] Did I position the lift over the person's center of mass, route and attach the sling loops correctly without twists, and check that nothing was trapped or pinched before lifting?
- [ ] Did I lift just enough to check the person's posture, breathing, head support, and balance before moving, and adjust before transit?
- [ ] Did I operate the lift smoothly, keep the boom low, move with a second caregiver spotting, and avoid swinging the loaded boom near obstacles?
- [ ] Did I use two caregivers for a full hoist transfer, and reserve the sit-to-stand lift for a person who can bear partial weight and cooperate?
- [ ] Did I follow the manufacturer's brake and base-spread instruction for this specific model, rather than applying a habit from a different lift?
- [ ] Did I keep the person attended throughout, lower them fully onto the destination before detaching, and remove any damaged sling from service?
