# Post-Surgical Complication Recognition And Red-Flag Response

The days and weeks after surgery are a period of heightened risk, when the body is healing from a major controlled injury and complications can declare themselves suddenly or insidiously. The judgment problem is that surgical recovery has a wide spectrum of expected symptoms (pain, swelling, fatigue, appetite loss, bruising) that overlap with the early signs of serious complications (infection, bleeding, blood clots, wound breakdown, bowel obstruction), and a caregiver who treats every concern as routine may miss a developing emergency, while one who alarms at every symptom may exhaust the person and the system. The agent must understand the expected recovery trajectory for this surgery, the specific red flags that demand immediate attention, and the decision points for when to call, whom to call, and how fast. The skill is in pattern recognition: knowing what the normal post-surgical course looks like so that deviations become visible, and having a clear escalation pathway so that a complication is caught early, when it is still treatable, rather than after it has become a crisis.

## Core Rules

### Know The Expected Recovery Trajectory For This Surgery

Every surgery has an expected recovery course, and knowing it is the baseline against which complications are judged. Obtain from the surgeon and the discharge instructions: what pain level and pattern to expect, and for how long (pain should gradually improve, not worsen, over the days after surgery); what wound appearance is expected (some redness, bruising, swelling, and drainage are normal early, but should improve); what appetite, energy, sleep, and bowel function to expect (often reduced for several days); what temperature is acceptable (a low-grade fever in the first day or two is common; a rising or persistent fever is not); and what activity level is expected (gradually increasing, not sudden). Without this baseline, you cannot distinguish expected recovery from a complication. Ask the surgeon explicitly: "What should be getting better each day, and what would worry you?"

### Monitor The Surgical Site And Recognize Infection And Dehiscence

The surgical site is the most direct window into recovery, and it must be inspected (with the dressing changed as instructed) on the schedule the surgeon specifies. Know the signs of surgical site infection: increasing redness that spreads beyond the wound edges, increasing or new pain, swelling, warmth, purulent (cloudy, yellow, green, or foul-smelling) drainage, and opening of the wound edges (dehiscence). Know the signs of deeper infection: fever (especially rising or persistent above the expected low-grade early fever), chills, increasing pain, and systemic illness. A wound that opens, drains pus, or shows spreading redness with fever is a surgical emergency — photograph it, note the time, and call the surgeon immediately or seek emergency care. A small amount of clear or serosanguineous (pink) drainage early is normal; copious, purulent, or increasing drainage is not.

### Recognize Bleeding, Hematoma, And Fluid Collections

Post-surgical bleeding can be external (visible at the wound or drain) or internal (forming a hematoma or causing shock). External bleeding that soaks a dressing quickly, is bright red and pumping, or does not stop with firm pressure is an emergency — apply pressure, keep the person still, and call for help. Internal bleeding may show as swelling or a firm, painful mass near the site (a hematoma), increasing abdominal pain and distension after abdominal surgery, a rapid heart rate, pale skin, dizziness, fainting, or a drop in blood pressure. A surgical drain that suddenly puts out much more blood, or blood that is bright red and clotting, needs urgent attention. Report any signs of bleeding promptly; internal bleeding can become life-threatening quickly.

### Watch For Venous Thromboembolism: DVT And Pulmonary Embolism

Surgery, immobility, and certain procedures (especially orthopedic, abdominal, and cancer surgery) create a high risk for blood clots. Recognize deep vein thrombosis: swelling (often one-sided) of a leg (or arm), pain or tenderness in the calf or along the vein, warmth, and redness — measure and compare, and report new or worsening one-sided swelling. Recognize pulmonary embolism: sudden shortness of breath, chest pain (often sharp, worse with deep breath), a fast heart rate, coughing up blood, dizziness, or fainting. A pulmonary embolism is a life-threatening emergency — call emergency services immediately. Do not wait to see if it improves, do not attribute new shortness of breath to anxiety or deconditioning, and do not massage a swollen leg (which can dislodge a clot). Ensure prescribed clot prevention (blood thinners, compression, leg exercises, early mobility) is used exactly as ordered.

### Monitor Bowel And Urinary Function After Surgery

Surgery, anesthesia, opioids, and immobility slow the gut and bladder, and dysfunction can become serious. Constipation is nearly universal with opioids; prevent it aggressively with hydration, fiber, stool softeners, and laxatives as prescribed, and report no bowel movement for several days, severe abdominal pain, nausea and vomiting, or abdominal distension, which can signal an ileus (paralyzed bowel) or obstruction. Inability to pass gas, vomiting, and a swollen abdomen after abdominal surgery is an obstruction and needs urgent evaluation. Urinary retention (inability to urinate) may occur, especially after certain surgeries or with opioids; report no urination for the expected period, lower abdominal discomfort, or a dribbling stream. A person who has not urinated since surgery or who is uncomfortable and unable to void needs prompt evaluation.

### Distinguish Expected Fatigue And Deconditioning From Emerging Problems

Fatigue, weakness, and reduced appetite are expected after surgery, but they should gradually improve. Watch for fatigue that is profound, worsening, or accompanied by other symptoms (fever, pallor, dizziness, shortness of breath), which may signal anemia (from bleeding), infection, dehydration, or another complication. Do not assume that exhaustion is just normal recovery; if it is out of proportion to the surgery or not improving, report it. Encourage hydration, nutrition as tolerated, gradual activity, and deep breathing, and report a downward trajectory.

### Have A Clear Escalation Pathway: Surgeon, Primary Care, Or Emergency

Know in advance whom to call for what. Surgical site problems, drain issues, wound concerns, and questions about the surgical recovery belong to the surgeon or surgical team. General medical problems (a fall, a fever that may be from a cold, medication side effects) may belong to the primary care. Emergencies (severe bleeding, signs of a pulmonary embolism, a wound that opens with fever, severe abdominal pain with vomiting, sudden collapse) belong to emergency services. Have the surgeon's after-hours number, the primary care's number, and the emergency number posted. When calling, have ready: the surgery date and type, the current symptoms and when they started, the vital signs if available (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, breathing rate), the medications and last doses, and a description of the wound or drain output. When in doubt, call — surgeons and hospitals would rather hear about a concern early than manage a crisis late.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Worsening Symptom As Normal Recovery

The caregiver assumes that increasing pain, redness, or fever is part of healing. The mechanism is the assumption that recovery is uncomfortable. The false signal is that some discomfort is expected. The harm is that the early signs of infection, bleeding, or other complications are dismissed, and the complication progresses. Recovery should trend toward improvement; worsening is a red flag.

### Assuming Leg Swelling Is Expected Post-Surgical Fluid

The caregiver attributes one-sided calf swelling to fluid retention or gravity. The mechanism is the assumption that swelling is normal after surgery. The false signal is that some swelling is common. The harm is that a deep vein thrombosis is missed and may progress to a pulmonary embolism. New or one-sided swelling, especially with calf pain, needs evaluation.

### Waiting To See If Shortness Of Breath Improves

The caregiver attributes new breathlessness to deconditioning or anxiety and delays calling. The mechanism is the hope that the symptom is minor. The false signal is that breathlessness can have many causes. The harm is that a pulmonary embolism, heart problem, or fluid overload progresses and becomes fatal. New shortness of breath after surgery is a pulmonary embolism until proven otherwise; call immediately.

### Missing An Ileus Or Obstruction As Constipation

The caregiver treats post-surgical abdominal symptoms as ordinary constipation. The mechanism is the assumption that opioids cause constipation. The false signal is that constipation is expected. The harm is that an ileus or bowel obstruction, which needs specific treatment, is missed and may lead to bowel ischemia. No gas, vomiting, and distension are not ordinary constipation; report them.

### Calling The Wrong Team Or Waiting Because Of Uncertainty

The caregiver is unsure whether to call the surgeon or primary care and so calls no one. The mechanism is fear of bothering the wrong person. The false signal is that uncertainty means waiting. The harm is that a complication progresses while the caregiver waits. When in doubt, call the surgical team first (they know the recovery) and let them direct you.

## Self-Check

Before treating post-surgical complication recognition and red-flag response as complete, verify:

- The expected recovery trajectory for this surgery (pain pattern, wound appearance, appetite, energy, temperature, activity) was understood as the baseline for judging complications.
- The surgical site was inspected and dressed as instructed, with signs of infection (spreading redness, pus, fever, opening) and dehiscence recognized and reported immediately.
- Bleeding, hematoma, and fluid collections (external and internal signs, drain output) were monitored and reported.
- Venous thromboembolism signs (one-sided leg swelling and pain for DVT; sudden shortness of breath, chest pain, coughing blood for pulmonary embolism) were recognized, and pulmonary embolism was treated as an emergency.
- Bowel and urinary function were monitored, with ileus, obstruction, and retention distinguished from ordinary constipation and reported.
- Fatigue and deconditioning were distinguished from anemia, infection, and dehydration, with a worsening trajectory reported.
- A clear escalation pathway (surgeon for surgical concerns, primary care for general, emergency for red flags) was established and used, with relevant information ready when calling.
- Did the work stay within scope, recognizing and escalating complications while deferring diagnosis and treatment to the surgical and medical team?
