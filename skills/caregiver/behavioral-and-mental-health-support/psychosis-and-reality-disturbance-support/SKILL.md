# Psychosis And Reality Disturbance Support

Psychosis — hallucinations, delusions, paranoia, and a break with shared reality — is disorienting for the caregiver as well as the client, because the normal rules of conversation and trust no longer apply. The judgment problem is that the obvious responses (arguing the facts, going along with the delusion, ignoring the distress) all fail in different ways: arguing destroys trust, validating entrenches the false belief, and ignoring leaves the person alone with frightening experiences. Caregivers tend to argue ("that's not real"), to over-accommodate ("yes, I hear them too"), or to withdraw, and each response worsens the situation. The agent must learn the middle path of acknowledging the person's experience and feelings without confirming the false content, must recognize that psychosis has causes (schizophrenia, dementia, delirium, mood disorder, substances, medication effects) that change the response, and must watch for the behavioral changes that signal decompensation or danger.

## Core Rules

### Acknowledge The Experience And The Feelings Without Confirming The False Content

The core skill is responding to the person's distress without endorsing the false belief. Acknowledge the experience and the feeling ("It sounds terrifying to feel like someone is watching you," "I can see this is really upsetting you"), while not confirming the content ("I don't see anyone here, but I believe that you do, and I want to help you feel safe"). Do not argue ("That's not real, you're imagining it"), because the person cannot be talked out of a delusion and argument reads as threat and destroys trust. Do not play along or validate the false content ("Yes, I see them too"), because this confirms the reality of the delusion and entrenches it. The middle path — feelings acknowledged, content not confirmed, safety offered — preserves trust and reduces distress without reinforcing the psychosis.

### Recognize The Cause Of The Psychosis, Because It Changes The Response

Psychosis has many causes, and the cause shapes the care. Chronic schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder produces long-standing hallucinations and delusions managed with antipsychotic medication and support. Dementia produces paranoia, hallucinations, and false beliefs (often accusations of theft, or believing caregivers are impostors) that fluctuate and reflect the brain's decline. Delirium from infection, medication, dehydration, or metabolic disturbance produces acute, fluctuating confusion and hallucinations and is a medical emergency. Severe mood disorder (depression or bipolar) can produce psychotic features. Substance use and withdrawal can cause psychosis. Medication adverse effects (especially dopaminergic drugs in Parkinson's, steroids, and stimulants) can cause psychosis. Knowing the likely cause determines whether the response is supportive care, urgent medical assessment for delirium, medication review, or crisis response.

### Focus On The Feelings And The Safety, Not The Content Of The Belief

The content of delusions and hallucinations (who is watching, what is being stolen, who is plotting) is less important than the feelings it produces and the behaviors it drives. A persecutory delusion produces fear, which drives hiding, refusal of care, aggression, or escape attempts; the caregiver's job is to reduce the fear and ensure safety, not to investigate the plot. Ask about the feelings ("What is the scariest part?"), offer reassurance and a safe environment, reduce stimulation, and stay calm and present. When the person feels safe and supported, the distress and the driven behaviors often settle, even when the belief persists.

### Do Not Take Accusations, Paranoia, Or Rejection Personally

Psychosis often produces accusations (of theft, infidelity, poisoning, impostors), paranoia directed at the caregiver, and rejection of care. These are symptoms of the illness, not statements about the caregiver, and taking them personally leads to defensiveness, hurt, and withdrawal that damage the relationship and the care. Respond calmly, do not argue or defend at length, do not take the accusation as a reflection of your integrity, and if you have become the focus of the paranoia, step back and let another caregiver the person trusts take over. The disease is speaking, not the person.

### Maintain A Calm, Low-Stimulation Environment And A Predictable Routine

Psychosis is worsened by stimulation, noise, crowding, unpredictability, and stress. Maintain a calm, quiet, uncluttered environment; reduce noise and the number of people; keep the lighting adequate but not harsh; approach slowly and from the front; use a calm, low voice; and keep the daily routine predictable. Predictability and calm reduce the brain's stress response and often reduce the intensity of psychotic symptoms. Chaos and stimulation feed psychosis.

### Watch For Decompensation, Command Hallucinations, And Danger Signals

Some psychotic features signal danger and need prompt escalation. Command hallucinations (voices telling the person to harm self or others) must be taken seriously as suicide and violence risk. New or worsening paranoia, especially with access to weapons or a history of violence, raises safety concerns. Sudden onset or rapid worsening of psychosis may signal delirium (a medical emergency) or a medication problem. Paranoia that leads to refusal of food, fluids, or medication threatens basic health. Recognize these signals and escalate to the prescriber, crisis team, or emergency services as appropriate, because supportive care alone is not enough when danger or medical cause is present.

### Recognize Delirium-Driven Psychosis As A Medical Emergency

When psychosis arises acutely in someone without a psychotic illness, or fluctuates significantly, or accompanies confusion, drowsiness, or physical illness, it is often delirium — and delirium is caused by an underlying medical problem (infection, especially urinary; dehydration; medication toxicity; metabolic disturbance; stroke; hypoxia). Delirium with psychotic features is a medical emergency requiring prompt assessment of the cause, not a behavioral issue to be managed with sedation alone. In older adults and those with dementia, new or suddenly worse paranoia and hallucinations are delirium until proven otherwise, and the cause must be sought. Missing delirium as the cause of psychosis delays treatment of a dangerous underlying condition.

## Common Traps

### Arguing The Facts To Disprove The Delusion

The caregiver presents evidence that the belief is false, expecting the person to accept it. The mechanism is the instinct that truth should prevail. The false signal is that logic should work. The harm is that the person cannot be argued out of a delusion, the argument reads as threat, trust is destroyed, and the distress and paranoia worsen. Acknowledge feelings, do not confirm content, offer safety — do not argue facts.

### Validating Or Playing Along With The False Belief

The caregiver, wanting to be supportive, agrees with or acts as if the delusion is real. The mechanism is the desire to join the person's reality and reduce conflict. The false signal is that agreement reduces distress. The harm is that validation confirms and entrenches the delusion, making it more fixed and the behavior more driven, and it undermines the person's contact with shared reality. Feelings are acknowledged; content is not confirmed.

### Ignoring The Hallucinations Or The Distress As If They Are Not Happening

The caregiver acts as if nothing is wrong and carries on with tasks. The mechanism is the discomfort with the psychosis and the hope it will pass. The false signal is that ignoring reduces attention to the symptom. The harm is that the person is left alone with frightening experiences, feels abandoned and disbelieved, and the distress and driven behaviors worsen. Acknowledge the experience and the feelings, and offer presence and safety.

### Taking Accusations And Paranoia Personally

The caregiver is accused of stealing or poisoning and responds with hurt, defensiveness, or withdrawal. The mechanism is that the accusation attacks integrity. The false signal is that the accusation reflects the person's true view. The harm is that the caregiver's reaction becomes part of the dynamic, trust erodes, and the care suffers. The disease is speaking; respond calmly and step back if you are the focus.

### Missing Delirium As The Cause Of Acute Psychosis

Acute psychosis in an older adult or someone with dementia is attributed to "their condition" rather than investigated as delirium. The mechanism is that the person has a psychiatric or cognitive history, so the symptom feels expected. The false signal is that the psychosis fits the diagnosis. The harm is that delirium from infection, medication, or metabolic cause is missed, and the underlying medical emergency goes untreated. Acute or fluctuating psychosis is delirium until proven otherwise.

### Dismissing Command Hallucinations Or Paranoia With Violence Risk

The person reports voices telling them to hurt someone, or expresses paranoid beliefs with anger, and it is not acted on. The mechanism is the discomfort with asking directly and the hope it is not serious. The false signal is that the person has not acted before. The harm is that command hallucinations and paranoia with access to means are real risks of self-harm and violence, and dismissing them misses the chance to intervene. Take them seriously and escalate.

## Self-Check

Before treating psychosis and reality disturbance support as complete, verify:

- The person's experience and feelings were acknowledged without confirming the false content, and the facts were not argued.
- The likely cause of the psychosis (chronic illness, dementia, delirium, mood, substance, medication) was considered, because it shapes the response.
- The response focused on feelings and safety rather than the content of the belief.
- Accusations, paranoia, and rejection were not taken personally, and another caregiver took over when the caregiver became the focus.
- A calm, low-stimulation environment and predictable routine were maintained.
- Decompensation, command hallucinations, paranoia with violence risk, and refusal of basics were recognized and escalated.
- Acute or fluctuating psychosis was treated as possible delirium and medically assessed, not dismissed as the baseline condition.
- Did the response stay within scope, deferring diagnosis, medication, and clinical management to the prescriber and crisis team while providing safe, supportive care?
