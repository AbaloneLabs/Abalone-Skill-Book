# Advance Directives And Care Preferences

Advance directives and care preferences are how a client's voice survives the moment they can no longer speak for themselves. They are not paperwork to file; they are the moral and legal foundation for every decision made when capacity fades, and mishandling them produces some of the most damaging conflicts in care. Caregivers tend to treat directives as a nurse or doctor matter and disengage, to defer to whichever family member is loudest, or to assume a generic default when the document is unclear. The harm is that a client who explicitly asked for comfort-only care is subjected to aggressive treatment they did not want, or a client who wanted everything done is under-treated, and families fracture in the aftermath. The agent has latitude to help clarify and honor preferences, but it must recognize that interpreting or contesting a directive is a clinical and legal matter requiring the right people, and that the client's own expressed wishes outrank family opinion whenever they conflict.

## Core Rules

### Know Whether A Directive Exists And Where It Is Recorded

At intake and after any transition, establish whether the client has an advance directive, a living will, a resuscitation order, a healthcare power of attorney, or documented goals of care, and confirm where the current version lives. A directive that no one can find at the critical moment is functionally absent. Make sure the care team and family know the document exists and what it says before a crisis forces a rushed decision.

### Honor The Client's Own Wishes Above Family Preference

When a client has clearly expressed wishes, in a directive, in goals-of-care conversations, or through consistent prior statements, those wishes govern, even if a family member disagrees. The role of family is to represent the client's values, not to substitute their own. If a family member's request conflicts with the documented preference, escalate to the nurse and the designated decision-maker rather than quietly complying with the family.

### Distinguish Capacity From Agreement

A client who disagrees with you is not automatically lacking capacity. Capacity is decision-specific, time-specific, and must be properly assessed, not assumed because the client makes an unconventional choice. Do not treat a refusal of care as evidence of incapacity, and do not let family pressure override a client who has capacity. Conversely, when capacity is genuinely lost, the documented directive and the legal substitute decision-maker take over.

### Route Ambiguity And Conflict To The Right Authority

When the directive is unclear, when family members disagree, when the clinical situation was not anticipated by the document, or when the designated decision-maker is unsure, do not improvise. Escalate to the nurse, the prescriber, the care manager, and where needed ethics or legal channels. These situations are exactly what those structures exist for, and a caregiver who resolves them alone exposes the client to harm and themselves to liability.

### Keep Preferences Specific And Operationally Usable

A directive that says no extraordinary measures is open to interpretation at the worst moment. Work with the team to translate preferences into concrete, actionable instructions: resuscitation status, hospital transfer yes or no, antibiotics yes or no, artificial hydration and nutrition, comfort-focused care. Vague preferences are honored inconsistently because every reader fills in their own meaning, so specificity protects the client's intent.

### Revisit Preferences As Conditions Change

A directive written years ago may not reflect the client's current situation, and preferences can evolve as illness progresses. When a major change occurs, a new diagnosis, a decline, a hospitalization, or entry into a new phase of care, flag the need to revisit goals of care with the client and decision-maker. Directives are living documents, and a stale one can direct care the client would no longer choose.

## Common Traps

### Deferring To The Loudest Family Member Instead Of The Documented Wish

The trap begins in a moment of pressure, when one family member is vocal and insistent and the documented preference points elsewhere. The mechanism is that conflict is uncomfortable and the vocal relative feels like the obvious authority, so the caregiver aligns with the loudest voice to keep the peace. The false signal is a smooth, conflict-free decision that everyone present seems to accept. The harm is that the client's explicitly stated wishes are overridden by someone who never had the authority to override them, the client receives care they spent effort refusing, and other family members who knew the real wishes are silenced and later resentful. Peace at the moment of decision becomes a fracture that lasts for years.

### Treating The Directive As Someone Else's Responsibility

The caregiver assumes advance directives belong to nurses and doctors and never learns what the document says. The mechanism is scope-narrowing, the belief that knowing the preferences is above the caregiver's role. The false signal is that the clinical staff have it handled. The harm is that the caregiver, who is often the person present when a client deteriorates or when a family member makes a request, cannot recognize when a proposed action contradicts the directive, cannot advocate for the client, and may unknowingly participate in care the client refused. A directive no one on the floor has read cannot protect the client.

### Assuming A Refusal Means The Client Lacks Capacity

A client with capacity refuses a treatment or care, and the caregiver, believing the refusal is unwise, treats it as proof the client cannot decide. The mechanism is conflating disagreement with incompetence, often driven by protective intent. The false signal is that a competent person would obviously accept the recommended care. The harm is that a capable adult's autonomous decision is overridden, sometimes leading to unwanted treatment, and the client loses agency over their own body and life. Capacity is about the ability to understand and decide, not about whether the decision matches what staff would choose.

### Acting On A Stale Directive Without Revisiting It

The directive was written five years ago in very different circumstances, and it is applied literally to the current situation. The mechanism is treating the document as fixed and authoritative regardless of context. The false signal is that following the written instruction is the legally safe and correct path. The harm is that the client may have changed their mind as their condition changed, and a preference made when they were well may not reflect what they would want now that they are seriously ill. Honoring a directive means honoring the person's current intent, which requires confirming that the old document still speaks for them.

### Leaving Preferences Vague And Hoping They Get Interpreted Right

The care plan records comfort-focused care or no aggressive measures without defining what that means in practice. The mechanism is avoidance of difficult, specific conversations. The false signal is that a preference has been documented and therefore the client is protected. The harm is that at the critical moment, each reader interprets the vague phrase differently, the same directive produces opposite decisions across shifts, and the client receives inconsistent care that probably does not match what they actually wanted. Vague preferences are a form of no preference, because they leave the real decision to whoever happens to be present.

### Letting A Crisis Force A Decision Before The Directive Is Confirmed

The client deteriorates suddenly, everyone is urgent, and decisions are made before anyone locates or reads the directive. The mechanism is that emergencies compress time and push people toward default action. The false signal is that acting fast is the responsible thing to do. The harm is that the default, usually full treatment, may be exactly what the client asked to avoid, and once aggressive measures begin they are hard to walk back. Confirming the directive before a crisis, and having it immediately accessible, is what allows the client's wishes to actually govern when seconds matter.

## Self-Check

Before treating advance-directive handling as complete, verify:

- The existence, location, and current content of any advance directive, resuscitation order, and designated decision-maker are known to the care team.
- The client's own documented or consistently expressed wishes govern decisions, even when a family member requests something different.
- Capacity has been properly assessed rather than assumed from a refusal, and a client with capacity is not overridden.
- Any ambiguity, family conflict, or unanticipated clinical situation has been escalated to the nurse, prescriber, or ethics channel rather than resolved alone.
- Preferences have been translated into concrete, actionable instructions, not left as vague phrases open to interpretation.
- The directive has been revisited after any major change in the client's condition, to confirm it still reflects current wishes.
- The directive is immediately accessible so that a crisis does not force a decision before it can be confirmed.
