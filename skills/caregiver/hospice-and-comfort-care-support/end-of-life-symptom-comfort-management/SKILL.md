# End-Of-Life Symptom Comfort Management

When care shifts from cure to comfort, the entire framework of decision-making changes, and the caregiver who does not make that shift continues to act in ways that cause suffering. The judgment problem is that end-of-life care prioritizes comfort and dignity over prolongation of life, which means that symptoms are treated aggressively for relief, that routine monitoring and interventions that add burden without benefit are stopped, and that the emergency department and 911 are no longer the default response. Caregivers trained in curative care tend to call 911 for symptoms that are expected in dying, to under-treat pain and breathlessness out of fear, to pursue lab draws and vitals that no longer serve the client, and to miss the signs that death is approaching. The agent must understand the comfort-focused frame, must know the hospice contact pathway as the first call, and must recognize that the goal is a peaceful, comfortable death, not a resuscitation.

## Core Rules

### Shift The Frame Entirely: Comfort And Dignity Replace Cure And Prolongation

In hospice and comfort care, the goals of care have changed, and every decision is made against the new goal of comfort and quality, not length of life. This means that medications, treatments, monitoring, hospital transfers, and interventions are evaluated by whether they add comfort, and those that add burden without comfort benefit are stopped. Routine vital signs, lab draws, weight checks, and disease-focused treatments that no longer serve comfort are discontinued per the plan. The caregiver must internalize this shift, because continuing curative-frame habits (chasing numbers, sending to the hospital, withholding comfort medication) causes suffering at the end of life. When uncertain whether an intervention serves comfort, ask the hospice team.

### Know The Hospice Contact Pathway And Use It As The First Call, Not 911

In hospice care, the hospice nurse and on-call team are the first contact for symptom changes, not the emergency services and not the primary provider. The client and family should have the hospice 24-hour number, and the caregiver should know it and use it for any comfort concern: worsening pain, breathlessness, agitation, secretions, restlessness, bleeding, or any change that distresses the client or family. Calling 911 instead of hospice typically results in a hospital transfer, resuscitation attempts, and invasive interventions that contradict the comfort goal and cause suffering. The exception is a specific emergency the hospice plan directs to 911, but the default first call for a hospice client is hospice.

### Manage Pain Aggressively For Comfort, Within The Prescribed Plan

Pain at the end of life is common and is treated aggressively for relief, because the goal is comfort and the fears that limit pain medication in curative care (dependence, respiratory depression, sedation) are reframed at end of life in favor of relief. Follow the prescribed pain plan exactly: scheduled and as-needed opioid and non-opioid medications, the rescue or breakthrough doses for worsening pain, and the route adjustments (liquid, sublingual, topical, or suppository) as swallowing becomes difficult. Do not withhold or under-dose pain medication out of fear when the client is in pain and the plan permits the dose, and do not wait for pain to become severe before treating — preventing pain is more effective than chasing it. Report uncontrolled pain to hospice promptly so the plan can be adjusted.

### Manage Breathlessness, Agitation, Secretions, And Nausea As Comfort Symptoms

The common end-of-life symptoms each have comfort-focused management. Breathlessness (dyspnea) is treated with prescribed opioid or anti-anxiety medication, positioning upright and forward, fresh air, a fan, and calm reassurance — oxygen is used only if it adds comfort, not as a default. Agitation and restlessness are treated with prescribed medication, a calm and quiet environment, reassurance, gentle touch, and addressing causes such as pain or a full bladder. Terminal secretions (the rattling sound from pooled saliva in a client who can no longer swallow or clear secretions) are treated with prescribed anticholinergic medication, repositioning to the side, and gentle mouth care; the secretions usually do not bother the client but distress the family, who should be reassured. Nausea is treated with prescribed antiemetics and small, appealing offerings. Each symptom has a plan, and the hospice team adjusts it.

### Recognize The Signs Of Approaching Death And Prepare The Family

Active dying has recognizable signs: decreased intake of food and fluid (natural and not a cause of forced feeding), increased sleeping, withdrawal, changes in breathing pattern (Cheyne-Stokes, with periods of apnea), mottling or blotching of the skin especially on the legs, coolness of the extremities, confusion or visions, restlessness, loss of urine or bowel control, and a change in skin color. These are expected, not emergencies, and the family should be prepared for them so they are not frightened. The caregiver's role is to keep the client clean, comfortable, positioned, and supported, to manage symptoms per the plan, to reassure the family about what is normal, and to notify hospice of the changes so they can support and confirm the stage.

### Continue Mouth Care, Positioning, Skin Care, And Presence As Core Comfort Measures

Even as disease-focused treatments stop, core comfort measures continue and become central: meticulous mouth care (moistening dry lips and mouth with swabs, as the dying client's mouth becomes dry and uncomfortable), regular turning and positioning for comfort and skin integrity, keeping the client clean and dry, maintaining a calm and comfortable environment, and the simple presence of a caregiver. These measures are the heart of end-of-life dignity, and they are within the caregiver's scope regardless of the medical plan. A clean, comfortable, well-positioned, gently cared-for client is the goal.

### Support The Family Through The Process, Not Just The Client

End-of-life care is family care as much as client care. The family is grieving, frightened, exhausted, and often unprepared for what dying looks like. Provide clear, compassionate explanations of what is happening and what to expect, encourage their presence and participation at the level they want, respect their cultural and religious practices around dying, support their decisions within the care plan, and connect them to hospice bereavement and spiritual support. The way the family experiences the death shapes their grief for years, and the caregiver's calm, respectful, communicative presence is part of good end-of-life care.

## Common Traps

### Calling 911 For An Expected End-Of-Life Symptom

The client becomes agitated or breathless, and the caregiver or family calls 911. The mechanism is the reflex that distress equals emergency. The false signal is that the symptom is severe. The harm is a hospital transfer, resuscitation attempts, and invasive interventions that contradict the comfort goal and cause suffering and trauma for the client and family. For a hospice client, the first call is hospice, who can manage the symptom at home.

### Under-Treating Pain Out Of Fear Of Medication

The client is in pain, but the caregiver withholds or under-doses the opioid for fear of dependence, sedation, or hastening death. The mechanism is the curative-care fear of opioids carried into comfort care. The false signal is that the lower dose feels safer. The harm is untreated pain at the end of life, which is both cruel and unnecessary, and the fear of hastening death with appropriately dosed comfort medication is not supported by good practice. Follow the prescribed plan and treat pain for comfort.

### Forcing Food Or Fluid On A Dying Client Who Has Stopped Intake

The family, distressed by the client's refusal of food and water, tries to force intake or asks for IV fluids. The mechanism is the belief that not eating causes suffering and death. The false signal is that intake must be maintained. The harm is that the dying body can no longer process food and fluid, forced feeding causes nausea and aspiration, and IV fluids can cause edema and worsening breathlessness. Decreased intake is a natural part of dying and is not starving; mouth care is the comfort measure, not forced feeding.

### Pursuing Vitals, Labs, And Disease Treatments That No Longer Serve Comfort

The caregiver continues to check blood pressure, draw labs, or give disease-focused treatments as if the goal were still cure. The mechanism is the habit of curative care. The false signal is that monitoring is being thorough. The harm is added burden and discomfort for a client for whom the information no longer changes the plan, and the contradiction of the comfort goal. In comfort care, interventions serve comfort or they are stopped.

### Treating Terminal Secretions As Choking Or An Emergency

The client develops the rattling sound of pooled secretions, and the family is frightened that the client is choking or drowning. The mechanism is that the sound is dramatic and distressing. The false signal is that the client is suffering. The harm is that suctioning (which is uncomfortable and often ineffective) is attempted or 911 is called, when the client is usually not distressed by the secretions (they are semiconscious and not choking), and the comfort measure is repositioning, mouth care, and prescribed medication. Reassure the family and follow the plan.

### Withholding Presence Or Honest Communication From The Family

The caregiver, uncomfortable with dying, withdraws or gives vague reassurance instead of clear information. The mechanism is the discomfort with death and the desire not to frighten. The false signal is that vagueness is kinder. The harm is that the family is unprepared, frightened by normal changes, and deprived of the chance to be present and to grieve well. Clear, compassionate communication and presence are core to end-of-life care.

## Self-Check

Before treating end-of-life symptom comfort management as complete, verify:

- The frame was shifted entirely to comfort and dignity, with disease-focused monitoring and interventions stopped unless they served comfort.
- The hospice 24-hour contact pathway was known and used as the first call for symptom changes, with 911 reserved for what the hospice plan directs.
- Pain was managed aggressively for comfort within the prescribed plan, without under-dosing out of fear, and uncontrolled pain was reported to hospice.
- Breathlessness, agitation, secretions, and nausea were managed as comfort symptoms per the plan, with positioning, reassurance, and prescribed medication.
- The signs of approaching death were recognized as expected, and the family was prepared and reassured rather than frightened.
- Core comfort measures (mouth care, positioning, skin care, cleanliness, presence) continued and were treated as central, not optional.
- The family was supported with clear, compassionate communication, inclusion, and respect for their practices and grief.
- Did the response stay within scope, deferring medication changes and clinical decisions to the hospice team while providing comfort-focused care?
