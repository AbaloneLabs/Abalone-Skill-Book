# Comfort Care Decision-Making And Goal Alignment

When a client is seriously or terminally ill, almost every care decision becomes a judgment about what serves the client's goals, and those goals may be comfort, length of life, function, or a combination that shifts over time. The judgment problem is that decisions are made moment to moment — a medication, a hospital transfer, a feeding tube, an antibiotic, a lab draw — and without an explicit, shared understanding of the client's goals, each decision defaults to "do more," which often adds burden without benefit and can contradict what the client actually wanted. Caregivers tend to assume the goal is obvious, to defer to the most vocal family member, and to participate in interventions that have not been weighed against the client's stated preferences. The agent must understand that goals must be made explicit, that every intervention is weighed against them, and that the caregiver's role is to surface the question, not to decide it.

## Core Rules

### Make The Goals Of Care Explicit, Shared, And Documented

The foundation of aligned decision-making is an explicit, shared, documented understanding of the client's goals: what they want most (comfort, time, function, being home, specific outcomes), what they are willing to undergo for it (hospitalization, ICU, feeding tubes, dialysis, CPR), and what they do not want. These goals are captured in advance directives, physician orders for life-sustaining treatment (POLST or similar), goals-of-care conversations, and the care plan. Obtain and understand these documents before participating in decisions, and if the goals are not clear or documented, surface the need for a goals-of-care conversation with the team and family. Decisions made without explicit goals default to maximal intervention, which often contradicts what the client wanted.

### Weigh Every Intervention Against The Client's Goals, Not Against Default Habit

For every proposed intervention — a new medication, a hospital transfer, a feeding tube, dialysis, an antibiotic, a lab draw, a procedure, CPR — ask: does this serve the client's stated goals, and do the burdens (discomfort, hospitalization, side effects, time, away from home) outweigh the benefits for this client at this stage? An intervention that is routine in curative care (a feeding tube for someone no longer eating, an antibiotic for a pneumonia in a dying client, a hospital transfer for a worsening that is part of dying) may add burden without serving the comfort goal. The caregiver's job is not to decide whether to intervene, but to ensure the question is asked and routed to the decision-maker and team, because interventions that are not weighed against goals accumulate into unwanted, burdensome care.

### Recognize That The Shift From Cure To Comfort Is A Decision Point, Not A Drift

The transition from curative to comfort-focused care is a significant decision that should be made explicitly with the client (if able), the family, and the care team, based on the trajectory of the illness, the burden and futility of further curative treatment, and the client's goals. It is often made too late, after the client has suffered through burdensome interventions that did not serve them, because no one named the shift. The caregiver may be the first to notice that the client is declining despite treatment, that the burdens are growing, and that a goals-of-care conversation is needed. Surfacing this observation to the team and family is part of aligned care; the decision itself belongs to the client, family, and team.

### Defer To The Designated Decision-Maker And The Advance Directive, Not The Loudest Voice

When the client cannot decide for themselves, the legally designated decision-maker (healthcare proxy, surrogate, guardian, or next of kin per the hierarchy) decides, guided by the client's known wishes and the advance directive. Family conflict often arises when members disagree, and the caregiver must not take sides or be swayed by the most vocal member. Refer decisions to the documented decision-maker and the advance directive, and bring in the team, social worker, or ethics support when conflict persists. The caregiver's integrity depends on deferring to the proper authority, not to pressure.

### Surface The Question Rather Than Deciding It, And Route It To The Right Person

The caregiver's role in most decisions is not to decide but to recognize that a decision point exists and to route it to the right person: the hospice nurse, the prescriber, the designated decision-maker, the family. When a symptom worsens, an intervention is proposed, a transfer is considered, or a treatment is no longer clearly serving the client, surface the question ("Should we talk to the team about whether this still serves the goal?") rather than acting by default. Decisions made by default, without the right people, are how clients end up with care they would not have wanted.

### Support The Family Through Difficult Decisions Without Taking Over

Families facing goals-of-care and end-of-life decisions are under enormous emotional strain, and they need information, time, support, and permission to make the decision that fits the client's wishes — not pressure toward a particular choice. Provide honest information about prognosis and the likely outcomes of options, encourage them to consider what the client would want, support them through the guilt and grief of the decision, and bring in the team for the medical and ethical dimensions. Do not make the decision for them, and do not abandon them to make it alone; the middle path of informed, supported decision-making is the goal.

### Revisit The Goals As The Illness Progresses, Because Goals Shift

Goals of care are not fixed; they shift as the illness progresses, as the client's condition and preferences change, and as the balance of burden and benefit moves. A client who wanted "everything" at one stage may shift to comfort as the disease advances; a goal of time may shift to a goal of being home. Revisit the goals periodically and at major turning points (a hospitalization, a significant decline, a new treatment decision), and ensure the care plan and orders reflect the current goals. Care that continues on old goals, unvisited, drifts into misalignment.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To "Do More" Without Weighing Against The Client's Goals

Each decision is made in isolation as "more treatment," and the cumulative burden contradicts the client's wishes. The mechanism is that more feels like caring and less feels like giving up. The false signal is that intervening is the safe default. The harm is unwanted, burdensome care — hospitalizations, feeding tubes, procedures — that the client would not have chosen, and a death that looks nothing like what they wanted. Every intervention is weighed against the goals.

### Assuming The Goals Are Obvious And Shared When They Have Never Been Stated

The caregiver proceeds as if everyone knows the goal, when it has never been explicitly discussed or documented. The mechanism is the discomfort with the goals conversation. The false signal is that the family seems to agree. The harm is that unspoken goals diverge, and decisions made against assumed goals produce conflict and unwanted care. Make the goals explicit and documented.

### Letting The Shift From Cure To Comfort Drift Instead Of Naming It

The client declines despite treatment, the burdens grow, but no one names the shift to comfort, and maximal intervention continues past the point of benefit. The mechanism is the difficulty of the conversation. The false signal is that there is always one more treatment to try. The harm is prolonged suffering through interventions that do not serve the client. The shift is a decision point that is named and discussed, not a drift.

### Deferring To The Loudest Family Member Instead Of The Designated Decision-Maker

A vocal family member pushes for a particular decision, and the caregiver or team accedes to avoid conflict. The mechanism is the pressure of the moment. The false signal is that agreement ends the conflict. The harm is that the decision contradicts the client's wishes and the designated decision-maker's judgment, and it sets up worse conflict later. Refer to the documented decision-maker and directive.

### Making Or Steering The Decision Instead Of Routing It

The caregiver, seeing a decision point, makes the call or steers strongly toward a preferred outcome. The mechanism is the desire to resolve the situation. The false signal is that decisiveness helps. The harm is that the decision bypasses the client, family, and team, oversteps the caregiver's role, and can produce a decision the client would not have wanted. Surface the question and route it to the right person.

### Failing To Revisit Goals As The Illness Progresses

The goals were set once and never revisited, so the care plan reflects an earlier stage. The mechanism is that the conversation felt finished. The false signal is that the plan is set. The harm is that care continues on outdated goals, drifting into misalignment as the client's condition and preferences change. Goals are revisited at turning points.

## Self-Check

Before treating comfort care decision-making and goal alignment as complete, verify:

- The goals of care were made explicit, shared, and documented (advance directives, POLST, goals-of-care conversation), not assumed.
- Every intervention was weighed against the client's goals for burden versus benefit, rather than defaulting to "do more."
- The shift from cure to comfort was recognized as a decision point to be named and discussed, not a drift.
- Decisions were deferred to the designated decision-maker and the advance directive, not to the loudest family voice.
- Decision points were surfaced and routed to the right person (team, decision-maker, family) rather than decided by the caregiver.
- The family was supported through difficult decisions with information, time, and permission, without the decision being made for or abandoned to them.
- Goals were revisited as the illness progressed and at major turning points, so the care plan reflected the current goals.
- Did the response stay within scope, deferring prognosis, medical decisions, and ethical judgment to the team while surfacing and supporting aligned decision-making?
