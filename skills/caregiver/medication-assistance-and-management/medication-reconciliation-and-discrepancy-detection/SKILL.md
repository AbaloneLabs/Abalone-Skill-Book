# Medication Reconciliation And Discrepancy Detection

Medication reconciliation is the process of building an accurate, current picture of every medication a client is supposed to take and comparing it against what they actually take. It is where errors introduced by hospital discharge, multiple prescribers, over-the-counter additions, and stale records are caught before they cause harm. Caregivers tend to treat the medication list as a settled document rather than a moving target, and to assume that someone else has already reconciled it. The harm is serious: unintended discrepancies are a leading cause of adverse drug events, rehospitalization, and falls in older adults, many of whom see several prescribers who do not communicate. The agent has latitude to gather and compare information, but it must recognize that resolving discrepancies, deciding what to stop, start, or change, is a clinical decision that belongs to the prescriber or pharmacist, not the caregiver.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile After Every Transition Of Care

Hospital admission, discharge, emergency visits, specialist appointments, and changes in living situation all disrupt medication continuity. After any transition, obtain the current discharge or visit medication list and compare it line by line against the prior list and against what is physically in the client's home. Transitions are the highest-risk moments for medication error, and reconciliation is the specific defense against them.

### Compare Prescribed Against Actual, Not Just Prescribed Against Chart

A reconciled list is not complete if it only matches two documents. Clients often take less than prescribed, take leftover tablets from an old prescription, share medications with a spouse, or add over-the-counter drugs and supplements that no prescriber knows about. Ask what the client actually takes, including herbal and non-prescription products, because these are frequent sources of interactions and duplications.

### Look Specifically For Duplications, Omissions, And Interactions

When comparing lists, scan actively for the same drug under different names, two drugs in the same class, a stopped drug that is still being taken, a new drug missing from the home supply, and combinations that raise interaction risk such as multiple anticoagulants or sedatives. These patterns are where reconciliation finds real danger, and they require clinical judgment to resolve.

### Flag Discrepancies; Do Not Resolve Them Independently

When you find a mismatch, document it clearly and route it to the prescriber or pharmacist. Do not decide on your own to stop, start, change, or resume a medication, even when the discrepancy seems obvious. The caregiver's role is detection and escalation; the resolution is a clinical decision because the reasons behind a change may not be visible in the records.

### Keep A Single Current Source Of Truth And Update It Promptly

Maintain one current medication list, update it the moment a change is confirmed, and make sure every caregiver works from the same version. Stale copies, multiple conflicting lists, and handwritten additions are how clients end up taking both the old and new dose. The list should include drug name, dose, route, frequency, indication, prescriber, and start or stop dates.

### Treat Sudden Clinical Change As A Possible Reconciliation Failure

When a client develops new confusion, dizziness, bleeding, hypoglycemia, or falls, consider whether a recent medication change or an unreconciled discrepancy could be the cause. Many adverse events are discovered only after the harm, when a careful review reveals a duplication or interaction that reconciliation should have caught. Connecting symptoms to possible medication causes is part of vigilant caregiving.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Medication List As Settled Once Written

The trap begins because the list looks authoritative and complete, and no one has flagged a problem. The mechanism is that a written list feels like a stable truth rather than a snapshot that decays with every prescriber visit. The false signal is a tidy, signed document. The harm is that the list drifts out of sync with reality as prescribers make changes, the client adjusts their own intake, and hospital discharge introduces new drugs, and no one notices until an adverse event forces a review. Medication lists are never settled; they are only as good as the last reconciliation.

### Assuming The Hospital Already Reconciled On Discharge

The caregiver receives a discharge summary and assumes the hospital team reconciled the medications, so takes the list at face value. The mechanism is trust in the institution and avoidance of duplicate work. The false signal is a formal discharge document that looks complete. The harm is that discharge reconciliation errors are common: medications are often unintentionally continued, stopped, or omitted, and home medications the client was taking may not appear on the discharge list. Trusting the document without comparing it to the home supply is how clients end up double-dosed or abruptly withdrawn from a needed drug.

### Missing Over-The-Counter And Herbal Products

The reconciliation focuses on prescription medications and overlooks the client's painkillers, antacids, sleep aids, vitamins, and herbal supplements. The mechanism is that non-prescription products seem harmless and outside the medical record. The false signal is a complete prescription list. The harm is that over-the-counter products cause serious interactions, such as bleeding from added anti-inflammatories with anticoagulants, or serotonin problems from combinations, and hidden ingredients in herbal products can interfere with prescription drugs. A reconciliation that excludes what the client buys themselves is not a reconciliation.

### Resolving An Apparent Discrepancy Yourself To Be Helpful

The caregiver notices the client is still taking a medication that appears to have been stopped, or is not taking one that was started, and adjusts the home supply to match what seems correct. The mechanism is confidence that the right action is obvious and a desire to fix the problem quickly. The false signal is that the list now looks consistent. The harm is that the apparent discrepancy may reflect a deliberate clinical decision the caregiver cannot see, such as a tapered withdrawal or a drug held for a reason, and changing it independently can cause harm. Reconciliation findings must be escalated, not silently corrected.

### Keeping Multiple Conflicting Lists That No One Reconciles

The home has a pharmacy printout, a discharge summary, a handwritten list from a relative, and the caregiver's own notes, all slightly different. The mechanism is that each source was created for a purpose and none was retired. The false signal is that information exists and seems available. The harm is that caregivers work from different versions, the client receives inconsistent care across shifts, and there is no single authority to check against. Conflicting lists are functionally worse than no list, because each creates false confidence that someone knows the truth.

### Failing To Connect A Fall Or Confusion To A Recent Medication Change

The client falls or becomes confused a few days after a discharge or prescription change, and the event is treated as an isolated incident. The mechanism is that the symptom and the medication change are separated in time and seem unrelated. The false signal is that the client has fallen before or is generally frail. The harm is that an adverse drug effect, such as over-sedation from a new sleeping tablet or hypotension from an added antihypertensive, is missed and continues to injure the client. Any acute change following a medication transition must trigger a review of what changed.

## Self-Check

Before treating the medication list as reconciled and current, verify:

- Reconciliation was performed after the most recent hospital admission, discharge, emergency visit, or prescriber change.
- The comparison included what the client actually takes, including leftover tablets, shared medications, over-the-counter drugs, and herbal supplements.
- The review actively checked for duplications, omissions, same-class overlaps, stopped drugs still being taken, and high-risk interaction combinations.
- Every discrepancy found was documented and escalated to the prescriber or pharmacist, not resolved independently.
- A single current medication list exists, includes dose, route, frequency, indication, and prescriber, and is the version all caregivers use.
- Any recent acute change in the client's condition was considered as a possible consequence of a medication discrepancy or change.
- Stale copies and conflicting handwritten lists have been retired so only one source of truth remains in use.
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
