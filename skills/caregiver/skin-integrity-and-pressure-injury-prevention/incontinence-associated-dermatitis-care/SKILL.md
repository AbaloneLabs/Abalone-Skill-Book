# Incontinence-Associated Dermatitis Care

Incontinence-associated dermatitis (IAD) is the inflammation and breakdown of skin caused by prolonged contact with urine or feces. It is common, painful, and frequently confused with pressure injury, which leads to the wrong treatment. Unlike a pressure injury, IAD is driven by moisture and chemical irritation rather than by pressure over a bony prominence, and it is largely preventable with prompt, gentle, structured continence care. The caregiver is the person who either prevents IAD through consistent practice or allows it to develop through delayed changes, harsh cleansing, and rubbing. Once skin is denuded, the client is in significant pain, at higher risk of infection, and at much higher risk of a true pressure injury forming on top of the damaged skin. This skill builds the discipline of prompt moisture control, correct cleansing and barrier use, accurate description of what you see, and escalation when skin is broken or infected — while always preserving the client's dignity during an intimate and often embarrassing care task.

## Core Rules

### Treat Moisture Control as the Primary Intervention

IAD is prevented by keeping skin dry and free of irritants. Check and change soiled or wet briefs promptly — not on a fixed schedule — because damage begins within minutes of exposure, especially to liquid stool. Respond to known incontinence episodes immediately, and for clients with frequent leakage, schedule proactive checks. The single most effective prevention is that skin never sits in urine or feces for long.

### Cleanse Gently With the Right Products

Use a no-rinse, pH-balanced cleanser designed for incontinence care rather than plain soap, which is alkaline and strips the skin barrier. Avoid rubbing with a washcloth; pat or use a gentle wipe, and let the skin air-dry or pat dry rather than rubbing dry. Friction during cleansing is itself a cause of skin damage on already-irritated tissue.

### Apply Barrier Creams Correctly

A barrier cream (zinc oxide, petrolatum-based, or film-forming) protects skin from the next exposure to moisture. Apply a thin, even layer to intact or mildly reddened skin after cleansing and drying, and reapply after each change. Do not pile on thick layers that trap moisture or hide the skin from assessment, and remove build-up gently rather than scrubbing. Follow the care plan's specified product; barrier choice is a clinical decision.

### Describe What You See Accurately and Consistently

Document location, extent, color, and whether the skin is intact, reddened, denuded, or weeping. Use plain, objective description rather than guessing a diagnosis. Note whether redness blanches, whether it sits over a bony prominence (suggesting pressure) or spreads across perineal folds and buttocks (suggesting IAD), and whether there is any sign of infection such as increasing pain, odor, pus, or spreading redness. Accurate description lets the nurse distinguish IAD from pressure injury and choose the right treatment.

### Distinguish IAD From Pressure Injury — and Escalate When Unsure

IAD typically appears as diffuse redness over the perineum, buttocks, thighs, or scrotum, often with a "tide mark" edge and partial skin loss, and it is usually painful or burning. A pressure injury tends to be a localized, often non-blanching lesion over a bony prominence such as the sacrum, coccyx, or heel. These overlap, and IAD greatly increases the risk of pressure injury on top of it. If you are not certain, or if the area is over a bony prominence, escalate rather than labeling it yourself.

### Preserve Dignity and Comfort

Incontinence care is intimate and can be humiliating. Explain each step, ensure privacy, warm the wipes and water, and never make the client feel like a burden or express disgust. Pain from IAD is real; handle damaged skin with particular gentleness and offer analgesia before care if the care plan allows. Dignity is part of prevention, because a client who fears care will delay asking for changes and worsen the moisture exposure.

### Know Your Scope

Continence care, cleansing, barrier application per the care plan, and documentation are caregiver scope. Diagnosing IAD versus pressure injury, prescribing or changing barrier products, debriding, or treating infection is not. Escalate new or worsening skin damage, broken skin, signs of infection, or any lesion over a bony prominence to the nurse.

## Common Traps

**Using plain soap and a washcloth for perineal care.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver, using routine bathing supplies, cleans the incontinent perineum with alkaline soap and friction from a washcloth. The false signal is that "thorough scrubbing equals clean and healthy"; in reality, alkaline soap raises skin pH, destroying the acid mantle that resists irritation, and mechanical friction abrades skin already weakened by moisture. The harm is accelerated IAD, pain, and skin breakdown caused by the cleansing itself. The fix is to use a pH-balanced, no-rinse incontinence cleanser, pat rather than rub, and dry by patting or air, treating the perineum as fragile tissue rather than a surface to scrub.

**Deferring soiled-brief changes to the next scheduled round.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver, following a fixed check schedule, leaves a soiled brief in place until the next round to avoid disrupting the client. The false signal is "only twenty minutes to go"; in reality, liquid stool in particular begins damaging skin within minutes, and the combination of urine and feces creates ammonia and enzymes that are far more irritant together than either alone. The harm is rapid-onset IAD and denuded skin that is painful and slow to heal. The fix is to treat every known incontinence episode as an immediate change, regardless of the schedule, and to check proactively for clients who cannot report it.

**Piling on thick barrier cream and then failing to assess the skin.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver, intending to protect, applies heavy layers of barrier cream at each change without fully removing the previous layer. The false signal is that thick protection is better; in reality, accumulated cream traps moisture against the skin, masks developing redness or breakdown, and makes the next assessment unreliable. The harm is hidden, worsening dermatitis or an emerging pressure injury that goes unrecognized under the paste. The fix is to apply a thin even layer, gently remove build-up at each change so the skin can be seen and assessed, and never let cream substitute for looking at the skin.

**Labeling all perineal redness as IAD and missing a pressure injury.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver sees redness in the sacral or buttock area and assumes it is incontinence dermatitis because the client is incontinent. The false signal is that the client is incontinent, so the cause must be moisture; in reality, a localized, non-blanching lesion over the sacrum or coccyx is a pressure injury requiring a completely different prevention and treatment response. The harm is that a pressure injury is treated as simple irritation, pressure relief is not intensified, and the injury deepens while the wrong care continues. The fix is to check for blanching, note the location relative to bony prominences, and escalate any localized or non-blanching area for the nurse to differentiate.

**Expressing disgust or rushing through intimate care.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver, uncomfortable with incontinence, hurries the care, uses brusque movements, or allows non-verbal disgust to show. The false signal is that efficiency is professional; in reality, the client experiences shame and pain, learns to fear and avoid care, and begins to delay reporting or requesting changes. The harm is both immediate distress and worsening moisture exposure as the client suppresses requests, driving the very IAD the care was meant to prevent. The fix is to slow down, explain each step, ensure warmth and privacy, and treat continence care as a dignity-preserving interaction, not a task to rush.

## Self-Check

- Did I change soiled or wet briefs promptly rather than waiting for the next scheduled round?
- Did I cleanse with a pH-balanced, no-rinse product and pat rather than rub?
- Did I dry the skin fully before applying a thin, even layer of the prescribed barrier?
- Did I remove built-up cream so I could actually see and assess the skin?
- Did I describe the location, extent, color, and intactness of any redness objectively?
- Did I check whether redness blanches and whether it sits over a bony prominence, and escalate if unsure?
- Did I handle damaged skin with gentleness and preserve the client's dignity throughout?
- Did I stay within scope, escalating broken skin, infection signs, and any pressure-suspicious lesion to the nurse?
