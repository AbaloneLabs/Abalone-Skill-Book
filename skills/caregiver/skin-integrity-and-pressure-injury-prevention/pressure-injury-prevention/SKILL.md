# Pressure Injury Prevention

Pressure injuries (pressure ulcers, bedsores) are among the most preventable yet most damaging harms in care. They form when sustained pressure, shear, moisture, or friction starves tissue of blood, and a Stage 1 redness can progress to deep muscle and bone destruction in days. They are overwhelmingly a disease of immobility: the client who cannot reposition themselves, who slides down in a chair, who lies in wet or soiled briefs, or who is undernourished is the client at risk. The caregiver is the front line of prevention, because prevention is mostly about what happens shift to shift — turning, skin checks, continence care, and nutrition — not about a single clinical intervention. The judgment this skill builds is how to deliver consistent pressure relief, recognize the earliest skin changes, distinguish a pressure injury from other skin problems, and escalate the moment tissue compromise appears, because once a deep injury forms it may never fully heal.

## Core Rules

### Identify Risk and Focus the Plan

Know which clients are at risk: immobile or bedbound, chairbound without independent weight shift, incontinent, undernourished or dehydrated, with reduced sensation (diabetes, spinal cord injury, stroke), or with fragile skin. Use the care plan's risk assessment to direct how often to reposition and which surfaces to use. Risk is not static; a client who becomes acutely ill or less mobile moves into a higher-risk category and needs a tighter schedule.

### Reposition on a Schedule That Matches the Risk

For bedbound clients, reposition at least every two hours (more often for high risk), varying position between back, right side, left side, and a seated angle, and avoiding direct pressure on bony prominences. For chairbound clients, encourage or assist weight shifts every fifteen to thirty minutes. Do not drag the client across the sheet; use a slide sheet or hoist to lift, because shear forces tear tissue as effectively as direct pressure. Document position and time so the next shift continues the rotation rather than repeating it.

### Protect Bony Prominences and Vulnerable Skin

Pay particular attention to sacrum, coccyx, heels, hips, ankles, elbows, shoulders, ears, and the back of the head. Keep heels floated off the bed with a pillow or heel-lifting device rather than resting on the mattress. Use pressure-relieving support surfaces (mattresses, cushions) as prescribed, and check they are correctly inflated and positioned. Keep skin clean and dry, moisturize dry areas, and avoid massage over reddened or bony areas.

### Manage Moisture and Incontinence Relentlessly

Moisture from urine, feces, sweat, or wound drainage macerates skin and dramatically accelerates breakdown, especially combined with pressure. Check and change soiled briefs promptly, cleanse gently with pH-appropriate products, pat rather than rub dry, and apply barrier creams as prescribed. Do not leave a client in wet or soiled linen "until the next round"; the clock for skin damage starts immediately.

### Pair Skin Care With Nutrition and Hydration

Pressure injury prevention is not only a surface problem. Adequate protein, calories, and fluids maintain tissue tolerance and healing; a malnourished or dehydrated client breaks down faster and heals slower. Encourage intake, support mealtime and hydration, and escalate poor intake because it directly undermines skin integrity.

### Recognize and Escalate Early

Check skin at every turn and every personal-care episode. A Stage 1 pressure injury presents as non-blanchable redness over a bony prominence — when you press it, the color does not fade. In darker skin tones, look for color difference, warmth, firmness, or bogginess rather than redness. Any broken skin, blister, purple or maroon discoloration, or deep wound over a pressure point is a pressure injury until a clinician says otherwise. Report immediately; do not wait for the next scheduled assessment.

### Know Your Scope

Repositioning, continence care, skin checks, surface use, and documentation are caregiver scope. Staging a pressure injury, debriding, dressing wounds beyond the care plan, or changing a prevention regimen is not. Document what you see objectively (location, size, color, whether it blanches, presence of breakdown) and escalate to the nurse for assessment and treatment.

## Common Traps

**Trusting the two-hour turn schedule as automatically sufficient.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver follows a fixed two-hour interval mechanically even when the client's condition has changed. The false signal is "I turned them on time, so they are protected"; in reality, a high-risk client — acutely ill, very immobile, incontinent, undernourished — can develop non-blanchable erythema in well under two hours, and a single missed or delayed turn during deterioration can initiate breakdown. The harm is a Stage 1 injury that progresses to deep tissue loss because the schedule was treated as a ceiling rather than a minimum. The fix is to match repositioning frequency to current risk, check skin at every turn, and tighten the interval when risk rises.

**Dragging rather than lifting during repositioning.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver, working alone or in a hurry, pulls the client up the bed or across the chair by the arm or under the knee, creating shear force between skin and the underlying tissue and friction between skin and sheet. The false signal is that the client moved without complaint; in reality, shear is a leading cause of deep pressure injury and is invisible at the surface until serious damage exists. The harm is sacral and heel injuries that are deep on first appearance. The fix is to always use a slide sheet, hoist, or second caregiver to lift rather than drag, and to never reposition by pulling on the client's body.

**Missing non-blanchable erythema, especially in darker skin.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver checks skin, sees redness, and assumes it will fade, or fails to recognize early pressure injury in darker skin tones where redness is not visible. The false signal is that the area "looks pink but normal"; in reality, the defining sign of a Stage 1 injury is that the color does not blanch under pressure, and in darker skin the equivalent signs are warmth, firmness, bogginess, or a color different from surrounding skin. The harm is that an actionable early injury is missed until it becomes a deep wound. The fix is to press every reddened or changed area over a bony prominence and observe whether it blanches, and to escalate any non-blanching, warm, firm, or boggy area immediately.

**Leaving the client in wet or soiled briefs between rounds.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver, following a fixed check schedule, leaves incontinence until the next round rather than responding promptly. The false signal is "I will change them in twenty minutes anyway"; in reality, urine and feces, especially together, rapidly macerate skin and combine with pressure to cause breakdown far faster than pressure alone. The harm is incontinence-associated skin damage that progresses to pressure injury over the sacrum and buttocks. The fix is to treat continence care as immediate, check and change promptly, and never defer a soiled brief to the schedule.

**Assuming a pressure-relieving surface makes turning unnecessary.** The mechanism of harm is that a caregiver, once a specialized mattress or cushion is in place, reduces or skips repositioning on the belief that the surface does the work. The false signal is that the equipment is doing the relief; in reality, no surface eliminates pressure entirely, and immobility itself causes harm beyond pressure (contractures, respiratory compromise, discomfort). The harm is preventable pressure injury and broader immobility complications. The fix is to continue scheduled repositioning and skin checks regardless of the support surface, and to confirm the surface is correctly set up and inflated.

**Overlooking the edge case or exception.** The mechanism of harm is that the agent analyzes the typical scenario thoroughly but skips the unusual case. The false signal is that the common path is well-handled; in reality the exception silently produces the wrong outcome because the boundary was never tested. The harm is a failure that only appears in the case nobody checked. The fix is to always ask what the edge case is and whether the conclusion still holds there.

**Treating a confident conclusion as a substitute for evidence.** The mechanism of harm is that a well-written, confident-sounding conclusion is accepted as proof. The false signal is the tone of certainty; in reality it masks unstated assumptions, missing verification, or unresolved uncertainty. The harm is that the confident framing prevents anyone from asking the question that would expose the gap. The fix is to treat every conclusion as a claim that must be backed by stated evidence, not by its phrasing.

## Self-Check

- Did I identify the client's pressure-injury risk and reposition at a frequency that matches it?
- Did I lift rather than drag, using a slide sheet or hoist to avoid shear?
- Did I float the heels and protect bony prominences rather than resting them on the surface?
- Did I check and change soiled or wet briefs promptly, not on a fixed schedule?
- Did I check every reddened or changed area for blanching, and look for warmth, firmness, or color change in darker skin?
- Did I escalate any non-blanchable redness, broken skin, blister, or deep discoloration immediately?
- Did I support nutrition and hydration, knowing they underpin skin integrity?
- Did I document position, skin status, and care objectively, and stay within scope for staging and treatment?
