# Inhaled Respiratory Medication Technique And Adherence

Inhaled medications are the cornerstone of COPD and asthma management, and they are also among the most misused drugs in all of medicine. The judgment problem is that an inhaler only works if the medication reaches the small airways deep in the lungs, and that depends entirely on a specific, multi-step technique that most patients — and many caregivers — perform incorrectly without knowing it. A person can take their inhaler faithfully every day and get almost no medication, because their technique is wrong, and the result is poor control that gets attributed to severe disease, leading to more medications (and more side effects) when the real fix is better technique. The agent must understand that inhaled medication management is fundamentally a technique and adherence problem, that controller and rescue medications serve entirely different purposes, and that poor control should trigger a technique and adherence review before a medication escalation.

## Core Rules

### Master And Verify Inhaler Technique For Every Device

There are many inhaler types (metered-dose inhalers, dry-powder inhalers, soft-mist inhalers, breath-actuated devices), and each has a different technique, but all require a specific sequence to deliver the drug to the lungs: prepare the dose, exhale fully away from the device, seal the lips, breathe in at the right speed (slow and deep for most, fast and forceful for some dry-powder devices), hold the breath for 5 to 10 seconds, and exhale slowly. Common errors include not shaking a metered-dose inhaler, not exhaling before inhaling, breathing in too fast or too slow, not holding the breath, and not waiting between doses. Watch the person use each inhaler at every visit, compare their technique to the device-specific instructions, correct errors gently, and use a spacer with metered-dose inhalers (which dramatically improves delivery and reduces side effects). Technique drifts; recheck regularly.

### Distinguish Controller (Maintenance) From Rescue (Reliever) Medications, And Use Each Correctly

Respiratory medications fall into two functional groups, and confusing them causes both poor control and dangerous reliance. Controller (maintenance) medications — inhaled corticosteroids, long-acting bronchodilators (LABA, LAMA), and combination products — reduce inflammation and keep the airways open over time, and they only work if taken every day as scheduled, even when the person feels well. Rescue (reliever) medications — short-acting bronchodilators like albuterol — open the airways quickly for immediate relief of symptoms, and they are taken as needed for breathlessness. The critical rules: controllers must be taken daily regardless of symptoms; rescue inhalers are for acute symptoms and should not be needed more than twice a week in well-controlled disease (frequent rescue use is a sign of poor control that warrants a care-team contact). Ensure the person can name which is which, and never let a controller be skipped because the person feels fine.

### Use A Spacer With Metered-Dose Inhalers

A spacer is a simple chamber that attaches to a metered-dose inhaler and dramatically improves the amount of medication reaching the lungs while reducing the amount deposited in the mouth and throat (which causes side effects like thrush and hoarseness with inhaled steroids). For anyone using a metered-dose inhaler, especially older adults, children, and anyone with coordination difficulty, a spacer is standard of care, not an optional accessory. Ensure the person has and uses a spacer, knows the technique (spray into the spacer, then breathe in slowly and deeply from the spacer, hold, repeat), and rinses the mouth after inhaled steroids to prevent thrush.

### Assess Whether Poor Control Reflects Technique, Adherence, Or Disease Severity

When a person has persistent symptoms or frequent exacerbations despite being "on medication," the first question is not "what drug should we add" but "is the medication actually getting into the lungs." The three most common causes of poor control are incorrect technique, inconsistent adherence (especially skipping controllers when well), and environmental triggers — not severe disease. Before any medication escalation, verify technique by observation, ask about adherence honestly and without judgment (people often overreport), check for trigger exposure, and report all of this to the care team. Many "treatment failures" resolve when technique and adherence are fixed, without adding drugs.

### Recognize And Manage Inhaled Steroid Side Effects

Inhaled corticosteroids are safe and effective, but they have local side effects that bother patients and cause them to stop the medication: oral thrush (white patches, soreness), hoarseness, and sore throat. These are largely prevented by using a spacer and rinsing the mouth (and spitting, not swallowing) after each dose. Recognize thrush and report it for treatment, and reinforce rinsing. Long-term, high-dose inhaled steroids can have mild systemic effects (bone density, bruising), which the prescriber monitors. Do not let the person stop an inhaled steroid because of side effects without contacting the prescriber; there may be a lower-dose or different formulation.

### Maintain Nebulizer Equipment And Technique

For clients using nebulizers, the equipment and technique matter. Use the prescribed medication and dose, ensure the compressor works and the tubing and mask or mouthpiece are clean and unblocked, sit the person upright during the treatment, encourage slow deep breaths with a pause at the end of inspiration, and continue until the mist is gone (usually 5 to 15 minutes). Clean and air-dry the nebulizer parts after each use per instructions, and replace the disposable parts on schedule, because contaminated nebulizers can deliver bacteria to the lungs. A nebulizer that is sputtering or taking too long may have a clog or a failing compressor.

### Coordinate With The Pulmonary Team And Bring The Inhalers To Every Visit

Inhaled medication regimens change, and the only way the care team can assess technique and adherence is to see it. Bring all the person's inhalers (and spacers and nebulizer supplies) to every appointment, demonstrate the technique, and report any adherence challenges, side effects, or symptom patterns. The pulmonary team can adjust the regimen, simplify it (fewer devices, once-daily options), and provide technique education. Do not independently stop, start, or change inhaled medications; report and coordinate.

## Common Traps

### Assuming The Inhaler Is Working Because The Person Takes It

The caregiver counts the doses as given and assumes the medication reaches the lungs. The mechanism is equating taking with delivering. The false signal is that adherence equals effectiveness. The harm is that incorrect technique delivers little or no medication, the person has poor control, and the team escalates medications unnecessarily. Observe technique at every visit.

### Confusing Controller And Rescue Medications

The caregiver or person uses the rescue inhaler daily for control and skips the controller when well. The mechanism is not understanding the functional difference. The false signal is that the rescue inhaler that brings relief is the important one. The harm is that the controller is skipped, inflammation builds, control worsens, and the person relies more and more on the rescue inhaler. Teach the distinction clearly.

### Not Using A Spacer With A Metered-Dose Inhaler

The caregiver or person uses the inhaler directly without a spacer. The mechanism is treating the spacer as optional. The false signal is that the inhaler works on its own. The harm is that most of the medication lands in the mouth and throat, the lungs get little, and thrush and hoarseness are more common. Use a spacer.

### Escalating Medication Before Checking Technique And Adherence

The team adds a third or fourth inhaler for poor control. The mechanism is treating poor control as severe disease. The false signal is that more medications are needed. The harm is that the real cause (technique, adherence) goes unaddressed, the regimen gets complex and hard to follow, and side effects increase. Check technique and adherence first.

### Stopping An Inhaled Steroid Because Of Side Effects Or Feeling Well

The person stops the inhaled steroid because of hoarseness or because they feel fine. The mechanism is treating the controller as optional. The false signal is that no symptoms means no need. The harm is that inflammation rebuilds over weeks, control worsens, and an exacerbation follows. Report side effects for management and keep the controller daily.

### Using A Dirty Or Failing Nebulizer

The caregiver uses the nebulizer without cleaning or replacing parts. The mechanism is treating the equipment as maintenance-free. The false signal is that as long as it mists, it works. The harm is that a clogged or contaminated nebulizer delivers less medication and can deliver bacteria to the lungs. Clean, dry, and replace parts on schedule.

## Self-Check

Before treating inhaled respiratory medication technique and adherence as complete, verify:

- Inhaler technique for every device was observed at each visit, compared to device-specific instructions, and corrected gently, with a spacer used for metered-dose inhalers.
- Controller (daily, maintenance) and rescue (as-needed, reliever) medications were distinguished, and the controller was taken daily regardless of symptoms.
- A spacer was used with metered-dose inhalers, and the mouth was rinsed after inhaled steroids.
- Poor control triggered a technique, adherence, and trigger review before any medication escalation.
- Inhaled steroid side effects (thrush, hoarseness) were recognized, managed with spacer and rinsing, and reported rather than leading to discontinuation.
- Nebulizer equipment was maintained (clean, dry, parts replaced), and technique (upright, slow deep breaths, full treatment time) was supported.
- All inhalers and supplies were brought to every appointment, technique was demonstrated, and changes were coordinated with the pulmonary team.
- Did the work stay within scope, supporting technique and adherence while deferring diagnosis, medication changes, and regimen decisions to the pulmonary team?
