# Post-Discharge Medication Reconciliation And Adherence

The transition home from hospital is the single most medication-dangerous moment in a client's care, because the medication list changes at discharge, the home supply reflects the old regimen, and the gap between the two produces duplications, omissions, interactions, and missed high-alert drugs. The judgment problem is that reconciliation is tedious and easy to do superficially, and that the consequences of an unresolved discrepancy are silent until they cause a bleed, a fall, a readmission, or a death. Caregivers tend to trust the discharge list without comparing it to the bottles, to leave old bottles in the cabinet where they cause confusion, to assume the client will take the new regimen correctly, and to discover the discrepancy only at the next crisis. The agent must reconcile rigorously, must physically reorganize the home supply to match the current plan, and must build adherence support rather than assuming it.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile Every Medication, Including Over-The-Counter, Supplements, And As-Needed Drugs

Reconciliation means comparing the current prescribed list against everything the client actually takes, including prescription drugs, over-the-counter medications, vitamins, herbal supplements, and as-needed drugs. Each item must be checked: is it on both lists, is the dose and frequency the same, has anything been added, stopped, or changed, and are there duplications (the same drug under brand and generic names, or two drugs in the same class doing the same job). Over-the-counter and supplement items are routinely omitted from reconciliation and are a frequent hidden cause of interactions (for example, an over-the-counter NSAID stacked with a prescribed anticoagulant causing bleeding). The full picture is everything the client actually takes, not just the prescription list.

### Compare The Discharge List Against The Physical Bottles, Not Just Against Memory

Reconciliation is a physical act. Lay out every bottle, blister pack, and box in the home, and compare each against the discharge list one by one: name, dose, frequency, route, and whether it is to be continued, stopped, or changed. Old bottles from prior regimens, samples from specialists, and recently stopped drugs linger in cabinets and are easily confused with current ones, especially when the names or pills look similar. Do not rely on the client's or family's report of what is being taken, because their report often differs from what the bottles show. The bottles are the truth, and they must be matched to the list.

### Resolve Every Discrepancy With The Provider Or Pharmacist Before Giving The Dose

Discrepancies are the rule, not the exception, at discharge: a drug on the list but not in the home, a drug in the home but not on the list, a dose that differs, a frequency that changed. Each discrepancy must be resolved with the discharging provider, the primary provider, or the pharmacist before the medication is given, because guessing is how errors happen. Pay special attention to high-alert drugs (anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, cardiac drugs), where a discrepancy can cause immediate harm. Document each discrepancy, the resolution, and who clarified it, so the record is complete and the next caregiver inherits a verified plan.

### Physically Reorganize The Home Supply To Match The Current Plan

Once reconciled, reorganize the home supply so that the current regimen is clear and old medications are removed from active circulation. Remove or clearly separate stopped medications so they cannot be taken by mistake; consolidate duplicate bottles; set up a pill organizer or a clearly labeled system that reflects the current schedule; and ensure each current bottle is labeled and identifiable. A cabinet of mixed old and new bottles is a setup for error, especially for clients with cognitive impairment or many prescribers. The physical organization should make the right action easy and the wrong action hard.

### Build Adherence Support Rather Than Assuming The Client Will Follow The Regimen

A reconciled list is useless if the client does not take the medications as prescribed, and post-discharge adherence is notoriously poor. Build support into the daily routine: tie each dose to a habit (breakfast, bedtime), use pill organizers filled from the reconciled supply, set alarms or reminders, use a medication chart the client and family can read, and observe the client taking the dose rather than leaving it for them to take later. Identify and address specific adherence barriers — side effects, cost, complexity, misunderstanding, depression, cognitive impairment — because adherence fails for concrete reasons that can be addressed if they are known.

### Recognize The High-Risk Adherence Failures And Their Consequences

Some adherence failures are far more dangerous than others. Missing or doubling anticoagulants causes bleeding or clotting; missing insulin causes high glucose and doubling causes hypoglycemia; missing antibiotics causes treatment failure and resistance; stopping beta-blockers, steroids, benzodiazepines, thyroid, seizure, or antidepressant medications abruptly causes dangerous rebound and withdrawal; doubling opioids causes overdose. Know which medications on the client's list carry the highest stakes for missed or doubled doses, and apply the closest adherence monitoring to those. A missed dose of a low-stakes drug is different from a missed dose of an anticoagulant or insulin.

### Re-Reconcile After Every Appointment, Discharge, And Prescriber Change

Reconciliation is not a one-time discharge task; it must be repeated after every hospital discharge, emergency visit, specialist appointment, and change of prescriber, because each of these is a moment when the list changes. Each time, compare the new instructions against the home supply, remove stopped drugs, add new ones, and update the organizer and chart. The home supply drifts out of sync with the prescribed list continuously, and periodic re-reconciliation is what keeps it accurate.

## Common Traps

### Reconciling The Prescription List But Missing Over-The-Counter And Supplements

The caregiver checks the prescription drugs and considers reconciliation done, missing that the client also takes over-the-counter pain pills, sleep aids, allergy medications, or supplements. The mechanism is that non-prescription items feel harmless. The false signal is that the prescription list matches. The harm is that over-the-counter NSAIDs, anticholinergics, and supplements interact significantly with prescribed drugs and are a frequent hidden cause of bleeding, confusion, and toxicity. Reconciliation includes everything the client actually takes.

### Trusting The Discharge List Without Laying Out The Physical Bottles

The caregiver reads the discharge list and assumes it matches the home supply, without laying out the bottles. The mechanism is trust in the paperwork. The false signal is that the list looks right. The harm is that old bottles, stopped drugs, and duplicate supplies linger in the cabinet and are taken by mistake, and the discrepancy is invisible until a wrong dose is given. Reconciliation is physical: the bottles must be matched to the list.

### Guessing To Resolve A Discrepancy Rather Than Calling

The caregiver finds a mismatch between the list and the bottles and resolves it by choosing the more likely option. The mechanism is the desire to avoid delay. The false signal is that a reasonable choice was made. The harm is that medication discrepancies at discharge are a leading cause of harm, and a guess can be fatal with high-alert drugs. Every discrepancy is clarified with the provider or pharmacist before the dose is given.

### Leaving Old And Stopped Bottles In The Active Cabinet

The caregiver reconciles the list but leaves the old bottles in place, and the client or another caregiver continues to take the stopped drug. The mechanism is that removal feels unnecessary. The false signal is that the current list is known. The harm is that stopped drugs continue to be taken alongside their replacements, causing duplication, interaction, and overdose, especially when names or pills look similar. Stopped drugs are physically removed or clearly separated.

### Assuming The Client Will Take The New Regimen Correctly

The reconciled list is handed to the client, and adherence is assumed. The mechanism is that the instructions are clear. The false signal is that the client nodded agreement. The harm is that post-discharge adherence is poor for concrete reasons — side effects, complexity, cost, misunderstanding — and without support the client misses, doubles, or abandons doses, leading to treatment failure and readmission. Adherence is supported, not assumed.

### Not Re-Reconciling After Later Visits And Discharges

Reconciliation is done once at the first discharge and never repeated, so subsequent changes drift the home supply out of sync. The mechanism is treating reconciliation as a one-time task. The false signal is that it was done before. The harm is that each later visit and discharge introduces new discrepancies that, unreconciled, cause the same errors as the first discharge. Reconciliation is repeated after every change.

## Self-Check

Before treating post-discharge medication reconciliation and adherence as complete, verify:

- Every medication the client takes, including over-the-counter, supplements, and as-needed drugs, was included in the reconciliation.
- The discharge list was compared against the physical bottles laid out, not just against memory or report.
- Every discrepancy was resolved with the provider or pharmacist before the dose was given, with high-alert drugs given special attention.
- The home supply was physically reorganized so that current medications were clear, stopped drugs were removed or separated, and a labeled system or organizer reflected the current schedule.
- Adherence was supported through routine, organizers, reminders, observation, and barrier-addressing, not assumed.
- The highest-stakes adherence failures (anticoagulants, insulin, antibiotics, abrupt stops of beta-blockers, steroids, benzodiazepines, seizure and antidepressant drugs, opioid doubling) were identified and given the closest monitoring.
- Reconciliation was set up to be repeated after every future appointment, discharge, and prescriber change.
- Did the response stay within scope, deferring medication decisions, dose changes, and clinical judgment to the prescriber and pharmacist?
