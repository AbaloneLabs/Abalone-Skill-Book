# Account Ownership And Authorization Check

Authorization mistakes in support can expose data, transfer control, create billing disputes, or lock out the rightful owner. The person contacting support may be related to the account but not allowed to take the requested action. Agents often equate knowledge of account details with authority. This skill helps the agent decide who can do what before disclosing information or changing account state.

## Core Rules

### Identify the requested action first

Authorization depends on the action: viewing information, changing email, resetting MFA, canceling subscription, receiving invoice, approving refund, deleting data, adding users, changing admin, transferring ownership, or closing an account. A requester may be authorized for one action and not another.

Do not make a general "authorized" decision without tying it to the specific request.

### Verify role, not just identity

Identity verification answers who the requester is. Authorization answers what that person may do. Check owner, admin, billing contact, authorized user, parent or guardian, legal representative, customer success contact, reseller, marketplace buyer, estate executor, or organization delegate according to policy.

Knowing the password, invoice number, or email inbox may not prove authority.

### Respect organizational ownership

For business, school, nonprofit, or team accounts, access and data may belong to the organization rather than the individual requester. Ex-employees, contractors, founders, agency partners, and former admins can create complicated claims. Follow organization verification, admin recovery, domain ownership, contract, or legal paths.

Do not transfer organizational control based on personal familiarity or urgency alone.

### Handle proxy and family requests carefully

A spouse, parent, child, caregiver, assistant, colleague, or friend may have a legitimate reason to help, but privacy and authorization still apply. Determine whether proxy support is allowed, what proof is required, and what information can be shared.

If the account holder cannot act due to disability, death, medical issue, travel, or incarceration, use the designated policy path rather than improvising.

### Limit disclosure during failed authorization

When a requester is not authorized, avoid confirming private account details or explaining exactly what failed. Provide a safe path: ask the account holder to contact support, use admin tools, submit legal documentation, use secure verification, or contact the organization owner.

Do not leak information while explaining why support cannot proceed.

### Beware social engineering

Attackers often use urgency, sympathy, technical knowledge, public pressure, executive names, partial account details, or threats to bypass authorization. Treat these as context, not proof. If the pattern is suspicious, route to security or account takeover procedures.

Do not lower the bar because the requester is convincing.

### Document authorization reasoning

Record the requested action, requester identity status, role evidence, policy applied, information disclosed or withheld, decision, escalation, and customer-facing next path. If the decision is conditional or pending, say so.

Good records prevent later agents from granting access after a denied request without new evidence.

### Recheck authorization after context changes

Authorization can change when admins update roles, ownership is transferred, invoices are paid, legal documents are provided, or an organization confirms a delegate. It can also become invalid when employment ends or compromise is suspected.

Do not rely on stale authorization notes for high-impact actions.

### Treat disputes as ownership conflicts, not speed problems

When two parties claim ownership or authority, support should not choose the most persuasive requester casually. Preserve evidence, freeze high-risk changes where policy allows, route to legal, security, admin recovery, or account ownership review, and communicate only what can safely be shared.

Disputed access often appears as urgent support, but the real problem is authority conflict.

## Common Traps

- Treating identity verification as full authorization.
- Letting knowledge of private details substitute for role proof.
- Allowing a billing contact to make admin or data-deletion decisions without policy support.
- Transferring business account control to a former employee or contractor.
- Sharing account status with family, assistants, or coworkers because they sound helpful; explaining failed authorization in a way that reveals private account facts
- Accepting urgency, public pressure, executive names, or threats as authorization evidence; failing to route suspicious proxy requests to security or account takeover review
- Documenting "authorized" without saying for which action; relying on old authorization notes after role, ownership, employment, or risk status changed
- Resolving competing ownership claims by helping whoever contacted support most recently; continuing account changes during a dispute when policy requires review or temporary restriction

## Self-Check

- Is the authorization decision tied to a specific action such as viewing, changing email, resetting MFA, billing, refund, deletion, admin, transfer, or closure?
- Was requester identity verified separately from role authority?
- Was the requester role checked against owner, admin, billing contact, user, guardian, legal representative, reseller, marketplace buyer, executor, or organization delegate rules?
- For organization accounts, were admin, domain, contract, or legal ownership paths respected?
- Are proxy, family, caregiver, assistant, colleague, and estate requests handled through approved proof and disclosure limits?
- If authorization failed, does the response avoid confirming private account facts?
- Were social-engineering signals considered, including urgency, sympathy, partial details, public pressure, executive names, and threats?
- Is suspicious activity routed to security or account takeover review?
- Are requested action, identity status, role evidence, policy, disclosure, decision, escalation, and next path documented?; was authorization rechecked if role, ownership, employment, evidence, or risk status changed?
- If ownership or authority is disputed, were high-risk changes paused or routed to the correct review path?; does the response avoid revealing one party's private account details to another claimant?
