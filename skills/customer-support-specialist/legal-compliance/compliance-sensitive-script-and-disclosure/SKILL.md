# Compliance Sensitive Script And Disclosure

Some support messages are not free-form writing. They contain required disclosures, regulated wording, warranty boundaries, safety notices, privacy language, consent statements, or limits on professional advice. Agents often improve tone by editing away legally important language, or paste scripts without adapting the case facts. This skill helps the agent preserve required meaning while still communicating clearly.

## Core Rules

### Identify when approved language is required

Required wording may apply to refunds, warranties, recalls, subscriptions, auto-renewals, privacy, data requests, medical or financial information, regulated products, safety notices, consent, claims, promotions, legal complaints, or accessibility rights. Check whether the case requires an approved script, template, macro, disclosure, or legal-reviewed response.

Do not rewrite regulated language from memory.

### Preserve mandatory meaning

When adapting a script, keep required conditions, limitations, rights, disclaimers, deadlines, eligibility criteria, and next paths intact. Improve readability around the required language, but do not remove or soften legally material terms unless an approved alternate version exists.

Plain language is good; changing obligations is not.

### Do not overextend disclaimers

Disclaimers should match the case. Adding broad legalistic language to routine cases can confuse customers and create unnecessary distrust. Conversely, omitting a required disclosure in a regulated case creates risk.

Use the smallest approved compliance language that fits the request.

### Keep facts case-specific and accurate

Even when using a script, update dates, amounts, plan names, product versions, warranty status, region, customer action, and next owner. A legally correct disclosure can still be misleading if the case facts are wrong.

Do not let boilerplate override evidence.

### Avoid unauthorized professional advice

Support agents should not provide legal, medical, tax, financial, insurance, safety engineering, or regulatory advice unless the product and policy explicitly authorize it. Provide general support information, approved disclaimers, and escalation paths to qualified owners.

If a customer asks for professional interpretation, route or suggest qualified advice without pretending to be that advisor.

### Treat consent and opt-out language carefully

Consent, marketing preferences, recording notices, tracking, data sharing, auto-renewal, subscription cancellation, and communication opt-outs may require precise wording and process. Do not imply consent exists, opt-out is complete, or cancellation is final unless system state supports it.

Confirm what was actually done and what remains pending.

### Coordinate with policy changes

Compliance scripts can become stale after policy, law, product, region, or pricing changes. If an approved macro appears inconsistent with current facts, do not silently edit it. Escalate for updated guidance or use an approved current source.

Outdated scripts are a hidden compliance defect.

### Document approved-language use

When a compliance-sensitive response is sent, note the script, source, version if available, case-specific facts, and any approved deviation. This helps quality and legal reviewers understand that wording was intentional.

Do not record unnecessary legal analysis in ordinary notes.

### Preserve required disclosures across format changes

Approved language may need to move between email, chat, phone script, SMS, in-app message, help-center article, or translated response. Changing the format can accidentally remove timing, eligibility, consent, opt-out, warranty, safety, or limitation language. When adapting format, preserve required meaning and confirm whether shorter-channel versions are approved.

Do not compress a regulated disclosure into a casual summary just because the channel is short or conversational.

## Common Traps

- Editing required disclosure language for friendliness and changing its meaning.
- Pasting legalistic language into cases where it is not required.
- Using an old macro after policy, region, product, or law changed.
- Leaving placeholder dates, amounts, product names, or plan details in a script.
- Treating a disclaimer as a substitute for answering the customer's operational question; providing legal, medical, financial, tax, insurance, or safety advice from support
- Saying consent, opt-out, cancellation, or deletion is complete before system state confirms it; improvising alternate warranty, refund, privacy, or safety language
- Failing to escalate when approved language conflicts with the facts; not recording which approved script or deviation was used
- Moving approved wording into chat, SMS, phone notes, or translation and accidentally dropping required conditions; treating short-channel constraints as permission to omit required disclosures

## Self-Check

- Was the case checked for required language involving refund, warranty, recall, subscription, auto-renewal, privacy, data request, medical, financial, regulated product, safety, consent, claims, promotion, legal complaint, or accessibility?
- Is the response based on approved current language rather than memory?
- Are mandatory conditions, limits, rights, deadlines, eligibility criteria, disclaimers, and next paths preserved?
- Is the disclosure no broader or narrower than the case requires?
- Are dates, amounts, plans, products, warranty status, region, customer action, and next owner accurate?
- Does the response avoid unauthorized legal, medical, tax, financial, insurance, safety, or regulatory advice?
- Are consent, opt-out, recording, tracking, data sharing, auto-renewal, and cancellation statements supported by system state?
- If the script seems stale or inconsistent, was it escalated rather than silently rewritten?
- Is any approved deviation documented?; would quality, legal, or compliance review understand why this wording was used?
- If the response changed channel, format, or language, were required disclosures preserved or replaced with an approved short-form version?; was any translation or localization checked for preserving compliance meaning rather than only tone?
