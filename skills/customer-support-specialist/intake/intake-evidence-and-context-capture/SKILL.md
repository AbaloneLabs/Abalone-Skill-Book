# Intake Evidence And Context Capture

Good intake captures enough context for the next action without making the customer do unnecessary work or exposing sensitive data. Weak evidence capture causes repeated questions, bad escalations, wrong refunds, privacy issues, and lost history. This skill helps the agent gather the right facts early while respecting customer effort and safety.

## Core Rules

### Start with the decision the evidence must support

Before asking for information, identify the next decision: troubleshoot, route, verify identity, assess refund, reproduce a bug, confirm entitlement, investigate fraud, escalate an incident, or explain policy. Evidence should support that decision.

Do not collect information just because a macro asks for it.

### Use existing context before asking the customer

Check prior tickets, account notes, order history, subscription status, known issues, safe logs, product version, device or environment records, and prior commitments. Customers lose trust when support asks for information they already provided.

If information is visible but uncertain, ask for confirmation rather than making the customer repeat everything.

### Ask questions in a usable sequence

Group questions by purpose and avoid overwhelming the customer. Start with facts that unblock routing or immediate safety. For technical issues, ask for reproduction steps, error message, timing, environment, expected behavior, actual behavior, and recent changes. For billing, ask for charge date or invoice reference through safe channels.

Do not ask ten unrelated questions when only two determine the next path.

### Avoid unsafe evidence requests

Never request passwords, full card numbers, unnecessary identity documents, secrets, private keys, full access tokens, or sensitive personal data through ordinary support channels. Use approved upload, verification, redaction, or secure-link processes when sensitive evidence is required.

If the customer sends sensitive data unprompted, follow privacy handling rules and avoid quoting it back.

### Capture customer impact and urgency

Record what the issue prevents, who is affected, deadline, financial harm, safety concern, business impact, accessibility impact, and prior support effort. Impact shapes priority and escalation more than raw category.

If the customer does not state impact, infer cautiously and ask if the answer changes handling.

### Preserve prior commitments and customer history

Look for promises already made: refund, callback, bug follow-up, replacement, manual workaround, escalation, deadline, or exception. Record whether they are verified, unverified, fulfilled, missed, or need review.

Ignoring prior commitments makes support seem inconsistent even when the current agent is technically correct.

### Document evidence for the next owner

Notes should include confirmed facts, customer claims, evidence links, missing information, actions tried, policy consulted, risk flags, and next decision needed. Distinguish facts from assumptions.

The next owner should not need to reread the entire conversation to understand the case.

### Keep evidence minimal and relevant

More evidence is not always better. Excessive screenshots, logs, transcripts, and personal data increase privacy risk and review burden. Collect the least amount needed for the decision and retain it according to policy.

If evidence is not needed, do not ask for it.

### Make evidence usable across channels and time

Evidence often arrives fragmented across email, chat, phone notes, community posts, social messages, screenshots, attachments, and internal logs. The agent should consolidate the decision-relevant facts into the case record instead of assuming the next owner will find every fragment. Where the source matters, link or cite it in the note.

Preserve time context carefully. For incidents, billing, fulfillment, access, and fraud, the exact time zone, event time, charge time, delivery window, or lockout moment can change the interpretation. Avoid vague notes such as "recently" or "yesterday" when a concrete timestamp is available or needed.

## Common Traps

- Asking for a standard list of information before deciding what the case needs.
- Making the customer repeat data already in the record.
- Overwhelming the customer with many questions at once.
- Asking for passwords, full payment data, secrets, tokens, or unnecessary documents.
- Quoting sensitive customer-provided information back into the conversation; recording symptoms without customer impact and urgency
- Missing prior promises made by another agent or channel; blending customer claims, confirmed facts, and agent assumptions in the notes
- Creating escalation notes that force the next owner to reread everything; collecting excessive evidence that increases privacy risk without improving the decision
- Leaving evidence scattered across channels without a concise case record for the next owner; recording relative time references that become ambiguous after handoff or across time zones

## Self-Check

- Is the evidence tied to a specific next decision such as troubleshooting, routing, verification, refund, bug reproduction, entitlement, fraud, incident, or policy?
- Were prior tickets, account notes, order history, subscription, known issues, safe logs, product version, environment, and prior commitments checked first?
- Are questions grouped, minimal, and sequenced by routing, safety, and decision value?
- Are technical questions limited to relevant reproduction steps, error, timing, environment, expected behavior, actual behavior, and recent changes?
- Are billing or identity details requested only through safe channels?
- Are passwords, full card numbers, documents, secrets, private keys, tokens, and unnecessary sensitive data avoided?
- Is customer impact captured for affected users, deadline, financial harm, safety, business, accessibility, and prior effort?
- Are prior promises documented as verified, unverified, fulfilled, missed, or needing review?
- Do notes separate confirmed facts, customer claims, assumptions, evidence links, missing information, actions tried, policies, risk flags, and next decision?; is evidence collection minimal, relevant, and compliant with retention and privacy expectations?
- Are relevant facts consolidated from email, chat, phone notes, social, community, attachments, and logs so the next owner does not hunt through every channel?; are important times recorded with enough precision, including time zone where it affects incidents, billing, fulfillment, access, or fraud interpretation?
