# Entitlement And Support Scope Check

Support scope decisions shape trust. A customer may be asking for help they are entitled to, help that belongs to a different team, or help the company cannot safely provide. Agents often either over-help beyond authority or refuse too quickly. This skill helps the agent verify entitlement and boundaries without making false promises, exposing private data, or abandoning the customer.

## Core Rules

### Verify entitlement before granting or refusing support

Check account status, plan, contract, support tier, warranty, order history, subscription state, region, product version, purchase channel, service status, and prior commitments. Do not rely only on the customer's statement or a single visible flag if the case affects money, access, warranty, safety, or enterprise commitments.

If entitlement data conflicts, explain internally what is uncertain and route to the owner who can decide.

### Distinguish support scope from ownership

A request can be in scope but owned by another team, or out of support scope but still deserving of a safe referral. For example, billing may own refunds, engineering may own defects, legal may own subpoenas, customer success may own account strategy, and warranty operations may own repairs.

Do not tell the customer "we cannot help" when the correct answer is "this needs the right team."

### Identify policy and contract boundaries

Support boundaries may come from policy, plan terms, warranty, region, compliance, data privacy, security, product lifecycle, or professional advice limits. The agent should explain the boundary in plain language and avoid improvising exceptions.

When legal, medical, financial, safety, or regulated advice is requested, keep the response conservative and route to qualified owners where available.

### Avoid creating unsupported obligations

Promising manual work, special handling, free service, reimbursement, custom troubleshooting, engineering review, or expedited replacement can create expectations the company cannot meet. Before offering an exception, confirm authority, criteria, expiration, and impact on other teams.

If an exception is approved, document who approved it and what exactly was promised.

### Offer the next best path

Even when the request is out of scope, provide a constructive next step when safe: relevant help article, paid support option, sales path, account owner, repair channel, community forum, product feedback route, or general guidance. The goal is not to over-serve; it is to avoid dead-end refusal.

Do not direct customers to unsafe, unofficial, or privacy-risky routes.

### Treat legacy, trial, and third-party cases carefully

Customers may have trial accounts, expired subscriptions, reseller purchases, marketplace orders, older product versions, unsupported integrations, or secondhand devices. These cases often create confusing entitlement. Check who owns service obligations and what evidence is needed.

Do not assume that lack of direct purchase record means the customer has no rights.

### Keep scope explanation neutral and respectful

Customers may experience scope limits as unfair. Explain the boundary without blaming the customer, quoting dense policy, or sounding punitive. A strong response states what can be done, what cannot be done, why, and what path remains.

Avoid saying "policy says no" without explaining the operational reason enough for the customer to understand.

### Update records for future support

Record entitlement checks, plan status, boundary explanation, referrals, exceptions, approvals, and customer commitments. Future agents need to understand why the case was handled a certain way.

Bad records lead to inconsistent answers and repeated customer effort.

### Revisit scope when facts change

Entitlement is not always a one-time decision. A payment can clear, an order can be found under another email, a warranty proof can arrive, a contract clause can be clarified, or a previously unsupported issue can become part of a known incident. The agent should keep scope decisions conditional when evidence is incomplete and should not let an early "not eligible" label become permanent without review.

When new evidence changes scope, correct the record and explain the change plainly. Customers should not have to argue against stale internal notes. If scope remains denied after new evidence, acknowledge the evidence and explain why it does not change the decision.

## Common Traps

- Refusing support based on one incomplete account flag.
- Providing work that exceeds authority because the customer seems sympathetic.
- Confusing out-of-scope with owned-by-another-team.
- Explaining boundaries with internal policy language the customer cannot use.
- Creating exceptions without approval, criteria, expiration, and documentation; assuming reseller, marketplace, trial, legacy, or secondhand customers have no entitlement
- Providing professional, legal, medical, financial, or safety advice outside support scope; sending customers to unofficial or insecure workarounds
- Failing to document scope decisions, referrals, and promises; treating scope checks as gatekeeping instead of expectation clarity
- Letting an early denial persist after new proof, payment, contract, or order evidence appears; ignoring evidence because it arrives through a different identity, channel, reseller, or account owner

## Self-Check

- Were account status, plan, contract, support tier, warranty, order history, subscription, region, product version, channel, service status, and prior commitments checked where relevant?
- Are conflicting entitlement signals labeled and routed to a decision owner?
- Is the issue out of scope, in scope, or in scope but owned by another team?
- Are policy, contract, region, lifecycle, compliance, privacy, and professional-advice boundaries respected?
- Are manual work, special handling, free service, reimbursement, engineering review, and expedited actions within authority?
- If an exception is offered, is approval, scope, criteria, expiration, and promise documented?
- Is the customer given a safe next path when support cannot perform the request?
- Are legacy, trial, reseller, marketplace, unsupported integration, and secondhand cases handled with evidence instead of assumption?
- Is the scope explanation plain, respectful, and specific about what can and cannot be done?; are entitlement checks, referrals, approvals, exceptions, and customer commitments recorded for future support?
- Is the entitlement decision labeled as final only when evidence is complete enough to support that confidence?; if new evidence appeared, was the scope decision, record, and customer explanation updated rather than relying on stale notes?
