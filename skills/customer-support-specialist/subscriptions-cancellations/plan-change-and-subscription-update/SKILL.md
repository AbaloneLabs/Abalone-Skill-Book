# Plan Change And Subscription Update

Subscription changes look simple in the UI but can affect billing, access, data, contract terms, support tier, discounts, integrations, and team members. Agents often focus on the requested button click and miss proration, loss of features, authority, or renewal effects. This skill helps the agent make subscription updates with clear consent and accurate operational impact.

## Core Rules

### Define the requested change precisely

Clarify whether the customer wants upgrade, downgrade, pause, resume, billing-cycle change, seat change, add-on removal, owner transfer, plan migration, region change, trial conversion, or contract renewal update. Each has different timing and risk.

Do not describe multiple subscription actions with vague language like "change my plan."

### Verify authority and affected scope

Check whether the requester can alter billing, seats, owner, plan, add-ons, contracts, or organization-wide access. In team and enterprise accounts, a change may affect other users, integrations, data retention, support tier, or contractual obligations.

Do not let a non-admin or unauthorized user make a change that affects others.

### Explain billing effects before action

Plan changes may create immediate charges, credits, proration, next-cycle changes, tax differences, coupon loss, annual commitment effects, unpaid invoice interactions, or marketplace billing constraints. Explain what will happen now and later.

Do not surprise customers with charges or assume proration works the way they expect.

### Explain access and data effects

Downgrades, pauses, and add-on removals may disable features, reduce limits, remove seats, restrict exports, affect integrations, alter support tier, or change data retention. Upgrades may unlock features but require setup, admin action, or migration.

Feature access is part of the decision, not an afterthought.

### Preserve discounts, trials, and contract terms deliberately

Changing plans can end trials, remove legacy pricing, invalidate discounts, restart commitments, or conflict with enterprise terms. Check before action and state if the change is irreversible or requires approval.

Do not trade a customer's legacy benefit away by accident.

### Handle marketplace, reseller, and enterprise ownership

Some subscriptions must be changed through app stores, marketplaces, resellers, procurement, or account teams. Support may not control billing or plan state directly. Route to the right owner and explain what can be done through support.

Do not promise a direct update when another merchant or contract owner controls it.

### Confirm consent and timing

Before applying changes with financial or access impact, confirm the effective date, amount, affected users, features lost or gained, and whether the change is reversible. For low-risk self-service guidance, still ensure the customer understands the final step.

Consent should be specific, not assumed from a vague request.

### Document the before and after state

Record previous plan, new plan, effective date, billing impact, access impact, authority checked, customer consent, confirmation sent, and follow-up. If the change fails, record error, owner, and next path.

Future agents need a clear subscription history.

### Validate dependent settings and downstream systems

Plan changes can leave dependent state behind: old add-ons, seat limits, billing cycle, usage meters, entitlements, invoices, tax profile, renewal date, marketplace subscription, or integration permissions. After the change, verify the downstream state that matters for the customer's request.

Do not assume the billing plan, product entitlement, and invoice state updated at the same time.

## Common Traps

- Treating upgrade, downgrade, pause, resume, owner transfer, and billing-cycle change as equivalent.
- Letting an unauthorized user affect organization billing or access.
- Failing to explain immediate charge, credit, proration, tax, and next-cycle effects.
- Overlooking lost features, limits, integrations, exports, data retention, or support tier.
- Ending a trial, coupon, legacy price, or contract benefit without warning; promising changes controlled by marketplace, reseller, procurement, or account team
- Applying changes before customer consent is specific; saying a change is reversible without checking
- Failing to record before and after state; treating a failed plan change as completed because instructions were sent
- Forgetting old add-ons, usage meters, seats, renewal date, or entitlement flags after a plan change; assuming billing, product access, and marketplace state synchronize immediately

## Self-Check

- Is the exact change identified: upgrade, downgrade, pause, resume, cycle, seats, add-on, owner, migration, region, trial, or contract?
- Is the requester authorized for the affected billing, plan, seats, owner, contract, and organization scope?
- Are immediate charges, credits, proration, tax, invoice, coupon, unpaid balance, and next-cycle effects explained?
- Are access, data, feature, limit, integration, export, support tier, and retention effects explained?
- Are trial, legacy price, discount, annual commitment, and enterprise terms protected or clearly disclosed?
- Are marketplace, reseller, app-store, procurement, and account-team ownership constraints checked?
- Is consent specific to effective date, amount, affected users, features, and reversibility?
- Is the result confirmed to the customer?
- Are previous plan, new plan, timing, billing, access, authority, consent, confirmation, and follow-up documented?; if the change failed or is pending, is owner and next path clear?
- Were add-ons, seats, usage meters, entitlements, invoices, tax profile, renewal date, marketplace state, and integrations checked after the change where relevant?; if billing and product access are out of sync, is the customer told what is pending and who owns correction?
