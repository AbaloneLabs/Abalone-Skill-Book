# Launch Support Readiness

Launch support readiness is not a last-minute macro. A launch changes what customers can do, what they expect, what can fail, and what support must know before volume arrives. Weak readiness causes inconsistent answers, missed incidents, bad promises, and avoidable customer frustration. This skill helps the agent prepare support to handle launches with enough context, routing, and feedback loops.

## Core Rules

### Define what is launching and for whom

Clarify product, feature, region, plan, device, segment, rollout percentage, beta status, eligibility, exclusions, and whether the launch is public, invite-only, staged, or internal. Support needs the same scope boundaries customers will experience.

Do not write launch guidance as if every customer sees the same thing on day one.

### Map expected customer questions

Prepare for access, eligibility, pricing, migration, compatibility, permissions, data effects, setup, billing, limits, cancellation, downgrade, integrations, localization, and troubleshooting. Include questions from customers who are excluded or who expected different behavior.

Launch readiness should cover confusion, not only promotion.

### Identify known risks and guardrails

Ask product what may break, what is not supported, which workflows are risky, what rollback would look like, what monitoring exists, and which customer actions should be avoided if issues appear. Turn that into support-facing guardrails.

Agents need to know when not to recommend a workaround.

### Prepare routing and escalation

Define owners for bugs, billing issues, eligibility disputes, privacy questions, legal concerns, accessibility barriers, VIP accounts, social complaints, and launch incidents. Include evidence needed for escalation and expected response time.

If support does not know where to send launch issues, customers become the routing system.

### Align customer-facing language

Macros, help center, bot flows, release notes, status page, sales, customer success, community, and social responses should agree. If launch marketing uses broad claims, support needs approved language for limitations and exclusions.

Do not let agents soften overpromises with improvised explanations.

### Train on edge cases

Use realistic scenarios: customer not eligible, missing feature flag, failed migration, old plan conflict, accessibility blocker, integration break, pricing surprise, beta bug, region restriction, and customer demanding roadmap commitment.

Agents should practice saying no, escalating, and explaining uncertainty.

### Establish launch monitoring

Track ticket volume, tags, issue patterns, sentiment, social posts, feature adoption blockers, repeat contacts, refunds, cancellations, escalations, QA defects, and incident signals. Compare against expected launch behavior.

The first support contacts are part of launch telemetry.

### Define feedback cadence

Set a cadence for support to report patterns back to product during the launch window. Include who attends, what data is reviewed, how decisions are logged, and how updated guidance reaches agents across shifts.

Readiness continues after launch day.

### Prepare excluded-customer communication and validate support operations capacity

Launches create disappointment for customers who are not eligible, not migrated yet, on the wrong plan, in the wrong region, using unsupported devices, or waiting for enterprise approval. Prepare clear language for why they do not have access and what options exist.

Do not let agents improvise fairness explanations under pressure. Excluded customers often create complaints, social posts, cancellation threats, and sales escalations.

Readiness includes staffing, queue routing, chat and phone scripts, escalation coverage, outsourced-team briefings, language coverage, weekend support, and after-hours incident paths. A launch can fail in support even when the product works.

If the expected volume exceeds capacity, prepare triage rules and customer expectation language before launch.

## Common Traps

- Preparing support for the marketed feature but not the eligibility and exclusion rules.
- Assuming all regions, plans, devices, and rollout cohorts see the same behavior.
- Giving agents only release notes and no failure modes.
- Omitting escalation paths for billing, privacy, accessibility, legal, VIP, and incident issues.
- Letting sales, marketing, help center, and support language diverge; training only happy-path setup
- Forgetting customers who are not eligible or who lose an old workflow; treating early support contacts as noise instead of launch telemetry
- Updating launch guidance in one channel while outsourced or regional teams keep old answers; closing readiness when the launch ships rather than when issue patterns stabilize
- Forgetting customers excluded by plan, region, cohort, device, or enterprise approval; launching without checking support staffing, routing, language coverage, weekend coverage, or after-hours escalation

## Self-Check

- Are product, feature, region, plan, device, segment, rollout, beta, eligibility, and exclusions defined?
- Are expected questions mapped across access, pricing, migration, setup, compatibility, billing, limits, cancellation, integrations, localization, and troubleshooting?
- Are known risks, unsupported workflows, monitoring, rollback, and unsafe workaround boundaries documented?
- Are routing owners and evidence requirements clear for bugs, billing, privacy, legal, accessibility, VIP, social, and incidents?
- Do macros, help center, bots, release notes, sales, success, community, social, and status language align?
- Are edge cases practiced, including ineligible customers, failed migration, pricing surprise, beta bug, and region restriction?
- Are launch support metrics and tags ready?
- Is there a feedback cadence with product and a way to update guidance across shifts?
- Are agents prepared to say no or escalate without overpromising?; will readiness continue through the launch stabilization period?
- Is excluded-customer language prepared with options and safe expectation-setting?; is support capacity, routing, coverage, outsourced-team readiness, and after-hours escalation validated?
