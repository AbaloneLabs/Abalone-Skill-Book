# Proactive Outreach For Affected Customers

Proactive outreach can reduce harm and build trust, but poor outreach creates confusion, privacy risk, unnecessary panic, or inconsistent promises. Agents may contact too broadly, too narrowly, too late, or with language that confirms more than the company knows. This skill helps the agent decide who should be contacted, what should be said, and how follow-up will be owned.

## Core Rules

### Define the affected population

Identify who is affected by account, product, plan, region, shipment batch, invoice, payment method, release version, feature flag, incident, vendor, or time window. Separate confirmed affected customers from potentially affected customers.

Do not send broad outreach because it is easier than defining scope, and do not under-contact when evidence shows impact.

### Confirm outreach authority and owner

Proactive outreach may require approval from support leadership, product, legal, privacy, security, billing, operations, PR, customer success, or incident command. Decide who owns messaging, list accuracy, delivery, replies, and follow-up actions.

Multiple teams sending separate messages creates confusion.

### State what is known and unknown

Tell customers what happened, what they may experience, what action support has taken, what the customer should do, and what remains under investigation. Avoid overstating cause, scope, liability, fix timing, or compensation.

Honest uncertainty is better than a confident message that later needs correction.

### Protect privacy and confidentiality

Do not reveal that other customers are affected in a way that exposes identities, account relationships, security posture, health, financial, legal, or personal data. Use approved channels and segment messages carefully.

Outreach should not create the next privacy incident.

### Make the customer action clear

If the customer must update payment, stop using a product, check an order, rotate credentials, confirm address, apply workaround, or wait for a fix, state that clearly. If no action is needed, say so.

Customers should not have to infer whether they are at risk or expected to act.

### Coordinate remedies and eligibility

If outreach includes refunds, credits, replacements, repairs, recalls, service extensions, or support priority, define eligibility, timing, approval, and how customers claim or receive the remedy. Avoid open-ended language that every recipient can interpret differently.

Remedy promises must be operationally ready before outreach.

### Prepare reply handling

Outreach generates replies, confusion, escalations, complaints, and edge cases. Prepare macros, routing, ownership, self-service links, and escalation criteria before sending. Track who has been contacted and what follow-up remains.

Proactive outreach without response capacity can worsen support load.

### Close the loop after outreach

Track delivery, bounces, customer replies, unresolved cases, remedy completion, and lessons learned. Update knowledge base, incident records, product feedback, and customer records where appropriate.

Outreach is a workflow, not a single message.

### Choose channel and cadence deliberately and plan for customers who were missed

The right channel depends on urgency, sensitivity, customer preference, region, consent, and likelihood of action. Email may be best for detail; SMS or in-app may be needed for urgent action; phone or account-team outreach may be needed for high-impact enterprise cases. Cadence should avoid both silence and noisy repeated alerts.

Do not use a public or broad channel for sensitive issues just because it is faster.

Affected-customer lists are rarely perfect. Prepare a path for customers who did not receive outreach, received it late, use another email, share accounts, bought through a reseller, or were affected indirectly. Support should know how to validate eligibility without forcing customers to prove everything from scratch.

Outreach quality includes the exception path.

## Common Traps

- Contacting customers without a clear affected-population definition.
- Waiting for perfect certainty while harm continues.
- Letting several teams send inconsistent outreach.
- Over-confirming cause, scope, liability, or fix timing.
- Revealing sensitive information about other affected customers; sending an action-required message without a clear action
- Promising compensation before operations can deliver it; failing to prepare reply routing and support capacity
- Not tracking who was contacted and what happened next; treating outreach as done when the message is sent
- Choosing a channel that is fast but unsafe for the sensitivity of the issue; having no path for eligible customers who were missed by the outreach list

## Self-Check

- Is the affected population defined by account, product, plan, region, batch, invoice, payment, version, feature flag, vendor, incident, or time window?
- Are confirmed and potentially affected customers separated?
- Is messaging authority clear across support, product, legal, privacy, security, billing, operations, PR, customer success, or incident command?
- Does the message distinguish known facts, unknowns, customer action, company action, and investigation status?
- Does it avoid unsupported cause, scope, liability, fix timing, and compensation promises?
- Are privacy and confidentiality protected through segmentation and approved channels?
- Is customer action explicit, including when no action is needed?
- Are remedy eligibility, timing, approval, and claim path operationally ready?
- Are reply handling, macros, routing, ownership, capacity, and escalation criteria prepared?; are delivery, replies, unresolved cases, remedy completion, records, and lessons learned tracked?
- Is channel and cadence appropriate for urgency, sensitivity, consent, customer preference, region, and action required?; is there a validation path for missed, late, alternate-email, shared-account, reseller, or indirectly affected customers?
