# Trust Repair After Support Mistake

When support itself causes or worsens the problem, the next response must do more than apologize. It must identify what went wrong, correct the operational state, repair the expectation, and prevent repeat failure. Agents often minimize support mistakes to avoid blame or over-apologize without action. This skill helps the agent rebuild trust with precision and follow-through.

## Core Rules

### Name the support mistake accurately

Identify whether support gave wrong information, missed a promised update, asked repeated questions, lost evidence, closed too early, routed incorrectly, used inappropriate tone, failed verification, mishandled privacy, or made an unauthorized promise. Be specific enough for the customer to know support sees the issue.

Do not hide behind vague language like "sorry for any inconvenience" when a concrete support failure is known.

### Correct the operational consequence

Determine what the mistake caused: wrong charge, delayed shipment, missed refund, lost access, repeated effort, bad escalation, privacy exposure, expired deadline, or customer confusion. Repair the actual consequence where possible.

An apology without correction is incomplete.

### Use ownership language carefully

Take responsibility for support's process failure without over-admitting unverified product fault, legal liability, or compensation entitlement. For example, support can own that it missed an update even if root cause remains under investigation.

Precision protects both trust and policy.

### Reset expectations honestly

If support previously promised something that cannot be delivered, correct the expectation and explain the current path. Do not repeat a false promise to avoid discomfort. If support missed a deadline, give a new checkpoint only if it can be owned.

Trust repair depends on the next promise being reliable.

### Reduce customer effort immediately

Do not ask the customer to repeat the history support already has. Summarize what you reviewed and state what support will do next. If new information is needed, explain why it is necessary despite prior effort.

The customer should feel the burden shifting back to support.

### Consider appropriate recovery

Depending on harm and authority, recovery may include priority review, refund or credit, replacement, fee waiver, callback, escalation, corrected record, data handling action, or manager review. Match recovery to harm and policy.

Do not use compensation to avoid explaining or fixing the mistake.

### Feed the failure back internally

Support mistakes may reveal bad macros, training gaps, unclear policy, broken routing, tool defects, or staffing issues. Record actionable feedback for QA, knowledge, training, product, billing, or operations.

Trust repair for one customer should reduce repeat harm.

### Follow through visibly

After repair actions, confirm what was completed, what remains, and who owns it. If the case stays open, track commitments. If closed, document why the trust repair and operational correction are complete.

The final proof of trust repair is reliable follow-through.

### Decide whether the customer needs explanation or just correction and prevent the customer from paying twice for support's mistake

Some mistakes require a clear explanation so the customer can trust the new path. Others are better repaired with concise correction and minimal internal detail, especially when privacy, security, personnel, or legal constraints apply. Match transparency to the customer's need and safe disclosure boundaries.

Do not over-share internal process details to prove sincerity.

If support caused extra shipping, duplicate payment, missed deadline, wasted troubleshooting, data re-entry, or repeated verification, consider whether those added burdens need remedy or removal. The recovery should not require the customer to absorb avoidable cost created by support.

Where support cannot waive the burden, explain why and route for review if the harm is material.

## Common Traps

- Using generic apology language when a specific support mistake occurred.
- Over-admitting liability or product fault beyond evidence.
- Apologizing without correcting the consequence.
- Repeating an impossible promise because the prior agent said it.
- Asking the customer to restate history after repeated contacts; offering compensation without fixing the underlying support failure
- Hiding internal process issues so no one learns from them; blaming a previous agent or team by name
- Closing after the apology while follow-up remains open; failing to document the mistake, correction, and prevention feedback
- Over-sharing internal details, employee blame, or security process while trying to sound transparent; making the customer pay again in time, money, or effort to correct a support-created problem

## Self-Check

- Is the support mistake specifically identified?
- Are the operational consequences of the mistake understood and corrected or routed?
- Does ownership language acknowledge support failure without unsupported legal, product, or compensation admission?
- Are incorrect prior promises reset honestly?
- Is the next checkpoint reliable and owned?
- Has customer effort been reduced by reviewing prior context before asking more?
- Is recovery matched to harm, authority, and policy?
- Are internal feedback signals routed to QA, knowledge, training, product, billing, or operations?
- Are completed actions, remaining work, owner, and commitments visible to the customer?; are mistake, correction, recovery, prevention feedback, and closure reason documented?
- Is the explanation transparent enough for trust but limited enough for privacy, security, personnel, and legal boundaries?; were extra costs, repeated work, duplicate payment, shipping, verification, or missed deadlines created by support considered for removal or remedy?
