# Failure Remediation And Compensation Plan

Service recovery is not simply giving money back. A good recovery plan identifies the failure, reduces current harm, chooses an authorized remedy, prevents repeat failure, and communicates clearly. Agents often jump to compensation before fixing the underlying issue, or deny remedy because policy is narrow while support caused real harm. This skill helps the agent design recovery that is fair, documented, and operationally complete.

## Core Rules

### Identify the failure and harm

Clarify what failed: product, billing, delivery, support response, escalation, accessibility, security, privacy, policy explanation, or expectation setting. Capture the harm: money, time, access, business interruption, missed deadline, safety risk, emotional strain, or repeated effort.

Remedy should match the harm, not just the customer's volume or visibility.

### Correct the underlying issue first

Before compensation, determine whether account state, invoice, shipment, product access, data, subscription, support note, or escalation path must be corrected. Compensation without correction creates another contact.

If correction is impossible, explain what alternative remedy can and cannot address.

### Match remedy to failure type

Remedies may include correction, refund, credit, replacement, fee waiver, service extension, expedited handling, callback, manual review, documentation, apology, accessibility accommodation, or proactive monitoring. Choose a remedy that addresses the actual failure and customer need.

Do not use one default credit for every service failure.

### Confirm authority and precedent

Check policy, amount limits, approval owner, region, plan, contract, prior remedies, fraud risk, and fairness. Exceptions should have criteria, scope, expiration, and documented approval.

Uncontrolled goodwill becomes inconsistent treatment and future broken promises.

### Separate apology from liability and remedy

Acknowledge customer impact and confirmed support failure without over-admitting unverified cause or legal responsibility. Explain remedy based on evidence and authority. Do not imply compensation is guaranteed because an apology was given.

Careful language can still be humane.

### Consider non-financial recovery

Some customers need a working account, correct documentation, replacement before a deadline, accessible alternative, assurance of process change, or one owner more than money. Ask what outcome matters when not obvious.

Financial compensation is not always the strongest repair.

### Avoid double recovery and abuse exposure

Check previous refunds, credits, replacements, chargebacks, insurance claims, vendor remedies, warranty claims, and service extensions. If fraud or abuse signals exist, route for review without accusing the customer.

Recovery should not create duplicate compensation or reward exploitation.

### Document the recovery plan

Record failure, evidence, harm, remedy, authority, approval, customer message, operational correction, prevention or feedback, follow-up, and final state. If no compensation is granted, document why and what alternative was provided.

The plan should be reviewable and repeatable for similar cases.

### Consider timing and urgency of recovery and coordinate visible and hidden remediation

The same remedy can have different value depending on timing. A replacement after an event, credit after churn, or callback after a missed legal deadline may not repair the harm. Match the recovery timeline to the customer's deadline, business dependency, safety concern, travel, accessibility need, or billing cycle.

If the standard remedy is too slow to matter, escalate for an alternate authorized path rather than pretending it is adequate.

Some recovery work is customer-visible, such as refund or replacement. Other work is internal, such as correcting account notes, fixing entitlement, removing a bad flag, updating a macro, or escalating a bug. Both may be necessary.

Do not close recovery after the visible remedy if hidden state remains wrong and may harm the customer later.

## Common Traps

- Offering compensation before fixing account, billing, delivery, or product state.
- Matching remedy to customer anger rather than actual harm.
- Treating every failure as a refund problem.
- Denying service recovery because standard refund policy is narrow, while support caused separate harm.
- Creating exceptions without authority, scope, or precedent review; apologizing in a way that implies unauthorized liability or remedy
- Ignoring non-financial remedies that better solve the problem; missing duplicate refunds, credits, replacements, chargebacks, or warranty remedies
- Revealing fraud concerns while explaining delayed recovery; failing to document why the remedy was fair and authorized
- Offering a remedy too late to address the customer's actual deadline or dependency; fixing the customer-visible issue while leaving internal account, entitlement, or support records incorrect

## Self-Check

- Is the failure identified across product, billing, delivery, support, escalation, accessibility, security, privacy, policy, or expectation setting?
- Is customer harm captured across money, time, access, business, deadline, safety, emotion, and repeated effort?
- Has the underlying operational issue been corrected or routed before compensation?
- Is the remedy matched to failure type and customer need?
- Are authority, amount, approval, region, plan, contract, prior remedies, fraud risk, and fairness checked?
- Are exception criteria, scope, expiration, and approval documented?
- Does apology acknowledge impact without unsupported legal or causal admission?
- Were non-financial remedies considered?
- Were double recovery and abuse signals checked?; are failure, evidence, harm, remedy, authority, correction, prevention, follow-up, and final state documented?
- Is the remedy timeline meaningful for the customer's deadline, business, safety, travel, accessibility, or billing-cycle need?; are hidden internal corrections such as notes, flags, entitlement, macros, bugs, or routing completed or owned?
