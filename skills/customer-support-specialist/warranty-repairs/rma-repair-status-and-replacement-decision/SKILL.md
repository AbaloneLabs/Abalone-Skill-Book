# RMA Repair Status And Replacement Decision

Repair status cases become frustrating when the customer cannot tell where the product is, who owns the next step, or why replacement is or is not available. Agents may overpromise timelines, misread depot status, or treat inspection decisions as final without explaining options. This skill helps the agent manage RMA status and replacement decisions with evidence, expectation control, and customer continuity.

## Core Rules

### Establish the current RMA state

Identify whether the case is label issued, awaiting shipment, in transit, delivered to depot, awaiting inspection, parts pending, repair in progress, estimate awaiting approval, replacement approved, replacement shipped, returned unrepaired, denied, lost, or closed.

Do not answer from an old status if carrier, depot, and support systems differ.

### Reconcile carrier and repair records

Carrier delivery does not always mean depot intake. Depot receipt does not always mean inspection. Replacement shipment may have a separate tracking ID. Compare systems before telling the customer where the product is.

If records conflict, open the correct investigation path.

### Explain decision criteria

Repair, replacement, refund, paid repair, return unrepaired, or denial may depend on warranty status, inspection findings, parts availability, safety, region, product age, prior repairs, damage type, inventory, and policy.

Customers need to understand the basis, not only the outcome.

### Avoid timeline certainty without confirmation

Repair time depends on transit, intake backlog, inspection, parts, technician availability, quality check, replacement stock, customs, and holidays. Provide approved ranges and next checkpoints.

Do not promise expedite, replacement, or date unless confirmed.

### Manage customer dependency

The product may be essential for work, accessibility, health, safety, school, or business operations. Capture dependency and route for approved loaner, expedite, replacement, or alternative process where available.

Do not invent options, but do not ignore high-impact dependency.

### Handle estimates and approvals clearly

If paid repair or out-of-warranty estimate is required, state amount, currency, tax, approval deadline, what happens if declined, and whether diagnostic or return fees apply. Use approved billing language.

Avoid surprise charges.

### Preserve condition and inspection evidence

Inspection photos, diagnostics, damage notes, serial checks, tamper evidence, and repair logs may be needed for disputes. Do not discard or summarize away evidence when denying coverage.

### Close with delivery and warranty continuity and use proactive status cadence

When a repaired or replacement product ships, explain tracking, signature, warranty continuation, refurbished status if applicable, setup steps, data considerations, and what to do if the issue recurs.

Closure is not complete until the customer knows how to resume use.

Repair customers often become frustrated because they hear nothing between milestones. Set a status cadence for delayed intake, inspection, parts wait, estimate pending, replacement stock, lost shipment investigation, or quality check.

If there is no change, say that and provide the next checkpoint. Silence makes customers assume the product is lost or ignored.

### Handle disputed inspection outcomes and manage inventory substitutions carefully

Customers may dispute damage findings, warranty denial, missing accessories, returned-unrepaired status, or depot photos. Provide the appeal or review path, evidence considered, what new evidence can change the decision, and whether the product can be returned, repaired for a fee, or held pending review.

Do not turn disputes into arguments with the depot.

Replacement may involve same model, equivalent model, refurbished unit, color substitution, regional variant, accessory exclusion, or unavailable stock. Explain substitution rules before shipment when policy requires it.

Do not imply the customer will receive a new or identical product unless that is confirmed.

## Common Traps

- Treating carrier delivered status as depot inspection complete.
- Giving status from one system without reconciling repair and shipment records.
- Saying replacement is unavailable without explaining criteria or alternatives.
- Promising repair completion dates from generic estimates; ignoring high-impact dependency on the product
- Sending paid repair estimates without taxes, deadline, or decline consequences; losing inspection evidence needed for disputes
- Failing to investigate lost inbound or outbound shipments; forgetting refurbished replacement disclosure or warranty continuation rules
- Closing after shipment without setup or recurrence guidance; leaving customers without updates during intake, inspection, parts wait, estimate review, or stock delays
- Treating disputed depot findings as unreviewable without explaining review path or evidence; promising identical, new, or immediate replacement when substitution, refurbished stock, or regional variants may apply

## Self-Check

- Is the current RMA state clearly identified?
- Were carrier, depot, support, and replacement shipment records reconciled?
- Are conflicting records routed for investigation?
- Are repair, replacement, refund, paid repair, return unrepaired, and denial criteria explained?
- Are timelines based on approved ranges and current evidence?
- Is customer dependency assessed for work, accessibility, health, safety, school, or business operations?
- Are loaner, expedite, replacement, or alternative options approved before being offered?
- Are paid estimate amount, currency, tax, deadline, fees, and decline consequences clear?; is inspection and condition evidence preserved for disputes?
- Does closure explain tracking, signature, warranty continuation, refurbished status, setup, data, and recurrence path?; is a proactive status cadence set for delays and no-change periods?
- Are disputed inspection outcomes handled with review path, evidence, options, and customer-safe language?; are replacement substitution, refurbished status, color, model, accessory, stock, and regional variant rules explained before shipment?
