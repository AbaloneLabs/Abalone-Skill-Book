# Warranty Eligibility And Repair Intake

Warranty intake is where customer frustration, policy, evidence, product safety, logistics, and regional rules meet. A casual approval can create cost and fraud risk. A careless denial can be unfair or violate obligations. Agents need enough information to route the case without turning intake into an interrogation. This skill helps the agent evaluate warranty and repair requests with consistency and care.

## Core Rules

### Identify product and warranty basis

Confirm product model, serial number, purchase date, seller, region, warranty term, registration, order ID, and whether the customer bought directly, through reseller, marketplace, retailer, or secondhand channel. Warranty eligibility often depends on purchase path.

Do not apply direct-purchase policy to every product.

### Classify the issue and condition

Determine whether the problem is defect, accidental damage, wear, misuse, consumable, cosmetic issue, missing part, software issue, accessory issue, prior repair, or safety complaint. Ask for photos, symptoms, error codes, and usage context only where needed.

Classification drives repair, replacement, denial, or safety escalation.

### Collect evidence proportionately

Proof of purchase, serial photo, defect photo, video, diagnostics, or return inspection may be needed. Explain why evidence is required and how to provide it securely.

Do not ask for excessive personal data or unsafe testing.

### Check regional and channel terms

Warranty periods, consumer rights, repair obligations, shipping responsibility, return windows, refurbished products, and authorized repair options can vary by region and seller channel. Use approved policy sources.

Escalate legal or regional uncertainty rather than guessing.

### Separate warranty from goodwill

A product may be out of warranty but eligible for paid repair, goodwill, recall, safety review, or replacement exception. Conversely, an in-warranty product may require inspection before approval.

Use the right remedy category and approval path.

### Set repair process expectations

Explain RMA steps, shipping label, packaging, backup or data removal, inspection, estimate, approval, repair time, replacement availability, and what happens if damage is not covered. Include customer responsibilities and deadlines.

For products with stored data, explain privacy and backup guidance.

### Watch for fraud and abuse without accusing

Repeated claims, mismatched serials, altered receipts, inconsistent photos, resale patterns, or suspicious damage may require fraud review. Keep communication neutral and evidence-based.

Do not accuse the customer without review.

### Escalate safety signals and handle counterfeit, resale, and ownership complexity

Overheating, smoke, fire, injury, electrical shock, battery swelling, contamination, sharp edges, or hazardous malfunction require safety or recall routing, not ordinary warranty handling.

Warranty may be affected by counterfeit goods, gray-market imports, unauthorized sellers, secondhand purchases, gifts, missing receipts, refurbished products, rentals, or transferred ownership. These cases need careful policy application and neutral language.

Do not accuse the customer of fraud because the purchase path is complex. Explain what evidence is needed and what remedies may still exist.

### Explain inspection uncertainty and protect customer data and accessories

Intake eligibility is often preliminary. Final coverage may depend on depot inspection, diagnostic result, serial verification, condition, liquid damage, tamper evidence, or parts availability. Make that uncertainty clear before the customer ships the product.

This prevents customers from interpreting intake approval as guaranteed free repair or replacement.

Repair intake should tell customers what to remove, back up, or include: memory cards, SIM cards, personal files, accessories, chargers, cases, locks, passwords, or activation settings. Some items may not be returned if not part of the RMA.

For connected devices, explain reset or data handling guidance using approved language.

## Common Traps

- Applying one warranty rule without checking product, region, and seller channel.
- Treating every product problem as either approved warranty or denied warranty.
- Asking for excessive evidence or unsafe customer tests.
- Ignoring consumer rights or regional repair obligations; confusing warranty, paid repair, goodwill, recall, and safety review
- Promising replacement before inspection or approval; forgetting shipping, packaging, data backup, and inspection expectations
- Accusing fraud based on suspicious evidence before review; routing safety complaints through ordinary repair intake
- Not documenting proof, serial, condition, decision, and customer communication; accusing customers with secondhand, gifted, gray-market, or missing-receipt products instead of applying policy neutrally
- Letting customers believe intake approval guarantees final coverage before inspection; failing to warn customers about backups, personal data, locks, accessories, and items that may not be returned

## Self-Check

- Are model, serial, purchase date, seller, region, warranty term, registration, order, and purchase path verified?
- Is the issue classified as defect, damage, wear, misuse, consumable, cosmetic, missing part, software, accessory, prior repair, or safety complaint?
- Is evidence requested proportionately and securely?
- Are regional and seller-channel warranty rules checked from approved sources?
- Are legal or regional uncertainties escalated?
- Is warranty distinguished from paid repair, goodwill, recall, safety review, and replacement exception?
- Are RMA, shipping, packaging, data backup, inspection, estimate, repair time, replacement, responsibilities, and deadlines explained?
- Are fraud indicators handled neutrally through review?; are safety signals routed outside ordinary warranty intake?
- Are proof, serial, condition, decision, approval, and customer communication documented?; are counterfeit, unauthorized seller, secondhand, gift, refurbished, rental, and ownership-transfer issues handled through approved policy?
