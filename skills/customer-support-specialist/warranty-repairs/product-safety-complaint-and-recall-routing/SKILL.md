# Product Safety Complaint And Recall Routing

Product safety complaints are not ordinary warranty issues. They may involve injury, fire, hazardous malfunction, regulatory reporting, recall obligations, evidence preservation, and customer safety instructions. Agents can cause harm by troubleshooting casually, asking the customer to keep using the product, or routing the case as a standard repair. This skill helps the agent recognize safety signals and route them with urgency and restraint.

## Core Rules

### Treat safety signals as escalation triggers

Escalate reports of injury, burn, smoke, fire, sparking, electrical shock, battery swelling, chemical leak, contamination, sharp breakage, choking hazard, overheating, unexpected motion, structural failure, or near miss. A near miss can still be reportable or important.

Do not wait for proof that the product caused the harm before using the safety route.

### Give only approved immediate guidance

Use approved safety language, such as stop use, unplug if safe, isolate product, keep away from flammable materials, do not ship damaged batteries without instruction, seek emergency help if needed, or preserve product for inspection. Do not invent troubleshooting steps.

Avoid telling the customer to recreate the issue.

### Preserve evidence and chain of custody

Collect product model, serial, purchase source, photos, date, location, circumstances, injury description, property damage, accessories used, environment, prior repairs, and whether the product is still available. Use approved upload and storage channels.

Do not ask for graphic or excessive personal information beyond policy needs.

### Route to safety, legal, quality, or compliance

Safety complaints may need product quality, legal, regulatory, recall, insurance, engineering, or executive handling. Follow the specialized intake path and mark urgency.

Ordinary support should not decide reportability or recall implications.

### Avoid admissions and blame

Acknowledge concern and explain process without stating cause, fault, defect, liability, or recall status unless approved. Customers need care, but premature conclusions can create legal and regulatory problems.

Use neutral wording: "we need our safety team to review this."

### Handle recall and field action questions precisely

If a product is under recall, safety notice, service bulletin, or field action, use approved eligibility and remedy language. Confirm model, serial, region, purchase path, and prior remedy. If not under recall, do not imply safety clearance without review.

Recall communication must be consistent and traceable.

### Protect shipping and handling safety

Damaged batteries, contaminated products, sharp items, or hazardous materials may require special return instructions or may not be safe to ship. Do not issue ordinary return labels until safety guidance allows it.

### Track customer follow-up

Safety cases require clear owner, next contact, evidence status, and customer safety instruction. Do not leave the customer waiting in a normal warranty queue.

### Respect medical and legal boundaries and monitor recall eligibility and prior remedy

If the customer reports injury, illness, property damage, police involvement, insurance claim, attorney contact, or regulator complaint, collect only approved intake facts and route to the proper team. Do not advise on medical treatment, legal rights, insurance coverage, or whether the product caused the injury.

Use emergency language only as approved, such as seeking emergency services when immediate danger exists.

Recall cases may depend on serial range, production batch, region, prior repair, ownership transfer, or whether the customer already received a remedy. Verify these before promising replacement, repair, refund, or field action.

If the customer is outside eligibility but reports a similar hazard, still route the safety complaint for review.

## Common Traps

- Treating overheating, smoke, shock, battery swelling, or injury as standard warranty defect.
- Asking the customer to keep using or reproduce a hazardous issue.
- Giving improvised safety advice outside approved language.
- Issuing ordinary return labels for potentially hazardous products.
- Making statements about cause, fault, defect, liability, or recall status before review; failing to collect model, serial, purchase source, photos, date, circumstances, and product availability
- Asking for unnecessary graphic injury details; letting frontline support decide regulatory reportability
- Saying a product is safe because it is not currently recalled; losing safety follow-up in a standard repair queue
- Asking for or giving medical, legal, insurance, or causation advice outside approved intake; denying safety routing because the serial is outside a current recall range

## Self-Check

- Were injury, burn, smoke, fire, sparking, shock, battery swelling, leak, contamination, breakage, choking, overheating, unexpected motion, structural failure, and near-miss signals checked?
- Was the specialized safety route used before ordinary repair handling?
- Is immediate guidance limited to approved safety language?
- Did the response avoid asking the customer to recreate the hazard?
- Are model, serial, purchase source, photos, date, location, circumstances, injury, property damage, accessories, environment, prior repair, and product availability captured appropriately?
- Are evidence upload, storage, and access handled through approved channels?
- Are safety, legal, quality, compliance, regulatory, recall, insurance, or engineering owners involved where required?
- Does the message avoid admissions of cause, fault, defect, liability, or recall status unless approved?
- Are recall or field action eligibility and remedy language traceable to approved sources?; are return or shipping instructions safe for hazardous materials?
- Are injury, property damage, insurance, attorney, police, regulator, and emergency signals routed without unsupported medical or legal advice?; are serial, batch, region, prior remedy, and ownership checked while still escalating similar hazards outside recall eligibility?
