# Queue Priority And Aging Review

Queue priority determines which customers wait and which risks receive attention. Sorting only by age, customer status, or noise can bury safety, money, access, and legal risk. Sorting only by severity can leave old routine cases to rot. This skill helps the agent balance fairness, urgency, impact, and ownership when reviewing support queues.

## Core Rules

### Prioritize by risk and consequence

Look for cases involving safety, security, privacy, legal, account access, billing harm, fraud, regulated complaints, enterprise SLA, accessibility, missed commitments, and customer deadlines. These may need attention before older low-risk cases.

Do not treat first-in-first-out as fairness when consequences differ.

### Watch aged tickets for hidden blockers

Old tickets may be waiting on customer, internal team, vendor, carrier, engineering, approval, or no one. Review whether the waiting state is accurate and whether a follow-up, escalation, closure, or expectation reset is needed.

An aged ticket is often a failed ownership signal.

### Distinguish customer waiting from support waiting

If support is waiting on the customer, the ticket should show what was requested and when. If the customer is waiting on support, the owner and next update should be clear. Ambiguous waiting states create repeat contacts.

Do not let "pending" become a dumping ground.

### Protect quiet high-impact cases

Some customers do not escalate loudly but still face serious harm: locked accounts, medical delivery, business outage, privacy concern, unpaid refund, or accessibility barrier. Search for impact signals, not only public pressure or repeated replies.

Queue review should counteract noise bias.

### Review priority changes and downgrades

Priority can change as facts change, but downgrades should be supported by evidence. If a case was lowered because it is old, complex, or inconvenient, correct it. If urgency has passed, update the customer expectation.

Priority changes should be auditable.

### Identify unowned escalations

Escalated cases may still lack accepted ownership. Check whether the receiving team acknowledged, requested evidence, rejected the route, or missed the handoff. Reassign or follow up rather than letting the ticket age.

Escalation is not ownership until someone accepts it.

### Balance aging fairness with batch efficiency

Batching similar low-risk tickets can improve throughput, but do not let batching ignore high-risk outliers. Use queue filters that preserve severity, deadlines, and commitments.

Operational efficiency should not flatten risk.

### Document queue decisions and sample for quality, not just speed

When priority is changed, escalated, skipped, batched, or held, document why. Include impact, owner, next step, and customer expectation. This protects fairness and helps later agents understand queue state.

Queue management should leave a trail.

Queue review should include spot checks of recently answered, closed, and merged cases. A queue can look healthy because agents sent fast but shallow replies. Review whether responses resolved the issue, preserved promises, and routed risks correctly.

If fast closure produces reopens or complaints, adjust the queue strategy rather than blaming customers.

### Account for specialist capacity and rebalance when volume shifts

Some cases can only be handled by billing, security, legal, language, technical, accessibility, or enterprise specialists. If specialist capacity is constrained, create a visible triage path, interim updates, and escalation criteria. Do not leave specialist-needed cases mixed into general queues where no one can act.

Priority only matters if the right capability is available.

Queue priority is not static. A billing event, outage, release, policy change, or social complaint can change the risk mix within hours. Revisit filters and staffing assumptions when new patterns appear instead of relying on yesterday's queue rules.

## Common Traps

- Sorting only by oldest ticket and missing severe newer cases.
- Serving loud or public customers before quiet high-risk customers without reason.
- Letting "pending" hide unowned cases.
- Failing to distinguish waiting-on-customer from waiting-on-support; downgrading priority because a case is hard or inconvenient
- Assuming an escalated ticket has an owner; ignoring missed promises when reviewing queue age
- Batching easy tickets while high-impact tickets age; treating queue priority as a metric problem rather than customer-risk problem
- Not documenting why priority changed; reviewing only open backlog and ignoring quality of recent closures
- Letting specialist cases age in general queues without capability or owner; keeping old priority filters after volume source or risk mix changed

## Self-Check

- Are safety, security, privacy, legal, access, billing, fraud, regulated, enterprise, accessibility, missed commitment, and deadline risks surfaced?
- Are aged tickets checked for true blocker and owner?
- Is waiting state clear: customer, support, internal team, vendor, carrier, engineering, approval, or no one?
- Are quiet high-impact cases protected from noise bias?
- Are priority changes and downgrades evidence-based?
- Are unowned escalations identified and corrected?
- Does batching preserve high-risk outliers and commitments?
- Are skipped or held cases still given expectation management where needed?; are impact, owner, next step, and customer expectation documented?
- Would the queue order be defensible if reviewed against risk, fairness, and SLA obligations?; were recently closed, merged, and answered cases sampled for quality, reopens, and missed risk?
- Are specialist capacity constraints visible with triage and interim update paths?; were filters and staffing assumptions revisited after volume source or risk mix changed?
