# Missing Damaged Or Wrong Item Support

Missing, damaged, and wrong-item cases combine customer frustration, warehouse evidence, carrier liability, fraud risk, product safety, and remedy policy. Agents often ask for too much proof, approve remedies before checking ownership, or ignore patterns that affect warehouse quality. This skill helps the agent identify the failure type and choose a fair, documented remedy path.

## Core Rules

### Classify the fulfillment failure precisely

Separate missing item, missing package, partial shipment, wrong SKU, wrong variant, damaged packaging, damaged product, empty box, defective on arrival, quantity discrepancy, temperature damage, or quality concern. The failure type determines evidence, owner, and remedy.

Do not treat every complaint as "lost package" if warehouse or product quality may be responsible.

### Check order and packing evidence

Review order lines, shipment splits, pick/pack records, weight, scan logs, serial or lot numbers, carrier proof, delivery photo, customer photos, prior contacts, inventory adjustments, and known warehouse issues. If evidence conflicts, route rather than forcing a premature remedy.

Avoid making the customer prove facts support can check internally.

### Ask for evidence proportionally

Photos, packaging, labels, damage detail, serial numbers, and delivery context may be necessary, but the request should match the value, risk, and remedy. Give redaction and safety instructions when evidence includes address, minors, private spaces, or hazardous goods.

Do not require burdensome evidence for low-risk cases unless policy requires it.

### Consider safety and product risk

Damaged electronics, chemicals, food, medical items, batteries, children's products, recalled goods, or sharp/broken materials may require stop-use guidance, product safety escalation, disposal instructions, or restricted evidence handling. Do not ask customers to keep using or test unsafe items.

Product safety can be more important than replacement speed.

### Choose remedy based on failure, policy, and customer need

Options include replacement, reshipment, missing-part shipment, refund, partial refund, returnless refund, return label, repair, warranty path, carrier claim, warehouse investigation, or vendor escalation. Consider availability, timing, customer deadline, safety, and fraud risk.

Do not default to refund if replacement solves the customer's actual need, or replacement if the customer no longer needs the item.

### Preserve carrier, warehouse, and vendor ownership

Carrier damage, warehouse mispick, marketplace seller error, vendor defect, dropship issue, or customer address problem require different routing and evidence. Use the correct owner and avoid blaming publicly without evidence.

The customer needs ownership clarity, not internal finger-pointing.

### Watch for abuse patterns without accusing

Repeated missing items, high-value claims, empty-box reports, address patterns, many returnless refunds, inconsistent photos, or multiple accounts may require review. Handle neutrally and route through fraud or operations review.

Do not reveal thresholds or deny solely on suspicion.

### Document evidence and remedy

Record failure type, evidence checked, customer-provided proof, internal records, safety flags, owner, remedy decision, shipment or refund details, and follow-up. Capture product or warehouse feedback where patterns emerge.

Good documentation helps both the customer and operations improve.

### Preserve packaging and inspection instructions

For damage, empty box, wrong item, or quality cases, the packaging may be important evidence. Tell the customer whether to keep box, label, packing slip, damaged item, photos, serial number, or lot code until review is complete. If disposal is necessary for safety, document why and follow approved instructions.

Do not tell customers to discard evidence that warehouse, carrier, vendor, or safety review may need.

## Common Traps

- Treating missing item, lost package, wrong item, damage, and defect as the same case.
- Asking the customer for proof already available in packing or carrier records.
- Demanding excessive photos or documents for low-risk claims.
- Ignoring product safety risks in damaged goods.
- Blaming the carrier, warehouse, vendor, or customer before evidence supports it; sending a replacement when the customer needs refund or deadline relief
- Issuing a refund when replacement or missing-part shipment is the right remedy; missing repeated claim patterns or returnless-refund abuse signals
- Exposing address or private-space details in broad notes; failing to feed warehouse, vendor, or product-quality patterns back to operations
- Telling the customer to throw away packaging or damaged goods before evidence requirements are clear; forgetting serial, lot, batch, or label evidence needed for vendor, safety, or carrier review

## Self-Check

- Is the failure type identified: missing item, package, partial shipment, wrong SKU, variant, damaged packaging, damaged product, empty box, defect, quantity, temperature, or quality?
- Were order lines, shipment splits, pack records, weight, scans, serials, lots, carrier proof, delivery photo, customer photos, prior contacts, inventory, and known issues checked?
- Is customer evidence requested only when necessary and proportional?
- Are privacy and safety instructions provided for photos, labels, addresses, private spaces, hazardous goods, and sensitive details?
- Were product safety risks checked for electronics, chemicals, food, medical items, batteries, children's products, recalls, or sharp/broken materials?
- Is the remedy matched to failure, policy, availability, timing, deadline, safety, and customer need?
- Is the correct owner identified across carrier, warehouse, marketplace seller, vendor, dropshipper, and customer address issue?
- Were abuse patterns checked without accusing or revealing thresholds?
- Are evidence, safety flags, owner, remedy, shipment or refund detail, and follow-up documented?; are operational patterns fed to warehouse, vendor, product, or quality owners where appropriate?
- Were packaging, labels, packing slip, serial, lot, batch, and damaged item preservation instructions given where relevant?; if disposal was advised for safety, is the reason and approved instruction documented?
