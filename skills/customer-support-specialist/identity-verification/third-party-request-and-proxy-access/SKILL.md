# Third Party Request And Proxy Access

Third-party requests create a conflict between helpfulness and privacy. The requester may be genuinely assisting the customer, but support may not be allowed to disclose data or make changes through them. Agents often rely on relationship claims or urgency instead of verified authority. This skill helps the agent handle proxy requests without exposing account information or blocking legitimate delegated support paths.

## Core Rules

### Identify who is asking and for whom

Clarify whether the requester is a family member, caregiver, assistant, coworker, manager, admin, reseller, marketplace seller, legal representative, executor, parent, guardian, agency, customer success contact, or another role. Then identify the account holder, organization, data subject, or billing owner affected.

Different proxy roles have different authority and evidence requirements.

### Tie permission to the requested action

A proxy may be allowed to receive general troubleshooting guidance but not account status, billing history, refund approval, data export, deletion, access reset, ownership transfer, or cancellation. Determine exactly what the requester wants support to do.

Do not treat "helping the customer" as blanket permission.

### Use approved authorization evidence

Authorization may require signed-in customer approval, admin invitation, support PIN, written consent, power of attorney, guardianship document, executor paperwork, reseller agreement, business contract, marketplace workflow, or legal intake. Use the approved path for the relationship and request type.

Do not accept forwarded emails, screenshots, or personal familiarity as proof unless policy permits.

### Share safe general guidance when possible

If the proxy is not authorized for account-specific details, support can often provide general product guidance, self-service links, instructions for the account holder, secure contact path, or explanation of what proof is needed. This keeps the conversation helpful without disclosure.

Avoid saying more than needed about whether an account exists or what its state is.

### Protect vulnerable and sensitive situations

Proxy requests may involve minors, elderly customers, disability, illness, domestic abuse, death, incarceration, workplace conflict, or legal disputes. These require careful routing and may have special privacy, safety, or legal obligations.

Do not assume a family or workplace relationship makes disclosure safe. In some cases, the proxy is the risk.

### Treat disputed authority conservatively

If multiple people claim authority, an ex-employee requests access, a spouse contests ownership, or an agency and client disagree, pause high-risk changes where policy allows and route to ownership, legal, security, or admin review. Keep customer-facing language neutral and do not disclose one party's private facts to another.

Support should not adjudicate conflicts informally.

### Avoid turning proxy support into account takeover

Attackers may pose as assistants, relatives, IT staff, executives, or vendors. Be alert to pressure, partial account details, urgency, refusal to use official channels, requests to change contact methods, or attempts to bypass MFA and ownership checks.

Route suspicious proxy behavior to the appropriate security path.

### Document authority and disclosure limits

Record the requester role, requested action, evidence provided, policy path, what was disclosed or withheld, approved next step, and any escalation. Avoid storing unnecessary legal, medical, or identity documents in normal notes.

Future agents must know whether the proxy is approved, denied, or limited.

### Recheck expiration, revocation, and scope

Proxy authority can expire, be revoked, apply only to one account, or allow only limited actions. A support PIN, consent note, admin role, legal document, reseller agreement, or agency relationship may not authorize every future request. Check effective dates, scope, account match, and revocation status before relying on it.

Do not let an old proxy approval become permanent access by inertia.

## Common Traps

- Assuming a spouse, parent, assistant, manager, or coworker can receive account details.
- Providing billing, access, deletion, or ownership help through a proxy authorized only for general guidance.
- Accepting forwarded messages or screenshots as consent without checking policy.
- Confirming account existence or status while explaining that the proxy is not authorized.
- Ignoring abuse, coercion, elder care, domestic safety, workplace dispute, or minor-related risk; letting the most persistent claimant win an authority dispute
- Treating a reseller, agency, marketplace partner, or vendor as the account owner without evidence; missing social-engineering signals in proxy requests
- Recording sensitive legal or medical documents in broad notes; failing to document exactly what the proxy may and may not do
- Reusing expired, revoked, or single-purpose proxy authority for a new request; treating authorization for one account, product, or action as authorization for all related accounts

## Self-Check

- Is the requester role identified and distinguished from the account holder, organization, data subject, or billing owner?
- Is permission tied to the specific requested action?
- Does the request involve account details, billing, refund, data export, deletion, access reset, ownership transfer, cancellation, or general guidance?
- Was approved authorization evidence used for the relationship and action type?
- If authorization is missing, is safe general guidance offered without account-specific disclosure?
- Are vulnerable contexts considered, including minors, elder care, disability, illness, domestic safety, death, incarceration, workplace conflict, and legal disputes?
- If authority is disputed, are high-risk changes paused or routed rather than decided informally?
- Are social-engineering signals checked, including urgency, partial details, executive pressure, contact-change requests, and refusal to use official paths?
- Are disclosure limits, evidence, policy path, approval, denial, and escalation documented?; are unnecessary legal, medical, identity, and sensitive proxy documents kept out of ordinary notes?
- Was proxy authority checked for expiration, revocation, scope, account match, and action limits?; if prior consent exists, is it valid for this specific request rather than only historical context?
