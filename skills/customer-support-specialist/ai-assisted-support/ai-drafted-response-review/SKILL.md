# AI Drafted Response Review

AI-drafted support messages can speed writing but can also invent facts, flatten customer context, over-apologize, promise remedies, or expose sensitive details. The human agent remains responsible for what is sent. This skill helps the agent review AI output as a draft requiring evidence, policy, and judgment checks, not as a completed answer.

## Core Rules

### Verify every case-specific fact

Check account state, order status, charge amount, plan, policy, prior promise, escalation status, customer name, dates, and any claimed action. AI may infer plausible details from the thread that are not true.

Do not send AI-generated facts unless they are supported by the record or approved source.

### Compare against customer intent

AI drafts often answer the most obvious issue and miss secondary asks, emotional context, deadlines, prior effort, or requested remedy. Compare the draft to the customer's actual message and case history.

The draft should not make the customer feel support ignored key details.

### Remove unsupported commitments

Look for promises about refunds, credits, replacements, timelines, bug fixes, account restoration, escalation outcomes, legal interpretations, policy exceptions, or service recovery. Replace them with controlled next steps unless authority and evidence exist.

AI wording can create obligations even when it sounds soft.

### Check policy and approved language

For billing, privacy, safety, legal, security, warranty, subscriptions, regulated complaints, and data requests, compare the draft with approved policy or scripts. Do not trust AI to paraphrase compliance-sensitive language correctly.

If approved wording is required, use it.

### Protect privacy and sensitive data

AI drafts may quote internal notes, full identifiers, sensitive evidence, payment details, security signals, fraud hints, or another customer's information from context. Remove unnecessary private data and use approved secure channels for sensitive requests.

Do not expose internal reasoning to sound transparent.

### Match tone to the case

Adjust tone for harmed, angry, anxious, repeat-contact, high-value, safety, or routine customers. AI drafts may be overly cheerful, generic, apologetic, or formal. Empathy should be specific and proportionate.

Tone polish cannot compensate for inaccurate substance.

### Preserve uncertainty honestly

If cause, timing, eligibility, or owner is unknown, say so. AI tends to convert uncertainty into confident prose. Label what is known, what is being checked, and what happens next.

Customers can handle uncertainty better than false confidence.

### Review structure and customer action and compare the draft to the full thread, not only the latest message

Make sure the final reply has clear next action, owner, timing, customer task, and closure state. Remove unnecessary explanation. If asking for information, ensure the request is minimal, safe, and not already available.

AI drafts often sound complete while leaving the workflow ambiguous.

AI may overweight the latest customer message and miss earlier attachments, promises, denials, escalation notes, emotional context, or prior agent mistakes. Review the complete thread for conflicts before sending the draft.

If the draft contradicts earlier support guidance, resolve the contradiction explicitly rather than sending a cleaner-sounding answer.

### Preserve human accountability and review AI translation and tone shifts

If AI generated or transformed the draft, the agent should still know what was verified, what was changed, and why. For sensitive cases, document that policy, evidence, and authority were checked. Do not hide behind AI if the answer is challenged.

The final message belongs to support, not the drafting tool.

If AI rewrites or translates a response, check that policy limits, urgency, empathy, refusal, and customer action survived the transformation. Translation can soften denial, strengthen promises, or lose safety nuance.

## Common Traps

- Sending AI-inferred order, billing, account, or policy facts without checking.
- Letting the draft ignore secondary issues or prior support history.
- Keeping promises the agent cannot authorize.
- Paraphrasing compliance-sensitive language inaccurately; exposing private account details, internal notes, security signals, or fraud rationale
- Using cheerful AI tone for harmed or frustrated customers; letting AI over-apologize in a way that implies fault or remedy
- Presenting uncertain cause or timeline as fact; asking for information already in the ticket
- Treating grammar quality as response quality; reviewing only the latest message and missing earlier commitments or contradictions
- Sending an AI draft without knowing which claims were verified or changed; letting AI translation or tone rewriting change policy meaning, urgency, or promise strength

## Self-Check

- Are all case-specific facts verified against record or approved source?
- Does the reply address the customer's actual intent, secondary asks, impact, deadline, and prior effort?
- Are unsupported commitments about money, replacement, access, timeline, fix, escalation, legal outcome, or exception removed?
- Is policy-sensitive wording checked against approved language?
- Are private details, internal notes, security signals, fraud hints, payment data, and third-party information protected?
- Is tone specific and proportionate to customer harm or emotion?
- Are unknown cause, timing, eligibility, and owner stated honestly?
- Is the next action, owner, timing, customer task, and closure state clear?; are information requests minimal, safe, and not already answerable from context?
- Would the agent defend the final message as their own judgment, not AI output?; was the full thread checked for attachments, promises, denials, escalations, prior mistakes, and contradictions?
- For sensitive cases, is there a record that evidence, policy, and authority were reviewed by a human?; if AI translated or rewrote tone, did policy meaning, urgency, empathy, refusal, and customer action remain intact?
