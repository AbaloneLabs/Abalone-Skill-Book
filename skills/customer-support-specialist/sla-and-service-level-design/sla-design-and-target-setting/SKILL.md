# SLA Design And Target Setting

A service level agreement (SLA) is the contract—internal or external—that defines what the support service commits to deliver, and the targets set in the SLA shape the customer's expectation, the team's behavior, and the operation's economics. An SLA that is too aggressive (the sub-minute response target for a low-margin product) creates the unsustainable operation and the inevitable breach; an SLA that is too loose (the 48-hour response for a critical issue) creates the customer dissatisfaction and the churn. The SLA design is the calibration of the commitment to the customer's need, the operation's capability, and the business's economics, and the targets are the levers that drive the team's priorities. Support leaders often set the SLAs by the industry benchmark or the competitor's published target, without the analysis of their own operation's capacity and their own customer's tolerance, and the misaligned SLA produces the operation that cannot meet the commitment or the customer who is not satisfied by the meeting.

The harm this skill prevents is an SLA that the operation cannot meet consistently (the target that produces the chronic breach and the penalty or the trust loss), an SLA that drives the wrong behavior (the response-time target that produces the rushed and the unhelpful first response, the resolution-time target that produces the premature closure), or an SLA that is measured in the way that does not reflect the customer's experience (the clock that pauses, the definition that excludes). The SLA is the most influential design decision in the support operation, because it determines what the team optimizes for, and the wrong target optimizes for the metric at the expense of the outcome.

## Core Rules

### Anchor The SLA To The Customer's Need And The Issue's Urgency

The SLA must be anchored to the customer's need (how quickly does the customer need the response and the resolution for this type of issue?) and the issue's urgency (the severity of the impact, the criticality of the function affected). A critical issue (the service outage, the data loss, the payment failure) requires the aggressive SLA (the minutes for the response, the hours for the resolution), because the customer's operation is affected. A routine issue (the how-to question, the minor configuration) tolerates the less aggressive SLA (the hours for the response, the days for the resolution), because the customer's operation continues.

Segment the SLA by the issue's severity and the customer's tier, not the single SLA for all issues and all customers. The severity-based SLA (the critical, the high, the normal, the low) matches the commitment to the urgency; the tier-based SLA (the enterprise, the business, the standard) matches the commitment to the customer's value and the contract. The single SLA for all issues either over-commits on the routine issues (the wasted capacity) or under-commits on the critical issues (the customer loss).

### Set The Targets Based On The Capacity And The Feasibility

The SLA targets must be set based on the operation's capacity (the staffing, the hours, the channel mix) and the feasibility (the historical performance, the process complexity, the dependency on the other teams), not on the aspiration or the benchmark. A target that exceeds the capacity (the two-hour response target with the staffing that supports the four-hour response) produces the chronic breach; a target that is far below the capacity (the 24-hour response when the operation can deliver the two-hour) leaves the customer's expectation unmet and the competitive position weakened.

Analyze the historical performance (the current response and resolution times, the distribution, the variance) to understand the current capability, and model the capacity (the staffing, the volume forecast, the shrinkage) to understand the feasible target. Set the target that stretches the operation (the improvement from the current performance) but that is achievable with the planned capacity. The target without the capacity plan is the commitment without the resource, and the breach is the inevitable result.

### Define The Measurement Methodology Precisely

The SLA measurement methodology (what starts the clock, what pauses the clock, what stops the clock, what counts as the response, what counts as the resolution) must be defined precisely, because the methodology determines whether the SLA is met and whether the measurement reflects the customer's experience. The ambiguous methodology (the undefined start, the generous pause, the loose definition of the resolution) produces the SLA that is met on paper but not in the customer's experience.

Define the start (the ticket creation, the customer's message, the business hours or the calendar hours), the pause (the customer's pending response, the third-party dependency, the off-hours), the stop (the agent's response, the customer's confirmation of the resolution), and the definitions (the response is the substantive reply, not the auto-acknowledgement; the resolution is the confirmed fix, not the agent's closure). The methodology must be documented, consistent, and aligned with the customer's experience, because the SLA that is met by the methodology but not by the experience erodes the trust.

### Balance The Response Time And The Resolution Quality

The response time SLA (how quickly the customer receives the first response) and the resolution quality (whether the issue is actually resolved) must be balanced, because the over-emphasis on the response time can undermine the resolution quality. The response-time target that is too aggressive drives the agent to send the fast but the unhelpful response (the "we have received your message" without the substance), which meets the SLA but frustrates the customer. The resolution-quality target (the first contact resolution, the customer satisfaction) must complement the response-time target.

Design the SLA to drive the substantive first response (the response that addresses the issue, not merely acknowledges it), and the resolution (the confirmed fix, not the premature closure). The first contact resolution (FCR) and the customer satisfaction (CSAT) are the quality SLAs that balance the speed SLAs, and the balanced scorecard (the speed and the quality) prevents the optimization for the speed at the expense of the quality. The SLA that drives the fast but the unhelpful response is the SLA that harms the customer experience while appearing to meet the commitment.

### Include The Availability And The Coverage Commitments

The SLA includes the availability (the hours and the days the support is available) and the coverage (the channels, the languages, the geographies), not only the response and the resolution times. The availability commitment (the 24/7, the business hours, the extended hours) must match the customer's need and the operation's capacity, and the coverage commitment (the channels and the languages) must match the customer's expectation.

Define the availability and the coverage in the SLA, and manage the customer's expectation for the outside-the-coverage events (the off-hours emergency, the unsupported channel). The customer who expects the 24/7 support and receives the business-hours support is the dissatisfied customer, regardless of the response time within the business hours. The availability and the coverage are the SLA components that shape the customer's expectation, and the mismatch produces the dissatisfaction.

### Review And Revise The SLA Based On The Performance And The Feedback

The SLA is not the static commitment; it must be reviewed and revised based on the actual performance (are we meeting the SLA, and at what cost?) and the customer feedback (is the SLA meeting the customer's need?). The performance review (the SLA attainment rate, the cost per contact, the agent workload) reveals whether the SLA is sustainable; the customer feedback (the CSAT, the complaint themes, the churn reasons) reveals whether the SLA is sufficient.

Revise the SLA when the conditions change (the volume increase, the complexity increase, the customer expectation shift), and communicate the revision to the customers and the team. The SLA that is not revised becomes the outdated commitment (the target that no longer reflects the operation or the customer), and the uncommunicated revision erodes the trust. The SLA review is the periodic discipline that keeps the commitment aligned with the reality.

## Common Traps

### The Benchmark-Based Target

The SLA set by the industry benchmark without the capacity and the feasibility analysis. The trap is the unachievable commitment.

### The Single SLA For All Issues

The same response and resolution target for the critical and the routine issues. The trap is the undifferentiated commitment.

### The Methodology That Does Not Reflect The Experience

The generous pause or the loose definition that meets the SLA on paper but not in the experience. The trap is the gaming.

### The Response Time Over The Quality

The aggressive response target that drives the fast but the unhelpful first response. The trap is the speed optimization.

### The Undefined Availability And Coverage

The customer's expectation mismatched with the actual availability and coverage. The trap is the expectation gap.

### The Static SLA

The commitment not revised for the changing conditions and the feedback. The trap is the outdated target.

### The SLA Without The Capacity Plan

The target set without the staffing and the volume analysis. The trap is the commitment without the resource.

## Self-Check

- Is the SLA anchored to the customer's need and the issue's urgency, segmented by the severity and the tier?
- Are the targets set based on the capacity and the feasibility (the historical performance, the volume forecast, the staffing), not the benchmark or the aspiration?
- Is the measurement methodology defined precisely (the start, the pause, the stop, the definitions), aligned with the customer's experience?
- Is the response time balanced with the resolution quality (the FCR, the CSAT), preventing the fast but the unhelpful response?
- Are the availability and the coverage commitments defined and communicated, matching the customer's expectation?
- Is the SLA reviewed and revised based on the performance and the feedback, with the communication of the changes?
- Is the SLA designed to drive the customer outcome, not merely the metric attainment?
- Is the capacity plan in place to support the SLA, not just the target on the page?
