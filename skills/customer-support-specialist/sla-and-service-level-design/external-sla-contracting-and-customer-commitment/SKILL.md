# External SLA Contracting And Customer Commitment

An external SLA is a contractual commitment, not an operational target, and the distinction changes everything about how it is designed, managed, and breached. An internal SLA that is missed produces the operational review; an external SLA that is missed produces the contractual consequence (the service credit, the penalty, the termination right) and the legal exposure. The external SLA is the commitment the organization makes to the customer in the contract, and the wording of the commitment (the scope, the measurement, the exclusions, the remedies) determines the organization's liability and the customer's recourse. Support and legal teams often treat the external SLA as the marketing term (the "99.9% uptime" and the "4-hour response" presented in the sales), without the rigorous definition of what the commitment actually requires and what happens when it is not met, and the ambiguity produces the disputes and the unexpected liability.

The harm this skill prevents is an external SLA that is over-committed (the promise the operation cannot consistently meet, producing the chronic service credits or the termination risk), an SLA that is ambiguously defined (the measurement and the exclusions unclear, producing the dispute when the breach occurs), or an SLA whose remedies (the service credits, the penalties) are disproportionate to the business value and the failure's impact. The external SLA is the legal and the commercial commitment, and the design must reflect the operational reality, the contractual risk, and the customer relationship.

## Core Rules

### Align The Contractual SLA With The Operational Capability

The external SLA committed in the contract must align with the operational capability, not with the sales aspiration or the competitor's marketing. The contractual commitment that exceeds the operational capability (the 99.99% uptime that the infrastructure supports only at the 99.5%, the one-hour response that the staffing supports only at the four-hour) produces the chronic breach and the contractual consequence. Before the contractual commitment, assess the operational capability (the historical performance, the capacity, the failure modes) and commit to what is achievable.

Build the margin into the contractual commitment (the committed SLA that is slightly less aggressive than the operational target), so the operational variance does not immediately produce the contractual breach. The operational target (the internal goal) and the contractual SLA (the external commitment) are the different numbers, and the contractual SLA must be the commitment the operation can meet even in the adverse conditions, not the target it meets in the ideal conditions.

### Define The SLA Scope And The Exclusions Precisely

The external SLA's scope (what services, what issues, what channels are covered) and the exclusions (what is not covered, what pauses the clock, what is force majeure) must be defined precisely in the contract, because the ambiguity produces the dispute. The scope definition (the "critical issues" defined by the specific severity criteria, the "business hours" defined by the specific timezone and the days) prevents the disagreement about what the SLA applies to. The exclusion definition (the customer-caused delay, the third-party dependency, the scheduled maintenance, the force majeure) prevents the breach that is not the organization's responsibility.

Define the scope and the exclusions in the specific, the objective, and the verifiable terms, not the vague language (the "reasonable efforts," the "material breach"). The customer's expectation (shaped by the sales conversation) and the contract's definition (shaped by the legal wording) must be aligned, because the gap between the expectation and the definition produces the dissatisfaction and the dispute, even when the contract is technically met.

### Structure The Measurement And The Reporting

The external SLA's measurement methodology (how the performance is calculated) and the reporting (how the performance is communicated to the customer) must be structured in the contract, because the customer must be able to verify the commitment is met. The measurement methodology (the start and the stop of the clock, the definition of the response and the resolution, the data source) must be defined and agreed, and the reporting (the frequency, the format, the access to the underlying data) must be specified.

Provide the customer with the transparent and the verifiable reporting (the monthly SLA report, the real-time status page, the access to the ticket data), not the assertion that the SLA was met. The customer who cannot verify the SLA performance assumes the worst, and the assumption erodes the trust. The transparent reporting (even when the SLA is breached, with the honest acknowledgement and the remedy) builds the trust more effectively than the opaque assertion of the compliance.

### Define The Remedies And The Service Credits

The external SLA's remedies (what the customer receives when the SLA is breached) must be defined in the contract, and the most common remedy is the service credit (the percentage of the fee credited back based on the breach's severity). The service credit structure (the breach threshold, the credit percentage, the cap, the accumulation) must be defined precisely, and the structure must be proportionate to the failure's impact and the business value.

Structure the service credits to provide the meaningful remedy without the disproportionate liability: the tiered credits (the small credit for the minor breach, the large credit for the major breach), the cap (the maximum total credits, to limit the liability), and the exclusion of the consequential damages (to prevent the breach from producing the unlimited liability). Balance the customer's recourse (the remedy that makes the breach meaningful) with the organization's protection (the cap and the exclusion that prevent the existential liability). The service credit structure that is too weak (the token credit) provides no real recourse; the structure that is too strong (the uncapped credit) creates the excessive liability.

### Manage The Customer's SLA Expectation In The Sales And The Renewal

The customer's SLA expectation is shaped during the sales (the commitments discussed, the capabilities presented) and confirmed during the renewal (the performance reviewed, the terms renegotiated), and the expectation management is the ongoing discipline. The sales team must sell the SLA that the operation can deliver, not the SLA that closes the deal, because the over-committed SLA produces the breach and the churn. The renewal must review the actual performance and the customer's evolving need, and the renegotiation must align the commitment with the reality.

Coordinate the sales, the support, and the legal teams on the SLA commitment, so the contractual wording, the operational capability, and the customer's expectation are aligned. The sales team that commits the SLA the operation cannot deliver, the legal team that defines the SLA the customer does not understand, and the support team that operates the SLA without the knowledge of the commitment are the misaligned teams that produce the breach and the dispute.

### Handle The Breach And The Remedy Professionally

When the external SLA is breached, the breach handling (the acknowledgement, the remedy, the customer communication) must be professional and the contractual. Acknowledge the breach honestly (the admission that the SLA was not met, with the specific data), apply the remedy (the service credit calculated per the contract, the corrective action), and communicate with the customer (the explanation, the apology, the improvement plan). The breach that is handled professionally (the honest acknowledgement, the prompt remedy, the credible improvement) often preserves the customer relationship; the breach that is hidden, disputed, or poorly remedied destroys the relationship.

Document the breach and the remedy (the calculation, the credit, the communication) for the contractual record and the audit trail. The breach that is not documented produces the dispute (the customer's claim that the credit was insufficient or the breach was larger), and the documentation is the defense. The professional breach handling is the customer relationship management, not merely the contractual compliance.

## Common Traps

### The Over-Committed Contractual SLA

The SLA committed in the contract that the operation cannot consistently meet. The trap is the sales-driven commitment.

### The Ambiguous Scope And Exclusions

The vague language that produces the dispute when the breach occurs. The trap is the imprecise definition.

### The Non-Verifiable Measurement

The SLA performance that the customer cannot verify, breeding the distrust. The trap is the opaque reporting.

### The Disproportionate Remedies

The service credits that are too weak (no recourse) or too strong (excessive liability). The trap is the unbalanced structure.

### The Misaligned Sales, Legal, And Support

The commitment sold, defined, and operated by the teams that do not coordinate. The trap is the siloed commitment.

### The Hidden Or Disputed Breach

The breach that is not acknowledged or is disputed, destroying the relationship. The trap is the defensive breach handling.

### The Operational Target Confused With The Contractual SLA

The internal target treated as the external commitment, with no margin. The trap is the conflation.

## Self-Check

- Is the contractual SLA aligned with the operational capability, with the margin between the operational target and the external commitment?
- Are the scope and the exclusions defined precisely (the severity criteria, the business hours, the customer-caused delay, the force majeure), not the vague language?
- Is the measurement methodology defined and agreed, and the reporting transparent and verifiable (the monthly report, the status page, the data access)?
- Are the remedies (the service credits) structured to be proportionate (the tiered credits, the cap, the consequential damage exclusion)?
- Are the sales, the legal, and the support teams coordinated on the SLA commitment, with the aligned wording, capability, and expectation?
- Is the breach handled professionally (the honest acknowledgement, the prompt remedy, the credible improvement plan), with the documentation?
- Is the customer's SLA expectation managed in the sales and the renewal, with the performance review and the renegotiation?
- Is the external SLA treated as the contractual commitment with the legal and the commercial weight, not the operational target?
