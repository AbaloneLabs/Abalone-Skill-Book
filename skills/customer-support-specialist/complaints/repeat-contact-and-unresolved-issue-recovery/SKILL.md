# Repeat Contact And Unresolved Issue Recovery

Repeat-contact complaints mean the support process itself may have become part of the harm. The customer is no longer only asking about the original product or billing problem; they are reacting to wasted time, broken promises, inconsistent answers, or failed ownership. This skill helps the agent stop the loop, recover trust, and identify the process failure.

## Core Rules

### Reconstruct the support journey

Review all related tickets, channels, promises, responses, escalations, troubleshooting steps, policies cited, remedies offered, and closure reasons. Identify when the customer had to repeat themselves or when support contradicted itself.

Do not ask the customer to recap the whole history before you read it.

### Name the unresolved issue and the support failure

Separate the original issue from the support-process failure. The original issue might be a bug, refund, shipment, account access, or policy decision. The support failure might be delay, bad handoff, missed update, wrong macro, repeated questions, or premature closure.

Both may need action.

### Establish a single owner

Repeat-contact recovery needs visible ownership. Identify who will coordinate the case, what they can decide, which teams are involved, and when the customer will hear back. If ownership must transfer, make the transfer explicit and complete.

Do not create another queue bounce.

### Stop repeating failed actions

List what has already been tried and what changed. Do not repeat troubleshooting, verification, policy explanation, or escalation unless the context has changed or the prior attempt was incomplete. Explain why any repeated step is necessary.

The customer should see that support learned from history.

### Reset expectations honestly

If previous agents promised too much, gave wrong timing, or implied a remedy that is not approved, correct it directly. Apologize for the process failure where supported by facts, then set a realistic path.

Do not preserve false hope to avoid discomfort.

### Consider service recovery

Repeated support failure may justify apology, priority review, goodwill credit, expedited handling, escalation, or coaching feedback. These remedies still require authority and policy fit. Do not use compensation as a substitute for solving the root issue.

Service recovery should address the support harm, not hide the unresolved work.

### Capture systemic signal

Repeat contacts often reveal missing knowledge content, broken macros, routing gaps, product defects, billing edge cases, or training issues. Feed the pattern to the right owner after the individual case is stabilized.

If the same loop keeps happening, the system needs repair.

### Document recovery plan

Record journey summary, unresolved issue, process failure, owner, actions stopped, new plan, commitments, service recovery, systemic feedback, and final outcome. This prevents the next agent from restarting the loop.

The record should make another repeat contact less likely.

### Make the recovery plan different from the failed path and decide when escalation is about process, not difficulty

Recovery requires a concrete change: different owner, better evidence packet, revised policy interpretation, new escalation route, manual correction, senior review, product defect link, or clearer customer instruction. If the new response uses the same macro, queue, and troubleshooting steps, the loop likely continues.

The customer should be able to see what changed in support's approach.

Repeated contact should escalate when the process failed, not merely because the customer is persistent. Escalation may be needed for missed commitments, contradictory answers, ownership gaps, or unresolved cross-team dependencies even if the technical issue is not severe.

Do not wait for customer anger to justify process escalation.

## Common Traps

- Treating the repeat contact as a normal new ticket.
- Making the customer prove the history support already has.
- Solving the original issue but ignoring the support-process failure.
- Apologizing for delay without assigning a single owner.
- Repeating failed steps because the current agent does not read history; keeping an incorrect prior promise alive instead of resetting expectations
- Offering compensation without fixing the issue or documenting authority; escalating again with no better packet than before
- Missing systemic signals in repeated complaints; closing after one update while the recovery plan remains incomplete
- Calling something a recovery plan when it repeats the same owner, macro, or queue path; escalating only after anger increases instead of when process failure becomes visible

## Self-Check

- Were all related tickets, channels, promises, responses, escalations, steps, policies, remedies, and closures reviewed?
- Is the original issue separated from the support-process failure?
- Is there a single owner or explicit accepted handoff?
- Are already-tried steps listed and avoided unless context changed?
- Are prior wrong promises, timing, or expectations corrected honestly?
- Is service recovery considered with authority and policy fit?
- Does the plan solve the root issue rather than only smooth over frustration?
- Are systemic signals routed to knowledge, QA, product, billing, operations, or training owners?
- Are journey, unresolved issue, failure, owner, plan, commitments, recovery, feedback, and outcome documented?; would the next agent be able to continue without restarting the loop?
- Is the new plan materially different from the failed path?; was process escalation considered for missed commitments, contradictions, ownership gaps, and cross-team dependency failure?
