# Chargeback And Unauthorized Transaction Response

Unauthorized transaction and chargeback cases combine customer harm, payment rules, fraud risk, account security, and evidence deadlines. Agents may promise refunds that conflict with a bank dispute, discourage legitimate fraud reporting, or reveal risk details. This skill helps the agent respond with clarity while preserving dispute process integrity and customer protection.

## Core Rules

### Identify the payment path

Determine whether the customer reports an unrecognized charge, unauthorized transaction, duplicate charge, merchant refund request, active chargeback, bank dispute, card fraud claim, marketplace transaction, or processor reversal. These states have different owners and consequences.

Do not treat a bank dispute as a normal refund ticket.

### Protect the customer without prejudging the claim

A customer who reports unauthorized payment may be a victim of fraud, confused by descriptor names, sharing payment with family, seeing an authorization hold, or attempting abuse. Acknowledge concern and route according to evidence and policy without accusing or automatically accepting the claim.

Neutral handling protects both legitimate customers and risk controls.

### Check account and security links

Unauthorized payment may indicate account takeover, stolen payment method, unauthorized user, family misuse, reseller issue, subscription confusion, or marketplace mismatch. Check whether account access, email, MFA, shipping address, order history, and recent changes suggest security risk.

Do not resolve only the payment if the account remains compromised.

### Avoid double recovery

If a chargeback or bank dispute is active, issuing a separate refund, credit, or goodwill remedy may create duplicate recovery or evidence conflict. Check dispute state and follow approved coordination with billing, finance, or fraud before taking action.

Explain that bank and merchant processes may interact without giving legal or card-network advice.

### Preserve dispute evidence

Evidence may include invoice, terms, cancellation record, delivery proof, usage logs, account activity, prior communications, refund decisions, order details, and customer claim. Store evidence safely and meet internal deadlines. Redact unnecessary sensitive data.

Do not omit customer-support context that could explain the dispute fairly.

### Communicate safe next steps

Tell the customer what support can review, what information is needed, whether a secure path is required, and who controls bank dispute timing. If they believe their card is stolen, suggest contacting their bank or card issuer using approved language.

Do not tell customers to cancel a legitimate dispute or promise a bank outcome.

### Restrict risk and dispute details

Do not disclose representment strategy, evidence thresholds, fraud rules, risk scores, internal notes, or exact reasons a dispute is contested or accepted unless approved. Keep customer-facing language factual and limited.

Dispute operations are sensitive and can be abused.

### Document status and ownership

Record dispute state, customer claim, payment references where allowed, account security checks, evidence gathered, actions taken or paused, owner, deadline, and customer-facing message. Avoid storing full payment credentials.

Future agents need to know whether support, fraud, finance, processor, marketplace, or the bank owns the next step.

### Avoid retaliation or service disruption without policy

An active chargeback or unauthorized claim may affect access, subscriptions, shipments, or future purchases, but support should not threaten or punish the customer informally. Any account restriction, collection action, cancellation, or service hold must follow policy and consider fraud, legal, finance, and customer communication requirements.

Do not use service access as leverage to make a customer withdraw a dispute.

## Common Traps

- Treating unauthorized transaction claims as either obviously true or obviously abusive.
- Handling an active chargeback like an ordinary refund request.
- Issuing a refund while a bank dispute is active without checking duplicate recovery risk.
- Ignoring account takeover or unauthorized user signals.
- Telling the customer to withdraw or start a chargeback without approved language; promising timing controlled by card networks, banks, marketplaces, or processors
- Revealing dispute strategy, fraud thresholds, or risk rationale; preserving only merchant evidence and ignoring prior support failures
- Copying full card or bank data into notes; leaving ownership unclear between support, fraud, finance, processor, marketplace, and bank
- Threatening cancellation, collections, or account restrictions without approved policy and owner; pressuring the customer to withdraw a bank dispute by implying support will retaliate

## Self-Check

- Is the payment path identified: unrecognized charge, unauthorized transaction, duplicate, refund request, chargeback, bank dispute, card fraud, marketplace, or processor reversal?
- Does the response acknowledge concern without prejudging truth or abuse?
- Were account takeover, stolen payment, unauthorized user, family use, reseller, subscription confusion, and marketplace mismatch checked?
- Was active dispute or chargeback status checked before refund, credit, or goodwill action?
- Are duplicate recovery and evidence-conflict risks controlled?
- Is dispute evidence preserved, including invoice, terms, cancellation, delivery, usage, activity, communications, refund decisions, order details, and customer claims?
- Does the response give safe next steps without legal, card-network, or bank-outcome promises?
- Are representment strategy, thresholds, fraud rules, risk scores, and internal notes kept restricted?
- Are status, claim, references, security checks, evidence, actions, owner, deadline, and message documented?; is next ownership clear across support, fraud, finance, processor, marketplace, and bank?
- Are service restrictions, collections, cancellations, or purchase holds applied only through approved policy?; does the customer-facing message avoid pressuring the customer to withdraw a dispute?
