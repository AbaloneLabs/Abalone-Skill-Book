# Boundary Setting For Non Supported Requests

Saying no is part of good support. Weak boundaries create unsafe advice, unsupported commitments, endless debates, and unfair exceptions. Harsh boundaries make customers feel dismissed. This skill helps the agent decline or redirect non-supported requests with clarity, dignity, and a useful next path where possible.

## Core Rules

### Identify why the request is unsupported

The request may be outside product scope, require professional advice, bypass policy, violate law or safety, need custom development, depend on a third party, exceed contract, require unavailable data, or conflict with privacy, security, fraud, abuse, or compliance controls.

Naming the reason helps choose the right boundary.

### Separate cannot from will not

"Cannot" may mean technically impossible or no access. "Will not" may mean policy, safety, legal, fairness, or security restriction. "Do not support" may mean the company has not built the service. These need different wording.

Precise language prevents customers from arguing against the wrong issue.

### Provide the supported alternative

Where possible, offer the closest supported path: help article, standard feature, export, formal request, partner contact, professional advisor, feature feedback, appeal, manual review, or approved escalation.

Do not create false hope with alternatives that cannot solve the need.

### Avoid unauthorized professional advice

Customers may ask for legal, tax, medical, financial, security, engineering, HR, or business advice. Explain product behavior and process, but direct them to their qualified advisor for decisions outside support scope.

Do not hide professional advice inside casual wording.

### Hold boundaries under pressure

Customers may escalate, threaten churn, invoke account value, demand managers, or repeat the request. Re-check whether new evidence changes the answer. If not, restate the boundary and next path without arguing endlessly.

Boundaries should be consistent across agents.

### Escalate unsafe or abusive requests

Requests involving illegal activity, evading controls, harassment, fraud, self-harm, threats, data scraping, credential sharing, sanctions bypass, or abuse of others may need trust and safety, security, legal, or supervisor escalation.

Do not provide procedural tips that enable harm.

### Document the boundary

Record request, reason unsupported, policy source, alternatives offered, escalation, customer reaction, and final message. This prevents the customer from receiving a different answer through another channel.

### Keep tone respectful and concise and use final-answer boundaries when needed

Avoid over-explaining, moralizing, or blaming. A clear no with one or two reasons and a supported next step is usually better than a long defensive message.

After the boundary has been explained, alternatives offered, and escalation or review completed, repeated debate may need a final-answer message. State that the decision will not change through further messages unless new relevant evidence is provided.

This protects agents from endless loops while still leaving room for legitimate new information.

### Protect agents and other customers and reopen boundaries only for material change

Non-supported requests sometimes become abusive, discriminatory, threatening, or manipulative. Follow contact-limiting, abuse, safety, or supervisor procedures where appropriate. Do not require agents to absorb unlimited hostility to prove customer care.

Boundary setting should include workplace safety and consistent service for other customers.

A final boundary can be revisited when the customer provides new evidence, a policy changes, an error is found, or an approved escalation owner reopens review. It should not be reopened merely because the customer changes channel or repeats the same argument.

State what kind of new information would matter so the customer has a fair path.

## Common Traps

- Saying "we cannot help" without explaining the support boundary.
- Using vague language that makes a policy restriction sound like a temporary inconvenience.
- Offering an alternative that does not actually address the need.
- Giving legal, tax, medical, financial, security, or business advice to be helpful; changing the answer because the customer is loud, high-value, or persistent
- Arguing after the decision is final; providing steps that enable fraud, abuse, evasion, scraping, or unsafe behavior
- Failing to escalate threats, self-harm, illegal requests, or privacy/security risks; not documenting the boundary, leading to answer shopping
- Using scolding or moral judgment in denials; continuing endless debate after the support boundary and review path are exhausted
- Treating abusive or threatening behavior as something agents must tolerate indefinitely; reopening final boundaries through channel switching rather than material new evidence

## Self-Check

- Is the unsupported reason identified: scope, professional advice, policy, law, safety, custom work, third party, contract, data, privacy, security, fraud, abuse, or compliance?
- Is the wording precise about cannot, will not, and unsupported?
- Is there a supported alternative or safe next path where one exists?
- Does the response avoid unauthorized professional advice?
- Has new evidence been considered before restating a final boundary?
- Are unsafe, abusive, illegal, fraud, evasion, self-harm, threat, scraping, sanction, privacy, and security signals escalated?
- Does the message avoid procedural details that enable harm?
- Is the boundary documented with source, alternatives, escalation, reaction, and final message?; is the tone respectful, concise, and non-moralizing?
- Will another agent have enough context to avoid inconsistent answer shopping?; is a final-answer boundary used when review is complete and no new evidence exists?
- Are agent safety, contact limits, abuse, discrimination, threat, and supervisor procedures considered where needed?; is the boundary reopenable only for material new evidence, policy change, error, or approved review?
