# Exception Request Evaluation

Exception requests are moments where fairness, precedent, risk, and customer care collide. A rigid denial can be unfair when policy did not anticipate the case. A casual exception can create inconsistency, abuse, financial loss, legal risk, or future customer distrust. This skill helps the agent evaluate exceptions with evidence and approval rather than impulse.

## Core Rules

### Define the rule being excepted

State the exact policy, deadline, eligibility condition, plan limit, verification requirement, refund rule, warranty term, support scope, or process requirement the customer wants changed. Do not evaluate an exception against a vague sense of "standard policy."

If the rule is unclear, resolve the policy ambiguity before treating the case as an exception.

### Understand the customer circumstance

Collect the facts that matter: what happened, when, customer action, company action, prior promises, documentation, impact, delay reason, accessibility or hardship factor, incident involvement, billing history, and whether the customer relied on company guidance.

Do not demand irrelevant personal detail.

### Evaluate fairness and precedent

Ask whether similarly situated customers would reasonably receive the same treatment. Consider whether the exception corrects company error, resolves policy ambiguity, addresses hardship, honors a promise, or creates unfair advantage.

A fair exception can be repeatable in principle even if rare.

### Check risk surfaces

Exceptions may affect privacy, security, fraud prevention, legal compliance, tax, finance, warranty, regulated services, abuse enforcement, contractual terms, or customer safety. High-risk exceptions require specialist review.

Do not waive controls that protect other customers or the platform without authority.

### Confirm decision authority

Know who can approve the exception and what evidence is required. Frontline agents may be able to approve small gestures but not policy overrides, legal exceptions, identity bypass, enforcement reversal, or large credits.

If approval is pending, do not imply likely outcome.

### Consider alternatives

If the requested exception is not allowed, consider supported alternatives: appeal, partial credit, payment plan, deadline extension, manual review, goodwill gesture, training, accessible process, or escalation. Alternatives should match the customer's need, not merely soften the denial.

### Document rationale

Record request, rule, facts, evidence, decision, approver, amount or action, customer communication, and precedent note. If denied, record why and what alternatives were offered.

Documentation protects consistency and future agents.

### Communicate with dignity and distinguish appeal, complaint, and exception

Whether approving or denying, explain the decision plainly. Avoid moral judgment, suspicion, or excessive legalism. If the exception is one-time, state boundaries clearly.

An appeal challenges whether the original decision was correct. A complaint challenges the experience or fairness. An exception asks to depart from the rule even if the rule was applied correctly. These paths can overlap, but they require different owners and evidence.

Do not use an exception as a shortcut to avoid reviewing a potentially wrong decision.

### Watch for repeat exception pressure and review equality and protected-class risk

Repeated exception requests may indicate customer abuse, unclear policy, poor self-service, unfair deadlines, inaccessible process, or frontline inconsistency. Track patterns by customer and by issue type.

The goal is not to punish repeat requests automatically, but to understand whether policy needs repair or controls need strengthening.

Exceptions can create discrimination or accessibility risk if similar customers are treated differently based on language fluency, disability, region, age, name, persistence, or social status. Use objective criteria and documented approval.

If hardship, disability, or accessibility is part of the request, route through the appropriate accommodation or vulnerability process rather than casual discretion.

## Common Traps

- Evaluating an exception without naming the exact rule.
- Approving because the customer is loud, high-value, or sympathetic without evidence.
- Denying because "policy says no" when company error or hardship is relevant.
- Requesting sensitive hardship evidence that policy does not require; waiving security, privacy, fraud, legal, or enforcement controls casually
- Letting frontline agents approve beyond authority; imlying approval while review is pending
- Offering alternatives that do not address the customer's actual need; failing to document precedent and rationale
- Writing denials that shame or accuse the customer; treating an appeal or complaint as an exception and skipping decision-quality review
- Ignoring repeated exception requests as either pure abuse or pure hardship without pattern analysis; letting persistence, status, language fluency, or agent sympathy determine who receives exceptions

## Self-Check

- Is the exact policy, deadline, eligibility rule, plan limit, verification, refund, warranty, scope, or process requirement identified?
- Are relevant facts collected without unnecessary sensitive detail?
- Are company error, prior promises, reliance, hardship, accessibility, incident impact, and documentation considered?
- Would similarly situated customers be treated consistently?
- Are privacy, security, fraud, legal, tax, finance, warranty, regulated, abuse, contract, and safety risks checked?
- Is the approver and evidence requirement clear?
- Does pending review language avoid implied approval?
- Are supported alternatives considered if the exception is denied?; is the rationale documented with rule, facts, evidence, decision, approver, action, communication, and precedent?
- Is the customer message dignified and clear?; is the case correctly routed as appeal, complaint, exception, or a combination?
- Are repeat exception patterns assessed for abuse, policy ambiguity, accessibility, process failure, or inconsistent prior handling?; are equality, accessibility, vulnerability, and protected-class risks considered with objective criteria?
