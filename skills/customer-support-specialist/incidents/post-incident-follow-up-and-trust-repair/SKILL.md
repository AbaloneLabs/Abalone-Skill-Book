# Post Incident Follow-Up And Trust Repair

Post-incident support is where customers decide whether the organization is reliable after failure. A short apology may close a ticket, but it may not answer what happened, whether the customer's work recovered, what commitments remain, or whether similar harm will be prevented. This skill helps the agent handle post-incident communication as trust repair, not only incident cleanup.

## Core Rules

### Confirm customer recovery, not just system recovery

The system may be healthy while customer work remains damaged: failed payments, missing exports, duplicate orders, delayed messages, incomplete records, broken automations, unprocessed cancellations, or lost time. Before closing, verify what recovery means for the affected customer segment.

If recovery cannot be confirmed at customer level, say what has been restored and what the customer should check. Provide a path for reporting residual impact.

### Segment the follow-up

Not all affected customers need the same message. Separate customers who had brief degradation, failed transactions, data inconsistency, missed deadlines, premium SLA impact, safety or security concerns, public complaints, or open support promises.

High-impact customers may need account-owner involvement, custom remediation, contractual review, or executive communication. A generic bulk apology can look dismissive when the impact was severe.

### Explain enough without speculation

Customers need a credible explanation, but they do not need unapproved technical detail or uncertain root cause. State what happened in customer-understandable terms, when it happened, what was affected, what was done to restore service, and what remains under review.

If the root cause analysis is not complete, do not pretend it is. Promise a later update only if the organization will actually send one.

### Reconcile commitments and remedies

Review promises made during the incident: updates, manual fixes, refund review, credits, replacements, data restoration, SLA credits, account reviews, or escalation responses. Track which are complete and which still need action.

Remedies should match impact and policy. Do not offer credits or refunds casually during frustration, but do not ignore approved recovery obligations because the outage ended.

### Repair trust through specificity

"We apologize for the inconvenience" is rarely enough. Good trust repair names the customer impact, acknowledges effort or disruption, explains what changed, and gives a clear next step if the customer still sees an issue.

For repeated incidents, acknowledge the pattern. Customers often react more strongly to recurrence than to a single failure.

### Align legal, security, privacy, and account teams

Some incidents require controlled language or formal notice. Data exposure, security concerns, regulated services, safety matters, payment failures, contractual SLA issues, and public company claims may need review before broad follow-up.

Do not turn a support follow-up into an unauthorized admission, legal notice, or security statement.

### Feed learnings back into support

Post-incident follow-up should improve macros, detection, watchlists, status-page templates, QA rubrics, escalation paths, and proactive outreach criteria. Capture what customers asked that the team could not answer during the incident.

The support team often sees the trust damage before engineering sees the operational pattern.

### Handle customers who no longer trust routine answers

Some customers will not be reassured by standard closure language, especially after repeated incidents, missed updates, financial loss, public embarrassment, or operational disruption. Treat that reaction as a support need, not as impatience.

Offer a credible route for unresolved concerns: account review, escalation owner, evidence submission, billing or SLA review, technical confirmation, or a clearer explanation of limitations. If the organization cannot provide more detail, say what cannot be shared and why. Do not keep sending the same macro after the customer has made clear that the issue is trust, not only functionality.

### Decide whether proactive outreach is required

Some affected customers may never open a ticket. If the incident involved failed transactions, data inconsistency, security concern, missed deliveries, account access, safety, contractual SLA, or high-value operations, assess whether proactive notification is required or appropriate. Coordinate with privacy, legal, customer success, and incident leadership where needed.

## Common Traps

- Closing tickets because monitoring shows green while customer work remains unrecovered.
- Sending the same apology to low-impact and high-impact customers.
- Providing a confident root cause before it is approved.
- Promising a postmortem that no one will deliver.
- Forgetting refunds, credits, replacements, SLA reviews, or manual fixes promised during the incident; using vague apology language that does not acknowledge actual customer impact
- Ignoring repeat-incident context; sending security, privacy, legal, or regulated statements without review
- Treating public complaints as solved because a private ticket was updated; reusing closure macros for customers whose main issue is broken trust after repeated or severe impact
- Waiting only for inbound tickets when the incident clearly affected customers who may need proactive outreach; failing to convert incident lessons into better support guidance

## Self-Check

- Does the follow-up distinguish system recovery from customer recovery?
- Are residual impacts such as failed transactions, delayed workflows, lost work, or data inconsistency addressed?
- Are customers segmented by impact severity, SLA, account type, public visibility, and open promises?
- Does the explanation state what happened, when, what was affected, what was restored, and what remains under review?
- Is root-cause language approved and free of speculation?
- Are promised updates, fixes, refunds, credits, replacements, SLA reviews, and escalations tracked to completion?
- Does the message acknowledge the specific disruption, not just inconvenience?
- Are repeat incidents handled with appropriate candor?
- Have legal, security, privacy, compliance, and account teams reviewed where needed?; are incident lessons fed back into support macros, watchlists, QA, escalation paths, and outreach criteria?
- Is there a credible escalation or review path for customers who no longer trust routine closure language?; was proactive outreach considered for silent but materially affected customers?
