# Safety Critical Support Triage

Safety-critical support is not ordinary troubleshooting with a more serious tone. A customer may mention harm, injury, unsafe product behavior, violence, coercion, medical dependency, or emergency conditions while asking a normal support question. Agents can fail by either overstepping into emergency advice or under-reacting because the ticket category looks routine. This skill helps the agent detect safety signals, route urgently, and keep language inside the support role.

## Core Rules

### Look for safety signals in ordinary language

Customers may say a product is smoking, a device shocked them, medicine is unavailable, someone is trapped, a child is involved, a driver is stranded, an abusive person controls the account, a customer cannot access emergency information, or a platform is being used for threats. These may appear inside billing, account, delivery, or technical tickets.

Do not rely only on the selected category or first sentence.

### Separate immediate danger from support inconvenience

Ask whether delay could increase physical harm, medical risk, violence, exposure, unsafe operation, or inability to reach help. Immediate danger requires emergency guidance or escalation according to policy, not normal queue handling. Non-immediate safety concerns may still require specialized review.

Do not treat every high-emotion case as safety-critical, but do not wait for perfect wording when harm is plausible.

### Keep within the support role

Support agents should not provide medical, legal, emergency response, engineering safety, mental health, or law enforcement advice unless approved policy explicitly provides wording. Use approved emergency language, safety disclaimers, and escalation paths.

The agent can direct the customer to emergency services or qualified help without diagnosing, investigating, or instructing risky actions.

### Do not ask the customer to create more risk

Avoid troubleshooting steps that require touching unsafe equipment, reusing a dangerous product, confronting an abuser, driving while distracted, disclosing sensitive location, or delaying emergency help. For suspected safety defects, prioritize stopping use and routing to product safety where approved.

Normal diagnostic curiosity can be harmful in safety cases.

### Capture minimum critical facts

When safe, record what happened, who is affected, location or region where relevant, time, product or service involved, injury or harm status, ongoing risk, vulnerable people, evidence available, and whether emergency help is already contacted. Keep questions minimal if the customer is in danger.

Do not make the customer complete a full support form before safety routing.

### Route to specialized owners quickly

Safety-critical cases may belong to emergency response, product safety, trust and safety, legal, security, abuse operations, medical escalation, field operations, or leadership. Use the approved urgent path and include enough context for immediate review.

Do not leave safety cases in normal backlog, public comments, or broad queues.

### Communicate clearly and calmly

Use short, direct language. If there is immediate danger, direct the customer to local emergency services or approved crisis resources. State what support can do next and what it cannot do. Avoid speculation, blame, or reassurance that the situation is safe.

Safety messages should be understandable under stress.

### Document restrictions and next ownership

Record safety signals, actions taken, escalation owner, customer-facing guidance, evidence preserved, and unresolved risk. Avoid broad sharing of sensitive location, health, minor, abuse, or security details.

Future agents need to know the case is not ordinary support.

### Reassess safety after each new message

Safety state can change quickly. A customer who first reports a past injury may later mention current symptoms, an unsafe household member, a child nearby, or a product still in use. Re-read new messages for escalation changes instead of relying on the first triage decision.

If a case is downgraded from safety-critical, document what changed and what residual support work remains.

## Common Traps

- Missing safety signals because the ticket is categorized as billing, delivery, technical, or account access.
- Treating all emotional urgency as safety-critical without checking actual harm.
- Giving medical, legal, emergency, engineering, or mental-health advice beyond approved language.
- Asking the customer to keep using or test a potentially unsafe product.
- Encouraging confrontation with an abusive person or unsafe actor; collecting a full diagnostic packet before urgent routing
- Sending safety cases through normal queues because ownership is unclear; saying "you should be safe now" without evidence or authority
- Sharing sensitive safety details too broadly; closing the support case after escalation without documenting unresolved risk or ownership
- Leaving the original safety classification unchanged after new messages increase or reduce risk; treating a past incident as closed even when the customer may still be exposed to the hazard

## Self-Check

- Were safety signals checked across physical harm, medical risk, product safety, abuse, violence, self-harm, emergency access, vulnerable people, and unsafe use?
- Was immediate danger separated from non-immediate safety concern?
- Does the response stay within approved support, emergency, safety, legal, medical, and mental-health boundaries?
- Does the agent avoid troubleshooting steps that increase risk?
- Are minimum critical facts captured without burdening a customer in danger?
- Is the case routed to emergency, product safety, trust and safety, legal, security, abuse, medical, field, or leadership owner as appropriate?
- Is customer-facing language short, calm, direct, and free of unsupported reassurance?
- Are emergency services or crisis resources mentioned only according to approved policy and context?
- Are sensitive safety details protected from broad exposure?; are safety signals, actions, owner, guidance, evidence, and unresolved risk documented?
- Were later customer messages reviewed for changes in immediacy, exposure, vulnerable people, or ongoing hazard?; if safety severity changed, is the reason and remaining support work documented?
