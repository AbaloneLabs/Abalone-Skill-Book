# Harmful Content Or Self Harm Escalation

Harmful content and self-harm signals can appear in support tickets about moderation, account access, billing, reviews, messaging, community posts, or product misuse. Agents may over-focus on policy enforcement and miss immediate safety, or overstep into counseling and investigation. This skill helps the agent preserve safety signals, route to trained owners, and respond with careful, non-escalating language.

## Core Rules

### Identify the harm category

Classify the concern at a high level: self-harm, suicide risk, threat of violence, harassment, stalking, sexual exploitation, child safety, hate or targeted abuse, extremist content, illegal activity, doxxing, non-consensual intimate content, scam, coercion, or product-enabled abuse. The category determines urgency and owner.

Do not treat all upsetting content as the same support problem.

### Escalate immediate risk quickly

If there is imminent self-harm, violence, child danger, exploitation, active stalking, or credible threat, use approved crisis, trust and safety, security, legal, or emergency escalation paths. Do not wait for complete evidence if delay could increase harm.

Frontline support should not personally investigate dangerous situations beyond approved intake.

### Use supportive but bounded language

For self-harm or distress, use approved supportive language and crisis-resource guidance where policy provides it. Do not diagnose, debate, shame, give therapy, promise confidentiality, or claim the agent can provide emergency help. For threats or abuse, keep language firm and procedural.

The goal is to avoid escalating harm while routing to qualified response.

### Preserve evidence without spreading harmful material

Capture links, message IDs, screenshots, timestamps, usernames, affected users, report source, and risk description in approved restricted locations. Avoid quoting graphic, exploitative, hateful, or self-harm content broadly. Use summaries where possible and raw evidence only where required.

Evidence preservation should not amplify harmful content.

### Protect reporters and victims

Do not expose the reporter's identity to the reported user unless the process requires it and safeguards exist. Consider retaliation, domestic abuse, workplace conflict, minors, marginalized groups, or vulnerable users. Encourage safe reporting channels rather than public confrontation.

Support should not make the reporter less safe.

### Avoid policy overreach and underreach

Not every unpleasant comment is a safety emergency, but some mild-looking messages are serious when combined with context. Check threat specificity, target, means, timing, pattern, vulnerable person, private information, and prior reports. Route uncertain cases conservatively.

Do not let personal discomfort replace policy criteria.

### Coordinate account, content, and customer support actions

Harm cases may require content removal, account restriction, law enforcement path, customer safety response, refund, identity protection, device or location privacy, or product feedback. Keep ownership clear across trust and safety, security, legal, and support.

Do not resolve only the ticket while leaving harmful behavior active.

### Document final disposition carefully

Record category, evidence, urgency, escalation owner, customer guidance, reporter protection, action taken, restrictions, and unresolved risks in appropriate fields. Avoid unnecessary graphic details or sensitive victim information in broad notes.

Future reviewers need a record that is complete but not needlessly harmful.

### Avoid amplifying or normalizing harmful behavior and keep reporter updates limited but useful

Support responses should not repeat methods, threats, slurs, graphic details, or instructions unless required for restricted review. Do not debate ideology, validate abusive framing, or provide procedural tips that help evade moderation or safety controls.

When explaining policy action to a reporter or affected user, focus on safety, reporting path, and next steps rather than reproducing harmful content.

Reporters often want to know what happened. Share only approved status: report received, under review, action taken if policy allows, or unable to share details for privacy/safety reasons. Avoid revealing moderation thresholds, enforcement details, or another user's private information.

Useful updates can reduce repeat contacts without compromising safety processes.

## Common Traps

- Treating self-harm or violence language as ordinary emotional frustration.
- Personally counseling, diagnosing, or debating a distressed customer.
- Waiting for perfect proof before escalating imminent harm.
- Quoting harmful, graphic, exploitative, or hateful content into broad notes.
- Revealing reporter identity or details that increase retaliation risk; treating all offensive content as equally urgent
- Missing threat specificity, means, target, timing, pattern, or vulnerable-person context; applying moderation policy while ignoring account security, legal, or support needs
- Promising confidentiality or emergency intervention that support cannot guarantee; closing the case without recording action taken and unresolved risk
- Repeating methods, slurs, threats, or graphic details in customer-facing replies or broad notes; revealing enforcement logic or another user's private information while updating a reporter

## Self-Check

- Is the harm category identified: self-harm, suicide, violence, harassment, stalking, exploitation, child safety, hate, extremist, illegal, doxxing, non-consensual intimate content, scam, coercion, or abuse?
- If imminent harm is plausible, was the approved crisis, trust and safety, security, legal, or emergency path used quickly?
- Does language remain supportive or firm without counseling, diagnosing, shaming, debating, or promising unavailable help?
- Are crisis resources used only according to approved policy and context?
- Are links, IDs, screenshots, timestamps, users, source, and risk description preserved in restricted locations?
- Is harmful raw content minimized in broad notes and summaries?
- Are reporter and victim identities protected from unnecessary exposure?
- Were retaliation, domestic abuse, workplace conflict, minors, marginalized groups, and vulnerable users considered?
- Are account, content, legal, security, and support actions coordinated with clear owners?; are final disposition, customer guidance, action taken, restrictions, and unresolved risks documented safely?
- Does the response avoid amplifying harmful methods, threats, slurs, graphic details, or evasion guidance?; are reporter updates limited to approved status and free of private enforcement details?
