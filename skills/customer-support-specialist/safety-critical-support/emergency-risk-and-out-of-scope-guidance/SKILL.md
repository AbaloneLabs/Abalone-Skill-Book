# Emergency Risk And Out Of Scope Guidance

Customers sometimes bring emergencies to support because it is the channel in front of them. Support may not be able to provide emergency response, medical care, legal protection, or real-time rescue, but a careless reply can delay help or imply expertise the agent does not have. This skill helps the agent communicate limits while still acting responsibly.

## Core Rules

### Recognize emergency-risk language

Look for mentions of immediate danger, injury, fire, smoke, electric shock, overdose, medical crisis, violence, stalking, child danger, inability to reach emergency services, missing person, active threat, unsafe driver, trapped person, domestic abuse, or urgent location-based danger. Customers may express this indirectly or inside another complaint.

When in doubt, treat plausible immediate harm as needing safer guidance.

### Direct to appropriate immediate help

If there is imminent danger, use approved language directing the customer to local emergency services or crisis resources. Do not ask them to keep chatting with support instead of seeking help. If the company has an emergency escalation process, use it immediately.

Support should not become a substitute for emergency services.

### State support limits plainly

Explain that support cannot provide emergency, medical, legal, law-enforcement, or real-time safety intervention unless the approved workflow says otherwise. Keep the limitation clear but not dismissive. Pair the limit with the safest next step.

"We cannot help" is too blunt; "we cannot provide emergency assistance through this channel; please contact local emergency services now" is safer.

### Avoid unsafe troubleshooting or advice

Do not tell customers to test hazardous products, drive while using the app, confront a threatening person, stop medication, ignore symptoms, move injured people, share sensitive location publicly, or delay emergency help. If the product may be unsafe, use approved stop-use or safety escalation language.

Normal support steps can be dangerous in emergency contexts.

### Use approved resources, not invented ones

If policy provides crisis, emergency, product safety, law enforcement, or regional resource language, use it. Do not invent hotline numbers, legal advice, medical steps, or safety plans. If resources vary by location and the location is unknown, use general local-emergency wording unless policy provides otherwise.

Incorrect resource guidance can be harmful.

### Keep asking minimal and purposeful

When someone may be in danger, do not ask a long sequence of verification or diagnostic questions. Ask only what is needed to route safely, such as whether they are in immediate danger, whether emergency services have been contacted, or which product is unsafe, if safe to ask.

Escalation should not depend on perfect form completion.

### Preserve privacy and safety in public channels

If the emergency appears on social, community, or public review channels, do not ask the customer to post personal location, medical details, identity documents, or abuse details publicly. Move to secure channels only if doing so does not delay emergency help.

Public helpfulness can create exposure.

### Continue the support case only after immediate risk is addressed

Once immediate risk is routed or the customer is safe enough to continue, support can handle product issue, refund, replacement, account protection, complaint, or documentation. Keep emergency handling and ordinary support action distinct.

Do not close operational issues simply because emergency guidance was sent.

### Handle location uncertainty carefully

Emergency resources vary by country, region, and sometimes city. If the customer's location is unknown and policy does not provide a regional resource, use general language directing them to local emergency services or local crisis resources. Do not guess a hotline based on phone number, language, currency, or profile assumptions.

If location is needed for company escalation and it is safe to ask, request only the minimum location detail required by policy.

## Common Traps

- Treating emergency language as dramatic wording instead of possible immediate harm.
- Asking the customer to continue troubleshooting before seeking emergency help.
- Giving medical, legal, law-enforcement, or rescue advice from support.
- Saying only "this is out of scope" with no safe next step.
- Inventing crisis resources or hotline numbers; asking for detailed personal, medical, or location information in public channels
- Delaying urgent escalation because verification is incomplete; giving product advice that requires using a potentially unsafe item
- Assuming emergency guidance resolves the underlying support issue; forgetting to document what emergency guidance and escalation were used
- Guessing emergency resources or jurisdictions from weak signals; asking for detailed location publicly when a general emergency instruction or secure channel is safer

## Self-Check

- Was the message checked for immediate danger, injury, fire, shock, overdose, medical crisis, violence, stalking, child danger, active threat, unsafe driver, trapped person, domestic abuse, or urgent location risk?
- If imminent danger is plausible, does the response direct the customer to local emergency services or approved crisis resources?
- Does the response state support limits without abandoning the customer?
- Does it avoid medical, legal, law-enforcement, rescue, and real-time safety advice beyond approved wording?
- Are unsafe troubleshooting steps avoided?
- Are approved resources used instead of invented numbers, steps, or safety plans?
- Are questions minimal and tied to safe routing?
- Are public-channel privacy and safety risks handled carefully?
- After emergency handling, is the ordinary support issue still tracked separately if needed?; are emergency guidance, escalation, customer-facing message, and remaining support obligations documented?
- If location is uncertain, does the response avoid guessing specific resources and use approved general language?; if location is requested, is it minimal, necessary, safe, and not collected publicly unless policy allows?
