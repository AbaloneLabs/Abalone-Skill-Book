# Sales Billing And Support Alignment

Customers experience sales, billing, and support as one company, even when internal teams own different systems and promises. Misalignment creates disputes over discounts, renewal dates, invoice corrections, refund eligibility, plan features, and contract terms. Agents can harm trust by denying a promise they cannot see or honoring a promise without authority. This skill helps the agent align teams before giving financial or contractual answers.

## Core Rules

### Establish the source of the promise

Determine whether the customer expectation came from contract, order form, quote, sales email, chat, marketplace listing, invoice, renewal notice, help article, account manager, promotion, reseller, or prior support case. Ask for evidence when needed, but also search internal records.

Do not dismiss a customer claim simply because it is not in the support ticket.

### Separate billing facts from policy decisions

Billing facts include charges, invoices, credits, tax, renewal dates, payment status, plan, usage, and refund history. Policy decisions include whether to refund, credit, honor a quote, waive a fee, extend a discount, or adjust terms.

Support may be able to explain facts but not approve the financial outcome.

### Check contract and plan boundaries

Enterprise, annual, reseller, marketplace, promotional, nonprofit, education, trial, and legacy plans may not follow standard support policy. Contract terms can define SLA, cancellation, renewal, price increase, invoicing, and support entitlements.

Escalate before applying default consumer or self-serve rules to contracted accounts.

### Avoid unauthorized financial commitments

Do not promise refunds, credits, discounts, price locks, invoice voids, tax changes, payment extensions, cancellation backdating, or renewal changes unless you have approval. If review is pending, say what is being reviewed and when the customer will hear back.

A well-intended promise can create revenue, legal, and trust problems.

### Coordinate customer messaging

Sales, billing, support, and customer success should not send conflicting explanations. Decide who will respond, what facts are confirmed, what is still under review, and what wording should be used.

If the customer has an account owner, align before sending a sensitive financial denial or approval.

### Preserve audit trails

Financial changes need records: approval owner, reason, customer evidence, invoice ID, credit memo, refund ID, plan change, tax document, contract reference, and customer notice. Do not rely on chat memory.

Auditability matters for finance, disputes, chargebacks, and customer trust.

### Handle disputes with neutrality

Customers may be angry because they believe they were misled. Acknowledge the concern and investigate without accusing sales, billing, or the customer. Internal blame should not leak into customer communication.

If the company made a mistake, use approved recovery language and ownership.

### Feed recurring misalignment back upstream

Repeated disputes over the same promotion, invoice language, renewal notice, plan limit, or sales claim indicate systemic misalignment. Route patterns to sales enablement, billing operations, legal, marketing, or product packaging.

### Preserve customer evidence without overexposure and align before renewal or cancellation deadlines

Customers may provide contracts, screenshots, emails, recorded promises, purchase orders, tax documents, or bank statements. Store them in approved systems, restrict access, and reference the relevant facts without spreading sensitive financial or personal information in broad channels.

If evidence appears altered, incomplete, or from another account, do not accuse the customer in the first response. Escalate for review and ask for the needed source document through a safe channel.

Financial disputes often become urgent near renewal, cancellation windows, payment failure, collections, or contract deadlines. State the deadline, what action is paused or not paused, who owns review, and whether the customer must take action to preserve rights or service.

Do not let an internal review accidentally allow a renewal, suspension, or fee to proceed without clear customer expectation.

## Common Traps

- Denying a customer's pricing or feature claim without checking sales and billing records.
- Honoring a verbal or screenshot claim without approval or contract review.
- Confusing billing fact explanation with authority to approve a remedy.
- Applying self-serve cancellation or refund rules to enterprise or reseller accounts.
- Letting sales, billing, support, and success send separate messages; promising credit, refund, tax correction, or price change while review is pending
- Failing to document approvals and financial identifiers; blaming another internal team in the customer reply
- Treating repeated disputes as individual confusion rather than upstream messaging failure; ignoring chargeback, legal, tax, or revenue-recognition risk
- Sharing contracts, tax documents, bank evidence, or sales emails too broadly during review; letting renewal, cancellation, suspension, collections, or payment deadlines pass while teams debate ownership

## Self-Check

- Is the customer's expectation traced to contract, quote, email, invoice, promotion, marketplace, reseller, article, account manager, or prior support case?
- Were internal records checked before accepting or denying the claim?
- Are billing facts separated from policy or financial approval decisions?
- Are contract, plan, reseller, marketplace, annual, legacy, trial, promotion, nonprofit, education, and enterprise boundaries checked?
- Are refunds, credits, discounts, tax changes, invoice corrections, extensions, and cancellation changes approved before being promised?
- Is one team assigned to customer messaging with aligned facts and wording?
- Are account owners involved for sensitive contracted accounts?
- Are approval owner, reason, invoice, credit, refund, plan, tax, contract, and customer notice recorded?
- Is the customer communication neutral and ownership-oriented?; are repeated disputes routed to sales, billing, legal, marketing, enablement, or packaging owners?
- Is customer evidence stored and summarized through approved channels with appropriate access limits?; are renewal, cancellation, payment, suspension, and collections deadlines explicitly managed during review?
