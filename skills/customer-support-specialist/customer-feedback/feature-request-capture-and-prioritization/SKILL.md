# Feature Request Capture And Prioritization

Feature requests are easy to collect badly. Customers state solutions, but the real signal is the job they cannot complete, the cost of the gap, and the segment affected. Support teams often forward raw requests, promise roadmap consideration, or count votes without separating strategic fit, workaround quality, severity, and customer value. This skill helps the agent capture feature requests as evidence for product decisions without overpromising.

## Core Rules

### Capture the underlying job

Record what the customer is trying to accomplish, why current functionality fails, what workaround they use, and what happens if the request is not solved. Do not only record the requested button, field, report, integration, or setting.

Product teams can evaluate problems better than isolated proposed solutions.

### Identify customer and segment context

Feature value depends on segment: plan, region, industry, company size, role, workflow maturity, accessibility need, integration stack, revenue tier, regulated status, marketplace role, or strategic account. Capture relevant context without exposing private details.

A request from one enterprise admin and a request from many small customers may require different interpretation.

### Distinguish request types

Separate missing feature, usability friction, bug disguised as request, policy limitation, documentation gap, pricing packaging issue, integration limitation, performance problem, accessibility barrier, and compliance need.

Routing a bug or documentation gap as a future feature delays relief.

### Avoid roadmap promises

Thank the customer, capture the request, and explain the feedback process in approved terms. Do not promise release, timeline, priority, design, beta access, or direct product follow-up unless confirmed.

If the customer is blocked or high-value, route through customer success or product escalation rather than implying support can set roadmap.

### Include evidence and impact

Useful feature capture includes frequency, severity, affected workflow, lost time, lost revenue, churn risk, manual workaround, support volume, competitive pressure, contractual mention, and whether the customer would pay or adopt if solved.

Do not inflate impact. State evidence and confidence.

### Prioritize with more than vote count

Vote count can be useful, but it can favor loud segments. Consider strategic fit, customer harm, accessibility, compliance, revenue, retention, product direction, implementation complexity, and whether a simpler support or documentation fix exists.

Support should inform prioritization, not reduce it to popularity.

### Close the loop responsibly

If the feature ships, changes, or is declined, update affected customers only when appropriate and approved. Do not revive old requests with vague news that creates new support expectations.

Keep feedback records clean so product can trace decisions.

### Maintain taxonomy quality

Feature request systems become useless when tags are inconsistent. Use standard categories, merge duplicates, link related requests, and avoid creating a new bucket for every phrase.

Good taxonomy lets patterns emerge without hiding nuance.

### Capture the "why now" signal and avoid turning feedback into negotiation

Some requests are long-standing irritants, while others become urgent because of a contract renewal, policy change, competitor move, market expansion, regulation, launch, incident, or workflow growth. Record why the request matters now.

Timing helps product distinguish evergreen backlog from immediate risk. It also helps customer-facing teams decide whether a workaround, executive review, or customer success intervention is needed while roadmap decisions remain open.

Customers may use a feature request to seek a discount, extension, roadmap commitment, or custom work. Keep the feedback capture separate from commercial negotiation. Route commercial or contractual matters to the right owner without implying that feature volume creates entitlement.

Support can acknowledge importance without bargaining over product direction.

## Common Traps

- Recording the customer's proposed solution but not the job or pain.
- Treating bugs, documentation gaps, plan limitations, and accessibility barriers as feature requests.
- Promising roadmap attention, timeline, or beta access casually.
- Counting votes without segment, severity, revenue, churn, compliance, or accessibility context.
- Letting strategic-account pressure override evidence without escalation; failing to record workarounds and their cost
- Creating duplicate request entries with slightly different wording; ignoring requests from low-volume but high-harm customer groups
- Leaving customers thinking support can personally influence roadmap; not closing the loop when a shipped change clearly affects prior requests
- Missing why the request is urgent now, such as renewal, regulation, launch, incident, or competitor pressure; letting feature feedback become an unofficial commercial negotiation or custom-work promise

## Self-Check

- Does the record capture the customer's underlying job, blocker, workaround, and consequence?
- Is segment context included where relevant: plan, region, industry, size, role, workflow, accessibility, integration, regulated status, or account tier?
- Is the request type classified correctly as feature, usability, bug, documentation, pricing, integration, performance, accessibility, compliance, or policy issue?
- Does customer-facing language avoid roadmap promises?
- Is impact evidence included with frequency, severity, lost time, revenue, churn, support volume, competition, contract, or adoption signal?
- Is prioritization informed by more than vote count?
- Are high-value or blocked customers escalated through the right path without false promises?
- Are duplicates merged or linked using standard taxonomy?
- Is follow-up planned only when approved and useful?; can product understand the problem without reading the raw ticket?
- Is the timing or "why now" signal recorded?; are commercial, contractual, discount, and custom-work asks routed separately from product feedback?
