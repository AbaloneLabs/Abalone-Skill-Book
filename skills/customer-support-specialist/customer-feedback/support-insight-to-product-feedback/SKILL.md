# Support Insight To Product Feedback

Support teams see product friction before it appears in roadmap discussions. But product teams cannot act on vague claims like "customers are confused" or "this creates tickets." Good feedback translates support observations into customer jobs, evidence, severity, affected segments, and possible interventions. This skill helps the agent convert support insight into product feedback that is credible without pretending support owns product decisions.

## Core Rules

### Frame the customer problem

Start with what customers are trying to do, what blocks them, what they currently do instead, and why it matters. Avoid leading with the internal support burden unless that burden reflects customer effort.

Product feedback should make the user problem legible.

### Include evidence, not just anecdotes

Evidence can include ticket examples, contact volume, repeat contacts, CSAT comments, churn notes, account escalations, QA findings, community posts, social complaints, product analytics, session recordings, incident data, and support time spent. Use representative examples with privacy redaction.

If evidence is thin, label it as an emerging signal rather than a proven pattern.

### Separate fix idea from problem

Support may have good solution ideas, but product needs to understand the problem before choosing a fix. Present possible interventions as hypotheses: UI change, copy change, onboarding, guardrail, automation, policy adjustment, documentation, plan packaging, or product capability.

Do not make the feedback only a demand for a specific button.

### Assess customer and business impact

Include who is affected, how often, severity, time lost, revenue or retention risk, accessibility or compliance risk, support cost, operational workaround, and customer emotion. A low-volume issue may be urgent if it blocks core use or creates risk.

Avoid exaggeration. Product teams need calibrated impact to trust support.

### Name the current support mitigation

State how support handles the issue today: macro, workaround, escalation, manual fix, refund, training, help center article, or no safe path. Include whether mitigation is scalable and what risks it creates.

This helps product decide whether the issue needs product work, documentation, policy, or support process change.

### Route to the right owner

Product feedback may belong to product management, design, research, engineering, data, policy, growth, billing, trust and safety, accessibility, localization, or operations. Route based on root domain, not the team the agent knows best.

If ownership is uncertain, state the likely candidates and why.

### Ask for a decision or next investigation

Effective feedback requests a clear next step: confirm whether this is known, review examples, add instrumentation, test copy, prioritize research, update docs, define workaround, or decide no action.

Do not send feedback into a void and call it done.

### Close the loop back to support

When product responds, update macros, help articles, QA guidance, triage tags, known issues, or escalation criteria. If no change will happen, support still needs accurate explanation and boundaries.

Feedback is only useful if it changes either product or support behavior.

### Preserve the customer's words without losing structure and identify reversible support fixes

Raw customer language can reveal emotional cost, expectation mismatch, and vocabulary that product teams would not invent. Include short redacted snippets or paraphrases where they illuminate the problem, but pair them with structured fields so the feedback is searchable and comparable.

Do not sanitize feedback so much that the pain disappears. Do not include long private transcripts when a concise example would work.

Some product insights can be partially addressed before product changes: clearer macro, warning banner, help article, onboarding step, validation message, routing rule, known-issue tag, or support workflow. Note these reversible mitigations separately from product requests.

This prevents product feedback from becoming an excuse to leave customers unsupported while waiting for roadmap work.

## Common Traps

- Writing "customers are confused" without the task, blocker, segment, or evidence.
- Forwarding raw tickets and expecting product to infer the pattern.
- Treating a support team's preferred fix as the only acceptable product solution.
- Inflating impact because the issue is frustrating to agents.
- Ignoring support mitigations and their risks; routing everything to engineering when the issue may be design, policy, docs, billing, or localization
- Sending feedback without a requested decision or next step; failing to redact private customer data
- Not updating support content after product responds; repeating the same feedback every month without adding new evidence or asking for a decision
- Removing customer language so completely that product cannot hear the expectation mismatch; waiting for product work when a reversible support mitigation could reduce harm now

## Self-Check

- Is the customer problem framed as job, blocker, workaround, and consequence?
- Does the feedback include evidence beyond anecdote where available?
- Are examples redacted and representative?
- Are proposed fixes separated from the underlying problem?
- Is impact calibrated across frequency, severity, segment, time, revenue, retention, accessibility, compliance, support cost, and emotion?
- Is current support mitigation described with scalability and risk?
- Is the likely owner identified across product, design, engineering, data, policy, billing, trust, accessibility, localization, or operations?
- Does the feedback ask for a decision or next investigation?
- Is uncertainty labeled honestly?; will product response feed back into macros, docs, QA, tags, known issues, or escalation criteria?
- Are short customer examples or paraphrases included where they clarify pain without exposing private data?; are reversible support-side mitigations identified separately from product changes?
