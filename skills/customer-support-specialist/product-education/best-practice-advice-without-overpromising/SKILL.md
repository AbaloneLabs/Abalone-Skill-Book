# Best Practice Advice Without Overpromising

Customers often ask support not only "how do I do this" but "what should I do." That request can be useful, but it can also pull agents into consulting, legal, financial, medical, security, tax, compliance, or business advice they are not authorized to give. Agents may overpromise outcomes to be helpful. This skill helps the agent provide practical product guidance while staying inside evidence, policy, and support scope.

## Core Rules

### Separate product guidance from professional advice

Support can explain product behavior, common configurations, tradeoffs, known limits, and where customers can find settings. It should not decide the customer's legal compliance, tax treatment, investment strategy, medical workflow, hiring decision, security posture, or business policy unless the organization explicitly provides that service.

Use boundary language when needed: "This explains how the setting works, but your team should decide whether it meets your obligations."

### State assumptions and context

Best practices depend on company size, risk tolerance, region, workflow, user roles, data sensitivity, budget, technical capacity, and customer objectives. Do not present one configuration as universally best.

If context is missing, either ask a targeted question or provide options with the conditions where each fits.

### Explain tradeoffs, not only benefits

A recommended setting may improve security but add friction, speed onboarding but reduce control, automate work but increase error blast radius, or reduce cost but limit support. Make the tradeoff visible.

Customers trust advice more when it includes conditions and downsides.

### Avoid outcome guarantees

Do not guarantee revenue, conversion, compliance, uptime, deliverability, fraud prevention, SEO ranking, dispute outcomes, customer satisfaction, or risk elimination. If examples or benchmarks exist, cite them as illustrative and within approved language.

Best practice is not a promise of result.

### Use approved sources and current behavior

Recommendations should align with current product documentation, policy, known limitations, pricing, support scope, and customer contract. If the advice depends on a beta, roadmap, third-party integration, or experimental feature, label that clearly.

Outdated best-practice advice can create expensive rework.

### Respect customer ownership

Support can advise, but the customer chooses. Do not pressure customers into configurations that make support easier at the expense of their needs. Present options and consequences so the customer can decide.

For enterprise or regulated customers, encourage internal review by their admin, security, legal, compliance, finance, or operations owners.

### Escalate high-impact recommendations

Advice that affects data access, security controls, billing, public content, customer communications, regulated workflows, production integrations, mass imports, or irreversible changes may need specialist review.

Do not improvise architecture, migration, or compliance recommendations in a routine ticket.

### Document recommendations and caveats

When advice may affect important configuration, record what was recommended, assumptions, caveats, sources, and customer decision. Future agents need to know whether a later issue came from support guidance or customer choice.

### Offer decision criteria instead of hidden judgment and be careful with examples from other customers

When there is no single best option, give the customer criteria they can use: risk tolerance, number of users, data sensitivity, need for auditability, cost constraints, support capacity, reversibility, user training burden, and downstream dependencies. This is more useful than making a decision on their behalf.

Decision criteria also make support boundaries clearer. The agent can explain how the product behaves under each option while the customer chooses based on their organization.

Agents may know common patterns from prior tickets, but those examples can be misleading or confidential. Do not imply that another customer's setup is a benchmark unless it is approved, anonymized, and comparable.

If examples are helpful, describe them as generic patterns and state the condition that makes them relevant.

## Common Traps

- Answering "what should I do" with a universal recommendation despite missing context.
- Giving legal, tax, medical, financial, compliance, or security advice under the label of best practice.
- Mentioning benefits without friction, cost, risk, or maintenance tradeoffs.
- Promising outcomes such as higher revenue, compliance, deliverability, or fraud prevention.
- Recommending beta, roadmap, or third-party behavior as stable; pushing the customer toward the option easiest for support
- Advising production changes without considering data, billing, security, or customer-facing blast radius; failing to involve customer success, solutions, legal, security, or product specialists for high-impact choices
- Not documenting assumptions and caveats; using anecdotes from other customers as proof of fit
- Choosing for the customer instead of offering decision criteria and consequences; revealing or overgeneralizing another customer's implementation pattern

## Self-Check

- Is the response clearly product guidance rather than unauthorized professional advice?
- Are legal, tax, medical, financial, compliance, and security boundaries respected?
- Are assumptions about customer size, region, workflow, roles, data sensitivity, technical capacity, and goals stated?
- Are multiple options provided where context could reasonably change the answer?
- Are tradeoffs, downsides, maintenance needs, and risks explained?
- Are outcome guarantees avoided?
- Does the advice align with current documentation, policy, pricing, limits, contract, and support scope?
- Are beta, roadmap, experimental, or third-party dependencies labeled?
- Are high-impact recommendations escalated or reviewed where needed?; are recommendation, assumptions, caveats, source, and customer decision documented?
- Does the advice provide decision criteria where context determines the best option?; are examples generic, approved, anonymized, and comparable rather than confidential anecdotes?
