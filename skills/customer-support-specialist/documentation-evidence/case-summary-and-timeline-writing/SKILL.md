# Case Summary And Timeline Writing

Long support cases become hard to reason about because evidence, promises, decisions, and customer messages are scattered across channels and time. A poor summary can hide missed commitments, distort sequence, or make a current decision look unfair. This skill helps the agent compress a case without losing the facts that determine outcome and accountability.

## Core Rules

### Start with the current question

A useful summary is shaped by the decision at hand: refund review, escalation, complaint investigation, legal review, incident analysis, coaching, closure, account recovery, fraud assessment, or customer success handoff. Include the history needed for that decision and avoid turning the summary into a full transcript.

Do not summarize everything equally. Relevance depends on the next use.

### Preserve chronology precisely

Order events by when they happened, not when the agent found them. Include dates, times, time zones, channel, actor, and event where timing affects eligibility, service level, charge windows, delivery, access, incident scope, or missed commitments.

Avoid vague phrases like "recently," "last week," or "later" when exact timestamps are available or material.

### Separate customer claims, system evidence, and support actions

The timeline should show what the customer said, what systems show, and what support did. These are different evidence types. A customer may report cancellation on a date; the system may show no cancellation; support may have given unclear instructions. The decision depends on the tension between them.

Do not collapse contested facts into a single narrative.

### Highlight commitments and misses

Identify promised updates, callbacks, refunds, replacements, escalation, investigation, workaround, or exception review. Note whether they were fulfilled, missed, changed, or still pending. Support failures often appear only when the timeline shows the sequence.

This is especially important for complaints, service recovery, and repeated-contact cases.

### Include impact, not only events

The summary should capture what the issue prevented, who was affected, financial amount, business consequence, safety or accessibility concern, deadline, emotional impact, and prior customer effort. Events without impact can make high-risk cases look ordinary.

Impact also explains why the customer reacted strongly or why escalation was appropriate.

### Account for all relevant channels

Customers may contact email, chat, phone, social, community, sales, customer success, self-service, or app feedback. Consolidate material events across channels and note where evidence came from. Do not assume the current ticket contains the whole story.

If a channel cannot be checked, state that limitation rather than implying completeness.

### Show status and open questions

End the summary with current state: resolved, pending customer action, pending internal owner, awaiting third party, denied, escalated, incident-linked, or reopened. List open questions that block the next decision.

A summary that lacks current status forces the next person to repeat analysis.

### Keep summaries neutral and reviewable

Use objective language and avoid advocacy, blame, or unsupported motive. If the customer was abusive, state observable facts. If support failed, state what happened. If evidence conflicts, show the conflict.

The goal is not to defend support or the customer; it is to enable a fair decision.

### Mark source confidence

When a timeline depends on multiple sources, identify which source supports each important event: system log, customer message, payment record, shipment scan, call note, social post, sales note, status page, or agent note. Some sources are stronger than others, and some may be delayed or incomplete.

If a timestamp comes from customer memory, carrier tracking, or a third-party system with known lag, say that. Source confidence helps reviewers decide whether a decision should be final, provisional, or escalated for verification.

## Common Traps

- Writing a chronological transcript instead of a decision-focused summary.
- Ordering events by ticket view order rather than real-world sequence.
- Omitting time zones or exact dates when eligibility, service level, delivery, or incident scope depends on timing.
- Merging customer claims and system evidence into one unqualified statement.
- Missing promised updates, callbacks, refunds, escalation, or exception reviews; summarizing operational events but omitting customer impact
- Ignoring chat, phone, social, sales, customer success, self-service, or community context; ending the summary without current status or open questions
- Using blameful or defensive language that distorts review; hiding uncertainty to make the case look cleaner
- Listing events without identifying whether they came from logs, customer reports, agents, vendors, or third parties; treating low-confidence or delayed source data as if it were definitive

## Self-Check

- Is the summary shaped by the current decision or review purpose?
- Are events ordered by when they happened, with dates, times, time zones, channels, actors, and event details where relevant?
- Are customer claims, system evidence, support actions, and assumptions separated?
- Are promised updates, callbacks, refunds, replacements, escalations, investigations, workarounds, and exception reviews marked as fulfilled, missed, changed, or pending?
- Does the summary capture customer impact, affected people, amount, business consequence, safety, accessibility, deadline, emotion, and prior effort?
- Were all relevant channels checked or limitations stated?
- Does the summary identify current status and open questions blocking the next decision?
- Are evidence conflicts preserved rather than smoothed over?
- Is the language neutral, reviewable, and free of unsupported motive or blame?; are important events tied to their source, such as system log, customer message, payment record, shipment scan, call note, social post, sales note, status page, or agent note?
- Is source confidence clear when data may be delayed, incomplete, customer-reported, or third-party supplied?; could a reviewer make the next decision without reading the entire case history first?
