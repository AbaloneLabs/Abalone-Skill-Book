# Review Site And Community Reputation Response

Reviews and community posts are support artifacts, marketing signals, and public records at once. A reply that only tries to win the argument can damage trust, while a reply that only apologizes may fail to route the customer or correct a misleading impression. This skill helps the agent respond in public reputation spaces with privacy discipline, useful resolution paths, and attention to what future readers will infer.

## Core Rules

### Write for two audiences

The original customer needs to feel heard and know what to do next. Future readers need to see whether the organization is fair, responsive, and honest. A good response is specific enough to be credible but not so specific that it exposes private details.

Do not optimize only for the star rating. Optimize for a public record that shows responsible support behavior.

### Classify the review before replying

Different posts require different handling: unresolved support case, product bug, shipping issue, billing dispute, policy disagreement, safety allegation, accessibility concern, abuse report, competitor spam, fake review, repeated incident, or general dissatisfaction.

Classification decides whether the response should route privately, escalate, correct misinformation, thank the customer, request evidence, or report platform abuse.

### Avoid privacy leaks and case debates

Never disclose the customer's purchase history, account status, enforcement history, refund eligibility, health or safety details, identity verification, or private support notes. Even when the customer shared some details publicly, the company should not add more private context.

If correction is needed, keep it general and invite secure follow-up.

### Make the next step realistic

Do not say "contact support" if the customer already did and felt ignored. Provide a specific route, reference instruction, escalation phrase, or form that helps the case be found. If the platform hides contact details, explain what information the customer should include privately.

For app store or marketplace reviews, know what the platform allows and whether support can initiate contact.

### Do not manipulate reviews

Do not pressure customers to change ratings, offer undisclosed incentives for positive reviews, ask only happy customers to post in a misleading way, or bury legitimate criticism with scripted replies. Follow platform rules and consumer protection expectations.

It is acceptable to ask a customer to update their review after resolution only if the request is neutral, compliant, and not tied to a benefit.

### Escalate reputation-sensitive risk

Reviews that allege safety harm, discrimination, fraud, data exposure, regulatory violations, systemic outage, harassment, or vulnerable-customer mistreatment need more than a public reply. Escalate and document the signal.

High-visibility creator, press, investor, or enterprise posts may also need coordinated handling, but coordination should not distort the facts.

### Feed themes back into operations

Review replies are not only individual responses. Track themes by product, policy, billing, fulfillment, support quality, accessibility, localization, and repeated incident. Compare public themes with ticket data to find blind spots.

Public reviews often surface customer effort and emotional damage that internal metrics flatten.

### Separate response from investigation

A public review reply can acknowledge and route, but investigation may need a separate internal record. Capture the review URL, platform, rating, date, poster handle, claimed issue, product or location, prior case reference if available, risk signals, and routing owner. Do not assume the reply itself is enough documentation.

This matters when a review alleges safety harm, discrimination, fraud, counterfeit goods, data exposure, or repeated failed support. The public response should remain privacy-safe while the internal investigation collects the evidence needed to act.

### Handle unverifiable or malicious reviews carefully

Some reviews are incomplete, mistaken, spam, competitor activity, coordinated brigading, or posted by someone who is not a customer. Respond in a way that invites verifiable follow-up without accusing the poster unless platform policy and evidence support reporting. Use platform dispute tools when appropriate, but avoid public threats.

## Common Traps

- Writing a reply that tries to impress future readers but gives the customer no real path.
- Treating all negative reviews as reputation problems instead of possible unresolved support issues.
- Revealing private support history to defend the company.
- Telling the customer to contact support when that is exactly what failed.
- Using the same apology macro for safety, billing, product, and shipping complaints; offering incentives or pressure to change a rating
- Ignoring platform-specific rules for review responses; failing to escalate allegations of safety harm, discrimination, fraud, data exposure, or regulatory breach
- Over-correcting misinformation in a way that sounds combative; treating the public reply as the only record and failing to open an internal investigation path
- Publicly accusing a reviewer of being fake without enough evidence or platform basis; not converting repeated review themes into product, policy, or support improvements

## Self-Check

- Does the reply serve both the original customer and future readers?
- Has the review type been classified before responding?
- Does the response avoid private account, purchase, enforcement, refund, identity, health, safety, and support-note details?
- If correction is needed, is it narrow, general, and non-combative?
- Is the next step specific enough to overcome the customer's prior friction?
- Does the reply comply with platform rules and avoid review manipulation?
- Are safety, discrimination, fraud, data exposure, regulatory, harassment, and vulnerable-customer allegations escalated?
- Are high-visibility posts coordinated without changing facts?
- Are repeated themes captured for product, policy, fulfillment, billing, accessibility, localization, or support-quality improvement?; would the response look fair and useful if read out of context by a future customer?
- Is there an internal record with review URL, rating, date, handle, claimed issue, risk signals, and owner where investigation is needed?; are unverifiable, spam, malicious, or non-customer reviews handled through careful language and platform tools rather than public accusation?
