# Privacy Request Routing And Safe Response

Privacy requests are not ordinary support preferences. A customer may be exercising legal rights, asking about personal data, requesting deletion, challenging consent, or reporting a privacy concern. Agents often answer casually, promise deletion manually, disclose account details before verification, or route too late. This skill helps the agent recognize privacy work and move it through the proper process.

## Core Rules

### Recognize privacy intent even when phrased casually

Customers may say "delete my data," "send me everything you have," "stop tracking me," "remove my child," "change my personal info," "what data do you sell," "unsubscribe me," or "I did not consent." These may trigger privacy rights or regulated handling depending on region and account context.

Do not reduce these requests to normal cancellation, marketing preference, or account support without checking privacy intent.

### Verify identity before disclosure or action

Data access, export, correction, deletion, portability, and account-specific privacy answers require identity and sometimes authority verification. The requester may not be the data subject, account owner, parent, guardian, employee, or authorized representative.

Do not disclose what personal data exists or start deletion until the proper verification path is satisfied.

### Route through the designated privacy process

Use the organization's privacy intake, data subject request workflow, legal/compliance queue, self-service portal, or regional process. Frontline support may acknowledge and route but may not be authorized to decide scope, exemptions, timing, or legal applicability.

Do not create manual deletion or export promises outside the approved workflow.

### Preserve timelines and jurisdiction context

Privacy workflows often have deadlines and jurisdiction-specific requirements. Capture request date, requester region if known, account identifiers, request type, channel, verification status, and any urgency. Do not promise a deadline unless the privacy process supports it.

If the customer mentions a law or location, record it without pretending to provide legal interpretation.

### Avoid over-disclosure in acknowledgement

A safe acknowledgement confirms receipt or next path without revealing data categories, account state, tracking details, or internal retention practices beyond approved language. If the requester is unverified, keep the response general.

Do not answer "what data do you have on me" in ordinary support chat.

### Distinguish privacy requests from support outcomes

Deletion may affect account access, subscriptions, order history, warranty, fraud records, legal retention, tax records, safety logs, or product functionality. Correction may require verification. Opt-out may not stop all transactional messages. Marketing unsubscribe is not the same as data deletion.

Explain operational effects only through approved language and route edge cases.

### Handle sensitive categories conservatively

Requests involving children, health information, financial data, biometrics, precise location, government IDs, employee data, domestic safety, protected classes, or regulated industries require extra care. Escalate to privacy/legal paths and avoid improvising.

Do not collect more sensitive data while trying to process a privacy request.

### Document and hand off cleanly

Record request type, date received, requester, account or identifiers where safe, verification status, jurisdiction signal, channel, customer-facing acknowledgement, and routing destination. Avoid copying sensitive personal data into broad notes.

The privacy team should be able to start from the handoff without reasking basic routing facts.

### Do not conflate privacy preference with legal request

Some messages are operational preferences, such as changing email settings, hiding a profile, correcting a typo, or closing an account. Others may be formal privacy rights requests. When intent is ambiguous, clarify or route rather than either over-lawyering a routine task or under-routing a regulated request.

Support may complete a simple product setting change while still preserving the customer's broader privacy request if they asked for one.

## Common Traps

- Treating "delete my data" as only account cancellation.
- Treating "unsubscribe" as a full privacy opt-out or deletion request without clarification.
- Disclosing personal data before identity and authority are verified.
- Promising deletion, export, correction, or timing outside the approved process.
- Ignoring jurisdiction, request date, channel, and statutory timeline signals; giving legal interpretations of privacy rights from frontline support
- Over-explaining internal data retention, tracking, or sale practices without approved language; missing sensitive categories such as child data, health, financial, biometrics, location, IDs, employee data, or domestic safety risk
- Collecting unnecessary personal data to process the privacy request; failing to document the request clearly enough for privacy/legal review
- Treating every preference change as a formal legal request and slowing routine support unnecessarily; treating a formal rights request as a simple product setting because it resembles one

## Self-Check

- Was privacy intent recognized even if the customer used casual language?
- Is the request type identified: access, export, deletion, correction, opt-out, consent, tracking, retention, child data, employee data, or privacy concern?
- Was identity and authority verified before account-specific disclosure or action?
- Was the request routed through the designated privacy, legal, compliance, self-service, or regional workflow?
- Are request date, channel, region or jurisdiction signal, account identifiers, verification status, and urgency captured where appropriate?
- Does customer-facing language avoid unauthorized account facts, data categories, retention details, legal interpretation, and unsupported deadlines?
- Are operational effects distinguished from privacy rights, including subscription, warranty, fraud, legal retention, tax, safety, and transactional messaging constraints?
- Are sensitive categories escalated conservatively?
- Is unnecessary personal data avoided during intake?; can the privacy owner act from the handoff without rereading the entire support thread?
- If the request mixes product preference and legal privacy language, are both parts separated and routed or handled appropriately?; is the response careful not to imply that a routine setting change satisfies a broader privacy rights request unless the approved process says so?
