# Vulnerable Customer And Hardship Handling

Vulnerability and hardship change the support risk profile. A routine billing, cancellation, access, delivery, or complaint case may become high impact when the customer is grieving, displaced, unsafe, disabled, financially distressed, elderly, digitally excluded, or emotionally overwhelmed. Agents can harm customers by demanding unnecessary proof, rushing the interaction, applying policy mechanically, or making promises outside approved hardship paths. This skill helps the agent provide careful support without becoming a social worker, lawyer, or clinician.

## Core Rules

### Recognize vulnerability signals

Customers may directly mention hardship, or they may show signals: inability to pay, sudden life event, illness, bereavement, disaster, unstable housing, domestic abuse, cognitive difficulty, elder exploitation, language barrier, inaccessible technology, panic, confusion, repeated failed verification, or dependence on the service for essential needs.

Do not require customers to use formal words like "vulnerable" before adjusting support.

### Slow down and reduce burden

Use clear language, fewer steps, written summaries, extra time, callback options, authorized helper paths, accessible channels, and confirmation of understanding. Avoid making the customer repeat distressing details across teams.

Reducing burden does not mean skipping security, privacy, or policy. It means choosing the least harmful compliant path.

### Ask only for necessary evidence

Some hardship programs require documentation, but many support adjustments do not. Ask for the minimum evidence required by policy and explain why it is needed. Do not request medical, financial, legal, or family details out of curiosity.

If evidence is sensitive, use approved upload or specialist channels.

### Use approved hardship and exception paths

Financial relief, payment plans, fee waivers, extensions, cancellation support, delivery changes, account recovery, accessibility accommodations, or complaint escalation should follow approved policies. If the situation does not fit policy but harm is significant, escalate for exception review.

Do not invent benefits or deny reflexively because the first policy answer is narrow.

### Protect safety and privacy

Hardship cases can involve domestic abuse, stalking, elder abuse, coercive control, shared accounts, unsafe addresses, or unauthorized helpers. Be careful with notifications, account changes, billing visibility, and who receives updates.

If the customer mentions immediate danger, self-harm, abuse, or exploitation, follow safety escalation guidance. Do not attempt counseling beyond support scope.

### Preserve dignity and agency

Customers in hardship should not be treated as helpless or suspicious. Offer options, explain consequences, and let them choose where possible. Avoid pity, moral judgment, or excessive sympathy that delays practical help.

The goal is a respectful path through a difficult process.

### Document sensitively

Record the operational facts needed for continuity: hardship category if policy uses one, requested adjustment, evidence received, approved action, owner, deadline, and safety considerations. Avoid graphic or unnecessary personal details.

Future agents need enough context to avoid making the customer retell the situation.

### Plan handoffs with continuity

Vulnerable customers are harmed when each team restarts the same script. If the case moves to billing, account recovery, complaints, legal, accessibility, safety, or a supervisor, the handoff should include what the customer has already explained, what must not be repeated unnecessarily, what accommodation or safety constraint applies, and what promise was made.

Mark sensitive notes carefully so they are visible to authorized agents but not overexposed. The goal is continuity without spreading private hardship details.

### Separate empathy from qualification

Empathy is appropriate, but it does not determine eligibility for hardship relief, refunds, extensions, or exceptions. Qualification should follow policy and approved escalation. Conversely, a customer who does not sound emotional may still qualify.

This prevents inconsistent outcomes based on how distressed or persuasive the customer appears.

## Common Traps

- Applying a routine billing, cancellation, or verification script without noticing vulnerability.
- Asking for detailed medical, financial, legal, or family evidence when policy does not require it.
- Skipping security because the customer is distressed.
- Sending notifications or account changes that could expose someone experiencing abuse.
- Treating an authorized helper as safe without verifying consent and authority; promising hardship relief, refunds, extensions, or exceptions before approval
- Denying support because the case does not match the first policy category; using pitying or infantilizing language
- Recording excessive sensitive personal detail in ticket notes; trying to provide therapy, legal advice, or emergency guidance outside support scope
- Making the customer repeat hardship details during every transfer; approving or denying help based on emotional presentation rather than policy and evidence

## Self-Check

- Were hardship or vulnerability signals recognized even if indirect?
- Did the response reduce customer burden with clearer steps, accessible channels, summaries, extra time, or callback options?
- Were security, privacy, and policy controls maintained through a less harmful path?
- Is evidence limited to what policy requires and collected through approved channels?
- Are hardship, exception, accessibility, payment, cancellation, delivery, or complaint paths used where relevant?
- Are domestic abuse, stalking, elder abuse, coercion, unsafe address, shared account, notification, and helper-authority risks considered?
- Are immediate danger, self-harm, abuse, or exploitation signals escalated appropriately?
- Does the language preserve dignity and agency without pity or judgment?
- Are operational facts documented without unnecessary sensitive detail?; does the customer have a clear next step, owner, and expected timing?
- Does any handoff preserve continuity while limiting sensitive detail to authorized agents?; are hardship decisions based on policy, evidence, and approved escalation rather than how emotional the customer sounds?
