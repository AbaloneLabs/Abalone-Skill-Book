# Accessibility Support And Accommodation

Accessibility support is not a courtesy add-on. It determines whether a customer can use the product, understand the answer, complete a required process, or exercise the same rights as other customers. Agents often miss accessibility needs because the customer does not use formal disability language or because standard workflows assume vision, hearing, mobility, cognition, language, or time flexibility. This skill helps the agent respond with dignity, practicality, and appropriate escalation.

## Core Rules

### Listen for barrier signals

Customers may describe accessibility problems indirectly: "I cannot read this," "the button is not reachable," "the audio has no captions," "the form times out," "I use a screen reader," "I cannot call," "I need plain language," or "the color makes it impossible." Treat these as access barriers, not inconvenience.

Ask what would make the task possible without requiring the customer to disclose more medical or disability information than needed.

### Offer reasonable support adjustments

Adjustments may include written instead of phone support, phone instead of chat, captions, transcripts, plain-language summary, extended time, accessible document format, larger text guidance, step-by-step navigation, alternate verification path, or help completing a form.

Use approved accommodation paths when available. If no formal path exists, escalate rather than forcing the customer through an inaccessible process.

### Do not make the customer prove disability casually

For many support adjustments, the customer should not need to provide medical details. Verification, if required for a formal accommodation or legal reason, should be handled through approved channels and minimum necessary data.

Avoid intrusive questions. Focus on the barrier and the requested support.

### Preserve security and privacy while adapting

Accessibility needs do not remove account-security requirements, but they may require an equivalent method. For example, a customer who cannot use voice calls may need a secure written path; a customer who cannot complete visual CAPTCHA may need an alternative challenge.

Do not ask customers to share passwords, codes, IDs, or private data with helpers as a workaround.

### Route product defects clearly

Accessibility bugs need strong reports: assistive technology, browser, device, OS, steps, expected behavior, actual barrier, affected workflow, severity, and customer impact. Include whether the barrier blocks account access, payment, cancellation, legal rights, safety information, or core product use.

Accessibility defects are not cosmetic if they prevent task completion.

### Communicate with dignity

Use respectful, direct language. Do not pity, praise, infantilize, blame, or make assumptions about capability. Mirror the customer's preferred terms when appropriate and avoid unnecessary labels.

The customer should not feel like an exception being tolerated.

### Follow through on accommodation commitments

If an alternate process, callback, accessible file, manual review, or specialist escalation is promised, track it. Accessibility support often fails not because the first agent is rude, but because the promised adaptation is not operationally owned.

### Do not make accessibility depend on a single channel

Support programs sometimes say they are accessible while requiring the customer to call, upload an inaccessible PDF, solve a visual challenge, use a mouse, wait in a timed chat, or read dense legal text. Check the entire path, not only the final answer.

If a customer reports that the accommodation path itself is inaccessible, treat that as a priority defect. Provide an alternate contact route and document the blocked step so the process can be fixed.

### Coordinate compliance without delaying basic help

Some accessibility issues may require legal, compliance, product, or specialized accessibility review. That review should not prevent the support team from giving safe immediate help, such as an accessible summary, alternate channel, deadline extension, or escalation owner.

Separate urgent customer access from longer-term compliance assessment.

## Common Traps

- Treating accessibility barriers as user preference or inconvenience.
- Requiring medical details for ordinary support adjustments.
- Saying "use a friend or family member" when that exposes private account information.
- Offering a workaround that bypasses security controls.
- Reporting accessibility bugs without assistive technology, device, steps, or impact; assuming phone is accessible to everyone or written support is accessible to everyone
- Using patronizing language or praising the customer for basic task attempts; forgetting that timeouts, CAPTCHAs, PDFs, color contrast, keyboard traps, and unlabeled controls can block service
- Closing after giving an alternate path without tracking whether it worked; treating accessibility as lower priority than other product defects
- Offering an accommodation path that contains another inaccessible step; waiting for legal or product review before providing safe immediate access support

## Self-Check

- Were barrier signals recognized even if the customer did not use formal disability language?
- Did the response ask what support would make the task possible without requesting unnecessary medical details?
- Are support adjustments offered through approved or escalated paths?
- Is equivalent security maintained through accessible verification or account processes?
- Does the response avoid asking the customer to reveal private data to helpers?
- Are accessibility defects documented with assistive technology, browser, device, OS, steps, expected behavior, actual barrier, severity, and impact?
- Is the impact assessed for account access, payment, cancellation, legal rights, safety information, or core use?
- Is the language respectful, direct, and free of pity or assumptions?
- Are promised alternate processes, files, callbacks, manual reviews, or escalations tracked?; does the customer have a usable path to complete the task?
- Was the full accommodation path checked for inaccessible steps such as phone-only, timed chat, inaccessible PDFs, visual challenges, or mouse-only controls?; is longer-term compliance review separated from immediate customer access help?
