# Severity Routing And Approval Path

Severity decisions determine response speed, team attention, authority, and customer expectation. Agents often use emotion, queue age, customer status, or personal discomfort as a proxy for severity. That creates unfair prioritization and misses real risk. This skill helps the agent route by impact, urgency, authority, and safety rather than by noise.

## Core Rules

### Classify severity by consequence

Start with what happens if support does not act quickly: money loss, account lockout, safety risk, business interruption, data exposure, legal deadline, accessibility barrier, active abuse, fraud, shipment deadline, or many affected users. Severity should reflect customer and business consequence, not only the customer's tone or ticket age.

Document the impact in concrete terms. "Very upset" is not a severity reason by itself; "enterprise admin locked out during payroll processing" is.

### Distinguish urgency from importance

Some cases are important but not time-critical, such as product feedback or a complex refund review. Others are urgent because delay increases harm, even if the final fix is simple. Route urgent cases to time-sensitive paths and important cases to durable owners.

Do not inflate severity simply because a case is complicated or visible.

### Use approved severity criteria

Check local definitions for priority, incident severity, safety escalation, enterprise service level, fraud review, legal intake, billing approval, warranty authority, and engineering escalation. If the case sits between levels, choose the more conservative temporary route and ask a lead or owner to confirm.

Severity labels become operational commitments. Do not invent custom meanings.

### Route to authority, not only expertise

The right owner must have permission to decide. Technical support may understand a bug but not approve a refund. Billing may see a charge but not judge fraud. Customer success may own relationship context but not security restoration. Legal may need to own subpoenas or regulated requests.

Escalation should reach the team that can act, approve, or formally decide, not merely the team that is sympathetic.

### Protect sensitive routes

Account takeover, privacy, security vulnerability, abuse, harassment, self-harm, illegal activity, payment fraud, chargeback, regulated data, minors, medical or legal deadlines, and safety-critical issues require restricted handling. These routes may have special evidence, language, confidentiality, and audit requirements.

Do not keep sensitive cases in ordinary queues because the visible customer request sounds routine.

### Avoid customer-status distortion

VIP, enterprise, public, influencer, or high-revenue customers may have service commitments that affect routing. That does not mean low-status customers with high-risk issues should wait. Use customer tier as one factor only where policy or contract says it matters.

Severity must remain defensible if audited against impact and policy.

### Record approval requirements

Refunds, credits, replacements, manual access restoration, warranty exceptions, expedited fulfillment, custom engineering work, public statements, and legal responses may require approval. Identify who can approve, what evidence is needed, and whether the customer should be told review is pending.

Do not imply approval by starting work that depends on a decision not yet made.

### Reassess severity as facts change

Triage is provisional. New evidence may reveal a broader incident, reduced impact, fraud signal, customer workaround, missed deadline, or incorrect account context. Update severity, owner, customer expectation, and notes when facts change.

Leaving stale priority on a case causes either under-response or queue distortion.

### Downgrade with the same care as escalation

Reducing severity is a decision that affects customer trust and queue fairness. Downgrade only when impact, urgency, or risk has genuinely changed or was initially misclassified. Record the evidence for the downgrade, what work remains, and whether the customer expectation needs to be reset.

Do not quietly downgrade a case because it is difficult, noisy, old, or inconvenient for the receiving team.

## Common Traps

- Using customer anger or executive visibility as the main severity signal.
- Treating complicated cases as urgent when no time-sensitive harm exists.
- Under-prioritizing quiet customers with serious account, money, safety, or access issues.
- Escalating to a team with expertise but no authority to decide.
- Keeping security, privacy, fraud, abuse, legal, or safety cases in normal support paths.
- Letting VIP status override impact-based severity without policy basis; starting refunds, replacements, exceptions, or access work before approval is confirmed
- Failing to update priority after new facts reduce or increase risk; routing a case without recording the consequence that justified severity
- Describing a case as escalated to the customer before an owner has accepted it; quietly downgrading cases without evidence, note updates, or customer expectation reset

## Self-Check

- Is severity based on concrete consequence such as money, access, safety, business interruption, data, legal deadline, accessibility, abuse, fraud, shipment, or affected users?
- Is urgency separated from general importance or complexity?
- Were approved severity, incident, safety, fraud, legal, billing, warranty, and enterprise criteria checked where relevant?
- Does the receiving route have authority to act, approve, or formally decide?
- Are sensitive cases routed through restricted paths with proper evidence and confidentiality?
- Is customer tier used only where contract, service level, or policy supports it?
- Are approval requirements identified for refunds, credits, replacements, access restoration, exceptions, fulfillment, engineering work, public statements, and legal responses?
- Has the customer been told only what is true about escalation status?
- Are severity, owner, notes, and customer expectations updated when facts change?
- If severity was reduced, is the downgrade supported by evidence and are remaining work and customer expectations clear?; would the priority be defensible if audited against impact and policy?
