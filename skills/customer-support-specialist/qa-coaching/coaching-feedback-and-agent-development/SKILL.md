# Coaching Feedback And Agent Development

Coaching should change future support behavior. Vague praise, generic criticism, or score-focused feedback rarely helps agents improve. Poor coaching can also blame agents for unclear policy, broken tools, or impossible queue conditions. This skill helps the agent write feedback that is evidence-based, fair, specific, and tied to customer outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Anchor feedback in observable behavior

Use case evidence: missed verification, repeated question, unclear next step, unsupported promise, privacy-safe handling, strong de-escalation, or complete escalation packet. Avoid personality judgments.

Feedback should describe what the agent did and what effect it had.

### Link behavior to customer or risk impact

Explain why the behavior matters: customer repeated effort, policy risk, delayed resolution, privacy exposure, trust repair, or successful ownership. This helps the agent generalize beyond one case.

Do not say "do better" without the operational consequence.

### Separate skill gap from system gap

Before coaching, check whether the agent had correct macro, knowledge, tool access, staffing, policy clarity, training, language support, and escalation path. If the system caused the miss, coach differently and route the system defect.

Fair coaching improves both person and process.

### Provide a concrete next behavior

Give the agent a specific action to try next time: verify billing authority before invoice details, summarize prior history before asking questions, state update checkpoint, route privacy requests, or include evidence in escalation packet.

The agent should leave knowing what to do differently.

### Balance strengths and corrections honestly

Name strengths when they are real and relevant, not as filler. If the case had a severe defect, be direct. If the agent handled hard context well but missed one step, say that.

Coaching should be precise, not artificially balanced.

### Adapt coaching to experience and pattern

New agents may need explanation and examples. Experienced agents may need pattern recognition, calibration, or accountability. A one-off miss needs different handling from repeated behavior.

Do not treat every QA finding as the same coaching moment.

### Protect privacy and dignity and track whether coaching works

Do not shame agents publicly or include unnecessary customer-sensitive details in coaching records. Keep feedback professional and focused on behavior. If the case involves trauma, harassment, safety, or legal material, limit exposure.

Coaching quality includes how coaching is delivered.

Document the behavior, action plan, owner, resources, and follow-up evidence. Review later cases to see whether behavior improved. If not, adjust training, tools, or expectations.

Coaching is incomplete without follow-through.

### Make feedback psychologically safe but direct and coach judgment, not scripts alone

Agents need to understand the defect without feeling personally attacked. Use neutral language, specific evidence, and shared goals. For severe errors, be direct about risk and required change while keeping the conversation professional.

Softening feedback until the behavior is unclear is not kindness.

If the agent used the wrong macro, explain the decision signal they missed: sensitivity, customer impact, policy boundary, prior promise, or ownership gap. Otherwise they may simply memorize a different template and miss the next variant.

Good coaching improves pattern recognition.

### Distinguish performance, conduct, and wellness concerns and invite evidence-based response

Some patterns reflect skill gaps; others reflect workload, burnout, unclear tools, conduct problems, or repeated disregard for policy. Route each appropriately. Coaching should not substitute for staffing support, policy clarification, or formal conduct process where needed.

Misclassifying the problem leads to unfair or ineffective action.

Agents should be able to add context, missing evidence, or policy ambiguity to coaching discussions. This is not a debate club; it is a way to catch incomplete QA and system defects.

## Common Traps

- Giving feedback based on tone impressions instead of case evidence.
- Criticizing personality rather than behavior.
- Failing to explain customer or risk impact; coaching an agent for a macro, policy, or tool failure they could not control
- Providing vague advice like "be clearer" or "take ownership."; padding severe feedback with irrelevant praise
- Treating one-off and repeated defects the same; sharing sensitive case details unnecessarily in coaching
- Not giving the agent a next behavior to practice; never checking whether coaching changed later work
- Making feedback so gentle that the required behavior change is invisible; coaching only the macro choice instead of the judgment signal behind it
- Treating burnout, workload, unclear tooling, or conduct issues as ordinary skill gaps; giving coaching with no channel for the agent to surface missing context or system blockers

## Self-Check

- Is feedback grounded in observable case behavior?
- Does it explain customer, policy, privacy, safety, trust, or resolution impact?
- Were macro, knowledge, tool, staffing, policy, language, training, and escalation conditions considered?
- Is the next behavior concrete enough to practice?
- Are strengths and corrections specific and honest?
- Is coaching adapted to agent experience and whether the issue is one-off or repeated?
- Are privacy, dignity, and sensitive case limits protected?; are resources, examples, or practice opportunities provided where useful?
- Is follow-up evidence defined?; are system defects routed separately from agent coaching?
- Is the feedback direct enough to change behavior while avoiding personal attack?; does it teach the judgment signal, not only the correct script?
- Were performance, conduct, workload, wellness, tooling, and policy clarity distinguished?; can the agent provide evidence-based context without derailing accountability?
