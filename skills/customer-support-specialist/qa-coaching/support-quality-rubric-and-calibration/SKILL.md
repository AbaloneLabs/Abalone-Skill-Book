# Support Quality Rubric And Calibration

A support quality rubric defines what the organization rewards and corrects. If the rubric overweights tone, speed, or policy recitation, agents may miss privacy, ownership, customer outcome, and follow-through. This skill helps the agent build and apply rubrics that evaluate real support quality, not just surface polish.

## Core Rules

### Measure outcome and risk, not just writing quality

Rubrics should evaluate whether the issue was understood, correctly resolved or routed, privacy was protected, policy was applied accurately, commitments were tracked, and customer effort was reduced. Tone matters, but it is not the whole quality picture.

Do not let a well-written wrong answer score highly.

### Define observable criteria

Each score item should be based on evidence in the case: facts checked, policy cited, next step stated, sensitive data avoided, escalation packet complete, or promise documented. Avoid vague criteria such as "good ownership" unless examples define it.

Reviewers need to score behavior, not impressions.

### Calibrate against edge cases

Use examples covering billing, access, safety, privacy, legal, fraud, emotional complaints, technical troubleshooting, incidents, and routine cases. Calibration should include cases where tone is good but decision is wrong, and cases where answer is firm but correct.

Without edge cases, reviewers drift toward personal preference.

### Weight high-risk failures appropriately

Privacy leaks, unauthorized refunds, unsafe advice, missed safety signals, legal mishandling, account takeover risk, or false promises should carry heavier consequences than minor phrasing issues. The score should reflect harm.

A single severe miss may outweigh several style strengths.

### Separate agent error from system error

If the agent followed a bad macro, stale policy, broken tool, missing translation, or unclear escalation path, record both the case score and the system defect. Coaching the agent alone will not fix systemic causes.

Rubrics should produce operational learning.

### Include customer effort and continuity

Evaluate whether the agent used existing context, avoided repeated questions, preserved promises, documented handoff, and made the next step clear. Customers experience quality across the case, not one message.

Do not score a single reply in isolation when history matters.

### Maintain reviewer alignment

Reviewers should compare sample scores, discuss disagreements, update examples, and document rule interpretations. Calibration is ongoing because products, policies, and support channels change.

Uncalibrated QA becomes reviewer opinion.

### Use scores for improvement, not punishment alone and define critical failures and recovery credit

The rubric should identify coachable behaviors, training needs, knowledge gaps, macro defects, and process risks. If scores only rank agents, quality signals may be hidden.

QA should improve the system as well as individual performance.

Some failures should cap or fail a score regardless of other strengths: privacy exposure, unsafe advice, unauthorized money movement, legal mishandling, account takeover enablement, or discriminatory behavior. Also define when an agent deserves credit for catching and correcting earlier support mistakes.

Rubrics need severity logic, not just additive points.

### Include appeal and reviewer notes and watch rubric drift over time

Agents and leads should be able to challenge scores when evidence was missed, policy changed, system context was unavailable, or reviewer interpretation was inconsistent. Reviewer notes should explain score reasoning enough for calibration and appeal.

QA credibility depends on transparent reasoning.

As products, policies, automation, customer expectations, and channels change, rubric examples can become stale. Review whether the rubric still rewards the behavior the organization wants, especially after incidents, policy changes, or major launches.

A once-good rubric can become a source of bad incentives.

## Common Traps

- Giving high scores to polished but inaccurate responses.
- Using vague categories that reviewers interpret differently.
- Calibrating only on routine cases.
- Underweighting privacy, safety, legal, security, and money-risk failures; blaming agents for broken macros, stale knowledge, or tool gaps
- Scoring one message without ticket history; letting reviewer preferences about tone dominate
- Failing to update rubric after policy or product changes; using QA only for discipline rather than learning
- Ignoring customer outcome after the reviewed response; letting severe safety, privacy, legal, or money failures be offset by tone points
- Having no appeal path when reviewer interpretation or policy context is disputed; keeping old rubric examples after product, policy, or automation changes

## Self-Check

- Does the rubric measure understanding, resolution, routing, privacy, policy, commitments, effort, and outcome?
- Are criteria observable in the case record?
- Are examples provided for ambiguous items?
- Does calibration include billing, access, safety, privacy, legal, fraud, emotional, technical, incident, and routine cases?
- Are high-risk failures weighted according to harm?
- Are system defects separated from agent behavior?
- Does scoring account for prior history, repeated questions, handoffs, and promises?; are reviewer disagreements discussed and documented?
- Is the rubric updated after product, policy, channel, or process changes?; do scores feed coaching, training, knowledge, macro, and process improvements?
- Are critical failures and score caps defined for severe risk?; is recovery credit defined when agents catch and repair earlier mistakes?
- Are reviewer notes sufficient for calibration and appeal?; is rubric drift reviewed after launches, incidents, automation changes, and policy updates?
