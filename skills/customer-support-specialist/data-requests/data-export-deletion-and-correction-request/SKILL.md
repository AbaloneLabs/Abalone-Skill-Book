# Data Export Deletion And Correction Request

Data rights requests are not ordinary account support. They can affect legal obligations, security, retention rules, billing records, fraud evidence, product functionality, and the rights of other people. Agents often move too quickly because the request sounds simple: "send me my data" or "delete my account." This skill helps the agent handle data export, deletion, and correction requests with careful scope, verification, and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Classify the request before acting

Determine whether the customer is asking for access, export, deletion, correction, restriction, portability, account closure, marketing opt-out, support transcript copy, or a narrower operational change. Similar wording can imply different obligations and workflows.

Do not collapse all privacy requests into "delete account." A customer may want to stop emails, remove a payment method, correct a name, export invoices, delete optional profile data, or exercise a formal legal right.

### Verify identity and authority

Before disclosing, deleting, or correcting data, follow the approved verification path. Check whether the requester is the account owner, admin, authorized user, parent or guardian, legal representative, enterprise contact, reseller, estate representative, or someone asking about another person.

Verification should be strong enough for the sensitivity of the data and action. A low-friction support identity check may be insufficient for exporting data, deleting records, or changing personal information.

### Define scope precisely

Clarify which account, product, workspace, region, data type, time period, user, order, device, or communication channel is included. Multi-user accounts, organizations, family accounts, shared inboxes, marketplace orders, and enterprise tenants often contain data relating to more than one person.

Do not disclose another person's data inside an export or erase records that the requester has no authority to control.

### Check retention and exception rules

Some data cannot be deleted immediately or completely because of legal retention, tax, accounting, fraud prevention, chargebacks, security, disputes, warranty, safety, abuse enforcement, backups, or contractual obligations. Other data may be anonymized, restricted, or scheduled for deletion rather than removed instantly.

Explain limits carefully and avoid legal conclusions unless approved. If the request touches regulated or disputed records, escalate to privacy, legal, compliance, or security.

### Explain product consequences

Deletion or correction can break access, remove history, cancel subscriptions, affect invoices, disable personalization, close cases, alter team visibility, or prevent recovery. Make consequences clear before irreversible action.

For enterprise or shared accounts, explain which effects apply to the requester versus the organization. Do not let one user unintentionally destroy shared business records.

### Keep an auditable record

Record request type, verification method, scope, decision, data categories, exclusions, owner, timestamps, customer notices, and completion status. Privacy requests may later be reviewed through audits, complaints, regulatory inquiries, or disputes.

The record should prove that the team followed process without storing unnecessary sensitive data.

### Communicate deadlines and handoffs

Data requests may have statutory or policy deadlines. Tell the customer what was received, what is needed, expected timing, and how they will be updated. If the request moves to a privacy team, make clear that support is not deciding the legal outcome.

Do not let a privacy request sit inside a generic queue with no owner.

### Handle partial fulfillment explicitly

Many data requests are not simply approved or denied. The team may export some categories, withhold records involving other people, retain invoices, anonymize analytics, preserve fraud evidence, or schedule deletion after a retention period. Explain partial outcomes in plain language using approved reasons.

Do not make the customer infer what happened from a silent export file or a generic completion notice. If some data was excluded, corrected, retained, or queued for later deletion, the response should state that a limit exists and where the customer can ask follow-up questions.

### Coordinate processors and downstream systems

Customer data may live in billing tools, marketing systems, support platforms, analytics, warehouses, backups, fulfillment partners, app stores, marketplace partners, and identity providers. Support should not promise completion across all systems unless the privacy workflow covers those systems.

If downstream deletion or correction is asynchronous, track status and customer messaging. A request is weakly handled if the main account is changed but connected systems keep sending messages or using stale information.

## Common Traps

- Treating "delete my data" as ordinary account cancellation without privacy review.
- Exporting data after weak identity verification.
- Sending organization, family, or shared-account data to one requester without authority.
- Forgetting data in support transcripts, logs, backups, invoices, marketing systems, or third-party processors.
- Promising complete deletion despite retention, fraud, legal, tax, chargeback, safety, or security obligations; correcting records without preserving the basis for the correction
- Failing to explain account, subscription, workspace, or recovery consequences; giving legal interpretations beyond approved language
- Losing the request in a general support queue; sending a completion notice without explaining partial fulfillment, exclusions, retained records, or delayed deletion
- Forgetting processors, marketing tools, support systems, backups, warehouses, and partners that may keep stale data; keeping too much sensitive evidence in the support note while trying to document the request

## Self-Check

- Has the request type been classified as access, export, deletion, correction, restriction, portability, closure, opt-out, or transcript request?
- Is requester identity and authority verified at a level appropriate to the data and action?
- Are account, product, workspace, region, data type, time period, user, and channel scope defined?
- Have shared-account, enterprise, family, marketplace, reseller, and third-party data boundaries been checked?
- Are retention, tax, accounting, fraud, chargeback, security, warranty, safety, abuse, backup, and legal exceptions considered?
- Are privacy, legal, compliance, or security teams involved where required?
- Are product and account consequences explained before irreversible action?
- Is the request record auditable without excessive sensitive data?
- Are deadlines, missing information, handoff owner, and update expectations clear?; does the response avoid legal advice while accurately explaining process and limits?
- Are partial approvals, denials, exclusions, retained records, anonymization, and delayed deletion explained using approved reasons?; are downstream processors and connected systems included in tracking or clearly excluded from the support promise?
