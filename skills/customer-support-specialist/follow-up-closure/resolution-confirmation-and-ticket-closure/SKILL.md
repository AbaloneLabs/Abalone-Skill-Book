# Resolution Confirmation And Ticket Closure

Closing a ticket is an operational decision, not a housekeeping step. Premature closure hides unresolved harm, breaks promises, distorts metrics, and forces customers to repeat context. Over-delayed closure can also clutter queues and confuse ownership. This skill helps the agent decide when closure is justified and how to leave a clear final state.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the customer's actual issue is addressed

Compare closure against the customer's original request, later clarifications, described impact, and any additional issues discovered. A ticket may have an answer but not a resolution. For example, explaining a charge does not resolve a refund request, and sending a workaround does not fix the underlying bug.

Do not close because the agent completed their own task if the customer outcome remains open.

### Distinguish resolved, mitigated, denied, and transferred

Resolved means the issue is fixed or request completed. Mitigated means the customer has a temporary path. Denied means the request cannot be granted and the decision has been explained. Transferred means another owner has accepted responsibility. These should not be collapsed into "closed."

Use the right final state so reporting and future agents understand the outcome.

### Check open commitments

Before closure, review promised updates, refunds, credits, replacements, callbacks, investigations, bug follow-ups, escalations, documentation, and customer actions. If any promise remains open, either complete it, transfer it with owner, or keep the case open.

A closure that loses a promise creates avoidable trust damage.

### Confirm customer-facing expectation

The final response should state what was done, what remains unavailable, what the customer can do next, and how to reopen or follow up if applicable. If no response is needed due to automation or duplicate handling, the record should still explain why.

Do not let closure happen silently after a confusing or high-impact case.

### Preserve unresolved risks

Security, privacy, safety, legal, billing, fraud, incident, and accessibility issues may have residual risks even after the immediate support action. Ensure those are routed, documented, or explicitly resolved before closing the support-facing case.

Ticket closure should not erase risk ownership.

### Avoid metric-driven closure

Closing to reduce backlog, hit response metrics, or avoid aging can make customer experience worse. Conversely, leaving solved cases open to avoid CSAT triggers or personal accountability distorts queue health. Close based on outcome and ownership.

Metrics are useful only when closure means something real.

### Handle no-response cases carefully

If support needs customer information and the customer does not reply, closure may be appropriate after clear requests and timing. The final note should state what was requested, when, what remains unresolved, and how the customer can continue later.

Do not close high-risk cases solely because the customer went silent if safety, security, legal, or money risk remains.

### Document the final state

Record outcome, evidence, actions completed, customer message, open items transferred, promises fulfilled, unresolved risk, and closure reason. Future agents should quickly know whether to reopen, start fresh, or route elsewhere.

Closure documentation is what makes the next contact less painful.

### Check automation and survey effects

Many systems auto-close after inactivity, send satisfaction surveys, suppress replies, or change SLA state when a ticket is marked solved. Before closure, consider whether automation will misrepresent the outcome, trigger a survey during unresolved harm, or prevent the customer from replying easily.

If automation may confuse the customer, set the correct status, pause closure, or explain the next route.

## Common Traps

- Closing after answering one question while related issues remain unresolved.
- Treating workaround, denial, transfer, and resolution as the same state.
- Missing open promises from earlier in the thread.
- Closing escalated cases before the receiving owner accepts responsibility.
- Letting security, privacy, safety, legal, billing, or fraud risk disappear under closure; closing to improve backlog or aging metrics
- Leaving solved cases open indefinitely with no owner; closing no-response cases without a clear final request and continuation path
- Sending a final message that says "resolved" when the customer still needs action; failing to document why closure was appropriate
- Letting auto-close mark a high-risk case solved because the customer went silent; triggering closure surveys or automation before the customer-facing outcome is clear

## Self-Check

- Does closure address the customer's original request, later clarifications, impact, and related issues?
- Is the final state correctly labeled as resolved, mitigated, denied, transferred, duplicate, or no-response?
- Are all promised updates, refunds, credits, replacements, callbacks, investigations, bug follow-ups, escalations, and customer actions complete or owned?
- Does the final response state what was done, what remains, next path, and reopening route where appropriate?
- Are residual security, privacy, safety, legal, billing, fraud, incident, and accessibility risks routed or resolved?
- Is closure based on outcome and ownership rather than metrics?
- If customer is silent, were requests clear and timing appropriate?
- Are high-risk silent cases kept routed or owned as required?
- Are outcome, evidence, actions, message, transfers, promises, risks, and closure reason documented?; would a future agent know whether to reopen, start fresh, or route elsewhere?
- Were auto-close, survey, SLA, reply suppression, and solved-status effects considered?; if automation may mislead the customer or hide risk, was status adjusted or explanation provided?
