# Reopening And Repeat Issue Handling

A reopened or repeat issue is a signal that the prior resolution may have been incomplete, misunderstood, temporary, or wrong. Agents often treat the new contact as a fresh ticket and make the customer repeat everything. This skill helps the agent use history, identify why the issue returned, and avoid another shallow closure.

## Core Rules

### Start with the prior case history

Read the previous issue, actions taken, promises, customer response, evidence, escalation, closure reason, and final state. Identify what was supposed to happen after closure. Do not ask the customer to restate details already available.

Repeat contact should lower customer effort, not reset it.

### Classify why the case reopened

Reopen reasons include unresolved original issue, workaround failed, issue recurred, customer misunderstood, support gave wrong answer, promise missed, new related issue, duplicate ticket, customer added evidence, or downstream owner did not act. The response depends on the reason.

Do not treat every reopen as customer confusion.

### Acknowledge repeat effort

If the customer has contacted support multiple times, acknowledge the repeated effort and state that you reviewed the prior context. This is not an empty apology; it reduces the customer's need to prove history.

Avoid saying "as previously stated" in a way that sounds dismissive.

### Look for pattern and severity change

Repeated contacts may reveal incident, product defect, billing system problem, account takeover, accessibility issue, fraud, process gap, or training problem. Check whether impact has increased, deadlines passed, money moved, access is still blocked, or prior workaround failed.

Do not keep applying the same low-severity handling if repetition shows deeper risk.

### Avoid repeating failed steps

Before suggesting troubleshooting, policy explanation, refund review, or escalation, check what was already tried and whether it worked. If repeating a step is necessary, explain why the context changed.

Repetition without reason feels like support is not listening.

### Decide whether to reopen, merge, escalate, or start new

If the issue is the same and unresolved, reopen or continue the existing case. If it is related but distinct, link context. If several duplicates exist, merge or cross-reference. If prior handling failed, escalate with history.

Ticket hygiene should serve customer continuity.

### Repair broken expectations

If support missed a promise, closed prematurely, gave conflicting answers, or routed poorly, address that directly. Set a new owner and checkpoint. Do not hide process failure behind another generic response.

Service recovery may be needed in addition to solving the original issue.

### Document repeat-contact learning

Record reopen reason, prior actions, failed steps, new evidence, severity change, escalation path, customer expectation, and final resolution. Feed patterns to knowledge, QA, product, billing, or operations where appropriate.

Repeat issues are process data.

### Distinguish duplicate noise from repeated harm and identify the failed control point

Multiple tickets may come from impatience, channel switching, automation, or unclear ownership; they may also show that the customer is still harmed. Before closing duplicates, check whether each contact contains new evidence, changed impact, missed deadline, or a different affected user.

Duplicate cleanup should not erase important new information.

When an issue repeats, ask which control failed: intake classification, evidence capture, troubleshooting, policy explanation, escalation acceptance, ownership transfer, customer instruction, product defect, billing state, fulfillment action, or closure. Naming the failed control point helps prevent the same loop from happening again.

Do not simply apologize for recurrence without changing the process step that failed.

## Common Traps

- Treating reopened tickets as unrelated new tickets.
- Asking the customer to repeat information already in history.
- Assuming the customer misunderstood rather than checking support failure; repeating troubleshooting steps that already failed
- Missing increased severity because the case is old; closing duplicates without linking or preserving context
- Ignoring missed promises and conflicting prior answers; apologizing for repeat contact but not changing ownership or path
- Failing to escalate after multiple unsuccessful support loops; not feeding repeated patterns to operations or product owners
- Closing duplicates mechanically and losing new evidence or severity changes; treating repeat contact as customer impatience when support ownership was unclear
- Reopening the ticket but leaving the same failed owner, queue, macro, or troubleshooting path in place; treating repeat contact as a customer-communication problem when the operational control failed

## Self-Check

- Were previous issue, actions, evidence, promises, escalation, closure reason, and final state reviewed?
- Is the reopen reason classified: unresolved, recurrence, failed workaround, misunderstanding, wrong answer, missed promise, new related issue, duplicate, new evidence, or downstream failure?
- Does the response acknowledge repeated effort and prior context?
- Were pattern and severity changes checked across incident, defect, billing, access, fraud, accessibility, and process risk?
- Are failed steps avoided unless a reason for retry is explained?
- Is ticket handling correct: reopen, merge, link, escalate, or start new?
- Are broken expectations, missed promises, premature closure, and conflicting answers repaired directly?; is a new owner, checkpoint, or escalation path clear?
- Are reopen reason, prior actions, new evidence, severity, path, expectation, and resolution documented?; are repeat-contact patterns fed to knowledge, QA, product, billing, or operations where useful?
- Before closing duplicates, was each contact checked for new evidence, changed impact, missed deadline, or different affected user?; does duplicate handling preserve continuity and customer-visible ownership?
- Is the failed control point identified across intake, evidence, troubleshooting, policy, escalation, ownership, instruction, product, billing, fulfillment, or closure?; did the new handling change the failed control point rather than repeat it?
