# Customer Action Request And Instruction Writing

Support often needs the customer to do something: confirm details, try a step, send evidence, restart a device, change a setting, approve a charge, or use a secure form. Poorly written requests create repeated contacts, failed troubleshooting, privacy risk, and customer frustration. This skill helps the agent ask for action only when needed and write instructions that customers can safely complete.

## Core Rules

### Confirm that customer action is necessary

Before asking the customer to act, check whether support can resolve or verify the issue internally. Review account data, order records, logs, attachments, prior tickets, known issues, subscription state, and tool access. A request is justified when it changes the next decision or cannot be performed safely by support.

Do not ask the customer to do work because the macro expects it or because it is faster for the agent.

### Ask for the smallest useful action

Minimize steps and information. If one screenshot, one timestamp, or one confirmation will unblock the case, do not ask for a full diagnostic package. If a troubleshooting step is only relevant after another result, sequence it rather than sending a long list.

Small, targeted requests improve completion rates and reduce errors.

### Explain the purpose of the request

Customers are more likely to comply when they understand why the action matters. Briefly connect the request to the decision: verifying ownership, matching a charge, reproducing a bug, ruling out a local setting, protecting the account, or confirming eligibility.

Do not over-explain internal mechanics. Give enough reason for trust and accuracy.

### Write steps in the customer's environment

Use product names, platform labels, device type, browser, app version, language, region, and permission level that match the customer's context when known. If instructions vary, state the branch clearly. Avoid assuming the customer has admin access, desktop access, current app version, strong connectivity, or technical vocabulary.

If context is unknown, ask the one or two questions needed before giving brittle instructions.

### Keep instructions ordered and verifiable

Use numbered steps for sequences. Put warnings before risky actions. Tell the customer what result to expect and what to report back. For troubleshooting, ask for the exact error, behavior, timestamp, or screenshot after the step if it fails.

Do not write paragraphs that hide multiple actions in one sentence.

### Protect privacy and security

Never request passwords, full payment numbers, private keys, tokens, recovery codes, unnecessary government IDs, or sensitive files through ordinary support replies. If evidence is needed, instruct the customer to redact irrelevant sensitive data or use the approved secure channel.

If a step could expose data, log the customer out, change permissions, delete content, affect billing, or interrupt service, warn them first and confirm the action is appropriate.

### Account for accessibility and capacity

Some customers cannot complete visual, audio, dexterity-heavy, time-sensitive, or technically complex steps. Offer alternatives where possible: written instructions, secure upload, assisted workflow, callback, accessible format, delegated admin, or internal review.

Do not assume unwillingness when the barrier may be disability, language, device limitation, workplace policy, or stress.

### Make failure paths explicit

Instructions should say what to do if the step does not work. Provide the exact information to send back, the fallback route, or the condition for escalation. This prevents customers from disappearing after a failed step or sending unhelpful replies like "still broken."

If a workaround is temporary, state its limits and what long-term resolution remains open.

## Common Traps

- Asking the customer to provide information support already has.
- Sending a long generic checklist when one targeted action would decide the next step.
- Omitting why the request matters and making it feel like busywork.
- Assuming platform, language, permissions, technical skill, or accessibility needs.
- Combining several actions in one paragraph so the customer misses a step.
- Putting warnings after the risky instruction rather than before it.
- Asking for sensitive evidence through an unsafe channel; failing to tell the customer what result to expect or what to report back
- Treating inability to complete a step as non-cooperation; giving a workaround without explaining whether it is temporary and what remains unresolved

## Self-Check

- Was internal context checked before asking the customer to act?
- Does each requested action support a specific decision or next step?
- Is the request the smallest useful action rather than a broad checklist?
- Does the message explain why the action, evidence, or confirmation is needed?
- Are instructions adapted to the customer's product, platform, device, language, region, permissions, and version where known?
- Are steps ordered, numbered when sequential, and written with expected results?
- Are warnings placed before actions that could affect data, access, billing, permissions, or service continuity?
- Are privacy and security protected through redaction, secure upload, and avoidance of passwords, full payment data, tokens, keys, and unnecessary documents?
- Are accessibility, language, device, workplace, and capacity barriers considered with alternatives where possible?
- Does the customer know what to send back or what will happen if the step fails?
