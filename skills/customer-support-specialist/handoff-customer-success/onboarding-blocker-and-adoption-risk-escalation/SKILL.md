# Onboarding Blocker And Adoption Risk Escalation

Onboarding blockers are not just setup questions. They can signal that a customer will never reach value, will churn before renewal, will misuse the product, or will blame support for a failed rollout. Agents often answer the immediate ticket but miss the broader adoption risk. This skill helps the agent recognize and escalate onboarding blockers with enough context for customer success and operations to act.

## Core Rules

### Define the blocked milestone

Identify what milestone the customer cannot complete: account creation, user invite, SSO, billing activation, data import, integration, first transaction, first report, workflow launch, training, migration, or stakeholder approval. A vague "setup problem" is not enough.

Milestone clarity lets teams judge urgency and owner.

### Assess adoption impact

Ask whether the blocker prevents first value, affects many users, delays launch, risks renewal, blocks revenue, creates executive concern, or causes the customer to revert to another tool. Include deadlines and business context.

The same technical blocker may be low impact for one account and critical for another.

### Separate customer readiness from product failure

Some blockers come from missing permissions, unclear internal ownership, lack of training, dirty data, stakeholder disagreement, or customer process gaps. Others come from product bugs, plan limits, missing features, documentation gaps, or integration failures.

Escalate to the owner that can change the outcome, not only the team that answers fastest.

### Preserve the setup sequence

Record steps already completed, current configuration, failed step, error, workaround, dependencies, and what should not be changed while investigation continues. This prevents success or implementation teams from restarting setup blindly.

For migrations or integrations, include data state and rollback concerns.

### Escalate with a requested action

Customer success may need to schedule training, align stakeholders, reset expectations, involve implementation, review use case fit, or manage relationship risk. Product or engineering may need to investigate defects. Billing may need to unblock payment.

State the requested action, not just "please advise."

### Avoid masking risk with one-off fixes

A support workaround can get past today's blocker while leaving the customer unable to operate independently. If the customer needs repeated manual help, custom steps, or unsupported configuration, escalate adoption risk.

First value should be repeatable by the customer.

### Set customer expectations for handoff

Tell the customer who will help next, what remains under support ownership, what information has been shared, and when they can expect contact. Avoid creating a mystery transfer.

If support is still investigating, keep the update cadence.

### Close the loop after intervention

Verify whether the blocker was removed, first value happened, training was delivered, product issue was logged, or the adoption risk remains. Update tags and success notes.

### Define risk tiers and triggers and preserve customer project timeline

Not every setup issue needs the same escalation. Define triggers such as executive sponsor blocked, launch date within a week, no first value after repeated contacts, data migration at risk, payment activation blocked, security review stalled, high user count affected, or customer threatening to abandon rollout.

Risk tiers help support escalate consistently instead of relying on whichever customer sounds most urgent.

Onboarding exists inside the customer's project plan. Capture go-live date, training date, migration window, stakeholder meeting, renewal date, procurement milestone, or public launch if known. If support cannot meet that timeline, escalate expectation management early.

This turns setup support from reactive troubleshooting into adoption-risk management.

## Common Traps

- Treating onboarding blockers as isolated how-to tickets.
- Escalating without naming the blocked milestone.
- Ignoring customer deadline, launch date, stakeholder pressure, or renewal risk.
- Blaming customer readiness when the product or documentation is unclear.
- Sending success a ticket link with no sequence, state, or requested action; providing manual workarounds that hide long-term adoption failure
- Letting implementation, product, billing, and support each assume another team owns the blocker; telling the customer someone will reach out without confirming owner or timing
- Closing after first value without checking whether the customer can repeat the workflow; failing to tag onboarding blocker patterns for product or operations
- Escalating based on agent intuition instead of defined risk triggers; missing the customer's project timeline and discovering too late that support timing broke adoption

## Self-Check

- Is the blocked milestone clearly named?
- Does the escalation explain first value, user impact, launch delay, renewal risk, revenue, executive concern, and fallback risk where relevant?
- Is the cause classified as customer readiness, product defect, plan limit, missing feature, documentation gap, integration failure, billing block, or stakeholder issue?
- Are completed steps, current configuration, failed step, error, workaround, dependencies, and do-not-change warnings recorded?
- Are migration, integration, data state, and rollback concerns included?
- Is the requested action explicit for success, implementation, product, engineering, billing, or operations?
- Are one-off support workarounds assessed for long-term adoption risk?
- Does the customer know next owner, timing, shared context, and remaining support ownership?
- Was the outcome checked after intervention?; are repeated blockers tagged for product, training, knowledge, or operations review?
- Are escalation triggers and risk tier defined for the blocker?; is the customer's project timeline captured and used for expectation management?
