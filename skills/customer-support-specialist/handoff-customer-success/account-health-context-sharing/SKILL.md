# Account Health Context Sharing

Support records contain strong account-health signals, but raw ticket counts rarely tell the story. One severe data-loss case may matter more than twenty password questions. A frustrated admin may signal renewal risk. Repeated billing confusion may reveal packaging misfit. This skill helps the agent share account-health context that is useful to customer success without violating privacy or overwhelming account teams.

## Core Rules

### Summarize patterns, not ticket dumps

Share the main support themes, severity, customer goals, unresolved blockers, repeated contacts, sentiment, and commitments. Link tickets for evidence, but do not make customer success read every case to understand the risk.

An account-health note should be concise enough to act on.

### Distinguish volume from risk

High ticket volume can mean poor product fit, rapid adoption, many users, training gaps, or a single noisy issue. Low volume can hide silent churn or executive dissatisfaction. Explain what the support pattern likely means and what evidence supports it.

Do not equate contact count with health without context.

### Include customer impact and business context

Where known, state affected teams, workflows, deadlines, revenue, launch plans, compliance concerns, executive attention, or customer-facing impact. This helps success prioritize outreach.

If business context is unknown, say so rather than inventing it.

### Track unresolved commitments

Account teams need to know open promises: engineering update, refund review, bug fix, onboarding call, workaround, escalation, incident follow-up, data recovery, or policy decision. Include owner and date.

Untracked promises create relationship damage when the customer repeats them in another meeting.

### Share sensitive context carefully

Support may contain private data, identity verification, security, abuse, legal, health, hardship, employee complaints, or payment details. Share only what account teams need and are authorized to know. Redact or summarize sensitive signals.

If a sensitive issue changes account handling, route through the proper owner rather than spreading details broadly.

### Use account-health taxonomy

Classify signals such as adoption blocker, renewal risk, executive escalation, repeated defect, billing friction, sentiment decline, training need, product gap, incident impact, support quality concern, or expansion blocker.

Consistent taxonomy lets account teams and operations compare accounts.

### Include recommended next action

Suggest what customer success should do: outreach, stakeholder alignment, training, renewal risk review, executive brief, product feedback, billing review, implementation help, or no immediate action. Make clear whether support expects response.

### Update context after major events and include positive recovery signals

After incident resolution, bug fix, refund decision, escalation closure, or successful onboarding, update the health context. Old support risk can linger in account records and distort future decisions.

Account health is not only risk. If a customer recovered after a difficult incident, adopted a workaround successfully, praised a support interaction, completed onboarding, or confirmed a renewal blocker was removed, include that context. Positive recovery prevents old support pain from overstating current risk.

Be specific. "Looks better" is less useful than "admin confirmed import completed and training scheduled."

### Guard against stale or duplicated context and make support confidence visible

Account teams may read notes long after the original case. Date health notes, mark outdated risks, and merge duplicate summaries where possible. A stale note about an unresolved bug or unhappy executive can mislead sales, success, and leadership.

If support does not know whether the risk remains, label it as unknown and assign follow-up.

Account-health context should state whether the signal is confirmed, likely, emerging, or anecdotal. A single angry ticket, a repeated pattern across admins, and a documented renewal blocker should not be weighted the same way.

Confidence labels help customer success decide whether to contact the customer immediately, monitor, ask support for more evidence, or wait for a clearer pattern. They also prevent support from overstating risk when the evidence is still thin.

## Common Traps

- Sending account teams a pile of ticket links without synthesis.
- Treating ticket count as account health.
- Omitting business impact, affected workflow, deadlines, or sentiment.
- Forgetting open promises and owners; sharing private security, abuse, legal, hardship, identity, or payment details unnecessarily
- Using inconsistent labels that account teams cannot aggregate; providing risk context with no recommended action
- Overstating churn risk because a single customer sounded angry; failing to update account notes after resolution
- Letting account teams contact customers with stale or incomplete support context; sharing only negative risk signals and omitting recovery or resolved-blocker evidence
- Leaving old account-health notes undated, duplicated, or unmarked after risk changes; presenting anecdotal, emerging, likely, and confirmed account risk with the same weight

## Self-Check

- Does the account note summarize themes, severity, goals, blockers, repeat contacts, sentiment, and commitments?
- Are ticket links used as evidence rather than the whole message?
- Is volume interpreted with context rather than treated as health by itself?
- Are affected teams, workflows, deadlines, revenue, launch, compliance, executive, and customer-facing impacts included where known?
- Are unresolved promises listed with owner and date?
- Are privacy, security, abuse, legal, hardship, identity, and payment details minimized or routed properly?
- Is account-health taxonomy applied consistently?
- Is a recommended next action included?; is it clear whether support needs a response from success?
- Is account context updated after major resolution or risk change?; are positive recovery, completed onboarding, praised support, and removed-blocker signals included where relevant?
- Are account-health notes dated, deduplicated, and marked current, resolved, or unknown?; is confidence labeled so success can distinguish anecdote, emerging signal, likely risk, and confirmed risk?
