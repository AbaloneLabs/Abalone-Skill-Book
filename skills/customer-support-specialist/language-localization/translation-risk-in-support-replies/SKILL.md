# Translation Risk In Support Replies

A translated support reply can create a commitment the original did not make. Small changes in tense, politeness, negation, or policy terms can turn "we will review" into "we will approve," or "may be eligible" into "is eligible." Translation risk is highest when replies involve money, legal rights, safety, identity, data, enforcement, warranties, or public complaints. This skill helps the agent review translated replies for meaning, tone, and commitment control.

## Core Rules

### Translate intent before style

The translated reply should preserve the support intent: acknowledge, ask for evidence, deny, approve, escalate, explain policy, provide steps, or set a boundary. Do not prioritize fluent style if it changes the action or commitment.

If the source message is unclear, fix the source before translating. Ambiguity becomes harder to detect after translation.

### Protect conditional language

Words such as may, might, eligible, pending review, if, after verification, estimated, expected, and subject to policy often carry risk. Ensure the translation preserves uncertainty and conditions.

This matters for refunds, replacements, cancellations, chargebacks, account reinstatement, warranty coverage, shipping dates, legal requests, and incident restoration.

### Review tone in cultural context

Tone does not translate directly. A direct denial may sound rude in one language; a warm apology may sound like liability in another; informal language may feel disrespectful in a formal support culture.

Use approved localization guidance or native review for sensitive cases. Avoid humor, idioms, and emotional overstatement unless the organization has localized them deliberately.

### Keep placeholders and variables safe

Check names, pronouns, dates, currency, tax, addresses, plan names, product names, ticket IDs, order IDs, and links after translation. Placeholders can move, break, or become grammatically wrong.

Never let machine translation alter URLs, codes, legal names, or product identifiers.

### Preserve policy and legal boundaries

Do not translate legal, privacy, safety, tax, warranty, or enforcement language casually. Use approved localized language where available. If not available, escalate or send a plain process explanation without adding legal interpretations.

Avoid translating "not legal advice" or required disclosures in a way that weakens the boundary.

### Adapt examples and instructions

Screens, menu names, address formats, date formats, payment methods, phone numbers, and regional settings may differ. A translated step-by-step reply should match the customer's localized interface where possible.

If the product is not localized, explain which English term the customer will see.

### Validate the final answer as a support artifact

Read the translated reply as if it were the only message the customer sees. Does it state the right decision, next step, owner, timeline, evidence, and limitation? Does it avoid new promises?

Translation review should check operational meaning, not just grammar.

### Document translation assumptions

When a sensitive reply is translated or reviewed by a bilingual agent, record the source language, target language, reviewer if applicable, and any unresolved uncertainty. This helps later audits or escalations understand why wording was chosen.

### Maintain approved phrase libraries and review numbers, dates, and formatting separately

Repeated support concepts should have approved translations: refund eligibility, cancellation, account deletion, verification, warranty, enforcement, safety escalation, privacy request, legal process, subscription renewal, and incident update. If agents translate these independently each time, commitment language will drift.

When no approved phrase exists, flag the gap for localization or policy review. Do not let one urgent case create unofficial wording that other agents copy.

A translated sentence can be grammatically correct while dates, decimal separators, currency placement, tax labels, address order, phone format, or time zones become wrong. Review these elements as data, not prose.

This is especially important for deadlines, refund amounts, payment dates, shipping estimates, warranty periods, and legal response windows.

## Common Traps

- Translating a vague source message instead of clarifying it first.
- Changing conditional eligibility into a promise.
- Losing "pending review," "estimated," "if verified," or "subject to policy" wording.
- Adding apologies or warmth that imply fault, liability, or guaranteed remedy.
- Letting placeholders, dates, currency, addresses, or links break; translating product names, legal names, URLs, codes, or policy categories incorrectly
- Sending machine-translated legal, privacy, tax, safety, warranty, or enforcement language without review; giving steps for the wrong localized interface
- Reviewing grammar but not operational commitments; failing to record translation review for a sensitive case
- Letting agents create ad hoc translations for high-risk recurring policy phrases; missing date, decimal, currency, address, phone, and timezone formatting errors because the prose reads well

## Self-Check

- Does the translated reply preserve the original support intent?
- Was unclear source language fixed before translation?
- Are conditions, uncertainty, eligibility, estimates, verification requirements, and policy limits preserved?
- Does the tone fit the language and context without creating liability or disrespect?
- Are names, pronouns, dates, currency, tax, addresses, plan names, product names, IDs, links, and placeholders correct?
- Are legal, privacy, tax, safety, warranty, and enforcement phrases approved or escalated?
- Are required disclosures and professional-advice boundaries preserved?
- Do instructions match the localized interface, or explain customer-visible English terms?
- Does the final translated answer state the correct decision, next step, owner, timeline, evidence, and limitation?; are language, reviewer, and unresolved translation uncertainty documented for sensitive cases?
- Are high-risk recurring phrases taken from approved translation sources or flagged for localization review?; were numbers, dates, currency, tax labels, addresses, phone formats, and timezones reviewed separately from prose?
