# Abuse Report Intake And Risk Triage

Abuse reports are not ordinary complaints. They may involve immediate safety risk, legal obligations, targeted harm, coordinated behavior, vulnerable people, or evidence that can disappear. Agents can cause harm by asking the reporter to keep engaging, dismissing context, over-promising enforcement, or routing a high-risk report as a routine policy question. This skill helps the agent collect enough information to act while protecting the reporter and preserving escalation paths.

## Core Rules

### Triage for immediacy first

Look for threats of violence, self-harm, child safety, sexual exploitation, stalking, doxxing, credible real-world harm, active account compromise, extortion, dangerous products, or ongoing harassment. These signals may require urgent escalation, emergency workflows, or specialized review.

Do not bury urgent risk under ordinary queue order. If the process has emergency categories, use them. If there is no clear category, escalate to the nearest trust and safety, legal, security, or supervisor path.

### Protect the reporter

Do not require the reporter to confront the accused party, gather more evidence through risky engagement, or publicly repeat private details. Offer safe reporting channels and explain what information helps without making the reporter responsible for enforcement.

Use trauma-aware and nonjudgmental language. Avoid implying that the reporter caused the abuse or must prove intent beyond what policy requires.

### Collect structured evidence

Gather links, usernames, account IDs, timestamps, screenshots, message IDs, transaction IDs, group names, prior reports, affected users, and the reporter's immediate concern. Ask for original links where safe because screenshots may be incomplete or edited.

Preserve evidence according to policy. Some content can be deleted quickly or changed by the actor after a report.

### Classify policy domain and confidence

Separate harassment, spam, fraud, impersonation, hate, sexual content, child safety, violent threat, self-harm, privacy violation, intellectual property, marketplace abuse, platform manipulation, account takeover, and illegal content. Label confidence and uncertainty.

Misclassification can send a serious case to the wrong queue or apply the wrong enforcement standard.

### Avoid enforcement promises

Tell reporters that the team will review under policy, but do not promise removal, suspension, refund, disclosure, investigation details, or action against another user. Many platforms cannot reveal enforcement outcomes or internal signals.

Set expectations about what the reporter may or may not hear back.

### Consider retaliation and repeated harm

Some reports create retaliation risk. Think about whether notifications, public actions, direct contact, or visible moderation may expose the reporter. Note repeat behavior, multiple affected users, ban evasion, coordinated campaigns, and escalation after prior reports.

Pattern evidence can change priority and response.

### Route with enough context

Escalation should include risk category, urgency, evidence, reporter safety needs, accused actor identifiers, prior actions, jurisdiction or region if relevant, and any customer-facing promises. Trust and safety reviewers need context, not just a ticket link.

If legal, emergency, child safety, or law-enforcement rules may apply, use the approved specialized route.

### Watch for counter-reports and abuse of reporting

Reporting systems can be misused to harass, silence, retaliate, or overwhelm another user. A report may be genuine, malicious, incomplete, or part of a conflict where both sides submit claims. Intake should preserve neutrality while still prioritizing immediate safety.

Do not dismiss a report because there is conflict history, and do not accept it uncritically because the reporter sounds credible. Capture context, prior reports, linked actors, timing, and evidence quality so reviewers can evaluate policy fairly.

### Minimize exposure for agents and reviewers

Abuse evidence can include violent, sexual, hateful, exploitative, or otherwise disturbing content. Follow internal handling rules for sensitive media, content warnings, restricted access, and wellness escalation. Do not paste graphic content into broad channels when a link or controlled evidence reference is sufficient.

## Common Traps

- Treating threats, self-harm, exploitation, or doxxing as routine complaints.
- Asking the reporter to keep interacting with the abusive actor for proof.
- Collecting vague narratives without links, IDs, timestamps, or affected users.
- Promising that an account will be banned or content removed.
- Revealing enforcement details to a reporter or accused user when policy forbids it; ignoring retaliation risk after a report
- Misrouting spam, impersonation, fraud, harassment, safety, and legal issues into the same generic queue; dismissing repeated low-level behavior that forms a serious pattern
- Failing to preserve disappearing evidence; assuming every report is either fully true or malicious instead of preserving context for review
- Broadcasting graphic or sensitive abuse evidence into channels that do not need it; using skeptical or blame-focused language with a distressed reporter

## Self-Check

- Were immediate safety, self-harm, child safety, violence, doxxing, extortion, stalking, exploitation, fraud, and account-compromise signals checked?
- Is the reporter protected from unsafe evidence gathering or confrontation?
- Is the language nonjudgmental and free of blame?
- Are links, IDs, usernames, timestamps, screenshots, messages, transactions, affected users, and prior reports captured where safe?
- Is evidence preserved according to policy?
- Is the policy domain classified with confidence and uncertainty?
- Does the response avoid enforcement promises or prohibited disclosure?
- Are retaliation, repeated harm, ban evasion, and coordinated behavior considered?
- Does the escalation packet include urgency, risk category, evidence, identifiers, prior actions, safety needs, region, and promises?; are legal, emergency, child safety, security, or law-enforcement paths used where required?
- Are counter-report, retaliation, malicious-reporting, and conflict-history signals captured without losing safety priority?; is sensitive or graphic evidence handled with restricted access and appropriate care for reviewers?
