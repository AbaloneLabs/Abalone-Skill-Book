# Harassment Spam And Platform Policy Support

Customers dealing with harassment or spam often need practical safety help before formal enforcement is complete. They may be scared, angry, confused by policy, or trying to stop repeated unwanted contact. Agents can harm customers by minimizing the issue, promising enforcement, suggesting unsafe confrontation, or giving generic settings advice that does not fit the threat. This skill helps the agent provide practical support within platform policy boundaries.

## Core Rules

### Start with the customer's immediate safety and control

Ask whether the customer is facing threats, doxxing, stalking, self-harm concerns, sexual exploitation, financial scam, account takeover, or real-world danger. If urgent risk is present, escalate through the appropriate safety workflow.

For non-urgent cases, help the customer reduce exposure: block, mute, report, restrict replies, adjust privacy settings, change contact controls, secure account access, or preserve evidence. Make sure advice does not destroy needed evidence.

### Explain reporting and enforcement limits

Tell the customer how to report, what information helps, and what the review may consider. Avoid promising that content will be removed, accounts banned, messages blocked forever, or money recovered.

If the platform cannot disclose enforcement outcomes, say that clearly. Customers may still receive guidance on protective tools.

### Recognize pattern and escalation signals

Repeated low-level messages can become harassment when persistent, targeted, coordinated, sexualized, discriminatory, threatening, or tied to offline identity. Spam can become fraud when it includes payment requests, phishing, impersonation, malware, counterfeit goods, or investment claims.

Do not evaluate each message in isolation when the harm is cumulative.

### Avoid unsafe confrontation advice

Do not advise customers to respond to harassers, negotiate with scammers, threaten exposure, retaliate, or collect evidence through further engagement. Provide safer alternatives such as blocking after evidence capture, using platform reports, preserving URLs, and contacting local emergency resources where appropriate.

The customer should not carry enforcement risk alone.

### Use policy language carefully

Customers may ask whether something is "illegal," "defamation," "harassment," or "a scam." Support can explain platform policy categories and reporting routes, but should avoid legal determinations unless approved.

Use clear language about what the platform can review and what may require outside help.

### Support targets without guaranteeing invisibility

Blocking, muting, privacy settings, keyword filters, or account changes may reduce exposure but may not stop screenshots, new accounts, off-platform contact, or coordinated behavior. Explain realistic limits so customers can make informed safety decisions.

For vulnerable customers, minors, public figures, creators, or employees facing targeted campaigns, escalate to the appropriate specialized path.

### Document for policy and product improvement

Track abuse type, volume, evasion pattern, reporting friction, tool confusion, false positives, and customer safety concerns. Repeated reports may indicate gaps in moderation tools, anti-spam systems, onboarding, or policy language.

Support patterns can reveal platform risks before dashboards do.

### Account for off-platform movement

Harassment and scams often move across channels: email, phone, social media, payment apps, marketplace messages, review sites, or physical addresses. Support may not control those spaces, but it can help the customer understand platform reporting boundaries, preserve relevant evidence, secure the account, and avoid unsafe engagement.

Do not promise action on behavior the platform cannot review, but do not ignore off-platform context if it changes risk, such as doxxing, extortion, threats, stalking, or coordinated fraud.

### Support false-positive and blocked-user confusion

Customers may also contact support because spam filters, blocked messages, account restrictions, or moderation tools affected legitimate communication. Explain what can be reviewed, what evidence helps, and what cannot be disclosed about detection systems.

This prevents safety tooling from becoming a silent customer-experience failure.

## Common Traps

- Treating harassment or spam as merely annoying when threats, doxxing, fraud, or targeted abuse are present.
- Promising enforcement outcomes the review team has not decided.
- Suggesting the customer confront or bait the abusive actor for evidence.
- Ignoring cumulative harm across many low-level contacts.
- Giving generic block or mute advice without preserving evidence first; making legal conclusions about defamation, illegality, or criminal conduct
- Failing to explain that blocking and privacy controls have limits; missing impersonation, phishing, malware, counterfeit, or payment-scam signals inside "spam."
- Dismissing reports from creators, minors, vulnerable customers, or public figures as ordinary support; not capturing recurring abuse patterns for trust and safety or product teams
- Ignoring off-platform movement that changes risk even if enforcement authority is limited; explaining spam or moderation false positives in a way that exposes detection logic

## Self-Check

- Were immediate safety, doxxing, stalking, threats, self-harm, sexual exploitation, scam, account takeover, and real-world danger signals checked?
- Does the response give protective steps without destroying needed evidence?
- Are report paths, helpful evidence, and review limits explained?
- Does the message avoid enforcement promises and prohibited disclosure?
- Are repeated, coordinated, targeted, discriminatory, sexualized, or offline-linked patterns considered?
- Does the advice avoid unsafe confrontation, retaliation, or further engagement?
- Is platform policy explained without unsupported legal conclusions?
- Are limits of blocking, muting, privacy tools, filters, and account changes clear?
- Are vulnerable users, minors, creators, public figures, and targeted campaigns escalated where needed?; are abuse patterns, evasion, tool friction, and policy gaps documented for improvement?
- Are off-platform channels considered for risk, evidence preservation, account security, and boundary-setting?; are false-positive or blocked-user cases handled without revealing detection details?
