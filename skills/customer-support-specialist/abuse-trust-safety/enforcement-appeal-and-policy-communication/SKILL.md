# Enforcement Appeal And Policy Communication

Enforcement communication is difficult because customers want specifics, but too much specificity can expose detection systems, reporter identity, victim information, or abuse-prevention signals. Too little specificity feels arbitrary and unfair. This skill helps the agent explain policy enforcement and appeal paths with enough clarity for due process while protecting privacy and platform integrity.

## Core Rules

### Separate notice, appeal, and final decision

An initial enforcement notice should explain the action, relevant policy category, immediate effect, and appeal path if available. An appeal intake should explain what information can be submitted. A final decision should state whether the action changed and what options remain.

Do not mix these stages. Customers become confused when a final denial reads like an initial warning or when an appeal path is implied after it is exhausted.

### Give usable policy context

Provide the policy category, general behavior at issue, date range or content type when allowed, and the affected account or feature. Avoid vague phrases such as "violated our terms" when a more specific approved category exists.

At the same time, do not reveal private reporter information, exact detection thresholds, evasion signals, internal tooling, or evidence that could help future abuse.

### Protect third parties

Enforcement cases may involve reporters, victims, minors, employees, marketplace buyers, sellers, moderators, or other users. Do not disclose identities, private messages, personal details, safety context, or investigative notes unless policy and law allow it.

Even when the enforced user demands proof, privacy and safety limits remain.

### Explain appeal standards

Tell the customer what an appeal can address: mistaken identity, missing context, policy misapplication, compromised account, restored compliance, duplicate enforcement, or new evidence. Do not invite irrelevant arguments that cannot change the decision.

If the action is not appealable, say so clearly and give any available next step, such as waiting period, account security review, or policy education.

### Avoid negotiating policy in support

Agents should not improvise exceptions to trust and safety rules because a customer is upset, high-value, public, or persuasive. Escalate edge cases through the correct review path and record the reason.

Consistency is part of fairness. Exceptions without policy basis undermine other users' safety.

### Handle account compromise carefully

If the customer claims enforcement resulted from account takeover, route through account-security and enforcement review. Do not automatically reverse action just because compromise is alleged, and do not ignore the possibility either.

The decision may need both account recovery and policy review.

### Keep tone firm and respectful

Enforcement messages should be direct, non-moralizing, and free of argument. The goal is to communicate the decision and path, not to shame the user or debate every fact.

If the customer becomes abusive or threatening, follow safety and escalation policy while preserving the enforcement record.

### Manage repeat appeals and boundaries

Some users repeatedly reopen appeals, switch channels, contact executives, post publicly, or pressure unrelated support teams after a final decision. Define when a case is still open to new evidence and when the correct response is a boundary message.

A boundary should be clear, respectful, and consistent: what has been reviewed, whether new evidence is accepted, what channels remain available, and what behavior may lead to contact limits. Do not let repeated pressure create an unofficial extra appeal path for persistent or high-profile users.

### Coordinate with customer-facing teams

Sales, customer success, billing, community, and executive support may receive questions about enforcement. Provide them with approved language and disclosure limits. They should not promise reinstatement, reveal policy details, or pressure trust and safety for exceptions without the formal path.

## Common Traps

- Sending only "terms violation" with no usable policy category.
- Revealing reporter identity, victim details, detection logic, or internal evidence.
- Imlying an appeal is available when the decision is final.
- Treating every angry response as an appeal without required evidence.
- Reversing or softening enforcement because the customer is high-value or public; ignoring account-compromise claims or accepting them without security review
- Using moralizing, sarcastic, or accusatory language; disclosing enforcement outcomes to third parties who are not authorized
- Failing to document appeal evidence and decision rationale; allowing repeat appeals through new channels after the final decision without new evidence
- Letting sales, success, billing, or executives promise enforcement outcomes outside the review path; creating inconsistent precedents through ad hoc exceptions

## Self-Check

- Is the communication stage clear: notice, appeal intake, appeal update, final decision, or post-final boundary?
- Does the message state the action, effect, policy category, and appeal path when available?
- Is the policy context specific enough to be usable without exposing protected evidence or abuse-prevention systems?
- Are reporter, victim, minor, employee, buyer, seller, and other third-party details protected?
- Does the appeal guidance explain what evidence or arguments are relevant?
- If not appealable, is that stated clearly with any available next step?
- Are high-value, public, or emotional cases routed consistently rather than negotiated casually?
- Are account-compromise claims handled through both security and enforcement review?
- Is the tone firm, respectful, and non-moralizing?; is the decision rationale documented for audit, fairness, and future review?
- Are repeat appeals handled through a defined boundary that still allows genuinely new evidence if policy permits?; do other customer-facing teams have approved language and disclosure limits?
