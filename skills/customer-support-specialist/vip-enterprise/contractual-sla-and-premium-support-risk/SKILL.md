# Contractual SLA And Premium Support Risk

SLA and premium support cases are not ordinary timing questions. The answer may affect service credits, renewal trust, legal exposure, executive escalation, and internal accountability. Agents can create risk by misclassifying severity, promising resolution instead of response, or discussing credits without approval. This skill helps the agent manage support obligations with precise timekeeping, contract awareness, and careful communication.

## Core Rules

### Locate the governing obligation

Check the customer's contract, support tier, order form, service description, premium plan terms, regional addendum, incident policy, or marketplace terms. Do not rely on generic website SLA language if the customer has negotiated terms.

If the governing document is unclear, escalate to account, legal, or support operations before making claims.

### Separate SLA types

SLAs may cover first response, update cadence, workaround, resolution, uptime, incident notification, escalation availability, or service credit eligibility. Customers often say "SLA" while meaning any of these.

Clarify which obligation is in question before evaluating compliance.

### Start and stop clocks precisely

Record when the case was opened, when severity was assigned, when required information was received, when business hours apply, when the first response occurred, when updates were sent, and when workaround or resolution happened. Include timezone and holiday basis.

Clock rules may pause for customer response, unavailable evidence, or non-business hours depending on contract.

### Classify severity from criteria

Severity should be based on contract definitions and customer impact, not customer anger alone. Consider production outage, data loss, security risk, critical workflow, number of users, workaround, revenue, safety, regulatory impact, and executive exposure.

If severity changes, document why and when.

### Communicate obligations carefully

Do not say an SLA was met, missed, or credit-eligible unless approved. You can say what the team is reviewing, what times are recorded, and when the customer will receive a formal response.

Avoid casual admissions or denials in chat that conflict with later review.

### Coordinate internally before credit discussion

Service credits, refunds, fee waivers, or contractual remedies may require account, finance, legal, or customer success review. Support should not calculate or promise credits unless policy allows.

If the customer asks directly, route to the approved process and keep the support issue moving.

### Maintain premium support experience

Premium support often includes clearer communication, priority routing, named contacts, after-hours paths, or proactive incident updates. Even if a credit is not owed, poor communication can damage the relationship.

Meet the experience expectation as well as the formal clock.

### Audit and improve after misses

If an SLA was missed or nearly missed, identify whether routing, severity assignment, staffing, tooling, holiday coverage, handoff, or escalation ownership failed. Update process and account notes.

Premium obligations should improve operational discipline.

### Preserve evidence for credit or dispute review and distinguish remedy review from customer recovery

If the customer claims a missed SLA or premium obligation, preserve the full timeline: ticket creation, automated acknowledgements, agent responses, internal routing, severity changes, customer replies, requested evidence, incident updates, workaround offers, and resolution messages. Include system timestamps and timezone.

Do not edit or summarize away uncomfortable timing facts. A credible review depends on complete evidence.

Service credit or SLA review can run in parallel with fixing the customer's issue. Do not let a contractual remedy debate delay troubleshooting, workaround, escalation, or customer updates.

Likewise, do not imply that a credit fully resolves operational harm if the customer still needs recovery, root cause, or prevention.

## Common Traps

- Applying public SLA terms to a negotiated contract.
- Treating first response, update cadence, resolution, uptime, and service credits as the same obligation.
- Using relative times without timezone or business-hour basis.
- Classifying severity based only on customer emotion or account value.
- Saying "we missed the SLA" or "you are not eligible" before formal review; promising credits, refunds, or waivers without approval
- Ignoring customer-provided evidence that changes the clock; letting premium customers receive ordinary vague updates
- Failing to document severity changes and why they occurred; not performing operational review after a miss or near miss
- Preserving only selected timeline facts that support the internal position; letting credit review distract from active troubleshooting and customer recovery

## Self-Check

- Is the governing contract, tier, order form, service description, regional addendum, incident policy, or marketplace term identified?
- Is the SLA type clear: first response, update, workaround, resolution, uptime, notification, escalation, or credit?
- Are all clock events recorded with timezone, business-hour, holiday, customer-waiting, and evidence-received basis?
- Is severity classified from contract criteria and actual impact?
- Are severity changes timestamped and justified?
- Does the customer communication avoid unauthorized conclusions about SLA compliance or credits?
- Are account, finance, legal, support operations, and customer success involved where needed?
- Is the premium support experience maintained through clear updates and routing?
- Are misses or near misses reviewed for routing, staffing, tooling, handoff, and escalation defects?; are process and account notes updated after the review?
- Is the full SLA evidence timeline preserved with system timestamps and timezone?; is remedy review separated from ongoing recovery, workaround, root cause, and prevention work?
