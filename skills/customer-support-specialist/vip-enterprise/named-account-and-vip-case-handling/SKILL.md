# Named Account And VIP Case Handling

VIP handling is not simply responding faster. High-visibility accounts may carry revenue, public, contractual, or relationship risk, but they also raise privacy, fairness, and over-escalation problems. Agents can overpromise, expose sensitive context, or distort policy because a customer is important. This skill helps the agent provide appropriate priority while preserving process integrity.

## Core Rules

### Identify why the case is VIP

VIP status may come from enterprise contract, named account ownership, revenue, executive visibility, public figure, safety risk, partner relationship, strategic launch, investor attention, or premium support entitlement. Record the basis.

Do not treat "loud" or "angry" as the same as VIP. Also do not ignore formal entitlement because the customer is polite.

### Separate priority from outcome

VIP status may affect response speed, coordination, communication detail, or escalation visibility. It should not automatically change policy eligibility, security requirements, privacy rules, refund approval, enforcement outcome, or legal process.

If an exception is being considered, use the approved exception path and document the reason.

### Coordinate with account owners

Named accounts often have customer success managers, account executives, technical account managers, or executive sponsors. Align on customer context, promises, channel, timing, and who speaks to the customer.

Do not surprise account owners with a sensitive denial, public statement, or technical claim if coordination is required.

### Protect privacy despite visibility

VIP cases attract internal curiosity. Limit access to need-to-know teams and avoid sharing private details in broad channels. Public figures, executives, employees, and strategic customers deserve the same privacy discipline as any other customer.

High visibility is not permission for internal gossip.

### Maintain accurate escalation packets

Escalation should include impact, entitlement, contract or SLA reference, customer sentiment, business consequence, prior commitments, evidence, owner, and requested decision. Do not escalate only with "VIP customer."

Specialists need facts, not status labels.

### Avoid special promises under pressure

Do not promise direct engineering access, guaranteed fix time, refund, credit, roadmap commitment, custom feature, executive response, or policy override unless approved. VIP customers often have more internal attention, but the product reality may remain uncertain.

Use checkpoint commitments rather than false certainty.

### Consider fairness and precedent

If a VIP receives treatment unavailable to other customers, confirm whether it is contractually entitled, approved as an exception, or a standard premium-support process. Document the basis.

Uncontrolled VIP exceptions can undermine policy and frontline morale.

### Close the internal loop and protect queue fairness and escalation capacity

After resolution, update account owner, support record, known issue, SLA review, and any leadership channel. Include what was promised, what was delivered, and what remains open.

VIP visibility makes stale internal assumptions especially risky.

VIP prioritization can consume specialist time and delay other severe cases. When escalating a VIP case, state the real impact and entitlement so triage can compare it against safety, data loss, security, and broad outage work.

Do not let internal pressure crowd out non-VIP customers with higher harm. Fairness includes using scarce escalation paths for the right reasons.

### Control internal communications and watch for impersonation and social engineering

High-profile cases often create side threads in chat, email, and leadership channels. Designate one internal owner and source of truth. Keep updates factual, timestamped, and free of speculation.

Side-channel confusion can produce contradictory customer messages and unauthorized promises.

VIP and executive identities can attract impersonators or people attempting to pressure agents into bypassing verification. Treat requests involving account access, payment, security, personal data, travel, public figures, or executive names with normal or heightened verification, not less.

Internal urgency should never replace identity checks. If a request arrives through an unusual channel, validate it before action.

## Common Traps

- Equating anger, social reach, or internal pressure with legitimate VIP entitlement.
- Letting VIP status change privacy, security, legal, or enforcement requirements without approval.
- Escalating with status label but no evidence or requested decision.
- Allowing broad internal access to sensitive VIP details; sending customer messages that conflict with account owner expectations
- Promising fix timelines, executive attention, credits, or roadmap outcomes casually; ignoring fairness and precedent when making exceptions
- Over-prioritizing VIP inconvenience while missing severe harm for non-VIP customers; closing support without updating account and leadership channels
- Failing to document why special handling occurred; allowing VIP pressure to displace non-VIP cases with greater safety, data, security, or outage harm
- Letting multiple internal side channels create conflicting facts and promises; reducing verification because the requester claims to be a VIP, executive, assistant, or public representative

## Self-Check

- Is the VIP basis documented: contract, revenue, executive, public figure, safety, partner, launch, investor, or premium entitlement?
- Is priority separated from outcome eligibility?
- Are policy, security, privacy, enforcement, refund, and legal rules preserved unless an approved exception exists?
- Are account owners aligned on context, promises, channel, timing, and customer communication?
- Is internal access limited to need-to-know?
- Does the escalation packet include impact, entitlement, SLA or contract reference, sentiment, business consequence, promises, evidence, owner, and requested decision?
- Are direct engineering, fix time, credit, refund, roadmap, executive, and policy commitments approved before being made?
- Is fairness or precedent documented for special handling?; are severe non-VIP harms still escalated appropriately?
- Is the account and support record updated after resolution?; is escalation capacity used based on real impact and entitlement rather than visibility alone?
- Is there one internal source of truth and owner for VIP case communication?; are impersonation, unusual-channel, assistant, representative, and social-engineering risks handled with proper verification?
