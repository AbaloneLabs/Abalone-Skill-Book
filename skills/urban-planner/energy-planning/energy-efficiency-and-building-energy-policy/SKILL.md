# Energy Efficiency and Building Energy Policy

Building energy policy is the lever with the largest effect on a jurisdiction's emissions and on the affordability of housing, and it is also the lever most often adopted in a form that cannot be enforced or that produces inequitable outcomes. The judgment problem is that energy codes, benchmarking ordinances, and electrification rules operate on a building stock that is enormously varied — new versus existing, owned versus rented, large versus small, affluent versus cost-burdened — and a policy designed for one segment routinely fails or harms another. Planners commonly fail by adopting a code without the inspection and enforcement capacity to make it real, by passing a benchmarking ordinance that generates data no one uses, by pursuing electrification without checking whether state law preempts local gas restrictions, or by designing retrofit programs that flow to owners who can already afford the work while the households most burdened by energy costs are excluded. The deeper failure is treating energy efficiency as a technical matter of kilowatt-hours when it is also a housing-affordability, tenant-protection, and equity question. A retrofit subsidy that flows only to owners who can front the cost, or an electrification mandate adopted without checking state preemption, can leave the highest-burden households untouched while producing litigation that sets the whole policy back. The recurring mistake is adopting the visible policy instrument without building the enforcement, financing, and equity scaffolding that makes it actually work. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether building energy policy actually reduces emissions and eases energy burden, or only documents an ambition.

## Core Rules

### Adopt Codes With Enforcement Capacity, Not Just on Paper

The International Energy Conservation Code (IECC), whether the base version or a stretch code such as the International Code Conservation (ICC) family's optional high-efficiency appendices or a state-developed stretch code, is only as effective as the inspection regime that verifies compliance. A jurisdiction that adopts the latest code without trained inspectors, without plan-review capacity, and without a culture of field verification gets compliance rates far below the code's potential, because contractors default to familiar, lower-cost practices unless inspections force otherwise. The rule is to assess enforcement capacity before adoption, to train inspectors and plan reviewers on the new provisions (especially the blower-door, insulation, and mechanical-system requirements that distinguish stretch codes), and to budget for the field verification that makes the code real. A code adopted without enforcement capacity is a paper reduction: the emissions savings are claimed in the climate plan but never realized in the building stock, and the gap between modeled and actual performance widens with every project.

### Choose Between Base Code, Stretch Code, and Reach Code Deliberately

The choice among the base IECC, a state or model stretch code, and a locally drafted reach code is a decision about ambition, feasibility, and legal authority, and it should be made explicitly rather than by default. The base code is the minimum the jurisdiction can adopt and is enforceable through standard permitting, but it delivers the least savings. A stretch code raises the bar (often requiring higher insulation, air-sealing, or electrification-ready provisions) and is appropriate where the political will and the construction industry capacity exist, but it can raise housing costs and may require builder education. A locally drafted reach code beyond the state-adopted maximum may exceed the jurisdiction's legal authority in states that set a uniform statewide code and preempt local amendments, so the first check is whether local reach-code authority exists at all. Match the code choice to the legal authority, the enforcement capacity, the construction-market readiness, and the emissions goal, and document the rationale, because a reach code adopted without authority or capacity is unenforceable and a base code adopted where a stretch code was feasible leaves savings on the table.

### Design Benchmarking Ordinances to Produce Action, Not Just Data

Building energy benchmarking and transparency ordinances require owners of larger buildings to measure and report energy use, on the theory that measurement drives management and that public disclosure drives market pressure. The frequent failure is that the ordinance generates a dataset that no one analyzes, no owner acts on, and no market participant consults, so the administrative cost is incurred with no emissions benefit. A benchmarking ordinance should be paired with the elements that convert data to action: a threshold for mandatory energy-use intensity targets or audits and retro-commissioning for poor performers, technical assistance for owners who lack the capacity to interpret their data, public reporting that allows tenants and buyers to compare buildings, and a clear escalation path for owners who do not improve. Cover the building segments where disclosure will actually shift behavior (typically larger commercial and multifamily buildings where owners and tenants can act on the information), and build the analytic and assistance capacity alongside the reporting requirement, because data without a path to action is compliance theater.

### Verify Legal Authority Before Pursuing Electrification or Gas Restrictions

Building electrification and restrictions on new gas connections are among the most powerful emissions-reduction tools, but they are also among the most legally constrained, and pursuing them without verifying authority invites preemption rulings that set the policy back years. Many states have enacted laws preempting local restrictions on utility service or fuel choice, and in those jurisdictions a local gas ban is unenforceable regardless of its climate merit. Even where preemption does not bar the policy outright, the authority to restrict fuel choice may rest with a state utility commission rather than the local building department. Before adopting an electrification requirement, map the legal landscape: does state law preempt local fuel-choice rules, does the local code-enabling statute permit it, and is there a pathway (such as a building-code amendment framed around equipment efficiency rather than an explicit fuel ban) that achieves the goal within the jurisdiction's authority? Where full electrification is preempted, pursue what is lawful — electrification-ready new construction, all-electric incentives, or code provisions that do not facially restrict fuel — rather than adopting a rule that will be struck down.

### Design Retrofit Programs to Reach the Energy-Burdened, Not Just the Capable

Energy-efficiency and electrification retrofit programs frequently flow to property owners who have the credit, the cash, and the motivation to undertake the work, while the households with the highest energy burden — low-income owners and renters who pay a disproportionate share of income for energy — are excluded by upfront cost, split incentives, or program design. The split-incentive problem is central: in rental housing, the owner pays for the retrofit but the tenant pays the energy bill, so the owner has no financial reason to invest, and tenants cannot authorize building improvements. A retrofit program that does not address this structurally will subsidize efficiency in affluent owner-occupied housing while the burdened rental stock is untouched. The rule is to design programs with no-to-low upfront cost (on-bill financing, grants, credit-enhanced loans), to target the highest-burden neighborhoods explicitly, to structure incentives that work for rental housing (owner grants paired with tenant-protection clauses that prevent rent increases attributable to the improvement), and to use energy-burden data rather than first-come-first-served to allocate resources, so the program reduces inequity rather than reinforcing it.

### Build Compliance, Verification, and Ongoing Performance Into Every Policy

Energy policies fail silently when no one checks whether they are working. A code whose compliance is never measured, a benchmarking dataset never analyzed, a retrofit program whose savings are never verified against bills, and an electrification mandate whose installation quality is never inspected all produce the same outcome: claimed savings that do not appear in actual consumption. Build verification into each policy: field inspection and blower-door testing for code compliance, analysis and public reporting for benchmarking, measurement-and-verification or utility-bill analysis for retrofits, and commissioning for electrification installations. Establish performance indicators, report them publicly, and create triggers for course correction when savings fall short. A building energy policy without verification is a statement of intent; one with verification and adaptation is a program that actually reduces emissions and burden over time.

## Common Traps

### Code Adoption Without Enforcement Capacity

The jurisdiction adopts the latest stretch code to demonstrate climate leadership but does not train inspectors, add plan-review capacity, or budget for the field verification the new provisions require. The trap mechanism is that adoption is politically visible and enforcement capacity is invisible, so the gap goes unnoticed until compliance rates reveal that buildings are being built to the old standard under the new code's name. The false signal is an ambitious adopted code. The harm is that the emissions savings are claimed in the climate plan but never realized, the gap between modeled and actual performance widens, and the jurisdiction's credibility erodes when measured savings consistently miss projections.

### Benchmarking Data That No One Acts On

The benchmarking ordinance is adopted, buildings report their energy use, the dataset is published, and then nothing happens — no analysis, no performance targets, no technical assistance, no consequences for poor performers. The trap mechanism is that the reporting requirement is treated as the policy rather than as the input to a policy, and the absence of a follow-on action path means the administrative cost is incurred with no emissions benefit. The false signal is a transparency ordinance with a growing dataset. The harm is that owners report but do not improve, the dataset accumulates without shifting behavior, and the policy is cited as evidence of climate action while delivering none.

### Electrification Mandate Adopted Without Authority Check

The jurisdiction passes an all-electric new-construction requirement or a gas-connection restriction to cut emissions, without verifying whether state law preempts local fuel-choice rules or whether the authority rests with a state utility commission. The trap mechanism is that the policy is adopted on climate merit and political momentum, and the legal vulnerability surfaces only when a utility or developer challenges it, at which point a preemption ruling invalidates the policy and often constrains future local action. The false signal is bold local climate leadership. The harm is a struck-down policy, years of lost progress, legal precedent that weakens local energy authority, and the demoralization of the coalition that pushed the mandate.

### Retrofit Programs That Subsidize the Already-Capable

The retrofit program offers rebates and financing that flow disproportionately to affluent owners who can front the cost and navigate the application, while high-energy-burden households — low-income owners and renters — are excluded by upfront cost, credit requirements, the split incentive, or program complexity. The trap mechanism is that first-come-first-served and owner-initiated program designs systematically advantage those with the resources to participate, so the public subsidy reinforces existing inequity rather than relieving it. The false signal is a popular program with high participation. The harm is that the households most burdened by energy costs receive the least benefit, the emissions reductions concentrate in already-efficient buildings, and the program's equity rationale is contradicted by its actual distribution.

### Stretch Code Treated as a Free Emissions Win

The jurisdiction adopts a stretch code on the assumption that it delivers automatic savings, without considering the housing-cost implications, the builder-capacity gap, or the enforcement burden, and presents it as costless climate action. The trap mechanism is that stretch codes raise construction costs (which can affect housing affordability and may be passed to renters), require builders to learn new practices, and demand more inspection — and ignoring these effects produces pushback that can reverse the code. The false signal is an adopted ambitious code with projected savings. The harm is builder noncompliance, cost-driven exemptions that gut the code, political backlash that weakens future updates, and savings that never materialize because the supporting capacity was never built.

### Ignoring the Split Incentive in Rental Housing

The retrofit program is designed for owner-occupants who pay their own energy bills, and rental housing — where the owner invests but the tenant saves — is left out because the owner has no financial reason to participate. The trap mechanism is that the program's incentive structure assumes the energy payer is the decision-maker, which is false for a large share of the housing stock, so the highest-burden tenants in the least-efficient units are systematically excluded. The false signal is a program open to all property types. The harm is that the rental stock remains inefficient, the tenants with the highest energy burden see no relief, and the program's emissions and equity goals both go unmet because the structural barrier was never addressed.

## Self-Check

- Was enforcement capacity — trained inspectors, plan review, field verification — assessed and budgeted before code adoption, rather than treating the adopted code as the achievement?
- Was the choice among base code, stretch code, and reach code made deliberately against legal authority, enforcement capacity, and market readiness, rather than by default?
- Does the benchmarking ordinance include analysis, performance targets or audit triggers, technical assistance, and public reporting — the elements that convert data to action — rather than merely a reporting requirement?
- Before pursuing electrification or gas restrictions, was state preemption and the locus of fuel-choice authority verified, with a lawful pathway chosen if direct restriction is preempted?
- Does the retrofit program reach energy-burdened households through no-upfront-cost financing, targeted outreach, and rental-sector incentives that address the split incentive, rather than flowing to already-capable owners?
- Is verification built into every policy — inspection for codes, analysis for benchmarking, bill-based savings for retrofits, commissioning for electrification — with public reporting and course-correction triggers?
- Did you avoid adopting codes without enforcement, benchmarking without action, electrification without an authority check, and retrofit programs that subsidize the already-capable while excluding the energy-burdened?
- Will the policy actually reduce emissions and ease energy burden as measured by verified outcomes, or does it only document an ambition whose effects are never confirmed?
