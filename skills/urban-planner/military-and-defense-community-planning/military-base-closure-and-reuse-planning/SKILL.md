# Military Base Closure and Reuse Planning

A military base closure is a shock to the host community — the loss of the jobs, the payroll, the population, and the identity — and the reuse planning is the opportunity to convert the base to the civilian uses that replace the economic loss and the create the new future. The judgment problem is that the base closure involves the complex property transfer (the federal-to-local, the environmental cleanup, the conveyance mechanisms), the economic recovery (the job replacement, the business attraction, the diversification), and the land-use planning (the civilian reuse, the infrastructure, the compatibility), and the planner must navigate these dimensions to produce the reuse that replaces the loss and creates the opportunity. A base that is reused well (the clean transfer, the diversified economy, the compatible uses) becomes the asset that replaces the loss, while a base that is reused poorly (the delayed cleanup, the undiversified economy, the incompatible uses) becomes the liability that compounds the loss. This skill covers the base closure and the reuse planning decisions that determine whether the closure becomes the opportunity or the liability. It complements the broader military-and-defense skill by focusing on the closure and the reuse dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Reuse Authority and the Community Vision

The base reuse requires the organizational structure (the reuse authority, the planning team) and the community vision (the desired future, the economic strategy, the land-use concept), and the early establishment of both determines the trajectory of the reuse. The rule is to establish the reuse authority and the community vision, creating the reuse authority (the local redevelopment authority, the economic development authority) with the clear mandate, the capacity, and the funding, and developing the community vision through the inclusive planning process that engages the community, the businesses, and the stakeholders. The reuse authority should be established early, because the base closure process moves quickly, and the community without the authority is the community that is left behind. The reuse authority should have the capacity (the staff, the consultants, the legal and the financial expertise), because the base reuse is the complex undertaking that the capacity requires. The community vision should be developed through the inclusive process, because the vision that the community owns is the vision that is implemented, and the vision that is imposed is the vision that is opposed. The vision should address the economic strategy (the job replacement, the diversification), the land-use concept (the civilian uses, the infrastructure), and the community integration (the connection to the surrounding community, the avoidance of the enclave).

### Manage the Environmental Cleanup and the Property Transfer

The base property is often contaminated (the military training, the industrial activities, the fuel storage), and the environmental cleanup must be completed before the property can be transferred and redeveloped, and the management of the cleanup and the transfer is the critical path of the reuse. The rule is to manage the environmental cleanup and the property transfer, coordinating with the military (the responsible party for the cleanup), the regulators (the EPA, the state environmental agency), and the community (the restoration advisory board), and using the transfer mechanisms (the early transfer, the interim lease, the economic development conveyance) that allow the redevelopment to proceed. The cleanup should be coordinated with the reuse plan, because the cleanup level should match the intended use (the residential cleanup is more stringent than the industrial), and the coordination ensures the cleanup supports the reuse. The property transfer should use the appropriate mechanism, because the different mechanisms (the public benefit conveyance, the economic development conveyance, the negotiated sale) have the different requirements and the different costs, and the selection affects the community's cost and the control. The transfer should include the interim leasing, because the interim lease allows the community to use the property and to generate the revenue before the final transfer, and the interim use maintains the property and the momentum.

### Replace the Jobs and Diversify the Economy

The base closure causes the loss of the military and the civilian jobs, and the economic recovery requires the job replacement and the diversification, because the community that depends on the single employer (the base) is the community that is vulnerable to the next closure, and the diversification reduces the vulnerability. The rule is to replace the jobs and diversify the economy, using the business attraction (the targeting of the employers that match the workforce and the facilities), the business retention and the expansion (the support of the existing businesses), the entrepreneurship (the incubators, the accelerators, the small business support), and the sector diversification (the targeting of the multiple sectors rather than the single sector). The job replacement should target the employers that match the workforce skills (the military and the civilian workers who lost the jobs), because the matching workforce attracts the employers, and the mismatched workforce does not. The diversification should target the multiple sectors, because the single-sector economy is the vulnerable economy, and the multiple sectors provide the resilience. The economic recovery should include the workforce development (the retraining, the education, the placement), because the workforce that is not retrained cannot fill the new jobs, and the development bridges the gap. The economic recovery should be coordinated with the regional economy, because the base closure affects the region, and the regional coordination maximizes the recovery.

### Plan the Civilian Reuse and the Infrastructure

The civilian reuse plan converts the military base to the civilian uses, and the plan should address the land uses (the residential, the commercial, the industrial, the institutional, the open space), the infrastructure (the roads, the water, the sewer, the utilities), and the community integration (the connection to the surrounding street network, the avoidance of the gated enclave). The rule is to plan the civilian reuse and the infrastructure, developing the reuse plan that is based on the market analysis (the demand for the different uses), the infrastructure assessment (the capacity and the condition of the existing systems), and the community integration (the street connections, the mixed uses, the open space). The reuse plan should be market-based, because the uses that the market does not demand will not be developed, and the market analysis identifies the feasible uses. The infrastructure should be assessed and upgraded, because the military infrastructure may not meet the civilian standards, and the upgrade is the prerequisite for the redevelopment. The community integration should connect the base to the surrounding community, because the base that is the enclave (the single entrance, the internal street network) does not integrate, and the multiple connections and the grid network integrate. The reuse plan should include the phasing, because the redevelopment occurs over the years, and the phasing manages the infrastructure and the market absorption.

### Coordinate the Housing and the Population Transition

The base closure causes the population loss (the military personnel and the families who leave), and the housing market is affected (the vacancy, the price decline), and the reuse plan should address the housing and the population transition. The rule is to coordinate the housing and the population transition, analyzing the housing impact (the vacancy, the price, the oversupply), planning the housing reuse (the residential development on the base, the adaptive reuse of the military housing), and managing the transition (the timing, the phasing, the market absorption). The housing analysis should identify the impact of the closure on the housing market, because the impact informs the housing strategy, and the strategy that does not account for the impact worsens the market. The housing reuse should include the mix of the housing types (the market-rate, the affordable, the senior, the workforce), because the diverse housing serves the diverse population, and the single-type housing does not. The housing transition should be phased, because the simultaneous release of the military housing and the new residential development would flood the market, and the phasing manages the absorption. The housing should be integrated with the other uses, because the mixed-use base is the vibrant base, and the single-use residential is the dormitory.

## Common Traps

### Reuse Authority Not Established, Community Left Behind

The reuse authority is not established early, and the community is left behind in the base closure process, because the community without the authority cannot participate effectively, and the military and the other agencies make the decisions without the community input. The trap is that the absent authority leaves the community behind. The mechanism is that the authority was not established, and the community was unrepresented. The false signal is that "the military will handle it." The harm is that the community's interests are not represented, the transfer is delayed, the cleanup is inadequate, the reuse does not serve the community, and the closure that should be the opportunity becomes the liability.

### Cleanup Not Coordinated With the Reuse

The environmental cleanup is not coordinated with the reuse plan, and the cleanup level does not match the intended use, and the property cannot be redeveloped for the planned uses, because the cleanup is inadequate for the residential or the cleanup is excessive for the industrial. The trap is that the uncoordinated cleanup delays or prevents the reuse. The mechanism is that the coordination was not conducted, and the cleanup proceeded on the default basis. The false signal is that "the cleanup is underway." The harm is that the property cannot be used for the planned uses, the redevelopment is delayed, the additional cleanup is required, and the reuse that should proceed does not.

### Economy Not Diversified, Vulnerability Persists

The economy is not diversified, and the community replaces the base with the single employer or the single sector, and the vulnerability to the next closure or the downturn persists, because the diversification was not pursued. The trap is that the undiversified economy maintains the vulnerability. The mechanism is that the diversification was not targeted, and the replacement focused on the single employer. The false signal is that "the jobs are replaced." The harm is that the vulnerability persists, the next downturn or the closure causes the same loss, the economy is not resilient, and the recovery that should diversify does not.

### Base Reused as the Enclave, Not Integrated

The base is reused as the enclave (the single entrance, the internal network, the gated community), and the reuse does not integrate with the surrounding community, because the integration was not planned. The trap is that the enclave reuse does not integrate. The mechanism is that the street connections were not planned, and the base retained the military layout. The false signal is that "the base is redeveloped." The harm is that the base is the enclave, the surrounding community does not benefit, the traffic is concentrated at the single entrance, and the reuse that should integrate does not.

### Housing Market Flooded, Transition Not Managed

The housing market is flooded with the simultaneous release of the military housing and the new residential development, and the market crashes, because the transition was not phased. The trap is that the flooded market crashes. The mechanism is that the phasing was not implemented, and the housing was released at once. The false signal is that "the housing is available." The harm is that the market crashes, the prices decline, the existing homeowners lose the equity, the developers cannot compete, and the transition that should be managed is not.

## Self-Check

- Is the reuse authority established early, with the mandate, the capacity, the funding, and the community vision?
- Is the environmental cleanup coordinated with the reuse plan, with the cleanup level matching the intended use and the transfer mechanism selected?
- Are the jobs replaced and the economy diversified, with the business attraction, the retention, the entrepreneurship, and the sector diversification?
- Is the civilian reuse planned, with the market-based uses, the infrastructure upgrade, the community integration, and the phasing?
- Is the housing and the population transition coordinated, with the impact analysis, the housing mix, and the phased absorption?
- Is the authority established, or is the community left behind?
- Is the cleanup coordinated, or does it delay the reuse?
- Is the economy diversified, or does the vulnerability persist?
- Is the base integrated, or is it the enclave?
- Is the housing transition managed, or is the market flooded?
