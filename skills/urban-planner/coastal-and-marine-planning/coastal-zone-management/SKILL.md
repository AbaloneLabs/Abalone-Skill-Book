# Coastal Zone Management

Coastal zone management is the effort to govern the strip of land and water where terrestrial and marine jurisdictions, uses, and authorities overlap, and it is defined by a structural problem that inland planning does not face: the coastal zone is governed by a patchwork of federal, state, and local authorities, each with jurisdiction over a different slice (the water column, the submerged lands, the shore, the upland), and no single authority controls enough of the system to manage it coherently. The judgment problem is that the most consequential coastal decisions — siting an energy facility, opening a navigation channel, permitting an aquaculture operation, managing a wetland — cross these jurisdictional lines, and the management program's influence depends on coordination mechanisms (federal consistency, joint permitting, regional partnerships) that are slow, contested, and only as strong as the weakest party's commitment. A coastal program can produce comprehensive plans and detailed regulations and still fail to shape outcomes, because the decisions are made in the gaps between the authorities. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a coastal zone management program will coordinate the fragmented authorities into coherent outcomes or merely document their fragmentation.

## Core Rules

### Map the Full Jurisdictional and Authority Landscape Before Planning

Before preparing or amending a coastal zone management program, map the complete authority landscape: which federal agencies (NOAA, the Army Corps, EPA, USFWS, FEMA, BOEM) have jurisdiction over which activities and waters; which state agencies (coastal program, environmental, natural resources, transportation, public lands) regulate which resources and uses; which local governments control the upland zoning and permitting that shapes the terrestrial coast; and which special districts and regional bodies (estuary programs, port authorities, watershed associations) hold implementation authority. For each major use and resource, identify which authorities must align for coherent management, and where the gaps and overlaps are. A coastal program that does not begin from this map will plan for outcomes it cannot deliver, because the implementing authorities were not identified or engaged.

### Use Federal Consistency as the Primary Lever, and Understand Its Limits

For state coastal programs operating under the federal Coastal Zone Management Act, the federal consistency authority — which requires federal actions (and federally licensed or permitted activities) affecting the coastal zone to be consistent with the state's enforceable coastal policies — is the primary lever for influencing decisions that would otherwise be made by federal agencies without state input. Understand both the power and the limits of this lever: it applies to federal actions and federal permits, it requires the state's policies to be enforceable (not aspirational), it operates within defined timelines and review scopes, and it is subject to appeal to the Secretary of Commerce. Draft enforceable coastal policies (statewide program policies or local implementation plan policies, depending on the program structure) that are specific enough to support consistency determinations, and build the consistency review capacity (staff, process, coordination with the federal permitting agencies) to use the authority effectively. A program with strong policies but no consistency review capacity cannot exercise its primary lever.

### Define the Land-Water Boundary and the Management Zone Deliberately

The coastal zone's boundaries — both the landward and the seaward — determine what the program can regulate, and they are often defined by statute or regulation in ways that create management gaps. The landward boundary may stop at the mean high water line (leaving the upland to local zoning), at a fixed distance inland, or at a jurisdictional feature (a coastal highway, a contour line); the seaward boundary may extend to the territorial sea, the state waters, or a resource-based line. Define the management zone deliberately, recognizing that a zone that is too narrow leaves the upland (where the land-use pressure originates) outside the program, and a zone that is too wide overextends the program's capacity. Where the statutory boundary creates gaps (the program controls the water but not the upland that pollutes it), use coordination, consistency, and partnership to extend influence into the gap, because the coastal system does not respect jurisdictional lines.

### Prioritize Uses Through a Deliberate Allocation Framework

The coastal zone is the site of intense competing uses — ports and navigation, energy facilities, aquaculture and fishing, recreation and tourism, habitat and ecosystem protection, military uses, residential and commercial development — and the program's core function is to allocate space and priority among them. Develop a use-allocation framework (based on marine spatial planning, special-area management plans, or zoning) that designates priority use areas, identifies where uses are compatible and where they conflict, and establishes the criteria for resolving conflicts. Resist the default of case-by-case permitting without a spatial framework, because that default allocates use to whichever applicant arrives first and generates cumulative conflicts that a spatial framework would have prevented. The framework should be based on the best available resource and use data, and it should be revisable as conditions and uses change.

### Build the Multi-Party Coordination and Governance Architecture

Because no single authority controls the coastal system, the program's outcomes depend on coordination among the federal, state, local, and regional authorities whose decisions must align. Build the coordination architecture explicitly: interagency agreements that define the roles and procedures for joint permitting, memoranda of understanding with the federal agencies whose consistency determinations the program reviews, partnerships with the estuary programs and regional bodies that implement watershed-scale actions, and a governance structure (a coastal commission, an interagency council) that provides a forum for resolving conflicts and aligning priorities. A program without this architecture will produce plans that the implementing agencies do not adopt and consistency determinations that the federal agencies resist, because the coordination was assumed rather than built.

### Ground the Program in the Best Available Science and Adaptive Management

Coastal systems are dynamic and incompletely understood, and the program's credibility and effectiveness depend on its use of the best available science — resource inventories, habitat mapping, water-quality monitoring, sea-level-rise projections, species and fisheries data, and use and economic data — and on its willingness to revise management as conditions and understanding change. Invest in the monitoring and data infrastructure that supports evidence-based management, build adaptive management into the regulatory framework (so standards and allocations are revised as conditions change), and be honest about the uncertainty, particularly for long-horizon projections under climate change. A program that overstates its certainty invites dismissal when conditions diverge; a program that defers management pending certainty defaults to the status quo and loses the resources it was meant to protect.

## Common Traps

### The Plan Without Implementing Authority

The coastal program produces a comprehensive plan that allocates uses, protects resources, and coordinates authorities — but the implementing agencies were not engaged, the plan is not enforceable, and the decisions continue to be made by the individual authorities in their separate silos. The trap mechanism is that the plan is comprehensive at the program level but has no binding effect on the authorities whose alignment it requires, and the planning effort substitutes for the harder work of building the coordination and governance that would actually align decisions. The false signal is a comprehensive, adopted plan. The harm is that the coastal outcomes do not change, the plan is ignored by the implementing agencies, and the program's credibility erodes because its central product had no effect.

### Aspirational Policies That Cannot Support Consistency Review

The program's coastal policies are written as aspirations ("protect coastal water quality," "promote sustainable use") rather than as enforceable standards, and when the program attempts to use federal consistency to influence a federal permit, the policies are too vague to support a consistency objection. The trap mechanism is that aspirational policies are easy to adopt (no one objects to protection and promotion) but useless for consistency review, which requires policies specific enough to determine whether a proposed action is or is not consistent. The false signal is a comprehensive set of adopted coastal policies. The harm is that the program's primary lever (federal consistency) is unusable because the policies cannot be applied, and the federal actions affecting the coast proceed without effective state influence.

### Case-by-Case Permitting Without Spatial Allocation

The program reviews permits for coastal uses one at a time, without a spatial framework that allocates uses and identifies conflicts, and over time the coastal zone fills with uses that are individually defensible but cumulatively in conflict (an aquaculture site adjacent to a navigation channel, a wind energy area overlapping a fishing ground, a recreational marina displacing habitat). The trap mechanism is that the absence of a spatial framework means each permit is evaluated in isolation, and the cumulative pattern of uses is the emergent result of the sequence of applications rather than a deliberate allocation. The false signal is responsive, case-by-case permitting. The harm is a coastal zone of accumulated conflicts, the loss of the resource and use values that a spatial framework would have protected, and the increasingly intractable disputes that arise once incompatible uses are established.

### Coordination Assumed Rather Than Built

The program assumes that the federal, state, and local authorities whose decisions affect the coast will coordinate because the program exists, but no interagency agreements, joint procedures, or governance forums are established, and the authorities continue to act in their silos. The trap mechanism is that coordination is treated as a natural consequence of the program rather than as a specific architecture that must be built and maintained, and without that architecture the program's plans and policies have no delivery mechanism. The false signal is that the program is "coordinating" because it convenes meetings. The harm is that the implementing agencies do not adopt the program's priorities, the federal consistency determinations are resisted or appealed, and the coastal outcomes reflect the separate authorities' decisions rather than the program's coordinated strategy.

## Self-Check

- Have I mapped the complete authority landscape — federal, state, local, and regional — and identified, for each major use and resource, which authorities must align?
- Are the program's coastal policies specific and enforceable enough to support federal consistency review, rather than aspirational?
- Is there the staff and process capacity to exercise federal consistency as the primary lever, with documented procedures for review and coordination with federal permitting agencies?
- Is the management zone defined deliberately, with the landward and seaward boundaries chosen to capture the system, and the gaps addressed through coordination?
- Is there a spatial use-allocation framework (marine spatial plan, special-area management plan, zoning) that prioritizes uses and resolves conflicts, rather than case-by-case permitting?
- Is the coordination architecture built explicitly — interagency agreements, MOUs with federal agencies, regional partnerships, a governance forum — rather than assumed?
- Is the program grounded in the best available science, with monitoring infrastructure and adaptive management for revising standards as conditions change?
- If a major coastal decision (energy siting, channel deepening, aquaculture expansion) arose tomorrow, would the program have the policies, the consistency authority, and the coordination architecture to influence it — or would it be sidelined?
