# Working Waterfronts and Water-Dependent Uses

Working waterfronts — the piers, wharves, boatyards, fish processors, marine suppliers, and water-dependent industries that line a community's shore — are among the most economically and culturally significant assets a coastal community holds, and they are also among the most threatened, because the same waterfront that is essential to marine industry is the most desirable location for residential and recreational conversion. The judgment problem is that the land-use economics overwhelmingly favor conversion: a waterfront parcel converted to condominiums generates orders of magnitude more per square foot than a working pier, and the market pressure to convert is relentless and structural. A community can lose its working waterfront not through a single decision but through a sequence of conversions, each defensible, until the marine industry that defined the community no longer has the shore access it requires and cannot be reconstituted once lost. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a working waterfront strategy will sustain water-dependent industry or preside over its conversion to residential and recreational use.

## Core Rules

### Define and Protect Water-Dependent Uses as a Distinct Regulatory Category

The foundation of working-waterfront protection is a regulatory definition of "water-dependent use" — a use that requires direct access to the water to function (commercial fishing, marinas, boat building and repair, marine research, aquaculture, waterborne freight, ferry terminals) — and a zoning framework that prioritizes these uses on the waterfront and restricts or prohibits non-water-dependent uses (residential, general retail, office) that do not require water access and that outcompete water-dependent industry for limited waterfront land. Define the category operationally (with specific use inclusions and a test for water-dependency), designate water-dependent zoning districts on the working waterfront, and require that non-water-dependent uses locate inland where they do not consume the limited shore access. Without this framework, the market will convert the waterfront to its highest-value residential and recreational use, and the water-dependent industry will be displaced regardless of its economic and cultural importance.

### Address the Conversion Pressure With Specific Preservation Instruments

The market pressure to convert working waterfronts to residential and recreational use cannot be addressed by zoning alone, because zoning is politically reversible and because the conversion economics are overwhelming. Deploy a layered set of preservation instruments: water-dependent zoning (restricting the district to water-dependent uses), density bonuses or transfer of development rights that send residential density inland, acquisition of working waterfront parcels (fee or easement) by the public or a land trust, covenants and deed restrictions that bind the parcel to water-dependent use in perpetuity, and tax abatements or current-use assessment that reduce the property-tax pressure to convert. Each instrument addresses a different pressure (zoning the use, acquisition the ownership, tax policy the fiscal burden), and the combination is more durable than any single tool. Recognize that working-waterfront preservation is a multi-decade effort, because the conversion pressure is permanent and the preservation must be as well.

### Sustain the Land-Water Infrastructure Network, Not Just the Parcels

A working waterfront functions as a network: the pier requires the adjacent boatyard, the boatyard requires the marine supplier, the supplier requires the processor, the processor requires the transport link, and the whole requires upland support area for parking, gear storage, repair, and truck access. Preserving the parcels without preserving the network produces a waterfront with isolated protected uses that cannot function because the supporting uses and upland area have been converted. Map the working waterfront as a network of interdependent parcels and upland support areas, and protect the network — including the upland support area that is often the first to convert to residential — not just the most visible water-dependent parcels. A pier without truck access, gear storage, and repair support is a museum piece, not a working waterfront.

### Manage the Conflict Between Working Waterfront and Adjacent Residential

Working waterfronts are noisy, smelly, early-morning, and industrial, and the residential and recreational uses that encroach on them generate complaints that pressure the working waterfront to curtail its operations, modify its hours, or relocate. This conflict is predictable and structural, and managing it requires proactive regulation rather than reactive complaint response. Adopt right-to-farm-style protections for water-dependent industry (recognizing the operational noise, odor, and hours as protected characteristics of the use), require disclosure to residential buyers adjacent to working waterfronts (so the conflict is known before the purchase, not discovered after), and maintain buffers and transitions between the working waterfront and adjacent residential rather than allowing residential to abut industrial uses directly. Resist the political pressure to curtail water-dependent operations in response to complaints from residents who chose to live adjacent to a working waterfront, because each curtailment weakens the industry's viability and accelerates its loss.

### Plan for the Marine Industry's Economic Viability, Not Just Its Land Protection

Protecting the land base for working waterfronts is necessary but not sufficient; the marine industry must remain economically viable, or the protected parcels will sit empty or be converted at the first political opportunity to weaken the protection. Address the industry's viability through complementary strategies: harbor and channel dredging to maintain navigational access (without which the piers are useless), infrastructure investment (piers, bulkheads, utilities, fish-pier refrigeration), workforce housing (so the industry's workers can afford to live in the community), business technical assistance and financing for marine businesses, and coordination with state and federal fishery and maritime policy. A working-waterfront strategy that protects the land but ignores the industry's economics is preserving a stage set, and the protected but empty waterfront will eventually be converted despite the protection.

### Engage the Marine Industry and the Community in the Strategy's Design

A working-waterfront strategy designed without the active engagement of the marine industry will miss the operational realities (the need for truck turning radii, gear-drying area, 24-hour access, specific water depths) that determine whether the protected parcels can actually function. Engage the marine industry — fishermen, boatyard operators, processors, marine suppliers — in the strategy's design, and engage the broader community on the waterfront's cultural and economic significance, because the political support for working-waterfront protection depends on the community understanding what is at stake. Build the strategy on the industry's operational needs and the community's values, and maintain the engagement over time, because the conversion pressure is permanent and the political defense of the waterfront must be as well.

## Common Traps

### Zoning Alone Treated as Sufficient Protection

The community adopts water-dependent zoning on the working waterfront and treats the protection as complete, but the zoning is politically reversible and the conversion economics are overwhelming, so over time the zoning is weakened (variances, text amendments, rezones) parcel by parcel until the protection is hollow. The trap mechanism is that zoning feels like a durable protection but is subject to the same political pressure that drives conversion, and without complementary instruments (acquisition, easements, covenants, tax policy) the zoning erodes under sustained pressure. The false signal is a protected zoning designation. The harm is the gradual conversion of the waterfront despite the zoning, the loss of the marine industry, and the discovery — too late — that the zoning was not the durable protection it appeared to be.

### Preserving the Parcels, Losing the Network

The community protects the visible water-dependent parcels (the piers, the fish houses) but allows the upland support area (gear storage, truck access, marine suppliers, parking) to convert to residential, and the protected piers become nonfunctional because the supporting network is gone. The trap mechanism is that the support area is less visible and less politically salable as "working waterfront," so it converts first, and the protected parcels cannot operate without it. The false signal is that the iconic waterfront structures are preserved. The harm is a waterfront of protected but nonfunctional piers — a museum of marine industry rather than a working one — and the eventual conversion of even the protected parcels once they are demonstrably unused.

### Residential Encroachment Followed by Operational Curtailment

Residential development is allowed adjacent to the working waterfront, the residents complain about the noise, odor, and early-morning activity of the marine industry, and the political pressure leads to operational restrictions (limited hours, noise caps, odor controls) that make the industry nonviable. The trap mechanism is that the conflict is predictable and the resolution consistently favors the residential complainants, because they are more numerous and politically organized than the marine industry, and each restriction is framed as a reasonable compromise. The false signal is responsive government addressing resident complaints. The harm is the gradual curtailment of water-dependent operations until the industry is nonviable, the loss of the working waterfront through complaint rather than conversion, and the reinforcement of the residential pattern that caused the conflict.

### Protection Without Economic Viability

The community protects the working-waterfront land base but does not address the marine industry's economics (navigational access, infrastructure, workforce housing, business viability), and the protected parcels sit underused or empty because the industry cannot afford to operate on them. The trap mechanism is that land protection without economic support preserves the form of the working waterfront without the function, and the empty protected parcels become evidence that the waterfront "isn't viable" — generating the political argument for conversion. The false signal is preserved waterfront parcels. The harm is the eventual conversion of the protected parcels (because they are "unused"), the loss of the marine industry, and the self-fulfilling prophecy that protection failed when the failure was the absence of complementary economic strategy.

## Self-Check

- Is "water-dependent use" defined operationally, with zoning that prioritizes these uses on the waterfront and restricts non-water-dependent uses to inland locations?
- Is the conversion pressure addressed with layered instruments (zoning, acquisition, easements, covenants, TDR, tax abatement) rather than zoning alone?
- Is the working waterfront mapped and protected as an interdependent network of parcels and upland support area, not just the visible water-dependent parcels?
- Are right-to-farm-style protections, buyer disclosure, and buffers in place to manage the conflict between working waterfront and adjacent residential?
- Does the strategy address the marine industry's economic viability (dredging, infrastructure, workforce housing, business support), not just the land protection?
- Were the marine industry and the community actively engaged in the strategy's design, and is the engagement maintained over time?
- If the zoning on the waterfront were challenged or weakened in five years, would complementary instruments (acquisition, easements, covenants) preserve the water-dependent uses?
- If the protected parcels were viewed in ten years, would they be functioning as a working waterfront, or would they be preserved but empty — a museum rather than an industry?
