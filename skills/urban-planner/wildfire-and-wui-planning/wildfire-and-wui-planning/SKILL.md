# Wildfire and Wildland-Urban Interface Planning

Wildfire and wildland-urban interface (WUI) planning combines land-use authority, fire science, building codes, vegetation management, and evacuation engineering in a way that few other planning specialties do. The judgment problem is that wildfire risk is dynamic and non-linear: it depends on fuel loading, topography, weather, ignition sources, structure characteristics, and the spatial arrangement of the built environment, all of which interact. Planners frequently borrow from floodplain or hazard-mitigation templates and treat wildfire as a mapped hazard zone with a fixed boundary, which produces regulations that are both too blunt for the varied exposure within the zone and too weak at the ignition-to-structure pathway where most losses actually occur.

The stakes are high and rising. WUI development, longer fire seasons, drought stress on vegetation, and insurer withdrawal from high-risk markets have made wildfire a primary driver of community risk in much of the western and southern United States and in analogous regions abroad. The planner's latitude is significant because model codes (the International Wildland-Urban Interface Code, NFPA 1140, and state fire codes) are adoptable in many configurations, and because comprehensive plans and subdivision ordinances can shape exposure decades ahead of the building code. The skill is in using that latitude before the next fire, not after.

## Core Rules

### Use the Right Risk Basis, Not Just a Fuel Map

A defensible WUI plan begins with a multi-layer risk characterization: the relevant state or federal wildfire hazard map, LANDFIRE or equivalent fuel and vegetation data, slope and aspect from best-available elevation data, historical fire occurrence and ignition density, ember exposure modeling where available, and locally calibrated fire weather data including wind patterns that drive extreme fire behavior. A single fuel-model or vegetation map is not a risk map. Where the community has a Community Wildfire Protection Plan (CWPP) or a state wildlife action plan with fire-return-interval data, reconcile those with the regulatory hazard layer and document where they disagree. The plan should make clear which layer governs regulatory triggers and which informs non-regulatory guidance.

### Distinguish the Three WUI Exposure Types

The WUI is not uniform. The three recognized types - intermix (structures scattered within wildland vegetation), interface (structures adjacent to contiguous wildland vegetation), and occluded (vegetation embedded within the built environment, such as urban canyons and parks) - have different risk profiles and different regulatory responses. Intermix exposure is dominated by direct flame and radiative heat and by the difficulty of suppression access; interface exposure is dominated by ember showers across the boundary; occluded exposure is dominated by rapid fire spread through contiguous vegetation in dense neighborhoods. A plan or ordinance that applies one uniform standard across all three will either over-regulate low-risk interface lots or under-protect high-risk intermix and occluded areas. Map the three types and calibrate standards to each.

### Regulate the Structure-Ignition Pathway, Not Just the Fuel Buffer

Most structure losses in WUI fires are caused by embers igniting fine fuels in and around the building - roof and gutter debris, siding, decks, fences, and near-structure vegetation - rather than by flame front contact. A defensible-space standard alone (the typical 30/100-foot fuel-reduction zone) addresses one pathway but not the others. Effective WUI regulation combines defensible space with ignition-resistant construction (Class A roofing, ember-resistant vents, noncombustible siding and fencing within a defined distance, enclosed eaves, dual-pane tempered glass), access and water-supply standards for suppression, and management of community-scale fuel breaks. When advising on code adoption, sequence the provisions so that the highest-impact, lowest-cost items (roofing and vents) are not buried behind the most politically difficult items (large-lot fuel modification).

### Treat Evacuation and Access as a Life-Safety Constraint

WUI developments are often single-access subdivisions on narrow winding roads in steep terrain, which makes simultaneous evacuation under fire conditions dangerous or impossible. Evaluate evacuation capacity as a primary design constraint, not an afterthought. Two points of access should be the default for any new WUI subdivision; where terrain makes that infeasible, the plan or ordinance must require compensating measures such as widened roads, turnouts, secondary emergency-only access, neighborhood safe refuge or temporary relocation areas, and coordination with the fire agency on shelter-in-place feasibility. Model traffic demand against worst-case concurrent evacuation plus inbound fire-suppression traffic, and do not accept a road network that cannot clear the subdivision in the time window implied by fire spread modeling.

### Address Existing Development and the Retrofit Problem

New WUI development is the easier problem because ignition-resistant standards can be imposed at permitting. The harder and larger problem is the existing housing stock built before modern WUI codes, much of it with wood-shake roofing, open eaves, single-pane windows, and flammable decking. A plan that ignores existing structures will not meaningfully reduce community risk. Identify retrofit pathways: point-of-sale disclosure of wildfire risk and defensible-space status, retrofit triggers at the time of substantial improvement or re-roofing, voluntary cost-share defensible-space programs, and prioritized public investment in vegetation management around the highest-risk neighborhoods. Be explicit about which retrofits are mandatory, which are incentivized, and which are educational only.

### Coordinate Water Supply and Suppression Infrastructure

WUI fires stress water systems in ways that urban fires do not: power loss disables pumps, multiple simultaneous structure ignitions overwhelm hydrant demand, and storage designed for domestic peak flow is inadequate for wildfire suppression. Confirm that the water system serving new WUI development has storage and delivery capacity for suppression under power-loss conditions, or require on-site suppression water (cisterns, draft sources, or dedicated storage with backup power). Coordinate hydrant spacing and flow with the responding fire agency, and do not assume that municipal water service exists in all WUI areas - many intermix areas rely on tanks and draft sites that must be engineered and maintained.

### Integrate Vegetation Management Across Jurisdictions

Vegetation does not respect parcel or jurisdictional lines. A defensible-space requirement on private lots is undermined by unmanaged fuels on adjacent public land, utility corridors, transportation rights-of-way, or HOA open space. The plan should identify the major landowners in the WUI, assign or confirm fuel-management responsibilities, and establish a coordination mechanism - typically a fuels-management committee, an MOU among agencies, or a designated WUI coordinator. Prescribed fire, mechanical treatment, grazing, and biomass removal each have ecological and logistical tradeoffs; do not prescribe a single method without reference to the local fire ecology and air-quality constraints.

### Plan for the Insurance and Rebuilding Dynamics

In markets where insurers are withdrawing or non-renewing coverage, the planning implications are substantial: properties become uninsurable or unaffordable to insure, lending is constrained, and post-fire rebuilding is slower and more contentious. Address insurance explicitly in the plan's risk narrative, and recognize that post-fire rebuilding is a critical window for risk reduction. Pre-adopt a post-disaster rebuilding framework that clarifies which standards apply to rebuilds, whether nonconforming status is reset, and whether the community will offer expedited permitting for rebuilds that exceed code. Failing to plan for this window results in reconstruction of the same exposure that just burned.

## Common Traps

### Mapping the WUI as a Single Hazard Polygon

The mechanism is that GIS produces a clean WUI boundary from housing-density and vegetation data, and the ordinance adopts that polygon as the regulatory trigger. The false signal is a boundary that appears to separate safe from unsafe areas. The harm is that lots just outside the line receive no ignition-resistant standards despite identical ember exposure, while lots inside the line are all regulated identically regardless of whether they are interface, intermix, or occluded. Use the polygon as a starting point but regulate based on site-specific exposure and the three WUI types.

### Assuming Defensible Space Alone Prevents Loss

The mechanism is that the 100-foot defensible-space standard is the most visible and most enforced WUI requirement, so it is treated as the primary loss-prevention measure. The false signal is a cleared lot that looks fire-safe. The harm is that structures with defensible space but combustible roofing, open eaves, and ember-entry vents still ignite from ember showers, and the community believes it has solved the problem. Pair defensible space with ignition-resistant construction standards in the same ordinance.

### Designing Roads to Urban Standards in Steep WUI Terrain

The mechanism is that standard subdivision road standards (width, grade, radius) are applied in WUI areas because they are already in the code, and deviating requires engineering justification. The false signal is a compliant road network. The harm is that roads meeting urban geometric standards may still be single-access, too narrow for simultaneous evacuation and suppression, or too steep for apparatus under fire conditions. Evaluate WUI roads against fire-access and evacuation criteria, not only the standard subdivision geometric criteria.

### Ignoring Ember Transport Across the Defensible-Space Boundary

The mechanism is that defensible space is measured from the structure outward, and the plan focuses on the 100-foot zone. The false signal is that fuels beyond 100 feet are not a structure-ignition concern. The harm is that ember showers from vegetation, outbuildings, and neighboring structures hundreds of feet away ignite receptive fuels on and near the target structure. Address community-scale fuel breaks, parcel-level structural hardening, and the cumulative effect of neighboring parcels.

### Deferring Existing-Structure Retrofit to "Voluntary Programs" Without Funding

The mechanism is that mandatory retrofit is politically difficult, so the plan lists voluntary cost-share programs without identifying a funding source. The false signal is an action item that appears to address existing exposure. The harm is that participation is low, the highest-risk structures are never touched, and the plan's risk reduction is negligible. Either fund the voluntary program at a level that produces measurable participation, or use point-of-sale and substantial-improvement triggers that reach existing structures without a standalone mandate.

### Conflating Prescribed Fire With Wildfire Risk Reduction Without Ecological Basis

The mechanism is that prescribed fire is a recognized fuel-reduction tool, so plans recommend it broadly. The false signal is a vegetation-management strategy that appears science-based. The harm is that prescribed fire applied in the wrong fire regime (for example, in vegetation types that did not historically burn frequently) can damage ecosystems, invade non-native species, and erode public trust. Tie prescribed fire recommendations to the local fire-return interval and ecological condition, and coordinate with ecologists and air-quality regulators.

## Self-Check

- Does the risk characterization combine fuel, slope, weather, ignition history, and ember exposure, and does it distinguish the regulatory hazard layer from best-available science where the two diverge?
- Are the three WUI exposure types (intermix, interface, occluded) mapped separately, with standards calibrated to each type rather than a single uniform requirement?
- Do the adopted or recommended regulations address both defensible space and ignition-resistant construction, with the highest-impact, lowest-cost provisions (roofing, vents) sequenced for early adoption?
- Has evacuation capacity been modeled for worst-case concurrent evacuation plus inbound suppression traffic, and are two points of access required or compensated for where infeasible?
- Does the plan address existing development through retrofit triggers, point-of-sale disclosure, or funded voluntary programs, rather than regulating only new construction?
- Are water-supply and suppression-infrastructure requirements coordinated with the responding fire agency, including power-loss and storage scenarios?
- Are the major WUI landowners identified, with fuel-management responsibilities assigned and a coordination mechanism established?
- Does the plan address the insurance and post-fire rebuilding dynamics, including a pre-adopted rebuilding framework that clarifies applicable standards and nonconforming status?
- For each recommended vegetation-management method, is there a reference to local fire ecology and air-quality constraints rather than a generic recommendation?
- Can the planner articulate, for each higher standard, the specific exposure or loss-history justification rather than citing a model code generically?
