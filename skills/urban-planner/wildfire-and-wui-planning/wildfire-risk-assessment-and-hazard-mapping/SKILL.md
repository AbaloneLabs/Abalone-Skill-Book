# Wildfire Risk Assessment and Hazard Mapping

The wildfire risk assessment and the hazard mapping identify where the wildfire threatens the community, and the assessment and the mapping guide the mitigation, the regulation, and the response. The judgment problem is that the wildfire risk is the complex (the fuel, the weather, the topography, the ignition, the exposure), the WUI is the interface where the development meets the wildland, and the assessment and the mapping must integrate these factors to identify the risk and the priority, and the poor assessment produces the inadequate mitigation. A risk assessment that is conducted well (the comprehensive factors, the WUI mapping, the modeling, the prioritization) guides the effective mitigation, while an assessment that is conducted poorly (the incomplete factors, the no WUI mapping, the no modeling) fails. This skill covers the wildfire risk assessment and the hazard mapping decisions that determine whether the community understands and addresses its wildfire risk. It complements the broader wildfire skill by focusing on the assessment and the mapping dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Integrate the Fuel, the Weather, the Topography, and the Ignition

The wildfire risk assessment should integrate the fuel (the vegetation, the density, the continuity), the weather (the wind, the temperature, the humidity, the drought), the topography (the slope, the aspect, the elevation, the chimneys), and the ignition (the human, the lightning), because the wildfire behavior depends on all these factors, and the integration produces the comprehensive risk. The rule is to integrate the factors, using the fuel assessment (the fuel models, the fuel loading, the fuel continuity), the weather analysis (the fire weather, the red flag conditions, the climate trends), the topographic analysis (the slope, the aspect, the terrain features), and the ignition analysis (the ignition history, the ignition probability). The fuel assessment should map the fuel models and the loading, because the fuel is the fire's energy, and the dense and the continuous fuel produces the intense fire. The weather analysis should assess the fire weather patterns, because the hot, the dry, and the windy conditions drive the fire, and the analysis identifies the risk. The topographic analysis should assess the slope and the aspect, because the fire moves faster uphill, and the south-facing slopes are the drier, and the terrain channels the fire. The ignition analysis should assess the ignition history and the probability, because the fire requires the ignition, and the analysis identifies the likely sources.

### Map the WUI and the Exposure of the Development

The WUI mapping should identify where the development meets the wildland, because the WUI is the zone of the risk, and the mapping guides the regulation and the mitigation. The rule is to map the WUI and the exposure, using the WUI delineation (the intermix, the interface, the occluded), the exposure analysis (the ember, the radiant heat, the direct flame), the structure inventory (the structures in the WUI, the vulnerable structures), and the community assessment (the communities at risk, the priority communities). The WUI delineation should identify the intermix (the development scattered in the wildland), the interface (the development adjacent to the wildland), and the occluded (the wildland within the development), because the WUI types differ in the risk, and the delineation guides the response. The exposure analysis should assess the ember exposure (the embers that travel the miles), the radiant heat (the heat from the adjacent fire), and the direct flame (the flame contact), because the exposure types differ in the mitigation, and the analysis guides it. The structure inventory should identify the structures in the WUI and the vulnerable structures, because the structures are the values at risk, and the inventory guides the prioritization. The community assessment should identify the communities at risk, because the communities are the priority, and the assessment guides the resources.

### Model the Fire Behavior and the Spread

The fire behavior modeling should simulate the fire spread and the intensity, because the modeling predicts the fire's path and the impact, and the prediction guides the mitigation and the response. The rule is to model the fire behavior and the spread, using the fire behavior models (the FlamMap, the FARSITE, the FSPro), the scenario analysis (the different weather, the different ignition), the intensity analysis (the flame length, the rate of spread, the fireline intensity), and the probability analysis (the likelihood of the fire). The fire behavior models should simulate the fire under the specific conditions, because the models integrate the fuel, the weather, and the topography, and the simulation predicts the behavior. The scenario analysis should model the different scenarios (the worst-case weather, the likely ignition), because the fire behavior varies by the conditions, and the scenarios capture the range. The intensity analysis should assess the flame length and the rate of spread, because the intensity determines the controllability and the impact, and the analysis informs the mitigation. The probability analysis should assess the likelihood of the fire, because the probability informs the priority, and the high-probability areas are the priority.

### Prioritize the Mitigation and the Fuel Treatments

The risk assessment should prioritize the mitigation and the fuel treatments, because the resources are the limited, and the prioritization directs them to the highest-risk areas, and the unprioritized mitigation wastes the resources. The rule is to prioritize the mitigation and the fuel treatments, using the risk ranking (the high-risk areas identified), the treatment prioritization (the fuel breaks, the thinning, the prescribed fire), the community prioritization (the communities at risk), and the landscape prioritization (the strategic landscape treatments). The risk ranking should identify the high-risk areas, because the ranking guides the prioritization, and the high-risk areas are the priority. The treatment prioritization should identify the fuel breaks, the thinning, and the prescribed fire, because the treatments reduce the risk, and the prioritization directs them. The community prioritization should identify the communities at risk, because the communities are the values, and the prioritization protects them. The landscape prioritization should identify the strategic landscape treatments, because the landscape-scale treatments (the fuel breaks, the restoration) protect the broader area, and the prioritization implements them.

### Use the Assessment for the Regulation and the Planning

The risk assessment should inform the regulation and the planning, because the assessment identifies the risk, and the regulation and the planning respond to it, and the unused assessment is the wasted effort. The rule is to use the assessment for the regulation and the planning, using the WUI regulation (the building codes, the defensible space, the materials), the comprehensive plan (the wildfire element, the risk policies), the land use planning (the development standards in the WUI), and the evacuation planning (the routes, the capacity). The WUI regulation should adopt the building codes and the defensible space standards, because the regulation reduces the risk, and the assessment guides it. The comprehensive plan should include the wildfire element, because the plan guides the community, and the element addresses the risk. The land use planning should apply the development standards in the WUI, because the development in the WUI is the risk, and the standards mitigate it. The evacuation planning should use the assessment, because the evacuation routes and the capacity depend on the risk, and the assessment informs them.

## Common Traps

### Factors Incomplete, Risk Misunderstood

The risk factors are incomplete (the fuel assessed, the weather and the topography ignored), and the risk is misunderstood, because the integration was not conducted. The trap is that the incomplete factors produce the misunderstood risk. The mechanism is that the integration was not conducted, and the one factor was assessed. The false signal is that "we assessed the fuel." The harm is that the risk is incomplete, the weather and the topography are ignored, the assessment misleads, and the factors that should be integrated are not.

### WUI Not Mapped, Development Exposure Unknown

The WUI is not mapped, and the development exposure is unknown, because the WUI delineation was not conducted. The trap is that the unmapped WUI produces the unknown exposure. The mechanism is that the WUI mapping was not conducted, and the development was not assessed. The false signal is that "we know the wildfire risk." The harm is that the WUI is not identified, the exposure is unknown, the regulation is unguided, and the WUI that should guide the mitigation does not.

### Fire Behavior Not Modeled, Spread Unknown

The fire behavior is not modeled, and the spread is unknown, because the modeling was not conducted. The trap is that the unmodeled behavior produces the unknown spread. The mechanism is that the modeling was not conducted, and the behavior was assumed. The false signal is that "we assessed the risk." The harm is that the spread is unknown, the intensity is unknown, the mitigation is unguided, and the modeling that should predict the behavior does not.

### Mitigation Unprioritized, Resources Wasted

The mitigation is unprioritized, and the resources are wasted on the low-risk areas, because the risk ranking was not conducted. The trap is that the unprioritized mitigation wastes the resources. The mechanism is that the ranking was not conducted, and the mitigation was distributed. The false signal is that "we are mitigating." The harm is that the high-risk areas are not addressed, the low-risk areas are treated, the resources are wasted, and the prioritization that should direct the resources does not.

### Assessment Unused, Regulation Unguided

The assessment is unused, and the regulation is unguided, because the assessment was not connected to the regulation and the planning. The trap is that the unused assessment produces the unguided regulation. The mechanism is that the assessment was not connected, and the regulation was developed separately. The false signal is that "we have an assessment." The harm is that the assessment is wasted, the regulation does not address the risk, the mitigation is inadequate, and the assessment that should guide the regulation does not.

## Self-Check

- Are the fuel, the weather, the topography, and the ignition integrated, with the fuel assessment, the weather analysis, the topographic analysis, and the ignition analysis?
- Is the WUI mapped and the exposure analyzed, with the delineation, the exposure, the structure inventory, and the community assessment?
- Is the fire behavior modeled, with the models, the scenarios, the intensity, and the probability?
- Is the mitigation prioritized, with the risk ranking, the treatment prioritization, the community prioritization, and the landscape prioritization?
- Is the assessment used for the regulation and the planning, with the WUI regulation, the comprehensive plan, the land use, and the evacuation?
- Are the factors integrated, or are they incomplete?
- Is the WUI mapped, or is the exposure unknown?
- Is the fire behavior modeled, or is the spread unknown?
- Is the mitigation prioritized, or are the resources wasted?
- Is the assessment used, or is the regulation unguided?
