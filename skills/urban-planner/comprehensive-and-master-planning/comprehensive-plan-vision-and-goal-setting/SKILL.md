# Comprehensive Plan Vision and Goal Setting

The vision and goals section of a comprehensive plan is not decorative preamble; it is the legal and analytical anchor for every subsequent recommendation, and in jurisdictions where the plan is binding or quasi-binding, it is the standard against which zoning, capital, and regulatory decisions are tested for consistency. The judgment problem is that vision statements are easy to write vaguely and almost impossible to write usefully. A vision full of aspirational adjectives — "vibrant," "sustainable," "welcoming," "thriving" — satisfies everyone at the adoption hearing and provides no guidance to the planner who must later decide whether a specific rezoning, road project, or code amendment advances or contradicts the plan. The skill is in converting diffuse community aspirations into directional, testable, and prioritized goals that actually constrain and inform decisions.

## Core Rules

### Make Goals Directional and Testable, Not Aspirational

A goal that cannot be tested against a specific decision is not a goal; it is a slogan. "Promote a vibrant downtown" tells a planner nothing about whether a proposed surface parking lot on Main Street advances or contradicts the goal. "Direct growth and public investment to the downtown core and existing transit corridors, and discourage auto-oriented strip development at the city edges" is testable: a downtown housing project advances it, an edge-of-town strip mall contradicts it. The rule is that every goal should imply a direction of change (more of X, less of Y, growth here not there), and a planner reading the goal two years later should be able to classify a typical project as advancing, contradicting, or neutral toward it. If a goal cannot be so classified, it is too vague to function as a plan standard.

### Resolve Competing Goals Explicitly Through Prioritization

Communities want everything: growth and no change, affordability and neighborhood character, economic development and environmental protection. These goals genuinely conflict, and a plan that lists them all without acknowledging the conflict provides no guidance when they collide in a real decision. The rule is to surface the tradeoffs explicitly and establish priority order or geographic context. "In growth areas, housing production and affordability take priority over preservation of existing scale; in established low-density neighborhoods, preservation of character takes priority over densification" is a usable resolution. The alternative — listing both goals without priority — guarantees that every contested decision will relitigate the plan, because both sides can cite a goal. Explicit prioritization is politically harder at adoption but analytically essential afterward.

### Ground the Vision in Evidence About Current Conditions and Trends

A vision disconnected from the community's actual conditions is fantasy, and it will be ignored. Demographic trends, employment bases, housing markets, infrastructure capacity, environmental constraints, and fiscal realities all bound what the plan can plausibly achieve. The rule is that the vision and goals section must be built on a foundations analysis that documents where the community is and where it is heading under current policy, so that the goals represent a genuine change in direction rather than a wish. A goal to "preserve agricultural land" in a community where farmland is being consumed at 5% per year, with no analysis of the development pressure or the fiscal structure driving conversion, is a goal that will fail. The evidence base also gives the goals credibility: they are not staff preferences but responses to documented conditions.

### Connect Goals to Implementation Levers the Jurisdiction Actually Controls

A plan goal is only meaningful if the jurisdiction has a tool to advance it. "Reduce vehicle miles traveled" is a goal that depends on regional transportation policy, state fuel standards, and market behavior largely outside municipal control; "Adopt zoning that permits mixed-use, higher-density development within a half-mile of transit stations, and require new development to complete the pedestrian network" is a goal the jurisdiction can implement through its own zoning and capital codes. The rule is to map each goal to the specific implementation levers — zoning, capital improvements, regulations, incentives, partnerships — and to confirm those levers exist. A goal with no implementation lever is an aspiration the plan cannot deliver, and including it erodes the plan's credibility when it predictably fails.

### Sequence Goals to Reflect Phasing and Dependency

Comprehensive plans cover horizons of 10 to 25 years, and not everything can or should happen at once. Some goals are foundational (infrastructure capacity, land assembly) and must precede others (intensive development, economic revitalization). The rule is to sequence goals into phases — short-term, mid-term, long-term — that reflect these dependencies, so that the plan reads as a strategy rather than a wish list. Sequencing also forces honesty about capacity: a small planning department cannot pursue fifteen priorities simultaneously, and a plan that implies it can will accomplish none of them. Explicit phasing lets the community and decision-makers track progress and adjust, rather than discovering at year ten that nothing was implemented because everything was listed as immediate.

## Common Traps

### The Adjective-Heavy Vision That Means Anything and Nothing

The vision statement is polished until it is rhetorically pleasing — "a vibrant, sustainable, and inclusive community where all residents thrive" — and adopted unanimously because no one disagrees with the adjectives. The trap is that the statement provides no decision guidance. The mechanism is that vague language is politically safe because it is untestable, but it is analytically useless for the same reason. The false signal is that unanimous adoption means the community agrees on direction. The harm is that when a real decision arises, every faction cites the vision in support of its preferred outcome, and the plan provides no arbiter, so the decision is made on other grounds and the plan is revealed as decorative.

### Goals as a List Without Priority or Conflict Resolution

The plan lists fifteen goals covering housing, economy, environment, transportation, equity, character, and fiscal health, all stated as equally important. The trap is that real decisions force tradeoffs among these goals, and a plan that treats them as co-equal provides no resolution mechanism. The mechanism is that avoiding explicit prioritization avoids political conflict at adoption. The false signal is that the plan is "comprehensive" and "balanced." The harm is that the first contested decision relitigates the plan, because both sides can cite a goal, and the decision is made by political power rather than plan policy, defeating the purpose of having a plan.

### Vision Disconnected from Conditions and Capacity

The plan articulates an ambitious vision — doubling downtown employment, becoming a regional arts destination, achieving carbon neutrality — without analyzing whether the conditions, market, infrastructure, and institutional capacity exist to support it. The trap is that the vision is aspirational rather than strategic. The mechanism is that ambition is confused with planning. The false signal is that "we set a bold goal." The harm is that the goals are not implemented, the plan loses credibility, and the community becomes cynical about planning generally, making future planning harder. A modest, achievable vision that is actually implemented serves the community far better than an ambitious one that is not.

### Ignoring the Plan-to-Decision Linkage

The plan is adopted, placed on the shelf, and consulted only when a staff report needs a citation. The trap is that the plan was never designed to be used in decisions; it was designed to be adopted. The mechanism is that the vision and goals were not written with decision-testing in mind. The false signal is that adoption is the endpoint. The harm is that the plan's legal and analytical function — to guide and constrain decisions — is never realized, and the zoning, capital, and regulatory decisions that determine the community's future are made without the plan's direction. The remedy is to design the goals from the outset to be applied to decisions, and to build plan-consistency review into the decision process.

## Self-Check

- Can a planner reading each goal two years later classify a typical project as advancing, contradicting, or neutral toward it, or is the goal too vague to function as a standard?
- Have I surfaced and resolved the genuine conflicts among goals through explicit prioritization or geographic context, rather than listing them as co-equal?
- Is the vision grounded in a foundations analysis of current conditions and trends, so that goals represent a change in direction rather than a wish?
- Is each goal mapped to specific implementation levers the jurisdiction actually controls, and have I confirmed those levers exist?
- Are goals sequenced into phases that reflect dependencies and institutional capacity, rather than presented as simultaneous priorities?
- Have I stripped adjective-heavy language in favor of directional, testable statements of preferred change?
- Does the plan include a mechanism for testing decisions against the goals, so the plan is used rather than shelved?
- Have I distinguished goals the jurisdiction can advance through its own tools from aspirations that depend on external actors, and been honest about the difference?
- Would a decision-maker citing this plan to justify a contested decision be able to point to a specific goal that the decision advances or contradicts?
- Have I confirmed the goals are calibrated to local conditions and capacity, rather than borrowed from another community's plan without adaptation?
