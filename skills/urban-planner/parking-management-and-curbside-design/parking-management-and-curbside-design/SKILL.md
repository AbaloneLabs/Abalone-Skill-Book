# Parking Management and Curbside Design

Parking is the hidden variable that shapes cities. The supply of parking, the rules that govern it, and the price charged for it determine how much land is devoted to storing cars, whether development is feasible, which modes people choose, and how the curb — the most valuable and most contested piece of public right-of-way — functions. Conventional planning treats parking as a requirement to be met: each use must provide a fixed number of spaces based on outdated suburban standards, and the result is abundant, free, or underpriced parking that subsidizes driving, consumes land, renders development infeasible, and hollows out walkability. Modern parking management inverts this: it treats parking as a system to be managed, with supply calibrated to actual demand, prices set to manage availability, and the curb allocated among competing uses based on its highest value. The judgment problem is that parking is politically charged — residents and merchants perceive free, abundant parking as a right and a business necessity — and the evidence that parking management improves outcomes runs against these perceptions. A planner who applies evidence-based parking policy without managing the political transition generates backlash that can reverse the policy; a planner who defers to perceptions perpetuates a system that undermines the community's own goals. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether parking management achieves its outcomes or generates conflict that defeats it.

## Core Rules

### Replace Minimums With Calibrated, Context-Sensitive Approaches

Conventional parking minimums — fixed requirements per use, derived from suburban peak-demand studies — over-supply parking in most contexts, particularly in walkable, transit-served areas where car ownership and trip generation are lower. The rule is to replace minimums with approaches calibrated to context: eliminate minimums in walkable, transit-served areas where the market will provide what is needed; reduce minimums in moderate contexts; and where requirements remain, base them on local demand data rather than borrowed standards. The calibration should account for the use's actual trip generation (which is lower in walkable areas), the availability of shared parking (which reduces per-use requirements), the transit service (which reduces car access), and the management strategies (pricing and permits that manage spillover). A blanket minimum applied regardless of context over-supplies parking, renders development infeasible, and subsidizes the driving that the community's other policies are trying to reduce. The shift from minimums to calibrated approaches is the single highest-leverage parking policy change.

### Price Parking to Manage Availability, Not to Maximize Revenue

The purpose of parking pricing is to manage availability — to ensure that one or two spaces are available on every block, so drivers do not circle searching for spaces (which generates congestion) — not to maximize revenue. The rule is to set prices at the level that achieves target availability (typically 85 percent occupancy, leaving spaces open), with prices that vary by time of day, location, and demand. Underpriced or free parking produces full lots and circling drivers, because the price does not ration the scarce resource. The revenue from pricing should be transparently reinvested in the district where it is generated (streetscape, transit, security), because visible local benefit builds political support for pricing that would otherwise be perceived as a tax. Performance pricing — adjusting prices based on measured occupancy — is more effective and more defensible than fixed prices, because the price is tied to an observable outcome rather than to a policy preference.

### Allocate the Curb by Highest Value, Not by Default

The curb is the most valuable piece of public right-of-way, because it is where access, delivery, transit, parking, and pedestrian activity converge, and conventional allocation — virtually all of it to free or cheap parking — wastes that value. The rule is to allocate the curb based on its highest-value use in each location and time: transit stops and lanes where transit operates; loading zones for delivery and ride-hail where commercial and access demand is high; short-term parking for retail access where turnover supports businesses; bike parking and parklets where active transportation and public realm are priorities; and longer-term parking where that is the highest use. The allocation should vary by time of day (loading in the morning, parking in the evening) and by context (transit on arterials, loading in commercial districts). Curb management — using signage, paint, pricing, and enforcement to allocate the curb deliberately — replaces the default of universal parking with an allocation that serves the district's actual needs and generates more value per foot of curb.

### Manage Spillover Proactively When Supply or Pricing Changes

When parking minimums are reduced or pricing is implemented, the concern is always spillover — drivers parking in adjacent residential streets to avoid fees or because supply is constrained — and unmanaged spillover generates the political opposition that reverses parking policy. The rule is to manage spillover proactively: implement residential permit parking in adjacent neighborhoods before or alongside the policy change, with pricing or limits that prevent all-day commuter storage; monitor spillover after implementation and adjust permits as needed; and communicate that spillover management is part of the policy, not an afterthought. Spillover is real but manageable, and a policy that addresses it explicitly is more defensible than one that denies it. The failure mode is implementing parking reform without spillover management, experiencing the spillover that was predicted, and reversing the reform in response to the opposition it generated — when the spillover could have been managed from the start.

### Use Shared Parking and District Approaches to Reduce Total Supply

Individual uses providing their own parking produces oversupply, because each use sizes for its own peak, and peaks rarely coincide. A restaurant's peak is evening, an office's peak is daytime, a theater's peak is weekend — and shared parking, where multiple uses draw from a common supply, requires fewer total spaces than the sum of individual requirements. The rule is to promote shared parking through district approaches: shared lots or structures serving multiple uses, shared parking agreements recorded against properties, and parking maximums that prevent individual over-supply. District approaches also enable the unbundling of parking from rent or sale price — charging for parking separately — which reveals the true cost of parking and allows households and businesses to choose how much to consume. Shared, unbundled parking reduces total supply, reduces cost, and supports the walkability and density that per-use minimums prevent.

## Common Traps

### Minimums Maintained Because of Perceived Demand

The parking minimums are retained because residents and merchants perceive that more parking is needed, even though the actual demand — measured by occupancy — shows oversupply. The trap is that perception overrides evidence. The mechanism is that the political pressure to maintain minimums is based on the experience of peak demand and scarcity at specific moments, generalized to all times. The false signal is that "we need the parking." The harm is that development remains infeasible (because the parking cost is prohibitive), land is wasted on underused lots, driving is subsidized, and the walkability and density the community wants are prevented by the parking policy it refuses to change.

### Pricing Set to Maximize Revenue, Triggering Backlash

Parking is priced, but the price is set to maximize revenue rather than to manage availability, and the high price generates backlash from residents and merchants who perceive it as a tax. The trap is that revenue-maximizing pricing defeats the political sustainability of pricing. The mechanism is that the price exceeded the level needed for availability, and the excess was perceived as extraction. The false signal is that "pricing is working — revenue is up." The harm is that the backlash reverses the pricing policy, the availability management is lost, and the credibility of parking management is damaged, making future reforms harder.

### Curb Left to Default Parking Allocation

The curb is managed as parking by default, and the competing uses — transit, loading, bike access, public realm — are accommodated only through ad hoc exceptions, while the highest-value uses go un-served. The trap is that default allocation wastes the curb's value. The mechanism is that parking is the incumbent use, and reallocating requires active decision-making that is avoided. The false signal is that "the curb is for parking." The harm is that transit is slowed (no transit lanes), delivery is chaotic (no loading zones), bike access is unsafe (no protected facilities), and the public realm is diminished (no parklets or widened sidewalks), because the curb's value is captured by low-turnover parking rather than by the uses that would generate more value for the district.

### Spillover Unmanaged, Generating Reversal

Parking minimums are reduced or pricing is implemented without spillover management in adjacent neighborhoods, and the predicted spillover occurs, generating opposition that reverses the policy. The trap is that an unmanaged consequence defeats a sound policy. The mechanism is that the policy change shifted parking demand to unprotected adjacent streets, and the affected residents organized against the change. The false signal is that "the policy failed." The harm is that the reform is reversed, the conditions the reform was meant to address persist, and the political capital invested is wasted because a foreseeable consequence was not managed.

### Oversupply Through Individual Per-Use Requirements

Each use is required to provide its own parking, sized for its own peak, and the total supply far exceeds what shared parking would require, consuming land and capital. The trap is that individual requirements produce systemic oversupply. The mechanism is that peaks do not coincide, but per-use requirements assume they do. The false signal is that "each use is providing its required parking." The harm is that land and capital are wasted on underused parking, development is more expensive and less feasible, and the oversupply subsidizes driving and undermines the walkability the community wants, because the parking system is structured to over-supply regardless of actual demand.

## Self-Check

- Have parking minimums been eliminated or reduced in walkable, transit-served areas, and where requirements remain, are they based on local demand data rather than borrowed standards?
- Is parking priced to achieve target availability (approximately 85 percent occupancy), with prices varying by time and location, and is revenue transparently reinvested in the district?
- Is the curb allocated by highest-value use — transit, loading, short-term access, bike, public realm — varying by location and time, rather than defaulted to parking?
- Is spillover managed proactively through residential permit parking and monitoring, before and alongside pricing or supply changes?
- Are shared parking and district approaches used to reduce total supply, and is parking unbundled from rent and sale price to reveal its true cost?
- Are parking decisions based on measured occupancy and demand data, or on perception and borrowed standards?
- Is pricing set for availability management with local revenue reinvestment, or for revenue maximization that risks backlash?
- Does the curb allocation serve the district's actual needs, or does it preserve parking as the default at the expense of higher-value uses?
- If minimums are reduced or pricing implemented, is there a spillover management plan that prevents the opposition that reverses reform?
- Does the parking system support the community's walkability, transit, and density goals, or does it subsidize driving and undermine them through oversupply and underpricing?
