# Parking Requirements and Supply Management

The off-street parking minimums — the mandatory number of spaces per unit or per square foot — have been the dominant parking policy for decades, and they have produced the oversupply, the wasted land, the increased housing costs, the degraded walkability, and the induced driving that the research now documents. The judgment problem is that the parking minimums were adopted to solve the shortage, but they created the oversupply, and the reform of the minimums (the reduction, the elimination, the replacement with the maximums) faces the political opposition and the implementation challenges that the status quo does not. The planner must navigate the evidence (the parking utilization studies, the land and cost impacts), the politics (the neighborhood opposition, the developer preferences), and the implementation (the transition, the spillover management, the monitoring) to reform the parking requirements in the way that produces the appropriate supply and the quality places. This skill covers the parking requirements and the supply management decisions that determine whether the community mandates the oversupply or manages the appropriate supply. It complements the broader parking skill by focusing on the requirements and the supply dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Base the Requirements on the Actual Demand, Not the Peak or the Assumption

The parking requirements should be based on the actual demand (the observed utilization), not the peak demand (the worst-case scenario) or the assumed demand (the generic standard from the manual), because the peak and the assumed demand overstate the need, and the overstated need produces the oversupply. The rule is to base the requirements on the actual demand, conducting the parking utilization studies (the observation of the occupied spaces at the different times, the days, the seasons), the shared parking analysis (the different peak times of the different uses), and the local calibration (the requirements adjusted to the local conditions, not the national manual). The utilization studies should capture the actual use, because the actual use is the evidence, and the assumed use is the guess. The shared parking analysis should account for the mix of the uses, because the mixed-use development shares the parking across the uses with the different peaks, and the shared use reduces the total demand. The local calibration should adjust for the local conditions (the transit access, the walkability, the demographics, the pricing), because the local conditions affect the demand, and the national manual does not reflect the local conditions. The requirements should be set at the level that ensures the availability without the oversupply, because the availability is the goal, and the oversupply is the cost.

### Eliminate the Minimums or Replace Them With the Maximums

The parking minimums should be eliminated (the no-minimum approach) or replaced with the maximums (the cap on the supply), because the minimums mandate the oversupply, and the elimination or the maximum allows the market to provide the appropriate supply. The rule is to eliminate the minimums or replace them with the maximums, using the elimination in the transit-rich and the walkable areas (where the car ownership is lower and the alternatives are available), the reduction in the other areas (the lower minimums that reflect the actual demand), and the maximums in the areas where the oversupply is the concern (the downtowns, the transit corridors). The elimination should be comprehensive where appropriate, because the partial elimination (the elimination for some uses but not others) creates the inconsistencies, and the comprehensive elimination is the simpler and the more effective approach. The maximums should be set at the level that prevents the oversupply, because the developers may overbuild the parking (the lender requirement, the market expectation), and the maximum caps the supply. The elimination and the maximum should be paired with the spillover management (the curb pricing, the permitting), because the development without the on-site parking may shift the demand to the curb, and the management addresses the shift. The reform should be phased or piloted, because the phased reform allows the learning and the adjustment, and the pilot demonstrates the effect.

### Manage the Shared and the Unbundled Parking

The shared parking (the spaces shared across the uses and the buildings) and the unbundled parking (the parking sold separately from the housing or the commercial space) are the strategies that reduce the total supply and the cost, and the management of these strategies determines whether they produce the efficiency or the conflict. The rule is to manage the shared and the unbundled parking, using the shared parking agreements (the legal arrangements for the shared use), the unbundled pricing (the parking priced and sold separately), and the technology (the access control, the payment, the reservation). The shared parking should be formalized through the agreements, because the informal sharing may not be reliable, and the agreement ensures the access and the maintenance. The unbundled parking should be priced at the market rate, because the market rate reveals the true cost of the parking, and the below-market rate does not reduce the demand. The unbundled parking should be required in the new development, because the developer may bundle the parking to simplify the marketing, and the requirement ensures the unbundling. The shared and the unbundled parking should be coordinated with the curb management, because the shared and the unbundled parking may shift the demand to the curb, and the coordination manages the shift.

### Address the Spillover and the Transition

The elimination or the reduction of the parking requirements may shift the parking demand to the curb and the neighboring properties (the spillover), and the management of the spillover and the transition determines whether the reform succeeds or generates the backlash. The rule is to address the spillover and the transition, using the curb management (the permit parking, the pricing, the time limits), the monitoring (the observation of the spillover), the phased implementation (the gradual reform that allows the adjustment), and the community engagement (the communication and the involvement). The curb management should include the residential permit parking, because the spillover into the residential neighborhoods is the most common concern, and the permit parking reserves the curb for the residents. The monitoring should track the spillover, because the monitoring provides the evidence, and the evidence-based management is the defensible management. The phased implementation should allow the adjustment, because the reform that is implemented all at once may generate the backlash, and the phased reform allows the learning. The community engagement should address the concerns, because the concerns (the spillover, the access, the safety) are the legitimate concerns, and the engagement builds the support.

### Transition to the Market-Based Provision

The parking should be transitioned from the mandated provision (the minimums) to the market-based provision (the developer provides what the market demands), because the market-based provision produces the appropriate supply, and the mandated provision produces the oversupply. The rule is to transition to the market-based provision, using the elimination of the minimums, the private provision (the commercial parking operators), the pricing (the market-rate parking that reveals the cost), and the demand management (the promotion of the alternatives). The market-based provision should allow the developer to decide the supply, because the developer knows the market, and the mandated supply does not reflect the market. The private provision should include the commercial operators, because the operators provide the public parking that the individual developments do not, and the operators manage the supply efficiently. The pricing should be at the market rate, because the market rate reveals the cost and manages the demand, and the below-market rate does not. The demand management should promote the alternatives (the transit, the bicycling, the walking, the carpooling), because the alternatives reduce the parking demand, and the reduction allows the lower supply.

## Common Traps

### Requirements Based on the Peak or the Manual, Oversupply Results

The parking requirements are based on the peak demand or the generic manual, and the requirements overstate the need, and the oversupply results, because the actual demand is lower than the peak or the manual assumes. The trap is that the peak- or manual-based requirements produce the oversupply. The mechanism is that the utilization studies were not conducted, and the requirements were set by the default. The false signal is that "the requirements are standard." The harm is that the parking is oversupplied, the land is wasted, the housing costs increase, the walkability degrades, and the requirements that should ensure the availability produce the oversupply.

### Minimums Retained, Oversupply Mandated

The minimums are retained, and the oversupply is mandated, because the reform was not pursued, and the developers are required to provide the parking the market does not demand. The trap is that the retained minimums mandate the oversupply. The mechanism is that the elimination or the reduction was not adopted, and the minimums persisted. The false signal is that "the parking is required." The harm is that the parking is oversupplied, the development costs increase, the housing is less affordable, the land is wasted, and the minimums that should ensure the availability mandate the oversupply.

### Shared and Unbundled Parking Not Managed, Conflicts Result

The shared and the unbundled parking are not managed, and the conflicts result (the shared parking disputes, the unbundled parking ignored), because the management was not pursued. The trap is that the unmanaged shared and unbundled parking causes the conflicts. The mechanism is that the agreements and the pricing were not implemented, and the sharing and the unbundling were informal. The false signal is that "the parking is shared." The harm is that the shared parking is unreliable, the unbundled parking is not priced, the conflicts result, and the strategies that should reduce the supply and the cost do not.

### Spillover Not Managed, Backlash Follows

The spillover from the reduced or eliminated requirements is not managed, and the backlash follows, because the curb management and the monitoring were not implemented. The trap is that the unmanaged spillover generates the backlash. The mechanism is that the curb management was not implemented, and the spillover was not monitored. The false signal is that "the reform is working." The harm is that the spillover occurs, the neighborhoods complain, the political backlash follows, the reform is reversed, and the spillover that should have been managed undermines the reform.

### Market-Based Provision Not Transitioned, Mandated Oversupply Persists

The market-based provision is not transitioned, and the mandated oversupply persists, because the transition was not pursued. The trap is that the untransitioned provision maintains the oversupply. The mechanism is that the minimums were not eliminated, and the market was not allowed to provide the supply. The false signal is that "the parking is provided." The harm is that the oversupply persists, the land is wasted, the costs increase, the walkability degrades, and the provision that should be market-based remains mandated.

## Self-Check

- Are the requirements based on the actual demand, with the utilization studies, the shared parking analysis, and the local calibration?
- Are the minimums eliminated or replaced with the maximums, with the comprehensive elimination in the transit-rich areas and the maximums where the oversupply is the concern?
- Is the shared and the unbundled parking managed, with the agreements, the pricing, and the technology?
- Is the spillover addressed, with the curb management, the monitoring, the phased implementation, and the community engagement?
- Is the provision transitioned to the market-based, with the elimination, the private provision, the pricing, and the demand management?
- Are the requirements demand-based, or are they peak- or manual-based?
- Are the minimums eliminated, or is the oversupply mandated?
- Is the shared parking managed, or do the conflicts result?
- Is the spillover managed, or does the backlash follow?
- Is the provision market-based, or is it mandated?
