# Parking Technology and Curbside Innovation

The curb is being transformed by the technology and the new mobility services — the sensors, the payment apps, the ride-hailing, the delivery, the micromobility, the electric vehicles, the automated vehicles — and the management of this transformation determines whether the curb becomes the efficient multimodal asset or the chaotic conflict zone. The judgment problem is that the technology offers the capabilities (the real-time data, the dynamic pricing, the flexible allocation) that the conventional management lacks, but the technology requires the policy framework (the data standards, the privacy, the equity, the public benefit) that the conventional management did not need, and the planner must integrate the technology and the policy to produce the curb that serves the public. A community that manages the curb innovation well (the data-driven allocation, the dynamic pricing, the multimodal priority) creates the efficient and the equitable curb, while a community that manages it poorly faces the congestion, the conflict, and the private capture of the public asset. This skill covers the parking technology and the curbside innovation decisions that determine whether the curb transformation serves the public or the private interests. It complements the broader parking skill by focusing on the technology and the innovation dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Deploy the Sensors and the Data Systems Strategically

The sensors (the in-ground, the camera-based, the mobile) and the data systems (the collection, the analysis, the reporting) provide the real-time and the historical data on the parking and the curb use, and the strategic deployment of these systems determines whether the data informs the management or is wasted. The rule is to deploy the sensors and the data systems strategically, selecting the technology that fits the context (the in-ground for the fixed spaces, the camera-based for the broader coverage, the mobile for the periodic survey), the data architecture (the open standards, the interoperability, the integration with the other systems), and the use cases (the occupancy monitoring, the enforcement, the wayfinding, the analytics). The technology selection should be based on the needs, not the novelty, because the technology that does not serve the management is the wasted investment. The data architecture should use the open standards (the APIs, the data sharing), because the proprietary systems lock the community into the vendor, and the open standards allow the flexibility. The data should be used for the management (the pricing adjustments, the allocation changes, the enforcement) and the policy (the performance metrics, the trend analysis, the planning), because the data that is not used is the wasted data. The data should be protected (the privacy, the security), because the curb data may include the personal information, and the protection is the obligation.

### Design the Dynamic Curb for the Multiple Users

The curb serves the multiple users — the parking, the loading, the ride-hailing, the delivery, the micromobility, the transit, the pedestrians — and the dynamic curb (the flexible allocation that changes by the time of day and the demand) serves the multiple users more efficiently than the static curb (the fixed allocation that does not change). The rule is to design the dynamic curb for the multiple users, using the flexible zones (the spaces that change function by the time of day), the digital signage (the real-time information on the curb use), the pricing (the dynamic pricing that manages the demand), and the prioritization (the high-value uses prioritized over the low-value uses). The dynamic curb should allocate the space based on the demand, because the demand changes (the morning delivery, the midday parking, the evening ride-hailing), and the dynamic allocation matches the curb to the current demand. The prioritization should favor the high-value uses (the transit that moves the most people, the loading that serves the businesses), because the high-value uses contribute more to the street efficiency than the low-value uses (the individual car storage). The dynamic curb should be managed through the technology (the sensors, the apps, the enforcement), because the dynamic management requires the real-time information and the response, and the manual management cannot provide it. The dynamic curb should be evaluated and adjusted, because the demand changes, and the evaluation identifies the adjustments.

### Manage the Ride-Hailing, the Delivery, and the Micromobility

The ride-hailing (the Uber and the Lyft), the delivery (the freight, the package, the food), and the micromobility (the bikes and the scooters) generate the curb activity that the conventional management did not anticipate, and the management of these services determines whether they contribute to or conflict with the street function. The rule is to manage the ride-hailing, the delivery, and the micromobility, using the designated zones (the geofenced pickup and drop-off, the loading zones, the micromobility parking), the regulation (the permits, the fees, the data requirements), the pricing (the curb access pricing, the congestion pricing), and the coordination (the agreements with the companies, the data sharing). The designated zones should be provided for the ride-hailing and the delivery, because the services need the curb access, and the unmanaged access creates the congestion (the double parking, the circling). The micromobility parking should be designated, because the abandoned bikes and scooters block the sidewalks, and the designated parking manages the placement. The regulation should include the data requirements (the trip data, the curb use data), because the data informs the management, and the companies should share the data. The pricing should reflect the cost of the curb access, because the free curb access overuses the curb, and the pricing manages the use. The coordination should include the agreements with the companies, because the companies operate across the jurisdictions, and the coordination ensures the consistent management.

### Use the Data to Inform the Management and the Policy

The parking and the curb data should inform the management (the day-to-day operations) and the policy (the long-term decisions), because the data provides the evidence, and the evidence-based management and the policy are the defensible management and the policy. The rule is to use the data to inform the management and the policy, using the data for the performance-based pricing (the price adjusted to the target occupancy), the allocation optimization (the curb allocated based on the observed use), the trend analysis (the changes over time), the equity analysis (the distributional impacts), and the reporting (the public dashboards, the annual reports). The data should drive the pricing, because the performance-based pricing (the price adjusted to maintain the 85-percent occupancy) is the data-driven pricing, and the fixed pricing is not. The data should drive the allocation, because the observed use identifies the high-demand and the low-demand uses, and the allocation should match the demand. The data should be analyzed for the trends, because the trends (the declining parking demand, the increasing ride-hailing) inform the policy, and the policy should anticipate the trends. The data should be analyzed for the equity, because the pricing and the allocation may affect the low-income and the minority communities differently, and the equity analysis ensures the fairness. The data should be reported publicly, because the transparency builds the trust, and the public data enables the research and the innovation.

### Protect the Public Interest and the Equity

The curb is the public asset, and the technology and the innovation should serve the public interest (the efficiency, the safety, the equity, the revenue), not the private interest (the vendor lock-in, the company data capture, the surveillance). The rule is to protect the public interest and the equity, using the public-benefit requirements (the data ownership, the open standards, the revenue return), the equity protections (the affordable access, the nondiscriminatory pricing, the universal design), the privacy protections (the data minimization, the consent, the security), and the oversight (the public review, the audit, the accountability). The public-benefit requirements should ensure the community owns the data and the system, because the private capture of the public asset is the loss, and the public ownership preserves the asset. The equity protections should ensure the technology does not disadvantage the low-income and the minority users, because the technology may require the smartphone and the credit card that not everyone has, and the protections ensure the access. The privacy protections should ensure the data is minimized and protected, because the curb data may track the individuals, and the tracking is the surveillance. The oversight should ensure the technology serves the public, because the technology without the oversight may serve the vendor, and the oversight ensures the public benefit.

## Common Traps

### Technology Deployed Without the Strategy, Data Wasted

The technology is deployed without the strategy, and the data is collected but not used, because the use cases were not defined, and the data sits in the system. The trap is that the strategy-less technology wastes the data. The mechanism is that the use cases were not defined, and the technology was deployed for the novelty. The false signal is that "we have the smart parking." The harm is that the data is wasted, the management is not improved, the investment does not return, and the technology that should inform the management does not.

### Static Curb Retained, Multiple Users Conflict

The static curb is retained, and the multiple users (the parking, the ride-hailing, the delivery, the micromobility) conflict, because the dynamic allocation was not implemented. The trap is that the static curb causes the conflicts. The mechanism is that the dynamic curb was not designed, and the fixed allocation persisted. The false signal is that "the curb is managed." The harm is that the users conflict, the congestion results, the double parking and the circling occur, and the curb that should serve the multiple users serves none well.

### New Mobility Unmanaged, Curb Chaos

The ride-hailing, the delivery, and the micromobility are unmanaged, and the curb chaos results (the double parking, the abandoned scooters, the circling), because the zones and the regulation were not implemented. The trap is that the unmanaged new mobility causes the chaos. The mechanism is that the zones and the regulation were not implemented, and the services operated without the rules. The false signal is that "the services are available." The harm is that the curb is chaotic, the congestion results, the safety is compromised, and the services that could contribute to the mobility conflict with the street function.

### Data Not Used, Management Not Improved

The data is collected but not used for the management or the policy, and the management is not improved, because the data analysis and the reporting were not implemented. The trap is that the unused data does not improve the management. The mechanism is that the data analysis was not conducted, and the data was collected without the application. The false signal is that "we have the data." The harm is that the pricing is not adjusted, the allocation is not optimized, the trends are not identified, the equity is not analyzed, and the data that should inform the management does not.

### Public Interest Not Protected, Private Capture

The public interest is not protected, and the technology and the data are captured by the private vendors and the companies, because the public-benefit requirements and the oversight were not implemented. The trap is that the unprotected public interest enables the private capture. The mechanism is that the requirements were not imposed, and the technology was deployed without the oversight. The false signal is that "the technology is working." The harm is that the data is captured, the vendor is locked in, the equity is not protected, the privacy is compromised, and the public asset that should serve the public serves the private interest.
