# Economic Development Strategy and Targeting

Economic development strategy determines where a jurisdiction directs its limited resources — staff, incentives, infrastructure, marketing — to grow employment, income, and tax base. The judgment problem is that the field is dominated by imitation (every city wants biotech, tech, and advanced manufacturing), by measurement traps (chasing job counts that mask low quality or transient employment), and by incentive competition that subsidizes location decisions that would have happened anyway. A strategy that targets the same industries every other city targets, with the same incentives, competes on a crowded field and wastes public resources. A strategy grounded in the region's genuine assets, focused on retention and entrepreneurship alongside attraction, and disciplined about what incentives can and cannot buy, produces durable growth that benefits residents. The skill is in targeting honestly, measuring what matters, and resisting the subsidy arms race.

## Core Rules

### Target From Genuine Competitive Advantage, Not Aspiration

Industry targeting should begin from the region's actual assets — its existing industry clusters, workforce skills, research institutions, infrastructure, natural amenities, and market access — not from a list of "desirable" industries disconnected from local conditions. The rule is to conduct a strengths-and-assets analysis that identifies what the region genuinely does well, what its existing clusters and supply chains support, and what growth sectors align with those assets, and to target sectors where the region has a defensible advantage. Targeting biotech in a region without a research university, a wet-laboratory workforce, or venture capital is aspiration, not strategy, and the resources spent pursuing it are wasted. Targeting where the region is strong builds on what exists and produces durable clusters; targeting where it is not strong chases industries that will locate elsewhere regardless of incentives.

### Balance Attraction With Retention, Expansion, and Entrepreneurship

Business attraction (bringing firms from elsewhere) receives disproportionate attention because it is visible and politically rewarding, but most net new jobs in most regions come from existing business expansion and from new firm formation (entrepreneurship), not from attraction. The rule is to balance the strategy across all three: retain and support existing businesses (which are already invested in the region and at risk of leaving if neglected), support entrepreneurs and small business formation (which generates local, rooted growth), and pursue attraction selectively (where the region has a genuine competitive offering). A strategy that overweights attraction underweights the sectors that produce most of the growth, neglects the existing employers who may leave, and misses the entrepreneurs who could scale. The balance should be informed by data on where the region's jobs actually come from, not by the visibility of attraction deals.

### Evaluate Incentives by Their Return, Additionality, and Fiscal Risk

Tax abatements, grants, and other incentives are the currency of economic development, and they should be evaluated rigorously: by their return (do the jobs, investment, and tax revenue generated exceed the public cost?), by their additionality (would the project have happened without the incentive, making the incentive a windfall rather than a catalyst?), and by their fiscal risk (does the incentive commit future revenue in ways that could strain the budget if the project underperforms?). The rule is to subject every incentive to a cost-benefit analysis, to require that the incentive be material to the decision (not a giveaway to a project that was coming anyway), and to include clawback provisions (job and investment commitments enforceable through recapture) that protect the public if the project fails to deliver. Incentives granted without these disciplines subsidize decisions that would have been made anyway, transfer public revenue to private balance sheets, and expose the jurisdiction to fiscal risk.

### Measure Quality of Jobs and Benefit to Residents, Not Just Job Counts

Job counts are the most cited economic development metric, and they are the most misleading, because they treat all jobs equally regardless of wage, benefit, career ladder, or who gets them. The rule is to measure job quality (wages, benefits, advancement, stability), the match between the jobs created and the skills of existing residents (so the growth benefits the current population, not just in-commuters), and the displacement effects (does the new growth raise housing costs and displace residents, undermining the income gains?). A strategy that adds low-wage, transient jobs may increase the job count while leaving residents' economic condition unchanged or worsened. A strategy that adds jobs accessible to existing residents, at wages that support households, with career ladders, produces genuine economic improvement. The metrics should capture what matters to residents, not what is easy to count.

### Coordinate Economic Development With Land Use, Housing, and Workforce

Economic development does not occur in isolation; it depends on the availability of suitable land and buildings, on housing affordable to the workforce, on transportation access, and on workforce skills. The rule is to coordinate the economic development strategy with the land-use plan (zoning for the targeted industries' space needs), the housing strategy (ensuring the workforce can afford to live in the region), the transportation plan (ensuring access to employment), and the workforce development system (ensuring residents have the skills the targeted industries need). A strategy that targets an industry without the land, housing, or workforce to support it will fail to attract or retain that industry, and the resources spent will be wasted. Coordination requires the planner to engage the economic development agency, the workforce board, and the housing and transportation planners, breaking the silos that fragment the strategy.

## Common Traps

### Targeting the Same Industries Every City Targets

The strategy lists biotech, technology, advanced manufacturing, and clean energy as targets, identical to the strategies of dozens of other regions, with no analysis of why this region can win in those sectors. The trap is that the region competes on a crowded field with no advantage. The mechanism is that aspiration substitutes for asset analysis. The false signal is that "we're targeting high-growth sectors." The harm is that the resources are spent chasing industries that locate elsewhere, the genuine local assets are neglected, and the strategy produces no durable growth.

### Chasing Job Counts Without Quality or Access

The strategy celebrates the number of jobs created, without examining the wages, benefits, career ladders, or whether existing residents can access them. The trap is that the headline metric hides the actual economic effect. The mechanism is that quantity is easier to measure and celebrate than quality and access. The false signal is that "we created X thousand jobs." The harm is that the jobs may be low-wage, transient, or filled by in-commuters, leaving residents' economic condition unchanged, while housing costs rise with the new employment, potentially worsening residents' net position.

### Incentives for Projects That Would Have Happened Anyway

The incentive is granted to a project that, on analysis, would have located in the region without it, because the region's assets (market, workforce, infrastructure) already made it the rational choice. The trap is that the incentive is a windfall to a decision already made, not a catalyst. The mechanism is that additionality is not evaluated; the incentive is granted to "close the deal" regardless of whether it was needed. The false signal is that "the incentive brought the project." The harm is that public revenue is transferred to a private balance sheet with no public benefit, the fiscal cost is real, and the precedent invites every project to demand an incentive.

### Neglecting Existing Businesses and Entrepreneurs

The strategy focuses on attraction, and the existing employers and local entrepreneurs receive little attention or support, until the existing employers leave (citing neglect) and the entrepreneurs relocate to regions that support them. The trap is that the strategy underweights the sectors that produce most of the growth. The mechanism is that attraction is visible and politically rewarding while retention and entrepreneurship are not. The false signal is that "we're growing jobs." The harm is that the region loses its rooted employers and entrepreneurs, the growth is concentrated in footloose attracted firms that may leave when incentives expire, and the economic base becomes less durable.

### Economic Development Disconnected From Housing and Workforce

The strategy targets an industry, but the workforce it requires cannot afford to live in the region (housing costs) or does not have the skills (workforce gaps), and the industry cannot grow or locate there. The trap is that the strategy is uncoordinated with the housing and workforce systems it depends on. The mechanism is that economic development, housing, and workforce are planned in silos. The false signal is that "we have an economic strategy." The harm is that the targeted growth cannot materialize (the preconditions are absent), the resources are wasted, and the region's genuine constraints (housing, skills) go unaddressed.

## Self-Check

- Is industry targeting grounded in the region's genuine assets and existing clusters, rather than in a list of desirable industries disconnected from local conditions?
- Does the strategy balance attraction with retention, expansion, and entrepreneurship, informed by where the region's jobs actually come from?
- Are incentives evaluated for return, additionality, and fiscal risk, with clawbacks that protect the public if the project underperforms?
- Do the metrics measure job quality, wages, and access for existing residents, not just job counts?
- Is the economic development strategy coordinated with land use, housing, transportation, and workforce development?
- Have I avoided targeting the same industries every other region targets without a defensible local advantage?
- Are the jobs created accessible to existing residents at wages that support households, or do they fill with in-commuters?
- Are incentives reserved for projects where they are material to the decision, not granted as a matter of course?
- Are existing businesses and entrepreneurs supported, or neglected in favor of attraction?
- Does the strategy address the housing, land, and workforce preconditions the targeted growth depends on?
