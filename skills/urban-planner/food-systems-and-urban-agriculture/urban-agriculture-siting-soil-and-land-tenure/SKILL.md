# Urban Agriculture Siting, Soil, and Land Tenure

Urban agriculture — community gardens, urban farms, market gardens, orchards — delivers multiple benefits: fresh food for the growers and the neighborhood, community building, green space, stormwater absorption, heat reduction, and the productive use of vacant land. But urban agriculture is uniquely vulnerable because it operates on land that the urban land market values for other uses, and the conditions that make a site suitable for agriculture (sun, soil, water, access, security of tenure) are the same conditions that make it attractive for development. The judgment problem is that urban agriculture is often established on vacant land through informal or short-term arrangements, thrives for years, and is then lost when the land is developed, because the tenure was never secure and the agriculture was always understood as interim. The result is that urban agriculture is systematically ephemeral: gardens are created and lost, the soil and the community investment are destroyed, and the food production never achieves the scale or stability to be a reliable component of the food system or the neighborhood. This skill covers the siting, soil, water, and tenure decisions that determine whether urban agriculture is a stable land use or a temporary one. It complements the food-access skill by focusing on the production dimension that retail and distribution alone cannot provide.

## Core Rules

### Site Urban Agriculture Based on Suitability and Network Value

Urban agriculture siting is often opportunistic — a garden goes where a vacant lot is available — but the suitability of a site for agriculture depends on multiple factors that opportunistic siting may not evaluate, and the network value of a site (its contribution to a system of urban agriculture) is often ignored. The rule is to site urban agriculture based on a suitability analysis that evaluates the site's sun exposure (food production needs full sun, and shaded sites fail), soil (the existing soil's quality and contamination, discussed below), water access (agriculture requires reliable water, and sites without access fail), access (the growers need to reach the site, and the community needs to benefit), and security (the site needs protection from vandalism and theft), and on a network analysis that identifies where urban agriculture would provide the greatest benefit (food access, community building, green space in underserved areas). The suitability analysis should identify the sites that can support productive agriculture and exclude the sites that cannot, because a garden on an unsuitable site (shaded, contaminated, waterless) will fail, and the failure discourages future efforts. The siting should also consider the land's development trajectory: a site that will be developed in five years may be suitable for interim agriculture but not for the soil-building and perennial plantings that require longer tenure, and the siting should match the agriculture type to the tenure horizon.

### Test and Remediate Soil for Safe Food Production

Urban soil is often contaminated — by lead from paint and gasoline, by arsenic from pesticides, by industrial contaminants from past uses — and growing food in contaminated soil can transfer the contaminants to the produce, posing health risks particularly to children and to the gardeners who work the soil. The rule is to test the soil before establishing urban agriculture, analyzing for the contaminants likely present based on the site's history (lead for older residential, arsenic for orchard or treated lumber, industrial contaminants for industrial or brownfield sites), and to remediate or manage the contamination to enable safe food production. The testing should be done by a certified laboratory, with samples taken from multiple locations and depths, because contamination is unevenly distributed. The remediation options include capping (bringing in clean soil and growing in raised beds, which isolates the crops from the contaminated native soil), phytoremediation (using plants to extract contaminants, which is slow and limited), and soil replacement (excavating and replacing the contaminated soil, which is expensive). The management options include the crops grown (fruiting crops like tomatoes accumulate less lead than leafy greens or root crops), the practices (washing produce, peeling root crops, avoiding soil contact), and the exposure reduction (mulching to reduce dust, hand washing). The soil safety should be documented and communicated to the gardeners, because the health risk is real and the gardeners need to know the conditions and the precautions.

### Secure Land Tenure Matched to the Agriculture's Investment Horizon

The most critical decision for urban agriculture is land tenure, because the tenure determines whether the agriculture can invest in the soil, the infrastructure, and the perennials that require years to mature, or whether it must operate as a temporary use that can be displaced at any time. The rule is to secure land tenure matched to the agriculture's investment horizon: a community garden that builds soil and plants perennials needs long-term tenure (10 years or more, ideally permanent), while an interim farm on a site awaiting development needs tenure matched to the development horizon (with a clear end date and relocation assistance). The tenure options include ownership (the garden or a land trust acquires the site, providing permanent tenure), long-term lease (10-30 years, providing the security for soil-building and perennials), short-term lease or license (1-5 years, suitable for interim use but not for long-term investment), and informal permission (no security, suitable only for the most temporary agriculture). The tenure should be documented, because informal arrangements are easily revoked, and the documentation should address the conditions (the rent, the maintenance obligations, the access, the improvements) and the termination (the notice, the relocation assistance, the compensation for the improvements). The tenure strategy should prioritize the long-term security for the agriculture that requires it, because the loss of a mature garden — with its built soil, its perennials, its community — is a loss that cannot be quickly replaced, and the systematic ephemerality of urban agriculture is a direct consequence of insecure tenure.

### Ensure Water Access and Infrastructure

Agriculture requires reliable water, and the lack of water access is a primary reason urban agriculture sites fail, because the gardeners cannot sustain the labor of hand-watering, and the crops cannot survive the droughts. The rule is to ensure water access and infrastructure for every urban agriculture site, providing a reliable water source (a metered connection, a well, a rainwater harvesting system), the distribution infrastructure (hoses, irrigation, spigots), and the funding for the water (the cost of the meter and the usage, which can be substantial for a productive garden). The water access should be evaluated in the suitability analysis and secured before the agriculture is established, because a garden planted without water access will fail in the first drought, and the failure wastes the soil and the community investment. The water infrastructure should be designed for the agriculture's needs (the volume, the pressure, the distribution), and it should be metered and funded, because unmetered or unfunded water access is a liability that the property owner will eventually revoke. The water access should also consider the conservation dimension: rainwater harvesting, drip irrigation, and mulching reduce the water demand, and the drought-tolerant crops reduce the risk, because the water cost and the drought risk are real constraints on urban agriculture's productivity.

### Balance Urban Agriculture with Competing Land Uses and Development Pressure

Urban agriculture competes with other land uses — housing, commercial, open space — and in a developing urban context, the land's value for development often exceeds its value for agriculture, creating pressure to convert the agriculture to development. The rule is to balance urban agriculture with the competing land uses, recognizing that the agriculture provides benefits (food, community, green space, stormwater) that the development may not, and that the balance should be struck deliberately through the planning and zoning, not through the land market alone. The balance should be struck in the comprehensive plan and the zoning, which can designate land for urban agriculture (through agriculture zoning, urban agriculture overlay districts, or the designation of permanent community garden sites), and which can require or incentivize the inclusion of agriculture in development (through urban agriculture requirements, density bonuses, or the integration of agriculture into open space). The balance should also address the displacement: when urban agriculture is displaced by development, the policy should provide for relocation (a comparable site, soil and infrastructure support) and compensation (for the improvements and the community investment), because the systematic loss of urban agriculture without relocation or compensation is what makes the use ephemeral. The balance should recognize that urban agriculture is a legitimate land use that deserves protection, not merely an interim use that fills the gap until development arrives.

## Common Traps

### Opportunistic Siting on Unsuitable Sites

The urban agriculture is sited on a vacant lot without a suitability analysis, and the site is shaded, waterless, or otherwise unsuitable, and the agriculture fails, discouraging future efforts. The trap is that opportunistic siting ignores the conditions agriculture requires. The mechanism is that the suitability analysis was not done, and the site was selected for availability rather than suitability. The false signal is that "we are using the vacant land." The harm is that the agriculture fails, the soil and the community investment are wasted, the gardeners are discouraged, and the failure is misread as a lack of interest in urban agriculture rather than a consequence of unsuitable siting.

### Soil Contamination Untested, Food Safety Risk

The urban agriculture is established without soil testing, the soil is contaminated, and the food produced accumulates the contaminants, posing health risks to the gardeners and the consumers. The trap is that untested soil is an invisible health risk. The mechanism is that the soil testing was not done, and the contamination was assumed absent. The false signal is that "the garden is producing fresh food." The harm is that the gardeners and consumers are exposed to contaminants, the health risk is unrecognized, the liability may fall on the property owner or the sponsoring agency, and the urban agriculture is associated with health risk rather than health benefit.

### Short-Term Tenure Destroying Long-Term Investment

The urban agriculture is established on a short-term or informal arrangement, the gardeners invest years in building the soil and planting perennials, and the land is developed, destroying the garden and the investment, with no relocation or compensation. The trap is that insecure tenure makes urban agriculture systematically ephemeral. The mechanism is that the tenure was not secured, and the development proceeded without provision for the agriculture. The false signal is that "the garden was a temporary use." The harm is that the soil and the perennials are destroyed, the community investment is lost, the gardeners are discouraged, and the urban agriculture never achieves the scale or stability to be a reliable component of the food system or the neighborhood.

### Water Access Assumed Rather Than Secured

The urban agriculture is established without securing water access, and the garden fails in the first drought, because the gardeners cannot sustain the hand-watering and the crops cannot survive without irrigation. The trap is that water access is assumed but not provided. The mechanism is that the water access was not evaluated or secured, and the garden relied on the gardeners' labor or the informal access. The false signal is that "the garden is planted." The harm is that the garden fails, the crops are lost, the soil and the community investment are wasted, and the failure is misread as a lack of commitment rather than a consequence of the missing water infrastructure.

### Development Pressure Converting Agriculture Without Relocation

The urban agriculture is displaced by development, and there is no relocation or compensation, so the gardeners lose their investment and the neighborhood loses its food production, and the systematic loss makes urban agriculture ephemeral. The trap is that development pressure without relocation policy destroys urban agriculture. The mechanism is that the planning and zoning did not protect the agriculture, and the development proceeded without provision for relocation. The false signal is that "the land is more valuable for development." The harm is that the urban agriculture is lost, the community investment is destroyed, the food production declines, and the urban agriculture is understood as interim rather than as a legitimate land use deserving protection.

## Self-Check

- Is the urban agriculture sited based on a suitability analysis (sun, soil, water, access, security) and a network analysis (where it provides the greatest benefit)?
- Is the soil tested for the contaminants likely present, and is the remediation or management documented and communicated to the gardeners?
- Is the land tenure secured and matched to the agriculture's investment horizon, with long-term tenure for the soil-building and perennial agriculture?
- Is the water access secured and the infrastructure provided, with the funding for the meter and the usage committed?
- Is the urban agriculture balanced with the competing land uses through the planning and zoning, with protection, relocation, and compensation for displaced agriculture?
- Is the site suitable for productive agriculture, or was it selected for availability regardless of the conditions?
- Is the soil safe for food production, tested and managed, or is the contamination an unrecognized health risk?
- Is the tenure secure enough for the agriculture's investment, or is the garden vulnerable to displacement that would destroy the investment?
- Is the water access reliable and funded, or is the garden dependent on unsustainable hand-watering or informal access?
- Is the urban agriculture protected from development pressure, with relocation and compensation if displaced, or is it systematically ephemeral?
