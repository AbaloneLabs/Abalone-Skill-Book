# Food Access and Food Security Planning

Food is the most basic human need, yet the food system that delivers it is one of the least planned elements of the built environment, left largely to the market with the result that food access is profoundly unequal. Lower-income neighborhoods, communities of color, and rural areas often lack full-service grocery stores, have an overabundance of fast food and convenience outlets, and are served by a food environment that makes healthy eating difficult and unhealthy eating easy. These patterns are not accidental; they are produced by decades of disinvestment, retail redlining, zoning that excludes or fails to attract grocery, and transportation systems that assume car access for food shopping. The judgment problem is that food access is a system problem — it involves where stores locate, what they stock, how residents travel to them, what they can afford, and what the alternatives are — and addressing it requires interventions across land use, transportation, economic development, and social services, not a single silver bullet. A grocery store attraction that ignores transportation will not serve the car-less household; a farmers market that accepts SNAP but is open during working hours will not serve the working family; a food pantry that provides emergency food does not address the chronic access deficit. This skill covers the analysis and intervention decisions that determine whether food planning reduces or merely documents food insecurity. It complements the urban-agriculture skill by focusing on the retail, distribution, and access dimensions that production alone cannot solve.

## Core Rules

### Map Food Access Comprehensively, Not Just Store Locations

Food access analysis often reduces to a map of grocery store locations, but access is more than proximity — it is the combination of availability (is healthy food available nearby), affordability (can residents afford it), accessibility (can residents reach the store), and acceptability (does the store meet the residents' cultural and quality needs). The rule is to map food access comprehensively, using the USDA Food Access Research Atlas or a local analysis that identifies low-income, low-access census tracts (food deserts), but supplementing it with the food environment (the food swamp analysis that maps the overabundance of unhealthy outlets), the affordability analysis (the cost of a market basket relative to income), the accessibility analysis (can residents reach the store by the modes available to them — transit, walking, biking — within a reasonable time), and the acceptability analysis (does the store stock the culturally appropriate foods the community wants). The comprehensive mapping should disaggregate by population, because food access disparities correlate with income, race, and vehicle availability, and the analysis should identify the specific populations and neighborhoods with the greatest access deficits. The mapping should also account for the dynamic food environment, which changes as stores open and close, and a snapshot analysis may miss the closure of a key store that suddenly eliminates access for a neighborhood.

### Distinguish the Access Barriers to Target the Right Intervention

Food access deficits have different causes, and the intervention must match the cause, because a mismatched intervention will not work. The rule is to distinguish the access barriers and target the right intervention: if the barrier is retail absence (no grocery store in the area), the intervention is grocery attraction (incentives, site assembly, zoning); if the barrier is transportation (the store exists but residents cannot reach it), the intervention is transportation (transit routes to the store, shuttle services, delivery); if the barrier is affordability (the store exists and is reachable but residents cannot afford the food), the intervention is affordability (SNAP and WIC enrollment, double-bucks programs, food subsidies); if the barrier is acceptability (the store exists but does not stock the needed foods), the intervention is retail improvement (stocking requirements, technical assistance, small-store initiatives). The barrier analysis should identify which barriers affect which populations, because the barriers often compound: a lower-income, car-less household may face retail absence, transportation, and affordability barriers simultaneously, and addressing only one will not resolve the access deficit. The intervention targeting should be evidence-based, drawing on the food access research that identifies which interventions work for which barriers, rather than defaulting to the intervention the jurisdiction has used before regardless of the barrier.

### Site Grocery and Healthy Food Retail Strategically

Grocery store attraction is the most visible food access intervention, and its success depends on strategic siting that matches the store to the community and the site to the store's economics. The rule is to site grocery and healthy food retail strategically, identifying the underserved areas with sufficient population density and purchasing power to support a store, assembling the sites that meet the store's requirements (size, access, parking, visibility), and providing the incentives (tax abatements, grants, low-cost financing, infrastructure) that close the gap between the store's return and the site's cost. The siting should recognize that grocery is a low-margin business and that underserved areas are often underserved because the sites do not pencil without public support, so the incentive is not a subsidy for an otherwise-profitable store but the difference that makes the store viable. The siting should also address the small-store and alternative retail: a full-service grocery may not be viable in every underserved area, but a corner store initiative (upgrading existing small stores to stock healthy food), a food cooperative, a mobile market, or a farmers market with SNAP acceptance may provide access where a grocery cannot. The strategic siting should be coordinated with the transportation planning, because a store that is sited but not reachable by the car-less residents does not solve the access deficit, and the site selection should prioritize locations that are transit-accessible and walkable from the underserved neighborhoods.

### Build the Food Distribution and Recovery Infrastructure

Food access depends not only on retail but on the distribution infrastructure that moves food from producers to retailers to consumers, and the recovery infrastructure that captures surplus food for distribution to those in need. The rule is to build the food distribution and recovery infrastructure, supporting the food hubs, distribution networks, and food banks that aggregate, process, and distribute food, particularly the local and regional food that supports local producers and provides fresh food to underserved areas. The distribution infrastructure should address the cold chain (refrigeration and storage that preserves fresh food), the aggregation (food hubs that consolidate local producers' output for distribution), and the last-mile distribution (getting the food to the retailers, pantries, and consumers who need it). The food recovery infrastructure should capture the surplus food from farms, retailers, and institutions that would otherwise be wasted, and redirect it to food banks and pantries, reducing both food waste and food insecurity. The distribution and recovery infrastructure is often invisible in food planning, which focuses on retail, but it is the backbone that determines whether the retail can stock healthy food at a price residents can afford, and whether the emergency food system can meet the need.

### Integrate Food Security into Comprehensive Planning and Cross-Sector Policy

Food security is a cross-cutting issue that intersects with land use (zoning for grocery and urban agriculture), transportation (access to food retail), economic development (grocery attraction and food business support), housing (the income available for food after rent), health (the diet-related disease burden), and social services (the food assistance programs). The rule is to integrate food security into comprehensive planning and cross-sector policy, so that the food system is planned as a system rather than addressed through isolated interventions. The comprehensive plan should include a food system element or food access policies that address the production, processing, distribution, retail, and waste dimensions, and the element should be integrated with the other elements (land use, transportation, economic development, housing) that affect food access. The cross-sector policy should coordinate the agencies (planning, public works, health, economic development, social services) whose decisions affect food access, because the siloed approach produces contradictions (transportation that does not serve food retail, zoning that excludes grocery, economic development that does not prioritize food access). The integration should also engage the food system stakeholders (grocers, producers, food banks, community organizations, residents), because the food system is largely private and nonprofit, and the public sector's role is to enable and coordinate, not to deliver the food directly.

## Common Traps

### Proximity Mistaken for Access

The food access analysis maps grocery store locations and identifies the areas more than a mile from a store, but it does not account for transportation, affordability, or acceptability, and the "food desert" designation misstates the actual access deficit. The trap is that proximity is the easiest measure but the most incomplete. The mechanism is that the analysis used the USDA atlas or a simple buffer without the supplementary analysis. The false signal is that "we have identified the food deserts." The harm is that the interventions target proximity (attracting a grocery) when the actual barrier is transportation or affordability, the intervention does not resolve the access deficit, and the food insecurity persists despite the investment.

### Single-Barrier Intervention That Ignores Compounding Barriers

The food access intervention addresses one barrier (attracting a grocery store) but ignores the others (transportation, affordability, acceptability), and the residents who face multiple barriers remain food insecure despite the intervention. The trap is that single-barrier interventions are easier but insufficient. The mechanism is that the barrier analysis was not done, and the default intervention was applied. The false signal is that "we brought a grocery to the neighborhood." The harm is that the car-less residents cannot reach the store, the lower-income residents cannot afford the food, the store does not stock the culturally appropriate foods, and the food insecurity persists because the compounding barriers were not addressed.

### Grocery Attraction Without Transportation Coordination

The grocery store is attracted to the underserved area, but the transportation is not coordinated, and the car-less residents who were the target of the intervention cannot reach the store, which then underperforms and may close. The trap is that a store without access fails for the population it was meant to serve. The mechanism is that the siting did not coordinate with transportation, and the store relied on the car access that the underserved residents lack. The false signal is that "the neighborhood has a grocery store." The harm is that the car-less residents remain food insecure, the store underperforms because its target customers cannot reach it, the store may close, and the investment is wasted while the food insecurity persists.

### Retail Focus Ignoring Distribution and Recovery

The food planning focuses on retail attraction and ignores the distribution and recovery infrastructure, and the retail cannot stock healthy food at an affordable price because the distribution backbone is missing, and the surplus food that could address insecurity is wasted. The trap is that retail without distribution is unsustainable. The mechanism is that the distribution and recovery infrastructure was not planned, and the retail was treated as sufficient. The false signal is that "we are addressing food access." The harm is that the retail struggles to stock affordable healthy food, the local producers lack the aggregation to reach the market, the surplus food is wasted rather than recovered, and the food system's backbone deficits undermine the retail interventions.

### Food Planning Isolated from Comprehensive and Cross-Sector Policy

The food access interventions are pursued in isolation, without integration into the comprehensive plan or coordination across the agencies, and the contradictions (zoning that excludes grocery, transportation that does not serve food retail) undermine the interventions. The trap is that isolated food planning cannot overcome the cross-sector barriers. The mechanism is that the food planning was not integrated, and the agencies acted in silos. The false signal is that "we have a food initiative." The harm is that the interventions are contradicted by the other policies, the food system is not planned as a system, the access deficits persist, and the resources invested in isolated interventions produce limited results because the systemic barriers remain.

## Self-Check

- Is food access mapped comprehensively, accounting for availability, affordability, accessibility, and acceptability, disaggregated by population?
- Are the access barriers distinguished and the interventions targeted to the actual barriers, accounting for compounding?
- Is grocery and healthy food retail sited strategically, with site assembly, incentives, and coordination with transportation, including small-store and alternative retail?
- Is the food distribution and recovery infrastructure supported, including food hubs, cold chain, aggregation, and food recovery?
- Is food security integrated into the comprehensive plan and coordinated across the agencies whose decisions affect food access?
- Does the access analysis account for transportation, affordability, and acceptability, or does it reduce to grocery proximity?
- Are the interventions matched to the barriers, or is a single default intervention applied regardless of the barrier?
- Can the car-less residents reach the grocery that was attracted to their neighborhood, or does the store rely on the access they lack?
- Does the food planning address the distribution and recovery infrastructure, or does it focus on retail alone?
- Is the food planning integrated with the comprehensive plan and the cross-sector policies, or is it isolated and vulnerable to contradiction?
