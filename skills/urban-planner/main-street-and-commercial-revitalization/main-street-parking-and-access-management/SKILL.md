# Main Street Parking and Access Management

Parking is the most contested resource on a main street, and the way it is managed — the supply, the pricing, the curbside allocation, the access by the multiple modes — determines whether the district is convenient for the customers, viable for the businesses, and walkable for the pedestrians. The judgment problem is that the conventional approach (the abundant free parking, the minimum parking requirements, the curb for the storage) undermines the walkability and the place-making that the main street needs, while the progressive approach (the managed parking, the shared supply, the multimodal access) requires the political will and the business support that are difficult to build. The planner must navigate the tension between the parking convenience (that the customers and the businesses demand) and the walkability (that the district vitality requires), and the management of this tension determines whether the main street is a place or a parking lot. This skill covers the parking and the access management decisions that determine whether the main street is convenient, viable, and walkable. It complements the broader main-street skill by focusing on the parking and the multimodal access dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Manage the Parking Supply Through the Shared and the District Approaches

The parking supply on a main street should be managed collectively (the shared parking, the district parking, the public lots) rather than provided individually (the on-site parking for each business), because the individual provision produces the oversupply, the wasted land, and the degraded walkability, while the collective provision matches the supply to the actual demand and frees the land for the productive uses. The rule is to manage the parking supply through the shared and the district approaches, using the shared parking analysis (the different peak times of the retail, the restaurant, the office, the residential that allow the shared use), the public parking lots and the structures (the centralized supply that serves the district), and the parking benefit districts (the revenue from the priced parking that funds the district improvements). The shared parking should account for the mixed-use synergy (the daytime office and the evening restaurant that share the same spaces), because the shared use reduces the total supply needed, and the reduced supply frees the land. The district parking should be funded through the parking benefit district, because the revenue from the priced parking funds the district improvements, and the self-funding mechanism sustains the management. The supply management should reduce or eliminate the minimum parking requirements, because the minimums force the oversupply, and the elimination allows the market to provide the appropriate supply.

### Price the Curb Parking to Manage the Demand

The curb parking on a main street should be priced to manage the demand, because the free curb parking creates the shortage (the spaces occupied by the long-term parkers), the cruising (the drivers circling for the free spaces), and the congestion, while the priced parking creates the availability (the spaces that turn over) and the revenue. The rule is to price the curb parking to manage the demand, using the performance-based pricing (the price adjusted to maintain the 85-percent occupancy, the target that ensures the availability), the technology (the meters, the sensors, the payment apps), and the revenue return (the revenue that funds the district). The performance-based pricing should target the 85-percent occupancy (one or two spaces available per block), because the 85-percent target ensures the availability without the oversupply, and the price should be adjusted (up or down) to maintain the target. The pricing should include the time limits and the enforcement, because the pricing without the enforcement is the pricing that is ignored, and the enforcement ensures the compliance. The pricing should return the revenue to the district, because the revenue that leaves the district generates the opposition, and the revenue that stays builds the support. The pricing should be coordinated with the off-street supply, because the curb and the off-street should be priced consistently, and the inconsistent pricing (the free curb and the priced off-street) undermines the management.

### Allocate the Curb for the Multiple Uses

The curb on a main street serves the multiple uses — the parking, the loading, the transit, the bicycle, the pedestrian, the outdoor dining — and the allocation of the curb among these uses determines whether the district functions for the businesses, the customers, and the residents. The rule is to allocate the curb for the multiple uses, using the curbside management plan (the allocation of the curb space by the time of day, the day of week, and the location), the loading zones (the dedicated spaces for the deliveries, the time-restricted to free the space for the other uses), the transit stops (the dedicated spaces for the buses and the shuttles), and the activation (the parklets, the outdoor dining, the public spaces). The curbside allocation should balance the competing demands, because the curb is the finite resource, and the allocation that favors one use over the others creates the conflicts. The allocation should prioritize the high-value uses (the transit that moves the most people, the loading that serves the businesses, the activation that creates the place), because the high-value uses contribute more to the district than the low-value uses (the free parking for the individual car). The allocation should be flexible (the time-of-day and the day-of-week changes), because the demands change, and the flexible allocation matches the curb to the current demand.

### Coordinate the Multimodal Access

The main street should be accessible by the multiple modes — the walking, the bicycling, the transit, and the driving — and the coordination of the multimodal access determines whether the district is accessible to the customers and the employees who arrive by the different means. The rule is to coordinate the multimodal access, providing the pedestrian access (the continuous sidewalks, the safe crossings, the walkable environment), the bicycle access (the bike lanes, the bike parking, the bike share), the transit access (the bus routes, the transit stops, the transit information), and the vehicular access (the parking, the drop-off, the wayfinding). The multimodal coordination should prioritize the modes by the efficiency and the place-making, because the walking and the bicycling and the transit move more people per unit of the street space than the driving, and the prioritization of the efficient modes increases the district's accessibility. The coordination should address the first-mile and the last-mile connections, because the transit that does not connect to the origins and the destinations is the transit that is not used, and the connections (the shuttles, the bike share, the walkable environment) complete the trip. The coordination should include the transportation demand management (the promotion of the non-driving modes, the employer programs, the visitor information), because the TDM shifts the trips to the efficient modes, and the shift reduces the parking demand and the congestion.

### Balance the Parking Convenience and the Walkability

The main street must balance the parking convenience (that the customers and the businesses demand) and the walkability (that the district vitality requires), because the excessive parking convenience undermines the walkability (the surface lots, the wide streets, the auto orientation), and the excessive walkability constraint (the reduced parking, the narrowed streets) may inconvenience the customers. The rule is to balance the parking convenience and the walkability, providing the adequate but not the excessive parking (the managed supply that ensures the availability without the oversupply), the walkable environment (the narrow streets, the wide sidewalks, the buildings at the street), and the transition (the gradual shift from the auto orientation to the walkable orientation). The balance should be calibrated to the district's stage of the revitalization, because the early-stage district may need the parking to attract the customers, and the mature district may prioritize the walkability. The balance should be communicated to the businesses, because the businesses often overestimate the parking demand and underestimate the walkability benefit, and the education builds the support for the balanced approach. The balance should be evaluated against the performance metrics (the parking occupancy, the sales, the foot traffic), because the metrics provide the evidence, and the evidence-based balance is the defensible balance.

## Common Traps

### Oversupplied and Free Parking, Walkability Undermined

The parking is oversupplied (the minimum requirements, the surface lots) and free, and the walkability is undermined, because the oversupply and the free parking create the auto orientation that degrades the pedestrian environment. The trap is that the oversupplied free parking undermines the walkability. The mechanism is that the minimum requirements were not reduced, and the parking was provided individually. The false signal is that "the parking is convenient." The harm is that the walkability is degraded, the place is diminished, the land is wasted on the parking, and the district that should be walkable is auto-oriented.

### Curb Parking Not Priced, Shortage and Cruising

The curb parking is not priced, and the shortage and the cruising occur, because the free parking creates the demand that exceeds the supply, and the drivers circle for the spaces. The trap is that the free curb parking creates the shortage. The mechanism is that the pricing was not implemented, and the parking was left free. The false signal is that "the parking is free." The harm is that the spaces are not available, the cruising creates the congestion, the customers are frustrated, the businesses lose the sales, and the free parking that should be the convenience is the inconvenience.

### Curb Allocated Only for Parking, Other Uses Ignored

The curb is allocated only for the parking, and the loading, the transit, the bicycle, and the activation are ignored, because the curb is treated as the parking storage rather than the multimodal resource. The trap is that the single-use curb ignores the other demands. The mechanism is that the curbside management plan was not developed, and the curb was allocated by the default to the parking. The false signal is that "the curb is for the parking." The harm is that the loading occurs in the travel lane, the transit stops are inadequate, the bicycle access is missing, the activation is not provided, and the curb that should serve the multiple uses serves only one.

### Multimodal Access Not Coordinated, Auto Dependency Persists

The multimodal access is not coordinated, and the auto dependency persists, because the walking, the bicycling, and the transit are not provided, and the customers and the employees must drive. The trap is that the uncoordinated multimodal access maintains the auto dependency. The mechanism is that the multimodal facilities were not provided, and the access was left to the driving. The false signal is that "the parking is provided." The harm is that the auto dependency persists, the parking demand is high, the congestion continues, and the district that should be multimodal is auto-dependent.

### Walkability Sacrificed for the Parking Convenience

The walkability is sacrificed for the parking convenience, and the district becomes the auto-oriented place, because the parking demand overrides the walkable design. The trap is that the sacrificed walkability undermines the place. The mechanism is that the parking demand was prioritized, and the walkable design was compromised. The false signal is that "the customers want the parking." The harm is that the walkability is degraded, the place is diminished, the customers who want the walkable district do not come, and the district that should be the place is the parking lot.

## Self-Check

- Is the parking supply managed through the shared and the district approaches, with the shared parking analysis, the public lots, and the parking benefit district?
- Is the curb parking priced to manage the demand, with the performance-based pricing, the technology, and the revenue return?
- Is the curb allocated for the multiple uses, with the loading, the transit, the bicycle, and the activation?
- Is the multimodal access coordinated, with the pedestrian, the bicycle, the transit, and the vehicular access?
- Is the parking convenience balanced with the walkability, with the managed supply, the walkable environment, and the performance evaluation?
- Is the supply managed, or is it oversupplied and free?
- Is the curb priced, or is it free and short?
- Is the curb multimodal, or is it single-use?
- Is the access multimodal, or is it auto-dependent?
- Is the walkability balanced, or is it sacrificed for the parking?
