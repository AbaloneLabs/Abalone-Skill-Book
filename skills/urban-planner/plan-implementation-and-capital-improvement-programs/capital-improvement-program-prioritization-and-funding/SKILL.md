# Capital Improvement Program Prioritization and Funding

The capital improvement program (CIP) is the multi-year plan for the public infrastructure — the roads, the water, the sewer, the parks, the buildings, the technology — and the prioritization and the funding of the CIP determines whether the comprehensive plan is implemented or shelved. The judgment problem is that the CIP involves the competing projects (the roads versus the parks, the maintenance versus the new construction), the competing funding sources (the general fund, the bonds, the grants, the fees), and the competing priorities (the plan alignment, the political influence, the equity, the fiscal capacity), and the planner must navigate these dimensions to produce the CIP that implements the plan within the fiscal constraints. A CIP that is prioritized well (the criteria-based ranking, the funding alignment, the plan consistency) implements the plan, while a CIP that is prioritized poorly (the political ranking, the funding mismatch, the plan inconsistency) builds the projects that the plan did not envision and leaves the plan unimplemented. This skill covers the CIP prioritization and the funding decisions that determine whether the capital program implements the comprehensive plan. It complements the broader plan-implementation skill by focusing on the capital programming dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Align the CIP With the Comprehensive Plan and the Strategic Priorities

The CIP should be aligned with the comprehensive plan and the strategic priorities, because the CIP is the implementation tool of the plan, and the CIP that is not aligned builds the projects the plan did not envision. The rule is to align the CIP with the comprehensive plan and the strategic priorities, using the plan-based project identification (the projects that the plan calls for), the policy alignment (the projects that advance the plan goals), and the gap analysis (the plan elements that the CIP does not address). The alignment should be documented, because the documentation provides the trail from the plan to the project, and the undocumented alignment is the asserted alignment. The alignment should address all the plan elements (the land use, the transportation, the housing, the environment, the economic development), because the CIP that addresses only some elements implements only those elements, and the unaddressed elements are not implemented. The alignment should be evaluated, because the evaluation identifies the gaps and the inconsistencies, and the evaluation drives the adjustment. The alignment should be the primary criterion, because the CIP that is not aligned with the plan is the CIP that does not implement the plan, and the alignment is the purpose of the CIP.

### Use the Criteria-Based Prioritization, Not the Political Ranking

The CIP projects should be prioritized through the criteria-based scoring, not the political ranking, because the criteria-based scoring is the transparent and the defensible method, and the political ranking is the opaque and the challengeable method. The rule is to use the criteria-based prioritization, developing the criteria (the plan alignment, the condition, the capacity, the equity, the economic benefit, the funding availability), the weights (the relative importance of the criteria), the scoring (the objective scoring of each project against the criteria), and the ranking (the projects ranked by the score). The criteria should be adopted publicly, because the adopted criteria are the transparent criteria, and the unadopted criteria are the hidden criteria. The criteria should include the equity (the distributional impact, the benefit to the underserved communities), because the CIP that does not include the equity may perpetuate the inequity, and the equity criterion addresses it. The criteria should include the condition (the state of repair, the remaining life), because the maintenance of the existing infrastructure is the priority, and the new construction should not displace the maintenance. The scoring should be objective and documented, because the subjective scoring is the challengeable scoring, and the documentation provides the defense. The ranking should be the starting point, not the final word, because the judgment and the tradeoffs may adjust the ranking, but the adjustment should be transparent and justified.

### Match the Funding Sources to the Project Types

The CIP projects should be matched to the appropriate funding sources, because the different sources (the general fund, the bonds, the grants, the impact fees, the user fees) have the different eligibility, the different timing, and the different constraints, and the mismatch generates the unfunded projects or the wasted sources. The rule is to match the funding sources to the project types, using the general fund for the small and the ongoing projects, the bonds for the large and the long-lived projects, the grants for the projects that meet the grant criteria, the impact fees for the growth-related projects, and the user fees for the enterprise-funded projects (the water, the sewer, the parking). The matching should account for the eligibility, because the grant has the specific eligibility, and the ineligible project cannot use the grant. The matching should account for the timing, because the bond has the voter approval cycle, and the grant has the application cycle, and the timing affects the project schedule. The matching should account for the constraints, because the impact fee has the proportionate share constraint, and the user fee has the rate-setting constraint, and the constraints affect the use. The matching should include the local match, because the grants often require the local match, and the match should be identified. The matching should be realistic, because the optimistic matching (the grant we might get) is the matching that may not materialize, and the realistic matching is the matching that funds the project.

### Address the Maintenance and the Lifecycle Cost

The CIP should address the maintenance and the lifecycle cost, because the new project that the CIP builds must be maintained, and the maintenance that is not funded leads to the deterioration, and the deterioration leads to the more expensive replacement. The rule is to address the maintenance and the lifecycle cost, including the maintenance budget in the CIP (the ongoing cost of the new project), the lifecycle cost analysis (the total cost of the project over its life, including the maintenance and the replacement), and the maintenance backlog (the accumulated deferred maintenance). The maintenance budget should be included for each new project, because the new project adds to the maintenance burden, and the unfunded maintenance leads to the deterioration. The lifecycle cost analysis should compare the alternatives (the higher initial cost with the lower maintenance versus the lower initial cost with the higher maintenance), because the lifecycle cost reveals the true cost, and the initial cost alone does not. The maintenance backlog should be addressed, because the backlog is the accumulated underfunding, and the unaddressed backlog grows. The CIP should prioritize the maintenance over the new construction when the backlog is large, because the new construction that is built while the existing infrastructure deteriorates is the misplaced priority.

### Manage the Fiscal Capacity and the Debt

The CIP should be managed within the fiscal capacity and the debt constraints, because the CIP that exceeds the capacity generates the fiscal stress, and the stress leads to the service cuts, the tax increases, or the project cancellations. The rule is to manage the fiscal capacity and the debt, using the fiscal forecast (the revenue and the expenditure projections), the debt capacity analysis (the debt service as the percent of the budget, the credit rating implications), and the affordability analysis (the CIP the community can afford). The fiscal forecast should project the revenue and the expenditure, because the forecast identifies the capacity, and the CIP should fit within the capacity. The debt capacity analysis should assess the debt service burden, because the excessive debt service crowds out the operations, and the credit rating affects the borrowing cost. The affordability analysis should identify the CIP the community can afford, because the CIP that exceeds the affordability is the CIP that generates the stress. The fiscal management should include the reserve, because the reserve provides the cushion, and the reserve that is spent on the CIP is the reserve that is not available for the emergency. The fiscal management should be conservative, because the optimistic revenue projections and the low interest rate assumptions generate the stress when they do not materialize, and the conservative assumptions provide the margin.

## Common Traps

### CIP Not Aligned With the Plan, Plan Not Implemented

The CIP is not aligned with the comprehensive plan, and the CIP builds the projects the plan did not envision, and the plan is not implemented, because the alignment was not pursued. The trap is that the unaligned CIP does not implement the plan. The mechanism is that the alignment was not documented, and the CIP was developed independently. The false signal is that "the CIP is adopted." The harm is that the plan is not implemented, the projects the plan called for are not built, the projects the plan did not call for are built, and the CIP that should implement the plan does not.

### Political Ranking, Merit Not Considered

The CIP is ranked by the political influence, not the criteria-based scoring, and the merit is not considered, because the transparent criteria were not used. The trap is that the political ranking ignores the merit. The mechanism is that the criteria were not adopted, and the ranking was set by the influence. The false signal is that "the projects are prioritized." The harm is that the high-merit projects are not funded, the low-merit projects are funded, the transparency is absent, and the prioritization that should be merit-based is influence-based.

### Funding Mismatched, Projects Unfunded

The funding sources are mismatched to the project types, and the projects are unfunded, because the eligibility, the timing, or the constraints were not considered. The trap is that the mismatched funding leaves the projects unfunded. The mechanism is that the matching was not conducted, and the funding was assumed. The false signal is that "the funding is identified." The harm is that the projects are unfunded, the sources are wasted, the grants do not materialize, and the funding that should match the projects does not.

### Maintenance Not Funded, Infrastructure Deteriorates

The maintenance is not funded, and the infrastructure deteriorates, because the lifecycle cost and the maintenance backlog were not addressed. The trap is that the unfunded maintenance causes the deterioration. The mechanism is that the maintenance was not budgeted, and the new construction was prioritized. The false signal is that "the new projects are built." The harm is that the existing infrastructure deteriorates, the maintenance backlog grows, the replacement cost increases, and the new construction that is built while the existing decays is the misplaced priority.

### Fiscal Capacity Exceeded, Stress Results

The fiscal capacity is exceeded, and the fiscal stress results, because the forecast and the debt capacity were not managed. The trap is that the exceeded capacity generates the stress. The mechanism is that the forecast was optimistic, and the debt was not managed. The false signal is that "the CIP is funded." The harm is that the service cuts or the tax increases follow, the credit rating declines, the projects are cancelled, and the CIP that should fit within the capacity exceeds it.

## Self-Check

- Is the CIP aligned with the comprehensive plan and the strategic priorities, with the documented alignment and the gap analysis?
- Is the prioritization criteria-based, with the adopted criteria, the weights, the objective scoring, and the transparent ranking?
- Are the funding sources matched to the project types, with the eligibility, the timing, the constraints, and the realistic matching?
- Is the maintenance and the lifecycle cost addressed, with the maintenance budget, the lifecycle analysis, and the backlog?
- Is the fiscal capacity and the debt managed, with the forecast, the debt capacity, the affordability, and the conservative assumptions?
- Is the CIP plan-aligned, or does it build the unenvisioned projects?
- Is the ranking merit-based, or is it political?
- Is the funding matched, or are the projects unfunded?
- Is the maintenance funded, or does the infrastructure deteriorate?
- Is the capacity managed, or does the stress result?
