# Transit-Oriented Development

Transit-oriented development (TOD) is the practice of concentrating mixed-use, higher-density, walkable development around transit stations so that the transit investment and the land use reinforce each other. The premise is sound — transit works best where density and walkability concentrate riders, and density and walkability are most viable where transit provides access — but the execution is difficult, because TOD requires aligning transit service, land use regulation, real estate markets, infrastructure, and community interests around a shared vision for a specific place. The judgment problem is that TOD is often pursued as a land use strategy on the assumption that transit will follow, or as a transit strategy on the assumption that land use will follow, when in fact both must be coordinated or neither delivers. A station with high-density zoning but no market produces no development; a station with market demand but zoning that prohibits density produces no ridership benefit; a station with both but no pedestrian access produces a park-and-ride that generates none of the walkable, mixed-use character TOD promises. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether TOD produces the intended synergy between transit and land use or produces one more underperforming station area.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate Density and Mix to Actual Transit Service Level

The density and intensity appropriate for a station area depend on the transit service it receives — frequency, span, speed, reliability, and connections — and calibrating development to a service level that does not exist or is not committed produces either over-development (density with insufficient transit to support car-free living) or under-development (low density around high-capacity transit that wastes the investment). The rule is to match the planned density to the committed transit service: heavy rail and high-frequency light rail support the highest density; commuter rail and lower-frequency light rail support moderate density; bus rapid transit supports moderate density along the corridor; local bus supports modest intensification. The calibration should account for the service that will exist over the life of the development, not just the service today, but planned service should be committed (funded, scheduled) before it is used to justify density, because development built for transit that never arrives is stranded. The density should be highest closest to the station and transition down with distance, reflecting the walkshed's gradient of transit access.

### Design the Walkshed as the Primary Planning Unit

TOD functions at the scale of the walkshed — the area within a comfortable walking distance of the station, typically a half-mile, modified by the street network's connectivity and the pedestrian environment's quality. The walkshed, not the Euclidean radius, is the planning unit, because barriers (highways, arterials, rivers, superblocks, hostile streetscapes) reduce the effective walkshed dramatically. The rule is to map the actual walkshed — accounting for barriers, street connectivity, slope, and pedestrian quality — and to plan the density, mix, and pedestrian improvements within that functional area. A station with a half-mile radius but a quarter-mile walkshed (due to barriers) has half the development potential its map suggests, and planning the full radius overstates the opportunity and misallocates investment. Pedestrian access improvements — connections across barriers, street grid completion, complete streets — expand the walkshed and are often the highest-leverage TOD investment, because they multiply the land that can support transit-supportive density.

### Eliminate or Reduce Parking to Enable Density and Ridership

Parking is the variable that most often defeats TOD, because conventional parking requirements consume the land and capital that density requires, and because abundant parking enables the car use that transit is meant to reduce. The rule is to minimize parking in station areas: eliminate minimums, cap maximums, unbundle parking from rent or sale price, and provide shared parking rather than per-use requirements. Reduced parking is feasible in TOD because transit access and walkability reduce car dependence, and because the density that reduced parking enables is what makes transit viable. The calibration should account for the station's position (terminal stations and those with limited service may need more parking for park-and-ride), the market (residential and office markets may expect parking), and the tradeoff (every parking space consumes land that could be development). Parking that is required but underused wastes the site's potential; parking that is too scarce drives spillover into adjacent neighborhoods, which generates political opposition that can unwind the TOD.

### Address Displacement Risk in Corridors Where TOD Raises Land Values

TOD increases land values, because transit access is valuable, and rising land values can displace existing residents and businesses who cannot afford the increased rents — particularly in lower-income and minority communities where transit dependence is highest and where TOD is most needed and most disruptive. The rule is to assess displacement risk before TOD is implemented, using indicators of vulnerability (renter population, lower-income households, tenants without protection, small businesses on month-to-month leases), and to pair the upzoning and investment with anti-displacement strategies: affordable housing requirements, preservation of existing affordable units, tenant protections, small business stabilization, and community ownership models. TOD that produces displacement undermines its equity purpose and its ridership purpose (displaced transit riders are replaced by higher-income residents who drive more), and it generates political opposition that can block future TOD. Equity must be designed into the TOD from the start, not added after displacement has begun.

### Coordinate Implementation Across Transit, Zoning, Infrastructure, and Phasing

TOD requires coordinated action across systems that operate on different timelines and under different governance: the transit agency must commit service, the municipality must adopt zoning, the infrastructure agencies must fund and build the pedestrian, water, sewer, and stormwater capacity, and the development community must build the projects. The rule is to build an implementation framework that sequences these actions: confirm transit service commitment, adopt station area zoning, fund infrastructure, assemble land where necessary, and phase development to match market absorption. A TOD plan that adopts zoning without infrastructure funding produces development that cannot be served; one that builds infrastructure without zoning produces capacity that cannot be used; one that does neither produces a plan on a shelf. The implementation framework should identify the responsible party, funding source, and timeline for each action, and it should be monitored and updated, because TOD is a multi-decade effort that requires sustained coordination across changes in political leadership, market conditions, and transit funding.

## Common Traps

### Density Without Committed Transit Service

The station area is upzoned for high density on the expectation that transit service will be improved, but the service is never committed or is reduced, and the development is stranded without the transit access that justified the density. The trap is that density was calibrated to a service level that does not exist. The mechanism is that planned transit was treated as committed. The false signal is that "we are planning for future transit." The harm is that residents move in expecting transit and find none, car dependence and traffic increase, the development underperforms, and the credibility of TOD is damaged because the promised synergy never materialized.

### Walkshed Overstated Due to Barriers

The station area plan is built on a half-mile radius, but highways, arterials, or hostile streetscapes reduce the actual walkshed to a fraction of the radius, and the planned density and investment are misallocated. The trap is that the map overstates the opportunity. The mechanism is that the planning unit was a Euclidean distance rather than a functional walkshed. The false signal is that "the station has a large catchment area." The harm is that development is planned in areas residents cannot walk from, the station's actual ridership potential is lower than projected, and investment is wasted on land that is functionally remote from the station.

### Parking Requirements That Defeat Density

Conventional parking minimums are retained in the station area, consuming the land and capital that density requires, and the TOD produces auto-oriented development that generates none of the walkable, mixed-use character intended. The trap is that parking requirements undermine the TOD's premise. The mechanism is that parking consumes site area and capital, reducing the density that is feasible, and abundant parking enables car use that transit was meant to reduce. The false signal is that "we upzoned the station area." The harm is that the development is low-density and car-oriented despite the zoning, transit ridership does not increase, and the TOD is indistinguishable from conventional suburban development.

### TOD That Displaces the Transit-Dependent Population

The station area is upzoned and invested in, land values rise, and the lower-income, transit-dependent residents who would benefit most from TOD are displaced by higher-income residents who drive more. The trap is that TOD produces gentrification without ridership benefit. The mechanism is that rising land values raise rents, unprotected tenants are displaced, and the new residents have higher car ownership and lower transit use. The false signal is that "the station area is being redeveloped." The harm is that the community loses its transit-accessible affordable housing, the transit-dependent population is pushed to car-dependent areas, transit ridership does not increase as projected, and the equity purpose of TOD is defeated.

### Plan Without Implementation Coordination

A station area plan is adopted with vision, density, and mix, but no framework coordinates the transit service, zoning, infrastructure, and phasing, and nothing is built. The trap is that a plan without implementation is an aspiration, not a strategy. The mechanism is that the systems whose coordination TOD requires operate independently, and without a framework they do not align. The false signal is that "we have a TOD plan." The harm is that the plan sits unimplemented, the transit investment does not generate the land use benefit that justified it, and the planning effort is wasted because the coordination that would have produced development never occurred.

## Self-Check

- Is the planned station area density calibrated to the committed transit service level, not to a service level that is merely hoped for?
- Has the actual walkshed been mapped accounting for barriers and street connectivity, and is the plan built on the functional walkshed rather than a Euclidean radius?
- Have parking minimums been eliminated or reduced, maximums capped, and parking unbundled, so that parking does not consume the land and capital density requires?
- Has displacement risk been assessed, and are anti-displacement strategies paired with the upzoning and investment?
- Is there an implementation framework that sequences transit service, zoning, infrastructure, and development, with responsible parties, funding, and timelines for each action?
- If transit service is reduced or delayed, will the density still be appropriate, or will the development be stranded?
- Do pedestrian improvements address the barriers that constrain the walkshed, expanding the functional station area?
- Is parking reduced to a level that supports density without creating spillover that generates neighborhood opposition?
- Are affordable housing and anti-displacement measures funded and committed, or merely aspirational?
- Is the implementation framework monitored and updated, with a mechanism to sustain coordination across political and market changes over the TOD's multi-decade timeline?
