# Transit-Oriented Development Financing and Implementation

The TOD requires the financing and the implementation that the conventional development does not, because the TOD involves the higher costs (the structured parking, the public realm, the infrastructure), the complex land (the multiple parcels, the fragmented ownership, the public land), and the market timing (the transit may precede or follow the development), and the financing and the implementation determine whether the TOD is built or stalled. The judgment problem is that the TOD financing requires the public tools (the value capture, the tax increment, the public land), the private partnerships (the developer selection, the risk sharing, the public benefit), and the phasing (the sequencing of the transit, the infrastructure, and the development), and the planner must structure these dimensions to produce the TOD that the market alone would not deliver. A TOD that is financed and implemented well (the appropriate tools, the strong partnership, the realistic phasing) is built, while a TOD that is financed and implemented poorly (the wrong tools, the weak partnership, the unrealistic phasing) stalls. This skill covers the financing and the implementation decisions that determine whether the TOD is built. It complements the broader TOD skill and the station area skill by focusing on the financial and the implementation dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Use the Value Capture to Fund the TOD and the Transit

The value capture (the public recovery of the land value increase the transit generates) is the tool that funds the TOD infrastructure and the transit, because the transit increases the land value, and the capture of the increase recycles the benefit into the investment that created it. The rule is to use the value capture to fund the TOD and the transit, using the tax increment financing (the TIF that captures the property tax increase), the special assessment (the assessment on the benefiting properties), the development impact fees (the fees on the new development), the joint development (the development of the transit agency's land), and the air rights (the development above the transit). The TIF should be used to fund the infrastructure (the streets, the utilities, the public realm), because the infrastructure is the prerequisite for the development, and the TIF funds it from the future tax revenue. The TIF should be calibrated to the revenue projection, because the TIF that overestimates the revenue generates the shortfall, and the conservative projection avoids it. The joint development should be used on the transit agency's land, because the agency's land is the prime TOD site, and the development generates the revenue and the ridership. The air rights should be used above the transit, because the air rights are the development opportunity the transit creates, and the development capitalizes on it.

### Assemble the Land and Manage the Public-Private Partnership

The land assembly and the public-private partnership (the PPP) are the implementation tools that overcome the fragmented ownership and the market reluctance, because the TOD often requires the assembled land (the multiple parcels combined) and the private development capacity the public sector does not have. The rule is to assemble the land and manage the public-private partnership, using the land assembly (the acquisition, the consolidation, the land banking), the developer selection (the RFP, the qualification, the negotiation), the partnership structure (the risk sharing, the revenue sharing, the public benefit), and the disposition (the ground lease, the sale, the development agreement). The land assembly should be pursued where the ownership is fragmented, because the fragmented ownership prevents the coherent development, and the assembly enables it. The land assembly should use the voluntary acquisition first and the eminent domain as the last resort, because the voluntary acquisition builds the goodwill, and the eminent domain generates the opposition. The developer selection should be competitive and the qualification-based, because the competitive selection produces the best proposal, and the qualification-based selection ensures the capacity. The partnership should share the risk and the return, because the TOD involves the risks (the market, the construction, the permitting) and the returns, and the sharing aligns the interests. The disposition should use the ground lease (the public retains the land, the developer builds), because the ground lease retains the public control and the revenue, and the sale does not.

### Phase the Development to Match the Transit and the Market

The TOD should be phased to match the transit service and the market demand, because the TOD that precedes the transit or the market stalls, and the phased TOD that matches the transit and the market succeeds. The rule is to phase the development to match the transit and the market, using the transit-sequencing analysis (the development before, with, or after the transit), the market phasing (the phases matched to the absorption), the infrastructure phasing (the infrastructure preceding the development), and the catalytic projects (the early projects that build the momentum). The transit sequencing should determine whether the development precedes the transit (the development that creates the demand for the transit), coincides with the transit (the development that leverages the transit construction), or follows the transit (the development that responds to the transit service). The market phasing should match the development to the absorption, because the development that exceeds the absorption generates the vacancy, and the matched development does not. The infrastructure phasing should precede the development, because the development without the infrastructure stalls, and the preceding infrastructure enables it. The catalytic projects should be the early investments that build the momentum, because the TOD often needs the catalyst to start, and the catalytic project provides it.

### Implement the TOD in the Weak Market

The TOD in the weak market (the market that does not support the development without the public support) requires the additional tools, because the conventional financing does not work, and the public tools must fill the gap. The rule is to implement the TOD in the weak market, using the gap financing (the public funding that fills the gap between the cost and the market), the tax abatement (the reduced property tax that improves the feasibility), the public land (the free or the reduced-cost land that lowers the cost), the infrastructure investment (the public infrastructure that enables the development), and the anchor tenants (the public or the institutional anchors that attract the private investment). The gap financing should be calibrated to the minimum needed, because the excessive gap financing wastes the public resources, and the calibrated financing uses them efficiently. The tax abatement should be time-limited, because the permanent abatement forgoes the revenue forever, and the time-limited abatement bridges the gap. The public land should be used strategically, because the free land lowers the development cost, and the strategic use maximizes the benefit. The infrastructure investment should precede the development, because the infrastructure is the prerequisite, and the preceding investment enables. The anchor tenants should be recruited, because the anchor (the government office, the university, the major employer) attracts the private development, and the anchor de-risks the project.

### Address the Affordable Housing and the Community Benefits

The TOD should address the affordable housing and the community benefits, because the TOD often raises the property values and the rents, and the existing residents are displaced if the affordable housing is not preserved and the benefits are not provided. The rule is to address the affordable housing and the community benefits, using the inclusionary zoning (the affordable units in the TOD), the affordable housing set-aside (the percentage of the TOD that is affordable), the community benefits agreement (the developer's commitment to the community), and the anti-displacement strategies (the preservation, the protection, the production). The inclusionary zoning should require the affordable units in the TOD, because the TOD is the high-opportunity area, and the affordable units in the TOD advance the equity. The set-aside should be meaningful (the 15 to 20 percent), because the token set-aside does not produce the significant affordable housing, and the meaningful set-aside does. The community benefits agreement should be negotiated with the community, because the community knows the benefits it needs, and the negotiated agreement ensures the delivery. The anti-displacement strategies should protect the existing residents, because the TOD that displaces the existing residents is the TOD that fails the community, and the strategies prevent the displacement.

## Common Traps

### Value Capture Not Used, TOD Infrastructure Unfunded

The value capture is not used, and the TOD infrastructure is unfunded, because the TIF and the other tools were not implemented. The trap is that the unused value capture leaves the infrastructure unfunded. The mechanism is that the value capture was not adopted, and the infrastructure was left unfunded. The false signal is that "the TOD will happen." The harm is that the infrastructure is not built, the development does not occur, the transit investment is underutilized, and the value capture that should fund the TOD does not.

### Land Not Assembled, Development Fragmented

The land is not assembled, and the development is fragmented, because the assembly and the partnership were not pursued. The trap is that the unassembled land produces the fragmented development. The mechanism is that the assembly was not pursued, and the fragmented ownership prevented the coherent development. The false signal is that "the parcels are available." The harm is that the development is fragmented, the coherent TOD is not achieved, the parcels are developed individually, and the assembly that should enable the coherent TOD does not.

### Phasing Mismatched, Development Stalls

The phasing is mismatched (the development precedes the market or the transit), and the development stalls, because the sequencing was not analyzed. The trap is that the mismatched phasing stalls the development. The mechanism is that the sequencing was not analyzed, and the development was built at the wrong time. The false signal is that "the development is underway." The harm is that the development stalls, the vacancy occurs, the investors lose, the momentum is lost, and the phasing that should match the transit and the market does not.

### Weak Market Not Addressed, TOD Does Not Occur

The weak market is not addressed, and the TOD does not occur, because the gap financing and the public tools were not provided. The trap is that the unaddressed weak market prevents the TOD. The mechanism is that the gap financing was not provided, and the market could not support the development. The false signal is that "the market will come." The harm is that the TOD does not occur, the transit investment is underutilized, the community does not benefit, and the tools that should enable the weak-market TOD do not.

### Affordable Housing Not Addressed, Displacement Results

The affordable housing is not addressed, and the displacement results, because the inclusionary zoning and the anti-displacement were not implemented. The trap is that the unaddressed affordable housing causes the displacement. The mechanism is that the inclusionary zoning was not required, and the TOD raised the rents. The false signal is that "the TOD is built." The harm is that the existing residents are displaced, the equity is not advanced, the community is disrupted, and the affordable housing that should protect the residents does not.

## Self-Check

- Is the land assembled and the public-private partnership managed, with the acquisition, the developer selection, the risk sharing, and the disposition?
- Is the development phased to match the transit and the market, with the sequencing, the market absorption, the infrastructure, and the catalytic projects?
- Is the TOD implemented in the weak market, with the gap financing, the tax abatement, the public land, the infrastructure, and the anchors?
- Is the affordable housing and the community benefits addressed, with the inclusionary zoning, the set-aside, the community benefits agreement, and the anti-displacement?
- Is the value capture used, or is the infrastructure unfunded?
- Is the land assembled, or is the development fragmented?
- Is the phasing matched, or does the development stall?
- Is the weak market addressed, or does the TOD not occur?
- Is the affordable housing addressed, or does the displacement result?
