# Public Art Maintenance, Conservation, and Deaccession

The public art collection is the long-term asset that requires the ongoing stewardship — the maintenance, the conservation, the repair — and the collection that is not maintained deteriorates, and the deteriorated artwork becomes the liability, the embarrassment, or the controversy. The judgment problem is that the public art stewardship requires the technical expertise (the conservation, the materials science), the financial resources (the maintenance budget, the conservation funding), and the difficult decisions (the repair, the deaccession, the removal), and the planner must manage these dimensions to maintain the collection's value and the public trust. A collection that is stewarded well (the maintenance plan, the conservation, the deaccession policy) retains its value, while a collection that is stewarded poorly (the neglect, the deferred conservation, the ad hoc removal) loses its value and generates the controversy. This skill covers the maintenance, the conservation, and the deaccession decisions that determine whether the public art collection retains its value. It complements the broader public-art skill and the commissioning skill by focusing on the stewardship dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Develop the Collection Management Plan and the Inventory

The public art collection should be managed through the collection management plan and the inventory, because the plan provides the framework for the stewardship, and the inventory provides the record of the collection, and the collection without the plan and the inventory is the collection that is not maintained. The rule is to develop the collection management plan and the inventory, documenting each artwork (the artist, the title, the date, the medium, the location, the condition, the value, the maintenance requirements), establishing the maintenance schedule (the routine cleaning, the periodic inspection, the conservation), and identifying the funding (the maintenance budget, the conservation fund). The inventory should be comprehensive and current, because the comprehensive inventory is the record that guides the stewardship, and the incomplete inventory is the record that misses the artworks. The inventory should include the condition assessment, because the condition identifies the maintenance and the conservation needs, and the unassessed condition is the unknown condition. The maintenance schedule should be based on the artist's recommendations and the material requirements, because the different artworks require the different maintenance, and the uniform schedule does not address the differences. The collection management plan should be reviewed and updated, because the collection changes (the new acquisitions, the condition changes), and the plan should reflect the current collection.

### Budget for the Maintenance and the Conservation

The maintenance and the conservation should be budgeted, because the stewardship requires the funding, and the unfunded stewardship is the stewardship that is not done. The rule is to budget for the maintenance and the conservation, including the maintenance in the operating budget (the annual cost of the routine maintenance), the conservation in the capital budget (the periodic cost of the major conservation), and the contingency for the emergency (the damage, the vandalism, the weather). The maintenance budget should be funded from the ongoing revenue, because the maintenance is the ongoing cost, and the ongoing revenue funds it. The conservation budget should be planned for the periodic conservation, because the conservation is the periodic cost (every 10 to 20 years), and the planned funding ensures the conservation. The contingency should be available for the emergency, because the public art is exposed to the damage and the vandalism, and the contingency funds the response. The budget should include the percentage for the maintenance in the initial commissioning (the one to two percent of the project cost set aside for the maintenance), because the maintenance funding from the start ensures the ongoing care, and the unfunded maintenance leads to the neglect.

### Address the Deterioration and the Damage Promptly

The deterioration and the damage should be addressed promptly, because the prompt response prevents the further deterioration, and the delayed response allows the deterioration to worsen, and the worsened deterioration is more expensive to repair. The rule is to address the deterioration and the damage promptly, using the condition monitoring (the periodic inspection, the reporting), the prompt assessment (the evaluation of the damage by the conservator), the conservation response (the repair, the restoration), and the documentation (the record of the condition and the treatment). The condition monitoring should identify the deterioration early, because the early identification allows the early response, and the late identification allows the worsening. The prompt assessment should be conducted by the qualified conservator, because the conservator has the expertise, and the unqualified assessment may worsen the damage. The conservation response should follow the conservation standards (the American Institute for Conservation, the professional guidelines), because the standards ensure the quality, and the non-standard treatment may damage the artwork. The documentation should record the condition and the treatment, because the documentation provides the record for the future, and the undocumented treatment is the treatment that is not traceable.

### Develop the Deaccession Policy and the Criteria

The deaccession (the formal removal of the artwork from the collection) should be governed by the policy and the criteria, because the deaccession is the significant decision that affects the collection and the public, and the policy and the criteria ensure the decision is principled, while the ad hoc deaccession is the decision that is challenged. The rule is to develop the deaccession policy and the criteria, defining the grounds for the deaccession (the deterioration beyond the repair, the safety hazard, the site change, the community opposition, the artistic significance decline), the process (the review, the hearing, the decision), and the disposition (the sale, the donation, the return to the artist, the relocation, the destruction). The grounds should be specific and the documented, because the specific grounds guide the decision, and the vague grounds enable the ad hoc. The process should be public and the deliberate, because the public art is the public asset, and the public process ensures the accountability. The disposition should follow the professional standards (the American Alliance of Museums, the Association for Public Art), because the standards ensure the ethical disposition, and the non-standard disposition may violate the artist's rights or the public trust. The deaccession proceeds should be used for the collection (the acquisition, the conservation), because the proceeds used for the other purposes violate the trust, and the proceeds used for the collection advance the collection.

### Manage the Removal and the Community Response

The removal of the deaccessioned artwork should be managed, because the removal is the physical and the political act that affects the site, the community, and the artist, and the unmanaged removal generates the conflict. The rule is to manage the removal and the community response, notifying the artist (the artist's moral rights, the notification requirement), engaging the community (the explanation, the dialogue), documenting the removal (the record of the artwork and the removal), and restoring the site (the repair of the site after the removal). The artist notification should respect the artist's moral rights (the right of attribution, the right of integrity), because the moral rights are the legal rights, and the notification is the obligation. The community engagement should explain the deaccession, because the community may have the attachment to the artwork, and the explanation builds the understanding. The documentation should record the artwork before the removal, because the documentation preserves the record, and the undocumented removal loses the record. The site restoration should repair the site, because the removal may damage the site, and the restoration returns the site to the usable condition.

## Common Traps

### Collection Not Inventoried, Artworks Forgotten

The collection is not inventoried, and the artworks are forgotten, because the inventory and the management plan were not developed. The trap is that the uninventoried collection is the forgotten collection. The mechanism is that the inventory was not conducted, and the collection was not tracked. The false signal is that "we have the public art." The harm is that the artworks are forgotten, the maintenance is not done, the condition deteriorates, and the collection that should be stewarded is neglected.

### Maintenance Not Budgeted, Conservation Deferred

The maintenance is not budgeted, and the conservation is deferred, because the funding was not allocated. The trap is that the unfunded maintenance leads to the deferred conservation. The mechanism is that the maintenance was not budgeted, and the conservation was postponed. The false signal is that "the art is installed." The harm is that the maintenance is not done, the conservation is deferred, the deterioration worsens, the cost increases, and the stewardship that should be funded is not.

### Deterioration Not Addressed, Artwork Lost

The deterioration is not addressed promptly, and the artwork is lost, because the monitoring and the conservation were not implemented. The trap is that the unaddressed deterioration loses the artwork. The mechanism is that the monitoring was not conducted, and the conservation was delayed. The false signal is that "the artwork is durable." The harm is that the deterioration worsens, the artwork is damaged beyond the repair, the artwork is lost, and the deterioration that should have been addressed is not.

### Deaccession Ad Hoc, Decision Challenged

The deaccession is ad hoc, and the decision is challenged, because the policy and the criteria were not developed. The trap is that the ad hoc deaccession is the challenged deaccession. The mechanism is that the policy was not adopted, and the deaccession was decided case-by-case. The false signal is that "the artwork is removed." The harm is that the decision is challenged, the community opposes, the artist sues, the public trust is eroded, and the deaccession that should be principled is ad hoc.

### Removal Mismanaged, Conflict Results

The removal is mismanaged, and the conflict results, because the artist notification, the community engagement, and the site restoration were not managed. The trap is that the mismanaged removal generates the conflict. The mechanism is that the notification and the engagement were not conducted, and the removal proceeded abruptly. The false signal is that "the artwork is removed." The harm is that the artist's rights are violated, the community is alienated, the site is damaged, the conflict follows, and the removal that should be managed is mismanaged.

## Self-Check

- Is the collection management plan and the inventory developed, with the comprehensive record, the condition assessment, and the maintenance schedule?
- Is the maintenance and the conservation budgeted, with the operating budget, the capital budget, the contingency, and the initial percentage?
- Is the deterioration and the damage addressed promptly, with the monitoring, the conservator assessment, the standard treatment, and the documentation?
- Is the deaccession policy and the criteria developed, with the grounds, the process, the disposition, and the proceeds use?
- Is the removal and the community response managed, with the artist notification, the community engagement, the documentation, and the site restoration?
- Is the collection inventoried, or are the artworks forgotten?
- Is the maintenance budgeted, or is the conservation deferred?
- Is the deterioration addressed, or is the artwork lost?
- Is the deaccession principled, or is it ad hoc?
- Is the removal managed, or does the conflict result?
