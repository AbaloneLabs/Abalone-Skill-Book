# Small Town and Village Planning

Small-town planning fails most often not from lack of vision but from the silent mismatch between the tools used and the community's actual capacity. A village of 2,000 residents governed by a part-time board, a volunteer planning commission, and a contracted building inspector cannot administer the form-based code, the tiered discretionary review process, or the elaborate GIS-based suitability analysis that a metropolitan planner would default to. The judgment problem is that the dominant planning literature, software, and training are produced by and for larger jurisdictions, so the planner working in a small town imports instruments that look professional but require staffing, data, and political infrastructure the community does not have — and the plan collapses the moment the consultant leaves or the one staff planner retires. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a small-town plan will be implementable by the people and budget that will actually exist, or whether it will become a document the community cannot use.

## Core Rules

### Design Every Process to the Community's Real Administrative Capacity

Before selecting any tool or procedure, inventory the human and fiscal capacity that will implement it: how many paid staff, their hours and qualifications, the volunteer board's experience and turnover, the budget for consulting engineers and attorneys, and the software and data infrastructure available. A zoning ordinance that requires a trained planner to interpret every application is unenforceable in a town where the code enforcement officer is also the road commissioner and works twenty hours a week. A development review process with three tiers of discretionary hearings will bottleneck because the planning commission meets monthly and has no staff reports. Calibrate every instrument to the capacity that exists: prefer by-right standards over discretionary review where judgment capacity is thin, prefer simple dimensional tables over complex form-based codes where no one can administer them, and prefer paper-and-spreadsheet workflows over GIS platforms no one is trained to run. Capacity is the binding constraint; elegance is not.

### Anchor the Plan to the Fiscal and Economic Reality of the Tax Base

Small towns plan within a fiscal envelope that metropolitan jurisdictions do not face: a narrow tax base, dependence on a single large employer or industry, state-aid volatility, and infrastructure (water, sewer, roads) that is often aged and undercapitalized relative to its replacement cost. A plan that calls for new parks, streetscape reconstruction, and expanded facilities without reconciling against the capital improvements program and the bonding capacity is a wish list, not a plan. Build the plan around the town's actual revenue structure and its capital backlog: prioritize the infrastructure that protects the existing tax base (a failing sewer main that threatens downtown businesses) before the infrastructure that aspirationally grows it, and be explicit about which projects depend on grants or partnerships the town cannot fund alone. Plans that ignore fiscal reality breed cynicism when none of the projects materialize.

### Protect the Functional and Economic Core of the Community

Most small towns have a single functional core — a main street, a village center, a courthouse square — that concentrates the community's civic, commercial, and social life, and whose loss to disinvestment, highway relocation, or big-box edge development is existential rather than incremental. The plan's first economic-development priority is typically to protect and reinvigorate this core, because once the main street empties, the community loses the density of interaction that makes it a community rather than a collection of driveways. Focus zoning, infrastructure investment, façade programs, and small-business support on the core before pursuing fringe commercial development that competes with it. Resist the pressure to rezone highway corridors for strip commercial on the argument that it generates tax revenue, because the revenue often comes at the cost of the core's viability and produces a net fiscal and social loss over time.

### Sequence Engagement Around Volunteer Board and Civic Rhythms

Small-town engagement is not a scaled-down version of metropolitan outreach. The community's civic life runs through volunteer boards, service clubs, churches, the coffee shop, the library, and the weekly newspaper, and a plan that holds a single evening charrette at the municipal building will hear from the same handful of regulars who attend every meeting. Sequence engagement to meet people where the civic networks already are: service-club lunches, farm bureau meetings, school events, the post office, the diner. Recognize that volunteer boards have limited meeting frequency and stamina, and do not load them with a review schedule that exceeds their capacity. Build the plan's legitimacy through the existing social infrastructure rather than parallel engagement channels that disappear when the planning process ends.

### Preserve Community Character Without Weaponizing It Against Needed Housing

Small towns face a specific tension: residents value the rural or small-town character that brought or kept them there, and that character is often invoked to oppose the very housing and development the community needs to sustain its population, schools, and businesses. The trap is not that character does not matter — it does, deeply — but that "character" is frequently used as a proxy for exclusion without any operational definition, so any change can be blocked as inconsistent with it. The rule is to define character operationally (the scale, massing, setbacks, materials, and street relationship that produce the town's distinctive pattern) and then design standards that allow new housing consistent with that pattern, rather than treating character as a veto on all growth. A village that preserves its appearance while losing its young families and its school has preserved a stage set, not a community.

### Build Implementation Around Tools the Town Can Sustain Without Consultants

The implementation chapter should privilege instruments the town can run with its own staff and budget over instruments that require recurring consultant engagement. A simple zoning map amendment, a checklist-based site-plan review, a capital improvements list tied to the annual budget, and a small-project grant pipeline are sustainable; a complex form-based code requiring specialized administration, a discretionary design review requiring trained commissioners, and a multi-year GIS build-out are not, unless external funding for administration is secured. When a sophisticated tool is genuinely needed, pair it with a capacity-building plan — training, shared services with the county or region, or a regional planning agency that provides ongoing support — rather than assuming the town will figure it out.

## Common Traps

### Importing the Metropolitan Plan Wholesale

The planner, trained in metropolitan practice, brings a comprehensive plan structure, a zoning template, and a public-engagement design calibrated to a city of 100,000 and applies it to a town of 3,000. The trap mechanism is that the imported tools are professionally familiar and look rigorous, but their staffing, data, and procedural requirements exceed what the town can sustain, and their assumptions about market depth, infrastructure, and demographic trends do not match rural reality. The false signal is a polished plan that reads as modern and complete. The harm is that the town adopts instruments it cannot administer, the plan's ambitious elements never move, and within a few years the document is ignored — leaving the town worse off because staff and political capital were spent on a plan that did not fit.

### The Consultant Plan With No Transfer of Capacity

The town hires a consultant to write the comprehensive plan, the consultant delivers a strong document, and the engagement ends. The trap mechanism is that the plan's intelligence resides in the consultant's institutional memory and files, not in the town's staff or boards, so when implementation questions arise no one in the town can interpret or defend the plan's analysis. The false signal is a high-quality deliverable. The harm is that the plan becomes a static reference rather than a living instrument, the next amendment cycle starts from scratch because the analytical foundation was never transferred, and the town has spent scarce funds on a product it cannot maintain or use strategically.

### Highway Commercial as the Default Economic Strategy

Facing a stagnant tax base, the town rezones its highway edge for gas stations, dollar stores, and fast food, on the theory that any development is good development and the highway has the traffic. The trap mechanism is that fringe highway commercial directly competes with the main street for the same limited consumer dollars, and because the highway sites have cheaper land and easier parking, they win, hollowing out the core. The false signal is new construction and apparent tax-base growth at the edge. The harm is the slow death of the downtown, the loss of the walkable civic and social concentration that defines the town, and a net fiscal position that is often worse because the edge development requires road, water, and public-safety extensions whose costs are rarely fully captured in the project's fiscal analysis.

### Character as an Undefined Veto

Residents organize against a small infill housing project on the grounds that it is "inconsistent with community character," and because the plan and code never define character operationally, the objection is unanswerable on the merits and the project is denied or scaled to irrelevance. The trap mechanism is that an undefined standard gives every opponent a veto that cannot be evaluated against evidence, and the veto is wielded disproportionately against the affordable and multifamily housing the town needs most. The false signal is that the community is "protecting its character." The harm is that the town preserves its appearance while losing the demographic diversity, school enrollment, and workforce housing that sustain its actual functioning, and the character being protected becomes a museum of affluence rather than a living community.

## Self-Check

- Have I inventoried the town's real staff, volunteer-board, fiscal, and data capacity, and calibrated every tool and procedure to that capacity rather than to metropolitan defaults?
- Is the plan's project list reconciled against the capital improvements program, the bonding limit, and the existing infrastructure backlog, with grant-dependent projects clearly identified?
- Does the economic-development strategy prioritize protecting and reinvigorating the functional core (main street, village center) before pursuing fringe commercial that would compete with it?
- Was engagement sequenced through existing civic networks (service clubs, churches, the weekly paper, the post office) rather than a single charrette that reaches only the usual attendees?
- Is "community character" defined operationally — in scale, massing, setback, material, and street-relationship terms — so that new housing consistent with the pattern can be approved rather than vetoed?
- Does the implementation chapter privilege tools the town can administer with its own staff, and pair any sophisticated instrument with a documented capacity-building or shared-services plan?
- If a consultant produced the plan, is there a documented transfer of the analytical foundation, data, and interpretive capacity to town staff and boards?
- Has the plan been stress-tested against the loss of a single staff person or the turnover of a volunteer board, since small-town institutional memory is often concentrated in one or two people?
