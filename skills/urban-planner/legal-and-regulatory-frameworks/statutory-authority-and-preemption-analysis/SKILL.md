# Statutory Authority and Preemption Analysis

Before a jurisdiction can regulate, it must have the legal authority to do so, and even with authority, its regulation cannot conflict with superior law. The judgment problem is not "is this a good policy" but whether the jurisdiction has been granted the power to adopt it, whether state or federal law preempts it, and how the local regulation fits within the hierarchy of governing law. Planners commonly fail by adopting regulations that exceed the jurisdiction's delegated authority, by missing state preemption of local rent control, tenant protections, or energy regulations, or by drafting local provisions that conflict with state environmental, transportation, or housing law. Authority and preemption analysis is what determines whether a carefully drafted ordinance is enforceable or will be struck down; getting it wrong wastes years of work and exposes the jurisdiction to liability.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Source of Authority Before Drafting

Local governments are creatures of state law, and their power derives from the state constitution, enabling legislation, and the local charter. Under Dillon's Rule (followed in many states), localities have only those powers expressly granted, necessarily implied, or essential to the declared purpose; under home rule (broader in others), localities have plenary authority except where limited. Determine which rule applies in your state and identify the specific grant of authority for the proposed regulation — the zoning enabling act, the police power delegation, a specific statute authorizing the tool (inclusionary zoning, rent stabilization, short-term rental regulation). If you cannot identify the source of authority, the regulation is vulnerable no matter how good the policy. Cite the authority in the ordinance's purpose section and the staff report.

### Analyze Preemption in Both Directions

Preemption cuts both ways and must be checked in both directions. Vertical preemption: does state or federal law occupy the field, expressly preempt local regulation, or conflict with the local provision such that compliance with both is impossible? State law often preempts local rent control, eviction regulation, building energy codes, firearm regulation, and telecommunications siting, and federal law preempts areas like airline noise, cable franchising, and certain environmental programs. Horizontal preemption and conflict: does the local regulation conflict with another local, regional, or state plan or law that governs the same subject? Identify every potentially preemptive statute and regulation before adoption, and where preemption exists, either do not regulate, regulate only within the space left open, or seek state enabling legislation.

### Reconcile Local Regulation with State Housing and Environmental Law

Many states have housing and environmental statutes that constrain local authority in specific ways: housing element laws requiring zoning for a regional housing need, density bonus laws, streamlining of certain housing types, state environmental review requirements, and coastal or critical area acts. A local regulation that impedes housing production in violation of state housing law, or that fails to apply required environmental review, can be challenged and overturned, sometimes with mandatory remedies (e.g., a court rezoning the property). Map the applicable state housing and environmental framework before drafting, and ensure the local regulation complies — or use the state framework as the basis for the local action where it supports the goal. Ignoring these statutes is the most common source of housing litigation.

### Distinguish Legislative, Quasi-Judicial, and Ministerial Acts

The nature of the decision determines its procedural requirements and the standard of review. Legislative acts (adopting a zoning ordinance, a comprehensive plan amendment) are subject to deferential review and broad public process; quasi-judicial acts (variances, conditional use permits, subdivisions) require due process protections and record-based decisions; ministerial acts (building permits issued against objective standards) are reviewed for compliance with the standards, not for discretion. Misclassifying a decision — treating a quasi-judicial decision as legislative to avoid due process, or a discretionary decision as ministerial to avoid review — creates procedural and substantive vulnerability. Classify each decision correctly and apply the matching procedures.

### Handle Conflicts Between Local Regulations and Other Provisions

Internal consistency matters: a new regulation must not conflict with the comprehensive plan, with other sections of the zoning code, or with other local ordinances. Many states require zoning to be "in accordance with" the comprehensive plan, so a zoning amendment inconsistent with the plan must be preceded by a plan amendment or it is vulnerable. Scan for conflicts with other code provisions (signs, parking, environmental, building) and reconcile them as part of the same adoption, because latent conflicts surface as interpretation disputes and enforcement problems later. A code that is internally inconsistent is harder to administer and easier to challenge.

### Document the Authority Analysis in the Record

The authority and preemption analysis should be documented in the staff report and the legislative record, so that the jurisdiction's legal basis is clear and defensible. Cite the enabling act, address potential preemption, explain consistency with the plan and state law, and identify the classification of the act (legislative, quasi-judicial, ministerial). This documentation protects the regulation against challenge and guides future interpretation. An ordinance adopted without a documented authority analysis is defended after the fact, often poorly, by attorneys who must reconstruct what the planners intended.

## Common Traps

### Regulating Without Identifiable Authority

Adopting a regulation because the policy is desirable, without identifying the specific grant of authority, is vulnerable to challenge under Dillon's Rule or even home rule where the subject is preempted. The false signal is that local government has broad police power; the harm is invalidation when the authority gap is exposed. Identify and cite the source of authority before drafting.

### Missing State Preemption

Failing to identify a state statute that preempts the local regulation — on rent control, energy codes, tenant protections, or other subjects — produces an ordinance that cannot legally stand. The false signal is a complete-looking local regulation; the harm is invalidation and, often, liability. Conduct a preemption analysis for every novel regulation and consult counsel on ambiguous cases.

### Conflicts with State Housing and Environmental Law

Local regulations that impede housing production or skip required environmental review, in violation of state statutes, are a primary source of litigation and can result in court-ordered remedies. The false signal is local control over land use; the harm is that the state framework overrides the local action, sometimes dramatically. Map and comply with the state housing and environmental framework.

### Misclassifying the Decision Type

Treating a quasi-judicial decision as legislative (to avoid due process) or a discretionary decision as ministerial (to avoid review) creates procedural vulnerability and incorrect standards of review. The false signal is procedural efficiency; the harm is decisions overturned for using the wrong process. Classify decisions correctly and apply the matching procedures.

### Internal Inconsistency with the Plan or Code

Adopting a zoning amendment inconsistent with the comprehensive plan, or a regulation that conflicts with other code provisions, creates interpretation disputes and enforcement problems. The false signal is a focused, targeted amendment; the harm is a code that cannot be administered coherently. Reconcile conflicts and amend the plan first where required.

## Self-Check

- Have you identified the specific source of authority (enabling act, charter, statute) for the proposed regulation and cited it in the record?
- Have you analyzed vertical preemption (state and federal) and confirmed the local regulation does not conflict with or intrude on preempted subjects?
- Does the regulation comply with applicable state housing and environmental law, including housing element, density bonus, streamlining, and environmental review requirements?
- Is the decision correctly classified as legislative, quasi-judicial, or ministerial, with the matching procedures applied?
- Is the regulation consistent with the comprehensive plan and with other provisions of the local code, with conflicts reconciled?
- Is the authority and preemption analysis documented in the staff report and legislative record, not merely assumed?
- Did you avoid adopting regulation without an identifiable grant of authority?
- Did you avoid missing state preemption of subjects like rent control, energy codes, or tenant protections?
- Did you avoid conflicts with state housing and environmental statutes that could trigger court-ordered remedies?
- If challenged, would the regulation withstand review for authority, preemption, consistency, and correct procedural classification?
