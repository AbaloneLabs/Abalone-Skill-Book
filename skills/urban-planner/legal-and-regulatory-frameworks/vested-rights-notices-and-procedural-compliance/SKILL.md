# Vested Rights, Notices, and Procedural Compliance

Land use decisions live or die on procedure and timing. A developer's rights can vest at a specific moment, locking in the rules that applied then; a notice published one day late can void a hearing; a deadline missed under a streamlining statute can result in deemed approval. The judgment problem is not "follow the rules" but how to identify the exact moment rights vest, how to compute and meet the multiple overlapping notice and timing requirements that govern each decision, and how to manage the transition when regulations change so that both applicants and the jurisdiction are treated lawfully. Planners commonly fail by misjudging vesting points, by botching notice (radius, timing, method, language), by missing statutory deadlines, or by mishandling nonconforming uses during code amendments. Procedural precision is what keeps decisions enforceable; procedural error is the most common, and most avoidable, source of overturned approvals.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Vesting Point Under the Applicable Standard

Vesting — the moment when a developer's rights lock in against later regulatory changes — varies by state and determines whether a project is judged under the code in effect when the application was submitted, when it was approved, when permits issued, or when construction began. Some states follow a "development agreement" or "preliminary approval" vesting standard; others vest only at building permit or substantial construction; some provide statutory vesting for valid applications in process. Identify the applicable standard and the specific vesting date for every project, because a regulation adopted after vesting cannot apply to the vested project, while one adopted before can. Document the vesting determination, because disputes about which code version applies are common and turn on the exact facts of when applications were deemed complete and what approvals issued.

### Compute and Document Every Notice Requirement

Notice requirements are multiple and specific: type (published, posted, mailed), timing (a fixed number of days before the hearing, often with separate windows for publication, posting, and mailing), radius (who must receive mailed notice, based on distance from the site), content (project description, hearing date and location, information on how to comment and appeal), and sometimes language (in languages other than English where required by state law or local policy). For each hearing, build a notice compliance checklist that captures every requirement, complete it with documentation (affidavits of publication, posting, and mailing; the mailing list and radius used), and retain it in the record. A notice defect discovered after the hearing can require re-noticing and re-hearing, and a notice defect discovered after the decision can invalidate it. Notice is not a formality; it is the jurisdictional foundation of the hearing.

### Track and Meet Statutory Deadlines

Many states impose streamlining and timing deadlines on land use decisions, particularly for housing: a fixed number of days to deem an application complete, to schedule a hearing, to issue a decision after the hearing, and to act on subsequent permits. Missing these deadlines can have consequences ranging from deemed approval (the project is approved by inaction) to loss of fee recovery to exposure to damages. Build a deadline-tracking system that flags each statutory clock, assigns responsibility, and escalates as deadlines approach. When a deadline cannot be met, use the legally available extensions (often by mutual agreement with the applicant, in writing) rather than letting the clock expire. Silent deadline misses are among the most expensive procedural errors a planning agency can make.

### Manage Nonconforming Uses When the Code Changes

When a zoning amendment changes the rules, existing uses and structures that complied before but do not comply after become nonconforming, and the code must specify how they are treated: can they continue, be repaired, be expanded, be rebuilt after damage, be resumed after abandonment? The legal principle (in most jurisdictions) is that nonconforming uses may continue but should not be encouraged to expand, and the code's treatment must be applied consistently and documented. When drafting the amendment, anticipate the nonconformities it will create and specify their treatment; when administering it, track nonconforming status on the parcel and apply the rules consistently. Mishandling nonconforming uses — allowing undocumented expansions, applying inconsistent damage-rebuild rules — creates disputes and equal protection problems.

### Preserve the Procedural Record at Every Step

The procedural record — notice affidavits, completeness determinations, hearing notices, staff reports, testimony, exhibits, the resolution with findings and conditions, the vote, and the appeal — is what an appellate body or court reviews. Build and retain this record contemporaneously, not reconstructed after the fact. Each procedural step should be documented in the file with its date and the responsible party, so the record shows a complete, compliant process from intake to final decision. A procedurally sound decision supported by a thin or disorganized record is harder to defend than one with a clean, complete record; treat record-keeping as part of the decision, not as clerical aftermath.

### Coordinate Vesting and Code Transitions Deliberately

When a major code amendment is adopted, there is typically a transition period during which applications submitted before the effective date are reviewed under the old code and those after under the new. Define the transition rules clearly (the trigger date, whether applications must be complete or merely submitted, how related applications are handled), publish them, and apply them consistently. A poorly defined transition invites a rush of last-minute applications under the old code and disputes about which applications were "in process." Clear transition rules protect both the jurisdiction's policy goals and applicants' reasonable expectations.

## Common Traps

### Misjudging the Vesting Point

Assuming vesting occurs at a particular milestone (application submittal, approval, permit) without confirming the state standard can lead to applying the wrong code version to a project. The false signal is that the current code governs; the harm is that a vested project is subjected to rules that do not apply, or a non-vested project escapes rules that do. Confirm the vesting standard and document the determination for each project.

### Notice Defects

Errors in notice — wrong radius, missed publication window, inadequate content, missing required languages — can invalidate a hearing or decision, sometimes discovered only on appeal. The false signal is that notice was "sent"; the harm is jurisdictional defect that voids the outcome. Use a notice checklist, document compliance with affidavits, and verify the mailing list and radius.

### Missing Statutory Deadlines

Letting a streamlining or decision deadline pass without action can result in deemed approval, fee shifting, or damages, undoing the jurisdiction's substantive position. The false signal is that the timeline is flexible; the harm is automatic legal consequences. Track every statutory clock and use written extensions when needed.

### Inconsistent Treatment of Nonconforming Uses

Allowing some nonconforming uses to expand or rebuild while denying others, without a documented basis, creates equal protection exposure and administrative chaos. The false signal is case-by-case flexibility; the harm is inconsistent decisions that cannot be defended. Apply the code's nonconforming use rules consistently and document the basis for each determination.

### Disorganized or Incomplete Procedural Records

A decision supported by a scattered or incomplete procedural record is difficult to defend on appeal, because the jurisdiction cannot demonstrate compliance with notice, completeness, hearing, and findings requirements. The false signal is that the right decision was reached; the harm is that it cannot be proven in the record. Build and retain the procedural record contemporaneously at every step.

## Self-Check

- Have you identified the applicable vesting standard and the specific vesting date for the project, and documented the determination?
- For each hearing, did you complete and document a notice checklist covering type, timing, radius, content, and required languages, with affidavits retained?
- Are all statutory deadlines (completeness, hearing scheduling, decision, permits) tracked, with written extensions used before clocks expire?
- When the code changes, have you anticipated the nonconformities created, specified their treatment, and applied the rules consistently?
- Is the procedural record — notice, completeness, staff report, testimony, exhibits, resolution, vote, appeal — built and retained contemporaneously?
- Are code transition rules clearly defined and consistently applied when major amendments take effect?
- Did you avoid assuming the vesting point without confirming the state standard?
- Did you avoid notice defects by using a checklist and documenting compliance?
- Did you avoid letting statutory deadlines pass without action or written extension?
- If the decision were appealed, would the procedural record demonstrate complete compliance with every notice, timing, and process requirement?
