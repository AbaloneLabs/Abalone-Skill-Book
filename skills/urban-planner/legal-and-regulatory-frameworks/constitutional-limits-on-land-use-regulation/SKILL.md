# Constitutional Limits on Land Use Regulation

Land use regulation is an exercise of the police power, but that power is bounded by constitutional protections that, when violated, expose the jurisdiction to invalidation, damages, and attorneys' fees. The judgment problem is not "can we regulate this" but how to regulate in a way that survives constitutional challenge — respecting the takings clause, substantive and procedural due process, equal protection, and the First Amendment as it bears on signs, religious uses, and speech. Planners commonly fail by drafting regulations that lack a rational basis, by imposing conditions or exactions without the required nexus and proportionality, by treating signs or religious institutions as ordinary land uses, or by applying regulations in a way that targets or excludes disfavored groups. Constitutional literacy is what separates defensible regulation from regulation that collapses in litigation, often years after adoption.

## Core Rules

### Ground Every Regulation in a Legitimate Public Purpose with Rational Basis

Under the rational basis test that governs most economic and land use regulation, a regulation must bear a rational relationship to a legitimate government interest — health, safety, welfare, environmental protection, orderly development. While rational basis is deferential, it is not a rubber stamp, especially where a regulation is challenged as arbitrary, as excluding a lawful use without reason, or as bearing no real relationship to its stated purpose. For each regulation, be able to articulate the public harm it addresses and how the regulation rationally addresses it, and document that basis in the legislative record. Regulations adopted without an articulable purpose, or whose purpose is a pretext for exclusion or favoritism, are vulnerable even under rational basis review.

### Apply the Nollan/Dolan Nexus and Proportionality Test to Exactions

When a jurisdiction conditions development approval on the dedication of land, the payment of fees, or the construction of improvements (exactions), the Constitution requires both an essential nexus between the exaction and the impact of the proposed development (Nollan) and rough proportionality between the exaction and the impact, both in nature and extent (Dolan). These tests apply to adjudicative, discretionary conditions and, in some jurisdictions and under federal signals, to legislative exactions as well. For every exaction, document the specific project impact it addresses and the analysis showing proportionality; an exaction imposed without that analysis is an unconstitutional condition. Borrowed or boilerplate conditions that do not tie to the specific project's impacts are the most common source of exaction challenges.

### Respect Procedural Due Process in Adjudicative Decisions

Discretionary decisions — variances, conditional use permits, subdivisions — are adjudicative acts that require procedural due process: adequate notice, the opportunity to be heard before an impartial decision-maker, a decision based on the record with findings, and the right of appeal. Defects in notice (wrong radius, wrong timing, failure to publish in required languages), bias or ex parte communication by decision-makers, or decisions without findings grounded in the record can invalidate the outcome regardless of its substantive merits. Build the procedural requirements into the review workflow, document compliance at each step, and treat procedural regularity as substantive, because a procedurally defective decision is as vulnerable as a substantively wrong one.

### Avoid Equal Protection and Selective Enforcement Problems

Regulations must not be applied to treat similarly situated parties differently without a rational basis, and enforcement must not be selective or targeted. Patterns of denying permits to disfavored applicants while granting identical requests from favored ones, of enforcing code provisions against some property owners but not others, or of applying regulations in a way that correlates with race, ethnicity, or other protected status invite equal protection challenges and fair housing claims. Apply regulations consistently, document the basis for any differentiated treatment, and audit enforcement patterns for disparate impact. Inconsistency is not just poor practice; it is a constitutional vulnerability.

### Handle First Amendment and RLUIPA Land Uses with Care

Certain land uses carry heightened constitutional protection that ordinary zoning logic cannot override. Sign regulations are subject to First Amendment scrutiny (content-neutral standards, narrow tailoring), and content-based sign rules are presumptively invalid. Religious institutions are protected by the Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA), which prohibits land use regulations that impose a substantial burden on religious exercise without a compelling government interest, and also prohibits religious discrimination and exclusion. Treat these uses with specialized analysis, not generic zoning logic, and consult the agency attorney before denying or heavily conditioning a religious institution or applying content-based sign rules. Misapplying ordinary zoning to constitutionally protected uses is a predictable source of expensive litigation.

### Recognize When Regulation Crosses into a Takings

A regulation that goes "too far" in restricting use can constitute a taking requiring compensation, under frameworks that include physical takings, regulatory takings (the Penn Central multi-factor test for partial regulatory burdens, and the Lucas per-se rule for regulations that deprive all economic use), and exactions. While most zoning does not rise to a taking, regulations that eliminate essentially all economic use of a property, that authorize physical occupation, or that are imposed ad hoc without the nexus and proportionality analysis are vulnerable. When a regulation approaches eliminating viable use, analyze the takings implications before adoption, consider hardship exceptions or variances, and consult counsel. Ignoring the takings dimension does not make it go away; it surfaces later as a compensation claim.

## Common Traps

### Adopting Regulations Without an Articulable Rational Basis

Regulations drafted to address a political pressure or a neighborhood complaint, without a documented public purpose and rational connection, can fail rational basis review when challenged, especially if the record reveals a pretextual or exclusionary motive. The false signal is that zoning is entitled to deference; the harm is invalidation when the lack of basis is exposed. Document the public purpose and rational connection in the legislative record.

### Imposing Exactions Without Nexus and Proportionality Analysis

Conditions requiring dedications, fees, or improvements that are not tied to the specific project's impacts — borrowed from a standard list or extracted as community benefits — fail the Nollan/Dolan test and are unconstitutional conditions. The false signal is a robust mitigation package; the harm is that the conditions are struck down, potentially invalidating the approval. Analyze and document nexus and proportionality for every exaction.

### Procedural Defects in Adjudicative Decisions

Notice errors, ex parte communication, biased decision-makers, or decisions without findings can invalidate an otherwise sound substantive decision. The false signal is that the right outcome was reached; the harm is that the decision is overturned on procedural grounds regardless of merit. Build and document procedural regularity at every step.

### Inconsistent or Selective Application Inviting Equal Protection Claims

Applying regulations differently to similarly situated parties, or enforcing selectively, creates equal protection exposure and undermines the credibility of the regulatory system. The false signal is discretionary flexibility; the harm is litigation and invalidation based on disparate treatment. Apply regulations consistently and document the basis for any differentiation.

### Treating Signs and Religious Uses as Ordinary Land Uses

Applying generic zoning logic to signs (content-based distinctions) or religious institutions (substantial burdens, exclusion) ignores the heightened constitutional and statutory protections these uses carry. The false signal is even-handed treatment; the harm is presumptively invalid sign rules or RLUIPA violations with substantial damages exposure. Use specialized analysis for constitutionally protected uses and consult counsel.

## Self-Check

- Can you articulate the legitimate public purpose and rational basis for each regulation, and is that basis documented in the legislative record?
- For every exaction (dedication, fee, improvement), is there documented analysis of the essential nexus and rough proportionality to the specific project's impacts?
- Are adjudicative decisions supported by adequate notice, an impartial decision-maker free of ex parte communication, a record-based decision with findings, and appeal rights?
- Are regulations applied consistently to similarly situated parties, with any differentiated treatment documented and non-discriminatory?
- Are sign regulations content-neutral and narrowly tailored, and are religious institution decisions analyzed under RLUIPA before denial or heavy conditioning?
- Where a regulation approaches eliminating all economic use of property, has the takings dimension been analyzed and hardship exceptions considered?
- Did you avoid adopting regulations whose real motive is exclusion or favoritism rather than the stated public purpose?
- Did you avoid boilerplate exactions not tied to the specific project's impacts?
- Did you document procedural compliance (notice, impartiality, findings) at each step of adjudicative decisions?
- If challenged, would the regulation or decision withstand review on rational basis, exactions, due process, equal protection, and First Amendment/RLUIPA grounds?
