# Healthy Food Access Planning

Food-access planning is a field where the dominant framing — the "food desert," defined by distance to a supermarket — has shaped both the analysis and the interventions in ways that frequently miss the actual problem. The judgment problem is that food access is not solely a function of supermarket proximity; it is shaped by affordability, transportation, cultural fit, time, housing and kitchen conditions, and the economic structure of the food retail environment, and interventions calibrated only to the distance metric (attracting a supermarket to a defined area) often fail to change residents' diets or health because they did not address the binding constraints. A city can offer a grocery incentive, attract a store, declare the food desert solved, and find that diet-related health outcomes did not improve, because the residents who needed access could not afford the store, could not reach it, or shopped elsewhere for reasons the distance metric never captured. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a food-access strategy will improve residents' food security and health or merely satisfy a metric.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose the Actual Access Constraints Before Selecting Interventions

Before designing an intervention, diagnose what actually constrains food access for the target population. Distance to a supermarket is one constraint, but affordability (whether residents can buy the food the store sells), transportation (whether residents can reach the store without a car, especially for bulky groceries), cultural and dietary fit (whether the store stocks foods the population eats), time (whether working residents can shop during store hours), and housing conditions (whether residents have kitchens, refrigeration, and storage to prepare fresh food) are frequently the binding constraints, and each implies a different intervention. Conduct a multi-dimensional access analysis that includes price surveys, transit and walking access, store inventories, and resident-reported barriers, rather than relying on the distance-to-supermarket metric alone. An intervention that does not address the binding constraint will not change the outcome, regardless of how well it addresses a non-binding one.

### Evaluate the Full Range of Interventions Against the Diagnosed Constraint

Food-access interventions span grocery attraction and incentives (tax breaks, land assembly, loan funds), mobile markets and produce prescription programs, transit and delivery interventions (shuttle service to grocery, online ordering with SNAP), corner-store and small-retail healthy initiatives, farmers markets and double-up incentive programs, urban agriculture and community gardens, and food-pantry and food-bank capacity building. Each addresses a different constraint and carries different evidence of effectiveness, cost, and durability. Match the intervention to the diagnosed constraint: grocery attraction addresses distance but not affordability; incentive programs address affordability but not distance; transit interventions address distance for carless households; urban agriculture addresses access and community but not volume. Avoid the reflexive selection of the most visible intervention (attracting a supermarket) when the evidence and the diagnosis point elsewhere, and be honest that some interventions (urban agriculture, corner-store conversions) have thin evidence of diet and health impact despite their popularity.

### Design for the Economic Sustainability of the Intervention

Food retail is a low-margin business, and food-access interventions frequently fail because the store, market, or program they create is not economically sustainable without ongoing subsidy. A grocery store attracted with a five-year incentive closes in year six when the incentive ends; a mobile market dependent on grant funding stops when the grant ends; a corner-store healthy initiative reverts when the program officer leaves. Design interventions for economic sustainability from the outset: model the store's or program's revenue and cost structure, identify the customer base and its purchasing power, and confirm whether the intervention can sustain itself or requires permanent public support — and if permanent support is required, secure it before launching. Be honest about which interventions require ongoing subsidy (many do) and ensure the subsidy is funded, because launching an intervention that closes in three years harms the residents who came to depend on it and discredits the strategy.

### Measure Diet, Health, and Food-Security Outcomes, Not Just Retail Presence

The metric most food-access strategies track — presence of a grocery store, or counts of markets and gardens — is an input, not an outcome, and it can improve while the actual outcomes (diet quality, food security, diet-related health) do not. Commit to measuring the outcomes the strategy is meant to change: resident-reported food security (using validated instruments like the USDA module), diet quality indicators, and where feasible diet-related health measures, collected before and after the intervention for the target population. This discipline is demanding, because health outcomes change slowly and are influenced by many factors, but without it the strategy cannot distinguish interventions that work from those that do not, and it cannot defend its continuation or expansion against the inevitable question of whether it achieved anything. A strategy that tracks only retail presence is a strategy that cannot learn.

### Address Equity and the Structural Drivers of Food Insecurity

Food insecurity is not randomly distributed; it concentrates in low-income communities, communities of color, and neighborhoods shaped by disinvestment, redlining, and exclusionary retail patterns, and the structural drivers (poverty, housing cost burden, transportation access, wage levels) are more determinative of food security than retail proximity. A food-access strategy that addresses only retail presence without engaging the structural drivers will produce marginal change, because the residents it serves remain food-insecure due to affordability and income constraints they cannot shop their way out of. Coordinate the food strategy with the broader anti-poverty, housing, transportation, and economic-development strategies, and be honest with policymakers that food-access planning alone cannot solve food insecurity, because food insecurity is largely an income problem manifesting as a food problem. Overpromising what food-access interventions can achieve sets up the strategy for the disappointment that follows when health outcomes do not improve.

### Engage Residents on Their Actual Food Environment and Preferences

Food-access interventions are frequently designed without genuine engagement of the residents they are meant to serve, on the assumption that "access to healthy food" is a universally desired and understood good. Residents, however, have specific food traditions, shopping patterns, price sensitivities, and trust relationships with existing retailers (including small grocers, ethnic markets, and informal sources) that an externally designed intervention may ignore or displace. Engage residents on their actual food environment — where they shop, what they buy, what they would buy if it were available and affordable, what they distrust about proposed interventions — and design the strategy to fit the population rather than to impose a generic healthy-food model. A grocery store that does not stock the foods the population eats, or that displaces a trusted ethnic market, will not improve diets regardless of its proximity.

## Common Traps

### The Supermarket Incentive That Solves the Metric but Not the Problem

The city offers a tax abatement and land assembly to attract a supermarket to a designated food desert, the store opens, the distance metric is satisfied, and the food desert is declared solved — but the residents who needed access cannot afford the store, shop there only marginally, and continue to rely on the food sources they used before. The trap mechanism is that the intervention addressed the distance constraint, which was not the binding constraint, and the affordability, transportation, and cultural-fit constraints remain. The false signal is a new supermarket and a solved metric. The harm is that public resources were spent on an intervention that did not change diets or health, the residents' food security is unchanged, and the strategy is credited with success it did not achieve — foreclosing the more effective interventions that the diagnosis would have supported.

### Input Metrics Mistaken for Outcomes

The strategy reports the number of grocery stores, markets, gardens, and food programs as its achievements, and the metrics rise steadily, while diet quality, food security, and health outcomes for the target population do not improve. The trap mechanism is that input metrics are easy to count and politically satisfying, while outcome metrics are slow and confounded, and the gap between the two is invisible unless both are measured. The false signal is a growing list of programs and retail. The harm is that the strategy optimizes for the inputs (more stores, more programs) rather than for the outcomes (better diets, less insecurity), resources are spent on interventions that do not work, and the strategy cannot distinguish success from failure because it never measured what mattered.

### The Unsustainable Intervention

The mobile market, the corner-store conversion, the farmers-market incentive, or the produce-prescription program launches with grant funding, serves residents who come to depend on it, and then closes when the grant ends, because its economics never supported operation without subsidy. The trap mechanism is that food-access interventions are often funded by time-limited grants evaluated on launch rather than sustainability, and the operating economics (low margins, dispersed demand, high labor) are not viable without ongoing support. The false signal is a successful launch and early service. The harm is that residents who incorporated the intervention into their food strategy lose it, trust in future interventions erodes, and the underlying access problem returns worse, because the intervention displaced or did not develop alternatives.

### Imposing a Generic Healthy-Food Model

The intervention brings a conventional supermarket, a mainstream farmers market, or a standard healthy-corner-store model to a community whose food traditions, shopping patterns, and preferences differ from the generic model, and the residents do not use it because it does not fit their food culture. The trap mechanism is that "healthy food access" is treated as a universal concept rather than as a population-specific one, and the intervention's design reflects the planner's or funder's food culture rather than the residents'. The false signal is a store or market that meets the planner's healthy-food standard. The harm is an underused intervention that did not improve diets, the potential displacement of trusted existing food sources, and the reinforcement of a narrative that the residents "don't want" healthy food, when the actual issue is that the intervention did not fit.

## Self-Check

- Did I diagnose the actual access constraints (affordability, transportation, cultural fit, time, housing) through multi-dimensional analysis, not just distance to a supermarket?
- Is the selected intervention matched to the diagnosed binding constraint, with honest acknowledgment of the evidence base for its effectiveness?
- Has the economic sustainability of the intervention been modeled, with ongoing subsidy secured if required, rather than assumed from a launch evaluation?
- Am I measuring food security, diet quality, and (where feasible) health outcomes for the target population, not just retail presence and program counts?
- Is the food strategy coordinated with the broader anti-poverty, housing, transportation, and economic-development strategies that address the structural drivers of food insecurity?
- Did genuine resident engagement shape the intervention's design, including food traditions, shopping patterns, and existing trusted food sources?
- If the headline metric (stores, markets, programs) improved but food security did not, would the strategy detect the gap and adjust — or would it declare success on the input?
- Have I been honest with policymakers about what food-access planning can and cannot achieve, given that food insecurity is largely an income problem?
