# Health Impact Assessment

A Health Impact Assessment (HIA) is a structured process for evaluating the potential health effects of a proposed policy, plan, or project on a population, particularly on vulnerable groups, and for translating that analysis into recommendations for decision-makers. The judgment problem is that HIAs occupy an awkward position: they are advisory, non-regulatory, and often conducted outside the formal decision process, which means their influence depends entirely on their timing, rigor, and the political context — and a poorly timed, methodologically thin, or procedurally disconnected HIA can consume substantial resources and change nothing. Worse, an HIA can become a legitimating exercise, lending the appearance of health consideration to a decision that was already made, while its recommendations are ignored. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether an HIA will genuinely inform a decision or become an expensive gesture.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether an HIA Is the Right Tool for This Decision

Before initiating an HIA, assess whether it is the appropriate instrument for the decision at hand. An HIA is most valuable when the decision has significant but non-obvious health implications, when health is not already represented in the formal review (as it would be in an environmental impact assessment with a health section), when there is a genuine opportunity to influence the decision (the decision is not yet final, the decision-makers are receptive), and when the resources exist to do it credibly. An HIA is the wrong tool when the decision is already made, when the health effects are obvious and better addressed through direct advocacy, when the timeline is too short for credible analysis, or when the formal review process already requires health analysis. Be honest about this assessment, because a token HIA conducted to check a box harms the credibility of the method and consumes resources that could have gone to direct influence.

### Scope to the Decision-Relevant Health Determinants and Populations

Scoping is where an HIA is won or lost. A scope that is too broad attempts to analyze every possible health effect and produces a shallow, unpersuasive document; a scope that is too narrow misses the effects that matter. Scope to the health determinants the decision plausibly affects (air quality, physical activity, safety, access to services, noise, stress, displacement, economic opportunity), the pathways by which the decision affects them, and the populations affected differentially (children, elderly, low-income, chronically ill, racial and ethnic groups, residents of affected neighborhoods). Engage stakeholders — especially affected residents — in scoping, because they identify effects and populations the analyst would miss, and their involvement builds the legitimacy the HIA will need to be heard. Document the scoping rationale and the determinants excluded, so that the scope is defensible and the boundaries clear.

### Ground Findings in the Best Available Evidence and Be Honest About Uncertainty

HIA findings should rest on the best available evidence — peer-reviewed literature, local data, quantitative analysis where feasible, and structured expert and stakeholder input — and should be honest about the strength of that evidence and the uncertainty in the projections. Avoid the two opposite failures: presenting speculative effects as certain (which overstates the analysis and invites dismissal), and presenting only effects with strong evidence (which omits the real but harder-to-quantify effects, like stress, social cohesion, and displacement, that often dominate residents' concerns). Characterize findings with calibrated confidence (well-established, probable, possible, uncertain), cite the evidence base, and distinguish what the analysis shows from what it infers. An HIA that overstates its certainty will be demolished by a critic; one that is honest about uncertainty retains credibility and is harder to dismiss.

### Develop Actionable, Decision-Specific Recommendations

The most common reason an HIA fails to influence a decision is that its recommendations are vague, generic, or disconnected from the decision's actual levers. "Promote public health" is not a recommendation; "Require the project to include a sealed pedestrian connection to the transit station, funded through the development agreement, as a condition of approval" is. Develop recommendations that are specific to the decision's actual instruments (conditions of approval, plan policies, code amendments, mitigation requirements, design changes), that name the responsible party and the mechanism, and that are feasible within the decision's scope. Prioritize recommendations by health impact and feasibility, and engage the decision-makers in their development where possible, because recommendations developed with the people who must implement them are far more likely to be adopted than recommendations handed to them after the fact.

### Time the HIA to the Decision-Making Window

An HIA's influence depends on its timing: it must be completed while the decision is still open and while the decision-makers can still incorporate its findings. An HIA completed after the decision is final is an academic exercise; an HIA completed after the political alignment is set may be formally received but substantively ignored. Map the decision timeline (hearings, votes, comment periods, negotiations) and sequence the HIA's scoping, analysis, and reporting to deliver findings at the point of maximum influence — typically before the key hearing or vote, with enough lead time for decision-makers to absorb and for recommendations to be incorporated. If the decision timeline does not allow a credible HIA, advocate for a delay or a phased approach rather than producing a rushed analysis that lacks credibility, because a discredited HIA harms the broader cause of health-informed decision-making.

### Build the Stakeholder and Implementation Architecture Into the Process

An HIA's influence extends beyond its report through the stakeholders who carry its findings into the decision process. Build a stakeholder engagement plan that involves affected residents, public health agencies, community organizations, and the decision-makers themselves throughout the HIA, so that the findings are understood, owned, and advocated for by the people who will use them. Develop an implementation and monitoring plan that tracks whether the recommendations are adopted and whether the projected health effects materialize, because HIAs that include follow-up are taken more seriously by decision-makers (who know their actions will be reviewed) and contribute to the evidence base for future HIAs. An HIA delivered as a report to a passive audience is far less influential than an HIA delivered through an organized stakeholder network with a monitoring commitment.

## Common Traps

### The HIA as a Legitimating Exercise

The HIA is commissioned or conducted to demonstrate that health was "considered," but the decision has already been made or the decision-makers are not genuinely open to changing course, and the HIA's recommendations are received and ignored. The trap mechanism is that the HIA process lends the appearance of health-informed decision-making while the decision proceeds as planned, and the resources spent on the HIA are effectively spent on legitimation rather than influence. The false signal is a completed HIA and a formal response. The harm is that the affected residents' health concerns are documented and then dismissed, the HIA method is associated with tokenism, and future HIAs in the jurisdiction are harder to take seriously — while the health effects the HIA identified go unaddressed.

### Vague Recommendations Disconnected From the Decision

The HIA produces a list of recommendations — "promote active transportation," "minimize displacement," "protect air quality" — that are laudable but generic, that do not map to the decision's actual levers, and that the decision-makers can acknowledge without acting on. The trap mechanism is that generic recommendations are easy to generate and easy to ignore, and they allow the HIA to appear responsive while giving the decision-maker no specific obligation. The false signal is a thorough-looking recommendations section. The harm is that the HIA does not change the decision, the health effects it identified go unmitigated, and the HIA is filed as a completed process rather than an influential one.

### Overstating Certainty to Strengthen the Case

Under pressure to be persuasive, the HIA presents speculative or weakly supported effects as certain, projecting precise numbers from thin evidence, and a critic demolishes the overstated findings, discrediting the entire analysis. The trap mechanism is that the desire to influence creates an incentive to overstate, and overstatement is effective until it is challenged, at which point the loss of credibility extends to the well-supported findings. The false signal is a confident, quantified analysis. The harm is that the HIA is dismissed wholesale, the defensible findings are lost with the indefensible ones, and the decision proceeds without the health analysis that an honestly calibrated HIA could have provided.

### The Wrong Scope — Too Broad or Too Narrow

The HIA attempts to analyze every conceivable health effect (too broad) and produces a shallow document that persuades no one, or it analyzes only the effects with strong evidence (too narrow) and omits the stress, displacement, and community effects that are the residents' primary concerns. The trap mechanism is that scoping is a judgment call with no formula, and both errors — breadth at the expense of depth, and narrowness at the expense of relevance — produce HIAs that fail to influence. The false signal is a comprehensive-looking or rigorous-looking scope. The harm is an HIA that either cannot defend its shallow findings or that misses the effects that mattered most to the affected population, and in either case a document that does not change the decision.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess, before initiating, whether an HIA is the right tool — with a genuine opportunity to influence and resources to do it credibly — rather than defaulting to the method?
- Is the scope focused on the decision-relevant health determinants and the differentially affected populations, with stakeholder input and a documented rationale for what was excluded?
- Are findings grounded in the best available evidence, with calibrated confidence, honest characterization of uncertainty, and distinction between established and inferred effects?
- Are recommendations specific to the decision's actual instruments (conditions, policies, code amendments, mitigation), naming responsible parties and mechanisms, and prioritized by impact and feasibility?
- Is the HIA timed to deliver findings within the decision-making window, with enough lead time for decision-makers to absorb and act — not after the decision is effectively final?
- Is there a stakeholder engagement plan involving affected residents, public health, and decision-makers throughout, so findings are understood and advocated for?
- Is there an implementation and monitoring plan to track adoption of recommendations and the projected health effects, contributing to follow-up accountability?
- If the decision-makers received the HIA formally but acted contrary to its findings, would the affected community have the organizing capacity to hold them accountable — or was the HIA a one-time document?
