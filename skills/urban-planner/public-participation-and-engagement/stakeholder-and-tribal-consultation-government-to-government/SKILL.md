# Stakeholder and Tribal Consultation, Government-to-Government

Consultation is a category of engagement fundamentally different from general public participation. Where public participation invites input from whoever chooses to attend, consultation is a directed, often legally required, government-to-government or government-to-stakeholder relationship with entities that hold specific legal standing, jurisdictional authority, or substantive expertise — tribal nations, adjacent jurisdictions, state and federal agencies, special districts, and formally recognized stakeholder organizations. The judgment problem is that consultation is frequently treated as a procedural step (send the notice, log the response, check the box) when it is in fact a substantive obligation whose quality determines whether a project can proceed, whether cultural resources are protected, whether permits are issued on schedule, and whether the lead agency has satisfied duties that cannot be cured after the fact. A planner who confuses consultation with notification — who sends a form letter where a government-to-government dialogue was required — can invalidate a decision, trigger litigation, cause irreparable harm to cultural resources, and damage relationships that take decades to rebuild. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether consultation satisfies its legal and substantive purpose or merely documents its failure.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Consultation from Notification and Apply the Right Standard

The most common and most damaging error is to treat consultation as if it were notification — sending a notice, waiting for a response, and treating silence as assent. The rule is to distinguish the two and apply the correct standard to each relationship. Notification is one-way: the lead agency informs an entity of a pending action and invites comment, and the entity's failure to respond generally has no legal consequence. Consultation is two-way and relational: the lead agency engages the entity in a substantive exchange, with the obligation to seek the entity's input, consider it seriously, and respond to it, and in some frameworks (notably tribal consultation under Section 106 and many state laws) with the obligation to continue the dialogue toward a defined outcome. The trap is that notification is cheaper and faster, so there is constant pressure to characterize a consultation obligation as a notification, but the legal frameworks that require consultation do so precisely because notification was deemed insufficient. The rule is to identify, for each entity, whether the governing framework requires notification or consultation, and to perform the one that is actually required, documenting the substantive exchange rather than the mailing.

### Conduct Tribal Consultation as Government-to-Government, Not as Public Outreach

Tribal nations are sovereign governments, and consultation with them is a government-to-government relationship governed by specific legal frameworks — the federal trust responsibility, Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act (which requires consultation with tribes that attach religious and cultural significance to historic properties in the area of potential effect), the National Environmental Policy Act, executive orders on tribal consultation, and state-specific cultural-resource and consultation statutes. The rule is that tribal consultation is not a subset of public participation and cannot be discharged by inviting tribal members to a public meeting or posting a notice. It requires direct, early, and government-to-government contact with the tribal nation's designated consultation office; respect for the tribe's protocols and timelines (which may not match the project schedule); recognition that tribes may identify cultural resources and sacred sites whose locations are confidential and cannot be disclosed in public documents; and a good-faith effort to resolve adverse effects through avoidance, minimization, and mitigation, with the tribe's input given the weight the legal framework requires. The trap of treating tribal consultation as a procedural notice is not only a legal failure; it is a substantive failure that can lead to irreparable destruction of cultural resources and to project-stopping litigation or political conflict.

### Start Consultation Early Enough to Shape the Outcome

The timing of consultation determines whether it can influence the decision. The rule is to initiate consultation at the earliest stage at which the lead agency has enough information to consult meaningfully — typically during scoping, before alternatives are foreclosed and before the environmental review or design is advanced. Consultation initiated after the preferred alternative is selected, the design is fixed, or the environmental document is drafted is structurally hollow: the consulted entity is presented with a decision already made and invited to comment on the margins, which satisfies the form of consultation while defeating its purpose. The harm is compounded because the consulted entity often recognizes the hollow consultation, refuses to participate or files objections, and the lead agency has a record that shows consultation occurred but no substantive input that could improve the project. The rule is to build consultation into the project schedule from the beginning, with milestones that allow the input to alter the alternatives, the design, and the impact analysis, not merely the mitigation.

### Coordinate Across Agencies to Identify Permitting and Review Dependencies

Most significant projects require permits, approvals, or concurrences from multiple agencies — federal (Section 404 wetlands, Section 106 cultural resources, NEPA lead and cooperating agencies), state (environmental review, water quality, transportation), regional (MPOs, special districts), and local — and each has its own consultation requirements, review timelines, and decision criteria. The rule is to map the full universe of required consultations and permits at the outset, identify the dependencies among them (one agency's concurrence may be a prerequisite for another's permit; a Section 106 finding may be required before a Section 404 permit can be issued), and sequence the consultations to satisfy the dependencies without creating schedule-destroying delays. The trap is to discover a required consultation late in the process, when its outcome can delay or block a project that was otherwise ready to proceed. Early and comprehensive intergovernmental coordination — including inviting qualifying agencies to be cooperating agencies under NEPA, which integrates their input into a single review — is how a project avoids the serial-delay failure mode.

### Document the Consultation Record to Withstand Challenge

Because consultation obligations are legal duties, the consultation record is the evidence that the duty was satisfied, and it is the first thing a challenger or a reviewing court examines. The rule is to document not only that consultation occurred but what was consulted about, what input was received, how the lead agency considered and responded to that input, and how the input influenced (or did not influence) the decision. The record should include the consultation invitations and responses, meeting notes and summaries (shared with the consulted entity for accuracy), the substantive issues raised, the lead agency's analysis of those issues, and the resolution or non-resolution of each. A record that shows notices were sent but does not show substantive exchange, consideration, and response will not withstand a claim that consultation was procedural rather than substantive. The documentation discipline applies throughout the process, not as a final write-up, because the substance and the record are built together.

## Common Traps

### Discharging Tribal Consultation Through Public Notice

The lead agency posts a public notice and invites tribal input through the general public participation process, treating the notice as satisfying the tribal consultation obligation. The trap mechanism is that tribal consultation is a government-to-government duty that requires direct contact with the tribal nation's consultation office and substantive dialogue, and a public notice to individual tribal members neither satisfies the legal framework nor respects the sovereign relationship. The false signal is that the tribe "had the opportunity to comment" through the public process. The harm is that the consultation obligation is legally unsatisfied, the cultural resources the consultation was meant to identify may be damaged or destroyed before the tribe is meaningfully engaged, and the project faces litigation, permit denial, or political crisis when the failure surfaces — by which point the cultural harm may be irreparable. The corrective is to conduct direct, early, government-to-government consultation with the tribal nation's designated office, under the tribe's protocols.

### Consulting After the Decision Is Effectively Made

The lead agency consults the relevant agencies and tribes, but only after the preferred alternative is selected, the design is fixed, and the environmental document is in draft, so the consultation can influence only mitigation. The trap mechanism is that consultation that occurs after the formative decisions cannot shape those decisions, and the consulted entities recognize the hollow process, refuse to participate, or file objections that the lead agency then has to address under time pressure. The false signal is that consultation "occurred" and is documented. The harm is that the consultation record is vulnerable to challenge (the entities will testify that their input could not influence the outcome), the project bears avoidable impacts that early consultation would have prevented, and the relationships are damaged for the next project. The corrective is to initiate consultation at scoping, before alternatives are foreclosed.

### Treating Agency Coordination as a Permitting Checklist

The project team identifies the required permits and pursues each in sequence, treating each agency as a checkbox, without mapping the dependencies or inviting early coordination. The trap mechanism is that the agencies' review criteria interact — a wetlands permit may depend on a cultural-resources finding, a transportation concurrence may depend on an environmental document — and pursuing them in isolation creates serial delays, conflicting conditions, and late discoveries that one agency's input should have shaped another's review. The false signal is that all the permits are "in process." The harm is schedule slippage, rework, and sometimes project-killing conflicts between agency conditions that early coordination would have resolved. The corrective is to map the full consultation and permit universe at the outset, identify dependencies, and use tools like cooperating-agency status to integrate the reviews.

### Logging Silence as Concurrence

The lead agency sends consultation invitations, some entities do not respond, and the project proceeds on the assumption that silence equals no objection. The trap mechanism is that silence has many causes — the entity lacked capacity to respond in the timeframe, the notice went to the wrong office, the entity deferred expecting a later opportunity, or the entity's objection was never formally recorded — and none of them is legally equivalent to concurrence. The false signal is the absence of objection in the file. The harm is that the entity later objects (or a third party raises the entity's non-participation), the lead agency's claim of consultation is undermined, and the project faces delay or challenge from a failure that a follow-up contact would have cured. The corrective is to follow up on non-responses, document the follow-up, and where an entity remains non-responsive, document the good-faith effort rather than treating silence as assent.

### Disclosing Confidential Cultural Resource Information

The consultation identifies a sacred site or traditional cultural property, and the lead agency includes its location and description in the public environmental document to demonstrate that impacts were considered. The trap mechanism is that the legal frameworks for cultural resources (Section 304 of the National Historic Preservation Act and analogous provisions) recognize that disclosure of certain cultural information causes harm — by enabling looting, vandalism, or unwanted visitation — and provide for confidentiality, and public disclosure defeats the very protection the consultation was meant to provide. The false signal is that the agency was being transparent. The harm is that the disclosure itself becomes an adverse effect on the resource, the tribe's trust in the process is destroyed, and the agency has violated the confidentiality the framework requires. The corrective is to handle confidential cultural information under the applicable confidentiality provisions, consulting with the tribe on what may and may not be disclosed, and withholding sensitive locational information from public documents.

## Self-Check

- For each consulted entity, has the governing framework been identified as requiring notification or consultation, and has the correct standard (substantive consultation, not just notice) been applied where consultation is required?
- Is tribal consultation being conducted government-to-government with the tribal nation's designated consultation office, under the tribe's protocols, rather than through general public notice to tribal members?
- Was consultation initiated at scoping, before alternatives were foreclosed and the design fixed, so that the input can shape the decision rather than only the mitigation?
- Has the full universe of required agency consultations and permits been mapped, with dependencies identified and sequenced, and cooperating-agency status offered where appropriate?
- Does the consultation record document the substantive exchange — issues raised, lead-agency analysis, and how input influenced or did not influence the decision — not merely that notices were sent?
- Have non-responsive entities been followed up with, with the good-faith effort documented, rather than silence logged as concurrence?
- Has confidential cultural resource information been handled under the applicable confidentiality provisions, in consultation with the tribe regarding what may be disclosed?
- Where consultation identified adverse effects, has the lead agency pursued avoidance, minimization, and mitigation in that order, with the consulted entity's input given the weight the framework requires?
- Have the consultation timelines been aligned with the consulted entities' actual capacity and protocols, rather than imposed by the project schedule?
- If the decision is challenged, does the record demonstrate a good-faith substantive consultation, or only that the procedural notices were sent?
