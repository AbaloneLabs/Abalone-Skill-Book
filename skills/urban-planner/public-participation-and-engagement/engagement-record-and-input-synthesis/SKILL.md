# Engagement Record and Input Synthesis

The engagement record is the artifact that makes a plan defensible — to decision-makers, to courts, to funders, and to the public itself. The judgment problem is not how to "summarize comments" but how to convert a messy, partial, emotionally charged body of input into a structured record that honestly represents what was heard, transparently shows how it influenced the plan, and survives scrutiny from people who disagreed with the outcome. Planners commonly fail here by cherry-picking supportive comments, by collapsing diverse input into vague themes that cannot be traced to specific changes, or by writing a summary that reads as advocacy rather than documentation. A rigorous synthesis protects the plan, the agency, and the legitimacy of the engagement itself.

## Core Rules

### Treat the Record as Evidence, Not Narrative

Every comment, survey response, meeting note, and email is part of an evidentiary record that may be reviewed by a city council, a hearing examiner, a court, or an auditor. Organize and retain input so it can be traced: who said what, through which channel, on what date, and in response to which question or proposal. Keep raw data (individual comments, sign-in sheets, survey exports) alongside the synthesis, not just the polished summary. A record that cannot be traced back to source material is not a record; it is a claim about a record, and it will not hold up under challenge. Plan the documentation system before engagement begins, not after.

### Quantify Where You Can, Qualify Where You Cannot

Not all input is countable, but where it is, count it honestly. Report the number of unique commenters (not duplicate comments), the distribution of positions on key questions, and the demographic and geographic breakdown of who participated. Where input is qualitative — focus groups, open-ended testimony, workshop discussions — describe the themes, the range of views, and the intensity of feeling, without inflating a few voices into a majority. Be explicit about the limits of the data: a comment count is not a poll, a meeting is not a referendum, and strong turnout from one neighborhood does not equal citywide consensus. State the methodological limits in the report itself.

### Make the Influence Transparent and Specific

The most important and most often botched part of synthesis is showing how input changed the plan. For each major decision, document what the original proposal was, what concerns were raised, and what changed as a result — with specific references. "Public input was considered" is not documentation; "In response to 47 comments about pedestrian safety at the Elm/Maple intersection, the plan adds a protected crossing and reduces the corner radius from 15 to 5 feet" is documentation. Where input did not change the plan, explain why: conflicting with adopted policy, infeasible, outside scope, or outweighed by other evidence. Honesty about what did not change is as important as celebrating what did, because it shows the synthesis was genuine rather than decorative.

### Represent Dissent and Conflict Honestly

A synthesis that papers over disagreement reads as propaganda and destroys credibility the moment a council member or reporter compares it to the raw record. When input was divided, say so: "Opinion was split, with long-term residents predominantly opposed and newer residents and renters predominantly in favor." Name the factions, the stakes, and the tradeoffs the plan is making between them. A plan that has to balance competing legitimate interests is more defensible than one that pretends consensus existed. The record should help decision-makers see the real shape of the conflict, not hide it.

### Connect Input to the People Most Affected

Aggregate comment counts can obscure who is speaking for whom. A hundred comments opposing a housing project from people who live outside the affected neighborhood may carry different weight than twenty comments from residents who would be displaced. In synthesis, distinguish between input from those most affected by the decision and input from those with general interest, and report both. This is not about discounting outside voices — they may have valid points — but about giving decision-makers an accurate picture of whose interests are at stake and who is advocating for them.

### Build the Synthesis Iteratively with the Plan

Synthesis is not a final-week task; it must run alongside plan development so that input can actually shape decisions before they lock. Schedule synthesis checkpoints after each engagement round, feed findings to the plan team in real time, and adjust engagement if the input reveals blind spots. A synthesis written after the plan is essentially complete cannot influence it and is, at best, a record of consultation; a synthesis woven into the process is what makes engagement genuinely substantive.

## Common Traps

### Cherry-Picking Supportive Comments

When the plan team has a preferred direction, there is strong temptation to foreground comments that support it and bury those that oppose it. The false signal is a clean, on-message summary; the harm is that the record is dishonest and collapses the moment anyone checks the raw data, which undermines the entire plan's legitimacy. Summarize the full distribution of views, including opposition, and let the plan's rationale carry the argument for why certain input was weighted as it was.

### Counting Comments as Votes

Treating each comment as a vote — and reporting that "73% of comments opposed" — implies a representativeness that self-selected input does not have. The false signal is a precise-sounding percentage; the harm is that decision-makers treat it as a poll result and either over-defer to the majority or dismiss the whole exercise as unrepresentative. Report counts and distributions, but frame them clearly as the views of those who chose to participate, not a sample of the population, and supplement with demographic context about who that was.

### Collapsing Diversity into Vague Themes

Summarizing hundreds of comments as "residents are concerned about traffic and density" loses the specificity that makes input actionable and verifiable. The false signal is a tidy themes list; the harm is that the synthesis cannot be traced back to concrete concerns, so neither the plan team nor a reviewer can tell what was actually said or whether it was addressed. Preserve specificity: which intersections, which impacts, which populations, which alternatives were proposed. Quote representative comments directly so the texture survives.

### Writing the Summary as Advocacy

A synthesis written to persuade rather than document — emphasizing supportive input, minimizing opposition, using loaded framing — reads as a brief for a predetermined outcome. The false signal is a confident, well-argued report; the harm is that it destroys the agency's credibility as a neutral convener and hands opponents evidence of bad faith. Write in a documentary register: report what was heard, including what was heard that you wish had not been, and let the plan's merits make the case.

### Failing to Document What Did Not Change and Why

When input is ignored, the absence of explanation is conspicuous and reads as dismissal. The false signal is a report full of "input shaped the plan" with no acknowledgment of the input that did not; the harm is that participants whose ideas were rejected conclude they were not heard, and future engagement starts from a deeper trust deficit. Explicitly catalog the major pieces of input that did not change the plan and the reasons, so the record shows genuine consideration rather than selective adoption.

## Self-Check

- Can every claim in your synthesis be traced back to specific source material — individual comments, survey data, meeting notes — that is retained and retrievable?
- Did you report the number of unique commenters, the distribution of positions, and the demographic and geographic breakdown of who participated, with explicit methodological caveats?
- For each major plan decision, did you document what the original proposal was, what input was received, what changed as a result, and — where nothing changed — why?
- Did you represent dissent and divided opinion honestly, naming the factions and tradeoffs rather than implying consensus?
- Did you distinguish input from those most affected by the decision versus general interest, and report both separately?
- Did you avoid treating comment counts as votes or poll results, and frame participation-based findings with appropriate caveats?
- Did you preserve specificity (which locations, which impacts, which populations) rather than collapsing input into vague themes?
- Is the synthesis written in a documentary register rather than an advocacy one, including input that was inconvenient for the plan's direction?
- Did you run synthesis iteratively alongside plan development, so input could shape decisions before they locked, rather than only at the end?
- Could a skeptical reviewer compare your synthesis to the raw record and find it honest, complete, and traceable — even if they disagreed with the plan's conclusions?
