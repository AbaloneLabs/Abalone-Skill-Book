# Water, Sewer, and Utility Capacity Planning

Water, sewer, stormwater, and utility capacity are the physical determinants of where development can occur and at what intensity, and the infrastructure that provides them is the most expensive and most long-lived public investment a jurisdiction makes. The judgment problem is that infrastructure decisions are made by engineers focused on hydraulic and fiscal criteria, while their land-use consequences — where growth is enabled, where it is constrained, what pattern it takes — are often unexamined. A sewer line extended to the periphery enables sprawl that contradicts the comprehensive plan; a capacity constraint in an urban core where growth is desired stifles the very development the plan seeks. The planner's role is to ensure that infrastructure decisions serve the land-use strategy, not just the engineering and fiscal logic, and to recognize that the location and sizing of infrastructure is itself a growth-management decision of the first order.

## Core Rules

### Align Infrastructure Capacity and Extension With the Growth Strategy

Infrastructure extension is the most powerful growth-management tool available, because development follows water and sewer lines. The rule is to align infrastructure investment with the comprehensive plan's growth strategy: extend and upsize capacity where growth is desired (infill, transit corridors, designated growth areas), and constrain or withhold extension where growth is not desired (rural edges, conservation areas, agricultural land). This requires the planner to engage the utility and public works departments whose capital plans are often developed on engineering and fiscal criteria without reference to the land-use plan, and to reconcile the two so that the infrastructure serves the strategy. A jurisdiction whose plan directs growth inward but whose utility extensions reach outward is contradicting its own strategy with its own spending, and the plan will fail.

### Use Service Areas and Boundaries as Growth-Management Tools

The delineation of service areas — where urban services (water, sewer, urban roads) are provided and where they are not — is a powerful growth-management tool, because development at urban density requires urban services, and withholding those services constrains development to rural densities. The rule is to use service area boundaries deliberately, drawing them to encompass the designated growth area and to exclude the rural and conservation edges, and to enforce the boundary through utility policy (no extensions beyond the boundary) and through zoning (rural densities outside it). Urban growth boundaries, sewer service area boundaries, and similar tools, where authorized, allow the jurisdiction to concentrate growth where it can be efficiently served and to protect the areas it wants to conserve. A service area drawn without reference to the growth strategy, or eroded by ad hoc extensions, defeats the tool.

### Apply Concurrency and Adequate-Public-Facilities Requirements Honestly

Concurrency (or adequate public facilities) requirements mandate that infrastructure capacity be in place when development impacts occur, and they are a legitimate tool to prevent growth from degrading service. The rule is to apply concurrency honestly: measure capacity accurately, define the level of service that must be maintained, and deny or delay development that would exceed it — including in growth areas, where the requirement forces the infrastructure to be provided rather than allowing growth to degrade service. The trap is that concurrency is often applied selectively: enforced to slow growth in some areas, waived to allow growth in others, undermining its legitimacy and its analytical basis. Honest application means the same standard everywhere, with the growth strategy pursued through infrastructure provision (building the capacity) rather than through concurrency waivers (allowing the degradation).

### Calibrate Impact Fees to the Proportionate Share of Growth's Cost

Impact fees (or development impact fees) charge new development for its proportionate share of the infrastructure capacity it requires, and they are an essential tool for ensuring that growth pays its cost rather than burdening existing ratepayers. The rule is to calibrate impact fees through a defensible methodology (an infrastructure needs assessment tied to growth projections, with the cost allocated by land use type and demand unit), to charge the proportionate share required by state enabling law (often strictly limited), and to spend the fees on the capacity the growth requires, not on unrelated needs. Impact fees that are too low shift growth's cost to existing residents and ratepayers; fees that exceed the proportionate share are vulnerable to legal challenge. The methodology should be transparent and updated periodically, because outdated fees either undercharge (shifting cost) or overcharge (constraining feasible development).

### Plan Infrastructure for Resilience and Long-Term Conditions

Infrastructure is designed for decades of service, and the conditions it will face over its design life — population growth, climate change, regulatory standards, technological change — differ from the conditions at construction. The rule is to plan infrastructure for the projected conditions over its design life, not for the historical baselines or the current population: size water and sewer capacity for the growth it will serve (with phasing to avoid overbuilding), design stormwater for the intensified precipitation climate change brings, plan for the regulatory standards that will tighten, and consider the resilience of the system to disruption (single points of failure, dependence on vulnerable inputs). Infrastructure built to current conditions in a changing context will be prematurely inadequate, requiring expensive retrofit or degrading service; infrastructure built for the projected conditions serves its full design life.

## Common Traps

### Extending Infrastructure Without Land-Use Alignment

A sewer or water line is extended to serve a specific development or to address a capacity issue, with no analysis of the growth the extension will induce along its route, and sprawl results along the line, contradicting the growth strategy. The trap is that infrastructure creates its own demand. The mechanism is that the line makes previously unservable land developable, and the market responds. The false signal is that "we addressed the capacity need." The harm is that growth is enabled where the plan sought to prevent it, the pattern contradicts the strategy, and the public investment in the line subsidizes the unwanted development.

### Concurrency Applied Selectively

Concurrency is enforced to block development in areas where growth is opposed, but waived or relaxed to allow development in areas where it is desired, undermining the standard's legitimacy. The trap is that the analytical tool becomes a political instrument. The mechanism is that the level of service is treated as flexible when convenient. The false signal is that "we're managing growth." The harm is that the standard is discredited, growth degrades service where waivers were granted, and the selective application invites legal challenge for inconsistency.

### Impact Fees Set Below Proportionate Cost

Impact fees are kept low to encourage development or to avoid political conflict, and the gap between the fees collected and the infrastructure cost growth requires is shifted to existing ratepayers or to general funds. The trap is that growth is subsidized by the existing community. The mechanism is that the fee is set by political comfort rather than by the cost methodology. The false signal is that "we're keeping housing affordable" (the fee savings rarely reach the homebuyer). The harm is that existing residents subsidize new development, the infrastructure is underfunded and service degrades, and the fiscal strain accumulates until a rate increase or a service cut is required.

### Sizing Capacity to Current Rather Than Projected Demand

A water or sewer line is sized for current demand plus a modest margin, and within a few years growth exceeds the capacity, requiring an expensive upsizing or a parallel line. The trap is that the infrastructure is undersized for its design life. The mechanism is that the sizing analysis used current rather than projected demand, or that the projection was conservative to limit upfront cost. The false signal is that "we provided the capacity." The harm is that the capacity is inadequate sooner than planned, the upsizing is more expensive than initial over-sizing would have been, and development is constrained or service degrades in the interim.

### Planning Infrastructure in Silos Without System Integration

Water, sewer, stormwater, transportation, and other infrastructure are planned by separate departments on separate cycles, and the systems are not integrated, producing inefficiency, conflict, and missed opportunities (a road rebuilt without coordinating the sewer replacement beneath it, a stormwater system that ignores the water supply implications). The trap is that each system is optimized in isolation. The mechanism is that the providers plan in silos. The false signal is that "each system is well-planned." The harm is that the integrated cost is higher, opportunities for coordination are missed, and the systems may conflict (stormwater overwhelming sewer capacity, road construction disrupting water mains). The remedy is a coordinated capital planning process that reconciles the systems.

## Self-Check

- Is infrastructure capacity and extension aligned with the comprehensive plan's growth strategy, with capacity directed to growth areas and withheld from conservation areas?
- Are service area boundaries drawn deliberately to manage growth, and are they enforced through utility policy and zoning rather than eroded by ad hoc extensions?
- Is concurrency applied consistently and honestly, with the same level-of-service standard everywhere, and with growth pursued through capacity provision rather than waivers?
- Are impact fees calibrated through a defensible, transparent methodology to the proportionate share of growth's cost, updated periodically?
- Is infrastructure planned for projected conditions over its design life (growth, climate, regulation), not for current or historical baselines?
- Have I avoided extending infrastructure without analyzing the growth it will induce and its consistency with the land-use strategy?
- Is concurrency a consistent analytical standard, or a political instrument applied selectively?
- Do impact fees recover growth's proportionate cost, or is the gap shifted to existing ratepayers?
- Is capacity sized for the demand it will serve over its design life, or will it be inadequate before the system is retired?
- Are the infrastructure systems (water, sewer, stormwater, transportation) planned in coordination, or in silos that miss integration and conflict?
