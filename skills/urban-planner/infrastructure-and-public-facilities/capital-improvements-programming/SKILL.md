# Capital Improvements Programming

The capital improvements program (CIP) is where the comprehensive plan meets the budget, and it is the single most consequential implementation tool the jurisdiction controls directly. The judgment problem is that the CIP is developed through a fiscal and political process that often operates without reference to the adopted plan, so that the jurisdiction's actual spending — what it builds, where, and when — may contradict the plan's growth strategy, equity commitments, and infrastructure phasing. A plan that directs growth inward but whose CIP funds road extensions outward is a plan being defeated by its own government's budget. The planner's role is to ensure the CIP advances the plan, to bring plan-consistency discipline to the capital process, and to recognize that the budget, not the plan document, is where the jurisdiction's true priorities are revealed.

## Core Rules

### Make the CIP the Primary Implementation Vehicle for the Comprehensive Plan

The CIP should be the structured mechanism through which the comprehensive plan's infrastructure recommendations are funded, sequenced, and delivered. The rule is to build a direct linkage: each major plan recommendation with a capital component should appear in the CIP, with a project description, cost estimate, funding source, and timeline, and the CIP development process should begin from the plan's priorities rather than from departmental wish lists. This requires the planner to engage the budget process, which is often outside the planning department's routine, and to advocate for the plan's infrastructure needs against competing demands. A CIP developed without plan linkage will fund the projects with the most political or departmental momentum, not the projects that advance the strategy, and the plan will remain unimplemented.

### Prioritize Projects by Plan Consistency, Need, and Fiscal Capacity

Capital prioritization should rest on three explicit criteria: consistency with the comprehensive plan (does the project advance the growth strategy, equity commitments, and service levels?), demonstrated need (is there a measured capacity gap, safety issue, or service deficiency the project addresses?), and fiscal capacity (can the jurisdiction fund the project without compromising its fiscal health or other commitments?). The rule is to make these criteria explicit, to score or rank projects against them transparently, and to document the rationale for prioritization. Without explicit criteria, prioritization defaults to political influence and historical patterns, producing a CIP that funds the familiar and the favored rather than the strategic and the needed. Explicit criteria also create accountability: a project that scores poorly but is funded anyway is visibly an exception, and the exception must be justified.

### Sequence Investments to Match the Growth and Revenue Horizon

The CIP's multi-year horizon (typically five to six years, sometimes longer) requires sequencing investments so that capacity is available when growth arrives and so that the spending pace matches the revenue stream. The rule is to phase major projects to avoid fiscal spikes (concentrating too much debt or spending in one year), to align infrastructure provision with the development pipeline (capacity in place when the growth occurs, not years before or after), and to maintain a sustainable debt burden (debt service within prudent percentages of the operating budget). Sequencing also requires contingency: the CIP should preserve flexibility to respond to unforeseen needs (a failing facility, a regulatory mandate, an economic shift) without abandoning the planned projects, and the contingency should be explicit rather than absorbed by deferring maintenance.

### Protect Maintenance and Renewal From Being Deferred for New Projects

The most common and most damaging CIP failure is deferring maintenance and renewal of existing infrastructure to fund new projects, because new projects are visible and politically rewarded while maintenance is invisible until it fails. The rule is to protect maintenance and renewal funding — the ongoing investment that keeps existing assets functioning — with a dedicated allocation or a maintenance-first budgeting rule, and to assess the full lifecycle cost of new projects (including their ongoing maintenance) before committing to them. A jurisdiction that builds new infrastructure without funding its maintenance accumulates a maintenance backlog that eventually forces service degradation, emergency repairs at premium cost, or premature asset failure. The lifecycle cost perspective reveals that a project the CIP can afford to build may be one the operating budget cannot afford to maintain.

### Evaluate the Equity Distribution of Capital Investment

The pattern of capital investment — where parks, schools, roads, utilities, and facilities are built and maintained — encodes the jurisdiction's equity commitments, and historically disinvested areas often remain disinvested because the CIP follows political influence and existing infrastructure rather than need. The rule is to evaluate the CIP's geographic distribution for equity: map the proposed investments against income, race, and historical investment patterns, and confirm that the CIP directs investment to underserved areas, not just to growth areas and favored neighborhoods. An equity evaluation may reveal that the CIP, however unintentionally, concentrates investment in already-served areas, and the remedy is to reallocate toward the underserved, even when the political pressure runs the other way.

## Common Traps

### The CIP Developed Without Plan Linkage

The CIP is assembled from departmental requests, ranked by fiscal and political criteria, and adopted without reference to the comprehensive plan, so the spending contradicts the strategy. The trap is that the budget and the plan are developed on separate tracks. The mechanism is that the budget process does not consult the plan. The false signal is that "we have a capital budget." The harm is that the plan is unimplemented, the infrastructure built contradicts the growth strategy, and the plan is revealed as powerless against the spending that actually shapes the city.

### New Projects Crowding Out Maintenance

The CIP funds new, visible projects while maintenance and renewal of existing assets is deferred, and the maintenance backlog grows until assets fail. The trap is that new projects are politically rewarded while maintenance is invisible. The mechanism is that the budget prioritizes the ribbon-cutting over the upkeep. The false signal is that "we're investing in infrastructure." The harm is that existing assets degrade, the eventual repair or replacement is more expensive than ongoing maintenance would have been, and the service failures (a water main break, a bridge closure) fall on the public.

### Prioritization by Political Influence Rather Than Criteria

Projects are added to the CIP based on council member preference, developer pressure, or departmental momentum, without ranking against consistent criteria, and the CIP reflects influence rather than strategy. The trap is that the prioritization is opaque and unaccountable. The mechanism is that explicit criteria are avoided because they constrain discretion. The false signal is that "we funded the most needed projects." The harm is that the CIP funds the favored over the strategic, the plan's priorities are not advanced, and the underserved areas remain underserved because they lack the influence to direct spending.

### Ignoring Lifecycle Cost in Project Commitments

A new facility or infrastructure project is added to the CIP based on its capital cost, without analyzing the ongoing operating and maintenance cost it will impose, and the operating budget cannot absorb the new burden. The trap is that the project is "affordable" to build but unaffordable to operate. The mechanism is that capital and operating budgets are separate. The false signal is that "the project is funded." The harm is that the new asset is under-maintained (because the operating budget is strained), service degrades, or other operating needs are cut to maintain the new asset, and the cumulative effect of many such decisions is fiscal stress.

### Equity Blindness in Geographic Distribution

The CIP's investments are mapped and found to concentrate in growth areas and politically favored neighborhoods, while historically disinvested areas receive little, perpetuating the pattern of inequity. The trap is that the distribution was never evaluated for equity. The mechanism is that investment follows influence and existing infrastructure. The false signal is that "we fund projects by need." The harm is that the disinvestment persists, the equity gap widens, and the jurisdiction's stated equity commitments are contradicted by its own spending.

## Self-Check

- Is the CIP developed from the comprehensive plan's infrastructure recommendations, with a direct linkage between plan priorities and funded projects?
- Are projects prioritized against explicit, transparent criteria (plan consistency, demonstrated need, fiscal capacity), with documented rationale?
- Is investment sequenced to match the growth and revenue horizon, with sustainable debt and preserved contingency?
- Is maintenance and renewal of existing assets protected from deferral, with lifecycle cost assessed before new projects are committed?
- Has the CIP's geographic distribution been evaluated for equity, with investment directed to historically underserved areas?
- Have I avoided developing the CIP from departmental requests without plan linkage?
- Are existing assets being maintained, or is the maintenance backlog growing to fund new projects?
- Is prioritization transparent and criteria-based, or driven by political influence and momentum?
- Are the operating and maintenance costs of new projects analyzed before commitment, not just the capital cost?
- Does the equity evaluation confirm that investment reaches underserved areas, or does the CIP perpetuate historical disinvestment?
