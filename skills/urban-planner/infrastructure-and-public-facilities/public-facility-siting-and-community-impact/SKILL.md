# Public Facility Siting and Community Impact

Every community needs public facilities, but no neighborhood wants them nearby — particularly the locally unwanted land uses (LULUs) like shelters, treatment centers, public works yards, detention facilities, and infrastructure that provide region-wide benefits but concentrate local burdens. The judgment problem is that the siting decision determines who bears the burden, and the political path of least resistance routes LULUs to the communities with the least power to resist, which are often the communities already bearing disproportionate environmental and social burdens. A planner who sites by political convenience perpetuates environmental injustice; one who sites by regional equity and impact criteria faces intense opposition from whichever community is selected. The skill is in siting by transparent, equitable criteria, distributing burdens fairly across the region, mitigating local impacts genuinely, and resisting the default that concentrates unwanted facilities in disempowered neighborhoods.

## Core Rules

### Site by Transparent Criteria, Not Political Convenience

Facility siting should rest on explicit, documented criteria — service area need, accessibility, site suitability, cost, environmental constraints, and equity considerations — applied consistently across candidate sites, with the analysis and the scoring made public. The rule is to develop the criteria before evaluating sites, to apply them uniformly, and to document the rationale for the selected site against the alternatives. Siting by transparent criteria produces a defensible decision and allows the community to engage the analysis rather than fight the outcome; siting by political convenience (choosing the path of least resistance) produces a decision that concentrates burdens in disempowered communities and is vulnerable to challenge as arbitrary or inequitable. The criteria should include service effectiveness (does the site allow the facility to serve its function?) and equity (does the siting distribute burdens fairly?), not just cost and availability.

### Distribute Locally Unwanted Land Uses Equitably Across the Region

LULUs that provide regional benefits but impose local burdens should be distributed equitably across the region, not concentrated in the communities with the least power to resist. The rule is to assess the existing distribution of LULUs and environmental burdens, to identify communities already bearing disproportionate loads, and to prioritize siting in communities that are under-burdened, even when the political resistance there is greater. This inverts the default, which routes facilities to the path of least resistance, and it requires the jurisdiction to absorb the political cost of equitable distribution. A regional facility-siting framework, where communities accept some burden in exchange for regional benefits and where no community is asked to absorb a disproportionate share, is more just and more durable than ad hoc siting that concentrates burdens wherever opposition is weakest.

### Mitigate Local Impacts Genuinely, Not as a Public Relations Exercise

When a facility imposes local impacts — traffic, noise, emissions, perceived safety risk, aesthetic degradation — the mitigation should be genuine (designed to actually reduce the impact), enforceable (tied to conditions the jurisdiction can monitor), and adequate (proportional to the impact, not token). The rule is to analyze the local impacts specifically, to design mitigation that addresses each, and to commit to monitoring and adaptive management. Generic mitigation — "the facility will be designed to be compatible" — is a public relations exercise that does not reduce impact. Genuine mitigation may include buffering, operational restrictions, traffic management, design standards, and community benefits (park improvements, streetscape, programming) that offset the burden. The mitigation should be negotiated with the affected community, not imposed on it, and the commitments should be enforceable, not aspirational.

### Engage the Affected Community Early and Genuinely

Communities faced with a LULU siting are often engaged after the decision is made, which positions them as opponents of a fait accompli and confirms their suspicion that the process is rigged. The rule is to engage the affected community early, before the site is selected, to share the need and the criteria, to invite the community into the site evaluation, and to take the community's input seriously in the decision. Early engagement does not guarantee acceptance, but it produces better decisions (the community knows things about the site and the impacts that staff do not) and it builds the legitimacy that allows the facility to operate without ongoing conflict. Engagement that is genuine (the community's input can change the outcome) differs from engagement that is performative (the decision is fixed and the input is for the record), and communities can tell the difference.

### Recognize and Address the Cumulative Burden on Over-Burdened Communities

A community may already host multiple LULUs, environmental hazards, and stressors, and adding one more facility — even one that meets siting criteria in isolation — pushes the cumulative burden past a just level. The rule is to assess the cumulative burden on candidate communities, not just the suitability of the individual site, and to treat cumulative burden as a siting criterion that can disqualify an otherwise suitable site. A facility that is appropriate in an under-burdened community may be inappropriate in a community already saturated with burdens, even if the site is technically suitable and the political resistance is lower. Cumulative burden assessment requires mapping the existing facilities and hazards, and it requires the jurisdiction to acknowledge patterns it may have created over decades and to break them rather than deepen them.

## Common Traps

### Siting Where Resistance Is Lowest

The facility is sited in the community with the least political power to resist, regardless of whether that community is the most suitable or the most burdened. The trap is that the path of least resistance perpetuates environmental injustice. The mechanism is that political convenience substitutes for siting criteria. The false signal is that "the site was available and acceptable." The harm is that burdens concentrate in disempowered communities, the cumulative inequity deepens, and the jurisdiction's siting pattern becomes a record of who is sacrificed for regional benefit.

### Engagement After the Decision

The community is informed of the selected site and invited to comment, after the analysis and the decision are complete, and the engagement becomes a confrontation over a fait accompli. The trap is that the engagement confirms the community's distrust rather than building legitimacy. The mechanism is that the decision was made internally and the engagement is for the record. The false signal is that "we engaged the community." The harm is that the community fights the facility, the process is delayed and contested, and the facility operates under ongoing opposition that could have been mitigated by genuine upstream engagement.

### Mitigation as Token Gesture

The facility's local impacts are acknowledged and "mitigated" with a token gesture — a small buffer, a cosmetic design element, a one-time payment — that does not actually reduce the impact. The trap is that the mitigation is performative. The mechanism is that the commitment is designed to defuse opposition rather than to address the impact. The false signal is that "impacts are mitigated." The harm is that the impacts occur as analyzed, the community bears the full burden, the mitigation is unenforceable or inadequate, and the community's distrust of the jurisdiction's commitments is confirmed.

### Ignoring Cumulative Burden

Each facility is sited based on its individual suitability, without assessing the cumulative burden on the community, and an already-over-burdened community receives yet another facility because each individual siting met the criteria. The trap is that the cumulative pattern is never evaluated. The mechanism is that siting is project-by-project rather than cumulative. The false signal is that "this facility meets the criteria." The harm is that the community's cumulative burden crosses a just threshold, the environmental and health effects compound, and the jurisdiction deepens a pattern of inequity it should be breaking.

### Regional Benefit Cited to Override Local Burden

The facility's regional benefit is emphasized to justify imposing the local burden, and the affected community is told that the region's need outweighs their concern. The trap is that regional benefit becomes a conversation-stopper that dismisses legitimate local impact. The mechanism is that the regional framing shifts the burden to the local community without compensation. The false signal is that "the region needs this." The harm is that the community bears a burden for regional benefit without equitable mitigation or distribution, the cumulative pattern of sacrifice is reinforced, and the regional solidarity needed to site facilities equitably is undermined.

## Self-Check

- Is the facility sited by transparent, documented criteria applied consistently across candidate sites, with the analysis public?
- Are locally unwanted land uses distributed equitably across the region, with over-burdened communities protected from additional concentration?
- Is the mitigation genuine, enforceable, and proportional to the local impact, designed with the affected community rather than imposed on it?
- Is the affected community engaged early, before the site is selected, with input that can change the outcome?
- Has the cumulative burden on candidate communities been assessed, and has an over-burdened community been protected from an additional facility even if the individual site is suitable?
- Have I avoided siting where resistance is lowest, regardless of suitability or cumulative burden?
- Did engagement begin before the decision, or after, as a confrontation over a fait accompli?
- Does the mitigation actually reduce the impact, or is it a token gesture to defuse opposition?
- Has cumulative burden been treated as a siting criterion, not just individual site suitability?
- Is the regional benefit weighed against genuine local mitigation and equitable distribution, not used to dismiss the local burden?
