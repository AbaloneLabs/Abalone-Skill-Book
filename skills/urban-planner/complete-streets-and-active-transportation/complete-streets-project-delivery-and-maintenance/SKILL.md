# Complete Streets Project Delivery and Maintenance

The most common failure of complete streets is not in the policy or the design but in the delivery: a jurisdiction adopts a strong complete streets policy, designs exemplary corridors, and then builds projects that do not reflect the policy or the design, because the policy was never integrated into the agency's project delivery system. The gap between policy and pavement is created by institutional structures: the capital program that funds projects without complete streets criteria, the design manual that has not been updated to reflect the policy, the review process that exempts resurfacing and maintenance projects from complete streets requirements, the construction change orders that eliminate the complete streets elements to save cost or time, and the maintenance budget that does not fund the upkeep of the new facilities. The judgment problem is that complete streets are delivered through dozens of projects a year, by multiple departments and consultants, and unless the policy is embedded in the delivery system — the funding criteria, the design standards, the review checklists, the construction oversight, the maintenance commitments — the policy will produce a few showcase projects while the vast majority of streets are rebuilt to the old standards. This skill covers the project delivery, institutional integration, and maintenance decisions that determine whether a complete streets policy produces complete streets. It complements the policy and modal-tradeoff skills by focusing on the implementation system that converts policy into built projects.

## Core Rules

### Embed Complete Streets Criteria in the Capital Programming and Funding

The capital program — the list of projects the jurisdiction funds each year — is where the complete streets policy is either implemented or defeated, because the projects in the capital program are the projects that get built, and if they are selected and scoped without complete streets criteria, they will not produce complete streets. The rule is to embed complete streets criteria in the capital programming and funding, so that every street project (resurfacing, reconstruction, safety, operations) is evaluated for complete streets opportunities, and the projects that advance the complete streets network are prioritized. The criteria should require that every street project assess the complete streets elements needed (sidewalks, bike facilities, transit stops, crossings, accessibility) and incorporate them unless a documented exception is granted, and that the capital program prioritizes the projects that close network gaps or serve the adopted priority network. The criteria should also address the funding: complete streets elements should be funded as part of the project, not as a separate add-on that is cut when the budget is tight, and the funding sources (federal, state, local) should be aligned to support the complete streets elements. The capital programming integration is what converts a policy that applies to showcase projects into a policy that applies to every street project, because the vast majority of street miles are rebuilt through resurfacing and maintenance, not through standalone complete streets projects.

### Update the Design Manual and Standards to Reflect the Policy

The design manual is the document that governs how streets are designed, and if it has not been updated to reflect the complete streets policy, the designers (whether in-house or consultant) will design to the old standards, and the complete streets elements will be value-engineered out as deviations from the manual. The rule is to update the design manual and standards to reflect the policy, incorporating the complete streets elements (lane widths, bike facility types, sidewalk dimensions, crossing treatments, transit stop design, green infrastructure) as the default standards, not as optional enhancements, and aligning the manual with the national guidance (NACTO, AASHTO bike and ped guides, PROWAG) that supports complete streets design. The manual update should also address the exceptions process: when and how a project may deviate from the complete streets standards, what documentation is required, and who approves the exception, because an exceptions process that is too easy undermines the standards while one that is too rigid prevents context-sensitive design. The manual should be the reference that designers use daily, and if it does not reflect the policy, the policy will not be implemented regardless of the political commitment, because the designers will default to the manual they know.

### Apply Complete Streets to Resurfacing and Maintenance, Not Just Reconstruction

The trap that defeats most complete streets policies is the exemption of resurfacing and maintenance projects from complete streets requirements, because resurfacing is the most common street project, and the streets that are resurfaced without complete streets elements remain incomplete for another resurfacing cycle (often 15-20 years). The rule is to apply complete streets to resurfacing and maintenance, not just reconstruction, because the resurfacing project is the opportunity to restripe the street with bike lanes, to add crossings, to narrow the travel lanes, at a fraction of the cost of reconstruction, and missing the opportunity means the street remains incomplete for a generation. The integration should require that every resurfacing project assess the complete streets opportunities and incorporate the restriping and marking elements that do not require construction, with a documented exception only where the elements are infeasible. The maintenance projects (pothole repair, signal replacement, sign replacement) should similarly be assessed for complete streets integration, because the signal replacement is the opportunity to add leading pedestrian intervals, and the sign replacement is the opportunity to update the wayfinding. The resurfacing and maintenance integration is what produces a complete streets network over time, because the network is built incrementally through these projects, not through the rare reconstruction.

### Manage Construction to Preserve the Complete Streets Elements

The construction phase is where complete streets elements are often lost, through change orders that eliminate the bike facility to save cost, value engineering that narrows the sidewalk, or contractor decisions that simplify the intersection, and the project that was designed as complete streets is built as conventional. The rule is to manage construction to preserve the complete streets elements, through construction oversight that verifies the elements are built as designed, a change-order process that requires documented justification and approval for any elimination of complete streets elements, and a post-construction audit that verifies the as-built conditions match the design. The construction management should recognize that the complete streets elements are often the first things value-engineered because they are perceived as enhancements rather than core elements, and the oversight must treat them as core. The post-construction audit should be a standard practice, because the as-built conditions often differ from the design, and the differences (a narrower bike lane, a missing crossing, a steeper curb ramp) may not be discovered until a user is injured. The audit should compare the as-built to the design, document the deviations, and require correction where the deviations affect function or safety.

### Fund and Assign Maintenance for the Long-Term Function of the Facilities

A complete streets facility that is built but not maintained does not function: a bike lane filled with debris is unusable, a sidewalk covered in snow or overgrowth is impassable, a crossing with faded markings is unrecognized, and a green infrastructure feature that is not maintained fails. The rule is to fund and assign maintenance for the long-term function of the facilities, identifying the maintenance requirements (sweeping for bike lanes, snow removal for sidewalks, vegetation management, marking refreshment, green infrastructure upkeep), assigning the responsibility to a specific department or entity, and funding the maintenance as an ongoing obligation, not a one-time cost. The maintenance funding should be committed before the facility is built, because a facility built without maintenance funding will deteriorate, and the deterioration will be misread as the facility's failure rather than the maintenance's failure. The maintenance assignment should address the jurisdictions where multiple entities are responsible (the city, the state DOT, the adjacent property owner), because the maintenance gaps often occur at the boundaries where responsibility is unclear. The maintenance commitment should be sustained over the facility's life, because a facility that is maintained for the first year and then neglected fails as surely as one that was never maintained, and the users who depended on it are stranded.

## Common Traps

### Policy Adopted but Not Integrated into Capital Programming

The complete streets policy is adopted, but the capital program continues to select and scope projects without complete streets criteria, and the projects that are built do not reflect the policy, producing a few showcase projects while the majority of streets are rebuilt to the old standards. The trap is that a policy without capital integration is a statement, not a system. The mechanism is that the capital programming was not updated, and the projects were selected by the old criteria. The false signal is that "we have a complete streets policy." The harm is that the policy applies to showcase projects only, the network is not built through the routine projects, the vast majority of street miles remain incomplete, and the policy's credibility erodes as the gap between policy and pavement persists.

### Design Manual Not Updated, Designers Default to Old Standards

The design manual has not been updated to reflect the policy, and the designers (in-house and consultant) design to the old standards, with the complete streets elements value-engineered out as deviations from the manual. The trap is that the manual governs the design, and an outdated manual defeats the policy. The mechanism is that the manual update was not done, and the designers defaulted to the standards they know. The false signal is that "the designers are following the standards." The harm is that the projects are designed to the old standards, the complete streets elements are eliminated as deviations, the policy is not implemented, and the designers' practice does not change because the manual they use has not changed.

### Resurfacing Exempted, Network Not Built Incrementally

The resurfacing and maintenance projects are exempted from complete streets requirements, and the streets that are resurfaced without complete streets elements remain incomplete for a generation, because the network is built through resurfacing, not through the rare reconstruction. The trap is that the exemption defeats the incremental network-building that resurfacing enables. The mechanism is that the resurfacing exemption was written into the policy or the practice, and the opportunities were missed. The false signal is that "resurfacing is just maintenance." The harm is that the streets remain incomplete for 15-20 years, the network is not built, the low-cost opportunities are missed, and the complete streets network depends on the rare reconstruction that cannot build the network at the needed pace.

### Complete Streets Elements Lost in Construction

The project is designed as complete streets, but the complete streets elements are eliminated through change orders or value engineering during construction, and the as-built project does not reflect the design. The trap is that construction erodes the design. The mechanism is that the construction oversight did not preserve the elements, and the change-order process allowed the elimination. The false signal is that "the project is built." The harm is that the complete streets elements are missing, the facility does not function as designed, the investment is undermined, and the users who expected the facility are stranded by its absence.

### Facilities Built but Not Maintained

The complete streets facilities are built, but the maintenance is not funded or assigned, and the facilities deteriorate — bike lanes fill with debris, sidewalks are blocked by snow or overgrowth, crossings fade — and the facilities become unusable, with the deterioration misread as the facilities' failure. The trap is that unmaintained facilities fail regardless of their design. The mechanism is that the maintenance funding was not committed, and the responsibility was not assigned. The false signal is that "the facility is built." The harm is that the facility deteriorates, the users who depend on it are stranded, the deterioration is misread as low demand or design failure, and the political support for future complete streets erodes as the built facilities fail.

## Self-Check

- Are complete streets criteria embedded in the capital programming and funding, so that every street project is evaluated and the network-advancing projects are prioritized?
- Is the design manual updated to reflect the policy, with complete streets elements as default standards and a documented exceptions process?
- Are resurfacing and maintenance projects subject to complete streets requirements, so that the network is built incrementally through the most common projects?
- Is construction managed to preserve the complete streets elements, with oversight, change-order control, and post-construction audit?
- Is maintenance funded and assigned for the long-term function of the facilities, with the responsibility clear and the funding committed before construction?
- Does the capital program produce complete streets through routine projects, or only through rare showcase projects?
- Do the designers use a manual that reflects the policy, or do they default to outdated standards?
- Are the resurfacing projects assessed for complete streets opportunities, or are they exempted and the opportunities missed?
- Do the as-built conditions match the design, verified by post-construction audit, or have the complete streets elements been lost in construction?
- Are the built facilities maintained and functional over time, or are they deteriorating because the maintenance was not funded or assigned?
