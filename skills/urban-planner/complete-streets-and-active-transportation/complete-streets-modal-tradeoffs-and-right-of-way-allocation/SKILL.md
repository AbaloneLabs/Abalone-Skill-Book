# Complete Streets Modal Tradeoffs and Right-of-Way Allocation

A complete street serves all users — drivers, transit riders, cyclists, pedestrians, freight, emergency vehicles — but the right-of-way that contains these uses is finite, and serving all users fully is usually impossible. A street that maintains vehicle throughput, provides dedicated transit lanes, installs protected bike facilities, widens sidewalks, and preserves loading cannot fit in a typical right-of-way, and the planner must choose which modes get how much space. These choices are the substance of complete streets design, and they are politically charged because every inch allocated to one mode is taken from another, and the users who lose space experience the loss directly while the users who gain space may not attribute the gain to the project. The judgment problem is that modal tradeoffs are often resolved by defaulting to the existing allocation (preserving vehicle throughput and parking, because changing them generates opposition), which produces a street that is nominally complete but functionally prioritizes the car, with token facilities for other modes that do not actually serve them. A genuinely complete street requires explicit, defensible decisions about modal priority, based on the street's function, the users it serves, and the city's adopted goals, and the decisions must be made openly rather than through the accumulated defaults of conventional design. This skill covers the tradeoff analysis and right-of-way allocation that determine whether a complete streets project actually completes the street. It complements the complete-streets policy skill by focusing on the corridor-level design decisions the policy must operationalize.

## Core Rules

### Establish Modal Priority Based on Street Function and Adopted Goals

The first step in resolving modal tradeoffs is establishing the modal priority for the specific street, because the priority determines how the tradeoffs are resolved when the modes conflict. The rule is to establish modal priority based on the street's functional classification (is it a local street, an arterial, a transit corridor, a downtown street, a neighborhood main street), the users it serves (who travels on it, to what destinations, by what modes), and the city's adopted goals (the complete streets policy, the transit priority network, the bike network, the vision zero commitment). A transit corridor should prioritize transit (dedicated lanes, signal priority, stop design), a neighborhood main street should prioritize pedestrians and local access (wide sidewalks, slow speeds, crossings), a bike network spine should prioritize the bike facility (protection, continuity), and a freight corridor should prioritize goods movement (lane width, turning radius, loading). The modal priority should be established explicitly and defended, because the implicit alternative is to default to vehicle throughput, which is the existing priority and the path of least political resistance. The modal priority should be documented in the corridor plan or the design report, so that the tradeoffs are resolved consistently with the priority, and so that the opposition that inevitably arises can be answered with the adopted basis for the decision.

### Quantify the Tradeoffs So the Choices Are Explicit

Modal tradeoffs are quantifiable — a transit lane removes a vehicle lane and changes the throughput; a protected bike lane removes parking or a vehicle lane; a wider sidewalk narrows the others — and quantifying the tradeoffs makes the choices explicit and defensible, rather than leaving them to impression or political pressure. The rule is to quantify the tradeoffs for each allocation option, modeling the impacts on each mode (vehicle travel time and throughput, transit speed and reliability, bike level of stress and continuity, pedestrian space and crossing quality, loading and access), so that the decision-makers and the public can see what each option provides and what it costs. The quantification should use the established methods (Highway Capacity Manual for vehicles, Transit Capacity and Quality of Service for transit, Level of Traffic Stress for bikes, pedestrian level of service), and the results should be reported in comparable units, so that the tradeoff is visible: this option provides X for transit and costs Y for vehicles, that option provides X for bikes and costs Y for parking. The quantification should also address the safety dimension (how does each option affect crashes, particularly for vulnerable users), because safety is often the deciding factor and the Vision Zero commitment may override the throughput considerations. The quantified tradeoffs are what make the allocation a reasoned decision rather than a political compromise, and they are what defend the project against the opposition that focuses on the loss without acknowledging the gain.

### Design for the Vulnerable Users Who Bear the Consequence of Failure

The users who bear the consequence of a complete streets failure — the pedestrian struck in a crossing, the cyclist doored or merged into, the transit rider stranded by unreliable service — are the vulnerable users, and the design must protect them, not merely accommodate them. The rule is to design for the vulnerable users, recognizing that a design that works for the confident driver but fails the vulnerable pedestrian or cyclist is not a complete street, regardless of how many modes it nominally serves. The design should apply the safe-systems approach, which designs the street so that the inevitable human errors do not produce fatal consequences, through speed management (lower speeds reduce crash severity), separation (protected facilities for vulnerable users where speeds and volumes warrant), and intersection design (protected crossings, leading pedestrian intervals, no right-turn-on-red where conflicts exist). The design should meet or exceed the design standards for each mode, not the minimum, because minimum-standard design often produces facilities that nominally exist but do not function — a bike lane in the door zone, a sidewalk too narrow for two wheelchairs to pass, a crossing too wide for an older adult to traverse in the signal time. The vulnerable-user focus should also consider the specific populations: children near schools, older adults near senior centers, people with disabilities who rely on accessible infrastructure, and the design should be tested against their needs explicitly.

### Manage the Transition and the Network Effects Beyond the Corridor

A complete streets project on one corridor affects the network beyond the corridor, because the traffic, transit, and circulation patterns change, and the project's success depends on managing these network effects, not just the corridor's design. The rule is to manage the transition and the network effects, analyzing how the reallocated corridor affects the parallel streets (will diverted traffic burden a residential street?), the connecting transit (will the transit improvement connect to the network?), the bike network (does the facility connect to the existing or planned network?), and the loading and access (will the loading restrictions shift deliveries to less appropriate locations?). The network analysis should identify the mitigation needed: traffic calming on the parallel streets that receive diverted traffic, transit signal priority at the connecting intersections, wayfinding and connections for the bike network, and loading management that provides alternatives. The transition should also manage the construction period, which is often the most disruptive phase and the source of the most intense opposition, through phasing, communication, and temporary accommodations that maintain access for the businesses and residents. A complete streets project that designs the corridor well but ignores the network effects may shift the problems rather than solve them, generating opposition from the affected parallel streets and undermining the project's credibility.

### Build the Political and Public Case for the Reallocation

The technical analysis establishes what the reallocation should be, but the reallocation requires political and public support, because the users who lose space will oppose the project, and the users who gain space may not organize to support it. The rule is to build the political and public case for the reallocation, engaging the affected users (particularly those who lose space) early and honestly, presenting the quantified tradeoffs and the adopted basis for the decision, and building the coalition of users who benefit (transit riders, cyclists, pedestrians, the businesses that benefit from the increased access). The engagement should acknowledge the losses honestly, because denying them destroys credibility, and should present the gains and the rationale (the modal priority, the safety improvement, the adopted goals) that justify the reallocation. The political case should be built with the elected officials, who will face the opposition and need the analysis and the public support to sustain the decision. The case should be made before the decision, not after, because a reallocation that is decided and then defended generates more opposition than one that is developed through engagement. The political and public case is what converts a technically sound reallocation into an implemented project, and without it, the project may be designed but not built, or built and then reversed under opposition.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to Vehicle Throughput and Parking

The complete streets project resolves the tradeoffs by preserving the existing vehicle throughput and parking, and the facilities for other modes are token — a bike lane in the door zone, a narrow sidewalk, no transit priority — producing a street that is nominally complete but functionally car-prioritized. The trap is that defaulting to the existing allocation avoids opposition but defeats the project's purpose. The mechanism is that the modal priority was not established, and the tradeoffs defaulted to the existing vehicle priority. The false signal is that "we are completing the street." The harm is that the other modes are not actually served, the project's goals are not met, the investment is wasted on token facilities, and the complete streets policy is undermined by a project that demonstrates that complete streets do not work.

### Tradeoffs Resolved by Impression Rather Than Analysis

The modal tradeoffs are resolved by impression or political pressure, without quantified analysis, and the resulting allocation does not reflect the actual impacts on each mode, producing a design that is neither defensible nor optimal. The trap is that unquantified tradeoffs produce unaccountable decisions. The mechanism is that the tradeoffs were not modeled, and the allocation was decided by negotiation or default. The false signal is that "we balanced the modes." The harm is that the design does not serve the modes as intended, the opposition can challenge the allocation without a defensible basis, the project may not achieve its safety or mode-share goals, and the tradeoffs that were not analyzed emerge as problems after construction.

### Minimum-Standard Design Failing Vulnerable Users

The facilities are built to the minimum design standard, which nominally meets the requirement but does not function for the vulnerable users — a bike lane too narrow for comfort at speed, a crossing too wide for the signal time, a sidewalk too narrow for passing — and the vulnerable users are not actually served. The trap is that minimum-standard design excludes the users who need the facility most. The mechanism is that the design standard was treated as a target rather than a floor, and the vulnerable users' needs were not tested. The false signal is that "the facility meets the standard." The harm is that the vulnerable users cannot use the facility, the safety goals are not met, the mode shift does not occur, and the investment produces a facility that exists but does not function.

### Network Effects Ignored, Problems Shifted

The complete streets project reallocated the corridor well but did not analyze the network effects, and the diverted traffic, the shifted loading, or the disconnected bike facility shift the problems to the parallel streets or the connecting network, generating opposition from the affected areas. The trap is that corridor-level success can produce network-level failure. The mechanism is that the network analysis was not done, and the mitigation was not provided. The false signal is that "the corridor is improved." The harm is that the problems shift to the parallel streets, the affected residents and businesses oppose the project, the project's credibility is undermined, and the complete streets approach is associated with shifting rather than solving problems.

### Reallocation Decided Without Political or Public Support

The reallocation is decided on technical grounds and then announced, without prior engagement or political support, and the users who lose space organize opposition that delays, modifies, or reverses the project. The trap is that a technically sound decision without political support is vulnerable to reversal. The mechanism is that the engagement and political case were not built before the decision. The false signal is that "the analysis justifies the decision." The harm is that the project is delayed or reversed, the resources invested in the design are wasted, the opposition is energized for future projects, and the complete streets policy is undermined by a project that failed not on technical but on political grounds.

## Self-Check

- Is the modal priority for the corridor established explicitly, based on street function, users served, and adopted goals, rather than defaulting to vehicle throughput?
- Are the tradeoffs quantified for each allocation option, with the impacts on each mode reported in comparable units, including the safety dimension?
- Is the design tested against the vulnerable users' needs, exceeding the minimum standard where the minimum does not function for them?
- Are the network effects — parallel streets, connecting transit, bike network, loading — analyzed and mitigated, with the construction transition managed?
- Is the political and public case built before the decision, engaging the users who lose space honestly and building the coalition of users who benefit?
- Does the street actually prioritize the modes the modal priority established, or does it default to preserving vehicle throughput and parking?
- Can the allocation be defended with quantified analysis, or is it resolved by impression and political pressure?
- Would a vulnerable user — a child, an older adult, a person with a disability — be served by the design, or does the minimum standard exclude them?
- Does the project solve the problems on the corridor without shifting them to the parallel streets and the connecting network?
- Was the reallocation developed with engagement and political support, or decided and then defended against the opposition that emerged?
