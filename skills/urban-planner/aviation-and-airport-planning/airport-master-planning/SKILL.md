# Airport Master Planning

An airport master plan is the long-range blueprint that translates an airport's role and forecast demand into a phased set of facility decisions, depicted on the Airport Layout Plan (ALP) and backed by the justification needed for federal funding. The judgment problem is that every element of the plan is interdependent and every decision is expensive and long-lived. A runway extension built to the wrong length either fails to attract the service it was meant to enable or saddles the sponsor with pavement it cannot maintain. A terminal sized to an optimistic enplanement forecast becomes a financial burden; one sized too small chokes growth and triggers costly reconstruction. Forecasts drive facility requirements, facility requirements drive the ALP, and the ALP drives eligibility for the National Plan of Integrated Airport Systems (NPIAS) and FAA grant funding, so an error early in the process propagates through the entire plan and is extraordinarily difficult to reverse once concrete is poured. The planner's task is to ground the forecast in defensible assumptions, to size facilities to demand with appropriate reserve, to integrate airside, landside, and terminal planning so no element constrains another, and to position the ALP so the airport remains fundable and the sponsor remains in compliance with grant assurances. This skill covers aviation demand forecasting, facility requirements analysis, terminal and airside and landside planning, ground access and intermodal connectivity, the ALP, NPIAS, and FAA funding.

## Core Rules

### Anchor the Forecast in Transparent, Defensible Assumptions

Aviation demand forecasting is the foundation of the entire master plan, because the forecast of operations, enplanements, and based aircraft determines every facility requirement that follows. The rule is that the forecast must be transparent about its methodology (trend, market share, regression, or a combination), its drivers (population, income, fare levels, route decisions of carriers), and its scenarios, and it must be benchmarked against FAA Terminal Area Forecast (TAF) so that material divergence is explained. A forecast that simply extends a growth trend without examining the underlying market is not defensible, and a forecast that is optimistic to justify a desired project will produce facilities that are oversized and unfundable. The planner should present a preferred forecast bounded by low and high scenarios, should identify the assumptions to which the forecast is most sensitive, and should ensure the forecast horizon matches the planning horizon so that phased facilities are triggered by demand milestones, not by arbitrary dates.

### Derive Facility Requirements From the Forecast and the Design Aircraft

Facility requirements translate forecast demand into the physical capacity the airport must provide: runway length and strength, taxiway geometry, apron and gate capacity, terminal area, aircraft parking, fuel facilities, and access roads. The rule is that each requirement must trace back to a specific forecast output and to the design or critical aircraft, which determines runway length, weight-bearing capacity, taxiway width, and separation standards. Runway length in particular must be analyzed for the critical aircraft at the airport's elevation, temperature, and stage length, not taken from a generic table, because hot-and-high conditions and the design mission dramatically affect the length required. The planner should distinguish capacity-driven requirements (the facility is at its practical capacity and will fail the level of service) from standards-driven requirements (the facility does not meet current design standards), because the funding justification and the urgency differ.

### Integrate Airside, Terminal, and Landside So No Element Constrains Another

An airport is a system in which the capacity of the weakest element governs the whole. A runway with ample capacity is useless if the terminal cannot process passengers; a terminal with ample gates is useless if the apron and taxiway system cannot move aircraft; a landside with ample curb is useless if the access roads are gridlocked. The rule is to plan airside, terminal, and landside together, identifying the binding capacity constraint in each planning horizon and ensuring the phased improvements relieve the constraint before it chokes the system. A common failure is to over-invest in one element (often the runway or the terminal, because these are visible and politically attractive) while neglecting another (often the taxiway system, the apron, or the ground access), so that the investment is wasted because the system bottleneck simply moves. The planner should carry a capacity balance sheet through each horizon so the binding constraint is always explicit.

### Plan the Terminal for Processing Capacity and Passenger Experience

Terminal planning is where forecast enplanements meet passenger experience, and it is the element most prone to optimism and to obsolescence. The rule is to size the terminal from the peak-hour passenger volume, not the annual enplanement, because the terminal fails at the peak hour even if annual volume looks comfortable. Processing areas (check-in, security screening, baggage, gate holdrooms) must be sized to the peak-hour design load with an acceptable level of service, and circulation and concessions must be planned so that passengers move intuitively and dwell time is productive rather than congested. The planner should account for changing processing models (automated bag drop, expanded security checkpoints, larger aircraft at gates) that change the space required per passenger, and should build in flexibility to reconfigure, because terminal programs change faster than terminal buildings.

### Design Ground Access and Intermodal Connectivity for All Modes

Ground access is frequently the weakest link in airport planning, because the airport is often planned as if everyone arrives by private car. The rule is to plan ground access for the full modal split: private vehicles and parking, taxis and ride-hailing, shuttles and buses, rental cars, and where feasible rail or transit. The access road system, the curb frontage, the parking supply, and the rental car facility must be sized to the forecast passenger and employee volumes, and the connection to the regional surface transport network and to transit must be explicit on the ALP. Intermodal connectivity, whether a transit station, a bus rapid transit link, or an intermodal transfer center, should be planned even if not immediately built, so that the right-of-way and the terminal interface are preserved. Neglecting ground access produces curb congestion, parking shortages, and employee access problems that degrade the airport's function and its reputation.

### Maintain the ALP as the Controlling Document and the Funding Key

The Airport Layout Plan is the single drawing set that depicts the existing and ultimate development of the airport, and it is the document the FAA reviews for consistency with grant assurances and design standards. The rule is that the ALP must be internally consistent with the master plan, must depict all existing and proposed facilities with proper separations and runway safety areas, and must be kept current, because construction not shown on an approved ALP can jeopardize funding and compliance. The planner should ensure the ALP reflects the ultimate development so that interim construction does not foreclose future phases, should coordinate ALP approval with the FAA, and should recognize that an approved ALP is effectively a precondition for NPIAS inclusion and for AIP funding of the depicted projects.

### Position the Airport for NPIAS Inclusion and FAA Funding

Federal funding through the Airport Improvement Program (AIP) and the passenger facility charge (PFC) program flows to airports that are included in the National Plan of Integrated Airport Systems (NPIAS), and NPIAS inclusion depends on the airport's role, its activity levels, and the justification of its development needs. The rule is that the master plan must establish the airport's role (national, regional, local, basic, or unclassified), must demonstrate that proposed development meets a current need consistent with the NPIAS role, and must produce the benefit-cost and justification analysis that AIP requires. The planner should understand that funding prioritization favors projects that address safety, standards compliance, capacity at congested airports, and reconstruction of aging infrastructure, and should sequence the capital plan so that the highest-priority, best-justified projects are positioned for funding first. The sponsor's grant assurances, including the obligation to maintain the developed property and to keep it available for public use, must be understood as a condition of accepting federal funds.

## Common Traps

### Forecasting Optimistically to Justify a Desired Project

The forecast is built to support a runway extension or a new terminal that the sponsor already wants, using high-growth assumptions and ignoring adverse indicators such as carrier consolidation or fare increases. The trap is that the forecast is reverse-engineered rather than analytically derived. The mechanism is that the desired project sets the target demand and the forecast is tuned to reach it. The false signal is that "demand justifies the project." The harm is that the facility is oversized, the operating and maintenance burden falls on the sponsor, the project may not score well for AIP funding because the need is not current, and when the forecast demand fails to materialize the airport is left with stranded assets. The remedy is to derive the forecast independently and to let the requirements follow.

### Sizing the Terminal to Annual Enplanements Instead of Peak Hour

The terminal is sized to the annual enplanement forecast, producing a building that is overwhelmed during peak banks and empty the rest of the day. The trap is that annual volume masks the peak-hour stress. The mechanism is the use of an annual average rather than a design hour. The false signal is that "the terminal has capacity for the forecast." The harm is a poor passenger experience, missed connections, and pressure for costly expansion or reconstruction long before the annual forecast is reached. The remedy is to size processing and gate areas to the peak-hour design passenger load with a defined level of service.

### Overbuilding One Element While Neglecting the Binding Constraint

The sponsor builds a new runway but leaves the taxiway system and apron unchanged, so aircraft cannot get to the runway; or expands the terminal but leaves the access road and curb unchanged, so passengers cannot reach the terminal. The trap is that investment follows visibility rather than system capacity. The mechanism is that the binding constraint is not identified because airside, terminal, and landside are planned in isolation. The false signal is that "we added capacity." The harm is that the new facility cannot be used to its potential, the bottleneck moves rather than disappears, and the investment produces little benefit. The remedy is to carry a capacity balance sheet and to relieve the binding constraint at each horizon.

### Taking Runway Length From a Generic Table

The required runway length is read from a table for the aircraft category without adjusting for the airport's elevation, mean maximum temperature, and stage length, producing a runway that is too short for the design aircraft on hot days or for the intended route. The trap is that the table value is a simplification that hides the hot-and-high and mission effects. The mechanism is reliance on a generic reference instead of an aircraft performance analysis. The false signal is that "the runway meets the standard." The harm is that the critical aircraft is weight-limited, cannot reach its destination, or cannot operate at all, and the service the runway was meant to enable never materializes. The remedy is to perform a site-specific runway length analysis for the design aircraft.

### Letting the ALP Drift From the Master Plan

The ALP is updated infrequently, so it depicts facilities that have been built differently or omits facilities that are planned, and the master plan and the ALP tell different stories. The trap is that the ALP ceases to be a reliable controlling document. The mechanism is that construction proceeds on interim approvals without an ALP update. The false signal is that "we have an approved ALP." The harm is that the FAA review finds inconsistencies, funding is delayed or denied, and future phases are foreclosed because interim construction occupies the ultimate-development footprint. The remedy is to treat any significant change as triggering an ALP update and to keep the ALP and master plan synchronized.

### Neglecting Ground Access Until It Becomes a Crisis

Ground access is treated as a local road issue outside the airport plan, so curb, parking, and access road capacity are not sized to passenger growth, and no provision is made for transit. The trap is that access is planned by others with no airport input. The mechanism is that the airport plan stops at the property line. The false signal is that "access is the city's problem." The harm is curb congestion, parking shortages, employee access failure, and a degraded passenger experience that constrains the airport's growth long before the airside or terminal does. The remedy is to plan ground access as an integral element of the master plan and to preserve intermodal connections on the ALP.

## Self-Check

- Is the aviation demand forecast transparent about methodology, drivers, and scenarios, and is material divergence from the FAA Terminal Area Forecast explained?
- Does each facility requirement trace to a specific forecast output and to the design aircraft, with runway length analyzed for the airport's elevation, temperature, and stage length?
- Have airside, terminal, and landside been planned together with a capacity balance sheet that identifies the binding constraint at each horizon?
- Is the terminal sized to the peak-hour design passenger load with a defined level of service, rather than to annual enplanements?
- Does the ground access plan address the full modal split and preserve intermodal connections on the ALP, rather than stopping at the property line?
- Is the ALP current, internally consistent with the master plan, and approved by the FAA, with ultimate development depicted so interim construction does not foreclose future phases?
- Has the airport's NPIAS role been established and are proposed projects justified to a current need consistent with that role and with AIP prioritization?
- Does the capital plan sequence the highest-priority, best-justified projects first, and does the sponsor understand the grant assurances attached to federal funding?
