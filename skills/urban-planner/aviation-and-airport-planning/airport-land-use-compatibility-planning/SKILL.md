# Airport Land Use Compatibility Planning

Airports and the communities around them are locked in a permanent structural tension: the airport needs room to operate safely and grow, while surrounding land owners want to develop, build, and use property up to the fence line. Airport land use compatibility planning is the practice of resolving that tension through noise contours, airspace protection surfaces, and safety zones before incompatibility becomes a concrete-and-asphalt fact that cannot be undone. The judgment problem is that the most harmful outcomes are slow and diffuse. A few houses built inside the 65 DNL contour do not shut the airport overnight, but over twenty years they accumulate into a constituency that demands flight restrictions, curfews, and operational changes that erode the airport's utility and its federal funding eligibility. Height obstructions are even more insidious: a crane, a cell tower, or a building permitted without Part 77 review can trigger a finding of obstruction, force instrument approach procedures to be raised, and increase weather minimums for every flight thereafter. The planner's task is to treat the airport's operational footprint as a real, three-dimensional planning constraint, to translate abstract federal surfaces into enforceable local zoning, and to defend the compatibility regime against the constant pressure to chip away at it parcel by parcel. This skill covers noise exposure mapping and DNL contours, FAA Part 77 imaginary surfaces and height hazard zoning, runway protection zones, and the land use compatibility guidelines that tie them together.

## Core Rules

### Treat DNL Contours as a Planning Constraint, Not a Mere Disclosure

The Day-Night Average Sound Level (DNL, sometimes written Ldn) is the metric FAA and HUD use to describe cumulative noise exposure, with the 65 DNL contour generally treated as the threshold above which residential land use is incompatible without mitigation. The rule is that the contours produced by an Integrated Noise Model or AEDT run are planning inputs, not decorative maps. They should drive the land use plan, the zoning map, the subdivision review triggers, and the real estate disclosure requirements for parcels they cross. A common failure is to publish the contours in a compatibility study and then fail to translate them into regulations, so that development continues as if the contours did not exist. The contours are only as good as the aviation activity (operations, fleet mix, runway use, time of day) that generated them, so they must be refreshed when operations change materially, and the planning documents must reference a specific, dated contour set rather than a generic approximation.

### Translate Part 77 Imaginary Surfaces Into Local Height Hazard Zoning

FAA Part 77 defines the imaginary surfaces around an airport (primary, approach, transitional, horizontal, and conical surfaces) that determine whether an object is an obstruction, and it establishes the notice criteria for proposed construction. But Part 77 is a federal review and notice framework, not a self-executing local prohibition. The rule is that the local jurisdiction must adopt height hazard zoning or an airport overlay district that codifies the Part 77 surfaces as enforceable height limits, with a permit trigger for any construction, tree planting, or equipment that could penetrate them. The overlay should reference the runway ends, the precise surface dimensions, and the elevation data, and it should require FAA Form 7460-1 review as a condition of approval. Relying on the FAA's voluntary notice process alone is insufficient because the FAA cannot stop construction; only the local zoning authority can. The overlay must also address temporary objects (cranes, construction equipment, drones) which are a frequent source of unpermitted penetrations.

### Size and Protect Runway Protection Zones According to the Critical Aircraft

A Runway Protection Zone (RPZ) is a trapezoidal area off each runway end, intended to enhance the protection of people and property on the ground by keeping the most hazardous overflight area clear of incompatible uses. RPZ dimensions are a function of the runway's approach category (visual, nonprecision, precision) and the design aircraft, so they change when the critical aircraft or the approach type changes. The rule is that the RPZ must be sized to the current or planned critical aircraft and approach, that the airport sponsor should control the RPZ land in fee or by easement to the extent practicable, and that land uses within the RPZ must be compatible. FAA guidance is explicit that residences, places of assembly, fuel storage, and other concentrations of people are incompatible within the RPZ. When the RPZ extends beyond airport property, the planner must pursue acquisition, easement, or compatible-use agreements rather than allowing incompatible development to encroach, because an encroached RPZ can jeopardize federal grant assurances and force the airport to reconfigure or shorten the runway.

### Apply Land Use Compatibility Guidelines With Specificity, Not as Generalities

FAA, HUD, and many states publish land use compatibility matrices that rate land use types against DNL bands (typically 65, 70, and 75 DNL). The rule is that these matrices are starting points that must be applied with local specificity: the compatibility determination should consider not just the use category but the density, the building construction type, the presence of noise-sensitive receptors (schools, hospitals, worship), and the availability of sound insulation. A single-family home and a 200-unit apartment building are both "residential," but they represent vastly different exposures and different mitigation feasibility. The compatibility plan should define which uses are prohibited, which are conditional (permitted with sound insulation or avigation easements), and which are compatible, and it should tie those determinations to specific contour bands and RPZ locations rather than leaving them to case-by-case discretion that erodes under development pressure.

### Coordinate Across the Airport Sponsor, the Local Jurisdiction, and the State

Compatibility planning almost always spans multiple jurisdictions, because airports and their influence areas rarely respect municipal boundaries, and because the authority to zone is usually local while the authority to operate the airport is the sponsor's. The rule is to establish a standing coordination mechanism — an airport land use commission, a joint planning review, or a memorandum of agreement — that gives the airport sponsor a formal role in reviewing proposed development within the influence area. In many states an airport influence area or airport safety zone is defined in statute, and development within it must be referred to the sponsor for comment. The planner should know whether such referral is mandatory in the jurisdiction, should ensure it actually happens for every applicable application, and should document the sponsor's comments in the record so that compatibility decisions are defensible and consistent over time.

### Maintain the Compatibility Regime Against Incremental Erosion

The greatest threat to compatibility is not a single bad decision but the accumulation of small approvals, each of which looks minor in isolation. A variance here, a rezoning there, a cell tower that "barely" penetrates the surface, a subdivision that "just" clips the 65 DNL contour. The rule is to treat the compatibility boundaries as hard lines that are not subject to variance, to require a formal compatibility finding for every application within the influence area, and to resist the political pressure to grant exceptions because the immediate impact "seems small." Each exception establishes a precedent that makes the next exception easier, and the cumulative effect is the loss of the airport's operational envelope and the creation of a noise-sensitive population that will later demand operational restrictions. The planner should maintain a compatibility case log so that the cumulative effect of approvals is visible and defensible.

## Common Traps

### Publishing Contours Without Enforceable Regulations

The airport completes a noise study, produces 65, 70, and 75 DNL contours, and publishes them in a compatibility document, but the local zoning is never amended to reference them. The trap is that the contours are treated as the deliverable when they are only the analytical basis for regulation. The mechanism is that the study and the zoning are handled by different entities with no mandate to connect them. The false signal is that "we have a compatibility plan." The harm is that development continues inside the contours exactly as before, the noise-sensitive population grows, and when residents later complain, the airport is blamed for impacts that the land use process failed to prevent. The remedy is to close the loop by requiring that every adopted contour set trigger a corresponding zoning amendment.

### Relying on FAA Part 77 Notice Instead of Local Height Zoning

A developer proposes a building near the airport, and the jurisdiction assumes the FAA will catch any airspace problem through the Form 7460-1 process. The trap is that the FAA process is advisory and cannot prohibit construction; it only issues determinations of no hazard or hazard. The mechanism is a misunderstanding of federal versus local authority. The false signal is that "the FAA reviewed it." The harm is that an object the FAA flagged can still be built if local zoning permits it, creating an obstruction that degrades instrument approaches, raises minimums, and is expensive to resolve after the fact. The remedy is a local airport overlay district with enforceable height limits and a permit condition requiring a favorable FAA determination.

### Undersizing the RPZ for the Current or Future Critical Aircraft

The RPZ is sized to an older, smaller critical aircraft, and when the airport begins to serve larger aircraft or adds a precision approach, the RPZ is not re-examined. The trap is that the RPZ dimensions are treated as fixed when they are a function of the design and approach conditions. The mechanism is that the master plan's critical aircraft update does not flow through to the RPZ and the land use controls. The false signal is that "the RPZ is already protected." The harm is that land which should be inside the RPZ remains in incompatible use, the airport falls out of compliance with grant assurances, and a future safety event exposes the gap. The remedy is to review RPZ sizing whenever the critical aircraft or approach type changes and to acquire or control the additional area.

### Permitting Conditional Uses Without the Required Mitigation

A residential subdivision is approved inside the 65 DNL contour on the condition that units receive sound insulation, but the insulation is never built, never inspected, or built to no verifiable standard. The trap is that the mitigation condition is treated as satisfied by the approval rather than by its implementation. The mechanism is that the permit is issued and the construction oversight is weak or absent. The false signal is that "the homes are sound-insulated." The harm is that residents experience the full noise exposure, believe they were protected, and become a hostile constituency, while the airport bears the political cost of a mitigation program that existed only on paper. The remedy is to require certified sound insulation, an avigation easement, and recorded disclosure before occupancy.

### Allowing Incompatible Uses in the RPZ Through Easement Misunderstanding

The airport obtains an avigation or clearance easement over RPZ land and treats the easement as making the use compatible. The trap is that an easement grants overflight and clearance rights but does not remove the people or structures from the hazard area. The mechanism is confusion between airspace control and land use compatibility. The false signal is that "the RPZ is clear because we hold an easement." The harm is that incompatible concentrations of people remain in the most hazardous overflight zone, the RPZ does not serve its safety function, and the arrangement may not satisfy FAA grant assurances. The remedy is to distinguish fee acquisition and physical clearance from easement rights, and to prioritize removal of incompatible uses within the inner RPZ.

### Segmenting Compatibility Review Across Parcels

A large development is reviewed parcel by parcel, and no single parcel appears to have a significant compatibility problem, even though the aggregate development inside the contour is substantial. The trap is that segmentation hides the cumulative incompatibility. The mechanism is that each application is evaluated in isolation without reference to the others. The false signal is that "each parcel is compatible." The harm is that the cumulative noise exposure population grows to a level that drives operational restrictions, and the incremental approvals made it impossible to see the trend. The remedy is to maintain a running compatibility case log and to evaluate each new application against the cumulative exposure, not just the parcel.

## Self-Check

- Have the current DNL contours been translated into enforceable zoning, disclosure, and review triggers, rather than merely published in a study?
- Does the local height hazard zoning or airport overlay district codify the FAA Part 77 imaginary surfaces with specific dimensions and a Form 7460-1 permit condition?
- Is the RPZ sized to the current or planned critical aircraft and approach type, and is the land within it controlled by the sponsor in fee or by appropriate easement?
- Are land use compatibility determinations tied to specific contour bands, RPZ locations, density, and receptor sensitivity, rather than left to case-by-case discretion?
- Is there a formal referral or coordination mechanism that gives the airport sponsor a documented role in reviewing development within the influence area?
- Does the compatibility regime treat the contour and surface boundaries as hard lines not subject to variance, with a logged record of every approval?
- For any conditional use approved with sound insulation, is there a certified, inspected standard and a recorded disclosure or avigation easement before occupancy?
- Has the cumulative effect of incremental approvals been tracked so that erosion of the compatibility envelope is visible and defensible?
