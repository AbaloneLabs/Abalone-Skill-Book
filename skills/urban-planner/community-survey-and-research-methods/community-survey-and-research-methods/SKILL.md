# Community Survey and Research Methods

Community survey and research methods cover the systematic gathering, analysis, and interpretation of public input for planning decisions. The judgment problem is that survey work sits between two failure modes: treating any input as representative because it was gathered, and dismissing all input as unscientific because it was not a probability sample. Both errors corrupt planning decisions. The first over-weights loud, self-selected voices and calls them the community; the second discards legitimate signal because it did not come from a peer-reviewed instrument. The planner's job is to design research that is fit for purpose, to be honest about what each method can and cannot support, and to prevent engagement data from being used to justify decisions it cannot actually defend.

The latitude here is real but bounded by methodological integrity. A planning process rarely has the budget or time for academic-grade survey research, and many useful questions can be answered with pragmatic, lower-rigor methods. But every method has known weaknesses, and misrepresenting a weak method as a strong one - or letting decision-makers treat it as such - is the core professional risk. The skill is in matching method to question, documenting limitations, and ensuring that the conclusions drawn are supported by the evidence gathered.

## Core Rules

### Match the Method to the Decision Question

Different planning questions require different evidence. A question about whether a specific policy has majority support across the whole community requires a probability-based survey with known sampling error. A question about what concerns residents most requires a broader mix of qualitative and quantitative methods. A question about how a specific neighborhood experiences a corridor requires targeted geographic sampling and qualitative depth. A question about tradeoffs between values requires deliberative methods that let participants engage with information. Before selecting a method, state the decision question precisely and identify what kind of evidence would actually change the decision. Then choose the method that can produce that evidence, and be explicit when no affordable method can.

### Distinguish Scientific From Non-Scientific Input

The single most important classification in community research is whether the data come from a probability sample of the relevant population (scientific, generalizable) or from a self-selected or convenience sample (non-scientific, not generalizable). Open-comment forms, online polls, workshop dot-voting, comment letters, and most pop-up engagement produce non-scientific input. This does not make the input worthless - it can surface concerns, identify the range of opinion, and reveal intensity of feeling - but it cannot support claims about what the community as a whole thinks or wants. Label every dataset with its method and its generalizability, and never present self-selected results as representative. The harm from mislabeling is not just statistical; it transfers decision authority to whoever was motivated enough to respond.

### Design Probability Surveys for Validity, Not Just Response Rate

When a probability survey is warranted, its value depends on sampling quality, not response rate alone. Use a defensible sampling frame: address-based sampling, random-digit dialing, or a registered-voter list depending on the target population and the question. Track and report response rate, contact rate, and cooperation rate separately. Plan for nonresponse bias analysis: compare the demographic profile of respondents to the best available population benchmark (Census ACS for the general public, registered-voter file for electoral questions), and weight the data to correct measurable skew. A survey with a high response rate from a skewed frame is no more representative than one with a low response rate from a correct frame. Document the frame, the weighting, and the achieved margin of error.

### Write Instruments That Measure Rather Than Lead

Survey and interview instruments shape the answers they receive. Leading questions, loaded terms, double-barreled items, forced choices that omit real options, and question order effects all produce results that reflect the instrument more than the respondent's actual views. Pre-test instruments with cognitive interviewing to confirm that respondents understand the questions as intended. Balance question wording, randomize or rotate response options where order effects are likely, and avoid combining two questions into one. Provide a "don't know" or neutral option where forcing a position would distort the result. Document the full instrument and the rationale for key wordings, because the wording is part of the evidence and must be available for review.

### Use Qualitative Methods for Depth, Not for Headcounts

Focus groups, interviews, and open-ended survey questions produce rich, contextual understanding of how people think about an issue, what language they use, and what concerns them. They do not produce frequencies that can be generalized. A theme that appears in fifteen of twenty interviews is not "75 percent of the community"; it is a finding about the salience of that theme among the people interviewed. Analyze qualitative data systematically - using a codebook, multiple coders, and documented coding decisions - so that the analysis is reproducible and not simply the analyst's impressions. Report qualitative findings as themes, range of opinion, and illustrative quotes, with clear description of who was sampled and why, never as percentages of the whole.

### Recruit Inclusively and Document Who Was Reached

Representativeness in engagement is not only a sampling problem; it is an access problem. People differ in their availability, language, childcare needs, trust of government, digital access, and comfort with formal processes. Design recruitment and participation options to reduce barriers: multiple languages, multiple times and formats, childcare and stipends where appropriate, community-based partners as trusted intermediaries, and digital and non-digital channels. Track who participated against the population, and where the participation skews, say so. A process that reaches only the usual participants and reports their views as community input reproduces existing power imbalances. Document the recruitment effort, the achieved participation profile, and the gaps.

### Separate Preferences From Constraints

Community input is most useful when it is paired with the constraints that will shape the decision: legal authority, budget, market feasibility, environmental limits, and policy commitments already made. Presenting preferences without constraints produces wish lists; presenting constraints without preferences produces technocratic plans with no legitimacy. Structure engagement so that participants encounter the relevant constraints and can express informed preferences, and structure the analysis so that decision-makers see both the public's preferences and the constraints that bound the feasible options. Where a strongly expressed preference conflicts with a binding constraint, surface the conflict explicitly rather than quietly setting the preference aside.

### Analyze and Report With Methodological Transparency

The credibility of engagement data depends on the transparency of the methods and the honesty about limitations. Report the method, the sampling frame, the response and cooperation rates, the weighting approach, the achieved sample size and margin of error, the instrument, the coding approach for qualitative data, and the participation profile. State what the data can and cannot support. Where findings are contested or ambiguous, present the range rather than a single interpretation. Make raw data and instruments available for review where privacy permits. A report that omits methodology invites decision-makers and the public to treat the findings as more or less definitive than they deserve.

### Avoid Using Engagement Data to Ratify Predetermined Decisions

The most damaging misuse of community research is the collection of input that is then ignored or selectively cited to support a decision already made. This is corrosive because it consumes public trust and time while producing no genuine influence on the outcome. If a decision is genuinely constrained and input will not change it, do not frame engagement as if it will. If input is sought, commit in advance to how it will be used, report back to participants what was heard and what changed as a result, and document where input diverged from the decision and why. The integrity of the feedback loop is what distinguishes engagement from theater.

## Common Traps

### Presenting Self-Selected Input as Representative

The mechanism is that online comment forms and open surveys are easy to deploy and produce large response counts, which look authoritative. The false signal is a large dataset that appears to reflect the community. The harm is that the views of motivated respondents - often opponents of a change, organized groups, or digitally connected populations - are treated as the community's will, and quieter or less-connected constituencies are overridden. Always label self-selected data as non-scientific and report who responded.

### Treating High Response Count as High Representativeness

The mechanism is that response volume is visible and easy to report, while representativeness requires demographic analysis that is harder. The false signal is a big number that implies authority. The harm is that a thousand responses from a skewed population are treated as more valid than three hundred from a representative sample. Report and weight for representativeness, not volume.

### Leading Questions That Produce the Desired Answer

The mechanism is that question wording can be shaped, intentionally or not, to elicit a particular response, and the resulting numbers look objective. The false signal is a quantified result that appears to measure public opinion. The harm is that the decision is justified by data that actually reflects the instrument. Pre-test instruments and document wording rationale.

### Reporting Qualitative Themes as Percentages

The mechanism is that counting mentions of a theme across interviews or comments produces a number, and the number looks like a frequency. The false signal is a quantified share of opinion. The harm is that the intensity or prevalence of a theme among a small, purposive sample is reported as if it were the share of the community holding that view. Report qualitative findings as themes and range, not as generalizable percentages.

### Conflating the Loud With the Numerous

The mechanism is that opponents of change are typically more motivated to participate than supporters or the indifferent, so the visible input skews negative. The false signal is that the community opposes the change. The harm is that a silent or less-mobilized majority is overridden by an organized minority, or that a needed policy is abandoned based on non-representative intensity. Use scientific methods where the decision requires them, and interpret intensity as intensity, not as prevalence.

### Failing to Report Who Was Not Reached

The mechanism is that reporting focuses on who participated, and the gaps are less visible. The false signal is a complete picture of community input. The harm is that under-reached constituencies - often lower-income, non-English-speaking, younger, or less trusting of government - are silently excluded from the evidence base. Always report the participation profile against the population and name the gaps.

### Selectively Citing Input to Support a Predetermined Decision

The mechanism is that engagement produces a range of views, and the analyst or decision-maker cites only the views that support the preferred outcome. The false signal is evidence-based decision-making. The harm is that public trust is eroded and the engagement is exposed as performative. Commit in advance to how input will be used and report the full range of findings.

## Self-Check

- Has the decision question been stated precisely, and is the selected method capable of producing evidence that would actually change the decision?
- Is every dataset labeled with its method and its generalizability, with self-selected input clearly distinguished from probability-based results?
- For any probability survey, are the sampling frame, response and cooperation rates, weighting approach, sample size, and margin of error documented, and has nonresponse bias been assessed against a population benchmark?
- Have instruments been pre-tested, and is the full instrument with wording rationale available for review?
- Are qualitative findings reported as themes, range, and illustrative evidence with a documented coding approach, rather than as generalizable percentages?
- Does the participation profile match the relevant population on key demographics, and where it skews, are the gaps named and recruitment efforts documented?
- Are community preferences presented alongside the legal, fiscal, market, and environmental constraints that bound feasible options?
- Does the methodology report state what the data can and cannot support, and are raw data and instruments available for review where privacy permits?
- Has a commitment been made in advance to how input will be used, with a plan to report back to participants what was heard and what changed?
- Where strongly expressed input conflicts with a binding constraint, is the conflict surfaced and explained rather than quietly set aside?
