# Survey Sampling and Representativeness in Planning Research

A community survey produces numbers — percent support, percent opposition, average rating — and the numbers feel authoritative because they are quantitative, but their authority depends entirely on whether the respondents represent the population the survey claims to describe. A survey of the people who showed up to a public meeting, or who responded to an online link shared in a newsletter, or who answered a mailer sent to a non-random list, does not represent the community; it represents the people who were reachable and motivated through that channel, and that group is systematically different from the community as a whole. The judgment problem is that planning decisions are often justified by survey results that the sampling method cannot support, and the misrepresentation is invisible because the numbers look rigorous while the sample is biased. A survey that over-represents homeowners, older adults, long-term residents, English speakers, and higher-income groups — the groups that are easiest to reach and most likely to respond — will produce results that systematically misstate the community's preferences, and planning decisions based on those results will serve the over-represented groups while the under-represented groups (renters, younger adults, recent residents, non-English speakers, lower-income groups) are excluded from the findings. This skill covers the sampling, recruitment, and representativeness decisions that determine whether survey results can be generalized to the planning population. It complements the survey-design skill by focusing on who is in the sample, rather than on what is asked.

## Core Rules

### Select the Sampling Method to Match the Inference the Survey Must Support

The sampling method determines what the survey's results can support, and the method must be selected to match the inference the planning decision requires. The rule is to select the sampling method based on the inferential goal: if the survey must support a population-level inference (what does the community think), a probability-based sample (where every member of the population has a known, non-zero chance of selection) is required, because only probability samples support statistical generalization; if the survey is exploratory or developmental (gathering input to inform a plan, not to measure opinion), a non-probability sample (convenience, purposive, snowball) may be acceptable, but its results must be reported as input from the respondents, not as the community's opinion. The probability-based options include address-based sampling (mailing to a random sample of addresses, which covers households that lack phones or internet), random-digit-dial phone sampling (which under-covers cell-only and non-response households), and panel-based online sampling (which under-covers the offline population). The non-probability options include convenience sampling (whoever responds to an open link), purposive sampling (recruiting specific groups to ensure representation), and snowball sampling (respondents recruit peers). The method should be selected deliberately, and the limitations of whatever method is chosen should be reported with the results, because the most common error is using a non-probability sample (an open online survey) and reporting the results as if they represented the community.

### Determine Sample Size Through Power Analysis, Not Rule of Thumb

Sample size determines the precision of the survey's estimates — the margin of error — and the size must be determined through analysis of the precision the decision requires, not through a rule of thumb (a thousand respondents, five percent of the population). The rule is to determine sample size through a power analysis that considers the population size, the desired margin of error, the confidence level, and the expected variation in the responses, because these factors interact: a larger population requires only a slightly larger sample (the sample size does not scale linearly with population), a smaller margin of error requires a much larger sample (halving the margin quadruples the sample), and greater variation in responses requires a larger sample to estimate precisely. The power analysis should also consider the subgroups the survey must analyze: if the planning decision requires separate estimates for neighborhoods, income groups, or racial groups, the sample must be large enough in each subgroup to support the estimate, and the total sample must be correspondingly larger. A survey that is sized for a community-level estimate but used to compare neighborhoods will produce subgroup estimates with margins of error so large that the comparisons are meaningless, and the planning decisions based on those comparisons will be unsupported. The sample size should be determined before the survey is fielded, and the expected precision should be reported with the results, so that the decision-makers know what the numbers can and cannot support.

### Recruit to Reach the Under-Represented, Not Just the Easy to Reach

Even a probability sample will under-represent some groups, because response rates differ systematically: older adults, homeowners, and higher-income groups respond at higher rates, while younger adults, renters, lower-income groups, and non-English speakers respond at lower rates. The rule is to recruit actively to reach the under-represented, using targeted outreach (in-language materials, community-based recruitment, incentives, multiple contact modes) to raise the response rates among the groups that are typically under-represented, rather than accepting the response pattern that passive recruitment produces. The recruitment should be informed by an analysis of who is likely to be under-represented in the specific community and survey, and the outreach should be designed to reach those groups through the channels they use (not the channels the planning agency prefers). The recruitment should use incentives (monetary or non-monetary) to raise response rates, particularly among lower-income groups for whom the time cost of survey participation is a real burden, and the incentives should be calibrated to the population and the survey length. The recruitment should also use multiple modes (mail, phone, online, in-person) to reach populations that are not reachable through a single mode, because the cell-only household, the offline household, and the household that does not respond to mail each require a different approach. A survey that recruits passively will systematically over-represent the easy-to-reach groups, and the results will misstate the community's preferences in ways that the passive recruitment did not even attempt to correct.

### Evaluate and Report Representativeness Honestly

Every survey sample is imperfect, and the question is not whether the sample is representative but how unrepresentative it is, in what direction, and what the bias means for the results. The rule is to evaluate and report representativeness honestly, comparing the sample's demographics (age, income, race, tenure, geography) to the population's demographics (from the census or the American Community Survey), identifying the groups that are over- and under-represented, and reporting the direction and likely magnitude of the bias. The representativeness evaluation should be reported with the results, not buried in an appendix, because the decision-makers must know whether the survey over-represents the groups that benefit from or oppose the planning decision. Where the bias is substantial, the results should be weighted (adjusting the responses to match the population demographics) or reported with explicit caveats (the survey over-represents homeowners, so the results may overstate opposition to housing), because reporting unweighted, unexamined results as the community's opinion is a misrepresentation. The honest reporting should acknowledge what the sample cannot support: a survey of meeting attendees cannot support a community-level inference, regardless of how many attended; an online survey cannot represent the offline population; a survey in English only cannot represent the non-English-speaking population. The honesty about representativeness is what distinguishes a survey that informs the planning decision from a survey that misleads it.

### Interpret Results Within the Limits the Sample Supports

The final rule is to interpret the results within the limits the sampling method, the sample size, and the representativeness support, and to resist the temptation to overstate what the numbers mean. The rule is to interpret results with explicit attention to the margin of error (a 52-48 split may be within the margin and therefore not a meaningful majority), the subgroup sample sizes (a neighborhood comparison based on 30 respondents per neighborhood is not reliable), the representativeness (a result that over-represents homeowners may overstate opposition), and the mode effects (an online survey may produce different results than a phone survey of the same population). The interpretation should distinguish the strong findings (a 70-30 split with a representative sample and a small margin) from the weak findings (a 52-48 split with a biased sample and a large margin), and the planning decisions should be calibrated to the strength of the evidence. The interpretation should also integrate the survey results with other evidence (demographic data, qualitative engagement, existing conditions), because a survey is one source among several, and the planning decision should not rest on a single survey whose limitations are substantial. The discipline of interpreting within the limits is what prevents survey results from becoming a false authority that drives planning decisions beyond what the evidence supports.

## Common Traps

### Non-Probability Sample Reported as Community Opinion

An open online survey, a survey of meeting attendees, or a survey of a newsletter's subscribers is reported as "the community's opinion," when it represents only the respondents, who are systematically different from the community. The trap is that non-probability results feel quantitative and therefore authoritative. The mechanism is that the sampling method was non-probability, but the results were reported without the limitation. The false signal is that "the survey shows what the community thinks." The harm is that the planning decision is based on a biased sample, the under-represented groups are excluded from the findings, the decision serves the over-represented groups, and the survey provides false authority for an inequitable outcome.

### Sample Sized for the Community but Used for Subgroup Comparisons

The survey is sized to produce a community-level estimate, but the results are used to compare neighborhoods or demographic groups, and the subgroup samples are too small to support the comparisons, producing estimates with margins of error so large that the differences are meaningless. The trap is that subgroup analysis on an undersized sample produces misleading precision. The mechanism is that the sample size was determined for the community level, not for the subgroups. The false signal is that "we have neighborhood-level results." The harm is that the subgroup comparisons are unreliable, the planning decisions based on them are unsupported, and the apparent precision of the numbers masks the uncertainty that the small samples cannot resolve.

### Passive Recruitment Over-Representing the Easy to Reach

The survey relies on passive recruitment (an open link, a newsletter, a mailing to a general list), and the respondents over-represent the groups that are easy to reach and motivated to respond, while the under-represented groups are absent from the findings. The trap is that passive recruitment produces a biased sample without any deliberate bias. The mechanism is that the recruitment did not target the under-represented, and the response pattern reflected the differential response rates. The false signal is that "we publicized the survey widely." The harm is that the sample is systematically biased, the results misstate the community's preferences, the under-represented groups are excluded, and the planning decision serves the over-represented groups.

### Representativeness Not Evaluated or Reported

The survey's results are reported without any evaluation of whether the sample represents the population, and the decision-makers treat the numbers as authoritative without knowing the bias. The trap is that unreported bias is invisible bias. The mechanism is that the representativeness was not evaluated, or was evaluated but not reported. The false signal is that "the survey results are the findings." The harm is that the decision-makers do not know the sample's limitations, they treat biased results as community opinion, the planning decision is based on a misrepresentation, and the under-represented groups bear the consequences of a decision that excluded their input.

### Results Overstated Beyond What the Sample Supports

The survey's results are reported and interpreted with a precision and authority that the sampling method, sample size, and representativeness do not support, and the planning decision is driven by numbers that are weaker than they appear. The trap is that quantitative results invite overstatement. The mechanism is that the interpretation did not attend to the margin of error, the subgroup sizes, the representativeness, or the mode effects. The false signal is that "the numbers are clear." The harm is that the planning decision is based on overstated evidence, the decision may be wrong because the evidence was weaker than assumed, and the survey provides false confidence that displaces the other evidence (qualitative, demographic) that should inform the decision.

## Self-Check

- Is the sampling method selected to match the inference the planning decision requires, with probability sampling for population-level inference and the limitations of non-probability sampling reported?
- Is the sample size determined through power analysis for the community level and for the subgroups the decision requires, not through a rule of thumb?
- Does the recruitment actively target the under-represented groups, through targeted outreach, incentives, and multiple modes, rather than accepting passive recruitment's bias?
- Is the representativeness evaluated and reported honestly, comparing the sample to the population, identifying the bias, and reporting the direction and likely magnitude?
- Are the results interpreted within the limits the sample supports, attending to margin of error, subgroup sizes, representativeness, and mode effects, and integrated with other evidence?
- If the survey's results were reported as "the community's opinion," would the sampling method actually support that claim, or would it overstate what the sample can bear?
- Are the subgroup comparisons based on samples large enough to support them, or are the margins of error so large that the differences are meaningless?
- Do the respondents represent the community's demographics, or do they systematically over-represent the easy-to-reach groups?
- Is the representativeness evaluation reported with the results, so the decision-makers know the sample's limitations, or is it buried or omitted?
- Are the strong findings distinguished from the weak findings, and is the planning decision calibrated to the strength of the evidence?
