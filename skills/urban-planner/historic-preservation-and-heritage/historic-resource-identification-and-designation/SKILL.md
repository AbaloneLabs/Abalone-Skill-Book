# Historic Resource Identification and Designation

Historic designation is an irreversible legal act that constrains future owners and shapes a community's heritage for generations, so the decision of what to designate deserves far more rigor than "this building looks old." The judgment problem is not how to admire old buildings but how to apply consistent, defensible criteria to determine which resources possess the significance and integrity to warrant legal protection, how to survey comprehensively enough to avoid missing resources (especially those of underrepresented communities), and how to write designation documents that will withstand challenge and guide future review. Planners commonly fail by treating designation as an aesthetic preference rather than a significance determination, by surveying only architecturally grand resources while missing those significant for social, cultural, or community history, or by writing vague designation reports that cannot anchor later design review. Rigorous identification and designation is what makes preservation lawful, equitable, and durable.

## Core Rules

### Apply Significance Criteria Systematically, Not Aesthetically

Historic significance is a legal and analytical determination, not a taste judgment. Use the established criteria — association with significant events, association with significant persons, embodiment of distinctive design or construction, or potential to yield information — and evaluate each resource against them with evidence, not impression. A building can be unremarkable architecturally but highly significant for its association with a community's civil rights history, a labor movement, or an immigrant enclave; conversely, a handsome building may lack the significance or integrity to merit designation. Document the specific facts (dates, persons, events, design lineage, construction context) that establish significance, because the designation must be defensible against owners who will challenge it and must guide reviewers for decades.

### Assess Integrity Honestly Against the Period of Significance

Significance alone is not enough; the resource must retain enough integrity — in location, design, setting, materials, workmanship, feeling, and association — to convey that significance. A building significant for its 1920s commercial role that has been reskinned, expanded, and re-set among incompatible new construction may have lost the integrity to convey that significance, even if the history is real. Be honest about integrity losses, because over-designating resources that lack it weakens the whole system and invites successful challenges. Define the period of significance precisely and assess what the resource looked like then versus now; the gap between the two is the integrity question.

### Survey Comprehensively, Including Underrepresented Histories

A survey that captures only the grand civic buildings and architect-designed homes of the wealthy will systematically miss the heritage of communities whose history is embodied in more modest structures — workers' housing, minority business districts, sites of community organizing, places of worship of immigrant communities, LGBTQ+ landmarks, women's history sites. These resources are often the most threatened because they are not recognized as significant. Survey with an explicit commitment to identifying resources significant to underrepresented communities, partner with community historians and cultural organizations, and use thematic surveys (civil rights, labor, immigration, women's history) to surface resources that a building-by-building aesthetic survey would miss. An incomplete survey produces an incomplete — and inequitable — preservation program.

### Define the Boundaries and Character-Defining Features Precisely

A designation that does not specify exactly what is protected and why invites decades of dispute. Define the district or resource boundaries with precision and justify them (why this edge and not the next street?). Identify and describe the character-defining features — the specific materials, forms, massing, setbacks, streetscapes, or features that convey significance — so that future design review can distinguish changes that matter from changes that do not. A designation report that lists a building's features without distinguishing the character-defining ones from the incidental ones gives reviewers no anchor and gives owners no clarity. The precision of the designation document is the precision of all future preservation decisions.

### Distinguish the Levels of Designation and Their Consequences

Resources can be listed locally (under a municipal ordinance), nationally (National Register), or both, and the consequences differ sharply. Local designation typically carries the binding review authority over changes and demolition; National Register listing is primarily honorific for private owners but triggers review for federally assisted projects (Section 106). Be clear about which level of designation you are pursuing and what each does and does not do. Owners often fear National Register listing because they misunderstand it as controlling private actions; conversely, local designation that is described as merely "honorific" when it actually controls demolition invites legal challenge. State the consequences accurately.

### Anticipate Owner Opposition and Build a Defensible Record

Designation over owner objection is legally permissible in most jurisdictions but requires an exceptionally strong record: clear significance findings, integrity analysis, boundary justification, and compliance with procedural requirements (notice, hearing, vote thresholds). Anticipate that owners will challenge on significance, on integrity, on procedural grounds, and on economic hardship or takings theories. Build the designation document to withstand these challenges, document the methodology, and ensure procedural steps are followed meticulously. A designation that survives is one that was prepared as if it would be litigated, because it often is.

## Common Traps

### Designating for Aesthetics Rather Than Significance

Choosing resources because they are attractive or beloved, without establishing the historical, architectural, or cultural significance the criteria require, produces designations vulnerable to challenge and inconsistent to administer. The false signal is community affection; the harm is designations that cannot be defended and that erode the credibility of the preservation program. Anchor every designation in documented significance against the criteria.

### Missing Underrepresented Histories in the Survey

Surveys that focus on architectural pedigree systematically exclude the heritage of working-class, minority, immigrant, women's, and LGBTQ+ communities, whose significant sites are often modest or unmarked. The false signal is a "comprehensive" survey of notable buildings; the harm is a preservation inventory that protects the heritage of the powerful while erasing the heritage of the marginalized. Use thematic and community-based surveys to surface underrepresented resources.

### Over-Designating Resources That Lack Integrity

Designating resources that have lost too much of their historic fabric or setting, out of enthusiasm or political pressure, inflates the inventory with resources that cannot convey their significance and that generate disputes over every alteration. The false signal is a growing list of protected resources; the harm is a weaker, less defensible preservation system. Apply integrity standards honestly, even when it means not designating a beloved but altered resource.

### Vague Designation Documents

Reports that describe a building in general terms without identifying its specific character-defining features leave reviewers without guidance and owners without clarity, producing inconsistent decisions and appeals. The false signal is a completed designation; the harm is decades of avoidable dispute because no one knows exactly what is protected. Specify the features, materials, and forms that convey significance and must be preserved.

### Conflating Local and National Designation Consequences

Telling owners that National Register listing has no effect on their property, when federal undertakings may trigger review, or conversely treating an honorific listing as if it controlled private demolition, misrepresents the legal consequences and breeds either false fear or false security. The false signal is a clear "what designation means" summary; the harm is owner mistrust and procedural vulnerability. State the consequences of each designation level accurately and in writing.

## Self-Check

- Is each designation grounded in documented significance against established criteria (events, persons, design, information) rather than aesthetic preference?
- Have you honestly assessed the resource's integrity against its period of significance, and declined to designate where integrity is insufficient?
- Did the survey use thematic and community-based methods to identify resources significant to underrepresented communities, not just architecturally notable buildings?
- Does the designation document precisely define boundaries and identify the specific character-defining features that future review must protect?
- Are you clear about which level of designation (local, National Register, both) is being pursued and what each legally does and does not do?
- Was the designation prepared to withstand owner challenge, with a strong significance and integrity record and meticulous procedural compliance?
- Did you avoid designating resources primarily because they are attractive or beloved, without the required significance basis?
- Did you avoid inflating the inventory with resources that lack the integrity to convey their significance?
- Could a future reviewer use the designation document to distinguish changes that affect character-defining features from those that do not?
- If challenged, would the designation's significance findings, integrity analysis, boundary justification, and procedural record withstand review?
