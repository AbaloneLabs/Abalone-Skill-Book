# Demolition Review and Economic Hardship

Demolition is the irreversible outcome preservation review exists to prevent, and the decision to allow or deny it is where the system's integrity is tested. The judgment problem is not "never demolish" but how to apply the legal criteria for demolition honestly — distinguishing genuine hardship and public-safety cases from strategic demolition claims designed to clear a site for redevelopment — and how to weigh the loss of a resource against the public interest, the owner's legitimate constraints, and the availability of alternatives. Planners commonly fail by treating demolition review as a rubber stamp when an owner claims hardship, by failing to investigate neglect as a cause of "deterioration," or by denying demolitions without the evidentiary record needed to sustain the denial. Rigorous demolition review is what gives preservation teeth; careless review either loses resources or loses in court.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Demolition Criteria as an Evidentiary Test, Not a Discretionary Call

Most ordinances allow demolition only under specific criteria — typically that the resource cannot yield a reasonable return, cannot be adapted for a viable use, poses a safety threat that cannot be mitigated, or is of such low significance that its loss is acceptable. Treat each criterion as an evidentiary test requiring proof, not as a basis for a judgment call. Require the owner to submit the financial analysis, marketing history, rehabilitation estimates, and alternatives considered that the criteria demand, and evaluate that evidence critically. A claim of hardship unsupported by documentation is not a finding; it is an assertion. The rigor of the evidentiary record is what makes a demolition decision — either way — defensible.

### Investigate Demolition by Neglect and Hold Owners Accountable

A common pattern is an owner allowing a designated resource to deteriorate — through deferred maintenance, vacancy, or active neglect — and then citing the resulting deterioration as grounds for demolition. This is demolition by neglect, and many ordinances provide remedies: minimum maintenance requirements, code enforcement, receivership, or denial of a demolition permit where the condition was owner-caused. Investigate the maintenance history (permits, code violations, complaints, aerial photos over time) to determine whether the deterioration was avoidable. Where neglect is the cause, the appropriate response is enforcement and repair, not demolition. Failing to investigate neglect rewards the strategy and incentivizes more of it.

### Evaluate Economic Hardship with Real Financial Analysis

An economic hardship claim asserts that preserving the resource prevents any reasonable economic use of the property. Evaluate this with actual financial evidence: the owner's acquisition and investment history, operating revenues and costs, rehabilitation cost estimates from qualified professionals, marketing efforts to sell or lease, alternative uses analyzed, and the rate of return with and without preservation. Compare the return under preservation to a reasonable return standard, not to the maximum return the owner could achieve by demolishing and redeveloping. Hardship is not "I could make more money with a new building"; it is "preservation prevents any reasonable economic use." Many claims fail this test when examined, and the analysis should show why.

### Require Consideration of Alternatives Before Approving Demolition

Before demolition is permitted, the owner should demonstrate that preservation is genuinely infeasible, which requires showing that alternatives were considered and rejected for documented reasons: rehabilitation for the existing use, adaptive reuse for a new use, partial demolition retaining the character-defining features, relocation of the resource, sale to a preservation-minded buyer, or phased investment. An owner who has not explored alternatives has not established that demolition is necessary. Document which alternatives were considered, why each was rejected, and whether the rejections were substantive or pretextual.

### Distinguish Significance Levels in the Demolition Decision

Not all designated resources are equal. Demolition of a resource of exceptional significance — individually listed, pivotal to a district, the last survivor of an important type — warrants the highest scrutiny and the strongest denial absent truly compelling grounds. Demolition of a marginally contributing resource, where the loss would not undermine the district's character, may be more defensibly allowed, especially if mitigated. Calibrate the burden of proof and the weight of preservation to the resource's significance, and document the significance finding as part of the demolition decision. Treating all resources identically both over-protects the marginal and under-protects the exceptional.

### Mitigate Unavoidable Loss Through Documentation and Salvage

When demolition is approved, require mitigation that captures what will be lost: Historic American Buildings Survey (HABS) or equivalent documentation (photographs, drawings, history), salvage of significant materials and features for reuse, archaeological monitoring during demolition, and interpretive measures where appropriate. Mitigation does not justify the loss, but it ensures the resource is recorded for the historical record and that significant fabric is preserved. Make mitigation a condition of the demolition permit, enforceable before demolition proceeds.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Unsupported Hardship Claims

When an owner asserts that preservation is infeasible without submitting the financial evidence the criteria require, and the reviewer approves demolition on that assertion, the system has no integrity. The false signal is respecting owner property rights; the harm is that demolition becomes available to any owner willing to claim hardship, and designated resources have no real protection. Require and critically evaluate the evidentiary record before reaching a hardship finding.

### Failing to Investigate Demolition by Neglect

Accepting deterioration at face value, without examining its cause, rewards owners who allow resources to decay and then demolish them. The false signal is "the building is in poor condition"; the harm is that neglect becomes a reliable demolition strategy and the inventory erodes. Investigate maintenance history and apply minimum maintenance and code enforcement where the condition was owner-caused.

### Comparing Hardship to Maximum Rather Than Reasonable Return

Evaluating hardship by comparing preservation returns to the returns from new development sets the bar so high that any developable site would qualify for demolition. The false signal is a fair financial comparison; the harm is that hardship becomes synonymous with "redevelopment would be more profitable," which is always true and means preservation never wins. Compare to a reasonable return standard, not the highest-and-best-use return.

### Skipping Alternatives Analysis

Approving demolition without requiring the owner to show that rehabilitation, adaptive reuse, partial retention, relocation, or sale were considered and genuinely infeasible treats demolition as the default rather than the last resort. The false signal is efficiency; the harm is the loss of resources that could have been saved with an alternative the owner never explored. Require documented alternatives analysis before approving demolition.

### Treating All Designated Resources Identically

Applying the same demolition scrutiny to a marginally contributing resource and to a landmark of exceptional significance misallocates effort and undermines credibility. The false signal is consistent, even-handed review; the harm is that exceptional resources do not receive the heightened protection their significance warrants, while marginal cases consume disproportionate review capacity. Calibrate the burden of proof to the resource's significance.

## Self-Check

- Did you require the owner to submit and you critically evaluate the financial, marketing, and rehabilitation evidence the demolition criteria demand, rather than accepting unsupported claims?
- Did you investigate the maintenance history to determine whether deterioration was owner-caused (demolition by neglect) and apply enforcement remedies where it was?
- For a hardship claim, did you compare the return under preservation to a reasonable return standard, not to the maximum return achievable through redevelopment?
- Did you require documented consideration of alternatives (rehabilitation, adaptive reuse, partial retention, relocation, sale) before treating demolition as justified?
- Did you calibrate the burden of proof and the weight of preservation to the resource's significance, treating exceptional resources with the highest scrutiny?
- Where demolition was approved, did you require and enforce mitigation (HABS documentation, salvage, archaeological monitoring, interpretation) as a condition?
- Did you avoid approving demolition on assertion-based hardship claims lacking the required evidentiary record?
- Did you avoid accepting deterioration at face value without examining whether it resulted from owner neglect?
- Could a reviewer or court follow the evidentiary basis for the demolition decision — whether approval or denial — from the record?
- Does the decision treat demolition as the last resort after alternatives and hardship are genuinely tested, rather than as a default available on request?
