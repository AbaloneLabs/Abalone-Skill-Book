# Design Review in Historic Districts

Design review in a historic context is one of the most judgment-intensive tasks in planning, because it requires distinguishing between changes that harm a resource's historic character and changes that are compatible or even necessary — and explaining that distinction defensibly to owners who may see only obstruction. The judgment problem is not "preserve everything as it was" but how to apply the established standards to specific proposals, how to balance preservation against legitimate needs (accessibility, energy efficiency, safety, economic viability), and how to write review decisions that guide owners rather than frustrate them. Planners commonly fail by applying personal taste under the banner of standards, by treating the Standards as absolute prohibitions rather than guidance, or by opposing every contemporary intervention and thus driving owners to seek demolition or hardship exemptions. Skilled historic design review preserves what matters while allowing resources to remain useful and alive.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Secretary of the Interior's Standards as Guidance, Not Prohibition

The Standards for Rehabilitation are the prevailing framework, and they are guidance about how to undertake changes while preserving character — not a flat prohibition on alteration. Read each standard carefully and apply it to the specific proposal: distinguishing character-defining features from non-significant ones, prioritizing repair over replacement, minimizing damage to historic materials, ensuring additions are compatible but distinguishable, and reversing the false assumption that any visible change is prohibited. The Standards explicitly contemplate new use, new additions, and new code-required work; the question is how, not whether. Reviewers who treat the Standards as a veto misapply them and produce denials that cannot be sustained.

### Identify and Protect Character-Defining Features First

Before evaluating any proposal, identify the resource's character-defining features — the specific materials, forms, craftsmanship, spatial arrangements, and features that convey its significance (as established in the designation). Review the proposal against its effect on those features: a change to a character-defining storefront matters more than a change to a non-historic rear addition. This focus prevents both over-protection (treating every element as sacrosanct) and under-protection (allowing damage to the features that actually convey significance). Tie every review comment to a specific character-defining feature, so the owner understands the basis and the decision is defensible.

### Evaluate Compatibility of Additions and Infill Contextually

New construction and additions in historic districts should be compatible with their context in scale, massing, setback, rhythm, materials, and relationship to the street — but compatibility does not mean imitation. Contemporary design that respects the district's patterns (cornice lines, window proportions, storefront transparency, building-to-street relationship) can be entirely appropriate, while literal imitation that confuses the historic record is generally discouraged. Evaluate the proposal's relationship to its immediate neighbors and the district's broader patterns, and articulate specifically what makes it compatible or incompatible, rather than asserting taste-based preferences. Differentiate between a district with a strong, consistent character (where new work should defer more) and one with varied character (where more design latitude is appropriate).

### Balance Preservation with Accessibility, Safety, and Energy Goals

Historic buildings must be allowed to meet contemporary needs — accessibility (ADA), life-safety codes, seismic upgrades, energy efficiency, resilience — or they will become unusable and be lost. The Standards and most local ordinances provide for these upgrades; the question is how to accomplish them with minimal harm to character-defining features. Work with applicants to find solutions that meet the code or accessibility goal while preserving historic fabric: ramps placed sensitively, windows upgraded or secondary glazing added rather than wholesale replacement, seismic reinforcement that retains historic finishes, mechanical systems routed to minimize visible impact. Treating code and energy upgrades as enemies of preservation guarantees that buildings become nonfunctional and are demolished; integrating them skillfully keeps them in use.

### Differentiate Between Contributing and Non-Contributing Resources

Within a historic district, resources are classified as contributing (possessing significance and integrity) or non-contributing (lacking them, due to age or alteration). Review standards apply more strictly to contributing resources and more flexibly to non-contributing ones, which may be substantially altered or even replaced. Confirm the resource's status in the designation and calibrate review accordingly. Applying strict standards to a non-contributing resource over-protects it and frustrates owners; applying flexible standards to a contributing resource under-protects it and erodes the district. The distinction is not a loophole; it is the analytical backbone of district review.

### Document Review Decisions with Standards-Based Findings

Every review decision — approval, approval with conditions, or denial — should be tied to specific standards and specific features, with reasoning the owner can follow and a hearing body or court can review. "Incompatible" is not a finding; "the proposed addition's height, set back from the principal facade, would be visible above the historic roofline and would overwhelm the resource's scale, contrary to Standard 9 on additions" is a finding. Standards-based findings protect the decision from challenge, guide the owner toward a compliant revision, and create a consistent body of precedent that makes future review more predictable.

## Common Traps

### Applying Personal Taste as If It Were the Standards

Reviewers who impose their own aesthetic preferences — for traditional over contemporary design, for certain materials, for a particular style — under the banner of "the Standards" produce arbitrary decisions that owners cannot predict or satisfy. The false signal is confident, opinionated review; the harm is inconsistent decisions, appeals, and a perception that historic review is subjective obstruction. Anchor every decision in the specific standard and the specific character-defining feature, not in taste.

### Treating the Standards as Absolute Prohibition

Reading the Standards as forbidding any visible change, any new use, or any contemporary design ignores that they were written to guide rehabilitation and adaptive reuse. The false signal is rigorous preservation; the harm is that buildings cannot be adapted to contemporary needs, become nonviable, and are lost to demolition or neglect. Apply the Standards as they were intended: to guide change while preserving character.

### Opposing All Energy, Accessibility, and Code Upgrades

Resisting every window replacement, ramp, mechanical addition, or energy upgrade in the name of preservation makes historic buildings nonfunctional and accelerates their loss. The false signal is protecting historic fabric; the harm is that the building cannot meet code, serve users with disabilities, or achieve energy performance, and so is abandoned or demolished. Find solutions that accomplish the upgrade with minimal harm to character-defining features.

### Failing to Distinguish Contributing from Non-Contributing Resources

Applying the same review rigor to a non-contributing altered building as to a pristine contributing one wastes owner effort and over-protects resources that lack significance; applying flexible review to a contributing resource erodes the district. The false signal is uniform district review; the harm is misallocated protection and avoidable conflict. Confirm contributing status and calibrate review to it.

### Vague or Assertion-Based Findings

Decisions that assert "incompatible" or "out of character" without tying the conclusion to a specific standard and feature give the owner no path to compliance and invite appeal. The false signal is a clear decision; the harm is unpredictable review, frustrated applicants, and vulnerable approvals. Write findings that reference the standard, the feature, and the reasoning.

## Self-Check

- Are you applying the Secretary of the Interior's Standards as guidance for undertaking change, rather than as a flat prohibition on alteration?
- Did you identify the resource's character-defining features and focus review on the proposal's effect on those, rather than on every element equally?
- For additions and infill, did you evaluate compatibility in scale, massing, rhythm, and street relationship — while allowing distinguishable contemporary design rather than requiring imitation?
- Did you work with the applicant to integrate accessibility, code, seismic, and energy upgrades with minimal harm to character-defining features, rather than opposing them?
- Did you confirm whether the resource is contributing or non-contributing and calibrate the review strictness accordingly?
- Is each review decision documented with findings tied to specific standards and specific features, rather than assertion-based conclusions?
- Did you avoid imposing personal aesthetic preferences under the banner of the Standards?
- Did you avoid the pattern of resisting all contemporary intervention, which drives owners toward demolition or hardship claims?
- Could an owner read your findings and understand both the basis for the decision and what a compliant revision would look like?
- If the decision were appealed, would the standards-based findings and the record of character-defining features withstand review?
