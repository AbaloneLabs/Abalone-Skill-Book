# Bus Network Design and Service Planning

The bus network is the backbone of the local transit, and the design of the network — the routes, the frequencies, the stop spacing, the span, the coverage — determines whether the bus serves the community's needs or leaves the transit-dependent stranded and the choice riders unconvinced. The judgment problem is that the bus network involves the fundamental tradeoff between the coverage (the service spread across the community to serve the transit-dependent) and the ridership (the service concentrated on the high-demand corridors to attract the riders), and the allocation of the limited service hours between these goals determines the network's purpose and its performance. A bus network that is designed well (the clear purpose, the appropriate tradeoffs, the integrated structure) serves the community, while a bus network that is designed poorly (the unclear purpose, the inappropriate tradeoffs, the fragmented structure) fails the community. This skill covers the bus network design and the service planning decisions that determine whether the bus serves the community's needs. It complements the broader transit-corridor skill by focusing on the network and the service dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Clarify the Network Purpose and the Coverage-Ridership Tradeoff

The bus network should have the clear purpose, because the network that tries to be all things to all people (the high coverage and the high ridership) achieves neither, and the network with the clear purpose (the coverage-oriented or the ridership-oriented or the deliberate mix) achieves the purpose. The rule is to clarify the network purpose and the coverage-ridership tradeoff, defining the purpose (the coverage to serve the transit-dependent, the ridership to attract the choice riders, or the mix), allocating the service hours accordingly (the percentage to the coverage and the percentage to the ridership), and communicating the purpose to the community. The coverage-ridership tradeoff should be made deliberately, because the default allocation (the inherited network that serves both poorly) is the unexamined allocation, and the deliberate allocation is the examined one. The coverage goal serves the transit-dependent (the low-income, the senior, the disabled, the zero-car households), because the coverage ensures the access, and the access is the equity. The ridership goal serves the choice riders and the environment, because the high-ridership service attracts the riders from the cars, and the mode shift reduces the emissions. The mix should be based on the community's needs and the resources, because the community that values the equity may choose the more coverage, and the community that values the efficiency may choose the more ridership, and the resources constrain the choice.

### Design the Route Structure and the Stop Spacing for the Speed and the Access

The route structure and the stop spacing determine the speed and the access of the bus, and the design should balance the speed (the faster bus that attracts the riders) and the access (the closer stops that serve the walkers), because the excessive stops slow the bus and the excessive spacing distances the access. The rule is to design the route structure and the stop spacing for the speed and the access, using the route directness (the routes that go where the people want to go, without the excessive deviation), the stop spacing (the wider spacing on the high-demand corridors for the speed, the closer spacing in the low-demand areas for the access), the frequency (the high frequency on the high-demand routes, the lower frequency on the low-demand), and the span (the long span on the routes that serve the all-day demand). The route directness should prioritize the direct over the deviated, because the deviated route (the route that wanders to serve the few) slows the bus for the many, and the direct route serves the many efficiently. The stop spacing should be wider on the high-demand corridors (the quarter-mile to the half-mile), because the wider spacing speeds the bus, and the speed attracts the riders. The stop spacing should be balanced with the access, because the spacing that is too wide distances the access, and the balanced spacing serves both. The frequency should be high on the high-demand routes (the 10 to 15 minutes), because the high frequency makes the bus usable (the show-and-go), and the low frequency makes it unusable (the schedule-dependent). The span should match the demand, because the commuter-only span does not serve the all-day demand, and the all-day span does.

### Integrate the Bus With the Rail and the Other Modes

The bus should be integrated with the rail and the other modes, because the integrated network (the bus feeding the rail, the bus connecting the modes) provides the seamless travel, and the unintegrated network (the bus competing with the rail, the bus disconnected from the modes) provides the fragmented travel. The rule is to integrate the bus with the rail and the other modes, using the bus-rail integration (the bus routes feeding the rail stations, the timed transfers, the unified fare and the schedule), the bus-bike integration (the bike racks, the bike share at the stops), the bus-pedestrian integration (the safe and the accessible stops, the good sidewalks), and the bus-ride-hailing integration (the first-mile and the last-mile connections). The bus-rail integration should use the feeder routes (the bus routes that terminate at the rail stations and the timed transfers), because the feeder routes extend the rail's reach, and the competing routes duplicate the service. The bus-rail integration should include the unified fare and the schedule, because the separate fare and the uncoordinated schedule create the friction, and the unified fare and the coordinated schedule remove it. The bus-bike integration should include the bike racks and the bike share, because the bike extends the bus's reach, and the integration serves the bike-and-ride. The bus-pedestrian integration should include the safe and the accessible stops, because the stop is the pedestrian environment, and the safe and the accessible stop serves the walker.

### Use the Grid and the Frequent Network for the Usability

The grid network (the orthogonal routes that cross the city) and the frequent network (the routes with the 15-minute or better frequency) make the bus usable, because the grid allows the anywhere-to-anywhere travel (the transfer at any intersection), and the frequency makes the transfer painless (the short wait), and the combination creates the network the rider can use without the schedule. The rule is to use the grid and the frequent network for the usability, designing the grid where the street network allows (the orthogonal streets, the even spacing), identifying the frequent network (the high-demand routes with the 15-minute frequency), and branding the frequent network (the distinct color, the map, the stops). The grid should be used where the street network allows, because the grid requires the orthogonal street network, and the discontinuous street network does not allow the grid. The frequent network should be identified and the branded, because the branding communicates the usability, and the unbranded frequency is the hidden frequency. The frequent network should be the priority for the investment, because the frequency is the service quality, and the priority ensures the quality. The grid and the frequent network should be communicated, because the usability depends on the understanding, and the communication builds the understanding.

### Plan the Service for the Equity and the Access

The bus service should be planned for the equity and the access, because the bus is the lifeline for the transit-dependent, and the service that does not serve the equity and the access fails the community. The rule is to plan the service for the equity and the access, using the equity analysis (the service in the low-income and the minority communities, the Title VI compliance), the access analysis (the access to the jobs, the services, the opportunities), and the community engagement (the input from the transit-dependent, the riders, the non-riders). The equity analysis should ensure the service in the low-income and the minority communities is not disproportionately reduced (the Title VI), and the service is proportionally invested. The access analysis should measure the access to the jobs and the services, because the access is the purpose of the transit, and the measurement identifies the gaps. The community engagement should include the transit-dependent, because the transit-dependent are the core riders, and their input is the essential input. The service planning should address the reverse commute (the trips from the city to the suburban jobs), because the transit-dependent need the access to the suburban jobs, and the unidirectional service does not serve them. The service planning should include the night and the weekend service, because the transit-dependent travel at the all hours, and the day-only service does not serve them.

## Common Traps

### Purpose Unclear, Coverage and Ridership Both Served Poorly

The network purpose is unclear, and the coverage and the ridership are both served poorly, because the tradeoff was not made deliberately. The trap is that the unclear purpose serves both poorly. The mechanism is that the tradeoff was not made, and the network tried to be all things. The false signal is that "the network serves everyone." The harm is that the coverage is inadequate, the ridership is low, the transit-dependent are underserved, the choice riders are not attracted, and the network that should serve the purpose does not.

### Stops Too Close, Bus Too Slow

The stops are too close, and the bus is too slow, because the stop spacing was not widened. The trap is that the close spacing slows the bus. The mechanism is that the stop spacing was not adjusted, and the historical close spacing persisted. The false signal is that "the stops are convenient." The harm is that the bus is slow, the riders are deterred, the ridership declines, and the spacing that should balance the speed and the access does not.

### Bus Not Integrated With the Rail, Fragmented Travel

The bus is not integrated with the rail, and the travel is fragmented, because the feeder routes, the timed transfers, and the unified fare were not implemented. The trap is that the unintegrated bus fragments the travel. The mechanism is that the integration was not pursued, and the bus and the rail operated independently. The false signal is that "we have the bus and the rail." The harm is that the transfer is difficult, the fare is doubled, the schedule is uncoordinated, the rider is frustrated, and the integration that should provide the seamless travel does not.

### Frequent Network Not Identified, Usability Hidden

The frequent network is not identified or branded, and the usability is hidden, because the frequent network was not mapped or the branded. The trap is that the unidentified frequency hides the usability. The mechanism is that the branding was not implemented, and the frequency was the unmarked feature. The false signal is that "the frequency is provided." The harm is that the riders do not know the frequency, the usability is hidden, the ridership is lower than it could be, and the frequency that should make the bus usable does not.

### Equity Not Planned, Transit-Dependent Underserved

The equity is not planned, and the transit-dependent are underserved, because the equity analysis and the community engagement were not conducted. The trap is that the unplanned equity underserves the transit-dependent. The mechanism is that the equity analysis was not conducted, and the service was planned without the equity lens. The false signal is that "the service is provided." The harm is that the transit-dependent are underserved, the Title VI is violated, the access to the jobs and the services is inadequate, and the equity that should guide the service does not.

## Self-Check

- Is the network purpose clarified, with the deliberate coverage-ridership tradeoff and the community communication?
- Are the route structure and the stop spacing designed for the speed and the access, with the directness, the appropriate spacing, the frequency, and the span?
- Is the bus integrated with the rail and the other modes, with the feeder routes, the timed transfers, the unified fare, and the bike and pedestrian connections?
- Is the grid and the frequent network used for the usability, with the grid design, the frequent network identification, the branding, and the priority investment?
- Is the service planned for the equity and the access, with the equity analysis, the access analysis, the community engagement, the reverse commute, and the night and weekend service?
- Is the purpose clear, or are the coverage and the ridership both served poorly?
- Are the stops spaced for the speed, or is the bus too slow?
- Is the bus integrated, or is the travel fragmented?
- Is the frequent network identified, or is the usability hidden?
- Is the equity planned, or are the transit-dependent underserved?
