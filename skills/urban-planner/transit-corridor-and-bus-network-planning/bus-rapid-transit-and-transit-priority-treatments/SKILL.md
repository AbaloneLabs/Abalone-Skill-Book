# Bus Rapid Transit and Transit Priority Treatments

Bus rapid transit (BRT) and the transit priority treatments (the dedicated lanes, the signal priority, the queue jumps) are the tools that make the bus fast and reliable, and the quality of the BRT and the priority determines whether the bus competes with the car or loses to the congestion. The judgment problem is that the BRT involves the tradeoffs between the speed (the dedicated lanes, the wide stop spacing, the limited stops), the capacity (the large vehicles, the high frequency), and the cost (the infrastructure, the vehicles, the operations), and the choice among the BRT, the light rail, and the conventional bus depends on the demand, the corridor, and the resources. A BRT that is planned well (the dedicated running way, the strong priority, the appropriate stations) delivers the rail-like service at the bus cost, while a BRT that is planned poorly (the mixed traffic, the weak priority, the inadequate stations) is the bus in the branding, and it does not attract the riders. This skill covers the BRT and the transit priority decisions that determine whether the high-capacity transit delivers the speed and the reliability. It complements the broader transit-corridor skill by focusing on the BRT and the priority dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Select the Running Way for the Speed and the Reliability

The running way (the lane the bus operates in) is the primary determinant of the BRT speed and the reliability, and the dedicated running way (the bus-only lane) provides the speed and the reliability, while the mixed traffic does not. The rule is to select the running way for the speed and the reliability, using the dedicated lanes (the center-running, the side-running, the median-running) where the congestion is significant, the managed lanes (the high-occupancy vehicle, the high-occupancy toll) where the shared use is appropriate, and the mixed traffic (the conventional bus in the general lanes) only where the congestion is minimal. The center-running lanes (the lanes in the median) provide the best speed and the reliability, because the center lanes avoid the curb conflicts (the parking, the turning, the delivery), and the conflict-free operation is the fast operation. The side-running lanes (the lanes at the curb) are the compromise, because they are easier to implement but subject to the curb conflicts. The dedicated lanes should be enforced, because the unenforced lane is the lane that is violated by the cars, and the enforcement (the cameras, the police) ensures the bus-only use. The running way should be continuous, because the intermittent dedication (the dedicated lane that ends at the bottleneck) does not solve the bottleneck, and the continuous dedication does.

### Design the Stations for the Capacity and the Comfort

The BRT stations should be designed for the capacity (the handling of the boarding and the alighting) and the comfort (the amenities that attract the riders), because the station is the place the rider experiences the service, and the poor station deters the rider. The rule is to design the stations for the capacity and the comfort, using the platform width (the adequate width for the passenger volume), the level boarding (the platform-level boarding that speeds the boarding), the off-board fare collection (the fare paid before the boarding, not on the bus), the multiple doors (the all-door boarding that speeds the boarding), and the amenities (the shelter, the seating, the information, the security). The platform width should be adequate for the peak volume, because the narrow platform creates the crowding and the delay, and the adequate platform does not. The level boarding should be implemented, because the level boarding (the platform at the bus floor level) speeds the boarding (the no-steps boarding) and the accessibility (the wheelchair rolling), and the conventional boarding does not. The off-board fare collection should be implemented, because the on-board fare collection (the fare paid to the driver) is the bottleneck, and the off-board collection removes it. The multiple doors should be used, because the single-door boarding is the bottleneck, and the all-door boarding removes it. The amenities should be provided, because the station without the amenities is the uncomfortable station, and the amenities attract the riders.

### Implement the Transit Signal Priority and the Queue Jumps

The transit signal priority (the traffic signal that gives the bus the green) and the queue jumps (the lane and the signal that let the bus jump the queue) are the treatments that reduce the delay at the intersections, and the implementation of these treatments is the difference between the BRT that moves and the BRT that waits. The rule is to implement the transit signal priority and the queue jumps, using the transit signal priority (the TSP that extends the green or the early green for the approaching bus), the queue jump (the short bus lane at the intersection that lets the bus bypass the queue and the signal that gives the bus the head start), and the signal coordination (the synchronized signals along the corridor that provide the green wave for the bus). The TSP should be implemented at the congested intersections, because the TSP reduces the intersection delay, and the congested intersection is the high-delay intersection. The TSP should be the conditional priority (the priority only for the late bus), because the unconditional priority (the priority for every bus) disrupts the cross traffic, and the conditional priority minimizes the disruption. The queue jump should be implemented where the bus must merge through the queue, because the queue jump bypasses the queue, and the bypass reduces the delay. The signal coordination should be timed for the bus, because the signal timed for the car does not serve the bus, and the signal timed for the bus does.

### Choose Between the BRT and the Light Rail Deliberately

The choice between the BRT and the light rail should be made deliberately, because the BRT and the light rail serve the different contexts, and the wrong choice wastes the resources or underdelivers the capacity. The rule is to choose between the BRT and the light rail deliberately, evaluating the demand (the BRT for the moderate demand, the light rail for the high demand), the cost (the BRT at the lower cost, the light rail at the higher cost), the corridor (the BRT for the flexible routing, the light rail for the fixed corridor), the image (the light rail for the permanence and the development, the BRT for the flexibility), and the future (the BRT for the incremental, the light rail for the long-term). The demand evaluation should use the ridership projections, because the BRT handles the moderate demand (the 5,000 to the 15,000 passengers per hour per direction), and the light rail handles the high demand (the 10,000 to the 25,000). The cost evaluation should use the full cost (the capital and the operating), because the BRT is the lower cost, and the light rail is the higher cost, and the cost-effectiveness matters. The corridor evaluation should consider the flexibility, because the BRT can run on the different routes (the downtown distribution, the suburban branching), and the light rail is fixed. The image evaluation should consider the permanence, because the light rail signals the permanence that attracts the development, and the BRT may or may not. The choice should be documented and the justified, because the documented and the justified choice is the defensible choice.

### Brand and Market the BRT as the Premium Service

The BRT should be branded and the marketed as the premium service, because the BRT that is branded and the marketed as the premium service (the distinct identity, the unique name, the premium experience) attracts the riders who would not ride the conventional bus, and the BRT that is branded as the bus does not. The rule is to brand and market the BRT as the premium service, using the distinct identity (the unique name, the color, the logo), the premium stations (the branded stations with the amenities), the branded vehicles (the distinct vehicles with the BRT identity), and the marketing (the promotion of the BRT as the fast and the premium service). The distinct identity should differentiate the BRT from the conventional bus, because the rider who avoids the bus may ride the BRT, and the differentiation changes the perception. The premium stations should provide the amenities, because the station is the experience, and the premium station attracts the rider. The branded vehicles should be distinct, because the distinct vehicle reinforces the brand, and the conventional vehicle does not. The marketing should promote the BRT as the premium service, because the marketing shapes the perception, and the premium perception attracts the rider.

## Common Traps

### Running Way Not Dedicated, BRT Is the Bus in the Branding

The running way is not dedicated, and the BRT is the bus in the branding, because the dedicated lanes were not implemented. The trap is that the mixed-traffic BRT is the bus in the branding. The mechanism is that the dedicated lanes were not built (the political opposition, the cost), and the BRT operates in the mixed traffic. The false signal is that "we have the BRT." The harm is that the BRT is not fast or reliable, the riders are not attracted, the ridership is low, the investment does not return, and the BRT that should be the premium service is the conventional bus.

### Stations Inadequate, Boarding Slow

The stations are inadequate, and the boarding is slow, because the level boarding, the off-board fare, and the multiple doors were not implemented. The trap is that the inadequate stations slow the boarding. The mechanism is that the station treatments were not implemented, and the conventional boarding persisted. The false signal is that "the stations are built." The harm is that the boarding is slow, the dwell time is long, the speed is low, the reliability is poor, and the stations that should speed the boarding slow it.

### Signal Priority Not Implemented, Bus Waits at the Lights

The transit signal priority is not implemented, and the bus waits at the lights, because the TSP and the queue jumps were not installed. The trap is that the unimplemented TSP makes the bus wait. The mechanism is that the TSP was not installed, and the bus stops at the signals. The false signal is that "the bus has the dedicated lane." The harm is that the bus waits at the signals, the delay accumulates, the speed is low, the reliability is poor, and the TSP that should reduce the delay does not.

### BRT Chosen Without the Evaluation, Wrong Mode

The BRT is chosen without the evaluation, and it is the wrong mode for the corridor, because the demand, the cost, and the corridor were not evaluated. The trap is that the unevaluated choice is the wrong mode. The mechanism is that the evaluation was not conducted, and the mode was chosen by the preference. The false signal is that "the BRT is cheaper." The harm is that the BRT underdelivers the capacity (the high-demand corridor), or the light rail wastes the resources (the low-demand corridor), and the mode that should match the corridor does not.

### BRT Not Branded, Perceived as the Bus

The BRT is not branded, and it is perceived as the conventional bus, because the distinct identity and the marketing were not implemented. The trap is that the unbranded BRT is perceived as the bus. The mechanism is that the branding was not implemented, and the BRT was presented as the bus. The false signal is that "the service is fast." The harm is that the riders avoid the BRT (the perception of the bus), the ridership is low, the investment does not return, and the branding that should change the perception does not.
