# Transit Corridor and Bus Network Planning

A transit network is a system of routes, frequencies, and connections that determines who can live without a car, which jobs are accessible to whom, and how a region moves. Bus network planning is the design of that system, and it is governed by a fundamental tradeoff: every dollar of service spent on coverage — spreading routes across the geography to reach more areas — is a dollar not spent on ridership — concentrating service on the corridors where demand is highest and where transit is most competitive with driving. The judgment problem is that both goals are legitimate — coverage serves equity and access for those who depend on transit, ridership serves efficiency, mode shift, and fiscal sustainability — and every network represents an allocation between them that should be deliberate, not accidental. A network that pursues coverage without ridership produces low-ridership routes that are expensive per rider and that fail to attract choice riders; a network that pursues ridership without coverage abandons the transit-dependent in lower-density areas. The planner's role is to design a network whose coverage-ridership allocation is explicit, defensible, and matched to the community's goals, and to ensure the service, frequency, and reliability that make transit useful, not merely present. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a bus network serves its riders or serves its map.

## Core Rules

### Make the Coverage-Ridership Allocation Explicit and Documented

Every transit network allocates service between coverage (reaching more areas, especially lower-density and equity-priority areas, with lower-frequency service) and ridership (concentrating service on high-demand corridors with high frequency that attracts choice riders), and the allocation should be explicit, documented, and matched to the community's goals. The rule is to state the allocation as a policy — what percentage of service is dedicated to coverage goals, what percentage to ridership goals — and to design the network to achieve it, rather than allowing the allocation to emerge accidentally from incremental route decisions. An explicit allocation allows the community to debate the tradeoff consciously (do we want more frequency on the main corridors, or more coverage in the neighborhoods?), and it allows the network to be evaluated against its stated goals rather than against an unstated default. A network whose allocation is unstated tends to drift toward coverage (because every route has advocates and cutting routes is politically difficult), producing a network that is everywhere and useful nowhere.

### Design for Frequency, Span, and Reliability as the Primary Service Standards

Transit is useful when it is frequent (so riders do not plan their lives around the schedule), when it runs the span of the day (so it is available when needed), and when it is reliable (so it arrives when expected), and these service standards matter more than route coverage in determining whether people use transit. The rule is to set frequency, span, and reliability as the primary service standards, and to allocate service to meet them on the priority corridors: frequency of 15 minutes or better throughout the day on the core network (so transit is turn-up-and-go), span that covers early morning to late evening at minimum (so transit serves shift workers and evening trips), and reliability targets with monitoring and interventions (bus lanes, signal priority, holding strategies) that achieve them. A network with broad coverage but low frequency produces routes that are present but unusable, because riders cannot depend on them, and the coverage goal is defeated because the service does not provide access. Concentrating service to achieve high frequency on fewer corridors often serves more riders and more access than spreading it thinly across many.

### Structure Routes for Directness, Legibility, and Connectivity

Route structure determines how quickly and intuitively transit moves people, and the best networks use direct routes (following the path riders want to travel, not winding through neighborhoods), legible patterns (routes that follow major corridors and are easy to understand), and connectivity (routes that intersect and transfer at nodes that serve multiple destinations). The rule is to design routes for directness, legibility, and connectivity: avoid route deviations that slow the through-trip to serve a single destination (a common pattern that penalizes the majority of riders to serve the few), build a grid or radial network that allows transfer-based trips to many destinations, and concentrate service on corridors where the land use supports transit demand. The tradeoff is that direct routes may bypass some origins or destinations, but the speed and reliability gained serves more riders than the deviations lost. A network of winding, deviated routes is slow, illegible, and uncompetitive, and it drives away the choice riders whose presence makes the system fiscally sustainable.

### Integrate Service With Land Use and Street Design

Transit demand is generated by land use — the density and mix of origins and destinations along the corridor — and transit speed and reliability are determined by street design — the lanes, signals, and traffic conditions the bus operates in. The rule is to integrate transit planning with land use (concentrating density and mix on transit corridors, so the service has riders) and with street design (dedicated bus lanes, transit signal priority, queue jumps, and stop spacing that balances access and speed). A high-frequency bus route in a low-density, auto-oriented corridor produces low ridership regardless of service quality, because the land use does not generate transit trips; a high-density corridor without bus lanes or signal priority produces slow, unreliable service that drives away riders regardless of demand. The integration should be planned at the corridor level, with the transit agency, the municipality, and the land use planning coordinated, because transit success depends on all three.

### Plan the Network as a System, Not a Collection of Routes

A bus network is a system whose value lies in the connections it enables, not in the individual routes, and planning route-by-route produces a collection that is less useful than a designed network. The rule is to plan the network as a system: identify the core frequent network (the high-frequency corridors that form the spine), the supporting local routes (that feed the core), the crosstown connections (that allow non-downtown trips), and the transfer points (where routes intersect and where timed transfers may be warranted). The system design should ensure that the network serves the region's actual travel patterns (which are increasingly multi-destination, not just downtown-bound), that transfers are easy (with infrastructure, fare integration, and where useful, timed transfers), and that the network is legible (so riders can understand and use it). A network planned route-by-route tends to duplicate service on competitive corridors, leave gaps in connectivity, and produce a system that is less than the sum of its routes.

## Common Traps

### Coverage Pursued Without Ridership, Producing Low-Use Routes

The network spreads service across the geography to provide coverage, but the routes are low-frequency and low-ridership, and the cost per rider is high while the mode share remains low. The trap is that coverage without ridership produces an expensive network that serves few people. The mechanism is that low-density, low-frequency routes cannot attract riders, and the service investment does not generate the ridership that would justify it. The false signal is that "we provide transit citywide." The harm is that the network is fiscally unsustainable, the low-ridership routes are vulnerable to cuts, the transit-dependent riders who depend on them face instability, and the system does not achieve the mode shift that would serve regional goals.

### Frequency Too Low to Be Useful

Routes are spread for coverage, and the frequency on each is too low (30 or 60 minutes) for transit to be useful, and ridership remains low because the service is not dependable. The trap is that low frequency defeats the coverage goal. The mechanism is that transit is useful only when riders can depend on it, and low frequency requires schedule-based use that most choice riders will not accept. The false signal is that "we serve this area." The harm is that the service is present but unusable, the ridership remains low, the route is cited as evidence of low demand, and the coverage goal is not achieved because the service does not provide the access it was meant to provide.

### Route Deviations That Slow the Through-Trip

Routes deviate from the direct corridor to serve individual destinations — a hospital, a shopping center, a neighborhood — and the through-trip is slowed, driving away the riders who would use the direct service. The trap is that deviations serve the few at the expense of the many. The mechanism is that each deviation adds time to every through-trip, and the cumulative effect makes the route uncompetitive. The false signal is that "we are serving more destinations." The harm is that the route loses the choice riders who value speed, the ridership declines, and the route becomes a coverage route that serves few people at high cost, when a direct route would have served more.

### High-Frequency Service in a Low-Density Corridor

High-frequency bus service is provided on a corridor, but the land use is low-density and auto-oriented, and ridership is low regardless of the service quality. The trap is that the service exceeds the demand the land use generates. The mechanism is that transit demand is generated by density and mix, and a low-density corridor cannot fill high-frequency service. The false signal is that "transit doesn't work here." The harm is that the service is underused, the cost per rider is high, the route is vulnerable to cuts, and the experience is cited as evidence against transit investment, when the actual failure was the mismatch between service and land use.

### Network Planned Route-by-Route Without System Design

Routes are added, modified, and cut incrementally, without a system-level design, and the network is a collection of routes with duplication, gaps, and poor connectivity. The trap is that a collection is less useful than a designed network. The mechanism is that incremental decisions optimize each route without regard to the system, producing duplication on some corridors and gaps on others. The false signal is that "we are responding to demand." The harm is that the network is inefficient (duplicating service where it is not needed), incomplete (leaving gaps in connectivity), and illegible (so riders cannot understand or use it), and the system serves fewer riders than a designed network would with the same resources.

## Self-Check

- Is the coverage-ridership allocation stated as a policy, with the network designed to achieve it, rather than allowed to emerge accidentally?
- Are frequency, span, and reliability set as primary service standards, with service concentrated to achieve high frequency on the core network?
- Are routes designed for directness, legibility, and connectivity, with deviations minimized and transfers enabled at well-designed nodes?
- Is transit integrated with land use (density and mix on corridors) and street design (bus lanes, signal priority, stop spacing)?
- Is the network planned as a system — core frequent network, local feeders, crosstown connections, transfer points — rather than route-by-route?
- If a route serves coverage, is that goal stated, and is the route evaluated against coverage rather than ridership metrics?
- Is the frequency on each route high enough to be useful (15 minutes or better on the core), or is it so low that the service is present but unusable?
- Do routes follow direct paths, or do deviations slow the through-trip to serve individual destinations?
- Does the land use along the corridor generate the demand the service level assumes, or is the service mismatched to the corridor?
- Is the network legible and connected, with transfers enabled, or is it a collection of routes that riders cannot navigate as a system?
