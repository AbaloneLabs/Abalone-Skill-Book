# AICP Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct

The AICP Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct is the binding ethical framework for certified planners in the United States, and applying it is a judgment problem rather than a lookup exercise. The Code sets out aspirational principles, rules of responsibility to the public, and specific rules of conduct that can trigger suspension or revocation of certification, so a planner must constantly distinguish what they may do from what they should do, and what they must report from what they may privately dislike. The recurring failure is treating the Code as paperwork filed at certification renewal rather than as the standard against which everyday decisions, from a hallway conversation with an applicant to a recommendation in a staff report, are measured. Planners also tend to collapse the Code into the single duty of "serve the public interest" without recognizing that the Code layers that duty over obligations of integrity, competence, disclosure, and reporting that frequently pull in different directions. The stakes are high because an ethics violation can end a career, expose the planner and the employer to legal liability, and, more importantly, damage the public trust that gives the planning profession its authority. This skill is for the moments when a planner must decide what the Code requires, what to disclose, what to refuse, and what to escalate.

## Core Rules

### Treat the Public Interest as the Primary, Not the Only, Duty

The Code places the public interest above the interest of any client, employer, or personal gain, and this is the principle that most often conflicts with the planner's daily pressures. A private-sector planner serves a client, a public-sector planner serves an elected or appointed body, and both face direct or implicit pressure to advance that principal's interest, sometimes at the expense of the broader public. The judgment is not to pretend the employer's interest does not exist but to name it explicitly, refuse to subordinate the public interest to it, and document the reasoning when the two diverge. Where the conflict is irreconcilable, the Code contemplates that the planner may need to decline the assignment, withdraw, or resign rather than act against the public interest. Distinguish the public interest from the interest of the loudest or most powerful segment of the public, and from the planner's own policy preferences; the public interest is a reasoned judgment about long-term community welfare, not a slogan.

### Maintain Integrity and Independent Professional Judgment

Integrity under the Code means the planner's advice and recommendations reflect independent professional judgment, not the outcome the applicant, the developer, the elected official, or the planner's own career wants. Competence is its twin: the planner must not undertake work beyond their qualifications, must keep their knowledge current, and must not represent a conclusion as sound when the underlying analysis is weak or absent. The trap is the slow drift in which a planner, under pressure, softens a finding, omits an inconvenient analysis, or frames a recommendation to please a decision-maker, each step seeming minor until the cumulative product no longer reflects the planner's actual judgment. When external pressure threatens integrity, the response is to escalate internally, document the disagreement, and, if necessary, refuse to sign or submit the work product. A recommendation that the planner does not actually believe is an integrity failure regardless of whether anyone notices.

### Identify and Disclose Conflicts of Interest Proactively

A conflict of interest exists when the planner has a personal, financial, or relational interest that could be perceived to compromise their impartiality, and the Code's standard is broad because the harm is the erosion of public trust, not just actual bias. Identify conflicts early, before the matter advances, because a late disclosure looks like concealment discovered under pressure. Disclose in writing to the supervisor, the reviewing body, and, where appropriate, the public record, and let the employer or body decide whether recusal or reassignment is required rather than deciding unilaterally that the conflict is manageable. Remember that indirect conflicts, a spouse's employer, a prior client, property near the site, a friendship with an applicant, are still conflicts, and that the appearance standard means the planner need not have actually been influenced, only that a reasonable person could perceive influence. When in doubt, disclose; the cost of an unnecessary disclosure is small, and the cost of an undisclosed conflict that later surfaces is severe.

### Distinguish Aspirational Principles from Enforceable Rules

The Code is structured in layers: aspirational principles that describe the profession's ideals, principles of responsibility to the public that carry strong weight, and specific rules of conduct that are directly enforceable through the ethics process. A planner reasoning through a dilemma should identify which layer governs, because the consequences and the required response differ. Violating an aspirational principle is a failure of professional aspiration but not by itself a chargeable offense, while violating a rule of conduct, such as accepting a contingent fee, misrepresenting qualifications, or failing to disclose a conflict, is directly actionable. Do not comfort yourself with "this is only aspirational" when the conduct also touches a rule, and do not treat the rules as a ceiling when the principles call for more. Read the Code as a whole, because the principles give meaning to the rules and the rules give teeth to the principles.

### Report Ethical Violations Through the Proper Channel

The Code imposes a duty to report known or reasonably suspected violations of its rules, and this duty is one of the hardest to act on because it can mean reporting a colleague, a supervisor, or a client. The judgment is to report through the correct channel, which may be an internal supervisor, a human resources or ethics officer, a state licensing or certification board, or the AICP Ethics Officer directly, depending on the severity and the local structure. Report facts, not accusations, and distinguish a good-faith disagreement about planning judgment from a genuine ethical violation. Retaliation for good-faith reporting is itself a violation, and the planner should document the report and preserve evidence. Where the violation also involves criminal conduct, fraud, or imminent public harm, the duty to report may extend to law enforcement, and legal counsel should be consulted before acting.

### Understand and Prepare for the AICP Ethics Review Process

The AICP ethics process begins with a complaint or an inquiry and proceeds through review by the Ethics Committee, a possible investigation, a determination, and, if a violation is found, sanctions ranging from a private reprimand to revocation of certification. A planner facing a complaint should take it seriously, respond factually and completely, preserve all relevant records, and consult legal counsel and, if available, professional liability insurance before responding. Do not attempt to contact the complainant informally to resolve the matter, and do not destroy or alter records. Understand that the process evaluates conduct against the Code's standards, not against the planner's subjective good intentions, and that the documentation of the planner's reasoning, disclosures, and consultations is often the decisive evidence. Familiarity with the process before a complaint arises, not after, is what allows a planner to respond rather than react.

### Escalate to Ethics Counsel and Legal Referral When Stakes Rise

Planning ethics overlaps with legal exposure, and the planner must recognize when a question has crossed from an ethical judgment into a matter requiring professional legal advice. Allegations of fraud, bribery, open-meetings or public-records violations, discrimination, or misuse of public funds carry civil and criminal consequences that an AICP ethics opinion cannot resolve. In those cases, the planner should consult the employer's legal counsel, retain personal counsel if their own liability is implicated, and consider whether the matter must be reported to external authorities. An ethics complaint to AICP is not a substitute for legal referral, and legal compliance is not a substitute for ethical compliance; both must be satisfied. When in doubt about whether a matter has legal dimensions, treat it as if it does and seek counsel before making statements that could be used in either forum.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Code as Renewal Paperwork

The planner signs the Code at certification and re-affirms it at renewal, and the trap is to treat that signature as a periodic formality rather than as a continuing obligation tested against daily decisions. The mechanism is that ethics feels active only during the renewal cycle or when a complaint looms, so the planner stops consulting the Code for ordinary judgments and begins relying on habit and office culture. The false signal is that nothing has gone wrong in years, so the Code must be being followed, when in fact the planner has simply stopped checking. The harm is that drift accumulates undetected until a specific incident exposes a pattern of conduct that no longer conforms, at which point the gap is wide and the defense is weak. The corrective is to read the Code as a living standard applied to concrete decisions, not a document filed and forgotten.

### Rationalizing Pressure as "Just Doing My Job"

When a supervisor or applicant pressures a planner to soften a finding, omit an analysis, or accelerate a review past what competence allows, the planner often complies by reframing the compliance as professional loyalty or job performance. The mechanism is that refusal carries immediate career cost while compliance carries only diffuse future risk, so the planner convinces themselves that the requested change is within professional discretion. The false signal is that the change is small, defensible, and unlikely to be challenged, which feels true in the moment but ignores the cumulative erosion of independent judgment. The harm is that the work product no longer reflects the planner's actual professional view, the public interest is subordinated to a private interest, and, if challenged, the planner has personally signed a product they do not believe. The corrective is to recognize that "just doing my job" is not a defense to an ethics charge and that the duty to the public interest overrides the duty to the employer.

### Under-Disclosing Indirect and Appearance Conflicts

Planners readily disclose direct financial conflicts but routinely miss indirect ones: a spouse's firm that does work for the applicant, a prior employer with pending matters, ownership of nearby property, a social relationship, or a pending job offer from a firm with business before the jurisdiction. The mechanism is that the planner judges the conflict by their own confidence in their impartiality rather than by the appearance standard, concluding "I would never be influenced" and therefore not disclosing. The false signal is the planner's internal certainty, which is invisible to the public and irrelevant to the appearance test. The harm is that the undisclosed conflict later surfaces, often raised by an opponent of the project, and retroactively taints the decision, the planner, and the jurisdiction regardless of whether actual bias occurred. The corrective is to apply the reasonable-person appearance standard and disclose liberally.

### Failing to Report Because It Feels Disloyal

The duty to report violations is the most commonly avoided Code obligation because reporting a colleague, supervisor, or client feels like betrayal, and because the reporter fears social and professional consequences. The mechanism is that loyalty to people and to the team is immediate and felt, while the duty to the profession and the public is abstract and distant, so the planner rationalizes silence as discretion or as "not my place." The false signal is that the violation is isolated, minor, or already known, none of which relieves the reporting duty. The harm is that the conduct continues or escalates, the public is harmed, and the planner who knew and did not report becomes complicit under the Code's own terms. The corrective is to report facts through the proper channel and to recognize that the Code makes reporting a duty, not an option.

### Confusing Legal Compliance with Ethical Compliance

A planner may conclude that because an action is legal, permitted by local ordinance, or approved by counsel, it is therefore ethical, and proceed without separate ethical analysis. The mechanism is that legal review feels like the authoritative check, so the planner treats a clean legal opinion as closing the ethical question. The false signal is the legal green light, which addresses what is permissible, not what is right under the Code's principles. The harm is that conduct that is legal but inconsistent with the Code's aspirational principles or its rules, such as aggressively advocating for a client against the public interest within legal bounds, still constitutes an ethical failure. The corrective is to run the ethical analysis independently of the legal analysis and to satisfy both.

### Misjudging the Reporting Threshold Between Disagreement and Violation

Planners sometimes report, or fail to report, based on whether they agree with a colleague's planning judgment rather than whether a Code rule was violated. The mechanism is that professional disagreement feels like an ethical failing when one is on the losing side, and genuine violations feel like mere disagreement when committed by an ally. The false signal is the planner's emotional reaction to the outcome rather than the conduct measured against the Code. The harm of over-reporting is burdening the process and damaging relationships without cause; the harm of under-reporting is letting a real violation pass. The corrective is to distinguish good-faith planning judgment, which the Code protects, from conduct that violates a specific rule, and to report only the latter, on facts.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I identify which layer of the Code governs this decision, aspirational principle, public responsibility, or enforceable rule, and respond accordingly?
- [ ] When employer, client, or personal pressure conflicted with the public interest, did I name the conflict, document the reasoning, and refuse to subordinate the public interest?
- [ ] Did my recommendation reflect my independent professional judgment, and is there any analysis I softened, omitted, or framed to please a decision-maker?
- [ ] Did I identify and disclose in writing all direct, indirect, and appearance conflicts before the matter advanced, applying the reasonable-person standard rather than my own certainty of impartiality?
- [ ] If I suspected a violation of a Code rule, did I report the facts through the correct channel, preserve evidence, and document the report?
- [ ] Have I read the Code recently as a living standard, rather than treating my renewal signature as sufficient?
- [ ] Did I run the ethical analysis separately from the legal analysis, and recognize where legal compliance does not equal ethical compliance?
- [ ] Where the matter involved fraud, bribery, discrimination, or public-records or open-meetings issues, did I treat it as carrying legal exposure and seek counsel before making statements?
