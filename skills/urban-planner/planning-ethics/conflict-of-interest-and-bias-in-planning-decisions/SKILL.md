# Conflict of Interest and Bias in Planning Decisions

Land use decisions are made through discretionary processes that confer enormous financial value, and that discretion is exactly why conflicts of interest, bias, and ex parte communications are treated as threats to the legitimacy of the decision rather than as mere etiquette. The judgment problem is that a planner or decision-maker is never a blank slate: they have relationships, finances, prior employment, policy views, and cognitive biases that shape how they read an application, and the ethical task is to identify which of those rise to the level of a disqualifying conflict, which require disclosure, and which must simply be monitored. Planners commonly fail by recognizing only direct financial conflicts while missing personal, relational, ideological, and structural conflicts, by treating ex parte conversations as harmless information-gathering rather than as contamination of the record, and by assuming that good intentions neutralize bias when the law and the ethics codes judge by the appearance of impartiality. The harm of these failures is severe: a decision tainted by conflict or bias can be voided, can expose the jurisdiction and the individual to litigation, and can permanently damage public trust in the planning process. This skill is for the moments when a planner must decide whether to disclose, recuse, refuse a conversation, or escalate, and how to document the decision so it survives later scrutiny.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Financial, Personal, and Structural Conflicts

A financial conflict is a direct or indirect economic interest in the outcome: ownership of affected property, a contingent fee, employment or prospective employment with a party, a family member's stake. A personal conflict arises from relationships and affinity: friendship, enmity, family ties, membership in an organization with a position on the matter, or a prior professional relationship with the applicant or opponent. A structural conflict is built into the planner's role: the jurisdiction is both the regulator and a property owner, the planner drafted the policy now being applied, or the planner's performance is evaluated by an official with a stated position. Each type requires a different response. Financial conflicts almost always require disclosure and often recusal; personal conflicts require disclosure and case-by-case recusal based on the closeness of the relationship; structural conflicts usually require disclosure and transparent management rather than recusal, because they cannot be eliminated by stepping aside. Name the type of conflict explicitly, because conflating them leads to under-responding to financial conflicts and over-responding to unavoidable structural ones.

### Apply the Appearance Standard, Not the Honesty Standard

The governing standard for conflicts and bias in land use decisions is whether a reasonable person, knowing the facts, would question the decision-maker's impartiality, not whether the decision-maker subjectively believes they can be fair. This appearance standard is stricter than the planner's own sense of integrity, and it exists because public confidence in the process depends on decisions looking fair as well as being fair. Apply the test by imagining the facts disclosed on the front page of a local newspaper: if the disclosure would cause a reasonable resident to doubt the outcome, there is a conflict requiring disclosure or recusal. Do not reason from "I would never let this affect me," because that reasoning is invisible to the public and irrelevant to the legal and ethical test. When the appearance is present, disclose and let the appropriate authority decide on recusal; err toward disclosure because the cost of an unnecessary disclosure is far lower than the cost of an undisclosed conflict that later taints the decision.

### Treat Ex Parte Communications as Contamination, Not Information

An ex parte communication is a contact about the merits of a pending matter outside the established public process, typically between a decision-maker and an interested party without all parties present. In quasi-judicial land use proceedings, such communications are often prohibited or strictly limited because they place information and argument into the record that the other parties cannot see or rebut, violating due process. The trap is treating a hallway conversation, an email, or a lunch with an applicant as harmless context-gathering, when in fact it can require disclosure of the communication's content into the record, disqualification of the decision-maker, or remand of the decision. The rule for planners and commissioners is to decline substantive discussion of pending matters, to redirect the party to the public process, and to document any ex parte contact that does occur so it can be placed on the record. When in doubt about whether a proceeding is quasi-judicial and thus subject to ex parte limits, treat it as if it is.

### Apply Recusal Standards Based on the Proceeding Type and the Relationship

Recusal, stepping aside from a decision, is required when the conflict or bias is such that participation would compromise the fairness or the appearance of fairness of the proceeding. The standard is stricter in quasi-judicial proceedings, such as variances, special permits, and conditional use approvals, where the decision-maker acts like a judge and due process applies, than in legislative proceedings, such as comprehensive plan or zoning code adoption, where the body acts politically and broader participation is tolerated. Apply recusal when there is a direct financial interest, a close personal relationship with a party, prior involvement that predetermines the outcome, or any fact that would require the decision to be overturned on appeal. Document the recusal and the reason in the record, and do not discuss the matter with the remaining decision-makers after recusing. A late or reluctant recusal looks worse than an early and decisive one.

### Respect Post-Employment and Revolving-Door Restrictions

Planners who move between public agencies and private firms, or who consider doing so, face restrictions designed to prevent the use of public office for private gain and the appearance of continued influence. These may include bans on representing private clients before the former agency for a defined period, bans on working on matters the planner participated in personally and substantially while in public service, and disclosure or divestiture requirements. Identify the applicable restrictions before, not after, accepting private employment, because a violation can result in civil penalties, disqualification from the matter, and loss of professional standing. The mirror trap applies to private-sector planners entering public service: prior representation of clients with matters before the new agency can require screening or recusal from those matters. When a job transition is contemplated, consult the jurisdiction's ethics rules and obtain a written opinion before acting.

### Examine Implicit Bias in Land Use Decisions

Land use decisions are not made by neutral algorithms; they are made by people whose judgments are shaped by implicit biases about race, income, disability, family structure, and neighborhood character, and those biases have historically produced exclusionary zoning, concentrated disinvestment, and unequal protection. The ethical task is not to deny bias but to design decision processes that expose and counteract it: use objective, code-based criteria rather than impressionistic judgments about "fit" or "character," test discretionary decisions for disparate impact on protected classes, examine whether opposition to a project tracks the demographics of who would benefit, and scrutinize the language of staff reports for coded or subjective terms. Document the criteria applied and the evidence supporting each finding, so the decision rests on a defensible record rather than on unexamined assumptions. Implicit bias is not a personal accusation; it is a structural condition that responsible planning practice must actively counteract.

### Document the Decision and Its Rationale on the Record

The single strongest protection against allegations of conflict, bias, and arbitrary decision-making is a complete record that shows the decision was based on the applicable standards, supported by substantial evidence, and reached through a fair process. Document the criteria applied, the evidence considered, the findings made on each criterion, the conditions imposed and their justification, and the responses to public input. Where discretion was exercised, explain the reasoning that connected the evidence to the conclusion, because a bare approval or denial invites challenge. Include in the record any disclosures of conflict, any ex parte communications and their content, and any recusals, so the integrity of the process is visible. A well-documented decision is harder to overturn, easier to defend, and more respectful of the public whose interests it affects.

### Escalate to Legal Counsel and Ethics Authorities When the Stakes Rise

Conflict and bias questions frequently carry legal consequences that exceed what professional ethics alone can resolve. When a matter involves potential due process violations, open-meetings or public-records exposure, discrimination claims, bribery or honest-services fraud, or a decision that may be voided, the planner should involve the jurisdiction's legal counsel immediately and, where personal liability is possible, retain personal counsel. An ethics complaint to AICP or a state board may be appropriate alongside, not instead of, legal referral. Do not attempt to resolve a serious conflict or bias issue through informal conversation with the affected party, and do not alter or destroy records once an issue is identified. Recognize that the planner's duty runs to the integrity of the process, and that protecting the process sometimes requires escalating against the planner's own supervisor or client.

## Common Traps

### Recognizing Only Direct Financial Conflicts

Planners and commissioners readily spot a direct financial interest, such as owning the subject property, but miss the web of indirect and personal conflicts that are equally disqualifying. The mechanism is that financial conflicts are concrete and easy to inventory, while relational, ideological, and structural conflicts are diffuse and feel like ordinary life, so the planner screens only for the obvious. The false signal is the absence of a direct money interest, which is taken to mean there is no conflict at all. The harm is that decisions are made by people with undisclosed relationships, prior employment, or property interests near the site, and when those facts emerge, the decision is tainted regardless of the merits. The corrective is to inventory financial, personal, and structural conflicts separately and to apply the appearance test to each.

### Treating Ex Parte Contact as Harmless Context

A planner or commissioner who chats with an applicant at a community event, accepts a one-on-one briefing, or reads a detailed email outside the hearing often believes they are simply getting informed. The mechanism is that information-gathering feels responsible and the conversation seems too informal to matter, so the participant does not recognize that they have injected un-rebutted material into a quasi-judicial proceeding. The false signal is that the information was neutral or balanced, which is irrelevant because the other parties had no chance to respond. The harm is that the proceeding is compromised, the decision-maker may have to be disqualified, and the decision can be remanded or voided on due process grounds. The corrective is to refuse substantive discussion of pending matters and to route all communication through the public process.

### Recusing Too Late or Too Narrowly

A decision-maker who recognizes a conflict but waits to see whether it becomes a problem, or who recuses from the vote but continues to discuss the matter with colleagues, has not actually cured the conflict. The mechanism is that recusal feels costly and dramatic, so the decision-maker delays or partial-steps, hoping the issue will not materialize. The false signal is that the conflict did not affect the outcome, which is legally irrelevant in quasi-judicial proceedings. The harm is that the late or partial recusal looks like concealment discovered under pressure, undermines the appearance of fairness, and can still invalidate the decision. The corrective is to recuse early, completely, and on the record, and to cease all participation after recusing.

### Assuming Good Intentions Neutralize Implicit Bias

A planner who is confident in their own fairness may conclude that because they intend no discrimination, their discretionary judgments are free of bias. The mechanism is that implicit bias operates below conscious awareness, so the planner's sincere self-assessment of neutrality is itself unreliable evidence. The false signal is the planner's subjective sense of fairness, which neither the public nor a reviewing court can verify. The harm is that subjective, impressionistic decisions about "character," "compatibility," or "fit" reproduce historical patterns of exclusion, and the planner is unaware that this is happening. The corrective is to rely on objective criteria, test decisions for disparate impact, and document the evidence rather than the intention.

### Misjudging Post-Employment Restrictions as Mere Formalities

A planner leaving public service for a private firm may assume that the revolving-door rules are advisory or that they will not be enforced, and begin representing clients before the former agency immediately. The mechanism is that the restrictions feel like obstacles to a new career, and the planner, eager to start, reads them narrowly or ignores them. The false signal is that no one has objected yet, which reflects delay in enforcement rather than permission. The harm is civil penalty, disqualification from matters, reputational damage, and potential exposure for the new firm. The corrective is to identify the applicable restrictions before the transition, obtain a written ethics opinion, and observe the cooling-off periods strictly.

### Documenting the Outcome but Not the Reasoning

A staff report or decision that states the conclusion, "approved with conditions," without connecting it to the standards and the evidence, is nearly indefensible if challenged. The mechanism is that the reasoning is obvious to the planner at the time, so documenting it feels redundant, and only the outcome gets recorded. The false signal is that the decision is self-evidently correct, which it is not to a reviewing court or an aggrieved party. The harm is that the decision looks arbitrary, the findings cannot be reconstructed, and the jurisdiction must either defend a thin record or remake the decision. The corrective is to document the criteria, the evidence, the findings on each criterion, and the reasoning that connects them, at the time of the decision.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Did I identify and separately evaluate financial, personal, and structural conflicts, rather than screening only for direct money interests?
- [ ] Did I apply the reasonable-person appearance standard, rather than my own subjective confidence in my impartiality?
- [ ] Did I refuse and redirect ex parte communications about pending matters, and document any that occurred for the record?
- [ ] Did I determine whether the proceeding was quasi-judicial and apply the stricter recusal standard where it was, recusing early and completely where required?
- [ ] If a job transition was contemplated, did I identify and observe the post-employment and revolving-door restrictions before acting?
- [ ] Did I rely on objective, code-based criteria rather than impressionistic judgments, and test discretionary decisions for disparate impact on protected classes?
- [ ] Did I document on the record the criteria, evidence, findings, conditions, and the reasoning connecting them, plus any disclosures, ex parte contacts, and recusals?
- [ ] Where due process, open-meetings, discrimination, or fraud issues were present, did I involve legal counsel and consider ethics-board referral rather than handling it informally?
