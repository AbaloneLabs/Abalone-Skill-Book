# Displacement Risk and Anti-Gentrification Analysis

Displacement is the shadow side of urban investment: the same plan that brings amenities, transit, and housing to a disinvested neighborhood can raise rents and property taxes enough to push out the very residents the investment was meant to serve. The judgment problem is not whether to invest but how to analyze who is vulnerable to displacement, predict where investment will trigger it, and design interventions that capture the benefits of investment for existing residents rather than displacing them. Planners commonly fail by treating displacement as a side effect to be mourned rather than a predictable outcome to be modeled and prevented, by analyzing only direct (physical) displacement and ignoring cultural and business displacement, or by adopting vague "anti-displacement" language without binding strategies. Rigorous displacement analysis is what separates equitable development from greenwashed gentrification.

## Core Rules

### Model Displacement Risk Multidimensionally

Displacement vulnerability is not a single variable. It is a composite of: housing cost burden (renters paying more than 30-50% of income), tenure (renters are far more vulnerable than owners), income and income trend, presence of subsidized or rent-controlled units at risk of expiring, demographic indicators of marginalized groups, length of residency, linguistic isolation, and proximity to planned investment. Build a displacement risk index from these factors using census, assessor, and local data, and map it at the block group or parcel level. A single-metric analysis ("median rent is rising") misses the populations actually at risk. The map should show where vulnerable residents live relative to where investment is planned, so interventions can be targeted.

### Distinguish Direct, Indirect, Cultural, and Business Displacement

Direct displacement is physical removal (demolition, eviction, rent hike forcing a move). Indirect or exclusionary displacement is when residents can no longer afford to move into or stay in a neighborhood as costs rise, even without an eviction. Cultural displacement is the erosion of a community's identity, institutions, and networks as the original residents and businesses leave. Business displacement is the loss of minority- and immigrant-owned small businesses that serve the existing community when commercial rents rise. A complete analysis addresses all four, because a plan that prevents only direct displacement can still produce cultural and economic erasure that hollows out the community it claimed to serve.

### Time the Analysis to Influence the Plan, Not Decorate It

Displacement analysis performed after the plan's direction is set cannot change the outcome; it can only document the harm. Run the analysis early, in parallel with the plan's alternatives, so that displacement risk becomes a criterion for choosing between alternatives and calibrating interventions. If a rezoning alternative produces high displacement risk and another produces less, that should weigh in the choice. Analysis that arrives too late to shape decisions is, at best, an apology in advance.

### Pair Investment with Binding Anti-Displacement Strategies

Investment without protection accelerates displacement; investment with protection can produce equitable development. Binding strategies include: preserving and expanding affordable units (acquisition, rehab, long-term covenants), tenant protections (just-cause eviction, rent stabilization where legal, right of first refusal), small business stabilization (commercial rent protections, technical assistance, legacy business programs), land trusts and community ownership, home repair and tax relief for long-term owners, and targeted hiring and wealth-building for existing residents. Vague commitments ("we value existing residents") are not strategies; they are rhetoric. Tie each identified risk to a specific, funded intervention with measurable outcomes and an accountable implementer.

### Center the Existing Community in the Plan's Benefits

The default of market-driven redevelopment is that benefits (new housing, transit, amenities, jobs) accrue to new and higher-income residents while costs (displacement, disruption) fall on existing ones. Explicitly design the plan so existing residents benefit: affordable units reserved for area residents, local hiring and procurement, community-owned commercial space, public realm improvements in existing (not just new) areas, and programs that build wealth rather than extract it. Equity in development is not automatic; it must be engineered into the plan's incentives and conditions.

### Monitor and Adapt Over the Implementation Period

Displacement risk is dynamic; it shifts as investment lands, as markets react, and as policies take effect. Build monitoring into the plan — track rent trends, eviction filings, business turnover, demographic change, and affordable unit counts against baselines — and build in triggers for additional intervention if indicators move in the wrong direction. A plan that launches anti-displacement strategies but never checks whether they work is operating on faith. Adaptation based on monitored outcomes is what turns a one-time analysis into ongoing stewardship of an existing community.

## Common Traps

### Analyzing Only Direct Displacement

Focusing on demolition and eviction misses the far larger population displaced indirectly by rising costs and the cultural and business displacement that erodes community even without physical removal. The false signal is "the project itself displaces few residents"; the harm is that the surrounding market dynamics displace many more, unmeasured and unaddressed. Analyze all four forms of displacement to capture the real impact.

### Running the Analysis Too Late to Influence Decisions

A displacement study completed after the rezoning is approved can document risk but cannot redirect the plan. The false signal is that the plan "addressed displacement"; the harm is that the analysis was decorative rather than decision-shaping. Sequence displacement analysis to run alongside alternative development so it informs the choice.

### Substituting Rhetoric for Binding Strategies

Plans full of "preserve community character" and "prevent displacement" language, without funded, enforceable mechanisms, provide political cover while changing nothing. The false signal is a values-affirming plan; the harm is continued displacement under the banner of equity. Require that every stated anti-displacement goal be backed by a specific intervention, an implementer, a budget, and a measurable target.

### Ignoring Business and Cultural Displacement

Plans that protect residential tenants but leave commercial tenants exposed can still destroy a community's economic and cultural infrastructure — its restaurants, barbershops, places of worship, social service providers. The false signal is "we protected residents"; the harm is the loss of the institutions that made the community coherent. Include commercial stabilization and cultural preservation in the strategy set.

### Treating Displacement as Unavoidable Collateral Damage

A resigned framing — "some displacement is inevitable with investment" — naturalizes a preventable outcome and relieves the plan of responsibility. The false signal is realism; the harm is that preventable displacement is allowed to occur because the plan did not try hard enough to prevent it. Treat displacement as a predictable effect that proportional, well-designed interventions can substantially reduce, and be accountable for the gap between what was predicted and what occurred.

## Self-Check

- Have you built a multidimensional displacement risk index (cost burden, tenure, income, subsidized unit status, demographics, length of residency, linguistic isolation) and mapped it relative to planned investment?
- Does the analysis address direct, indirect, cultural, and business displacement — not just physical removal?
- Was the analysis timed to run alongside plan alternatives so displacement risk could shape the choice, rather than arriving after decisions were locked?
- Is every identified risk paired with a specific, funded, enforceable anti-displacement strategy with an implementer and a measurable target — not just rhetoric?
- Does the plan engineer benefits (affordable units, jobs, amenities, ownership) toward existing residents rather than defaulting them to new arrivals?
- Are tenant protections, business stabilization, community ownership, and wealth-building tools included where appropriate, not only housing production?
- Is there a monitoring framework to track rent, evictions, business turnover, demographics, and affordable units against baselines, with triggers for additional intervention?
- Did you avoid treating displacement as an inevitable side effect rather than a predictable, preventable outcome the plan is accountable for?
- Are the anti-displacement strategies calibrated to the specific vulnerable populations identified, rather than generic measures applied uniformly?
- Could existing residents review this plan and conclude it was designed to keep them in place and let them benefit from investment — rather than to replace them?
