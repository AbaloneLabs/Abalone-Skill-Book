# Development Agreements and Vesting Entitlements

A development agreement (DA) is a contract between a municipality and a developer that vests the developer's entitlements for a defined term — often ten to twenty years or more — in exchange for the developer's binding commitments on infrastructure, phasing, community benefits, and performance. It is the instrument that makes the promises exchanged at a hearing legally durable, and it is also the instrument by which a community can lock itself into a project's terms long after the officials who approved it have left office and the market conditions that justified it have changed. The judgment problem is that a DA is simultaneously a stability device (protecting the developer's investment against regulatory change) and a flexibility device (allowing negotiated exactions that exceed what the municipality could impose under its standard codes), and the negotiation must balance these without granting windfall entitlements, surrendering future regulatory authority, or accepting commitments that sound binding but are unenforceable when it matters. A DA drafted loosely vests the rights and dilutes the obligations; a DA drafted well aligns the developer's and the public's interests for the full term.

## Core Rules

### Vest Only What Is Necessary, for Only as Long as Is Justified

Vesting is the core benefit the developer receives: the entitlements in place at execution govern the project for the term, insulating it from subsequent zoning changes, code amendments, and fee increases. The rule is to vest only the specific entitlements the project actually requires (the rezoning, the density, the use approvals, the specific plan or PUD), for a term justified by the project's realistic buildout horizon, with extensions tied to defined milestones rather than granted automatically. An overbroad vest — "all regulations in effect on the date of execution" — freezes not just the project-relevant standards but every unrelated code provision, foreclosing the municipality's ability to adopt new stormwater, environmental, safety, or affordability standards for the site for a generation. The term should reflect the genuine development schedule (a phased project may need ten to fifteen years; a single-building project rarely needs more than five to seven), and extensions should be earned through substantial progress, not granted as of right, so that a stalled project does not hold vested rights indefinitely while the surrounding regulatory landscape evolves.

### Make Every Developer Commitment Specific, Measurable, and Enforceable

The community benefits negotiated into a DA — affordable housing units, park dedications, road improvements, design standards, phasing, open space, community fund contributions — are valuable only to the extent they are drafted as enforceable obligations rather than aspirations. The rule is that each commitment must specify what will be done, by whom, by when, to what standard, and with what consequence for non-performance. "The developer will construct affordable housing" is unenforceable; "The developer will construct, prior to issuance of the 200th certificate of occupancy, no fewer than 40 dwelling units affordable at 60 percent of area median income for a period of 30 years, secured by a recorded affordability covenant, with non-performance resulting in [specific remedy]" is enforceable. Every commitment should have a measurable trigger (a phase, a date, an occupancy count), a verifiable standard, and a default remedy (specific performance, liquidated damages, revocation of entitlements, escrow forfeiture) that the municipality can actually exercise. A commitment without a remedy is a statement of intent, and a DA full of statements of intent delivers the developer's rights without the public's benefits.

### Reserve the Municipality's Regulatory Authority for Health, Safety, and New Law

A DA that vests "all applicable regulations" can be read to bar the municipality from applying subsequently adopted health, safety, building-code, and environmental standards to the site, which is both bad policy and, in many jurisdictions, legally suspect. The rule is to carve out explicit reservations: the DA should not preclude the application of subsequently adopted health and safety regulations, building and fire codes, and standards required by federal or state law, and it should preserve the municipality's authority to impose new requirements necessitated by unforeseen health, safety, or environmental conditions. The reservation should be drafted specifically rather than as a general catch-all, because vague reservations invite dispute, and the dispute will arise years later when the municipality needs to apply a new standard the developer claims is vested out. The balance is to give the developer reasonable protection against arbitrary regulatory change while preserving the municipality's non-waivable duty to protect public health and safety.

### Tie Phasing and Infrastructure to Triggers That Protect the Public

Phased developments spread buildout over years, and the infrastructure (roads, water, sewer, schools, parks, fire service) must keep pace with each phase or the later phases will impose burdens the earlier phases did not. The rule is to define phasing triggers tied to development milestones — number of certificates of occupancy, building permits issued, dwelling units built — and to condition each phase's progression on the completion of the infrastructure required to serve it. The trap is the "build the profitable phases first, defer the infrastructure" pattern, where the residential phases proceed and the road widenings, school capacity, or parkland are deferred to a later phase that may never come. The review should require that infrastructure precede or accompany the development it serves, should define the consequences if a phase stalls (does the developer retain the right to the later phases, or do they lapse?), and should ensure that the DA's phasing aligns with the comprehensive plan's growth sequence and the capital facilities plan, so that the phased project does not outpace the public's capacity to serve it.

### Secure Performance Through Bonds, Escrows, and Recorded Instruments

A DA's commitments extend over many years and across ownership changes, and the security for those commitments must survive both. The rule is to secure performance through instruments that do not depend on the original developer's solvency or continued involvement: performance bonds and cash escrows for infrastructure construction, with amounts sized to complete the work on default; recorded covenants, deed restrictions, and easements for affordability, open space, design, and use commitments that run with the land; and assignment and successor provisions that bind purchasers and lenders. The review should confirm that the security is adequate (a bond sized to 50 percent of construction cost does not protect against default), that it remains in force for the full obligation period (an affordability covenant that expires with the DA term rather than the affordability period is a gap), and that the municipality's remedies are exercisable without litigation where possible. A DA whose commitments are unsecured is a set of promises that survive only as long as the developer chooses to honor them.

## Common Traps

### Vesting All Regulations Indefinitely

The DA vests "all applicable land use regulations in effect on the date of execution" for a term of twenty years with automatic extensions, and the municipality later discovers it cannot apply a newly adopted stormwater standard, a wildfire defensible-space requirement, or an updated building code to the site. The trap mechanism is that an overbroad vesting clause, drafted for developer certainty, sweeps in regulatory categories the negotiation never contemplated, freezing the site under a regulatory regime that ages out of relevance and safety. The false signal is that the clause is "standard" and protects the developer's investment. The harm is that the municipality cannot protect public health and safety at the site under modern standards, neighboring properties bear the spillover of the unregulated development, and the community questions why one developer was exempted from rules everyone else follows. The corrective is to vest only project-specific entitlements for a justified term, with explicit reservations for health, safety, building code, and new legal requirements.

### Commitments Without Triggers or Remedies

The DA recites that the developer "will provide affordable housing," "will construct the trail connection," and "will contribute to the parks fund," and these statements are presented as the public benefits justifying the entitlements. The trap mechanism is that without a measurable trigger (when), a verifiable standard (to what), and a remedy (what happens if not), the commitments are unenforceable, and a developer who later finds them inconvenient can defer or reinterpret them with little consequence. The false signal is that the benefits are "in the agreement." The harm is that the entitlements vest and the benefits never materialize, the public receives density and infrastructure burden without the offsetting commitments, and the next developer points to the unenforced DA as precedent for similar leniency. The corrective is to draft every commitment with a specific trigger, standard, and default remedy, and to secure it with a bond, escrow, or recorded covenant.

### Phasing That Front-Loads Revenue and Defers Infrastructure

The DA's phasing sequence builds the high-margin residential phases first and defers the road widening, the school site dedication, and the regional park to later phases, on the assumption that the later phases will proceed. The trap mechanism is that if the later phases stall (market downturn, developer decision, financing failure), the deferred infrastructure never arrives, but the early phases' impacts — traffic, school enrollment, park demand — are fully realized and borne by the existing public systems. The false signal is that the infrastructure is "required" by the DA. The harm is congested roads, overcrowded schools, and missing amenities, with no leverage to compel the deferred work because the developer has not technically breached (the trigger was a later phase that never started). The corrective is to require infrastructure to precede or accompany the development it serves, with phasing triggers tied to milestones and consequences for stalled phases.

### Unsecured Commitments Across Ownership Changes

The DA is executed with Developer A, who makes the affordability, open-space, and infrastructure commitments, and three years later the project is sold to Developer B, who disclaims the commitments as not binding on the successor. The trap mechanism is that without recorded covenants running with the land, assignment provisions binding successors, and security that does not depend on the original developer, the commitments are personal to the original party and dissolve on transfer. The false signal is that the DA "runs with the land." The harm is that the entitlements (which do transfer) proceed while the commitments (which do not) disappear, and the municipality has granted the value without receiving the consideration. The corrective is to secure commitments through recorded instruments that run with the land, assignment and successor clauses, and bonds or escrows that survive the original developer's involvement.

## Self-Check

- Does the DA vest only the specific project-relevant entitlements, for a term justified by the realistic buildout horizon, rather than "all regulations" indefinitely?
- Are extensions tied to defined milestones of substantial progress, rather than granted automatically, so stalled projects do not hold vested rights indefinitely?
- Does each developer commitment specify what, by whom, by when, to what standard, and with what default remedy — rather than reciting aspirations?
- Does the DA explicitly reserve the municipality's authority to apply subsequently adopted health, safety, building-code, and federal/state-law-mandated standards?
- Is phasing tied to development-milestone triggers, with infrastructure required to precede or accompany the development it serves and consequences for stalled phases?
- Are commitments secured by performance bonds or cash escrows sized to complete the work on default, and by recorded covenants and easements running with the land?
- Do assignment and successor provisions bind purchasers and lenders, so commitments survive ownership changes?
- Are affordability, open-space, and use restrictions recorded for their full intended duration (which may exceed the DA term), not coterminous with the DA?
- Has the DA been checked against state statutory authority for development agreements, including any statutory limits on term, scope, and exactions?
- Does the DA's phasing align with the comprehensive plan's growth sequence and the capital facilities plan, so the project does not outpace public service capacity?
