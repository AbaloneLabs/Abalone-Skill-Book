# Discretionary Review and Hearing Preparation

A discretionary hearing is the moment where analysis becomes a decision, and where the quality of the staff report, the presentation, and the process determine whether the outcome is defensible. The judgment problem is not how to "recommend approval or denial" but how to equip a lay decision-making body — volunteers, council members, or commissioners who were not part of the analysis — to make a lawful, well-supported decision under public scrutiny, often in a charged room. Planners commonly fail by writing staff reports that bury the key issues, by presenting findings the commission does not actually understand, by mishandling ex parte communications or procedural requirements, or by failing to build a complete record that can survive appeal. Hearing preparation is where the integrity of the entire review process is tested.

## Core Rules

### Write the Staff Report for the Decision-Maker, Not for the File

The staff report's primary audience is the hearing body, which needs to understand the project, the key issues, the applicable standards, the public input, and the basis for a decision — clearly enough to deliberate and vote intelligently. Lead with the decision and the decisive issues, not with background that buries them. Use plain language for the executive summary and key findings; reserve technical detail for appendices that a commissioner can consult but is not required to wade through. A report that a commissioner cannot follow in the reading period will produce a confused deliberation and a poorly reasoned decision. Test the report by asking whether a non-planner could explain the recommendation and its basis after one read.

### Frame the Discretionary Question Precisely

Discretionary review is not a free vote; it is a decision bounded by specific standards. The staff report must state exactly what the hearing body is being asked to decide, what findings are required, and what evidence supports each finding. If the project requires findings that it is consistent with the plan, compatible with surrounding uses, and supported by adequate facilities, the report should walk through each finding and explain whether the evidence supports it. Vague framing ("should we approve this project?") invites decisions based on personal preference rather than the standards, which is a recipe for overturn on appeal. Give the commission the legal structure of the decision, then the facts to fill it.

### Present the Decision Architecture, Not Just the Conclusion

At the hearing, your presentation should equip the body to deliberate, not just deliver a verdict. Explain the required findings, summarize the key evidence for and against each, identify the genuinely contested issues, and present the recommendation with its rationale. Be honest about where reasonable commissioners could disagree; pretending the decision is obvious disrespects the body and weakens the record. Anticipate the questions commissioners are likely to ask (traffic, density, compatibility, precedent) and prepare concise, evidence-based answers. A presentation that only argues for the conclusion leaves the commission unprepared to defend its vote.

### Build a Complete and Clean Record

The hearing record — application materials, staff report, public testimony, exhibits, correspondence, and the body's deliberation and vote — is what an appellate body or court will review. Ensure all exhibits are entered and referenced, testimony is captured (by court reporter, recording, or minutes), and the motion and vote are clearly recorded with the findings adopted. If conditions are modified at the hearing, document the modifications precisely and confirm they are incorporated into the resolution. A hearing that produced a sound decision but a sloppy record can still lose on appeal because the record does not show the basis for the decision.

### Manage Process, Ex Parte, and Procedural Requirements

Discretionary decisions are governed by procedural rules that, if violated, can invalidate the outcome regardless of the merits: proper notice (type, timing, radius, languages), quorum, abstentions and conflicts, limits on ex parte communications, and the right of applicants and opponents to present and rebut evidence. Brief commissioners in advance on conflicts and ex parte rules, ensure notice was correct and documented, and be prepared to address procedural objections at the hearing. A merit-based decision can be overturned purely for procedural error, so treat process as substantive. When in doubt, consult the agency attorney before the hearing, not during it.

### Prepare for and Navigate Appeals

Many entitlements can be appealed to the city council or a higher body, and the hearing record travels with the appeal. Write the staff report and conduct the hearing anticipating that the decision may be reviewed by a body that did not attend it. Make the reasoning explicit, the evidence citations precise, and the conditions clean, so an appellate body can affirm on the existing record rather than needing to re-litigate the issues. Understand the appeal standards (de novo review vs. substantial evidence review) and structure the record accordingly.

## Common Traps

### Burying the Decision in Background

Staff reports that open with pages of project description, site context, and code recitation before reaching the recommendation train commissioners to skim and miss the key issues. The false signal is thoroughness; the harm is that the body votes on a recommendation it does not fully understand and cannot defend. Lead with the decision, the contested issues, and the findings, and push background to its proper supporting role.

### Letting the Hearing Drift from the Standards

Without firm framing, a hearing can devolve into a referendum on the project in general — whether commissioners "like" it — rather than a decision against specific findings. The false signal is robust deliberation; the harm is a decision based on factors outside the legal standards, which is vulnerable to overturn and produces inconsistent precedent. Continually redirect deliberation to the required findings and the evidence relevant to each.

### Mishandling Ex Parte Communications and Conflicts

Commissioners who discuss the project with applicants or opponents outside the hearing, or who fail to disclose conflicts, create procedural defects that can invalidate the decision. The false signal is that the commissioner "already knows the issues"; the harm is a tainted record and a decision vulnerable to challenge for bias or prejudgment. Train commissioners on ex parte and conflict rules before each major hearing, and document disclosures and recusals in the record.

### Failing to Capture Modifications Made at the Hearing

When a commission amends conditions or the project at the dais, the changes must be precisely documented and incorporated into the final resolution; vague references ("as discussed") are not enough. The false signal is that the modifications are "on the record"; the harm is that the approved project does not match the recorded decision, leading to permit disputes and appeals. Read back substantive modifications, confirm them with the body, and ensure the resolution reflects them exactly.

### Presenting as an Advocate Rather Than an Analyst

When staff recommendations align with the applicant, the presentation can slide into advocacy, which undermines the staff's credibility as a neutral analyst and hands opponents a narrative of bias. The false signal is a confident, persuasive presentation; the harm is that the body and the public stop treating the analysis as objective, weakening the entire record. Present the analysis evenhandedly — including the evidence against the recommendation — and let the merits carry the case.

## Self-Check

- Does the staff report lead with the decision and the decisive issues, in language a non-planner commissioner can follow in one read?
- Does the report state precisely what the hearing body is being asked to decide, the required findings, and the evidence supporting each?
- Does your presentation equip the body to deliberate on the findings, including honest acknowledgment of contested issues, rather than merely arguing a conclusion?
- Is the hearing record complete — all exhibits entered and referenced, testimony captured, motion and vote and findings clearly recorded?
- Were notice, quorum, conflicts, and ex parte requirements verified and documented, with the agency attorney consulted on any uncertainty?
- Are any modifications made at the dais precisely documented and incorporated into the final resolution?
- Did you structure the record anticipating a possible appeal, with explicit reasoning and precise evidence citations?
- Did you continually redirect deliberation to the required findings rather than letting it drift into a general referendum on the project?
- Did you present the analysis evenhandedly, including evidence against the recommendation, rather than as advocacy?
- If this decision were appealed, would the record show a lawful, well-reasoned decision against the proper standards — or would gaps in the report or record create vulnerability?
