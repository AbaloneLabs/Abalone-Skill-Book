# Findings and Conditions of Approval

Findings and conditions are where a development decision becomes either legally bulletproof or dangerously exposed. The judgment problem is not how to "write up" a decision that has already been made but how to construct a record that ties the decision to specific code authority, specific evidence in the record, and conditions that are lawful, proportionate, and enforceable. Planners commonly fail by writing generic findings that recite the code without applying it to the facts, by stacking conditions borrowed from other projects without checking their nexus to this project's impacts, or by imposing requirements that exceed the agency's legal authority — any of which can invalidate an approval that took years to reach. Rigorous findings and conditions work is what separates an approval that survives appeal and litigation from one that collapses under scrutiny.

## Core Rules

### Build Findings as Reasoned Decisions, Not Code Recitations

A finding is not a copy of the code standard; it is the decision-maker's reasoned explanation of how the evidence in the record satisfies that standard for this specific project. For each required finding, the structure should be: the code requires X; the record contains evidence Y (citing specific exhibits, testimony, studies); based on that evidence, the decision-maker concludes Z. Generic findings ("the project is consistent with the comprehensive plan") that do not reference the specific plan policies, the specific project features, and the specific evidence are the most common ground for overturning an approval on appeal or in court. Every finding must be traceable: a reader should be able to follow it from the code requirement, through the evidence, to the conclusion.

### Tie Every Condition to Authority, Impact, and Proportionality

Each condition of approval must rest on three legs: legal authority (a specific code provision or police-power basis that allows the agency to impose it), a nexus to the project's specific impacts (the condition must address an impact the project actually creates), and proportionality (the condition must be roughly proportional in nature and extent to that impact). Conditions that fail any of these tests are vulnerable as unlawful exactions, especially when they require dedications of land, fees, or construction of improvements. For each condition, be able to articulate the impact it addresses and the code provision that authorizes it; if you cannot, the condition should not be imposed. Borrowed condition libraries are a starting point, not a substitute for project-specific calibration.

### Distinguish Mandatory, Discretionary, and Suggested Conditions

Not everything in a conditions list carries the same legal weight. Mandatory conditions are required by code and must be met for the project to be lawful; violating them invalidates the approval. Discretionary conditions are imposed under the agency's discretionary authority to mitigate project impacts and must meet nexus and proportionality tests. Suggested or "recommended" conditions are not legally binding and should not be presented as if they were. Conflating these categories confuses applicants, creates unenforceable requirements, and muddies the record on appeal. Label each condition by its basis and authority so the legal weight is clear.

### Sequence Conditions to the Project Lifecycle

Conditions apply at different points: prior to issuance of permits, during construction, prior to occupancy, and over the life of the operation. Organize conditions by phase so the applicant, the building official, and the code enforcement staff all know what must be satisfied when. A condition that is buried in a long list but actually gates permit issuance can be missed, leading to permits issued out of order and compliance failures. Clear sequencing also helps the applicant understand the true cost and timeline of compliance, which reduces disputes later.

### Make Conditions Clear, Enforceable, and Verifiable

A condition that is ambiguous cannot be enforced and invites dispute. Each condition should state specifically what must be done, by whom, by when, against what standard, and how compliance will be verified. Avoid subjective standards ("the building shall be attractive," "landscaping shall be appropriate") that cannot be measured; replace them with objective, verifiable standards (specific plant species, heights, opacity, materials). Vague conditions are either unenforceable or give staff discretion that can be challenged as arbitrary. If a condition cannot be written clearly, reconsider whether it should be a condition at all or whether it belongs in design guidelines applied at a different stage.

### Preserve the Record Linking Conditions to Findings

The conditions and the findings must be internally consistent: the findings establish the impacts and the basis for mitigation, and the conditions mitigate those specific impacts. A condition that addresses an impact not discussed in the findings, or a finding that identifies an impact not addressed by any condition, signals a disconnect that an appellant will exploit. Cross-reference conditions to the findings they implement, so the record reads as a coherent decision rather than a list of requirements stapled to a conclusion.

## Common Traps

### Reciting the Code Instead of Applying It to Facts

Findings that quote the code standard and then assert compliance, without referencing the specific evidence, read as conclusory and are routinely overturned. The false signal is that the finding "tracks the code"; the harm is that it provides no reasoned basis for the decision and fails the test of whether a reviewing court can follow the agency's logic. Always connect the code requirement to specific record evidence and explain the reasoning, not just the conclusion.

### Borrowing Conditions Without Checking Nexus and Proportionality

Standard condition libraries are efficient, but copying conditions wholesale from a different project without verifying that they address this project's actual impacts — at a proportional level — creates unlawful exactions. The false signal is a familiar, comprehensive-looking conditions list; the harm is that conditions may exceed the project's impacts and be struck down, potentially taking related approvals with them. Calibrate every condition to the specific project's impacts and document the nexus.

### Imposing Conditions Beyond the Agency's Authority

Enthusiasm to mitigate a project's effects can lead to conditions that exceed the agency's legal powers — requiring public improvements outside the project's impact zone, extracting community benefits unrelated to impacts, or regulating matters under another agency's jurisdiction. The false signal is a robust mitigation package; the harm is that the conditions are unenforceable or unlawful and can invalidate the approval. Stay within the four corners of the code's authority, and where ambition exceeds authority, pursue the goal through code amendment or voluntary agreements rather than conditions.

### Using Vague or Subjective Conditions

Conditions phrased as "shall be compatible," "shall be high quality," or "to the satisfaction of staff" cannot be objectively verified and invite disputes about whether compliance has been achieved. The false signal is flexibility; the harm is that the applicant cannot know what compliance looks like, staff discretion becomes a source of arbitrary decisions, and enforcement becomes impossible. Replace subjective language with specific, measurable standards wherever possible, and reserve subjective review for design guidelines where it is appropriate.

### Failing to Reconcile Conditions Across Reviewers

When multiple departments contribute conditions, the final set can contain redundancies, contradictions, or requirements that cannot be satisfied simultaneously. The false signal is thoroughness; the harm is an unworkable approval that the applicant cannot actually build and that exposes the agency to appeals. The lead planner must reconcile the full condition set for internal consistency before the decision is issued.

## Self-Check

- Does each required finding cite the specific code standard, reference specific evidence in the record, and explain the reasoning that connects them — rather than merely reciting the code?
- Can you articulate, for every condition, the legal authority, the specific project impact it addresses (nexus), and why it is proportionate to that impact?
- Are conditions clearly labeled as mandatory, discretionary, or suggested, so their legal weight is unambiguous?
- Are conditions organized by project phase (pre-permit, construction, pre-occupancy, operational) so compliance timing is clear to applicant and staff?
- Is each condition written with specific, objective, verifiable standards — avoiding vague or subjective language that cannot be enforced?
- Are the conditions cross-referenced to the findings, so the record shows each condition implementing a specific identified impact?
- Did you avoid copying conditions from other projects without verifying their nexus and proportionality to this project's actual impacts?
- Did you confirm that no condition exceeds the agency's legal authority or regulates matters outside its jurisdiction?
- Did the lead planner reconcile conditions from all reviewing departments into a consistent, non-contradictory set?
- If this approval were appealed or litigated, would the findings and conditions withstand review for reasoned decision-making, lawful authority, nexus, and proportionality?
