# Freight and Goods Movement Planning

Freight is the economic lifeblood of a region — every good consumed, produced, or traded moves through a freight system of trucks, rail, ports, warehouses, and distribution centers — but the same system generates congestion, emissions, noise, pavement damage, and safety conflicts that fall disproportionately on the communities it passes through. Freight planning is the effort to keep goods moving while managing these impacts, and it is governed by a fundamental tension: the freight system optimizes for efficiency and cost, which concentrates traffic on the fastest, cheapest routes, while the community optimizes for livability and safety, which seeks to disperse, reduce, or redirect that traffic. The judgment problem is that freight is essential and disruptive in equal measure, and the planning decisions — where trucks route, where warehouses locate, how deliveries occur, how rail and port interfaces are managed — determine whether the freight system serves the economy without degrading the communities it crosses. A plan that prioritizes freight efficiency without managing community impact generates opposition that can restrict freight movement; a plan that restricts freight without providing alternatives raises costs and suppresses economic activity. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether freight planning balances movement and community or produces conflict that serves neither.

## Core Rules

### Designate and Protect a Truck Route Network Based on Function

A designated truck route network channels freight traffic onto corridors designed for it — with adequate pavement, bridge clearance, turning radii, and intersection geometry — and away from local streets and sensitive uses where trucks create safety and livability problems. The rule is to designate the network based on function: identify the corridors that connect origins and destinations (ports, intermodal facilities, warehouses, industrial districts, commercial centers), confirm they have the physical capacity for truck traffic, and designate them as the primary truck routes with signage and enforcement that keeps trucks on them. The network should be coherent — trucks must be able to reach their destinations without being forced off the network onto local streets — and it should avoid routing through residential areas, school zones, and pedestrian-heavy districts where the conflict is highest. A truck route network that is incomplete (trucks must leave it to reach destinations) or unenforced (trucks cut through local streets for shortcut) fails to manage the impact it was designed to address.

### Separate Freight Land Uses From Sensitive Uses Through Zoning and Buffering

Warehouses, distribution centers, and logistics facilities generate intensive truck traffic, 24-hour operations, noise, and emissions, and locating them adjacent to residential areas, schools, or sensitive environmental areas creates conflicts that are difficult to mitigate after the fact. The rule is to use zoning and buffering to separate freight-intensive uses from sensitive uses: concentrate warehouses and distribution in designated industrial or logistics districts with access to the truck route network and rail, require buffer zones, landscaping, berms, or setbacks at the edges where industrial meets other uses, and use overlay districts or performance standards to manage external impacts (hours of operation, truck queuing, noise, lighting). The separation should be planned at the district level, because the cumulative impact of multiple facilities is greater than any single facility, and because the truck traffic they generate concentrates on the routes that serve them. Allowing freight-intensive uses to disperse into commercial or residential areas through zoning gaps creates conflicts that retroactive mitigation cannot resolve.

### Manage Last-Mile Delivery and Urban Loading

The growth of e-commerce has intensified last-mile delivery — the final leg from distribution center to doorstep — and the delivery vehicles (vans, trucks, cargo bikes) generate curb conflicts, double-parking, congestion, and safety issues in the very districts where pedestrian and commercial activity is highest. The rule is to manage last-mile delivery and urban loading through curb allocation (designated loading zones with time-of-day regulation), delivery windows (restricting large-truck delivery to off-peak hours in dense districts), consolidation (micro-fulfillment and consolidation centers that reduce the number of delivery vehicles in the final mile), and alternative modes (cargo bikes and electric vehicles for zero-emission zones). The management should be district-specific, because delivery patterns vary by land use (retail districts need daytime loading, residential needs evening), and it should balance the freight access that businesses depend on with the curb and street space that pedestrians and other modes require. Unmanaged last-mile delivery produces chaotic curbs, blocked travel lanes, and conflicts that degrade the district for all users.

### Coordinate Across Jurisdictions and Modes

Freight moves across jurisdictional boundaries and across modes — truck to rail to port to air — and planning that is confined to a single jurisdiction or mode produces gaps that the freight system routes around, often into the communities least able to absorb the impact. The rule is to coordinate freight planning across the jurisdictions and modes the system spans: regional freight plans that identify the corridors, facilities, and gateways; intergovernmental agreements that manage truck routing across boundaries; and multimodal integration that connects truck, rail, water, and air freight into a coherent system. Regional metropolitan planning organizations are typically the vehicle for this coordination, but it requires the participation of the ports, railroads, and logistics operators whose decisions shape the system. A jurisdiction that plans freight in isolation may restrict truck traffic only to push it onto a neighboring jurisdiction's streets, generating interjurisdictional conflict and solving nothing regionally.

### Address the Equity and Health Burdens on Affected Communities

Freight corridors and facilities are disproportionately located in lower-income and minority communities, and the impacts — diesel particulate emissions, noise, crash risk, pavement damage — fall disproportionately on these communities, creating environmental justice concerns that freight planning must address. The rule is to assess the equity and health burdens of freight infrastructure on affected communities, using air quality monitoring, health impact assessment, and demographic analysis, and to implement mitigation that reduces the burden: truck route restrictions in sensitive areas, low-emission zones or electric truck requirements, pavement and infrastructure repair funded by the freight users, and community benefits agreements that return value to the affected neighborhoods. Freight planning that ignores the equity dimension perpetuates the disproportionate burden and generates the opposition and litigation that increasingly delay or block freight projects, because the communities bearing the burden have legal and political tools to challenge projects that worsen their condition.

## Common Traps

### Truck Route Network That Is Incomplete or Unenforced

A truck route network is designated but has gaps (trucks must leave it to reach destinations) or is not enforced (trucks cut through local streets), and the freight traffic the network was meant to channel continues to affect local streets. The trap is that a network that cannot be used or is not enforced fails to manage impact. The mechanism is that trucks optimize for time and cost, and if the designated route is longer or slower, or if enforcement is absent, they use the local streets. The false signal is that "we have a designated truck route." The harm is that local streets continue to bear truck traffic they were not designed for, residents experience the safety and livability impacts the network was meant to prevent, and the designation provides no actual benefit.

### Warehousing Adjacent to Residential Without Buffer

A warehouse or distribution center is approved adjacent to a residential area without adequate buffering, and the truck traffic, noise, lighting, and emissions degrade the neighborhood. The trap is that the land use conflict was created at approval and cannot be fully mitigated after. The mechanism is that the zoning allowed the use without requiring the buffer, or the buffer was reduced under development pressure. The false signal is that "the use is permitted in the district." The harm is that residents experience chronic impacts, property values decline, the conflict generates complaints and litigation, and the mitigation that is possible after the fact is expensive and incomplete compared to the separation that should have been required at zoning.

### Last-Mile Delivery Unmanaged in Dense Districts

E-commerce delivery intensifies in a commercial or mixed-use district, and the curb and loading infrastructure is not adapted, producing double-parking, blocked travel lanes, and conflicts with pedestrians and transit. The trap is that the delivery system evolved faster than the curb management. The mechanism is that the curb was allocated for parking, not loading, and the delivery vehicles have no designated space. The false signal is that "delivery vehicles are just double-parking." The harm is that congestion increases, transit is delayed, safety conflicts multiply, and the district's function degrades for all users, because the freight access that businesses need and the street space that other users need were not reconciled.

### Freight Plan Confined to One Jurisdiction

A jurisdiction restricts truck traffic or denies a freight facility, and the freight system routes around the restriction into a neighboring jurisdiction or onto less suitable routes, solving nothing regionally and generating interjurisdictional conflict. The trap is that local restriction without regional coordination shifts rather than solves the problem. The mechanism is that freight moves across boundaries, and a single jurisdiction's restriction redirects the traffic. The false signal is that "we protected our community." The harm is that the neighboring community bears the shifted burden, the interjurisdictional relationship is damaged, and the regional freight system becomes less efficient, raising costs that are passed to consumers and businesses throughout the region.

### Equity Burdens Ignored Until They Block Projects

Freight projects proceed without assessing the equity and health burdens on affected communities, and the opposition, litigation, or regulatory challenge that arises delays or blocks the project. The trap is that ignoring the equity dimension creates the opposition that defeats the project. The mechanism is that the affected communities have legal tools (environmental justice requirements, civil rights, environmental review) and political tools (organizing, media, elected officials) that can stop projects. The false signal is that "the project is technically sound." The harm is that the project is delayed or cancelled, the freight need goes unmet, and the cost and delay could have been avoided by addressing the equity burden in the planning from the start.

## Self-Check

- Is the designated truck route network complete (trucks can reach destinations without leaving it), physically adequate, and enforced through signage and monitoring?
- Are freight-intensive uses (warehouses, distribution) separated from sensitive uses through zoning, buffering, and overlay standards, rather than allowed to disperse into residential or commercial areas?
- Is last-mile delivery managed through curb allocation, loading zones, delivery windows, consolidation, and alternative modes, calibrated to each district's patterns?
- Is freight planning coordinated across jurisdictions and modes, through regional plans and intergovernmental agreements, rather than confined to a single jurisdiction?
- Have the equity and health burdens on affected communities been assessed, and is mitigation (route restrictions, emission standards, infrastructure repair, community benefits) implemented?
- Can trucks reach their destinations on the designated network, or do gaps force them onto local streets?
- Do the buffer requirements at industrial-residential edges reflect the cumulative impact of the freight district, not just individual facilities?
- Is the curb in dense districts allocated for loading and delivery, or does the default parking allocation produce double-parking and conflict?
- If this jurisdiction restricts freight, where does the traffic go, and is that outcome acceptable regionally?
- Have the communities bearing the freight burden been engaged, and does the plan reduce rather than perpetuate the disproportionate impact?
