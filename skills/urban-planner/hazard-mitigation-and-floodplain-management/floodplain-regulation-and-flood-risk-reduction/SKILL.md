# Floodplain Regulation and Flood Risk Reduction

Floodplains are among the most heavily regulated land in the United States, governed by the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) and the local ordinances that implement it, yet flood damage continues to increase, because the regulatory minimums are inadequate, the flood maps are outdated, the cumulative impact of floodplain development is unmanaged, and the development that is permitted under the minimum standards generates the losses that follow. The judgment problem is that floodplain regulation is technically complex (the hydrology, the mapping, the construction standards), legally constrained (the NFIP requirements, the takings doctrine, the vested rights), and politically charged (the property owners who want to build, the flood risk that the building creates), and the regulator must navigate these dimensions to produce a floodplain management program that reduces risk rather than merely complies with the minimum. A community that adopts the NFIP minimums — the 100-year flood, the one-foot freeboard, the basic construction standards — participates in the program but does not meaningfully reduce its flood risk, because the minimums allow development that increases the flood elevation, the velocity, and the cumulative risk, and the losses accumulate. This skill covers the floodplain regulation and risk-reduction decisions that determine whether a community reduces its flood risk or merely manages its compliance. It complements the broader hazard-mitigation skill by focusing on the floodplain-specific regulatory and NFIP dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Adopt Standards Above the NFIP Minimum to Actually Reduce Risk

The NFIP minimum standards — the 100-year (one-percent-annual-chance) flood, the no-rise requirement for the floodway, the basic elevation and anchoring requirements — are a floor, not a ceiling, and a community that adopts only the minimums allows the development that generates the future losses. The rule is to adopt standards above the NFIP minimum, through the higher freeboard (the additional height above the base flood elevation, typically two to three feet, which accounts for the uncertainty and the future conditions), the expanded regulatory floodplain (the 500-year flood, which captures the larger events that are increasingly frequent), the limitations on the fill and the development in the floodway (which conveys the flood and cannot be compromised), and the cumulative substantial improvement tracking (which captures the improvements that accumulate over time to exceed the substantial improvement threshold). The higher standards should be calibrated to the community's flood risk and its tolerance, and they should be justified by the risk reduction they produce and the Community Rating System (CRS) credits they earn, which reduce the flood insurance premiums for the community's policyholders. The higher standards should be adopted before the development pressure arrives, because adopting them retroactively faces the vested rights and the political opposition that the existing development generates.

### Use the Best Available Data, Including Future Conditions

The Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRMs) that the NFIP produces are the regulatory baseline, but they are often outdated (the mapping may be decades old), they may not reflect the current and future conditions (the development that has increased the runoff, the climate change that has increased the rainfall), and they may not capture the local flood risks (the stormwater flooding, the dam failure, the levee failure). The rule is to use the best available data, including the future conditions, supplementing the FIRM with the local flood studies, the watershed models, the future-conditions hydrology (the increased rainfall, the sea-level rise), and the local knowledge of the flood history. The best-available-data approach should be codified in the ordinance, so that the local studies can be used for the regulatory decisions even when they show the flood risk that the FIRM does not. The future-conditions data should be integrated into the regulatory standard, because the development that is permitted today will exist for decades, and the flood conditions it will face are the future conditions, not the historical conditions the FIRM reflects. The best-available-data approach requires the technical capacity to develop and to apply the local studies, and the community should invest in this capacity, because the reliance on the outdated FIRM permits the development that the future conditions will flood.

### Manage the Cumulative Impact of Floodplain Development

Floodplain development is reviewed project-by-project, but the cumulative impact of the development — the combined fill, the combined runoff, the combined displacement of the flood storage — increases the flood elevation and the flood risk over time, and the project-by-project review that does not account for the cumulative impact permits the development that generates the losses. The rule is to manage the cumulative impact of the floodplain development, through the no-rise and the no-adverse-impact standards that require each project to demonstrate that it does not increase the flood elevation or the velocity, the flood storage compensation that requires the fill to be offset by the equivalent excavation, and the watershed-based mitigation that addresses the cumulative runoff through the regional stormwater management. The cumulative impact management should track the development over time, because the individual projects that each meet the standard may collectively exceed the floodplain's capacity, and the tracking allows the community to identify when the cumulative threshold is approaching and to adjust the standards. The cumulative impact management should also address the downstream and the upstream effects, because the floodplain development in one community affects the flood risk in the neighboring communities, and the watershed-based coordination is needed to manage the cumulative impact across the jurisdictions.

### Require Flood-Resistant Construction and Substantial Improvement Tracking

The construction standards for the floodplain buildings determine whether they survive the flood, and the NFIP minimums (the elevation, the anchoring, the flood-resistant materials below the base flood elevation) are a floor that the higher standards should exceed. The rule is to require the flood-resistant construction, including the elevation above the future-conditions flood (with the freeboard), the wet or dry floodproofing (appropriate to the building type and the flood depth), the utility protection (the elevation of the mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems), and the use of the flood-resistant materials below the flood elevation. The substantial improvement and the substantial damage tracking is the mechanism that brings the existing (pre-FIRM) buildings into compliance when they are substantially improved or damaged, and the rule is to track the improvements cumulatively, because the improvements that are below the substantial threshold individually may exceed it cumulatively, and the cumulative tracking captures the buildings that should be brought into compliance. The substantial improvement tracking requires the building permit records and the inspection, and the community should maintain the system that tracks the improvements over the life of the building, because the untracked improvements allow the non-compliant buildings to persist and to generate the losses.

### Participate in the Community Rating System to Incentivize Higher Standards

The Community Rating System (CRS) rewards the communities that exceed the NFIP minimums with the reduced flood insurance premiums for their policyholders, and the CRS provides the financial incentive that can justify the higher standards politically. The rule is to participate in the CRS and to pursue the credits that the higher standards earn, using the premium reductions to build the political support for the standards that would otherwise face the opposition of the property owners. The CRS credits are earned for the higher regulatory standards (the freeboard, the floodway, the substantial improvement tracking), the floodplain mapping and the data (the local studies, the future conditions), the public outreach and the education, the flood damage reduction (the acquisition, the elevation, the mitigation), and the stormwater management. The CRS participation should be pursued strategically, targeting the credits that provide the most risk reduction and the most premium reduction per unit of effort, and the community should document the activities that earn the credits, because the CRS verification requires the documentation. The CRS participation is not an end in itself but a means to the higher standards and the risk reduction, and the community should use the CRS to advance the floodplain management that the minimums do not require.

## Common Traps

### Minimum Standards Adopted, Risk Not Reduced

The community adopts the NFIP minimums and participates in the program, but the minimums allow the development that increases the flood risk, and the community's flood losses continue to increase despite its compliance. The trap is that the minimum compliance does not reduce the risk. The mechanism is that the higher standards were not adopted, and the development proceeded under the minimums. The false signal is that "we are in the NFIP." The harm is that the flood risk increases, the losses accumulate, the insurance claims rise, the community's CRS rating does not improve, and the development that the minimums permitted generates the losses that the higher standards would have prevented.

### Outdated FIRM Used, Current and Future Risk Missed

The community relies on the outdated FIRM, the development is permitted based on the historical flood that the FIRM reflects, and the current and future flood conditions (the increased development, the climate change) flood the development that the FIRM said was safe. The trap is that the outdated FIRM understates the risk. The mechanism is that the best-available-data and the future-conditions approaches were not used, and the FIRM was treated as accurate. The false signal is that "the FIRM says it's out of the floodplain." The harm is that the development is built to the outdated standard, the future conditions flood it, the losses occur, the community is liable for the permits it issued, and the development that the FIRM said was safe is not.

### Cumulative Impact Unmanaged, Flood Risk Accumulates

The floodplain development is reviewed project-by-project, each project meets the no-rise standard, but the cumulative fill, runoff, and displacement increase the flood elevation over time, and the flood risk accumulates despite the compliant individual projects. The trap is that the project-by-project review misses the cumulative impact. The mechanism is that the cumulative tracking and the no-adverse-impact standards were not applied, and the development proceeded without the cumulative accounting. The false signal is that "each project meets the standard." The harm is that the flood elevation increases, the flood risk accumulates, the buildings that were permitted flood at the higher elevation, and the community's flood losses increase despite the compliant project-by-project review.

### Substantial Improvement Not Tracked Cumulatively

The substantial improvement and the substantial damage are not tracked cumulatively, and the pre-FIRM buildings that are improved incrementally (each improvement below the substantial threshold) are never brought into compliance, and they generate the losses when they flood. The trap is that the untracked improvements allow the non-compliant buildings to persist. The mechanism is that the cumulative tracking was not implemented, and the improvements were reviewed individually. The false signal is that "each improvement is below the threshold." The harm is that the non-compliant buildings persist, they flood, the losses occur, the insurance claims rise, and the buildings that should have been brought into compliance continue to generate the losses.

### CRS Not Pursued, Higher Standards Lack the Incentive

The community does not participate in the CRS or does not pursue the credits that the higher standards earn, and the higher standards face the political opposition that the premium reductions could have overcome, and the community adopts the minimums instead. The trap is that the CRS incentive is not used to build the support for the higher standards. The mechanism is that the CRS participation was not pursued, and the premium reductions were not available to justify the standards. The false signal is that "the CRS is too much work." The harm is that the higher standards are not adopted, the flood risk is not reduced, the policyholders pay the higher premiums, and the community misses the financial incentive that would have advanced the floodplain management.

## Self-Check

- Are the floodplain standards adopted above the NFIP minimum, including the freeboard, the expanded floodplain, the floodway limitations, and the cumulative substantial improvement tracking?
- Is the best available data used, including the local studies and the future-conditions hydrology, with the approach codified in the ordinance?
- Is the cumulative impact of the floodplain development managed, through the no-adverse-impact standards, the flood storage compensation, and the watershed-based mitigation?
- Is the flood-resistant construction required, with the elevation, the floodproofing, the utility protection, and the substantial improvement tracking that brings the existing buildings into compliance?
- Is the CRS participation pursued strategically, with the credits that the higher standards earn used to build the political support and to reduce the premiums?
- Do the standards exceed the NFIP minimum, or does the community adopt the floor while the flood risk continues to increase?
- Does the regulatory floodplain reflect the current and future conditions, or does it rely on the outdated FIRM that understates the risk?
- Is the cumulative impact of the development tracked and managed, or does the project-by-project review permit the accumulation that increases the flood risk?
- Are the substantial improvements tracked cumulatively, or do the non-compliant buildings persist through the incremental improvements that escape the threshold?
- Is the CRS used to incentivize the higher standards, or is the incentive missed while the minimums are adopted?
