# Impact Fee Calculation and Nexus Study

Impact fees are the mechanism by which the new development pays for the infrastructure its growth requires, and the legal and the technical rigor of the fee calculation determines whether the fee is defensible, collectable, and effective. The calculation is governed by the dual rational nexus (the need for the infrastructure and the proportionate share the development pays), the proportionate share requirement (the development pays only for the infrastructure that serves it, not for the existing deficiencies), and the methodological choices (the plan-based, the incremental, the consumption-based) that determine the fee amount. The judgment problem is that the impact fee calculation requires the infrastructure planning (the facility needs, the costs, the timing), the demographic and the growth analysis (the new development, the existing population, the proportional allocation), and the legal defensibility (the rational nexus, the proportionate share, the credits), and the planner must integrate these dimensions to produce a fee that is technically sound and legally defensible. A fee that is too low does not fund the infrastructure, a fee that is too high faces the legal challenge and the political opposition, and the calculation must navigate these constraints to produce the fee that funds the growth without the overcharge. This skill covers the calculation and the nexus study decisions that determine whether the impact fee is defensible and effective. It complements the broader impact-fees skill by focusing on the technical and the legal dimensions of the fee calculation.

## Core Rules

### Establish the Rational Nexus Through the Infrastructure Plan

The rational nexus requires that the fee be tied to the infrastructure need that the development creates, and the infrastructure need must be documented through the capital facility plan that identifies the facilities, the costs, the timing, and the service population. The rule is to establish the rational nexus through the infrastructure plan, using the adopted capital facility plan (the water, the sewer, the road, the park, the school) that documents the facility needs, the costs, and the timing, and the level-of-service standard that defines the facility quantity per unit of population or development. The infrastructure plan should be adopted and current, because the fee calculation relies on the plan, and the outdated or the unadopted plan undermines the nexus. The infrastructure plan should address only the growth-related facilities (the facilities needed to serve the new development), not the existing deficiencies (the facilities needed to correct the current shortfalls), because the proportionate share requires that the development pay only for the infrastructure that serves it, and the existing deficiencies are the responsibility of the existing population. The infrastructure plan should be detailed enough to support the fee calculation (the facility quantities, the costs, the service areas), because the fee calculation depends on the plan, and the vague plan produces the vague fee.

### Select the Cost Allocation Methodology Deliberately

The cost allocation methodology determines how the infrastructure cost is distributed across the development units (the residential, the commercial, the industrial), and the choice of the methodology affects the fee amount and the defensibility. The rule is to select the cost allocation methodology deliberately, choosing among the plan-based (the total facility cost divided by the total service population), the incremental (the cost of the next increment of capacity divided by the development it serves), and the consumption-based (the cost based on the actual usage, such as the water or the sewer demand). The plan-based methodology is appropriate when the facility plan is comprehensive and the level-of-service is clear; the incremental methodology is appropriate when the facility is built in the discrete increments and the cost of the next increment is identifiable; the consumption-based methodology is appropriate when the usage varies significantly across the development types and the metering or the equivalent is available. The methodology should be documented and justified, because the legal challenge often targets the methodology, and the undocumented choice is the vulnerable choice. The methodology should be applied consistently across the facility types, because the inconsistent methodology (the plan-based for the water, the incremental for the roads) creates the internal contradictions that the challenge exploits.

### Apply the Proportionate Share and the Credits Correctly

The proportionate share requires that the development pay only for the infrastructure that serves it, and the credits require that the development receive the credit for the infrastructure it provides or the taxes it pays that fund the same infrastructure. The rule is to apply the proportionate share and the credits correctly, calculating the fee based on the development's proportional share of the facility cost (not the total cost), and applying the credits for the dedicated facilities (the roads, the parks, the water lines that the development builds), the taxes paid (the property taxes, the gas taxes that fund the same infrastructure), and the excess capacity (the capacity the development purchases that exceeds its need). The proportionate share calculation should account for the service area (the development pays for the facilities in its service area, not the facilities citywide), the level-of-service (the development pays for the adopted level-of-service, not the higher level), and the existing capacity (the development uses the existing capacity and pays only for the new capacity). The credits should be calculated and documented, because the uncredited fee overcharges the development, and the overcharge is the basis for the legal challenge. The credits should be applied to all the revenue sources that fund the same infrastructure, because the double-counting (the fee and the tax funding the same facility) violates the proportionate share.

### Update the Fee Schedule Regularly

The construction costs and the growth projections change over time, and the fee that was accurate at adoption erodes with the inflation and the changing conditions, and the fee that is not updated becomes the fee that underfunds the infrastructure or the fee that overcharges the development. The rule is to update the fee schedule regularly, at least annually for the cost adjustments (the construction cost index, the ENR index), and every three to five years for the comprehensive update (the facility plan, the growth projections, the methodology review). The annual update should adjust the fees for the cost inflation, because the unadjusted fee erodes, and the eroded fee underfunds the infrastructure. The comprehensive update should re-examine the facility plan, the growth projections, and the methodology, because the conditions change, and the fee should reflect the current conditions. The update should be documented and adopted, because the fee schedule is the regulatory document, and the undocumented or the unadopted update is not enforceable. The update should include the public review and the adoption process, because the fee affects the development community, and the transparency builds the acceptance and the defensibility.

### Document the Study for the Legal Defensibility

The impact fee study is the document that supports the fee, and the legal challenge targets the study, and the quality of the documentation determines the defensibility. The rule is to document the study for the legal defensibility, including the facility plan and the level-of-service, the cost allocation methodology and the justification, the proportionate share calculation and the credits, the fee schedule and the derivation, and the assumptions and the limitations. The documentation should be clear and complete, because the court and the hearing examiner will review it, and the incomplete documentation is the vulnerable documentation. The documentation should include the data sources and the calculations, because the reproducibility builds the credibility, and the undocumented calculation is the challengeable calculation. The documentation should be prepared by the qualified professional (the engineer, the economist, the planner), because the credibility of the preparer supports the credibility of the study, and the unqualified preparer undermines the defensibility. The documentation should be adopted by the governing body, because the adoption provides the legal authority, and the unadopted study is not the basis for the fee.

## Common Traps

### Infrastructure Plan Not Adopted or Outdated

The fee is calculated based on the infrastructure plan that is not adopted or that is outdated, and the rational nexus is undermined, because the plan does not document the facility needs, the costs, and the timing that the nexus requires. The trap is that the unadopted or the outdated plan undermines the nexus. The mechanism is that the plan was not adopted or was not updated, and the fee was calculated based on the assumption. The false signal is that "we have a facility plan." The harm is that the rational nexus is challenged, the fee is invalidated, the infrastructure is underfunded, and the study that should have documented the nexus does not.

### Methodology Selected Without the Justification

The cost allocation methodology is selected without the justification, and the methodology is challenged, because the choice is not documented and the alternatives are not evaluated. The trap is that the unjustified methodology is the vulnerable methodology. The mechanism is that the methodology was selected by the default or the precedent, and the justification was not documented. The false signal is that "we used the standard methodology." The harm is that the methodology is challenged, the fee is invalidated, the alternative methodologies are not evaluated, and the fee that depends on the methodology is not defensible.

### Credits Not Applied, Development Overcharged

The credits for the dedicated facilities, the taxes paid, and the excess capacity are not applied, and the development is overcharged, because the fee does not account for the infrastructure the development provides or the taxes it pays. The trap is that the uncredited fee overcharges the development. The mechanism is that the credits were not calculated, and the fee was set at the full proportionate share without the offset. The false signal is that "the fee is the proportionate share." The harm is that the development is overcharged, the legal challenge succeeds, the fee is invalidated, and the overcharge that the credits should have corrected is not corrected.

### Fee Not Updated, Erodes With the Inflation

The fee is not updated for the cost inflation, and the fee erodes, and the infrastructure is underfunded, because the fee that was accurate at adoption no longer reflects the current construction costs. The trap is that the unupdated fee underfunds the infrastructure. The mechanism is that the annual cost adjustment was not applied, and the fee was left at the original level. The false signal is that "the fee is current." The harm is that the fee erodes, the infrastructure is underfunded, the growth does not pay for the infrastructure it requires, and the existing ratepayers or the taxpayers fund the gap.

### Study Not Documented for the Defensibility

The study is not documented with the clarity and the completeness that the legal challenge requires, and the fee is vulnerable, because the documentation does not support the calculation and the assumptions. The trap is that the undocumented study is the vulnerable study. The mechanism is that the documentation was not prepared, and the study was filed without the detail. The false signal is that "we conducted the study." The harm is that the study is challenged, the fee is invalidated, the calculations are not reproducible, and the defensibility that the documentation should provide does not.

## Self-Check

- Is the rational nexus established through the adopted and current infrastructure plan, with the facility needs, the costs, the timing, and the level-of-service documented?
- Is the cost allocation methodology selected deliberately, with the justification and the evaluation of the alternatives?
- Is the proportionate share calculated correctly, with the credits for the dedicated facilities, the taxes paid, and the excess capacity applied?
- Is the fee schedule updated regularly, with the annual cost adjustments and the comprehensive updates every three to five years?
- Is the study documented for the legal defensibility, with the clarity, the completeness, the data sources, the calculations, and the qualified preparer?
- Is the infrastructure plan adopted and current, or is the nexus based on the assumption?
- Is the methodology justified, or is it selected by the default?
- Are the credits applied, or is the development overcharged?
- Is the fee updated, or has it eroded with the inflation?
- Is the study documented, or is it filed without the detail that the defensibility requires?
