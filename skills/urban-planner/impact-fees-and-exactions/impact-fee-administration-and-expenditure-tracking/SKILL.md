# Impact Fee Administration and Expenditure Tracking

The impact fee is calculated and adopted, but the fee that is collected and not spent — or spent on the wrong facility, in the wrong area, or after the deadline — generates the refunds, the legal challenges, and the political backlash that can undermine the entire program. The judgment problem is that the impact fee administration requires the financial tracking (the collection, the deposit, the interest, the expenditure by facility and area), the legal compliance (the time limits, the proportionate share, the eligible facilities), and the political management (the reporting, the transparency, the development community relations), and the administrator must integrate these dimensions to maintain the program that the calculation established. An impact fee program that is well-calculated but poorly administered fails, because the fees accumulate without the expenditure, the deadlines pass, the refunds are required, and the program loses the credibility that the calculation built. This skill covers the administration and the expenditure tracking decisions that determine whether the impact fee program delivers the infrastructure it promised. It complements the broader impact-fees skill and the fee calculation skill by focusing on the operational and the compliance dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Segregate the Fees by Facility Type and Service Area

The impact fees must be segregated by the facility type (the water, the sewer, the road, the park, the school) and the service area (the geographic area the facility serves), because the fees collected for one facility or area cannot be spent on another, and the commingling violates the proportionate share and generates the refunds. The rule is to segregate the fees by facility type and service area, maintaining the separate trust accounts or the separate accounting funds for each facility type and each service area, and tracking the collection, the interest, and the expenditure within each account. The segregation should be maintained at the level of detail the fee calculation used, because the fee was calculated by facility and area, and the expenditure must match the calculation. The segregation should be documented in the accounting system, because the audit and the legal challenge will examine the records, and the commingled funds are the vulnerable funds. The segregation should include the interest earned on the fees, because the interest belongs to the fee account, and the failure to credit the interest is the shortfall that the challenge targets.

### Spend the Fees Within the Statutory Time Limits

The impact fee statutes impose the time limits on the expenditure of the fees (typically five to seven years, varying by state), and the fees not spent within the deadline must be refunded, because the statute requires the expenditure or the refund. The rule is to spend the fees within the statutory time limits, tracking the collection date of each fee and the deadline for its expenditure, and prioritizing the expenditure of the fees approaching the deadline. The time limit tracking should be automated, because the manual tracking of the individual fees and the deadlines is error-prone, and the missed deadline generates the refund. The expenditure should be planned to match the collection, because the fees collected must be spent on the facilities they fund, and the accumulation without the expenditure generates the refund risk. The time limit compliance should be monitored and reported, because the governing body and the development community should know the status, and the unreported deadline is the missed deadline. The refund process should be established, because some fees will not be spent within the deadline, and the clear process (the notification, the calculation, the refund) reduces the dispute and the legal challenge.

### Spend the Fees Only on the Eligible Facilities

The impact fees may be spent only on the facilities identified in the fee calculation (the system improvements, the growth-related facilities), not on the other facilities (the project improvements, the existing deficiencies, the maintenance), and the expenditure on the ineligible facilities generates the refund and the challenge. The rule is to spend the fees only on the eligible facilities, using the fee calculation and the facility plan to define the eligible facilities, and verifying each expenditure against the eligibility. The eligible facilities are the system improvements that serve the development (the water plant expansion, the road widening, the regional park), not the project improvements that serve the individual project (the water service line, the driveway, the on-site park), and the distinction should be clear in the administration. The eligible facilities are the growth-related facilities (the facilities needed to serve the new development), not the existing deficiencies (the facilities needed to correct the current shortfalls), and the expenditure on the existing deficiencies is the proportionate share violation. The eligible facilities are the capital facilities (the construction, the expansion), not the maintenance (the operations, the repair), and the expenditure on the maintenance is the ineligibility. The eligibility should be verified before the expenditure, because the ineligible expenditure generates the refund and the challenge, and the verification prevents the problem.

### Track and Report the Program Performance

The impact fee program should be tracked and reported, because the governing body, the development community, and the public should know the collection, the expenditure, the fund balances, and the facility delivery, and the transparency builds the credibility and the support. The rule is to track and report the program performance, preparing the annual report that documents the collection by facility and area, the expenditure by facility and project, the fund balances and the deadlines, the facilities constructed and the capacity delivered, and the comparison to the fee calculation and the facility plan. The report should be clear and accessible, because the technical detail should be presented in the way the non-technical audience can understand, and the opaque report does not build the credibility. The report should include the performance metrics (the collection rate, the expenditure rate, the fund balance ratio, the deadline compliance), because the metrics provide the objective measure of the program performance, and the metrics should be compared to the targets and the peers. The report should identify the issues and the recommendations, because the program should improve, and the report that does not identify the issues does not drive the improvement.

### Manage the Legal and the Political Risks Proactively

The impact fee program faces the legal risks (the challenges to the fee, the refund demands, the compliance audits) and the political risks (the opposition from the development community, the pressure to reduce the fees, the demands to spend the fees on the priorities that are not eligible), and the administrator should manage these risks proactively. The rule is to manage the legal and the political risks proactively, maintaining the documentation that supports the fee and the expenditure, responding to the challenges with the evidence and the analysis, engaging the development community to address the concerns before they become the challenges, and educating the governing body and the public on the program and the benefits. The legal risk management should include the regular compliance review (the time limits, the eligibility, the segregation), because the proactive review identifies the issues before the challenge, and the reactive review is the late review. The political risk management should include the engagement of the development community, because the engaged community is less likely to challenge, and the disengaged community is the challenging community. The risk management should be documented, because the documentation supports the defense, and the undocumented management is the vulnerable management.

## Common Traps

### Fees Commingled Across the Facility Types or Areas

The fees are commingled across the facility types (the water fees spent on the roads) or the service areas (the north area fees spent in the south area), and the proportionate share is violated, because the fees collected for one facility or area are spent on another. The trap is that the commingling violates the proportionate share. The mechanism is that the segregation was not maintained, and the fees were spent from the combined account. The false signal is that "the fees are in the impact fee fund." The harm is that the proportionate share is violated, the refund is required, the legal challenge succeeds, and the program loses the credibility.

### Time Limit Missed, Refund Required

The fees are not spent within the statutory time limit, and the refund is required, because the statute mandates the expenditure or the refund, and the unspent fees must be returned. The trap is that the missed deadline generates the refund. The mechanism is that the time limit was not tracked, and the fees accumulated without the expenditure. The false signal is that "the fees are in the fund." The harm is that the refund is required, the infrastructure is not built, the fees are returned, the program loses the revenue, and the credibility is damaged.

### Fees Spent on the Ineligible Facilities

The fees are spent on the ineligible facilities (the project improvements, the existing deficiencies, the maintenance), and the expenditure generates the refund, because the fees may be spent only on the eligible system improvements. The trap is that the ineligible expenditure generates the refund. The mechanism is that the eligibility was not verified, and the fees were spent on the convenient but ineligible facility. The false signal is that "the fees are spent on the infrastructure." The harm is that the expenditure is challenged, the refund is required, the eligible facilities are not funded, and the program loses the credibility.

### Program Not Reported, Credibility Erodes

The program is not reported, and the collection, the expenditure, and the fund balances are not transparent, and the development community and the public do not know the status, and the credibility erodes. The trap is that the unreported program loses the credibility. The mechanism is that the reporting was not conducted, and the program operated without the transparency. The false signal is that "the program is running." The harm is that the credibility erodes, the development community challenges, the public does not understand, the governing body does not support, and the program that should have the transparency does not.

### Risks Not Managed Proactively

The legal and the political risks are not managed proactively, and the challenges and the opposition arrive without the preparation, and the program is on the defensive. The trap is that the reactive management is the late management. The mechanism is that the proactive management was not conducted, and the risks materialized before the preparation. The false signal is that "the program is compliant." The harm is that the challenges succeed, the opposition builds, the program is undermined, and the management that should have been proactive is reactive.

## Self-Check

- Are the fees segregated by facility type and service area, with the separate accounts and the interest credited?
- Are the fees spent within the statutory time limits, with the tracking and the prioritization of the fees approaching the deadline?
- Are the fees spent only on the eligible facilities, with the verification of each expenditure against the eligibility?
- Is the program performance tracked and reported, with the annual report, the performance metrics, and the issues and recommendations?
- Are the legal and the political risks managed proactively, with the documentation, the compliance review, the engagement, and the education?
- Are the fees segregated, or are they commingled across the facilities and the areas?
- Are the time limits tracked, or are the deadlines missed?
- Is the eligibility verified, or are the fees spent on the ineligible facilities?
- Is the program reported, or does it operate without the transparency?
- Are the risks managed proactively, or does the program operate on the defensive?
