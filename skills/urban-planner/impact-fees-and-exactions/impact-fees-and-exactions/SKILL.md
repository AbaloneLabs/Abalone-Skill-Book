# Impact Fees and Exactions

Impact fees and exactions are the mechanisms by which new development pays for the infrastructure and public facilities its occupants will use, and they sit at the intersection of fiscal policy, infrastructure planning, and constitutional law. An exaction is a condition of development approval — a dedication of land, construction of infrastructure, or payment in lieu — required to offset a project-specific impact. An impact fee is a charge imposed on new development, calculated to fund the system-level facilities the development will require. Both are constrained by constitutional doctrine: under the Nollan-Dolan-Koontz line of cases, an exaction must have an essential nexus to a legitimate state interest and rough proportionality to the development's impact, and impact fees must rest on a rational study that documents the cost of facilities attributable to new growth. The judgment problem is that these mechanisms are powerful tools for funding growth-related infrastructure, but they are easily drafted in ways that exceed legal authority, miscalculate impact, or suppress the development they are meant to accommodate. A fee that is too high renders projects infeasible and suppresses housing production; a fee that is too low shifts the cost of growth to existing residents; a fee that lacks a defensible study will be struck down. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether impact fees and exactions are legally defensible, financially adequate, and developmentally sound.

## Core Rules

### Build the Fee on a Defensible Methodology and Documented Study

An impact fee must be based on a study that documents the facilities required to serve new development, the cost of those facilities, and the proportionate share attributable to the development paying the fee. The methodology matters because the study is the legal and factual foundation of the fee, and a fee without a current, defensible study is vulnerable to challenge. The standard approach is to calculate the cost per unit of capacity (per gallon of water, per student, per park acre per capita), determine the demand each unit of development generates, and set the fee at the cost of the facility capacity required to serve that demand. The study must document its assumptions, data sources, cost estimates, and allocation methodology, and it must be updated periodically as costs and demand change. The rule is that the fee is only as defensible as the study behind it, and a fee adopted without a current study, or left in place after conditions have changed, is an invitation to legal challenge and a guarantee of fiscal inadequacy.

### Satisfy Nexus and Proportionality for Every Exaction

An exaction — whether a land dedication, infrastructure construction, or fee in lieu — must satisfy the constitutional tests of nexus and proportionality. Nexus requires that the exaction serve the same legitimate government interest as the permit condition (the required dedication must address an impact created by the development, not an unrelated public need). Proportionality requires that the exaction be roughly proportional in nature and extent to the development's impact, both quantitatively and qualitatively. The rule is to document, for every exaction, the specific impact the development creates, the facility or dedication required to address it, and the calculation showing the required exaction is proportionate to the impact. An exaction imposed without this analysis is vulnerable to a takings challenge, and the analysis must be done individually for each project, because proportionality cannot be assumed from a category. The documentation should be in the record before the exaction is imposed, not reconstructed after a challenge.

### Distinguish Project-Specific From System-Wide Impacts

Exactions and impact fees serve different purposes, and confusing them produces legal and fiscal errors. An exaction addresses a project-specific impact — the road widening needed at the project's access point, the water main extension to serve the project, the park land the project's residents will use that is within the service radius. An impact fee addresses the system-wide facilities required to serve cumulative growth — the water treatment plant expansion, the new fire station, the school capacity. The rule is to assign each facility cost to the correct mechanism: project-specific costs are addressed through exactions or conditions tied to the development, and system-wide costs are addressed through impact fees assessed proportionately across all new development. Assigning a system-wide cost to a single project through an exaction exceeds proportionality and is vulnerable to challenge; assigning a project-specific cost to the impact fee shifts the burden to all development when it should fall on the project that creates the impact.

### Account for Credits, Offsets, and Double Recovery

A development may contribute to required facilities through multiple channels — it may pay impact fees, construct on-site infrastructure, dedicate land, or contribute to a system through its taxes — and the fee structure must account for these contributions to avoid double recovery. Impact fee ordinances typically include credit provisions: if a developer constructs or dedicates facilities that are included in the impact fee study's facility list, the developer receives a credit against the fee equal to the value of the contribution. Without credits, a developer pays twice for the same facility — once through the fee and once through the direct contribution — which is both unfair and legally vulnerable. The rule is to build credit and offset provisions into the fee structure, document the methodology for calculating credits, and ensure the fee study does not count facilities that developers are required to construct as part of the fee basis. Double recovery is a common basis for challenge and a common source of development infeasibility.

### Calibrate the Fee to Market Conditions and Housing Affordability

Impact fees are a cost of development, and like all costs, they affect project feasibility, particularly for housing at lower price points. A fee calibrated to recover full infrastructure cost may be legally defensible but may render housing projects infeasible in weaker markets, suppressing the housing production the community needs. The rule is to evaluate the fee's effect on feasibility, using prototypical project pro formas, and to consider mechanisms that maintain infrastructure funding while preserving feasibility: phased fee schedules, fee waivers or reductions for affordable housing, fee financing through the development's long-term debt, or alternative funding sources that reduce the fee's share of total cost. The fee should be set at the level the market will bear while remaining defensible, and the tradeoff between full cost recovery and development feasibility should be a deliberate decision documented in the study, not an unintended consequence of a mechanical calculation.

## Common Traps

### Fee Without a Current, Defensible Study

The impact fee was adopted years ago, the underlying study is outdated, and costs and demand have changed, but the fee has not been updated. The trap is that an outdated fee is both legally vulnerable and fiscally inadequate. The mechanism is that the study is the foundation of the fee's defensibility, and a fee that no longer reflects current costs or demand lacks rational basis. The false signal is that "we have an impact fee ordinance." The harm is that the fee underfunds the facilities it is meant to support (shifting cost to existing residents or creating infrastructure deficits), and it is vulnerable to challenge on the grounds that it no longer bears a rational relationship to documented impact.

### Exaction Without Individualized Nexus and Proportionality

The jurisdiction imposes a standard exaction — a fixed dedication requirement or fee per unit — on all projects, without individualized analysis of each project's impact. The trap is that categorical exactions are vulnerable to proportionality challenges. The mechanism is that proportionality must be assessed for each development, because impacts vary by project type, location, and scale. The false signal is that "the requirement is in the ordinance." The harm is that the exaction is struck down on a takings challenge, the jurisdiction loses the infrastructure funding it was counting on, and the precedent weakens the jurisdiction's ability to impose exactions on future projects.

### Double Recovery Through Fee and Exaction

A developer pays impact fees and is also required to construct facilities that the impact fee study counts toward the fee basis, paying twice for the same infrastructure. The trap is that double recovery is both unfair and legally vulnerable. The mechanism is that the fee study and the exaction requirements were not coordinated, so the same facility cost appears in both. The false signal is that "the developer is paying their fair share." The harm is that the project becomes infeasible (because costs are double-counted), the developer challenges the fee or abandons the project, and the jurisdiction either loses the development or must refund fees, undermining the program's credibility.

### Fee Set Too High, Suppressing Development

The fee is calculated to recover full infrastructure cost without regard to its effect on project feasibility, and housing or commercial development becomes infeasible. The trap is that a legally defensible fee can still be counterproductive if it suppresses the development it is meant to serve. The mechanism is that impact fees are a significant cost component, and at higher levels they push projects below the feasibility threshold, particularly for lower-margin housing. The false signal is that "growth is paying its way." The harm is that housing production declines, the shortage the community faces worsens, and the fee revenue declines because fewer projects are built, defeating the fiscal purpose.

### System-Wide Cost Assigned to a Single Project

A development is required, through an exaction, to construct or fund a facility that serves the broader system — a regional pump station, an arterial road widening — that exceeds the project's proportionate impact. The trap is that the exaction exceeds proportionality. The mechanism is that system-wide costs should be allocated across all benefiting development through impact fees, not assigned to the project that happens to trigger the need. The false signal is that "the project created the need for the facility." The harm is that the exaction is vulnerable to a proportionality challenge, the project may become infeasible, and the cost allocation is inequitable because one project bears a cost that should be shared.

## Self-Check

- Is the impact fee based on a current, documented study that shows the facilities required, their cost, and the proportionate share attributable to new development?
- For every exaction, is there an individualized analysis in the record documenting nexus and rough proportionality to the specific development's impact?
- Have project-specific impacts (addressed by exactions) been distinguished from system-wide impacts (addressed by impact fees), with no cost assigned to the wrong mechanism?
- Does the fee ordinance include credit and offset provisions that prevent double recovery when developers construct facilities included in the fee study?
- Has the fee's effect on project feasibility been evaluated, and are there mechanisms to preserve feasibility for needed housing without undermining infrastructure funding?
- Is the fee study updated periodically to reflect current costs and demand, and is the update documented?
- If challenged, could the jurisdiction demonstrate that each fee component rests on a rational calculation tied to documented facility costs?
- Have I confirmed that no facility cost appears in both the impact fee study and a mandatory exaction requirement?
- Does the fee structure account for the different impacts of different development types, or does it apply a uniform fee that over- or under-charges some uses?
- Is the revenue from impact fees accounted for and spent on the facilities for which it was collected, with documentation that maintains the program's credibility?
