# Variance and Special Permit Administration

Variances and special permits are the quasi-judicial safety valves of the zoning system: the variance allows departure from a dimensional standard where strict application would cause unnecessary hardship, and the special permit allows a use that the ordinance permits conditionally where specified criteria are met. The judgment problem is that these decisions are made by boards of adjustment or zoning boards of appeals whose members are typically volunteers, under procedures that must satisfy quasi-judicial due process, against legal tests that are specific and unforgiving, and the consequences of error run both directions — an approval that lacks the required findings will be overturned on challenge by a neighbor, and a denial that does not engage the applicant's evidence will be overturned on challenge by the applicant. A board that grants variances loosely (the "hardship of the applicant's own making" problem) or denies special permits on general opposition rather than the criteria converts the quasi-judicial process into an unpredictable political one, exposes the jurisdiction to reversal, and erodes the integrity of the zoning scheme. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether variance and special-permit administration will be legally sound and fair or a source of reversible error and arbitrary outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Correct Legal Standard and Distinguish Variance Types

The foundational discipline is to apply the correct legal standard to each application, because the standards for variances and special permits are different and are frequently confused. A use variance (allowing a use prohibited in the district) requires, in most jurisdictions, the showing of unnecessary hardship and the absence of any reasonable use under the ordinance — an extremely high standard that most jurisdictions disfavor or prohibit. An area (dimensional) variance requires the balancing of statutory factors (unnecessary hardship, whether the hardship is self-created, the effect on the neighborhood, the spirit of the ordinance, substantial justice, minimum variance necessary) and is the more common application. A special permit (special exception, conditional use) does not require a hardship showing at all; it requires findings that the use meets the specific criteria in the ordinance and is appropriate at the proposed location. Applying the variance standard to a special permit, or vice versa, produces a decision that does not match the legal test and is vulnerable on that basis alone.

### Build the Evidentiary Record Before the Hearing

A defensible quasi-judicial decision rests on an evidentiary record — the application, the staff report, the evidence submitted by the applicant, the testimony and evidence at the hearing, and the board's findings — and the record must be complete before the decision is made. Prepare a staff report that analyzes the application against each required criterion, identifies the evidence supporting and contradicting each finding, and recommends a disposition with reasoning. Ensure the applicant has submitted the evidence needed to meet each criterion (hardship for a variance, the specific criteria for a special permit), because the board cannot grant an application the applicant did not prove. At the hearing, take testimony under oath or affirmation, allow cross-examination where the procedure provides for it, admit exhibits into the record, and ensure the record is closed before deliberation. A decision based on an incomplete record, or on evidence not in the record, will be reversed regardless of its merits.

### Draft Findings That Engage the Specific Evidence and Each Criterion

The findings of fact are the most important document the board produces, because they are the basis on which a court reviews the decision, and generic or conclusory findings are the most common reason quasi-judicial decisions are reversed. The findings must address each required criterion, cite the specific evidence in the record that supports the finding on each criterion, and explain the reasoning that connects the evidence to the conclusion. For a variance, address each statutory factor (the hardship and its source, the neighborhood effect, the spirit of the ordinance, substantial justice, the minimum variance necessary); for a special permit, address each ordinance criterion. Findings that recite the legal standard without engaging the evidence ("the Board finds unnecessary hardship") are conclusory and will be remanded; findings that walk through the evidence and explain the conclusion ("the lot's 25-foot width, combined with the 1940 subdivision creating a nonconforming parcel, and the wetland covering the rear half, establish that no reasonable use is possible without the variance") are defensible. Provide the board with a findings template or draft findings that they can adopt, modify, or reject, so that the findings meet the legal standard regardless of the board's drafting capacity.

### Impose Conditions That Are Tailored, Enforceable, and Within Authority

Conditions of approval are the board's primary tool for mitigating the impacts of an approved variance or special permit, and they must be tailored to the impacts identified in the record, enforceable through the ordinance's mechanisms, and within the board's statutory and ordinance authority. A condition must bear a substantial relationship to the impacts of the use (a condition unrelated to the record evidence is vulnerable as arbitrary), must be capable of enforcement (with clear standards, a responsible party, and a compliance mechanism), and must not exceed the board's authority (conditions that effectively rezone, that require dedications unrelated to the impact, or that reach beyond the ordinance's conditional-use provisions are vulnerable). Avoid the two opposite failures: conditions so vague ("the use shall be a good neighbor") that they cannot be enforced, and conditions so onerous or unrelated that they convert the approval into a denial or invite challenge. Document the basis for each condition in the findings, connecting it to the impact it addresses.

### Manage Ex Parte Communications and Conflicts Rigorously

Quasi-judicial decisions must be based on the record and the law, not on ex parte communications (contacts outside the hearing) or on the decision-maker's conflicts, and the failure to manage these is a common and avoidable source of reversal. Establish and enforce a strict no-ex-parte policy: board members do not discuss pending applications with applicants, opponents, or staff outside the hearing, and any inadvertent contact is disclosed on the record. Require board members to disclose any conflict of interest (financial, familial, positional) and to recuse where required by law or where the conflict would create an appearance of impropriety. Train board members on these requirements at appointment and periodically thereafter, because volunteer board members frequently do not understand them and the violations are often well-intentioned (a conversation with a neighbor, a site visit that includes discussion). The appearance of impartiality is as important as its substance, because a decision that appears compromised is vulnerable regardless of its merits.

### Sequence the Procedural Requirements and Meet Every Deadline

Quasi-judicial procedures are governed by statutory and ordinance requirements — notice (content, methods, timing, radius), hearing procedures (oath, cross-examination, record closure), decision timelines, and filing requirements — and each is a potential source of reversal if not met. Build a procedural checklist for every application (notice content and timing, hearing requirements, decision deadline, filing), verify each item before the hearing and before the decision, and document compliance in the record. Notice defects are the most common procedural reversal: a notice that omits a required element, is served by the wrong method, or is published outside the required window voids the hearing regardless of the decision's merits. Treat the procedural requirements as jurisdictional (because in most jurisdictions they are), and never proceed with a hearing or decision if a procedural requirement is unmet, because the defect cannot be cured after the fact.

## Common Traps

### The Self-Created Hardship Variance

The applicant purchased or subdivided the parcel knowing the dimensional constraints, the strict application of the ordinance creates a hardship, and the board grants the variance on the basis of the hardship — without examining whether the hardship was self-created, which in most jurisdictions is a statutory bar or a factor weighing against the variance. The trap mechanism is that the hardship is real (the parcel cannot be built on as configured), but it was created by the applicant's own decision to acquire or subdivide the constrained parcel, and granting the variance rewards the creation of the hardship and invites replication. The false signal is a sympathetic applicant and a real hardship. The harm is the erosion of the dimensional standards through variances for self-created hardships, the precedent that invites similar applications, and the reversal on challenge if the self-creation factor was not addressed in the findings.

### Findings That Recite the Standard Without Engaging the Evidence

The board's findings recite the legal criteria ("the Board finds unnecessary hardship," "the Board finds no substantial detriment to the neighborhood") without citing the specific evidence or explaining the reasoning, and the decision is reversed on challenge because the findings are conclusory. The trap mechanism is that volunteer board members are not trained to draft evidentiary findings, and the path of least resistance is to recite the standard, which feels sufficient but is not, because a court cannot review a conclusion it cannot trace to the record. The false signal is a complete set of findings addressing each criterion. The harm is that an otherwise defensible decision is reversed for lack of substantive findings, the application is remanded for a new hearing, and the jurisdiction's credibility and the applicant's resources are consumed by a defect that could have been avoided with a findings template.

### Conditions Unrelated to the Record or Beyond Authority

The board imposes conditions (landscaping, road improvements, dedications, operational restrictions) that address concerns not in the record, that exceed the ordinance's conditional-use provisions, or that effectively rezone the property — and the conditions are challenged as arbitrary or ultra vires. The trap mechanism is that conditions feel like reasonable mitigation, but a condition must bear a relationship to the impacts in the record and must be within the board's authority, and conditions that fail either test are vulnerable even when the underlying approval is sound. The false signal is a thorough set of conditions addressing every concern. The harm is the reversal of the conditions (and sometimes the approval), the exposure to a claim that the conditions are confiscatory or constitute an uncompensated taking, and the erosion of the board's credibility when its conditions cannot be sustained.

### The Ex Parte Communication That Taints the Decision

A board member discusses the pending application with the applicant, the opponent, or a neighbor outside the hearing, or visits the site and forms impressions not in the record, and the communication taints the decision or creates the appearance of taint, leading to reversal or recusal. The trap mechanism is that volunteer board members often do not understand the ex parte prohibition, and well-intentioned contacts (a conversation at the grocery store, a site visit with discussion) violate it, and the violation is discovered through disclosure or through the participant's testimony. The false signal is a well-informed board member. The harm is the reversal of a decision based on improper considerations, the recusal that deprives the board of a member, and the erosion of public confidence in the quasi-judicial process when the appearance of impartiality is compromised.

## Self-Check

- Is the correct legal standard being applied — use variance, area variance, or special permit — with the distinct tests for each, rather than a generic "balance the equities" approach?
- Is the evidentiary record complete before the decision, with the applicant's proof of each criterion, sworn testimony, admitted exhibits, and a closed record?
- Do the findings address each required criterion, cite the specific record evidence, and explain the reasoning — rather than reciting the standard conclusorily?
- Are conditions tailored to record-identified impacts, enforceable, and within the board's ordinance and statutory authority, with each condition's basis documented?
- Is there a strict, enforced no-ex-parte policy, with disclosure of inadvertent contacts and recusal for conflicts, and are board members trained on these requirements?
- Is a procedural checklist (notice content, methods, timing, radius; hearing requirements; decision deadline; filing) verified and documented for every application?
- For variances, do the findings address whether the hardship was self-created, and is the self-creation factor weighed rather than ignored?
- If this decision were reviewed by a court, could the court trace each finding to specific record evidence and each condition to a record-identified impact — or would the findings and conditions appear conclusory and unsupported?
