# Energy and Emissions Reduction Strategy

Climate action plans and energy strategies are where ambition meets the hard accounting of where emissions actually come from and what measures can credibly reduce them. The judgment problem is not "set a bold target" but how to build a greenhouse gas inventory that captures the emissions the jurisdiction actually influences, how to select measures whose reductions are quantifiable and additional (not double-counted or already happening), how to sequence measures by impact and feasibility, and how to avoid the common pattern of plans full of aspirational measures that do not add up to the target. Planners commonly fail by setting targets unanchored from the inventory, by listing measures without quantifying their reductions, by relying on undefined future technology or funding, or by ignoring the implementation and political pathway that determines whether measures are adopted. A rigorous emissions strategy is an honest accounting exercise that adds up; a weak one is a wish list dressed as a plan.

## Core Rules

### Build the Inventory Before the Target

A greenhouse gas inventory — typically following the Global Covenant of Mayors or ICLEI protocol, covering building energy, transportation, waste, and often consumption-based emissions — is the baseline against which any target and any measure must be measured. Build the inventory first, at a scope and sector resolution that matches the jurisdiction's influence (government operations, community-wide, or both), and use consistent methodology so progress can be tracked over time. A target set without an inventory is a number untethered from reality, and measures selected without knowing the sectoral breakdown of emissions will misallocate effort. The inventory reveals, for example, whether transportation or buildings dominate, which determines where the leverage is. Update the inventory periodically and reconcile methodological changes so trends are real, not artifacts.

### Quantify Each Measure's Reduction and Confirm the Portfolio Adds Up

For every emissions reduction measure, estimate the tons of greenhouse gases it will abate, using a defensible methodology (ICLEI, EPA, or peer-reviewed factors), the assumed adoption rate, and the activity baseline. Sum the measures and compare to the target trajectory: if the portfolio does not reach the target, either add measures, strengthen them, or revise the target — do not present a portfolio that falls short as if it achieves the goal. This arithmetic is the integrity test of a climate plan, and plans that skip it claim reductions they cannot deliver. Show the math, identify the gap if one exists, and be honest about whether the strategy meets the target on paper before considering implementation.

### Test Measures for Additionality, Feasibility, and Leakage

A credible measure reduces emissions that would not have been reduced without it (additionality), is implementable given legal authority, technology, cost, and political acceptance (feasibility), and does not simply shift emissions outside the jurisdiction's boundary (leakage). A building electrification mandate that the jurisdiction lacks legal authority to adopt is not a feasible measure; a vehicle-miles-traveled reduction strategy that shifts trips to a neighboring jurisdiction without reducing total VMT is leakage; a renewable energy credit purchase that funds projects that would have been built anyway lacks additionality. Test each measure against these criteria, and discount or exclude those that fail, because over-crediting non-additional or infeasible measures inflates the projected reductions and guarantees the target is missed.

### Sequence by Impact and Marginal Abatement Cost

Not all measures are equal: some deliver large reductions at low or negative cost (energy efficiency, mode shift), others deliver smaller reductions at high cost, and the political and implementation difficulty varies. Build a marginal abatement cost curve or equivalent ranking that orders measures by tons abated per dollar, and sequence implementation to capture the high-impact, low-cost measures first, both to maximize early reductions and to build momentum and savings that fund later, harder measures. A plan that front-loads the most expensive, lowest-impact measures (because they are visible or politically easy) underperforms and exhausts resources. Sequence for impact.

### Connect Measures to Implementation Authority and Funding

A measure without an identified implementer, legal authority, and funding source is a recommendation, not a commitment. For each measure, specify the implementing agency or actor, the legal mechanism (ordinance, code amendment, program, capital investment, incentive), the funding source (general fund, grant, utility program, fee, bond), and the timeline. Measures that lack these elements are unlikely to be implemented regardless of their technical merit, and a plan whose measures are mostly unfunded recommendations will not achieve its target. The implementation pathway is part of the strategy, not an afterthought.

### Build Monitoring, Reporting, and Course Correction

Emissions strategies fail silently if no one checks whether measures are being implemented and whether emissions are declining. Build a monitoring framework: track implementation of each measure (adopted, funded, on schedule), track emissions through periodic inventory updates, and compare actual to target trajectory. Establish triggers for course correction — if emissions are not declining as projected, additional measures must be brought forward. A climate plan without monitoring is a one-time document; one with monitoring and adaptation is an ongoing strategy that can actually reach its target.

## Common Traps

### Setting Targets Without an Inventory Basis

Adopting a net-zero or percentage-reduction target without a current inventory and sectoral breakdown detaches the goal from the emissions sources that must be addressed. The false signal is an ambitious, leadership-setting target; the harm is a goal that cannot be operationalized because the baseline and leverage points are undefined. Build the inventory before setting the target.

### Listing Measures Without Quantifying Reductions

Climate plans full of measures (efficiency, transit, solar, electrification) whose individual and cumulative reductions are never quantified cannot demonstrate that they meet the target. The false signal is a comprehensive strategy; the harm is a portfolio that looks robust but does not add up. Quantify each measure and show the sum against the target.

### Over-Crediting Non-Additional or Infeasible Measures

Counting reductions from measures that would happen anyway, that the jurisdiction cannot legally implement, or that shift emissions elsewhere inflates the projected impact and guarantees the target is missed. The false signal is a strong-looking portfolio; the harm is a credibility collapse when emissions do not decline. Test for additionality, feasibility, and leakage, and discount accordingly.

### Relying on Undefined Future Technology or Funding

Basing significant reductions on technologies not yet commercial, funding not yet identified, or measures not yet adopted builds the target on hypotheticals. The false signal is an innovative strategy; the harm is that the reductions do not materialize when the assumptions prove wrong. Ground the strategy in implementable measures with identified authority and funding, and treat speculative measures as upside, not baseline.

### Ignoring the Implementation and Political Pathway

A technically excellent strategy that lacks the legal authority, funding, and political support to be adopted and implemented is a paper plan. The false signal is a rigorous technical analysis; the harm is a target that is never pursued. Specify the implementer, authority, funding, and political strategy for each measure, and build monitoring to track implementation.

## Self-Check

- Is the emissions reduction target grounded in a current, methodologically consistent greenhouse gas inventory at a scope that matches the jurisdiction's influence?
- Has each measure's reduction been quantified with a defensible methodology, and does the portfolio sum to the target trajectory — or is there an acknowledged gap?
- Have measures been tested for additionality (real reductions), feasibility (legal, technical, political), and leakage (not shifting emissions elsewhere)?
- Are measures sequenced by impact and marginal abatement cost, with high-impact, low-cost measures prioritized?
- Does each measure have an identified implementer, legal authority, funding source, and timeline — or is it an unfunded recommendation?
- Is there a monitoring framework to track implementation and emissions against the target, with triggers for course correction?
- Did you avoid setting a target without an inventory basis?
- Did you avoid listing measures whose reductions are not quantified and summed against the target?
- Did you avoid relying on undefined future technology or funding as the basis for major reductions?
- Will this strategy actually reduce emissions to the target — or does it only document an aspiration whose arithmetic and implementation pathway do not hold up?
