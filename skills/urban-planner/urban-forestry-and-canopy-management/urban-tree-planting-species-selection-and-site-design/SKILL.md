# Urban Tree Planting Species Selection and Site Design

The species selection and the site design determine whether the planted tree survives and thrives, because the urban environment is harsh (the compacted soil, the limited space, the heat, the pollution), and the wrong species or the wrong site produces the tree that fails. The judgment problem is that the species selection is often casual (the familiar species, the available stock), the site design is often inadequate (the small pit, the poor soil), and the conflicts with the infrastructure are often ignored (the power lines, the sidewalks, the pipes), and these failures produce the short-lived, the costly, and the damaging urban forest. A planting program that is designed well (the right species, the right site, the conflict management) produces the thriving and the long-lived urban forest, while a program that is designed poorly (the wrong species, the inadequate site, the ignored conflicts) produces the failing and the costly forest. This skill covers the species selection and the site design decisions that determine whether the planted tree survives and thrives. It complements the broader urban-forestry skill by focusing on the planting and the design dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Select the Right Species for the Site and the Function

The species selection should match the species to the site conditions and the intended function, because the wrong species fails, and the right species thrives. The rule is to select the species based on the site analysis (the soil, the light, the space, the moisture), the hardiness (the climate zone, the heat tolerance, the cold tolerance), the function (the shade, the screening, the aesthetic, the stormwater), and the constraints (the overhead lines, the underground utilities, the sight lines, the pavement). The site analysis should assess the soil (the compaction, the pH, the drainage), the light (the full sun, the shade), the space (the canopy room, the root room), and the moisture (the wet, the dry), because the species must tolerate the site, and the mismatched species fails. The hardiness should match the climate zone, because the species that is not hardy fails in the cold or the heat, and the hardy species survives. The function should guide the selection, because the shade tree differs from the ornamental, and the street tree differs from the park, and the function determines the form. The constraints should rule out the incompatible species, because the tall species under the power lines conflicts, and the shallow-rooted species near the sidewalk heaves, and the constraint-based selection avoids the conflict.

### Diversify the Species to Avoid the Monoculture

The species selection should diversify the urban forest, because the monoculture (the one species, the one genus) is vulnerable to the pest and the disease, and the diversification reduces the risk. The rule is to diversify the species, using the diversity guidelines (the no more than 10 percent any species, the no more than 20 percent any genus, the no more than 30 percent any family), the species mix (the varied species across the planting), and the pest and disease consideration (the avoidance of the overplanted and the vulnerable species). The diversity guidelines should limit any one species, genus, and family, because the pest that targets one species (the Dutch elm disease, the emerald ash borer) can devastate the monoculture, and the diversification limits the loss. The species mix should vary across the planting and the city, because the varied mix reduces the risk, and the uniform mix increases it. The pest and disease consideration should avoid the overplanted species (the ash, the elm) and the vulnerable species, because the overplanted and the vulnerable species are the pest targets, and the avoidance reduces the risk.

### Design the Planting Site with the Adequate Soil Volume

The planting site should be designed with the adequate soil volume, because the tree needs the soil for the root growth, the water, and the nutrients, and the inadequate soil volume stunts the tree and the shortens the life. The rule is to design the site with the adequate soil volume, using the soil volume calculation (the mature canopy size, the soil volume needed), the soil quality (the structure, the organic matter, the drainage), the root management (the root barriers, the structural soil, the suspended pavement), and the infrastructure integration (the Silva cells, the tree pits, the continuous soil channels). The soil volume calculation should estimate the needed volume based on the mature canopy, because the large tree needs the large volume, and the small pit stunts it. The soil quality should provide the good structure and the drainage, because the compacted and the poorly drained soil suffocates the roots, and the good soil supports them. The root management should use the barriers and the structural solutions, because the roots damage the infrastructure, and the management prevents the damage. The infrastructure integration should use the Silva cells and the suspended pavement, because these systems provide the soil volume under the pavement, and the integration enables the large tree in the urban setting.

### Manage the Conflicts Between the Trees and the Infrastructure

The conflicts between the trees and the infrastructure (the power lines, the sidewalks, the underground utilities, the sight lines) should be managed, because the unmanaged conflicts damage the infrastructure and the tree, and the management preserves both. The rule is to manage the conflicts, using the overhead conflict management (the small species under the lines, the directional pruning, the utility coordination), the underground conflict management (the root barriers, the utility setbacks, the trenching standards), the pavement conflict management (the structural soil, the root barriers, the larger pits), and the sight line management (the species selection, the pruning, the setback). The overhead conflict management should use the small species under the lines and the directional pruning, because the tall species under the lines is topped, and the topping destroys the tree, and the small species and the directional pruning avoid the conflict. The underground conflict management should use the root barriers and the setbacks, because the roots damage the pipes, and the management prevents the damage. The pavement conflict management should use the structural soil and the larger pits, because the roots heave the pavement, and the management prevents the heave. The sight line management should use the appropriate species and the pruning, because the trees block the visibility, and the management preserves the safety.

### Address the Climate Adaptation and the Pest Resistance

The species selection should address the climate adaptation and the pest resistance, because the climate is changing (the hotter, the drier, the more extreme), and the pests are spreading (the new pests, the new diseases), and the species that is not adapted and not resistant fails. The rule is to address the climate adaptation and the pest resistance, using the climate-resilient species (the heat-tolerant, the drought-tolerant), the pest-resistant species (the resistant cultivars, the non-host species), and the assisted migration (the species from the warmer climate, the gradual introduction). The climate-resilient species should be selected for the heat and the drought tolerance, because the urban heat island and the climate change increase the stress, and the tolerant species survives. The pest-resistant species should be selected, because the pests and the diseases are spreading, and the resistant species avoids the loss. The assisted migration should consider the species from the slightly warmer climate, because the climate is shifting, and the warmer-climate species may be better adapted to the future, but the introduction should be gradual and monitored to avoid the invasiveness.

## Common Traps

### Species Selected Casually, Wrong Species for the Site

The species is selected casually (the familiar, the available, the cheap), and it is the wrong species for the site, because the site analysis was not conducted. The trap is that the casual selection produces the wrong species. The mechanism is that the site analysis was not conducted, and the species was selected by the familiarity. The false signal is that "we planted a tree." The harm is that the species fails (the wrong soil, the wrong light, the wrong space), the tree dies or the stunts, the money is wasted, and the species that should match the site does not.

### Monoculture Planted, Pest Vulnerability

The monoculture is planted (the one species, the one genus across the city), and the forest is vulnerable to the pest, because the diversity was not considered. The trap is that the monoculture is the pest target. The mechanism is that the diversity was not considered, and the one species was planted for the uniformity. The false signal is that "the planting looks uniform." The harm is that the pest arrives (the emerald ash borer, the Dutch elm disease), the monoculture is devastated, the canopy is lost, and the diversity that should protect the forest does not.

### Soil Volume Inadequate, Tree Stunted

The soil volume is inadequate (the small pit, the compacted soil), and the tree is stunted, because the soil volume was not calculated. The trap is that the inadequate volume stunts the tree. The mechanism is that the soil volume was not calculated, and the small pit was dug. The false signal is that "the tree was planted." The harm is that the tree is stunted, the growth is slow, the life is short, the benefits are reduced, and the soil volume that should support the tree does not.

### Infrastructure Conflicts Ignored, Damage and Topping

The infrastructure conflicts are ignored (the power lines, the sidewalks, the pipes), and the tree is topped or the infrastructure is damaged, because the conflicts were not managed. The trap is that the ignored conflicts produce the damage. The mechanism is that the conflicts were not assessed, and the tree was planted in the conflict zone. The false signal is that "the tree was planted." The harm is that the tree is topped (the power lines), the sidewalk is heaved (the roots), the pipe is invaded, the conflicts escalate, and the management that should prevent the conflicts does not.

### Climate and Pests Not Considered, Future Losses

The climate adaptation and the pest resistance are not considered, and the future losses occur, because the changing climate and the spreading pests were not addressed. The trap is that the unconsidered climate and pests produce the future losses. The mechanism is that the climate and the pest trends were not assessed, and the species was selected for the current conditions. The false signal is that "the species grows here now." The harm is that the climate changes (the hotter, the drier), the pest spreads, the species fails, the canopy is lost, and the adaptation that should protect the future forest does not.
