# Urban Tree Canopy Goal Setting and Targeting

The urban tree canopy — the layer of leaves and branches that covers the city when viewed from above — is the measure of the urban forest, and the canopy goal and the targeting determine whether the community grows and equitably distributes its forest. The judgment problem is that the canopy is not evenly distributed (the affluent neighborhoods have more, the low-income neighborhoods have less), the canopy changes over time (the development removes, the planting adds, the storms and the pests kill), and the goal-setting and the targeting must address the distribution and the change to produce the equitable and the growing canopy. A canopy program that is targeted well (the goals, the priority areas, the monitoring) grows and equitably distributes the canopy, while a program that is targeted poorly (the no goals, the random planting, the no monitoring) does not. This skill covers the canopy goal-setting and the targeting decisions that determine whether the community grows and equitably distributes its urban forest. It complements the broader urban-forestry skill by focusing on the canopy and the targeting dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Set the Canopy Goal Based on the Analysis and the Feasibility

The canopy goal should be based on the canopy analysis (the current cover, the available planting space, the feasibility) and the community priorities (the equity, the heat, the stormwater), because the goal that is not based on the analysis is the arbitrary goal, and the goal that is based is the achievable goal. The rule is to set the canopy goal based on the analysis and the feasibility, using the canopy assessment (the current canopy cover, the possible planting area, the achievable canopy), the benchmarking (the comparison to the peer communities), the feasibility analysis (the space, the cost, the maintenance), and the community engagement (the priorities, the acceptance). The canopy assessment should identify the current canopy and the possible canopy, because the gap between the current and the possible defines the achievable goal, and the assessment provides the basis. The benchmarking should compare to the peers, because the comparison provides the context, and the context informs the goal. The feasibility analysis should address the space (the available planting area), the cost (the planting and the maintenance), and the maintenance (the ongoing care), because the goal that does not account for the feasibility is the unachievable goal. The community engagement should involve the public, because the canopy affects the residents, and the engagement builds the support.

### Analyze the Canopy Distribution and the Equity

The canopy distribution should be analyzed for the equity, because the canopy is not evenly distributed, and the low-income and the minority communities often have less canopy, and the inequity contributes to the heat, the health, and the quality-of-life disparities. The rule is to analyze the canopy distribution and the equity, using the canopy cover by the neighborhood and the demographic (the income, the race, the age), the heat island analysis (the canopy and the land surface temperature), the health analysis (the canopy and the health outcomes), and the priority area identification (the low-canopy, the high-heat, the vulnerable populations). The canopy distribution should be mapped by the neighborhood and the demographic, because the mapping identifies the inequity, and the identification guides the targeting. The heat island analysis should correlate the canopy and the temperature, because the low-canopy areas are the high-heat areas, and the correlation identifies the heat-vulnerable communities. The health analysis should correlate the canopy and the health, because the canopy improves the air quality and the health, and the correlation identifies the health-vulnerable communities. The priority area identification should target the low-canopy, the high-heat, and the vulnerable communities, because these communities benefit most from the canopy, and the targeting advances the equity.

### Prioritize the Planting in the High-Need Areas

The planting should be prioritized in the high-need areas (the low-canopy, the high-heat, the vulnerable populations), because the equitable distribution requires the targeted planting, and the random planting perpetuates the inequity. The rule is to prioritize the planting in the high-need areas, using the priority map (the high-need areas identified), the targeted programs (the free trees, the neighborhood planting, the street tree planting in the priority areas), the partnerships (the community organizations, the schools, the faith communities in the priority areas), and the resources (the funding and the staff directed to the priority areas). The priority map should identify the high-need areas, because the map guides the targeting, and the untargeted planting does not address the inequity. The targeted programs should provide the free or the reduced-cost trees, because the cost is the barrier in the low-income communities, and the free trees remove the barrier. The partnerships should engage the community organizations, because the organizations have the trust and the reach, and the engagement ensures the participation. The resources should be directed to the priority areas, because the resources are the limited, and the direction ensures the equity.

### Manage the Canopy on the Public and the Private Land

The canopy is on both the public and the private land, and the management should address both, because the public canopy (the street trees, the park trees) is the significant portion but the private canopy (the yard trees, the commercial landscaping) is the larger portion, and the management of only the public canopy misses the majority. The rule is to manage the canopy on the public and the private land, using the public tree management (the street tree planting, the park tree management, the tree ordinance for the public trees), the private tree protection (the tree ordinance for the private trees, the removal permits, the mitigation), and the private tree planting (the giveaway programs, the technical assistance, the incentives). The public tree management should plant and maintain the street and the park trees, because the public trees are the direct responsibility, and the management ensures the public canopy. The private tree protection should regulate the removal of the large and the significant trees, because the unregulated removal loses the canopy, and the regulation protects it. The private tree planting should provide the trees and the assistance to the private property owners, because the private land is the larger planting opportunity, and the assistance enables the planting.

### Monitor the Canopy Change and the Progress

The canopy should be monitored for the change and the progress toward the goal, because the canopy changes (the growth, the removal, the mortality), and the monitoring tracks the change, and the progress toward the goal requires the tracking. The rule is to monitor the canopy change and the progress, using the remote sensing (the aerial and the satellite imagery, the LiDAR), the canopy assessment (the periodic measurement of the canopy cover), the change analysis (the gains and the losses), and the reporting (the public dashboard, the annual report). The remote sensing should be used for the canopy measurement, because the remote sensing provides the accurate and the comprehensive measurement, and the ground survey alone does not. The canopy assessment should be conducted periodically (every 5 to 10 years), because the canopy changes slowly, and the periodic assessment captures the change. The change analysis should identify the gains (the planting, the growth) and the losses (the development, the storms, the pests), because the net change is the progress, and the analysis identifies the drivers. The reporting should be public and the accessible, because the public should know the progress, and the transparency builds the accountability.

## Common Traps

### Goal Arbitrary, Not Based on the Analysis

The canopy goal is arbitrary (the round number, the political target), and it is not based on the analysis, because the canopy assessment and the feasibility were not conducted. The trap is that the arbitrary goal is the unachievable goal. The mechanism is that the assessment was not conducted, and the goal was set by the aspiration. The false signal is that "we have a canopy goal." The harm is that the goal is not achievable, the progress is not made, the credibility is eroded, and the goal that should be based on the analysis is arbitrary.

### Equity Not Analyzed, Inequity Perpetuated

The canopy distribution is not analyzed for the equity, and the inequity is perpetuated, because the equity analysis was not conducted. The trap is that the unanalyzed equity perpetuates the inequity. The mechanism is that the equity analysis was not conducted, and the planting was not targeted. The false signal is that "we plant trees." The harm is that the inequity persists, the low-canopy communities remain low-canopy, the heat and the health disparities continue, and the equity that should guide the planting does not.

### Planting Not Targeted, Random Distribution

The planting is not targeted to the high-need areas, and the distribution is random, because the priority areas were not identified. The trap is that the untargeted planting produces the random distribution. The mechanism is that the priority map was not developed, and the planting was distributed by the demand. The false signal is that "we plant trees." The harm is that the high-need areas are not addressed, the equity is not advanced, the resources are spread thin, and the targeting that should address the high-need areas does not.

### Private Canopy Not Managed, Majority Missed

The private canopy is not managed, and the majority of the canopy is missed, because the private tree protection and the planting were not implemented. The trap is that the unmanaged private canopy misses the majority. The mechanism is that the private tree ordinance and the planting programs were not implemented, and the private canopy was left to the market. The false signal is that "we manage the public trees." The harm is that the private canopy is lost (the development, the removal), the majority is missed, the canopy declines, and the management that should address the private canopy does not.

### Canopy Not Monitored, Progress Unknown

The canopy is not monitored, and the progress is unknown, because the periodic assessment and the reporting were not implemented. The trap is that the unmonitored canopy has the unknown progress. The mechanism is that the monitoring was not implemented, and the canopy was not measured. The false signal is that "we plant trees." The harm is that the change is not tracked, the losses are not identified, the progress is unknown, the accountability is absent, and the monitoring that should track the progress does not.

## Self-Check

- Is the canopy goal based on the analysis and the feasibility, with the assessment, the benchmarking, the feasibility, and the engagement?
- Is the canopy distribution analyzed for the equity, with the neighborhood and demographic mapping, the heat island, the health, and the priority identification?
- Is the planting prioritized in the high-need areas, with the priority map, the targeted programs, the partnerships, and the directed resources?
- Is the canopy managed on the public and the private land, with the public management, the private protection, and the private planting?
- Is the canopy monitored for the change and the progress, with the remote sensing, the periodic assessment, the change analysis, and the public reporting?
- Is the goal analysis-based, or is it arbitrary?
- Is the equity analyzed, or is the inequity perpetuated?
- Is the planting targeted, or is the distribution random?
- Is the private canopy managed, or is the majority missed?
- Is the canopy monitored, or is the progress unknown?
