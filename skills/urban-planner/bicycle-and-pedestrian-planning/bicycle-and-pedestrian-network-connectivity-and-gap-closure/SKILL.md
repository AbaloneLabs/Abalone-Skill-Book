# Bicycle and Pedestrian Network Connectivity and Gap Closure

A bicycle or pedestrian facility is only as useful as the network it connects to, and the defining failure of active transportation infrastructure is not poor design of individual segments but the gaps between them. A bike lane that ends at a hostile intersection, a sidewalk that terminates at a ditch, a trail that stops short of the destination it was meant to serve — each gap converts a potentially useful facility into a barrier, because a person walking or cycling must either abandon the trip, detour, or traverse a segment that feels unsafe. The judgment problem is that network connectivity is a system property that cannot be achieved by building good segments in isolation; it requires mapping the network as a whole, identifying the barriers and missing links that fragment it, and prioritizing investment to close the gaps that produce the greatest access gains. This is analytically harder than designing a single corridor, because it requires network-level analysis (where do people want to go, what routes can they take, where are the barriers), and it is politically harder, because gap-closure projects are often the most expensive and contentious (a bridge over a highway, a crossing of a rail line, a connection through a reluctant neighborhood). This skill covers the network-level decisions that determine whether active transportation investment produces a connected, low-stress system or a collection of impressive but isolated segments. It complements the facility-design and corridor-selection skill by focusing on the connectivity, access, and prioritization logic that operates above the individual project.

## Core Rules

### Map the Network as a System of Destinations, Routes, and Barriers

Connectivity analysis begins with a network map that is more than a inventory of existing facilities; it is a model of how people actually travel — where the destinations are (schools, jobs, transit, retail, parks), what routes connect origins to destinations, and where the barriers (highways, arterials, rivers, rail lines, large parcels with no through-connections) interrupt those routes. The rule is to map the network as a system of destinations, routes, and barriers, using GIS to model access (how many destinations are reachable within a given travel time or distance, by different population groups) and to identify the barriers and gaps that constrain access. The access analysis should be disaggregated by population (who can reach the destinations, and who cannot), because the equity dimension of connectivity is whether the people who depend most on walking and cycling — lower-income residents, children, older adults, people without cars — have access to a connected network, or whether the network serves the confident recreational cyclist while leaving the dependent user stranded. The network map should distinguish low-stress from high-stress facilities, because a route that is technically connected but requires cycling on a high-stress arterial is not a usable connection for most of the population, and the connectivity analysis should measure access via the low-stress network specifically.

### Prioritize Gap Closure by Access Gain, Not by Project Ease

The temptation in active transportation investment is to build the projects that are easy — the trail along the creek, the bike lane on the quiet street, the sidewalk in the new subdivision — and to defer the projects that are hard but high-value — the bridge over the highway, the protected crossing of the arterial, the connection through the established neighborhood. The rule is to prioritize gap closure by the access gain it produces, not by the ease of the project, because a single high-value gap closure can connect large segments of network and produce more access gain than many easy projects. The access-gain analysis should model the before-and-after: how many destinations become reachable, for how many people, when a specific gap is closed, and the prioritization should rank projects by access gain per dollar, not by the linear feet of facility built. The high-value gaps are often the barriers — the highway, the river, the rail line — because closing a barrier crossing connects entire sides of the network that are currently isolated, and a single crossing can transform the network's connectivity. The prioritization should also consider the equity dimension: does the gap closure serve the populations that depend on active transportation, or does it serve the populations that already have access? A gap-closure program that prioritizes ease over access gain produces a network of comfortable segments that do not connect, and the low use of the isolated segments is then misread as low demand.

### Use Level of Traffic Stress to Evaluate the Usable Network

A network that is connected on a map may not be usable in practice, because the stress of the facilities determines who will use them. Level of Traffic Stress (LTS) is a method for classifying facilities by the stress they impose on users, from LTS 1 (suitable for children) to LTS 4 (suitable only for confident, experienced cyclists), and it provides a more accurate picture of the usable network than a simple inventory of facilities. The rule is to use LTS (or a comparable stress-based method) to evaluate the usable network, mapping the low-stress network specifically and measuring access via that network, because the population that will use a connected low-stress network is far larger than the population that will use a connected high-stress network. The LTS analysis should identify the high-stress segments that break otherwise low-stress routes — the arterial crossing, the gap in protection, the high-speed merge — and these segments should be prioritized for treatment, because reducing the stress of a single segment can convert a high-stress route to a low-stress one and dramatically expand the usable network. The LTS framework should be applied to pedestrian facilities as well, adapted to the stressors that affect walking (traffic speed and volume, crossing distance, sidewalk presence and condition, intersection design), because the same logic applies: a sidewalk network that is technically complete but includes high-stress crossings is not a usable network for the people who need it most.

### Address Barriers and Crossings as the Highest-Leverage Interventions

Barriers — highways, arterials, rivers, rail lines, large unbroken parcels — are the structures that most severely fragment active transportation networks, and the crossings of those barriers are the highest-leverage interventions for connectivity, because a single crossing can reconnect sides of the network that are otherwise isolated. The rule is to address barriers and crossings as the highest-leverage interventions, identifying every barrier crossing in the network, evaluating the existing crossings (are they present, are they low-stress, are they adequately spaced), and prioritizing new or improved crossings where the access gain is greatest. Barrier crossings are often the most expensive and contentious projects (a pedestrian bridge over a highway can cost millions and face opposition from adjacent property owners), but they are also the projects that most transform the network, and deferring them in favor of easier projects perpetuates the fragmentation. The crossing analysis should consider spacing (how far apart are the crossings, and does the spacing force long detours), stress (are the existing crossings low-stress or do they require mixing with high-speed traffic), and accessibility (do the crossings meet accessibility standards for grade, width, and surface), because a crossing that is present but high-stress or inaccessible is not a usable connection.

### Sequence Investment to Build Continuous Low-Stress Routes Incrementally

A connected network is built over years, through many projects, and the sequencing of those projects determines whether the network builds toward connectivity or accumulates as isolated segments. The rule is to sequence investment to build continuous low-stress routes incrementally, identifying the spine routes that connect the most destinations and closing the gaps along those routes first, so that each project extends a usable route rather than standing alone. The sequencing should prioritize the gaps that complete a route over the gaps that start a new one, because a completed route generates use that justifies further investment, while an isolated segment generates little use and may be cited as evidence of low demand. The sequencing should also manage the political dimension: a network-building strategy that delivers a visible, usable route early builds the constituency for the harder projects later, while a strategy that defers all the high-value gaps to the end may lose political support before the network is complete. The incremental strategy should be documented in a network plan that shows the build-out sequence, so that each project is understood as a step toward a connected network rather than as a standalone investment, and so that the funding and political strategy can be sustained over the years required to complete the network.

## Common Traps

### Isolated Segments Mistaken for a Network

The jurisdiction builds impressive individual facilities — a scenic trail, a protected bike lane on a showcase corridor — but the facilities do not connect to each other or to the destinations people need to reach, and the low use is misread as low demand. The trap is that segment-level success masks network-level failure. The mechanism is that the projects were selected for visibility or ease rather than for connectivity, and the gaps between them were not addressed. The false signal is that "we are building bike infrastructure." The harm is that the facilities serve few users, the low use undermines the political case for further investment, and the network that would generate high use is never assembled because the connectivity logic was never applied.

### Prioritizing Easy Projects Over High-Value Gap Closures

The investment program funds the easy projects — trails along creeks, bike lanes on quiet streets — and defers the hard but high-value projects — barrier crossings, arterial protections, connections through reluctant neighborhoods — and the network remains fragmented despite years of investment. The trap is that ease of delivery substitutes for access gain in prioritization. The mechanism is that the prioritization did not model access gain, or the political process favored visible easy projects over contentious high-value ones. The false signal is that "we are making progress." The harm is that the network's fragmentation persists, the high-value gaps that would transform connectivity remain open, and the investment produces diminishing returns as the easy projects are completed without addressing the barriers.

### Connected-on-Map but High-Stress in Practice

The network map shows connected facilities, but the connections require cycling on high-stress arterials or crossing high-speed roads, and the network is usable only by the confident few, not by the population that would use a low-stress network. The trap is that map-level connectivity overstates usable connectivity. The mechanism is that the connectivity analysis counted all facilities equally, without distinguishing low-stress from high-stress, and the high-stress segments break the otherwise low-stress routes. The false signal is that "the network is connected." The harm is that the facilities serve only the confident users, the broader population does not use them, the access gains are smaller than projected, and the investment is judged a failure for not generating the use that a low-stress network would have generated.

### Barriers Deferred Because Crossings Are Expensive

The network plan identifies the barrier crossings as high-value, but the crossings are expensive and contentious, and they are deferred in favor of cheaper projects, leaving the network fragmented across the barriers indefinitely. The trap is that the highest-leverage interventions are never built because they are hard. The mechanism is that the funding and political strategy could not sustain the expensive crossings, and the easier projects were substituted. The false signal is that "we will address the barriers later." The harm is that the barriers continue to fragment the network, the access gains from the cheaper projects are limited by the persistent fragmentation, and the crossings become harder to build over time as adjacent development and political opposition accumulate.

### Equity Blind in Connectivity Analysis

The connectivity analysis measures aggregate access, but it does not disaggregate by population, and the network serves the confident recreational users in the connected areas while leaving the dependent users — lower-income residents, children, older adults — in the disconnected areas without access. The trap is that aggregate access metrics hide distributional inequity. The mechanism is that the analysis did not ask who has access, only how much access exists. The false signal is that "access is improving." The harm is that the populations that depend most on active transportation remain stranded, the equity goals of the investment are not met, and the network reinforces rather than reduces the existing access disparities.

## Self-Check

- Is the network mapped as a system of destinations, routes, and barriers, with access modeled and disaggregated by population group?
- Are gap-closure projects prioritized by access gain per dollar, including the high-value barrier crossings, rather than by project ease?
- Is Level of Traffic Stress (or a comparable method) used to evaluate the usable low-stress network, and are the high-stress segments that break low-stress routes identified for treatment?
- Are barriers and crossings addressed as the highest-leverage interventions, with crossings evaluated for spacing, stress, and accessibility?
- Is investment sequenced to build continuous low-stress routes incrementally, with each project extending a usable route rather than standing alone?
- If a person tried to travel from a typical origin to a typical destination on the network, would they encounter gaps, high-stress segments, or barriers that end or deter the trip?
- Are the highest-value gap closures — the barrier crossings — funded and scheduled, or are they deferred indefinitely in favor of easier projects?
- Does the connectivity analysis distinguish the low-stress usable network from the high-stress technical network, and is access measured via the former?
- Does the network serve the populations that depend on active transportation, or does it serve the populations that already have access?
- Is each project understood as a step toward a connected network, documented in a build-out sequence, or is it a standalone investment whose relationship to the network is unclear?
