# Active Transportation Equity and Access for Dependent Users

Active transportation planning has a recurring blind spot: it tends to serve the users who are easiest to plan for — confident recreational cyclists in connected neighborhoods, healthy adults walking for exercise in pleasant districts — while underserving the users who depend on walking and cycling because they have no alternative. The dependent users are lower-income residents who cannot afford a car, children who cannot drive, older adults who have stopped or never started driving, people with disabilities who rely on accessible pedestrian infrastructure, and workers who commute by bike or foot because transit does not serve their shift or their route. These users make the trips that active transportation is, in principle, designed to enable — to work, to school, to the grocery store, to the clinic — and yet the infrastructure that serves them is often the last to be built, because it runs through the neighborhoods with the least political voice, serves the trip types that generate the least enthusiasm, and competes for funding with showcase projects in higher-income areas. The judgment problem is that equity in active transportation is not achieved by building the same facilities everywhere; it is achieved by recognizing that different populations have different needs, different barriers, and different dependence on the network, and by prioritizing investment to serve the populations whose access is most constrained and whose alternatives are fewest. This skill covers the equity analysis and prioritization that determines whether active transportation investment reduces or reinforces existing access disparities. It complements the network-connectivity and facility-design skills by focusing on the distributional question of who the network serves.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Dependent Users and Their Actual Trip Patterns

Equity analysis begins with identifying who depends on active transportation and what trips they actually make, because the dependent user's trip pattern is often invisible in conventional transportation data, which is built around the car commuter and the peak-hour motor vehicle trip. The rule is to identify the dependent users and their trip patterns through data sources that capture them: census journey-to-work data disaggregated by income and vehicle availability (which reveals the car-free and car-light households and their commute modes), school travel data (which reveals how children get to school and the barriers they face), transit rider surveys (which reveal the walk and bike access trips that transit depends on), and direct engagement with the communities (which reveals the trips the data misses — to the laundromat, to the clinic, to the second job, to childcare). The trip-pattern analysis should identify the destinations that matter to dependent users, which are often different from the destinations that drive conventional planning (the regional job center, the downtown) — the grocery store in the food desert, the clinic with limited hours, the school with no safe walking route, the bus stop with no sidewalk or shelter. The dependent-user analysis should be disaggregated by population, because the needs differ: the child needs a low-stress route to school, the older adult needs a continuous accessible sidewalk with frequent resting places and adequate crossing time, the worker needs a direct protected route to the job site, the person with a disability needs infrastructure that meets accessibility standards throughout.

### Measure Access Disparities and Target Investment to Close Them

Once the dependent users and their trips are identified, the equity analysis measures the access disparities: which populations can reach which destinations, via what quality of network, and how does this compare across neighborhoods and income groups. The rule is to measure access disparities explicitly and to target investment to close them, because an investment strategy that does not measure disparities will not close them, and may widen them by directing resources to the neighborhoods that already have access. The access measurement should use the network-connectivity and level-of-stress methods, but disaggregated by population and neighborhood, so that the analysis reveals, for example, that lower-income residents on one side of the highway can reach half as many jobs by bike as higher-income residents on the other side, because the highway is a barrier and the crossing serves the higher-income side. The investment targeting should prioritize the populations and neighborhoods with the greatest access deficits, even when those neighborhoods have less political voice or the projects are less visible, because the access gain per dollar is greatest where the deficit is greatest. The targeting should be explicit and documented, because the political pressure to direct investment to higher-income, higher-voice neighborhoods is strong, and without an explicit equity target, the investment will drift toward the politically easy projects.

### Design for the Dependent User's Needs, Not the Recreational Standard

The design standards for active transportation facilities are often calibrated to the recreational or confident user — the cyclist who can handle a high-stress merge, the pedestrian who can wait for a gap in traffic, the user who can navigate a steep grade or a long crossing — and these standards produce facilities that the dependent user cannot use. The rule is to design for the dependent user's needs, which are more demanding: the child needs a fully protected, low-stress route with no high-stress segments; the older adult needs longer crossing times, refuge islands, gentle grades, and frequent resting places; the person with a disability needs infrastructure that meets or exceeds accessibility standards (PROWAG in the US), including curb ramps, detectable warnings, adequate width, firm and stable surfaces, and accessible signalization; the worker needs a direct route that does not detour around the barriers. Designing for the dependent user often raises the standard for all users, because a facility that works for a child or an older adult works for everyone, while a facility designed for the confident user works only for the confident user. The design should be tested against the dependent user's needs explicitly: would a child use this route to school, would an older adult cross this intersection, would a wheelchair user navigate this sidewalk — and if the answer is no, the design is inadequate regardless of whether it meets the minimum standard.

### Address the Barriers That Disproportionately Affect Dependent Users

The barriers that fragment active transportation networks — highways, arterials, rivers, rail lines — disproportionately affect dependent users, because dependent users cannot drive around the barrier the way a car user can, and the detour imposed by a barrier consumes a larger share of a walking or cycling trip than a driving trip. The rule is to address the barriers that disproportionately affect dependent users, prioritizing the crossings that serve the populations whose access is most constrained by the barrier. A highway that separates a lower-income neighborhood from its grocery store, school, and transit stop is a more urgent crossing priority than a highway that separates two higher-income neighborhoods with ample alternatives, because the lower-income residents have no alternative and the barrier constrains their access to essential destinations. The barrier analysis should identify which populations are affected by each barrier, what destinations the barrier separates them from, and what alternatives exist, and the crossing prioritization should weight the barrier's impact on dependent users heavily. Addressing these barriers is often expensive and contentious, but it is the investment that most reduces access disparities, because it reconnects the populations whose access is most constrained.

### Ensure Maintenance, Lighting, and Security for the Dependent User's Trips

A facility that is built but not maintained, not lit, or not secure is not usable for the dependent user, who cannot choose an alternative route or time of travel the way a recreational user can. The rule is to ensure maintenance, lighting, and security for the dependent user's trips, because the worker who commutes by bike at 5 AM needs a lit, maintained, secure route, the older adult who walks to the clinic needs a sidewalk that is clear of snow, debris, and overgrowth, and the child who walks to school needs a route that is safe from crime as well as from traffic. The maintenance standard should be higher for the facilities that serve dependent users, because a deferred-maintenance facility that a recreational user can avoid is a barrier for the dependent user who has no alternative. The lighting and security analysis should consider the dependent user's travel times, which often include early morning, evening, and night hours, and should address the personal-security dimension that deters use, particularly for women, older adults, and people in higher-crime neighborhoods. The maintenance, lighting, and security obligations should be funded and assigned, not assumed, because a facility that is not maintained, lit, or secured will fall into disuse, and the disuse will be misread as low demand.

## Common Traps

### Recreational Network Mistaken for a Transportation Network

The active transportation investment builds a network of scenic trails and showcase corridors that serve recreational users in connected, higher-income areas, while the utilitarian network that serves dependent users' trips to work, school, and services remains unbuilt. The trap is that recreational visibility substitutes for transportation function. The mechanism is that the investment prioritized visible, popular projects over the functional, dependent-user projects. The false signal is that "we have a great trail network." The harm is that the dependent users remain stranded, the equity goals are not met, and the network serves the users who already have alternatives while underserving the users who do not.

### Aggregate Metrics Hiding Distributional Disparities

The active transportation plan reports aggregate access gains — "X more miles of bike lane, Y more residents within a quarter-mile of a facility" — but the gains accrue disproportionately to higher-income neighborhoods, and the access disparities between neighborhoods widen even as the aggregate improves. The trap is that aggregate metrics mask inequity. The mechanism is that the analysis did not disaggregate by population or neighborhood. The false signal is that "access is improving for everyone." The harm is that the populations with the greatest access deficits are not served, the disparities widen, and the investment reinforces rather than reduces the existing inequity, while the aggregate metrics provide cover for the inequitable distribution.

### Design Standards Calibrated to the Confident User

The facilities are built to standards that the confident user can navigate but the dependent user cannot — unprotected bike lanes on arterials, intersections with no protection for cyclists or long crossing distances for pedestrians, grades and surfaces that exclude wheelchair users — and the facilities serve only the confident few. The trap is that minimum-standard design excludes the dependent user. The mechanism is that the design standards were calibrated to the confident or typical user, not to the dependent user's more demanding needs. The false signal is that "the facility meets the standard." The harm is that the dependent users cannot use the facility, the access gains are smaller than projected, and the investment serves the users who already have alternatives while excluding the users who do not.

### Barriers Affecting Dependent Users Left in Place

The barriers that most constrain dependent users' access — the highway that separates the lower-income neighborhood from its destinations, the arterial with no safe crossing — are left in place because the crossings are expensive, and the dependent users continue to face long detours, dangerous crossings, or abandoned trips. The trap is that the highest-impact equity investments are deferred because they are hard. The mechanism is that the barrier crossings were not prioritized for their impact on dependent users, and the easier projects were substituted. The false signal is that "we are addressing equity." The harm is that the dependent users' access remains constrained by the barriers, the equity goals are not met, and the investment produces less access gain than the barrier crossings would have produced.

### Maintenance and Security Assumed Rather Than Funded

The facilities are built but the maintenance, lighting, and security are not funded or assigned, and the facilities fall into disrepair, become dark or unsafe, and fall into disuse, particularly for the dependent users who travel at off-peak hours or have no alternative route. The trap is that the operating obligations were assumed rather than committed. The mechanism is that the capital project was funded but the operating budget was not. The false signal is that "the facility is built." The harm is that the facility becomes unusable for the dependent user, the access gains erode, the disuse is misread as low demand, and the investment is effectively wasted for the population it was meant to serve.

## Self-Check

- Have the dependent users (car-free, lower-income, children, older adults, people with disabilities) and their actual trip patterns been identified through data and engagement?
- Are access disparities measured and disaggregated by population and neighborhood, and is investment targeted to close the greatest deficits?
- Are facilities designed for the dependent user's more demanding needs (full protection, accessibility, longer crossing times, gentle grades), not merely for the confident user?
- Are the barriers that disproportionately affect dependent users identified and prioritized for crossing, weighted by their impact on the populations with the fewest alternatives?
- Are maintenance, lighting, and security funded and assigned for the facilities that serve dependent users, particularly for their off-peak travel times?
- Would a child use this route to school, would an older adult cross this intersection, would a wheelchair user navigate this sidewalk — and if not, is the design adequate?
- Does the network serve the trips that dependent users actually make (to work, school, grocery, clinic, transit), or only the trips that recreational users make?
- Do the access gains accrue to the neighborhoods with the greatest deficits, or do they widen the disparities by serving already-connected areas?
- Are the barrier crossings that most constrain dependent users' access funded and scheduled, or deferred in favor of easier projects?
- Is the facility maintained, lit, and secure for the dependent user's travel times, or does it fall into disuse for the population that depends on it most?
