# Neighborhood Identity and Place Attachment

A neighborhood's identity — the character, the history, the culture, the shared meaning that the residents associate with the place — is the foundation of the place attachment that makes the residents stay, invest, and participate, and the planning that ignores or manufactures the identity undermines the attachment it should strengthen. The judgment problem is that the identity is held by the community (the residents' experience, memory, and meaning), not by the planner, and the planner's attempt to define or to brand the identity can either strengthen the authentic identity or impose the manufactured identity that does not reflect the community and that erodes the attachment. The planner must work with the community to articulate and to reinforce the identity, managing the tension between the preservation (the existing character that the residents value) and the change (the new residents, the development, the evolution), and using the placemaking and the branding to strengthen rather than to replace the authentic identity. This skill covers the identity and the place attachment decisions that determine whether the neighborhood planning strengthens or erodes the community's connection to the place. It complements the broader neighborhood-planning skill by focusing on the identity and the meaning dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Articulate the Identity With the Community, Not for the Community

The neighborhood identity should be articulated with the community, because the identity is held by the residents, and the articulation that the planner imposes does not reflect the community and erodes the attachment. The rule is to articulate the identity with the community, using the community-led process (the storytelling, the oral history, the community workshops, the cultural asset mapping), the inclusive engagement (the long-term residents, the new residents, the businesses, the cultural institutions), and the documentation (the written, the visual, the spatial record of the identity). The articulation should capture the multiple identities (the different communities, the different histories, the different meanings), because the neighborhood is not the monolith, and the single identity erases the multiple. The articulation should address the contested identity (the different communities' claims to the neighborhood), because the identity may be contested, and the articulation should navigate the contestation rather than impose the single version. The articulation should be documented, because the documentation preserves the identity, and the undocumented identity is the identity that is lost. The articulation should be shared and validated, because the community should recognize the articulated identity, and the validation ensures the authenticity.

### Preserve the Character-Defining Elements

The neighborhood identity is carried by the character-defining elements (the buildings, the streets, the landmarks, the landscapes, the cultural institutions, the businesses), and the preservation of these elements preserves the identity, and the loss of the elements erodes the identity. The rule is to preserve the character-defining elements, using the historic preservation (the landmark designation, the historic district), the cultural resource protection (the cultural landmarks, the community institutions), the business preservation (the legacy businesses, the cultural businesses), and the landscape preservation (the significant trees, the public spaces, the views). The preservation should identify the character-defining elements through the community process, because the community knows the elements that carry the identity, and the planner's identification may miss the elements the community values. The preservation should be targeted to the elements that carry the identity, because the preservation of the wrong elements (the buildings the community does not value) does not preserve the identity, and the preservation of the right elements does. The preservation should be balanced with the change, because the preservation that prevents all the change is the preservation that fossilizes, and the balanced preservation allows the evolution while maintaining the identity. The preservation should include the intangible heritage (the events, the traditions, the practices), because the identity is carried not only by the physical elements but by the cultural practices, and the intangible preservation is the part of the identity preservation.

### Use the Placemaking to Reinforce the Authentic Identity

The placemaking (the creation of the public spaces that reflect and strengthen the community) should reinforce the authentic identity, because the placemaking that reflects the community strengthens the attachment, and the placemaking that imposes the generic design erodes the identity. The rule is to use the placemaking to reinforce the authentic identity, using the community-led design (the residents' input into the public space design), the cultural expression (the public art, the interpretive elements, the cultural programming), the local materials and the craftsmanship (the materials and the craft that reflect the place), and the programming (the events, the activities, the gatherings that express the community). The placemaking should be community-led, because the community knows the identity, and the community-led placemaking reflects the identity. The placemaking should use the cultural expression, because the public art and the interpretive elements express the identity, and the generic design does not. The placemaking should be maintained and programmed, because the public space that is not maintained or programmed is the public space that does not function, and the maintenance and the programming sustain the place. The placemaking should be evaluated against the community use and the satisfaction, because the evaluation provides the evidence, and the evidence-based placemaking is the defensible placemaking.

### Avoid the Manufactured Identity and the Generic Branding

The manufactured identity (the brand that the planner or the developer creates and imposes) and the generic branding (the logo, the slogan, the theme that does not reflect the community) erode the authentic identity, because the manufactured identity replaces the community's identity with the marketing construct, and the residents do not recognize or attach to the manufactured identity. The rule is to avoid the manufactured identity and the generic branding, resisting the temptation to create the brand that the community did not articulate, and using the branding only to communicate the identity that the community defined. The manufactured identity should be avoided because it does not reflect the community, and the residents recognize the inauthenticity, and the recognition erodes the trust and the attachment. The generic branding should be avoided because it is interchangeable (the same branding applied to the different neighborhoods), and the interchangeable branding does not differentiate, and the differentiation is the purpose of the branding. The branding should be used to communicate the authentic identity, because the branding that communicates the community's identity reinforces the identity, and the branding that replaces the identity erodes it. The branding should be community-developed, because the community-developed branding reflects the community, and the planner-developed branding may not.

### Manage the Identity Change and the Evolution

The neighborhood identity is not static; it evolves with the new residents, the new businesses, the new buildings, and the new meanings, and the management of the evolution determines whether the identity remains authentic (the community's lived experience) or becomes contested (the conflict between the old and the new). The rule is to manage the identity change and the evolution, using the inclusive engagement (the old and the new residents together), the bridging (the activities and the spaces that bring the communities together), the evolution (the identity that incorporates the new while maintaining the old), and the conflict resolution (the process for the identity conflicts). The management should acknowledge the change, because the denial of the change (the attempt to freeze the identity) does not prevent the change, and the acknowledgment allows the managed evolution. The management should bridge the communities, because the bridging (the shared activities, the shared spaces, the shared identity) reduces the conflict, and the bridged identity is the identity that the old and the new share. The management should protect the vulnerable identity (the communities of color, the immigrant communities, the low-income communities whose identity is threatened by the gentrification), because the vulnerable identity is the identity that is lost, and the protection preserves the identity. The management should be ongoing, because the identity evolves continuously, and the ongoing management adapts to the evolution.

## Common Traps

### Identity Imposed, Community Not Engaged

The identity is imposed by the planner or the developer, and the community is not engaged in the articulation, and the imposed identity does not reflect the community, and the attachment is eroded. The trap is that the imposed identity erodes the attachment. The mechanism is that the community process was not conducted, and the identity was defined by the planner. The false signal is that "the neighborhood has an identity." The harm is that the residents do not recognize the identity, the attachment erodes, the trust is lost, and the identity that should strengthen the community does not.

### Character-Defining Elements Lost, Identity Eroded

The character-defining elements (the buildings, the landmarks, the businesses) are lost to the development, and the identity is eroded, because the preservation was not pursued. The trap is that the lost elements erode the identity. The mechanism is that the preservation was not implemented, and the development removed the elements. The false signal is that "the neighborhood is changing." The harm is that the identity is eroded, the attachment weakens, the residents lose the connection to the place, and the elements that carried the identity are gone.

### Placemaking Generic, Identity Not Reflected

The placemaking is generic (the standard design, the interchangeable elements), and the identity is not reflected, because the community-led design was not pursued. The trap is that the generic placemaking does not reflect the identity. The mechanism is that the community-led design was not conducted, and the standard design was applied. The false signal is that "the public space is improved." The harm is that the public space does not reflect the community, the identity is not expressed, the attachment is not strengthened, and the placemaking that should reinforce the identity does not.

### Manufactured Brand Imposed, Authenticity Lost

The manufactured brand is imposed, and the authentic identity is replaced by the marketing construct, and the authenticity is lost, because the community-developed branding was not pursued. The trap is that the manufactured brand loses the authenticity. The mechanism is that the community-developed branding was not conducted, and the brand was created by the consultant. The false signal is that "the neighborhood is branded." The harm is that the brand does not reflect the community, the authenticity is lost, the residents do not attach to the brand, and the branding that should communicate the identity replaces it.

### Evolution Not Managed, Communities Conflict

The identity evolution is not managed, and the old and the new communities conflict, because the bridging and the conflict resolution were not pursued. The trap is that the unmanaged evolution causes the conflict. The mechanism is that the bridging was not conducted, and the communities did not integrate. The false signal is that "the neighborhood is changing." The harm is that the communities conflict, the identity is contested, the attachment erodes, the vulnerable identity is lost, and the evolution that should be managed causes the conflict.

## Self-Check

- Is the identity articulated with the community, through the community-led process, the inclusive engagement, and the documentation?
- Are the character-defining elements preserved, through the historic preservation, the cultural resource protection, and the business and landscape preservation?
- Is the placemaking used to reinforce the authentic identity, through the community-led design, the cultural expression, and the programming?
- Is the manufactured identity and the generic branding avoided, with the branding used only to communicate the community-defined identity?
- Is the identity evolution managed, through the inclusive engagement, the bridging, the protection of the vulnerable identity, and the conflict resolution?
- Is the identity community-articulated, or is it imposed?
- Are the character-defining elements preserved, or are they lost?
- Is the placemaking authentic, or is it generic?
- Is the brand community-developed, or is it manufactured?
- Is the evolution managed, or do the communities conflict?
