# Neighborhood Housing Affordability and Displacement Risk

Neighborhood planning often involves the investment that improves the physical conditions and the amenities, and this investment can increase the property values and the rents, and the increase can displace the existing residents who cannot afford the higher costs. The judgment problem is that the planner wants the investment (the improved housing, the amenities, the services) but must manage the displacement (the rent increases, the property tax increases, the condo conversions) that the investment can cause, and the navigation of this tension determines whether the neighborhood improves for the existing residents or replaces them. A neighborhood that manages the displacement well (the preservation, the protection, the inclusion) improves with the existing residents, while a neighborhood that manages it poorly improves by replacing them, and the improvement that displaces is the improvement that fails the community. This skill covers the affordability and the displacement risk decisions that determine whether the neighborhood investment benefits or displaces the existing residents. It complements the broader neighborhood-planning skill by focusing on the affordability and the displacement dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Analyze the Displacement Risk Before the Investment

The displacement risk should be analyzed before the neighborhood investment, because the analysis identifies the neighborhoods and the populations at the risk, and the strategies should be targeted to the risk, and the investment without the analysis may cause the displacement that the strategies could have prevented. The rule is to analyze the displacement risk before the investment, using the displacement risk indicators (the rent trends, the demographic change, the income, the tenure, the cost burden, the vulnerable populations), the market analysis (the property values, the investment patterns, the development pipeline), and the community input (the residents' experience of the pressure, the displacement stories). The analysis should identify the high-risk neighborhoods and the high-risk populations (the low-income renters, the seniors, the communities of color, the immigrants), because the strategies should target the high risk. The analysis should be mapped, because the spatial distribution of the risk guides the targeted intervention. The analysis should be updated regularly, because the displacement risk changes, and the strategies should reflect the current risk. The analysis should be shared with the community, because the community should know the risk, and the shared analysis builds the support for the strategies.

### Preserve the Existing Affordable Housing Stock

The existing affordable housing stock (the naturally occurring affordable housing, the subsidized housing, the rent-controlled units) is the most efficient and the most immediate protection against the displacement, because the preservation of the existing units is faster and cheaper than the production of the new units, and the loss of the existing units outpaces the production of the new. The rule is to preserve the existing affordable housing stock, using the preservation strategies (the acquisition of the at-risk properties, the rehabilitation with the affordability restrictions, the right of first refusal for the tenants), the regulatory tools (the rent stabilization, the just-cause eviction, the condo conversion controls), and the funding (the local housing trust fund, the state and federal programs, the philanthropic sources). The preservation should target the at-risk properties (the subsidized properties with the expiring affordability, the naturally affordable properties with the market pressure), because the at-risk properties are the properties that will be lost. The preservation should include the regulatory protections (the rent stabilization, the just-cause eviction), because the regulatory protections apply to the broader stock, and the protections prevent the displacement that the acquisition cannot address. The preservation should include the tenant protections (the relocation assistance, the legal aid, the rental assistance), because the tenants who are displaced need the support, and the support reduces the harm.

### Produce the New Affordable Housing

The new affordable housing production is needed to address the affordability gap that the market does not fill, and the production should be integrated into the neighborhood, because the affordable housing that is segregated in the low-opportunity areas does not advance the equity, and the integrated production does. The rule is to produce the new affordable housing, using the inclusionary zoning (the requirement that the new development include the affordable units), the density bonuses (the incentive for the affordable housing), the public land (the surplus property dedicated to the affordable housing), and the funding (the housing trust fund, the tax credits, the bonds). The production should target the range of the incomes (the extremely low, the very low, the low, the moderate), because the single-income production does not serve the range of the needs. The production should include the larger units (the two-bedroom, the three-bedroom, the four-bedroom), because the families need the larger units, and the all-studio production does not serve the families. The production should be integrated into the high-opportunity areas (the good schools, the jobs, the transit), because the affordable housing in the high-opportunity areas advances the equity, and the concentration in the low-opportunity areas does not.

### Manage the Gentrification Pressures

The gentrification (the influx of the higher-income residents and the investment that changes the neighborhood character) creates the pressures (the rent increases, the property tax increases, the cultural displacement) that can displace the existing residents, and the management of these pressures determines whether the gentrification benefits or displaces the community. The rule is to manage the gentrification pressures, using the anti-displacement strategies (the preservation, the production, the protection), the community benefits agreements (the commitments from the developers to the community), the cultural preservation (the support of the local businesses, the cultural institutions, the community organizations), and the equitable development (the investment that benefits the existing residents). The management should be proactive, because the gentrification that is managed early is the gentrification that does not displace, and the reactive management is the late management. The management should be community-led, because the community knows the pressures and the needs, and the community-led management is the management that serves the community. The management should address the cultural displacement (the loss of the community character, the local businesses, the cultural institutions), because the cultural displacement is the displacement that the housing strategies alone do not address, and the cultural preservation is the part of the management.

### Align the Investment With the Stabilization

The neighborhood investment (the streetscape, the transit, the parks, the services) should be aligned with the stabilization (the preservation, the production, the protection), because the investment without the stabilization causes the displacement, and the stabilization without the investment leaves the disinvestment, and the alignment produces the improvement that benefits the existing residents. The rule is to align the investment with the stabilization, coordinating the physical investment with the housing strategies, sequencing the investment to precede or coincide with the stabilization, and engaging the community to ensure the alignment serves the residents. The coordination should ensure that the transit investment includes the preservation along the corridor, because the transit increases the property values, and the preservation prevents the displacement. The sequencing should ensure that the stabilization precedes the investment, because the stabilization that follows the investment is the stabilization that is too late, and the preceding stabilization prevents the displacement. The community engagement should ensure that the alignment serves the residents, because the alignment that is imposed does not serve, and the community-led alignment does. The alignment should be evaluated against the displacement metrics (the displacement rate, the affordability, the tenure), because the metrics provide the evidence, and the evidence-based alignment is the defensible alignment.

## Common Traps

### Displacement Risk Not Analyzed, Investment Causes the Displacement

The displacement risk is not analyzed before the investment, and the investment causes the displacement that the strategies could have prevented, because the analysis was not conducted and the strategies were not targeted. The trap is that the unanalyzed risk causes the displacement. The mechanism is that the analysis was not conducted, and the investment proceeded without the strategies. The false signal is that "the investment is good." The harm is that the residents are displaced, the community is disrupted, the equity is not advanced, and the investment that should benefit the community displaces it.

### Existing Stock Not Preserved, Units Lost Faster Than Produced

The existing affordable housing stock is not preserved, and the units are lost to the market (the rent increases, the condo conversions, the expiring subsidies) faster than the new units are produced, because the preservation was not pursued. The trap is that the unpreserved stock is lost faster than the production replaces it. The mechanism is that the preservation was not funded or regulated, and the stock was lost. The false signal is that "we produce the new affordable housing." The harm is that the net affordable stock declines, the residents are displaced, the preservation that is faster and cheaper is not pursued, and the production that is slower and more expensive cannot keep up.

### New Housing Not Integrated, Segregation Continues

The new affordable housing is not integrated into the high-opportunity areas, and the production is concentrated in the low-opportunity areas, because the integration was not pursued. The trap is that the segregated production continues the segregation. The mechanism is that the high-opportunity targeting was not required, and the production followed the path of the least resistance. The false signal is that "we produce the affordable housing." The harm is that the affordable housing is concentrated, the segregation continues, the residents do not access the opportunity, and the production that should integrate does not.

### Gentrification Not Managed, Cultural Displacement Occurs

The gentrification is not managed, and the cultural displacement (the loss of the community character, the local businesses, the cultural institutions) occurs, because the management was not pursued. The trap is that the unmanaged gentrification causes the cultural displacement. The mechanism is that the management was not implemented, and the gentrification proceeded. The false signal is that "the neighborhood is improving." The harm is that the community character is lost, the local businesses close, the cultural institutions disappear, the residents feel displaced even if they stay, and the improvement that should benefit the community erases it.

### Investment Not Aligned With the Stabilization

The investment is not aligned with the stabilization, and the investment causes the displacement while the stabilization follows too late, because the alignment was not coordinated. The trap is that the unaligned investment causes the displacement. The mechanism is that the coordination was not conducted, and the investment and the stabilization proceeded separately. The false signal is that "the investment and the stabilization are both happening." The harm is that the investment causes the displacement, the stabilization is too late, the residents are displaced before the stabilization arrives, and the alignment that should prevent the displacement does not.

## Self-Check

- Is the displacement risk analyzed before the investment, with the indicators, the market analysis, the community input, and the mapping?
- Is the existing affordable housing stock preserved, with the acquisition, the rehabilitation, the regulatory tools, and the tenant protections?
- Is the new affordable housing produced, with the inclusionary zoning, the density bonuses, the public land, and the integration into the high-opportunity areas?
- Is the gentrification managed, with the anti-displacement strategies, the community benefits, the cultural preservation, and the equitable development?
- Is the investment aligned with the stabilization, with the coordination, the sequencing, the community engagement, and the displacement metrics?
- Is the risk analyzed, or does the investment cause the displacement?
- Is the stock preserved, or are the units lost faster than produced?
- Is the housing integrated, or does the segregation continue?
- Is the gentrification managed, or does the cultural displacement occur?
- Is the investment aligned, or does the stabilization follow too late?
