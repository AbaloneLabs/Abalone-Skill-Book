# Brownfield Liability Transfer and Environmental Insurance

The central obstacle to brownfield redevelopment is not the contamination itself but the liability that attaches to it, because under federal and state environmental law, liability for contamination can follow ownership regardless of who caused the release, and a developer who acquires a contaminated site may inherit cleanup obligations and third-party claims that dwarf the project's value. The liability regime is the reason brownfields sit idle: a developer who could profitably redevelop the site if the liability were resolved cannot proceed because the liability is uncertain, potentially unlimited, and not dischargeable through ordinary transactional structures. The judgment problem is that liability transfer is achieved through a set of legal instruments — prospective purchaser agreements, comfort letters, covenants not to sue, bona fide prospective purchaser status, environmental insurance — each of which allocates the liability among the parties (the current owner, the past owner, the developer, the agency, the insurer) in different ways, with different costs, different coverage, and different residual risks. Selecting and structuring these instruments is the work that determines whether the liability is resolved cleanly or whether it lingers to generate disputes, cost overruns, and litigation long after the redevelopment is complete. This skill covers the liability-allocation decisions and instruments. It complements the assessment-and-remediation skill by focusing on the legal and financial structures that resolve who pays, rather than on the technical cleanup.

## Core Rules

### Map the Liability Before Structuring the Transfer

Liability transfer begins with mapping who is currently liable, for what, and to whom, because the transfer strategy depends on the existing liability profile. The rule is to map the liability comprehensively before structuring the transfer, identifying the potentially responsible parties (current and past owners and operators, generators, transporters), the regulatory authorities (federal Superfund, state voluntary cleanup, state brownfield programs), the third-party claimants (adjacent property owners, downgradient water users, natural resource trustees), and the nature of each claim (cleanup cost, natural resource damages, personal injury, property diminution). The liability map should distinguish the liability that can be transferred or resolved through agreement (the agency's cleanup claim, addressed through a prospective purchaser agreement) from the liability that cannot (third-party toxic tort claims, which are not resolved by an agency agreement and require insurance or indemnification). The liability map should also identify the defenses available — bona fide prospective purchaser (BFPP) status under federal law, innocent landowner, contiguous property owner — which can shield a developer from liability if the pre-acquisition due diligence (the Phase I) meets the statutory standard and the post-acquisition obligations (no exacerbation, appropriate care, cooperation, institutional controls) are met. The liability map determines the instruments needed: if the agency claim is the primary risk, a prospective purchaser agreement resolves it; if third-party claims are the primary risk, environmental insurance or indemnification is needed; if the BFPP defense is available, it may reduce or eliminate the need for other instruments.

### Use Prospective Purchaser Agreements to Resolve the Agency Claim

The regulatory agency's claim for cleanup is the most direct liability, and it can be resolved through a prospective purchaser agreement (PPA) or similar instrument in which the agency agrees not to pursue the developer in exchange for the developer's commitment to perform specified cleanup and other consideration. The rule is to use a PPA (or the state-law equivalent — comfort letter, covenant not to sue, prospective purchaser agreement) to resolve the agency claim, negotiating the scope of the developer's cleanup obligation, the agency's covenant not to sue, and the contribution protection (which shields the developer from the agency's claims and from other parties' contribution claims for the matters covered). The PPA should be drafted to define precisely what cleanup the developer must perform (referencing the remediation action plan), what the agency's covenant covers (the specific contaminants and media), and what residual liability remains (third-party claims, contamination discovered later, natural resource damages, which may not be covered). The PPA should address the contingencies: what happens if the cleanup costs more than expected, if contamination is discovered that was not in the agreement, if the developer fails to complete the cleanup, if the property is sold before the cleanup is complete. The PPA is the instrument that converts an uncertain, potentially unlimited agency liability into a defined, manageable obligation, and its quality determines whether the developer can proceed with confidence or whether residual uncertainty undermines the project's financing.

### Secure Bona Fide Prospective Purchaser Status Through Compliant Due Diligence

Federal law provides a liability defense for the bona fide prospective purchaser who acquires property with knowledge of the contamination (after the appropriate due diligence) and meets ongoing obligations, and this defense is the foundation of liability protection for many brownfield developers. The rule is to secure BFPP status through compliant due diligence — an All Appropriate Inquiry (AAI) Phase I conducted before acquisition, by a qualified environmental professional, meeting the regulatory standard — and to maintain the ongoing obligations after acquisition (no exacerbation of the contamination, appropriate care to stop continuing releases, full cooperation with the agency, compliance with institutional controls, and the reporting and access obligations). The BFPP defense is only as strong as the due diligence that establishes it, and a Phase I that does not meet the AAI standard (conducted after acquisition, by an unqualified professional, or with inadequate scope) does not confer BFPP status and leaves the developer fully liable. The ongoing obligations are continuing duties, and a developer who acquires BFPP status but then exacerbates the contamination (through construction that spreads it), fails to provide agency access, or violates an institutional control loses the defense. The BFPP status should be documented and the ongoing obligations should be built into the property management, because the defense is asserted after the fact and must be supported by a record of compliant due diligence and ongoing compliance.

### Use Environmental Insurance to Cap Residual and Unknown Risk

Even with a PPA and BFPP status, residual risk remains: contamination that was not discovered in the Phase II and is found during or after construction, third-party claims that are not resolved by the agency agreement, cost overruns on the agreed cleanup, and natural resource damages. The rule is to use environmental insurance to cap this residual and unknown risk, purchasing a policy (pollution legal liability, cost cap, or a combination) that covers the specific risks the other instruments do not address. Environmental insurance is a specialized product, and the policy must be negotiated carefully: the coverage (what contaminants, what media, what claims), the exclusions (known contamination that exceeds a cap, pre-existing conditions not disclosed, ordinary construction risks), the limits and deductibles, the term (often 10 years, but the contamination may persist longer), and the claims process. The cost-cap policy, which caps the remediation cost overruns, is particularly valuable for brownfields where the remediation cost is uncertain, because it converts an open-ended cost into a defined maximum. The insurance decision should be based on the residual risk analysis: what liability remains after the PPA and BFPP, what is the probability and magnitude of each residual risk, and what coverage is available at what cost. Environmental insurance is not a substitute for the other instruments (it does not resolve the agency claim or confer BFPP status), but it is the instrument that addresses the residual risk the others leave, and a brownfield project that relies on PPA and BFPP without insurance bears the unknown-contamination and cost-overrun risk that has bankrupted many redevelopment projects.

### Draft Indemnification and Disclosure to Allocate Risk Between the Parties

The brownfield transaction involves multiple parties — the current owner, the past owner who may have caused the contamination, the developer, the lender, the agency, the insurer — and the risk allocation among them is determined by the indemnification, disclosure, and warranty provisions in the transaction documents. The rule is to draft these provisions deliberately to allocate the risk according to the agreed structure, rather than relying on boilerplate that may not reflect the brownfield-specific allocation. The indemnification should specify who pays for what: does the past owner indemnify the developer for contamination the past owner caused, and up to what limit; does the developer indemnify the current owner for contamination the developer exacerbates; how do the indemnities interact with the insurance and the PPA. The disclosure provisions should ensure that each party discloses what it knows about the contamination, because undisclosed contamination discovered later generates fraud and misrepresentation claims that are separate from the environmental liability. The warranty provisions should be drafted to reflect the brownfield reality (the property is sold "as is" with known contamination, not with warranties of environmental condition), because warranties that are inconsistent with the known condition create liability that the transaction structure was meant to resolve. The transaction documents should be integrated with the PPA, the BFPP documentation, and the insurance, so that the risk allocation is coherent across all instruments, rather than contradictory.

## Common Traps

### Liability Not Mapped Before Structuring the Transfer

The developer proceeds with the transaction without mapping the liability, acquires the site, and discovers that the liability is broader than expected — third-party claims, natural resource damages, contamination from an off-site source — that the transaction structure does not address. The trap is that unmapped liability produces inadequate transfer. The mechanism is that the liability analysis was not done before the transaction, and the instruments selected did not cover the full liability. The false signal is that "the Phase I identified the issues." The harm is that the residual liability is discovered after acquisition, the developer bears it without coverage, the project's economics are undermined, and the disputes among the parties consume years.

### Agency Claim Resolved but Third-Party Liability Left Open

The PPA resolves the agency's cleanup claim, but the third-party claims (adjacent owners, downgradient users) are not addressed, and the developer is sued by a third party for contamination the PPA did not cover. The trap is that resolving one liability source does not resolve all. The mechanism is that the PPA covers only the agency claim, and the third-party risk was not insured or indemnified. The false signal is that "the agency will not sue." The harm is that the third-party claims proceed, the developer bears the defense and any judgment without coverage, and the liability that the PPA was meant to resolve is merely shifted to a different claimant.

### BFPP Defense Lost Through Inadequate Due Diligence or Ongoing Obligations

The developer acquires the site intending to claim BFPP status, but the Phase I was conducted after acquisition or did not meet the AAI standard, or the developer exacerbates the contamination during construction, and the BFPP defense is lost, leaving the developer fully liable. The trap is that BFPP status requires both compliant acquisition and ongoing compliance. The mechanism is that the due diligence was non-compliant or the ongoing obligations were not met. The false signal is that "we did a Phase I." The harm is that the BFPP defense is unavailable, the developer is liable as an owner, the PPA may not provide full protection, and the liability exposure is far greater than the transaction structure anticipated.

### Insurance Purchased Without Matching the Residual Risk

The developer purchases environmental insurance, but the policy's coverage and exclusions do not match the actual residual risk, and when a claim arises (cost overrun, unknown contamination), the claim is excluded or under-limited, and the developer bears the loss. The trap is that insurance that does not match the risk provides false comfort. The mechanism is that the policy was purchased without analyzing the residual risk, and the coverage was not negotiated to address it. The false signal is that "we are insured." The harm is that the claim is denied or underpaid, the developer bears the loss that the insurance was meant to cap, and the project's economics are undermined by the very risk the insurance was purchased to address.

### Transaction Documents with Boilerplate That Contradicts the Risk Allocation

The transaction documents use boilerplate indemnification, disclosure, and warranty provisions that do not reflect the brownfield-specific risk allocation, and the contradictions generate disputes when contamination is discovered or costs overrun. The trap is that boilerplate substitutes for deliberate drafting. The mechanism is that the brownfield-specific provisions were not drafted, and the standard provisions were used without adaptation. The false signal is that "the contract is signed." The harm is that the risk allocation is incoherent, the parties dispute who bears which liability, the litigation consumes years and dollars, and the transaction structure that was meant to resolve the liability instead generates new disputes.

## Self-Check

- Has the liability been mapped comprehensively before the transaction, identifying all potentially responsible parties, regulatory authorities, third-party claimants, and the nature of each claim?
- Does the prospective purchaser agreement (or equivalent) resolve the agency claim, with the cleanup obligation, covenant not to sue, and contribution protection clearly defined?
- Has bona fide prospective purchaser status been secured through compliant AAI due diligence before acquisition, and are the ongoing obligations built into property management?
- Does environmental insurance cover the residual and unknown risk (cost overruns, unknown contamination, third-party claims), with coverage and limits matched to the actual risk profile?
- Are the transaction documents' indemnification, disclosure, and warranty provisions drafted deliberately to allocate risk coherently with the PPA, BFPP, and insurance?
- If contamination is discovered after acquisition that was not in the Phase II, who bears the cost, and is that allocation documented and insured?
- Are the third-party claims (adjacent owners, downgradient users) addressed by insurance or indemnification, or are they left open despite the PPA?
- Would the BFPP defense survive scrutiny — was the Phase I compliant, and are the ongoing obligations being met?
- Does the environmental insurance policy's coverage match the residual risk, or are the exclusions likely to deny the claims that actually arise?
- Are the transaction documents' risk-allocation provisions consistent with each other and with the other instruments, or do contradictions invite dispute?
