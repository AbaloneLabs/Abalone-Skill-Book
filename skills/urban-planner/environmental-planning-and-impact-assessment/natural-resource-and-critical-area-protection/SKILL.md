# Natural Resource and Critical Area Protection

Critical area protection — wetlands, streams, steep slopes, floodplains, fish and wildlife habitat, aquifer recharge areas — is where environmental planning becomes legally binding and physically consequential. The judgment problem is that these resources provide diffuse public benefits (flood storage, water quality, habitat, carbon sequestration) that are invisible until they are lost, while the development pressure on the land they occupy is concentrated, immediate, and politically salient. A planner who applies protection standards rigorously is accused of "taking" the property; one who applies them loosely allows incremental degradation that, parcel by parcel, eliminates the resource and the functions it provided. The skill is in designating protection based on the resource's function and the best available science, applying standards that are defensible and consistent, and recognizing that the cumulative loss of many small encroachments is how critical areas die.

## Core Rules

### Base Protection Designations on Best Available Science and Resource Function

Critical area protection must be grounded in the best available science, not in convenience or political compromise, both because the law requires it in many jurisdictions and because protection that is not science-based will not protect the resource. The rule is to designate critical areas based on documented resource function — what the wetland stores, what the stream supports, what the slope's failure risk is, what the habitat's role is — and to calibrate the protection (buffer width, allowed uses, review standard) to the sensitivity of that function. A blanket buffer applied without regard to resource function is either over-protective (imposing on land that does not need it) or under-protective (failing where the function is most sensitive). The science should be cited and documented so that the protection standard is defensible and so that future amendments can be evaluated against the same evidentiary basis.

### Apply Buffers That Serve Their Stated Function, Not Arbitrary Widths

Buffers — the vegetated setbacks between development and the critical area — are the primary protection tool, and their width should be calibrated to the function they serve: water quality filtration, habitat refuge, bank stabilization, flood conveyance. A buffer too narrow to filter runoff provides a false sense of protection; a buffer designed for water quality may be inadequate for habitat. The rule is to state the buffer's intended function, to size it based on the science for that function, and to allow variation (wider for high-value resources, narrower where conditions allow) only with documented justification. Buffers that are negotiated down to a politically acceptable width without functional basis protect nothing, and they create a precedent that every buffer is negotiable, which erodes the standard across the jurisdiction.

### Evaluate Cumulative Loss, Not Just the Individual Encroachment

The most insidious threat to critical areas is the cumulative loss of many small encroachments, each of which appears minor in isolation. A 500-square-foot buffer reduction on one permit, repeated across hundreds of permits, eliminates the buffer's function across the landscape, even though no single decision was significant. The rule is to evaluate each encroachment against the cumulative loss already permitted in the area, to track the total allowable impact, and to recognize when the resource is approaching the threshold beyond which incremental loss causes functional collapse. A jurisdiction that approves each encroachment in isolation, without tracking the cumulative total, will discover too late that the resource is gone, and the loss will be irreversible because critical areas, once developed, are rarely restored.

### Distinguish Avoidance, Minimization, and Mitigation in Sequence

Environmental protection follows a sequence: avoid the impact first, minimize what cannot be avoided, and mitigate (compensate) for the residual. The rule is to apply this sequence rigorously, not to jump to mitigation as the first response. Avoidance means siting the development outside the critical area or buffer where feasible; minimization means reducing the footprint and impact where some encroachment is unavoidable; mitigation means compensating for the residual through restoration, creation, or preservation elsewhere. The trap is that mitigation is often treated as a fee that buys the right to impact, which inverts the sequence and treats the critical area as developable for a price. Rigorous application of the sequence ensures that mitigation is reserved for genuinely unavoidable impacts, not used to justify avoidable ones.

### Require Mitigation That Is In-Kind, Proximate, and Monitored

When mitigation is required, it should be in-kind (replacing the same resource function lost), proximate (within the same watershed or ecosystem so the lost function is actually replaced where it mattered), durable (protected in perpetuity, not temporary), and monitored (with performance standards and adaptive management). The rule is to scrutinize mitigation proposals against these criteria and to reject mitigation that is out-of-kind, distant, temporary, or unmonitored, because such mitigation does not replace the lost function regardless of what the ratio suggests. Mitigation banking and in-lieu fees can be legitimate where they produce real, durable, in-kind restoration, but they can also become a mechanism for buying the right to destroy a local resource by paying for a distant one, which does not maintain the function at the site of impact.

## Common Traps

### The Buffer Negotiated to a Political Width

The applicant proposes a building that encroaches the buffer, the planning commission is sympathetic, and the buffer is reduced to a width that allows the project. The trap is that the reduced buffer no longer serves its function, but it is presented as a compromise. The mechanism is that political pressure substitutes for functional analysis. The false signal is that "we protected the resource." The harm is that the buffer's function is lost at that site, the precedent invites further reductions, and the cumulative effect across many such compromises eliminates the resource's protective function across the landscape.

### Mitigation as a Fee That Buys the Right to Impact

The applicant pays an in-lieu fee or buys mitigation bank credits, and the encroachment is approved on the theory that the fee compensates. The trap is that the fee may fund restoration elsewhere, but the local function is lost, and the restoration may be out-of-kind, distant, or never completed. The mechanism is that monetary compensation is treated as functional replacement. The false signal is that "the impact is offset." The harm is that the local resource is degraded, the distant compensation may not replace the same function, and the avoidance-minimization sequence is bypassed because the fee makes impact convenient. The remedy is to require the sequence and to scrutinize whether the compensation actually replaces the lost function at the site that matters.

### Approving Encroachments in Isolation Without Cumulative Tracking

Each buffer reduction is evaluated against the individual parcel, found to be minor, and approved, with no tracking of the total encroachment already permitted in the area. The trap is that the cumulative loss is never seen. The mechanism is that each decision is made in isolation. The false signal is that "this encroachment is small." The harm is that the buffer's function is eliminated across the landscape by accretion, the resource collapses, and no single decision is responsible, so the loss is unaccountable. The remedy is to track cumulative impacts and to deny encroachments when the total approaches the functional threshold.

### Treating Avoidance as Infeasible by Default

The avoidance analysis concludes that avoiding the critical area is "infeasible" because the upland portion of the site is already committed to other uses, or because the project's layout favors the critical area. The trap is that avoidance is dismissed without genuine evaluation, often because the applicant's preferred layout requires the encroachment. The mechanism is that feasibility is defined by the applicant's preferences rather than by genuine constraints. The false signal is that "we considered avoidance." The harm is that the avoidance step is bypassed, the sequence collapses to mitigation, and avoidable impacts are treated as unavoidable. The remedy is to require a genuine avoidance analysis that considers alternative site layouts, reduced footprints, and clustering, not just the applicant's preferred plan.

### Protecting the Resource Boundary but Ignoring the Surrounding Watershed

The wetland or stream is protected within its buffer, but the surrounding watershed is developed without regard to the increased runoff, pollutants, and fragmentation that degrade the protected resource from outside its boundary. The trap is that the protected area is degraded by its context. The mechanism is that protection is applied at the parcel rather than the watershed scale. The false signal is that "the critical area is protected." The harm is that the resource's function declines despite the protection, because the inputs it depends on (clean water, stable hydrology, connected habitat) are altered by the surrounding development. The remedy is to plan at the watershed scale, managing the land use that affects the resource, not just the resource itself.

## Self-Check

- Are critical area designations based on documented resource function and best available science, with the science cited and defensible?
- Are buffer widths calibrated to the function they serve (water quality, habitat, bank stability), with documented justification for any variation?
- Are encroachments evaluated against the cumulative loss already permitted in the area, with tracking that prevents functional collapse by accretion?
- Is the avoidance-minimization-mitigation sequence applied rigorously, with avoidance genuinely evaluated before mitigation is considered?
- Is required mitigation in-kind, proximate, durable, and monitored with performance standards, rather than a distant fee that does not replace local function?
- Has the buffer been protected at its functional width, or has it been negotiated to a political width that no longer serves its function?
- Is mitigation reserved for genuinely unavoidable impacts, or is it used as a fee that buys the right to avoidable encroachment?
- Has the cumulative impact of many small encroachments been tracked, or is each approved in isolation?
- Is the avoidance analysis genuine (considering alternative layouts and reduced footprints), or does it dismiss avoidance based on the applicant's preferred plan?
- Is the surrounding watershed managed to protect the resource's inputs, or is the resource protected at its boundary while degraded by its context?
