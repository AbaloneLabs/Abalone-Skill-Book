# Climate and Greenhouse Gas Impact Analysis

Climate impact analysis brings the global, long-term problem of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions into the local, project-level decisions where planners work. The judgment problem is that any single project's emissions are a negligible fraction of the global total, which makes it tempting to dismiss them as insignificant, while the cumulative global problem is built entirely from millions of such local decisions. A climate analysis that treats project-level emissions as too small to matter defeats the decarbonization goal at every decision point; one that demands impossible precision or impossible mitigation paralyzes development without advancing climate goals. The skill is in analyzing emissions at a level of rigor proportional to the decision, connecting project-level analysis to adopted climate goals, and designing mitigation that actually reduces emissions rather than papering over them.

## Core Rules

### Anchor the Analysis in Adopted Targets and the Carbon Budget

A project's GHG emissions are not significant or insignificant in the abstract; they are significant or not relative to the trajectory the jurisdiction has adopted to meet its climate commitments. The rule is to anchor the analysis in the adopted climate action plan, the emissions reduction target, and the remaining carbon budget, and to evaluate whether the project's emissions are consistent with that trajectory. A project that adds emissions above the trajectory is inconsistent with the goal, regardless of whether its individual contribution is "small." This framing defeats the "too small to matter" dismissal, because every project is small relative to the global total, and the global total is the sum of the small contributions. The carbon budget framing also forces honesty about cumulative emissions and the finite remaining atmospheric capacity, which a project-by-project significance test obscures.

### Analyze Both Operational and Embodied Emissions Across the Lifecycle

A complete climate analysis covers the emissions from the project's operation (energy use, transportation, waste) and the embodied emissions from its construction (materials, particularly concrete and steel, and the carbon released by land conversion). The rule is to scope the analysis to the major emissions sources, to use established emission factors and methods, and to be honest about what is and is not included. The most common gap is ignoring embodied emissions, which can be a large share of a building's lifetime total, particularly for materials like concrete. The most common distortion is double-counting or inconsistent boundaries (counting the building's electricity emissions while ignoring the grid's decarbonization trajectory, or vice versa). Documenting the analysis boundary and the methods makes the analysis reproducible and defensible.

### Evaluate Consistency With the Climate Action Plan, Not Just Significance

In jurisdictions with an adopted climate action plan (CAP), the relevant question for a project is often not whether its emissions are "significant" in isolation but whether the project is consistent with the CAP's assumptions and measures. The rule is to evaluate whether the project incorporates the CAP's applicable measures (energy efficiency, electrification, transit-oriented location, VMT reduction, renewable energy), whether its emissions are within the CAP's growth assumptions, and whether it would undermine the CAP's emissions trajectory. A project consistent with an adopted, adequately analyzed CAP can rely on the CAP's programmatic analysis; a project inconsistent with the CAP must provide its own analysis and mitigation. This framing connects the project decision to the plan, rather than treating each project as a free-standing climate question.

### Design Mitigation That Is Real, Additional, and Durable

Climate mitigation should reduce emissions that would otherwise occur (additional), in a verifiable and permanent way (durable), and prioritized at the source (avoidance and efficiency) before offsets. The rule is to scrutinize mitigation hierarchically: first, avoid emissions through location, design, and efficiency; second, reduce through electrification, renewable energy, and mode shift; third, sequester or offset the residual. Offsets — paying for emissions reductions elsewhere — are the weakest mitigation because they are often not additional (the reduction would have happened anyway), not permanent (forests burn, projects are abandoned), and not local (the climate benefit is global but the local co-pollution and land use effects are not addressed). A climate analysis that relies heavily on offsets is buying paper reductions; one that prioritizes on-site reduction is advancing the goal.

### Address Climate Adaptation Alongside Mitigation

Climate impact analysis is incomplete if it addresses only the project's contribution to emissions (mitigation) and ignores the project's exposure to climate impacts (adaptation). The rule is to analyze how sea level rise, increased heat, intensified storms, changed precipitation, wildfire, and other projected climate conditions will affect the project over its design life, and to design for resilience to those conditions. A project built to historical climate baselines in a changing climate will be prematurely obsolete, damaged, or dangerous. The adaptation analysis should use the best available projections (not historical data), consider the project's full design life, and incorporate resilience measures into the design rather than treating climate impacts as a future problem to be managed later.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Project Emissions as Too Small to Matter

The analysis notes that the project's emissions are a tiny fraction of global or even jurisdictional totals, and concludes they are insignificant. The trap is that this reasoning, applied at every project, defeats any project-level climate policy. The mechanism is that significance is evaluated in isolation rather than against the cumulative trajectory. The false signal is that "this project won't affect the climate." The harm is that emissions accumulate, the jurisdiction's climate target is missed, and each project is individually unaccountable for its contribution. The remedy is to evaluate consistency with the trajectory and the carbon budget, not significance in isolation.

### Ignoring Embodied Carbon

The analysis covers operational emissions (energy, transportation) but omits the embodied emissions from construction materials, particularly concrete and steel, which can be a large share of a building's lifetime emissions. The trap is that the analysis understates the project's climate impact. The mechanism is that embodied emissions are harder to quantify and often outside the conventional scope. The false signal is that "the building is efficient." The harm is that the climate impact is understated, mitigation is misdirected toward operational efficiency while the larger embodied emissions are ignored, and the carbon budget is consumed faster than the analysis suggests.

### Relying on Offsets That Are Not Additional or Permanent

The project's residual emissions are "offset" by purchasing credits from a forest, methane capture, or renewable energy project elsewhere, and the analysis concludes the project is carbon neutral. The trap is that the offset may not represent real, additional, permanent reductions. The mechanism is that offset markets are difficult to verify, and many credits fund reductions that would have occurred anyway. The false signal is that "the emissions are offset." The harm is that the project's actual emissions enter the atmosphere, the offset does not cancel them, and the climate goal is undermined while appearing to be met. The remedy is to prioritize on-site reduction and to scrutinize offsets for additionality, permanence, and verifiability.

### Using Historical Climate Baselines for Future Conditions

The adaptation analysis uses historical temperature, precipitation, and sea level data to design for the project's future conditions, ignoring that the climate is changing over the project's design life. The trap is that the project is designed for a climate that no longer exists by the time it is built. The mechanism is that historical data is available and familiar while projections are uncertain. The false signal is that "we designed to the standard." The harm is that the project is prematurely obsolete, vulnerable to conditions it was not designed for, and the cost of retrofit or failure is borne later, often publicly. The remedy is to use the best available projections for the project's design life and to design for resilience.

### Treating the Climate Action Plan as Satisfied by Citation

The project cites the jurisdiction's climate action plan and asserts consistency, without evaluating whether the project actually incorporates the plan's measures or whether its emissions are within the plan's assumptions. The trap is that the CAP is treated as a checkbox rather than a standard. The mechanism is that the citation substitutes for analysis. The false signal is that "the project is consistent with the CAP." The harm is that the project undermines the CAP's trajectory while appearing to comply, the cumulative effect of many such projects defeats the CAP, and the jurisdiction misses its climate target. The remedy is to evaluate consistency substantively, measure by measure.

## Self-Check

- Is the emissions analysis anchored in the adopted climate target and remaining carbon budget, evaluating consistency with the trajectory rather than significance in isolation?
- Does the analysis cover both operational and embodied emissions across the lifecycle, with documented boundaries and methods?
- Is the project evaluated for consistency with the climate action plan's measures and growth assumptions, not just cited as consistent?
- Is mitigation prioritized hierarchically (avoid, reduce, sequester/offset), with offsets scrutinized for additionality, permanence, and verifiability?
- Does the analysis address climate adaptation — exposure to sea level rise, heat, storms, wildfire — using projections for the project's design life, not historical baselines?
- Have I avoided dismissing the project's emissions as too small to matter, recognizing that the global total is the sum of local decisions?
- Are embodied emissions from materials included, or have they been silently omitted to understate the impact?
- Are offsets real and additional, or are they paper reductions that do not cancel the project's actual emissions?
- Is the project designed for projected future climate conditions, or for a historical baseline that will not hold over its design life?
- Would the project, replicated across the jurisdiction, meet or miss the adopted climate target, and does the analysis reflect that cumulative reality?
