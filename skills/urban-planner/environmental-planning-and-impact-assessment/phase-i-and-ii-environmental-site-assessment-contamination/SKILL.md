# Phase I and Phase II Environmental Site Assessment and Contamination

Contamination is the land-use risk that is invisible at the surface and irreversible in its consequences. A parcel can appear clean, carry full zoning entitlements, and pass every site-plan review, yet conceal soil, groundwater, or vapor contamination that transforms a feasible project into a multi-year, multi-million-dollar remediation — or, worse, transfers CERCLA strict, joint-and-several liability to the very owner or municipality that acquired or approved the site without knowledge of the condition. The judgment problem is that environmental site assessments (ESAs) are technical reports produced by consultants under defined standards (ASTM E1527 for Phase I), and planners, attorneys, and reviewing bodies routinely treat the existence of a "clean" Phase I as a warranty of cleanliness when it is in fact a structured inquiry with specific exclusions, opinion-based conclusions, and a shelf life. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether contamination risk is actually understood and allocated before it becomes a liability that cannot be unwound.

## Core Rules

### Read the Phase I for What It Is and Is Not

A Phase I ESA conducted under ASTM E1527 is a non-intrusive, records-and-observation-based inquiry designed to identify recognized environmental conditions (RECs) — that is, the likely presence of hazardous substances or petroleum products under conditions suggesting a release. It is explicitly not a determination that a site is clean, and it does not sample soil, groundwater, or vapor. The rule is to read the Phase I against its scope: confirm it was conducted within the regulatory shelf life (typically one year for the full report, with certain components valid for 180 days or six months for transaction purposes), confirm it followed the current ASTM practice and met all appropriate inquiry (AAI) requirements for CERCLA defenses, and read the conclusions section for the precise language used. A conclusion of "no recognized environmental conditions identified" supports an innocent landowner or bona fide prospective purchaser defense; a conclusion listing RECs, controlled RECs (CRECs), or historical RECs (HRECs) triggers the obligation to investigate further before relying on the site's environmental status. Treating a Phase I as a clean bill of health when it identified RECs, or when it has expired, forfeits the very liability protection the assessment was commissioned to secure.

### Escalate Recognized Environmental Conditions to a Properly Scoped Phase II

When a Phase I identifies RECs, the correct next step is a Phase II ESA — intrusive sampling to confirm or rule out the presence and extent of contamination. The rule is that a Phase II must be scoped to the specific RECs identified and to the contaminants plausibly associated with them (e.g., volatile organics for a former dry cleaner, metals and petroleum hydrocarbons for a former gas station, PFAS for certain industrial or AFFF-using sites), not a generic sampling grid. The scoping decisions — number and location of borings, depth intervals, analytical methods, detection limits — determine whether the Phase II can actually answer the risk question, and a poorly scoped Phase II can return non-detects that reflect sampling design rather than site condition. The planner's role is not to design the sampling but to confirm that the Phase II addresses each REC, that the data quality objectives are stated, and that the results are interpreted by a qualified professional in a report that distinguishes between "no contamination found," "contamination found below regulatory thresholds," and "contamination found requiring reporting and remediation." A Phase II that does not answer the question the Phase I raised is a gap, not a clearance.

### Map Contamination Findings Against the Proposed Land Use and Exposure Pathways

Contamination risk is not an absolute property of the site; it is a function of the contaminant, the exposure pathway, and the receptor — all of which depend on the proposed land use. The same residual soil contamination may be acceptable under a paved commercial use with an engineered cap and vapor barrier, and unacceptable under a residential use with gardens and basements where the exposure pathway is direct. The rule is to evaluate Phase II findings against the specific land use proposed: identify complete exposure pathways (soil contact, groundwater ingestion, vapor intrusion, indoor air), identify the receptors (residents, workers, construction crews, ecological), and determine whether the residual contamination can be managed to a risk-based standard for that use. Risk-based corrective action (RBCA) standards and state voluntary cleanup program (VCP) criteria are typically land-use-specific, and a cleanup goal calibrated to industrial use will not protect a residential receptor. The decision to rezone or approve a contaminated site for a more sensitive use must be accompanied by a remediation strategy adequate to that use, or the approval creates a future exposure the community will have to address.

### Allocate Liability Deliberately Through the Entitlement and Acquisition Structure

CERCLA imposes strict, joint-and-several, and retroactive liability on current and former owners and operators of contaminated property, with limited defenses (bona fide prospective purchaser, innocent landowner, contiguous property owner) that depend on having conducted AAI before acquisition. The rule is that liability allocation must be designed, not left to accident: a municipality acquiring property should complete AAI before taking title and should structure the acquisition (e.g., via foreclosure, eminent domain with care for the CERCLA exemptions, or a brownfield agreement) to preserve defenses; a developer should negotiate indemnities, purchase price adjustments, and potentially state VCP enrollment that caps or clarifies liability; an approving body should avoid actions (such as taking title through tax foreclosure without the statutory exemption) that inadvertently attach liability. The trap is that the land-use decision (rezoning, acquisition, approval) and the liability decision are often handled by different staff and on different timelines, and a planner focused on the entitlement can inadvertently commit the municipality to a liability it did not intend to accept. The rule is to treat contamination liability as a gating issue in any transaction or approval involving a site with known or suspected contamination.

### Coordinate Remediation with the Development Schedule and the Regulatory Pathway

Remediation is not instantaneous and not all remediation pathways fit all development timelines. A state VCP or brownfield agreement may cap liability but impose a remediation schedule that conflicts with a phased development; a federal CERCLA-based cleanup may involve years of negotiation with EPA; an engineered cap requires long-term operation and maintenance (O&M) and an environmental covenant or deed restriction that runs with the land. The rule is to align the remediation pathway with the development schedule and to make the entitlement contingent on the remediation milestones that protect the future receptors: for example, condition occupancy on completion of the vapor mitigation system, condition the residential phase on achieving residential-use cleanup goals, and record the institutional and engineering controls before the first certificate of occupancy. A development that proceeds ahead of its remediation, or that relies on controls that were never recorded, creates an exposure the approval was supposed to prevent.

## Common Traps

### Treating a "Clean" Phase I as a Warranty of Site Condition

The Phase I returns "no RECs identified," and the project proceeds on the assumption that the site is confirmed clean. The trap mechanism is that a Phase I is a non-intrusive records and observation inquiry with defined exclusions — it does not sample, it relies on the completeness of historical records, and its conclusion is a professional opinion, not a guarantee. The false signal is the word "clean" attached to a report that never tested anything. The harm is that subsurface contamination from an unrecorded release, an off-site plume migrating onto the property, or a contaminant not within the standard scope (such as asbestos in building materials, lead in soil, mold, radon, or emerging contaminants like PFAS) remains undiscovered until construction excavation, vapor intrusion complaints, or a future transaction — at which point the owner has acquired CERCLA liability the Phase I was supposed to defend against. The corrective is to understand the Phase I's exclusions and to commission supplemental inquiries (asbestos surveys, Phase II sampling, vapor assessments) where the proposed use or site history warrants, rather than treating the Phase I as comprehensive.

### Relying on an Expired Phase I for Transaction Defenses

A Phase I conducted three years ago is cited in a current transaction or approval as evidence of clean status. The trap mechanism is that the CERCLA defenses and the ASTM standard impose specific shelf lives — the full Phase I is generally valid for one year, and certain components (interviews, records review, site reconnaissance, lien search) must be updated within 180 days to support a bona fide prospective purchaser defense. The false signal is that a Phase I exists in the file and was once valid. The harm is that the expired report no longer supports the liability defense it was commissioned for, so the acquirer or approver loses the protection and may inherit strict liability for a condition that arose or was discovered after the report's date. The corrective is to verify the report date against the regulatory shelf life before relying on it and to commission an update where the report has aged out.

### Mis-Scoping the Phase II to the Wrong Contaminants or Locations

A Phase II is commissioned for a site with a former dry cleaner, and the sampling focuses on metals and total petroleum hydrocarbons because those are the lab's standard suite, missing the chlorinated solvents (PCE, TCE) that are the actual contaminant of concern. The trap mechanism is that Phase II scope is only as good as the conceptual site model driving it, and a generic or copy-paste scope will sample the wrong analytes, the wrong depths, or the wrong locations. The false signal is a set of non-detect results that looks like clearance. The harm is that the actual plume goes undetected, the project proceeds, and the contamination is discovered later during construction or, worse, through vapor intrusion into the completed building — a far more expensive and potentially health-significant failure. The corrective is to confirm the Phase II scope is derived from the Phase I RECs and a defensible conceptual site model, targeting the contaminants plausibly associated with the identified historical uses.

### Approving a Sensitive Use Without a Use-Adequate Remediation Strategy

A contaminated industrial site is rezoned to residential and approved with a cleanup to industrial risk-based standards, on the reasoning that any cleanup is better than none. The trap mechanism is that risk-based cleanup goals are land-use-specific, and a cleanup adequate for a paved commercial use with no soil contact is wholly inadequate for a residential use with gardens, basements, and child receptors. The false signal is that a remediation was completed and a no-further-action letter issued. The harm is that the residential receptors are exposed to residual contamination that the industrial cleanup left in place, the environmental covenant may prohibit the very residential use that was approved, and the municipality faces future liability and political crisis when the exposure surfaces. The corrective is to require that the remediation strategy and cleanup goals be calibrated to the most sensitive approved use, and that institutional and engineering controls be recorded and consistent with the entitlement before occupancy.

## Self-Check

- Was the Phase I conducted under the current ASTM E1527 practice and within the regulatory shelf life (one year full, 180 days for transaction components) before being relied upon?
- Does the Phase I conclusion clearly distinguish among no RECs, RECs, CRECs, and HRECs, and have any identified RECs been escalated to a properly scoped Phase II?
- Is the Phase II scope derived from the Phase I RECs and a conceptual site model, targeting the specific contaminants and locations plausibly associated with the site's historical uses?
- Have Phase II findings been evaluated against the complete exposure pathways and receptors for the proposed land use, not against an abstract clean-or-contaminated binary?
- Are the cleanup goals calibrated to the most sensitive approved use, such that residential approval is supported by residential-use remediation rather than industrial-use standards?
- If CERCLA or state liability is in play, has all appropriate inquiry been completed before acquisition, and has the acquisition structure preserved the bona fide prospective purchaser or innocent landowner defense?
- Has the remediation pathway (VCP, brownfield agreement, federal cleanup) been aligned with the development schedule, with occupancy and phase approvals contingent on the relevant remediation milestones?
- Have institutional controls (deed restrictions, environmental covenants) and engineering controls (caps, vapor barriers, O&M plans) been recorded before the first certificate of occupancy?
- Are contaminants outside the standard Phase I scope — asbestos, lead, mold, radon, PFAS, vapor intrusion — addressed by supplemental inquiries where site history or proposed use warrants?
- Does the approval or transaction record reflect a deliberate liability allocation, rather than an inadvertent assumption of strict liability through an unexamined acquisition or entitlement action?
