# Demographic and Population Projection

Population projections are the foundation that land use, housing, and infrastructure plans rest on, and because they look mathematical, they are often treated as predictions when they are actually conditional scenarios. The judgment problem is not how to "calculate the future population" but how to choose a projection method appropriate to the horizon and data, to build scenarios that capture genuine uncertainty, to avoid the false precision that turns a range into a false target, and to connect the projection to the decisions it is meant to inform. Planners commonly fail by relying on a single extrapolated trend, by ignoring demographic components (aging, household formation, migration) that drive change, by failing to reconcile local projections with regional and state forecasts, or by using projections to justify predetermined conclusions rather than to test them. Sound projection practice makes a plan's growth assumptions transparent and defensible.

## Core Rules

### Match the Method to the Horizon and the Decision

Different projection methods suit different horizons and purposes. Short-horizon projections (5-10 years) can rely on trend extrapolation and recent building-permit data because momentum dominates. Medium horizons (10-20 years, the typical comprehensive plan horizon) require cohort-component or structural models that account for aging, births, deaths, migration, and household formation, because demographic composition drives change. Long horizons (20-50 years) are inherently speculative and should be presented as scenarios, not forecasts. Match the method's sophistication to the decision's stakes: a sewer capacity decision that locks in infrastructure for 50 years deserves a more rigorous and scenario-based projection than a 5-year parks plan. State the method, its assumptions, and why it fits the purpose.

### Build Scenarios That Capture Real Uncertainty, Not a Single Point Forecast

A single population number for the plan horizon hides the uncertainty that is the most important thing for decision-makers to understand. Build at least three scenarios — a low, a central, and a high — with different assumptions about migration, household size, employment growth, and policy constraints, and explain what conditions would produce each. The spread between low and high is itself a finding: if the range is wide, the plan must be robust to very different futures, which argues for phased, adaptive strategies rather than fixed commitments. A point forecast presented as the future invites plans that fail when reality falls at either end of the range.

### Reconcile Local Projections with Regional, State, and Neighboring Jurisdictions

A jurisdiction's projection does not exist in isolation; it must reconcile with the regional forecast (from the MPO or council of governments), the state demographer, and neighboring jurisdictions, because migration between jurisdictions is a zero-sum component of regional growth. If your projection assumes your city captures a disproportionate share of regional growth, examine whether that is plausible given trends, infrastructure, and policy — or whether it reflects optimism. Document the relationship between the local and regional forecasts, and where they diverge, explain why. Unreconciled projections produce plans whose growth assumptions cannot coexist with neighbors' plans, which is especially problematic for shared infrastructure and housing allocation.

### Decompose Change into Demographic Components, Not Just Total Trends

Total population change is the product of natural increase (births minus deaths) and net migration, and each component has different drivers and different implications. A growing city driven by natural increase (young families) has different housing, school, and service needs than one driven by in-migration of retirees or young professionals. A shrinking city losing population to out-migration has different challenges than one losing population to aging and mortality. Decompose the projection into its components and household-formation dynamics (household size, tenure, age structure), because the composition of change shapes the plan's housing, school, transportation, and service recommendations more than the total does.

### Connect the Projection Explicitly to Plan Decisions

A projection is only useful if it drives decisions: how much housing land to allocate, what size sewer pipe to build, how many school seats to plan, what transportation capacity to provide. Trace the projection through to these decisions, showing the per-capita or per-household demand factors and the resulting facility needs. Make the chain of reasoning explicit and testable, so that if the projection is revised, the decisions can be revisited. A projection that sits in a plan's introduction but does not visibly drive the land use, housing, and infrastructure recommendations is decoration, not analysis.

### Update Projections and Revisit Plans as Reality Unfolds

Projections are wrong the moment they are made; the question is how wrong, and in which direction. Build monitoring into the plan: track actual population, permits, and migration against the projected trajectory, and establish triggers for plan amendment if reality diverges beyond a threshold. Treat the projection as a hypothesis to be tested, not a fixed truth, and design the plan to be adaptable to the range of outcomes. Plans that assume a single future and never check become obsolete or misleading within a few years.

## Common Traps

### Relying on a Single Trend Extrapolation

Projecting a straight line from past growth into the future ignores demographic transitions, market cycles, and policy changes, and produces a single number that conceals uncertainty. The false signal is a clean, mathematical forecast; the harm is plans built on a future that is unlikely to unfold as projected. Use structural or scenario methods for plan-horizon projections, and present ranges.

### False Precision in Point Forecasts

Presenting a 2045 population of "142,300" implies an accuracy the method cannot deliver and turns a scenario into a false target that drives over- or under-building. The false signal is authoritative specificity; the harm is decisions calibrated to a number that will be wrong. Round generously, present ranges, and describe the uncertainty so decision-makers understand the projection is conditional.

### Ignoring Demographic Composition

Focusing on total population while ignoring age structure, household size, tenure, and migration drivers misses the factors that actually determine housing, school, and service demand. The false signal is "we projected the population"; the harm is recommendations mismatched to who the residents actually are. Decompose the projection into components and household dynamics.

### Failing to Reconcile with Regional Forecasts

A local projection that assumes growth inconsistent with the regional forecast, without explaining the divergence, produces plans whose assumptions conflict with neighbors' and with shared infrastructure planning. The false signal is a self-consistent local forecast; the harm is plans that cannot be implemented because the regional growth does not materialize as assumed. Reconcile and document the relationship to regional and state forecasts.

### Using the Projection to Justify a Predetermined Conclusion

Selecting the high-growth scenario to justify expansive land allocations or infrastructure, or the low-growth scenario to resist housing requirements, uses the projection as advocacy rather than analysis. The false signal is a data-driven rationale; the harm is decisions distorted to serve a preferred outcome, with the projection providing cover. Present the full range of scenarios and let the plan's strategy be robust across them, rather than cherry-picking the convenient one.

## Self-Check

- Did you choose a projection method whose sophistication matches the horizon and the stakes of the decisions it informs?
- Did you build multiple scenarios (low, central, high) that capture genuine uncertainty, rather than presenting a single point forecast?
- Did you reconcile the local projection with regional, state, and neighboring jurisdiction forecasts, and document the relationship and any divergence?
- Did you decompose the projection into demographic components (natural increase, migration, aging, household formation) rather than relying on total trends?
- Is the projection explicitly connected to plan decisions — housing land, infrastructure capacity, school seats, transportation — through documented demand factors?
- Did you build monitoring and adaptation triggers into the plan so it can be revisited as actual growth diverges from the projection?
- Did you avoid relying on a single trend extrapolation for a plan-horizon forecast?
- Did you avoid false precision by presenting ranges and describing uncertainty rather than exact point estimates?
- Did you avoid selecting the convenient scenario to justify a predetermined conclusion, instead building a strategy robust across the range?
- Could a reviewer trace the projection from its data and method, through its scenarios and components, to the specific plan decisions it drives — and find the chain defensible?
