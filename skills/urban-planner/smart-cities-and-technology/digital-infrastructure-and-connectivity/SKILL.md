# Digital Infrastructure and Connectivity

Digital infrastructure — broadband, fiber, small-cell wireless, public Wi-Fi, and the conduit and facilities that carry them — is increasingly treated as essential public infrastructure, comparable to roads and water. The judgment problem is that most of this infrastructure is owned and operated by private carriers under franchise and right-of-way agreements the public controls only at the margins, and the planning decisions that determine who gets served, at what speed, at what price, and with what resilience are made through procurement, franchise negotiation, and siting review that planners frequently treat as routine rather than as the consequential public-policy choices they are. A city can approve a thousand small cells, sign a franchise that locks in a decade of terms, and deploy public Wi-Fi that captures no usage data — each appearing as a routine permit — while the equity, resilience, aesthetic, and competitive consequences accumulate invisibly. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a connectivity strategy will serve the public interest or merely accommodate the carriers'.

## Core Rules

### Define the Public-Policy Goals Before the Technology Procurement

Connectivity strategies fail when they begin from the technology ("we need a fiber ring," "we should deploy small cells") rather than from the public-policy goals the technology is meant to serve. Define the goals first: universal affordable access at defined speed tiers, redundancy and resilience for emergency and essential services, competition to restrain prices, digital equity for underserved populations, smart-city platform capacity for municipal operations. Each goal implies different infrastructure, different ownership models (municipal, private, public-private partnership, open access), and different regulatory and procurement strategies. A strategy that begins from the technology will deliver the technology and miss the goals, because the carriers' incentives (maximizing return on their capital) align with coverage of profitable areas and do not align with universal affordable access, resilience, or equity. State the goals, map each to an instrument, and evaluate every procurement and franchise against whether it advances them.

### Treat Broadband as Essential Infrastructure Requiring Equity Analysis

Broadband is now essential for employment, education, health, government access, and civic participation, and its absence is a meaningful deprivation, not merely an inconvenience. Treat broadband planning with the equity analysis appropriate to essential infrastructure: map availability, speed, price, and adoption (not just coverage — adoption lags coverage in low-income communities due to affordability and digital literacy) against income, age, language, and tenure; identify the unserved and underserved; and design interventions (municipal or cooperative broadband, open-access networks, subsidy programs, digital navigation and device access) targeted to the gaps. Resist the carrier's framing that coverage equals service, because a household with a wire to the curb at a price it cannot afford is not served. The equity analysis should drive the strategy, and the strategy should be measured by adoption among the underserved, not by miles of fiber deployed.

### Negotiate Franchise and Right-of-Way Agreements for Public Value

The franchise agreements, master license agreements, and right-of-way use authorizations that govern carriers' use of public property are among the most consequential and least scrutinized instruments in municipal planning. They determine the compensation the public receives for private use of the right-of-way, the build-out obligations (whether the carrier must serve underserved areas), the aesthetic and siting standards for facilities, the data the carrier must share, the duration and renewal terms, and the public's enforcement remedies. Negotiate these agreements for public value, not for administrative convenience: require build-out to underserved areas as a condition of the franchise, secure fair compensation indexed to inflation, retain authority over siting and aesthetics, require data sharing for coverage and performance, and limit duration so terms can be revisited as technology and market conditions change. Engage telecommunications counsel for every negotiation, because the carriers negotiate these daily and the city negotiates them once a decade.

### Site Telecommunications Facilities for Function, Safety, and Aesthetic Compatibility

Small cells, macro towers, distributed antenna systems, and related facilities are sited in the public right-of-way and on public and private property, and their siting determines both network function and community impact. Site facilities to serve the coverage and capacity need (which requires understanding the network engineering, not just the carrier's preferred locations), to avoid concentration in any single neighborhood or near sensitive uses (schools, daycares, residences) beyond what the engineering requires, and to integrate aesthetically with the streetscape (concealment, collocation on existing poles, design standards for new poles). Comply with the federal constraints on local authority over wireless siting (the Telecommunications Act and FCC shot-clock rules limit what municipalities can regulate and how long they can take), while exercising the authority that remains (aesthetics, spacing, safety, structural, and undergrounding requirements) consistently and defensibly. A siting program that is either too permissive (rubber-stamping carrier applications) or too restrictive (denying beyond legal authority and inviting preemption) fails the public interest.

### Build Resilience and Redundancy Into the Connectivity Strategy

Connectivity infrastructure is increasingly critical to emergency response, public safety, healthcare, and economic continuity, and its failure during a disaster (power loss, fiber cut, facility damage) can cascade into broader system failures. Build resilience into the strategy: require diverse routing and physical redundancy for critical fiber paths, backup power for essential facilities, interoperability across carriers and technologies so that no single carrier's failure isolates the city, and coordination with the emergency operations plan so that connectivity restoration is prioritized for essential services. Recognize that private carriers optimize their networks for typical conditions and profit, not for the city's disaster resilience, and that the public's resilience needs may require municipal investment in redundant infrastructure the market will not provide. A connectivity strategy without a resilience analysis is a strategy that will fail at the moment it is most needed.

### Govern Public Wi-Fi and Municipal Networks for Use, Equity, and Privacy

Public Wi-Fi, municipal fiber, and municipal networks are deployed to serve access and equity goals, and their value depends on whether they are governed for those goals. Design public Wi-Fi for actual use (coverage where underserved residents are, simple connection, adequate speed for real tasks, not just token coverage in downtowns and parks), measure adoption and usage by demographics to confirm equity outcomes, and govern the data collected (minimize collection, prohibit commercial reuse, protect user privacy) so that the network does not become a surveillance platform. For municipal fiber and networks, define the ownership, operations, and open-access model that best serves the goals (municipal operation, dark-lease to multiple carriers, public-private partnership), and resist the assumption that municipal ownership is always the answer or that private partnership is always the answer — the model should follow the goals and the jurisdiction's capacity.

## Common Traps

### Coverage Treated as Service

The strategy reports broadband coverage (the percentage of addresses with a wire or signal available) as its success metric, and the metric looks strong, while adoption (the percentage of households actually subscribing and using the service) lags far behind in low-income communities due to affordability. The trap mechanism is that coverage is easy to measure and flattering to the carriers and the city, while adoption reveals the affordability gap that coverage conceals, and measuring adoption requires household-level data the carriers do not voluntarily share. The false signal is high coverage percentages. The harm is that the strategy declares success while a substantial share of residents remain effectively unconnected, the digital divide persists behind a coverage statistic, and the interventions that would close it (affordability, devices, digital literacy) go unfunded because the problem appears solved.

### The Franchise Signed for Administrative Convenience

The franchise or master license agreement is negotiated under time pressure or by staff without telecommunications expertise, accepted largely as the carrier presents it, and locks the city into a decade or more of terms that under-compensate the public, impose no build-out obligations, cede siting control, and provide no data. The trap mechanism is that the agreement is routine to the carrier (who negotiates it daily) and novel to the city (who negotiates it rarely), and the asymmetry produces terms that serve the carrier unless the city invests in expertise. The false signal is that an agreement was reached and the network will be built. The harm is a decade of foregone public value, unserved areas the carrier has no obligation to reach, aesthetic and siting impacts the city cannot control, and a renewal cycle that starts from the unfavorable baseline.

### Siting That Concentrates or Over-Permits

The city applies minimal siting review and approves small cells and facilities wherever the carrier proposes, producing concentration in some neighborhoods (often lower-income or commercial corridors with less political resistance), facilities near sensitive uses beyond what the engineering requires, and an aesthetically degraded streetscape. The trap mechanism is that minimal review feels neutral and defers to the carrier's engineering, but it shifts the burden of the infrastructure onto the communities least able to resist and forgoes the aesthetic and safety authority the city retains. The false signal is efficient permitting and rapid deployment. The harm is inequitable distribution of the infrastructure's visual and perceived health impacts, a degraded public realm, and the loss of the leverage that careful siting review provides for better overall outcomes.

### Resilience Assumed Because the Network Exists

The city relies on private carrier networks for emergency communications, public safety, and essential services, on the assumption that the networks are resilient because they exist and are modern, without confirming the redundancy, backup power, and disaster-recovery commitments the city's actual needs require. The trap mechanism is that carriers design for typical conditions and profit, and their resilience investments reflect their business risk, not the city's public-safety risk, so the gap is invisible until a disaster. The false signal is modern, extensive network coverage. The harm is that during the disaster the network fails at the points of greatest need, essential services lose connectivity, and the city discovers — too late — that the resilience it assumed was never contractually committed or independently verified.

## Self-Check

- Does the strategy begin from defined public-policy goals (universal affordable access, resilience, competition, equity, municipal capacity) rather than from a technology deployment?
- Is broadband treated as essential infrastructure with an equity analysis mapping availability, speed, price, and adoption against income, age, language, and tenure?
- Are franchise and right-of-way agreements negotiated for public value — build-out obligations, fair compensation, siting and aesthetic control, data sharing, limited duration — with telecommunications counsel?
- Are telecommunications facilities sited for function, equitable distribution, sensitive-use protection, and aesthetic integration, within the limits of federal preemption?
- Does the strategy include a resilience and redundancy analysis for critical fiber paths, backup power, carrier interoperability, and emergency operations coordination?
- For public Wi-Fi and municipal networks, are deployment, adoption, and privacy governed for use and equity — with adoption measured by demographics and data collection minimized?
- Am I measuring adoption among the underserved, not just coverage, and adjusting the strategy when the two diverge?
- Have I confirmed, contractually and technically, that the resilience the city's emergency services depend on is actually committed and verifiable, not merely assumed from the network's existence?
