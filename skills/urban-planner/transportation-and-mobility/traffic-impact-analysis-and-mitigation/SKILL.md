# Traffic Impact Analysis and Mitigation

A traffic impact analysis (TIA), also called a traffic impact study (TIS), is the technical document that translates a development's trip generation into measurable effects on the surrounding network, and it is the basis on which a reviewing body decides whether the network can absorb the project, what mitigation is required, and whether the project meets concurrency or adequacy-of-public-facilities requirements. The judgment problem is that a TIA is only as honest as its scoping assumptions — the trip generation rates, the pass-by and internal capture reductions, the background growth, the study area, and the horizon year — and these assumptions are typically supplied or strongly influenced by the applicant's consultant, with the result that a TIA can be engineered to minimize the apparent impact long before any vehicle moves. A planner who reviews the TIA's conclusions without auditing its inputs is approving a number, not an analysis. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the TIA actually characterizes the impact and whether the mitigation actually addresses it, or whether both are artifacts of favorable assumptions.

## Core Rules

### Negotiate the Scope of Study Before the Analysis Begins, Not After

The most consequential decisions in a TIA are made at scoping, before any modeling: the study area boundaries, the analysis scenarios (existing, background, build, post-build), the horizon year, the peak hours, the trip generation methodology, the reductions claimed, and the intersections and links to be analyzed. Once these are set, the conclusions follow almost mechanically, and a TIA scoped to a small study area with aggressive reductions and a short horizon will show minimal impact regardless of the project's true effect. The rule is to require a written scope of study, agreed by the reviewing agency before fieldwork and modeling begin, that defines each of these elements and the rationale for each. The scope should err toward a study area large enough to capture the project's actual travelshed, a horizon year that captures buildout and a reasonable background-growth period, and analysis scenarios that isolate the project's incremental contribution from background and cumulative conditions. A TIA conducted under an unaudited scope is a consultant's deliverable, not a public-interest analysis.

### Audit the Trip Generation and Reduction Assumptions Line by Line

Trip generation is where a TIA's impact estimate is most sensitive to assumption choice, and it is where applicant-driven optimism most often enters. The rule is to audit each input: the generation rates or equations (typically from ITE Trip Generation, with attention to whether average rates or fitted-curve equations are appropriate and whether the data set is substantial or sparse for the land use), the reductions claimed for pass-by trips (trips already on the network that divert to the site), diverted linked trips, internal capture within a mixed-use site, travel demand management (TDM) measures, and mode split. Each reduction must be justified against the specific site context and against the methodology permitted by the reviewing agency; default maximum reductions (often capped, for example, at 10 to 20 percent for pass-by) exist precisely because unbounded reductions can drive the impact estimate to near zero. The trap is that reductions compound multiplicatively, so a 20 percent pass-by reduction combined with a 25 percent internal-capture reduction and a 15 percent TDM reduction removes nearly half the gross trips, and each must stand on its own evidence. A TIA whose reductions are not individually justified is reporting an engineered number.

### Choose the Right Performance Metric: Level of Service, Vehicle-Miles-Traveled, or Both

The performance metric determines what counts as an impact, and the choice between level of service (LOS, an intersection or link delay-based measure of auto congestion) and vehicle-miles-traveled (VMT, a measure of total auto travel and a proxy for environmental and induced-demand effects) reflects a policy decision about what the network is being protected for. LOS-based review protects auto throughput and can block compact, infill, transit-served development that generates VMT-reducing travel patterns; VMT-based review, increasingly required under state policy, captures the systemic and environmental effects that LOS ignores but requires different analytical tools (often a travel demand model rather than intersection analysis). The rule is to confirm which metric governs in the jurisdiction, whether both apply, and how conflicts between them are resolved — for example, a project that fails LOS but passes VMT in a VMT-priority jurisdiction may be approvable with a statement of overriding consideration, while the same project in an LOS-priority jurisdiction may be blocked. Applying the wrong metric, or applying both without a conflict-resolution rule, produces findings that do not answer the governing policy question.

### Define Impact Thresholds and Mitigation Triggers Explicitly

A TIA reports effects; the approval decision depends on whether those effects exceed thresholds that trigger mitigation, denial, or concurrency failure. The rule is to define the thresholds explicitly and in advance: what change in LOS (often a letter grade or a volume-to-capacity increment) constitutes a significant impact, at what point background-plus-project conditions fail the adopted LOS standard, what triggers a fair-share contribution versus full construction of an improvement, and what the concurrency or adequacy requirement is and whether the project meets it. Where thresholds are undefined, the mitigation negotiation becomes ad hoc, and similarly situated projects receive different requirements depending on negotiation skill rather than policy — which is both unfair and a legal vulnerability. The rule is to apply pre-committed thresholds consistently, to document the basis for any deviation, and to ensure that mitigation is proportional to the project's incremental contribution rather than assigned to solve pre-existing deficiencies the project did not cause.

### Tie Mitigation to the Identified Impact and Verify It Actually Addresses It

Mitigation must be causally connected to the impact the TIA identified, must be feasible and funded, and must be in place by the time the impact occurs. The rule is to evaluate each proposed mitigation against the impact it claims to address: a turn-lane addition that restores the pre-project LOS at the affected intersection is targeted mitigation; a contribution to a distant future interchange that may never be built is not. The review must also confirm that the mitigation is constructible (right-of-way, environmental clearance, agency agreement), that it is funded and scheduled to coincide with the project's impact, and that it does not simply shift the problem downstream or to another mode (for example, a turn lane that improves auto LOS but degrades pedestrian crossing safety). Where the impact cannot be fully mitigated on the network, the honest finding is that the project's residual impact is unmitigated, and the approval decision must then rest on an explicit policy choice (a statement of overriding consideration, a concurrency exception, or denial) rather than on a pretense that the mitigation resolved it.

## Common Traps

### Accepting the TIA's Conclusions Without Auditing the Inputs

The TIA concludes "no significant impact," the executive summary is clean, and the review proceeds on the conclusion without examining the trip generation, reductions, study area, and horizon year that produced it. The trap mechanism is that the conclusion is a deterministic output of the inputs, and the inputs were set at scoping under applicant influence, so a favorable conclusion can reflect favorable assumptions rather than a benign project. The false signal is the clean executive summary and the professional formatting. The harm is that the project is approved on an engineered impact estimate, the network experiences impacts the TIA understated, and the resulting congestion is then attributed to "unexpected growth" rather than to the unaudited analysis that enabled it. The corrective is to audit the inputs line by line and to require the scoping agreement in writing before analysis begins.

### Stacking Reductions to Drive Trip Generation Toward Zero

The TIA applies a pass-by reduction, an internal-capture reduction, a TDM reduction, and a transit mode-split reduction, each presented as standard, and the combined effect removes a large fraction of the gross trips. The trap mechanism is that each reduction may be individually defensible at its default value but the compounding is multiplicative and the combined reduction often far exceeds what the site context supports, especially where the reductions assume conditions (robust transit, a genuinely mixed-use internal capture, an enforced TDM program) that the project does not actually deliver. The false signal is that each reduction cites a methodology source. The harm is that the impact estimate is materially understated, the network receives trips the analysis said would not occur, and the mitigation — sized to the reduced trip estimate — is inadequate. The corrective is to cap combined reductions, require evidence for each beyond the default, and condition any TDM-based reduction on a recorded and enforceable TDM program.

### Using LOS to Block Infill and VMT-Reducing Development

A compact, mixed-use, transit-served infill project generates a TIA that fails the intersection LOS standard, and the project is denied or heavily mitigated with auto-capacity improvements that undermine its walkable character. The trap mechanism is that LOS measures auto delay in isolation and penalizes exactly the location-efficient development that reduces total VMT, while a greenfield project on an uncongested highway can pass LOS review despite generating far more total travel and emissions. The false signal is the LOS failure, which reads as an objective deficiency. The harm is the systematic suppression of infill and the channeling of growth toward auto-dependent peripheries — the opposite of the community's stated sustainability goals — plus the addition of turn lanes and widenings that further degrade the walkable environment. The corrective is to apply VMT as the governing or co-equal metric in jurisdictions that have adopted it, to allow LOS exceedances in designated infill and transit areas where VMT is favorable, and to avoid auto-capacity mitigation that defeats the project's mode-shift intent.

### Assigning Mitigation That Is Unfunded, Unschedule, or Disconnected From the Impact

The TIA identifies an impact, the conditions require a fair-share contribution to a future interchange or a list of improvements, and the approval is granted. The trap mechanism is that the listed improvements may lack right-of-way, environmental clearance, design, funding from other sources, or a construction schedule, so the contribution is collected but the improvement is never built, and the impact materializes unmitigated. The false signal is that mitigation was "required" as a condition. The harm is that the network degrades, the public attributes the congestion to growth the project "paid to mitigate," and the collected funds sit unused or are diverted. The corrective is to require that mitigation be feasible and funded, scheduled to coincide with the impact, and causally connected to the identified effect, and to treat unfunded future improvements as non-mitigation requiring an explicit overriding-consideration finding.

## Self-Check

- Was a written scope of study — defining study area, scenarios, horizon year, peak hours, trip generation method, and reductions — agreed by the reviewing agency before modeling began?
- Have the trip generation rates and equations been audited for appropriateness to the land use and data sufficiency, rather than accepted from the executive summary?
- Is each reduction (pass-by, internal capture, TDM, mode split) individually justified with site-specific evidence, and is the combined reduction capped at a defensible level?
- Does the TIA apply the governing performance metric (LOS, VMT, or both) consistent with current jurisdictional policy, with a conflict-resolution rule where both apply?
- Are the impact thresholds and mitigation triggers defined explicitly and applied consistently across similarly situated projects?
- Is each required mitigation causally connected to an identified impact, feasible, funded, and scheduled to coincide with the impact?
- Where mitigation cannot fully address the impact, is the residual impact acknowledged and the approval based on an explicit policy finding rather than a pretense of full mitigation?
- Does the mitigation avoid degrading other modes — for example, does a turn-lane addition preserve pedestrian crossing safety and bike facility continuity?
- If TDM-based reductions were claimed, is there a recorded and enforceable TDM program that will actually deliver the assumed mode shift?
- Has the background growth and cumulative scenario accounted for other pending and planned developments, so the project's incremental contribution is measured against realistic future conditions rather than today's network?
