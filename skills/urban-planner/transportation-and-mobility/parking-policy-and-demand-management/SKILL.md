# Parking Policy and Demand Management

Parking policy is the silent determinant of urban form, housing affordability, and mode share. Off-street parking requirements inflate housing costs, consume land that could be housing or commerce, and subsidize driving in ways that undercut every other transportation goal. Abundant free parking creates the congestion, sprawl, and emissions that transportation plans claim to reduce. The judgment problem is that parking is experienced acutely by drivers (who notice every shortage) and its costs are diffuse and invisible (buried in rent, in prices, in public subsidies), so the political pressure always runs toward more parking, while the analytical answer almost always runs toward less and better-managed parking. The planner's role is to surface the true costs of parking, to manage demand rather than endlessly supply it, and to resist the default that every trip must arrive by car and store for free.

## Core Rules

### Treat Parking Minimums as a Policy Choice With Documented Costs

Off-street parking minimums are not a neutral engineering standard; they are a policy decision to require each use to supply enough parking to satisfy peak free demand, and they impose documented costs: a surface space adds tens of thousands of dollars to a housing unit's cost, a structured space far more; parking lots consume land that could be housing or commerce; required parking renders small-lot and infill development infeasible. The rule is to treat parking minimums as a choice that must be justified against its costs, not as a default. Where the goal is housing affordability, walkability, or mode shift, the minimums should be reduced or eliminated, and the consequences managed through shared parking, on-street management, and transportation demand management. Eliminating minimums does not ban parking; it lets the market supply what each project needs rather than mandating a uniform oversupply.

### Use Shared Parking and Demand Management Before Building More Supply

Most parking supply is used inefficiently: office parking sits empty at night, residential parking sits empty by day, and each use is required to supply its own peak as if the others did not exist. Shared parking — using one supply to serve multiple uses with complementary peaks — can dramatically reduce the total supply needed. The rule is to analyze the actual demand profile of a district (by time of day, day of week, and use) and to manage the shared supply through pricing, permits, and wayfinding, before building additional structured or surface parking. A parking management district that prices curb parking by demand, shares off-street supply, and provides real-time information can serve the same demand with a fraction of the supply that minimums would require, at far lower cost and land consumption.

### Apply Performance Pricing to Curb Parking

Free or underpriced curb parking creates cruising for spaces (a significant share of downtown traffic), congestion, and a shortage that is read as "not enough parking" when the actual problem is mispricing. Donald Shoup's principle — price curb parking by demand, at the lowest rate that maintains one or two open spaces per block — eliminates cruising, ensures availability, and generates revenue that can fund district improvements. The rule is to set curb prices to maintain a target availability (typically 85% occupancy), to vary them by time and location as demand changes, and to dedicate the revenue to the district where it is collected, building political support. Performance pricing is the single most effective parking policy because it aligns the price with the demand and removes the shortage signal that drives overbuilding.

### Implement Transportation Demand Management as a System

Transportation demand management (TDM) reduces vehicle trips through strategies like transit passes, bike facilities, carshare, flexible work, and priced parking, applied to employers, developments, and districts. The rule is to implement TDM as a system with measurable trip-reduction targets, not as a menu of optional amenities. A development that offers a transit pass and a bike room but also supplies abundant free parking will see no mode shift, because the parking subsidy overwhelms the alternatives. Effective TDM pairs the incentives for alternatives (passes, facilities, information) with the disincentives for driving (priced or limited parking), measures the actual trip generation, and adjusts. TDM without measurement is unverifiable; TDM paired with free parking is ineffective.

### Evaluate the Cumulative Effect of Parking on Housing and Mode Share

Each parking requirement and each parking investment should be evaluated for its cumulative effect on housing costs and mode share, not just for its effect on the individual site. A city that requires two spaces per unit across hundreds of developments has silently added hundreds of millions of dollars to housing costs and subsidized driving across the entire market, contradicting its affordability and climate goals. The rule is to aggregate the effect: how much does the parking policy add to housing costs citywide, how many vehicle trips does it induce, and how does that compare to the stated goals? Surfacing the cumulative cost changes the policy conversation from "do we have enough parking at this site" to "is our parking policy consistent with our housing and climate goals."

## Common Traps

### Reading Shortage as a Supply Problem

A neighborhood reports "not enough parking," and the response is to require more parking or build a structure. The trap is that the shortage is almost always a pricing and management problem, not a supply problem. The mechanism is that free or underpriced parking generates demand that exceeds any reasonable supply, and the shortage persists regardless of how much is built. The false signal is that "we need more parking." The harm is that the new supply is expensive, consumes land, induces more driving (which refills the new supply), and the shortage reappears, because the underlying pricing was never addressed.

### Requiring Each Use to Supply Its Own Peak

The code requires each use to supply parking for its own peak demand, ignoring that adjacent uses have complementary peaks (office by day, residential by night). The trap is that the requirement produces massive oversupply when the uses are aggregated. The mechanism is that the requirement is calibrated to isolated, single-use development. The false signal is that "each use needs its parking." The harm is that the district is awash in underused parking, land is consumed, housing is more expensive, and the walkable, mixed-use character the plan sought is defeated by the parking lots the code required.

### Subsidizing Parking While Pursuing Mode Shift

The city adopts a mode-shift goal, invests in transit and bike infrastructure, and simultaneously requires abundant parking and prices it at zero. The trap is that the parking subsidy overwhelms the mode-shift investments. The mechanism is that free parking is a powerful inducement to drive, and travelers rationally choose the subsidized mode. The false signal is that "we're investing in alternatives." The harm is that mode share does not shift, the transit and bike investments underperform, and the city concludes that "people just want to drive here," when the actual cause is the parking policy that made driving the rational choice.

### Treating Curb Parking as Free Public Space

Curb parking is treated as a free public amenity, and any proposal to price it is met with opposition. The trap is that free curb parking is the most expensive parking policy a city can pursue, because it generates cruising, congestion, emissions, and the perception of shortage that drives overbuilding. The mechanism is that the costs of free curb parking are diffuse and invisible, while the price is visible and concentrated on the driver. The false signal is that "free parking is a public benefit." The harm is that the city suffers congestion and emissions from cruising, builds unnecessary parking structures to address the perceived shortage, and forgoes the revenue that could fund district improvements, all to preserve a subsidy that benefits drivers at the expense of the city's stated goals.

### Ignoring the Housing Cost of Parking Requirements

A parking minimum is adopted or retained without considering its effect on housing affordability. The trap is that each required space adds tens of thousands of dollars to a unit's cost, pricing households out of the market and rendering small-scale and infill development infeasible. The mechanism is that the cost is buried in rent and prices rather than paid at the parking space. The false signal is that "parking requirements are a technical standard." The harm is that housing is more expensive than it needs to be, the housing that gets built is biased toward larger projects that can absorb the parking cost, and the city's affordability goals are silently contradicted by its parking code.

## Self-Check

- Have I treated parking minimums as a policy choice with documented costs, rather than as a neutral default, and justified them against their effect on housing, land use, and mode share?
- Before recommending new parking supply, have I analyzed shared parking potential and demand management options that could serve the same demand with less supply?
- Is curb parking priced to maintain target availability (around 85% occupancy), with revenue dedicated to the district, rather than provided free?
- Is transportation demand management implemented as a system with trip-reduction targets and paired with priced or limited parking, not just optional amenities alongside free parking?
- Have I evaluated the cumulative effect of the parking policy on housing costs and mode share citywide, not just the effect on individual sites?
- When a shortage is reported, have I diagnosed whether it is a pricing and management problem before concluding it is a supply problem?
- Have I allowed shared parking across uses with complementary peaks, rather than requiring each use to supply its own peak?
- Are the city's parking requirements and investments consistent with its affordability, climate, and mode-share goals, or do they silently contradict them?
- Is curb parking managed as scarce, valuable public space, rather than treated as a free amenity whose costs are ignored?
- Would removing or reducing a parking requirement lower housing costs and enable development without creating an unmanaged shortage?
