# Multimodal Corridor and Street Design

Streets are the most permanent and most contested public space in a city, and the cross-section decisions made for a corridor lock in tradeoffs for generations. Every foot of right-of-way allocated to one mode is taken from another: a wider travel lane or a turn lane means a narrower sidewalk, a removed bike lane, or a lost planting strip. The judgment problem is that vehicle throughput is measurable in the short term and politically salient, while the harms of auto-oriented design — pedestrian deaths, suppressed cycling and transit, degraded public space, induced demand — accumulate slowly and diffusely. A planner who optimizes for the measurable (delay at the intersection) will systematically underweight the things that matter most (whether the street is safe to cross on foot). The skill is in making the right-of-way allocation explicit, defending it against the default of moving cars, and designing for the street's full function as a place, not just a pipe.

## Core Rules

### Start From the Street's Context and Function, Not From the Traffic Count

The first question is not "how much traffic does this street carry?" but "what is this street for, and what context does it pass through?" A street through a dense downtown, a residential neighborhood, a school zone, and a commercial strip are different design problems even at the same traffic volume, because the users, the speeds appropriate to the context, and the tradeoffs among modes differ. The rule is to designate the street's functional and contextual classification — a downtown main street, a neighborhood connector, an arterial through a mixed-use corridor — and to design the cross-section to serve that function, recognizing that a design calibrated purely to vehicle throughput will produce a street hostile to every other use. Context-sensitive design means the same traffic volume yields different streets in different places.

### Make Right-of-Way Allocation an Explicit Tradeoff, Not a Default

The most important analytical move is to surface the right-of-way allocation as a decision rather than letting it default to the existing or the vehicle-serving configuration. A 100-foot right-of-way can be allocated many ways: four wide travel lanes and minimal sidewalks; two travel lanes, center turn lane, bike lanes, generous sidewalks, and a planting strip; transit lanes and a median. Each allocation serves different users and produces different outcomes for safety, mode share, and land value. The rule is to present the allocation as alternatives with explicit tradeoffs — what each mode gains and loses, what the safety and mode-share implications are — so that the decision is made consciously rather than by inertia. The default of "preserve vehicle level of service" is a decision; making it explicit allows it to be challenged.

### Apply the Safe System Approach and Target Speed

The Safe System approach, now the prevailing paradigm in traffic safety, holds that humans will make mistakes and that the street system must be designed so those mistakes are not fatal. The central operational tool is target speed: the planner decides what speed is appropriate for the context (often 20-25 mph in dense urban areas, 30 mph on arterials with pedestrian activity), and designs the street so that drivers naturally travel at that speed, through lane width, intersection geometry, horizontal and vertical deflection, and friction from parking, trees, and buildings. The trap of conventional design is that wide lanes, generous curves, and cleared sight lines are "safer" by the old standard (more forgiving of driver error) but induce speeds that are lethal to pedestrians. The rule is to design for the target speed, accepting that this often means narrowing lanes and adding friction that conventional engineering resists.

### Design for the Network, Not Just the Corridor

A corridor redesign does not exist in isolation; it shifts traffic, redirects trips, and changes the function of adjacent streets. A "road diet" that removes through-lanes on one corridor may push traffic to parallel streets; a new bike lane that ends at a hostile intersection will not be used; a transit priority measure that is not sustained through the corridor's signals will not save time. The rule is to design the corridor within its network: consider diversion to parallel routes, ensure the multimodal facilities connect to a usable network rather than terminating at gaps, and coordinate signal timing and turn restrictions across the corridor so that the design functions as a system. A corridor designed without network awareness will either fail (the bike lane is unused because it leads nowhere) or create unanticipated problems elsewhere (diverted traffic degrades a residential street).

### Use Level of Service Honestly and Acknowledge Induced Demand

Vehicle level of service (LOS) is a legitimate engineering measure, but it is often misused as the sole decision criterion, and it carries hidden assumptions. The rule is to use LOS as one input among several, to apply it to all modes (multimodal LOS) where data allow, and to acknowledge induced demand: widening a road to reduce congestion in the short term generates additional trips that refill the road, so the long-term congestion relief is often negligible while the induced vehicle miles traveled, emissions, and sprawl are real. Conversely, reducing road capacity (road diets) often produces less diversion and congestion than predicted, because trips redistribute across modes, routes, and times. Honest use of LOS means reporting it transparently, not letting it silently veto multimodal improvements, and pairing it with safety, mode-share, and equity measures that capture what LOS misses.

## Common Traps

### Optimizing for Vehicle Level of Service as the Default

The traffic study reports that a proposed bike lane will increase vehicle delay from LOS B to LOS D at the peak hour, and the project is killed or the bike lane is removed. The trap is that LOS is treated as a veto over every other consideration, even though the project's purpose was to improve cycling safety and mode share. The mechanism is that LOS is quantified and the multimodal benefits are not, so the quantified measure dominates. The false signal is that "the project fails the traffic test." The harm is that streets are perpetually optimized for the mode the city claims to want less of, multimodal goals are never realized, and the city's adopted complete streets policy is contradicted by its engineering decisions.

### Designing for the Peak Hour and Ignoring the Other 23

The cross-section is sized to accommodate peak-hour vehicle demand, producing wide lanes and multiple through-lanes that are empty the other 23 hours, and that encourage speeding and degrade the pedestrian environment all day. The trap is that peak-hour design overbuilds for a condition that exists briefly. The mechanism is that the peak hour is the binding constraint in conventional analysis. The false signal is that "we have to handle the rush hour." The harm is that the street is hostile to its primary users (the people who live, work, and walk there) for the entire day, to serve a brief peak, and the overbuilt cross-section induces the very peak demand it was designed to accommodate.

### Terminating Facilities at Gaps

A bike lane is installed for most of a corridor but ends a block short of a major destination, or a sidewalk improvement stops at the municipal boundary. The trap is that the facility is effectively unusable because the gap is exactly where the danger concentrates. The mechanism is that the network, not the segment, determines whether a facility is used. The false signal is that "we built the bike lane." The harm is that the investment produces no mode shift (cyclists will not ride a lane that dumps them into a hostile intersection), the safety benefit is not realized, and the project is cited as evidence that "bike infrastructure doesn't work here."

### Ignoring Induced Demand in Capacity Analysis

A road widening is justified by a forecast showing reduced congestion, but the forecast assumes traffic volume stays constant when capacity increases, ignoring that wider roads generate more and longer trips. The trap is that the forecast is internally consistent but based on a false assumption. The mechanism is that conventional travel demand models often underrepresent induced demand. The false signal is that "the widening will solve congestion." The harm is that the congestion returns within a few years (induced demand refills the road), the public investment is wasted, and the induced vehicle miles traveled increase emissions and sprawl, contradicting the city's other goals.

### Treating Pedestrian and Bicycle Facilities as Add-Ons

After the vehicle lanes are sized, the remaining right-of-way is allocated to "complete streets" amenities — a narrow sidewalk, a striped bike lane — without reconsidering whether the vehicle allocation was appropriate. The trap is that multimodal design is treated as a residual rather than a co-equal input. The mechanism is that the vehicle cross-section is designed first and the rest fits in the margins. The false signal is that "we provided for all modes." The harm is that the multimodal facilities are substandard (a 4-foot sidewalk against a curb, a bike lane in the door zone), they are not used, and the project is presented as a complete streets success despite failing its multimodal intent.

## Self-Check

- Did I start from the street's context and intended function, rather than from the traffic count, and does the design serve that function?
- Have I presented the right-of-way allocation as explicit alternatives with tradeoffs, rather than defaulting to the existing or vehicle-serving configuration?
- Is the design calibrated to a target speed appropriate to the context, with geometry (lane width, curves, friction) that induces that speed, rather than to "forgiving" highway standards?
- Have I considered the network effects — diversion to parallel streets, gaps in the multimodal network, signal coordination — not just the corridor in isolation?
- Am I using vehicle level of service as one input among several, and have I acknowledged induced demand rather than assuming capacity expansion relieves congestion?
- Have I sized the cross-section for the full day and the primary users, not just the peak hour and through-traffic?
- Do the pedestrian and bicycle facilities connect to a usable network, or do they terminate at gaps that render them unusable?
- Are the multimodal facilities designed to standard, not fitted into the residual right-of-way after vehicle lanes are sized?
- Have I reported safety, mode-share, and equity measures alongside LOS, so the decision considers what LOS misses?
- Would a person walking, cycling, or taking transit through this corridor experience it as safe and continuous, or only as a vehicle corridor with token amenities?
