# Active Transportation, Bike and Pedestrian Network Planning

Active transportation — walking, cycling, and other human-powered mobility — is the mode that every transit trip, every parking strategy, and every complete-streets policy depends on at its first and last leg, and it is the mode most consistently underdesigned because it is treated as an amenity rather than as a network. The judgment problem is that bike and pedestrian facilities are usually evaluated project-by-project and segment-by-segment — a bike lane here, a sidewalk gap closure there — while their actual usefulness is a network property determined by connectivity, continuity, stress, and destination access, none of which a single segment can deliver alone. A community can spend millions on disjointed facilities and still have a place where no one rides or walks, because the segments do not connect, terminate at barriers, or force users into high-stress gaps. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether an active-transportation plan builds a usable network or accumulates disconnected infrastructure.

## Core Rules

### Design at the Network Level, Not the Segment Level

The unit of analysis for active transportation is the network, because a facility's value is determined by where it connects to, not by its length. A bike lane that runs for eight blocks and terminates at a high-speed arterial without a crossing does not transport anyone; a sidewalk that ends at a drainage ditch strands the pedestrian who used it. The rule is to map the existing and proposed facilities as a network and to evaluate each project by its contribution to connectivity: does it close a gap, cross a barrier (a river, a highway, a rail line), connect origins to destinations (residential to schools, retail, transit, employment), and complete a continuous low-stress route? Network-level analysis tools — level of traffic stress (LTS), connectivity indices, and isochrone-based access measures — quantify whether the system actually gets users where they need to go. A capital plan that lists segments without a network map is a list of projects, not a transportation plan, and it will produce the disconnected outcome it cannot see.

### Select the Facility Typology by Stress and User Population, Not by Available Right-of-Way

The choice among facility typologies — shared lanes (sharrows), conventional bike lanes, buffered bike lanes, protected (separated) bike lanes, shared-use paths, and pedestrian treatments — is the central design decision, and it must be driven by the target user population and the traffic stress of the corridor, not by what fits in the residual right-of-way after auto capacity is preserved. The rule is to apply the "all ages and abilities" standard: if the network is intended to serve the broad population (children, older adults, less confident riders), the facility must achieve low stress (LTS 1 or 2), which on a corridor with significant speed or volume requires separation — a protected bike lane or a path, not a painted lane. Conventional and shared-lane facilities serve only the confident "strong and fearless" and "enthused and confident" rider populations, which are a small minority; designing to them and calling the result a bike network excludes the majority of potential riders. The trap is selecting a lower-separation typology to avoid removing a travel lane or parking, then presenting it as adequate; the rule is to let the user-population and stress analysis drive the typology and to treat the right-of-way tradeoff as a separate, explicit decision.

### Solve the Barriers and the Gaps, Not Just the Easy Corridors

The highest-value active-transportation investments are almost always the hardest ones: crossings of major barriers (arterials, highways, waterways, rail corridors), intersections that break an otherwise continuous route, and the one- or two-block gaps that sever an otherwise usable network. The rule is to prioritize barrier crossings and gap closures over easy mileage, because a mile of pleasant trail along a greenway adds recreational value, while a single bridge or underpass across a highway can transform the connectivity of an entire district. The corollary is that the network's quality is set by its weakest link: a route that is low-stress for two miles and high-stress for one block is, for the target user population, a high-stress route. The review of an active-transportation plan should ask where the barriers and gaps are, whether the plan addresses them, and whether the funding prioritization reflects network value rather than ease of construction.

### Integrate With Transit, Land Use, and the First-and-Last-Mile Function

Active transportation does not compete with transit; it feeds it. Most transit trips begin and end as walk or bike trips, and the quality of the walk and bike access to the stop is often the binding constraint on transit ridership. The rule is to integrate active-transportation planning with transit access: map the walk- and bike-sheds around transit stations, identify the gaps and barriers within them, and prioritize the facilities that extend the effective catchment of the station. The same integration applies to land use: a network that connects residential areas to schools, retail, and employment generates utilitarian trips (the trips that justify transportation investment), while a network built only along greenways generates primarily recreational trips. The land-use and active-transportation plans must be designed together, because a walkable network in an auto-oriented land-use pattern has no destinations to walk to, and a mixed-use land-use pattern without a walkable network has destinations no one can reach without driving.

### Maintain Continuity of Treatment Through Intersections and Conflict Points

Intersections are where bike and pedestrian networks fail, not where they succeed, because the conflict with turning vehicles is concentrated exactly where the facility's protection typically ends. The rule is to design the intersection treatment with the same standard of protection as the midblock facility: protected intersections, dedicated signal phases, leading pedestrian intervals, bike signal heads, raised crossings, and corner refuge islands that maintain the separation through the conflict zone. A protected bike lane that drops to a shared lane at the intersection, or a sidewalk that channels pedestrians into a right-turn slip lane, has sacrificed the user at the point of highest risk. The review must trace each route through its intersections and confirm that the treatment is continuous, because a network evaluated only midblock will appear complete while failing at every crossing.

## Common Traps

### Building Disjointed Segments and Calling It a Network

The capital plan adds bike lanes wherever a street is being repaved, accumulating mileage over years, and the result is reported as a growing bike network. The trap mechanism is that segment-by-segment accumulation produces facilities that do not connect, terminate at barriers, or switch typologies unpredictably, so no continuous low-stress route exists despite substantial total mileage. The false signal is the rising mileage figure, which looks like progress. The harm is that the facilities are unused by the target population (because they do not go anywhere useful at low stress), the ridership data confirms low demand (justifying further underinvestment), and the community concludes that "people here just don't bike" when the actual cause is the disconnected network. The corrective is to plan and evaluate at the network level and to prioritize connectivity over mileage.

### Selecting the Facility Typology to Preserve Auto Capacity

The corridor analysis shows that a protected bike lane is needed to serve all-ages-and-abilities users, but the design is downgraded to a painted bike lane (or sharrows, or no facility) to avoid removing a travel lane or on-street parking. The trap mechanism is that the auto-capacity preservation is presented as a neutral engineering constraint, when it is actually a policy choice to prioritize auto throughput over the stated active-transportation goal, and the downgraded facility serves only the small confident-rider population. The false signal is that a bike facility was "provided." The harm is that the corridor remains high-stress, the majority of potential riders are excluded, and the investment produces neither ridership nor safety gains, reinforcing the belief that bike facilities do not work. The corrective is to let the user-population and stress analysis drive the typology and to make the right-of-way tradeoff an explicit, documented policy decision rather than a default.

### Terminating the Facility at the Barrier

A bike lane or sidewalk is built along a corridor and ends where the corridor meets a highway, a river, or a high-speed arterial, with no crossing treatment. The trap mechanism is that barriers are exactly where facilities are hardest and most expensive to continue, so they are the points most often deferred, and a facility that is excellent for its length and useless at its terminus is useless for transportation. The false signal is the completed segment, which photographs well and adds to the mileage total. The harm is that users who began the route discover it does not connect, the facility's utilitarian value is zero, and the barrier continues to sever the communities on either side. The corrective is to prioritize barrier crossings and to treat a facility that does not cross its barriers as incomplete regardless of its midblock quality.

### Dropping Protection at the Intersection

A protected bike lane runs the length of the corridor and merges into a shared lane or a right-turn lane at each intersection, on the assumption that bikes and cars can negotiate the conflict. The trap mechanism is that intersections concentrate exactly the conflict the midblock separation was designed to prevent, and dropping the protection at the conflict point protects the user at the safe location and abandons them at the dangerous one. The false signal is that the corridor has a protected facility. The harm is that crashes concentrate at the unprotected intersections, the safety record of the "protected" corridor disappoints, and the design is cited as evidence that separation does not improve safety. The corrective is to require continuous treatment through intersections — protected intersections, dedicated phases, leading intervals — evaluated at the same standard as the midblock facility.

## Self-Check

- Is the plan evaluated at the network level — with connectivity, barrier crossings, and destination access analyzed — rather than as a list of segment projects?
- Does the facility typology selection serve the all-ages-and-abilities user population, achieving low traffic stress (LTS 1 or 2) on corridors with significant speed or volume through separation rather than paint?
- Where a lower-separation typology was chosen, is the auto-capacity or parking preservation documented as an explicit policy tradeoff rather than presented as a neutral engineering constraint?
- Does the plan prioritize barrier crossings (highways, rivers, rail, arterials) and gap closures over easy mileage, recognizing that network value is set by the weakest link?
- Is each continuous route traced through its intersections, with protection maintained through the conflict zones rather than dropped at them?
- Are walk- and bike-sheds around transit stations mapped, with gaps and barriers within them prioritized for the first-and-last-mile function?
- Does the active-transportation network connect residential areas to actual destinations (schools, retail, employment, transit), supporting utilitarian trips rather than only recreational ones?
- Are pedestrian facilities — sidewalks, crossings, accessibility for people with disabilities — designed to the same network and continuity standard as the bikeways, not treated as residual?
- Have accessibility requirements (PROWAG or equivalent: curb ramps, detectable warnings, crossing times, accessible pedestrian signals) been integrated, not added as an afterthought?
- Does the funding prioritization reflect network connectivity value, or does it reflect ease of construction and available right-of-way?
