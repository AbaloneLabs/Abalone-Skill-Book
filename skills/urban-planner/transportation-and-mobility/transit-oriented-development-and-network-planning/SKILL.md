# Transit-Oriented Development and Network Planning

Transit-oriented development (TOD) is the deliberate coordination of land use and transit investment so that each reinforces the other: transit provides the access that supports higher density, and the density provides the ridership that supports the transit. The judgment problem is that the two sides are often planned separately — the transit agency builds the line, the city zones the land, and the result is a station surrounded by surface parking and low-density zoning that produces too few riders to justify the service, or housing built beyond walking distance of a station whose access is hostile to pedestrians. TOD succeeds only when the land use, the station design, the access network, and the service are planned as an integrated system, and when the planner confronts the political reality that the density required for transit success is often the density the surrounding neighborhood opposes.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate Density and Mix to the Transit Service Level

Different transit modes and service levels require different minimum densities and land use mixes to succeed. A frequent rail or bus rapid transit line can support and justify high-density, mixed-use development; an infrequent local bus route cannot, and zoning it for high density produces development that does not materialize or transit that is underused. The rule is to match the designated intensity to the transit service: identify the service level (frequency, span, speed, mode, connectivity to the regional network), determine the density and mix that service can support and that will in turn support it, and zone accordingly. Mismatch in either direction fails: too little density for the transit wastes the investment; too much density for weak transit produces empty buildings and disillusionment with TOD. The calibration should be documented so that the relationship between service and intensity is explicit and defensible.

### Design the Walkshed, Not Just the Station

A transit station's value is determined by what is within walking distance of it, and the walk is shaped by the street network, the block size, the street design, and the barriers (highways, arterials, parking lots, walls). A station with a half-mile radius drawn on a map may have an actual walkshed of a quarter-mile because superblocks, hostile arterials, and surface parking make the walk circuitous or unpleasant. The rule is to design and improve the pedestrian access network — fine-grained street connections, safe crossings of arterials, mid-block passages through large parcels, complete sidewalks, and active ground-floor uses along the walk — so that the actual walkshed approaches the theoretical one. A station with a great map radius and a hostile walkshed will underperform; a station with a modest radius and an excellent walk will outperform it.

### Reduce and Right-Size Parking to Enable Transit-Supportive Density

Abundant parking and TOD are in direct conflict: parking consumes the land and lowers the density that transit needs, and it makes driving convenient enough to undercut transit ridership. The rule is to reduce or eliminate parking minimums in station areas, to manage the parking that remains (shared, priced, unbundled), and to recognize that parking requirements are often the binding constraint on TOD feasibility — a project that works financially at 0.5 parking spaces per unit may be infeasible at 1.5. The political difficulty is that neighbors fear spillover parking, and the remedy is management (permit parking, pricing) rather than requiring each development to oversupply parking. Right-sizing parking is not anti-car; it is pro-transit, because transit cannot succeed if driving and parking are systematically subsidized and convenient at the station door.

### Plan the Phasing of Land Use and Transit Together

TOD is a long-term proposition, and the phasing of land use and transit must be coordinated. Opening high-capacity transit before the ridership exists requires operating subsidies and risks service cuts; zoning for TOD before the transit arrives requires developers to build for a future that may not materialize, suppressing investment. The rule is to align the phasing: plan the land use changes and the infrastructure (including the pedestrian network and the station) to mature together, use interim strategies (temporary uses, surface parking designed for future redevelopment, phased upzoning) to bridge the gap, and be honest about the timeline. A TOD plan that assumes instant transformation sets up disappointment; one that sequences the changes realistically builds the ridership and the development over time.

### Address Displacement and Equity Explicitly

TOD increases land value, which can displace exactly the lower-income residents who most depend on transit, producing a perverse outcome where transit investment benefits higher-income, lower-ridership residents while displacing the transit-dependent. The rule is to address displacement and equity explicitly in the TOD plan: include affordable housing requirements or incentives in station areas, preserve existing affordable housing, invest in anti-displacement strategies alongside the transit, and ensure that the access improvements (sidewalks, crossings, bike facilities) serve the existing community, not just new development. TOD that ignores equity will be challenged, will lose political support, and will fail to deliver the ridership and access benefits that justify public investment.

## Common Traps

### Zoning for TOD Without the Transit

The city upzones around a station in anticipation of future transit, but the transit is delayed, downgraded, or never built, and the upzoning produces no development or development that is auto-oriented because there is no transit to orient to. The trap is that land use and transit were planned separately and the linkage was assumed. The mechanism is that the upzoning precedes the service without a credible commitment. The false signal is that "we're planning for the future." The harm is that the upzoning is either wasted (no development) or produces development that contradicts the TOD intent, and when the transit finally arrives the station area is already built out at the wrong pattern.

### Building the Transit Without the Land Use

The transit line is built, but the station-area zoning remains low-density, parking is required, and the surrounding neighborhood opposes change, so the line opens to low ridership and is cited as a failure. The trap is that the transit investment was not matched by the land use changes that produce ridership. The mechanism is that the transit agency and the city operated independently. The false signal is that "transit doesn't work here." The harm is that the public investment is wasted, the service is vulnerable to cuts, and the region concludes that transit is infeasible, when the failure was in the land use coordination, not the transit.

### Drawing the Half-Mile Radius and Ignoring the Walk

The TOD plan shows a half-mile circle around the station and counts the parcels within it, but the actual walk is blocked by a highway, a river, a superblock, or a sea of surface parking, and the effective walkshed is a fraction of the circle. The trap is that the map overstates the accessible area. The mechanism is that the plan analyzed the radius rather than the network. The false signal is that "the station has a large catchment." The harm is that ridership projections based on the circle are too high, the development is built but underperforms, and the access barriers that actually limit the station are never addressed.

### Requiring Parking That Undercuts the TOD

The city upzones for TOD density but retains conventional parking minimums, and the resulting projects are feasible only with large parking structures that consume the density the upzoning allowed and make driving convenient enough to suppress ridership. The trap is that parking minimums silently defeat the TOD intent. The mechanism is that the binding constraint on density and mode share is parking, not zoning. The false signal is that "we upzoned for TOD." The harm is that the development is less dense than zoned, ridership is lower than projected, and the station area is dominated by parking structures rather than the active uses that would support the transit and the street life.

### Ignoring Displacement Until It Happens

The TOD plan focuses on attracting development and ridership, treats existing residents as obstacles or ignores them, and discovers years later that the transit investment has displaced the transit-dependent population and the station area is now higher-income and lower-ridership than before. The trap is that equity was treated as a secondary concern. The mechanism is that land value appreciation was not paired with preservation and affordability strategies. The false signal is that "the neighborhood is improving." The harm is that the transit investment produced the opposite of its access intent, the displaced residents are worse off, and the ridership justifying the investment is undermined.

## Self-Check

- Is the designated station-area density and mix calibrated to the actual transit service level (frequency, mode, connectivity), with the relationship documented?
- Have I analyzed and improved the actual pedestrian walkshed — the network, block size, crossings, barriers — rather than relying on a half-mile radius on a map?
- Have parking minimums been reduced or eliminated in the station area, and is remaining parking managed (shared, priced, unbundled) to avoid undercutting ridership?
- Are the land use changes and the transit investment phased together, with interim strategies to bridge the gap?
- Does the plan include explicit anti-displacement and affordable housing strategies, so the transit investment benefits rather than displaces the transit-dependent?
- If the transit is not yet built, is there a credible commitment and timeline, or is the upzoning speculative?
- If the transit is built, is the zoning aligned to produce the ridership, or does it remain at a pattern that will underperform?
- Have I addressed the access barriers (highways, superblocks, parking lots) that actually limit the walkshed, not just the parcels within the radius?
- Is parking right-sized to the TOD intent, or do minimums silently defeat the density and mode share the plan envisions?
- Would an existing lower-income resident near the station be better off after the TOD, or at risk of displacement, and have I planned accordingly?
