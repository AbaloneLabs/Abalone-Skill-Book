# Park Master Planning

A park master plan is the document that translates a community's recreation needs into specific land, facilities, and capital commitments, and it is also one of the most common sites of a particular planning failure: the plan that produces amenities the community cannot afford to operate, that serves the users who attended the meetings rather than the users who need the park, and that treats parkland as a generic commodity rather than as a specific ecological and social setting. The judgment problem is that park planning is dominated by level-of-service standards (acres per thousand residents) and by aspirational amenity lists that feel responsive but are detached from operating budgets, demographic reality, and the site's actual capacity to support the planned uses. A park built to such a plan looks successful at ribbon-cutting and declines steadily thereafter, because the maintenance and programming the plan assumed never materialized. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a park master plan will produce usable, durable, equitably distributed recreation assets or a catalog of under-maintained liabilities.

## Core Rules

### Ground the Needs Assessment in Demographics, Use Patterns, and Equity, Not National Averages

The starting point of most park plans is a level-of-service standard (typically 10 acres per 1,000 residents, derived from mid-twentieth-century NRPA guidance) applied to the population to derive an acreage deficit. This approach is almost always misleading, because it treats all residents as having identical needs, ignores the actual distribution of existing parks relative to where people live, and converts a complex question (what recreation does this community lack?) into a single number that drives land acquisition regardless of whether the land serves a need. Replace or supplement the per-capita standard with: a demographic analysis identifying age cohorts, family structures, and cultural recreation preferences; a park-equity analysis mapping park access (typically walking distance, ten minutes or a half-mile) against population density, income, and car ownership to identify underserved areas; and a use-pattern survey of existing parks to identify what is over- and under-used. The needs assessment should explain what specific recreation gaps exist where, for whom — not how many acres the formula demands.

### Calibrate the Program of Facilities to Operating Capacity From the Outset

The most consequential and most ignored question in park planning is: who will maintain and program this, and with what budget? Every facility added to the plan — a pool, a splash pad, a dog park, a community garden, sports fields with lights — carries a perpetual operating cost (staffing, utilities, supplies, lifecycle replacement) that is typically two to five times its capital cost over a twenty-year horizon, and park departments are routinely understaffed relative to the assets they already hold. Build the operating budget into the plan from the first facility decision: for each proposed facility, estimate annual operating cost, identify the funding source (general fund, enterprise fund, partnership, revenue generation), and confirm the department's capacity to deliver it. Facilities without an identified operating source become maintenance burdens that drag down the whole system. A plan that adds amenities without addressing operations is planning for decline.

### Balance Programmed and Unprogrammed Space to the Community's Actual Mix

Park plans tend to over-allocate to programmed, specialized facilities (sports fields, courts, dedicated dog areas, splash pads) because organized user groups advocate loudly for them, and to under-allocate to flexible, unprogrammed open space (lawns, shade, walking paths, informal gathering areas) because no single constituency demands it. Yet unprogrammed space is what serves the widest range of users across ages, cultures, and seasons, and it is what residents most often report wanting when asked open-endedly. Calibrate the programmed-to-unprogrammed ratio to the community's actual mix: inventory existing programmed capacity and avoid duplicating it; reserve generous flexible space for the uses that emerge organically (picnicking, informal play, resting, cultural events); and resist the pressure to fill every acre with a dedicated facility, because dedicated facilities serve their users only and exclude everyone else during their use. The most-used parks are typically the ones with the most flexible space.

### Site Parks for Equitable Walking Access, Not Just Available Land

The distribution of parkland is often inequitable, concentrated in wealthier, lower-density neighborhoods and absent in denser, lower-income areas where residents are most dependent on walking and transit access to green space. A park master plan's siting decisions are the primary lever for correcting this, and they should be driven by an access analysis: where do residents live, who lacks a ten-minute walk to a park, and where are the acquisition and redevelopment opportunities to close the gap? Prioritize acquisition in underserved, high-density, low-car-access areas even when land is more expensive or requires creative assembly (school joint-use, street conversions, vacant-lot activation), because a park where it is cheap but no one can walk to it does not serve equity. Treat park access as a measurable equity outcome and report it alongside the acreage total, because the acreage total can rise while access inequity worsens.

### Design to the Site's Ecology, Hydrology, and Cultural Context

Each park site has specific ecological, hydrological, and cultural conditions that should shape its design, and plans that ignore them produce parks that fight the site — requiring irrigation where the site is dry, drainage infrastructure where the site is wet, removal of the mature trees that would have given the park immediate character, and erasure of the historical or cultural meaning the site holds for its neighbors. Require a site assessment (soils, hydrology, existing vegetation, wildlife, contaminated soils, cultural resources, neighborhood context) before programming the design, and design the facilities, circulation, and planting to fit the site rather than to impose a standard park template. Preserve mature canopy and healthy soils as primary assets, manage stormwater on-site as a design feature rather than piping it away, and engage the site's cultural and historical meaning with the adjacent community. A park designed to its site costs less to build and operate and is more valued than one imposed on it.

### Sequence Capital, Operations, and Stewardship With Measurable Benchmarks

The implementation chapter should sequence capital investment, operations funding, and community stewardship with measurable benchmarks tied to the equity and needs-assessment findings. Prioritize the investments that close the most significant access and needs gaps first, rather than the most visible or politically supported projects; pair each capital project with its operating-funding commitment; and build a stewardship and programming partnership strategy (friends-of groups, school joint-use, nonprofit operators) that sustains the park after the capital ribbon-cutting. Include a periodic update cycle keyed to the benchmarks, because park needs, demographics, and operating budgets change, and a plan that is not revisited becomes a static document that the department works around rather than from.

## Common Traps

### Level-of-Service as the Driving Metric

The plan applies a per-capita acreage standard, derives a deficit, and pursues land acquisition to close the numeric gap, regardless of where the land is or who can reach it. The trap mechanism is that the metric is easy to compute and politically legible, but it measures the supply of acres rather than the distribution of access, and it can be satisfied by acquiring a large parcel far from where anyone lives. The false signal is a rising acres-per-thousand ratio that appears to prove progress. The harm is that the community invests in parkland that does not serve the residents who lack access, the equity gap persists or worsens, and the operating budget is stretched across more low-value acreage.

### The Signature Amenity the Department Cannot Operate

The plan includes a pool, a splash pad, lighted tournament fields, or a community center because residents asked for it and it will be a flagship, but the operating cost is not funded and the department's staffing is already thin. The trap mechanism is that capital funding (grants, bonds, donations) is often available for construction but not for operations, so the amenity gets built and then declines, or the department cannibalizes maintenance of other parks to keep the flagship open. The false signal is a ribbon-cutting and early heavy use. The harm is a deteriorating signature asset that becomes an embarrassment, a maintenance liability that drags down the rest of the system, and a constituency that feels betrayed when the amenity they advocated for is cut back.

### Programmed Facilities Crowding Out Flexible Space

Organized user groups — sports leagues, dog-park advocates, gardeners — advocate effectively for dedicated facilities, and the plan allocates the site to their needs, leaving little flexible open space. The trap mechanism is that each dedicated facility serves its constituency well but excludes everyone else during its use, and the cumulative effect is a park that is fully programmed for specific users and unavailable for the informal, multi-generational, multi-cultural uses that serve the broadest population. The false signal is that the park "responded to community input." The harm is a park that serves the organized few and under-serves the unorganized many, and that loses the flexible social space that is the most universal park function.

### The Standard Template Imposed on Every Site

The department applies a standard park template (playground, ballfield, pavilion, parking lot) to every site regardless of its ecology, size, or context, because the template is easy to design, bid, and maintain. The trap mechanism is that the template ignores the site's distinctive features — its mature trees, its stream, its view, its slope, its neighborhood relationship — and produces parks that are interchangeable and that destroy the assets that would have made each one distinctive. The false signal is consistency and cost control. The harm is a system of parks that could be anywhere, that miss the chance to leverage each site's unique value, and that cost more to operate (irrigation, drainage, tree replacement) than site-responsive designs would have.

## Self-Check

- Does the needs assessment rest on demographics, an equity-weighted access analysis, and use patterns — not solely on a per-capita acreage standard?
- Is every proposed facility paired with an identified operating-funding source and a realistic estimate of annual operating and lifecycle cost?
- Does the programmed-to-unprogrammed ratio reflect the community's broad needs, with generous flexible space reserved, rather than being dominated by the facilities organized groups advocated for?
- Are siting and acquisition decisions driven by an access-and-equity analysis identifying who lacks a ten-minute walk to a park, not by where land is cheapest?
- Was a site-specific ecological, hydrological, and cultural assessment completed before design, and does the design fit the site rather than impose a standard template?
- Are mature canopy, healthy soils, and on-site stormwater management treated as primary design assets rather than cleared and engineered around?
- Does the implementation chapter sequence projects to close the most significant access and needs gaps first, with measurable benchmarks and a defined update cycle?
- Is there a stewardship and programming partnership strategy (friends-of groups, joint-use, nonprofit operators) to sustain each park after the capital ribbon-cutting?
- If the plan were fully built, would the operating budget be able to maintain the resulting system at an acceptable level — or would it cannibalize existing parks to do so?
