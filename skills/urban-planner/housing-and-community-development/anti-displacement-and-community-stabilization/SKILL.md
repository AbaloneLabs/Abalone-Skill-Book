# Anti-Displacement and Community Stabilization

Displacement is the harm that planning most often inflicts in the name of improvement. Investment — transit, amenities, infrastructure, rezoning — raises land values, and rising land values displace the lower-income residents and small businesses who cannot absorb the rent increases, producing the perverse outcome that the public investment intended to benefit a neighborhood displaces exactly the people the plan claimed to serve. The judgment problem is that the same actions that improve a neighborhood (new transit, better services, new housing) are the actions that trigger displacement, and a planner who pursues improvement without anti-displacement strategy is engineering gentrification, however pure the intent. The skill is in pairing every investment with the preservation, protection, and production strategies that allow existing residents to remain and benefit, and in measuring success by whether the existing community stays, not by whether the neighborhood "improved."

## Core Rules

### Assess Displacement Risk Before, Not After, Investing

Before any major public investment, rezoning, or revitalization initiative in a vulnerable neighborhood, assess the displacement risk: who lives and works there now, what are their incomes and tenures, how cost-burdened are they, how much rent increase can they absorb, what is the affordable stock at risk, and what are the early warning signs (rising rents, evictions, property sales, business turnover)? The rule is to conduct this assessment as a prerequisite to the investment, not as an afterthought, and to design the investment's pace, phasing, and accompanying strategies to manage the identified risk. An investment pursued without a displacement risk assessment is an investment made blind to its most consequential effect on the existing community, and the displacement will be discovered after it is irreversible.

### Pair Every Investment With Preservation and Protection Strategies

Public investment that raises land values must be paired, simultaneously and adequately funded, with strategies that preserve the existing community's ability to remain. The rule is to identify, for each investment, the corresponding anti-displacement package: preservation of existing affordable housing (acquisition, rehabilitation, long-term restrictions), tenant protections (just-cause eviction, rent stabilization, right of first refusal), affordable housing production (inclusionary requirements, public investment), small business stabilization (technical assistance, lease protections, commercial rent control where available), and asset-building for existing residents (down payment assistance, cooperatives, land trusts). The package must be funded and committed before or alongside the investment, not promised for later, because displacement begins the moment values rise and cannot be retroactively undone.

### Distinguish Cultural, Economic, and Physical Displacement

Displacement takes multiple forms, and a strategy that addresses only one leaves the others operating. Physical displacement is direct eviction or demolition. Economic displacement is rent or cost increases that force residents or businesses out. Cultural displacement is the transformation of a neighborhood's identity, institutions, and social networks as the original community is replaced, even without direct eviction. The rule is to design strategy for all three: physical (anti-eviction, preservation of at-risk units), economic (affordability, income supports, commercial stabilization), and cultural (support for cultural institutions, legacy businesses, community ownership, and the social infrastructure that holds a community together). A strategy that prevents physical eviction but allows economic and cultural displacement will still lose the community, more slowly and less visibly, but just as completely.

### Target Investment to Stabilize Existing Residents, Not Attract New Ones

The framing of revitalization often centers on attracting new residents and investment, which positions the existing community as an obstacle or a precursor to the intended future. The rule is to reframe the goal: the investment should stabilize and benefit the existing residents, with new residents and investment as a secondary effect, not the primary objective. This reframing changes the metrics (are existing residents better off, or did the neighborhood change population?), the strategies (preservation and protection alongside production), and the political coalition (existing residents as the constituency, not opposition to be managed). A revitalization that measures success by rising property values and new arrivals has succeeded at gentrification, not at community stabilization, regardless of its stated equity goals.

### Build Community Ownership and Power, Not Just Tenancy

Anti-displacement is more durable when existing residents and businesses own assets — homes, commercial spaces, land — rather than rent them, because owners capture the rising values rather than being displaced by them. The rule is to include community ownership strategies in the anti-displacement package: limited-equity cooperatives, community land trusts, shared-equity homeownership, commercial property acquisition by tenant cooperatives, and public or nonprofit acquisition of at-risk properties. Ownership also builds political power: residents who own are harder to displace and more able to shape the neighborhood's future. Tenancy protections are essential, but a strategy that leaves the existing community as renters in a rising market remains vulnerable to the next rent increase or sale; ownership changes the underlying power dynamic.

## Common Traps

### Investment Without Anti-Displacement Strategy

A transit line, waterfront park, or rezoning is pursued to improve a neighborhood, with no accompanying preservation or protection strategy, on the assumption that improvement benefits everyone. The trap is that the improvement raises values and displaces the residents it was meant to benefit. The mechanism is that the displacement effect is ignored or assumed away. The false signal is that "the neighborhood is improving." The harm is that the existing community is displaced, the public investment benefits higher-income new arrivals, and the equity goal is inverted — the investment worsened the condition of the people it claimed to serve.

### Promises of Future Affordability Instead of Committed Protection

The investment proceeds with a promise that affordable housing will be built "as part of the project" or "in a later phase," but the affordability is not committed, funded, or timed to coincide with the value increase. The trap is that the displacement begins immediately while the affordability arrives later, if at all. The mechanism is that the political will to invest exists before the value increase, while the will to protect exists after, when it is more expensive and the community is already displaced. The false signal is that "affordability is part of the plan." The harm is that the community is gone by the time the affordability materializes, and the protection serves a different population.

### Measuring Success by Rising Values and New Investment

The revitalization is evaluated by rising property values, new construction, and new business openings, all of which indicate gentrification, not stabilization. The trap is that the metrics of success are the metrics of displacement. The mechanism is that improvement is conflated with community benefit. The false signal is that "the strategy is working." The harm is that the strategy is judged successful while the existing community is displaced, the metrics reward the harm, and the approach is replicated in other neighborhoods with the same effect. The remedy is to measure whether existing residents and businesses remain and benefit, not whether values rose.

### Focusing on Physical Eviction While Ignoring Economic and Cultural Displacement

The anti-displacement strategy prevents evictions and demolitions but does not address rising rents, business turnover, or the loss of cultural institutions, and the community disperses through economic and cultural pressure even without direct displacement. The trap is that the visible harm (eviction) is prevented while the invisible harms (economic and cultural displacement) proceed. The mechanism is that physical displacement is measurable and political, while the other forms are diffuse and slow. The false signal is that "no one was displaced." The harm is that the community is lost through attrition, the social fabric dissolves, and the strategy is cited as successful while the neighborhood is transformed.

### Treating the Existing Community as an Obstacle to Improvement

The planning process frames the existing residents and businesses as obstacles to the desired transformation — their preferences, their uses, their buildings are "in the way" of the higher-value future. The trap is that the plan positions displacement as progress. The mechanism is that the existing community is devalued relative to the intended future. The false signal is that "we're planning for the future." The harm is that the plan engineers the displacement of the community it should serve, the equity rhetoric is contradicted by the plan's substance, and the community's distrust of planning is confirmed, making future engagement harder.

## Self-Check

- Was a displacement risk assessment conducted before the investment, identifying who is vulnerable and how much pressure they can absorb?
- Is every public investment paired, simultaneously and adequately funded, with preservation, protection, and production strategies committed before the value increase?
- Does the strategy address physical, economic, and cultural displacement, or only the visible eviction?
- Is the investment's success measured by whether existing residents and businesses remain and benefit, rather than by rising values and new arrivals?
- Does the strategy include community ownership (cooperatives, land trusts, acquisition), not just tenancy protections, to change the underlying power dynamic?
- Have I avoided pursuing investment without a committed anti-displacement package, on the assumption that improvement benefits everyone?
- Is the affordability committed and timed to the value increase, or promised for a later phase that may not arrive?
- Are the metrics of success the metrics of stabilization (existing community remains), not the metrics of gentrification (values rise)?
- Have I addressed the slow, diffuse economic and cultural displacement, not just the measurable physical eviction?
- Is the existing community positioned as the constituency the plan serves, or as an obstacle to the intended future?
