# Community Facilities and Services Planning

Community facilities — schools, libraries, parks, recreation centers, public health clinics, fire and police stations, civic buildings — are the physical infrastructure of public life, and their location, capacity, and quality determine whether a community is served or underserved. The judgment problem is that facilities are expensive and long-lived, so siting decisions made under one set of conditions persist for generations, and the patterns of investment and disinvestment they encode become the geography of equity and inequity. A facility sited to serve new growth may bypass the underserved existing community; a facility sited by political influence rather than need may duplicate service in favored areas while leaving gaps elsewhere. The skill is in planning facilities by measured need and equitable distribution, coordinating their provision with growth, and recognizing that the pattern of public investment is itself a statement of community values.

## Core Rules

### Plan Facility Provision by Measured Need and Level-of-Service Standards

Facility decisions should be driven by measured need — current and projected population, demographic composition, usage patterns, and level-of-service (LOS) standards that define what adequate service means — rather than by political pressure, historical patterns, or available sites. The rule is to establish LOS standards for each facility type (acres of park per thousand residents, library square footage per capita, school capacity per enrolled student, response time for emergency services), to measure current service against those standards spatially, and to prioritize investment where the gaps are greatest. Without LOS standards, facility decisions are ad hoc and unaccountable; with them, the gaps are visible and the investment can be directed to need. The standards should be reviewed periodically, because what was adequate in one era may be inadequate as demographics, expectations, and technology change.

### Site Facilities to Advance Equity and Address Underserved Areas

The geography of public facilities often reflects historical patterns of investment that favored some neighborhoods and bypassed others, and those patterns persist because new investment tends to follow existing infrastructure and political influence. The rule is to evaluate facility distribution for equity — mapping facility locations against income, race, age, and access — and to prioritize investment in underserved areas, even when the political pressure runs toward the already-served. An equity lens reveals, for example, that parks in low-income neighborhoods may be smaller, less maintained, and less programmed than those in wealthier areas, and that the LOS standard, applied uniformly, may mask qualitative disparities. The remedy is to invest not just where the quantitative gap is greatest but where the historical disinvestment is deepest, recognizing that equitable distribution may require more than proportional allocation.

### Coordinate Facility Provision With Growth and Redevelopment

Facilities must be provided in step with the growth they serve, neither lagging (producing overcrowded schools, undersized parks, slow emergency response) nor leading (producing underused facilities and fiscal strain). The rule is to coordinate the facility provision schedule with the growth projections and the development pipeline, using concurrency requirements (where available) to ensure that facilities are in place when the growth arrives, and using impact fees and developer contributions to ensure that growth pays its proportionate share. The coordination requires the planner to engage the facility providers (school districts, library systems, public safety) whose capital plans operate on different cycles, and to reconcile the timing so that growth and facility provision advance together. Growth without facilities degrades service for everyone; facilities without growth waste public investment.

### Design Facilities as Multi-Use Community Anchors, Not Single-Purpose Buildings

Single-purpose public buildings — a library open limited hours, a school unused evenings and summers, a recreation center serving one age group — represent inefficient use of expensive public assets and miss the opportunity to create the gathering places that build community. The rule is to design facilities as multi-use community anchors: co-locate services (a library with a community center and a health clinic), design for shared use (school facilities available for community programs after hours), and site facilities to anchor the public realm (on a plaza, a main street, a transit stop) rather than on isolated campuses. Multi-use design increases the return on public investment, extends the hours the facility serves the community, and creates the civic presence that single-purpose buildings lack. It requires early coordination among the providers and design that accommodates multiple uses, but it produces facilities that are more than the sum of their parts.

### Engage the Community in Defining Need, Not Just Reacting to Proposals

Communities are often asked to react to facility proposals after the need has been defined and the site selected, which positions them as opponents or supporters of a fait accompli rather than as participants in defining the need. The rule is to engage the community upstream, in defining what need exists, what kind of facility would serve it, and where it should be located, recognizing that the community's knowledge of its own needs and patterns is essential to a facility that will be used. Engagement that begins after the decisions are made produces conflict and missed information; engagement that begins at the needs assessment produces better facilities and community ownership of the result. This is especially important in underserved communities, where a history of top-down decisions may have produced distrust that only genuine upstream engagement can overcome.

## Common Traps

### Siting by Available Land Rather Than by Need

A facility is sited on a parcel the jurisdiction already owns or that is cheaply available, regardless of whether the location serves the need. The trap is that the facility is built but underused or inaccessible to the population it was meant to serve. The mechanism is that land availability substitutes for need analysis. The false signal is that "we provided the facility." The harm is that the underserved area remains underserved, the investment is wasted on a poorly located asset, and the next investment is harder to justify because the first "didn't get used."

### Level-of-Service Standards That Mask Qualitative Disparities

The LOS standard reports adequate park acreage per capita citywide or in a neighborhood, but the parks in low-income areas are small, poorly maintained, lack amenities, or are unsafe, while the standard counts them equally. The trap is that the quantitative standard hides the qualitative gap. The mechanism is that LOS measures quantity, not quality or accessibility. The false signal is that "service is adequate." The harm is that investment continues to flow to areas with political influence while the underserved areas are told they meet the standard, and the equity gap persists behind the numbers.

### Growth Outpacing Facility Provision

New development is approved, but the schools, parks, and public safety facilities to serve it are not provided concurrently, and the existing community's service degrades as the new arrivals share the inadequate supply. The trap is that growth is allowed without the facilities to support it. The mechanism is that facility provision lags the development pipeline, often because the providers' capital cycles are slower than the market. The false signal is that "growth pays for itself eventually." The harm is that service degrades for years, the existing community bears the cost, and when the facilities finally arrive the population has already experienced the overcrowding and decline.

### Single-Purpose Facilities Underused Most of the Time

A school, library, or community center is built for a single use and sits empty outside its primary hours, representing a missed opportunity for community gathering and inefficient use of the public investment. The trap is that the facility is designed for one function when it could serve many. The mechanism is that providers plan in silos and design for their own program. The false signal is that "we built the facility." The harm is that the public realm lacks the gathering places that build community, the facility is a cost rather than an asset for most of its hours, and the next facility is harder to fund because the first is seen as underused.

### Engagement After the Decisions Are Made

The community is invited to comment on a facility proposal after the need, type, and site have been determined, and the engagement becomes a fight over a fait accompli. The trap is that the engagement is reactive rather than formative. The mechanism is that decisions are made internally and engagement is used to ratify them. The false signal is that "we engaged the community." The harm is that the facility may not match the community's actual needs, the community's distrust is confirmed, and the engagement produces conflict rather than ownership, making the project slower and more contested than genuine upstream engagement would have been.

## Self-Check

- Are facility decisions driven by measured need and level-of-service standards applied spatially, rather than by available land or political pressure?
- Has the facility distribution been evaluated for equity, with investment prioritized in historically underserved areas, including qualitative disparities that quantitative standards mask?
- Is facility provision coordinated with the growth and development pipeline, with concurrency or impact fees ensuring growth pays its share and is served when it arrives?
- Are facilities designed as multi-use community anchors (co-located services, shared use, civic presence), rather than single-purpose buildings underused most of the time?
- Does community engagement begin at the needs assessment, defining need and location, rather than after the decisions are made?
- Have I avoided siting facilities by available land rather than by measured need?
- Do the LOS standards capture quality and accessibility, not just quantity, so that disparities are visible rather than hidden?
- Are facilities provided concurrently with growth, or does growth degrade service for the existing community until facilities catch up?
- Are facilities designed to serve multiple uses and hours, maximizing the public return on the investment?
- Did the community help define the need, or only react to a proposal already decided?
