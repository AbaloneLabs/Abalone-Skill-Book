# Housing Needs Assessment and Strategy

A housing needs assessment is the analytical foundation of housing policy, and it determines what a jurisdiction believes its housing problem to be. The judgment problem is that the assessment's framing, methods, and assumptions predetermine the strategy that follows: an assessment that measures only aggregate demand produces a strategy focused on production at any price; one that disaggregates by income, tenure, and household type produces a strategy that can address the affordability crisis where it actually lives. A weak assessment documents that housing is expensive and recommends building more; a strong assessment identifies who is cost-burdened, at what incomes, where the gaps are, what is driving them, and what combination of production, preservation, and access will close them. The planner's role is to make the assessment honest about the scale and distribution of need, even when the findings are politically uncomfortable, because a strategy built on a dishonest assessment cannot solve the problem.

## Core Rules

### Disaggregate Need by Income, Tenure, and Household Type

Aggregate housing demand — "we need X thousand units" — is analytically useless for affordability strategy, because the market will produce units for the incomes it can profitably serve, and the shortfall is almost always at the lower end. The rule is to disaggregate housing need by income band (extremely low, very low, low, moderate, above moderate), by tenure (renter versus owner), and by household type (family, senior, single-person, special needs), and to compare the supply in each band to the demand in each band. This reveals the specific affordability gaps: a jurisdiction may have adequate total supply but a severe shortfall of units affordable to extremely low-income households, which is the gap the strategy must address. Disaggregation also surfaces the needs of populations hidden in aggregates — large families, the elderly, the disabled, the homeless — whose housing requirements differ from the median household.

### Distinguish the Affordability Gap from the Production Gap

Two distinct problems are often conflated: the production gap (too few units overall, driving up all prices) and the affordability gap (units exist but are unaffordable to lower-income households because the market cannot profitably serve them). The rule is to distinguish the two and to design strategy accordingly. The production gap is addressed by removing barriers to supply (zoning, parking, review) so the market builds more; the affordability gap requires subsidy, regulation, or public production, because the market will not serve incomes below the profitable threshold regardless of how much supply is added. A strategy that addresses only the production gap will not solve affordability for lower-income households, because the new supply will be priced for higher incomes (it may filter down over decades, but the need is now). A strategy that addresses only the affordability gap through subsidy will be overwhelmed if the production gap continues to drive up rents. Both must be addressed, and the assessment should quantify each.

### Project Future Need, Not Just Document Current Conditions

A housing needs assessment that documents current cost burden and stops there cannot inform a strategy for a future horizon. The rule is to project future housing demand — population and household growth, demographic shifts (aging, household formation), income trends, and the resulting affordability gaps — over the planning horizon, and to compare the projected supply (under current policy) to the projected need. This projection reveals whether the gap is widening or narrowing, which populations are growing fastest, and whether current policy is on track. The projection should be transparent about its assumptions (migration, household size, income) and should be tested against alternatives, because the projection's assumptions can drive the strategy as much as its conclusions.

### Address Production, Preservation, and Access as a Coordinated Strategy

A complete housing strategy operates on three fronts: production (building new affordable units), preservation (keeping existing affordable units affordable, preventing loss to market-rate conversion or demolition), and access (removing barriers — discrimination, source-of-income, credit, immigration status — that prevent households from obtaining the units that exist). The rule is to design the strategy across all three, calibrated to the assessment's findings. A jurisdiction that focuses only on production loses existing affordable units faster than it builds new ones; one that focuses only on preservation cannot meet growth; one that focuses only on access leaves the underlying shortage untouched. The three fronts interact: production without preservation churns the stock; preservation without production creates scarcity that access barriers then ration inequitably. The strategy should be explicit about how the three are balanced and why.

### Connect the Strategy to the Land Use and Regulatory Tools That Implement It

A housing strategy that is not connected to the zoning, permitting, funding, and regulatory tools that implement it is an aspiration. The rule is to map each strategy element to the specific implementation tools: which zoning changes will enable the production, which funding sources will subsidize the affordability, which regulations will preserve the existing stock, which programs will expand access. The implementation mapping forces honesty about capacity — a strategy relying on federal funds that may not materialize, or on zoning changes the council will not adopt, is a strategy that will not be implemented — and it creates accountability for the specific actions. The strategy should distinguish short-term actions (funding allocations, code enforcement, zoning amendments) from long-term structural changes, and should assign owners and timelines.

## Common Traps

### Aggregate Demand Masking the Affordability Gap

The assessment reports total housing demand and total supply, finds a shortfall, and recommends more production, without disaggregating by income. The trap is that the aggregate masks the specific affordability gap at the lower end. The mechanism is that the aggregate is easier to compute and politically safer. The false signal is that "we need more housing." The harm is that the strategy pursues production that serves higher incomes, the affordability gap for lower-income households is unaddressed, and the assessment is cited as evidence that the problem is being handled when it is not.

### Assuming Market Production Will Solve Affordability

The strategy relies on adding market-rate supply to bring down rents for all, without recognizing that the market cannot profitably serve lower incomes and that filtering takes decades. The trap is that the strategy defers affordability to a future that will not arrive for the households in need now. The mechanism is that supply-and-demand is invoked as a universal solvent. The false signal is that "more supply lowers all rents." The harm is that lower-income households remain unaffordably housed for the decades filtering requires, displacement continues, and the strategy is revealed as inadequate when the promised affordability does not materialize.

### Preservation Ignored While Focusing on Production

The strategy emphasizes building new affordable units while existing affordable units (naturally occurring affordable housing, subsidized units with expiring restrictions) are lost to market-rate conversion or demolition at a faster rate than new units are built. The trap is that the net affordable stock shrinks despite the production effort. The mechanism is that loss is invisible while construction is visible. The false signal is that "we're producing affordable units." The harm is that the strategy loses ground, the resources spent on production are offset by losses elsewhere, and the affordability crisis worsens despite the investment. The remedy is to track preservation alongside production and to target the loss rate.

### Projection Assumed Rather Than Analyzed

The assessment projects future housing need using assumptions about growth, household size, and income that are not examined, and the projection drives the strategy. The trap is that the assumptions — not the analysis — determine the conclusion. The mechanism is that the projection's inputs are buried and its outputs are emphasized. The false signal is that "the data shows the need." The harm is that the strategy is built on unexamined assumptions that may be wrong (overstating or understating growth, missing demographic shifts), and when the assumptions prove wrong the strategy fails, with the assessment blamed rather than the inputs that drove it.

### Strategy Disconnected From Implementation Tools

The strategy recommends ambitious housing goals but does not map them to the zoning, funding, and regulatory tools that would implement them, and the goals are not achieved. The trap is that the strategy is aspirational rather than operational. The mechanism is that implementation is treated as a separate, later problem. The false signal is that "we have a housing strategy." The harm is that the strategy is not implemented, the housing goals are missed, and the assessment's analytical work is wasted because it never reached the decisions that determine outcomes.

## Self-Check

- Does the assessment disaggregate housing need by income band, tenure, and household type, revealing the specific gaps rather than an aggregate total?
- Have I distinguished the production gap from the affordability gap, and designed strategy for each, recognizing that market production alone will not solve lower-income affordability?
- Does the assessment project future need over the planning horizon, with transparent assumptions tested against alternatives, rather than only documenting current conditions?
- Does the strategy address production, preservation, and access in a coordinated balance, with preservation tracked against the loss rate?
- Is each strategy element mapped to specific implementation tools (zoning, funding, regulations, programs), with assigned owners, timelines, and confirmed capacity?
- Have I avoided masking the affordability gap behind aggregate demand figures?
- Have I been honest that market production will not, by itself, serve lower-income households in the timeframe they need?
- Are existing affordable units being preserved, or is the strategy losing them faster than it produces replacements?
- Are the projection's assumptions examined and documented, so the strategy's dependence on them is visible?
- Would the strategy, if implemented through its mapped tools, actually close the disaggregated affordability gaps, or does it rest on assumptions about market behavior that may not hold?
