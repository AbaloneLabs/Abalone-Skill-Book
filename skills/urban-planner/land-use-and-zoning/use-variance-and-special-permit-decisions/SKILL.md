# Use Variance and Special Permit Decisions

Discretionary relief is where zoning law meets individual facts, and where the planner's analysis most directly determines whether someone can use their property as they wish. Variances and special permits look similar on the surface — both grant something the code does not allow by-right — but they rest on fundamentally different legal standards, require different findings, and create different precedents. The judgment problem is that the standards are easy to recite and hard to apply honestly: the variance "hardship" test in most jurisdictions is deliberately strict, and a board that grants variances liberally erodes the zoning code itself, while a board that denies every variance invites unnecessary litigation and produces arbitrary results. The planner's role is to produce findings that are legally defensible, factually grounded, and internally consistent — not to advocate for or against the application.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Use Variances from Area Variances and Apply the Correct Standard

The single most important classification in discretionary relief is whether the request is for a use variance (permission to use the land for a purpose the code prohibits in that district) or an area variance (permission to deviate from a dimensional standard — setback, height, lot area — for a use that is otherwise permitted). Use variances are disfavored in most jurisdictions and require a stricter hardship showing, often the "unnecessary hardship" standard, which demands that the property cannot yield a reasonable return under the code's permitted uses. Area variances use a balancing test that weighs the benefit to the applicant against the detriment to the community, often under a statutory "practical difficulty" standard. Conflating the two — applying the area variance balancing test to a use variance, or demanding use-variance-level hardship for a modest setback relief — produces findings that are legally wrong and reversible. The first analytical step is always to classify the request correctly.

### Apply the Statutory Hardship Factors to the Specific Property, Not the Applicant

Variance hardship is evaluated from the perspective of the property, not the owner. The question is not "Does this applicant need this variance?" but "Does this property, under the code as written, suffer a hardship that warrants relief?" This distinction defeats the most common variance argument: "I bought this property to build a duplex, and the code only allows single-family, so I have a hardship." The hardship is self-created, because the code was in place when the property was purchased, and the applicant's investment-backed expectation does not create a legal hardship. The rule is to test each hardship factor against the physical and legal characteristics of the parcel — its shape, topography, size, surrounding context, and the range of uses permitted by-right — and to document whether the hardship inheres in the property or was created by the applicant's choices.

### Evaluate Special Permits Against Stated Standards, Not Neighborhood Preference

Special permits (also called conditional use permits) differ from variances because the use is contemplated by the code but allowed only with review. The code should state the standards the use must meet, and the planner's job is to apply those standards to the specific proposal. The trap is that special permit review often becomes a referendum on whether the neighbors want the use, which is not a legal standard. A daycare, group home, or multi-family building that meets the code's stated standards must be granted the permit, even if neighbors object, unless the code provides a discretionary standard (such as compatibility with neighborhood character) that the objection satisfies. The rule is to identify the exact standards in the code, evaluate the application against each one with specific findings, and separate legally relevant concerns from generalized opposition.

### Build the Findings from Evidence in the Record, Not from Conclusion

Findings of fact are the document that survives judicial review, and they must connect the legal standard to the specific evidence. A finding that "the requested variance will not alter the essential character of the neighborhood" is a conclusion, not a finding; a finding that "the requested 5-foot side setback reduction is consistent with the prevailing setbacks on the block, which range from 4 to 8 feet, as documented in the site survey and staff field observation dated [date]" is a finding. The rule is that every legal conclusion must be supported by a specific factual finding, and every factual finding must reference evidence in the record — the application, plans, surveys, staff report, hearing testimony, correspondence. Findings that recite the legal standard and then assert compliance, without the factual bridge, are the most common reason a board decision is overturned.

### Consider Precedent and Cumulative Effect Across the District

A single variance or special permit does not exist in isolation; it sets a precedent that applicants and boards will cite. A 10-foot height variance granted on one parcel becomes the argument for the same variance on the adjacent parcel, and within a few years the dimensional standard has effectively shifted without any code amendment. The rule is to ask, in every discretionary relief case: "If I grant this, what is the argument I will hear next month, and will I be able to distinguish it?" If the answer is that the next case is indistinguishable, the grant has effectively amended the code by accretion — which is legally problematic because code amendments require a different process. Documenting what makes this case unique, if anything, protects both the integrity of the code and the defensibility of the decision.

## Common Traps

### Granting the Variance Because the Project Is "Nice"

The applicant presents an attractive, well-designed project that requires a variance, and the board grants it because the project is an improvement over what could be built by-right. The trap is that the quality of the proposed project is not a variance criterion. The legal question is whether the property suffers a hardship warranting relief, not whether the applicant's architecture is appealing. The mechanism is that aesthetic approval substitutes for legal analysis. The false signal is that the community is "getting a better building." The harm is that the variance standard erodes — every future applicant can argue their project is also nice — and the code's dimensional standards become advisory rather than binding, applied only to applicants who do not present appealing projects.

### Treating Neighbor Opposition as a Legal Standard

Neighbors organize, submit petitions, and testify against a special permit application, and the board denies the permit citing "neighborhood opposition" or "community concern." The trap is that opposition is not a legal standard unless the code defines one (such as a compatibility finding) that the opposition satisfies with specific evidence. The mechanism is that political pressure is converted into a legal finding without the factual basis. The false signal is that the board "listened to the community." The harm is twofold: the denial is vulnerable to legal challenge for lacking evidentiary support, and it signals to future applicants that the process is political rather than standards-based, discouraging investment and inviting litigation.

### Applying the Wrong Hardship Standard

A board applies the lenient "practical difficulty" area-variance test to a use-variance request, or applies the strict "unnecessary hardship" use-variance test to a modest area variance. The trap is that the standards sound similar and the distinction is easy to miss under time pressure. The mechanism is that the wrong standard produces either an unjustified grant (too lenient) or an unjustified denial (too strict). The false signal is that the board "considered the factors." The harm is that the decision is legally erroneous and reversible, and the applicant either receives relief they were not entitled to or is denied relief they should have received, with the cost and delay of remand or appeal.

### Self-Created Hardship Disguised as Inherent Hardship

The applicant purchased the property, made investments, or subdivided the lot in a way that created the condition they now claim as a hardship, and the board accepts the claim without tracing its origin. The trap is that self-created hardship is explicitly excluded from variance eligibility in most jurisdictions, but it is often presented as if it inheres in the property. The mechanism is that the chronology of the property and the applicant's decisions is not examined. The false signal is that the hardship "exists." The harm is that the variance rewards the applicant for creating their own noncompliance, and invites others to do the same — buy property, create a nonconforming condition, and then seek relief.

## Self-Check

- Have I correctly classified the request as a use variance, area variance, or special permit, and applied the corresponding legal standard rather than a generic "balancing"?
- Is the hardship analysis grounded in the physical and legal characteristics of the property, and have I ruled out self-created hardship by tracing the origin of the condition?
- For a special permit, have I identified the exact standards in the code and evaluated the application against each one with specific findings, rather than treating neighbor opposition as a standard?
- Does every legal conclusion in the findings rest on a specific factual finding that references evidence in the record?
- Have I considered the precedent this decision sets and whether I will be able to distinguish the next, similar request?
- If granting relief, have I confirmed the property cannot achieve a reasonable return or reasonable use under the code as written, not merely that the applicant prefers the variance use?
- Have I separated legally relevant concerns (specific compatibility impacts, documented standards violations) from generalized opposition in the hearing record?
- If denying, are the denial findings specific enough that the applicant understands what evidence would change the outcome, and are they supported by the record rather than by board preference?
- Have I documented what makes this property or application unique, if anything, so the decision does not silently amend the code by accretion?
- Would a reviewing court reading only the findings be able to follow the chain from legal standard to factual evidence to conclusion?
