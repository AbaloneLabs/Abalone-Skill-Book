# Nonconformity and Legal Nonconforming Use Management

Legal nonconforming uses and structures are the awkward inheritances of zoning: development that was lawful when built but does not comply with the current code. They are protected because the law recognizes that it is unfair to require immediate compliance with a regulation adopted after the investment was made, but they are also disfavored because they represent exactly the conditions the zoning was enacted to eliminate. The judgment problem is that every decision about a nonconformity — can it expand, can it rebuild after a fire, can it resume after abandonment, when must it terminate — sets a precedent that either honors the property owner's investment or undermines the code's long-term intent. The planner must apply the code's nonconformity provisions precisely, because both over-protection and under-protection create legal exposure: too little protection invites takings claims, too much protection defeats the zoning.

## Core Rules

### Establish Legal Nonconforming Status Before Granting Any Protection

Not every noncompliant use or structure is a legal nonconformity entitled to protection. A use that was established in violation of the code, that expanded without permits, or that cannot be documented as predating the current regulation is an illegal nonconformity subject to enforcement, not a legal nonconformity subject to protection. The first step in any nonconformity decision is to establish the legal status of the use or structure: when was it established, what was the zoning at that time, was it lawful then, and is there documentary evidence (permits, certificates of occupancy, tax records, aerial photography, utility hookups) proving it? A planner who assumes nonconforming status without verification may extend protection to a use that has no right to it, or — equally damaging — may fail to recognize a legitimate nonconformity and trigger an unnecessary enforcement action. The burden of proof typically rests on the property owner claiming the protection, but the municipality's records often hold the dispositive evidence.

### Distinguish Nonconforming Uses from Nonconforming Structures

The code's protections and restrictions differ for nonconforming uses (the activity does not conform to the use regulations) and nonconforming structures (the building does not conform to dimensional standards — setback, height, lot area — but the use is permitted). A nonconforming use is generally more restricted: it typically cannot be expanded, cannot be changed to another nonconforming use, and may lose its status after abandonment. A nonconforming structure is generally more protected: it can often continue, be repaired, and sometimes be expanded if the expansion itself complies with current standards. Conflating the two — applying use-nonconformity restrictions to a structure-nonconformity, or vice versa — produces findings that are legally wrong. The rule is to classify the nonconformity precisely, identify which article of the code governs that class, and apply those provisions specifically.

### Apply Expansion, Reconstruction, and Resumption Rules Literally

The most litigated nonconformity questions are whether the nonconformity can expand, whether it can be reconstructed after destruction, and whether it can resume after discontinuance. These rules are typically stated in precise terms in the code — a nonconforming use may not be expanded; a nonconforming structure destroyed beyond a stated percentage of value may not be rebuilt except in conformance; a nonconforming use discontinued for a stated period is deemed abandoned. The planner's job is to apply these rules literally, not equitably. "Equitable" application — granting an expansion because the owner is sympathetic, allowing reconstruction because the fire was not their fault — undermines the code and creates disparate treatment. The rule is to document the factual question (did the use expand? what percentage was destroyed? how long was the discontinuance?) against the code's stated threshold, and to reach the conclusion the code dictates, even when the outcome feels harsh. If the outcome is genuinely inequitable, the remedy is a code amendment, not an ad hoc exception.

### Evaluate Vested Rights and Grandfathering Claims Against the Statutory Standard

Property owners frequently claim that their project has "vested rights" or is "grandfathered" based on expenditures, permits, or applications filed before a code change. These claims have specific legal elements that vary by jurisdiction but generally require a substantial expenditure made in good faith reliance on a valid permit, before the regulatory change, such that applying the new regulation would be unfair. The trap is that "grandfathering" is a colloquial term, not a legal standard, and many claims that invoke it do not meet the vested-rights test. The rule is to evaluate each claim against the jurisdiction's actual elements: Was there a valid permit? Were expenditures substantial and incurred in reliance? Was the reliance reasonable and in good faith? Did the project proceed with diligence? A claim that fails any element is not vested, regardless of how much money was spent, and the planner must document the analysis so the decision is defensible.

### Use Amortization Deliberately and With Legal Counsel

Amortization — requiring a nonconforming use to terminate after a set period — is the most aggressive nonconformity tool, and it carries the highest legal risk. It is permitted in some jurisdictions and prohibited or limited in others, and where permitted it requires a period long enough to allow the owner to recoup their investment, or it risks being deemed a taking. The rule is that amortization should never be applied casually or as a staff-level decision; it requires a documented analysis of the remaining economic life of the use, the owner's unrecovered investment, and the public interest in termination, and it should be adopted and applied with legal counsel. A poorly calibrated amortization period — too short to allow recoupment — converts a legitimate regulatory tool into a compensable taking.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Old Means Legal

A use has existed for decades, and the assumption is that it must be a legal nonconformity entitled to protection. The trap is that duration does not establish legality. A use that was established without permits, that expanded illegally over time, or that was never lawful under the zoning in effect when it began is an illegal nonconformity regardless of its age. The mechanism is that "it's been there forever" substitutes for documentary proof of lawful establishment. The false signal is that longevity implies legitimacy. The harm is that protection is extended to uses with no legal right to it, undermining the code and disadvantaging compliant property owners, while also creating an expectation that cannot survive a careful legal challenge.

### Allowing Incremental Expansion Through "Repair" and "Maintenance"

A nonconforming structure is repaired repeatedly over years, each repair modest and seemingly within the right to maintain, until the cumulative effect is an expanded or substantially altered nonconformity. The trap is that the code distinguishes repair and maintenance (generally allowed) from expansion and reconstruction (generally restricted), but the line blurs over time and across multiple permits. The mechanism is that each individual permit looks innocuous, and the cumulative effect is never evaluated. The false signal is that "it's just maintenance." The harm is that the nonconformity grows without ever triggering the reconstruction or expansion thresholds, defeating the code's intent to phase nonconformities out, and creating a structure that is substantially different from what was legally protected.

### Misapplying the Abandonment Period

A nonconforming use ceases operation, and after a period the question is whether it has been abandoned and lost its protected status. The trap is that the abandonment period in the code is stated precisely (often six months, twelve months, or two years), but the factual question of when "discontinuance" began is contested. The mechanism is that the owner may have maintained intermittent activity to reset the clock, or the municipality may date discontinuance from a point the owner disputes. The false signal is that the use "stopped." The harm is that an incorrect abandonment determination either strips a legitimate nonconformity of its protection (a taking risk) or preserves a use that should have terminated (undermining the code). The defense is to document the factual basis for the discontinuance date with specific evidence — utility records, business activity, inspections — rather than asserting it.

### Treating Vested Rights as a Matter of Fairness

An owner has spent significant money on a project, the code changes, and the planner grants vested rights because "it would be unfair to stop them now." The trap is that vested rights is a legal doctrine with specific elements, not an equity standard. The mechanism is that sympathy for the expenditure substitutes for the element-by-element analysis. The false signal is that "they spent a lot, so they're vested." The harm is that the vesting is granted without legal basis, exposing the municipality to challenge from those who must comply with the new code, and creating a precedent that any substantial expenditure defeats a regulatory change — which would make zoning unenforceable against anyone with resources.

## Self-Check

- Have I established the legal nonconforming status with documentary evidence (permits, certificates, tax records, aerials), rather than assuming it from age or appearance?
- Have I classified the nonconformity as a use nonconformity or a structure nonconformity, and applied the code provisions specific to that class?
- When evaluating expansion, reconstruction, or resumption, have I applied the code's stated thresholds literally and documented the factual basis, rather than reaching an equitable exception?
- For a vested rights or grandfathering claim, have I evaluated each legal element (valid permit, substantial expenditure, good-faith reliance, diligence) and documented the conclusion?
- If the outcome feels harsh, have I confirmed that the remedy is a code amendment rather than an ad hoc exception that undermines the code?
- Have I traced the chronology of permits and alterations to determine whether cumulative "maintenance" has effectively expanded the nonconformity?
- For an abandonment determination, is the discontinuance date documented with specific evidence rather than asserted?
- If amortization is being considered, is it supported by an investment-recoupment analysis and adopted with legal counsel, rather than imposed at staff level?
- Have I distinguished between a legal nonconformity (protected) and an illegal nonconformity (subject to enforcement), and confirmed the evidence supports the classification?
- Would the decision, if challenged, survive review based on the documented record and the code's literal provisions?
