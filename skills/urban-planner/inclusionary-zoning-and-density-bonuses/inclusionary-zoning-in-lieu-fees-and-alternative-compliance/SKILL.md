# Inclusionary Zoning In-Lieu Fees and Alternative Compliance

Inclusionary zoning programs typically offer the developer the choice between the on-site affordable units and the alternative compliance (the in-lieu fee, the off-site production, the land dedication, the rehabilitation), and the design of the alternatives determines whether they advance or undermine the affordable housing goals. The judgment problem is that the in-lieu fee that is too low does not produce the equivalent affordable units, the off-site option that is too permissive allows the segregation, and the alternative that is not tied to the affordable housing production becomes the loophole that the developer uses to avoid the obligation. The planner must design the alternatives that are equivalent to the on-site production (the fee that funds the equivalent units, the off-site that produces the equivalent units in the comparable locations), that serve the affordable housing goals (the deeper affordability, the larger units, the high-opportunity locations), and that do not become the escape valve that undermines the program. This skill covers the in-lieu fee and the alternative compliance decisions that determine whether the options advance or undermine the inclusionary goals. It complements the broader inclusionary-zoning skill and the targeting skill by focusing on the alternative compliance dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Set the In-Lieu Fee to Be Equivalent to the On-Site Production

The in-lieu fee should be equivalent to the cost of the on-site affordable unit production, because the fee that is too low does not fund the equivalent units, and the developer rationally chooses the cheaper fee over the more expensive on-site production, and the affordable housing stock is not produced. The rule is to set the in-lieu fee to be equivalent to the on-site production, calculating the fee based on the subsidy per unit (the difference between the market-rate and the affordable rent or price, the land cost, the construction cost, the financing cost), and updating the calculation regularly to reflect the current market conditions. The fee calculation should account for the full cost of the on-site production (the lost revenue, the construction, the financing, the ongoing administration), because the fee that does not account for the full cost is the fee that underfunds the production. The fee should be set at the level that makes the on-site and the in-lieu equivalent, because the fee that is cheaper than the on-site becomes the default, and the fee that is more expensive than the on-site is never used. The fee should be updated regularly, because the market conditions change, and the fee that was equivalent at adoption erodes or inflates, and the regular update maintains the equivalence.

### Manage the Fee Revenue Through the Affordable Housing Trust Fund

The in-lieu fee revenue should be managed through the affordable housing trust fund, because the fund provides the mechanism to accumulate, to track, and to expend the revenue for the affordable housing production, and the revenue without the fund is the revenue that is not spent or is spent on the wrong purpose. The rule is to manage the fee revenue through the affordable housing trust fund, establishing the fund with the clear purpose (the affordable housing production, the preservation, the assistance), the governance (the oversight board, the staff), the accounting (the segregated account, the interest, the tracking), and the expenditure plan (the priorities, the criteria, the timeline). The trust fund should have the clear expenditure plan, because the revenue that accumulates without the expenditure is the revenue that does not produce the housing, and the plan ensures the revenue is spent on the affordable housing. The trust fund should target the expenditure to the priorities (the deeper affordability, the special needs, the high-opportunity areas), because the fee revenue can be targeted more precisely than the on-site units, and the targeting advances the goals. The trust fund should be reported and audited, because the transparency builds the credibility, and the fund that is not reported is the fund that is questioned.

### Evaluate the Off-Site and the Rehabilitation Alternatives

The off-site production (the affordable units built at the different location) and the rehabilitation (the existing units made affordable) are the alternatives that may serve the affordable housing goals better than the on-site production, and the evaluation of the alternatives should determine when they are appropriate. The rule is to evaluate the off-site and the rehabilitation alternatives, establishing the criteria that the alternatives must meet (the equivalent number of units, the comparable location and the access to the opportunity, the comparable or the deeper affordability, the comparable or the better unit types), and the process for the evaluation and the approval. The off-site alternative should be allowed when it produces the equivalent or the better affordable housing (the more units, the deeper affordability, the larger units, the better location), because the alternative that produces the better housing serves the goals. The off-site alternative should not be allowed when it produces the inferior housing (the fewer units, the shallower affordability, the segregated location), because the inferior alternative undermines the goals. The rehabilitation alternative should be allowed when it preserves or creates the affordable housing that is equivalent or better, and the rehabilitation should include the affordability restrictions that match the on-site duration. The alternatives should be evaluated against the on-site baseline, because the baseline defines the equivalence, and the alternative that does not meet the baseline should not be approved.

### Require the Equivalence and Prevent the Loophole

The alternative compliance should be equivalent to the on-site production, and the equivalence should be required and enforced, because the alternative that is not equivalent is the loophole that the developer uses to avoid the obligation. The rule is to require the equivalence and prevent the loophole, establishing the clear standards for the equivalence (the unit count, the affordability, the location, the timing), and the enforcement that ensures the alternative meets the standards. The equivalence should be verified before the approval, because the alternative that is approved without the verification is the alternative that may not meet the standards, and the verification prevents the problem. The loophole prevention should address the common evasion (the off-site that produces the fewer units, the rehabilitation that does not include the affordability, the fee that is not spent), and the prevention should close the loopholes through the clear standards and the enforcement. The alternative should be the option, not the default, because the on-site production is the primary obligation, and the alternative that becomes the default undermines the on-site production and the integration. The alternative should be evaluated case-by-case, because the alternatives are appropriate in some cases and not in others, and the case-by-case evaluation ensures the appropriate use.

### Target the Alternatives to the Goals

The alternative compliance should be targeted to the affordable housing goals, because the alternatives can advance the goals that the on-site production cannot (the deeper affordability, the special needs, the high-opportunity locations, the preservation), and the targeting maximizes the benefit. The rule is to target the alternatives to the goals, using the in-lieu fee to fund the deeper affordability (the units at the 30 percent and the 50 percent AMI that the on-site cannot provide), the off-site to produce the larger units (the three-bedroom and the four-bedroom that the on-site does not include), the rehabilitation to preserve the affordable stock (the existing affordable units that are expiring), and the land dedication to create the new affordable housing (the sites that the trust fund can develop). The targeting should be based on the housing needs analysis, because the alternatives should address the needs that the on-site does not, and the targeting that is not based on the needs does not address the needs. The targeting should be evaluated regularly, because the needs change, and the targeting that was appropriate at adoption may not be appropriate later, and the regular evaluation adjusts the targeting. The targeting should be coordinated with the other housing programs, because the alternatives are one tool, and the coordinated approach produces the housing system that the goals require.

## Common Traps

### In-Lieu Fee Too Low, Equivalent Units Not Produced

The in-lieu fee is set too low, and the fee does not fund the equivalent affordable units, because the fee does not account for the full cost of the on-site production, and the developer rationally chooses the cheaper fee. The trap is that the low fee does not produce the equivalent units. The mechanism is that the fee was calculated based on the simplified formula rather than the full cost, and the fee was not updated. The false signal is that "the fee is an option." The harm is that the fee revenue does not produce the equivalent units, the affordable housing stock is not built, the developer avoids the on-site obligation, and the program that should produce the housing does not.

### Fee Revenue Not Spent, Trust Fund Accumulates

The fee revenue accumulates in the trust fund without the expenditure, and the affordable housing is not produced, because the fund does not have the expenditure plan or the capacity to spend. The trap is that the accumulating fund does not produce the housing. The mechanism is that the expenditure plan was not established, and the revenue accumulated without the project pipeline. The false signal is that "the fee revenue is collected." The harm is that the affordable housing is not produced, the revenue accumulates, the public questions the fund, the developer questions the fee, and the program that should produce the housing does not.

### Off-Site Used for the Segregation

The off-site alternative is used to produce the affordable units in the segregated location, and the affordable residents are concentrated rather than integrated, because the off-site is allowed without the location standards. The trap is that the off-site enables the segregation. The mechanism is that the location standards were not established, and the off-site was approved for the cheaper land. The false signal is that "the off-site produces the units." The harm is that the affordable units are segregated, the fair housing is undermined, the concentration occurs, and the off-site that should produce the equivalent housing produces the inferior housing.

### Alternative Not Equivalent, Becomes the Loophole

The alternative compliance is not equivalent to the on-site production, and it becomes the loophole that the developer uses to avoid the obligation, because the equivalence is not required and enforced. The trap is that the non-equivalent alternative is the loophole. The mechanism is that the equivalence was not established, and the alternative was approved without the verification. The false signal is that "the alternative is an option." The harm is that the developer avoids the on-site obligation, the affordable housing is not produced equivalently, the program is undermined, and the alternative that should be the option becomes the default.

### Alternatives Not Targeted to the Goals

The alternatives are not targeted to the affordable housing goals, and the fee revenue, the off-site, and the rehabilitation are used for the purposes that do not advance the goals, because the targeting is not established. The trap is that the untargeted alternatives do not advance the goals. The mechanism is that the targeting was not established, and the alternatives were used for the convenient rather than the strategic purposes. The false signal is that "the alternatives produce the affordable housing." The harm is that the deeper affordability is not produced, the special needs are not addressed, the high-opportunity locations are not targeted, and the alternatives that should advance the goals do not.

## Self-Check

- Is the in-lieu fee set to be equivalent to the on-site production, with the full cost calculation and the regular update?
- Is the fee revenue managed through the affordable housing trust fund, with the clear purpose, the governance, the accounting, and the expenditure plan?
- Are the off-site and the rehabilitation alternatives evaluated against the criteria, with the equivalence and the location standards?
- Is the equivalence required and enforced, with the verification before the approval and the loophole prevention?
- Are the alternatives targeted to the goals, with the deeper affordability, the larger units, the high-opportunity locations, and the preservation?
- Is the fee equivalent to the on-site, or does it underfund the production?
- Is the fee revenue spent, or does it accumulate without the production?
- Is the off-site used for the integration, or for the segregation?
- Is the alternative equivalent, or is it the loophole?
- Are the alternatives targeted to the goals, or are they used for the convenient purposes?
