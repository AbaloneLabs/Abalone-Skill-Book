# Inclusionary Zoning Unit Type and Income Targeting

Inclusionary zoning requires that a share of the units in the new development be affordable, and the income targeting (the AMI levels the units serve), the unit-type mix (the bedrooms and the types the units include), and the affordability duration (the years the units remain affordable) determine whether the program produces the housing that the community needs. The judgment problem is that the income targeting that is too high (the 120 percent AMI units) does not serve the households that need the affordable housing, the unit-type mix that is too narrow (the one-bedroom units only) does not serve the families and the larger households, and the affordability duration that is too short (the 30 years) loses the units to the market, and the calibration of these dimensions determines the program's effectiveness. The planner must navigate the tradeoffs (the deeper affordability that serves fewer households versus the shallower affordability that serves more, the larger units that serve the families versus the smaller units that serve the individuals, the longer duration that preserves the stock versus the shorter duration that the development accepts) to produce the program that meets the community's housing needs. This skill covers the targeting and the mix decisions that determine whether the inclusionary zoning produces the housing the community needs. It complements the broader inclusionary-zoning skill by focusing on the program design dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Target the Incomes That the Market Underserves

The inclusionary zoning program should target the incomes that the market does not serve, because the market produces the housing for the higher incomes, and the inclusionary units should fill the gap that the market leaves. The rule is to target the incomes that the market underserves, using the area median income (AMI) thresholds that correspond to the community's housing needs (the extremely low income at 30 percent AMI, the very low income at 50 percent AMI, the low income at 80 percent AMI, the moderate income at 120 percent AMI), and calibrating the targets to the local housing market and the population. The income targeting should be based on the housing needs analysis (the cost-burdened households, the gap between the incomes and the housing costs), because the targeting that is not based on the needs does not serve the needs. The income targeting should include the deeper affordability (the extremely low and the very low income) when the community's needs include these incomes, because the moderate-income targeting does not serve the households that face the most severe housing stress. The income targeting should be layered (the different units at the different AMI levels), because the single AMI target does not serve the range of the incomes, and the layered targeting produces the income mix that the community needs.

### Require the Unit Types That Serve the Households

The inclusionary units should include the unit types (the studios, the one-bedroom, the two-bedroom, the three-bedroom) that serve the households the community houses, because the unit-type mix that is too narrow (the one-bedroom only) does not serve the families and the larger households, and the inclusionary program should serve the full range of the households. The rule is to require the unit types that serve the households, setting the bedroom distribution that reflects the community's household composition (the families with children, the seniors, the individuals, the large households), and requiring the larger units (the two-bedroom and the three-bedroom) when the community's needs include the families. The unit-type mix should be proportional to the market-rate units, because the inclusionary units should be integrated into the development and should provide the comparable unit types, and the all-one-bedroom inclusionary units segregated from the all-three-bedroom market units do not serve the families. The unit-type mix should address the special needs (the accessible units, the large households, the multigenerational households), because the inclusionary program should serve the households that the market does not, and the special needs are the underserved needs. The unit-type mix should be enforced through the development review, because the developer may default to the cheaper unit types (the studios and the one-bedroom), and the enforcement ensures the mix.

### Set the Affordability Duration to Preserve the Stock

The affordability duration (the years the units remain affordable) determines whether the inclusionary units are preserved or lost to the market, and the short duration (the 30 years) loses the units to the market, while the long duration (the 99 years or the permanent) preserves the stock. The rule is to set the affordability duration to preserve the stock, requiring the longest duration that the development will accept (the permanent affordability where feasible, the 99 years, the 55 to 60 years), and avoiding the short durations (the 30 years) that lose the units. The affordability duration should be secured by the deed restriction or the covenant that runs with the land, because the restriction ensures the affordability survives the ownership changes, and the unrecorded restriction is the vulnerable restriction. The affordability duration should include the monitoring and the enforcement mechanism, because the restriction without the monitoring is the restriction that is violated, and the monitoring (the annual certification, the tenant income verification) ensures the compliance. The affordability duration should address the resale and the succession, because the owner-occupied units need the resale restrictions (the shared appreciation, the right of first refusal) and the rental units need the tenant succession rules, and the unaddressed resale and the succession are the loopholes that lose the affordability.

### Integrate the Units Within the Development

The inclusionary units should be integrated within the development (the mixed floors, the mixed buildings, the comparable design and the finishes), because the segregated units (the separate building, the separate entrance, the inferior design) stigmatize the affordable residents and violate the fair housing principles. The rule is to integrate the units within the development, requiring the inclusionary units to be distributed throughout the development (not clustered on the lower floors or the separate building), the comparable design and the finishes (the same exterior, the same common areas, the comparable interior), and the access to the amenities (the same lobby, the same courtyard, the same parking). The integration should be required by the ordinance and enforced through the development review, because the developer may segregate the units to reduce the cost or to market the market-rate units, and the enforcement ensures the integration. The integration should address the "poor door" issue (the separate entrance for the affordable units), because the separate entrance is the stigmatization that the fair housing prohibits, and the integration prevents the segregation. The integration should be documented in the development agreement and the deed restriction, because the documentation ensures the integration survives the ownership changes.

### Align With the Fair Housing and the Income-Mix Objectives

The inclusionary zoning program should align with the fair housing objectives (the affirmatively furthering fair housing, the non-discrimination, the access to the opportunity) and the income-mix objectives (the deconcentration of poverty, the economic integration), because the program that produces the affordable units in the high-opportunity areas furthers the fair housing, and the program that concentrates the affordable units in the low-opportunity areas undermines it. The rule is to align with the fair housing and the income-mix objectives, targeting the inclusionary units in the high-opportunity areas (the good schools, the jobs, the transit), providing the mobility and the counseling (the housing choice vouchers, the search assistance) to help the households access the opportunity, and avoiding the concentration of the affordable units in the low-opportunity areas. The alignment should include the analysis of the impediments to the fair housing choice, because the impediments (the zoning, the NIMBY, the lending) prevent the affordable housing in the high-opportunity areas, and the program should address the impediments. The alignment should include the monitoring of the income mix and the demographic outcomes, because the program should produce the integration, and the monitoring identifies whether it does. The alignment should be coordinated with the other housing programs (the vouchers, the public housing, the LIHTC), because the inclusionary zoning is one tool, and the coordinated approach produces the housing system that the fair housing requires.

## Common Traps

### Income Targeting Too High, Market Gap Not Filled

The income targeting is set too high (the 120 percent AMI), and the units serve the moderate-income households that the market partially serves, and the extremely low and the very low income households that the market does not serve are not helped. The trap is that the high targeting does not fill the market gap. The mechanism is that the targeting was calibrated to the developer feasibility rather than the housing needs, and the deeper affordability was not required. The false signal is that "the program produces the affordable units." The harm is that the households that need the housing most are not served, the market gap is not filled, the program produces the units that the market would partially produce, and the housing needs that the program should address are not addressed.

### Unit-Type Mix Too Narrow, Families Not Served

The unit-type mix is too narrow (the one-bedroom and the studios only), and the families with children and the larger households are not served, because the units do not include the bedrooms they need. The trap is that the narrow unit-type mix excludes the families. The mechanism is that the bedroom distribution was not required, and the developer defaulted to the cheaper unit types. The false signal is that "the program produces the affordable units." The harm is that the families are not served, the larger households are excluded, the program produces the units that do not match the household composition, and the housing needs of the families are not met.

### Affordability Duration Too Short, Units Lost to the Market

The affordability duration is too short (the 30 years), and the units are lost to the market at the expiration, because the short duration does not preserve the stock. The trap is that the short duration loses the units. The mechanism is that the longer duration was not required, and the developer accepted only the short duration. The false signal is that "the units are affordable." The harm is that the units convert to the market, the affordable stock is lost, the program that built the stock does not preserve it, and the community loses the units that the program produced.

### Units Segregated, Fair Housing Violated

The units are segregated within the development (the separate building, the separate entrance, the inferior design), and the affordable residents are stigmatized, and the fair housing is violated. The trap is that the segregation stigmatizes and violates the fair housing. The mechanism is that the integration was not required, and the developer segregated the units to reduce the cost or to market the market-rate units. The false signal is that "the units are in the development." The harm is that the affordable residents are stigmatized, the fair housing is violated, the legal challenge follows, and the program that should integrate does not.

### Fair Housing and Income Mix Not Aligned

The inclusionary units are concentrated in the low-opportunity areas, and the high-opportunity areas are not targeted, and the fair housing and the income-mix objectives are undermined, because the program concentrates rather than integrates. The trap is that the concentration undermines the fair housing. The mechanism is that the high-opportunity targeting was not required, and the program proceeded without the fair housing alignment. The false signal is that "the program produces the affordable units." The harm is that the affordable units are concentrated in the low-opportunity areas, the high-opportunity areas are not integrated, the fair housing is not furthered, and the program that should deconcentrate the poverty concentrates it.

## Self-Check

- Are the income targets calibrated to the housing needs, with the deeper affordability for the extremely low and the very low income?
- Is the unit-type mix required to serve the households, with the bedroom distribution that reflects the household composition and the larger units for the families?
- Is the affordability duration set to preserve the stock, with the longest feasible duration, the deed restriction, and the monitoring and enforcement?
- Are the units integrated within the development, with the distribution, the comparable design, and the access to the amenities?
- Is the program aligned with the fair housing and the income-mix objectives, with the high-opportunity targeting, the mobility, and the monitoring?
- Do the income targets fill the market gap, or do they serve the incomes the market partially serves?
- Does the unit-type mix serve the families, or does it default to the smaller units?
- Does the affordability duration preserve the stock, or does it lose the units to the market?
- Are the units integrated, or are they segregated within the development?
- Is the program aligned with the fair housing, or does it concentrate the affordable units?
