# Scenario Preferred Plan Synthesis and Adoption

The scenario planning produces the alternative futures, and the synthesis of the preferred scenario — the future the community chooses and the plan adopts — is the decision point that converts the exploration into the commitment. The judgment problem is that the preferred scenario is not merely the scenario that scores the best on the indicators, but the scenario that the community supports, the decision-makers will adopt, and the implementation can deliver, and the synthesis requires the reconciliation of the community input, the technical analysis, and the political feasibility. A preferred scenario that is synthesized well (the community-owned, the evidence-based, the feasible) becomes the adopted plan that guides the implementation, while a preferred scenario that is synthesized poorly (the planner-imposed, the analysis-ignored, the infeasible) becomes the plan that is not adopted or not implemented. This skill covers the synthesis and the adoption decisions that determine whether the scenario planning produces the plan that guides the future. It complements the broader scenario-planning skill by focusing on the synthesis and the adoption dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Synthesize the Preferred Scenario From the Community Input and the Technical Analysis

The preferred scenario should be synthesized from the community input and the technical analysis, because the preferred scenario that is based only on the community input may not be technically sound, and the preferred scenario that is based only on the technical analysis may not be community-supported, and the synthesis of both produces the scenario that is sound and supported. The rule is to synthesize the preferred scenario from the community input and the technical analysis, using the community input (the preferences, the values, the priorities expressed through the engagement), the technical analysis (the indicator scores, the feasibility, the tradeoffs), and the reconciliation (the resolution of the conflicts between the input and the analysis). The community input should be weighted appropriately, because the community is the ultimate beneficiary, and the input reflects the community's values. The technical analysis should inform the input, because the analysis provides the information the community needs to make the informed choice, and the uninformed input is the less valuable input. The reconciliation should be transparent, because the conflicts between the input and the analysis should be acknowledged and resolved openly, and the hidden resolution erodes the trust. The synthesis should produce the hybrid scenario (the combination of the elements from the different alternatives), because the preferred scenario may combine the best elements, and the pure alternative may not be the best.

### Reconcile the Competing Visions and the Conflicts

The scenario planning often reveals the competing visions (the growth versus the preservation, the density versus the character, the economic development versus the environment), and the preferred scenario must reconcile these visions, because the unreconciled visions produce the plan that pleases no one, and the reconciled visions produce the plan that the community can support. The rule is to reconcile the competing visions and the conflicts, using the deliberation (the structured discussion of the tradeoffs), the compromise (the give-and-take that produces the acceptable middle), the prioritization (the ranking of the values that guides the choice), and the phasing (the sequencing that allows the different visions at the different times). The deliberation should be structured, because the unstructured discussion does not resolve, and the structured deliberation does. The compromise should be principled, because the principled compromise (based on the values and the evidence) builds the trust, and the unprincipled compromise (based on the power) erodes it. The prioritization should be explicit, because the explicit prioritization clarifies the choice, and the implicit prioritization obscures it. The phasing should allow the different visions at the different times, because the community may want the preservation now and the growth later, and the phasing accommodates the sequence.

### Test the Preferred Scenario for the Feasibility and the Implementation

The preferred scenario should be tested for the feasibility (the market, the fiscal, the regulatory, the political) and the implementation (the actions, the timeline, the responsibilities), because the preferred scenario that is not feasible or not implementable is the scenario that remains the vision, and the tested scenario becomes the plan. The rule is to test the preferred scenario for the feasibility and the implementation, conducting the market feasibility (the demand for the development the scenario envisions), the fiscal feasibility (the revenue and the cost), the regulatory feasibility (the zoning and the regulations the scenario requires), the political feasibility (the support and the opposition), and the implementation analysis (the actions, the timeline, the responsibilities, the funding). The market feasibility should test the demand, because the development that the market does not demand will not occur, and the test identifies the gap. The fiscal feasibility should test the revenue and the cost, because the scenario that the community cannot afford is the scenario that is not implemented, and the test identifies the shortfall. The regulatory feasibility should test the zoning, because the scenario that requires the zoning the community will not adopt is the scenario that is blocked, and the test identifies the barrier. The political feasibility should test the support, because the scenario that the decision-makers will not adopt is the scenario that is not adopted, and the test identifies the opposition. The implementation analysis should identify the actions, because the scenario without the actions is the vision, and the scenario with the actions is the plan.

### Convert the Preferred Scenario Into the Adopted Plan

The preferred scenario should be converted into the adopted plan, because the adoption is the legal and the political act that gives the scenario the authority, and the unadopted scenario is the vision that does not guide. The rule is to convert the preferred scenario into the adopted plan, translating the scenario into the plan goals, the policies, the land use map, the implementation actions, and the indicators, and adopting the plan through the formal process (the hearing, the review, the adoption by the governing body). The translation should be comprehensive, because the scenario contains the vision, the land use, the transportation, the housing, the environment, and the other elements, and the plan should reflect all the elements. The translation should be specific, because the specific plan (the clear goals, the measurable policies, the defined actions) guides the implementation, and the vague plan does not. The adoption process should be public and the deliberate, because the plan is the community's document, and the public process ensures the legitimacy. The adoption should include the decision-maker engagement, because the decision-makers adopt the plan, and the engagement builds the support. The adoption should be followed by the implementation, because the adopted plan that is not implemented is the plan that is wasted, and the implementation is the purpose.

### Transition From the Visioning to the Implementation

The scenario planning is the visioning, and the adopted plan is the transition to the implementation, and the management of this transition determines whether the vision becomes the reality. The rule is to transition from the visioning to the implementation, developing the implementation plan (the actions, the timeline, the responsibilities, the funding, the indicators), establishing the implementation structure (the staff, the committee, the partnerships), and committing to the monitoring and the reporting (the tracking of the progress, the reporting to the community). The implementation plan should be specific and the funded, because the specific and the funded plan is implemented, and the vague and the unfunded plan is not. The implementation structure should have the clear authority and the capacity, because the implementation requires the organization, and the structure provides it. The monitoring and the reporting should be ongoing, because the implementation is the long-term process, and the monitoring tracks the progress and the reporting maintains the accountability. The transition should include the community involvement, because the community that participated in the visioning should participate in the implementation, and the involvement sustains the engagement. The transition should include the plan amendment process, because the conditions change, and the plan should be amendable to reflect the changes, and the amendment process maintains the relevance.

## Common Traps

### Preferred Scenario Imposed, Community Not Owned

The preferred scenario is imposed by the planner or the decision-makers, and the community does not own it, because the synthesis was not community-based. The trap is that the imposed scenario is not owned. The mechanism is that the community input was not synthesized, and the scenario was selected by the planner. The false signal is that "the preferred scenario is selected." The harm is that the community does not own the scenario, the support is absent, the adoption is challenged, and the scenario that should be community-owned is imposed.

### Competing Visions Not Reconciled, Plan Pleases No One

The competing visions are not reconciled, and the plan pleases no one, because the reconciliation was not pursued. The trap is that the unreconciled visions produce the plan that pleases no one. The mechanism is that the deliberation and the compromise were not conducted, and the visions were left in the conflict. The false signal is that "the plan is adopted." The harm is that the plan pleases no one, the opposition arises from all sides, the implementation is blocked, and the visions that should have been reconciled are not.

### Feasibility Not Tested, Scenario Remains the Vision

The feasibility is not tested, and the scenario remains the vision, because the market, the fiscal, the regulatory, and the political feasibility were not assessed. The trap is that the untested scenario remains the vision. The mechanism is that the feasibility was not tested, and the scenario was adopted without the assessment. The false signal is that "the plan is adopted." The harm is that the development does not occur, the funding is not available, the zoning is not adopted, the implementation fails, and the scenario that should be the plan remains the vision.

### Plan Not Adopted, Scenario Lacks the Authority

The plan is not adopted through the formal process, and the scenario lacks the authority, because the conversion and the adoption were not pursued. The trap is that the unadopted plan lacks the authority. The mechanism is that the formal adoption was not conducted, and the scenario remained the planning document. The false signal is that "we have the plan." The harm is that the plan lacks the authority, the decisions do not follow it, the implementation is optional, and the plan that should guide does not.

### Implementation Not Transitioned, Vision Not Realized

The implementation is not transitioned from the visioning, and the vision is not realized, because the implementation plan and the structure were not established. The trap is that the untransitioned implementation does not realize the vision. The mechanism is that the implementation plan was not developed, and the structure was not established. The false signal is that "the plan is adopted." The harm is that the implementation does not proceed, the actions are not taken, the vision is not realized, and the plan that should be implemented is not.

## Self-Check

- Is the preferred scenario synthesized from the community input and the technical analysis, with the reconciliation and the hybrid combination?
- Are the competing visions reconciled, with the deliberation, the compromise, the prioritization, and the phasing?
- Is the preferred scenario tested for the feasibility, with the market, the fiscal, the regulatory, the political, and the implementation analysis?
- Is the preferred scenario converted into the adopted plan, with the comprehensive translation, the specific policies, and the public adoption?
- Is the visioning transitioned to the implementation, with the implementation plan, the structure, the monitoring, and the community involvement?
- Is the scenario community-owned, or is it imposed?
- Are the visions reconciled, or does the plan please no one?
- Is the feasibility tested, or does the scenario remain the vision?
- Is the plan adopted, or does it lack the authority?
- Is the implementation transitioned, or is the vision not realized?
