# Scenario Quantitative Modeling and Indicators

Scenario planning relies on the quantitative models and the indicators to compare the alternative futures, and the quality of the modeling and the indicator selection determines whether the comparison is informative or misleading. The judgment problem is that the models (the land use, the transportation, the fiscal, the environmental) are the simplifications of the complex reality, the indicators (the metrics that summarize the scenario performance) are the selective representation, and the choice of the models and the indicators shapes the conclusions, and the planner must select and apply the models and the indicators that illuminate rather than distort the tradeoffs. A scenario analysis that is modeled well (the appropriate models, the calibrated assumptions, the comprehensive indicators) informs the decisions, while a scenario analysis that is modeled poorly (the inappropriate models, the uncalibrated assumptions, the selective indicators) misleads the decisions. This skill covers the quantitative modeling and the indicator decisions that determine whether the scenario analysis informs or misleads. It complements the broader scenario-planning skill by focusing on the analytical and the modeling dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Select the Models That Fit the Questions and the Scale

The models should fit the questions the scenario planning asks and the scale at which it operates, because the model that does not fit the question or the scale produces the outputs that do not inform, and the model that fits produces the outputs that do. The rule is to select the models that fit the questions and the scale, choosing the land use model (the allocation of the growth, the development capacity) for the land use questions, the transportation model (the travel demand, the network performance) for the mobility questions, the fiscal model (the revenue, the cost) for the economic questions, and the environmental model (the emissions, the water, the habitat) for the environmental questions. The model selection should match the scale (the regional, the corridor, the neighborhood), because the regional model may not capture the neighborhood detail, and the neighborhood model may not capture the regional interactions. The model selection should match the data availability, because the model that requires the data the community does not have is the model that cannot be run, and the data-appropriate model can. The model selection should match the capacity (the staff, the budget, the time), because the complex model that exceeds the capacity is the model that is not run well, and the appropriate model is. The model selection should be documented, because the documentation provides the transparency, and the undocumented selection is the unexplained selection.

### Calibrate the Models and the Assumptions

The models should be calibrated to the local conditions, and the assumptions should be the documented and the justified, because the uncalibrated model and the undocumented assumption produce the outputs that do not reflect the local reality, and the calibrated model and the documented assumption do. The rule is to calibrate the models and the assumptions, using the local data (the demographics, the employment, the travel, the development), the validation (the model's ability to reproduce the observed conditions), and the assumption documentation (the growth rate, the density, the cost, the preference). The calibration should use the local data, because the default parameters (the national averages) do not reflect the local conditions, and the local data does. The validation should test the model against the observed conditions (the base-year validation), because the model that cannot reproduce the present cannot be trusted for the future, and the validation builds the credibility. The assumption documentation should record the assumptions and the sources, because the assumptions drive the outputs, and the undocumented assumption is the hidden assumption. The assumptions should be tested through the sensitivity analysis (the variation of the key assumptions to test the effect on the outputs), because the sensitivity analysis identifies the assumptions that matter most, and the identification focuses the attention.

### Choose the Indicators That Capture the Tradeoffs

The indicators should capture the tradeoffs among the scenarios, because the scenario comparison is the tradeoff analysis, and the indicators that do not capture the tradeoffs do not inform the comparison. The rule is to choose the indicators that capture the tradeoffs, selecting the indicators across the dimensions (the land use, the transportation, the environment, the equity, the fiscal, the health), the indicators that differentiate the scenarios (the indicators that show the meaningful differences), and the indicators that the community values (the indicators that resonate with the stakeholders). The indicator selection should cover the multiple dimensions, because the single-dimension indicators (only the transportation, only the fiscal) miss the tradeoffs across the dimensions, and the multi-dimension indicators capture them. The indicator selection should differentiate, because the indicators that show the same result for all the scenarios do not inform the choice, and the differentiating indicators do. The indicator selection should resonate with the community, because the indicators that the community does not understand or value do not engage, and the resonating indicators do. The indicators should be presented in the accessible format (the dashboards, the charts, the maps), because the accessible presentation communicates the tradeoffs, and the inaccessible presentation does not.

### Interpret the Model Outputs With the Appropriate Uncertainty

The model outputs should be interpreted with the appropriate uncertainty, because the models are the simplifications, the assumptions are the estimates, and the outputs are the projections, not the predictions, and the interpretation that does not acknowledge the uncertainty overstates the precision and misleads the decisions. The rule is to interpret the model outputs with the appropriate uncertainty, communicating the range (the high and the low estimates), the confidence (the level of the certainty), the limitations (the model's constraints), and the scenarios (the alternative futures the outputs represent). The range should be communicated, because the point estimate (the single number) overstates the precision, and the range conveys the uncertainty. The confidence should be communicated, because the high-confidence output and the low-confidence output warrant the different weight, and the confidence informs the weight. The limitations should be communicated, because the model cannot capture everything, and the limitation disclosure is the honest disclosure. The scenarios should be presented as the alternative futures, not the predictions, because the scenarios are the "what if" explorations, and the presentation as the predictions misleads. The uncertainty communication should be accessible, because the technical uncertainty (the confidence interval, the sensitivity) should be communicated in the way the non-technical audience understands, and the inaccessible communication does not inform.

### Use the Indicators to Compare and to Communicate the Tradeoffs

The indicators should be used to compare the scenarios and to communicate the tradeoffs, because the comparison and the communication are the purpose of the indicators, and the indicators that are not used for the comparison and the communication are the wasted indicators. The rule is to use the indicators to compare and to communicate the tradeoffs, preparing the scenario comparison (the side-by-side indicator values), the tradeoff analysis (the identification of the scenarios' strengths and weaknesses), and the communication (the presentation of the tradeoffs to the stakeholders and the public). The scenario comparison should present the indicators side-by-side, because the side-by-side presentation enables the comparison, and the separate presentation does not. The tradeoff analysis should identify the strengths and the weaknesses of each scenario, because no scenario is the best on all the indicators, and the tradeoff identification clarifies the choice. The communication should present the tradeoffs in the accessible and the honest way, because the tradeoffs are the difficult choices, and the honest communication builds the understanding. The communication should include the community engagement, because the community should weigh in on the tradeoffs, and the engagement ensures the community input.

## Common Traps

### Model Does Not Fit the Question, Outputs Mislead

The model does not fit the question or the scale, and the outputs mislead, because the model was selected without the fit consideration. The trap is that the unfit model misleads. The mechanism is that the fit was not assessed, and the model was selected by the availability. The false signal is that "we modeled the scenarios." The harm is that the outputs do not inform the questions, the scale is wrong, the comparison is misleading, and the model that should illuminate distorts.

### Model Not Calibrated, Outputs Do Not Reflect the Local

The model is not calibrated to the local conditions, and the outputs do not reflect the local reality, because the calibration was not conducted. The trap is that the uncalibrated model does not reflect the local. The mechanism is that the local data was not used, and the default parameters were applied. The false signal is that "we ran the model." The harm is that the outputs do not reflect the local conditions, the comparison is inaccurate, the decisions are misinformed, and the model that should reflect the local does not.

### Indicators Do Not Capture the Tradeoffs, Comparison Uninformative

The indicators do not capture the tradeoffs, and the comparison is uninformative, because the indicators were selected without the tradeoff consideration. The trap is that the non-tradeoff indicators make the comparison uninformative. The mechanism is that the single-dimension or the non-differentiating indicators were selected, and the tradeoffs were missed. The false signal is that "we compared the scenarios." The harm is that the tradeoffs are not identified, the comparison does not inform, the choice is not clarified, and the indicators that should capture the tradeoffs do not.

### Uncertainty Not Communicated, Precision Overstated

The uncertainty is not communicated, and the precision is overstated, because the range, the confidence, and the limitations were not presented. The trap is that the overstated precision misleads. The mechanism is that the uncertainty was not communicated, and the point estimates were presented. The false signal is that "the model shows the result." The harm is that the precision is overstated, the decisions are made with the false confidence, the limitations are not understood, and the communication that should convey the uncertainty does not.

### Indicators Not Used, Comparison Wasted

The indicators are not used to compare or to communicate, and the comparison is wasted, because the indicators were computed but not applied. The trap is that the unused indicators waste the comparison. The mechanism is that the comparison and the communication were not conducted, and the indicators were filed. The false signal is that "we computed the indicators." The harm is that the comparison is not presented, the tradeoffs are not communicated, the choice is not informed, and the indicators that should inform the comparison do not.

## Self-Check

- Are the models selected to fit the questions and the scale, with the documentation and the data and capacity match?
- Are the models calibrated to the local conditions, with the local data, the validation, the assumption documentation, and the sensitivity analysis?
- Are the indicators chosen to capture the tradeoffs, with the multi-dimension coverage, the differentiation, and the community resonance?
- Are the model outputs interpreted with the appropriate uncertainty, with the range, the confidence, the limitations, and the scenario presentation?
- Are the indicators used to compare and to communicate the tradeoffs, with the side-by-side comparison, the tradeoff analysis, and the accessible and honest communication?
- Does the model fit, or does it mislead?
- Is the model calibrated, or does it not reflect the local?
- Do the indicators capture the tradeoffs, or is the comparison uninformative?
- Is the uncertainty communicated, or is the precision overstated?
- Are the indicators used, or is the comparison wasted?
