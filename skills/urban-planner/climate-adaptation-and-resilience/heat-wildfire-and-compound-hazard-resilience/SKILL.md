# Heat, Wildfire, and Compound Hazard Resilience

Extreme heat and wildfire are the climate hazards that kill the most people, and they are intensifying and expanding into geographies and seasons unaccustomed to them. Compound hazards — heat during a power outage, wildfire smoke during a heat wave, flood after fire — interact in ways that magnify the harm and defeat single-hazard planning. The judgment problem is that heat and wildfire vulnerability are shaped by land use, building stock, vegetation, and social factors that planning can influence, but the response is often siloed in emergency management and public health, disconnected from the land use and design decisions that determine exposure. The skill is in integrating heat and wildfire resilience into planning — tree canopy, building design, land use in the wildland-urban interface, cooling infrastructure, evacuation capacity — and in addressing the compound hazards and the equity dimensions that determine who dies.

## Core Rules

### Plan for Heat as a Built Environment and Equity Problem

Extreme heat is not primarily a weather event; it is a built environment problem (the urban heat island, the building stock, the lack of shade and cooling) and an equity problem (lower-income communities, communities of color, the elderly, and outdoor workers are more exposed and less able to cool). The rule is to plan for heat at the built environment and equity scale: map heat exposure (surface and air temperature, heat island intensity) against tree canopy, impervious surface, building age and cooling availability, and social vulnerability, to identify the neighborhoods and populations most at risk; and to invest in cooling strategies (tree canopy and green space, cool and reflective surfaces, cooling centers and resilience hubs, building cooling requirements, energy assistance for cooling) prioritized by heat exposure and social vulnerability. Heat planning that treats heat as a public health warning system, without addressing the built environment that creates the exposure and the equity that determines the effect, will not reduce heat mortality.

### Manage the Wildland-Urban Interface Through Land Use and Design

Wildfire risk in the wildland-urban interface (WUI) — where development meets wildland vegetation — is fundamentally a land use problem, and the most effective mitigation is to limit new development in high-fire-risk areas and to require fire-resistant design where development exists or is allowed. The rule is to map the WUI and fire risk (using fire history, vegetation, topography, weather, and climate projections), to restrict or carefully regulate new development in the highest-risk areas (using zoning, building codes, and subdivision standards), to require defensible space and fire-resistant construction (ignition-resistant materials, ember-resistant vents, access for firefighting, water supply) in the WUI, and to avoid the pattern where new development in fire-prone areas exposes residents and firefighters to predictable risk. The political pressure to allow WUI development is intense (the demand for rural and scenic living), but development in high-risk areas creates predictable future casualties and public cost, and the planner's role is to surface the risk honestly in the land use decision.

### Plan for Compound Hazards and Cascading Failures

Single-hazard planning misses the compound events and cascading failures that produce the worst outcomes: a heat wave during a power outage (no air conditioning), wildfire smoke during a heat wave (cannot open windows), flood or landslide after wildfire (the burned slopes shed water), evacuation during concurrent events (overloaded routes and shelters). The rule is to plan for compound hazards: identify the combinations that could occur, analyze the cascading failures they would trigger (power, water, transportation, communication, healthcare), and design resilience for the compound scenario, not just the single hazard. This includes resilience hubs (facilities with backup power, water, and cooling that function during outages), redundant systems, mutual aid arrangements, and land use decisions that do not create compound vulnerabilities (development in both flood- and fire-prone areas, for example). Compound planning requires engaging the utility, public works, emergency management, and healthcare systems, because the cascading failures cross their jurisdictions.

### Integrate Hazard Mitigation With Equity and Public Health

Heat and wildfire disproportionately harm lower-income communities, communities of color, the elderly, the disabled, outdoor workers, and the unhoused — populations with greater exposure (less tree canopy, older housing, occupational exposure), greater sensitivity (health conditions, medications), and less adaptive capacity (cannot afford cooling, cannot evacuate, cannot miss work). The rule is to integrate equity and public health into hazard mitigation: prioritize cooling and resilience investment in the most vulnerable communities, design evacuation and sheltering plans for people without vehicles or with access and functional needs, protect outdoor workers (labor standards, scheduling), and address the unhoused population (who cannot shelter from heat or smoke) explicitly in the plan. Hazard mitigation that protects the general population without targeting the most vulnerable will reduce average risk while leaving the highest mortality untouched, because the deaths concentrate in the populations the general plan does not reach.

### Use Nature-Based Solutions for Hazard Mitigation

Nature-based solutions — urban tree canopy and green space for heat, wetland and floodplain restoration for flood, fuels reduction and prescribed fire for wildfire — provide hazard mitigation alongside co-benefits (air quality, recreation, habitat, carbon sequestration) and are often more durable and cost-effective than gray infrastructure. The rule is to integrate nature-based solutions into hazard mitigation planning: expand tree canopy and green space in heat-vulnerable neighborhoods, restore wetlands and floodplains for flood storage, manage vegetation and use prescribed fire to reduce wildfire fuels, and protect the natural systems that provide hazard mitigation. Nature-based solutions require long-term stewardship (trees must be maintained, ecosystems managed) and they are not always sufficient (a tree canopy reduces heat but does not eliminate it; prescribed fire reduces but does not eliminate wildfire risk), but they offer multi-benefit mitigation that gray infrastructure cannot match, and they should be the first option where feasible.

## Common Traps

### Heat Treated as a Warning, Not a Built Environment Problem

The heat plan consists of a public warning system and cooling centers, without addressing the urban heat island, the lack of tree canopy, the building stock without cooling, and the equity distribution of exposure. The trap is that the plan responds to heat events rather than reducing heat vulnerability. The mechanism is that heat is treated as an emergency management issue rather than a planning issue. The false signal is that "we have a heat plan." The harm is that heat mortality continues (because the built environment that creates the exposure is unchanged), the most vulnerable remain most at risk, and the plan is cited as inadequate when the next heat wave kills.

### WUI Development Allowed Without Honest Risk Disclosure

New development is approved in the wildland-urban interface with high fire risk, on the assumption that fire protection will be provided, without honest disclosure of the risk to residents or adequate mitigation requirements. The trap is that the development creates predictable future casualties. The mechanism is that the political pressure for development and the assumption of fire protection substitute for risk analysis. The false signal is that "we require defensible space." The harm is that residents and firefighters are exposed to predictable fire risk, the public cost of fire protection escalates, the development is destroyed in the inevitable fire, and the pattern of high-risk WUI development continues.

### Single-Hazard Planning Missing Compound Events

The plan addresses heat, wildfire, and flood separately, without analyzing the compound events (heat plus outage, smoke plus heat, flood after fire) that produce the worst outcomes, and the response fails when a compound event occurs. The trap is that single-hazard planning is simpler but inadequate. The mechanism is that the hazards were planned in silos. The false signal is that "we have hazard plans." The harm is that the compound event triggers cascading failures the plans did not anticipate (no cooling during the outage, no shelter from both heat and smoke, evacuation routes overloaded), the mortality is higher than single-hazard planning predicted, and the plans are revealed as inadequate under the conditions that actually occur.

### Equity Ignored in Hazard Mitigation

The hazard mitigation protects the general population or the influential areas, without targeting the most vulnerable communities, and the mortality concentrates in the populations the plan did not reach. The trap is that the plan reduces average risk while leaving the highest vulnerability untouched. The mechanism is that equity was not integrated into the mitigation prioritization. The false signal is that "we're protecting the community." The harm is that the elderly, the unhoused, outdoor workers, lower-income communities, and communities of color continue to die at disproportionate rates, the equity dimension of climate mortality is unaddressed, and the plan's headline success masks the concentrated harm.

### Gray Infrastructure Default Over Nature-Based Solutions

The hazard mitigation defaults to gray infrastructure (sea walls, cooling centers, fire breaks) without considering nature-based solutions (tree canopy, wetland restoration, prescribed fire) that provide mitigation alongside co-benefits. The trap is that the gray solution addresses only the hazard while the nature-based solution addresses the hazard and provides additional value. The mechanism is that gray infrastructure is familiar and fundable while nature-based solutions require ecological expertise and long-term stewardship. The false signal is that "we're mitigating the hazard." The harm is that the co-benefits (air quality, recreation, habitat, carbon) are lost, the mitigation is less durable and more expensive over time, and the community receives a single-purpose project where a multi-benefit solution was feasible.

## Self-Check

- Does the heat plan address the built environment (tree canopy, cool surfaces, building cooling) and equity, not just the warning system?
- Is WUI development restricted or carefully regulated based on mapped fire risk, with honest disclosure and adequate mitigation, rather than allowed on the assumption of protection?
- Does the plan address compound hazards and cascading failures (heat plus outage, smoke plus heat, flood after fire), not just single hazards?
- Is hazard mitigation prioritized to protect the most vulnerable populations (elderly, unhoused, outdoor workers, lower-income, communities of color), not just the general population?
- Are nature-based solutions (tree canopy, wetland restoration, prescribed fire) integrated as the first option where feasible, alongside gray infrastructure?
- Have I avoided treating heat as a warning rather than a built environment and equity problem?
- Is WUI development disclosing and mitigating the fire risk honestly, or creating predictable future casualties?
- Does the plan function under compound events, or only under the single hazards it was designed for?
- Does the mitigation reach the populations with the highest mortality, or only reduce average risk?
- Are nature-based solutions considered before defaulting to gray infrastructure, capturing the co-benefits gray cannot provide?
