# Municipal Service Extension and Infrastructure Financing

Annexation and boundary expansion are only meaningful if the municipality can actually deliver the services that justify its authority, and the capacity to extend those services is usually the binding constraint on whether growth pays for itself. A municipality that annexes territory without a credible plan for extending water, sewer, police, and fire service inherits obligations it cannot meet, while a municipality that over-builds infrastructure to attract development may strand assets whose rate base never materializes. The judgment problem is that service extension is simultaneously an engineering question (what capacity exists, what must be added, where does the pipe or main go?), a financial question (who pays, over what time horizon, and does the revenue cover the operating and debt service costs?), a legal question (what do state statutes and the annexation agreement require the municipality to provide, and on what timeline?), and a political question (will existing ratepayers subsidize new growth, and will the new residents accept the rates and taxes that result?). This skill covers the decisions that determine whether service extension supports fiscally sustainable growth or produces stranded assets, rate shocks, service gaps, and intergovernmental disputes. It complements the broader annexation skill by focusing on the infrastructure and fiscal mechanics that make a boundary change operable.

## Core Rules

### Size Infrastructure to the Real Demand Horizon, Not the Aspirational One

Service extension is driven by a demand forecast — how many equivalent residential units, how many gallons per day, how many calls for service — and the forecast determines the capacity that gets built. The trap is that demand forecasts for newly developing territory are often aspirational, built on developer pro formas or comprehensive-plan build-out assumptions that assume rapid absorption, and infrastructure sized to that forecast is oversized if absorption is slow. The rule is to size infrastructure to a realistic demand horizon that distinguishes committed demand (projects with approved plats, signed utility agreements, or construction schedules) from speculative demand (comprehensive-plan capacity that depends on market conditions the municipality does not control), and to phase capacity to track the committed demand while preserving the ability to expand. Phased capacity — building a line sized for near-term demand with easements and a plant design that allows expansion — costs more per unit in the short run but avoids the stranded-asset risk that comes with building full build-out capacity before the rate base exists. The sizing analysis should explicitly state the absorption assumption it relies on, the break-even point at which the infrastructure's revenue covers its cost, and the downside scenario if absorption is slower, because these are the numbers that determine whether the extension is fiscally defensible.

### Assign Costs to the Growth That Causes Them Through Defensible Financing

Service extension costs money, and the central financial question is who pays: the new growth that creates the need, the existing ratepayers and taxpayers, or some combination. The rule is to assign costs to the growth that causes them, using the financing tools available (impact fees, system development charges, tap fees, special assessments, developer extension agreements, revenue bonds backed by the new area's rates), and to design the financing so that existing customers do not subsidize new growth unless that subsidy is an explicit, adopted policy choice. This requires a cost-of-service analysis that separates the capacity created for new growth (which growth should pay for) from the capacity that benefits the existing system (which the system should pay for), because infrastructure projects often serve both functions and the allocation determines who bears the cost. Developer extension agreements, where the developer builds and dedicates the infrastructure and is reimbursed as additional customers connect, are a powerful tool for aligning cost with causation, but they require careful drafting of reimbursement terms, oversizing obligations, and the timeline over which reimbursements accrue. The financing structure should be defensible against legal challenge (impact fees must meet nexus and proportionality standards) and against political challenge (ratepayers will object if their rates rise to subsidize new growth they did not request), so the cost allocation must be documented and the methodology transparent.

### Analyze Revenue Adequacy Across the Full Life-Cycle, Not Just Construction

The fiscal case for service extension often rests on the construction cost and the initial connection revenue, but the binding constraint is whether the revenue covers the full life-cycle cost: debt service, operations and maintenance, eventual replacement, and the cost of serving customers who are expensive to serve (low-density, long-line, high-call areas). The rule is to analyze revenue adequacy across the full life-cycle, using a fiscal impact model that projects operating revenue (rates, taxes, fees) against operating and capital cost over a twenty- to thirty-year horizon, with explicit assumptions about inflation, rate increases, and the timing of replacement. A common failure is that the extension is justified by the connection revenue and the construction is grant- or bond-funded, but the operating revenue is insufficient to maintain the system as it ages, producing deferred maintenance and eventual failure that the original fiscal analysis did not anticipate. The revenue adequacy analysis should stress-test the assumptions: what happens if absorption is half the forecast, if rates cannot be raised politically, if a major customer leaves, if a regulatory upgrade is required mid-life? If the extension cannot cover its life-cycle cost under plausible stress scenarios, it is not fiscally sustainable regardless of how attractive the near-term numbers look.

### Honor the Service-Extension Commitments in Annexation Agreements and Statute

Annexation agreements and state statutes often impose service-extension obligations — the municipality must provide water and sewer within a stated period, must extend police and fire protection, must not charge differential rates — and these obligations are legally enforceable and politically visible. The rule is to honor the service-extension commitments in the annexation agreement and statute, and to verify before annexation that the municipality can actually meet them, because a failure to extend promised service generates litigation, de-annexation petitions, and political backlash that undermines future annexation. The verification should be concrete: does the water system have the capacity and pressure to serve the area, is the sewer main within reach or must it be extended, what is the police and fire response time and is it adequate, what is the cost of meeting the commitment and is it funded? Where the commitment cannot be met on the statutory timeline, the municipality should negotiate a realistic timeline in the agreement, secure the funding, or decline the annexation, rather than annexing and then failing to deliver. The agreement should also address the edge cases: what happens if the developer does not build, if the area does not develop as projected, if the cost of service exceeds the estimate — because these are the conditions under which the service-extension commitment becomes a liability.

### Coordinate Service Boundaries with Counties and Special Districts

Municipal service extension does not occur in a vacuum; the territory is often already served by a county, a special district (water, sewer, fire, park), or another municipality, and extending municipal service into that territory creates overlaps, gaps, and disputes over jurisdiction, revenue, and responsibility. The rule is to coordinate service boundaries with counties and special districts before extending service, through service agreements, boundary plans, or de-annexation of the area from the overlapping district, because uncoordinated service extension produces duplicate billing, conflicting standards, and intergovernmental conflict that persists for decades. The coordination should address the specific frictions: which entity provides which service, how are the costs and revenues allocated, what happens to the special district's debt and assets, how are standards reconciled, and what is the process for future boundary changes? In states with boundary review boards or municipal annexation authorities, the coordination may be a statutory requirement, and the planner should understand the process and use it. The goal is a coherent service map where every resident knows who provides what, where the boundaries are, and where the responsibility lies — not a patchwork of overlapping and underlapping districts that no one can administer.

## Common Traps

### Stranded Assets from Over-Building to Speculative Demand

The municipality builds water and sewer capacity sized to the comprehensive-plan build-out, the development does not materialize on the projected timeline, and the infrastructure sits underused while its debt service and maintenance costs are borne by the existing ratepayers. The trap is that oversized infrastructure is a stranded asset whose cost falls on those who did not cause the need. The mechanism is that the demand forecast was aspirational, the capacity was built to the forecast, and the absorption was slower than projected. The false signal is that "we are building for the future." The harm is that rates rise to cover the underused asset, existing ratepayers subsidize speculative growth, the political support for future extension erodes, and the municipality may face rating downgrades if the debt cannot be serviced from the weak rate base.

### Existing Ratepayers Subsidizing New Growth

The service extension is financed through system-wide rates or general-fund contributions rather than growth-pays-its-own-way tools, and the existing ratepayers and taxpayers bear the cost of serving new development that generates the need. The trap is that the cost allocation is implicit and invisible until a rate increase exposes it. The mechanism is that the financing structure did not assign costs to the growth that caused them, and the cost-of-service analysis was not done or was not used to set the financing. The false signal is that "growth pays for itself." The harm is that existing residents subsidize new growth, rates rise faster than they would under growth-pays financing, the political backlash targets both the growth and the officials who approved it, and the municipality's fiscal position weakens because the growth does not cover its marginal cost.

### Service Commitments That Cannot Be Met

The annexation agreement or statute requires the municipality to extend service within a stated period, the municipality annexes without verifying it can meet the commitment, and the service is not extended on time, generating litigation and de-annexation pressure. The trap is that the legal commitment is made before the operational capacity is confirmed. The mechanism is that the capacity analysis was not done, the funding was not secured, or the timeline was unrealistic. The false signal is that "we will figure out the service later." The harm is that the annexed residents receive inferior service or none, the municipality is sued for breach of the agreement, de-annexation petitions circulate, and the political credibility of future annexation is destroyed.

### Overlapping Service Boundaries Creating Duplicate and Conflicting Service

The municipality extends service into territory already served by a special district or county, without coordinating the boundary, and the result is duplicate billing, conflicting standards, and disputes over which entity is responsible for which service. The trap is that uncoordinated extension creates a service map no one can administer. The mechanism is that the intergovernmental coordination was not done before the extension, and the boundary overlap was not resolved. The false signal is that "the market will sort it out." The harm is that residents receive conflicting bills and conflicting requirements, the special district's revenue and debt service are undermined, intergovernmental litigation ensues, and the service quality declines because no entity has clear responsibility.

### Fiscal Analysis That Ignores Operating and Replacement Cost

The extension is justified by the construction cost and the connection revenue, but the fiscal model does not account for the operating cost, the maintenance, or the eventual replacement, and the system falls into deferred maintenance and failure as it ages. The trap is that the near-term numbers look attractive while the life-cycle cost is ignored. The mechanism is that the fiscal impact model covered only the capital cost and the initial revenue, not the ongoing cost of operating and eventually replacing the infrastructure. The false signal is that "the extension is revenue-positive." The harm is that the system's condition deteriorates, rates must rise to catch up on deferred maintenance, the asset fails before its replacement is funded, and the fiscal position that looked strong at construction is weak at mid-life.

## Self-Check

- Is the infrastructure sized to a realistic demand horizon that separates committed from speculative demand, with phasing that tracks absorption?
- Does the financing assign costs to the growth that causes them, through impact fees, developer agreements, or system development charges, rather than implicitly to existing ratepayers?
- Does the fiscal model project revenue against full life-cycle cost (debt service, operations, maintenance, replacement) over a twenty- to thirty-year horizon, with stress-tested assumptions?
- Have the service-extension commitments in the annexation agreement and statute been verified as achievable, with capacity confirmed and funding secured, before annexation is completed?
- Are service boundaries coordinated with overlapping counties and special districts, with service agreements or boundary plans that eliminate overlaps and gaps?
- If absorption is half the forecast, does the extension still cover its cost, or does it become a stranded asset borne by existing ratepayers?
- Can a resident in the annexed area identify which entity provides water, sewer, police, and fire, or is the service map a patchwork of overlapping jurisdictions?
- Does the cost-of-service analysis separate capacity created for new growth from capacity that benefits the existing system, and is that allocation documented and defensible?
- If the developer does not build as projected, is there a mechanism to recover the stranded capacity cost, or does it fall on the municipality?
- Would the extension survive a legal challenge to its financing under nexus and proportionality standards, and a political challenge from ratepayers who object to subsidizing growth?
