# Intergovernmental Boundary Agreements and Annexation Disputes

Boundaries are relationships, not lines. Every municipal boundary adjoins a county, a neighboring municipality, or a special district, and the relationship across that boundary determines whether growth is coordinated or contested. Annexation disputes are among the most litigated and politically volatile issues in local government, because they redistribute revenue, service responsibility, and regulatory authority among jurisdictions that often have competing interests. The judgment problem is that boundary decisions made unilaterally by one jurisdiction provoke defensive responses from its neighbors — counter-annexation, litigation, refusal to provide service — that consume years of staff time and millions of dollars, while boundary decisions made through negotiated agreement produce stability, predictability, and coordinated growth that benefits all parties. The difference between these outcomes is rarely the underlying facts; it is whether the jurisdictions have a framework for resolving boundary questions before they become disputes. This skill covers the negotiation, drafting, and administration of intergovernmental boundary agreements and the resolution of annexation disputes. It complements the core annexation skill by focusing on the multi-jurisdictional relationships and dispute-resolution mechanisms that a single-jurisdiction annexation analysis cannot address.

## Core Rules

### Establish a Boundary Framework Before the Dispute Arises

The most effective boundary management is preventive: an agreement that defines the growth boundary, the annexation process, the service responsibilities, and the dispute-resolution mechanism before a specific annexation creates conflict. The rule is to establish a boundary framework proactively, through a joint planning agreement, a boundary plan, or a memoranda of understanding, because a framework negotiated in peacetime is far more durable than one negotiated under the pressure of a live dispute. The framework should address the specific issues that generate disputes: which jurisdiction will annex which territory and on what timeline, how revenue will be shared during a transition, who provides which service and at what standard, how the boundaries will be adjusted as growth occurs, and what happens when the parties disagree. A framework that exists only in statute is weaker than one that is negotiated and adopted by the parties, because the negotiated framework reflects the parties' specific interests and creates a relationship that statute alone cannot. The preventive framework should be reviewed and updated periodically, because growth patterns, service capacities, and political leadership change, and an outdated framework becomes a source of dispute rather than a prevention of it.

### Define the Growth Boundary and Annexation Rights with Geographic Specificity

Boundary disputes most often arise from ambiguity about which jurisdiction has the right to annex a specific parcel, and the ambiguity exists because the growth boundary was never mapped or was mapped at a resolution too coarse to resolve parcel-level questions. The rule is to define the growth boundary and annexation rights with geographic specificity, mapping the boundary to parcel lines and recording it in a format both jurisdictions reference, so that when a development is proposed, the question of who annexes it is already answered. The geographic specificity should address the common ambiguity sources: the boundary along a road (does it follow the centerline, the right-of-way line, or a setback), the boundary at a water feature (does it follow the thread of the stream or the bank), the boundary at a section line (does it follow the section line or the recorded subdivision line), and the treatment of islands and peninsulas of one jurisdiction within another. Where the boundary cannot be drawn precisely, the agreement should specify a process for resolving the specific case, rather than leaving it to be litigated. A boundary that is mapped to parcels and mutually referenced eliminates the most common source of annexation litigation, which is the claim that one jurisdiction annexed territory the other believed was its own.

### Allocate Revenue and Service Responsibility to Reflect Causation and Capacity

Boundary agreements redistribute revenue and service responsibility, and the allocation determines whether the agreement is stable or whether one party perceives itself as subsidizing the other. The rule is to allocate revenue and service responsibility to reflect causation (who created the demand for the service) and capacity (who can actually provide the service), rather than to default to whichever jurisdiction annexes the territory. Revenue sharing during a transition period — where the annexing jurisdiction shares property or sales tax with the jurisdiction that previously served the area, for a defined period — can smooth the fiscal impact and reduce the incentive for defensive counter-annexation. Service responsibility should be allocated to the entity with the capacity to provide the service at the required standard, which may not be the annexing jurisdiction, and the agreement should specify the standard, the cost allocation, and the mechanism for adjusting the allocation over time. The allocation should be transparent and documented, because the perception of unfairness is as destabilizing as actual unfairness, and an allocation that one party believes is exploitative will be renegotiated or litigated at the first opportunity.

### Use the Statutory Dispute-Resolution Process Before Litigation

Most states provide a statutory process for resolving annexation disputes — boundary review boards, municipal annexation authorities, mediation, or arbitration — and these processes are faster, cheaper, and more likely to preserve the intergovernmental relationship than litigation. The rule is to use the statutory dispute-resolution process before litigation, exhausting the administrative and negotiated remedies, because a court decision produces a winner and a loser while a negotiated or mediated resolution can produce an outcome both parties can live with. The planner's role is to understand the statutory process, to prepare the case for it (the facts of the annexation, the service capacity, the fiscal impact, the consistency with the comprehensive plan), and to engage the process in good faith rather than as a procedural hurdle before the inevitable lawsuit. Where the statutory process is weak or absent, the parties should establish their own dispute-resolution mechanism in the boundary agreement, because the absence of a process forces every disagreement into court. Litigation should be the last resort, reserved for cases where the statutory process has failed or where a legal question (the validity of the annexation statute itself, a constitutional challenge) requires judicial resolution.

### Manage Islands, Peninsulas, and Pre-Annexation Review with Deliberate Process

Islands (unincorporated territory surrounded by a municipality) and peninsulas (unincorporated territory connected to a municipality on most sides) are chronic sources of boundary inefficiency, because the county must serve territory that is functionally part of the municipality, and the municipality cannot plan coherently for an area it does not control. Many states require or encourage the resolution of islands through annexation, and the rule is to manage island annexation through a deliberate process that identifies the islands, evaluates the service and fiscal implications of annexing them, engages the affected residents, and sequences the annexations to manage the workload and the political impact. Pre-annexation review — where a jurisdiction reviews a proposed annexation for its service capacity, fiscal impact, and consistency with planning before it is finalized — is a mechanism for preventing annexations that create service gaps or fiscal problems, and the rule is to build pre-annexation review into the boundary framework so that problematic annexations are identified and corrected before they take effect. The deliberate process should recognize that island and peninsula resolution is often politically sensitive (the residents may not want to be annexed) and should provide for resident engagement, phased implementation, and the protection of residents who may face rate or tax increases from the annexation.

## Common Traps

### Unilateral Annexation Provoking Defensive Counter-Measures

A municipality annexes territory without coordinating with the affected county or neighboring jurisdiction, and the affected jurisdiction responds with counter-annexation, litigation, refusal to provide service, or political opposition that consumes years and dollars. The trap is that unilateral action is faster in the short run but destabilizing in the long run. The mechanism is that the annexation was pursued without a framework or consultation, and the affected jurisdiction's interests were not considered. The false signal is that "we have the statutory right to annex." The harm is that the intergovernmental relationship is poisoned, the annexation is delayed or overturned by litigation, future annexations face automatic opposition, and the staff and legal resources consumed by the dispute are unavailable for other work.

### Ambiguous Growth Boundary Producing Parcel-Level Litigation

The growth boundary was never mapped to parcels, a development is proposed in the ambiguous zone, and both jurisdictions claim the right to annex it, producing litigation that turns on the interpretation of a vague agreement or statute. The trap is that ambiguity at the planning scale becomes conflict at the parcel scale. The mechanism is that the boundary was defined in general terms and never resolved to the parcel level. The false signal is that "the boundary is clear enough." The harm is that the specific development is delayed, the litigation is expensive and unpredictable, the outcome sets a precedent that affects future cases, and the jurisdictions' relationship is damaged by the adversarial process.

### Revenue Allocation Perceived as Exploitative

The boundary agreement allocates revenue in a way that one party perceives as unfair — the annexing jurisdiction captures the revenue while the serving jurisdiction bears the cost — and the aggrieved party seeks to renegotiate, withdraw, or litigate at the first opportunity. The trap is that an allocation that is technically agreed can still be politically unsustainable if it is perceived as exploitative. The mechanism is that the revenue allocation did not reflect causation or capacity, or the transition period was too short. The false signal is that "the agreement is signed." The harm is that the agreement is unstable, the aggrieved party seeks every opportunity to escape it, the relationship deteriorates, and the agreement is renegotiated under worse terms than if it had been fair initially.

### Skipping the Statutory Process and Going Straight to Court

A boundary dispute arises, the jurisdictions skip the statutory dispute-resolution process and file suit, and the litigation consumes years and produces a binary outcome that satisfies neither party and destroys the relationship. The trap is that litigation feels decisive but is usually slower, more expensive, and more damaging than the alternatives. The mechanism is that the parties did not use or trust the statutory process, or the process was not established. The false signal is that "we have a strong legal case." The harm is that the dispute consumes legal and staff resources for years, the outcome is unpredictable, the relationship is destroyed, and the underlying boundary issue may not even be resolved by the court's narrow ruling.

### Islands and Peninsulas Left to Fester

Unincorporated islands and peninsulas are identified but never resolved, and the county continues to serve territory that is functionally municipal, creating service inefficiency, planning incoherence, and chronic boundary friction. The trap is that island resolution is politically difficult and is deferred indefinitely. The mechanism is that the deliberate process for resolving islands was never established, and each island is treated as a one-off political problem. The false signal is that "the islands are not causing problems." The harm is that the service inefficiency accumulates, the planning for the island area is incoherent (the municipality plans around territory it does not control), and the islands become flashpoints when development pressure finally forces the issue.

## Self-Check

- Is there a boundary framework — a joint planning agreement, boundary plan, or MOU — that defines the growth boundary, annexation process, service responsibilities, and dispute-resolution mechanism before disputes arise?
- Is the growth boundary mapped to parcel lines and mutually referenced by the jurisdictions, so that parcel-level annexation questions are already answered?
- Does the revenue and service allocation reflect causation and capacity, with a transition period that smooths the fiscal impact and reduces the incentive for defensive counter-annexation?
- Is the statutory dispute-resolution process understood and committed to before litigation, with the case prepared for it in good faith?
- Are islands, peninsulas, and pre-annexation review managed through a deliberate process, rather than left to fester or addressed ad hoc?
- If a specific parcel in the boundary zone were proposed for development tomorrow, would both jurisdictions agree on who annexes it, or would it generate a dispute?
- Does the revenue allocation withstand scrutiny from the party that bears more cost, or is it perceived as exploitative and therefore unstable?
- Has the statutory dispute-resolution process been used in good faith, or has it been bypassed in favor of litigation that will consume years and destroy the relationship?
- Are the unincorporated islands identified, evaluated, and sequenced for resolution, or are they accumulating as sources of future conflict?
- Is the boundary framework reviewed and updated periodically to reflect changing growth patterns, service capacities, and leadership, or is it becoming outdated and a source of dispute itself?
