# Shock Protection and Approach Boundaries

Electrical shock kills through a different mechanism than arc flash. Current passing through the body disrupts the heart's electrical rhythm (ventricular fibrillation) or causes sustained muscle contraction that prevents the victim from releasing the conductor. The threshold for these effects is terrifyingly low — currents as small as 50 milliamperes across the chest can induce fibrillation, and the resistance of dry skin, which might limit current at low voltages, breaks down above roughly 500 volts or when the skin is wet, cut, or punctured. The judgment problem is that shock hazard is determined by proximity to exposed energized parts, and the safe distance depends on the voltage and the worker's qualifications. An electrician who does not know the approach boundaries for the voltage they are working near is navigating without a map — they do not know how close is too close until they make contact, at which point the lesson is fatal. This skill covers how to determine the shock boundaries, select voltage-rated PPE, and maintain safe distances from energized parts.

## Core Rules

### Know the Three Shock Boundaries for the Voltage Involved

NFPA 70E defines three concentric boundaries around exposed energized conductors: the limited approach boundary, the restricted approach boundary, and the prohibited approach boundary. Each boundary represents a different level of risk and requires different qualifications and controls. The limited approach boundary is the closest an unqualified person may approach; inside it, only qualified persons are permitted. The restricted approach boundary is the closest a qualified person may approach without energized-work permits and shock-protective equipment; crossing it requires justification, a permit, and insulated PPE. The prohibited approach boundary is, for practical purposes, the same as contact — crossing it is treated as the same risk as touching the energized part. The distances for each boundary are specified in tables indexed by voltage, and they increase with voltage. An electrician must know the boundaries for the voltage they are working near before beginning the task, because the boundaries determine where they may stand, what they may reach, and what PPE they must wear.

### Use Insulated Tools Rated for the Voltage

Insulated tools provide a layer of protection between the worker's hand and the energized conductor if accidental contact occurs. The tools must be rated for the system voltage — a 1000V-rated screwdriver is not adequate for work near 15kV switchgear. The insulation must be intact; a tool with cracked, cut, or degraded insulation provides no protection and may be worse than a bare tool because it creates a false sense of security. Insulated tools are tested and marked with their voltage rating and the testing standard, and they must be inspected before each use. The trap is treating insulated tools as a license to work closer or faster — the insulation is a backup for accidental contact, not a primary control that makes contact acceptable. The primary control is maintaining distance from energized parts; the insulated tool is the second line of defense.

### Test and Inspect Rubber Gloves Before Each Use

Rubber insulating gloves are the primary shock protection for work on or near energized conductors, and they are also the most failure-prone piece of PPE because the rubber degrades. Before each use, the gloves must be visually inspected for holes, cuts, ozone checking, chemical swelling, and other damage, and they must be electrically tested using an air inflation test (rolling the glove to trap air and pressurize it, then listening and feeling for leaks). Gloves that fail inspection or the air test must be removed from service immediately. Additionally, rubber gloves must be dielectrically tested by a certified laboratory at intervals specified by the standard (typically every six months), and the test date must be verified before use. Leather protectors must be worn over rubber gloves to prevent mechanical damage. The trap is using gloves past their test date, skipping the air test because "they were fine yesterday," or wearing gloves without leather protectors, which allows a tool or conductor to cut the rubber and defeat the insulation.

### Maintain the Minimum Approach Distance in the Field

The boundaries are theoretical lines; in the field, the worker must maintain the minimum approach distance from exposed energized parts at all times. This means planning the work to avoid reaching across energized parts, using barriers or blankets to cover adjacent energized parts that could be inadvertently contacted, and positioning the body so that a slip or stumble does not result in contact. The trap is that the worker's reach extends with their arm and tools — a person reaching with a 2-foot tool effectively reduces their clearance by 2 feet. The minimum approach distance must account for the longest dimension of the worker plus tools, not just the static position of the body. Blanketing (covering energized parts with insulating rubber blankets) is the primary method of extending effective clearance, because it converts an exposed energized part into a covered surface that can be approached more closely.

### Apply the One-Hand Rule in High-Voltage Work

When working near exposed high-voltage conductors, the practice of keeping one hand behind the back or in a pocket reduces the likelihood that current will pass through the chest, because it eliminates a hand-to-hand path. While this is a simplification (current can still pass through the chest via other paths), it reinforces the principle of avoiding simultaneous contact with two points at different potentials. The broader rule is to avoid creating any path through the chest — do not lean on grounded metal while working on energized conductors, do not hold a grounded conduit with one hand while touching an energized conductor with the other, and position the body so that a shock would pass through an extremity, not the torso. The trap is that the work environment often creates inadvertent ground paths — metal ladders, conduit, junction boxes, concrete floors — and the worker must be aware of all of them, not just the obvious conductor.

### Understand That Resistance Is Not Reliable Protection

The human body's resistance is often cited as a protective factor — dry skin may present 100,000 ohms or more, limiting current at 120V to a harmless 1 milliampere. But this resistance is variable and unreliable. Wet or cut skin drops to 1,000 ohms or less. Sweat, common during physical work, dramatically reduces skin resistance. Voltages above approximately 500 volts puncture the skin, eliminating its resistive barrier entirely. And the internal body resistance (blood, muscle, organs) is only about 500 ohms regardless of skin condition. The trap is calculating "safe" current based on nominal skin resistance and then working in conditions (sweat, humidity, cuts, high voltage) that invalidate the calculation. The defense is to treat any voltage above 50V as potentially lethal regardless of assumed body resistance, and to rely on PPE and distance, not on the hope that skin resistance will limit the current.

## Common Traps

### Approaching Based on "Feel" Rather Than the Boundary Table

An experienced electrician develops a sense of how close is "too close" based on years of work, and begins to rely on that sense rather than looking up the boundary for the specific voltage. The trap is that the sense is calibrated to the voltages they work on most frequently, and when they encounter a different voltage (higher, or in an unfamiliar configuration), their sense leads them inside the boundary. The defense is to look up the boundary for every task, because the tables are indexed by voltage and the distances change significantly across voltage ranges. Experience does not override the physics; it only creates the illusion that it does.

### Using Rubber Gloves Past Their Test Date

Rubber gloves are dielectrically tested every six months, and the test date is stamped on the cuff. An electrician grabs a pair of gloves from the truck, sees they look fine, and proceeds without checking the date. The gloves are three months past test, and the rubber has degraded through ozone exposure, flexing, and chemical contact. The dielectric strength that was verified at the last test is no longer guaranteed. The trap is that rubber degradation is not always visible — ozone checking (microscopic cracking from ozone attack) can be invisible to the eye but sufficient to compromise the insulation. The defense is to check the test date every time, no exceptions, and to remove any glove past its date from service immediately. A glove that "looks fine" but is past test is an untested piece of rubber being trusted with a life.

### Wearing Leather Protectors That Are Too Large

Rubber gloves require leather protectors to prevent mechanical damage, and the protectors must be sized to fit the rubber glove snugly. An electrician wears oversized protectors because they are more comfortable or because the correct size is not available. The oversized protector allows the rubber glove to shift and fold inside it, creating creases that concentrate stress and can crack the rubber. The trap is that the comfort of the worker is being prioritized over the integrity of the PPE, and the failure mode (creased rubber cracking under voltage stress) is not immediately apparent. The defense is to match the protector size to the rubber glove size exactly and to refuse to use mismatched pairs.

### Forgetting That Adjacent Parts Are Also Energized

An electrician is working on a de-energized circuit in a panel that also contains energized circuits. They focus on the de-energized conductors and forget that the adjacent energized bus, breaker, or wire is still live. Their hand, tool, or sleeve contacts the energized part while their attention is on the workpiece. The trap is that the workpiece being de-energized creates a false sense of safety in the entire panel, when in reality only the specific circuit is locked out. The defense is to blanket all energized parts in the work area with insulating covers before beginning, and to maintain awareness of the location of all energized parts, not just the ones being worked on.

### Trusting Insulated Tools With Damaged Insulation

An insulated screwdriver has a small nick in the insulation from years of use. The nick appears superficial, and the tool is otherwise functional. The electrician continues to use it near energized parts. The nick exposes bare metal, and if the tool contacts an energized conductor at that point, the current passes through the tool shaft to the worker's hand. The trap is that the damage appears minor and the tool "still works," but the insulation's function is to prevent exactly this contact, and a nick defeats it entirely. The defense is to inspect insulated tools before each use and to remove any tool with damaged insulation from service immediately. A damaged insulated tool is worse than a bare tool because it creates the illusion of protection where none exists.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Do I know the limited, restricted, and prohibited approach boundaries for the specific voltage of the exposed energized parts I will be working near, and have I looked them up rather than relying on experience?
- Am I using insulated tools rated for the system voltage, and have I inspected each tool's insulation for cracks, cuts, and degradation before use?
- Are my rubber insulating gloves within their dielectric test date, have I performed the air inflation test before use, and am I wearing correctly sized leather protectors over them?
- Have I blanketed or covered all adjacent energized parts in the work area to prevent inadvertent contact, not just the specific conductors I am working on?
- Am I maintaining the minimum approach distance accounting for my reach plus tools, and have I positioned my body so that a slip or stumble will not result in contact with energized parts?
- Have I identified all possible current paths through my body (including ground paths through ladders, conduit, and structural metal) and positioned myself to avoid a chest-crossing path?
- Am I treating the voltage as potentially lethal regardless of assumed body resistance, and relying on PPE and distance rather than on the hope that skin resistance will limit current?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
