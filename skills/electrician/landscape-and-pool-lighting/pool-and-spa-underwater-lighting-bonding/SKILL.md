# Pool and Spa Underwater Lighting and Bonding

Water and electricity together kill people quietly. The signature hazard of pool and spa lighting is not the dramatic arc-flash that an electrician expects on switchgear; it is a small voltage gradient across the pool water that paralyzes a swimmer's muscles so they cannot call for help or climb out. This is why NEC Article 680 is one of the most prescriptive articles in the code, and why the requirements for bonding, grounding, and GFCI protection in and around water are not optional refinements. The judgment problem is that the life-safety function depends on the equipotential bond, which is an invisible grid of connected metal buried in the concrete — it cannot be tested by whether the light turns on, and it cannot be seen after the deck is poured. Agents miss the issues because the lighting appears to work and because the bonding grid is easy to treat as a formality, when in fact it is the single feature that prevents the gradient that kills.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Equipotential Bonding, Not Just Grounding, Is the Life-Safety Function

Grounding provides a fault-current path back to the source so a breaker can trip. Bonding in a pool context does something different and more important: it ties all conductive parts of the pool structure — the rebar, the shell, the ladder, the light niche, the water itself — together so that no voltage difference can appear between any two points a body can touch. A swimmer bridging two points at different potentials becomes the conductor, and even a few volts across the chest can be fatal in water. The defense is to recognize that the equipotential bond is the primary shock protection, that grounding alone is insufficient, and that every metallic and conductive element in and around the pool must be bonded into one common grid.

### Apply NEC 680.26 Equipotential Bonding Requirements to the Shell and Water

NEC 680.26 requires the pool shell's reinforcing steel, or an alternative bonded conductor grid, to be bonded together and to all metallic parts including underwater light forming shells, ladder anchors, handrails, diving bases, and the pool water itself. The water is bonded through a designated bonded conductive surface in contact with the water. The intent is a single equipotential plane so that any fault or induced voltage is distributed evenly and no gradient appears across the body. The defense is to follow 680.26 literally: bond the rebar or install a code-compliant grid, bond every listed metallic component, bond the water via the listed fitting, and connect all of it to a common bonding point with the required conductor size.

### Distinguish Wet-Niche From Dry-Niche Fixtures and Apply the Correct Rules

A wet-niche light mounts in a forming shell that is flooded by the pool water, which cools the lamp; the niche is bonded and the fixture cord runs through a sealed conduit to a junction box. A dry-niche light is isolated from the pool water by a sealed housing, often behind a window. Wet-niche fixtures require a forming shell bonded to the pool grid, an 8 AWG or larger bonding conductor, and GFCI protection; their cords must run through a listed potting compound or sealed fitting at the niche. The defense is to identify the fixture type before installation and to apply the niche bonding, cord routing, and GFCI rules that match it, never mixing the requirements of one type onto the other.

### Provide GFCI Protection for All Pool Lighting and Verify It Trips

All pool, spa, and fountain lighting operating at over 15 volts must have GFCI protection, and the GFCI must be tested to confirm it trips at its rated threshold. A GFCI that does not trip provides no protection, and underwater lighting GFCIs are notorious for nuisance tripping from wet cords and moisture ingress, which tempts installers to bypass them. The defense is to install the GFCI as required, to verify it trips with the test button and with a calibrated tester, and never to defeat or uprate a tripping GFCI — if it trips, the moisture or leakage causing it must be fixed, not suppressed.

### Route the Light Cord and Wet-Niche Conduit Correctly

The cord from a wet-niche light runs through a conduit cast into the pool wall up to a junction box located the required distance above the water line and away from the pool edge. The conduit must slope downward to the niche so it drains, must be sealed at the niche end with an approved potting compound to prevent water travel, and the junction box must be listed for pool use with a grounded terminal for the equipment grounding conductor. The defense is to install the conduit with the correct slope and sealing, to use a listed pool junction box at the required height, and to ensure the equipment grounding conductor in the cord is continuous back to the source.

### Bond All Metallic Equipment and Maintain the Grid Through Every Penetration

The equipotential grid only works if every conductive element is part of it. A metal pump housing, a heater, a handrail, a cover track, or a nearby metallic fence within reach of the pool must all be bonded to the common grid. Each new piece of equipment or structural penetration is a chance to break the grid. The defense is to bond every listed metallic component with the required conductor, to verify continuity across the grid before energizing, and to treat any added metal — including reinforcing wire mesh in a later deck repair — as something that must be tied back into the bond.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Bonding Grid as Optional Because the Light Works

The installer wires the light, it turns on, and the bonding grid is skipped or left incomplete because it appears unnecessary. The false signal is that the functioning light proves the electrical work is done. The mechanism of failure is that the light working says nothing about whether a voltage gradient can appear across the pool water, which is exactly what the bond prevents. The harm is a pool that looks finished but can paralyze a swimmer the first time a fault or induced voltage appears.

### Relying on the Equipment Grounding Conductor Instead of the Equipotential Bond

The installer grounds the light niche to the equipment grounding conductor and believes the bonding requirement is satisfied. The false signal is that grounding and bonding sound similar and both connect metal to a reference. The mechanism of failure is that the equipment ground clears faults but does not equalize potential between the niche, the ladder, and the water, so a body bridging them can still conduct. The harm is a false sense of safety and a latent shock hazard that grounding alone cannot remove.

### Installing a Wet-Niche Light Without Sealing the Conduit at the Niche

The installer pulls the cord through the niche conduit to the junction box but does not seal the conduit at the niche with the listed potting compound. The false signal is that the cord is continuous and the box is above water. The mechanism of failure is that pool water migrates up the conduit into the junction box, corroding terminals and carrying current paths into the box. The harm is water in the junction box, corrosion, leakage that trips the GFCI, and eventual failure that is difficult and costly to repair.

### Bypassing or Uprating a GFCI That Nuisance Trips

The pool light GFCI trips intermittently and the installer, frustrated, replaces it with a higher-rated one or removes it to stop the tripping. The false signal is that the tripping is a nuisance rather than a warning. The mechanism of failure is that the tripping indicates real leakage from a wet cord, a damaged seal, or water in the niche, and removing the protection leaves the hazard in place. The harm is loss of the only fast-acting shock protection and a real underwater leakage path that can injure or kill.

### Failing to Bond the Water Itself

The installer bonds the niche, the ladder, and the rebar but omits the listed fitting that bonds the pool water to the grid. The false signal is that all the visible metal is bonded. The mechanism of failure is that the water, which is conductive and surrounds the swimmer, is left at a different potential from the bonded metal, so a gradient can exist through the water itself. The harm is that the very medium the swimmer is immersed in is not equalized, defeating the purpose of the equipotential plane.

### Ignoring Metallic Fences, Rails, and Nearby Structures Within Reach

The installer bonds the pool equipment but does not bond a nearby metal fence, handrail, or deck reinforcement that a wet person can touch while in the water. The false signal is that these items are "not part of the pool." The mechanism of failure is that any conductive object within reach of a person in the water can be at a different potential and deliver current through the body. The harm is a shock path that the pool-side bonding never addressed.

## Self-Check

- Did I bond the pool shell rebar or install a code-compliant equipotential grid, and bond all listed metallic parts and the water to a common bonding point per NEC 680.26?
- Did I distinguish wet-niche from dry-niche fixtures and apply the niche bonding, cord routing, and sealing rules that match the fixture type?
- Did I install and verify GFCI protection for all pool and spa lighting, confirming it trips on test, and did I avoid bypassing a tripping GFCI?
- Did I seal the wet-niche conduit at the niche with the listed potting compound and route the cord to a listed pool junction box at the required height?
- Did I bond the pool water itself through the listed fitting, not just the visible metal components?
- Did I bond every metallic object within reach of the pool, including ladders, handrails, heaters, pumps, and nearby fences or rails?
- Did I verify continuity across the equipotential grid before energizing the lighting?
- Is the bonding installation documented so that future modifications can be checked against the grid rather than assumed complete?
