# Power Quality Analysis and Harmonic Monitoring

Power quality problems are the hardest electrical faults to diagnose because the symptoms — overheating transformers, nuisance tripping, failed capacitors, flickering, motors that run hot for no obvious reason — look like ordinary equipment failures, and the meter that finds them is not the clamp-on ammeter most electricians carry but a power quality analyzer that captures waveforms over time. The judgment problem is that a distorted current waveform, a voltage sag that lasts six cycles, or a resonant capacitor failure leaves almost no evidence by the time someone investigates, and the default diagnostic — measuring RMS voltage and current and declaring the system fine — is blind to exactly the phenomena causing the damage. An electrician who measures 480 volts and 200 amps at a feeder, sees normal-looking numbers, and closes the investigation has missed the 18% current THD that is overheating the transformer and the fifth harmonic that is about to blow the correction capacitors. This skill covers how to conduct power quality monitoring that captures real events, interpret THD and the harmonic spectrum, identify the actual source of distortion, and specify mitigation that solves the cause rather than the symptom.

## Core Rules

### Monitor Over Time, Not at a Single Instant

Power quality events are transient and intermittent by nature. A voltage sag that trips a drive happens in a few cycles and is gone; a harmonic distortion that overheats a transformer may appear only when specific nonlinear loads run; a resonance that destroys capacitors may occur only at certain load combinations. A single instantaneous measurement — voltage, current, power factor — captures none of this. The defense is to deploy a power quality analyzer that logs continuously for a representative period (days to weeks, covering the full range of plant operation) and records waveforms on event triggers (sags, swells, transients, imbalances). The analyzer must be set to capture the right phenomena: fast enough sample rate for transients and harmonics up to the 50th, long enough recording window to catch intermittent events, and triggered to store the waveform of every disturbance. A diagnosis based on a one-time reading is a guess; a diagnosis based on a trended log is evidence.

### Interpret THD Against the Correct Limit and the Correct Quantity

Total harmonic distortion is reported separately for voltage (VTHD) and current (ITHD), and the two have different meanings and different limits. Voltage distortion is what the system delivers to loads, and it is governed by limits such as the IEEE 519 framework (generally 5% VTHD at the point of common coupling for systems under 69 kV, with lower limits on individual harmonics). Current distortion depends on the load and is far more variable — a six-pulse drive can produce 80% or higher ITHD at its input while the resulting VTHD at the point of common coupling remains within limits because the source impedance is low. The trap is reading one THD number, not knowing whether it is voltage or current, and applying the wrong limit. The defense is to always distinguish VTHD from ITHD, to measure both, to evaluate VTHD against the limit at the point of common coupling (where the utility and customer meet), and to recognize that high ITHD at a single nonlinear load is expected and is only a system problem insofar as it produces unacceptable VTHD or overloads equipment.

### Read the Harmonic Spectrum, Not Just the THD Number

The total distortion number tells you there is a problem; the spectrum tells you what is causing it and what to do about it. Different loads produce characteristic harmonic signatures: six-pulse rectifiers (most variable-frequency drives, UPS front-ends, and electronic power supplies) produce predominantly the 5th, 7th, 11th, and 13th harmonics; single-phase electronic loads (computers, LED drivers, electronics) produce high 3rd harmonic that adds on the neutral; arc furnaces and arc welders produce a broad, noisy spectrum including even harmonics. The spectrum also reveals resonance: a sharp peak at a single harmonic frequency, often the 5th or 7th, indicates that a power factor correction capacitor is resonating with the system inductance and magnifying that harmonic. The defense is to always examine the individual harmonic magnitudes, to match the dominant harmonics to the likely source type, and to recognize that a single-frequency spike points to resonance while a broad characteristic spectrum points to a load type.

### Identify the Harmonic Source Before Specifying Mitigation

The most expensive mistake in harmonic mitigation is installing filters or reactors on the wrong equipment because the source was never identified. Harmonics flow from the nonlinear loads (sources) into the system, but a measurement of high distortion at a transformer or main bus does not by itself tell you which load is producing it — multiple nonlinear loads contribute, and the distortion measured at one point is the aggregate. The defense is to use source-identification techniques: measure at the individual feeder or load level to isolate which loads produce the distortion, compare the harmonic spectrum at each load to the spectrum at the point of common coupling, and where loads cannot be individually metered, use the direction of harmonic power flow (the analyzer can compute whether harmonics flow toward or away from a load) to identify sources. Only after the source is identified can mitigation be correctly targeted — a line reactor at the offending drive, a passive filter tuned to the dominant harmonic, an active filter at the bus, or isolation of the offending load.

### Evaluate Power Factor as Displacement and Distortion, Not a Single Number

Power factor has two components: displacement power factor (the phase angle between fundamental voltage and current, caused by inductive loads like motors and transformers) and distortion power factor (the reduction caused by harmonic current that does no real work). The "power factor" reading on a basic meter is usually true power factor, which combines both, but the correction is different for each. Low displacement factor is corrected with capacitance (power factor correction capacitors). Low distortion factor cannot be corrected with capacitance — adding capacitors to a harmonic-rich system can cause resonance and make the problem worse — and requires harmonic mitigation (filters, reactors, or active compensation). The defense is to measure displacement and distortion factor separately, to correct displacement factor with appropriately sized and reactor-protected capacitors, and to recognize that a low true power factor in a system full of drives is a harmonic problem, not a capacitor problem. Installing standard correction capacitors on a high-harmonic system without detuning reactors is a classic cause of capacitor failure and resonance.

### Diagnose Sag and Swell Events by Source and Duration

Voltage sags (dips) and swells are the most common power quality complaints, and their cause is encoded in their magnitude, duration, and waveform. A sag of 10 to 13% lasting one to a few cycles is typically a utility remote fault cleared by protection. A sag correlated with the starting of a large local load (a large motor, a transformer inrush) is caused by inrush current dropping voltage across source impedance. A swell often accompanies the switching off of a large load or capacitor energization. The defense is to use the analyzer's event log to correlate sags and swells with their timing — does the sag coincide with a known load start, a utility recloser operation, or a lightning event? — and to capture the waveform of each event to see its shape. A recurring sag that trips a sensitive drive may be fixed by a smaller soft-starter, a dedicated feeder, or a UPS, but only if the source (utility vs. internal) is correctly identified, because the mitigation for an external utility sag (ride-through, UPS) is entirely different from the mitigation for an internal load-start sag (soft-start, reduced inrush).

### Treat Neutral Current in Harmonic-Rich Systems as a First-Class Problem

In a three-phase wye system feeding single-phase nonlinear loads (office buildings, data centers, LED lighting), the triplen harmonics (3rd, 9th, 15th) do not cancel in the neutral as the fundamental does — they add. The result is neutral current that can equal or exceed the phase current even when the loads are balanced, overheating the neutral conductor and the transformer. The trap is sizing the neutral at the traditional reduced cross-section (or assuming balanced loads mean negligible neutral current), which is valid only for linear loads. The defense is to measure neutral current directly in systems with significant nonlinear single-phase loads, to recognize that high neutral current with balanced phase currents is the signature of triplen harmonics, and to specify a full-sized or double-sized neutral and a K-rated or derated transformer for such systems. A standard transformer feeding heavy nonlinear load will overheat and fail prematurely even though the phase loading appears within rating.

## Common Traps

### Measuring RMS Values and Declaring Power Quality Good

The electrician measures 480 volts and balanced current at a main feeder, sees normal-looking RMS numbers, and reports the power quality is fine. The trap is that RMS values average out the distortion and the transients that cause the actual problems — a 480V feeder with 8% VTHD, recurring 6-cycle sags, and 60% ITHD reads a normal 480V on a basic meter. The false signal is the normal RMS reading; the harm is that the overheating transformer, the nuisance-tripping drives, and the failing capacitors are all attributed to "bad equipment" while the power quality cause is never investigated. The defense is to use a power quality analyzer that captures waveforms and harmonics over time, and to recognize that RMS voltage and current alone cannot diagnose power quality problems.

### Applying the VTHD Limit to a Current Measurement (or Vice Versa)

The technician measures current THD of 35% at a drive input and reports that the system violates the 5% IEEE 519 limit, triggering an expensive mitigation project. The trap is that the 5% limit applies to voltage distortion at the point of common coupling, not to current distortion at an individual load, and 35% ITHD at a six-pulse drive input is entirely normal and expected. The false signal is a high THD number compared to a remembered limit; the harm is unnecessary, expensive mitigation applied where none was required, or conversely, a real VTHD violation missed because the technician checked only current. The defense is to always distinguish voltage from current distortion, to apply each limit to the correct quantity at the correct measurement point, and to evaluate whether the current distortion is actually producing unacceptable voltage distortion at the system level before mandating mitigation.

### Adding Power Factor Correction Capacitors to a Harmonic-Rich System

The facility has a low power factor (0.75) and a high electric bill with reactive demand charges, so the electrician installs standard power factor correction capacitors. The trap is that the low power factor is largely distortion factor from a building full of drives and electronic loads, and the added capacitors resonate with the system inductance at a harmonic frequency (often near the 5th or 7th), magnifying that harmonic, blowing the capacitors, and overheating transformers. The false signal is the low power factor reading that "obviously" needs capacitors; the harm is destroyed capacitors, amplified harmonics, and a problem worse than the original. The defense is to measure displacement and distortion factor separately before specifying correction, and to always detune correction capacitors with series reactors (tuned below the lowest significant harmonic, typically near the 4th) so they cannot resonate at a harmonic frequency.

### Treating All Distortion as Coming From One Obvious Load

The analyzer shows high 5th and 7th harmonics at the main bus, and the electrician blames the largest drive and installs a filter on it. The trap is that the measured distortion is the aggregate of many nonlinear loads, and the largest drive may be only one contributor while dozens of smaller drives and power supplies add up to the dominant source. The false signal is the correlation of "big drive" with "big distortion"; the harm is a filter that does not solve the problem because the other sources remain, wasted money, and a recurring distortion level. The defense is to measure at the individual feeder or load level, to use harmonic power flow direction to identify sources, and to consider bus-level mitigation (an active harmonic filter) when the distortion is distributed across many small sources rather than concentrated in one large one.

### Diagnosing a Sag From Its Symptom Instead of Its Source

A drive trips intermittently and the operator reports "voltage problems." The electrician measures voltage at the drive, sees it is normal at the moment, and assumes the problem is resolved or is a drive defect. The trap is that the sag that trips the drive is intermittent and was not occurring during the measurement, and without a triggered event capture the cause is invisible. The false signal is the normal voltage at the time of measurement; the harm is a recurring nuisance trip whose real cause — a large motor starting elsewhere, a utility recloser, or a weak feeder — is never found, and the drive continues to trip unpredictably. The defense is to deploy a logging analyzer with event triggering at the drive location over a long enough period to capture several trips, and to correlate the captured sags with plant operations and utility events to identify whether the source is internal or external.

### Sizing the Neutral for Balanced Linear Loads in a Nonlinear System

An office building is wired with a reduced neutral (half the phase conductor cross-section) on the assumption that balanced three-phase loads produce negligible neutral current. The trap is that the building's single-phase nonlinear loads (computers, LED lighting, electronics) produce triplen harmonics that add on the neutral regardless of phase balance, and the reduced neutral overheats. The false signal is the balanced phase currents that "should" produce no neutral current under linear-load rules; the harm is an overheated neutral, voltage distortion, and risk of fire. The defense is to measure neutral current in any system with significant single-phase nonlinear load, to recognize that balanced phases do not cancel triplen harmonics, and to specify full-sized neutrals and K-rated transformers for such systems.

## Self-Check

- Did I deploy a power quality analyzer logging continuously over a representative operating period with event-triggered waveform capture, rather than relying on a single instantaneous RMS measurement?
- Did I distinguish voltage THD from current THD, measure both, and evaluate VTHD against the correct limit (e.g., IEEE 519) at the point of common coupling rather than applying a voltage limit to a current reading?
- Did I examine the individual harmonic spectrum to identify the dominant harmonics, match them to likely source types (six-pulse drives, single-phase electronics, arc loads), and detect single-frequency spikes that indicate capacitor resonance?
- Did I identify the actual harmonic source by measuring at individual feeders or loads and using harmonic power flow direction, rather than assuming the largest load is the source?
- Did I measure displacement and distortion power factor separately, and if correction capacitors are needed, did I specify detuning reactors to prevent resonance with system harmonics?
- For sag and swell complaints, did I capture and time-stamp the events and correlate them with internal load starts and utility operations to determine whether the source is internal or external?
- In systems with single-phase nonlinear loads, did I measure neutral current directly and recognize that triplen harmonics add on the neutral regardless of phase balance, sizing the neutral and transformer accordingly?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
