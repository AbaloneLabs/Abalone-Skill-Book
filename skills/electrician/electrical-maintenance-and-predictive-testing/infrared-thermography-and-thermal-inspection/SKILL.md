# Infrared Thermography and Thermal Inspection

A thermal camera does not find loose connections; it finds temperature differences, and an electrician who reads those differences without understanding emissivity, load conditions, and the physics of resistance heating will either chase false hot spots or miss the connection that ignites next month. The judgment problem in infrared thermography is that the image looks authoritative — a bright yellow blob over a breaker feels like proof — but thermal energy in an electrical assembly comes from normal load current, ambient reflections, solar loading, and adjacent equipment as much as from a degrading connection. An electrician who scans a panel at 20% load, flags nothing, and files a clean report has produced a document that proves nothing, because the very connection that is failing may be cold at low load and only reveal itself at full operating current. This skill covers how to conduct thermographic inspections under valid conditions, distinguish real connection heating from false signatures, and decide when a thermal anomaly is an incipient failure that demands action versus a normal operating condition.

## Core Rules

### Inspect Under Sufficient and Stable Load

Thermography on electrical connections is meaningful only when current is flowing, because resistive heating is proportional to the square of current. A loose lug carrying no load produces no heat and is invisible to the camera, and the same lug at 40% load runs warm enough to look benign while at 90% load it runs hot enough to be critical. The defense is to require a minimum load — conventionally at least 40% of rated current, with 70% or higher preferred for detecting marginal connections — and to record the actual load at the time of inspection alongside every thermal image. A scan performed during a plant shutdown, overnight, or on a backup feeder that is not carrying current produces a clean report that is worth nothing, because the absence of heat proves only the absence of load, not the absence of a fault. Where load cannot be brought up during the inspection window, the finding must be labeled "unloaded — inconclusive" and rescheduled, not filed as "no anomalies."

### Compare Like Components, Not Against an Absolute Threshold

A single temperature reading on one component tells you almost nothing, because a breaker running at 55°C may be perfectly normal at full load or dangerously degraded at light load. The diagnostic power of thermography comes from comparison: the suspect phase versus the other two phases of the same device, one breaker versus its identical neighbor in the same panel, one cell of a contactor versus the adjacent cells. A phase-to-phase temperature difference of more than about 15°C (some standards use 10°C) on a balanced three-phase device is a strong indicator of a connection or contact problem on the hot phase, even if the absolute temperature is below any damage threshold. The defense is to always frame a finding as a delta — "Phase B is 22°C warmer than Phases A and C" — rather than as an absolute, and to recognize that a component that is uniformly warm with its neighbors is usually normal even if the absolute temperature seems high.

### Correct for Emissivity Before Trusting a Temperature Value

The temperature a thermal camera reports is a calculation based on the infrared radiation it receives, which depends on the surface's emissivity — how efficiently it emits infrared compared to a perfect blackbody. Bare copper bus, polished metal, and painted surfaces have wildly different emissivities (roughly 0.05 to 0.95), and an uncorrected reading on a bare shiny lug can be wrong by tens of degrees, usually reading low because the shiny surface reflects cold surroundings. The defense is to set the camera's emissivity to match the target material, to apply electrical tape (emissivity ~0.95) or high-emissivity paint to bare metal targets where a precise reading is needed, and to be deeply skeptical of temperature values on shiny surfaces taken without correction. When emissivity is unknown or uncorrectable, rely on relative comparison between similar surfaces rather than the absolute number, and never condemn or clear a connection based on a single uncorrected absolute reading.

### Account for Reflections, Solar Loading, and Ambient Sources

A thermal camera sees reflected infrared as well as emitted infrared, and a curved or shiny surface can reflect the heat of the thermographer's own body, an adjacent hot bus, or sunlight through a window, producing a hot spot that has nothing to do with the equipment. Solar loading on an outdoor enclosure or a south-facing panel can raise surface temperatures by 20°C or more, masking a real internal hotspot or creating a false one. The defense is to change the viewing angle to see whether the hot spot moves with the camera (a reflection) or stays fixed on the component (a real source), to shield outdoor equipment from direct sun during inspection, and to allow enclosures to reach thermal equilibrium with ambient before scanning after a door is opened. A finding that cannot be reproduced from a second angle is likely a reflection and must not be reported as a defect.

### Recognize the Thermal Signatures of Specific Failure Modes

Different incipient failures produce characteristically different thermal patterns, and reading the pattern — not just the peak temperature — is what separates a skilled thermographer from a camera operator. A loose or corroded lug typically produces a single, sharply defined hot spot at the connection point that cools rapidly with distance along the conductor. A degraded contact inside a breaker or contactor produces a more diffuse warmth centered on the device body, often with the heat distributed across the whole pole. An overloaded conductor (too small for the load, or a single high-current circuit) warms uniformly along its entire length, not concentrated at a point. A load imbalance shows as one phase uniformly warmer than the others across multiple components, pointing upstream to the load distribution rather than to any single connection. A failing internal component such as a capacitor or coil produces warmth in a location where no current-carrying connection exists. The defense is to describe the shape and distribution of the thermal pattern, not just the temperature, because the pattern tells you what is failing and where to look when the equipment is de-energized.

### Severity-Classify Using Both Temperature and Context

A thermal finding should be classified by severity, but severity is not determined by temperature alone. The common frameworks (such as the NETA or InterNACHI tiers) combine absolute temperature rise above similar components, rise above ambient, and the consequence of failure. A 10°C rise on a non-critical lighting breaker is a "monitor" finding; the same rise on the main feeder lug of a data center is urgent. The defense is to apply a consistent severity rubric that considers the delta temperature, the absolute temperature relative to the component's rating, the criticality of the load, whether the load at inspection was representative of normal operation, and whether the trend is worsening from prior inspections. A finding that is marginal on a single scan but has risen steadily over three inspection cycles is more urgent than a finding that is warmer on one scan but has been stable for years.

## Common Traps

### Scanning at Low or Zero Load and Reporting "No Anomalies"

The thermographer scans a switchboard during a low-production period when most loads are off, sees a uniformly cool image, and files a report stating the equipment is in good condition. The trap is that resistive heating scales with current squared, so a connection that runs 60°C at full load runs barely above ambient at 10% load and is invisible. The false signal is the clean thermal image; the harm is that a degraded connection goes unreported, continues to worsen, and fails catastrophically during the next period of full load when it finally heats enough to be detected — by igniting. The defense is to require and record minimum load conditions, to refuse to file a "clean" report on unloaded equipment, and to reschedule inspections when load cannot be achieved.

### Trusting an Absolute Temperature on a Shiny Surface Without Emissivity Correction

The thermographer points the camera at a bare copper bus connection, reads "42°C," and compares it to the 30°C ambient, concluding the connection is only slightly warm. The trap is that bare copper has an emissivity near 0.05, and the camera, set to a default emissivity of 0.95, is reading mostly reflected background radiation, not the surface temperature — the actual surface temperature may be 90°C. The false signal is the reassuringly low number; the harm is a severely overheated connection that is cleared as normal and left to progress to failure or fire. The defense is to correct emissivity for every target material, to apply tape or paint to bare metal for accurate readings, and to never rely on an uncorrected absolute value on a low-emissivity surface.

### Chasing a Reflection as a Defect

The thermographer sees a bright hot spot on a curved bus bar or breaker face and reports a critical overheating connection. The trap is that the hot spot is the thermographer's own body heat (37°C) reflected off the shiny surface, or the reflection of an adjacent hot conductor, and it moves or disappears when the viewing angle changes. The false signal is a dramatic-looking hot spot in the image; the harm is a false defect report that triggers an unnecessary outage, de-energization, and re-termination of a connection that was fine, eroding confidence in the thermography program and wasting resources. The defense is to verify every finding from a second angle, to confirm that the hot spot is fixed to the component, and to recognize that a hot spot that moves with the camera is a reflection, not a fault.

### Flagging a Uniformly Warm Component as a Defect

The thermographer sees a breaker running at 65°C and flags it as overheating. The trap is that the breaker is carrying its full rated current in a warm ambient, and 65°C is within its normal operating range — the entire device is uniformly warm, with no single hot connection, because the heat is from normal load current, not from a fault. The false signal is a high absolute temperature; the harm is unnecessary repair work and the risk that the real defects (the sharply localized hot spots elsewhere in the panel) are overlooked because attention went to the normal-looking warm breaker. The defense is to compare each component to its identical loaded neighbors and to look for localized hot spots, not uniform warmth, as the signature of a connection fault.

### Treating a Single Scan as a Diagnosis

The thermographer performs a one-time scan, finds one warm connection, repairs it, and considers the inspection complete. The trap is that a single scan is a snapshot that cannot distinguish a connection that has always run slightly warm (due to geometry or load) from one that is actively degrading, and it cannot detect a connection that was cold on inspection day but heats under different load or ambient conditions. The false signal is the single data point; the harm is that the predictive value of thermography — early detection of degradation before failure — is lost when there is no trend to compare against. The defense is to establish a baseline and repeat scans under comparable load and ambient conditions, building a trend that reveals degradation before it becomes critical.

## Self-Check

- Did I confirm and record the actual load current at the time of inspection, and did I require at least 40% of rated load before accepting the scan as meaningful rather than filing a "clean" report on unloaded equipment?
- Did I compare each suspect component to its identical, similarly loaded neighbors (other phases, adjacent breakers) and express findings as a temperature delta rather than an absolute value?
- Did I set the camera emissivity to match each target material, and where bare or shiny metal was involved, did I apply tape or paint or rely on relative comparison rather than an uncorrected absolute reading?
- Did I verify each reported hot spot from at least two viewing angles to confirm it is fixed to the component and not a reflection of my body, an adjacent source, or solar loading?
- Did I describe the shape and distribution of the thermal pattern (sharp point at a lug, diffuse warmth across a device body, uniform warmth along a conductor, one phase warmer across multiple components) to identify the failure mode, not just the peak temperature?
- Did I classify severity using a consistent rubric that accounts for temperature delta, absolute temperature relative to rating, load criticality, whether load was representative, and the trend from prior inspections?
- Did I label any scan performed at low or unstable load as inconclusive and schedule a re-inspection under proper load, rather than reporting it as a clean bill of health?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
