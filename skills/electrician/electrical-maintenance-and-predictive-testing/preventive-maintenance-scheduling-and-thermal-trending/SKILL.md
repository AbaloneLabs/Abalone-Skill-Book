# Preventive Maintenance Scheduling and Thermal Trending

Preventive electrical maintenance fails in two opposite ways: it is either performed so rarely that connections degrade undetected until they fail, or it is performed so aggressively — tightening every lug on every visit — that it accelerates the very damage it is meant to prevent. The judgment problem is that a maintenance schedule is not a calendar exercise but a risk-based decision that depends on load duty, environment, equipment age, and the rate at which measured parameters are changing, and the most common "maintenance" action, re-torquing a live connection, is often the wrong action applied to the wrong connection at the wrong time. An electrician who torques every terminal on an annual visit and records "all tight" has not performed maintenance; they have overstressed soft metal terminations, masked the connection that was actually loosening, and produced a log that looks diligent while concealing the trend that would have predicted the next failure. This skill covers how to set maintenance intervals that reflect real risk, how to trend degradation through repeated measurement, and when tightening is the correct response versus when it causes harm.

## Core Rules

### Base Maintenance Intervals on Risk, Not on a Fixed Calendar

A uniform annual inspection applied to every panel treats a lightly loaded residential subpanel the same as a 24/7 industrial feeder running at 90% duty in a hot, vibrating environment, and both are wrong for different reasons. Maintenance interval should be driven by the factors that actually accelerate connection degradation: load magnitude and duty cycle (heavily loaded connections cycle more and loosen faster), thermal cycling (frequent load changes expand and contract metal), environmental stress (heat, vibration, corrosion, contamination), equipment age and technology (older bolted connections and certain spring-clamp technologies behave differently), and the consequence of failure (life-safety and critical-process equipment warrants shorter intervals). The defense is to assign each asset a maintenance interval derived from these factors — NETA and similar frameworks provide tiered recommendations (e.g., 6 to 18 months for critical rotating and high-stress equipment, longer for lightly loaded systems) — and to shorten the interval when trending shows a parameter worsening, rather than waiting for the next calendar date. A schedule that is never adjusted in response to findings is a schedule that is not learning.

### Trend Measurements Over Time, Do Not Evaluate Each Reading in Isolation

A single contact resistance or temperature reading is a snapshot that cannot distinguish a connection that has always measured that way from one that is actively degrading. The predictive power of maintenance comes from trending: measuring the same parameter at the same point under comparable conditions on each visit, recording the value against a baseline, and watching the trajectory. A connection whose contact resistance has risen from 0.05 mΩ to 0.08 mΩ to 0.14 mΩ over three cycles is failing even if 0.14 mΩ is below any absolute alarm threshold, because the rate of change predicts that it will cross the threshold before the next inspection. The defense is to maintain a persistent record per asset and per connection point, to plot or tabulate the trend, and to trigger action on the slope of degradation as much as on the absolute value. A maintenance program without trended data is reactive, not predictive — it catches failures only after they have already crossed an absolute threshold, which is often too late.

### Measure Contact Resistance With a Microhmmeter or DLRO, Not by Re-Torquing

The correct way to assess whether a bolted connection has degraded is to measure its contact resistance with a digital low-resistance ohmmeter (DLRO, microhmmeter) that injects a meaningful test current and resolves down to microhms. Contact resistance is the direct indicator of connection health: a clean, well-torqued joint reads in the tens of microhms, and a degraded joint reads in the hundreds or thousands. The defense is to use the DLRO as the diagnostic instrument and to treat re-torquing as a repair action applied only after the measurement confirms degradation, never as the inspection method itself. Re-torquing as inspection is doubly wrong: it cannot detect a connection that is tight but high-resistance due to oxidation or fretting, and it damages connections that were fine (see the trap below). Measure first; tighten only what the measurement proves needs it.

### Torque to Specification With a Calibrated Torque Tool, Never "By Feel"

When a connection does require tightening, the torque must be applied with a calibrated torque wrench or screwdriver to the manufacturer's specified value, never estimated by hand. The torque specification exists because the clamping force that makes a good joint depends on bolt size, material, and lubrication, and "snug" or "good and tight" by feel is irreproducible — it under-tightens large lugs and over-tightens small ones. The defense is to carry calibrated torque tools covering the range of fastener sizes in the equipment, to look up and apply the manufacturer's torque value (not a generic table), and to record the applied torque. A connection torqued by feel and recorded as "checked" provides no evidence and no trend, because the next person cannot know what force was applied or whether the value changed.

### Do Not Re-Tighten Connections That Are Already at Spec

The most common and most damaging maintenance error is re-tightening every lug on every visit regardless of its condition. Bolted electrical connections are designed to be torqued once to a specific value that yields the correct clamping force; repeatedly re-torquing the same fastener over-stresses the threads and the soft copper or aluminum of the lug, causes cold flow (the metal permanently deforms and thins under the bolt), and can leave the connection looser and higher-resistance than before the "maintenance." This is especially true of compression and mechanical lug connections and of older aluminum terminations. The defense is to verify the existing torque with a torque wrench set to the specification — if the fastener does not move under the specified torque, the connection is still correct and must not be tightened further; if it moves, tighten to spec, mark it, and flag it for closer trending because a connection that loosened once is likely loosening. Never apply more torque than the specification to "make sure."

### De-Energize Before Tightening — Do Not Tighten Live Connections

Torquing a connection requires direct tool contact with the energized terminal, which places the worker's hands and a conductive tool inside the arc-flash boundary and creates a real risk of phase-to-phase or phase-to-ground contact through the tool. Except for narrowly justified, documented, and PPE-covered live work (and in many jurisdictions, not even then), connections should be de-energized, verified dead, and locked out before any torque work. The trap is treating a quick "tighten this one hot lug" as routine; the harm is arc-flash exposure that a brief outage would have eliminated. The defense is to schedule torque work as a de-energized task, to plan outages to accommodate it, and to refuse to tighten live connections as a default practice. The convenience of avoiding an outage is not a justification for arc-flash exposure, and the thermography that identified the hot connection can guide the de-energized repair.

### Combine Mechanical, Thermal, and Electrical Tests Into One Coherent Cycle

Effective predictive maintenance integrates three measurement types: thermography (non-contact, under load, finds heating connections while running), contact resistance / DLRO (de-energized, confirms the connection health that thermography flagged), and torque verification (de-energized, ensures clamping force is correct). Each catches what the others miss: thermography finds the hot connection but cannot tell you the resistance value; DLRO gives the resistance but requires an outage; torque verification confirms mechanical integrity but says nothing about oxidation. The defense is to sequence these into a coherent cycle — thermograph under load to target suspect connections, then schedule an outage to DLRO and torque-verify those connections and clean or re-terminate as the measurements dictate — rather than performing each test in isolation and failing to connect the findings. A program that does only thermography misses high-resistance connections that are cold at inspection load; a program that does only torque checks misses oxidized but tight joints.

## Common Traps

### Re-Torquing Every Lug Annually and Calling It Maintenance

The electrician opens the panel, runs a wrench over every terminal, feels each is "tight," and records the inspection as complete. The trap is that re-tightening already-correct connections cold-flows the soft lug metal, reduces the clamping force over repeated cycles, and creates the very loosening the program is meant to prevent — the maintenance is causing the degradation. The false signal is the diligent-looking activity and the "all tight" log entry; the harm is connections that degrade faster than if they had been left alone, with no measurement to detect it. The defense is to measure contact resistance and verify torque against specification rather than re-tightening by default, and to recognize that a connection that does not move under its specified torque is correct and should be left alone.

### Tightening a Live Connection Because the Outage Is Inconvenient

The thermographer flagged a hot lug, and the electrician, unwilling to schedule an outage, reaches in with a wrench to "just snug it up" while the panel is energized. The trap is that torquing requires direct contact with an energized bus, placing the worker inside the arc-flash boundary with a conductive tool, and a slip or a tool drop causes a phase fault with potentially fatal arc-flash energy. The false signal is that the task feels small and quick; the harm is severe arc-flash injury or death from a task that a brief, planned outage would have made safe. The defense is to treat torque work as a de-energized, locked-out task, to plan outages to accommodate it, and to refuse live tightening as a default. The hot connection can wait for a safe outage far better than the worker can survive an arc flash.

### Evaluating Each Reading Against an Absolute Threshold Without Trending

The technician measures contact resistance on a connection, sees 0.20 mΩ, checks it against the pass/fail limit of 0.5 mΩ, marks it "pass," and moves on. The trap is that the same connection read 0.05 mΩ two years ago and 0.12 mΩ last year — it has tripled and is on a clear worsening trajectory that will exceed the limit before the next cycle, but the absolute-threshold evaluation sees only a "pass." The false signal is the green check against the limit; the harm is a failure that the trend would have predicted and that a scheduled re-termination could have prevented. The defense is to record every reading in a persistent per-connection history, to compare each new reading to the prior values, and to act on a rising trend even when the absolute value is still within limits.

### Over-Torquing to "Be Sure" the Connection Is Tight

The electrician, worried about loose connections, torques a lug well beyond the manufacturer's specification, reasoning that tighter is safer. The trap is that over-torque yields and thins the soft copper or aluminum lug, reduces the contact area, can crack the lug or break the bolt, and produces a connection that is mechanically over-stressed and electrically higher-resistance than a correctly torqued one. The false signal is the satisfying feel of a very tight fastener; the harm is a connection degraded by the maintenance itself, which then runs hotter and fails sooner. The defense is to torque only to the manufacturer's specified value with a calibrated tool, to stop when the wrench clicks, and to understand that more torque beyond spec reduces reliability rather than increasing it.

### Cleaning Without Re-Measuring, or Measuring Without Cleaning

The technician cleans and re-terminates a flagged connection but does not re-measure contact resistance afterward, assuming the repair self-evidently fixed it. Or the technician measures resistance on an oxidized connection, gets a high reading, and condemns the component without first cleaning the contact surfaces. The trap is decoupling the action from the verification: a re-termination can be improperly made and read worse than before, and a dirty connection can read high solely due to surface oxide that cleaning would resolve without replacement. The false signal is the assumption that the action had its intended effect (or that the measurement reflects the component rather than the surface film); the harm is either a repair that did not work or an unnecessary component replacement. The defense is to always re-measure contact resistance after any cleaning or re-termination and to compare the before-and-after values, confirming the repair before returning the equipment to service.

## Self-Check

- Did I set the maintenance interval for each asset based on load duty, environment, age, and failure consequence, and do I shorten the interval when trending shows a parameter worsening rather than waiting for the next calendar date?
- Did I measure contact resistance with a DLRO/microhmmeter as the primary diagnostic, rather than using re-torquing as the inspection method?
- Am I maintaining a persistent per-connection record of resistance and temperature readings, and am I acting on the slope of the trend — not just whether each reading is under an absolute limit?
- When tightening was required, did I use a calibrated torque tool set to the manufacturer's specified value, and did I leave connections that did not move under spec torque alone rather than re-tightening them?
- Did I refuse to tighten live connections as a default, scheduling a de-energized, locked-out outage for torque work instead of accepting arc-flash exposure for convenience?
- Did I sequence thermography (under load), contact resistance (de-energized), and torque verification into a coherent cycle, using each finding to target the next test rather than performing them in isolation?
- After any cleaning or re-termination, did I re-measure contact resistance and compare before-and-after values to confirm the repair actually improved the connection?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
