# Power Quality and Harmonic Diagnosis

Power quality problems are the most elusive electrical faults because their symptoms — equipment malfunctions, nuisance trips, overheating, premature failure — do not point clearly to the electrical cause, and the disturbances are often intermittent and invisible to ordinary meters. The judgment problem is that a facility experiencing unexplained breaker trips, transformer overheating, or sensitive-equipment lockups is often misdiagnosed as having defective equipment or inadequate capacity, when the actual cause is a power quality disturbance — a voltage sag, a transient, a harmonic distortion — that a standard multimeter cannot capture. Diagnosing power quality requires the right instrument (a power quality analyzer that records over time), the right measurement window (long enough to capture the intermittent event), and the right interpretive framework (distinguishing harmonic distortion from sags from transients, each with different causes and mitigations). An electrician who treats power quality symptoms as steady-state problems applies the wrong tools and the wrong fixes. This skill covers how to diagnose power quality disturbances, how to measure them correctly, and how to select mitigation matched to the actual disturbance.

This skill complements the motor-fault and systematic-diagnosis skills. Here the focus is the power quality domain and its measurement-driven method.

## Core Rules

### Match the Instrument to the Disturbance Being Investigated

A standard multimeter measures RMS voltage and current at an instant and cannot capture transients (microsecond to millisecond events), sags and swells (cycles to seconds), or harmonic spectra. Diagnosing power quality requires a power quality analyzer (PQA) that samples at high speed, computes the harmonic spectrum via FFT, and records events over time. Match the instrument to the suspected disturbance: a PQA with transient capture for switching-surge and lightning-remainder investigations; a PQA with sag/swell event logging for intermittent trip investigations; a PQA with harmonic analysis for overheating and neutral-current investigations. Using a multimeter to diagnose a transient guarantees the transient is missed, because the meter averages it away. Confirm the instrument can capture the disturbance's timescale and characteristic before deploying it, and verify it is rated for the measurement category (CAT III or CAT IV at the service level).

### Record Over a Window Long Enough to Capture the Intermittent Event

Power quality disturbances are often intermittent — a sag that occurs when a large motor starts, a transient that accompanies a capacitor switch, a harmonic that builds as nonlinear loads come online. A measurement taken at an instant, or over a few minutes, will likely miss the event. Record over a window that spans the operational cycle: at least one full production shift, and ideally several days, to capture the conditions under which the symptom occurs. Correlate the recorded events with the operational timeline (when the symptom was reported) to identify which disturbance caused which symptom. A short recording that captures no events is not evidence that no disturbance exists; it is evidence that the window was too short. Set the recording window to span the symptom's recurrence pattern.

### Distinguish Harmonic Distortion From Other Disturbances by the Symptom and the Spectrum

Harmonic distortion from nonlinear loads (variable frequency drives, switching power supplies, LED drivers, uninterruptible power supplies) produces a characteristic set of symptoms: transformer and neutral conductor overheating (from triplen harmonics adding in the neutral), capacitor fuse blowing (from harmonic resonance), and reduced equipment life. The spectrum (from the PQA's FFT) shows the individual harmonic magnitudes — typically the 3rd, 5th, 7th, and 11th dominate — and the total harmonic distortion (THD) quantifies the aggregate. Distinguish harmonics from sags (which cause dropouts, not overheating), from transients (which cause insulation stress and immediate failure, not gradual overheating), and from voltage unbalance (which causes motor heating but is a fundamental-frequency phenomenon). The symptom pattern and the spectrum together identify harmonics as the cause; treat the spectrum as diagnostic, not the symptom alone.

### Identify the Source of Harmonics Before Specifying Mitigation

Harmonic mitigation options — K-rated or derated transformers, harmonic filters (passive and active), line reactors, phase-shifting transformers, and active front-end drives — address harmonics at different points and with different effectiveness, and selecting the wrong one wastes cost and may not solve the problem. Before specifying mitigation, identify the harmonic sources (which nonlinear loads, and what is their spectrum and magnitude), the system characteristics that amplify them (capacitor resonance, transformer impedance, source stiffness), and the point at which mitigation is most effective (at the source, at a distribution bus, or at the transformer). A passive filter tuned to the 5th harmonic is useless if the dominant harmonic is the 3rd; a K-rated transformer addresses neutral overheating but does not reduce the harmonics themselves. Match the mitigation to the measured spectrum and to the specific symptom being addressed.

### Investigate Sags and Swells as System Events, Not as Defective Equipment

Voltage sags (dips below nominal, typically 0.5 cycles to a few seconds) and swells (rises above nominal) are caused by system events: large motor starts, fault clearing, capacitor switching, load changes, and utility system events. A sag that causes a sensitive controller to drop out is not a defective controller; it is a sag that exceeded the controller's ride-through capability. Investigate the source of the sag (what large load started, what fault cleared, what the utility did) and the ride-through capability of the affected equipment. Mitigation may be a UPS, a constant-voltage transformer, a soft starter on the offending motor, or a redesign of the starting sequence — but only after the source and the susceptibility are identified. Treating the sag symptom as a defective-equipment problem leads to replacing equipment that will fail the same way at the next sag.

### Treat Transients as Distinct Events With Specific Causes and Protections

Transients (surges, spikes, and impulsive events lasting microseconds to milliseconds) arise from lightning, capacitor switching, load switching, and fault clearing, and they stress insulation and can destroy electronic components. Unlike harmonics and sags, transients are not steady-state or cyclical; they are discrete events. Diagnose them with a PQA set for transient capture, identify the likely source (lightning season, switching events correlated with the transient), and specify surge protective devices (SPDs) matched to the location and the transient magnitude. A transient that destroys equipment is not an equipment defect; it is an unmitigated transient. Distinguish transients from sags (longer and lower-frequency) and from harmonics (continuous and spectral), because each requires different measurement and different mitigation.

### Interpret THD and Individual Harmonics Against the Correct Limits

Total harmonic distortion of voltage (THD-V) and of current (THD-I) have different significance and different recommended limits. IEEE 519 recommends voltage distortion limits at the point of common coupling (typically 5% THD-V for systems under 69kV) and current distortion limits that depend on the short-circuit ratio. Individual harmonic magnitudes matter because specific harmonics have specific effects (triplen harmonics in the neutral, 5th and 7th causing motor torque problems). Interpret the measured values against the appropriate limit (IEEE 519 at the PCC, or equipment-specific limits), not against a generic "anything over X% is bad." A THD-I of 15% at an individual load may be acceptable; the same value at the service entrance may violate IEEE 519 and indicate a system-level problem. Context determines the significance.

## Common Traps

### Diagnosing Power Quality With a Multimeter and Missing the Disturbance

An electrician investigates intermittent equipment malfunctions with a multimeter, measures the voltage as correct at the moment of measurement, and concludes the supply is fine and the equipment is defective, because the multimeter cannot capture the sag, transient, or harmonic that occurs intermittently. The mechanism is that the multimeter is the familiar tool and reads correctly in steady state, so it is trusted for conditions it cannot capture, and the intermittent disturbance is averaged away or missed entirely. The false signal is the correct instantaneous reading, which reads as a good supply. The harm is that the equipment is replaced or "repaired" without resolving the power quality cause, the malfunction recurs, and the actual disturbance continues to damage equipment. The defense is to use a power quality analyzer for intermittent or distortion problems, to record over a window that spans the symptom's recurrence, and to treat a correct instantaneous multimeter reading as insufficient evidence of supply health.

### Treating Harmonic Symptoms as Overload or Defective Equipment

A transformer overheats, a neutral conductor runs hot, or capacitors blow their fuses, and the electrician diagnoses an overload or a defective component, because the symptoms resemble those of fundamental-frequency problems, and harmonics are not measured. The mechanism is that the symptoms are familiar (overheating reads as overload) and harmonics are invisible to standard meters, so the harmonic cause is not investigated, and the component is replaced or the capacity is increased. The false signal is the overheating itself, which reads as overload. The harm is that the replacement transformer overheats the same way, the neutral continues to carry excessive triplen-harmonic current, and the root cause persists because it was never measured. The defense is to include harmonic measurement in any investigation of unexplained overheating, to measure neutral current and its spectrum, and to treat overheating with no apparent fundamental-frequency cause as a harmonic problem until measured otherwise.

### Recording Too Briefly and Concluding No Disturbance Exists

A power quality analyzer is set up for a short window — an hour or a shift — captures no events, and the electrician concludes the supply is clean and the problem is elsewhere, when the disturbance occurs on a cycle (a weekly process, a seasonal condition) that the window did not span. The mechanism is that a short recording is easier to set up and review, so it is accepted as sufficient, and the absence of events in the window is generalized to the absence of events overall. The false signal is the clean recording, which reads as evidence of a good supply. The harm is that the investigation is abandoned with a false negative, the symptom recurs, and the actual disturbance is never captured. The defense is to set the recording window to span the symptom's recurrence pattern (at least one full cycle, ideally several days), to correlate events with the operational timeline, and to treat a short clean recording as inconclusive, not exculpatory.

### Specifying Mitigation Without Measuring the Actual Spectrum

A facility has harmonic problems, and the electrician installs a standard harmonic filter or a K-rated transformer based on general knowledge, without measuring the actual harmonic spectrum, and the mitigation does not address the dominant harmonics or is installed at the wrong point. The mechanism is that harmonic mitigation is selected by category rather than by measurement, so a filter tuned for typical harmonics may not match the actual spectrum, and the symptom persists. The false signal is the installed mitigation hardware, which reads as a solution. The harm is the cost of ineffective mitigation, the persistence of the symptom, and sometimes new problems (a mistuned filter causing resonance and amplifying a harmonic). The defense is to measure the spectrum before specifying mitigation, to identify the dominant harmonics and their sources, and to match the filter tuning, the transformer type, and the installation point to the measured spectrum.

### Confusing Sags With Equipment Defects and Replacing Susceptible Equipment

A controller drops out or a drive faults during a voltage sag, and the equipment is diagnosed as defective and replaced, when the sag exceeded the equipment's ride-through capability and the replacement will fail the same way. The mechanism is that the equipment's failure is visible and the sag is not, so the equipment is blamed, and the sag — caused by a motor start or a fault elsewhere — is never measured. The false signal is the equipment fault indication, which reads as a defect. The harm is repeated equipment replacement, continued dropouts, and the uncorrected sag continuing to disrupt operations. The defense is to measure for sags when equipment drops out unexplainedly, to compare the sag magnitude and duration to the equipment's ride-through spec, and to address the sag source (soft starter, UPS, sequence change) rather than replacing the equipment.

### Applying IEEE 519 Limits at the Wrong Point or Without the Short-Circuit Ratio

An electrician measures harmonic current distortion at an individual load, compares it to IEEE 519 limits, and concludes the installation violates the standard, when IEEE 519 applies at the point of common coupling and its current limits depend on the short-circuit ratio that was not calculated. The mechanism is that the limits are cited without their context (the PCC and the Isc/IL ratio), so they are applied where they do not govern and without the parameter that determines which limit row applies. The false signal is the citation of the standard, which reads as rigorous. The harm is either false violation findings that drive unnecessary mitigation, or false compliance findings that miss a real PCC violation. The defense is to apply IEEE 519 at the point of common coupling, to calculate the short-circuit ratio, to select the limit row for that ratio, and to treat measurements at individual loads as diagnostic, not as compliance.

## Self-Check

- Did I match the instrument to the disturbance — a power quality analyzer with the appropriate capture capability, not a multimeter — and verify its measurement category rating?
- Did I record over a window long enough to span the symptom's recurrence pattern, and correlate recorded events with the operational timeline?
- Did I distinguish harmonic distortion (overheating, neutral current, spectral signature) from sags (dropouts), swells, and transients (insulation stress), using the symptom pattern and the spectrum together?
- Did I identify the harmonic sources and the system characteristics that amplify them before specifying mitigation, and match the mitigation to the measured spectrum?
- Did I investigate sags and swells as system events (motor starts, faults, switching) and assess the affected equipment's ride-through, rather than diagnosing defective equipment?
- Did I treat transients as discrete events with specific causes, capture them with transient-mode recording, and specify SPDs matched to the location and magnitude?
- Did I interpret THD and individual harmonics against the correct limits (IEEE 519 at the PCC with the short-circuit ratio, or equipment-specific limits), not against generic thresholds?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring mitigation design and licensed sign-off to the qualified engineer where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
