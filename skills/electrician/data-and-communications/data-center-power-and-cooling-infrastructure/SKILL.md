# Data Center Power and Cooling Infrastructure

A data center is the one place an electrician's work must stay alive while everything else fails, which means the rules that govern ordinary commercial power — single feed, sized to load, breaker coordination as an afterthought — are not merely insufficient, they are the exact opposite of what the facility requires. Power and cooling in a data center are a single coupled system: every watt delivered to a server becomes heat that must be removed, and the cooling equipment itself draws power that must survive the same outages. The judgment problem is that electricians trained on office and retail work treat the data hall like a dense office: they run one reliable feed, size breakers to the connected load, and let the mechanical team handle cooling independently. The result is a facility that runs fine at 30 percent load and collapses the first time a feed fails, a UPS is bypassed, or a cooling unit short-cycles. Redundancy, capacity headroom, airflow containment, and the bond between electrical and mechanical design are not optional refinements; they are the load-bearing logic of the space. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a data center power and cooling plant survives its design failure scenarios or fails the first time one occurs.

## Core Rules

### Design Redundancy by Tier, Not by Intuition

Data center reliability is specified by tier (the Uptime Institute tier classification or an equivalent) and the tier dictates the topology: a Tier I single non-redundant path; Tier II with redundant components but a single path; Tier III with multiple power and cooling paths and concurrent maintainability (any single component can be taken offline for service without dropping the load); Tier IV with full fault tolerance and multiple active paths. The tier is not a label applied after the fact — it determines whether feeds are N, N+1, or 2N, whether there is one UPS or two on independent buses, and whether a single failure drops load. The trap is building "redundant-looking" infrastructure — two feeds that share a common upstream source, two UPS units that share a common bypass, two PDUs whose feeds cross-couple somewhere — that satisfies the eye but not the failure analysis. The defense is to define the required tier first, to trace every load path to confirm it has no common-mode single point of failure consistent with that tier, and to verify redundancy by failure analysis, not by component count.

### Deliver True A/B Power to the Rack, End to End

Dual-corded IT equipment is designed so that each power supply feeds from an independent electrical bus (conventionally A and B), so that the loss of either bus leaves the server running on the other. True A/B power requires that the A and B paths remain independent from the utility service through the generator, UPS, PDU, and rack-level busway or whip all the way to the rack PDU and the server's second power supply. The trap is the hidden common point: both rack PDUs fed from the same floor PDU, both feeds sharing a common UPS module or static transfer switch, or A and B whips that share a conduit and a common fault exposure. The defense is to color-code and physically separate A and B throughout (separate raceways, separate panels, separate UPS systems where the tier requires), to land each server's two power supplies on different rack PDUs from different feeds, and to never cross-connect A and B, because a tie between them turns a single feed failure into a total outage.

### Size Power Capacity to Peak Load Plus Headroom, With Breaker Coordination

Data center loads are dense, dynamic, and inrush-heavy: a rack can draw 5 to 40+ kW, servers have high inrush on power-up, and breaker tripping on one circuit can cascade if coordination is poor. Power distribution must be sized to the actual nameplate and realistic peak load with headroom (typically targeting no more than 80 percent of breaker rating for continuous loads, per NEC 210.20 and 215.2), and overcurrent devices must be selectively coordinated so that a fault on a branch trips only the branch breaker, not the panel or PDU main. The trap is sizing branch circuits to the connected nameplate without headroom and ignoring coordination, so that a single server fault or an inrush event trips an upstream device and drops an entire PDU or row. The defense is to derate continuous loads to 80 percent, to perform a coordination study that staggers breaker curves, and to consider inrush from blade servers and PDUs at commissioning.

### Treat Cooling Capacity and Airflow Containment as Part of the Electrical Design

Every watt of IT load becomes approximately a watt of heat, and the cooling system (CRAC/CRAH units, in-row coolers, chilled-water or DX) must remove it or the IT equipment overheats and throttles or fails. The critical insight electricians miss is that cooling is not just a mechanical concern: the cooling units themselves are electrical loads that must be on the same redundant power as the IT load, because a cooling unit that loses power during an outage causes a thermal shutdown that is as total as a power loss. Beyond capacity, airflow management through hot-aisle/cold-aisle containment is what makes the rated cooling capacity usable: without containment, hot and cold air mix, supply temperatures rise, and the CRAC units short-cycle while racks at the top of the cold aisle overheat. The trap is wiring the cooling as ordinary mechanical load on a single feed and ignoring containment. The defense is to put cooling on redundant A/B power consistent with the tier, to size cooling capacity to the IT load with N+1 or 2N redundancy, and to coordinate with the mechanical design on containment and airflow so the installed capacity is actually deliverable.

### Ground and Bond for IT Equipment, Not Just for Fault Clearing

Data center grounding serves two purposes that ordinary power grounding does not emphasize: clearing faults (the NEC purpose) and providing a stable, low-noise reference for sensitive IT equipment (the TIA-607 / J-STD-607 purpose). The telecommunications grounding and bonding system — the TMGB (telecommunications main grounding busbar), TGBs, and the bonding backbone — must be installed and bonded to the building's grounding electrode system so that all racks, cabinets, and cable trays sit at the same potential. The trap is bonding each rack to the nearest convenient metal or leaving rack grounding to the IT team, creating potential differences that drive ground-loop currents on data cable shields and cause intermittent errors and surge damage. The defense is to install the telecommunications bonding system per TIA-607, to bond every rack and cabinet to the TGB with a dedicated bonding conductor, and to ensure a single reference to the grounding electrode system, avoiding multiple references that create loops.

### Seal Penetrations and Preserve Fire and Smoke Compartmentation

Data centers are typically fire- and smoke-rated compartments, and the dense cabling, conduit, and piping that penetrates the walls and floors (power feeds, chiller piping, cable trays, busway) each breach the rating unless sealed with a listed firestop system. The high density and the presence of large floor and ceiling penetrations for airflow and cable make this especially demanding: an unsealed or improperly sealed penetration allows fire, smoke, and hot gases to spread between compartments and defeats the suppression and compartmentation design. The trap is treating firestopping as cosmetic or deferring it to a sub. The defense is to seal every rated penetration with a listed firestop system matched to the penetrating item and assembly, to document each system, to install identification tags, and to re-inspect penetrations after any cable add, because new cables through an existing sleeve frequently reopen a sealed breach.

## Common Traps

### Two Feeds That Share a Hidden Common Upstream Source

An installer provides two feeds to a rack and labels them A and B, but both originate from the same upstream switchboard, transformer, or UPS module, so they appear redundant but share a single point of failure. The mechanism of the trap is that redundancy is only real if the two paths have no common-mode component upstream, and a shared source means a single fault on that source drops both feeds simultaneously, defeating the entire purpose of dual-corded equipment. The false signal is that there are two physically separate cables and two breakers, which looks redundant and passes a casual walk-through. The harm is a facility that fails exactly when redundancy is supposed to save it — during the upstream fault or maintenance event — dropping the entire load that was believed to be protected. The defense is to trace each feed to an independent source, to confirm independence by failure analysis, and to keep A and B on separate switchboards, transformers, and UPS systems where the tier requires.

### Sizing Branch Circuits to Nameplate Without Continuous-Load Headroom

An electrician sizes a rack's branch circuit to the sum of the server nameplate loads and fills the circuit to its full breaker rating, because the numbers add up and the breaker holds. The mechanism of the trap is that data center loads are continuous (three hours or more), and NEC 210.20 and 215.2 require continuous loads to be no more than 80 percent of the overcurrent device rating; a circuit loaded to 100 percent will run hot, age the breaker, and trip under normal sustained operation or during a brief load spike. The false signal is that the breaker has not tripped yet, which proves the circuit is holding today but not that it has the required margin. The harm is nuisance tripping under sustained load, accelerated breaker and conductor aging, and an overloaded circuit that cannot absorb a failover surge from an adjacent failed circuit. The defense is to derate continuous loads to 80 percent of the breaker, to size for realistic peak plus headroom, and to verify coordination so a branch trip does not cascade upstream.

### Putting Cooling Units on a Single Non-Redundant Feed

To save cost, the CRAC/CRAH units are placed on a single mechanical feed separate from the redundant IT power, on the assumption that cooling is less critical than servers. The mechanism of the trap is that during a utility outage the UPS or generator carries the IT load but the cooling units on the non-redundant feed lose power, so the data hall heats rapidly (often within minutes at high density) and the IT equipment thermally shuts down — making the carefully protected IT power useless. The false signal is that the cooling runs fine on normal power and the IT power is fully redundant, which suggests the facility is protected when the thermal path is the actual vulnerability. The harm is a thermal cascade outage during the precise event the redundancy was built to survive. The defense is to place cooling on redundant power consistent with the tier, to size cooling capacity with N+1 or 2N redundancy, and to include cooling in the generator and UPS strategy for the ride-through window.

### Ignoring Hot-Aisle/Cold-Aisle Containment So Installed Cooling Capacity Is Unusable

The mechanical team installs CRAC units sized to the IT load, but the data hall is laid out without hot/cold aisle containment, so supply and return air mix freely in the room. The mechanism of the trap is that without containment the cold supply air is diluted by hot return air before it reaches the server intakes, the supply temperature must be set lower to compensate, the CRAC units short-cycle, and racks at the top of the cold aisle and at the ends of rows draw in mixed (warmer) air and overheat even though total cooling tonnage appears adequate. The false signal is that the total installed cooling tonnage exceeds the IT load, which suggests surplus capacity when the deliverable capacity at the rack intake is far lower. The harm is localized hot spots, server throttling, and a facility that cannot reach its designed density despite apparently sufficient cooling. The defense is to coordinate containment (hot or cold aisle, blanking panels, under-floor or overhead ducted supply) with the electrical and mechanical layout so that installed capacity is actually delivered to the rack intakes.

### Bonding Each Rack to the Nearest Metal Instead of the TMGB/TGB

During rack installation, each cabinet is bonded to whatever grounded metal is nearest — a floor pedestal, a pipe, building steel — with no common reference, to save running bonding conductors back to a busbar. The mechanism of the trap is that different grounding points sit at different potentials, and connecting racks to multiple references creates ground-loop currents that flow on data cable shields and equipment chassis, injecting noise and transient voltages into the very signaling the cabling is meant to protect. The false signal is that each rack reads continuity to ground, which proves a connection but not a single common reference. The harm is intermittent data errors, unexplained equipment reboots, and vulnerability to surge events that a proper single-point reference would have equalized. The defense is to install the TIA-607 telecommunications bonding system, bond every rack to the TMGB/TGB with a dedicated conductor, and ensure a single reference to the grounding electrode system.

### Leaving Cable and Piping Penetrations Unsealed or Reopened After Adds

Power feeds, chilled-water piping, and cable trays penetrate the data hall's rated walls and floors, and the penetrations are either never firestopped or are reopened when new cables are pulled through existing sleeves. The mechanism of the trap is that firestop works only as a complete, listed assembly; a reopened or partial seal leaves a path for fire, smoke, and hot gases that defeats the compartmentation and suppression design the data hall depends on. The false signal is that the hole looks mostly filled or was sealed once, which addresses appearance but not the current integrity of the rated assembly. The harm is fire and smoke spread between compartments during an incident, failed inspection, and loss of the compartmentation that protects the rest of the facility. The defense is to seal every rated penetration with a listed firestop system, document it, tag it, and re-inspect and restore the seal after every cable or piping add.

## Self-Check

- Did I define the required reliability tier first, and trace every power and cooling load path to confirm it has no common-mode single point of failure consistent with that tier?
- Did I deliver true A/B power to every dual-corded rack, with the two paths independent from utility/generator through UPS, PDU, and rack PDU, physically separated and never cross-connected?
- Did I size branch circuits to continuous-load headroom (no more than 80 percent of breaker rating for continuous loads), account for server inrush, and perform selective coordination so a branch fault does not cascade upstream?
- Did I place cooling units (CRAC/CRAH, in-row) on redundant power consistent with the tier, include them in the generator/UPS ride-through strategy, and size cooling capacity with N+1 or 2N redundancy?
- Did I coordinate hot-aisle/cold-aisle containment (blanking panels, ducted supply, row containment) with the electrical and mechanical layout so installed cooling capacity is actually delivered to the rack intakes?
- Did I install the TIA-607 telecommunications grounding and bonding system (TMGB, TGBs, bonding backbone) and bond every rack and cabinet to it with a dedicated conductor to a single reference?
- Did I seal every fire- and smoke-rated penetration (power feeds, piping, cable trays, busway) with a listed firestop system, document each system, tag it, and re-inspect after any cable or piping add?
- Did I verify, at commissioning, that a simulated single-feed failure, UPS module failure, and cooling-unit failure each leave the load running, rather than assuming redundancy from component count?
- Did I coordinate electrical and mechanical capacity so that every watt of IT load has both redundant power and redundant cooling sized to remove the resulting heat?
- Does the design and documentation allow the owner and another practitioner to confirm the redundancy, capacity, grounding, and firestop integrity against the required tier and standards?
