# Emergency Communication and Mass Notification Systems

An emergency communication system (ECS) or mass notification system (MNS) does more than make a noise: it delivers a spoken or textual instruction that tells people what is happening and what to do, across threats that a fire alarm alone was never designed to address, from an active shooter to a weather event to a chemical release. The judgment problem is that these systems combine fire alarm, voice, digital signage, text, and outdoor high-power speakers, and each layer has its own intelligibility, coverage, synchronization, and priority rules that are easy to get half right. A voice message that is loud but unintelligible does not communicate; strobes that flash out of sync can trigger seizures in photosensitive occupants; a mass notification message that cannot override a lower-priority fire alarm, or that is overridden by one, can leave people with the wrong instruction at the wrong moment. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether an ECS or MNS actually communicates the right message, to the right people, with the right priority, including intelligibility, strobe synchronization, ADA compliance, fire-alarm integration and priority, and the commissioning and documentation that make the system legally accepted.

## Core Rules

### Design for Intelligibility, Not Just Audibility, Where Voice Is Used

Where an ECS or MNS uses voice evacuation or voice instruction, the requirement is intelligibility, the ability to understand the spoken message, not merely audibility, the ability to hear that sound is present. Intelligibility depends on the speaker spacing and power tapping, the reverberation time of the space, the ambient noise, and the overlap between adjacent speaker cones, and it is measured by methods such as the Speech Transmission Index (STI) or Common Intelligibility Scale (CIS) per NFPA 72. A system that is loud but reverberant, or that has speakers too close together so their delayed overlap blurs the consonants, scores poorly on intelligibility even though every seat is above the audibility threshold. High-ceiling, hard-surface spaces (gymnasiums, concourses, warehouses) are the hardest, and they require careful speaker selection, density, and aiming, often with directional horns rather than ceiling speakers.

The trap is spacing speakers for audibility and assuming the message will be understood, when reverberation or overlap makes it a wash of noise. The defense is to design speaker density and power tapping for the space's reverberation, to verify intelligibility with an STI or CIS measurement at acceptance, and to use directional or higher-density speakers in reverberant spaces.

### Synchronize Visible Notification Appliances to Avoid Photosensitive-Seizure Risk

Where more than one visible notification appliance (strobe) is visible from any single viewing position, the appliances must be synchronized so they flash in unison, within the synchronization tolerance required by NFPA 72 and the product listing. The reason is safety, not aesthetics: out-of-sync strobes flashing at slightly different rates create a flicker pattern that can trigger seizures in people with photosensitive epilepsy, and the synchronization requirement exists to prevent that harm. Synchronization is achieved by a synchronization module, by a panel that drives synchronized NACs, or by addressable appliances that sync over the data circuit. Any space where two or more strobes are visible to one viewer, including corridors with strobes on opposite walls and rooms with multiple strobes, must have those strobes synchronized.

The trap is adding strobes on separate circuits or zones without a sync source, so strobes in the same corridor flash independently. The defense is to identify every viewing position where multiple strobes are visible, to drive those strobes from a common synchronized source, and to verify synchronization at acceptance.

### Apply ADA and NFPA 72 Visible Notification Requirements to Every Required Space

Visible notification is required by the ADA and NFPA 72 in restrooms, common-use areas, and wherever occupants who are deaf or hard of hearing may be present, and in many occupancies the system must provide visible notification throughout. The candela rating must be selected from the NFPA 72 room and corridor tables for the actual room dimensions and strobe count, mounting must be at the listed height (wall-mounted typically 80 to 96 in. above the floor, or ceiling per listing), and the appliances must be listed and compatible with the driving circuit. Restrooms require a minimum candela based on the single-strobe rule regardless of size. The ADA also requires that the system not rely on audible-only notification in spaces where it serves people who are deaf or hard of hearing, so visible coverage is an accessibility obligation, not an option.

The trap is providing strobes only in corridors and common areas and omitting them from restrooms and required rooms, or installing the wrong candela for the room size. The defense is to map every required space against the ADA and NFPA 72 visible-notification rules, to select candela from the tables, to mount at the listed height, and to confirm listed compatibility.

### Define Priority and Override Logic Between Fire Alarm, ECS, and MNS

When a fire alarm system and an ECS or MNS share the same notification appliances or the same building, the priority and override logic must be defined so that the correct message reaches occupants for the actual threat. NFPA 72 recognizes that mass notification can take priority over fire alarm where the MNS threat (such as an active shooter or a localized hazard where evacuation would increase danger) requires a different action than fire evacuation. The priority logic must be designed deliberately: which signals override which, whether the MNS can silence the fire alarm audible signal, how manual fire alarm stations are handled during an MNS event, and how the system returns to normal. A system where the fire alarm always wins, or where the MNS cannot override, can deliver the wrong instruction, such as ordering evacuation into an active-shooter zone.

The trap is integrating fire alarm and MNS without defining the priority matrix, so the wrong signal wins during a multi-threat event. The defense is to define the priority and override logic with the authority having jurisdiction and the owner's emergency response plan, to program the cause-and-effect accordingly, and to test the override behavior at acceptance.

### Cover All Intended Audiences, Including Outdoor, Wide-Area, and At-Risk Populations

An ECS or MNS must reach every audience it is designed to protect, which often extends beyond the building to outdoor areas, wide-area campuses, and at-risk populations who may not hear or see a building-based signal. Outdoor high-power speaker arrays (HPSA) and giant voice systems cover assembly yards, parade grounds, and campus grounds; text and email alerts, digital signage, and two-way radios extend coverage to people away from speakers. The system design must account for ambient noise outdoors (traffic, wind), for the dispersion pattern and intelligibility of HPSA units, and for the populations who need redundant paths (a person who is deaf needs text or visual; a person outdoors needs the HPSA, not the indoor speaker). Coverage gaps at the edges of the campus or behind buildings must be identified and filled.

The trap is covering the building interior and assuming the campus is protected, while outdoor and edge areas remain outside the intelligibility or audibility contour. The defense is to map the coverage contour for each layer (in-building voice, outdoor HPSA, text and signage), to identify gaps, and to add layers or devices until every intended audience has a usable path.

### Commission, Test, and Document the ECS/MNS to the Required Standard

ECS and MNS installations must be commissioned, tested, and documented per NFPA 72 and the authority having jurisdiction, including intelligibility testing where voice is used, synchronization verification where strobes are visible together, priority and override testing for the fire-alarm integration, and a record of completion documenting the design basis, the coverage, and the test results. The testing must exercise the actual message paths, including live voice and pre-recorded messages, and must verify that the priority logic behaves as designed. Because these systems address threats beyond fire, the documentation and the recurring test schedule must be maintained so the system remains functional and the staff knows how to activate it under stress.

The trap is commissioning the audio as "loud enough" without an intelligibility measurement, or testing the MNS priority in isolation without verifying the fire-alarm override behavior. The defense is to perform and document intelligibility, synchronization, priority, and coverage testing at acceptance, and to establish the recurring test and staff-drill schedule.

## Common Traps

### Designing Voice Coverage for Audibility and Skipping Intelligibility Measurement

Speakers are spaced so that every seat is above the audibility threshold, and the system is accepted on the basis of a sound-level meter reading, but in a reverberant lobby the spoken message is a blur that no one can understand. The mechanism of the trap is that audibility measures sound pressure, while intelligibility measures the ability to distinguish words, and a reverberant or overlap-heavy design can be loud yet unintelligible. The false signal is the dBA reading at every seat, which proves loudness but not comprehension. The harm is occupants who hear that an announcement is happening but cannot act on it because they cannot understand it. The defense is to design for the space's reverberation and to verify intelligibility with an STI or CIS measurement at acceptance.

### Leaving Strobes in the Same Space on Unsynchronized Circuits

Strobes are added to a corridor and a large room on separate NACs or zones to balance the load, with no synchronization source between them, so from many viewing positions two or more strobes flash at different rates. The mechanism of the trap is that out-of-sync strobes create a flicker pattern that can trigger photosensitive seizures, and the synchronization requirement exists precisely to prevent this. The false signal is that each strobe flashes and is visible, which proves function but not safe synchronization. The harm is a seizure risk to photosensitive occupants, a safety defect in a life-safety system. The defense is to drive all strobes visible from any one position from a common synchronized source and to verify synchronization at acceptance.

### Omitting Visible Notification From Restrooms and Required Rooms

Strobes are installed in corridors and common areas but omitted from restrooms and certain rooms to save cost, on the assumption that the audible signal is enough. The mechanism of the trap is that the ADA and NFPA 72 require visible notification in restrooms and wherever occupants who are deaf or hard of hearing may be present, and audible-only notification fails those occupants. The false signal is that the building has strobes in the main spaces, which looks like compliance but leaves required spaces uncovered. The harm is occupants who are deaf or hard of hearing missing the alarm in those spaces. The defense is to map every required space against the ADA and NFPA 72 visible-notification rules and to install appropriately rated strobes in each.

### Integrating Fire Alarm and MNS Without a Defined Priority Matrix

A fire alarm and an MNS share the notification appliances, but the priority and override logic was never defined with the AHJ or the emergency response plan, so during a multi-threat event the fire alarm evacuation signal overrides the MNS shelter-in-place message. The mechanism of the trap is that the two systems serve different threats that require different actions, and without a defined priority matrix the wrong signal can win, ordering occupants into a hazard. The false signal is that both systems test functional in isolation, which proves each works but not how they interact. The harm is occupants receiving an instruction that increases their danger, such as evacuating into an active-shooter area. The defense is to define the priority and override logic with the AHJ and emergency response plan, program it, and test the override behavior at acceptance.

### Assuming the Building Interior Coverage Protects the Whole Campus

An MNS is designed with in-building speakers and strobes, and the campus is assumed protected, but the outdoor assembly area and the far side of the buildings fall outside the audibility and intelligibility contour. The mechanism of the trap is that in-building devices do not cover outdoor and wide-area audiences, and a system that protects the building leaves the grounds unprotected. The false signal is that the building is loud and clear, which proves indoor coverage but not outdoor. The harm is people outdoors who receive no usable alert during a campus-wide emergency. The defense is to map the coverage contour for each layer, identify gaps at the campus edges and outdoor areas, and add HPSA or other layers until every intended audience has a usable path.

### Accepting the System Without Intelligibility, Sync, or Priority Testing

The ECS/MNS is installed, the speakers and strobes operate on a test activation, and the system is signed off without intelligibility measurement, synchronization verification, or priority-and-override testing. The mechanism of the trap is that a functional activation proves the devices energize but not that the message is intelligible, the strobes are safe, or the priority logic is correct, and these are exactly the properties the acceptance test is meant to confirm. The false signal is that the system activated and sounded, which proves operation but not communication quality or safety. The harm is a system that passes a superficial test yet fails to communicate or fails the synchronization or priority requirements. The defense is to perform and document intelligibility, synchronization, priority, and coverage testing at acceptance and to establish the recurring test and drill schedule.

## Self-Check

- Where voice is used, did I design speaker density and power tapping for the space's reverberation and verify intelligibility with an STI or CIS measurement at acceptance, rather than accepting an audibility reading alone?
- Did I identify every viewing position where two or more strobes are visible, drive those strobes from a common synchronized source, and verify synchronization at acceptance to prevent photosensitive-seizure risk?
- Did I map every required space against the ADA and NFPA 72 visible-notification rules, install appropriately rated strobes in restrooms and all required rooms, select candela from the room and corridor tables, and mount at the listed height?
- Did I define the priority and override logic between fire alarm, ECS, and MNS with the authority having jurisdiction and the owner's emergency response plan, program the cause-and-effect accordingly, and test the override behavior at acceptance?
- Did I map the coverage contour for each layer (in-building voice, outdoor high-power speaker arrays, text and digital signage), identify gaps at campus edges and outdoor areas, and add layers until every intended audience has a usable alert path?
- Did I account for at-risk populations, including people who are deaf or hard of hearing and people outdoors, by providing redundant paths (visual, text, HPSA) rather than relying on a single audible channel?
- Did I perform and document intelligibility, synchronization, priority-and-override, and coverage testing at acceptance, complete the record of completion, and establish the recurring test and staff-drill schedule?
- Did I defer final design, threat-assessment input, priority-matrix approval, and acceptance to the licensed designer, the authority having jurisdiction, and the owner's emergency response planning where the system exceeds my competence?
