# Electrical Bid Strategy and Risk Assessment

A bid is not just a price; it is a bet on what the job will actually involve. The estimate quantifies the known work, but the bid must also price the unknown: the scope gaps in the documents, the schedule risk, the change-order potential, and the risk that conditions on site differ from the drawings. The judgment problem is that the lowest bid wins the work and the lowest bid is usually the one that missed the most risk, so an estimator who prices only the visible work and ignores the risk either wins jobs that lose money or loses jobs to competitors who underpriced the same risk. Agents miss the issues because the bid process rewards apparent precision, while the decisions that determine profit — scope analysis, markup, risk allowance, contract structure — are qualitative and easy to shortcut under deadline pressure.

## Core Rules

### Perform a Scope Gap Analysis to Find What the Documents Do Not Show

Before pricing, the estimator must compare the electrical scope against the contract documents and the other trades to find the gaps: work that is implied but not detailed, work that falls between trades, and work that the documents assign ambiguously. A homerun shown with no panel, a feed to equipment shown on the electrical plan but specified by the mechanical trade, a system integration that no one owns — these gaps are where profit is lost. The defense is to read the documents as an integrated set, to list every scope gap and ambiguity, to request clarifications through the formal process before the bid, and to carry an allowance for the gaps that remain, because the work not shown is still work someone will have to do.

### Set the Bid Markup to Reflect Risk, Not Just Desired Profit

Markup is the percentage added to the estimated cost to cover overhead, profit, and risk. The profit portion is what the contractor hopes to earn; the risk portion is what the contractor charges to accept the chance that the estimate is wrong or the job goes sideways. A bid with thin markup wins more work but has no cushion for overrun; a bid with heavy markup protects against risk but loses competitive work. The defense is to set markup deliberately based on the risk profile of the job — higher for ambiguous scope, tight schedule, or unknown conditions; lower for well-defined, repetitive work — and never to default to a single markup regardless of risk, because a flat markup prices easy and hard jobs the same.

### Carry a Risk Allowance Proportional to the Identified Risks

Beyond markup, the bid should carry a specific risk allowance for the identified risks: a scope gap that may materialize, a site condition that may differ, a schedule that may compress. The allowance is a quantified contingency, sized to the probability and impact of each risk, not a vague pad. The defense is to list the specific risks, estimate the likely cost of each if it occurs, weight by probability, and carry the sum as a documented risk allowance, because an undocumented pad is either stripped under competitive pressure or forgotten when the risk arrives and the money is needed.

### Assess Change-Order Potential and Schedule Risk Before Committing

Some jobs are built for change orders — design-build work where the owner is still deciding, fast-track jobs where design follows construction, occupied renovations where conditions are hidden. These jobs carry high change-order potential and the electrical contractor should price the base work to be competitive while positioning to recover scope additions through well-documented change orders. Schedule risk — a compressed or phased schedule that forces overtime, stacking of trades, or inefficient sequencing — raises the labor cost and should be priced in. The defense is to assess the change-order potential and schedule risk explicitly, to price the base work with those risks in mind, and to set up the documentation and notice procedures that protect the right to recover added costs.

### Choose Unit-Price Versus Lump-Sum Structure to Match the Job's Uncertainty

A lump-sum bid fixes the price for a defined scope; the contractor bears the risk of quantity errors but keeps any savings. A unit-price bid prices each unit of work and the total is the unit prices times the actual quantities; the owner bears the quantity risk and the contractor is paid for what is installed. Lump-sum suits well-defined scope; unit-price suits jobs where quantities are uncertain — trenching in unknown soil, wiring in an existing building, retrofit work. The defense is to match the bid structure to the job's uncertainty, to push for unit-price items where quantities are genuinely unknown, and to price unit items carefully because a low unit price on a quantity that grows is a guaranteed loss.

### Evaluate the Bid Against the Competition and the Market, Not Just the Cost

A bid that covers cost and risk may still be uncompetitive if the market is soft or if a competitor is pricing the work at a loss to keep a crew busy. Conversely, in a tight market the same bid may win comfortably. The defense is to consider the competitive landscape — who is bidding, how hungry they are, whether the work is in their wheelhouse — and to set the final price with that context, accepting that a bid priced purely on cost without market awareness will either leave money on the table or lose every close race.

## Common Traps

### Bidding Only the Visible Scope and Missing the Gaps

The estimator prices exactly what the drawings show and ignores the implied and ambiguous scope. The false signal is that the documents appear complete. The mechanism of failure is that the gaps — the unmapped feeds, the cross-trade work, the unowned integration — become the contractor's responsibility at no additional cost, and the job loses money on work that was never in the bid. The harm is profit consumed by scope that a gap analysis would have caught and priced.

### Applying a Flat Markup Regardless of Risk

The estimator applies the same markup percentage to every bid to keep the process simple. The false signal is that consistency is disciplined. The mechanism of failure is that a flat markup under-prices risky jobs and over-prices easy ones, so the contractor wins the risky jobs that lose money and loses the easy jobs that would have been profitable. The harm is a portfolio weighted toward the jobs most likely to overrun, the exact opposite of a profitable selection.

### Carrying No Risk Allowance and Hoping for the Best

The estimator adds a standard profit margin and no risk allowance, believing that carrying contingency makes the bid uncompetitive. The false signal is that the bid is lean and competitive. The mechanism of failure is that the identified risks — a scope gap, a site condition, a schedule slip — materialize as they usually do, and there is no money set aside to absorb them. The harm is margin erosion or loss on risks that were foreseeable and should have been priced.

### Ignoring Schedule Risk and Pricing at Straight-Time Rates

The estimator assumes normal sequencing and straight-time labor for a job whose schedule is compressed or phased. The false signal is that the labor estimate looks efficient. The mechanism of failure is that the actual schedule forces overtime, trade stacking, and inefficient sequencing, all of which raise the real labor cost above the straight-time estimate. The harm is a labor overrun driven by a schedule risk the estimate never acknowledged.

### Pricing Unit-Price Items Low to Win, Then Watching Quantities Grow

The estimator prices unit items aggressively low to keep the total competitive, assuming the quantities in the bid will hold. The false signal is that the low total wins the job. The mechanism of failure is that the actual quantities grow — more trench, more wire, more devices — and the low unit prices multiply into a large loss. The harm is a job that loses more money the more work is added, because the unit prices were never profitable.

### Bidding Without Regard to the Competitive Market

The estimator prices purely on cost plus standard margin and submits, ignoring who else is bidding and what the market will bear. The false signal is that the price is correct relative to the cost. The mechanism of failure is that in a soft market the bid is uncompetitive and loses, while in a tight market the bid leaves profit on the table that a market-aware price would have captured. The harm is either a lost opportunity or a winning bid priced below what the market would have paid.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform a scope gap analysis across all contract documents and trades, list every gap and ambiguity, request clarifications, and carry an allowance for unresolved gaps?
- Did I set the bid markup deliberately based on the job's risk profile, rather than applying a flat markup to every bid?
- Did I carry a documented, quantified risk allowance for the specific identified risks, weighted by probability and impact?
- Did I assess change-order potential and schedule risk, and price the base work with overtime, stacking, and sequencing impacts in mind?
- Did I choose unit-price versus lump-sum structure to match the job's uncertainty, and are unit prices profitable at the quantities likely to occur?
- Did I consider the competitive landscape and market conditions when setting the final price, rather than pricing purely on cost?
- Did I set up the change-order documentation and notice procedures that protect the right to recover added scope and time?
- Is the bid file documented with scope assumptions, risk allowances, and markup rationale so the basis can be reviewed and the job handed off to operations cleanly?
