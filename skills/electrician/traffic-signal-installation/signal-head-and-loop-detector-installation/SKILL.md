# Signal Head and Loop Detector Installation

The visible part of a traffic signal — the heads, the loops in the pavement, and the pedestrian push-buttons — is what drivers and pedestrians actually interact with, and the quality of its installation determines whether the intersection works correctly and safely. The judgment problem is that these components are mechanical, geometrical, and electrical at once: a signal head aimed at the wrong lane, a loop cut with the wrong geometry or depth, or a pedestrian button mounted outside ADA reach will produce a system that functions electrically but fails operationally. An agent that mounts a head wherever the bracket reaches, saw-cuts a loop without a layout, or pulls conductors without protecting them from abrasion will produce an intersection that passes a continuity test but serves the wrong lanes, misses vehicles, or is inaccessible to disabled pedestrians.

## Core Rules

### Mount and Aim the Signal Head for the Correct Lane and Visibility

Each signal head must be positioned and aimed so the intended lane of traffic sees the indication clearly from the stopping sight distance, and adjacent lanes do not confuse it for their own. The defense is to mount the head at the height and horizontal position per the agency standard and the MUTCD, aim the head vertically and horizontally toward its target lane (using the aim line or the adjustable bracket), install the correct visor (tunnel visor for approach visibility, louver or cut-off visor where the head must not be visible to a conflicting approach), and verify from a vehicle in the target lane that the indication is clear and from the conflicting lane that it is not visible. A head aimed at the wrong lane causes drivers to respond to the wrong signal.

### Select the Visor and Backplate for the Approach and Background

Visors control stray light and prevent a driver from seeing a head meant for another approach; backplates improve contrast against the sky. The defense is to specify tunnel visors for most overhead and pole-mounted heads, louver or directional visors where heads are closely spaced and bleed between approaches is a risk, full-circle or louvered visors for far-side heads, and yellow or black backplates per the agency standard to improve conspicuity. A head without the correct visor can be read by a driver on a conflicting approach who mistakes it for their own green.

### Cut the Inductive Loop With Correct Geometry, Depth, and Turn Count

An inductive loop detector is a coil of wire cut into the pavement; its sensitivity and detection zone depend on the number of turns, the loop dimensions, the saw-cut depth, and the lead-in twist. The defense is to lay out the loop per the design (typically 6x6 foot or 6x50 foot rectangles, with the number of turns dictated by the loop size — three or four turns for larger loops, more for small), cut the slot to the specified depth (typically 1.5 to 2 inches, deep enough to seat the wire below the surface), clean the slot thoroughly, install the specified number of turns, and twist the lead-in pair a minimum of 4 to 6 turns per foot from the loop to the pull box. A loop with the wrong turn count, shallow cut, or untwisted lead-in will miss vehicles or false-call.

### Protect the Loop Wire and Seal the Saw-Cut Against Water

The loop wire in the pavement is immersed in a harsh environment of traffic, water, freeze-thaw, and pavement movement, and any breach leads to failure. The defense is to use loop wire with the correct insulation (typically THHN/THWN with XHHW or polyethylene jacket rated for direct burial in pavement), seat the wire at the bottom of the clean, dry slot, backfill the slot with the specified sealant (loop sealant, epoxy, or polyurethane), verify there are no air voids or wire protrusions, and protect the lead-in at the pavement edge from abrasion. A loop that is shallow, poorly sealed, or abraded at the edge fails within a season.

### Terminate the Lead-In Cable and Set Detector Sensitivity by Testing

The loop lead-in connects to the detector card in the cabinet, and the detector sensitivity must be set to detect the target vehicles reliably without false-calling on adjacent lanes or overhead objects. The defense is to run continuous lead-in cable from the loop to the cabinet (minimizing splices, which add capacitance and failure points), terminate per the detector channel, measure the loop inductance to verify it is in the expected range (confirming the turns and continuity), and set the detector sensitivity by driving a motorcycle, car, and truck over the loop and confirming each is detected without false calls when the lane is empty. A sensitivity set by guesswork either misses small vehicles or false-calls.

### Wire the Pedestrian Push-Button to ADA Height, Reach, and Reach Requirements

Pedestrian push-button stations must be accessible per the Americans with Disabilities Act and the MUTCD: mounted at the correct height (typically 38 to 42 inches), within reach of a level landing, with a level clear space for a wheelchair, and with accessible pedestrian signals (APS) where required. The defense is to mount the push-button at the ADA-specified height and reach from a firm, level surface, install the APS audible/tactile indicator where the crossing requires it, wire the button to the correct pedestrian phase input, and verify the button calls the pedestrian phase and the APS announces correctly. A button mounted too high, too far from the curb, or without APS excludes disabled pedestrians and violates the standard.

## Common Traps

### Aiming the Head at the Wrong Lane From an Adjustable Bracket

The head is mounted on a mast arm with an adjustable bracket, and the installer leaves it at the shipping angle instead of aiming it at the target lane. The mechanism of the failure is that the beam pattern points at the adjacent lane or into the sky, so the target lane sees a dim or off-axis indication and drivers cannot read the signal from the stopping distance. The false signal is that the head "is mounted," implying it is aimed, when the aim was never verified from a vehicle. The harm is poor visibility that can cause a driver to miss a signal change.

### Omitting the Visor and Letting a Conflicting Approach Read the Green

A head is installed without a visor, or with the wrong visor, and a driver on a conflicting approach can see the green indication meant for the target lane. The mechanism of the failure is that without a cut-off or tunnel visor, the wide beam of the LED or incandescent indication is visible off-axis, so a driver waiting on the cross street sees a green that is not theirs and proceeds into the intersection against a conflicting movement. The false signal is that the head "is bright enough," implying it is controlled, when the stray light reaches the wrong approach. The harm is a right-angle conflict from a driver acting on the wrong green.

### Cutting the Loop Too Shallow or With the Wrong Turn Count

The saw-cut is run at one inch instead of two, or the installer lays two turns instead of four in a small loop. The mechanism of the failure is that a shallow loop is exposed to traffic wear and freeze-thaw, and the wire is damaged or the slot opens within months; a loop with too few turns has low inductance and poor sensitivity, missing small vehicles or motorcycles. The false signal is that the loop "is in the pavement," implying function, when the depth or turns are wrong. The harm is a loop that fails quickly or misses vehicles, requiring a recut.

### Poorly Sealing the Loop and Letting Water Cause Failure

The loop wire is installed and the slot is sealed with generic caulk or an incompatible sealant, leaving voids. The mechanism of the failure is that water enters the voids, the wire insulation is submerged in standing water, the insulation absorbs moisture or the wire corrodes at a nick, and the loop shorts to ground or changes inductance, causing false calls or failure. The false signal is that the slot "is filled," implying a seal, when the sealant is wrong or voids remain. The harm is a loop that fails in the first wet season and must be recut.

### Setting Detector Sensitivity by Guess and Missing Motorcycles

The detector sensitivity is set to a default without driving test vehicles, and the loop detects cars and trucks but not motorcycles. The mechanism of the failure is that a motorcycle has a small metal mass and produces a small inductance shift, and a sensitivity set for cars is below the motorcycle threshold, so motorcycles are not detected and their phase is never called. The false signal is that the loop "detects vehicles," implying all vehicles, when only larger ones register. The harm is motorcycles trapped at a demand-actuated signal or running the red out of frustration.

### Mounting the Pedestrian Button Outside ADA Reach and Height

The push-button is mounted on the pole at a convenient height for a standing adult, far from the curb ramp, with no level landing. The mechanism of the failure is that a wheelchair user cannot reach the button (too high, or the pole is in the landscape strip with no paved approach), so they cannot call the pedestrian phase, and the installation violates the ADA and MUTCD pedestrian accessibility requirements. The false signal is that the button "works," implying accessibility, when it is out of reach for the users it must serve. The harm is exclusion of disabled pedestrians and an ADA compliance failure.

## Self-Check

- Did I mount and aim each signal head at the height and position per the agency standard, and verify from a vehicle in the target lane that the indication is clear and not visible to conflicting approaches?
- Did I select the correct visor (tunnel, louver, or cut-off) and backplate for each head based on the approach geometry and bleed risk, and install them per the standard?
- Did I lay out, cut to depth (1.5 to 2 inches), and install the correct number of turns in each loop, and twist the lead-in 4 to 6 turns per foot to the pull box?
- Did I use loop-rated wire, seat it at the bottom of a clean dry slot, backfill with the specified loop sealant without voids, and protect the lead-in at the pavement edge from abrasion?
- Did I run continuous lead-in cable to the cabinet (minimizing splices), measure the loop inductance to confirm turns and continuity, and set detector sensitivity by driving a motorcycle, car, and truck over each loop?
- Did I mount the pedestrian push-button at the ADA height (38 to 42 inches) within reach of a firm level landing, install APS where required, wire it to the correct pedestrian phase, and verify the call and announcement?
- Did I pull all conductors through poles and bases without abrasion, protect them at conduit edges, and leave sufficient slack and loop-form for future service?
- Is the installation documented with head aim, visor type, loop layouts and turns, detector settings, and push-button locations, so the field work is traceable and verifiable?
