# Demand Factor and Diversity Application

Demand factors are the NEC's recognition that not everything connected to an electrical system operates at the same time or at full rating, and applying them correctly is what separates a service sized for the actual conditions from one sized for a theoretical maximum that will never occur. The judgment problem is that demand factors are granted for specific, code-defined circumstances and are routinely misapplied in two opposite directions: an electrician applies a demand factor where the NEC does not permit one, under-sizing a feeder or service so it operates near or beyond its rating under real load, or an electrician refuses a demand factor the code explicitly grants, over-sizing the system at unnecessary cost and sometimes creating protection-coordination problems. Both errors stem from the same root: treating demand factors as rules of thumb rather than as specific allowances tied to specific load characteristics. This skill covers when demand factors apply, how to compute diversified loads correctly, and where diversity must not be assumed.

This skill complements the continuous-load skill, which addresses the 125% sizing rule. Here the focus is the diversity allowance and its limits.

## Core Rules

### Apply a Demand Factor Only Where the NEC Explicitly Grants One

A demand factor is the ratio of the maximum demand to the total connected load, and the NEC permits its use only in the specific articles and tables that define it. The general method for services and feeders (Article 220 Part III) grants demand factors for general lighting and receptacles through Table 220.42, for ranges through Table 220.55, for dryers through Table 220.54, and for specific equipment types in defined contexts. The optional method (Article 220 Part IV) grants different, often more generous demand factors but only for qualifying occupancies and with its own rules. The discipline is to identify the specific table or paragraph that authorizes the factor, to confirm the load qualifies under that provision, and to apply the factor exactly as written. A demand factor applied because "it seems reasonable" or "we always do it this way" is an engineering assumption, not a code allowance, and it leaves the installation non-compliant and potentially unsafe. When in doubt, the connected load governs.

### Distinguish Connected Load, Calculated Load, and Demand

These three quantities are different and confusing them is the source of most errors. Connected load is the sum of the nameplate ratings of all equipment — the theoretical maximum if everything ran at full capacity simultaneously. Calculated (or computed) load is the result of applying the NEC's rules, including demand factors, to arrive at the value used for sizing. Demand is what the system actually draws at any moment, which the calculated load is meant to estimate as a safe upper bound. The NEC does not permit sizing to measured demand alone, because a short measurement window may miss the peak; sizing is to the calculated load, which is the code-defined estimate of the maximum demand the system will see. Understand which quantity you are computing at each step, label it correctly, and never substitute a measured demand for the calculated load when sizing, because the measured value may not represent the design peak.

### Verify the Conditions That Justify a Demand Factor Before Applying It

Every demand factor in the NEC is conditioned on assumptions about how the loads behave, and applying the factor without confirming the conditions invalidates it. Table 220.42's lighting demand factors assume that not all lighting in a large building operates simultaneously; if a building's operational pattern means most lighting is on at once (a 24-hour facility, an open-plan office with full daytime lighting), the factor may overstate the diversity and the feeder runs hotter than designed. The range and dryer tables assume residential usage patterns and do not apply to commercial installations. The optional method assumes a mix of loads and has minimum size requirements. Before applying any factor, confirm the load type, the occupancy, the usage pattern, and any area or count thresholds, and document the basis. If the actual usage pattern diverges from the assumption behind the factor, apply engineering judgment to increase the sizing even though the code minimum permits the factor.

### Account for the Largest Motor Separately

NEC 220.50 requires that the largest motor in a group be calculated at 125% of its full-load current, reflecting the inrush and the running load of the motor that dominates the demand. This is not a demand factor; it is an increase applied to one motor before any diversity is considered for the motor group. Failing to apply the 125% to the largest motor under-sizes the feeder for the motor's contribution; applying it to every motor over-sizes the system. Identify the largest motor by full-load current, apply 125% to it, and calculate the remaining motors at 100%. This interacts with the motor load demand factors where applicable, and the order of operations matters: apply the 125% first, then any permitted diversity.

### Do Not Apply Residential Demand Factors to Commercial or Industrial Loads

The demand factor tables for ranges (220.55), dryers (220.54), and the residential optional method are written for dwelling unit usage patterns and explicitly do not apply to commercial or industrial installations. A commercial kitchen with multiple ranges cannot use Table 220.55; a laundromat cannot use Table 220.54. Commercial and industrial loads are generally calculated at connected load unless a specific article grants a demand factor (for example, receptacle loads in some contexts, or the noncoincident load provisions of 220.60). The trap is carrying over a familiar residential factor into a commercial context because the equipment looks similar. Confirm the occupancy classification and use only the factors permitted for that occupancy.

### Use Noncoincident Load Provisions Only When Loads Cannot Operate Simultaneously

NEC 220.60 permits omitting the smaller of two or more loads that are unlikely to operate simultaneously, sizing to the largest. This is a diversity allowance, but it is conditioned on the loads genuinely not being able to run at the same time — for example, heating and cooling in a system that is interlocked so both cannot run, or two pieces of equipment where operation of one precludes the other. The provision is not a license to assume loads won't coincide merely because they usually don't; it requires that they cannot. Verify the interlock or the operational impossibility before claiming noncoincidence, and document the basis. If the loads can run together, even if they rarely do, both must be included in the calculation.

### Reconcile the Standard and Optional Methods Deliberately

The standard method (Part III) and the optional method (Part IV) can yield different results for the same installation, and the optional method is often more generous. The choice is not arbitrary: the optional method has specific applicability limits (it is available for dwelling units, schools, certain existing installations, and others as defined), and mixing rules between the methods is prohibited. Choose the method based on applicability, apply it consistently throughout the calculation, and do not cherry-pick the most favorable provision from each. Document which method was used and why. When both methods are available, the choice is a design decision informed by cost and by the conservatism appropriate to the installation; the optional method's greater diversity is acceptable when the load mix justifies it, but understand that it produces a smaller service.

## Common Traps

### Applying a Demand Factor Because It Seems Reasonable

An electrician faces a large connected load and applies a demand factor to bring the service size down to a manageable and economical level, reasoning that "not everything will run at once," without confirming that the NEC grants a factor for that load type in that context. The mechanism is that the reasoning feels sound because diversity is real in many systems, so the electrician generalizes a valid concept into an unauthorized application, and the code's specific conditions are overlooked. The false signal is the sensible-looking reduced load and the economical service size, which read as good engineering. The harm is that the installation is non-compliant and the service or feeder may be under-sized for the actual peak demand, causing overheating, nuisance tripping, voltage drop, and accelerated conductor insulation degradation, with the error hidden until the load grows or the peak occurs. The defense is to apply a demand factor only where the NEC explicitly grants one, to cite the specific table or paragraph, and to size to connected load when no factor applies.

### Carrying a Residential Demand Factor Into a Commercial Context

An electrician accustomed to residential work calculates a small commercial kitchen and applies Table 220.55 (range demand) or the residential dryer factor, because the equipment is similar and the factor is familiar, without recognizing that those tables are dwelling-unit-specific. The mechanism is that pattern recognition from residential work supplies the factor automatically, and the occupancy distinction in the table heading is not checked, so a factor meant for intermittent household use is applied to a commercial load that runs continuously and simultaneously. The false signal is the familiar calculation, which reads as routine. The harm is a severely under-sized feeder that overheats under the commercial load profile the factor was never meant to cover, creating a fire risk and a code violation. The defense is to confirm the occupancy classification before selecting any table, to treat residential and commercial factors as non-interchangeable, and to calculate commercial loads at connected load absent a specific commercial allowance.

### Confusing Measured Demand With Calculated Load

An electrician measures the actual current on an existing feeder with a clamp meter, finds it well below the conductor rating, and concludes the feeder has ample spare capacity for added load, using the measured value as the basis for the addition. The mechanism is that the single measurement is concrete and available while the calculated load requires reconstruction from connected equipment, so the measurement is substituted for the calculation, and the measurement may have been taken during a low-demand period that does not represent the design peak. The false signal is the meter reading, which reads as empirical and reliable. The harm is that the added load pushes the real peak above the conductor rating, and the measurement that looked authoritative provided false comfort because it captured the wrong moment. The defense is to size from the calculated load per the NEC, to use measurements only to validate that the calculated load is reasonable, and to recognize that a single demand measurement is a snapshot, not a design basis.

### Forgetting the Largest Motor 125% Factor

In a calculation with multiple motors, the electrician sums the full-load currents and applies any motor demand factor, forgetting that NEC 220.50 requires 125% of the largest motor's full-load current, and the feeder is sized to the sum at 100%. The mechanism is that the 125% rule is a specific provision easily overlooked in a multi-step calculation, and the sum-at-100% feels complete, so the step is skipped. The false signal is the completed calculation that accounts for all motors, which reads as thorough. The harm is that the feeder is under-sized for the inrush and running demand of the largest motor, the breaker may trip on startup, and the conductors carry more than their rating during motor operation. The defense is to build the 125% largest-motor step into every motor calculation as a standard sub-procedure, to identify the largest motor explicitly, and to re-check the step before finalizing.

### Claiming Noncoincidence for Loads That Merely Tend Not to Run Together

An electrician omits a heating load from a calculation because "the cooling runs in summer and the heating in winter," but the system has no interlock and both can operate, so the noncoincident provision of 220.60 is applied without its condition being met. The mechanism is that the seasonal pattern makes noncoincidence feel true on average, so the provision is applied based on typical behavior rather than on the impossibility of simultaneous operation that the code requires. The false signal is the logical seasonal argument, which reads as sound engineering judgment. The harm is that on a day when both run — a cold morning with air conditioning started early, or a control failure — the feeder carries both loads and is overloaded, with the violation hidden by the "reasonable" assumption. The defense is to apply 220.60 only when an interlock or physical impossibility prevents simultaneous operation, to document the interlock, and to include both loads when coincidence is merely unlikely.

### Mixing Standard and Optional Method Provisions

To achieve the smallest service size, an electrician uses the optional method's demand factors for some load categories and the standard method's provisions for others, combining the most favorable rules from each. The mechanism is that each method is internally consistent but the combination is not, so cherry-picking produces a result more generous than either method permits, and the violation is obscured by the complexity of the calculation. The false signal is the detailed calculation that cites real code provisions, which reads as diligent. The harm is a non-compliant, under-sized service, and the mixed-method error is difficult to detect in plan review because each individual provision looks correct. The defense is to choose one method based on applicability, apply it consistently, document the choice, and treat any calculation that mixes provisions from both methods as non-compliant regardless of how each provision looks in isolation.

## Self-Check

- Did I apply each demand factor only where the NEC explicitly grants it, citing the specific table or paragraph, and size to connected load where no factor applies?
- Did I distinguish connected load, calculated load, and measured demand, and base sizing on the calculated load rather than on a single demand measurement?
- Did I verify the conditions behind each factor — load type, occupancy, usage pattern, area or count thresholds — and document the basis before applying it?
- Did I apply 125% to the largest motor's full-load current before any motor diversity, and calculate remaining motors at 100%?
- Did I confirm the occupancy classification and avoid carrying residential demand factors (ranges, dryers, optional method) into commercial or industrial calculations?
- Did I use the noncoincident load provision (220.60) only where an interlock or physical impossibility prevents simultaneous operation, not where coincidence is merely unlikely?
- Did I choose one calculation method (standard or optional) based on applicability, apply it consistently, and avoid mixing provisions between methods?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final load calculation and licensed sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
