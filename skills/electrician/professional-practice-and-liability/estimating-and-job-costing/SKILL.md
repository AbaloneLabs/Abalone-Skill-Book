# Estimating and Job Costing

An estimate is a prediction of cost, and a bid built on a bad estimate becomes a binding commitment to lose money. The judgment problem is that estimating feels like arithmetic — count the devices, multiply by the price, add labor — but the errors that bankrupt contractors are not arithmetic, they are assumptions: assuming the drawing is accurate, assuming standard labor applies to non-standard conditions, assuming overhead is covered by a flat markup, assuming a change order will be approved. Each assumption that goes unexamined converts uncertainty into a fixed price that the contractor must then deliver at a loss. This skill covers how to perform material takeoff, estimate labor, allocate overhead, apply markup, and manage change orders in a way that produces estimates grounded in the actual demands of the job rather than optimistic defaults.

## Core Rules

### Perform Material Takeoff From the Drawings, Not From Memory

Material takeoff is the process of counting and measuring every item required for the installation, directly from the construction drawings and specifications. The trap is estimating material from a similar previous job or from a walk-through impression, which omits the items that differ and the details that the drawing makes explicit. The defense is to perform a systematic takeoff — counting devices, measuring conduit and cable runs, listing panels and breakers, quantifying supports and fittings — directly from the drawings, and to reconcile the takeoff against the specification book, which often lists requirements not shown on the plans.

### Estimate Labor Against Actual Job Conditions, Not Standard Productivity

Labor is the largest and most variable cost in an electrical estimate, and standard labor units assume ideal conditions: accessible work, new construction, no existing interference. Real jobs rarely match the ideal. The trap is applying book labor units without adjusting for the conditions that slow the work — working overhead, working in an occupied building, existing conduit congestion, restricted access, phasing that fragments the crew. The defense is to walk the job if possible, to identify the conditions that affect productivity, and to adjust the labor units with documented factors that reflect the actual difficulty of the installation.

### Allocate Overhead by Driver, Not by a Flat Percentage

Overhead includes all the costs of being in business that are not directly tied to a job — insurance, vehicles, office, tools, supervision, and non-productive time. The trap is covering overhead with a flat markup applied equally to every bid, which under-recovers overhead on labor-intensive jobs and over-recovers on material-intensive jobs, distorting the bid and hiding the true cost. The defense is to calculate the annual overhead, identify what drives it — often labor hours and project duration — and allocate it to the estimate based on the job's actual consumption of overhead resources, so that each bid carries its fair share.

### Distinguish Markup From Margin and Apply Each Correctly

Markup is the percentage added to cost to arrive at price; margin is the percentage of the price that is profit. A contractor who wants a ten percent margin and applies a ten percent markup achieves only about nine percent margin, because the math differs. The trap is confusing the two and systematically under-pricing, so that the business operates at a lower margin than intended and cannot cover the overhead it believes it is covering. The defense is to decide whether the target is expressed as markup or margin, to use the correct conversion — margin equals markup divided by one plus markup — and to apply the calculation consistently across every line of the estimate.

### Document the Scope and Exclusions in Every Bid

A bid is only comparable to competing bids if the scope is identical, and scope ambiguity is where estimates are won and lost in the wrong direction. The trap is bidding a vague scope to keep the number low, winning the job, and then discovering that the customer's scope assumption was broader — which converts the apparent profit into a loss when the additional work must be performed at the bid price. The defense is to state explicitly what is included and, equally important, what is excluded — specific systems, specific phases, specific equipment — so that the customer and the contractor share the same understanding of what the price covers.

### Price Change Orders at Full Cost, Not at Cost-Plus Habit

Change orders arise when the scope changes after the contract is signed, and they are an opportunity to recover cost and margin — or a trap that erodes profitability if priced casually. The trap is pricing change orders at "cost plus ten percent" out of habit, which under-prices the disruption, the remobilization, and the lost productivity of inserting work into an ongoing project. The defense is to price each change order at the full cost of the additional material and labor, plus a labor burden for the disruption, plus overhead allocation, plus a margin that reflects the risk and the fact that the customer has limited leverage to shop the price once the work is in progress.

### Track Actual Cost Against the Estimate to Calibrate Future Bids

The estimate is a prediction, and the only way to improve future predictions is to compare the estimate against the actual cost incurred. The trap is moving from job to job without reconciling actuals, so that systematic estimating errors — consistently under-counting fittings, consistently under-estimating labor on existing work — persist undetected and compound across every bid. The defense is to track actual material and labor cost against the estimate line by line, to identify where the estimate was wrong and why, and to feed those corrections back into the labor units, material counts, and productivity factors used in future estimates.

## Common Traps

### The Takeoff That Trusts the Drawing Quantities

The estimator uses the quantities shown on the drawing or in a schedule provided by the designer, without independently counting or measuring. The trap is that drawing quantities are frequently wrong — the designer may have used an earlier revision, the count may exclude accessories, or the schedule may not reflect a plan note that adds devices. The false signal is the appearance of an authoritative quantity list; the harm is a material order that is short, triggering expensive field expediting and delays, or a bid that is based on fewer devices than the contract actually requires, converting the difference into an unrecoverable loss.

### Applying Book Labor to a Job With Difficult Conditions

The estimator applies standard labor units — from a estimating manual or historical average — to a job in an occupied hospital, a tight ceiling, or a phased renovation, without adjustment. The trap is that standard labor assumes open new construction, and difficult conditions can double or triple the labor for the same device count, because the crew must work around operations, set up protection, and coordinate access. The false signal is the neat labor number that looks competitive; the harm is a job where the actual labor exceeds the estimate by a wide margin, consuming the margin and then the overhead, and turning a winning bid into a losing project.

### The Flat Markup That Misallocates Overhead

The contractor applies a flat thirty percent markup to every bid regardless of composition. The trap is that overhead is driven largely by labor — payroll taxes, workers' compensation, supervision — and a labor-intensive job with a flat markup under-recovers overhead, while a material-intensive job over-recovers it and prices the bid uncompetitively high. The false signal is a simple, consistent markup that feels fair; the harm is a systematic mispricing that loses labor-intensive work at a loss and material-intensive work at an uncompetitive price, undermining the business across its entire portfolio.

### Confusing Markup and Margin

The contractor wants a fifteen percent profit margin and applies a fifteen percent markup to cost, believing the two are equivalent. The trap is that fifteen percent markup produces only about thirteen percent margin, because the profit is a smaller fraction of the larger price. Across a year of bidding, the contractor operates at two percent less margin than intended, which on a thin-margin electrical business can be the difference between profit and loss. The false signal is that the markup number matches the desired margin number; the harm is a systematic under-pricing that quietly erodes profitability until the year-end financials reveal the gap.

### The Vague Scope That Wins the Bid and Loses the Job

The estimator bids a low number by excluding items that competitors included, without clearly stating the exclusions, and wins the job on price. The trap is that the customer expected the excluded items to be included — the fire alarm interface, the low-voltage cabling, the testing and commissioning — and the contract language is ambiguous enough that the contractor must absorb the additional work. The false signal is the low bid that won the work; the harm is a job performed at a loss because the scope gap was exploited by the customer rather than clarified by the contractor, and the contractor has no recourse because the exclusions were never documented.

### Change Orders Priced at Cost-Plus Without Overhead or Disruption

The contractor prices every change order at material and labor cost plus a small percentage, treating the change as a minor addition. The trap is that a change order disrupts the planned workflow, requires remobilization, often forces work out of sequence, and consumes supervision and coordination time that the flat percentage does not cover. The false signal is that the change looks small on paper; the harm is a series of under-priced change orders that collectively erode the job margin, because each one was priced as an isolated addition rather than as a disruption to an ongoing operation with its own overhead and risk.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform a systematic material takeoff directly from the current drawing revision and reconcile it against the specification book, rather than relying on drawing quantities or memory?
- Did I walk the job and adjust labor units for actual conditions — access, occupancy, congestion, phasing — rather than applying standard productivity without modification?
- Did I allocate overhead based on the job's actual consumption of overhead resources, rather than applying a flat markup that misallocates overhead between labor- and material-intensive work?
- Did I convert correctly between markup and margin, and does the applied calculation achieve the target margin rather than the target markup?
- Did I document in the bid exactly what is included and what is excluded, so that the scope is unambiguous and comparable to competing bids?
- For each change order, did I price the full cost including disruption, remobilization, overhead allocation, and margin, rather than defaulting to cost-plus?
- Did I reconcile actual material and labor cost against the estimate after job completion, and did I feed the discrepancies back into the labor units and factors used in future estimates?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
