# Generator Setup and Bonding

A generator is a separately derived power source only under specific conditions, and whether it is treated as one determines how it must be grounded, bonded, and switched. The judgment problem is that the same physical generator can be correctly installed two completely different ways depending on whether its neutral is switched in the transfer means, and getting the bonding decision wrong produces one of two opposite but equally serious hazards: an ungrounded system during generator operation, or multiple neutral-to-ground bonds that put load current on grounding conductors. Layered on top of that is generator sizing, which is governed not by running load but by motor starting inrush, and transfer switch selection, which must match the load's tolerance for interruption. An electrician who treats a generator as "a plug-in backup" will mis-size it, mis-bond it, or mis-switch it, and the result is either a generator that stalls when the compressor starts, a frame that stays energized on the first fault, or a utility line that is backfed and kills a lineworker. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a generator installation protects the load, the utility, and the people who depend on it.

## Core Rules

### Size the Generator to Starting Load and Motor Inrush, Not to Running Nameplate

Generator sizing must account for the largest step load, which is almost always a motor starting event, and for the continuous running load with realistic diversity. Motors draw five to seven times running current during the first few cycles of start, and a generator sized to running load will droop in voltage and frequency, drop out contactors, and stall when the largest motor starts. The correct method is a step-load analysis: list every load with its running kW, apply power factor to get kVA, identify the largest motor's starting kVA (using the locked-rotor code letter or a soft-start reduction), and confirm the generator recovers each step within voltage and frequency tolerance. Sizing must also apply altitude and temperature derating, because a generator rated at sea level at 25 C loses roughly 3-4% of output per 1000 feet above sea level and proportionally with ambient temperature. The defense is to perform the step-load analysis, apply the derating, and verify recovery, rather than summing nameplates and rounding up.

### Determine Separately Derived Status From Whether the Neutral Is Switched

The single decision that governs generator bonding is whether the transfer switch switches the neutral. If the neutral is solidly connected between the generator and the utility service (a 3-pole transfer switch on a single-phase system, or the equivalent on three-phase), the generator is not a separately derived system, the neutral-to-ground bond is made only at the service, and no bond is made at the generator. If the neutral is switched (a 4-pole transfer switch), the generator is a separately derived system, and a neutral-to-ground bond must be made at the generator or its first disconnect, with the equipment grounding conductors tied to that bond. The trap is assuming every generator is separately derived and bonding the neutral at the generator when the neutral is not switched, which creates a parallel neutral path through the grounding conductor. The defense is to determine separately derived status from the transfer switch pole count, and to match the bonding decision to that status.

### Ground and Bond Separately Derived Systems at Their Source

A separately derived generator must establish its own system reference to earth at its source: a grounding electrode connection at the generator or its first disconnect, bonded to the building grounding electrode system, and a neutral-to-ground bond at the source. The equipment grounding conductors of the loads connect to this bonded neutral, exactly as they connect to the neutral at the utility service. This bond creates the fault-clearing path for the generator system. Without it, a ground fault during generator operation has no reliable return path, the breaker will not trip, and the frame of the generator and any bonded metal can stay energized. The defense is to install a grounding electrode at the separately derived generator, bond it to the building electrode system, establish the neutral-to-ground bond at the generator or its disconnect, and verify the equipment grounding conductors connect to the bonded neutral.

### Select the Transfer Switch Type for the System's Required Performance

Transfer switches are automatic (ATS) or manual, and open-transition (break-before-make) or closed-transition (make-before-break). The type is chosen by the system's required performance. Life-safety and legally required standby systems generally require automatic transfer within a defined time (often 10 seconds for life safety). Optional standby systems may use manual transfer. Closed-transition switches are used where the load cannot tolerate even a momentary interruption (data centers, process loads) and the utility permits momentary paralleling. The switch must be rated for the available fault current at the point of connection and for the load type, including inductive and non-linear loads. The trap is using a manual transfer switch on a system that requires automatic life-safety transfer, or an open-transition switch on a load that cannot tolerate the interruption. The defense is to determine the required transfer performance from the system classification, to select the switch type accordingly, and to verify the fault current and load ratings.

### Prevent Backfeed to the Utility Through Listed Transfer Means

The transfer switch's safety function is to prevent the generator from energizing the utility lines, which would backfeed the utility transformer and energize the service drop at lethal voltage for lineworkers who expect it de-energized. This is achieved by a listed transfer switch that mechanically or electrically guarantees the generator and utility sources cannot be connected simultaneously, except for permitted momentary closed-transition paralleling. Portable generator installations using a manual transfer switch or an interlock kit must use listed equipment that prevents simultaneous connection; backfeeding through a receptacle with a suicide cord (double-male cord) or relying on a homemade interlock is lethal and is a code violation. The defense is to use only listed transfer equipment that prevents simultaneous sources, to install it per its listing, and to never backfeed through a receptacle.

### Specify Cord and Cable for Ampacity, Voltage Drop, and the Abuse Environment

Generator feeders and cord sets must be sized for ampacity per the cable type and for voltage drop over the run length, and they must be a type rated for the environment. SOOW (oil and water resistant, extra-hard usage) is the standard for portable generator cord sets; Type W is used for heavy service; listed cable assemblies are used for fixed feeders. Voltage drop over a long run is the hidden sizing factor: a cable sized for ampacity may still deliver undervoltage at the load if the run is long, which causes motors to overheat and electronics to malfunction. As a rule of thumb, total voltage drop should be held under 3% from the source to the load on most installations, and the cable must be sized to meet that target, which often means upsizing one or two sizes above the ampacity minimum. The defense is to size the cable for ampacity and then check voltage drop, upsizing as needed, and to use a cable type rated for the physical and environmental conditions.

### Match Generator Voltage, Frequency, and Phase to the Building System

Generators must match the load's voltage, frequency, and phase configuration. A single-phase generator on a three-phase load, a 60 Hz generator on 50 Hz equipment, or a 120/240 V generator on a 120/208 V load will not work and can damage equipment. Reconnection generators can be reconfigured for different voltages, but the configuration must match the building system before connection. Frequency stability and waveform quality matter for sensitive electronic loads; small inverter generators produce clean power but limited output, while larger conventional generators may have more distortion. The defense is to verify the generator's voltage, frequency, and phase match the load, to confirm the reconnection settings if applicable, and to check waveform quality for sensitive loads.

## Common Traps

### Sizing the Generator to Running Load and Stalling on Motor Start

A generator is sized to the running load of a building, but when the air conditioner compressor starts, the voltage droops, the contactor drops out, the compressor cycles, and the generator stalls. The mechanism of the trap is that motor starting inrush is many times running current, and a generator adequate for running load cannot supply the momentary starting kVA. The false signal is that the running load fits the generator rating, which is true for steady state but not for the step load. The harm is failure to start critical motors, contactor chatter, potential generator stall, and in severe cases damage to the generator or the motor from repeated unsuccessful starts. The defense is to perform a step-load sizing that includes the largest motor's starting kVA and to verify recovery within tolerance.

### Bonding the Neutral at a Non-Separately-Derived Generator

A generator is installed with a 3-pole transfer switch (neutral not switched), so the generator neutral is solidly connected to the utility neutral, and the electrician also bonds the neutral to ground at the generator, creating a second neutral-to-ground bond. The mechanism of the trap is that with the neutral continuous between systems, a second bond creates a parallel path for neutral current through the equipment grounding conductor and any bonded metal piping. The false signal is that the generator "needs to be grounded," which conflates equipment grounding with neutral bonding. The harm is neutral current on grounding conductors, elevated touch voltage on bonded metal, and interference with fault-clearing coordination. The defense is to bond the neutral at the generator only when the generator is separately derived (neutral switched), and to keep the neutral isolated from ground otherwise.

### Leaving a Separately Derived Generator Ungrounded

A separately derived generator with a 4-pole transfer switch is installed without a grounding electrode connection or a neutral-to-ground bond at the generator, so when the system transfers to generator power the reference to earth is lost. The mechanism of the trap is that a separately derived system must establish its own system ground at its source, and omitting it leaves the system floating with no defined reference and no reliable fault-clearing path. The false signal is that the generator frame is grounded, which is equipment grounding but not system grounding. The harm is a system with no fault-clearing path during generator operation, so a ground fault does not trip the breaker and the frame stays energized until someone touches it. The defense is to install the grounding electrode and neutral-to-ground bond at the separately derived source.

### Using an Unlisted Interlock or Suicide Cord to Backfeed

To avoid buying a transfer switch, the owner uses a double-male cord to backfeed a receptacle from a portable generator, or an unlisted breaker interlock is fabricated. The mechanism of the trap is that simultaneous connection of generator and utility backfeeds the utility transformer, energizing the service drop at lethal voltage for lineworkers who expect it de-energized. The false signal is that the main breaker is off so the circuits are isolated, which the operator believes but which fails if the main is accidentally closed or the interlock is defeated. The harm is electrocution of utility workers and destruction of the generator. The defense is to use only listed transfer equipment that mechanically prevents simultaneous sources.

### Sizing the Cord for Ampacity and Ignoring Voltage Drop

A generator is placed 150 feet from the building and connected with a cable sized to the ampacity of the load, but the voltage drop over the long run delivers undervoltage at the panel, causing motors to overheat and electronics to malfunction. The mechanism of the trap is that ampacity sizing protects the cable from overheating but does not guarantee adequate voltage at the load, and on long runs voltage drop governs. The false signal is that the cable "is big enough for the amps," which is true for heating but not for delivered voltage. The harm is motor overheating, shortened equipment life, and nuisance undervoltage trips. The defense is to size the cable for ampacity and then verify voltage drop, upsizing as needed to hold total drop under roughly 3%.

### Selecting an Open-Transition Switch for a Load That Cannot Tolerate Interruption

A data center loads an optional standby generator with an open-transition transfer switch, and the momentary interruption during transfer drops every server even though the generator starts and runs fine. The mechanism of the trap is that open-transition switches break before make, creating a brief power gap that IT and process loads cannot tolerate, and the generator selection did not account for transfer type. The false signal is that the generator carries the running load, which is true after transfer but irrelevant to the gap during transfer. The harm is load drop precisely during the transfer the system was installed to prevent. The defense is to select closed-transition transfer or a UPS bridging for loads that cannot tolerate the interruption, and to confirm the utility permits momentary paralleling for closed transition.

### Mismatching Generator Voltage or Phase to the Building System

A 120/240 V single-phase generator is connected to a 120/208 V three-phase building, or a reconnection generator is left in the factory voltage setting rather than the building's voltage. The mechanism of the trap is that voltage, frequency, and phase must match exactly, and a mismatch either prevents operation or applies the wrong voltage to the load. The false signal is that the generator produces power and the plug fits, which proves generation but not compatibility. The harm is undervoltage or phase imbalance that damages motors and electronics, or a system that simply cannot carry the load. The defense is to verify voltage, frequency, and phase match before connection and to set reconnection generators to the building configuration.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the generator using a step-load analysis that includes the largest motor's starting kVA, apply altitude and temperature derating, and verify recovery within voltage and frequency tolerance for each step?
- Did I determine whether the generator is a separately derived system from whether the neutral is switched in the transfer means, and did I match the neutral-to-ground bonding decision to that status?
- For a separately derived generator, did I install a grounding electrode at the generator bonded to the building electrode system and establish the neutral-to-ground bond at the source, with equipment grounding conductors tied to that bond?
- Did I select the transfer switch type (automatic/manual, open/closed transition) based on the system classification and the load's tolerance for interruption, and verify its fault current and load ratings?
- Did I use only listed transfer equipment that prevents simultaneous connection of generator and utility, and avoid any backfeed through receptacles or unlisted interlocks?
- Did I size the cord or cable for ampacity and then verify voltage drop over the run, upsizing as needed to hold total drop under roughly 3%, and did I use a cable type (SOOW, Type W, listed assembly) rated for the environment?
- Did I verify the generator's voltage, frequency, and phase match the building system, confirm reconnection settings if applicable, and check waveform quality for sensitive loads?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
