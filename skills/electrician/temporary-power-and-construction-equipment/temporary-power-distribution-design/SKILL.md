# Temporary Power Distribution Design

Temporary power on a construction site is not a relaxed version of permanent wiring. It is a high-abuse environment where cords are driven over, panels are exposed to weather and impact, loads change daily as trades plug in different tools, and the people using the system have no electrical training. The judgment problem is that NEC Article 590 relaxes some permanent-wiring rules (it permits flexible cords for branch circuits, it permits certain cable assemblies exposed, and it allows shorter wiring methods) precisely because the installation is short-lived — but it does not relax the rules that keep people alive: overcurrent protection, grounding, GFCI, working clearances, and conductor ampacity. An electrician who reads "temporary" as "anything goes" will build a system that passes the first inspection and then degrades into a shock and fire hazard within weeks as cords are crushed, panels are overloaded, and phases drift out of balance. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a temporary distribution system delivers power safely for the life of the project.

## Core Rules

### Size the Temporary Service to the Real Construction Load Profile, Not a Guess

Temporary service sizing must account for the actual construction load, which is dominated by motor-starting inrush, welders, and simultaneous tool use rather than the diversity assumptions used for a finished building. A common method is to estimate connected load by trade and project phase, then apply a demand factor appropriate to the work sequencing. The service must also carry the largest step load: when a large compressor or pump starts, the voltage drop at the panel must not collapse other tools or trip the main. Undersizing shows up as nuisance tripping, dimming lights, and overheated conductors at the meter base; oversizing wastes money on a service that will be removed. The defense is to list the expected loads by phase of construction, apply realistic demand factors, size the service and the feeders for the calculated load plus a margin for the largest motor start, and document the basis so the inspector and the next electrician can follow the reasoning.

### Apply NEC Article 590 Correctly: Know What It Permits and What It Does Not Relax

Article 590 governs temporary installations for construction, remodeling, maintenance, repair, demolitions, and decorative holiday lighting, and it permits flexible cords and cables as a wiring method where permanent wiring would otherwise be required. However, 590 does not relax ampacity rules, overcurrent protection, grounding, bonding, GFCI, working space around panels, or the requirement that conductors be protected from physical damage. Temporary feeders and branch circuits must still be sized per Article 220 load calculations, protected per Article 240, and grounded per Article 250. The trap is treating 590 as a blanket exemption; it is a targeted permission for specific wiring methods during a defined period. The defense is to read 590 as permitting flexible cords and listed cable assemblies for the duration of the temporary use, while applying every other applicable article as if the installation were permanent.

### Select Distribution Panels and Spider Boxes for the Environment and the Load

Distribution panels for construction must be rated for the environment: NEMA 3R or better for outdoor and wet locations, with rain-tight enclosures and in-use covers where receptacles are exposed. Spider boxes (listed portable power distribution boxes) must be listed for the application, rated for the amperage and voltage, and equipped with the required GFCI protection. The number and rating of receptacles must match the expected load density: a box feeding multiple high-draw tools needs 20A or 30A receptacles, not a daisy-chain of 15A outlets that will overload. The trap is using a residential outdoor panel or an unlisted power strip for construction distribution, which lacks the environmental rating and the overcurrent coordination for the abuse. The defense is to use listed, environmentally-rated distribution equipment sized to the load, and to verify each receptacle branch is protected at or below its ampacity.

### Choose and Protect Cords and Cables for the Abuse They Will Receive

Cord and cable selection on a construction site is a decision about physical survival. SOOW (oil and water resistant, heavy-duty rubber) and SC-type (extra-hard usage) portable power cables are the standard for temporary feeders and branch circuits because their jacket resists abrasion, oil, sunlight, and crushing. Type W mining cable is used for the heaviest service. The cable must be sized for ampacity and for voltage drop over the run length, and it must be protected from physical damage: where cords cross vehicle or foot traffic, they must be in listed cable ramps or buried at the depth required by the AHJ — never laid across a driveway to be run over. Splices in flexible cords are generally not permitted except on continuous lengths, and any connection must be in an enclosure rated for the location. The defense is to specify extra-hard-usage listed cord, size it for ampacity and voltage drop, protect it at traffic crossings, and eliminate field splices except where a listed connector in a proper enclosure is used.

### Balance Single-Phase and Three-Phase Loads Across the Phases

Load balancing matters on any multi-phase temporary service because an unbalanced system drives neutral current, causes voltage imbalance that overheats three-phase motors, and leaves capacity stranded on the lightly loaded phases. On a 120/240V single-phase service, the two ungrounded legs should carry roughly equal load so the neutral carries only the difference. On a 120/208V three-phase service, the single-phase loads (tools, lights, receptacle spider boxes) must be distributed across A-B, B-C, and C-A so that line-to-neutral and line-to-line loads are spread evenly. The trap is letting one trade pull all its power from whichever receptacle is closest, creating a phase that is loaded to its limit while others sit idle, which causes voltage imbalance and nuisance tripping. The defense is to assign circuits to phases deliberately, measure phase currents under typical load, and redistribute as the work and the trades change through the project.

### Ground and Bond the Temporary System as a Complete, Continuous Path

Grounding and bonding on a temporary system must form a continuous equipment grounding conductor path from every tool frame and every receptacle box back to the service grounding point, with the neutral bonded to ground only at the service or at a separately derived source. The equipment grounding conductor in every cord and feeder must be intact and correctly sized; a missing or broken ground in a long cord run is the single most common defect that turns a tool fault into a lethal shock. Bonding of metal raceways, panel enclosures, and spider box frames must be positive, and any separately derived system (a generator or transformer) must have its own grounding electrode and neutral-to-ground bond. The defense is to verify continuity of the equipment grounding conductor on every cord set and feeder with a tester, to confirm the single neutral-to-ground bond at the service, and to treat any separately derived source as requiring its own electrode and bond.

### Treat Permits and Inspections as Applicable to Temporary Installations

Temporary installations are not exempt from permits and inspections. Most jurisdictions require a permit for temporary construction service, and the utility will not energize the service without an inspection release. The installation must meet the code in effect at the time, and re-inspection may be required when the service is modified or relocated. The trap is assuming a temporary service is informal and skipping the permit, which delays energization, creates liability, and guarantees a correction notice when the inspector arrives. The defense is to pull the permit before setting the temporary service, schedule the rough and service inspections, and call for re-inspection whenever the distribution is materially changed.

## Common Traps

### Sizing the Service to the "Typical" Load and Ignoring Peak Step Loads

The electrician sizes the temporary service to the average running load of the expected tools and lights, but on the day the concrete subcontractor starts, three vibrators and a pump start simultaneously and the main trips or the voltage sags enough to drop out the other tools. The mechanism of the trap is that construction loads are peaky and motor-dominated, and the average masks the step load that actually governs service and feeder sizing. The false signal is that the calculated average fits the service rating, which is true for steady state but not for the inrush of the largest simultaneous start. The harm is lost productivity, nuisance tripping, and overheated conductors at the service head. The defense is to size for the largest realistic step load and to apply demand factors only after confirming the peak is covered.

### Using a Residential Outdoor Panel or Power Strip as Construction Distribution

To save cost, an unlisted power strip or a residential outdoor receptacle panel is used to distribute power around the site. The mechanism of the trap is that residential equipment is not rated for the physical abuse, the continuous load, or the environmental exposure of construction, and its overcurrent devices are not coordinated for the downstream loads. The false signal is that the outlets work and the breaker holds, which proves energization but not suitability. The harm is enclosure failure, water ingress, breaker failure under continuous load, and a shock or fire hazard that develops over the project. The defense is to use only listed, environmentally-rated portable power distribution equipment rated for extra-hard usage.

### Laying Cords Across Driveways and Walkways Without Protection

A 100-foot SOOW cord is run from the panel to a work area straight across the site driveway, and within days the jacket is crushed and the conductors are exposed. The mechanism of the trap is that flexible cords, however heavy-duty, are not designed for vehicular crushing, and unprotected crossings fail predictably under construction traffic. The false signal is that the cord "is heavy SOOW, it can take it," which overstates the jacket's resistance to point loading from truck tires. The harm is exposed energized conductors at a traffic point — exactly where a person or a vehicle will contact them. The defense is to route cords overhead where possible, to use listed cable protectors (speed bumps with cable channels) at any crossing, or to bury the feeder at the AHJ-required depth.

### Letting One Trade Monopolize a Phase and Creating Voltage Imbalance

All the receptacle spider boxes for the framing and roofing crews are landed on phase A, while phases B and C carry only lighting. The mechanism of the trap is that unbalanced single-phase loading drives high neutral current and produces voltage imbalance that overheats any three-phase motor on the site (saws, compressors, hoists). The false signal is that each tool runs fine individually, which is true at light load but masks the imbalance that appears under simultaneous use. The harm is motor overheating, shortened tool life, and neutral conductor heating. The defense is to distribute receptacle circuits across all phases, measure phase and neutral currents under working load, and rebalance as trades change.

### Relying on a Broken or Removed Equipment Grounding Conductor

A long cord set has been repaired with a field splice that omitted the grounding conductor, or a ground pin has been broken off a plug so it fits a worn receptacle. The mechanism of the trap is that without the equipment grounding conductor, a fault inside a tool frame has no return path to trip the breaker, and the frame stays energized at line voltage until someone touches it. The false signal is that the tool runs normally, which is true until a fault occurs and is exactly the condition the ground exists to clear. The harm is electrocution on the first fault. The defense is to test every cord set for grounding continuity before use, to repair or remove any cord with a damaged ground, and to never defeat a ground pin.

### Skipping the Permit and Inspection Because the Job Is "Just Temporary"

The contractor treats the temporary service as informal and does not pull a permit, assuming the utility will connect on request. The mechanism of the trap is that utilities and jurisdictions require an inspection release before energizing a temporary service, and working without a permit creates code violations that surface later as correction notices, fines, or refusal to energize. The false signal is that the service is small and short-lived so the rules do not apply, which misreads both the code and the utility's process. The harm is schedule delay, liability for an unpermitted installation, and potential disconnection. The defense is to pull the permit, schedule inspections, and treat the temporary service with the same documentation as a permanent one.

## Self-Check

- Did I size the temporary service using a load list by construction phase, apply realistic demand factors, and confirm the service and feeders carry the largest expected step load including motor starting?
- Did I apply NEC Article 590 correctly — permitting flexible cords and listed cable assemblies for the temporary period while enforcing ampacity, overcurrent, grounding, GFCI, and working space rules from the other applicable articles?
- Did I select distribution panels and spider boxes that are listed, environmentally rated (NEMA 3R or better), and sized so each receptacle branch is protected at or below its ampacity?
- Did I specify extra-hard-usage listed cord (SOOW, SC, or Type W), size it for ampacity and voltage drop over the run, and protect it at every traffic crossing with listed cable ramps or burial?
- Did I distribute single-phase and three-phase loads across the phases to keep current balanced, and did I measure phase and neutral currents under working load to confirm the balance?
- Did I verify continuity of the equipment grounding conductor on every cord set and feeder, confirm the single neutral-to-ground bond at the service, and treat any generator or transformer as a separately derived system requiring its own electrode and bond?
- Did I pull the permit before setting the service, schedule the required inspections, and plan for re-inspection when the distribution is materially changed?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
