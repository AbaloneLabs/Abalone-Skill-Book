# Separately Derived System and Transformer Grounding

A separately derived system (SDS) is a source that has no direct electrical connection to another system's grounded circuit conductor — most commonly a step-down transformer, a generator with a separately derived neutral, or a UPS output — and grounding it correctly is one of the most error-prone tasks in the NEC because it requires establishing a new neutral-to-ground bond at exactly one point and sizing the grounding and bonding conductors to the source. The judgment problem is that the consequences of a grounding error at an SDS are severe and subtle: a missing bond leaves the system floating with respect to ground, so a line-to-ground fault has no return path and the case stays energized; an extra bond creates parallel neutral current paths that put current on grounding conductors, raceways, and equipment frames, causing magnetic fields, overheated raceways, and nuisance breaker operation. The discipline is to determine whether the source is an SDS, to establish the single neutral-to-ground bond at the source, to size the grounding electrode conductor and the bonding jumper to the source's characteristics, and to verify there is no second bond. This skill covers the grounding and bonding decisions for SDS installations.

This skill complements the grounding electrode system design skill. Here the focus is the separately derived source and its neutral-to-ground relationship.

## Core Rules

### Determine Whether the Source Is Separately Derived Before Grounding It

The NEC defines an SDS as a premises wiring system whose power is derived from a source with no direct electrical connection (including a solidly connected grounded circuit conductor) to another system. A transformer with no direct neutral connection to the primary is an SDS. A generator is an SDS only if its neutral is not solidly connected to the utility-supplied neutral — for example, a 4-pole transfer switch that breaks the neutral, or a 3-wire generator output. A generator with a solidly connected neutral through a 3-pole transfer switch is not an SDS, and grounding it as one creates a second neutral-to-ground bond. The first decision is whether the source qualifies, based on the actual wiring (the transfer switch type, the transformer connection, the UPS bypass arrangement), and this decision determines the entire grounding scheme. Do not assume a source is an SDS by type; verify it from the wiring that establishes or breaks the neutral connection.

### Establish the Neutral-to-Ground Bond at the Source, and Only at the Source

For an SDS, the NEC requires a bonding jumper (the main bonding jumper) that connects the grounded circuit conductor (the neutral) to the equipment grounding conductor and to the grounding electrode at the source or at the first disconnecting means, and this bond must be the only neutral-to-ground connection on that system. The bond establishes the reference to ground that allows overcurrent devices to clear line-to-ground faults and that stabilizes the system voltage. Establish it at the source (the transformer, the generator, the UPS output), bond it to a local grounding electrode, and ensure that no downstream panel or load creates a second bond. The single-bond rule is absolute: a second bond anywhere on the system creates parallel neutral return paths through the grounding system, with all the problems that follow. Verify, after installation, that the neutral is isolated from ground at all sub-panels and load equipment on the SDS.

### Size the Grounding Electrode Conductor to the SDS, Using the Correct Reference

The grounding electrode conductor (GEC) for an SDS is sized per NEC Table 250.66, based on the size of the derived phase conductors or the equivalent area, not on the ungrounded conductor of the primary. The GEC connects the SDS's grounded neutral to the local grounding electrode (building steel, a ground ring, a concrete-encased electrode, or a made electrode). Size it from Table 250.66 using the derived phase conductor size, and where the SDS is a transformer, the reference is the secondary conductor size. For large systems, the GEC may be substantial. Confirm the electrode type and the GEC sizing together, because the electrode requirements (and exceptions, such as the concrete-encased electrode minimum) interact with the conductor sizing. Do not size the GEC to the primary conductors or to a default size; size it to the SDS's derived conductors per the table.

### Size the Main Bonding Jumper and Equipment Bonding Jumpers to Carry Fault Current

The main bonding jumper at the SDS and the equipment bonding jumpers that connect equipment grounding conductors must be sized to carry the available fault current and to ensure rapid operation of the overcurrent device. For the main bonding jumper at the source, NEC 250.28 requires it to be not smaller than the sizes in Table 250.66 (based on the derived phase conductors) and, where it is a wire type, not smaller than specified. For equipment grounding conductors on the secondary side, Table 250.122 applies (based on the overcurrent device rating). The bonding path must be continuous, low-impedance, and capable of carrying the fault current long enough for the breaker or fuse to clear. An undersized or high-impedance bond delays clearing or fails during the fault, leaving equipment energized. Verify the bond sizing against both tables and the available fault current.

### Do Not Create a Second Neutral-to-Ground Bond Downstream

The single most common SDS grounding error is a second neutral-to-ground bond at a downstream panel — a sub-panel where the neutral bar is bonded to the enclosure, or a piece of equipment where the neutral is landed on the grounding terminal. The mechanism is usually an installer who bonds the neutral at every panel out of habit (a carryover from service-entrance practice) or a panel shipped with the neutral-ground bond installed that is not removed when used as a sub-panel. The consequence is that neutral current divides between the intended neutral conductor and the parallel path through the equipment grounding conductor, the raceway, and the building steel. This causes voltage on equipment frames, overheated raceways and connectors, magnetic fields, and interference with ground-fault detection. After installation, verify at every downstream panel and load that the neutral is isolated from ground, and remove any bond that should not be there.

### Ground the SDS to a Local Electrode and Bond It to the Building Grounding Electrode System

The SDS must be connected to a local grounding electrode (NEC 250.30), and where the building has an existing grounding electrode system (building steel, a ground ring, a concrete-encased electrode), the SDS electrode must be bonded to it to create a common ground reference. The bonding between electrodes prevents voltage differences between the SDS reference and the building reference during faults or transients. Do not ground the SDS to an isolated electrode that is not bonded to the building system, because a fault or surge can raise the SDS reference to a dangerous voltage relative to the building steel and equipment. Identify the local electrode, bond it to the building electrode system with a conductor sized per 250.53 or 250.66 as applicable, and verify the bond is continuous.

### Coordinate Generator and UPS Grounding With the Transfer and Bypass Arrangement

Generators and UPS systems introduce grounding complexity because their status as an SDS depends on the transfer switch and bypass arrangement, and the grounding scheme must be consistent across all operating modes. A generator with a 4-pole transfer switch is an SDS in standby mode and requires its own neutral-to-ground bond and grounding electrode; the same generator with a 3-pole switch is not an SDS and must not have a separate bond. A UPS with a maintenance bypass that connects the load directly to the utility may change the SDS status depending on bypass position. Design the grounding for all operating modes, ensure the neutral-to-ground bond exists in exactly one place in each mode, and verify with transfer and bypass operations that no mode creates a second bond or removes the only bond. Coordinate with the transfer switch and UPS manufacturer's instructions, which often specify the required grounding arrangement.

## Common Traps

### Grounding a Non-SDS Source as If It Were Separately Derived

A generator with a 3-pole transfer switch (neutral solidly connected to the utility neutral) is grounded as an SDS with its own neutral-to-ground bond and electrode, creating a second bond on the same neutral system. The mechanism is that the generator is assumed to be an SDS by type, without checking the transfer switch wiring, so the SDS grounding scheme is applied to a source that is not separately derived. The false signal is the installed grounding hardware at the generator, which reads as correct. The harm is a second neutral-to-ground bond that diverts neutral current onto grounding conductors and raceways, causing the overheating, voltage, and interference problems the single-bond rule prevents. The defense is to determine SDS status from the transfer switch and neutral wiring, not from the source type, and to ground only true SDS sources as SDS.

### Omitting the Neutral-to-Ground Bond at the SDS

A transformer is installed as an SDS but the neutral-to-ground bond at the secondary is omitted, leaving the system ungrounded (floating), and the error is not detected because the system appears to function. The mechanism is that the bond is a specific connection that is easy to overlook in a complex installation, and the system operates without it until a ground fault occurs, so the omission is invisible in normal operation. The false signal is the operating system, which reads as correctly installed. The harm is that a line-to-ground fault has no low-impedance return path, the overcurrent device does not clear, and the faulted enclosure remains energized at line voltage, creating a shock hazard that persists until someone contacts it. The defense is to verify the neutral-to-ground bond at every SDS at commissioning, to test for the bond (continuity between neutral and ground at the source), and to treat an ungrounded SDS as a serious defect.

### Creating a Second Bond at a Downstream Panel

A sub-panel fed from an SDS is installed with the neutral bar bonded to the enclosure (a bond that is correct at a service panel but wrong at a sub-panel), and the second bond diverts neutral current onto the equipment grounding system. The mechanism is that service-entrance bonding practice is carried over to sub-panels, or a panel shipped with the bond installed is not reconfigured, so the neutral is grounded at two points. The false signal is the panel's neutral and ground bars both present and landed, which reads as normal. The harm is neutral current on grounding conductors and raceways, causing voltage on equipment frames, raceway heating, magnetic fields, and ground-fault sensor misoperation, with the cause hidden because the current is on conductors it should not be on. The defense is to isolate neutral from ground at every downstream panel, to remove factory-installed bonds when a panel is used as a sub-panel, and to verify isolation at commissioning.

### Sizing the GEC to the Primary Conductors Instead of the Derived Conductors

The grounding electrode conductor for a transformer SDS is sized to the primary (line-side) conductor, because that is the conductor the electrician sees first, rather than to the derived phase conductors per Table 250.66, and the GEC is undersized for the SDS. The mechanism is that the primary conductor is the obvious reference, and the derived conductor sizing step is overlooked, so the GEC is selected for the wrong conductors. The false signal is the installed GEC, which reads as adequate because it is present. The harm is that the GEC cannot carry the fault current the SDS can deliver, and during a fault the electrode connection may fail, leaving the SDS ungrounded at the moment it needs the reference most. The defense is to size the GEC from Table 250.66 using the derived phase conductor size, to document the reference, and to verify the sizing against the secondary, not the primary.

### Isolating the SDS Electrode From the Building Grounding System

A transformer SDS is grounded to a local driven rod that is not bonded to the building steel or the existing grounding electrode system, on the theory that the SDS should have an "independent" ground, and the isolation creates a voltage difference between the two references during a fault or surge. The mechanism is that an independent ground sounds clean and is easy to install, so it is chosen without considering that the SDS and the building share the same physical structure and that a fault or surge will raise one reference relative to the other. The false signal is the dedicated electrode, which reads as thorough grounding. The harm is that during a fault or lightning event, the SDS reference and the building reference diverge by hundreds or thousands of volts, and the voltage appears between SDS-served equipment and building-grounded equipment, creating shock and insulation hazards. The defense is to bond the SDS electrode to the building grounding electrode system, to treat isolation as a defect, and to verify the bond at commissioning.

### Inconsistent Grounding Across Generator or UPS Operating Modes

A generator SDS with a 4-pole transfer switch is grounded correctly in standby mode, but when the transfer switch operates and the neutral breaks, the grounding scheme that was correct in one mode creates a second bond or removes the only bond in another, and the error appears only during a transfer. The mechanism is that the grounding is designed for one operating mode without checking all modes, and the transfer or bypass operation changes the neutral topology in a way that the grounding does not accommodate. The false signal is the correct grounding in normal mode, which reads as compliant. The harm is that during transfer or bypass, the system is either ungrounded (no bond) or multiply grounded (two bonds), and the hazard appears exactly when reliability matters most. The defense is to analyze the grounding for every operating mode, to verify the neutral-to-ground bond is single in each mode, and to test the grounding under transfer and bypass operations at commissioning.

## Self-Check

- Did I determine whether the source is an SDS from the actual wiring (transfer switch type, transformer connection, UPS bypass), not from the source type alone?
- Did I establish the neutral-to-ground bond at the source and verify it is the only bond on the system?
- Did I size the grounding electrode conductor from Table 250.66 using the derived phase conductor size, not the primary conductor size?
- Did I size the main bonding jumper and equipment bonding conductors per 250.28 and 250.122 to carry the available fault current?
- Did I verify at every downstream panel and load that the neutral is isolated from ground, and remove any second bond?
- Did I ground the SDS to a local electrode and bond that electrode to the building grounding electrode system, avoiding an isolated electrode?
- Did I analyze the grounding across all operating modes (normal, standby, transfer, bypass) and verify the bond is single in each mode?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final grounding design and licensed sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
