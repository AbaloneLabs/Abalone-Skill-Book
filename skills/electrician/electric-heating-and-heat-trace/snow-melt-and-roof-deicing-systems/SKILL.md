# Snow Melt and Roof De-Icing Systems

Snow melt and roof de-icing systems are among the most electrically demanding residential and light-commercial loads, because melting snow requires not just warming a surface but supplying the latent heat of fusion for the water, and that energy is large. The judgment problem is that these systems are sized by watts per square foot of surface (often 30 to 50 W/sq ft for snow melt, and a specific layout for roof de-icing), they are activated by sensors that must actually detect the snow or ice condition, and their demand is large enough that a whole-building service upgrade is often required to support them. An installer who lays cable by guess, places the snow sensor under an eave where it never sees snow, or connects a 15 kW driveway system to an already-loaded 200 A service will build a system that either never melts the snow, runs up an enormous bill without clearing the surface, or trips the main on the first storm. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a snow melt or de-icing system actually clears the surface or merely consumes power.

## Core Rules

### Lay Out the Cable or Mat to the Required Watts per Square Foot for the Area Classification

Snow melt output is specified in watts per square foot, and the required density depends on the area classification and the design snowfall. ASHRAE and the cable manufacturers classify snow melt areas as Class I (0-30 W/sq ft, residential, idle, low priority, where a small amount of standing snow is acceptable), Class II (roughly 30-40 W/sq ft, commercial/institutional, where the surface should be clear during operation), and Class III (40-50+ W/sq ft, critical, hospital ramps, aircraft, where the surface must be continuously snow-free). The cable or mat spacing must produce the required watt density across the entire surface, with no cold spots at the edges or between cable runs. The trap is spacing the cable for convenience or to fit a fixed-length roll, which produces a watt density below the class requirement and a surface that never fully clears. The defense is to determine the area classification, calculate the required watt density, and lay out the cable spacing to deliver that density uniformly across the surface, ordering the correct total length for the area.

### Place Slab-Mounted Sensors Where They Detect the Actual Snow Condition

Snow melt systems are activated by moisture and temperature sensors, and the sensor placement determines whether the system turns on during the storm. Slab-mounted sensors (a moisture/temperature sensor installed flush in the slab surface) detect snowfall and slab temperature directly, and they must be located where snow actually falls on them — typically in the open area of the slab, away from overhangs, drains, or sheltered spots. Aerial snow sensors (mounted on a pole above the slab) detect falling snow and ambient temperature and are an alternative where a slab-mounted sensor is impractical. The trap is placing the sensor under an overhang or next to a heated wall where it never sees snow, so the system never activates during a real storm, or placing it in a low spot where meltwater keeps it wet and the system runs continuously. The defense is to locate the sensor in the open slab area where it experiences the same snow and temperature as the surface being melted, and to verify its activation during commissioning.

### Select the Control Panel and Activation Method for the Required Performance

Snow melt control panels range from simple manual switches (the operator turns the system on when it snows) to fully automatic systems with aerial or slab-mounted snow sensors, slab temperature feedback, and an idle-maintenance mode that keeps the slab just above freezing to reduce the start-up energy spike. The activation method is chosen by the use: a residential driveway may use a manual or automatic aerial sensor; a commercial entrance or hospital ramp typically requires automatic slab-sensing control with idle maintenance, because the surface must clear without an operator. The control panel must be rated for the load (the total amperage of all heated zones), must provide the required ground-fault protection (NEC 426.28 for fixed outdoor electric de-icing and snow-melting equipment), and must be compatible with the sensor type. The defense is to match the control and activation method to the required performance, to size the panel to the total load, and to provide the required ground-fault protection.

### Calculate the Demand Load and Confirm the Service Can Support It

Snow melt systems are large loads, and their demand must be calculated and checked against the available service capacity. A 500 sq ft driveway at 40 W/sq ft is 20,000 W (20 kW), which at 240 V is roughly 83 A — a substantial continuous load that, added to an existing service, may exceed the service capacity or require demand-factor coordination under NEC 220. The load calculation must treat the snow melt as a continuous load (the conductors and overcurrent device sized at 125%), and the service or feeder must support the snow melt plus the other building loads, with the diversity and demand factors the code allows. The trap is connecting a large snow melt system to an existing service without checking the total demand, which results in a main breaker that trips on the first storm or, worse, a service conductor that overheats. The defense is to calculate the snow melt load at 125%, add it to the building load calculation, and upgrade the service or feeder where the total exceeds the available capacity.

### Design Roof and Gutter De-Icing for Ice Dam Prevention With Correct Layout

Roof and gutter de-icing systems are designed to prevent ice dams by maintaining a melt path through the eaves and gutters so that roof snowmelt can drain rather than refreeze at the cold eave. The cable is laid out in a zigzag pattern along the roof edge (typically extending up the roof about 2-3 feet past the exterior wall line, with the loops following the roof edge and into the gutter and downspout), and the wattage per foot must be sufficient to maintain a melt path at the design temperature. The cable must be rated for roof and gutter use (self-regulating or constant-wattage, listed for wet outdoor locations), must follow the manufacturer's spacing and pattern, and must extend into and along the gutters and down the downspouts to keep the melt path continuous. The trap is laying the cable in a single straight line along the eave, which does not create the melt path, or skipping the gutters and downspouts, where the meltwater refreezes and rebuilds the dam. The defense is to follow the manufacturer's zigzag pattern, extend the cable into the gutters and downspouts, and size the cable output for the design roof temperature.

### Provide Ground-Fault Protection and Proper Environmental Rating for All Components

Snow melt and de-icing systems operate outdoors in wet, freezing conditions, and every component — the cable, the connections, the junction boxes, the control panel — must be rated for the environment. NEC 426.28 requires ground-fault protection for fixed outdoor electric de-icing and snow-melting equipment, typically a GFPE device set per the code and the manufacturer. The cable must be listed for embedded (in concrete or asphalt) or exposed (roof) use as appropriate, the connections must be made with listed kits in rated enclosures, and the control panel must be NEMA-rated for the location. The trap is using indoor-rated junction boxes or field-fabricated connections in a wet, freezing environment, which fail by moisture ingress and create a shock or fire hazard. The defense is to use listed cable for the application, listed connection kits in environmentally-rated enclosures, and ground-fault protection per the code.

### Sequence the Installation With the Slab or Roof Construction

Snow melt cable embedded in a slab or under pavers must be installed in coordination with the concrete pour or the paver installation, because the cable must be at the correct depth, secured against floatation during the pour, and protected from damage by the concrete crew or the paver placement. Roof de-icing cable is installed on the finished roof and gutters. The embedded installation requires the cable to be tested for continuity and insulation resistance before, during, and after the pour, because a break or damage during the pour is invisible and irreversible once the concrete is set. The trap is installing the cable and then losing it to a damaged section during an uncoordinated pour, with no way to repair it without demolishing the slab. The defense is to coordinate the installation with the trades, to secure the cable per the manufacturer, and to test continuity and insulation resistance at each stage of the construction.

## Common Traps

### Spacing Cable to Fit a Roll Rather Than to the Required Watt Density

The installer has a fixed-length roll of snow melt cable and spaces it to cover the driveway, rather than to deliver the required watts per square foot. The mechanism of the trap is that watt density, not total cable length, determines whether the surface clears, and spreading the cable to fit the area reduces the density below the class requirement. The false signal is that the cable "covers the whole driveway," which addresses coverage but not output. The harm is a system that runs continuously during a storm and never fully clears the snow, wasting energy and failing its purpose. The defense is to calculate the required watt density for the area classification, determine the total cable length needed to deliver it, and order the correct length even if it means more than one roll.

### Placing the Snow Sensor Where It Never Sees Snow

The slab-mounted snow sensor is installed under a roof overhang or next to a heated wall, where snow never accumulates on it. The mechanism of the trap is that the sensor activates the system based on the moisture and temperature it experiences, and a sensor that never sees snow never turns the system on, while a sensor in a low wet spot keeps the system running. The false signal is that the sensor is wired and calibrated, which is true but irrelevant if it is in the wrong location. The harm is a system that fails to activate during a real storm, leaving the surface covered, or that runs continuously and wastes energy. The defense is to locate the sensor in the open slab area where it experiences the same snow and temperature as the surface being melted.

### Connecting a Large Snow Melt System Without Checking the Service Capacity

A 20 kW driveway snow melt system is connected to a 200 A residential service that already carries the house load, and on the first storm the main breaker trips. The mechanism of the trap is that snow melt is a large continuous load, and adding it to an existing service without a load calculation can exceed the service capacity, especially when the house heating and other loads are also running. The false signal is that the panel has a spare breaker space, which addresses physical space but not capacity. The harm is a main breaker that trips during storms (defeating the system), or an overloaded service conductor that overheats. The defense is to calculate the snow melt load at 125%, add it to the building load, and upgrade the service or feeder where the total exceeds the available capacity.

### Laying Roof De-Icing Cable in a Straight Line Instead of a Zigzag

The roof de-icing cable is run in a single straight line along the eave, and the ice dam rebuilds because the meltwater has no path through the cold eave. The mechanism of the trap is that ice dam prevention requires a melt path that traces the roof edge and extends into the gutter and downspout, and a straight cable does not create that path. The false signal is that the cable is warm and running, which proves energization but not the correct melt path. The harm is an ice dam that reforms despite an operating cable, with water backing up under the shingles. The defense is to follow the manufacturer's zigzag pattern, extend the cable up the roof past the exterior wall line, and continue it into the gutters and downspouts.

### Skipping Ground-Fault Protection or Using Indoor-Rated Components Outdoors

The snow melt or de-icing circuit is protected only by a standard breaker, and the junction boxes and connections are indoor-rated, in a wet freezing environment. The mechanism of the trap is that outdoor de-icing cable is subject to moisture ingress and physical damage, and without ground-fault protection a fault energizes the slab or the roof with no clearing path; indoor-rated enclosures fail by water entry. The false signal is that the system works when first installed, which is true in dry conditions but fails with moisture and freeze-thaw cycling. The harm is an energized slab or roof that shocks someone, or a fire at a failed connection. The defense is to provide ground-fault protection per NEC 426.28, to use listed cable and connection kits in environmentally-rated enclosures, and to use a NEMA-rated control panel.

### Damaging Embedded Cable During an Uncoordinated Concrete Pour

The snow melt cable is laid in the slab area, the concrete crew pours without coordination, and a section of cable is damaged by a screed, a boot, or a vibrator, with the damage invisible once the concrete is set. The mechanism of the trap is that embedded cable is irreparable after the pour, and damage during the pour disables a zone with no practical remedy short of demolishing the slab. The false signal is that the cable was intact when laid, which is true before the pour but not necessarily after. The harm is a dead zone in the snow melt system that cannot be repaired. The defense is to coordinate the pour with the electrician, to secure the cable against floatation and displacement, and to test continuity and insulation resistance before, during (where possible), and after the pour.

## Self-Check

- Did I determine the area classification (Class I, II, or III) for the snow melt surface, calculate the required watts per square foot, and lay out the cable spacing to deliver that density uniformly across the entire surface?
- Did I place the slab-mounted or aerial snow sensor where it actually experiences the snow and temperature condition of the surface being melted, away from overhangs and sheltered spots?
- Did I select a control panel and activation method (manual, automatic aerial sensor, or slab-sensing with idle maintenance) matched to the required performance, sized to the total load, and providing the required ground-fault protection?
- Did I calculate the snow melt load at 125% as a continuous load, add it to the building load calculation, and confirm the service or feeder can support the total — upgrading where necessary?
- For roof and gutter de-icing, did I follow the manufacturer's zigzag pattern, extend the cable up the roof past the exterior wall line, continue it into the gutters and downspouts, and size the cable output for the design roof temperature?
- Did I provide ground-fault protection per NEC 426.28, use listed cable and connection kits in environmentally-rated enclosures, and use a NEMA-rated control panel appropriate for the location?
- For embedded cable, did I coordinate the installation with the concrete or paver crew, secure the cable against floatation and displacement, and test continuity and insulation resistance before, during, and after the pour?
