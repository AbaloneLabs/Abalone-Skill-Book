# Baseboard and Unit Heater Wiring

Electric resistance heating converts the entire circuit into a continuous, high-wattage load that runs for hours at a time at near 100% of its rating, which makes the wiring decisions around it different from general-purpose branch circuits. The judgment problem is that a heating circuit is governed by continuous-load rules (the conductors and the overcurrent device must be sized at 125% of the load), it must be dedicated (no lighting, no receptacles, no other loads sharing the circuit), and the thermostat and heater selection determine whether the system can be fully switched off, whether the load is controlled safely, and whether the heater is mounted where it can actually heat the space without creating a fire hazard. An electrician who treats a baseboard heater like any other receptacle load will undersize the conductors, mix loads onto the circuit, install a single-pole thermostat that leaves the heater partially energized, or mount the heater against drapes or furniture. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether electric heating is safe, code-compliant, and actually effective.

## Core Rules

### Calculate the Heating Load by Heat Loss and Confirm Branch-Circuit Sizing Under Continuous-Load Rules

Fixed electric space heating is calculated two ways: the heat loss of the space (which determines how many watts of heat are needed to maintain the target temperature) and the branch-circuit load (which determines conductor and breaker sizing). The heat loss is typically estimated by watts per square foot (roughly 10 W/sq ft for well-insulated construction, more for poor insulation or cold climates) or by a proper Manual J calculation that accounts for insulation, glazing, infiltration, and design temperature difference. BTU-to-watt conversion is 1 watt = 3.413 BTU/hr, so a 5000 BTU/hr heater is roughly 1465 watts. Once the connected load is known, NEC Article 424 and 220 require that fixed electric space heating be treated as a continuous load: the branch-circuit conductors and the overcurrent protection must be sized at 125% of the load. A 1500 W heater at 240 V draws 6.25 A; at 125% that is 7.8 A, which fits a 15 A circuit — but two such heaters on one circuit (12.5 A, 125% = 15.6 A) requires a 20 A circuit. The defense is to calculate the heat loss, sum the connected heater wattage, and size the circuit at 125% under continuous-load rules.

### Dedicate the Circuit to Heating: No Other Loads on a Heating Branch

A fixed electric space heating branch circuit must supply only heating loads — no lighting, no receptacles, no general-purpose loads. NEC 424.3(B) prohibits any other loads on a branch circuit supplying fixed electric space-heating equipment. The reason is that heating circuits are loaded to their continuous rating for hours, and adding a receptacle or a light invites a user to plug in a high-draw appliance that overloads the circuit, or it creates confusion about what is on the circuit during troubleshooting. The trap is landing a receptacle or a light on a spare heating circuit "because there is room in the panel," which violates the dedicated-circuit rule and creates an overload path. The defense is to keep every heating branch strictly to heaters, and to run a separate general-purpose circuit for any receptacle or lighting load in the same space.

### Select the Thermostat Type and Pole Count for the Required Control and Full-Off

Thermostat selection has two axes: line-voltage (the thermostat switches the full heater current directly) versus low-voltage (a 24 V thermostat controls a relay contactor that switches the heater), and single-pole versus double-pole. Line-voltage thermostats are common for baseboard heaters and are rated for the heater current (typically 22 A at 240 V); they must be rated for the load they switch. Low-voltage thermostats with a contactor are used for larger loads or where the thermostat is located away from the heater, and they allow smaller, safer control wiring. The pole count matters for the off state: a single-pole thermostat switches only one leg of the 240 V circuit, so the heater element remains energized to ground even when "off" — a shock hazard during servicing and a cause of parasitic warmth. A double-pole thermostat switches both legs, providing a true full-off and a positive disconnect for servicing. The defense is to use a double-pole thermostat for full-off capability, to verify the thermostat's current rating against the heater load, and to use low-voltage control with a contactor for larger or multi-heater installations.

### Wire Baseboard Heaters With Correct Circuiting, Splicing, and Thermostat Integration

Baseboard heater wiring must follow the manufacturer's instructions exactly, because the heater is designed for a specific conductor entry, a specific thermostat (built-in or wall-mounted), and a specific series/parallel arrangement if multiple sections are fed from one circuit. Conductors must enter the heater through the listed knockouts with the proper connectors, and the connections must be made in the heater's listed wiring compartment — never spliced in the wall and stuffed behind the unit. The thermostat may be built into the heater end cap or wall-mounted; a wall-mounted thermostat requires a separate 2-wire or 4-wire run depending on whether it is a single-pole or double-pole device and whether it interrupts one or both legs. The conductors must be rated for the temperature at the heater (typically 90 C THHN in the listed compartment), and the circuit must be bonded through the equipment grounding conductor to the heater frame. The defense is to follow the manufacturer's wiring diagram, make connections only in listed compartments, use conductors rated for the compartment temperature, and verify the equipment grounding path to each heater frame.

### Respect Clearances and Mounting Restrictions for Fire Safety

Baseboard and unit heaters are installed to heat a space, and their location determines both effectiveness and fire safety. Baseboard heaters must not be installed below receptacles (cord-connected appliances can hang over the heater), must maintain clearance to drapes and furniture per the manufacturer's listing (typically at least 12 inches of clearance above and in front), and must not be installed where towels or combustibles will be draped over them. Wall and unit heaters must maintain clearance to combustibles, must be mounted per the listing (some require a specific wall construction), and must not be blocked by furniture. The trap is mounting a baseboard under a window with a drape that will hang over it, or in a bathroom where towels will be placed on it — both of which create a fire hazard and reduce the heater's effectiveness. The defense is to read the manufacturer's clearance requirements, to locate heaters where airflow and combustible clearance are maintained, and to coordinate the heater location with the building's use.

### Size the Overcurrent Protection and Conductors as a Continuous Load

Because fixed electric space heating is a continuous load, the branch-circuit overcurrent protection and the conductors must be sized at 125% of the load. A 4000 W heater at 240 V draws 16.7 A; at 125% that is 20.8 A, which requires a 25 A or 30 A breaker and #10 conductors (a 20 A breaker at 125% of 16.7 A would be 20.8 A, exceeding the 20 A rating). The next standard size up is permitted for the overcurrent device. The conductors must have an ampacity at least equal to the load, and after derating for temperature and conduit fill, must still carry the continuous current. The trap is sizing the breaker to the connected load without the 125% continuous factor, which results in a breaker that trips under sustained heating operation or, worse, conductors that overheat before the breaker trips. The defense is to apply the 125% continuous-load factor to both the conductors and the overcurrent device, and to verify the conductor ampacity after all derating.

### Install Wall and Unit Heaters With Proper Mounting, Clearance, and Control

Wall heaters (in-wall fans with a heating element) and unit heaters (ceiling or surface-mounted fans with elements) require correct mounting, clearance, and control wiring. Wall heaters must be installed in the listed enclosure, with the listed clearance to combustible framing, and with the airflow path unobstructed. Unit heaters must be mounted to support their weight, with clearance for airflow and service, and with the disconnect means accessible. Both types often include a built-in thermostat or a fan delay, and the control wiring must follow the manufacturer's diagram. The trap is mounting a wall heater in a stud bay without the listed fire-rated enclosure, or mounting a unit heater where the airflow is blocked, both of which create a fire hazard and reduce effectiveness. The defense is to use the listed enclosure and mounting hardware, maintain the listed clearances, and follow the manufacturer's control wiring.

## Common Traps

### Sizing the Breaker to the Connected Load Without the 125% Continuous Factor

A 3840 W baseboard heater at 240 V draws 16 A, so the electrician installs it on a 20 A breaker with #12 wire and considers the job done. The mechanism of the trap is that fixed electric space heating is a continuous load, and the breaker and conductors must be sized at 125% of 16 A, which is 20 A — right at the breaker rating, with no margin, and the breaker will trip under sustained cold-weather operation. The false signal is that "16 amps fits a 20 amp breaker," which ignores the continuous-load rule. The harm is nuisance tripping on cold nights, repeated breaker operation, and eventual breaker failure or conductor overheating. The defense is to apply the 125% continuous-load factor to both the conductors and the overcurrent device and to round up to the next standard breaker size.

### Landing a Receptacle or Light on a Spare Heating Circuit

The panel has a spare heating circuit with capacity, and the electrician lands a convenience receptacle on it "because there is room." The mechanism of the trap is that NEC 424.3(B) prohibits any load other than fixed electric space heating on a heating branch circuit, and the receptacle invites an unknown future load that, combined with the heater's continuous draw, overloads the circuit. The false signal is that the breaker holds for now, which proves nothing about future use. The harm is an overload path that violates the code and creates a fire risk when a high-draw appliance is plugged in during heating season. The defense is to keep every heating branch strictly dedicated and to run a separate general-purpose circuit for receptacles and lighting.

### Using a Single-Pole Thermostat and Leaving the Heater Energized to Ground When Off

A 240 V baseboard is controlled by a single-pole wall thermostat that switches only one leg, so when the thermostat is "off," the heater element is still energized from the other leg to ground. The mechanism of the trap is that a single-pole thermostat on a 240 V circuit does not provide a full disconnect, and the element remains live — a shock hazard for anyone servicing the heater who relies on the thermostat being off. The false signal is that the heater is not producing heat, which is true but does not mean it is de-energized. The harm is a shock injury during cleaning or servicing, and a small parasitic warmth that wastes energy. The defense is to use a double-pole thermostat for a true full-off and to treat the breaker, not the thermostat, as the servicing disconnect.

### Mounting a Baseboard Heater Where Drapes or Furniture Will Block It

A baseboard is installed under a window with floor-length drapes, or in a bathroom where towels will be hung over it. The mechanism of the trap is that the heater's listing depends on unobstructed airflow and clearance to combustibles, and drapes or towels both block the heat distribution and create a combustible in contact with a hot surface. The false signal is that the heater fits the wall space, which addresses aesthetics but not safety or effectiveness. The harm is a fire hazard from combustibles in contact with the heater, and a space that never reaches temperature because the heat is trapped. The defense is to read the manufacturer's clearance requirements, to coordinate the heater location with window treatments and room use, and to relocate or protect the heater where combustibles will be present.

### Splicing Heater Conductors in the Wall Instead of in the Listed Compartment

The circuit is run to a baseboard heater, but the conductors are spliced in the wall cavity behind the unit and only the tails are brought into the heater compartment. The mechanism of the trap is that splices outside a listed enclosure are not accessible and not protected, and a connection that loosens over years of thermal cycling cannot be inspected or tightened. The false signal is that the heater works, which is true until the splice fails. The harm is a hidden high-resistance connection that overheats inside the wall — a leading cause of heater-related fires. The defense is to make all connections inside the heater's listed wiring compartment, to use the listed knockouts and connectors, and to never bury a splice in the wall.

### Undersizing Conductors After Temperature Derating in a Hot Location

Heater conductors are run in a hot attic or in conduit along a warm wall, and the temperature derating reduces the ampacity below the continuous load, but the conductor size is not increased. The mechanism of the trap is that conductor ampacity must be derated for ambient temperature, and a #12 conductor rated for 30 A at 90 C in a 60 C attic may be derated below the 20 A continuous-load requirement. The false signal is that "#12 is good for 20 amps," which is true at standard conditions but not after derating. The harm is conductor overheating inside the wall or attic. The defense is to apply the temperature correction factor to the conductor ampacity and to upsize the conductor where the derated ampacity is below the continuous-load current.

## Self-Check

- Did I calculate the heat loss for the space (watts per square foot or Manual J) and size the branch-circuit conductors and overcurrent protection at 125% of the connected heater load under continuous-load rules?
- Did I keep the heating branch circuit strictly dedicated to fixed electric space heating, with no receptacles, lighting, or other loads on the same circuit?
- Did I select a thermostat rated for the heater current, use a double-pole thermostat for a true full-off on 240 V circuits, and use low-voltage control with a contactor for larger or multi-heater installations?
- Did I follow the manufacturer's wiring diagram, make all connections inside the heater's listed wiring compartment, use conductors rated for the compartment temperature, and verify the equipment grounding path to each heater frame?
- Did I read and respect the manufacturer's clearance requirements, locate baseboard heaters where drapes, furniture, and towels will not obstruct airflow or contact the heater, and coordinate the location with the room's use?
- Did I apply temperature derating and conduit-fill derating to the conductor ampacity and upsize the conductors where the derated ampacity is below the continuous-load current?
- Did I use the listed enclosure and mounting hardware for wall and unit heaters, maintain the listed clearances to combustibles, and keep the airflow path unobstructed?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
