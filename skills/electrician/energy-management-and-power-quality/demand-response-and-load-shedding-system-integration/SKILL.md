# Demand Response and Load Shedding System Integration

Demand response and automated load shedding look like a control problem: watch the demand, and when it crosses a threshold, drop some loads. The judgment problem is that demand response is a contract before it is a control system, and the control system is a safety-critical interlock before it is a convenience. The decisions that determine whether a demand-response installation saves money or causes outages, penalties, and process disruption are almost never visible on the control wiring diagram: they live in the interruptibility terms of the utility tariff, in the priority and recovery sequencing of the shed loads, in the distinction between a curtailable load and one that cannot be interrupted without consequence, and in the fail-safe behavior when communications or the controller fails. An electrician who wires the relays without understanding the contract terms, without classifying loads by interruptibility, and without designing for failure will build a system that either never sheds (and never earns the credit) or sheds the wrong load at the wrong time and triggers a process upset, a comfort complaint, or a life-safety exposure. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether demand response delivers its promised value or becomes a liability.

## Core Rules

### Understand the Utility Program Structure Before Designing Any Shed Logic

Demand-response and interruptible-load programs are not interchangeable, and the control strategy must match the program the facility is enrolled in. A peak-demand-reduction (demand limiting) program bills on the highest 15- or 30-minute demand interval, so the objective is to keep the rolling demand below a target by shaving load proactively. A curtailable-load or emergency demand-response program pays the customer to reduce load on utility dispatch, with a required reduction amount, a notification lead time (minutes to hours), a response time, and a minimum duration (often 2 to 6 hours), and penalties for non-performance. An interruptible-load program offers a lower rate in exchange for the utility's right to interrupt supply, sometimes with little notice. The trap is wiring a generic "drop load when demand is high" controller without reading the program's performance requirements, then failing to meet the required reduction or duration and incurring penalties that wipe out a year of credits. The defense is to obtain the program tariff or contract, extract the required kW reduction, the notification and response times, the minimum duration, the number of events per year, and the penalty for non-performance, and to design the shed logic and the sheddable-load inventory to meet those specific terms.

### Classify Every Candidate Load by Interruptibility, Not Just by Size

The load to shed is selected by a combination of size, priority, and the cost of interruption — not by size alone. Loads fall into categories: hard-interruptible loads that can be dropped instantly with no consequence (decorative lighting, empty-zone HVAC, electric heaters in unoccupied spaces); soft-interruptible loads that can be dropped for a bounded time before consequence (comfort HVAC, process pre-heating, non-critical pumping); and non-interruptible loads that cannot be shed without safety, regulatory, or process consequence (life safety, refrigeration of perishables, critical process, IT load in a non-redundant data center, medical equipment). The trap is shedding by ampere rating alone — dropping a large chiller because it is the biggest load, only to discover that shutting it down causes the process it serves to trip, or that restarting it later causes a simultaneous inrush that creates a new demand peak. The defense is to build a load inventory that records, for each candidate load, the kVA, the interruption consequence, the maximum acceptable off-time, the restart inrush, and the recovery time, and to sequence shedding by priority within that inventory rather than by size.

### Set the Demand Target and the Shed Threshold With Margin Below the Billing Interval

Demand is billed on a rolling or fixed time interval (commonly 15 or 30 minutes), and the demand-response controller must predict the interval demand and begin shedding before the interval closes, because once the interval has elapsed the demand is locked in. The trap is setting the shed threshold equal to the contracted demand target, so the controller reacts only after the demand has already crossed the limit, and by the time loads are shed the interval has recorded the peak. The defense is to set the shed threshold below the target with margin proportional to the load's response time and the interval length — typically starting to shed at 85 to 90 percent of the rolling-interval predicted demand — and to use a predictive controller that forecasts the interval-ending demand from the current trend rather than a simple instantaneous threshold.

### Design the Shed and Recovery Sequencing to Avoid New Peaks and Inrush

Shedding is half the problem; recovery is the other half. When a demand event ends and shed loads are restored, restarting multiple large motors or chillers simultaneously produces an inrush that can exceed the peak the system was trying to avoid, creating a new demand peak and negating the event's benefit. The trap is wiring all shed relays to restore together when the event clears, producing a coordinated inrush that spikes the demand and trips overcurrent devices. The defense is to restore loads in a staggered sequence with time delays between steps, ordered so that the largest inrush loads restart last and individually, and to verify that the recovery sequence does not produce a combined inrush that exceeds the demand target the system is meant to hold.

### Make the Control System Fail-Safe and Verify the Communications Path

Demand-response systems depend on a communications path — a utility dispatch signal, a network link to a building management system, or a smart meter data feed. The trap is assuming the communications path is reliable and designing the system to shed only when commanded, so that when the link fails during a peak the system does nothing and the facility incurs a demand penalty, or conversely assuming the link is reliable and designing the system to shed on a "no message" fail-safe that drops load on every transient communication loss. The defense is to define the fail-safe behavior explicitly: for a curtailable program, fail-safe may be "do not shed on loss of signal" to avoid nuisance process interruption; for a peak-demand-limiting program using local meter data, fail-safe may be "continue shedding on local demand threshold" so the facility is protected even if the central controller is unreachable. The choice depends on the program and the cost of each failure mode, and it must be documented and tested.

### Coordinate the Shed Relays With the Existing Control and Safety Interlocks

A load-shed command is an override imposed on equipment that already has its own control logic and safety interlocks. The trap is wiring a shed relay as a hard disconnect in series with the equipment's start circuit, bypassing the equipment's controlled shutdown sequence, so that shedding a chiller mid-cycle causes a refrigerant fault, or shedding a process heater causes a thermal shock, or shedding a motor causes a coast-down that trips a downstream interlock. The defense is to integrate the shed command through the equipment's normal control interface — a BMS point, a programmable input, or a controlled stop command — so the equipment shuts down in its designed sequence, and to verify that the shed interface does not defeat any safety interlock or override a manual emergency stop.

### Verify the Enrolled Load Baseline and the Metering Point Against the Program

Demand-response credits and penalties are calculated against a baseline (the facility's normal consumption) and measured at a defined metering point. The trap is enrolling based on an estimated baseline that does not reflect actual operations, so the required reduction is unreachable, or installing the shed controller to read from a submeter that does not match the utility billing meter, so the controller's view of demand diverges from the billed demand and the facility misses its target. The defense is to verify the baseline methodology in the program (typically an average of recent non-event days), to confirm the metering point the controller reads is the same meter the utility uses for settlement, and to reconcile the controller's demand reading against the utility meter during commissioning.

## Common Traps

### Shedding a Non-Interruptible Load and Triggering a Process or Safety Upset

The electrician configures the demand controller to drop the largest loads first, and the largest load is a process refrigeration compressor. The mechanism of the failure is that the compressor supports a cold storage that must hold temperature, and a 4-hour demand event drops the compressor, the cold room warms, the product spoils, and the spoilage cost dwarfs the demand-response credit. The false signal is that the load "is large and cyclic," which made it look sheddable on a size basis, masking the consequence of interruption. The harm is product loss, regulatory exposure for food or pharmaceutical cold chain, and a demand-response program that now costs more than it earns. The defense is to classify every load by interruptibility and consequence before assigning it to a shed priority, and to exclude non-interruptible loads from the shed inventory regardless of size.

### Setting the Threshold at the Target and Missing the Interval

The demand target is 500 kW and the controller is set to begin shedding at 500 kW instantaneous demand. The mechanism of the failure is that demand is billed on a 15-minute interval, the load ramps over several minutes, the controller detects 500 kW only after the ramp is advanced, and by the time it identifies and executes the shed the interval has already recorded a peak above target. The false signal is that the threshold "equals the target," which feels correct but ignores the interval and the shed execution latency. The harm is repeated demand peaks above target despite a functioning shed system, and the facility pays demand penalties it believed the system would prevent. The defense is to set the shed threshold below the target with margin, to use a predictive controller that forecasts interval-ending demand, and to begin shedding at 85 to 90 percent of the target.

### Restoring All Loads Simultaneously and Creating a New Demand Peak

A 2-hour demand event ends and the controller re-energizes all shed loads at once. The mechanism of the failure is that the simultaneous restart of chillers, large motors, and electric heaters draws a combined inrush and pickup load that exceeds the demand target, the billing interval records this as a new peak, and the demand charge the event was meant to avoid is incurred anyway at the recovery point. The false signal is that "the event is over, restore everything," which is correct for service restoration but wrong for demand management. The harm is a demand peak at recovery that negates the event's savings. The defense is to restore loads in a staggered sequence with time delays, restarting the largest inrush loads individually and last, and to verify the recovery profile does not exceed the target.

### Wiring the Shed Relay as a Hard Disconnect That Bypasses the Equipment's Shutdown Logic

The electrician wires the shed command as a contact in series with the chiller's compressor contactor coil, so a shed command drops the compressor instantly. The mechanism of the failure is that the chiller's controller expects to execute a controlled pump-down and ramp-down; an instant contactor drop leaves refrigerant in an uncontrolled state, logs a fault, and on restart the chiller refuses to run until a manual reset clears the fault — which may not happen during the event, so the load stays off longer than intended. The false signal is that the relay "drops the load," which is true electrically but ignores the equipment's control sequence. The harm is equipment faults, extended downtime, and a shed that lasts longer than commanded. The defense is to integrate the shed command through the equipment's control interface (a BMS point or programmed input) so the equipment shuts down in its designed sequence.

### Assuming the Communications Link Is Reliable and Losing the Dispatch Signal

The facility is enrolled in a utility dispatch program that sends a shed command over a network link, and the controller is designed to shed only on receipt of the command. The mechanism of the failure is that the network link fails during a dispatched event, the command never arrives, the controller does nothing, the facility does not reduce load, and the utility assesses a non-performance penalty based on the missed reduction. The false signal is that the controller "is installed and tested," which was true in commissioning but does not cover a real-time communications failure during an event. The harm is a penalty that can exceed the annual program credit. The defense is to define the fail-safe behavior for communications loss, to provide a redundant notification path (the utility typically also calls or texts), and to test the failure mode during commissioning by dropping the link and confirming the defined response.

### Enrolling Without Verifying the Baseline and Then Failing to Meet the Required Reduction

The facility enrolls in a curtailable program based on an estimated baseline, and the required reduction is 200 kW. The mechanism of the failure is that the baseline was estimated high, the facility's actual sheddable load is only 120 kW, and during every event the facility cannot meet the 200 kW reduction and incurs a per-event penalty. The false signal is that the facility "has plenty of load to shed," based on connected load rather than on the sheddable inventory against the program baseline. The harm is chronic non-performance penalties. The defense is to verify the baseline methodology, to inventory the genuinely sheddable load against the required reduction before enrolling, and to decline or downsize enrollment if the sheddable load cannot reliably meet the commitment.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I obtain the utility program tariff or contract and extract the required kW reduction, notification and response times, minimum event duration, annual event count, and non-performance penalty, and did I design the shed logic to meet those specific terms?
- Did I classify every candidate load by interruptibility and consequence (hard-interruptible, soft-interruptible, non-interruptible), recording the kVA, maximum off-time, restart inrush, and recovery time, and did I exclude non-interruptible loads from the shed inventory regardless of size?
- Did I set the shed threshold below the demand target with margin, and did I use a predictive controller that forecasts interval-ending demand rather than reacting to an instantaneous threshold?
- Did I design the recovery sequence to restore loads in a staggered order with time delays, so that the combined restart inrush does not create a new demand peak?
- Did I define and document the fail-safe behavior for communications or controller failure, provide a redundant dispatch notification path, and test the failure mode during commissioning?
- Did I integrate the shed command through each equipment's normal control interface so the equipment shuts down in its designed sequence, rather than wiring a hard disconnect that bypasses shutdown logic or safety interlocks?
- Did I verify the program baseline methodology and confirm the controller reads the same metering point the utility uses for settlement, and did I reconcile the controller's demand reading against the utility meter during commissioning?
- Are the program terms, shed priorities, fail-safe behavior, and recovery sequence documented clearly enough that another practitioner could operate and maintain the system?
