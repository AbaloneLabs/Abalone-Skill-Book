# Power Factor Correction and Capacitor Installation

Power factor correction looks like a simple arithmetic problem: measure the displacement power factor, size a capacitor to bring it to 0.95 or higher, and install the bank. The judgment problem is that a capacitor is not a passive component bolted onto a bus — it is a dynamic element that interacts with the source inductance, the harmonic spectrum, the motor population, and the utility metering tariff, and an installation that ignores those interactions will create the very problems it was meant to solve. A capacitor bank installed without a harmonic study can resonate with the source and explode; a bank switched with the wrong contactor can generate transients that trip drives across the plant; a correction sized to the nameplate motor load can overcorrect when motors cycle off and raise the voltage; and in a building dominated by nonlinear loads, displacement correction may address the wrong power factor entirely while the utility bills for true power factor. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether PF correction reduces costs and stabilizes voltage or becomes a source of failures and nuisance trips.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Displacement Power Factor From True Power Factor Before Sizing Anything

Displacement power factor is the cosine of the angle between the fundamental voltage and current, and it is what capacitors correct. True (or total) power factor is the ratio of real power to apparent power including harmonics, and it is what most modern utility meters record for billing. In a facility with significant nonlinear load — VFDs, UPS rectifiers, switch-mode power supplies, LED drivers — the true power factor is degraded by harmonic distortion that capacitors cannot correct and may worsen. The trap is measuring displacement PF with a simple meter, computing a capacitor size, installing it, and finding the utility bill barely changed because the meter was recording true PF degraded by harmonics. The defense is to measure both displacement and true power factor with an instrument that reports total harmonic distortion of current (THDI), and if the two diverge significantly, to recognize that harmonic mitigation (filters, active front-end drives, line reactors) is the relevant intervention, not capacitors alone.

### Calculate Capacitor Size From Measured Data, Not From Motor Nameplates

The reactive power to be corrected is the difference between the present reactive load and the target, computed as Qc = P × (tan(φ_present) − tan(φ_target)), where P is the real power measured at the billing interval. The trap is sizing the bank from motor nameplate data, summing the reactive power of every motor, and installing a bank far larger than the running load requires. The result is overcorrection when the motors cycle off: the leading power factor raises the system voltage, the capacitors push current back toward the transformer, and utility penalties for leading PF may apply. The defense is to measure the real power and displacement power factor at the service during representative load conditions (not just peak), compute the required kvar from the measured data, and target a corrected PF that leaves margin — typically 0.92 to 0.97 — rather than chasing 0.99 or unity, which maximizes resonance and overcorrection risk.

### Perform a Harmonic Resonance Study Before Installing Any Capacitor Bank

This is the single most consequential decision in PF correction, and it is the one most often skipped. A power factor capacitor in parallel with the source inductance forms a parallel resonant circuit. The resonant harmonic order is approximately h_r = sqrt(kVA_short_circuit / kvar_capacitor), where the short-circuit kVA is the stiffness of the supply at the capacitor location. If h_r coincides with a characteristic harmonic produced by the nonlinear loads on the system — commonly the 5th (300 Hz), 7th (420 Hz), or 11th — the harmonic current circulates between the source and the capacitor with high gain, the capacitor sees amplified current and voltage, and it overheats, fails, or explodes. The trap is installing a standard capacitor bank on a bus that feeds VFDs or other nonlinear loads, where the resonant frequency lands near the 5th or 7th harmonic, and experiencing capacitor fuse blowing, swelling, or rupture within weeks. The defense is to calculate the parallel resonant frequency for the proposed bank size and the system short-circuit capacity, identify the harmonic spectrum of the loads, and if the resonance is near a significant harmonic, to detune the bank with a series reactor (typically tuned below the 5th harmonic, near the 3.8th to 4.2nd order) so the bank presents inductive impedance at the problematic harmonic frequencies.

### Select Fixed Versus Automatic (Switched) Banks Based on Load Variability

A fixed capacitor bank is permanently connected and suits a load whose reactive demand is roughly constant, such as a group of continuously running motors. An automatic bank uses contactors to switch capacitor steps in and out under the control of a PF controller that tracks the measured reactive power. The trap is installing a large fixed bank where the load varies widely — a plant where motors start and stop, or where shifts change the load — and overcorrecting during light load, raising voltage and producing leading PF. The converse trap is installing an automatic bank where the load is steady and paying for switching complexity and contactor maintenance that adds failure modes without benefit. The defense is to profile the reactive load over time: if it varies by more than roughly 30 percent, use an automatic bank with enough steps to track the variation; if it is steady, a fixed bank is simpler and more reliable.

### Use Capacitor-Rated Contactors and Transient Inrush Limiting on Switched Banks

When a capacitor step is switched onto a live bus, the inrush current is limited only by the system inductance and the capacitor's internal inductance, and it can reach hundreds of times the rated current for a few milliseconds. This inrush stresses the contactor, welds contacts, and generates voltage transients that can trip sensitive loads. The trap is using a standard motor contactor to switch capacitor steps, finding the contacts weld or pit rapidly, and replacing contactors on a recurring schedule. The defense is to use contactors specifically rated for capacitor switching (with early-make auxiliary contacts that insert damping resistors during the closing transient), to limit the number of switching operations per hour per the manufacturer, and on larger banks to consider zero-crossing solid-state switches that close at the voltage peak to minimize inrush.

### Apply Detuned Reactors Where Any Nonlinear Load Is Present

A detuned reactor is a series inductor installed with each capacitor step that lowers the combined LC resonant frequency to well below the lowest significant harmonic — typically tuned to the 3.8th to 4.2nd order, below the 5th. This makes the capacitor-reactor branch appear inductive (not capacitive) at the 5th, 7th, and higher harmonics, so it cannot resonate with them and does not amplify their currents. The detuned bank still provides displacement power factor correction at the fundamental. The trap is treating detuning as an optional accessory and installing a non-detuned bank because "the harmonic content isn't that high," then discovering after a VFD is added or a load changes that the resonance shifted into a problem frequency. The defense is to specify detuned reactors on any capacitor bank installed on a bus that feeds, or may ever feed, nonlinear loads — the cost is modest and the protection is permanent, while retrofitting reactors after a failure is expensive and the failure itself can be dangerous.

### Place the Capacitor Bank at the Point That Matches the Correction Objective

Capacitor placement determines what is corrected and what is relieved. A bank at the service entrance corrects the power factor seen by the utility meter and relieves the transformer and service conductors, but it does not relieve the feeders and branch circuits downstream. A bank at the motor corrects at the motor terminals and relieves the feeder and transformer, but it switches with the motor and must be sized to avoid self-excitation when the motor disconnects. A distribution-level bank corrects a group of loads. The trap is placing a large bank at the service entrance to fix the utility bill while leaving overloaded feeders and transformers unrelieved, or placing a capacitor at each motor without checking that the motor's leading PF at part load does not overvoltage the motor terminals. The defense is to define the objective — utility billing relief, conductor and transformer capacity relief, or voltage support — and place the bank accordingly, verifying that motor-connected capacitors are sized below the motor's no-load reactive power to prevent self-excitation overvoltage.

## Common Traps

### Installing a Capacitor Bank That Resonates With the 5th or 7th Harmonic

The facility has a poor power factor from a mix of induction motors and six-pulse VFDs, and the electrician installs a standard 400 kvar capacitor bank at the main switchboard. The mechanism of the failure is that the bank and the transformer source inductance form a parallel resonant circuit whose resonant frequency, for this bank size and this short-circuit capacity, lands near the 5th harmonic (300 Hz). The six-pulse VFDs generate characteristic 5th harmonic current, and at the resonant frequency the parallel circuit presents high impedance, so the harmonic voltage amplifies, the capacitor draws amplified harmonic current, and within days the capacitor fuses blow, the cans swell from internal heating, or the bank ruptures. The false signal is that the bank "corrected the power factor" initially, suggesting it was working, and the failure is attributed to a capacitor defect rather than to resonance. The harm is equipment destruction, fire risk, and a plant that now has worse power quality than before the correction. The defense is to calculate the resonant harmonic order before selecting the bank size, and to detune the bank with series reactors tuned below the 5th harmonic whenever nonlinear loads are present or anticipated.

### Sizing the Bank From Motor Nameplates and Overcorrecting at Light Load

The electrician tabulates every motor's nameplate reactive power, sums it to 600 kvar, and installs a 600 kvar fixed bank. The mechanism of the failure is that nameplate reactive power assumes full load, but motors cycle off and the real running reactive demand is half the nameplate sum; the oversized fixed bank overcorrects, the displacement power factor goes leading, the system voltage rises at light load, and the utility applies a penalty for leading PF or the elevated voltage stresses insulation and shortens equipment life. The false signal is that the bank was sized to the connected motor load, which feels thorough but ignores duty cycle. The harm is voltage rise, leading-PF penalties, and potential overvoltage tripping of sensitive loads. The defense is to measure the running reactive power under representative conditions and size the bank to that, targeting a corrected PF in the 0.92 to 0.97 range, and to use a switched bank where load varies.

### Correcting Displacement PF in a Harmonic-Dominated Facility and Seeing No Bill Reduction

A data center or office building has a true power factor of 0.82 recorded by the utility meter, and the electrician installs capacitors to correct it. The mechanism of the failure is that the low true PF is caused by harmonic distortion (distortion power factor) from switch-mode power supplies and UPS rectifiers, not by displacement, and capacitors correct only displacement. The displacement PF may already be near 0.95, so the capacitors do little, and in the worst case they create resonance that worsens the harmonics and the true PF. The false signal is that the power factor "was 0.82 and needs correction," which conflates true and displacement PF. The harm is wasted expenditure on capacitors that do not reduce the bill and may introduce resonance. The defense is to measure displacement and true PF separately with a harmonic-capable analyzer, and if distortion is the dominant cause, to specify harmonic mitigation (passive filters, active filters, active front-end drives, or a 12-pulse/18-pulse rectifier) rather than capacitors.

### Using Standard Contactors on a Switched Bank and Welding the Contacts

An automatic capacitor bank uses standard motor-duty contactors to switch the steps. The mechanism of the failure is that capacitor inrush at closing reaches 100 to 300 times rated current for a few milliseconds, far exceeding the current a motor-start contactor is designed to make, and the contacts pit, weld, or fail to open on command. The false signal is that the contactor "is rated for the current," which addresses continuous current but not the capacitive inrush duty. The harm is steps stuck in or out, unbalanced correction, and recurring contactor replacement. The defense is to use capacitor-duty contactors with damping resistors on early-make auxiliary contacts, or solid-state zero-crossing switches, and to observe the manufacturer's maximum operations-per-hour limit.

### Placing a Motor-Connected Capacitor Too Large and Causing Self-Excitation Overvoltage

The electrician installs a capacitor at each motor terminal to correct at the load, sizing each to the motor's full-load reactive power. The mechanism of the failure is that when the motor is disconnected from the line while still spinning, the capacitor's charge and the motor's residual magnetism cause the motor to self-excite as an induction generator, and the terminal voltage can rise to 150 percent or more of rated, damaging the motor windings and any connected controls. The false signal is that the capacitor "matches the motor," which is true for running correction but dangerous at disconnect. The harm is insulation failure and overvoltage damage to the motor and adjacent equipment. The defense is to limit motor-connected capacitors to roughly 80 to 90 percent of the motor's no-load reactive power, so the residual magnetism cannot sustain self-excitation, or to ensure the capacitor disconnects with the motor starter.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I measure both displacement and true power factor with a harmonic-capable instrument, and did I identify whether the low PF is caused by displacement (correctable by capacitors) or by harmonic distortion (requiring filters or active mitigation)?
- Did I calculate the capacitor kvar from measured real power and present/target displacement PF at representative load, rather than from summed motor nameplate reactive power, and did I target 0.92 to 0.97 rather than unity?
- Did I calculate the parallel resonant harmonic order h_r = sqrt(SCkVA / kvar) for the proposed bank and the system short-circuit capacity, and identify whether it lands near a characteristic harmonic (5th, 7th, 11th) of the loads?
- Where any nonlinear load is present or anticipated, did I specify a detuned bank with series reactors tuned below the 5th harmonic, rather than a standard non-detuned bank?
- Did I choose fixed versus automatic (switched) based on the measured variability of the reactive load, and did I use capacitor-duty contactors or zero-crossing switches with the correct operations-per-hour limit on switched banks?
- Did I place the bank at the point that matches the objective (utility billing, conductor/transformer relief, or voltage support), and did I limit motor-connected capacitors below the no-load reactive power to prevent self-excitation overvoltage?
- Did I document the harmonic study, the resonance calculation, and the basis for the bank size and tuning so another practitioner can review and reproduce the conclusion?
