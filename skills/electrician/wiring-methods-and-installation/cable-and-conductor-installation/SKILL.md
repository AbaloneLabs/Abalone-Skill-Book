# Cable and Conductor Installation

Cable installations fail not because the cable itself is defective, but because the installer treats the cable as a flexible commodity that can be routed anywhere and supported casually. In reality, each cable type has strict rules about where it may be used, how it must be protected from physical damage, how often it must be supported, and how its ampacity must be derated when bundled or run in hot environments. The judgment problem is that cable looks forgiving — it bends, it fits through small holes, it lies flat against framing — and the installer is tempted to take shortcuts that look harmless but create fire hazards, code violations, and latent failures. A cable stapled too loosely chafes and shorts; a cable run unprotected through a stud bay gets nailed by the drywall installer; a bundle of cables run through a hot attic overheats because derating was never applied. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a cable installation is safe, compliant, and durable over its service life.

## Core Rules

### Match the Cable Type to the Permitted Location and Occupancy

The cable types are not interchangeable, and each has specific permitted and prohibited uses defined by the NEC. Nonmetallic cable (NM, commonly called Romex) is permitted in one- and two-family dwellings and multifamily dwellings of certain construction types, but it is prohibited in buildings of Types III, IV, and V construction above three floors, and it may not be used in commercial occupancies where the construction type excludes it. Metal-clad cable (MC) has a metal armor that provides mechanical protection and is permitted in a broader range of occupancies including many commercial applications. Armored cable (AC) has a flexible metal armor with an internal bonding strip and is permitted in similar locations to MC but with its own restrictions. The trap is using NM cable in a commercial or multi-story building where the construction type prohibits it, or assuming that any cable with a metal jacket is acceptable everywhere. The defense is to verify the construction type and occupancy classification, confirm the cable type is permitted for that location, and never substitute a residential cable type into a commercial installation without checking the article.

### Support and Staple Cables at the Required Intervals

Cable must be supported and secured at intervals that prevent sagging and movement, and the intervals differ by cable type. NM cable must be secured by staples, straps, or other approved means within 12 inches of every box or fitting and at intervals not exceeding 4.5 feet. MC and AC cables have their own support spacing requirements, typically every 6 feet and within 12 inches of boxes. Cables run through holes in framing do not require additional support between the framing members because the wood or metal provides the support. The trap is leaving long unsupported runs of cable in an attic or between joists, relying on the cable's stiffness to hold it in place, when vibration, thermal movement, or subsequent construction will cause it to sag or shift. The defense is to staple or strap at the required intervals, to secure cable within the required distance of every box, and to recognize that cables run horizontally through bored holes in studs are supported by the studs but still need securing where they exit the framing.

### Protect Cables From Physical Damage in Exposed and Concealed Locations

Cables are vulnerable to physical damage wherever they are exposed, run through framing where fasteners may penetrate them, or buried without adequate protection. NM and AC cables installed exposed must be protected from physical damage, which in practice means running them along the sides or bottom of framing members rather than on the face where they can be struck, or installing guard strips or plates where they pass through studs within reach of fasteners. MC cable's armor provides a degree of physical protection but is still subject to damage limitations. Cables run through bored holes in studs must keep a minimum distance from the stud face, or nail plates must be installed to prevent drywall screws and trim nails from penetrating the cable. The trap is running cable through a stud bay close to the face of the stud, assuming that because the cable is concealed it is safe, when the drywall installer or trim carpenter will drive fasteners into that stud and hit the cable. The defense is to bore holes at the required depth from the stud face, install nail plates where the cable is closer than the minimum distance, and route exposed cable along protected surfaces.

### Apply Ampacity Derating for Bundling and Ambient Temperature

The ampacity of a conductor is not a fixed number from the table — it must be corrected for ambient temperature and derated for the number of current-carrying conductors bundled together. When more than three current-carrying conductors are bundled or run in the same raceway or cable for more than 24 inches, the ampacity must be derated per NEC Table 310.15(C)(1). When the ambient temperature exceeds 30 degrees Celsius (86 degrees Fahrenheit), the ampacity must be corrected per the temperature correction factors in Table 310.16. Both adjustments reduce the ampacity, and they compound when applied together. The trap is treating the table ampacity as the usable ampacity, particularly for bundled cables in hot attics where the combination of bundling derating and temperature correction can reduce a 20-amp conductor to 15 amps or less. The defense is to count the current-carrying conductors in every bundle, apply the bundling derating when the count exceeds three, apply temperature correction based on the actual ambient temperature, and verify that the final derated ampacity still supports the intended load.

### Protect Cables From Edge Abrasion at Penetrations

Where a cable passes through a metal stud, a metal box knockout, or any sharp edge, the cable jacket and insulation must be protected from abrasion. Plastic or nylon bushings are installed in metal studs and at metal box openings to prevent the sharp edge from cutting into the cable jacket over time as the cable vibrates or shifts. Without the bushing, the metal edge saws into the jacket with every movement, eventually exposing the conductor and creating a fault. The trap is pulling cable through a metal stud or knockout without a bushing because the cable went through without visible damage, when the damage develops slowly over years of thermal cycling and vibration. The defense is to install listed bushings at every metal penetration, to inspect the cable jacket after pulling for any scoring, and to recognize that metal framing members require the same protection as metal boxes.

### Size and Terminate Conductors to Match the Breaker and the Terminal Rating

The conductor must be sized to carry the load and protected by a breaker sized to the conductor's ampacity, and the conductor's insulation temperature rating must match or exceed the terminal rating of the equipment it connects to. A 90-degree-rated conductor (THHN) connected to a terminal rated for 60 degrees (as on much older equipment) must use the 60-degree column ampacity, not the 90-degree column, because the terminal cannot dissipate the heat that a higher-rated conductor would carry. The trap is selecting conductor size from the 90-degree column because the wire is THHN, ignoring that the terminal rating limits the usable ampacity to the lower column. The defense is to identify the terminal temperature rating of the connected equipment, use the ampacity column that matches the lowest-rated termination in the circuit, and recognize that modern equipment rated 75 degrees allows use of the 75-degree column but not the 90-degree column for termination ampacity.

## Common Traps

### Using NM Cable in a Prohibited Commercial or Multi-Story Occupancy

An electrician installs nonmetallic cable throughout a small commercial tenant space because it is cheaper and faster than metal-clad cable, and the job looks like a residential buildout. The trap is that NM cable is permitted by occupancy and construction type, not by the appearance of the space, and many commercial occupancies and construction types prohibit it. The mechanism of harm is that the installation fails inspection and must be removed and replaced, or worse, it passes inspection incorrectly and creates a fire-spread path because the nonmetallic jacket lacks the fire resistance of metal-clad cable in the required construction type. The false signal is that the space "feels residential," so the cable choice seems appropriate. The defense is to determine the occupancy classification and construction type before selecting the cable, and to default to MC or a raceway method when the classification is uncertain.

### Bundling Cables in an Attic Without Derating

An electrician runs six separate two-wire NM cables through a hot attic, bundling them together with cable ties as they cross the attic floor toward a panel. The attic ambient temperature reaches 45 degrees Celsius in summer. The trap is that six cables means twelve current-carrying conductors bundled for more than 24 inches, triggering a significant derating from Table 310.15(C)(1), and the 45-degree ambient triggers a temperature correction, and the two adjustments compound. The original 20-amp circuits may now be limited to well below 20 amps, and the breakers will not trip because they are sized to the original ampacity. The mechanism of harm is conductor overheating, jacket degradation, and potential ignition in the insulation-packed attic. The false signal is that each cable individually is within its ampacity. The defense is to count current-carrying conductors in every bundle, apply both derating factors, and either reduce the bundle size or upsize the conductors to maintain the required ampacity.

### Running Cable on the Face of a Stud Without Protection

An electrician runs NM cable along the face of a stud in an unfinished basement or garage to save the effort of boring through the stud, stapling it flat against the wood. The cable is exposed on the face where it can be struck by tools, ladders, stored materials, or the building occupant. The trap is that the cable is stapled securely and looks neat, so it appears properly installed, but the NEC requires exposed NM cable to be protected from physical damage, and running on the face of a stud in an accessible location does not meet that requirement. The mechanism of harm is mechanical damage to the cable jacket and conductors from everyday activity in the space. The false signal is that the cable is supported and looks intentional. The defense is to run exposed cable along the sides or bottom of framing members, install guard strips or raceway where the cable must run on an exposed surface, or relocate the cable behind finished protection.

### Omitting Nail Plates Where Cable Passes Close to a Stud Face

An electrician bores a hole through a stud to run NM cable, but the hole is closer to the face of the stud than the code-required minimum distance because the stud is shallow or the boring drifted. The drywall installer later drives screws into the stud and the trim carpenter drives finish nails, and one of those fasteners penetrates the cable, creating a short circuit or a latent fault. The trap is that the cable is concealed and the hole looks adequately deep, so the installer does not install a nail plate. The mechanism of harm is that fasteners driven from the finished side have no way to avoid a cable that sits too close to the face, and the damage is invisible behind the drywall until a fault appears. The false signal is that the hole looked deep enough at the time. The defense is to measure the distance from the cable to the stud face, install nail plates wherever the cable is closer than the minimum required distance (typically 1.25 inches), and bore holes at the center of the stud depth whenever possible.

### Terminating 90-Degree Wire Using the 90-Degree Ampacity Column

An electrician selects a 6 AWG THHN conductor and reads the ampacity from the 90-degree column of Table 310.16, sizing the breaker to match. But the conductor terminates at a circuit breaker and at equipment terminals rated for 75 degrees. The trap is that the termination, not the conductor, limits the ampacity, and the 90-degree column overstates the usable ampacity at the terminals. The mechanism of harm is that the terminal overheates because it is carrying more current than its rating allows, even though the conductor itself is within its rating. The overheated terminal can damage the breaker, melt the insulation at the connection, and create a fire at the panel. The false signal is that the wire is rated for 90 degrees, so the installer assumes the full ampacity is available. The defense is to identify the terminal temperature rating of every device the conductor connects to, and use the ampacity column corresponding to the lowest-rated termination in the circuit.

### Overlooking the edge case or exception

The typical or textbook scenario is analyzed thoroughly, but the unusual case is skipped. The trap is that the standard path is well-handled while the exception silently produces the wrong outcome, because the agent stopped at the common case and never tested the boundary.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify that the selected cable type (NM, MC, AC) is permitted for the occupancy classification and construction type of the building, not just for the appearance of the space?
- Did I staple, strap, or otherwise secure the cable within the required distance of every box and at intervals not exceeding the limit for the cable type?
- Did I protect exposed cable from physical damage by routing along protected surfaces, installing guard strips, or running through framing rather than on the face of studs?
- Did I count the current-carrying conductors in every bundle exceeding three, apply the bundling derating from Table 310.15(C)(1), and apply temperature correction based on the actual ambient temperature?
- Did I install nail plates wherever cable passes through framing closer than the minimum required distance from the stud face, and bushings wherever cable penetrates metal studs or box knockouts?
- Did I select conductor size using the ampacity column that matches the lowest terminal temperature rating in the circuit, rather than defaulting to the 90-degree column because the wire is THHN?
- Did I inspect every cable jacket after pulling for scoring or abrasion, and replace any cable whose jacket is damaged?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
