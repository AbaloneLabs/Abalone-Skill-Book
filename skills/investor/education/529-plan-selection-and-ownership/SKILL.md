# 529 Plan Selection And Ownership

A 529 plan is a tax-advantaged account for education savings, but the decision of which plan to use and who owns it has consequences far beyond investment selection. The state of the plan affects tax deductions, the ownership structure affects financial aid assessment, and the beneficiary and account owner rules affect control, transfers, and penalties. The judgment problem is that the best investment menu is not always the best plan once tax deductions and aid impact are included, and the most convenient ownership is not always the most aid-efficient.

The harm this skill prevents is choosing a plan for its expense ratio while ignoring a valuable state tax deduction, or choosing an ownership structure that maximizes financial aid damage, or overfunding an account whose penalties on non-qualified withdrawals then trap the family. 529 rules, deduction limits, and financial aid formulas vary by state and change over time, and recent FAFSA simplification changed how some distributions are treated. No output here is personalized advice. The agent should frame the tax, aid, and control tradeoffs and direct the user to current state rules and a qualified advisor.

## Core Rules

### Compare The State Tax Deduction Against Investment Quality

Many states offer an income tax deduction or credit for contributions to the home state's plan, and some extend it to any state's plan. The deduction can be worth more than the difference in fund expense ratios over the saving period, so the cheapest out-of-state plan is not always the best after-tax choice. The agent should compute the value of the deduction, check whether the home state requires using its own plan, and weigh it against the plan's investment options and fees. For residents of states with no income tax or no deduction, the best national plan on cost and quality usually wins.

### Decide Ownership By Control And Financial Aid Impact

The account owner controls the 529, can change the beneficiary, and decides distributions, so ownership is a control decision as well as an aid decision. A parent-owned 529 is generally favorable for financial aid because it is treated as a parent asset assessed at a low rate, and qualified distributions for student education are not counted as student income under current FAFSA rules. A grandparent-owned 529 is not reported as an asset on the FAFSA, and under recent simplification qualified distributions are also not counted as student income, which can be advantageous, though CSS Profile schools may treat it differently. The agent should match ownership to both the control the contributor wants and the aid formula that applies.

### Understand The FAFSA Treatment Of Assets And Distributions

On the FAFSA, parent assets including parent-owned 529s are assessed at a low rate, while student assets such as custodial accounts are assessed at a much higher rate. Qualified 529 distributions for the student are not added back as student income under current rules, which is a major advantage over custodial accounts. The agent should position 529 assets favorably relative to student-owned assets and should note that the treatment of grandparent distributions has changed, so outdated advice may no longer apply.

### Use Ownership To Preserve Contributor Control

A contributor who wants to ensure funds are used for education, retain the ability to change beneficiaries, or reclaim funds, should be the account owner rather than contributing to a student-owned or custodial account. Grandparents who want control should open their own 529 rather than contributing to a parent-owned plan, accepting the aid-treatment tradeoff. The agent should clarify who needs control before recommending where contributions go.

### Check Contribution Limits And Gift Tax Coordination

529 plans have high lifetime contribution limits set by each state, and contributions qualify for the annual gift tax exclusion, with a special election that allows front-loading several years of exclusions in one year. Large contributions can use lifetime gift exemption and require reporting. The agent should coordinate 529 funding with the broader gifting and estate strategy, and should note the front-loading election for contributors who want to move assets out of the estate efficiently.

### Plan The Beneficiary Flexibility Early

A 529's beneficiary can be changed to another qualifying family member without tax consequence, which is the main escape valve for overfunding. The agent should consider whether siblings, the account owner themselves for continuing education, or other relatives are available as alternative beneficiaries, because this flexibility reduces the risk of penalties if the original beneficiary does not need all the funds. Plans without a clear alternate beneficiary carry more overfunding risk.

### Account For K-12, Student Loan, And Apprenticeship Uses

Qualified expenses now include not just college costs but also a limited amount of K-12 tuition per year and a lifetime amount of student loan repayment, and certain apprenticeship costs. These expanded uses give 529 funds more outlets and reduce overfunding risk, but the limits are specific. The agent should include these uses in the funding plan and should not assume the funds can only pay for four-year college.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The Cheapest Plan And Forgoing A State Deduction

A low expense ratio does not always beat a state tax deduction. The agent should compute the after-tax comparison, not just the fund cost.

### Using A Custodial Account Where A 529 Would Be Aid-Friendlier

Student-owned custodial assets are assessed at a high aid rate. The agent should prefer parent-owned 529 treatment where aid matters.

### Applying Outdated Grandparent-Distribution Rules

FAFSA simplification changed how grandparent 529 distributions are treated. The agent should use current rules, not pre-simplification advice.

### Overfunding Without An Alternate Beneficiary

Without a qualifying alternate beneficiary, excess funds face tax and penalty on withdrawal. The agent should plan beneficiary flexibility early.

### Ignoring The Front-Loading Election For Estate Reduction

Contributors can move several years of gift exclusion into a 529 at once. The agent should consider this for estate-reduction goals.

### Forgetting K-12 And Student Loan Qualified Uses

Expanded qualified uses reduce overfunding risk. The agent should include them rather than assuming only college qualifies.

### Confusing Account Owner Control With Beneficiary Interest

The account owner, not the beneficiary, controls the funds. The agent should clarify control before recommending a contribution structure.
