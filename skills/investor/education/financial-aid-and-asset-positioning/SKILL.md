# Financial Aid And Asset Positioning

Financial aid is determined by formulas that assess different assets and income streams at different rates, which means where a family holds its savings can change the aid award by thousands of dollars. The judgment problem is that the asset that is best for taxes or growth is not always the asset that is best for aid, and the moves that improve aid can harm the family's overall finances if made for the formula alone. Asset positioning for aid is a narrow optimization that must be weighed against the family's real financial picture.

The harm this skill prevents is parking savings in student-owned accounts that are assessed at a punitive rate, triggering large income in a base year that collapses aid, or making costly financial contortions to game a formula that may not even apply to the schools in question. Aid formulas, asset protection allowances, and income brackets change over time, and FAFSA and CSS Profile schools use different methodologies. No output here is personalized advice. The agent should frame the assessment mechanics and direct the user to each school's net price calculator and a qualified advisor.

## Core Rules

### Know Which Formula Each School Uses

The FAFSA determines federal aid and uses the Student Aid Index methodology. Many private colleges also use the CSS Profile, a institutional methodology that assesses assets and income differently, often including home equity, non-custodial parent income, and retirement account balances in some cases. The agent must identify which schools use which formula before recommending positioning, because a move that helps on the FAFSA can hurt on the CSS Profile. The two methodologies are not interchangeable, and advice that ignores the distinction is incomplete.

### Position Assets By Assessment Rate

Under the FAFSA, parent assets are assessed at a low rate while student assets are assessed at a much higher rate. This makes parent-owned accounts, including parent-owned 529s, far more aid-friendly than custodial or student-owned accounts. The agent should prefer parent ownership for education savings and should flag that moving a custodial account into a 529, which is generally not allowed directly, or spending it down on the student's behalf, can reduce the high-rate assessment. The assessment rate difference is the single most actionable positioning lever.

### Time Income Around The Base Year

Income is assessed more heavily than assets in the aid formulas, and the relevant income is from the base year, the tax year before the aid year. Realizing a large capital gain, converting a traditional account to a Roth, taking a retirement distribution, or exercising stock options in a base year can spike assessed income and collapse aid even if the family's actual cash flow is unchanged. The agent should identify the base years for each child and should avoid recommending income-accelerating moves in those years where aid is in play.

### Protect Retirement Assets Where The Formula Allows

Qualified retirement accounts such as 401ks and IRAs are generally not assessed as assets on the FAFSA, though contributions to them in the base year are added back to income. The agent should recognize that retirement accounts are sheltered from the asset assessment, which argues for maximizing retirement contributions over taxable savings for aid purposes, while noting that the contribution add-back limits the benefit in the base year. CSS Profile schools may assess retirement balances differently.

### Treat Home Equity Carefully By Methodology

Home equity is not assessed on the FAFSA but is often assessed on the CSS Profile, sometimes capped at a multiple of income. Families applying to CSS schools should understand that paying down the mortgage or rising home values can increase their assessed assets. The agent should not assume home equity is universally sheltered, and should check the methodology of the target schools.

### Avoid Formula Gaming That Harms The Real Plan

Some aid strategies, such as shifting assets to relatives, taking on debt to reduce cash, or timing asset sales, can improve the formula but harm the family's finances or carry audit and ethical risk. The agent should weigh the aid benefit against the real cost and should avoid contortions that trade genuine financial health for a formula advantage, especially when the school's methodology may not even reward the move.

### Use Each School's Net Price Calculator

Every college is required to provide a net price calculator that estimates the actual cost after aid for that specific institution. Because aid policies vary widely, the agent should direct the family to run the calculators for their target schools rather than relying on generic formula estimates. The calculator output is more decision-relevant than abstract positioning theory.

## Common Traps

### Keeping Savings In A Student-Owned Custodial Account

Student assets are assessed at a punitive rate. The agent should prefer parent-owned vehicles for education savings.

### Triggering Large Income In A Base Year

Capital gains, Roth conversions, and distributions in a base year spike assessed income. The agent should time income-accelerating moves around base years.

### Applying FAFSA Rules To CSS Profile Schools

CSS schools assess home equity, retirement balances, and non-custodial income differently. The agent should not generalize FAFSA treatment.

### Assuming Home Equity Is Always Sheltered

Home equity is sheltered on the FAFSA but often assessed on the CSS Profile. The agent should check the target schools' methodology.

### Gaming The Formula At Real Financial Cost

Shifting assets or taking on debt to reduce the assessment can harm the family's actual finances. The agent should weigh real cost against aid benefit.

### Ignoring The Contribution Add-Back For Retirement Accounts

Retirement contributions in the base year are added back to income even though the balances are sheltered. The agent should account for the add-back.

### Relying On Generic Estimates Instead Of Net Price Calculators

Aid policies vary by school. The agent should use each school's net price calculator for decision-relevant estimates.
