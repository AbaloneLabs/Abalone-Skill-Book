# Mean Reversion

Mean reversion is the tendency of asset prices, valuations, and returns to move back toward their long-run averages after extended departures. The thesis is that extremes are unsustainable, whether on the upside (bubbles, overvaluation) or downside (panic, undervaluation), and that positioning for reversion captures the correction. The judgment problem is that mean reversion is powerful in the long run but treacherous in application. Investors routinely mistake a genuine regime change or structural shift for a temporary departure, fade trends far too early, and suffer enormous losses as the "overvalued" asset becomes more overvalued for years. The famous adage applies: markets can remain irrational longer than the investor can remain solvent. The discipline is to distinguish temporary extremes from structural shifts, time entries with patience and risk control, and size positions so that being "early" (which is indistinguishable from being wrong) does not cause ruin.

This skill is for applying mean-reversion logic with discipline about timing, risk, and the distinction between cyclical and structural change.

## Core Rules

### Establish A Credible Long-Run Average To Revert Toward

Mean reversion requires a defensible estimate of the "mean." Without it, the concept is meaningless. The mean itself can shift over time.

Define the mean:

- use long-run historical averages of valuation metrics (e.g., CAPE, price-to-book, yield) over full market cycles;
- account for structural changes (interest rates, inflation, regulation, business models) that may justify a different equilibrium;
- distinguish cyclical departures (which revert) from structural shifts (which establish a new mean);
- use multiple metrics and timeframes to avoid dependence on a single, potentially distorted average.

A mean based on a short or unusual period is unreliable. The credibility of the reversion thesis depends on the robustness of the average and the argument that the current departure is temporary.

### Distinguish Cyclical Departures From Structural Shifts

The most dangerous error in mean reversion is fading a structural shift as if it were a cyclical extreme. Not every departure reverts.

Test the nature of the departure:

- is the elevated or depressed level driven by temporary factors (sentiment, cyclical earnings, liquidity) that will normalize?
- or is it driven by structural change (technology disruption, regulatory shift, permanently lower rates, business model transformation) that establishes a new equilibrium?
- what is the evidence for each interpretation, and what would confirm or refute the structural case?
- how long has the departure persisted, since very long departures may signal a regime change rather than a temporary extreme?

Cyclical departures are candidates for reversion trades. Structural shifts are not, and fading them can produce large losses as the new equilibrium persists or extends.

### Time Entries With Patience And Confirmation

Mean reversion strategies are notoriously early, because extremes can extend far beyond rational levels. Entering on the first sign of extension often means suffering before the reversion occurs.

Improve timing:

- wait for confirmation that the extreme is rolling over, rather than fading the trend while it is still accelerating;
- use valuation extremes combined with technical or sentiment confirmation (e.g., capitulation volume, sentiment surveys, insider buying);
- scale in gradually rather than establishing the full position at once, to average in if the extreme extends;
- accept that perfect timing is impossible and that some early pain is the cost of the strategy;
- define the horizon over which reversion is expected, since reversion can take years.

Patience and scaling reduce the risk of being wiped out by an extending extreme. Confirmation signals, while imperfect, improve the odds relative to pure valuation-based fading.

### Size Positions To Survive Being Early

Because mean-reversion trades can move against the investor for extended periods, position sizing must ensure survival. Being "early" is the defining risk.

Size defensively:

- limit the allocation to any single reversion trade so that extended adverse movement is survivable;
- use stop-losses or defined-risk structures where appropriate, while recognizing that stops can trigger on noise before the reversion;
- maintain diversification across independent reversion opportunities to reduce idiosyncratic risk;
- account for the potential for the extreme to extend significantly beyond historical norms;
- preserve liquidity and dry powder to add if the extreme extends further.

The reversion thesis may be correct, but if the position is sized so large that the investor is forced to liquidate before the reversion occurs, the correct thesis produces a loss. Sizing for survival is paramount.

### Understand The Time Horizon For Reversion

Mean reversion operates over different horizons depending on the asset and the metric. The investor must match the strategy to the relevant horizon.

Align horizons:

- short-term reversion (days to weeks) often reflects liquidity, sentiment, and positioning, and is exploited by statistical arbitrage and market-making;
- medium-term reversion (months to a year) can reflect earnings surprises and analyst adjustment cycles;
- long-term reversion (years to decades) reflects valuation and business cycle normalization, and is the domain of value and contrarian investing;
- the strategy's holding period must match the horizon of the reversion it targets.

An investor expecting long-term valuation reversion must have a multi-year horizon and the patience to hold through extended adverse movement. Mismatched horizons lead to premature abandonment.

### Prepare For The Behavioral Challenge

Mean reversion is psychologically demanding because it requires buying what is hated and selling what is loved, often while the trend continues against the position.

Build behavioral defenses:

- a written thesis with the reversion rationale, the mean estimate, and the expected horizon;
- pre-commitment to the holding period and risk limits, decided in calm conditions;
- acceptance that social and peer pressure will oppose the contrarian position;
- regular review of the thesis (not the mark-to-market) to confirm the reversion logic remains intact;
- the understanding that being early is normal and expected, not a sign of error.

The investor who cannot hold a losing reversion position through its adverse movement will not capture the reversion. Behavioral resilience is the implementation of the strategy.

## Common Traps

### Fading A Structural Shift As Cyclical

Not every extreme reverts. Structural changes (disruption, regulation, rate regimes) establish new equilibria, and fading them causes large losses.

### Being Too Early

Extremes can extend far beyond rational levels. Entering on the first sign of extension often means suffering for years.

### Overreliance On A Single Metric

A single valuation metric can be distorted or structurally outdated. Use multiple metrics and timeframes.

### Ignoring The Time Horizon

Reversion operates over specific horizons. Mismatched strategy and horizon lead to premature abandonment.

### Poor Position Sizing

Reversion trades can move against the investor for extended periods. Oversized positions force liquidation before the reversion.

### Confusing Mean Reversion With Value

Mean reversion and value investing overlap but differ. Reversion targets departures from averages; value targets discounts to intrinsic value. The distinction matters for the thesis.

### Abandoning The Thesis On Mark-To-Market

Holding a losing reversion position is painful, but abandoning on price rather than thesis destroys the strategy.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A credible long-run average is established using multiple metrics over full cycles, with structural changes accounted for.
- [ ] The departure is assessed as cyclical (likely to revert) versus structural (likely a new equilibrium), with evidence for each interpretation.
- [ ] Entries are timed with patience and confirmation signals, and positions are scaled gradually to manage the risk of being early.
- [ ] Positions are sized to survive extended adverse movement, with diversification across independent reversion opportunities.
- [ ] The strategy's time horizon matches the horizon of the targeted reversion (short, medium, or long term).
- [ ] Behavioral defenses (written thesis, pre-commitment, thesis-based review) are in place to sustain the position through adverse movement.
- [ ] The investor distinguishes mean reversion from intrinsic-value investing and applies the correct framework.
- [ ] The guidance flags that extremes can extend for long periods, that being early is indistinguishable from being wrong, that structural shifts may not revert, that past performance does not guarantee future results, and that professional advice may be warranted for contrarian or reversion strategies.
