# Commission And Fee Analysis

Commissions and fees are the explicit, posted charges for trading and holding accounts. The widespread move to zero-commission retail trading has created a common misconception that trading is now free, but the cost has not disappeared; it has shifted to less visible mechanisms, principally payment for order flow, where brokers are paid to route orders to specific market makers, and that payment is priced into the fill. The judgment problem is that the advertised commission is only one component of trading cost, and focusing on it while ignoring how the broker is actually compensated leads to choosing a broker on a metric that does not capture the real expense.

The harm this skill prevents is assuming zero commission means zero cost, choosing a broker on advertised price while ignoring execution quality and payment for order flow, overlooking account-level and regulatory fees that accumulate, and underestimating the total explicit cost of an active trading pattern. The agent's job is to identify all the ways a broker is compensated, to compare advertised commissions against the less visible costs, and to evaluate whether a low-commission structure actually delivers the best after-cost outcome. Fee structures, payment for order flow rules, and regulations vary by jurisdiction and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Treat Zero Commission As A Shift, Not An Elimination

Zero-commission trading is marketed as free, but brokers still earn money, principally through payment for order flow, securities lending, interest on cash balances, and premium services. The cost is embedded in the fill price and in the spread the investor effectively pays, rather than billed as a line item. The agent should never present zero commission as zero cost, and should explain that the compensation model has changed, not disappeared. Understanding how the broker is paid is essential to judging the true cost.

### Evaluate Payment For Order Flow And Its Effect On Fills

Payment for order flow means the broker receives a rebate for routing orders to particular wholesale market makers, who then execute the orders. The concern is that this creates an incentive to route orders for the broker's benefit rather than the best execution, and that the cost of the rebate may be recovered through slightly worse fill prices or wider effective spreads. The agent should ask whether the broker routes for best execution, should compare effective spreads and fill quality against benchmarks, and should recognize that payment for order flow is legal and regulated in some jurisdictions and banned in others, with different implications.

### Compare Advertised Commission Against Total Cost

A broker with a small per-share or per-trade commission may deliver better net execution than a zero-commission broker if its fills are better, just as a zero-commission broker may be cheaper overall if its payment-for-order-flow model still produces good prices. The agent should compare the total cost, commission plus the effective spread cost plus any other fees, rather than the headline rate alone. The right comparison is after-cost execution quality, not the advertised commission.

### Identify All Account-Level And Regulatory Fees

Beyond per-trade commissions, accounts carry other explicit costs: maintenance fees, inactivity fees, wire and transfer fees, margin interest, annual account fees, mutual fund and ETF expense ratios, and regulatory and exchange fees that may be passed through. The agent should inventory the full fee schedule, not just the commission, because these accumulate and can exceed the trading commission for some account types and activity levels. A low-commission broker with high account fees can be more expensive overall.

### Match The Fee Structure To The Trading Pattern

The best broker and fee structure depends on how the investor trades. A frequent trader of small sizes is sensitive to per-trade commissions and to effective spreads; a buy-and-hold investor is barely affected by commissions but sensitive to expense ratios and account fees; a large-order trader is sensitive to impact and spread, not commission. The agent should match the fee analysis to the investor's actual pattern, because a structure that is cheap for one pattern can be expensive for another.

### Scrutinize Margin Interest And Cash Sweep Rates

For investors who use margin or hold cash balances, margin interest and the interest rate on uninvested cash, the sweep rate, are significant costs and revenue sources. Brokers often pay well below market rates on cash, profiting from the spread, which is a hidden cost for investors who hold cash. The agent should consider margin interest for leveraged positions and should compare cash sweep rates to alternatives, because these can dominate the explicit commission cost for some accounts.

### Review Fee Disclosures And Confirm Current Terms

Fee schedules change, and the marketed rate may not match the current disclosure. The agent should direct the investor to the official fee schedule and best-execution reports, should confirm that quoted terms are current, and should periodically re-check, because a broker that was cheapest may no longer be. Fee analysis is not a one-time decision; it should be revisited as terms change.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Zero Commission Means Free Trading

The cost shifted to payment for order flow and spreads. The agent should never present it as free.

### Choosing A Broker On Advertised Commission Alone

Total cost includes spreads, fees, and execution quality. The agent should compare after-cost outcomes.

### Ignoring Payment For Order Flow Incentives

PFOH creates routing incentives that may not serve best execution. The agent should check fill quality.

### Overlooking Account And Regulatory Fees

Maintenance, transfer, and pass-through fees accumulate. The agent should inventory the full schedule.

### Applying One Fee Analysis To All Trading Patterns

The cheapest structure depends on trade frequency and size. The agent should match analysis to the pattern.

### Forgetting Margin Interest And Cash Sweep Rates

These can exceed commission cost for leveraged or cash-heavy accounts. The agent should include them.

### Treating Fee Analysis As One-Time

Schedules change. The agent should confirm current terms periodically.
