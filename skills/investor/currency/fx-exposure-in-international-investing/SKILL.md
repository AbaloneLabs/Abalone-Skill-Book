# FX Exposure In International Investing

Every foreign investment is two bets: the asset, and the currency. The judgment problem is that investors focus on the foreign asset and treat the currency as neutral, when over horizons of months to a few years currency moves can dominate, erase, or double the asset's local return. Agents and investors misunderstand when currency exposure diversifies and when it concentrates risk, apply the same hedging logic to equities and bonds despite their very different volatilities, and ignore that the investor's base currency and spending currency define what "foreign" even means. The skill is recognizing that currency exposure is an explicit position with its own risk and return, and deciding deliberately whether to hold it rather than inheriting it by default.

This skill is for evaluating currency exposure in international portfolios. It is not personalized financial advice; currency effects depend on base currency, horizon, and individual circumstances.

## Core Rules

### Treat Currency As A Separate Position, Not A Neutral Residual

A foreign stock bought in another currency produces a return equal to the local asset return plus (or minus) the currency movement against the investor's base currency. The currency leg is a distinct exposure with its own volatility and its own expected return.

Decompose every international return:

- the local-currency asset return (what the foreign market delivered);
- the currency return (how the foreign currency moved against the base currency);
- the interaction term (usually small but present in multiplicative returns).

Over one to three years, currency volatility can exceed equity volatility contribution, and can flip a gain into a loss or vice versa. Ignoring the currency leg is ignoring half the risk.

### Define The Base And Spending Currency Before Judging Exposure

What counts as "foreign" currency exposure depends on the investor's base currency and, more importantly, on the currency of their future spending. An investor who will spend in euros experiences dollar exposure as risk; an investor who will retire to a foreign country has a different liability currency.

Anchor to the spending currency:

- the relevant base currency is the currency of future obligations and spending, not necessarily the accounting currency;
- an investor with multi-currency spending (education abroad, foreign property, international family obligations) has a blended liability currency profile;
- a temporary expatriate may have a base currency that differs from their long-term spending currency, changing what exposure means.

Judging currency exposure without defining the spending currency leads to hedging the wrong risk.

### Recognize When Currency Diversifies And When It Concentrates

Currency exposure is not always harmful. Over long horizons, currency moves can diversify equity risk, and a portfolio of global equities held unhedged has historically had comparable or slightly lower volatility than its hedged counterpart over multi-decade periods. But over short horizons, currency adds volatility without expected return.

Distinguish by role:

- over long horizons, unhedged equity currency exposure can provide diversification, because currency moves are roughly mean-reverting and decorrelate with equity returns over time;
- over short horizons, currency adds volatility and can sharply reduce returns, with no expected return premium to compensate;
- currency exposure concentrates risk when the foreign currency is correlated with the investor's other risks (commodity exposure, job, regional economy).

The diversification benefit is horizon-dependent and uncertain; the added short-term volatility is reliable. Match the decision to the horizon.

### Apply Different Hedging Logic To Equities And Bonds

The case for hedging currency differs sharply by asset class because currency volatility interacts differently with asset volatility.

By asset class:

- **international bonds** — bond volatility is low and comparable to currency volatility, so unhedged currency can dominate and often double the volatility of a foreign bond holding; currency hedging is usually appropriate for the bond sleeve;
- **international equities** — equity volatility is several times currency volatility, so currency contributes a smaller fraction of total risk, and the diversification benefit of unhedged exposure is more arguable; partial hedging or no hedging is often defensible;
- **real assets and alternatives** — the interaction depends on the asset's own currency sensitivity.

A common error is applying one hedging policy across all asset classes. Bonds and equities warrant different currency decisions because currency means something different to each.

### Understand That Currency Has No Reliable Long-Run Expected Return

Unlike equities (expected real return from earnings) or bonds (yield), currencies do not offer a reliable long-run real return premium. Over long horizons, exchange rates tend to reflect inflation differentials (purchasing power parity), leaving no systematic real return to holding currency exposure.

Implications:

- currency exposure adds volatility without adding expected return over the long run;
- the case for unhedged exposure rests on diversification and mean-reversion, not on a currency return premium;
- speculative currency positions are zero-sum against other currency market participants and rarely reward non-specialists.

Treat currency exposure as a risk decision, not a return source, unless there is a specific, well-founded view.

### Account For Uncovered Interest Rate Parity And The Cost Of Hedging

Hedging currency is not free. The cost of a forward hedge reflects the interest rate differential between the two currencies (uncovered and covered interest rate parity). Hedging a low-rate currency into a high-rate currency incurs a cost; hedging a high-rate currency into a low-rate currency may generate a yield pickup.

Price the hedge:

- the forward points embed the interest rate differential, so hedging is not a zero-cost transaction;
- over time, hedging a currency with lower rates than the base tends to drag returns;
- the cost interacts with the asset's yield, so high-yielding foreign assets hedged into a lower-rate base currency can lose their yield advantage.

A hedged international bond fund's yield can differ materially from the unhedged yield because of this cost. Model the net, not the gross.

### Decide A Deliberate Currency Policy Rather Than Inheriting One

The worst currency decision is the unconscious one — holding whatever exposure the international allocation happens to produce, without examining it. Make the policy explicit.

Set the policy by:

- choosing a target hedge ratio by asset class (often near-full hedging for bonds, partial or zero for equities);
- defining the base currency as the spending currency;
- reviewing the policy when the spending currency changes (relocation, retirement abroad, new obligations);
- recognizing that currency-hedged and unhedged funds behave differently and selecting the right share class or vehicle.

A deliberate, reviewed currency policy is far better than an inherited default, even if the chosen policy is imperfect.

## Common Traps

### Treating Currency As Neutral

Currency is a separate position with its own volatility. Ignoring it ignores half the risk of international investing.

### Defining Foreign Relative To The Wrong Currency

The relevant base is the spending currency. Hedging to the accounting currency may hedge the wrong risk.

### Applying One Hedging Policy To All Asset Classes

Bonds and equities warrant different currency decisions because currency contributes very different fractions of their total risk.

### Assuming Currency Offers A Long-Run Return Premium

Currencies have no reliable long-run real return. Unhedged exposure is a diversification argument, not a return argument.

### Ignoring The Cost Of Hedging

Forward points embed the interest rate differential. Hedging can drag returns, especially for low-rate currencies.

### Inheriting Currency Exposure By Default

Holding whatever exposure the allocation produces, without a policy, is an unconscious bet. Make the policy explicit and reviewed.

### Confusing Short-Term And Long-Horizon Currency Effects

Currency adds reliable short-term volatility but may diversify over long horizons. The decision depends on the horizon.
