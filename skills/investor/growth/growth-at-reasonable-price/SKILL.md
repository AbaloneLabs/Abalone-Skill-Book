# Growth At A Reasonable Price

Growth at a reasonable price (GARP) seeks companies with above-average, sustainable growth purchased at valuations that do not require perfection. The thesis is a middle path: pure growth investors often overpay for the future, and pure value investors miss compounding businesses, so GARP targets growth that is fairly compensated by the price paid. The judgment problem is that "reasonable price" is subjective and growth is uncertain. Investors routinely use the PEG ratio mechanically, accept growth forecasts that are optimistic, and fail to distinguish growth that creates value (above the cost of capital) from growth that destroys it. GARP done well captures compounding at fair prices; done poorly, it overpays for disappointing growth while telling itself it was disciplined.

This skill is for applying GARP with rigor about growth quality, sustainability, and the true reasonableness of the price.

## Core Rules

### Understand What PEG Does And Does Not Capture

The PEG ratio (price-to-earnings divided by growth rate) is the signature GARP metric, but it is a crude shorthand, not a complete valuation.

Use PEG carefully:

- it normalizes the P/E for growth, allowing comparison across growth rates;
- it assumes growth is real, sustainable, and value-creating, which is often not tested;
- it ignores the duration of growth, the quality of earnings, and the cost of capital;
- it treats all growth equally, whether funded by high-return reinvestment or dilutive issuance;
- a low PEG is not automatically cheap, and a high PEG is not automatically expensive.

PEG is a screening tool and a cross-check, not a valuation. Always build a discounted cash flow that reflects the growth's duration, quality, and capital intensity.

### Assess Whether The Growth Is Sustainable

GARP depends on growth continuing. Unsustainable growth, paid for at a growth premium, leads to disappointment and multiple compression.

Test sustainability:

- is the growth driven by secular tailwinds (market expansion, adoption, demographics) or cyclical/one-time factors?
- is the company gaining share, or merely riding a rising market?
- what is the remaining runway, and at what rate will growth decelerate as the base grows?
- are margins and returns on capital stable or improving, suggesting a durable franchise?
- is management reinvesting at attractive incremental returns, or buying growth destructively?

Sustainable growth is structural and value-creating. Cyclical or acquisition-fueled growth that mean-reverts is a trap when priced as permanent.

### Distinguish Value-Creating From Value-Destroying Growth

Not all growth is worth paying for. Growth funded at returns below the cost of capital destroys shareholder value even as revenue rises.

Examine:

- the incremental return on invested capital for new investment;
- whether margins are expanding, stable, or compressing as the company grows;
- whether growth requires heavy capital expenditure or working capital build;
- whether share count is stable or diluting equity holders;
- the quality of earnings, including one-time items, stock-based compensation, and aggressive accounting.

GARP requires growth that is both sustainable and value-creating. Growth that burns capital or dilutes shareholders is not reasonably priced at any PEG.

### Evaluate The Reasonableness Of The Price Holistically

A reasonable price is not defined by PEG alone. It requires multiple valuation cross-checks and scenario analysis.

Apply:

- discounted cash flow with conservative growth and margin assumptions;
- relative valuation against peers and history, with adjustments for growth and quality differences;
- reverse DCF to infer the growth implied by the current price, and judge whether it is achievable;
- scenario analysis (bull, base, bear) with probability weighting;
- sensitivity to interest rates, since growth valuations are duration-sensitive.

The price is reasonable only if the base-case scenario delivers an acceptable return with downside protection in the bear case. A single metric cannot establish reasonableness.

### Stress-Test For Growth Disappointment And Multiple Compression

GARP's primary risk is that growth disappoints and the valuation multiple contracts simultaneously, producing a double loss.

Model:

- the return if growth comes in 20-30% below expectations and the multiple compresses;
- the interaction between deceleration and multiple, since growth stocks are re-rated on deceleration;
- whether the business can maintain margins and returns if growth slows;
- the behavioral risk of holding through the drawdown as the thesis is questioned.

A GARP position that survives a growth miss with limited downside is reasonably priced. One that collapses on any disappointment was speculative, not GARP.

### Match The Holding Period To The Growth Trajectory

GARP works best over multi-year horizons, because the growth must compound and the entry premium must be earned through.

Align:

- the holding period with the expected duration of above-average growth;
- the return expectation with the entry valuation and the compounding rate;
- the review cadence with checkpoints on growth sustainability and margin trajectory;
- the position size with conviction in growth durability and valuation cushion.

Frequent trading of GARP positions forfeits the compounding and incurs taxes. The strategy rewards patience and thesis-based review.

## Common Traps

### Using PEG Mechanically As A Complete Valuation

PEG normalizes for growth but ignores growth duration, quality, capital intensity, and cost of capital. It is a screen, not a valuation.

### Accepting Optimistic Growth Forecasts

Sell-side and management growth forecasts are often optimistic. Sustainability and value creation must be independently tested.

### Paying For Growth That Destroys Value

Growth below the cost of capital, or funded by dilution, destroys shareholder value. GARP requires value-creating growth.

### Ignoring Multiple Compression On Deceleration

Growth stocks are re-rated when growth slows. The combination of slower growth and a lower multiple can cause large losses.

### Confusing Cyclical Growth With Secular Growth

Cyclical or one-time growth priced as permanent is a classic GARP trap. Normalize through the cycle.

### Neglecting Earnings Quality

Stock-based compensation, aggressive revenue recognition, and one-time items can flatter growth and margins. Adjust for quality.

### Overpaying Because Growth Feels Safe

GARP investors may relax price discipline because the growth story is compelling. Reasonableness requires downside protection, not just a good narrative.

## Self-Check

- [ ] PEG is used as a screening and cross-check tool, not as a complete valuation, and is supplemented by DCF and relative valuation.
- [ ] Growth sustainability is tested for secular versus cyclical drivers, market runway, share dynamics, and margin and return stability.
- [ ] Growth is confirmed as value-creating (incremental returns above the cost of capital, stable or expanding margins, no excessive dilution).
- [ ] The price is evaluated as reasonable through multiple methods, including reverse DCF and scenario analysis, with downside protection in the bear case.
- [ ] The position is stress-tested for growth disappointment combined with multiple compression, and the downside is survivable.
- [ ] The holding period, return expectation, review cadence, and position size are aligned with the growth trajectory.
- [ ] Earnings quality is adjusted for stock-based compensation, one-time items, and aggressive accounting.
- [ ] The analysis flags that GARP can result in loss if growth disappoints, that multiples can compress, that growth forecasts are uncertain, and that professional advice may be warranted for concentrated growth holdings.
