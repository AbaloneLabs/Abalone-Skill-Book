# Rights Offering Analysis

A rights offering distributes to existing shareholders the right, but not the obligation, to subscribe to new shares at a discount to the current market price, typically to raise capital while giving current holders the chance to avoid dilution. Rights offerings appear in recapitalizations, bank capital raises, distressed refinancings, and spin-offs. They are mechanically simple but economically subtle: the theoretical ex-rights price, the subscription ratio, the discount, the tradability of the rights, and the presence of a standby underwriter all determine whether existing shareholders are made whole, diluted, or given an arbitrage opportunity. Investors who treat a rights notice as boilerplate, or who let rights lapse without analyzing exercise versus sale, give away value; investors who buy the rights without understanding the standby backstop and the issuer's capital needs take on risk they did not intend.

Use this skill when analyzing a rights offering, deciding whether to exercise or sell rights, assessing the dilution impact, or evaluating a standby underwriting arrangement. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating rights as a formality and from missing the ex-rights price, the subscription economics, the tradability, and the standby structure that determine shareholder outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Compute The Theoretical Ex-Rights Price And Dilution

A rights offering raises capital at a discount, and the new share price after the offering (ex-rights) reflects the blend of old and new shares:

- Subscription price: the price at which rights holders can buy new shares, set below the current market.
- Subscription ratio: how many rights are needed to subscribe to one new share.
- Theoretical ex-rights price (TERP): the weighted average of the old price and the new subscription price, accounting for the share count change; the price at which the stock should trade after the offering.

The TERP tells shareholders what their holding is worth after the offering if they do nothing. The discount to the current price is not free money; it is offset by the dilution from new shares unless the shareholder exercises or sells rights.

### Decide Between Exercise, Sale, And Lapse Based On Value

A rights holder has three choices, each with different outcomes:

- Exercise: subscribe to new shares at the subscription price, maintaining (or increasing) ownership percentage and avoiding dilution.
- Sell the rights: if the rights are tradable, sell them in the market, capturing their value and offsetting the dilution's economic effect.
- Lapse: do nothing; the rights expire worthless (if they have value) and the shareholder is diluted.

Letting valuable rights lapse is equivalent to giving away value. The economic difference between exercising and selling tradable rights is small if the market prices rights efficiently; the difference between either action and lapsing is the full value of the rights.

### Assess Whether Rights Are Tradable And The Market For Them

Tradability determines the shareholder's options:

- Tradable rights (nil-paid rights): can be bought and sold on the exchange during the subscription period, allowing shareholders who do not want to subscribe to capture the rights' value.
- Non-tradable rights: must be exercised or they lapse; shareholders who cannot or do not want to subscribe are diluted with no compensation.
- Oversubscription privilege: the right to subscribe to additional shares not taken up by other holders, allowing shareholders to increase their stake.

For non-tradable rights, the dilution cost to non-subscribing shareholders is unavoidable; for tradable rights, selling captures the value. Know which structure applies before deciding.

### Evaluate The Standby Underwriting And Backstop

Many rights offerings include a standby underwriter (backstop provider) who commits to purchase any unsubscribed shares:

- Backstop fee: the standby underwriter receives a fee for committing capital, paid by the issuer regardless of uptake.
- Standby commitment: if shareholders do not subscribe fully, the underwriter takes up the remaining shares at the subscription price.
- Implications: the backstop guarantees the capital raise but transfers risk to the underwriter, who may then sell the shares into the market, creating overhang.

The standby arrangement affects the offering's certainty and the post-offering share dynamics. An underwriter stuck with a large position may sell over time, pressuring the stock.

### Understand The Issuer's Capital Need And Use Of Proceeds

The reason for the rights offering shapes the risk:

- Recapitalization or debt reduction: strengthens the balance sheet; the dilution buys financial stability.
- Distressed refinancing: the issuer needs capital to survive; existing shareholders subscribe to avoid wipeout, but the business risk remains.
- Growth funding: the capital funds expansion; the dilution is justified if the growth generates returns above the cost of capital.
- Regulatory capital (banks): required to meet capital ratios; the offering is mandatory rather than opportunistic.

A rights offering to fund a failing business transfers additional risk to subscribers; one to fund profitable growth or strengthen a sound balance sheet is different. Assess the use of proceeds, not just the mechanics.

### Analyze The Effect On Existing Shareholders And Control

A rights offering, if fully taken up pro-rata, preserves existing shareholders' ownership percentages. But outcomes vary:

- Full pro-rata subscription: ownership maintained; economic effect is neutral aside from injecting more capital.
- Partial subscription or lapse: non-subscribing shareholders are diluted; ownership and voting power shift to subscribers and the standby underwriter.
- Oversubscription: shareholders who oversubscribe increase their stake at the expense of those who lapse.

For controlling shareholders, a rights offering can be a way to maintain control while raising capital, or, if they do not subscribe, to lose control. Assess the control implications alongside the economic ones.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Subscription Discount As Free Money

The discount is offset by dilution. The TERP, not the current price, is the relevant post-offering value.

### Letting Valuable Rights Lapse

Lapsing tradable or valuable rights gives away value equivalent to the dilution cost. Always evaluate exercise versus sale versus lapse.

### Ignoring Whether Rights Are Tradable

Non-tradable rights force a subscribe-or-be-diluted choice. Tradable rights allow capturing value by selling. The structure determines the optimal action.

### Overlooking The Standby Backstop And Resulting Overhang

A standby underwriter who takes up unsubscribed shares may sell them over time, pressuring the stock. The backstop guarantees capital but creates post-offering selling pressure.

### Assessing Mechanics Without The Use Of Proceeds

A rights offering to save a failing business is riskier than one to fund growth or strengthen a sound balance sheet. The capital need and use of proceeds drive the risk.

### Forgetting Control And Ownership Implications

Non-subscription dilutes ownership and voting power. For controlling holders, the offering can preserve or transfer control depending on participation.

## Self-Check

- Is the theoretical ex-rights price computed, recognizing that the subscription discount is offset by dilution?
- Is the choice between exercise, sale, and lapse analyzed based on the rights' value, with the cost of lapsing valuable rights made explicit?
- Is the tradability of the rights (nil-paid vs non-tradable) and the oversubscription privilege understood before deciding?
- Is the standby underwriting (backstop fee, commitment, and potential post-offering overhang) assessed for its effect on certainty and share dynamics?
- Is the issuer's capital need and use of proceeds evaluated, distinguishing recapitalization, distress, growth, and regulatory cases?
- Is the effect on existing shareholders' ownership and control analyzed, including dilution from non-subscription and shifts from oversubscription?
- Is the rights offering compared to alternative capital-raising methods (follow-on offering, convertible, debt) for cost and dilution?
- Does the conclusion guide the shareholder to exercise, sell, or lapse based on value and circumstances, rather than treating the rights notice as a formality?
