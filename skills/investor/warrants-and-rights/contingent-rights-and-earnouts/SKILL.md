# Contingent Rights And Earnouts

Not all rights pay off immediately or predictably. Contingent value rights (CVRs), earnouts, milestone payments, escrows, and holdbacks are contractual claims whose value depends on the occurrence of future events: a drug receiving regulatory approval, a business hitting revenue targets, a litigation resolving favorably, or a milestone being achieved within a deadline. These instruments bridge gaps in valuation between buyers and sellers, compensate for uncertainty, and allocate risk, but they are also where deals go wrong: milestones are missed or gamed, earnout disputes consume years of litigation, CVRs expire worthless despite real effort, and escrows release too little too late. Investors who value contingent rights at their face amount or headline probability, without modeling the milestone mechanics, the incentives of the party controlling the outcome, and the dispute risk, misprice them badly.

Use this skill when analyzing CVRs, earnouts, milestone payments, or escrow provisions in a deal, or when valuing a contingent claim whose payoff depends on future events. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating contingent rights as simple probability-weighted payoffs and from missing the control, incentive, measurement, dispute, and timing risks that determine whether the contingent value is ever realized.

## Core Rules

### Model The Contingency Realistically, Not At Headline Probability

The payoff of a contingent right depends on a specific event, and the probability of that event is usually uncertain and contested:

- Regulatory and clinical milestones (CVRs in pharma): probability of approval depends on trial results, FDA action, and timing; use informed probability estimates, not optimism.
- Commercial milestones and earnouts: revenue or EBITDA targets depend on business performance post-close, which the buyer controls.
- Litigation or regulatory resolution: outcomes are binary and timing-dependent; model a range of outcomes and delays.

Avoid valuing a CVR or earnout at a single "management's probability." Build a scenario-based model with base, upside, and failure cases, and stress the assumptions that drive the payoff.

### Identify Who Controls The Outcome And Their Incentives

The party that controls whether the milestone is achieved has enormous influence over the contingent right's value:

- Earnouts: the buyer controls the business post-close and can under-invest, divert resources, or restructure in ways that make targets harder to hit, even unintentionally. Sellers lose earnouts to buyer-controlled decisions.
- CVRs: the company (or a third party) controls the development effort; if they deprioritize the asset, the CVR expires worthless despite technical feasibility.
- Milestone payments: the party responsible for achieving the milestone may have incentives to delay or avoid it.

Assess the controller's incentives. A contingent right is worth less when the party that controls the outcome has little reason to pursue it. Structure the right to align incentives or to protect against misaligned ones.

### Scrutinize The Measurement And Definition Of Milestones

Earnouts and milestone disputes usually arise from ambiguous definitions:

- Revenue vs. gross profit vs. EBITDA: targets measured on revenue can be hit while profit collapses; definitions matter.
- Inclusions and exclusions: whether certain costs, intercompany transactions, or one-time items count toward the metric changes whether targets are met.
- Accounting policies: changes in accounting methods post-close can move metrics; specify the policies in the agreement.
- Carve-outs and related-party: exclude or include related-party transactions and non-recurring items explicitly.

Ambiguous measurement invites disputes and gaming. Read the definitions in the agreement, not the headline target, and recognize that the party controlling accounting has leverage to affect outcomes.

### Account For Timing, Caps, And Expiration

Contingent rights have time limits and caps that constrain value:

- Earnout periods: typically 1-5 years; longer periods increase uncertainty and expose the seller to prolonged buyer control.
- Caps: maximum payments limit upside even if the business vastly exceeds targets.
- Expiration: CVRs and milestone rights expire if the event does not occur by a deadline; a technically achievable milestone that misses the deadline pays nothing.
- Discounting: contingent payoffs in the future must be discounted for time value and risk.

A contingent right with a 3-year earnout, a cap, and a binary expiration is worth far less than the headline amount. Model the timing, cap, and expiration explicitly.

### Evaluate Escrow, Holdback, And Indemnity Structures

Escrows and holdbacks are contingent in reverse: they withhold a portion of purchase price pending the resolution of indemnity claims:

- Escrow amount and release schedule: how much is held back and when it releases to the seller if no claims arise.
- Indemnity triggers: the breaches (misrepresentations, undisclosed liabilities) that allow the buyer to claim against the escrow.
- Deductibles, baskets, and caps: the thresholds and limits on indemnity claims that affect how much the seller ultimately receives.

Escrows reduce the seller's immediate proceeds and expose them to post-close claims. Assess the escrow size, release terms, and indemnity scope to understand the true net proceeds and lingering risk.

### Assess Dispute Risk And Resolution Mechanisms

Contingent rights frequently lead to disputes, and the resolution mechanism affects value:

- Earnout disputes: buyers and sellers disagree on whether targets were met, on measurement, and on buyer conduct; litigation or arbitration can consume years.
- Dispute resolution clauses: specify arbitration, expert determination (e.g., an accounting firm resolving metric disputes), or litigation; the mechanism affects cost and speed.
- Good faith and best efforts covenants: require the buyer to use efforts to achieve milestones; enforceability varies.

A contingent right without a clear, efficient dispute mechanism is worth less because realizing its value may require costly litigation. Factor dispute risk into valuation and prefer structures with expert determination over open-ended litigation.

## Common Traps

### Valuing Contingent Rights At Headline Probability

Management's optimistic probability overstates value. Model scenarios with base, upside, and failure cases and stress the key assumptions.

### Ignoring Who Controls The Outcome

The party controlling the milestone has incentives that may not favor the contingent right holder. A right controlled by a misaligned party is worth less.

### Overlooking Ambiguous Milestone Definitions

Revenue, profit, and accounting definitions determine whether targets are met. Ambiguity invites disputes and gaming.

### Forgetting Caps, Expiration, And Discounting

Caps limit upside, expiration zeroes the right if the deadline is missed, and future payoffs must be discounted. Headline amounts overstate present value.

### Treating Escrows As Guaranteed Proceeds

Escrows are subject to indemnity claims and release schedules. The seller's net proceeds may be less than the headline price.

### Underestimating Dispute Risk

Earnout and milestone disputes are common and costly. Without a clear resolution mechanism, realizing value may require litigation.

### Assuming The Contingent Right Is Symmetric To Cash

A contingent right carries event risk, control risk, measurement risk, and dispute risk that cash does not. Discount accordingly.

## Self-Check

- Is the contingency modeled with scenario-based probabilities (base, upside, failure), not a single headline probability?
- Is the party controlling the outcome identified, and are their incentives assessed for alignment with the contingent right holder?
- Are the milestone definitions (revenue vs profit, inclusions, accounting policies, related-party) scrutinized for ambiguity and gaming risk?
- Are timing, caps, expiration, and discounting applied to compute present value, not headline amount?
- Are escrow and holdback structures assessed for size, release schedule, and indemnity triggers that affect net proceeds?
- Is dispute risk evaluated, with the resolution mechanism (arbitration, expert determination, litigation) factored into the value?
- Are best-efforts or good-faith covenants assessed for enforceability and protection against controller misalignment?
- Does the conclusion value the contingent right on its actual risk profile, including control, measurement, timing, and dispute risks, rather than as a simple probability-weighted payoff?
