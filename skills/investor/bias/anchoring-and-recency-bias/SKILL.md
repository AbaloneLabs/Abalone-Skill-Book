# Anchoring And Recency Bias

Anchoring is the tendency to rely too heavily on an initial reference point, such as a purchase price, a 52-week high, or an analyst target, when judging value. Recency bias is the tendency to overweight recent events, recent returns, and recent volatility when forecasting the future. In investing these biases distort valuation because the mind grabs the nearest or most memorable number and adjusts insufficiently from it, rather than deriving value from fundamentals. A stock looks cheap because it is down from its high, not because its cash flows justify the price; a strategy looks safe because the recent decade was calm, not because the underlying risk is low.

The harm this skill prevents is recommending a buy because a price has fallen from a memorable peak, treating a recent calm period as proof of low risk, or projecting recent returns indefinitely into the future. These biases are the engine behind buying falling knives, extrapolating bull markets, and underestimating tail risk after long quiet stretches. The agent's job is to identify the anchor and the recent window, test whether they reflect value or mere salience, and re-derive judgment from fundamentals and full-cycle evidence. Past performance does not guarantee future results, and recent returns are a particularly weak predictor of future ones.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Anchor Before Judging Value

Before concluding an asset is cheap or expensive, the agent should identify what reference point is anchoring the judgment. Common anchors include the purchase price, the all-time or 52-week high, the price a year ago, an analyst target, and the historical average multiple. Each of these is salient but none is inherently a measure of value. The agent should ask whether the valuation conclusion would change if the anchor were removed, and should re-derive fair value from fundamentals such as cash flows, growth, and risk, independent of where the price has been.

### Do Not Treat A Price Decline From A High As A Bargain

A stock down sharply from its peak is not necessarily cheap; it may simply be returning to fair value or below. The high itself may have been inflated by euphoria, and measuring from it anchors judgment to an arbitrary point. The agent should value the asset on its own fundamentals, not relative to a peak, and should recognize that buying because something is down is momentum reasoning dressed as value. The question is whether the current price is below intrinsic value, not whether it is below a memorable number.

### Re-Derive Valuation From Fundamentals, Not Reference Points

The antidote to anchoring is to build valuation from the ground up: estimate future cash flows, growth, margins, and an appropriate discount rate, and compare the result to the current price. This intrinsic exercise is independent of where the asset traded recently. The agent should prefer this approach over rules of thumb that reference historical multiples, because historical multiples are themselves anchors that may not reflect current fundamentals or interest rates.

### Use Full-Cycle Evidence, Not Just The Recent Window

Recency bias causes investors to forecast from the last several years, which may be unrepresentative of the full cycle. A strategy that worked in a low-volatility bull market may fail in a bear market; an asset class that looked safe after a calm decade may carry hidden risk. The agent should examine evidence across full market cycles, including bear markets and crises, and should explicitly ask what the worst recent-memory period looked like. Stress-testing against history that predates the current regime guards against extrapolating a favorable anomaly.

### Question Whether Recent Returns Predict Future Returns

Recent strong returns often reflect valuation expansion or a favorable cycle rather than durable skill, and valuation expansion cannot continue indefinitely. The agent should decompose recent returns into fundamentals, multiple change, and yield, and should be skeptical that past returns will repeat. Mean reversion in valuations and in factor returns means that the recent leader is often not the future leader. The agent should avoid recommending an asset primarily because it has performed well recently.

### Adjust Risk Assessment For Calm Periods

Volatility observed in a recent calm window understates the risk that appears in stress. The agent should use longer history and explicit stress scenarios to estimate downside, and should not report a low risk figure derived from a quiet period as if it were stable. Risk models fit on recent data fail exactly when they are needed most, because correlations and volatilities spike in crises. The agent should supplement recent-window risk with full-cycle and tail-scenario analysis.

### Use Pre-Commitment Rules To Resist Anchor-Driven Trades

Because anchors exert pull in real time, pre-commitment rules help. The agent should encourage setting buy and sell criteria based on fundamental value and position rules before reviewing current prices, so that the anchor does not silently set the threshold. A decision rule defined in advance is harder for a salient price to distort than a judgment made in the moment.

## Common Traps

### Buying Because A Price Is Down From Its High

A decline from a peak is not value. The agent should re-derive intrinsic worth, not measure from the high.

### Anchoring On The Purchase Price

The price paid is irrelevant to whether to hold or add. The agent should judge the position on current fundamentals and opportunity cost.

### Extrapolating Recent Returns Into The Future

Recent returns often reflect multiple expansion or cycle, not skill. The agent should decompose returns and discount repetition.

### Estimating Risk From A Calm Recent Window

Calm periods understate stress risk. The agent should use full-cycle history and tail scenarios.

### Using Historical Average Multiples As Fair Value

Historical averages are anchors that may not fit current fundamentals or rates. The agent should build valuation from the ground up.

### Recommending An Asset Because It Has Done Well Recently

Recency bias favors recent winners. The agent should check whether outperformance is sustainable or mean-reverting.

### Letting A Memorable Target Price Set Expectations

Analyst targets and round numbers are salient but arbitrary. The agent should derive price targets from valuation, not anchors.
