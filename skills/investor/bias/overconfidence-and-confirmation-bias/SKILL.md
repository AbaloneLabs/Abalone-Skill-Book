# Overconfidence And Confirmation Bias

Overconfidence is the systematic tendency to believe one's own judgments, forecasts, and skill are more accurate than they are. Confirmation bias is the tendency to seek, notice, and weight evidence that supports a prior belief while ignoring or discounting evidence that contradicts it. In investing these two biases compound: an investor forms a thesis, becomes more confident than the evidence warrants, then selectively gathers information that reinforces the thesis while filtering out the disconfirming data that would correct it. The result is positions held too long, sized too large, and abandoned too late.

The harm this skill prevents is presenting a confident-sounding investment conclusion built on one-sided evidence, failing to seek the strongest case against a favored idea, and treating a few confirming data points as proof while a mountain of disconfirming evidence is dismissed. These biases are the root of many blown-up positions and concentrated losses. The agent's job is not to eliminate confidence, which is impossible and not even desirable, but to discipline it against evidence and to actively hunt for the case that proves the thesis wrong. Past performance does not guarantee future results, and a track record of correct calls does not prove skill rather than luck.

## Core Rules

### Separate Skill From Outcome

A correct prediction does not prove skill, and an incorrect one does not prove incompetence, because markets involve large amounts of luck. The agent should evaluate decisions by the quality of the reasoning and the process at the time, not only by the outcome. A thesis that worked for the wrong reason teaches the wrong lesson, and a thesis that failed despite sound logic should not be discarded. Distinguishing skill from luck requires a large sample of decisions, which most investors never accumulate, so humility about a short track record is warranted.

### Actively Seek Disconfirming Evidence

The single most powerful countermeasure to confirmation bias is to deliberately search for the strongest case against the thesis. The agent should ask who holds the opposite view and why, what data would prove the thesis wrong, and what the bear case looks like in its most persuasive form. Reading only the bull case produces a confident but fragile thesis; steel-manning the bear case produces a more robust one. If the disconfirming case cannot be articulated, the research is incomplete.

### Pre-Define What Would Make The Thesis Wrong

A thesis without falsification criteria is not a thesis; it is a story that can explain any outcome. The agent should specify, before the position is taken, the evidence or price or fundamental outcome that would invalidate the thesis and trigger an exit. Without pre-defined falsification, every adverse development gets reinterpreted as temporary, and the position is held until the loss is large. Falsification criteria force intellectual honesty before emotions and sunk costs intervene.

### Calibrate Confidence Against Base Rates

Most forecasts are wrong more often than forecasters believe. Base rates, the historical frequency of outcomes for similar situations, provide a reality check on confidence. The agent should compare the thesis to how often similar theses have worked historically, and should discount confidence when the base rate is unfavorable. A thesis that depends on a rare outcome should be held with less conviction and smaller size than one aligned with a favorable base rate.

### Track And Review Predictions Honestly

Overconfidence is hard to see in real time but visible in a record. The agent should encourage maintaining a decision journal that records the thesis, the confidence level, the falsification criteria, and the outcome, and should review it periodically for calibration. Investors who never review their track record cannot learn whether their confidence is justified. Honest record-keeping is uncomfortable because it exposes errors, which is exactly why it is valuable.

### Size Positions According To Confidence Calibration

If confidence is systematically miscalibrated, position sizing should reflect that uncertainty. The agent should avoid maximum-size positions based on high subjective confidence, should diversify across independent theses to reduce reliance on any single judgment, and should size according to the strength of evidence and the consequences of being wrong, not the strength of conviction. Overconfident sizing is the mechanism by which a bad thesis becomes a catastrophic loss.

### Beware Of Narrative Coherence As A Substitute For Evidence

A compelling story feels like proof but is not. Markets are full of coherent narratives that explain the past and predict the future, most of which are constructed after the fact. The agent should distinguish a narrative that fits known facts from evidence that supports a forecast, and should discount stories that cannot be falsified. The most dangerous theses are the ones that feel obviously true.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Few Correct Calls As Proof Of Skill

A short winning streak is consistent with luck. The agent should not let recent success inflate confidence in future predictions.

### Reading Only Sources That Agree

Confirmation bias thrives on selective information gathering. The agent should actively read the opposing view.

### Failing To Define Falsification Criteria

A thesis that can explain anything protects nothing. The agent should specify what would prove it wrong before buying.

### Ignoring Base Rates In Favor Of A Unique Story

Every situation feels unique. The agent should check how often similar situations have ended favorably.

### Maximum Sizing On High Conviction

High subjective confidence is often miscalibrated. The agent should size for evidence strength and downside, not conviction.

### Reinterpreting Adverse News As Temporary

Without falsification criteria, every setback gets explained away. The agent should hold the pre-defined invalidation triggers.

### Trusting Narrative Coherence Over Evidence

A good story is not proof. The agent should separate explanatory narrative from predictive evidence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The investment conclusion evaluates reasoning quality, not only outcome, and acknowledges the role of luck.
- [ ] The strongest disconfirming case was actively sought and articulated, not just the bull case.
- [ ] Falsification criteria, the evidence that would prove the thesis wrong, are specified before the position is taken.
- [ ] Confidence is calibrated against base rates for similar situations.
- [ ] A decision journal or record of predictions and outcomes is encouraged for honest calibration review.
- [ ] Position sizing reflects evidence strength and downside, not subjective conviction alone.
- [ ] Narrative coherence is distinguished from predictive evidence.
- [ ] Opposing views and bear cases were read and weighed, not dismissed.
- [ ] The output acknowledges that past performance does not guarantee future results and that short track records are weak evidence of skill.
- [ ] No conclusion is presented as high-confidence without disconfirming evidence being addressed.
