# Risk Tolerance And Capacity

How much risk an investor should take depends on two different things that are constantly confused: risk capacity, the financial ability to bear loss without ruining goals, and risk tolerance, the psychological willingness to hold through losses. An investor can have high capacity but low tolerance (a wealthy investor who panics in a 20% drawdown) or low capacity but high tolerance (a near-retiree who is unfazed by volatility but cannot afford the loss). The binding constraint is the lower of the two, and agents routinely set allocation from only one, producing portfolios the investor abandons or that ruin goals.

This skill is for assessing the true risk an investor can and will bear before setting allocation.

## Core Rules

### Separate Capacity From Tolerance From Need

Three distinct questions, often conflated.

Define each:

- Risk capacity, the financial ability to absorb losses and still meet goals, driven by wealth, income, time horizon, obligations, and reserves.
- Risk tolerance, the psychological willingness to endure volatility and drawdown without abandoning the plan.
- Risk need, the return required to meet goals, which may demand more risk than capacity or tolerance allow.

The right risk level is bounded by capacity and tolerance, and it must also satisfy need. When need exceeds capacity or tolerance, the goals themselves must change, because taking more risk than the investor can bear does not solve the problem.

### Assess Capacity From The Full Financial Picture

Capacity is an objective financial question. Gather the whole balance sheet.

Assess:

- net worth and liquid assets;
- income stability, source, and human-capital value;
- time horizon to each goal and flexibility of that horizon;
- fixed obligations and spending needs;
- emergency reserves and insurance;
- existing concentrated exposures and liabilities;
- pension, social safety net, and guaranteed income.

A young high-earner with stable income and a long horizon has high capacity. A near-retiree dependent on the portfolio for spending has low capacity, regardless of how calm they feel.

### Assess Tolerance Honestly, Not Aspirationally

Tolerance is psychological and most investors overestimate it before a loss. Stated tolerance in a calm market is unreliable.

Probe:

- past behavior in real drawdowns, did they sell, freeze, or buy?
- the maximum loss they have actually experienced and held through;
- sleep, stress, and decision-making under loss;
- the source of their confidence (experience versus optimism);
- stated versus revealed tolerance, what they did, not what they say.

The best evidence of tolerance is behavior in a prior crash. Absent that, discount stated tolerance substantially. A questionnaire score is a starting point, not a verdict.

### Use The Binding Constraint

The investor can only bear the lower of capacity and tolerance. Set allocation from that, not from the higher.

Examples:

- high capacity, low tolerance: a wealthy investor who will panic in a 20% drawdown should hold less equity than capacity allows, because the behavioral cost of abandonment exceeds the return benefit.
- low capacity, high tolerance: a near-retiree who is unfazed by volatility still cannot afford a 50% drawdown, because the goal would fail.

Optimizing for the higher number produces portfolios that fail financially or behaviorally. Always bind by the lower.

### Account For Time Horizon And Goal Sensitivity

Capacity and tolerance change with the horizon and the goal.

Consider:

- short, fixed goals (a house down payment in two years) demand low risk regardless of tolerance;
- long, flexible goals (retirement in thirty years) allow more risk if tolerance permits;
- goals with little margin for error (funding essential retirement income) demand conservatism even with long horizons;
- sequence-of-returns risk in decumulation lowers effective capacity near and in retirement.

The same investor can rationally hold different risk levels across different goals and accounts.

### Reassess After Life Changes And Drawdowns

Risk capacity and tolerance are not fixed. They shift with circumstances and experience.

Reassess after:

- job loss, income change, or career shift;
- marriage, divorce, dependents, or inheritance;
- approaching a major withdrawal or retirement;
- a real drawdown that tests tolerance;
- changes in health, insurance, or obligations.

Crucially, a drawdown often lowers revealed tolerance. An investor who discovers they cannot hold their allocation through a crash should de-risk, not pretend the discovery did not happen.

### Connect Risk To A Survivable Drawdown

Translate risk level into a concrete drawdown the investor must be able to hold.

Estimate:

- the worst historical drawdown for the proposed allocation;
- the dollar loss that represents;
- the time to recover in past cycles;
- whether the investor can hold through that, both financially and psychologically.

A 70% equity allocation that could draw down 40% must be acceptable to the investor in both dollars and emotion. If it is not, the allocation is too aggressive, full stop.

## Common Traps

### Equating Tolerance With Capacity

A calm, risk-loving investor near retirement still has low capacity. Willingness to take risk does not create ability to absorb loss.

### Trusting Stated Tolerance In A Bull Market

Investors overstate tolerance when markets are calm. Revealed tolerance in a crash is far lower.

### Setting Allocation From Need Alone

Taking more risk than capacity or tolerance allow because the goals demand a high return does not make the goals achievable; it makes a blow-up more likely.

### Ignoring The Binding Constraint

Using the higher of capacity or tolerance produces portfolios that fail behaviorally or financially.

### Treating Risk As Fixed

Capacity and tolerance change with life events and market experience. A one-time assessment goes stale.

### Optimizing For Average Returns, Ignoring Path

A high-equity allocation may have a good average return but a drawdown path the investor cannot hold. Path, not average, determines whether the plan survives.

### Confusing Risk Tolerance With Risk Comprehension

An investor may tolerate risk in principle but not understand what a 40% drawdown feels like. Without a concrete dollar and time framing, tolerance is abstract.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Risk capacity, tolerance, and need are assessed separately, and the allocation is bound by the lower of capacity and tolerance.
- [ ] Capacity is assessed from the full financial picture, including income stability, horizon, obligations, reserves, and concentrated exposures.
- [ ] Tolerance is assessed from past behavior in real drawdowns, with stated tolerance discounted where behavior is unknown.
- [ ] Time horizon, goal flexibility, goal margin for error, and sequence risk are reflected in the risk level.
- [ ] A reassessment plan exists for life changes and post-drawdown revealed tolerance.
- [ ] The risk level is translated into a concrete dollar drawdown and recovery time the investor can hold.
- [ ] The recommendation does not push risk beyond the binding constraint to chase returns, and flags that capacity and tolerance are personal and that professional advice may be warranted for major allocation decisions.
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
