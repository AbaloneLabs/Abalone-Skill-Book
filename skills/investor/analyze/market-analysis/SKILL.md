# Market Analysis

Market analysis is not a prediction exercise. It is a discipline for separating what is happening, what the market appears to be pricing, what could change, and how much confidence the agent should place in any conclusion.

Use this skill before answering broad questions like:

- What should be considered when analyzing the market?
- Is this a good environment for buying stocks?
- What market conditions matter before entering a trade?
- How should a portfolio be positioned in the current regime?
- What should be checked before making an investment view from macro or sector data?

This skill should help the agent avoid narrative-driven, one-factor, or hindsight-biased market commentary.

## Core Rules

### Separate Observation, Interpretation, And Action

Do not collapse market facts into an investment conclusion.

Keep these layers separate:

- Observation: what data, price action, valuation, liquidity, or positioning shows.
- Interpretation: what those observations might mean.
- Market pricing: what may already be reflected in prices.
- Action implication: what, if anything, changes for allocation, risk, timing, or sizing.

Bad:

- "Rates are falling, so stocks should go up."
- "The economy is strong, so this is a good market."
- "AI demand is growing, so tech stocks are attractive."

Better:

- "Rates are falling. This may ease discount-rate pressure, but the market may already price part of that benefit. The implication depends on earnings revisions, valuation, and positioning."

### Identify The Market Level Being Analyzed

Be explicit about the level of analysis.

Possible levels:

- Global macro
- Country or region
- Asset class
- Sector or industry
- Factor or style
- Liquidity regime
- Credit cycle
- Currency or rates backdrop
- Volatility regime

Do not mix levels without saying so. A bullish sector view can still be wrong for a specific stock. A positive macro view can still fail if valuations are stretched. A weak economy can still coincide with rising markets if policy, liquidity, or expectations move faster than earnings.

### Ask What Is Already Priced In

Market analysis must include expectations, not only fundamentals.

A fact is not automatically useful if the market already knows it. The important question is often:

- Is the data better or worse than expectations?
- Are expectations rising or falling?
- Is the market priced for perfection, pessimism, or uncertainty?
- Is valuation already compensating for the risk?
- Is positioning crowded or under-owned?

Avoid treating widely known information as an edge.

### Separate Direction From Magnitude

It is not enough to say that a factor is positive or negative. Estimate whether it is large enough to matter.

Examples:

- A small decline in rates may not offset a large decline in earnings expectations.
- Strong revenue growth may not matter if valuation already assumes very high growth.
- A policy tailwind may not matter if implementation is slow or diluted.
- A sector rotation may not matter if the portfolio exposure is small.

Whenever possible, convert narrative into magnitude:

- impact on earnings
- impact on margins
- impact on multiples
- impact on default risk
- impact on liquidity
- impact on portfolio drawdown

### Use Multiple Lenses

A market view built from one indicator is fragile.

Check at least several of these:

- Growth: activity, demand, production, employment, sales, earnings
- Inflation: goods, services, wages, commodities, shelter, expectations
- Policy: central bank stance, fiscal impulse, regulation, liquidity provision
- Rates: nominal yields, real yields, curve shape, credit spreads
- Valuation: absolute, relative, historical, forward-looking, scenario-based
- Earnings: revisions, margins, breadth, cyclicality, operating leverage
- Liquidity: funding markets, market depth, issuance, redemptions, leverage
- Positioning: flows, sentiment, crowded trades, hedge fund exposure, retail participation
- Technicals: trend, breadth, volatility, dispersion, correlations
- Geopolitics: sanctions, conflict, trade policy, supply chain risk

The goal is not to mention every category. The goal is to prevent a single attractive story from dominating the analysis.

### Document the Basis and the Reasoning

Every conclusion should be traceable to its evidence, assumptions, and alternatives considered. Record not only the outcome but the reasoning path: what was checked, what was ruled out, what uncertainty remains, and what would change the conclusion. Documentation that captures the basis allows another practitioner to review, reproduce, or challenge the work, and it prevents confident conclusions from becoming unrepeatable assertions. A decision made without a recorded basis cannot be audited, improved, or safely handed off.

## Common Traps

### Treating Macro Commentary As Investment Analysis

A macro story is not automatically an investable view.

Common failure:

- "The economy is slowing, so sell equities."

Missing questions:

- Is the slowdown already priced?
- Are policy expectations changing?
- Are earnings estimates still too high?
- Which sectors are most exposed?
- Is the risk better expressed through equities, credit, duration, currency, or cash?

### Ignoring Time Horizon

The same data can imply different actions across horizons.

Examples:

- A weak labor print may be negative for near-term earnings but positive for rate-cut expectations.
- A valuation extreme may matter little over one month but matter greatly over five years.
- A liquidity shock may dominate fundamentals in the short term.

Always state the time horizon:

- days to weeks
- months
- one year
- multi-year

If the time horizon is unclear, the market view is usually too vague to be useful.

### Confusing Good Conditions With Good Entry Price

A strong market, strong sector, or strong company can still be a poor investment at the wrong price.

Check:

- What return is implied by the current valuation?
- What has to go right from here?
- What multiple, margin, or growth assumption is embedded?
- What happens if conditions normalize?

### Overweighting Recent Price Action

Recent market moves can inform analysis, but they should not become the entire analysis.

Avoid:

- assuming a rising market is safe
- assuming a falling market is cheap
- using price movement as proof of a thesis
- explaining every move after the fact with a convenient narrative

Use price action as evidence of changing expectations, liquidity, positioning, or risk appetite, not as a complete argument.

### Using Averages Without Regime Awareness

Historical averages can mislead when the regime has changed.

Check whether the current period differs in:

- inflation level
- interest-rate level
- policy reaction function
- leverage
- demographic trend
- market structure
- fiscal deficit
- globalization or trade regime
- sector composition

Do not compare today's valuation, margin, or yield to long-term averages without asking whether the base period is comparable.

### Missing Cross-Asset Confirmation

Market stories should be checked against related markets.

Examples:

- Equity optimism with widening credit spreads may signal inconsistency.
- Falling yields may reflect growth fear, not only easier discount rates.
- A strong currency may pressure exporters even if domestic equities look healthy.
- Commodity weakness may contradict a strong cyclical-growth story.

Cross-asset evidence does not need to agree perfectly, but conflicts should be named.

### Treating Correlation As Permanent

Correlations change by regime.

Examples:

- Stocks and bonds may diversify each other in low-inflation growth shocks.
- Stocks and bonds may fall together in inflation or rate-shock regimes.
- Defensive sectors may fail as defensives if valuation or positioning is crowded.

Do not rely on stale correlation assumptions without checking the current driver of risk.

## Analysis Structure

When producing a market analysis, use a structure like this:

1. Market scope
2. Current observations
3. Main drivers
4. What appears priced in
5. Bull case
6. Bear case
7. Key indicators to monitor
8. Portfolio or decision implications
9. Confidence level and what would change the view

### 1. Market Scope

State exactly what market is being analyzed.

Examples:

- US large-cap equities
- global developed-market bonds
- Korean technology exporters
- high-yield credit
- emerging-market currencies
- semiconductor capital equipment

### 2. Current Observations

List the important current facts without immediately turning them into a conclusion.

Possible observations:

- index level and recent performance
- valuation level
- earnings revisions
- rate movement
- credit spreads
- volatility
- sector breadth
- market concentration
- flows and positioning
- policy changes
- economic surprises

### 3. Main Drivers

Identify the few drivers that matter most.

Examples:

- discount rates
- earnings growth
- margin pressure
- liquidity
- policy support
- credit risk
- currency pressure
- commodity prices
- sector rotation
- geopolitical risk

Do not list ten drivers as if they matter equally. Rank them.

### 4. What Appears Priced In

State what the market seems to expect.

Useful prompts:

- What does valuation imply?
- What do earnings estimates imply?
- What do rates or spreads imply?
- What does positioning imply?
- What would surprise the market?

### 5. Bull Case

The bull case should include mechanism, not optimism.

Good bull case:

- inflation cools without a sharp earnings recession
- rates stabilize, allowing multiple pressure to ease
- earnings revisions broaden beyond a narrow group
- liquidity remains supportive

Weak bull case:

- "sentiment improves"
- "stocks keep going up"
- "the economy is resilient"

### 6. Bear Case

The bear case should be concrete.

Possible bear drivers:

- earnings estimates are too high
- margins normalize lower
- valuation multiple compresses
- credit spreads widen
- inflation remains sticky
- policy is tighter than expected
- positioning unwinds
- liquidity deteriorates

The bear case should explain what would be observed if it starts playing out.

### 7. Indicators To Monitor

Name what would update the view.

Examples:

- earnings revision breadth
- real yield trend
- credit spread widening
- market breadth
- unemployment claims
- inflation surprise
- default rates
- currency movement
- fund flows
- volatility term structure

Avoid saying "monitor macro data" without naming what matters.

### 8. Portfolio Or Decision Implications

Translate analysis into implications, but do not overstate certainty.

Possible implications:

- increase or reduce risk exposure
- prefer quality over cyclicals
- prefer duration or cash
- avoid crowded trades
- rebalance concentrated winners
- wait for better risk/reward
- hedge currency or rate exposure
- size positions smaller due to uncertainty

Market analysis should not always end in action. Sometimes the correct implication is "no trade" or "watch these indicators."

### 9. Confidence And Invalidation

State confidence level and what would change the view.

Examples:

- Low confidence because signals conflict across rates, credit, and equities.
- Medium confidence because valuation and earnings revisions point in the same direction.
- High confidence only when data, pricing, positioning, and risk/reward are aligned.

Name invalidation conditions:

- credit spreads break higher
- earnings revisions turn negative
- inflation reaccelerates
- market breadth deteriorates
- policy support fails to appear
- valuation reaches a level that removes margin of safety

## Special Cases

### When The Prompt Is Very Broad

For prompts like "what should be considered when analyzing the market," avoid pretending there is one answer.

Return a framework:

- scope
- drivers
- valuation
- expectations
- scenarios
- risk
- indicators
- decision implications

### When The Prompt Is About Timing

Timing requires extra caution.

Check:

- whether the question is about short-term entry or long-term allocation
- whether the signal is valuation, momentum, liquidity, or macro
- whether the risk of waiting is higher or lower than the risk of entering
- whether position sizing can solve timing uncertainty

Avoid making a precise timing claim from broad market commentary.

### When The Prompt Is About A Specific Sector

Sector analysis should include both market-level and sector-specific drivers.

Examples:

- demand cycle
- pricing power
- supply growth
- margin structure
- regulation
- capex cycle
- inventory cycle
- currency exposure
- valuation relative to history and market

Do not assume sector strength means every company in the sector is attractive.

### When The Prompt Is About Portfolio Positioning

Market analysis for positioning should include exposure and correlation.

Check:

- current portfolio concentration
- factor exposures
- asset-class diversification
- liquidity needs
- drawdown tolerance
- rebalancing rules
- tax or transaction cost constraints, if relevant

## Self-Check

- [ ] The market scope is explicit.
- [ ] Observation, interpretation, pricing, and action implication are separated.
- [ ] The analysis states what appears to be priced in.
- [ ] Bull case and bear case are both concrete.
- [ ] The time horizon is explicit.
- [ ] The main drivers are ranked, not merely listed.
- [ ] Valuation or risk/reward is considered, not only narrative direction.
- [ ] Cross-asset or related-market evidence is checked when relevant.
- [ ] The analysis names indicators that would update or invalidate the view.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids false precision and states confidence level.
