# Relative Valuation Traps

Relative valuation, valuing a company by comparing its multiples to those of peers, is the most widely used valuation method because it is fast, intuitive, and requires fewer assumptions than a discounted cash flow model. It is also one of the most misleading methods when used carelessly, because every step contains a trap that can flip the conclusion. The judgment problem is that a multiple is not a fact but a ratio of price to a denominator that can be defined many ways, and the comparison group, the adjustments, and the interpretation all carry assumptions that are rarely examined. A stock that looks cheap on a multiple may be cheap because the market has priced in deterioration the analyst has not, or because the peer group is mischosen, or because the denominator is inflated by accounting choices. A stock that looks expensive may be fairly priced for superior growth that the static multiple cannot capture. The skill is using relative valuation with awareness of its traps, choosing peers and denominators deliberately, adjusting for real differences, and never treating a multiple comparison as conclusive without understanding why the spread exists.

The harm this skill prevents is the value trap and the growth mirage that naive relative valuation produces. Investors buy stocks that screen cheap on price-to-earnings or price-to-book, only to discover the cheapness reflected secular decline, accounting distortion, or a cyclical peak in earnings. Investors avoid stocks that screen expensive, missing durable compounders whose high multiples were justified by growth and returns that persisted for years. Both errors stem from treating the multiple as the answer rather than as a question: why does this spread exist, and is the market right or wrong about the reason? The agent must help the investor choose appropriate peers and denominators, adjust for genuine differences, and interpret the spread with skepticism about both the market and the analyst's own assumptions.

Use this skill when valuing a company with multiples, when screening for cheap or expensive stocks, when selecting peer groups, when adjusting multiples for growth or risk, or when diagnosing why a relative valuation conclusion may be wrong. The goal is to prevent the agent from presenting a multiple comparison as conclusive and to ensure the traps of relative valuation are examined before acting on the conclusion.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Peer Group Deliberately, Not By Sector Label

The peer group determines what cheap or expensive means, and it is the most consequential and most abused choice in relative valuation. A company screened against the wrong peers will look cheap or expensive for reasons that have nothing to do with its value. The peer group must be chosen by comparable economics, not by sector label or index membership.

Choose peers by:

- matching business model, revenue model, and value drivers, not just industry classification;
- matching growth rate, margin structure, and return on capital, since these drive multiples;
- including direct competitors, close substitutes, and companies with similar economics;
- excluding companies with fundamentally different models even if they share a sector;
- recognizing that the "right" peer group may be small, which limits the method's power.

A software company compared to a mix of software and IT services will look expensive because services carry lower multiples; compared to pure software peers, it may be average. The peer group is the foundation, and a weak foundation invalidates everything above it.

### Match The Multiple To The Value Driver

Different multiples capture different value drivers, and using the wrong multiple produces misleading conclusions. Price-to-earnings captures earnings power, price-to-book captures asset value, enterprise-value-to-EBITDA captures operating cash generation, and price-to-sales captures revenue with no profitability adjustment. The choice must match how the business creates value.

Match by:

- using earnings or cash-flow multiples for profitable, mature businesses;
- using revenue multiples only when profitability is temporarily depressed or emerging, with caution;
- using asset-based multiples for financials and asset-heavy businesses where book value drives returns;
- using enterprise-value multiples to neutralize capital structure differences;
- avoiding multiples based on denominators that are negative, distorted, or non-recurring.

A common error is using price-to-earnings for a company with temporarily depressed or negative earnings, which makes it look expensive or unmeasurable when revenue or forward earnings multiples would be more informative. Match the multiple to the economics.

### Adjust For Growth, Risk, And Return Differences

A static multiple comparison assumes peers are identical in growth, risk, and returns, which they never are. A company with superior growth, lower risk, or higher returns on capital deserves a higher multiple, and failing to adjust makes superior companies look expensive and inferior ones look cheap.

Adjust by:

- using forward multiples that incorporate expected growth, not just trailing;
- applying PEG or similar growth adjustments where growth differences are material;
- adjusting for risk differences, since higher-risk businesses warrant lower multiples;
- accounting for return on capital, since high-return businesses deserve higher multiples;
- normalizing the denominator for cyclical or one-time effects before comparing.

A company trading at twenty times earnings with fifteen percent growth is not necessarily more expensive than one at twelve times with two percent growth. The multiple must be interpreted against the differences that justify spreads.

### Investigate Why The Spread Exists

The most important question in relative valuation is not what the multiple is, but why it differs from peers. A spread exists for a reason, and the reason may be that the market is wrong, which is the opportunity, or that the market sees something the analyst has not, which is the trap. Assuming the spread is an opportunity without understanding it is the core relative-valuation error.

Investigate by asking:

- is the company cheaper because of deterioration the market has priced in;
- is the denominator inflated by cyclical peaks, one-time gains, or aggressive accounting;
- is the company more expensive because of superior growth, returns, or durability;
- what would have to be true for the spread to close, and is that plausible;
- what does the market know that the analyst's model may have missed.

A cheap multiple is an opportunity only if the reason for the cheapness is wrong. Most of the time, the market's reason is better than the analyst's, and the cheap stock is a value trap.

### Beware The Cyclical Denominator Trap

For cyclical businesses, the denominator of a multiple swings with the cycle, producing multiples that give exactly the wrong signal. At cyclical peaks, earnings are high and multiples look cheap, signaling a buy at the worst time. At cyclical troughs, earnings are depressed and multiples look expensive, signaling a sell at the best time.

Handle cyclicality by:

- normalizing earnings across the cycle rather than using peak or trough denominators;
- using mid-cycle or through-cycle multiples rather than point-in-time;
- recognizing that low multiples in cyclical sectors often reflect peak earnings, not cheapness;
- understanding that high multiples in cyclicals may reflect trough earnings, not expensiveness;
- cross-checking with enterprise-value-to-through-cycle-EBITDA or asset-based measures.

The classic value trap is buying a cyclical at a low price-to-earnings at the cycle peak. The denominator collapses as earnings revert, and the "cheap" stock gets cheaper.

### Normalize The Denominator For Accounting And One-Time Effects

The denominator of a multiple is only as reliable as the earnings or cash flow figure behind it, and reported figures are often distorted by accounting choices, one-time items, and non-recurring effects. Comparing multiples on unadjusted denominators imports those distortions into the conclusion.

Normalize by:

- removing genuine one-time gains and losses from earnings;
- retaining genuinely recurring costs that management labels one-time, like frequent restructuring;
- adjusting for stock-based compensation, which is a real cost often excluded from adjusted figures;
- using consistent accounting across peers, adjusting for differences in capitalization and recognition;
- preferring cash-flow-based denominators like EBITDA or free cash flow where earnings are noisy.

An adjusted EBITDA that excludes stock-based compensation overstates the denominator and makes the multiple look cheaper than it is. Rebuild the denominator before trusting the multiple.

### Use Relative Valuation As A Cross-Check, Not A Standalone

Relative valuation is most powerful as a cross-check against intrinsic valuation, not as a standalone method. Multiples tell you what the market is implying; intrinsic valuation tells you what the business is worth. Comparing the two reveals whether the market's implied assumptions are reasonable.

Use as a cross-check by:

- deriving the implied growth or return assumptions in the current multiple and testing their plausibility;
- comparing the relative valuation conclusion to a DCF or intrinsic valuation;
- using relative valuation to sanity-check, not to replace, fundamental analysis;
- recognizing that relative valuation can only tell you cheap or expensive relative to peers, not absolutely;
- being especially skeptical when relative and intrinsic valuation disagree, and understanding why.

A stock can be cheap relative to peers and still overvalued absolutely, if the whole peer group is expensive. Relative valuation without an intrinsic anchor can lead into overvalued sectors and asset bubbles.

## Common Traps

### Wrong Peer Group

Comparing to companies with different economics, making the target look cheap or expensive for irrelevant reasons. The trap is sector-label peers instead of comparable-economics peers.

### Mismatched Multiple

Using price-to-earnings for a cyclical at peak earnings, or price-to-sales for a profitable company, producing misleading signals. The trap is a multiple that does not match the value driver.

### Unadjusted Denominator

Comparing multiples on reported or management-adjusted earnings without normalizing for one-timers, stock-based compensation, or accounting differences. The trap is importing distortion into the comparison.

### Ignoring Growth And Risk Differences

Treating a static multiple comparison as conclusive without adjusting for superior growth or higher risk. The trap is calling a superior grower expensive or a declining business cheap.

### The Cyclical Value Trap

Buying cyclical stocks at low multiples that reflect peak earnings. The trap is cheapness that evaporates as earnings revert.

### Assuming The Spread Is An Opportunity

Concluding a cheap multiple means the market is wrong, without investigating why the spread exists. The trap is acting on the spread without understanding it.

### Relative Without Intrinsic Anchor

Concluding a stock is cheap relative to peers without checking whether the peers themselves are overvalued. The trap is buying into an overvalued group.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The peer group was chosen by comparable economics, growth, and returns, not by sector label or index membership.
- [ ] The multiple was matched to the business's value driver, with the denominator appropriate to the economics.
- [ ] Multiples were adjusted for growth, risk, and return-on-capital differences among peers.
- [ ] The reason for any spread to peers was investigated, not assumed to be an opportunity.
- [ ] Cyclical denominators were normalized across the cycle rather than taken at peak or trough.
- [ ] The denominator was rebuilt to remove distortions, including one-timers, recurring restructuring, and stock-based compensation.
- [ ] Relative valuation was used as a cross-check against intrinsic valuation, not as a standalone.
- [ ] Implied growth and return assumptions in the multiple were extracted and tested for plausibility.
- [ ] No conclusion of cheapness was reached without checking whether the peer group itself is overvalued.
- [ ] The recommendation states that multiples reflect market sentiment that can be wrong, that relative valuation has inherent limitations, and that this is not investment advice and professional valuation expertise may be warranted for material decisions.
