# Sum-Of-Parts Valuation

Sum-of-the-parts (SOTP) valuation values a multi-business company by appraising each segment independently with the method and multiple appropriate to that business, then aggregating and adjusting for corporate-level items. The logic is compelling: a conglomerate containing a fast-growing software unit, a mature industrial unit, and real estate should not be valued on a single blended multiple that fits none of them. The judgment problem is that SOTP is precise in appearance and fragile in practice. Each segment valuation carries its own assumptions and errors, the aggregation requires judgment about corporate overhead, cross-holdings, debt, and tax, and the market often prices the whole at a persistent discount to the parts that may never close. Agents tend to build an elaborate SOTP, declare the stock undervalued, and ignore that the discount may be rational and permanent. The skill is building a SOTP that is internally consistent and honestly assessing whether the gap to market price is real and closable.

This skill is for valuing a company by its parts with awareness of aggregation traps and the conglomerate discount.

## Core Rules

### Value Each Segment With The Method Appropriate To That Business

The core discipline of SOTP is matching the valuation method to each segment's economics. Applying one blended multiple to unlike businesses defeats the purpose.

For each segment, choose:

- a DCF for segments with predictable cash flows and distinct growth;
- a relative multiple (EV/EBITDA, P/E, EV/sales) benchmarked to pure-play peers for that business;
- an asset-based or real-estate appraisal for property or capital-heavy units;
- a separate treatment for financial-services segments (often price-to-book or excess return);
- a distinct discount rate reflecting each segment's risk, not a single weighted-average cost of capital applied uniformly.

A software unit deserves a growth multiple; a cyclical industrial unit deserves a through-cycle multiple; real estate deserves an appraisal. The method must fit the business, or the SOTP is just a more elaborate guess.

### Use Pure-Play Peers And Adjust For Differences

Relative multiples in SOTP are only as good as the peer set. The ideal is a pure-play public company whose only business matches the segment. Pure plays are rare, and adjustments are necessary.

Address:

- the availability and quality of pure-play peers (often thin or nonexistent);
- differences in growth, margin, scale, and risk between the segment and its peers;
- cyclicality and where the peer multiples sit in their own cycle;
- private-market or transaction multiples where public peers are absent, with appropriate illiquidity and control discounts.

Do not apply a sector average multiple blindly. A segment growing half as fast as its peer group does not deserve the peer multiple. Adjust for the differences, and widen the value range where peers are weak.

### Apply A Segment-Specific Discount Rate

Each segment has a different risk profile, and discounting all of them at the company's blended WACC distorts the result. A stable utility-like segment should be discounted at a lower rate than a volatile early-stage unit.

Set rates by:

- the segment's own beta and business risk, not the parent's blended cost of capital;
- the segment's capital structure where it is separately financed;
- the geographic and currency risk of the segment;
- consistency between the cash flow currency and the discount rate.

Using one WACC for everything overvalues the risky segments and undervalues the stable ones, producing a SOTP that looks precise but is internally inconsistent.

### Handle Corporate-Level Items Explicitly

The aggregation step is where most SOTP errors occur. Segment values must be combined with corporate-level adjustments to reach equity value.

Adjust for:

- unallocated corporate overhead and its present value as a drag (often material and growing);
- consolidated debt and lease obligations, allocated sensibly or subtracted in total;
- cash, investments, and marketable securities, added at fair value;
- minority interests and cross-holdings, added or subtracted as appropriate;
- pension and off-balance-sheet liabilities;
- the tax effects of separating the businesses (tax leakage on a spin-off or sale can be large).

A SOTP that sums segment equity values and ignores debt, overhead, and minority interests is not a valuation; it is an arithmetic error. The bridge from segment enterprise values to consolidated equity value must be complete.

### Decide On A Conglomerate Discount Honestly

The market frequently prices diversified companies below the sum of their parts. The question is whether this discount is an inefficiency (an opportunity) or rational (a permanent feature). Most agents assume the former; the truth is often the latter.

Consider why a discount may be rational and persistent:

- the complexity and opacity of analyzing many businesses deters investors and lowers demand;
- corporate overhead and capital misallocation destroy value relative to focused peers;
- the inability of investors to invest in the segment they want (they must buy the whole);
- governance and agency risks of a sprawling conglomerate;
- the tax and friction costs of actually separating the businesses.

A discount is only an opportunity if a credible catalyst (spin-off, breakup, activist, sale) can close it. Without a catalyst, the discount can persist indefinitely, and the "undervaluation" is a trap.

### Require A Catalyst To Realize The Value

SOTP value is theoretical until realized. The market must be induced to re-rate the stock, which usually requires an event.

Identify:

- a planned or plausible spin-off, split-off, or sale of a segment;
- activist pressure or management change toward simplification;
- a divestiture program or portfolio review;
- improved disclosure that lets the market see the parts clearly.

Without a catalyst, the gap between SOTP value and market price can remain for years. Value investors who buy conglomerates "below SOTP" without a catalyst often wait a very long time.

### Stress-Test The Aggregation And Show The Range

Because each segment valuation carries error, the aggregated SOTP carries compounded error. Present it as a range with sensitivities, not a point estimate.

Show:

- a bear, base, and bull case per segment, aggregated;
- sensitivity to the key assumptions (growth, margin, multiple, discount rate);
- the share of total value from each segment, to show concentration of assumption risk;
- the implied conglomerate discount at each case and versus the market price.

A SOTP presented as a single precise number overstates confidence. The honest output is a range plus a view on whether the gap is closable.

## Common Traps

### Applying One Blended Multiple To Unlike Segments

A single multiple across a conglomerate fits none of the businesses. Each segment needs its own method and peer set.

### Using Weak Or Mismatched Peers

Sector averages or distant comparables distort segment value. Pure plays adjusted for differences, or private-market multiples, are required.

### Discounting All Segments At The Parent WACC

A blended rate misvalues risky and stable segments alike. Each segment needs its own risk-adjusted discount rate.

### Ignoring Corporate Overhead, Debt, And Cross-Holdings

Summing segment values without the corporate bridge produces a meaningless number. Overhead, debt, minorities, and tax leakage must be handled.

### Assuming The Conglomerate Discount Is Always An Opportunity

The discount is often rational and persistent, reflecting complexity, overhead, and lack of focus. Without a catalyst it may never close.

### Buying Below SOTP Without A Catalyst

Theoretical undervaluation without an event to realize it can persist indefinitely. A catalyst is usually required.

### False Precision In The Aggregation

Compounding the errors of multiple segment valuations produces wide uncertainty. A point estimate overstates confidence; show a range.

### Forgetting Tax Leakage On Separation

Actually breaking up a company triggers tax on gains, transaction costs, and lost synergies. Net realization is below gross SOTP.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each segment is valued with the method and multiple appropriate to its own economics, not a single blended approach.
- [ ] Peer sets are pure-play where possible, adjusted for differences in growth, margin, scale, and cycle position, with private-market multiples used where public peers are absent.
- [ ] Segment-specific discount rates reflect each unit's risk, capital structure, and currency, rather than a uniform parent WACC.
- [ ] Corporate overhead, debt, cash, minority interests, cross-holdings, pensions, and tax effects are handled explicitly in the bridge from segment enterprise values to consolidated equity value.
- [ ] The conglomerate discount is assessed honestly, including the rational reasons it may persist (complexity, overhead, governance, separation costs), not assumed to be a pure inefficiency.
- [ ] A credible catalyst (spin-off, sale, activist, improved disclosure) is identified as the mechanism to close any gap to market price.
- [ ] The SOTP is presented as a range with sensitivities and per-segment value share, not a single point estimate.
- [ ] The recommendation states that SOTP is a framework dependent on many assumptions, that segment valuations carry compounded error, that discounts may persist without a catalyst, and that this is not investment advice and professional valuation expertise may be warranted for complex multi-business companies.
