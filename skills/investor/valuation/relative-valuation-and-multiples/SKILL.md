# Relative Valuation And Multiples

Relative valuation values a company by comparing it to similar companies, its own history, or the market, using multiples like price-to-earnings, EV-to-EBITDA, or price-to-book. It is the most widely used valuation approach because it is quick and intuitive. It is also the most abused. An investing agent often declares a stock "cheap" because its P/E is below the sector average, or "expensive" because it is above, without asking whether the multiples are comparable, whether the differences reflect real differences in growth, risk, and quality, or whether the peer set itself is fairly valued. A multiple is a summary of many assumptions; comparing two multiples without understanding what drives them is not analysis.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "is this stock cheap or expensive", "how does its valuation compare to peers", "what multiple should I use", or "is the sector overvalued". The goal is to prevent the agent from mechanically comparing headline multiples and to force it to choose the right multiple for the business, ensure comparability, interpret differences through growth, risk, and quality, and anchor against absolute valuation, not just relative.

Relative valuation is a comparison tool, not an intrinsic-value measure. A cheap stock can get cheaper, and a cheap sector can be cheap for good reason. Conclusions should disclose limitations and investor-specific context.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Right Multiple For The Business

Different multiples suit different businesses. Using the wrong multiple produces misleading conclusions.

Match the multiple:

- P/E suits stable, profitable companies with consistent earnings.
- EV/EBITDA suits capital-intensive or leveraged businesses and removes capital-structure and tax differences.
- P/S suits early-stage or unprofitable companies where sales growth is the relevant metric, but ignores profitability.
- P/B suits asset-heavy businesses like banks and insurers where book value is meaningful.
- EV/FCF or EV/EBIT suits cash-generative businesses and adjusts for capital intensity.
-PEG ratios adjust P/E for growth but embed growth assumptions that may be wrong.

A multiple that uses a denominator distorted by cyclicality, one-time items, or accounting choices misleads. Pick the multiple whose denominator best reflects normalized economic earnings for that business.

### Ensure Comparability Before Concluding

Two multiples are only comparable if the companies are genuinely similar. Differences in growth, risk, quality, accounting, and capital structure explain legitimate multiple gaps.

Check:

- Are the peers in the same business with similar growth, margins, and cyclicality?
- Are accounting policies, revenue recognition, and capitalization consistent?
- Is the capital structure comparable, or does leverage distort equity-based multiples?
- Are the denominators normalized, or distorted by one-time items, cyclicality, or write-downs?
- Is the peer set itself fairly valued, or is the whole sector mispriced?

A stock can look cheap relative to overvalued peers and still be expensive in absolute terms. Always question whether the comparison set is a valid benchmark.

### Interpret Multiple Differences Through Fundamentals

A higher or lower multiple is not automatically a buy or sell signal. The difference often reflects real differences in fundamentals.

Explain the gap:

- Higher growth justifies a higher multiple; lower growth justifies a lower one.
- Higher quality, stability, and returns on capital justify a premium.
- Lower risk, leverage, and cyclicality support a higher multiple.
- Stronger competitive position and moat warrant a richer valuation.
- Differences in capital intensity, working capital, and tax rates affect the right multiple.

If a stock trades at a discount and the discount is explained by weaker growth, lower quality, or higher risk, it may be fairly valued, not cheap. If the discount is unexplained, it may be an opportunity, or it may reflect a risk the market sees that the analysis missed.

### Anchor Relative Valuation Against Absolute Measures

Relative valuation only tells you how a stock compares to others, not whether it is cheap in absolute terms. A whole sector or market can be overvalued, making relative cheapness meaningless.

Combine:

- Compare the multiple to the company's own long-run history to assess where it sits in its range.
- Cross-check with absolute measures like DCF, dividend yield, or free cash flow yield.
- Assess whether the peer set and the market are themselves expensive or cheap by historical standards.
- Use the equity risk premium and interest-rate environment to contextualize multiple levels.

A stock cheap relative to peers in an overvalued market may still offer poor returns. Relative and absolute valuation answer different questions; use both.

### Normalize The Denominator For Cyclicality And One-Time Items

Multiples based on peak or trough earnings are misleading. Normalize the denominator.

Adjust:

- Use normalized, mid-cycle earnings rather than peak-cycle profits for cyclical businesses.
- Strip out one-time gains and losses from the denominator.
- For commodity or financial businesses, average earnings over a cycle.
- Adjust for stock-based compensation and other non-cash distortions where relevant.
- Use forward estimates cautiously, since they embed analyst optimism or pessimism.

A low P/E on peak earnings often signals a cyclical top, not a bargain. A high P/E on trough earnings can signal a cyclical bottom, not overvaluation.

### Use Multiple Multiples To Triangulate

No single multiple captures all dimensions of value. Use several to build a fuller picture.

Triangulate:

- P/E for earnings-based value.
- EV/EBITDA for capital-structure-neutral operating value.
- EV/FCF for cash-generation-based value.
- P/B or P/S as cross-checks for specific business types.
- Growth-adjusted measures like PEG, while acknowledging their embedded assumptions.

When several multiples point the same way, the conclusion is more robust. When they conflict, the conflict itself is informative and should be explained.

### Remember That Relative Valuation Can Perpetuate Mispricing

If everyone values by comparison to peers, and peers are mispriced, the mispricing propagates. Relative valuation can justify any price in a bubble.

Guard against:

- Using relative valuation to justify extreme prices during manias.
- Assuming the peer set is correctly priced when it may be in a bubble.
- Circular reasoning where expensive peers justify expensive targets.

Always ask whether the comparison set is reasonable, not just whether the target is cheaper than it.

## Common Traps

### Using The Wrong Multiple For The Business

A multiple whose denominator does not reflect the business's economics misleads. Match the multiple to the business model.

### Comparing Non-Comparable Companies

Growth, risk, quality, accounting, and capital-structure differences explain legitimate multiple gaps. Ensure comparability before concluding.

### Declaring Cheapness From A Low Multiple Without Explaining The Gap

A low multiple often reflects weak growth, low quality, or high risk. It is only a bargain if the gap is unexplained by fundamentals.

### Using Peak Or Trough Earnings In The Denominator

Cyclical and one-time distortions make multiples misleading. Normalize the denominator to mid-cycle earnings.

### Anchoring Only On Relative Valuation

A stock cheap relative to overvalued peers can still be expensive in absolute terms. Combine relative with absolute measures.

### Justifying Bubble Prices With Relative Valuation

In a mania, expensive peers justify expensive targets, perpetuating the mispricing. Question whether the peer set is fairly valued.

### Ignoring Growth And Quality Adjustments

Failing to adjust for growth and quality differences makes every premium look expensive and every discount look cheap. Adjust before concluding.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The multiple is chosen to match the business model and its denominator reflects normalized economic earnings.
- [ ] Comparability is verified across growth, risk, quality, accounting, and capital structure before concluding.
- [ ] Multiple differences are interpreted through fundamental drivers, not taken at face value.
- [ ] Relative valuation is anchored against absolute measures like DCF, FCF yield, and historical ranges.
- [ ] The denominator is normalized for cyclicality, one-time items, and non-cash distortions.
- [ ] Multiple multiples are used to triangulate, and conflicts are explained.
- [ ] The analysis questions whether the peer set and market are fairly valued, not just whether the target is cheaper.
- [ ] Growth and quality adjustments are made before judging a premium or discount.
- [ ] Relative valuation is not used to justify extreme or bubble prices.
- [ ] The conclusion discloses that relative valuation is a comparison tool, not an intrinsic-value measure, and flags investor context and professional advice.
