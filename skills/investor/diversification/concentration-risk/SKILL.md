# Concentration Risk

Concentration risk is the risk that a single exposure, or a set of exposures driven by one factor, can cause loss large enough to derail the portfolio. It is the most common source of catastrophic, permanent loss in personal investing, and it is also the risk agents most often underweight because concentration frequently arises from good fortune (a rising employer stock, a successful business, a hot sector). The judgment problem is that concentration feels like reward until it becomes ruin, and the moment of ruin is usually impossible to predict.

This skill is for identifying, measuring, and deciding about concentration before it forces a distressed sale.

## Core Rules

### Measure Concentration On Multiple Dimensions

Concentration is not only "too much of one stock." It hides in sectors, geographies, factors, currencies, issuers, managers, and time.

Measure across:

- single issuer or position;
- sector and industry;
- country and region;
- risk factor (growth, value, momentum, quality, duration, credit);
- currency;
- issuer or counterparty (including fund managers and platforms);
- liquidity and time (everything maturing or tradable at once);
- correlated exposures that are separate in name but linked in risk.

A portfolio can look diversified by label and be concentrated by factor. Always check the factor layer.

### Weight By Risk Contribution, Not Only Market Value

A position that is a modest share of market value can dominate portfolio risk if it is volatile or highly correlated with the rest.

Compute:

- each position's share of portfolio volatility;
- the marginal risk contribution of large holdings;
- how risk concentration differs from dollar concentration.

A 15% position in a high-volatility stock can contribute more risk than a 40% position in short bonds. Risk-weighted limits catch what dollar limits miss.

### Identify Hidden And Aggregate Concentration

Concentration often hides across accounts. The same company appears in employer stock, an RSU plan, a mutual fund, an index fund, and a spouse's account.

Aggregate across:

- taxable and tax-advantaged accounts;
- employer compensation and stock plans;
- mutual funds and ETFs that hold the same issuer;
- private business equity and real estate;
- options, warrants, and convertible exposure;
- household-level exposure including a spouse.

The whole-household view is the only honest view. A position that is "only 5%" in one account can be 40% of net worth in aggregate.

### Set Limits By Source Of Concentration

Different sources of concentration warrant different limits and different urgency.

- Single stock from employer compensation: high urgency, often the largest single risk in a household.
- Sector concentration from a hot market: medium urgency, but prone to mean reversion.
- Geographic home bias: structural, often accepted but should be explicit.
- Factor concentration: invisible until a factor reverses; set limits by factor exposure.
- Liquidity concentration: dangerous because it only matters when you need to sell.

Limits should reflect the source, the investor's ability to act, and the cost of reducing the position (taxes, lockups, restrictions).

### Account For The Asymmetry Of Permanent Loss

Diversification is often described as reducing average return, but its deeper purpose is reducing the probability of permanent, unrecoverable loss. A single concentrated position can go to zero or near-zero and never recover.

Ask:

- could this position lose most of its value permanently (fraud, bankruptcy, obsolescence, expropriation)?
- if it did, would the investor's goals still be achievable?
- is the upside the investor hopes for worth that downside?

Concentration is rational only when the investor can survive the worst case and the expected payoff justifies the tail risk.

### Consider The Cost And Tax Of Reduction

Reducing concentration often triggers taxes, restrictions, and emotional resistance. These are real but should not freeze the portfolio.

Weigh:

- the tax cost of selling now versus the expected risk reduction;
- staged reduction over time to spread tax and market impact;
- hedging (collars, exchange funds, prepaid forwards) where available;
- charitable or gift strategies where appropriate;
- the risk of waiting for a "better" price that never comes.

Tax friction is a reason to plan carefully, not a reason to hold unacceptable risk indefinitely.

### Distinguish Deliberate Concentration From Accidental

Some concentration is a deliberate bet on an edge (a founder's belief, a deep-value thesis). Much concentration is accidental, the residue of success, inertia, or restricted stock.

For deliberate concentration, require:

- a genuine, testable edge;
- a survival plan for the worst case;
- a pre-committed reduction trigger.

For accidental concentration, the bar to keep it should be high, because there is usually no edge, only inertia and tax avoidance.

## Common Traps

### Measuring Concentration Only Within One Account

The most common blind spot. A stock that is small in the brokerage account may be enormous across the household. Always aggregate.

### Ignoring Factor Concentration Behind Label Diversity

A portfolio of 30 tech stocks is concentrated in one factor regardless of issuer count. Label diversity masks factor concentration.

### Letting Tax Fear Freeze Unacceptable Risk

Avoiding a tax bill by holding a 60% single-stock position can lead to a far larger loss when the stock falls. Tax is a cost of risk reduction, not a reason to avoid it.

### Treating A Rising Stock As Validation Of Concentration

A concentrated position that has risen feels like proof of a good decision. It is usually luck plus momentum. The higher it climbs, the larger the embedded risk.

### Assuming Liquidity Will Be There When Needed

Concentrated positions in less liquid stocks, private assets, or locked-up funds can become unsellable exactly when selling matters most. Liquidity concentration is invisible until a crisis.

### Overconfidence In A Single Thesis

Concentration built on "I understand this company" often ignores unknown unknowns, fraud, regulation, management change, and disruption that no analysis foresees.
