# PE Fund Structure And Commitments

A private equity (buyout) fund commitment is not an investment you fund today. It is a legal obligation to provide capital on demand over a decade, deployed by a general partner (GP) you cannot easily replace, into companies you will not individually select, with returns that follow a J-curve (early losses, later gains) and cluster by vintage year. Treating a PE commitment like a stock purchase — capital deployed immediately, returns tracked daily — fundamentally misreads the structure. LPs who do not model the call schedule, the J-curve, and the vintage concentration end up with liquidity shortfalls, uneven returns, and exposure to the wrong years.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do private equity funds work", "what is a capital commitment", "what is the J-curve", or "should I invest in a PE fund". The goal is to prevent the agent from presenting PE as a conventional investment, and from ignoring the commitment obligation, the cash-flow timing, and the vintage and J-curve dynamics that define the asset class.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Commitment, Call, And Deployment Mechanics

A PE commitment is an obligation, not an immediate investment:

- Commitment: the LP pledges a fixed amount of capital to the fund over its life (typically 10+ years with extensions).
- Capital calls: the GP calls capital as deals are sourced and funded; calls happen over the first 4-6 years (investment period), with timing and pace set by deal flow, not by the LP.
- Distributions: as portfolio companies are sold (exits), capital is returned to LPs, typically in years 4-10+.
- Net cash flow: early years are negative (calls exceed distributions); later years are positive (distributions exceed calls). The LP must hold liquid capital to meet calls throughout.

The LP commits the full amount but funds it over years. This creates "deployment drag" (committed capital sitting in low-yield liquid assets awaiting calls) and funding risk (the obligation to meet calls on short notice).

### Model The J-Curve And Its Implications

PE returns follow a characteristic J-curve:

- Early years: negative or low returns due to management fees (on committed capital), deal and transaction fees, and the time value of unrealized investments. NAV often dips below cost in years 1-3.
- Middle years: value creation through operational improvement, multiple expansion, and deleveraging begins to show; NAV recovers and grows.
- Later years: exits realize gains; distributions accelerate; IRR is pulled up by the timing of cash flows.

The J-curve means short-horizon performance is uninformative and misleading. Judging a fund in years 1-3 penalizes the structure. Conversely, IRR (which weights the timing of cash flows) can flatter funds that return capital early, even if total value created is modest. Use both IRR and multiple of invested capital (MOIC / TVPI) to judge performance.

### Treat Vintage Year As The Dominant Driver Of Returns

PE returns cluster by vintage — the year the fund began investing:

- Vintage effect: funds that invest into cheap markets (post-crash, distressed) tend to produce strong returns; funds that invest into expensive, capital-abundant markets tend to underperform.
- Entry multiples matter: buying companies at low EV/EBITDA and exiting at higher multiples drives much of PE return; buying high and selling low destroys it.
- Concentration risk: a single large commitment to one vintage exposes the LP to that year's entry conditions. A diversified program across vintages smooths this.

Vintage diversification is the primary structural risk control in PE. A one-off commitment is a bet on a single vintage's entry timing.

### Analyze The Distribution Waterfall And Carry Mechanics

Returns are split between LPs and the GP via the waterfall:

- Return of capital: LPs receive their invested capital first.
- Preferred return (hurdle): LPs receive a preferred return (often 8%) before the GP earns carry.
- Catch-up: the GP catches up to its carry percentage (often 100% catch-up to 20% carry), which significantly affects the LP's share of profits in the catch-up zone.
- Carry (80/20): profits above the hurdle and catch-up are split 80% LP / 20% GP (typical).
- Deal-by-deal versus whole-fund: deal-by-deal pays carry on each profitable exit even if other deals lose; whole-fund computes carry on aggregate. Whole-fund is more LP-favorable.
- Clawback: if the GP has been overpaid carry (early wins, later losses), the clawback requires repayment at fund end. A strong, GP-funded clawback protects LPs.

Model net-of-carry returns under scenarios. Deal-by-deal carry with full catch-up can transfer significant value to the GP relative to whole-fund carry.

### Assess Fees On Committed Versus Invested Capital

PE fees compound and are charged in ways that affect net return:

- Management fee: typically 2% of committed capital during the investment period, sometimes stepping down to invested capital thereafter. Committed-capital fees accrue even before deployment, deepening the J-curve.
- Deal, transaction, monitoring, and financing fees: charged to portfolio companies and/or the fund; some are offset against management fee, some are not.
- Fund expenses: legal, audit, administration passed through to LPs.

Sum all fee layers. A 2% management fee on commitments plus deal and monitoring fees can consume a large share of gross returns, especially in low-return vintages.

### Plan Liquidity And Over-Commitment Strategy

Because calls and distributions are mismatched, LPs manage liquidity actively:

- Over-commitment: sophisticated LPs commit more than their liquid capital (e.g., 120-140%) to reduce deployment drag, relying on distributions to fund later calls. This increases funding risk if distributions slow.
- Liquidity reserves: LPs must hold liquid assets to meet calls; the size depends on the call schedule and distribution pace.
- Secondary market: LPs can sell fund interests on the secondary market, typically at a discount to NAV, providing partial liquidity.

The LP's liquidity plan must survive scenarios where distributions slow (delayed exits, market closures) while calls continue. Funding a call failure is a default with severe penalties.

### Evaluate Term, Extensions, And Key-Person Provisions

The fund's legal term governs the lockup:

- Term: typically 10 years (investment period ~5, harvest ~5), often with GP-discretionary extensions.
- Key-person provisions: if named key people leave or reduce involvement, the investment period can suspend pending LP vote; protects against the brand remaining without the talent.
- No-fault divorce: some agreements allow LPs to remove the GP or wind down the fund with a supermajority, without cause.
- Transferability: LP interests are difficult to transfer; secondary sales require GP consent and buyer qualification.

Read the term, extension, key-person, and transfer provisions. A 10-year fund with GP extensions and weak key-person protection can lock capital far longer than expected.

## Common Traps

### Treating A Commitment As An Immediate Investment

A commitment is funded over years via calls. The LP must hold liquid capital and plan the call schedule; treating it as deployed capital misstates the cash-flow profile.

### Judging Early-Year Performance

The J-curve makes years 1-3 uninformative. Penalizing or praising a fund on early NAV is premature; judge on realized returns and MOIC over the full life.

### Concentrating In A Single Vintage

Vintage drives PE returns. A single commitment is a bet on one entry year; diversify across vintages to smooth entry-multiple risk.

### Ignoring Deal-By-Deal Carry And Catch-Up

Deal-by-deal carry with full catch-up transfers more profit to the GP than whole-fund carry. Model the net difference.

### Underestimating Committed-Capital Fees

Fees on commitments accrue before deployment, deepening the J-curve. Sum all fee layers, including deal and monitoring fees.

### Failing To Plan For Funding Risk

Calls must be met on short notice. LPs without liquidity reserves or who over-commit without distribution assumptions face funding shortfalls and default.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The commitment, call, and distribution mechanics are understood, and the LP's funding liquidity is planned.
- [ ] The J-curve is modeled, and early-year performance is not over-weighted; IRR is paired with MOIC/TVPI.
- [ ] Vintage year is treated as the dominant return driver, and vintage diversification is planned.
- [ ] The distribution waterfall (hurdle, catch-up, deal-by-deal versus whole-fund, clawback) is modeled for net-of-carry returns.
- [ ] All fee layers (committed-capital management fee, deal, monitoring, financing, expenses) are summed.
- [ ] Liquidity and over-commitment strategy accounts for call-distribution mismatch and slow-exit scenarios.
- [ ] Term, extensions, key-person, no-fault divorce, and transferability provisions are read from the LPA.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting PE as a conventional investment and references investor-specific liquidity, vintage diversification, and risk tolerance.
