# PE Due Diligence And Selection

Private equity due diligence is the decision to lock up capital for a decade based on evidence that is incomplete, backward-looking, and heavily influenced by market conditions. The central challenge is separating skill from luck and market beta: a GP's stellar track record may reflect a single rising market and entry-multiple tailwind rather than repeatable operational value creation. Selecting a PE fund on gross IRR and brand reputation, without decomposing the sources of return, benchmarking net-to-LP performance against peers, and assessing team and operational quality, is how LPs end up in funds that fail to repeat.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do I choose a PE fund", "is this GP any good", "what should I check before committing to a buyout fund", or "how do I evaluate a private equity manager". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating track record as proof of skill, and from missing the team, strategy, benchmarking, and operational dimensions that determine whether returns repeat.

## Core Rules

### Decompose The Track Record Into Skill, Market Beta, And Leverage

A GP's track record is the headline evidence, but it must be decomposed:

- Market beta and entry multiples: returns from buying at low EV/EBITDA and exiting at higher multiples reflect market timing, not skill. Strong vintages often coincide with cheap entry.
- Leverage: PE uses significant debt; leverage amplifies equity returns in good times and destroys them in bad. Returns driven by leverage are not operational skill.
- Operational value creation: margin expansion, revenue growth, and operational improvement are the repeatable skill components. Ask how much of the return came from EBITDA growth versus multiple expansion and leverage.
- Exit timing and market conditions: exits into a bull market flatter returns; the skill is repeatable across cycles, not cycle-dependent.

Ask the GP to attribute returns across multiple expansion, EBITDA growth, leverage, and multiple sources. A track record dominated by multiple expansion and leverage is less repeatable than one driven by operational improvement.

### Benchmark Net-To-LP Returns Against Relevant Peers

Track record must be compared, not viewed in isolation:

- Net IRR and MOIC/TVPI: use net-to-LP (after fees and carry), not gross.
- Peer benchmarks: compare to same-vintage, same-strategy peers and to public market equivalents (PME) that replicate the cash-flow timing in public markets.
- PME (public market equivalent): what would the LP have earned investing the same cash flows in a public index? PME above 1.0 indicates the GP beat public markets; below 1.0 indicates underperformance.
- Quartile ranking: top-quartile status is meaningful; mid- and bottom-quartile funds often fail to beat public markets net of fees.

A 15% net IRR looks strong until benchmarked against a 13% public market return with the same cash-flow timing. Always benchmark, net of fees, against relevant peers and PME.

### Assess Strategy Coherence, Edge, And Capacity

Strategy determines what the GP buys and how value is created:

- Strategy type: buyout (large/mid/small cap), growth equity, special situations, distressed; each has different risk, return, and cycle sensitivity.
- Defined edge: sourcing (proprietary deal flow), operational improvement (in-house operating partners), sector specialization, or financial engineering. A coherent, repeatable edge is more valuable than generic deal-doing.
- Capacity: can the strategy absorb the fund size without diluting quality or pushing into larger/riskier deals? Fund size creep often precedes performance decline.
- Differentiation: what does this GP do that peers cannot easily replicate?

A coherent strategy with a defined edge and disciplined capacity is more repeatable than a generic approach chasing the latest deal.

### Evaluate Team Depth, Continuity, And Key-Person Risk

The team executes the strategy; assess who they are and whether they stay:

- Senior deal team: experience across cycles, leadership of prior deals, continuity at the firm.
- Depth beyond founders: a team dependent on one or two founders is key-person risk; mid-level and junior depth matters for execution and succession.
- Alignment and retention: carry vesting, equity in the firm, non-competes, and retention of senior dealmakers.
- Turnover: departures of senior investment professionals can break track-record portability and signal internal problems.

Track records built by departed teams are not portable. Meet the current team and assess whether the people who generated the returns are still there and aligned.

### Analyze Portfolio Construction And Risk Concentration

The fund's portfolio is where risk materializes:

- Number of deals and concentration: a fund with 8-10 deals is highly concentrated; one or two failures can sink the fund. More deals diversify idiosyncratic risk.
- Sector concentration: focus on one or two sectors can be an edge or a risk, depending on cycle.
- Geographic concentration: exposure to one region's economic and political risk.
- Leverage policy: fund-level and deal-level leverage, fixed/floating mix, covenant-lite exposure.
- Hold periods and exit pathways: how the GP plans to exit (sale, IPO, recap, secondary) and the sensitivity to market conditions.

Stress the portfolio for concentration. A "diversified" buyout fund with 60% of capital in two deals is not diversified.

### Review Operational, Legal, And Governance Infrastructure

Operational failures destroy value and can mask fraud:

- Independent fund administrator, auditor, and custodian: reduce operational and fraud risk.
- Valuation policy: fair-value methodology, independent valuation input, consistency across funds.
- Reporting transparency: property/deal-level detail, fee disclosure, realized versus unrealized breakdown.
- Compliance and regulatory history: prior regulatory actions, litigation, investor disputes.
- ESG and responsible investing: measurement, management, and reporting; increasingly demanded by LPs and relevant to exit values.

Weak operations (related-party administration, opaque reporting, no independent audit) are red flags. Strong infrastructure is necessary; its absence is a warning.

### Check References, Conflicts, And Concurrent Fund Activity

References and conflict checks reveal what marketing does not:

- LP references: prior fund investors on communication, problem handling, alignment in stress, and whether the GP met expectations.
- Deal references: portfolio company CEOs, lenders, and co-investors on the GP's operational value-add and behavior.
- Concurrent funds: multiple funds in the same strategy create deal-allocation conflicts; how does the GP decide which fund gets which deal?
- Related-party transactions: fees, services, and transactions with affiliates that transfer value from LPs to the GP.
- Side letters: preferential terms for large LPs (fee discounts, MFN, information) that may disadvantage others.

References and conflict checks are where hidden problems surface. Conduct them independently; do not rely on the pitch.

## Common Traps

### Treating Gross IRR As Evidence Of Skill

Gross IRR conflates skill, market beta, leverage, and vintage effects. Decompose the sources and benchmark net of fees.

### Ignoring Vintage And Market Beta In The Track Record

Strong vintages reflect cheap entry and rising markets. Ask whether the track record is repeatable in a different cycle.

### Accepting Mid-Pack Performance Because "PE Returns Are High"

Mid- and bottom-quartile PE funds often fail to beat public markets net of fees. Benchmark against PME and peers.

### Overlooking Key-Person And Team Turnover Risk

Track records built by departed teams are not portable. Assess current team depth and retention.

### Missing Portfolio Concentration

A fund with few deals or heavy sector/geographic concentration is riskier than it appears. Stress concentration.

### Relying On The Pitch Instead Of References

Marketing shows wins; references and conflict checks reveal problems. Conduct independent reference checks.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The track record is decomposed into skill (operational value creation), market beta (multiple expansion), leverage, and vintage effects.
- [ ] Net-to-LP IRR and MOIC/TVPI are benchmarked against same-vintage peers and public market equivalents (PME).
- [ ] Strategy coherence, defined edge, capacity, and differentiation are assessed for repeatability.
- [ ] Team depth, continuity, key-person risk, alignment, and turnover are evaluated.
- [ ] Portfolio construction (deal count, sector/geographic concentration, leverage, exit pathways) is stress-tested.
- [ ] Operational, legal, and governance infrastructure (administrator, auditor, valuation, reporting, compliance, ESG) is reviewed.
- [ ] LP and deal references are conducted independently, and concurrent-fund and related-party conflicts are examined.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a GP on track record alone and references investor-specific liquidity, vintage diversification, and risk tolerance.
