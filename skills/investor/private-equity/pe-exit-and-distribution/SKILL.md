# PE Exit And Distribution

Private equity returns are only realized at exit. Until a portfolio company is sold, IPO'd, or recapitalized, the gains are unrealized marks that can change. The exit pathway, timing, and structure determine the actual cash returned to LPs, the tax bill, and whether the GP's carry was earned on durable value or temporary marks. Analyzing PE as if NAV equals distributable cash — ignoring realization risk, exit-market dependence, and the tax and reinvestment mechanics of distributions — overstates the return the LP will actually receive.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do PE funds return money", "what are the exit options", "when will I get distributions", or "are unrealized gains reliable". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating NAV as cash, and from ignoring the exit-market, timing, tax, and reinvestment dynamics that determine realized LP returns.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Exit Pathways And Their Tradeoffs

PE realizes value through several pathways, each with different dynamics:

- Strategic sale (to a corporate buyer): often the highest price; synergies justify a premium; depends on M&A activity and corporate confidence.
- Sponsor-to-sponsor sale (to another PE fund): common in good markets; price driven by continuing PE demand and leverage availability; sensitive to credit conditions.
- IPO: public markets exit; can deliver high value but exposes to market timing, lockups, and post-IPO price risk; not always achievable.
- Recapitalization / dividend recap: the company re-leverages and pays a dividend to the sponsor; realizes partial cash without a full exit; increases company leverage and risk.
- Continuation vehicle (CV): the GP transfers the asset to a new fund it manages, "selling" to itself/LPs; provides liquidity but raises conflict and valuation questions.

Each pathway depends on market conditions. A fund planning IPO exits in a closed IPO market must hold, sell at a discount, or pursue an alternative. Exit flexibility is a real source of value.

### Separate Realized From Unrealized Returns

PE fund reporting blends realized and unrealized gains:

- Realized: cash returned to LPs from completed exits; durable and final.
- Unrealized: mark-to-fair-value of remaining portfolio companies; can change with markets, revaluations, and exit conditions.
- TVPI (total value to paid-in): realized + unrealized; overstates distributable cash to the extent unrealized marks are optimistic.
- DPI (distributed to paid-in): realized cash only; the conservative measure of what LPs have actually received.

Late in a fund's life, a large unrealized portion signals realization risk: the marks must be converted to cash through exits that depend on market conditions. Weight DPI more heavily as the fund matures.

### Model Exit-Market Dependence And Realization Risk

Exits depend on M&A, IPO, and credit markets, which cycle:

- Strong markets: multiple exits, high multiples, full realization; unrealized converts to realized at favorable prices.
- Weak/closed markets: exits stall, multiples compress, the GP holds or sells at discounts; unrealized marks may not be achievable.
- Forced exits: if the fund is nearing term, the GP may sell into weak markets to return capital, realizing losses versus marks.
- Hold-and-extend: GPs may extend the fund or use continuation vehicles to avoid weak-market exits, lengthening the lockup.

A fund's reported IRR can flatter early-realized deals while later unrealized holdings face a different market. Assess the exit environment for the remaining portfolio, not just historical exits.

### Assess Dividend Recaps And Partial Realizations

Dividend recaps realize cash without a full exit:

- Mechanics: the portfolio company issues additional debt and pays a special dividend to the sponsor (and thus LPs).
- Effect: returns capital early (boosting IRR), but increases company leverage and risk; the remaining equity is more leveraged and vulnerable.
- IRR flattery: early recaps boost IRR even if total value created is modest; the LP receives cash but holds a riskier residual.
- Sustainability: recaps depend on credit market access; in tight credit, they are unavailable.

Recaps are a legitimate tool but shift risk to the remaining holding. Evaluate whether the distribution came from value creation or from re-leveraging the company.

### Evaluate Continuation Vehicles And Conflict Risk

Continuation vehicles (CVs) are increasingly common and carry conflicts:

- Mechanics: the GP transfers a portfolio company from an aging fund to a new fund it manages, "selling" to itself.
- Process: ideally run through a competitive auction with independent valuation and LP election to roll or cash out.
- Conflict: the GP sets the price as both seller (fiduciary to the old fund) and buyer (manager of the new fund); valuation is contested.
- LP election: LPs may choose to receive cash (exit) or roll into the new CV; the choice depends on the offered price and the LP's liquidity needs.

CVs can provide liquidity but require scrutiny of valuation, process, and conflict. An LP rolling into a CV at an inflated price bears the overpayment.

### Account For Tax On Distributions

PE distributions carry tax consequences for LPs:

- Capital gains: most PE gains are long-term capital gains for taxable LPs (favorable rates), but holding period and structure matter.
- Carried interest taxation: the GP's carry is generally taxed as capital gains (in most jurisdictions), a politically contested treatment; changes could affect after-tax GP economics and alignment.
- UBTI and ECI: tax-exempt and foreign LPs may face unrelated business taxable income or effectively connected income from leverage or US operations, requiring blocker structures.
- Withholding and cross-border: foreign LPs face withholding and treaty considerations.

Model after-tax returns, not pre-tax distributions. Tax-exempt and foreign LPs should check UBTI/ECI exposure and structure accordingly.

### Consider Reinvestment, Recycling, And Subscription Lines

Several mechanisms affect distributions and reported returns:

- Recycling: the LPA may allow the GP to reinvest certain proceeds (sale costs, early exits) into new deals without counting against the commitment; can extend the investment period and vintage exposure.
- Subscription (capital call) lines: short-term credit lines drawn on LP commitments to fund deals or accelerate investments; smooths calls and can flatter IRR by delaying LP cash outflows, but adds cost and leverage.
- Reinvestment of distributions: some structures allow LPs to reinvest distributions, compounding exposure.

Subscription lines, in particular, can materially inflate IRR by changing the timing of cash flows without changing total value. Compare IRR to MOIC/TVPI to see whether timing or value is driving the reported return.

## Common Traps

### Treating NAV Or TVPI As Distributable Cash

NAV includes unrealized marks that depend on exit markets. DPI (realized cash) is the conservative measure of what LPs have received.

### Ignoring Exit-Market Dependence

Exits require M&A, IPO, and credit markets. Unrealized gains in a closed market may not be realizable at reported values.

### Being Flattered By IRR From Early Recaps

Dividend recaps boost IRR by returning cash early but increase residual company leverage. Compare IRR to MOIC to see total value created.

### Overlooking Continuation Vehicle Conflicts

CVs let the GP sell to itself; valuation and process conflicts require scrutiny. LPs rolling at inflated prices bear the overpayment.

### Forgetting Tax On Distributions

PE distributions are taxable (capital gains, UBTI, ECI, withholding). After-tax return, not gross distribution, is what the LP keeps.

### Missing Subscription-Line IRR Flattery

Subscription lines delay LP cash outflows, inflating IRR without changing total value. Always pair IRR with MOIC/TVPI/DPI.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Exit pathways (strategic, sponsor-to-sponsor, IPO, recap, continuation vehicle) and their market dependence are analyzed.
- [ ] Realized (DPI) and unrealized returns are separated, and realization risk is assessed for the remaining portfolio.
- [ ] Exit-market conditions for unrealized holdings are evaluated, and forced-exit or hold-and-extend scenarios are considered.
- [ ] Dividend recaps are distinguished from value-creating exits, and their effect on residual leverage and IRR is modeled.
- [ ] Continuation vehicle valuation, process, conflict, and LP election terms are scrutinized.
- [ ] Tax on distributions (capital gains, UBTI/ECI, withholding, carry taxation) is modeled for after-tax LP return.
- [ ] Recycling, reinvestment, and subscription-line effects on cash-flow timing and IRR are accounted for; IRR is paired with MOIC/TVPI/DPI.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting NAV as distributable cash and references investor-specific liquidity, tax, and risk tolerance.
