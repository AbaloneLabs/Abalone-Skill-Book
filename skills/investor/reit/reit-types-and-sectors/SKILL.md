# REIT Types And Sectors

REITs are not a single asset class. A data center REIT, an office REIT, and a shopping mall REIT have different tenants, lease structures, supply dynamics, growth drivers, and sensitivities to the economy, inflation, and interest rates. Treating "REITs" as one homogeneous income vehicle leads to owning the wrong sectors for the wrong reasons — chasing a dividend yield without understanding that the underlying business can be in secular decline or secular boom.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "should I buy REITs", "which REIT sectors are best", "are REITs interest-rate sensitive", or "what is the difference between office and industrial REITs". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending REITs at the index level without sector analysis, and from treating a high dividend yield as a signal of safety.

## Core Rules

### Analyze Each Sector's Demand Drivers And Supply Dynamics

Each property sector has distinct fundamentals. Understand what drives occupancy and rent:

- Retail (shopping centers, malls, freestanding): demand from consumer spending and retailer health; threat from e-commerce; supply discipline matters; location and co-tenancy quality differentiate.
- Office: demand from employment and white-collar work; structurally disrupted by remote/hybrid work in some markets; long leases create lag but also slow recovery; location, building quality, and amenity matter.
- Residential (apartments, single-family rental): demand from demographics, household formation, and affordability versus buying; supply cycles matter; rent regulation in some markets.
- Industrial (logistics, warehouses): demand from e-commerce, supply chains, and manufacturing; benefited from structural shifts; supply waves can pressure rents.
- Healthcare (senior housing, hospitals, skilled nursing, medical offices): demand from aging demographics; reimbursement, regulatory, and operator risk.
- Data centers, cell towers, self-storage: specialized demand (cloud, mobile, life events); often longer-duration growth, but valued accordingly.

A REIT's return is driven by its sector's NOI growth, not by the dividend yield. A sector in secular decline can destroy the dividend.

### Understand Lease Structure And Its Cycle Sensitivity

Lease length and structure determine how quickly a REIT can capture rising rents or gets stuck with falling rents:

- Short leases (apartments, self-storage, hotels): rents reprice quickly, capturing inflation and cycle moves in both directions; more cyclical, more inflation-responsive.
- Long leases (office, retail, net-lease): rents are locked for years; slow to capture inflation, but stable income in downturns; re-leasing risk at renewal.
- Net lease (tenant pays expenses): lower volatility but credit exposure to the tenant.
- Hotels (room nights): effectively daily leases; the most cyclical, most rate- and demand-sensitive.

Lease structure explains why apartment and hotel REITs behave differently from office and net-lease REITs across the cycle. Match the lease profile to the investor's cycle and inflation view.

### Separate Secular Trends From Cyclical Moves

REIT performance blends secular and cyclical forces, and confusing them leads to bad timing:

- Secular (multi-year structural): e-commerce benefiting industrial/logistics; remote work pressuring office; aging demographics supporting healthcare; cloud/AI supporting data centers; urbanization and affordability affecting residential.
- Cyclical (business cycle): employment, consumer confidence, interest rates, cap rates, transaction volume.

A cyclical recovery can lift a structurally challenged sector temporarily (value trap), and a cyclical downturn can depress a structurally strong sector temporarily (opportunity). Identify which force dominates for the sector in question before buying or selling.

### Assess Interest-Rate Sensitivity And Cap-Rate Transmission

REITs are sensitive to interest rates through several channels:

- Discount rate: higher rates raise the cap rate used to value properties, lowering NAV (like long-duration assets).
- Cost of capital: REITs rely on debt and equity issuance to grow; higher rates raise financing costs and can slow acquisitions.
- Relative attractiveness: higher bond yields make REIT dividends less attractive on a yield basis.
- Offset: REITs with short leases and inflation-linked rents (apartments, self-storage) can grow NOI to offset rate pressure; long-lease REITs cannot.

Rate sensitivity varies by sector and lease structure. Do not treat all REITs as equally rate-sensitive, and recognize that rate cuts can help REITs through both discount-rate and growth channels.

### Evaluate The Dividend As A Function Of FFO/AFFO, Not Coupon

A REIT dividend is not a bond coupon. It is paid from operating cash flow and can be cut if fundamentals deteriorate. (See the AFFO and dividend skill for detail.) Key points:

- REITs are required to distribute most taxable income to maintain tax status, which means dividends are high but also means payouts can strain cash flow during stress.
- Dividend coverage by AFFO, payout ratios, and balance-sheet flexibility determine dividend safety.
- A high dividend yield often signals market concern about the dividend's sustainability, not a bargain.

Judge the dividend against AFFO coverage and sector fundamentals, not in isolation.

### Check Valuation: P/FFO, P/AFFO, NAV Premium/Discount

REIT valuation uses sector-appropriate metrics:

- P/FFO and P/AFFO: price multiples of funds from operations; compare within sector and to history.
- NAV premium/discount: stock price versus estimated net asset value of the underlying properties; a discount can signal value or distress.
- Cap rate implied: the yield the market is assigning to the portfolio.
- Dividend yield: relevant but subordinate to FFO/AFFO coverage.

High growth (data centers, cell towers) trades at higher P/AFFO; mature income sectors trade lower. Compare within sector and assess whether the multiple reflects growth expectations that may or may not materialize.

### Consider Corporate Governance, Balance Sheet, And External Management

- Balance sheet: leverage, debt maturity ladder, fixed/floating mix, credit rating. Highly leveraged REITs are more rate-sensitive and more vulnerable in downturns.
- Governance: internally managed (employee team) versus externally advised (a separate manager earns fees); external management creates conflicts and should be scrutinized.
- Capital allocation: track record of acquisitions, developments, and dispositions; value-add versus empire-building.
- ESG and obsolescence: building energy efficiency, location resilience, sector-specific risks (e.g., mall obsolescence).

A cheap REIT with a weak balance sheet or poor governance can be a value trap. Quality matters across the cycle.

## Common Traps

### Buying REITs For The Dividend Yield Without Sector Analysis

A high yield often reflects a challenged sector (office, malls) where the dividend is at risk. Yield is not safety.

### Treating All REITs As Equally Rate-Sensitive

Short-lease, inflation-linked REITs (apartments, self-storage) can offset rate pressure with NOI growth; long-lease REITs cannot. Rate sensitivity is sector-specific.

### Confusing Secular Decline With Cyclical Value

A structurally challenged sector (e.g., lower-quality office, B-malls) can look cheap cyclically but keep declining. Cheap on a multiple is not cheap on a trend.

### Ignoring Lease Structure

Long-lease REITs offer stability but re-leasing risk and slow inflation capture. Short-lease REITs are cyclical but inflation-responsive. The lease profile must fit the thesis.

### Overlooking External Management Conflicts

Externally advised REITs can transfer value to the manager through fees and related-party transactions. Governance quality affects long-term returns.

### Comparing P/FFO Across Sectors Mechanically

Growth sectors deserve higher multiples; mature sectors lower. A high P/FFO in a growth sector may be fair; the same multiple in a declining sector is a warning.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each REIT sector's demand drivers, supply dynamics, and secular trends are analyzed, not treated as homogeneous.
- [ ] Lease structure (short versus long, gross versus net) is matched to the cycle and inflation view.
- [ ] Secular trends are distinguished from cyclical moves to avoid value traps and missed opportunities.
- [ ] Interest-rate sensitivity is assessed by sector and lease structure, not applied uniformly.
- [ ] The dividend is judged against FFO/AFFO coverage and payout sustainability, not treated as a coupon.
- [ ] Valuation uses P/FFO, P/AFFO, NAV premium/discount, and implied cap rate, compared within sector.
- [ ] Balance sheet, governance, external management, and capital allocation are assessed for quality.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending REITs at the index level without sector analysis and references investor-specific income needs, rate view, and risk tolerance.
