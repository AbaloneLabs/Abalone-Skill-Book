# AFFO And Dividend Analysis

REITs are required to distribute most of their taxable income as dividends, which makes the dividend central to the investment case. But a REIT dividend is not a bond coupon — it is paid from operating cash flow, and its sustainability depends on whether the REIT generates enough cash after maintaining its properties. Funds from operations (FFO) and adjusted funds from operations (AFFO) exist precisely because net income (with its depreciation charge) understates a REIT's cash earnings. Quoting a dividend yield without checking AFFO coverage treats a discretionary, at-risk cash distribution as guaranteed income.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "is this REIT dividend safe", "what is FFO and AFFO", "can the REIT keep paying its dividend", or "how do I evaluate REIT income quality". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending REITs on dividend yield alone, and from confusing accounting earnings with distributable cash.

## Core Rules

### Use FFO And AFFO, Not Net Income, For REIT Earnings

Net income is misleading for REITs because real estate depreciation is a large non-cash charge that often overstates true economic decline of well-located properties.

- FFO: net income excluding gains/losses on property sales, plus depreciation and amortization of real estate. A standardized measure (NAREIT defines it) that better reflects operating performance.
- AFFO: FFO further adjusted for recurring capital expenditures (maintenance capex), straight-line rent adjustments, and other non-cash items. AFFO is the closest proxy to distributable cash flow.

AFFO is the right denominator for dividend coverage and for P/AFFO valuation. Always check whether the metric being quoted is FFO or AFFO, and prefer AFFO for sustainability analysis.

### Assess Dividend Coverage By AFFO And The Payout Ratio

Dividend safety is measured by how comfortably AFFO covers the dividend:

- Payout ratio = dividends per share / AFFO per share. Lower ratios (e.g., 70-80%) provide cushion; ratios near or above 100% are warning signs.
- Coverage trends matter: a rising payout ratio over time signals deteriorating cushion even if the absolute dividend is growing.
- Sector norms differ: stable, long-lease REITs can sustain higher payout ratios; cyclical, short-lease REITs need lower ratios to absorb downturns.

A REIT paying out more than it earns in AFFO is funding the dividend from debt, equity issuance, or asset sales — unsustainable over time. Identify the funding source if coverage is thin.

### Distinguish Maintenance Capex From Growth Capex

AFFO subtracts maintenance capex (required to keep properties competitive) but not growth capex (acquisitions, developments that expand income). This distinction matters:

- Maintenance capex is a real, recurring cost of preserving NOI; excluding it overstates distributable cash.
- Growth capex is optional and should earn a return above the cost of capital; it should not be needed to fund the dividend.
- Some REITs classify capex aggressively to inflate AFFO; scrutinize the reconciliation from FFO to AFFO.

A REIT whose "AFFO" requires reclassifying necessary upkeep as growth capex is overstating its distributable cash. Read the capex breakdown.

### Adjust For Non-Cash And One-Time Items

FFO/AFFO can be inflated or distorted by adjustments:

- Straight-line rent: accounting recognition of rent increases over lease term that exceeds cash collected early; AFFO should adjust this down for cash analysis.
- Free rent and tenant improvement allowances: upfront costs that depress near-term cash but support future rents.
- Non-recurring items: legal settlements, insurance recoveries, gains on sale (already excluded from FFO but watch for other one-offs).
- Stock-based compensation: a real cost often added back; consider whether it should reduce AFFO.

Compare the REIT's reported AFFO to a "cash AFFO" that strips out favorable adjustments. The gap reveals earnings quality.

### Evaluate Dividend Growth, Not Just The Current Yield

A sustainable, growing dividend compounds; a flat or cut dividend destroys the income thesis. Assess:

- Historical dividend growth rate and its relationship to AFFO growth.
- Whether AFFO growth is organic (same-property NOI growth) or driven by issuance and acquisitions.
- The reinvestment rate: how much AFFO is retained to fund growth versus paid out.
- Balance-sheet capacity to fund growth without excessive leverage or dilution.

A REIT that pays out nearly all AFFO may have a high current yield but little capacity to grow the dividend. The total return depends on growth, not just the starting yield.

### Check Balance-Sheet And Financing Capacity

Dividend sustainability also depends on the balance sheet:

- Leverage (debt/EBITDA, debt to gross assets): highly leveraged REITs have less flexibility and more rate sensitivity.
- Debt maturity ladder and fixed/floating mix: refinancing risk and rate exposure.
- Credit ratings and access to capital: investment-grade REITs have more flexibility in stress.
- Liquidity: undrawn revolvers and available capacity to ride out downturns.

A well-covered dividend at a highly leveraged REIT is less safe than the same coverage at a conservatively financed one, because refinancing stress can force a cut.

### Recognize The REIT Distribution Requirement And Its Tension

REITs must distribute at least 90% of taxable income to avoid corporate tax. This creates a structural push toward high payouts, but it also means:

- Dividends can exceed free cash flow when taxable income is high but cash is tight (e.g., large gains).
- REITs may need to issue stock or debt to fund both the dividend and growth, diluting existing shareholders.
- Special dividends from gains can be misleading as indicators of recurring income.

The distribution requirement is a feature (tax efficiency) and a constraint (limited retention for growth). Balance the income benefit against the dilution/growth tradeoff.

## Common Traps

### Quoting Dividend Yield Without AFFO Coverage

A high yield with weak AFFO coverage is a warning, not a bargain. The market often prices dividend risk into the yield.

### Using Net Income Or EPS For REIT Valuation

Depreciation makes net income understated. FFO and especially AFFO are the correct earnings measures for REITs.

### Treating FFO As Distributable Cash

FFO does not subtract maintenance capex. AFFO is the better measure of cash available for distribution.

### Ignoring Straight-Line Rent And Non-Cash Adjustments

Straight-line rent and other adjustments inflate reported AFFO relative to cash. Cash AFFO is the conservative check.

### Assuming Past Dividend Stability Implies Future Safety

Payout ratios can deteriorate as AFFO flatlines or falls. A stable dividend with a rising payout ratio is a deteriorating situation.

### Forgetting The Distribution Requirement Forces High Payouts

The 90% rule structurally limits retention for growth. High payouts are not always a choice; they can constrain reinvestment and force dilutive issuance.

## Self-Check

- [ ] AFFO (not net income or raw FFO) is used as the measure of distributable cash and as the valuation denominator.
- [ ] Dividend coverage by AFFO and the payout ratio are computed, and coverage trends over time are checked.
- [ ] Maintenance capex is separated from growth capex, and aggressive capex classification is scrutinized.
- [ ] Non-cash and one-time items (straight-line rent, stock comp, free rent) are adjusted to compute cash AFFO.
- [ ] Dividend growth, its source (organic NOI versus issuance), and the reinvestment rate are assessed, not just the current yield.
- [ ] Balance-sheet leverage, debt maturities, fixed/floating mix, and liquidity are evaluated for dividend resilience.
- [ ] The REIT distribution requirement is recognized as both a tax benefit and a constraint on growth retention.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a REIT on dividend yield alone and references AFFO coverage, growth, and investor-specific income needs and risk tolerance.
