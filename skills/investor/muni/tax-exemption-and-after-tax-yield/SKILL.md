# Tax Exemption And After-Tax Yield

The defining feature of most municipal bonds is that their interest is exempt from federal income tax, and often from state and local tax for residents. This tax exemption is the entire reason munis exist as a separate asset class. But "tax-free" is not a blanket statement — the benefit depends on the investor's marginal tax bracket, the bond's specific tax treatment, the account it is held in, and whether alternative minimum tax applies. Comparing a muni's coupon to a taxable bond's coupon directly is one of the most common and costly errors in fixed income.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "are municipal bonds a good investment", "how do I compare a muni yield to a Treasury yield", "is this muni really tax-free", or "should I hold munis in my retirement account". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending munis based on the tax-free label without checking whether the investor's tax situation makes the exemption valuable, and from comparing pre-tax to post-tax yields.

## Core Rules

### Compare On A Tax-Equivalent Or After-Tax Basis, Never On Coupon Alone

A muni yielding 3.5% and a corporate bond yielding 5% cannot be compared by coupon. The relevant comparison is after-tax yield. Two equivalent approaches:

- Tax-equivalent yield (TEY): the taxable yield that would equal the muni's after-tax yield. TEY = tax-exempt yield / (1 − marginal tax rate). A 3.5% muni for an investor in a 37% federal bracket has a TEY of about 5.56%.
- After-tax yield: the taxable bond's yield × (1 − marginal tax rate), compared to the muni's tax-exempt yield.

Always compute this for the investor's actual combined marginal rate (federal plus state plus local where applicable). The higher the bracket, the more attractive munis become; in low brackets, taxable bonds often win after tax.

### Stack All Relevant Tax Layers

The exemption is not uniform. Identify each layer:

- Federal exemption: applies to most ("public-purpose") muni interest.
- State exemption: typically only for bonds issued by the investor's state of residence (or its subdivisions). Out-of-state munis are usually taxable at the state level.
- Local tax: where applicable.
- Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT): interest on certain "private activity bonds" (PABs) is tax-exempt for regular tax but is a tax preference item for AMT. For AMT-liable investors, these bonds are not truly tax-free.
- Net investment income tax (NIIT) and Medicare surtaxes: muni interest is generally exempt from NIIT, an additional advantage for high earners.

Failing to stack state tax is a common error: a high-yield out-of-state muni can be worse after state tax than a lower-yielding in-state bond.

### Confirm The Bond Is Actually Tax-Exempt

Not all munis are tax-exempt, and not all tax-exempt interest is fully exempt. Check:

- Public-purpose general obligation and essential-purpose revenue bonds: generally fully federally tax-exempt.
- Private activity bonds (PABs): federally tax-exempt for regular tax, but AMT preference items.
- Build America Bonds and taxable munis: explicitly federally taxable; they are not tax-exempt at all.
- Bank-qualified versus non-bank-qualified: affects dealer and bank demand, not individual tax exemption, but influences pricing.
- Direct-subsidy and refunding bonds: read the offering document for the specific tax treatment.

Do not assume "muni" means "tax-free." Read the tax treatment in the official statement.

### Match Muni Suitability To The Investor's Tax Bracket

The economic value of the exemption rises with the marginal tax rate. Rules of thumb:

- High marginal bracket (top federal plus state): munis are often attractive; the TEY frequently beats high-grade corporates after tax.
- Middle brackets: the comparison is close and depends on the specific yield curve and state treatment.
- Low brackets: taxable bonds usually provide higher after-tax yield; the exemption is wasted.
- Tax-advantaged accounts (IRA, 401(k)): holding munis inside a tax-deferred account wastes the exemption because all withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. Munis belong in taxable accounts.

A muni's benefit is investor-specific. The same bond can be excellent for one investor and value-destroying for another.

### Account For Capital Gains, Market Discount, And Premium

The tax treatment of munis extends beyond interest:

- Capital gains and losses on sale or redemption are taxable (at capital gains rates), even though interest is exempt.
- Market discount (bought below par in the secondary market) can create taxable ordinary income on sale or redemption, partially eroding the exemption.
- Premium amortization affects yield and basis; tax-exempt premium bonds require adjustment.

Investors buying seasoned munis below par should understand that part of the return may be taxable as market discount, not pure tax-exempt interest.

### Consider Tax-Law And Fiscal-Policy Risk

The tax exemption is a policy choice, not a law of nature. It has been periodically targeted for reform (e.g., capping or eliminating the exemption, or limiting the benefit to lower brackets). Changes would reprice the entire muni market. While the exemption has been durable, investors concentrated in munis should be aware of this policy risk, especially in deficit-reduction debates.

## Common Traps

### Comparing Muni Coupon To Taxable Coupon Directly

This overstates the muni's attractiveness for low-bracket investors and understates it for high-bracket investors. The comparison must be on a tax-equivalent or after-tax basis.

### Holding Munis In A Tax-Advantaged Account

The exemption is wasted inside an IRA or 401(k), where all income is taxed on withdrawal. Munis should generally sit in taxable accounts; tax-efficient equities or growth assets are better fits for tax-advantaged space.

### Ignoring State Tax On Out-Of-State Munis

A high-coupon out-of-state muni can be worse after state tax than a lower in-state bond. National muni funds mix states and may be state-taxable for the investor.

### Treating All Tax-Exempt Bonds As AMT-Free

Private activity bonds are AMT preference items. An AMT-liable investor holding PAB-heavy funds is not getting the expected tax benefit.

### Assuming A Muni Fund Is Equivalent To A Single Tax-Exempt Bond

Muni funds hold hundreds of issues across states and structures, including some AMT and taxable bonds. The fund's stated yield is not a clean tax-exempt yield; check the fund's tax composition.

### Forgetting Capital Gains And Market Discount Taxation

Interest may be exempt, but gains, market discount, and premium amortization have their own tax rules that affect after-tax return.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Yields are compared on a tax-equivalent or after-tax basis using the investor's combined marginal rate (federal, state, local, NIIT), never coupon-to-coupon.
- [ ] State exemption is applied only to in-state bonds, and out-of-state munis are adjusted for state tax.
- [ ] The bond's specific tax status (public-purpose, private activity/AMT, taxable muni) is confirmed from the offering document, not assumed.
- [ ] Muni suitability is matched to the investor's marginal bracket, and munis are not recommended for tax-advantaged accounts or low brackets where the exemption is wasted.
- [ ] AMT exposure is checked for the investor and for PAB-heavy funds.
- [ ] Capital gains, market discount, and premium amortization tax effects are addressed for seasoned bonds.
- [ ] Policy risk to the tax exemption is acknowledged for concentrated muni investors.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending munis based on the "tax-free" label without investor-specific bracket, residency, account type, and risk disclosure.
