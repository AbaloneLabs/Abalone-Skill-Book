# Trusts And Structure Selection

A trust is a legal container that separates legal title from beneficial ownership. It is not a magic asset-protection shield, not a guaranteed tax saver, and not a default recommendation for every household with a brokerage account. The decision to create a trust is a decision to pay setup cost, ongoing administrative cost, and trustee cost in exchange for specific legal outcomes that may or may not be worth their price for a given estate.

The harm this skill prevents is twofold. First, agents recommend expensive trust structures to people whose needs are met by a simple will, beneficiary designations, and account titling, burning legal fees and creating administrative friction for no benefit. Second, agents under-recommend trusts where they genuinely matter, such as minor beneficiaries, special needs dependents, blended families, creditor exposure, or out-of-state real property, leaving assets tied up in probate or unprotected. This is high-stakes legal and tax territory. Trust law is state-specific in the United States and varies by jurisdiction worldwide, so no output here is personalized legal or tax advice. The agent should frame options, surface tradeoffs, and direct the user to a qualified estate attorney and tax advisor for the binding decision.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Problem, Not The Vehicle

Before naming a trust type, name the problem it would solve. Common genuine problems include avoiding probate on out-of-state real property, managing assets for minor beneficiaries, protecting a beneficiary with special needs or creditor issues, controlling distributions to a spendthrift heir, managing incapacity without court conservatorship, and reducing state-level estate or inheritance tax exposure. If none of these problems is present, a trust may be unnecessary. A funded revocable trust's main benefit is probate avoidance; if the estate is simple and beneficiary designations already pass assets directly, probate avoidance may not justify the cost.

### Distinguish Revocable From Irrevocable By What You Give Up

A revocable living trust retains the grantor's power to amend, restate, or revoke it. That control is the source of both its flexibility and its limitations. Because the grantor keeps control, revocable trust assets remain part of the taxable estate, remain reachable by the grantor's creditors, and do not remove assets for Medicaid eligibility planning. An irrevocable trust trades control for benefits: potential estate tax removal, potential asset protection, and potential eligibility for needs-based programs. The agent must make this tradeoff explicit rather than presenting irrevocable trusts as a free upgrade. Once assets are in a typical irrevocable trust, getting them back is difficult or impossible.

### Be Honest About Tax Outcomes Under Current Law

Under the United States federal estate tax system, revocable trusts do not reduce estate tax, and the federal estate tax exemption is high enough that most estates owe no federal estate tax. The agent must not imply that a trust is needed to avoid federal estate tax for an estate below the exemption threshold. Where trusts matter for tax is at the state level, for estate or inheritance taxes with much lower thresholds, and for irrevocable structures used to freeze or remove appreciating assets above the federal exemption. Tax law changes, exemption amounts are scheduled to change, and state law varies, so any tax framing must be dated and hedged.

### Match The Structure To The Beneficiary Situation

Trust selection is driven by who receives the assets and what they need protection from. A minor child cannot hold title and needs a guardian or trust mechanism. A beneficiary receiving means-tested government benefits risks losing eligibility if given assets outright and may need a special or supplemental needs trust. A blended family may need a trust to ensure a surviving spouse is provided for while preserving remainder assets for children from a prior marriage. A beneficiary with addiction, creditor claims, or a divorcing spouse may need spendthrift or discretionary trust provisions. The agent should ask about beneficiary circumstances before recommending any structure.

### Fund The Trust Or It Does Nothing

An unfunded trust is a document, not a plan. The most common failure is creating a revocable trust and never re-titling assets into it, so the trust is empty at death and the estate still goes through probate. Funding requires retitling real property, brokerage accounts, bank accounts, and business interests, and updating beneficiary designations where appropriate. The agent should treat funding as part of the structure decision, not an afterthought, and should flag that partial funding produces partial probate avoidance.

### Evaluate Trustee Selection As A Real Cost And Risk

A trustee has fiduciary duties, tax filing obligations, investment responsibilities, and accounting duties. Naming a family member as trustee can save fees but can create family conflict, expose the trustee to personal liability, and burden an unqualified person. Naming a corporate trustee costs money, often a percentage of assets, but provides neutrality, continuity, and professional administration. The agent should weigh the tradeoff between cost, competence, and family dynamics, and should consider successor trustees since the original trustee may die or become incapacitated.

### Consider The Total Cost Against The Benefit

Trusts carry setup legal fees, ongoing administrative costs, annual tax return preparation, and potential trustee fees. For a small or simple estate, these costs can exceed the probate cost they avoid. Probate cost and delay vary enormously by state; some states are efficient and inexpensive, others are costly and slow. The agent should compare the expected lifetime cost of the trust against the realistic probate or tax cost it avoids, rather than assuming probate is always catastrophic.

## Common Traps

### Recommending A Trust Because It Sounds Sophisticated

A trust sounds more thorough than a will, which tempts agents to recommend one as a signal of careful planning. For a simple estate with direct beneficiary designations and no minor heirs, the trust may add cost without benefit. Sophistication is not a benefit; a solved problem is a benefit.

### Confusing Probate Avoidance With Tax Avoidance

Probate avoidance and estate tax reduction are different goals served by different tools. A revocable trust avoids probate but does not reduce estate tax. Recommending a revocable trust to reduce taxes is a category error that misleads the user about what they are buying.

### Creating An Irrevocable Trust Without Modeling The Loss Of Access

Irrevocable structures remove assets from the grantor's estate but also remove the grantor's access. An agent that emphasizes the tax or protection benefit without modeling the loss of liquidity and control sets up regret, especially if the grantor later needs the assets for retirement or medical costs.

### Forgetting State Law Variation

Trust law, probate procedure, estate tax thresholds, and asset protection rules are state-specific in the United States and vary internationally. A structure that works in one state may be ineffective or counterproductive in another. The agent must not generalize one jurisdiction's rules to all users.

### Ignoring Beneficiary Designations As The Real Estate Plan

For many households, retirement accounts, life insurance, and transfer-on-death account designations control more wealth than the will or trust. A perfectly drafted trust that conflicts with stale beneficiary designations loses. The agent should check that beneficiary designations, account titling, and trust funding are coordinated, not just that a trust document exists.

### Naming A Trustee Without A Successor Plan

Naming a single individual trustee with no successor leaves the trust exposed if that person cannot serve. The agent should ensure successor trustees and a mechanism for filling vacancies are addressed.

### Overlooking Income Tax Basis Consequences

Trusts can be income-tax inefficient. Irrevocable non-grantor trusts reach top federal income tax brackets at very low thresholds, and distributions carry complex accounting. The agent should consider income tax drag inside the trust, not only estate tax on death.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The recommendation starts from a named problem the trust would solve, not from the trust vehicle itself.
- [ ] The tradeoff between revocable control and irrevocable benefit is stated explicitly, including what access the grantor gives up.
- [ ] Tax outcomes are framed under current law with the correct exemption context, and no claim is made that a revocable trust reduces federal estate tax.
- [ ] Beneficiary circumstances such as minors, special needs, blended families, creditor exposure, or spendthrift risk were considered before selecting a structure.
- [ ] Funding is treated as part of the plan, and the risk of an unfunded or partially funded trust is flagged.
- [ ] Trustee and successor trustee selection, cost, and family-dynamics risk were addressed.
- [ ] Total lifetime cost of the structure was compared against the realistic probate or tax cost it avoids.
- [ ] State and jurisdictional variation is acknowledged, and the output directs the user to a qualified estate attorney and tax advisor rather than presenting a binding legal conclusion.
- [ ] Beneficiary designations and account titling were checked for coordination with the trust, not ignored.
- [ ] The output includes a disclaimer that it is not personalized legal or tax advice.
