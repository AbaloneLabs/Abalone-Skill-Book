# Roth Conversion Strategy

A Roth conversion moves assets from a traditional tax-deferred account (where contributions were pre-tax and growth is tax-deferred) to a Roth account (where withdrawals are tax-free), by recognizing the converted amount as ordinary income in the year of conversion. The trade is paying tax now to secure tax-free growth and withdrawals forever after. The judgment problem is that Roth conversions are powerful but conditional, and they are routinely recommended too broadly or sized too aggressively. The benefit depends entirely on the comparison of the current tax rate paid on conversion versus the future tax rate avoided, modified by the time horizon, the source of funds to pay the tax, the five-year rules, and interactions with RMDs, IRMAA, and estate goals. Agents tend to treat conversions as universally good (because tax-free sounds appealing) or to size them to the maximum bracket without modeling the downstream effects. The skill is evaluating conversions as a rate-arbitrage and timing decision with honest modeling of the interacting variables.

This skill is for deciding whether, when, and how much to convert, with awareness of the conditions under which it helps.

## Core Rules

### Anchor The Decision On Current Versus Future Tax Rates

The core logic of a Roth conversion is a tax-rate arbitrage: pay tax now at rate X to avoid tax later at rate Y. The conversion is beneficial when the future rate avoided exceeds the current rate paid, and detrimental when it does not.

Assess the rate comparison:

- the current marginal rate that the conversion income will be taxed at;
- the expected future marginal rate on the traditional balances if not converted (during retirement, during RMDs, or to heirs);
- the factors that could raise future rates (RMDs, large traditional balances, tax law changes, surviving-spouse filing-status change);
- the factors that could lower future rates (low income in early retirement, charitable giving, medical deductions).

If the future rate is expected to be higher, conversion favors paying now. If lower, deferral favors leaving the balance traditional. The decision is a forward-looking rate comparison, not a reflex toward tax-free accounts.

### Model The Conversion Across Multiple Years

A conversion is rarely a one-time event; the largest benefit comes from a multi-year program of conversions, especially in the low-income years of early retirement before Social Security and RMDs begin. Sizing a single conversion in isolation misses the opportunity.

Plan multi-year conversions by:

- mapping the income trajectory across retirement (low early, higher once Social Security and RMDs begin);
- converting in the low-income years to fill target brackets each year;
- spreading conversions across years to avoid spiking into higher brackets in any one year;
- projecting the cumulative tax paid versus the cumulative future tax avoided over the horizon.

A series of conversions filling the 12% or 22% bracket across several early-retirement years often dominates a single large conversion or no conversion at all. The optimization is multi-year, not single-year.

### Pay The Conversion Tax From Outside Sources

How the conversion tax is paid dramatically affects its value. Paying the tax from the converted balance undermines the conversion; paying from taxable funds preserves it.

Understand:

- paying tax from outside (taxable savings, cash) leaves the full converted amount growing tax-free in the Roth;
- paying tax by withholding from the converted balance reduces the Roth balance and, if the owner is under 59.5, can trigger early-distribution penalties on the withheld amount;
- the source of tax funds is itself a tax decision (selling appreciated taxable assets realizes gains);
- conversions are most valuable when the investor has taxable funds to pay the tax without disrupting the portfolio.

A conversion funded by withholding from itself is a partial conversion with a penalty risk, and it often fails the rate-arbitrage test. Convert only what can be taxed from outside sources.

### Respect The Five-Year Rules And Conversion Buckets

Roth accounts carry multiple five-year clocks that affect when converted and earnings amounts can be withdrawn tax- and penalty-free. Mishandling these clocks can create unexpected tax or penalties.

Track:

- the five-year rule for conversions (each conversion has its own five-year clock for penalty-free withdrawal of the converted principal, relevant before age 59.5);
- the five-year rule for Roth earnings (earnings are tax-free only after the account has been open five years and the owner is 59.5);
- the ordering of Roth distributions (contributions, then conversions, then earnings), which determines what comes out first;
- the separate five-year clocks for each conversion in some interpretations.

For older retirees converting late, the five-year clocks may limit the benefit if the funds are needed soon. For younger investors with decades, the clocks are easily satisfied. Align conversion timing with the expected need for the funds.

### Manage The Interactions With RMDs, IRMAA, And Social Security

Conversions do not happen in isolation; they add to income and interact with other tax and benefit systems. The interactions can create hidden costs that exceed the apparent conversion tax.

Account for:

- conversions after RMD age must be done in addition to (not in lieu of) the RMD, which can spike income;
- conversion income can trigger or increase IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges two years later;
- conversion income increases combined income, raising the taxable portion of Social Security;
- conversion income can phase out deductions, credits, and other income-sensitive benefits.

A conversion that fits the bracket on paper can still cost more through IRMAA and Social Security taxation. Model the full combined-income effect, not just the marginal rate on the conversion.

### Consider Estate And Beneficiary Planning

Roth accounts have powerful estate-planning advantages: they have no RMDs during the original owner's life, and they pass to heirs tax-free (though most non-spouse heirs face a 10-year distribution window). Conversions can be an estate-efficiency tool.

Factor in:

- Roth accounts reduce the future RMD burden and the taxable income of the estate and heirs;
- the heir's tax rate versus the retiree's (converting at the retiree's lower rate to benefit a higher-bracket heir can be very efficient);
- the 10-year distribution rule for most non-spouse beneficiaries, which concentrates inherited Roth withdrawals;
- the use of conversions to reduce a large traditional balance that would otherwise force high-RMD, high-bracket withdrawals.

For investors focused on legacy, conversions can transfer significant wealth tax-efficiently. For those who will spend most of the balance, the lifetime rate arbitrage matters more than the estate angle.

### Reassess Annually As Circumstances And Rates Change

The conversion decision is sensitive to assumptions about future rates, income, and law, all of which change. A conversion plan set once and never revisited drifts out of optimality.

Reassess each year:

- the current income and bracket and the conversion capacity;
- changes in tax law, brackets, and thresholds;
- the evolving balance of traditional versus Roth accounts;
- life changes (health, retirement timing, marital status, inheritances) that shift the rate trajectory.

The conversion program is a year-by-year decision, not a one-time commitment. Convert in the years it is clearly beneficial, defer in the years it is not, and keep the optionality to respond to change.

## Common Traps

### Treating Conversions As Universally Good

Tax-free growth is appealing, but the conversion is only beneficial if the future rate avoided exceeds the current rate paid. A conversion into a higher current bracket can destroy value.

### Sizing To The Maximum Bracket Without Modeling Downstream

Converting to the top of a bracket without considering IRMAA, Social Security taxation, and future RMDs can cost more than the apparent conversion tax.

### Paying Conversion Tax From The Converted Balance

Withholding from the conversion reduces the Roth balance and can trigger penalties. Conversions are most valuable when funded from outside sources.

### Ignoring The Five-Year Rules

Each conversion has its own clock, and earnings have a separate clock. Mishandling these can create tax or penalties on early withdrawals.

### Overlooking IRMAA And Social Security Interactions

Conversion income can trigger Medicare surcharges and increase Social Security taxation two layers of hidden cost that exceed the marginal rate.

### Converting When Future Rates Will Be Lower

If the retiree will be in a lower bracket in retirement (low income, large deductions), deferral beats conversion. The rate comparison governs.

### One-Time Instead Of Multi-Year Planning

The largest benefit comes from a series of conversions across low-income years, not a single conversion. Single-year sizing misses the opportunity.

### Forgetting Estate And Heir Considerations

Roth accounts pass tax-free and reduce heir RMD burden. Converting at a lower rate to benefit higher-bracket heirs can be highly efficient.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The conversion decision is anchored on a forward-looking comparison of the current marginal rate paid versus the expected future rate avoided, not on the appeal of tax-free growth alone.
- [ ] Conversions are modeled across multiple years, filling target brackets in low-income early-retirement years, not sized as a single event.
- [ ] The conversion tax is planned to be paid from outside (taxable) sources, preserving the full converted balance in the Roth and avoiding withholding penalties.
- [ ] The five-year rules for conversions and earnings, and the Roth distribution ordering, are understood and aligned with the expected need for the funds.
- [ ] Interactions with RMDs (conversions are in addition to, not instead of, RMDs after RMD age), IRMAA surcharges, and Social Security taxation are modeled via combined income, not just the marginal rate.
- [ ] Estate and beneficiary planning (no lifetime RMDs, tax-free inheritance, 10-year heir distribution, heir versus retiree rate) is factored into the conversion decision.
- [ ] The conversion plan is reassessed annually as income, tax law, balances, and life circumstances change, preserving the optionality to convert in good years and defer in others.
