# Three-Fund Portfolio

The three-fund portfolio is a minimalist construction that holds a domestic total stock market fund, an international total stock market fund, and a total bond market fund. The thesis is that three broad, low-cost funds capture global diversification across equities and high-quality fixed income, control risk through the bond allocation, and do so with minimal cost, complexity, and behavioral friction. The judgment problem is that the simplicity is deceptive. The real decisions, allocation weights, international exposure, rebalancing discipline, and tax placement, determine whether the portfolio succeeds, and investors routinely get these wrong by chasing performance, drifting from targets, or overcomplicating what should be simple. The three-fund portfolio works not because it is elaborate but because it is complete, low-cost, and holdable, and the discipline is to keep it that way.

This skill is for constructing and maintaining a three-fund portfolio with attention to the decisions that actually drive outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Choose Broad, Low-Cost, Total-Market Funds

The power of the three-fund portfolio comes from capturing entire markets at minimal cost. Fund selection should prioritize breadth and low fees.

Select:

- a domestic total stock market index fund covering the entire investable market (large, mid, and small cap);
- an international total stock market index fund covering developed and emerging markets;
- a total bond market index fund covering investment-grade government and corporate bonds;
- funds with the lowest expense ratios for comparable exposure, favoring index mutual funds or ETFs;
- funds from reputable providers with sufficient assets, liquidity, and tracking precision.

Avoid substituting narrow, sector, or actively managed funds, which sacrifice diversification and raise costs. The "total market" breadth is what delivers the diversification benefit.

### Set The Allocation By Horizon And Risk Capacity

The split between equities and bonds is the primary driver of both return and risk. It must match the investor's time horizon and ability to bear loss.

Determine allocation:

- longer horizons (decades to retirement) support higher equity allocations, since equities offer higher expected returns and time absorbs volatility;
- shorter horizons or near-term liquidity needs require higher bond allocations to reduce sequence risk;
- stable human capital (secure income) supports higher equity allocations; unstable or cyclical income argues for more bonds;
- the investor's behavioral tolerance for drawdowns sets a ceiling, since an allocation sold in a panic was too aggressive.

A common heuristic is "bonds equal to age" or "110 minus age in equities," but these are starting points. The allocation must be personalized to horizon, capacity, and tolerance, and it must be holdable through a bear market.

### Decide International Exposure Deliberately

The domestic-to-international split within equities is a meaningful decision that investors often make by default or by recent performance.

Consider:

- domestic bias is common but concentrates risk in a single country's economy, currency, and market;
- international exposure diversifies across economies, currencies, and market cycles;
- the appropriate split depends on the investor's home country, its market's global share, and currency considerations;
- a range of 20-40% international within equities is common, but the right level is personal;
- currency exposure is partly a diversifier and partly a volatility source; consider whether to hedge.

The decision should be deliberate and based on diversification logic, not on which segment performed best recently. Performance chasing in the domestic-international split is a common error.

### Establish A Clear Rebalancing Discipline

Rebalancing maintains the target allocation and controls risk, but it must be rule-based to avoid emotional drift.

Choose a rebalancing approach:

- calendar rebalancing (e.g., annually or semi-annually) on a fixed schedule;
- threshold rebalancing (e.g., when any asset class drifts 5 percentage points from target);
- a hybrid that checks on a calendar but rebalances only when thresholds are breached;
- rebalancing with new contributions or withdrawals, which is tax-efficient and low-cost.

The specific rule matters less than consistency. The discipline is to rebalance back toward target, which means selling what has risen and buying what has fallen, a behaviorally difficult but risk-controlling action.

### Place Assets Tax-Efficiently

For investors using both taxable and tax-advantaged accounts, asset location improves after-tax returns without changing the overall allocation.

Apply location principles:

- bonds, which generate ordinary income, are generally most tax-efficient in tax-advantaged accounts;
- broad equity index funds, which generate qualified dividends and long-term gains, are relatively tax-efficient in taxable accounts;
- international equity in taxable accounts may allow the foreign tax credit;
- match the most tax-inefficient assets to the most tax-advantaged accounts, while keeping the overall target allocation.

Asset location is a refinement that adds value over time but should not override the core allocation or add excessive complexity.

### Keep The Portfolio Simple And Avoid Drift

The three-fund portfolio's strength is its simplicity. Adding funds, tilts, or thematic exposures erodes the benefit and introduces tracking error and behavioral temptation.

Maintain simplicity:

- resist adding a fourth, fifth, or tenth fund unless there is a compelling, evidence-based reason;
- avoid performance-chasing additions after a segment outperforms;
- re-evaluate the allocation periodically but avoid frequent changes based on market conditions;
- keep the portfolio understandable and maintainable over decades.

An investor who holds three funds for thirty years, rebalances consistently, and minimizes costs and taxes will outperform most investors who add complexity and chase performance. Simplicity is the strategy, not a compromise.

## Common Traps

### Overcomplicating With Additional Funds

Each added fund increases complexity, cost, and the temptation to tinker. The three-fund design is intentionally minimal.

### Setting Allocation By Recent Performance

Chasing the asset class that performed best recently leads to buying high and selling low. Allocation should be based on horizon and risk capacity.

### Neglecting International Diversification

Home-country bias concentrates risk. International exposure should be a deliberate decision, not a default.

### Drifting From The Target Allocation

Without rebalancing, rising asset classes dominate and raise risk. Drift silently transforms a balanced portfolio into an aggressive one.

### Abandoning Bonds In Bull Markets

Bonds feel like a drag during equity bull markets, but they stabilize the portfolio and provide rebalancing reserves in bear markets.

### Letting Taxes Erode Returns

Poor asset location and high turnover generate unnecessary tax drag. Tax efficiency matters over long horizons.

### Confusing Simplicity With Inadequacy

The three-fund portfolio captures global markets at low cost. Its simplicity is a strength, not a limitation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The portfolio uses broad, low-cost, total-market index funds for domestic equity, international equity, and total bonds.
- [ ] The equity-bond allocation is set by time horizon, risk capacity (income, liabilities), and behavioral tolerance, and is holdable through a bear market.
- [ ] International exposure is decided deliberately based on diversification and currency logic, not recent performance.
- [ ] A clear, consistent rebalancing rule (calendar, threshold, or contribution-based) is established and followed.
- [ ] Assets are placed tax-efficiently across taxable and tax-advantaged accounts where applicable.
- [ ] The portfolio remains simple, with additions resisted unless evidence-based and cost-justified.
- [ ] The allocation is reviewed periodically but not changed based on short-term market movements or performance chasing.
- [ ] The guidance flags that markets decline sharply at times, that discipline is required to hold and rebalance through drawdowns, that the allocation must match personal risk capacity, and that professional advice may be warranted for complex tax or estate situations.
