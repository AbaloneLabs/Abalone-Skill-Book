# Inflation-Linked Bonds

Inflation-linked bonds (such as US TIPS, UK index-linked gilts, and euro area linkers) are bonds whose principal or coupon is adjusted for inflation, providing a return that is defined in real (purchasing-power) terms rather than nominal terms. Their central promise is protection: the investor is guaranteed a real return regardless of inflation, which makes them unique among liquid assets. The judgment problem is that inflation-linked bonds are widely misunderstood. Agents confuse the real yield with the nominal yield, misread the breakeven inflation rate, ignore the indexation lag and deflation floor, and assume linkers always protect against inflation when in fact they outperform nominal bonds only when realized inflation exceeds the breakeven. The skill is analyzing inflation-linked bonds in real terms, understanding breakevens as the market's inflation expectation, and judging when linkers genuinely hedge inflation risk.

This skill is for evaluating inflation-linked bonds with awareness of their mechanics and their conditional protection.

## Core Rules

### Think In Real Yields, Not Nominal Coupons

The defining feature of an inflation-linked bond is that its return is specified in real terms. The real yield is the true compensation for lending; the inflation adjustment preserves purchasing power on top of that.

Understand:

- the real yield is the return above inflation that the bond pays;
- the coupon and principal are inflation-adjusted, so nominal cash flows rise with inflation;
- the real yield, not the nominal coupon, is the figure to compare across linkers and to nominal bonds;
- a negative real yield means the investor is paying (in real terms) for the inflation protection.

An investor who compares a linker's low nominal coupon to a nominal bond's higher coupon and concludes the linker is inferior has made the category error. The comparison is real yield versus nominal yield minus expected inflation, not coupon versus coupon.

### Use The Breakeven Inflation Rate As The Decision Metric

The breakeven inflation rate is the inflation level at which an inflation-linked bond and a comparable nominal bond deliver the same return. It is the market's implied inflation expectation, derived from the gap between nominal and real yields.

Apply breakevens by:

- computing breakeven as nominal yield minus real yield for matched-maturity bonds;
- comparing the breakeven to your own inflation expectation: if you expect inflation above breakeven, linkers should outperform; below, nominals should;
- tracking breakevens over time and across maturities to read the market's inflation view;
- recognizing that breakevens embed an inflation risk premium, so they overstate pure expectations somewhat.

The breakeven is the single most important number for deciding between linkers and nominals. Linkers are not automatically good inflation hedges; they outperform only when realized inflation exceeds the breakeven the market has priced.

### Understand The Indexation Mechanism And Its Lag

The inflation adjustment is mechanical, but it is not instantaneous. Indexation lag means the adjustment reflects past inflation, not current, which affects the hedge quality.

Examine:

- the index used (CPI, RPI, HICP) and its construction, since different indices measure inflation differently;
- the indexation lag (typically two to three months), which means a sudden inflation spike is not fully reflected immediately;
- the frequency and method of principal adjustment;
- whether the adjustment is to principal (US TIPS) or to the coupon (some other markets).

A linker with a three-month lag provides an imperfect hedge against a sudden inflation surprise, because the adjustment trails the inflation. The mechanism details determine how precise the real-return guarantee actually is.

### Account For The Deflation Floor

Most inflation-linked bonds have a deflation floor: at maturity, the investor receives at least the original par, even if cumulative inflation has been negative. This protects against deflation but affects the bond's behavior.

Consider:

- the floor provides downside protection in deflation, making the bond valuable when deflation is a risk;
- the floor's value depends on the cumulative inflation since issuance (a seasoned bond with high cumulative indexation has a distant floor; a newly issued bond has a near floor);
- near the floor, the bond's convexity and behavior change, behaving more like a nominal bond.

The deflation floor is a valuable embedded option that agents often overlook. It makes newly issued linkers particularly attractive when deflation is plausible.

### Judge Whether Linkers Genuinely Hedge The Specific Inflation Risk

Not all inflation is the same, and a CPI-linked bond may not hedge the inflation that actually threatens the investor. The indexation must match the liability or concern.

Assess:

- whether the index (CPI) reflects the investor's actual cost inflation (medical costs, education, housing may inflate faster or differently than headline CPI);
- whether the investor's liabilities or spending are wage-linked, commodity-linked, or CPI-linked;
- the basis risk between the bond's index and the investor's real exposure;
- whether equities, real assets, or commodities might hedge the specific inflation better.

A TIPS bond hedges CPI inflation, not necessarily the investor's personal inflation. Mismatched indexation leaves residual real risk even with a full linker allocation.

### Recognize The Liquidity And Behavioral Differences

Inflation-linked bonds often trade in thinner, less liquid markets than nominal government bonds, and their behavior differs in stress.

Account for:

- lower liquidity and wider bid-ask spreads in the linker market, especially off-the-run issues;
- the tendency for breakevens to widen and linkers to underperform in liquidity-driven flights to quality (when nominal bonds rally but real yields rise);
- the tax treatment of inflation adjustments in some jurisdictions (phantom income tax on accrued inflation in taxable US TIPS accounts);
- the longer effective duration of real-yield exposure, which behaves differently from nominal duration.

Linkers are not always a safe haven. In liquidity crises, nominal bonds often outperform as real yields rise and breakevens widen. Liquidity and tax treatment matter as much as the inflation math.

### Match Linker Exposure To Objectives And Horizon

Inflation-linked bonds suit some objectives and not others. The allocation should reflect the investor's real liabilities, horizon, and view on breakevens.

Decide:

- whether the investor has real (inflation-linked) liabilities that warrant matching (retirement spending, real income needs);
- whether current real yields and breakevens offer fair compensation;
- the horizon: linkers' inflation protection compounds over long horizons but is noisy over short ones;
- the role in the portfolio: a strategic inflation hedge, a tactical bet on breakevens, or a real-yield duration position.

Linkers are powerful for long-horizon real-liability matching and for investors who fear inflation above breakevens. They are not a universal holding; the decision depends on real yield, breakeven, and the investor's specific inflation exposure.

## Common Traps

### Comparing Linker Coupons To Nominal Coupons

The comparison is real yield versus nominal yield minus expected inflation, not coupon versus coupon. Low nominal coupons on linkers are not inferior by definition.

### Assuming Linkers Always Hedge Inflation

Linkers outperform nominals only when realized inflation exceeds the breakeven. If inflation comes in below breakeven, nominals win.

### Ignoring The Breakeven Inflation Rate

The breakeven is the decision metric. Buying linkers without comparing breakevens to your inflation expectation is uninformed.

### Overlooking Indexation Lag

The inflation adjustment lags actual inflation by months. A sudden spike is not fully hedged immediately.

### Forgetting The Deflation Floor

The floor is a valuable embedded option, especially for newly issued bonds when deflation is possible. Ignoring it understates the linker's value.

### Mismatching The Index To The Exposure

A CPI linker hedges CPI inflation, not necessarily the investor's personal or liability inflation. Basis risk remains.

### Ignoring Liquidity And Tax Effects

Linker markets are thinner, breakevens widen in liquidity flights, and some jurisdictions tax phantom inflation income. These reduce the realized benefit.

### Treating Linkers As A Risk-Free Safe Haven

In liquidity crises, real yields can rise and linkers underperform nominal bonds. They are real-return assets, not unconditional safe havens.

- [ ] The breakeven inflation rate is computed and compared to the investor's own inflation expectation, recognizing that linkers outperform only when realized inflation exceeds breakeven.
