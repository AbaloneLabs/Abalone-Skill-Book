# Money Market And Sweep Yields

Where idle cash sits is not a trivial detail. A brokerage sweep account, a government money market fund, and a prime money market fund differ in yield, in the quality of their backing, in how they behave under stress, and in who guarantees them. The judgment problem is that investors routinely leave large balances in default sweep options that pay a fraction of the available market yield, while others chase a few extra basis points by taking hidden liquidity or credit risk inside an instrument they believe is cash. Both errors are invisible in calm markets and expensive in stressed ones.

This skill is for deciding where to hold safe cash to capture a fair yield without silently taking risk or breaching insurance limits. It is not personalized financial advice; product availability, yields, and rules vary by provider and jurisdiction and change frequently.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Default Sweep Cash Usually Pays Well Below Market

Most brokerages default uninvested cash into a proprietary sweep option — often an affiliated bank deposit or an in-house money fund — whose yield is set by the firm, not the market. These defaults frequently pay far less than comparable government money market funds available on the same platform.

The cost compounds:

- a 2 to 4 percentage point gap on a large balance is thousands per year;
- the gap is easy to miss because the cash appears to be "invested" and earning something;
- the default exists for the firm's economics, not the investor's yield.

Identify the current sweep yield explicitly and compare it to the best available government money fund on the platform before leaving balances idle.

### Compare Yields On A Like-For-Like Basis Using The 7-Day SEC Yield

Money market fund yields are quoted in inconsistent ways. The standardized 7-day SEC yield (with or without fee waivers) is the closest to a comparable figure.

When comparing:

- use the 7-day SEC yield, not the trailing distribution or a teaser introductory rate;
- check whether the quoted yield relies on a fee waiver that will expire;
- confirm the expense ratio, since two funds holding the same paper can differ only on cost;
- account for any state tax advantage of government-only funds, which hold US Treasury paper often exempt from state and local income tax.

A fund quoting a higher gross yield but carrying higher expenses or taxable paper may deliver less after tax.

### Match The Fund Type To The Risk Tolerance For Stress

Money market funds are not all the same in a crisis.

- **Government money funds** hold US Treasury and agency paper; lowest credit risk; historically the most stable; often state-tax advantaged.
- **Treasury-only funds** hold direct US government obligations; the safest and most liquid.
- **Prime funds** hold commercial paper, certificates of deposit, and corporate IOUs; higher yield; higher credit and liquidity risk; subject to floating NAV, liquidity fees, and redemption gates.

For cash that must be available on demand in any market condition, government or Treasury-only funds are the conservative choice. Prime funds may be acceptable for cash where a slightly higher yield is worth accepting gating risk, but only if the investor understands that access could be restricted.

### Understand The Floating NAV, Gates, And Fees For Prime And Tax-Exempt Funds

Post-crisis reforms changed how non-government money funds behave under stress.

Know the rules:

- prime institutional and retail funds, and tax-exempt funds, generally have a floating net asset value rather than a fixed one dollar;
- if weekly liquid assets fall below thresholds, the fund's board can impose liquidity fees of up to 2 percent on redemptions or temporarily suspend redemptions (gates);
- government funds are exempt from these mandatory gates and fees, which is a structural reason they are considered safer for emergency access.

An investor who believes prime cash is "just like a bank account" may be surprised when a gate blocks access during the exact period they need the money. Match the instrument to the need for certainty.

### Get FDIC Coverage Right Across Accounts

Bank sweep deposits rely on FDIC insurance, which has limits and aggregation rules that investors frequently misunderstand.

Key points:

- the standard maximum is a fixed dollar amount per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category;
- balances swept across a network of banks can extend coverage, but only if the program is structured and documented correctly;
- coverage is per bank, so multiple accounts at the same insured bank under the same ownership category aggregate together;
- SIPC coverage for brokerage accounts protects against the firm's failure and missing customer securities, not against a decline in the value of a money fund — it is a different kind of protection.

Do not assume "the government insures it" without checking which insurer, which limit, and which ownership category applies. For balances above the insured limit, a government money fund holding direct Treasury obligations carries essentially no credit risk to the US government regardless of the dollar amount.

### Separate The Need For Yield From The Need For Certainty

The right cash vehicle depends on what the cash is for.

- emergency and operating cash should prioritize certainty of access over the last basis point;
- larger balances held for months may justify the small operational effort of a Treasury bill ladder or a government fund;
- cash meant to be deployed soon should sit where redemption is instant and free.

Decide the purpose first, then choose the instrument. Chasing yield without naming the purpose leads to risk in the wrong place.

### Watch For Teaser Rates And Tiered Pricing

Some platforms offer promotional yields on new deposits or on balances above thresholds that later revert. Confirm whether a high advertised rate is permanent, time-limited, or conditional on balance size, and re-check periodically.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Cash In The Default Sweep Indefinitely

The sweep default pays the firm, not the investor. The single most common cash error is never moving idle balances to a higher-yielding government fund on the same platform.

### Comparing Gross Yields And Ignoring Expenses

Two funds holding identical paper can differ only by cost. A higher headline yield with a higher expense ratio can deliver less net.

### Assuming All Money Funds Are Equally Safe

Prime and tax-exempt funds carry credit, liquidity, gating, and floating-NAV risk that government and Treasury funds do not. Treating them as interchangeable is a hidden risk in the cash bucket.

### Forgetting The State Tax Advantage Of Government Funds

For investors in high-tax states, the state-tax exemption on Treasury interest can make a government fund's after-tax yield higher than a prime fund's higher pre-tax yield.

### Overlooking FDIC Aggregation Across Accounts

Multiple accounts at the same bank under the same ownership category share one limit. A sweep across affiliated banks only extends coverage if properly structured.

### Confusing SIPC With FDIC

SIPC does not protect against investment losses, including a money fund's NAV declining. It protects against the broker's failure and missing customer assets. Different protection, different purpose.

### Chasing A Few Basis Points Into Complexity

Moving cash into complex "cash plus" strategies, floating-rate bank loan funds, or ultra-short credit funds for a small yield pickup reintroduces the volatility and liquidity risk the cash bucket was meant to avoid.

- [ ] Yields were compared on a like-for-like 7-day SEC yield basis, accounting for fee waivers and expense ratios.
- [ ] The fund type (Treasury, government, prime, tax-exempt) was matched to the investor's need for certainty, with prime gating and floating-NAV risk understood.
