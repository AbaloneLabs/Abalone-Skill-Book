# Sector And Geographic Concentration

Sector and geographic concentration is the overexposure of a portfolio to a single industry, country, region, currency, or regulatory regime. It is often invisible because a portfolio can hold hundreds of names and still be concentrated by sector or country, especially through market-cap-weighted indices that load on dominant sectors or a home market. The judgment problem is that this concentration can be deliberate (a thesis) or accidental (drift, home bias, index construction), and the two call for opposite responses. Agents often see "many holdings" and assume diversification, missing that the risk is one layer up.

This skill is for identifying, measuring, and deciding about sector and geographic concentration.

## Core Rules

### Measure Concentration Above The Security Level

Issuer count is misleading. A portfolio of 500 US large-cap tech stocks is concentrated in one sector and one country regardless of name count.

Measure at multiple levels:

- sector and industry weights versus a global benchmark;
- country and region weights;
- currency exposure;
- market-cap segment (large versus small);
- regulatory and legal-jurisdiction exposure;
- common risk factors that span the sector or region.

Always compare to a relevant diversified benchmark to see whether the concentration is intentional tilt or accidental drift.

### Distinguish Deliberate Tilt From Accidental Drift

Concentration that reflects a reasoned thesis is a bet the investor chose. Concentration that arose from drift, home bias, or index construction is a bet the investor did not choose and may not know they hold.

Investigate the source:

- home bias from familiarity, access, or tax;
- market-cap-weighted index concentration in dominant sectors or names;
- drift from a sector or region outperforming;
- currency concentration from a home-currency bias;
- factor concentration behind sector labels.

Deliberate concentration requires an edge, a thesis, and a survival plan. Accidental concentration should usually be reduced, because there is no edge, only inertia.

### Assess Home Bias Honestly

Home bias, over-weighting one's own country, is nearly universal and sometimes rational (familiarity, lower costs, tax), but it is concentration and should be explicit.

Weigh:

- the rationale (information advantage, currency alignment, lower cost, tax efficiency);
- the cost (foregone diversification, single-country risk);
- the size of the bias versus a global market portfolio;
- the interaction with the investor's human capital and home-currency obligations.

Some home bias may be justified, but it should be a conscious decision with a defined size, not a default that grows unnoticed. A retiree spending in the home currency has a stronger case for home bias than a young global investor.

### Evaluate Sector Risk Across The Cycle

Sectors have different cyclical behavior. A sector concentration is also a bet on the cycle and the regime.

Analyze:

- the sector's cyclicality and sensitivity to growth, rates, and inflation;
- current position in the sector's cycle;
- valuation relative to history and the market;
- the concentration of the sector in a few dominant names;
- regulatory, technology, and disruption risk.

A sector that has outperformed for years is often expensive and crowded. Concentration in a recent winner is frequently late-cycle and prone to mean reversion.

### Evaluate Geographic And Country Risk

Country concentration exposes the portfolio to a single economy, currency, legal system, and policy regime.

Assess:

- economic diversification and concentration within the country;
- political, regulatory, and expropriation risk;
- currency risk and the investor's spending currency;
- market development, liquidity, and capital controls;
- the country's weight in global benchmarks (to detect unintentional concentration).

A single country, however familiar, carries sovereign and regime risk that diversification across countries reduces. Geographic concentration should be deliberate and sized to the investor's risk capacity.

### Account For Market-Cap Concentration

Even a diversified sector or country allocation can be concentrated if it is market-cap-weighted and dominated by a few huge names.

Check:

- the share of the sector or country held in its top few names;
- whether the concentration reflects the index or an active choice;
- the crowding and valuation of the dominant names;
- the effect of equal-weighting or capping if concentration is a concern.

A market-cap-weighted portfolio can have a large fraction of its risk in a handful of mega-cap stocks. This is concentration even if the holding count is large.

### Set Limits And A Review Cadence

Concentration should be bounded by limits and reviewed, not left to drift.

Define:

- maximum sector and country weights relative to benchmark or absolute;
- a review cadence and triggers (drift, regime change, valuation extreme);
- whether tactical deviations are allowed and budgeted;
- the action when a limit is breached (reduce, hedge, or consciously accept).

Limits convert concentration from an unnoticed accumulation into a managed exposure.

### Connect To The Investor's Broader Balance Sheet

Sector and geographic concentration interacts with the investor's human capital, business, and real estate.

Aggregate:

- the investor's employer and industry;
- business ownership and its sector;
- real estate location and its economy;
- pension and guaranteed income and their sovereign exposure.

An investor working in tech, holding tech-heavy equity, and owning a home in a tech hub is concentrated in tech across the balance sheet, regardless of the fund labels.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Issuer Count With Diversification

Many holdings in one sector or country is still concentration. Measure above the security level.

### Unrecognized Home Bias

Over-weighting the home country by default, without acknowledging the concentration and its rationale.

### Market-Cap Concentration Mistaken For Breadth

A cap-weighted portfolio dominated by mega-caps is concentrated even with hundreds of holdings.

### Drift Mistaken For Thesis

Concentration from a sector outperforming is drift, not a deliberate bet. It should be reviewed, not assumed intentional.

### Chasing Recent Sector Or Country Winners

Concentrating in what just outperformed is often late-cycle. Mean reversion can punish the concentration.

### Ignoring Balance-Sheet Concentration

Human capital, business, and real estate add sector and geographic exposure that fund analysis misses.

### No Limits, So Concentration Grows

Without explicit limits and review, concentration drifts upward until it becomes a dominant, unmanaged risk.

### Treating Currency As Neutral

Geographic concentration brings currency concentration. A home-currency investor with foreign concentration bears FX risk that may not align with obligations.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Concentration is measured at sector, industry, country, region, currency, cap-segment, and jurisdiction levels, compared to a diversified benchmark.
- [ ] Deliberate tilt (with thesis and edge) is distinguished from accidental drift, home bias, and index-driven concentration.
- [ ] Home bias is assessed for rationale, size, cost, and interaction with human capital and spending currency.
- [ ] Sector risk is evaluated across the cycle, including cyclicality, valuation, crowding, and disruption risk.
- [ ] Country risk is evaluated for economy, politics, regulation, currency, liquidity, and capital controls.
- [ ] Market-cap concentration within sectors and countries is checked, including the share in dominant names.
- [ ] Limits and a review cadence are defined, with action rules for breaches.
- [ ] Balance-sheet concentration (employer, business, real estate, pension) is aggregated with portfolio exposure.
- [ ] The recommendation flags that sector and geographic concentration can cause large losses, that home bias and index concentration are often unintentional, and that professional advice may be warranted for cross-border and concentrated portfolios.
