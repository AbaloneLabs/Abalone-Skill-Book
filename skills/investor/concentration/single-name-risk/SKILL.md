# Single Name Risk

A large single-stock position is the most common source of catastrophic, permanent loss in personal investing, and it usually arrives through success: an IPO, founder shares, vested RSUs, exercised options, or an inheritance. The position feels like reward and validation, so the investor holds it, and the longer it is held the larger the embedded risk grows. The judgment problem is that reducing the position triggers taxes, restrictions, and emotional resistance, while holding it gambles the investor's financial future on one company's survival. Agents often let tax fear or loyalty freeze an unacceptable risk.

This skill is for planning the reduction or hedging of a large single-name position.

## Core Rules

### Treat Single-Name Risk As Permanent-Loss Risk

A single stock can go to zero or near-zero through fraud, bankruptcy, obsolescence, regulatory action, or expropriation, and it may never recover. This is fundamentally different from a diversified index drawdown.

Recognize:

- idiosyncratic risk that diversification would eliminate is being borne uncompensated;
- the probability of a permanent, unrecoverable loss is non-trivial for any single name;
- the upside the investor hopes for is rarely worth the downside of ruin;
- the longer the position is held, the more the outcome depends on one company's fate.

The first step is reframing: this is not "my winner," it is a concentrated bet whose risk grows with its size.

### Measure The Whole-Household Exposure

Single-name risk hides across accounts. Aggregate before deciding.

Map:

- direct shares across brokerage, retirement, and joint accounts;
- unexercised options, RSUs, and unvested awards;
- shares held by a spouse or family members;
- exposure through mutual funds and ETFs that hold the stock;
- any derivative exposure (options, convertible notes);
- the human-capital link if the investor also works for the company.

A stock that is "only 10%" in the brokerage account can be 50% of net worth in aggregate. The whole-household number is the only honest basis for the decision.

### Quantify The After-Tax Cost Of Reduction

Selling triggers capital-gains tax, which is real but finite. Quantify it precisely, then weigh it against the risk reduction.

Compute:

- the unrealized gain and its tax character (long-term versus short-term);
- the tax cost of a full sale versus staged sales over multiple years;
- the effect of lot selection on realized gains;
- state taxes, surtaxes, and any applicable exemptions.

The tax cost is a one-time, knowable number. The risk of holding is an unknowable but potentially far larger loss. Tax is a factor in the pacing, not a veto on the decision.

### Choose Among Reduction Tools

There are several ways to reduce single-name exposure, each with tradeoffs.

Options:

- outright sale (simplest, triggers tax, full reduction);
- staged sale over years (spreads tax, gradual reduction, Rule 10b5-1 plans for insiders);
- exchange funds or swap pools (defers tax by pooling with other concentrated holders, limited eligibility and lockup);
- collars and prepaid forwards (hedge downside, may defer or monetize, complex);
- charitable gifts or trusts (donate appreciated shares for tax efficiency and diversification);
- gifting to family (shifts exposure, may use exemptions).

Match the tool to the investor's tax situation, insider status, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance. Each has legal, tax, and restriction implications that require expert review.

### Account For Insider Status And Restrictions

If the investor is an insider or holds restricted securities, additional rules apply.

Consider:

- lockup periods after an IPO or secondary;
- Rule 10b5-1 trading plans for insiders to trade without insider-trading concerns;
- blackout periods and trading windows;
- Section 16 reporting and short-swing profit rules;
- Rule 144 volume limits for restricted or control securities.

Insider status does not prevent reduction but constrains how and when. A 10b5-1 plan is often the right tool for insiders to diversify systematically.

### Plan The Behavioral And Emotional Path

Selling a position tied to identity, loyalty, or optimism is psychologically hard. The plan must address behavior.

Build:

- a pre-commitment to a reduction schedule set when objective;
- framing the sale as risk management, not as betraying the company;
- a rule that separates the decision from the stock's recent price;
- a review of the worst-case scenario the diversification prevents.

Investors who sell "on feel" tend to hold through the rise and sell after a decline, the worst of both. A pre-committed schedule removes the emotional timing.

### Address The Human-Capital Link

If the investor works for the company, the concentration is doubled: both income and wealth depend on one employer.

Assess:

- the correlation of human capital and financial capital to the same company;
- the effect of a company downturn on both salary and stock;
- the importance of diversifying away from the employer;
- the role of the position as both asset and compensation.

Holding employer stock concentrates both income and wealth in one entity. Diversifying the financial position reduces the double exposure even if the investor keeps working there.

### Decide The Target Residual Exposure

The question is not only whether to reduce but how much to retain. Some retention may be rational; a huge position is not.

Set:

- a target residual weight that reflects conviction, tax, and restrictions;
- a maximum acceptable single-name weight as a hard cap;
- a schedule to reach the target over a defined period;
- a review if the stock's rise pushes the weight back above the cap.

A common disciplined outcome is to reduce the position to a small fraction of net worth over a few years, retaining some upside while eliminating the ruin risk.

## Common Traps

### Letting Tax Fear Freeze Unacceptable Risk

Avoiding a known tax cost by holding a position that can lose far more is the classic single-name failure. Tax is finite; ruin is not.

### Measuring Only One Account

A stock small in the brokerage account may be dominant across the household. Aggregate before deciding.

### Treating A Rising Stock As Validation

A concentrated position that has risen feels like proof of a good decision. It is usually luck plus momentum, and the higher it climbs, the larger the risk.

### Ignoring The Human-Capital Link

Holding employer stock concentrates income and wealth in one entity, doubling the exposure to a single failure.

### Insider Inaction

Insiders often hold concentrated positions out of inertia or loyalty, even when 10b5-1 plans and other tools allow systematic diversification.

### Selling On Emotion After A Decline

Investors who sell on feel tend to hold through the rise and sell after a drop, realizing losses and missing recovery. A pre-committed schedule avoids this.

### Assuming "It Could Never Happen Here"

Every ruined single-name holder believed their company was exceptional. Idiosyncratic risk is real for every name, however strong.

### Over-Relying On Hedges

Collars and prepaid forwards can help but have cost, complexity, and basis risk. They are tools, not substitutes for outright diversification when sale is possible.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The position is treated as permanent-loss risk, not as a validated winner.
- [ ] Whole-household exposure across accounts, options, RSUs, family, funds, and human capital is aggregated.
- [ ] The after-tax cost of full and staged reduction is quantified and treated as a pacing factor, not a veto.
- [ ] Reduction tools (sale, staged sale, 10b5-1, exchange funds, collars, charitable or family gifting) are evaluated with their tradeoffs.
- [ ] Insider status, lockups, blackouts, Section 16, and Rule 144 restrictions are accounted for.
- [ ] A pre-committed reduction schedule and behavioral framing address the emotional resistance.
- [ ] The human-capital link is considered, and diversifying away from the employer is prioritized.
- [ ] A target residual exposure and hard cap are set, with a schedule and review rule.
- [ ] The recommendation flags that single-name positions can cause permanent loss, that tax and insider rules are complex and jurisdiction-specific, that this is not legal or tax advice, and that qualified professional advice is essential for concentrated positions.
