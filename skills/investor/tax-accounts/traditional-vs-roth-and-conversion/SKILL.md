# Traditional Versus Roth And Conversion

The choice between traditional (tax-deferred) and Roth (tax-exempt) retirement accounts, and the decision to convert traditional assets to Roth, hinge on one comparison: the marginal tax rate paid now versus the marginal rate avoided later. When the rate now is lower than the rate in retirement, Roth (pay now) wins; when the rate now is higher than the rate in retirement, traditional (defer and pay later) wins. This sounds simple, but the comparison is full of traps: future tax rates are uncertain, conversions can push income into higher brackets, pro-rata rules complicate partial conversions, and multi-year timing and legacy considerations change the answer.

Agents tend to give a blanket recommendation (often "always Roth when young, traditional when older") without modeling the investor's actual current and expected marginal rates, the interaction with other income, and the rules that govern conversions. The judgment problem is estimating future tax rates honestly, modeling the conversion at the correct marginal rate, and recognizing when a conversion is value-destroying.

This skill applies to traditional-versus-Roth contribution choices, Roth conversion analysis, tax-bracket arbitrage, and conversion timing. It is not tax or investment advice; tax laws vary by jurisdiction and change, conversion outcomes depend on uncertain future rates and individual circumstances, and consult a qualified tax professional.

## Core Rules

### Compare Marginal Rates Now Versus In Retirement

The core decision rule: if your marginal rate on the contribution (or conversion) is lower than the effective marginal rate you will pay on withdrawal, pay the tax now (Roth); if higher, defer (traditional). The "marginal rate now" is the rate on the last dollar of income the contribution or conversion adds. The "rate in retirement" is the marginal rate on the withdrawn dollars, which depends on retirement income, bracket structure, and required minimum distributions.

Estimate the retirement marginal rate realistically, not optimistically. Many retirees face higher-than-expected marginal rates due to RMDs, Social Security taxation, and IRMAA Medicare surcharges. Do not assume retirement income will be low; a large traditional balance generating RMDs can keep marginal rates high. Model the expected withdrawal-phase marginal rate including these effects.

### Model The Conversion At The Correct Marginal Rate, Not Average

A conversion adds to ordinary income in the year converted. The tax cost is determined by the marginal rate on the converted dollars, which may span brackets. Converting too much in one year pushes income into higher brackets, increasing the average rate paid and eroding the benefit. The optimal conversion amount often fills a bracket up to its limit without spilling into the next.

Model conversions year by year, filling brackets to their edges (for example, filling the top of the 24% bracket without entering 32%). Spreading conversions across multiple years at lower marginal rates usually beats a single large conversion at a high rate. The "fill the bracket" approach is the core of conversion optimization.

### Respect The Pro-Rata Rule For Conversions From Mixed Balances

When an investor has both pre-tax (traditional IRA) and after-tax (non-deductible contributions) balances, any conversion is treated pro-rata across the two, not as a selective conversion of only the after-tax amount. A large pre-tax balance makes the pro-rata rule convert mostly taxable assets even if the intent was to convert only after-tax money. This can create a large unexpected tax bill.

Before any conversion, assess the full traditional IRA balance across all accounts (the aggregation rule treats all traditional IRAs as one). If a large pre-tax balance exists, the backdoor Roth strategy can be undermined by pro-rata taxation. Plan conversions with the aggregation rule in mind, and consider rolling pre-tax IRA assets into an employer plan first to isolate after-tax money if eligible.

### Account For The Five-Year Rule And Substantial Equal Period Payments

Roth conversions have a five-year clock for each conversion, during which the converted principal can be withdrawn penalty-free but earnings may be subject to rules, and early withdrawal of converted amounts within five years can trigger penalties if under 59.5. Roth contributions also have a five-year rule for qualified earnings. These clocks affect liquidity and early-retirement planning.

Track the five-year clocks for each conversion separately. For early retirees using Roth conversions to bridge to age 59.5, a conversion ladder (converting each year to fund spending five years later) requires multi-year advance planning. Ignoring the five-year rule can create penalties or force taxable withdrawals from other accounts.

### Evaluate Conversion When The Account Value Is Depressed

A conversion taxes the value converted at the time of conversion. Converting after a market decline converts more shares for the same tax cost, and if the assets recover inside the Roth, all recovery is tax-free. This makes downturns a favorable window for conversion, though it requires confidence the decline is temporary and the cash to pay the tax.

Consider converting when balances are depressed, but do not try to time the market precisely. The tax-rate comparison dominates; the valuation effect is a secondary enhancement. Ensure the tax is paid from outside the converted account (paying tax from the converted amount reduces the conversion's benefit and, before 59.5, can trigger penalties).

### Consider Legacy And Estate Planning Effects

Roth accounts have advantages for legacy planning: no required minimum distributions during the owner's lifetime (for the original owner), and beneficiaries (under current rules) must generally empty inherited Roth accounts within 10 years but pay no tax on withdrawals. Traditional accounts force RMDs that can create taxable income the owner does not need, and inherited traditional accounts are taxable to beneficiaries.

For investors focused on legacy, Roth conversions can reduce lifetime RMD burden and leave a more tax-efficient inheritance, even if the simple rate comparison is close. Weigh estate considerations alongside the marginal-rate math, especially for investors who will not need to spend the full traditional balance.

### Re-Evaluate As Tax Law And Circumstances Change

The optimal traditional-versus-Roth choice depends on tax law, which changes, and on the investor's circumstances (income, marital status, state of residence, retirement plans), which also change. A decision made once may need revisiting. State tax matters: converting while in a high-tax state and withdrawing while in a no-tax state favors traditional; the reverse favors Roth.

Revisit the contribution choice and conversion opportunity periodically. Treat the decision as a multi-year optimization, not a one-time election. Changes in tax law (contribution limits, Roth income limits, RMD ages) can shift the optimum.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Roth Is Always Better Because Tax-Free Sounds Superior

Tax-free growth is only better if the rate paid now is lower than the rate avoided later. The trap is choosing Roth without comparing rates. For high earners now expecting lower retirement income, traditional often wins.

### Converting A Lump Sum And Pushing Into A Higher Bracket

A large single conversion can spike the marginal rate, raising the average tax paid. The trap is converting without bracket-aware modeling. Spread conversions to fill brackets.

### Ignoring The Pro-Rata Aggregation Rule

Mixed pre-tax and after-tax IRAs make conversions mostly taxable. The trap is attempting a backdoor Roth without accounting for the aggregated balance. Check all traditional IRA balances first.

### Overlooking The Five-Year Clock On Conversions

Early withdrawals of converted amounts can trigger penalties. The trap is assuming converted money is immediately accessible penalty-free. Track each conversion's clock.

### Paying Conversion Tax From The Converted Account

Paying tax from the converted amount reduces principal and may trigger penalties. The trap is converting without outside funds for the tax. Pay tax from taxable sources.

### Assuming Retirement Marginal Rate Will Be Low

RMDs, Social Security taxation, and IRMAA can keep retirement marginal rates high. The trap is underestimating the withdrawal rate. Model retirement income realistically.

### Ignoring State Tax Differences

State tax at contribution/conversion versus withdrawal changes the math. The trap is using only federal rates. Include state tax in the comparison.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Conversions are modeled year by year to fill brackets to their edges rather than as a single lump sum.
- [ ] The pro-rata aggregation rule is applied across all traditional IRA balances before any conversion or backdoor Roth.
- [ ] Five-year rules for each conversion and for Roth earnings are tracked, especially for early-retirement ladders.
- [ ] Conversion opportunities during market downturns are considered as a secondary enhancement, with tax paid from outside the converted account.
- [ ] Legacy and estate effects (RMDs, inherited account taxation) are weighed alongside the rate comparison.
- [ ] State tax at contribution/conversion versus withdrawal is included in the analysis.
