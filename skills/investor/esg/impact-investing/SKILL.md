# Impact Investing

Impact investing seeks to generate measurable, positive social or environmental impact alongside financial return, with the explicit intention of creating real-world change. The thesis is that capital can be deployed to address social and environmental challenges (clean energy, affordable housing, healthcare access, financial inclusion) while earning a return, and that the intentionality and measurability distinguish impact investing from conventional ESG integration. The judgment problem is that impact is hard to define, harder to measure, and easy to claim. Many investments marketed as impact generate no additional real-world benefit (they lack additionality), or they sacrifice return without proportional impact, or they measure outputs (money deployed) rather than outcomes (lives improved). The discipline is to demand intentionality, verify additionality, measure outcomes rigorously, and be honest about the tradeoff between financial return and impact, rather than accepting the comforting but false premise that impact and return always align.

This skill is for evaluating impact investments with rigor about intentionality, additionality, measurement, and the return-impact tradeoff.

## Core Rules

### Require Explicit Intentionality Of Impact

Impact investing is defined by the intention to generate positive impact. Without explicit intentionality, an investment is conventional, regardless of any incidental benefit.

Assess intentionality:

- is the impact objective stated explicitly in the investment thesis and strategy?
- is the investment designed and structured to achieve the impact objective, or is impact a post-hoc rationalization?
- does the manager or company have a theory of change linking the investment to the intended outcome?
- is impact considered in investment decisions, or only in marketing?

Intentionality distinguishes impact investing from conventional investing that happens to have positive externalities. If the impact objective is not embedded in the strategy and decisions, it is not impact investing.

### Verify Additionality, Not Just Allocation

Additionality is the core test of real impact: does the investment create a benefit that would not have occurred otherwise? Many impact investments lack additionality.

Test additionality:

- does the capital enable an activity that would not have happened without it (e.g., new clean energy project, new affordable housing)?
- or is the capital simply buying an existing asset (e.g., secondary market purchase of a green bond), with no new real-world impact?
- would the underlying project or company have proceeded regardless of this specific investment?
- is the investment providing capital to underserved markets, sectors, or populations that lack alternative funding?

Secondary market purchases and investments in large, well-funded companies often lack additionality. Genuine impact requires capital that enables new beneficial activity, not just capital allocated to existing impact-aligned assets.

### Measure Outcomes, Not Just Outputs

Impact measurement must focus on real-world outcomes (lives improved, emissions reduced), not just outputs (capital deployed, projects funded). Outcome measurement is harder but essential.

Assess measurement:

- are the impact metrics tied to real-world outcomes, with baselines and targets?
- is the measurement methodology rigorous, using established frameworks (IRIS, GIIN, SDG indicators)?
- is the impact attributable to the investment, or would it have occurred anyway (additionality in measurement)?
- are negative or unintended consequences measured, not just positive ones?
- is the impact verified by independent third parties, or self-reported?

Output metrics (dollars deployed, number of projects) are easy to report but tell little about actual impact. Outcome measurement, with attribution and verification, is the standard for genuine impact investing.

### Be Honest About The Return-Impact Tradeoff

Impact investing spans a spectrum from market-rate returns (impact and return align) to concessionary returns (return is sacrificed for impact). The investor must be honest about where on this spectrum a given investment sits.

Clarify expectations:

- does the investment target market-rate returns, with impact as a co-benefit?
- or does it accept below-market returns as a deliberate tradeoff for greater impact?
- is the return concession explicit and justified by the impact, or hidden and unexpected?
- what is the evidence on whether impact and return align or conflict in this sector and strategy?

The comforting claim that impact always comes with market-rate returns is often false. Some impact requires accepting lower returns, and honesty about this tradeoff is essential for setting realistic expectations and making deliberate choices.

### Evaluate The Evidence For Impact Effectiveness

Not all impact strategies are equally effective. The investor must assess the evidence that the approach actually achieves its intended outcomes.

Assess effectiveness:

- is there evidence (academic studies, evaluations, track record) that the intervention works?
- is the theory of change plausible and supported, or speculative?
- what are the scale and durability of the impact, and do they justify the capital and any return concession?
- are there perverse incentives or unintended consequences that undermine the impact?
- how does the impact per dollar compare to alternative approaches or direct philanthropy?

Impact effectiveness varies enormously across strategies and sectors. The investor should compare the impact efficiency of the investment to alternatives, including whether direct giving might achieve more impact per dollar.

### Consider Market Development And Systemic Impact

Some impact investments aim not just at project-level impact but at developing markets and creating systemic change. Understanding the level of impact shapes expectations.

Distinguish:

- direct impact, where the investment creates measurable benefit in specific projects or populations;
- market development, where the investment helps build a market (e.g., proving a financial model, attracting follow-on capital) that scales impact beyond the direct investment;
- systemic impact, where the investment contributes to policy, norm, or system change that transforms how problems are addressed.

Direct impact is most measurable; systemic impact is most ambitious but hardest to attribute. The investor should understand which level of impact the strategy targets and how it will be evidenced.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Intentionality With Incidental Benefit

Investments that happen to have positive externalities are not impact investing unless impact is an explicit objective embedded in the strategy.

### Ignoring Additionality

Buying existing impact-aligned assets on the secondary market creates no new real-world impact. Additionality is the core test.

### Measuring Outputs Instead Of Outcomes

Capital deployed and projects funded are outputs. Real impact is measured in outcomes, with attribution and verification.

### Claiming Market-Rate Returns Always Align With Impact

Some impact requires accepting below-market returns. Honesty about the tradeoff is essential.

### Neglecting Evidence Of Effectiveness

Not all interventions work. The theory of change must be supported by evidence, not aspiration.

### Overlooking Unintended Consequences

Impact strategies can have negative side effects (displacement, dependency, perverse incentives) that must be measured and managed.

### Accepting Self-Reported Impact Without Verification

Self-reported impact is prone to exaggeration. Independent verification and rigorous methodology are needed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Impact intentionality is explicit, embedded in the investment thesis and decisions, with a clear theory of change, not a post-hoc rationalization.
- [ ] Additionality is verified: the capital enables new beneficial activity that would not have occurred otherwise, rather than simply buying existing impact-aligned assets.
- [ ] Impact is measured in real-world outcomes (with baselines, targets, attribution, and independent verification), not just outputs like capital deployed.
- [ ] The return-impact tradeoff is honestly assessed, with clarity on whether the investment targets market-rate returns or accepts a deliberate concession for impact.
- [ ] Evidence for the effectiveness of the intervention is evaluated, with comparison to alternative approaches and direct philanthropy for impact per dollar.
- [ ] The level of impact (direct, market development, systemic) is understood, with appropriate measurement and expectations for each.
- [ ] Unintended consequences and perverse incentives are considered and managed.
- [ ] The guidance flags that impact is hard to measure and attribute, that additionality is often lacking, that returns may be concessionary, that impact claims are prone to exaggeration, and that professional advice may be warranted for impact-aligned and mission-driven portfolios.
