# Direct Lending Underwriting

Direct lending, making loans directly to middle-market companies without a syndicate of banks, has become the dominant form of private credit. The underwriting decisions, borrower selection, covenant structuring, collateral protection, and pricing determine whether the loan generates the promised yield or suffers losses that erase years of income. Unlike public high-yield bonds, where the lender accepts whatever terms the market offers and trades out if credit deteriorates, a direct lender negotiates terms upfront and holds the loan to maturity, making the initial underwriting the central determinant of outcome. Underwriters who price for yield and neglect covenants, who rely on adjusted EBITDA that overstates cash flow, or who accept thin collateral coverage in competitive deals set up losses that the floating-rate coupon cannot offset.

Use this skill when underwriting a direct lending loan, evaluating a private credit portfolio's credit quality, or assessing whether loan terms adequately protect the lender. The goal is to prevent the agent from reducing underwriting to a yield calculation and from missing the borrower's true cash flow, the covenant package, the collateral, and the downside protection that determine loss given default.

## Core Rules

### Analyze The Borrower's Real Cash Flow, Not Adjusted EBITDA

Middle-market borrowers present EBITDA adjusted for addbacks, pro forma synergies, and one-time charges that can overstate the true cash-generating capacity:

- Adjustments scrutiny: challenge each addback; many "one-time" expenses recur, and pro forma synergies may never materialize.
- Cash conversion: EBITDA is not cash flow. Account for working capital needs, capex required to sustain the business, taxes, and interest to derive free cash flow available for debt service.
- Sustainability: assess whether current performance is cyclical peak or sustainable trough; leverage on peak EBITDA understates risk.

A loan sized on adjusted EBITDA that overstates cash flow by 15-20% has materially higher true leverage than the headline ratio suggests. Always bridge from reported EBITDA to a conservative, sustainable cash flow figure.

### Size Leverage Against Conservative EBITDA And Assess Debt Service Capacity

Leverage ratios (debt to EBITDA) are the headline credit metric, but their meaning depends on the EBITDA basis and the debt service:

- Senior leverage: first-lien debt to EBITDA, typically 3.5-5.0x for middle-market direct loans.
- Total leverage: all debt to EBITDA, including subordinated and mezzanine.
- Fixed charge coverage: free cash flow to (interest plus scheduled amortization), indicating whether the borrower can service debt from operations without refinancing.

A borrower at 5.0x leverage on conservative EBITDA with thin fixed charge coverage has little margin for error. Stress the coverage under revenue and margin declines to confirm debt service survives a downturn.

### Structure Covenants That Provide Early Warning And Control

Covenants are the direct lender's primary protection, far more important than in broadly syndicated loans where covenants have eroded:

- Financial maintenance covenants: maximum leverage and minimum interest coverage tested quarterly, giving the lender early warning and the right to act before default.
- Affirmative and negative covenants: restrictions on additional debt, asset sales, distributions to sponsors, and mergers, preserving the lender's position.
- Covenant looseness: "covenant-lite" terms, increasingly common in competitive deals, remove early warning and leave the lender exposed until payment default.

Strong covenants let the lender renegotiate or restructure when performance deteriorates, often recovering value that a covenant-lite lender loses. In a competitive market, resist the pressure to strip covenants for yield.

### Secure Collateral And Understand Recovery Prospects

First-lien collateral improves recovery in default, but the quality and coverage of collateral varies:

- Collateral coverage: the value of assets (accounts receivable, inventory, equipment, real estate, intellectual property) relative to the loan.
- Lien perfection and priority: ensure liens are properly filed and the lender is first priority on the relevant assets.
- Asset liquidity: receivables and inventory are more recoverable than specialized equipment or intangibles; assess realistic liquidation values, not book values.
-ABL vs. cash flow: asset-based loans size borrowing against collateral values; cash flow loans rely on the business's earnings, with collateral as secondary support.

A first-lien loan on liquid collateral has better recovery prospects than a first-lien loan on illiquid assets or a cash flow loan with thin collateral. Model loss given default on realistic recovery assumptions, not optimistic appraisals.

### Price For Risk Including Floating-Rate And Floor Mechanics

Direct loans are typically floating-rate (SOFR or LIBOR plus a spread), with features affecting yield:

- Base rate floors: a floor on the reference rate protects yield if rates fall below the floor; valuable in low-rate environments.
- Spread (margin): the credit spread over the base rate, typically 400-700 basis points for middle-market loans depending on risk.
- OID and upfront fees: original issue discount and fees increase the effective yield above the coupon.
- Prepayment penalties: yield maintenance or make-whole provisions protect yield if the borrower refinances early.

Price the all-in yield (coupon plus OID plus fees, adjusted for floors) against the credit risk, and recognize that floating-rate exposure means the borrower's interest burden rises with rates, potentially stressing coverage.

### Assess The Sponsor And The Exit Dynamics

Most middle-market direct loans finance private equity-sponsored buyouts, and the sponsor's behavior affects outcomes:

- Sponsor quality: experienced sponsors with a track record of supporting portfolio companies in distress are more likely to inject capital or restructure than to walk away.
- Sponsor equity cushion: the amount of equity beneath the loan affects recovery; a thin equity cushion means the loan is impaired with modest enterprise value decline.
- Refinancing risk: the loan's maturity relative to the sponsor's hold period and market conditions affects whether the loan is repaid by refinancing or requires extension or restructuring.

A loan to a strong sponsor with a substantial equity cushion has better downside protection than one to a weak sponsor with minimal equity, even at similar leverage.

## Common Traps

### Trusting Adjusted EBITDA At Face Value

Addbacks and pro forma adjustments can overstate cash flow by 15-30%. Underwriting on inflated EBITDA understates true leverage and overstates coverage.

### Accepting Covenant-Lite Terms For Yield

Stripping maintenance covenants removes early warning and lender control. The extra spread does not compensate for the loss of restructuring optionality in distress.

### Overstating Collateral Recovery Values

Book values and appraisals overstate liquidation recoveries. Model recovery on realistic distressed sale values, especially for illiquid assets.

### Ignoring Floating-Rate Stress On The Borrower

Rising rates increase the borrower's interest burden, potentially breaching coverage. Stress debt service under higher-rate scenarios, not just the current rate.

### Neglecting The Sponsor's Equity Cushion And Behavior

A thin equity cushion means the loan is impaired quickly, and a weak sponsor may not support the company in distress. Sponsor quality and equity cushion materially affect recovery.

### Pricing For Yield Without Modeling Loss Given Default

The yield premium matters only net of expected losses. A high-yield loan with weak covenants and thin collateral can deliver lower realized return than a lower-yield loan with strong protection.

## Self-Check

- Is the borrower's cash flow analyzed on a conservative basis, with adjusted EBITDA addbacks scrutinized and free cash flow derived after working capital, capex, taxes, and interest?
- Is leverage sized against conservative EBITDA, with fixed charge coverage and debt service capacity stressed under revenue and margin declines?
- Are financial maintenance covenants (leverage, coverage) and restrictive covenants structured to provide early warning and lender control, resisting covenant-lite terms?
- Is collateral coverage assessed on realistic liquidation values, with lien priority confirmed and the distinction between ABL and cash flow lending recognized?
- Is the all-in yield priced (coupon, OID, fees, floors) against credit risk, with floating-rate stress applied to the borrower's debt service?
- Is the sponsor's quality, equity cushion, and likely behavior in distress assessed for its effect on recovery and restructuring outcomes?
- Is loss given default modeled on realistic recovery assumptions, with expected loss netted against the yield premium?
- Does the conclusion frame the loan's attractiveness on risk-adjusted return including downside protection, not on headline yield alone?
