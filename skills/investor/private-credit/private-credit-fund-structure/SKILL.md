# Private Credit Fund Structure

Private credit has grown into a major asset class as banks retreated from middle-market lending and institutional investors searched for yield beyond public bonds. Funds that make direct loans, mezzanine investments, or specialty finance exposures to private companies operate through structures that differ fundamentally from public bond funds or traditional private equity. Investors commit capital that is drawn over time, pay fees on committed (not just invested) capital, receive income from floating-rate loans, and face long lockups with limited liquidity. The fund structure, its terms, its liquidity provisions, and its fee mechanics determine whether the headline yield translates into net investor return. Allocators who read the yield and ignore the structure commit to illiquidity, fee drag, and valuation opacity that can erode returns and trap capital when conditions deteriorate.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how does a private credit fund work", "what is a BDC", "why is the yield different from my net return", or "what happens if I need liquidity from a private credit fund". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating private credit as a higher-yielding bond fund and from missing the commitment, drawdown, lockup, fee, and valuation features that define the investor's actual experience.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Commitment And Drawdown Model

Private credit funds do not take investor capital up front. Investors commit a total amount, and the fund draws capital over time as it originates loans:

- Commitment: the total an investor agrees to fund.
- Drawdowns (capital calls): periodic requests for capital as loans are funded.
- Unfunded commitment: the remaining obligation, which the investor must meet on demand.

An investor's exposure is not the capital deployed but the full commitment, because the unfunded portion can be called at any time. Liquidity planning must account for the entire commitment, and defaulting on a capital call carries severe penalties ( forfeiture of prior investments, litigation ).

### Model The Fund Life, Lockup, And Liquidity Terms

Private credit funds are long-lived and illiquid:

- Term: typically 7-10 years with possible extensions, far longer than a bond fund.
- Lockup: investors generally cannot redeem during the term; liquidity is limited to distributions.
- Interim liquidity: some vehicles (BDCs, interval funds, tender-offer funds) offer periodic liquidity at NAV, subject to limits and discounts.

The yield premium over public bonds partly compensates for this illiquidity. An investor who needs liquidity before the fund winds down may face tender offers at discounts, secondary sales at steep haircuts, or no exit at all. Match the lockup to the liability horizon before committing.

### Analyze The Fee Structure And Its Impact On Net Yield

Private credit fund fees are substantial and structured similarly to private equity:

- Management fee: typically 1.5-2.0% on committed capital (not invested), meaning fees accrue even before capital is deployed.
- Incentive fee (carry): typically 15-20% of profits above a hurdle, often with a high-water mark.
- BDC structures: may charge both management and incentive fees, with additional fees on loans originated.

Because fees accrue on committed capital during the slow deployment phase, the effective fee drag in early years is high relative to income received. Compute net yield to investors after all fees, not the portfolio's gross yield, which can be 200-400 basis points higher.

### Distinguish Vehicle Types And Their Tradeoffs

Private credit is accessed through several vehicle types with different liquidity, regulation, and fee profiles:

- Closed-end funds (limited partnerships): traditional structure, full lockup, highest illiquidity premium.
- Business Development Companies (BDCs): publicly registered, often exchange-listed, providing daily liquidity but trading at premiums/discounts to NAV; regulated under the Investment Company Act.
- Interval funds and tender-offer funds: continuous or periodic liquidity subject to limits (e.g., quarterly repurchases of 5% of NAV).
- Evergreen structures: open-ended with periodic liquidity, blending features.

Each vehicle trades off liquidity, transparency, fee level, and the illiquidity premium captured. An exchange-traded BDC offers liquidity but may trade at a persistent discount and carries higher fees; a closed-end LP locks capital but may capture more illiquidity premium.

### Assess Origination Capacity And Deal Flow

A private credit fund's return depends on its ability to originate attractive loans:

- Sourcing: relationships with private equity sponsors, direct borrower relationships, or intermediated platforms.
- Underwriting quality: the rigor of credit analysis, covenants, and structuring.
- Scale and competition: as the asset class grows, competition compresses spreads and weakens terms.

A fund's track record and sponsor relationships drive its ability to access deals. A fund without strong origination may deploy into lower-quality loans at tight spreads, eroding the risk-adjusted return.

### Understand Valuation And Reporting Opacity

Private loans are not marked to market daily; NAV is based on model valuations:

- Fair value (Level 3): management or a third party values loans using models, with limited market input.
- Stale valuations: NAV may lag actual credit deterioration, masking losses until defaults crystallize.
- Reporting lag: quarterly reporting with a delay means investors learn of problems late.

The smooth NAV lines of private credit funds partly reflect valuation opacity, not low risk. Stress the portfolio under default and recovery assumptions rather than relying on reported NAV stability.

## Common Traps

### Treating Private Credit As A Higher-Yielding Bond Fund

The yield premium reflects illiquidity, complexity, and credit risk, not free return. Comparing gross yield to public bond yield without adjusting for fees, lockup, and risk overstates the benefit.

### Ignoring The Commitment And Capital Call Obligation

The unfunded commitment must be funded on demand. Liquidity planning that considers only deployed capital understates the cash obligation.

### Underestimating Fee Drag On Committed Capital

Fees on committed capital during deployment, plus incentive fees, can consume a large share of the yield premium. Net yield, not gross yield, is what matters.

### Assuming NAV Stability Means Low Risk

Smooth, model-based NAV reflects valuation opacity and reporting lag, not absence of credit risk. Defaults and recoveries, not reported NAV, determine realized return.

### Choosing Liquidity (BDC) Without Accounting For Discounts And Fees

Exchange-traded or interval-fund liquidity is valuable but comes with NAV discounts, higher fees, and potential forced selling in stress. The liquidity is not free.

### Overlooking Origination Quality And Spread Compression

As the asset class grows, competition tightens spreads and weakens covenants. A fund's ability to originate attractive, well-structured loans drives sustainable returns.

## Self-Check

- Is the commitment and drawdown model understood, with liquidity planning accounting for the full unfunded commitment, not just deployed capital?
- Are the fund term, lockup, and interim liquidity provisions matched to the investor's liability horizon before committing?
- Is the fee structure (management on committed capital, incentive fee, origination fees) modeled to compute net yield, not gross yield?
- Is the vehicle type (closed-end LP, BDC, interval fund, evergreen) distinguished, with its liquidity, regulatory, and fee tradeoffs explicit?
- Is the fund's origination capacity, sponsor relationships, and exposure to spread compression assessed for sustainable returns?
- Is valuation opacity recognized, with the portfolio stressed under default and recovery assumptions rather than relying on reported NAV?
- Is the realized return modeled net of fees, defaults, recoveries, and illiquidity, rather than compared to public bond yield on a gross basis?
- Does the conclusion frame private credit as an illiquid, complex credit allocation requiring due diligence and horizon matching, not as a simple yield enhancement?
