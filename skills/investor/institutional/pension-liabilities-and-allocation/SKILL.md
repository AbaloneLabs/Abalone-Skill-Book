# Pension Liabilities And Allocation

When the investor's purpose is to pay a defined set of future obligations, the benchmark stops being a market index and becomes the liability itself. The judgment problem is that pension and insurance allocators, and individuals funding a specific spending stream, often optimize assets for return rather than for the match to what they owe, then discover that a rising-rate environment or falling discount rate has blown a hole in the funding ratio even though the assets performed "well." The skill is thinking in terms of surplus — assets minus liabilities — and deciding deliberately how much of the portfolio should match the liabilities and how much should reach for return to close a deficit, rather than treating the asset side in isolation.

This skill is for allocating assets against defined liabilities. It is not personalized financial advice; actuarial assumptions, regulations, and liability specifics vary by jurisdiction and situation.

## Core Rules

### Make The Liability The Benchmark, Not A Market Index

A pension or insurance portfolio exists to pay obligations. Its performance must be measured against the growth and discounting of those obligations, not against a generic equity or bond index.

Reframe the objective:

- the true measure is the funding ratio (assets divided by the present value of liabilities) and surplus or deficit;
- asset returns matter only insofar as they change the surplus relative to the liabilities;
- a portfolio that returns 8 percent while liabilities grow 10 percent has lost ground, even though the asset return looks strong;
- the relevant risk is surplus risk — the volatility of the funding ratio — not asset volatility alone.

Set the strategic benchmark as a liability proxy (typically a portfolio of high-quality, long-duration bonds whose value moves with the liabilities), then measure performance and risk against it.

### Understand What Drives The Liability

Liabilities valued using a discount rate move inversely to interest rates: when rates fall, the present value of future obligations rises, and a portfolio of ordinary assets may not keep pace. This interest-rate sensitivity is the central risk to manage.

Map the liability drivers:

- the discount rate (often linked to high-quality corporate or government bond yields) and its sensitivity (duration);
- inflation linkage for obligations that escalate with prices or wages;
- longevity and demographic assumptions that extend the obligation;
- benefit formulas, accruals, and future service for ongoing plans.

The liability behaves like a short position in a long-duration bond (and, where inflation-linked, a short position in inflation). Hedging it requires holding assets that move the same way.

### Use Liability-Driven Investing To Match The Interest-Rate Sensitivity

Liability-driven investing (LDI) hedges the interest-rate component of the liability by holding long-duration, high-quality bonds whose value rises when rates fall, offsetting the rise in the liability.

Apply LDI deliberately:

- match the duration of the asset portfolio to the duration of the liabilities, so rate moves affect both sides similarly;
- use high-quality bonds (government or high-grade corporate) and, where available, interest-rate swaps or overlays to extend duration efficiently;
- recognize that perfect matching is rarely achievable or costless, and that residual basis risk remains;
- separate the LDI (hedging) sleeve from the growth (return-seeking) sleeve, each with a clear purpose.

The LDI sleeve is not there to maximize return; it is there to stabilize the funding ratio against rate moves. Its low expected return is the cost of reducing surplus risk.

### Decide The Hedge Ratio Based On Funding Status And Risk Tolerance

The fraction of liabilities hedged (the hedge ratio) is a deliberate decision that trades surplus stability against return-seeking. It should not be set by default or left to drift.

Set the hedge ratio against:

- the funding ratio — well-funded plans can hedge more, locking in the surplus; underfunded plans may need more growth assets to close the deficit, accepting more surplus risk;
- the sponsor's risk tolerance and ability to make contributions if the deficit widens;
- the regulatory and accounting treatment of funded status volatility;
- the plan's horizon and whether it is open (ongoing accruals) or closed to new accruals.

A common glide path increases the hedge ratio as funding improves, locking in gains and reducing surplus risk as the plan approaches full funding.

### Balance Hedging With Return-Seeking To Close Deficits

A plan that is underfunded cannot close the deficit by hedging alone, because hedging assets earn the discount rate, which by construction just matches the liability growth. Closing a deficit requires return-seeking assets whose expected return exceeds the discount rate.

Balance the two sleeves:

- size the growth sleeve to the deficit-closing need and the sponsor's risk capacity;
- recognize that reaching for return introduces surplus risk — the growth assets may underperform and widen the deficit;
- avoid taking so much equity risk that a single bad period jeopardizes the plan's viability or forces large contributions;
- use diversifying growth strategies (private markets, real assets, absolute return) where appropriate, sized to liquidity and governance capacity.

The split between LDI and growth is the central allocation decision for a liability-driven investor, and it should be made explicitly, not by inertia.

### Account For Inflation Linkage Where Obligations Escalate

Where benefits or spending escalate with inflation or wages, nominal duration matching is insufficient. The portfolio must also hedge inflation to keep the surplus stable in real terms.

Address inflation linkage:

- use inflation-linked bonds or swaps to match the inflation sensitivity of the liabilities;
- recognize that real-asset and equity allocations provide partial, uncertain inflation hedging over long horizons but not a precise match;
- separate the inflation hedge from the interest-rate hedge, since they are distinct risks.

Ignoring inflation linkage produces a surplus that looks stable nominally but erodes in real terms whenever inflation surprises.

### Manage The Sponsor Covenant And Governance Realities

A pension does not exist in a vacuum; its allocation must reflect the strength and circumstances of the sponsor (the employer or entity funding it) and the governance that oversees it.

Consider:

- the strength of the sponsor covenant — a strong sponsor can run more return-seeking risk knowing contributions are available; a weak sponsor must hedge more to protect against default;
- the cyclicality of the sponsor's business and its correlation to the growth assets (avoid doubling the sponsor's risk in the portfolio);
- governance capacity to oversee complex strategies, derivatives overlays, and private markets;
- regulatory constraints on allocations, contributions, and benefit changes.

The right allocation for an identical liability differs depending on whether the sponsor is a growing, diversified employer or a stressed entity in a cyclical industry.

## Common Traps

### Benchmarking Assets To A Market Index Instead Of The Liability

A portfolio can beat a market index while losing ground against its obligations. The liability is the benchmark.

### Ignoring Interest-Rate Sensitivity Of The Liability

When rates fall, liabilities rise. A portfolio that does not match duration can see its funding ratio collapse despite decent asset returns.

### Confusing Hedging With Return-Seeking

LDI stabilizes the surplus; it does not close a deficit. Closing a deficit requires growth assets, which introduce surplus risk.

### Setting The Hedge Ratio By Default Or Inertia

The hedge ratio is a deliberate decision tied to funding status, risk tolerance, and sponsor strength. Leaving it static ignores changing conditions.

### Reaching For Return Without Surplus-Risk Discipline

Underfunded plans that load on equity to close a deficit can widen it sharply in a downturn, risking sponsor distress and large contributions.

### Overlooking Inflation Linkage

Nominal matching leaves the surplus exposed to inflation surprises where benefits escalate in real terms.

### Ignoring The Sponsor Covenant And Correlation

A portfolio that doubles the sponsor's business risk maximizes the chance that both the sponsor and the plan weaken together.
