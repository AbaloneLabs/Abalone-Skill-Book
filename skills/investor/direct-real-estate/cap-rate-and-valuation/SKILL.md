# Cap Rate And Valuation

The capitalization rate (cap rate) is the primary valuation shorthand for income-producing real estate: cap rate = net operating income / property value. It is the real-estate equivalent of an earnings yield. But a cap rate is not a return — it is a valuation multiple inverse that bundles a risk-free rate, a risk premium, expected NOI growth, and liquidity assumptions. Quoting a cap rate as "the return" without unpacking these components leads to overpaying for properties whose income is declining, whose expenses are understated, or whose risk premium is too thin.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "is this property a good deal", "what cap rate should I use", "how do I value a rental property", or "are real estate prices justified". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating a cap rate as a guaranteed yield, and from comparing properties on headline cap rates without normalizing NOI, growth, and risk.

## Core Rules

### Define NOI Accurately And Normalize It

Cap rate is only as meaningful as the NOI in the numerator. Net operating income must be computed consistently:

- Gross potential rent minus vacancy and credit loss minus operating expenses.
- Operating expenses include property taxes, insurance, maintenance, management, utilities, and reserves for replacement — but not debt service or depreciation.
- Normalize for below- or above-market leases, deferred maintenance, and one-time items.
- Distinguish in-place NOI from stabilized NOI (after lease-up or renovation).

A seller's pro forma NOI often overstates true NOI by underestimating vacancies, capex reserves, and management. Always reconstruct NOI from leases and expenses, not from a marketing pro forma.

### Unpack The Cap Rate Into Its Components

A cap rate is not arbitrary; it reflects market pricing of several factors:

- Risk-free rate (long-term government bond yield): the baseline opportunity cost.
- Risk premium: compensation for illiquidity, tenant credit, lease rollover, location, property quality, and market risk.
- Expected NOI growth: a lower cap rate is justified if rents and NOI are expected to grow; a higher cap rate if NOI is flat or declining.
- Inflation linkage: leases that adjust with inflation support lower cap rates.

The cap rate should make sense relative to these. A 3% cap rate when the 10-year Treasury yields 5% implies either strong expected growth or a very thin risk premium — both of which are vulnerable. Decompose the cap rate before accepting it as fair.

### Compare Cap Rates Within Property Type, Quality, And Market

Cap rates vary systematically and must be compared like-for-like:

- Property type: industrial and multifamily typically trade at lower cap rates than office or retail, reflecting growth and risk.
- Quality and location: prime assets in supply-constrained, high-growth markets trade at lower cap rates than secondary assets in weaker markets.
- Tenant credit and lease term: long leases to credit tenants support lower cap rates; short leases or weak tenants require higher.
- Market cycle: cap rates compress in capital-rich booms and expand in downturns.

Comparing a Class A industrial cap rate to a Class C office cap rate is meaningless. Compare within segment and adjust for quality, location, and lease structure.

### Separate Going-In Cap Rate From Stabilized And Exit Cap Rates

Different cap rates answer different questions:

- Going-in (in-place) cap rate: year-one NOI / purchase price. A snapshot of current yield.
- Stabilized cap rate: NOI after lease-up, renovation, or normalization / price. Reflects the property's potential, not its current state.
- Exit cap rate: the cap rate assumed at sale; critical to development and value-add returns.

Value-add and development deals often show low going-in cap rates but attractive stabilized yields. The return depends on the gap between going-in and stabilized, and on the exit cap rate assumption. Stress-test the exit cap rate — over-optimistic exit assumptions inflate projected returns.

### Model NOI Growth And Its Drivers

A property's value depends on future NOI, not just current. Assess growth drivers:

- Contractual rent escalations in leases (fixed bumps, CPI linkage).
- Market rent growth from supply/demand, employment, and demographics.
- Occupancy improvements and expense control.
- Value-add opportunities (renovation, re-tenanting, redevelopment).
- Risks: supply waves, tenant rollover to lower rents, expense inflation outpacing rent growth, obsolescence.

A low cap rate is justified only if NOI growth is strong and durable. If growth is uncertain or negative, a higher cap rate (lower price) is required to compensate.

### Assess The Risk Premium Adequacy

The cap rate's risk premium must compensate for real estate-specific risks:

- Illiquidity: real estate cannot be sold quickly; transactions take months and cost 5-8%.
- Tenant concentration and credit: a single-tenant property is equity-like exposure to that tenant.
- Lease rollover and downtime: re-leasing risk, free rent, and tenant improvements.
- Capital expenditure: aging properties require ongoing investment that erodes NOI.
- Market and location risk: neighborhood decline, zoning, environmental, climate.
- Leverage amplification: most property is leveraged, magnifying equity volatility.

If the cap rate barely exceeds the risk-free rate, the risk premium is thin and the property offers little compensation for illiquidity and risk. Judge whether the premium is adequate for the specific property.

### Account For Inflation, Interest Rates, And Cap-Rate Cycles

Cap rates move with the rate environment and capital flows:

- Rising interest rates without NOI growth push cap rates up and values down.
- Inflation can support NOI growth for properties with short, CPI-linked leases (apartments, hotels), partially offsetting rate pressure.
- Capital flows (foreign, institutional) compress cap rates independent of fundamentals; reversal expands them.
- Cap-rate cycles mean-revert; buying at cycle-low cap rates risks value decline when rates or risk appetite normalize.

Time the purchase relative to the cap-rate cycle and the rate environment. A great property bought at a cycle-low cap rate can be a poor investment if cap rates rise.

## Common Traps

### Quoting Cap Rate As The Investor's Return

Cap rate is a property-level unlevered yield, not the investor's return. Actual return depends on leverage, financing costs, capex, taxes, transaction costs, and exit cap rate.

### Trusting The Seller's Pro Forma NOI

Marketing pro formas routinely overstate NOI by understating vacancies, capex reserves, and management. Reconstruct NOI from leases and actual expenses.

### Comparing Cap Rates Across Different Property Types Or Qualities

A 4% multifamily cap rate and a 7% office cap rate are not directly comparable. Compare within segment, quality, and location.

### Ignoring The Growth Implied By A Low Cap Rate

A low cap rate implies the market expects strong NOI growth. If growth is unlikely, the price is too high.

### Using An Optimistic Exit Cap Rate In Development Returns

Value-add and development returns are highly sensitive to the exit cap rate assumption. Optimistic exit caps inflate returns and hide downside.

### Forgetting That Cap Rates Move With Interest Rates

A cap rate appropriate when the 10-year Treasury yields 1% is too low when it yields 5%. Real estate values fall when rates rise without NOI growth.

## Self-Check

- [ ] NOI is reconstructed from leases and normalized for vacancies, capex reserves, management, and one-time items, not taken from the seller's pro forma.
- [ ] The cap rate is decomposed into risk-free rate, risk premium, expected growth, and inflation linkage, and the components are judged adequate.
- [ ] Cap rates are compared within property type, quality, location, and lease structure, not across unlike properties.
- [ ] Going-in, stabilized, and exit cap rates are distinguished, and the exit cap rate is stress-tested.
- [ ] NOI growth drivers (escalations, market rents, value-add) and risks (supply, rollover, obsolescence) are modeled.
- [ ] The risk premium is assessed for adequacy given illiquidity, tenant concentration, capex, and leverage.
- [ ] The rate environment and cap-rate cycle are considered for purchase timing.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a cap rate as a guaranteed return and references investor-specific leverage, horizon, tax, and risk tolerance.
