# Leverage And Financing

Leverage is what transforms real estate from a stable income asset into a high-return, high-risk equity. A property bought with 75% debt behaves nothing like the same property bought for cash: returns are amplified in both directions, refinancing becomes a recurring risk, and the lender — not just the market — can trigger a loss of the asset. Quoting a cap rate or cash-on-cash return without stating the leverage behind it hides the dominant risk driver in direct real estate.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how much should I borrow against a property", "is this a good mortgage", "what happens if rates rise", or "how does leverage affect my real estate return". The goal is to prevent the agent from presenting leveraged returns as if they were property returns, and from ignoring refinancing, coverage, and downside risk.

## Core Rules

### Separate Property Return From Leveraged Equity Return

The unlevered property return (cap rate plus NOI growth minus capex) is a function of the real estate. The levered equity return is a function of the financing layered on top. Leverage magnifies the property return:

- Positive spread (property return > cost of debt): leverage boosts equity return (positive leverage).
- Negative spread (property return < cost of debt): leverage destroys equity return (negative leverage).

Always model both the unlevered property return and the levered equity return. A "20% cash-on-cash return" usually means a 5% property return amplified by aggressive leverage — and the same leverage produces a large loss if NOI falls or rates rise. State the leverage ratio and the spread explicitly.

### Use LTV, DSCR, And DYR Together To Assess Debt Capacity

Three ratios govern debt sustainability:

- Loan-to-value (LTV): debt / property value. Higher LTV means more risk and less equity cushion. Lenders cap LTV (often 65-75% for commercial, higher for agency residential).
- Debt-service coverage ratio (DSCR): NOI / debt service. Measures ability to cover payments from operations. Below 1.2x is thin; below 1.0x means the property cannot cover debt from operations.
- Debt yield ratio (DYR): NOI / loan amount. The lender's cap rate if they foreclose; a floor on lending regardless of DSCR.

A loan that passes one test can fail another. A high-LTV loan with adequate DSCR at a low rate can fail when rates reset. Check all three under stress, not just at origination.

### Model The Full Effect Of Leverage, Including Downside

Leverage is symmetric in theory but asymmetric in practice because of foreclosure and refinancing:

- Upside: NOI growth and cap-rate compression are amplified into large equity gains.
- Downside: NOI decline or cap-rate expansion is amplified into large equity losses; equity can go to zero or negative (underwater) while the property still operates.
- Foreclosure: if debt service cannot be met and reserves are exhausted, the lender takes the property; the equity is wiped out.
- Recourse: some debt is personally recourse, exposing other assets beyond the property.

Model downside scenarios (NOI decline, rate rise, cap-rate expansion) and check whether the equity survives. "Stress-test the leverage" means computing equity value under adverse conditions, not just the base case.

### Assess Refinancing Risk And Debt Structure

Most commercial real estate debt is not held to maturity; it is refinanced every 5-10 years. Refinancing risk is a recurring, structural feature:

- Maturity wall: debt coming due in a given period must be refinanced at then-current rates and terms.
- Rate environment at refinance: if rates have risen or lending standards tightened, refinancing may be unavailable or punitive.
- Value decline at refinance: if property value has fallen, the new loan may be smaller than the old, requiring a capital injection or equity dilution.
- Amortization: interest-only loans maximize cash flow but build no equity and create a larger maturity balloon; amortizing loans reduce principal but reduce cash flow.

Map the debt maturity schedule and model refinancing under stress. A property that is profitable but cannot refinance its maturing debt is forced into sale or foreclosure.

### Match Fixed/Floating Mix And Term To The Strategy

Debt structure should match the property's cash flow stability and the investor's risk tolerance:

- Fixed-rate, long-term: predictable payments, protection from rate rises, but prepayment penalties and no benefit from rate falls.
- Floating-rate: lower initial cost, flexibility, but payment volatility and direct exposure to rate rises; often used for short-hold or value-add strategies.
- Interest-only periods: boost near-term cash flow and returns, but increase refinance balloon and reduce amortization.
- Term: shorter term means more frequent refinance risk; longer term means more rate lock-in.

A floating-rate loan on a stable, long-hold property takes uncompensated rate risk; a fixed-rate loan on a short value-add flip may incur prepayment costs. Align the debt structure with the hold strategy.

### Account For Guarantees, Covenants, And Prepayment

Loan terms beyond rate and LTV carry real risk:

- Personal guarantees (carve-outs and full recourse): expose the borrower's other assets; "bad-boy" carve-outs can spring into full recourse on certain defaults.
- Financial covenants: DSCR, LTV, and reporting requirements; covenant breaches trigger default or reserves.
- Prepayment penalties, yield maintenance, and defeasance: restrict early repayment and impose costs to exit or refinance early.
- Assumability: whether a buyer can assume the loan affects sale flexibility and value.

Read the loan documents. A favorable-looking rate can come with covenants and penalties that materially increase risk or reduce exit flexibility.

### Consider The Cost Of Capital And Optimal Leverage

Optimal leverage is not "as much as the lender will give." It balances:

- Return enhancement from positive spread.
- Risk of foreclosure and equity wipeout in downside.
- Flexibility for capex, leasing, and downturns (reserves and covenant headroom).
- Tax shield from interest deductibility (jurisdiction-dependent, with limitations).
- Personal risk tolerance and the consequences of losing the property.

Conservative leverage (lower LTV, fixed-rate, amortizing) sacrifices some return for resilience. Aggressive leverage maximizes returns in good times and maximizes losses in bad times. Match leverage to the investor's ability and willingness to bear downside, not to the maximum available.

## Common Traps

### Presenting Leveraged Cash-On-Cash Return As The Property Return

A 20% cash-on-cash return often reflects 5% property return amplified by 75% leverage. The leverage, not the property, drives the return — and the same leverage drives the loss in downturns.

### Ignoring Refinancing Risk

Debt matures and must be refinanced. A profitable property that cannot refinance at maturity is forced into distress. Model the maturity wall.

### Using Floating-Rate Debt Without Modeling Rate Rises

Floating-rate debt maximizes exposure to rate increases. A 2% rate rise can turn positive cash flow negative and trigger covenant breaches.

### Overleveraging To Maximize Returns

Maximum LTV maximizes upside and downside. It leaves no cushion for NOI declines, capex, or refinance shortfalls, and turns volatility into foreclosure risk.

### Overlooking Personal Guarantees And Covenants

Recourse carve-outs and financial covenants expose assets beyond the property and can trigger default on technical breaches.

### Forgetting Prepayment And Assumability Constraints

Yield maintenance and defeasance make early exit costly; non-assumable loans reduce buyer pool and sale value.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Unlevered property return and levered equity return are both modeled, and the leverage ratio and spread are stated.
- [ ] LTV, DSCR, and debt yield are checked under stress, not just at origination.
- [ ] Downside scenarios (NOI decline, rate rise, cap-rate expansion) are modeled and equity survival is tested.
- [ ] The debt maturity schedule is mapped, and refinancing is modeled under stress (higher rates, lower values).
- [ ] Fixed/floating mix, amortization, and term are matched to the property's cash flow stability and the hold strategy.
- [ ] Personal guarantees, covenants, prepayment penalties, and assumability are read from the loan documents.
- [ ] Leverage is chosen based on the investor's downside tolerance and the cost of capital, not maximized to the lender's limit.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting leveraged returns without the leverage risk and references investor-specific risk tolerance, liquidity, and horizon.
