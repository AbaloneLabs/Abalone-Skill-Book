# Rental Income And Occupancy

Rental income is the engine of direct real estate, but gross rent is not what the investor keeps. Between the lease and the bank sit vacancy, credit loss, turnover costs, operating expenses, capital expenditures, and management — all of which erode gross rent into net operating income (NOI). Properties that look attractive on gross yield can deliver poor net returns because of high turnover, weak tenants, expense inflation, or deferred maintenance. Analyzing rental income means analyzing the gap between gross potential rent and durable NOI.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how much income will this property generate", "is the rent sustainable", "what vacancy should I assume", or "how do I evaluate a rental property's cash flow". The goal is to prevent the agent from quoting gross rent or an optimistic pro forma as the return, and from ignoring the operating realities that determine actual cash flow.

## Core Rules

### Start From Gross Potential Rent, Then Subtract Realistically

NOI is built bottom-up from gross potential rent (GPR) — the rent if every unit were leased at market with no concessions — then reduced by real-world frictions:

- Vacancy and credit loss: unleased units and uncollected rent.
- Concessions: free rent, tenant improvements, leasing commissions.
- Operating expenses: taxes, insurance, maintenance, utilities, management, administration.
- Reserves for replacement: capital reserves for roof, HVAC, and other recurring capex.

A common error is to use the seller's or agent's stabilized vacancy and expense assumptions, which are almost always optimistic. Reconstruct each line from leases, market data, and the property's actual condition.

### Model Vacancy And Credit Loss Conservatively

Vacancy is not a single number; it varies by property type, market, and tenant quality:

- Market vacancy: the structural vacancy rate for the property type and submarket (apartments 5%, office 10-15%, retail varies, single-tenant near 0% if leased).
- Frictional vacancy: the downtime between tenants as leases roll.
- Credit loss: tenants who stop paying; higher for weaker credit and small businesses.
- Turnover vacancy: the period to clean, repair, and re-lease a vacated unit.

For single-tenant properties, vacancy is binary: 0% while leased, 100% while vacant. The risk is concentrated at lease rollover. For multi-tenant properties, vacancy is averaged but still cyclical. Use market-appropriate vacancy and stress it for downturns.

### Analyze Lease Terms, Rollover, And Renewal Economics

The lease structure determines income stability and growth:

- Lease length: short leases (apartments, annual) reprice quickly but turn over often; long leases (office, retail, 5-10y) are stable but lock in below-market rents if the market rises.
- Rent escalations: fixed bumps, CPI linkage, or flat; flat long leases lose real value to inflation.
- Renewal probability and economics: renewing tenants cost less (no downtime, lower TI/LC) but may demand concessions; new tenants bring market rent but cost leasing commissions and improvements.
- Below- or above-market rents: rolling below-market leases up is growth; rolling above-market leases down is decline.

Map the lease rollover schedule. A property with many leases expiring simultaneously faces concentrated re-leasing risk; staggered expirations smooth income.

### Separate Operating Expenses From Capital Expenditures

Operating expenses (opex) are recurring and deducted in NOI; capital expenditures (capex) are periodic and lumpy, and proper NOI should reserve for them:

- Opex: taxes, insurance, routine maintenance, management, utilities — recurring annual costs.
- Capex reserves: roof, HVAC, parking lot, structural, unit turnover renovations — large, periodic costs that erode long-run NOI if not reserved.
- Net leases shift opex to the tenant (lower landlord opex, but tenant credit risk).
- Gross leases leave opex with the landlord (higher opex, expense inflation risk).

A property showing strong NOI with no capex reserve is overstating cash flow. Always reserve for capex based on the property's age and condition, even if the seller's pro forma omits it.

### Assess Tenant Quality And Concentration

Income durability depends on who pays it:

- Tenant creditworthiness: financial strength, payment history, business viability.
- Concentration: single-tenant or top-tenant concentration makes the property equity-like exposure to that tenant.
- Industry diversification: tenants in the same industry correlate (e.g., a retail center full of discretionary retailers in a recession).
- Lease guaranties: corporate guarantees versus unit-level entities; franchisee strength.

For single-tenant or high-concentration properties, analyze the tenant like an equity investment. A great building with a failing tenant is a problem, not an asset.

### Evaluate Market Rent Trends And Supply

NOI growth depends on market rents, which depend on supply and demand:

- Demand drivers: employment, population, household formation, business activity.
- Supply pipeline: new construction in the submarket; oversupply pressures rents and occupancy.
- Rent versus affordability: residential rents bounded by local incomes; commercial rents by tenant profitability.
- Competing properties: new Class A supply can pull tenants from older Class B/C.

A property in a market with heavy new supply faces rent declines and higher concessions even if its own operations are sound. Assess the submarket, not just the property.

### Account For Management, Operations, And Investor Involvement

Direct real estate is operationally intensive:

- Property management: professional management costs 3-5% of revenue but is essential; self-management saves cost but demands time and expertise.
- Tenant relations: handling complaints, late rent, evictions, renewals.
- Maintenance and vendors: coordinating repairs, contracts, quality control.
- Regulatory compliance: fair housing, building codes, rent regulation, licensing.

A property that "cash flows" on paper but requires extensive unpaid owner labor is not delivering the modeled return. Include the true cost of management, whether paid to a third party or valued as the owner's time.

## Common Traps

### Quoting Gross Rent Or Pro Forma NOI As The Return

Gross rent overstates income; pro forma NOI overstates net cash flow. Reconstruct NOI from leases and realistic expenses, including capex reserves.

### Underestimating Vacancy And Turnover

Marketed vacancy assumptions are optimistic. Single-tenant properties face binary vacancy at rollover; multi-tenant properties face cyclical and frictional vacancy.

### Omitting Capex Reserves

Lumpy capital expenditures (roof, HVAC, turnover renovations) erode NOI over time. A pro forma without capex reserves overstates cash flow.

### Ignoring Lease Rollover Concentration

Many leases expiring at once create concentrated re-leasing risk and potential income disruption. Stagger expirations where possible.

### Treating A Single-Tenant Property As Diversified Real Estate

Single-tenant income is equity-like exposure to that tenant. Tenant failure wipes out income regardless of the building's quality.

### Forgetting The Owner's Time And Management Cost

Self-management is not free; it is unpaid labor. Professional management is a real expense. Either way, the cost belongs in the return calculation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] NOI is reconstructed from gross potential rent minus realistic vacancy, credit loss, concessions, operating expenses, and capex reserves, not from a seller's pro forma.
- [ ] Vacancy and credit loss are modeled conservatively for the property type, including binary single-tenant rollover risk.
- [ ] Lease terms, rollover schedule, escalations, and renewal economics are analyzed for income stability and growth.
- [ ] Operating expenses and capex reserves are separated, and capex is reserved based on property age and condition.
- [ ] Tenant quality, concentration, industry diversification, and lease guaranties are assessed.
- [ ] Market rent trends, supply pipeline, and competing properties are evaluated for the submarket.
- [ ] Management cost (professional or owner time) and operational involvement are included in the return.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting gross rent or pro forma NOI as the return and references investor-specific horizon, operational capacity, and risk tolerance.
