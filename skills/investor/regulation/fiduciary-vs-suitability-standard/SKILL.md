# Fiduciary Versus Suitability Standard

The label on the person giving you financial advice is not a detail — it determines what legal obligation they owe you when their interest conflicts with yours. The judgment problem is that investors assume anyone called a "financial advisor" is legally required to act in their best interest, when in many jurisdictions a broker operating under a suitability standard is only required to recommend something merely "suitable," which permits recommendations that pay the advisor more at the client's expense. Agents and investors who do not understand this distinction cannot evaluate whether the advice they receive is trustworthy or merely permissible, and they routinely accept conflicted recommendations that look like guidance.

This skill is for understanding the standard of care an advisor owes and the conflicts that shape their recommendations. It is not legal advice; the rules differ by jurisdiction, role, and account type and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Two Standards And What Each Actually Requires

The distinction is not semantic; it defines what the advisor may legally do.

- **Fiduciary standard** — the advisor must act in the client's best interest, place the client's interest above their own, avoid or fully disclose conflicts, and charge fair compensation. Applies to registered investment advisors (RIAs) and to certain retirement accounts under specific rules.
- **Suitability standard** — the advisor must only recommend an investment that is reasonable for the client given their profile (age, goals, risk tolerance, finances). It does not require the recommendation to be the best or lowest-cost option, and it permits conflicts if disclosed.

A suitable recommendation can still be more expensive and inferior to available alternatives. "Suitable" is a low bar; "best interest" is a higher one. Know which one your advisor is held to for the specific advice and account in question.

### Recognize That The Same Person Can Owe Different Standards In Different Contexts

Many advisors are dual-registrants — registered both as an investment advisor (fiduciary) and as a broker (suitability) — and the standard they owe can change depending on the account and the product.

Watch for:

- a fiduciary standard on ongoing advisory or managed-money relationships;
- a suitability standard on one-time brokerage transactions, annuity sales, or commission products;
- "hat-switching," where the advisor wears the fiduciary hat to earn trust and the broker hat to sell a commission product.

Ask, for each specific recommendation, under which capacity and standard the advisor is acting. The answer changes the protection you have.

### Identify The Compensation Model Behind The Advice

How an advisor is paid predicts the conflicts they face. Compensation is not a side issue; it shapes recommendations.

Common models:

- **fee-only** — paid only by the client (a percentage of assets, a flat fee, or hourly); fewest inherent conflicts; the cleanest alignment with the client's interest.
- **fee-based** — a mix of client fees and commissions or other third-party compensation; carries potential conflicts that must be disclosed and managed.
- **commission** — paid by product providers for selling their products; creates a direct conflict between the advisor's income and the client's cost, and incentivizes more and higher-commission transactions.

Fee-only does not guarantee good advice, and commission does not guarantee bad advice, but the structure of incentives is a fact that must be understood and weighed.

### Map The Specific Conflicts That Affect Recommendations

Conflicts are not always disclosed in a way the investor understands. Identify the ones most likely to distort advice.

Typical conflicts:

- selling a higher-cost share class or annuity when a cheaper equivalent exists;
- recommending a proprietary fund or in-house product over better third-party options;
- churning or excessive trading to generate commissions;
- recommending a rollover from a lower-cost employer plan into a higher-cost IRA that pays the advisor;
- pushing insurance or annuity products with high commissions regardless of fit;
- steering assets to a custodian or platform that pays the advisor.

Where a conflict exists, ask what the advisor would recommend if they were paid identically regardless of the choice. If the answer differs, the conflict is shaping the advice.

### Distinguish Ongoing Advice From One-Time Transactions

A fiduciary relationship is usually ongoing and covers the totality of the advice. A suitability transaction is often point-in-time. This matters because the protection you have for a single transaction may be much weaker than for a managed relationship, even with the same advisor.

For significant one-time decisions — a rollover, an annuity, a large purchase — confirm in writing under what standard the recommendation is being made and how the advisor is compensated.

### Use Disclosure As A Floor, Not A Ceiling

Disclosure of conflicts is required in many contexts, but disclosure does not neutralize a conflict; it merely transfers the burden of vigilance to the client. Do not treat a disclosed conflict as a resolved one. A disclosed conflict still affects the recommendation, and the investor still bears the job of asking whether a less conflicted alternative exists.

### Verify The Advisor's Registration And Disciplinary History

The standard of care is only useful if the advisor is actually registered and accountable. Verify registration and check for disciplinary history through the relevant regulator's public database before engaging, and periodically thereafter.

## Common Traps

### Assuming "Financial Advisor" Implies A Fiduciary

The title is not regulated in many jurisdictions. A person called an advisor may be operating under a suitability standard, not a fiduciary one.

### Trusting Disclosure To Neutralize Conflicts

Disclosure informs but does not remove the conflict. The recommendation can still be shaped by incentives the investor now merely knows about.

### Ignoring The Compensation Model

Fee-only, fee-based, and commission arrangements create different incentives. Not asking how the advisor is paid leaves the largest conflict invisible.

### Overlooking Hat-Switching By Dual-Registrants

The same person can be a fiduciary for some advice and a broker for other advice. The protection varies by the capacity in which the specific recommendation is made.

### Accepting A Rollover Without Comparing Costs

Rolling an employer plan into an advisor-managed IRA often raises costs and generates advisor compensation. Suitability permits it even when staying put is better for the client.

### Treating Suitability As Best Interest

A suitable recommendation can be more expensive and inferior to available alternatives. Suitability is a low bar, not a guarantee of the best choice.

### Skipping Background Verification

Registration and disciplinary history are publicly available. Failing to check them before engaging is an avoidable risk.
