# Accredited Investor And Private Access

Qualifying as an accredited investor opens a door, but it does not make what lies behind that door suitable. The judgment problem is that investors treat accreditation as a mark of competence or a license for superior opportunities, when it is only a regulatory threshold above which certain disclosures and protections no longer apply. Behind that door sit illiquid, opaque, high-fee investments whose risks are precisely the ones the public-market protections were designed to address. Agents and investors routinely underestimate lockups, overestimate the manager's edge, and treat "exclusive access" as evidence of quality — the same instinct that fraudsters exploit. The skill is separating genuine access to a real edge from paying a high price for illiquidity and opacity.

This skill is for evaluating whether private access is appropriate for a specific investor, not whether they merely qualify. It is not personalized financial advice; qualification rules, offering terms, and risks vary by jurisdiction and change over time.

## Core Rules

### Treat Accreditation As A Threshold, Not An Endorsement

Accredreditation is a regulatory line that assumes the investor can afford to lose the money or has the sophistication to evaluate the risk without the protections offered to the public. It is not a certification that the investor understands the investment, nor that the investment is good.

Understand:

- the financial thresholds (income, net worth, often excluding primary residence, and in some jurisdictions professional certification or knowledgeable-employee status);
- that crossing the threshold removes certain mandatory disclosures and protections, transferring more diligence burden to the investor;
- that qualifying says nothing about the quality, liquidity, or fairness of any specific offering.

An accredited investor can lose everything in a legitimate-but-bad private deal. The qualification protects the issuer, not the investor.

### Price The Illiquidity Before Committing

Private investments are illiquid by design, and the illiquidity is a cost, not a feature. The capital is typically locked for years, often a decade or more in private equity and venture funds, with no guaranteed exit.

Map the liquidity terms:

- the initial lockup period before any redemption is possible;
- gates, suspension rights, and side-pocketing that can extend or freeze access further;
- the absence of a public market, meaning exit depends on the manager's discretion, a fund wind-down, a sale, or an IPO that may never come;
- the J-curve, where early years show negative returns before later gains, and the investor cannot leave during the bad stretch.

An investor who might need the capital within the lockup should not commit it. Liquidity needs and lockup horizons must be matched at the outset, because the mismatch cannot be fixed later.

### Demand A Credible Source Of Edge, Not Just Access

Private markets charge high fees for access. The investor must identify why the manager can earn returns above public-market alternatives after those fees.

Questions to answer:

- what is the specific edge — information, structuring, operational control, distressed specialization, or network access to deals the public cannot reach;
- is the edge durable, or a one-time factor (a famous name, a single relationship, a legacy portfolio);
- does the net return, after fees and carry, still exceed a comparable public benchmark over a full cycle;
- is the apparent outperformance real after adjusting for survivorship, stale pricing, and selection bias in the track record.

Access without a credible, durable edge is just an expensive way to own illiquid beta. Past performance of private funds does not guarantee future results, and reported returns are often smoothed and biased.

### Account For The Asymmetry Of Fees And Carry

Private fund fees are back-loaded against the investor in bad outcomes and front-loaded to the manager in good ones.

Understand the structure:

- a management fee charged every year, including during the lockup and during loss years, often on committed (not invested) capital;
- carried interest that gives the manager a large share of profits above a hurdle, with limited or no clawback if later years reverse the gains;
- fees on capital called, transaction and monitoring fees, and other loads that compound;
- the net effect that the manager can do very well even when the investor merely matches public-market returns.

Compute the breakeven gross return the strategy must deliver for the investor to beat a simple public alternative after all fees. If that breakeven is implausibly high, the structure favors the manager.

### Stress The Lack Of Price Transparency And Reporting Lag

Private assets are marked infrequently and often using the manager's or an affiliated party's estimates. This produces smoothed returns that understate true volatility and mask drawdowns.

Consequences:

- the reported volatility and correlation of private assets are artificially low, flattering diversification claims;
- stale marks delay the recognition of losses, so the investor may not know a position is impaired until long after the fact;
- comparisons to public-market volatility are apples-to-oranges because public marks move daily and private marks do not.

Do not treat the smoothness of a private track record as evidence of low risk. It is often evidence of infrequent marking.

### Size Private Exposure Against Total Wealth And Concentration

Because private investments are illiquid and opaque, their size should reflect the investor's ability to absorb a total loss without disrupting their plan.

Consider:

- limiting private and illiquid exposure to a defined minority of investable assets, with enough liquid reserves to cover all needs through the lockup;
- the interaction with other illiquid commitments (real estate, a private business, employer stock) that already reduce liquidity;
- the concentration risk if the private investment fails alongside the investor's other risks (job, sector, geography);
- capital-call risk in fund commitments, where the investor must fund additional capital on the manager's schedule regardless of personal circumstances.

A private commitment that is small relative to total wealth and staggered over time is very different from one that concentrates liquidity and timing risk.

### Apply Heightened Diligence Because Public Protections Are Absent

Private placements are exempt from the registration and disclosure requirements that protect public investors. The diligence burden shifts entirely to the investor.

Verify independently:

- the manager's background, registration, and disciplinary history through public regulators;
- the custody arrangement and whether an independent qualified custodian holds the assets;
- the audit, the fund administrator, and whether reported figures are independently verified;
- the offering documents' conflicts, related-party transactions, and fee waterfall;
- the legal structure and the investor's limited rights and remedies if things go wrong.

Where protections are absent, skepticism must be higher, not lower.

## Common Traps

### Equating Accreditation With Suitability

Qualifying to invest does not mean the investment is suitable. The threshold protects the issuer; suitability is a separate judgment the investor must make.

### Underestimating The Lockup And Capital Calls

Committing capital that might be needed during the lockup, or failing to reserve for capital calls, creates forced-sale risk in other parts of the portfolio.

### Treating Access As Quality

Exclusivity is a marketing lever, not evidence of an edge. Fraudsters exploit the same instinct that legitimate managers use to sell access.

### Trusting Smoothed Private Returns As Low Risk

Infrequent marking understates volatility. A smooth private track record is often a reporting artifact, not genuine low risk.

### Ignoring The Fee And Carry Asymmetry

Management fees accrue in bad years, and carry rewards the manager in good years with limited downside sharing. The structure favors the manager across outcomes.

### Overstating Diversification From Correlation Of Smoothed Marks

Reported low correlation between private and public assets is partly an artifact of stale pricing. True stress correlations are higher than reported.

### Skipping Diligence Because It Feels Exclusive

The absence of public protections means diligence matters more, not less. Exclusivity is not a substitute for verification.

- [ ] The illiquidity was priced explicitly, including lockup, gates, side pockets, the J-curve, and the absence of a guaranteed exit.
- [ ] A credible, durable source of edge was identified, with net-of-fee returns compared to public alternatives over a full cycle.
- [ ] The fee and carry asymmetry was modeled, including management fees on committed capital, transaction and monitoring fees, and limited clawback.
