# Hedge Fund Due Diligence

Hedge fund due diligence must answer two questions: does the manager generate genuine alpha, and can the investor trust the operation with their capital? The first requires decomposing returns into beta, factor exposure, and alpha, and stress-testing drawdowns, leverage, and liquidity. The second requires scrutinizing the operational infrastructure, valuation, service providers, and the manager's integrity — because hedge funds, with their opacity, leverage, and performance fees, have been the site of repeated frauds. Diligence that checks the track record but not the operations, or that trusts the auditor without verifying independence, is how investors end up in funds whose returns were fabricated or whose operations collapse.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do I evaluate a hedge fund", "is this hedge fund legitimate", "what due diligence is needed", or "how do I avoid hedge fund fraud". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating track record as sufficient evidence, and from missing the operational, leverage, liquidity, and integrity dimensions that determine whether the returns are real and the capital is safe.

## Core Rules

### Decompose Returns Into Alpha, Beta, And Factor Exposure

The headline track record is the start, not the end:

- Beta decomposition: regress returns against market, size, value, momentum, credit, and other factors to identify systematic exposures. Much "alpha" is disguised beta.
- Net exposure and directionality: persistent net-long equity exposure earns equity beta; market-neutral funds should show low beta.
- Factor tilts: value, quality, momentum, and carry explain much of long-short performance; these are not alpha.
- True alpha: the residual return after accounting for beta and factors. This is what should justify performance fees.

A fund that returns 10% with 0.8 equity beta and a value tilt is largely earning replicable factor returns, not skill. Demand factor-adjusted alpha evidence, not headline returns.

### Analyze Drawdowns, Volatility, And Return Consistency

Risk metrics reveal what averages hide:

- Drawdowns: depth, duration, and frequency; how the fund performed in specific crises (2008, 2020, rate shocks).
- Volatility and Sharpe/Sortino: risk-adjusted return, but Sharpe can be gamed by selling tail risk (steady gains, occasional crashes).
- Calmar and MAR ratios: return relative to drawdown; measures downside-aware performance.
- Return distribution: skewness and kurtosis; negative skew and fat tails (common in short-vol and arbitrage strategies) signal tail risk not captured by volatility.

A high Sharpe ratio with negative skew is often a red flag: the strategy sells insurance, earning steady gains until a crash. Examine the full distribution, not just the average.

### Assess Correlation And Diversification Value Across Regimes

The fund's role depends on its correlation to the portfolio:

- Normal-regime correlation: low correlation adds diversification value.
- Crisis correlation: many "diversifying" strategies correlate in crises; test 2008, 2020, and rate-shock periods specifically.
- Regime dependence: arbitrage strategies depend on stable spreads and liquidity; macro and trend-following depend on directional moves; their correlation changes with regime.
- Crisis alpha: some strategies (managed futures, certain macro) perform in crises; others (relative value, arb) lose as spreads widen and leverage unwinds.

A fund that diversifies in normal times but correlates in the exact crisis the investor is hedging provides false comfort. Stress-test crisis behavior explicitly.

### Evaluate Leverage, Liquidity, And Capacity

These determine whether returns are sustainable and capital is safe:

- Leverage: gross and net exposure; the strategy's reliance on leverage to magnify small spreads; leverage amplifies losses and creates margin and funding risk.
- Liquidity: the fund's holdings (liquid futures versus illiquid credit/distressed); redemption terms and gates; whether the fund can meet redemptions without forced sales.
- Capacity: whether the strategy can absorb assets without eroding returns; arbitrage strategies have finite capacity.
- Funding and prime broker risk: reliance on prime brokers for leverage and custody; prime broker failure or margin changes can force liquidation.

High leverage on illiquid positions is a classic hedge fund failure mode: in stress, margins rise, liquidity vanishes, and the fund is forced to sell at distressed prices. Map leverage, liquidity, and funding together.

### Scrutinize Operational Infrastructure And Service Providers

Operational failures and fraud destroy capital as surely as bad trades:

- Independent fund administrator: computes NAV independently of the manager; a related-party administrator is a major red flag.
- Reputable auditor: Big Four or recognized firm; annual audit; auditor changes or obscure auditors are warnings.
- Custody and prime broker: independent custody of assets; the manager should not control asset movement.
- Valuation policy: fair-value methodology for illiquid positions; independent pricing sources; manager discretion in valuation is a fraud risk.
- Compliance and regulatory history: prior regulatory actions, litigation, investor disputes.

Independent administrator, reputable auditor, and independent custody are baseline requirements. Their absence — especially a related-party administrator or manager-controlled valuation — is a serious red flag.

### Screen For Fraud And Integrity Red Flags

Hedge fund frauds share common patterns; screen for them:

- Inconsistent or unverifiable returns: returns too smooth, too good, or inconsistent with stated strategy; demand position-level and trade-level evidence.
- Related-party transactions: the manager trading with affiliates, parking positions, or using side pockets to hide losses.
- Valuation manipulation: illiquid positions marked favorably by the manager; stale or non-independent marks.
- Opaque or complex structures: excessive complexity that obscures holdings, fees, or risk.
- Manager lifestyle and background: criminal history, undisclosed conflicts, lifestyle inconsistent with legitimate fees.
- Auditor and administrator issues: obscure, changed, or related-party service providers; late or restated audits.

The Madoff and other frauds featured smooth returns, related-party administration, and obscure auditors. Treat any of these as disqualifying until independently verified.

### Conduct Reference And Background Checks

References and background checks reveal what track records do not:

- Prime broker and service provider references: on the manager's behavior, risk management, and operational conduct.
- Prior employer and regulatory background: criminal, regulatory, and litigation history; employment verification.
- LP references: existing investors on communication, problem handling, and whether expectations were met.
- Personal and professional references: on the manager's character and integrity.

Conduct independent background and reference checks. Do not rely on the manager's references or the pitch.

### Assess Risk Management And Stress Testing

How the manager manages risk determines survival:

- Risk limits: gross, net, leverage, concentration, liquidity limits; whether they are enforced.
- Stress testing and scenario analysis: how the manager tests for tail events; whether positions are sized for stress.
- Risk culture: whether risk officers are independent and empowered; whether the manager has breached limits and how it was handled.
- Historical risk events: how the fund performed in prior crises; whether losses were within modeled risk.

A manager without disciplined, enforced risk limits is one stress event from disaster. Assess risk management as a core competency, not an afterthought.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Track Record As Sufficient Evidence

Headline returns may be beta, factor exposure, or fabricated. Decompose alpha and verify operations.

### Ignoring Negative Skew And Tail Risk

High Sharpe with negative skew often signals short-volatility strategies that crash. Examine the full distribution.

### Overlooking Leverage And Liquidity Mismatch

High leverage on illiquid positions fails in stress. Map leverage, liquidity, and funding together.

### Accepting Related-Party Administration Or Valuation

Manager-controlled NAV and valuation are fraud risks. Demand independent administrator, auditor, and custody.

### Missing Common Fraud Red Flags

Smooth returns, related-party transactions, obscure auditors, and complex structures are fraud patterns. Screen for them.

### Relying On The Pitch Instead Of Background Checks

Background, regulatory, and reference checks reveal integrity issues the pitch hides. Conduct them independently.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Returns are decomposed into beta, factor exposure, and true alpha, and headline returns are not accepted as skill.
- [ ] Drawdowns, volatility, skewness, and crisis performance are analyzed, not just average returns and Sharpe.
- [ ] Correlation and diversification value are tested across regimes, including explicit crisis periods.
- [ ] Leverage, liquidity, capacity, and prime-broker/funding risk are mapped together.
- [ ] Operational infrastructure (independent administrator, reputable auditor, independent custody, valuation policy) is verified; related-party arrangements are red flags.
- [ ] Fraud red flags (smooth returns, related-party transactions, valuation manipulation, obscure service providers, background issues) are screened.
- [ ] Independent background, regulatory, and reference checks are conducted.
- [ ] Risk management (limits, stress testing, risk culture, historical events) is assessed as a core competency.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a fund on track record alone and references investor-specific liquidity, fraud-risk tolerance, and due-diligence capacity.
