# Merger Arbitrage

Merger arbitrage is an event-driven strategy that seeks to capture the spread between a target company's current share price and the consideration offered in an announced acquisition. The thesis is that, because deals carry completion risk, the target trades at a discount to the deal price until closing, and the arbitrageur earns the spread if the deal completes. The judgment problem is that merger arbitrage is not true arbitrage. The spread exists precisely because the deal can fail, and deal failure typically causes a sharp drop in the target's price back toward (or below) its pre-announcement level. The strategy's returns come from correctly assessing completion probability, timeline, and break risk, not from a risk-free capture of the spread. Investors who treat the spread as free money, without rigorously pricing break risk and timeline, will suffer occasional large losses that wipe out many small gains.

This skill is for analyzing merger arbitrage opportunities with discipline about completion probability, break risk, and position sizing.

## Core Rules

### Decompose The Spread Into Its Components

The arbitrage spread is compensation for multiple risks. Decomposing it reveals whether the opportunity is attractive.

Identify the components:

- the deal price versus the current target price, which defines the gross spread;
- the time to expected closing, which annualizes the return;
- the probability of deal completion, which is the core judgment;
- the downside if the deal breaks, which defines the loss scenario;
- dividends, interest, and financing costs over the holding period.

A 5% spread over six months (roughly 10% annualized) is attractive only if the completion probability is high and the break downside is limited. A spread that looks large may be inadequate compensation for a high break risk.

### Assess Deal Completion Probability Rigorously

The core skill in merger arbitrage is estimating the probability that the deal will complete on the announced terms. This requires analyzing multiple risk factors.

Evaluate:

- shareholder approval risk, including the voting threshold, insider holdings, and the stance of major shareholders and proxy advisors;
- regulatory risk, including antitrust review, foreign investment approval, and sector-specific regulators, with attention to overlap and remedy requirements;
- financing risk, whether the acquirer has committed financing and the risk of financing failure in adverse markets;
- material adverse change (MAC) clauses and the conditions under which the acquirer can walk away;
- litigation risk from target shareholders seeking higher prices.

Each risk factor has a probability and a potential deal-breaking impact. The arbitrageur must aggregate these into an overall completion probability and compare it to the spread.

### Price The Break Scenario And Downside

When a deal breaks, the target's price typically falls sharply, often back to or below its pre-announcement level. The break downside defines the loss scenario.

Assess:

- the pre-deal share price, which is a reasonable estimate of the break price absent other bidders;
- the fundamental value of the target standalone, since the break price may differ from pre-deal if conditions changed;
- the presence of termination fees (breakup fees) paid by the target to the acquirer, which provide partial downside cushion;
- the reaction of other potential bidders, since a broken deal may attract rival offers;
- the speed of the price adjustment, since the arbitrageur may be able to exit before full reversion.

The expected return of the position is the probability-weighted average of the deal-completing gain and the deal-breaking loss. A strategy with many small gains and occasional large losses must be sized and diversified to survive the losses.

### Annualize The Return And Compare To Alternatives

Merger arbitrage returns must be annualized and compared to the investor's hurdle and alternative uses of capital.

Calculate:

- the annualized return if the deal completes on time, accounting for the spread, holding period, dividends, and costs;
- the annualized return if the deal closes late, since delays reduce the annualized return;
- the expected return after weighting by completion probability;
- the capital efficiency, since capital is tied up until closing;
- the comparison to risk-free rates and other absolute return strategies.

A deal with an attractive gross spread may have a modest annualized return if the timeline is long, or a poor expected return if break risk is significant. The analysis must be on an annualized, probability-weighted, cost-adjusted basis.

### Monitor For Competing Bids And Dynamic Developments

Merger arbitrage is dynamic. Competing bids, regulatory developments, and shareholder actions can change the spread and the completion probability rapidly.

Monitor:

- the potential for a topping bid from a rival acquirer, which can increase the target price and the spread;
- regulatory decisions, remedy requirements, and conditions that affect completion probability;
- shareholder activism and proxy contests that may oppose or seek to improve the deal;
- changes in financing conditions and acquirer commitment;
- the arbitrageur's ability to adjust or exit the position as developments unfold.

The strategy requires active monitoring and the flexibility to adjust positions as the risk-reward changes. Static analysis at announcement is insufficient.

### Diversify Across Independent Deals

Because individual deals have binary outcomes, merger arbitrage works best as a diversified portfolio of independent deals.

Apply:

- hold multiple uncorrelated deals across sectors and deal types;
- size each position so that a single deal break does not impair the portfolio;
- account for correlation in stress, since deal breaks can cluster in market-wide risk-off events;
- recognize that the strategy's return distribution (many small gains, occasional losses) requires diversification to achieve a smooth return.

Concentration in a single deal converts the strategy into a binary bet. A diversified book captures the statistical edge of pricing completion probability across many deals.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Spread As Risk-Free

The spread exists because of break risk. It is not free money; it is compensation for bearing deal-failure risk.

### Underestimating Regulatory Risk

Antitrust, foreign investment, and sector regulators can block or delay deals. Regulatory risk is a leading cause of breaks.

### Ignoring The Break Downside

When a deal breaks, the target falls sharply. The loss scenario must be priced, not just the completion gain.

### Over-Concentrating In A Single Deal

Individual deals are binary. A diversified book is required to achieve a smooth return.

### Neglecting Timeline And Annualization

A large spread over a long timeline may have a modest annualized return. Time is a cost.

### Failing To Monitor Dynamic Developments

Competing bids, regulatory actions, and shareholder votes change the risk-reward. Active monitoring is required.

### Overlooking Financing And MAC Risk

Acquirer financing failure and material adverse change clauses can provide walk-away rights that break the deal.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The spread is decomposed into gross spread, timeline, completion probability, break downside, and costs, and evaluated on an annualized, probability-weighted basis.
- [ ] Deal completion probability is assessed rigorously across shareholder approval, regulatory, financing, MAC, and litigation risks.
- [ ] The break scenario is priced, including the pre-deal price, termination fees, and potential rival bids, and the expected return accounts for the loss scenario.
- [ ] The annualized return is compared to risk-free rates and alternative absolute return strategies, with capital efficiency considered.
- [ ] The position is monitored for competing bids, regulatory developments, and dynamic changes, with flexibility to adjust.
- [ ] The strategy is diversified across independent, uncorrelated deals, with position sizing that prevents any single break from impairing the portfolio.
- [ ] Correlation in stress (clustering of breaks in risk-off events) is accounted for in portfolio construction.
- [ ] The guidance flags that merger arbitrage carries deal-break risk, that losses can be sharp, that returns are uncertain, that past performance does not guarantee future results, and that professional advice may be warranted for event-driven strategies.
