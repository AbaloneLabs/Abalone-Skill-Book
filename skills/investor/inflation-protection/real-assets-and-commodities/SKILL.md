# Real Assets And Commodities

Real assets are the classic inflation hedge, but the label hides enormous differences in how, when, and whether each asset actually protects purchasing power. The judgment problem is that investors lump real estate, commodities, infrastructure, gold, and equities together as "inflation protection," when their inflation sensitivity varies by horizon, by the type of inflation (demand-pull versus cost-push, expected versus unexpected), and by whether the investor holds the asset directly or through a security whose price is driven by other factors. Agents reach for the familiar hedge — often gold or a commodity fund — without asking whether it protects against the specific inflation risk over the relevant horizon, and are surprised when a commodity futures roll yield destroys the inflation hedge, or when equities fall during inflation because multiples compress faster than earnings rise. The skill is matching the real asset to the inflation scenario and horizon, not collecting hedges by category.

This skill is for evaluating real assets and commodities as inflation hedges. It is not personalized financial advice; structures, tax treatment, and outcomes vary, and past performance does not guarantee future results.

## Core Rules

### Match The Hedge To The Type And Horizon Of Inflation

Different real assets hedge different kinds of inflation over different horizons. There is no universal inflation hedge.

Map the hedge to the risk:

- **unexpected, near-term inflation** — commodities and commodity-linked equities tend to respond fastest, because spot prices adjust quickly;
- **persistent, long-term inflation** — real estate, infrastructure, and equities with pricing power protect purchasing power as cash flows and asset values reprice over years;
- **demand-pull inflation** (strong growth driving prices up) — equities and real estate often do well, because the same growth supports earnings and rents;
- **cost-push or supply-shock inflation** (rising input costs, weak growth) — commodities may rise while equities suffer, because margins compress.

Identify which inflation the portfolio needs to hedge, over what horizon, and choose assets whose cash flows actually reprice in that scenario.

### Understand Why Equities Are A Long-Horizon Real Asset But A Poor Short-Term Hedge

Equities are claims on real cash flows that can rise with nominal prices over time, making them a strong long-horizon inflation hedge. But over short horizons, equities often fall during inflation surprises because the discount rate rises and multiples compress faster than earnings adjust.

Distinguish horizons:

- over decades, equities have historically preserved and grown real purchasing power, as companies pass through inflation in prices and wages;
- over months to a few years, equities can be hurt by inflation surprises, rising rates, and margin compression, especially for long-duration growth stocks;
- the equity inflation hedge works through earnings and dividend growth, not through price stability.

For short-horizon inflation protection, equities are unreliable; for long-horizon real-return, they are central. Do not use equities as a precise near-term inflation hedge.

### Treat Real Estate As A Cash-Flow Hedge, Not A Hard Asset

Real estate protects against inflation when rents and property values reprice with inflation, ideally through short-lease structures and replacement-cost support. It fails as a hedge when leases are long and fixed, when occupancy falls, or when financing costs rise faster than rents.

Evaluate the repricing mechanism:

- short-lease or mark-to-market leases (apartments, hotels, self-storage) reprice quickly with inflation;
- long-lease structures (some commercial, triple-net) lock in nominal rents and hedge inflation poorly;
- replacement cost and constrained supply support property values as construction costs rise;
- leverage cuts both ways — fixed-rate debt can be inflation-friendly (paying back in cheaper real dollars), but floating-rate or refinancing risk can destroy the hedge.

Direct real estate, REITs, and real estate funds differ in liquidity, pricing, and leverage. Match the structure to the inflation hedge sought, and price the leverage explicitly.

### Distinguish Commodity Spot Exposure From Commodity Futures Exposure

This is the most important and most missed distinction in commodity investing. The inflation hedge lives in spot prices; many investors access commodities through futures, whose returns are dominated by the roll, not the spot price.

Understand the difference:

- **spot exposure** (direct holdings, commodity producers, physical-backed structures) tracks the commodity price directly and is the cleaner inflation hedge;
- **futures exposure** (most commodity index funds and ETFs) earns the spot return plus or minus the roll yield, plus collateral return;
- in contango (longer-dated futures above spot), rolling contracts produces a negative roll yield that can overwhelm spot gains — the reason many commodity funds lost money during periods when the underlying commodities rose;
- backwardation (spot above longer-dated futures) produces a positive roll yield that adds to returns.

A commodity futures fund is not the same as owning commodities. Evaluate the roll, the curve structure, and the cost of maintaining the position, or the "inflation hedge" may underperform the very inflation it was meant to catch.

### Evaluate Infrastructure And Regulated Assets As Inflation-Linked Cash Flows

Infrastructure (utilities, pipelines, toll roads, airports) and other regulated assets often have revenues explicitly or implicitly linked to inflation through tariff escalators, regulated returns, or concession terms. They can provide a durable, long-horizon inflation hedge with lower volatility than commodities.

Assess the linkage:

- whether revenues are contractually or regulatorily tied to inflation, and with what lag;
- the stability of demand (essential services hedge better than discretionary);
- the leverage and refinancing exposure, since rising rates can offset inflation-linked revenue;
- whether the investor holds the asset directly, through a listed security whose price is driven by equity-market factors, or through a fund.

Listed infrastructure is partly an equity and partly a real asset; its inflation hedge is diluted by equity-market correlation. Direct or unlisted infrastructure provides a cleaner but illiquid hedge.

### Treat Gold As A Store Of Value, Not A Precise Inflation Hedge

Gold is often marketed as the inflation hedge, but its relationship with inflation is weak over most horizons. Gold's real return over long periods is near zero; it preserves purchasing power in the very long run but does not track inflation closely year to year.

Understand what gold actually does:

- it tends to perform best during extreme stress, currency debasement, loss of faith in fiat, and real-rate declines, not during ordinary inflation;
- it pays no cash flow, so its return depends entirely on what someone else will pay, making it volatile and sentiment-driven;
- as a small allocation, it can diversify tail scenarios (currency crisis, deflationary stress) more than it hedges measured inflation.

Hold gold for what it is — a long-horizon store of value and tail diversifier — not as a reliable annual inflation hedge.

### Price The Cost And Structure Of Access

Each real-asset exposure carries a different access cost that affects net inflation protection.

Cost factors:

- commodity futures carry roll and management costs that erode returns;
- direct real estate and infrastructure carry illiquidity, leverage, and operational costs;
- REITs and listed real assets carry equity-market correlation and beta;
- private real-asset funds carry fees, carry, and lockups;
- gold carries storage, insurance, or ETF expense.

The net inflation hedge is the gross hedge minus the cost of access. A high-cost structure can turn a real-asset allocation into a poor inflation protector.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Real Assets As Equivalent Inflation Hedges

Each asset hedges a different inflation type over a different horizon. Lumping them together produces the wrong protection.

### Using Equities As A Short-Term Inflation Hedge

Equities preserve real value over decades but can fall during near-term inflation surprises as multiples compress.

### Ignoring The Roll In Commodity Futures

Futures returns are dominated by the roll yield, not the spot price. Contango can destroy the inflation hedge.

### Assuming Real Estate Hedges Inflation Regardless Of Lease Structure

Long fixed leases and refinancing risk can make real estate a poor inflation hedge despite being a "hard asset."

### Believing Gold Reliably Tracks Inflation

Gold's correlation with measured inflation is weak. It is a store of value and tail diversifier, not a precise inflation hedge.

### Overlooking The Cost And Structure Of Access

Roll costs, fees, carry, illiquidity, and equity-market correlation all reduce the net inflation protection a real-asset allocation delivers.

### Confusing Direct Asset Exposure With Listed Security Exposure

Listed real assets carry equity-market beta that dilutes their inflation hedge relative to direct holdings.
