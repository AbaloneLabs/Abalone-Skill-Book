# Life Insurance Needs And Type

Life insurance is a tool for replacing income or capital that would be lost at the death of an insured person. It is not an investment, not a default savings vehicle, and not something every household needs in the same amount. The two judgment problems are sizing, how much death benefit is actually required, and type selection, whether term or permanent insurance best serves the need at an acceptable cost. These are separate questions that agents often collapse into a single recommendation, leading to over-insurance with expensive permanent products or under-insurance with too-short term policies.

The harm this skill prevents is selling or recommending permanent cash-value insurance to households whose need is pure income replacement that term covers cheaply, and conversely failing to recommend insurance where dependents or debts genuinely create a gap. Life insurance is a long-duration, high-stakes financial commitment. Policy terms, illustrations, and tax treatment vary by jurisdiction and product, and illustrations can project optimistic returns. No output here is personalized advice. The agent should frame the need, the type tradeoff, and the cost, and should direct the user to evaluate illustrations and suitability carefully, ideally with an independent advisor.

## Core Rules

### Quantify The Need Before Choosing The Product

Start with the death benefit amount, not the product. The need is usually some combination of income replacement for dependents, paying off debts such as a mortgage, funding future obligations such as children's education, and final expenses. A common approach is a multiple of income adjusted for years of dependency, or a more precise present-value calculation of future needs net of existing assets. The agent should size the gap explicitly before discussing term versus permanent, because the type decision is meaningless without an amount. A household with no dependents and no shared debt may need little or no life insurance.

### Match The Policy Duration To The Need Duration

Term insurance covers a defined period and is cheap because most terms expire unused. The right term length is the length of the financial obligation: until children are independent, until the mortgage is paid, or until retirement assets can self-insure the survivor. Buying a ten-year term for a thirty-year obligation creates a coverage cliff when the insured is older and potentially uninsurable. The agent should align the term to the dependency horizon, and should flag that renewability and convertibility options have real value when health or circumstances could change.

### Apply Buy-Term-And-Invest-The-Difference Honestly

For pure income-replacement needs, term insurance plus investing the premium difference is usually cheaper than permanent insurance for the same death benefit, because permanent policies bundle insurance with a savings or investment component that carries higher fees. The agent should present this comparison transparently, including the cost per thousand of death benefit for term versus permanent. The honest version acknowledges that buy-term-and-invest-the-difference only works if the household actually invests the difference and earns a reasonable return, which is a behavioral assumption, not a guarantee.

### Identify The Narrow Cases Where Permanent Insurance Is Justified

Permanent insurance, including whole life, universal life, and variable life, is not inherently wrong, but its justification is narrow. Plausible cases include lifelong dependents such as a special needs child, estate liquidity for an illiquid taxable estate, business buy-sell funding, legacy or charitable bequest motives, and high-income households who have maxed other tax-advantaged accounts and value the tax-deferred cash value. The agent should name the specific justification rather than defaulting to permanent, and should be skeptical when the only justification is forced savings, since that benefit can be achieved more cheaply through automated investing.

### Scrutinize Illustrations And Assumptions

Permanent policy illustrations project cash value growth based on assumed interest rates, dividend scales, or market returns. These assumptions can be optimistic, and the gap between illustrated and actual performance can be large over decades. The agent should insist on seeing guaranteed values alongside illustrated values, should ask what assumptions drive the illustration, and should treat non-guaranteed elements as uncertain. A policy that only works at the illustrated rate is fragile.

### Account For Insurability And Health Underwriting

Life insurance cost and availability depend on health underwriting at the time of application. The agent should consider the applicant's current health, family history, and risky activities, and should value the option to lock in coverage while insurable. For term policies, convertibility to permanent without new underwriting can be valuable if health could deteriorate. Waiting to buy insurance to save premium can backfire if health changes make coverage unaffordable or unavailable.

### Consider Ownership, Beneficiary, And Estate Liquidity

Who owns the policy and who is the beneficiary affects estate tax, creditor protection, and control. Policies owned by the insured are included in the taxable estate; policies owned by an irrevocable trust or by adult beneficiaries may remove the proceeds from the estate. Contingent beneficiaries and regular reviews matter because life events such as divorce, remarriage, and births change the intended recipient. The agent should coordinate ownership and beneficiary designations with the estate plan, not treat them as paperwork.

## Common Traps

### Recommending Permanent Insurance For A Pure Income-Replacement Need

Permanent insurance costs many times what term costs for the same death benefit. Recommending it where the need is temporary income replacement over-insures the household at high cost and crowds out other savings.

### Sizing Coverage By A Rule Of Thumb Without Modeling The Gap

Ten times income is a starting point, not an answer. The agent should model dependents, debts, education funding, and existing assets rather than applying a multiple blindly.

### Buying Too Short A Term For A Long Obligation

A term that expires before the obligation ends leaves the family uncovered when coverage is most needed and most expensive to renew. Match the term to the dependency horizon.

### Trusting Illustrated Returns As Guaranteed

Non-guaranteed cash value projections can fall short. The agent should compare guaranteed and illustrated values and stress-test the policy at lower returns.

### Justifying Permanent Insurance As Forced Savings

The forced-savings argument ignores that the same discipline can be applied to cheaper automated investing. If forced savings is the only benefit, the cost is hard to justify.

### Ignoring Insurability Risk By Delaying

Delaying purchase to save premium can result in uninsurability or much higher cost if health changes. The option value of locking in coverage while healthy is real.

### Letting Beneficiary Designations Go Stale

Life events change the intended beneficiary. An outdated designation can pay an ex-spouse or skip children. The agent should ensure designations are reviewed regularly and coordinated with the estate plan.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The death benefit amount was quantified from income replacement, debts, education, and final expenses before selecting a product type.
- [ ] The policy duration is matched to the financial obligation or dependency horizon.
- [ ] Buy-term-and-invest-the-difference was compared transparently against permanent insurance on cost per unit of death benefit, including the behavioral caveat.
- [ ] Any recommendation of permanent insurance names a specific lifelong justification rather than defaulting to it.
- [ ] Guaranteed values were examined alongside illustrated values, and non-guaranteed assumptions were treated as uncertain.
- [ ] Insurability, health underwriting, and the option value of locking in coverage were considered.
- [ ] Policy ownership, beneficiary designations, and estate liquidity effects were coordinated with the estate plan.
- [ ] The recommendation reflects the household's actual dependents and debts rather than a blanket rule that everyone needs life insurance.
- [ ] The output includes a disclaimer that it is not personalized advice and that policy illustrations and suitability should be reviewed independently.
- [ ] No claim is made that cash-value life insurance is a superior investment without comparing net cost and returns to alternatives.
