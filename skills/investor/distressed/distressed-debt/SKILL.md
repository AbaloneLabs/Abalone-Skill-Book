# Distressed Debt Investing

Distressed debt investing targets the debt of companies in or near default, purchased at deep discounts to par, with returns driven by recovery rather than coupon. The thesis is that distressed debt offers asymmetric returns when the investor can estimate recovery with more accuracy than the market, buying claims at prices below their likely recovery value. The judgment problem is that distressed debt is complex, illiquid, and uncertain, with recovery depending on seniority, collateral, the bankruptcy process, and negotiation among creditors. Investors who buy distressed debt based on yield or cheapness, without rigorously analyzing the capital structure, collateral, and recovery scenarios, routinely overpay for claims that recover far less than expected, or find their priority undermined by structural and legal complexities. The discipline is to analyze the position in the capital structure, estimate recovery across scenarios, and demand a margin of safety between price and expected recovery.

This skill is for analyzing distressed debt opportunities with rigor about seniority, collateral, and recovery.

## Core Rules

### Anchor On Recovery, Not Yield

In distressed debt, the coupon is often unpaid or irrelevant. Returns come from the difference between the purchase price and the eventual recovery. Recovery analysis is the core.

Focus on:

- the purchase price as a percentage of par (e.g., 40 cents on the dollar);
- the expected recovery as a percentage of par, based on the capital structure and asset value;
- the return as the difference between purchase price and recovery, annualized over the expected timeline;
- the probability-weighted recovery across scenarios (reorganization, liquidation, exchange);
- the downside if recovery is lower than expected.

A bond purchased at 40 with expected recovery of 70 offers a strong return if the thesis holds, but if recovery comes in at 20, the loss is severe. Recovery, not yield, drives returns.

### Analyze Seniority And Position In The Capital Structure

Recovery depends heavily on the seniority of the claim and its position in the priority waterfall. Seniority is the primary determinant of recovery.

Map the structure:

- first-lien secured debt, with the strongest collateral position and highest recovery;
- second-lien and unsecured secured debt, with weaker collateral and lower recovery;
- senior unsecured notes, with no specific collateral but priority over subordinated claims;
- subordinated debt, with the lowest priority among creditors and minimal recovery in severe distress;
- the amount of debt senior to the investor's position, which determines how much value must be distributed before the investor receives anything.

The farther down the capital structure, the lower the expected recovery and the higher the uncertainty. Seniority analysis is non-negotiable in distressed debt.

### Assess Collateral Quality And Coverage

For secured debt, the quality and value of the collateral determine recovery. Collateral analysis is essential for secured claims.

Evaluate:

- the nature of the collateral (real estate, equipment, inventory, receivables, intellectual property) and its realizable value in distress;
- the loan-to-value ratio and the coverage of the collateral over the claim;
- the priority of liens and any intercreditor issues that affect the investor's claim on the collateral;
- the liquidity and marketability of the collateral, since specialized assets fetch far less in fire sales;
- the potential for collateral value to deteriorate during the bankruptcy process.

Collateral that looks adequate on a balance sheet may realize far less in a distressed sale. Conservative, hair-cut collateral valuation is essential.

### Model Recovery Across Multiple Scenarios

Recovery in distressed debt is uncertain and path-dependent. A single point estimate is misleading; scenario analysis is required.

Build scenarios:

- a base case reorganization with estimated enterprise value distributed through the priority waterfall;
- a liquidation scenario, where assets are sold piecemeal, typically at lower values;
- an exchange or consensual restructuring scenario, where creditors agree to a haircut outside of court;
- a downside scenario where enterprise value is lower than expected or the process is unfavorable;
- probability-weight the scenarios and compare the expected recovery to the purchase price.

The margin of safety is the difference between the purchase price and the probability-weighted expected recovery. Demand a meaningful margin to absorb estimation error and adverse outcomes.

### Evaluate Workout, Exchange, And Litigation Paths

Distressed debt outcomes are not limited to formal bankruptcy. The investor must consider the range of resolution paths.

Consider:

- consensual exchanges or tender offers, where creditors accept a haircut to avoid bankruptcy;
- pre-packaged or pre-negotiated bankruptcies, where the plan is agreed before filing;
- litigation, including challenges to valuation, priority, intercreditor disputes, and fraudulent conveyance claims;
- the role of activist distressed investors who may agitate for better recoveries;
- the potential for a sale of the company or its assets as a resolution.

Each path has different timelines, costs, and recovery implications. The investor must understand which path is likely and how it affects the specific claim.

### Account For Illiquidity, Time, And Complexity Costs

Distressed debt is illiquid, slow, and complex. These characteristics impose costs that must be priced into the return.

Assess:

- the bid-ask spreads and the difficulty of exiting positions before resolution;
- the expected timeline to recovery, which can be years, and the annualized return implication;
- the legal, analytical, and monitoring costs of following a complex bankruptcy;
- the capital tie-up and the opportunity cost relative to other investments;
- the need for specialized expertise or advisors to analyze and monitor distressed positions.

Distressed debt is suitable for investors with the expertise, capital, and patience to handle illiquidity and complexity. For most investors, the costs and risks exceed the benefit.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On Yield Instead Of Recovery

In distressed debt, the coupon is often unpaid. Returns come from recovery, and recovery analysis is the core.

### Ignoring Seniority And Capital Structure Position

Recovery depends on priority. Buying junior claims when seniors are impaired leads to minimal or zero recovery.

### Overestimating Collateral Value

Collateral realizes far less in distress than on the balance sheet. Conservative, hair-cut valuation is essential.

### Using A Single Recovery Estimate

Recovery is uncertain. Scenario analysis with probability weighting is required, with a margin of safety.

### Neglecting Timeline And Illiquidity Costs

Distressed debt is slow and illiquid. The annualized return must account for time and the cost of capital tie-up.

### Underestimating Legal Complexity

Intercreditor disputes, lien priority, fraudulent conveyance, and jurisdictional issues create risks. Legal expertise is required.

### Confusing Distressed Debt With Equity-Like Upside

Distressed debt has capped upside (recovery at par or the reorganization value) but real downside. It is not an equity substitute.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Returns are anchored on recovery (purchase price versus expected recovery), not yield or coupon, and the analysis is on a probability-weighted, annualized basis.
- [ ] The seniority and position in the capital structure are mapped, with the amount of debt senior to the investor's claim quantified.
- [ ] Collateral quality, coverage, lien priority, and realizable value in distress are assessed with conservative haircuts.
- [ ] Recovery is modeled across multiple scenarios (reorganization, liquidation, exchange, downside), probability-weighted, with a margin of safety demanded.
- [ ] Workout, exchange, litigation, and sale paths are considered for their effect on the specific claim's recovery.
- [ ] Illiquidity, timeline, complexity, and monitoring costs are factored into the return and suitability assessment.
- [ ] The capped-upside, real-downside nature of distressed debt is understood, and it is not treated as an equity substitute.
- [ ] The guidance flags that distressed debt carries high risk of loss, that recoveries are uncertain and often lower than expected, that the process is slow and complex, that specialized expertise is required, and that professional legal and financial advice is strongly recommended for distressed debt investing.
