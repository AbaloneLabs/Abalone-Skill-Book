# Due Diligence On Managers And Funds

A fund investment has two risks: the strategy risk (will the investments perform) and the operational risk (will the firm lose or misappropriate the money regardless of strategy). The judgment problem is that investors spend almost all their effort on the first and almost none on the second, when operational failure — fraud, misvaluation, custody breach, side-pocket abuse, related-party self-dealing — destroys capital just as completely as a bad trade, and is far more predictable if checked. Agents and investors accept impressive track records at face value, never verifying whether the numbers correspond to real assets held by an independent custodian and audited by a real firm. The skill is treating operational due diligence as equal in weight to strategy analysis, because a sound strategy inside a fraudulent or negligent operation still loses everything.

This skill is for conducting due diligence on a manager or fund before committing capital. It is not legal advice; structures, terms, and regulatory protections vary by jurisdiction, and independent professional verification is essential.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Track Record Against Independent Evidence

A track record presented in marketing materials is a claim, not evidence. Many fraudulent and failed funds showed apparently excellent records that did not survive verification.

Verify independently:

- whether the track record is for the actual fund, a predecessor vehicle, the manager personally at a prior firm, or a hypothetical back-test (each is a weaker signal);
- whether returns are audited and reconcilable to custodian and administrator records;
- whether the record reflects the full set of funds the manager ran, including failed or wound-down vehicles that may be omitted;
- whether performance fees, fee waivers, and subscription lines flatter the reported net return.

A track record that cannot be tied to audited financials and independent custodian statements is a claim, not evidence. Treat survivorship and selection in any composite skeptically.

### Confirm Independent Custody, Audit, And Administration

These three controls are the backbone of operational integrity. Their absence or related-party status is the leading indicator of operational fraud.

Confirm:

- assets are held by an independent qualified custodian, not the manager or an affiliate, with statements sent directly to investors;
- financial statements are audited annually by a reputable, independent, recognized accounting firm;
- NAV is calculated by an independent fund administrator, not the manager's own team;
- the manager cannot move investor assets without independent checks.

Where any of these is internal, related-party, performed by an unknown firm, or absent, the risk that reported assets and returns are fictitious or misvalued rises to an unacceptable level. The Madoff failure is the canonical case: no independent custodian, no reputable auditor.

### Scrutinize The Fee Waterfall And Incentive Alignment

Fee structure predicts behavior. A poorly aligned waterfall rewards the manager for outcomes that do not benefit the investor and can persist even in loss years.

Examine:

- management fees, including whether they are charged on committed or invested capital and during the lockup;
- the carried interest or performance fee, the hurdle (hard or soft, with or without a high-water mark), and catch-up provisions;
- whether the manager's own capital is meaningfully co-invested so they share downside, not just upside;
- transaction, monitoring, breaching, and other fees loaded onto the fund or portfolio companies;
- the net effect across realistic return scenarios, including poor ones.

Compute what the manager earns in a flat or down year. If the manager does well when the investor does not, the alignment is broken.

### Examine Side Pockets, Gates, And Redemption Terms

The terms governing liquidity reveal where the manager can trap capital. These terms are often buried and are decisive in a stress event.

Review:

- side pockets that segregate illiquid or hard-to-value assets, which can lock a portion of capital indefinitely and at the manager's discretion;
- gates and suspension rights that can halt or limit redemptions, often precisely when investors most want out;
- lockup periods and the notice period for redemption;
- the manager's history of invoking gates or side pockets during prior stress periods.

A fund that has never gated in calm markets may still gate in a crisis. The terms, not the recent behavior, define the risk.

### Evaluate Operational Infrastructure And Key Person Risk

Beyond the controls, the firm's infrastructure determines whether it can operate reliably and survive the loss of key people.

Assess:

- the depth and experience of the back office, compliance, and risk functions, not just the portfolio team;
- business continuity, disaster recovery, and cyber controls;
- key person risk — what happens to the strategy and the firm if the founder or lead PM leaves, becomes incapacitated, or departs;
- the firm's regulatory history, examinations, and any prior operational incidents.

A brilliant strategist with no operational depth is a single point of failure. Infrastructure and succession matter as much as the edge.

### Investigate Related-Party Transactions And Conflicts

Conflicts are where the manager's interest diverges from the investor's. They are routine in the industry and must be identified, not assumed away.

Look for:

- the manager allocating favorable deals to favored funds or themselves (cherry-picking);
- transactions with affiliated brokers, valuation firms, or service providers;
- the manager buying from or selling to the fund entities they control;
- cross-fund allocations where one fund's loss is another's gain;
- personal trading by the manager in securities the fund trades.

Related-party transactions are not always harmful, but they must be disclosed, policed, and limited. Undisclosed or unmanaged conflicts are a warning.

### Require Transparency And Test Responsiveness

A manager's behavior under diligence predicts their behavior as an investor. Evasion, delayed answers, and resistance to standard questions are operational red flags independent of the strategy.

Expect:

- timely, complete responses to standard due diligence questions;
- willingness to provide audited financials, the offering documents, and access to service providers;
- clear, non-evasive answers about valuation, conflicts, and liquidity terms;
- consistency between marketing materials and the offering documents.

A manager who resists standard diligence before receiving capital will be harder to deal with after they hold it.

## Common Traps

### Accepting The Track Record At Face Value

Marketing track records often omit failed funds, mix personal and fund performance, or rely on back-tests. Verify against audited financials and custodian records.

### Skipping Independent Custody And Audit

Internal custody, related-party auditors, or unknown audit firms are the leading operational fraud indicators. Independent controls are essential.

### Ignoring The Fee Waterfall In Bad Scenarios

Fees that reward the manager in flat or down years, or carry without a high-water mark, break alignment. Model the poor scenarios, not just the good ones.

### Overlooking Side Pockets And Gates

Liquidity terms define what the manager can trap in a crisis. Recent calm-market behavior is not the test.

### Concentrating On Strategy, Ignoring Operations

A sound strategy inside a fraudulent or negligent operation still loses everything. Operational due diligence is equal in weight.

### Trusting Related-Party Arrangements Because They Are Disclosed

Disclosure does not neutralize a conflict. Disclosed related-party transactions still shape behavior and must be limited and policed.

### Mistaking Responsiveness In Marketing For Operational Quality

Pre-investment responsiveness is a minimum. Evasion during diligence predicts worse behavior afterward.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The track record was verified against audited financials and custodian/administrator records, with predecessor, personal, and back-tested components distinguished and survivorship considered.
- [ ] Independent qualified custody, reputable independent audit, and independent fund administration were confirmed, with direct custodial statements to investors.
- [ ] The fee waterfall was modeled across realistic scenarios including flat and down years, with management fees on committed capital, carry, hurdle, high-water mark, and other fees quantified.
- [ ] Side pockets, gates, suspension rights, lockups, and redemption notice periods were reviewed, and the manager's history of invoking them in stress was checked.
- [ ] Operational infrastructure, back-office depth, compliance, cyber and continuity controls, and key person and succession risk were assessed.
- [ ] Related-party transactions and conflicts (cherry-picking, affiliated service providers, cross-fund allocation, personal trading) were identified and evaluated for disclosure and policing.
- [ ] The manager's responsiveness, transparency, and consistency between marketing and offering documents were tested during diligence.
- [ ] The conclusion flags that due diligence reduces but does not eliminate operational risk, that structures and protections vary by jurisdiction, that past performance does not guarantee future results, that this is not legal advice, and that independent professional verification is essential.
