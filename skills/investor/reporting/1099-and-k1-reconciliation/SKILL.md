# 1099 And K-1 Reconciliation

The tax form a broker sends is a starting draft, not a final answer. The judgment problem is that investors treat the 1099 as authoritative and transcribe it directly onto a return, when broker-reported basis is frequently incomplete, wash-sale adjustments differ across brokers, corporate-action basis shifts are missing, and partnership K-1s arrive months later with their own basis adjustments that supersede the broker's numbers. The result is overpaid tax from overstated gains, underpaid tax and penalties from missed adjustments, or the chaos of an amended return when a corrected 1099 or a late K-1 arrives after filing. The skill is reconciling the broker's draft to the investor's own records and to the partnership's numbers before anything is filed.

This skill is for reconciling investment tax reporting. It is not tax advice; reporting rules, forms, and deadlines vary by jurisdiction and change over time, and a qualified tax professional should be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Treat The 1099 As A Draft Pending Reconciliation

A broker 1099 reports what the broker's systems recorded, which is often incomplete or wrong relative to the investor's actual tax position. Do not file from the 1099 without reconciling it to your own lot-level records.

Common 1099 inaccuracies:

- basis reported as zero or "noncovered" for older or transferred lots the broker did not track;
- wash-sale adjustments computed only within that broker's accounts, missing wash sales across brokers or account types;
- corporate-action basis adjustments missing or applied to the wrong lots;
- proceeds or basis split incorrectly across multiple lots on a single sale;
- classification errors between short-term and long-term, or between covered and noncovered.

Reconcile every sale: proceeds, basis, holding period, gain character, and wash-sale flags, line by line, against your own records.

### Reconcile Proceeds And Basis To The Lot Level

Aggregate totals hide errors. Reconcile at the lot level so each sale's proceeds and basis can be traced to a specific acquisition.

Steps:

- match each reported sale to the specific tax lot sold, using lot identification rather than average cost where available;
- confirm the acquisition date and price for each lot, including reinvested dividends that created small lots;
- verify the holding period that determines short-term versus long-term character, paying attention to lots near the one-year boundary;
- confirm the gain or loss character and amount per lot before rolling up to totals.

Lot-level reconciliation catches the errors that aggregate totals disguise and is essential where specific identification was elected.

### Handle Wash Sales Across All Accounts And Substantially Identical Securities

Wash-sale rules disallow a loss when a substantially identical security is repurchased within the prohibited window, and the rule applies across all of the investor's accounts — including across brokers, between taxable and retirement accounts in many jurisdictions, and through automatic dividend reinvestment.

Reconcile wash sales by:

- identifying repurchases of substantially identical securities within the window around each loss sale, across all accounts you control;
- computing the disallowed loss and the basis adjustment to the replacement shares;
- recognizing that a wash sale into certain retirement accounts can make the loss permanently disallowed with no basis recovery;
- reconciling the broker's reported wash-sale adjustments against your cross-account analysis, since the broker only sees its own accounts.

The broker's wash-sale reporting is incomplete by construction because it cannot see your other accounts. Cross-account reconciliation is the investor's job.

### Apply Corporate Action Basis Adjustments Explicitly

Splits, reverse splits, mergers, spinoffs, and reorganizations change share counts and per-share basis, and these adjustments are frequently missing or misapplied on the 1099.

For each corporate action during the year:

- obtain the issuer's basis allocation guidance (often a Form 8937 or equivalent) and apply it to the affected lots;
- adjust share counts and per-share basis so the total basis is preserved but correctly distributed;
- confirm the broker's 1099 reflects the adjustment; if not, correct it on the return with appropriate explanation;
- carry the adjusted basis forward so future sales are computed correctly.

Ignoring corporate actions breaks the basis chain and produces wrong gains on every future sale of the affected position.

### Integrate K-1 Basis Adjustments That Supersede Broker Basis

Partnership, S-corporation, and trust interests are reported on K-1s, and their basis rules differ from securities basis. A K-1 often adjusts the investor's outside basis in ways the broker never records, and the K-1 typically arrives months after the broker 1099.

Reconcile K-1 impacts:

- track outside basis separately from broker-reported basis, adjusting for income, losses, distributions, and liabilities passed through on the K-1;
- recognize that selling a partnership interest is reported based on tax basis, which may differ greatly from the broker's cost;
- wait for the final K-1 before finalizing the return where the partnership interest was sold, since late-arriving K-1s change the numbers;
- account for the different holding-period rules that may apply to partnership interests.

Filing from the broker's basis for a partnership interest, then receiving a K-1 that changes the basis, is a common trigger for amended returns.

### Plan For Corrected 1099s And Late K-1s

Brokers issue corrected 1099s well after the original, sometimes months later, as errors are found or issuers finalize corporate-action guidance. K-1s from partnerships and trusts routinely arrive after the normal filing deadline.

Manage timing by:

- filing on extension where corrected forms or K-1s are expected, rather than filing early and amending;
- tracking which positions are subject to late K-1s and proactively requesting estimated figures;
- maintaining a process to receive and act on corrected 1099s, since they can change gain, loss, or character;
- documenting the reconciliation so an amended return, if needed, is straightforward.

Filing early to "get it done" and then amending twice is more costly and error-prone than waiting for complete information.

### Document Every Adjustment For Audit Defense

Every difference between the 1099 and the filed return should be documented with its source, so an examination or notice can be answered without reconstruction.

Keep:

- the reconciliation worksheet showing reported versus adjusted figures;
- the lot-level basis records and corporate-action guidance supporting each adjustment;
- the wash-sale cross-account analysis;
- the K-1s and outside-basis computation for partnership interests;
- the corrected 1099s and the reasoning for any amended return.

A documented adjustment is defensible; an undocumented one is a liability.

## Common Traps

### Transcribing The 1099 Directly Onto The Return

Broker basis is often incomplete or wrong. Filing without reconciliation overpays tax on overstated gains or underpays and invites penalties on missed adjustments.

### Trusting The Broker's Wash-Sale Reporting

The broker only sees its own accounts. Cross-account and cross-broker wash sales, and wash sales into retirement accounts, are the investor's responsibility.

### Missing Corporate Action Basis Adjustments

Splits and spinoffs change basis. Without the adjustment, every future sale of the position is miscomputed.

### Filing From Broker Basis For A Partnership Interest

K-1 outside basis differs from broker cost. Filing early and ignoring the K-1 triggers amended returns and potential errors.

### Filing Early And Then Amending For Corrected Forms

Corrected 1099s and late K-1s are predictable. Filing on extension is usually cheaper and cleaner than amending.

### Using Average Cost When Specific Identification Was Intended

Average cost is often a default that forfeits lot-level control and can produce different gain character than intended.

### Forgetting State Differences

Federal treatment may differ from state treatment of basis, gains, and wash sales. Reconciliation should consider the relevant jurisdictions.

- [ ] Proceeds, basis, holding period, and gain character were reconciled at the lot level, not only in aggregate.
- [ ] Wash sales were analyzed across all accounts, brokers, account types, and substantially identical securities, not only within the reporting broker.
- [ ] Corporate-action basis adjustments were applied using issuer guidance (such as Form 8937) and reconciled to the 1099.
