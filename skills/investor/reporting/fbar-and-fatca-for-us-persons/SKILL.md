# FBAR And FATCA For US Persons

For US persons, foreign financial accounts carry reporting obligations that are easy to miss and catastrophic to violate. The judgment problem is that these rules are not about tax owed — they are about disclosure — and the penalties for failing to disclose can dwarf any tax consequence, including civil and criminal exposure even when no tax was evaded. Agents and investors routinely assume that "I already pay tax on the foreign income" satisfies the obligation, when the FBAR and FATCA regimes are separate, threshold-based, and unforgiving. A single overlooked foreign account, an inherited account abroad, or a signature authority over a foreign entity can trigger reporting that, if missed, produces penalties orders of magnitude larger than the account itself.

This skill is for recognizing when US foreign-account reporting applies and understanding its severity. It is not legal or tax advice; the rules are complex, fact-specific, jurisdiction-dependent, and change frequently. A qualified professional must be consulted for any actual cross-border situation.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Reporting Is Separate From Taxation

The central misconception is that paying tax on foreign income discharges all obligations. It does not. FBAR and FATCA are disclosure regimes that exist independently of whether any tax is due.

Understand the separation:

- income tax on foreign earnings is one obligation;
- FBAR (a Treasury/FinCEN bank-secrecy reporting form) is a separate disclosure of foreign financial accounts;
- FATCA (an IRS reporting regime) is yet another disclosure with its own thresholds and forms;
- foreign trust and foreign corporation reporting (PFIC rules, Form 5471, Form 3520) add further layers.

Satisfying one does not satisfy the others. Each must be evaluated on its own thresholds and rules.

### Know The FBAR Threshold And What Counts As An Account

FBAR generally applies when the aggregate value of foreign financial accounts exceeds a defined threshold at any point during the year. The "at any point" rule is a common trap: a brief peak above the threshold triggers reporting for the year.

What often counts as a reportable foreign financial account:

- foreign bank and brokerage accounts;
- foreign mutual funds and many foreign pooled investment vehicles;
- certain foreign insurance products with cash value;
- foreign retirement and pension accounts;
- accounts over which the person has signature authority, even if they do not own the funds.

Aggregate all such accounts and apply the threshold to the peak combined value, not to each account separately or to a year-end average.

### Know The FATCA Threshold And How It Differs From FBAR

FATCA reporting (typically Form 8938) applies to specified foreign financial assets above thresholds that vary by residency, filing status, and whether the assets are held directly or through an entity. The thresholds differ from FBAR thresholds and are based on year-end value and peak value during the year.

Key distinctions:

- FATCA thresholds are generally higher than FBAR and depend on the taxpayer's situation;
- FATCA captures a broader category of "specified foreign financial assets," including some interests not captured by FBAR;
- an asset can be reportable on FBAR, on FATCA, on both, or on neither — the regimes overlap imperfectly.

Do not assume that filing one form covers the other. Evaluate each regime independently.

### Identify The People And Situations Most At Risk

The investors most often caught by surprise are not sophisticated offshore structurers but ordinary people with cross-border lives.

High-risk situations:

- immigrants who retained home-country bank, pension, or brokerage accounts;
- US persons who inherited foreign accounts or foreign trusts;
- expatriates and dual residents with accounts in their country of residence;
- signatories on foreign entity accounts (business, family, charity) who do not own the funds;
- holders of foreign mutual funds or insurance products unaware they are reportable;
- US persons whose foreign accounts were disclosed automatically to the IRS under FATCA by foreign institutions, creating a mismatch if the person did not also self-report.

A foreign institution reporting an account to the IRS under FATCA, while the US person fails to self-report, is a high-visibility mismatch that frequently triggers examination.

### Understand The Penalty Asymmetry And Willfulness Distinction

The defining feature of these regimes is that penalties are severe and asymmetric, and they turn sharply on whether the failure is deemed willful or non-willful.

Appreciate the stakes:

- non-willful penalties can still be substantial per year and can multiply across accounts and years;
- willful violations carry far larger penalties, and can reach a large fraction of the account balance;
- criminal exposure exists in willful cases;
- the distinction between willful and non-willful is fact-specific and contested, and reckless disregard can be treated as willful.

Because the downside is so large relative to the modest effort of reporting, the rational posture is to over-report rather than under-report when in doubt, and to seek professional help immediately if a prior failure is discovered.

### Address Foreign Mutual Funds And PFICs Specially

Foreign mutual funds and similar pooled vehicles are usually classified as Passive Foreign Investment Companies (PFICs) for US persons, triggering a punitive tax and reporting regime distinct from ordinary investments.

Recognize:

- PFIC taxation can be far harsher than equivalent US-domiciled funds, with interest charges on deferral;
- separate PFIC reporting forms are required annually and on disposition;
- many investors hold PFICs unknowingly through foreign brokerage accounts;
- the cost of professional help to compute PFIC inclusion is often high, adding to the burden.

A foreign-domiciled index fund held by a US person can be a costly trap compared to an economically equivalent US-domiciled fund. The reporting and tax treatment, not the underlying exposure, drives the harm.

### Handle Foreign Trusts, Gifts, And Inheritances With Heightened Care

Foreign trusts and large foreign gifts carry their own reporting and tax regimes that interact with FBAR and FATCA. Inheritances of foreign accounts, signature authority over a foreign family entity, and distributions from foreign trusts all have specific forms and deadlines. These are among the most penalty-exposed areas and almost always require professional handling.

### Use Voluntary Disclosure For Past Failures, Not Silence

If a past failure is discovered, the worst response is to continue ignoring it. The regimes generally offer streamlined filing, voluntary disclosure, or delinquent submission pathways that can significantly reduce penalties for non-willful cases. The availability and terms change over time and depend on the facts, so prompt professional advice is essential. Silence is the highest-risk strategy.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Tax Paid Means Reporting Done

Income tax, FBAR, FATCA, and PFIC reporting are separate. Satisfying one does not discharge the others.

### Missing The "At Any Point" Aggregation Rule

FBAR turns on the peak aggregate value during the year, not year-end or per-account values. A brief peak triggers reporting.

### Overlooking Signature Authority

Accounts a person controls but does not own can still be reportable. Ownership is not the only trigger.

### Confusing FBAR And FATCA Thresholds

The two regimes have different thresholds, scopes, and forms. Filing one does not cover the other.

### Ignoring Foreign Mutual Funds As PFICs

Foreign pooled vehicles usually trigger the punitive PFIC regime, often unknowingly. Economic equivalence to a US fund does not mean tax equivalence.

### Dismissing An Inherited Or Family Account

Foreign inheritances, gifts, and family-entity signature authority carry their own reporting and penalty exposure.

### Staying Silent After Discovering A Past Failure

Continued non-disclosure is the highest-risk response. Voluntary disclosure pathways generally exist and should be explored promptly with professional help.
