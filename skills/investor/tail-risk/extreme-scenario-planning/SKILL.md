# Extreme Scenario Planning

Extreme scenario planning goes beyond historical stress testing to imagine systemic disruptions: liquidity crises where markets freeze, correlation breakdowns where diversification fails, credit seizures where funding stops, bank runs where institutions fail, sovereign defaults, and policy shocks where the rules change overnight. The judgment problem is that these scenarios are low-probability but high-consequence, they interact and cascade in unpredictable ways, and most portfolios and plans are built for normal functioning markets that may not exist during the event. Agents often plan for isolated market declines and miss the systemic, institutional, and operational dimensions that define real crises.

This skill is for planning portfolio and investor responses to severe systemic disruption.

## Core Rules

### Enumerate Systemic, Not Just Market, Scenarios

Market declines are only one class of extreme event. Systemic scenarios involve the plumbing of the financial system itself.

Plan across categories:

- liquidity crises where bids disappear and markets freeze;
- correlation breakdowns where diversifiers fall together;
- credit seizures where funding markets stop and spreads blow out;
- bank and broker failures where custodians or counterparties collapse;
- sovereign defaults or currency crises where the monetary regime shifts;
- policy shocks where capital controls, freezes, or rule changes occur overnight;
- operational crises where platforms, clearing, or settlement fail.

Each category threatens a different part of the portfolio and requires a different response. Enumerate them explicitly rather than assuming "a market crash" covers everything.

### Plan For Cascading And Simultaneous Failure

Real crises cascade. A market decline triggers margin calls, which trigger forced selling, which triggers liquidity withdrawal, which triggers counterparty failure, which triggers a credit seizure. Diversifiers fail together because the same funding stress hits everyone.

Model:

- second- and third-order effects, not just the first shock;
- simultaneous failure of multiple mechanisms (market + funding + counterparty);
- feedback loops where selling begets illiquidity begets more selling;
- the breakdown of relationships (correlation, funding, custody) assumed stable in calm.

A plan that handles each scenario in isolation will fail when they cascade. Stress the combinations and the feedback.

### Prioritize Survival And Obligation-Meeting

In an extreme scenario, the first objective is survival: meeting obligations, avoiding forced liquidation, and retaining the capacity to act. Return is irrelevant if the portfolio is ruined.

Ensure:

- enough cash and near-cash to meet all obligations through a prolonged freeze;
- no dependence on frozen funding, margin, or redemption-sensitive structures;
- no leverage that forces selling into a bidless market;
- access to liquidity that does not require functioning markets.

The investor who can meet obligations through a liquidity freeze survives to the recovery. The investor who must sell into the freeze is ruined at the bottom.

### Plan For Institutional And Custodial Failure

Extreme scenarios include the failure of the institutions holding the assets. A sound portfolio in a failed custodian is inaccessible.

Diversify and verify:

- custodial diversification across institutions and, if warranted, jurisdictions;
- counterparty limits and collateral protection for derivatives and lending;
- the safety and segregation of cash (not swept into bank general assets);
- the resolution and continuity of access if a primary platform fails;
- documentation and records so assets can be identified and claimed.

Operational resilience is as important as market resilience. An institutional failure can lock up a perfectly hedged portfolio.

### Plan For Policy And Regulatory Shocks

Governments change the rules in crises. Capital controls, redemption gates, transaction taxes, position limits, and asset freezes have all occurred.

Consider:

- jurisdictional diversification to reduce single-government risk;
- the history of policy interventions in past crises and their likelihood;
- the effect of capital controls or gates on liquidity and exit;
- the legal robustness of structures (trusts, entities, account types) to rule changes;
- the political risk to specific asset classes or sectors.

A portfolio that is sound under existing rules can be impaired by a rule change. Policy risk is real and should be considered for concentrated or jurisdiction-specific exposures.

### Build Contingency Playbooks, Not Just Analysis

Analysis that does not produce action plans is wasted. Each scenario should have a playbook.

For each material scenario, define:

- the early warning indicators to watch;
- the immediate protective actions to take;
- the obligations to meet and the liquidity source for each;
- the opportunities to exploit (dry powder deployment, rebalancing);
- the decision-makers and authorities if the primary investor is incapacitated.

A playbook converts panic into execution. The investor with a plan acts; the investor without one freezes or capitulates.

### Preserve Optionality And Agency

In extreme scenarios, the ability to act when others cannot is a major source of value. Preserve the resources and flexibility to act.

Maintain:

- dry powder (cash, unused credit lines) to deploy at dislocated prices;
- the psychological and financial capacity to hold through panic;
- the knowledge, access, and relationships to execute when markets are disrupted;
- flexibility in spending, contributions, and allocation to adapt.

The investor who survives a systemic crisis with liquidity and agency can buy at the bottom. The investor who is leveraged, locked up, or frozen is a forced seller or a passive victim.

### Reconcile Preparation With Cost And Probability

Extreme scenario preparation costs money and complexity. Not every scenario warrants equal preparation.

Prioritize by:

- the severity of the consequence (ruin versus discomfort);
- the plausibility given current conditions;
- the cost and complexity of the protection;
- the investor's specific exposures and vulnerabilities.

Focus preparation on scenarios that could cause ruin and that the investor's structure is actually exposed to. Do not over-engineer for implausible events, but never leave ruin paths open.

## Common Traps

### Planning Only For Market Declines

Market crashes are one scenario. Liquidity freezes, institutional failures, and policy shocks are different and often more dangerous.

### Isolated Rather Than Cascading Scenarios

Real crises cascade and combine. Plans that handle each shock in isolation fail when they interact.

### Assuming Liquidity Will Be Available

Liquidity vanishes in crises. Portfolios that depend on selling into functioning markets fail when markets freeze.

### Ignoring Institutional And Custodial Risk

A sound portfolio in a failed custodian or counterparty is inaccessible. Operational concentration is a hidden ruin path.

### Assuming The Rules Stay Fixed

Governments change rules in crises. Capital controls, gates, and freezes have happened and can happen again.

### Analysis Without Playbooks

Identifying scenarios without action plans leaves the investor to improvise under panic, which typically fails.

### Over-Leverage And Forced Selling

Leverage converts a survivable crisis into ruin via margin calls and forced liquidation. It is the most common ruin path in systemic events.

### Dismissing Scenarios As Implausible

Low-probability events that cause ruin must be planned for regardless of probability. The cost of ruin is infinite; the math of expected value breaks down.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The plan enumerates systemic scenarios (liquidity, correlation, credit, institutional, sovereign, policy, operational), not only market declines.
- [ ] Cascading and simultaneous failure, with feedback loops and relationship breakdowns, is modeled.
- [ ] Survival and obligation-meeting are prioritized, with cash and near-cash to endure a prolonged freeze and no forced-selling paths.
- [ ] Institutional and custodial risk is addressed via diversification, segregation, counterparty limits, and continuity planning.
- [ ] Policy and regulatory shocks (capital controls, gates, freezes) are considered, with jurisdictional diversification where warranted.
- [ ] Each material scenario has a contingency playbook with indicators, actions, liquidity sources, opportunities, and decision authorities.
- [ ] Optionality and agency (dry powder, capacity to hold and act) are preserved.
- [ ] Preparation is prioritized by severity, plausibility, cost, and the investor's specific exposures, focusing on ruin paths.
- [ ] The recommendation flags that systemic scenarios can exceed any preparation, that cascades are unpredictable, that no plan guarantees survival, and that professional legal, operational, and financial advice may be warranted for resilience planning.
