# Strategic Asset Allocation

Strategic asset allocation (SAA) is the long-term policy mix of asset classes that is meant to drive most of a portfolio's risk and return. It is not a market-timing tool and it is not a security-selection tool. The most common and most expensive mistake is to treat the policy mix as a one-time arithmetic exercise, set it once, and then let it drift or change it under stress. A sound SAA is a living decision that encodes the investor's goals, time horizon, risk capacity, and beliefs about asset-class behavior over full cycles.

Agents tend to underweight three things here. First, the policy mix must be survivable: an allocation the investor abandons in a drawdown is worse than a less ambitious allocation they hold. Second, the asset classes chosen must be defined carefully, because two funds with the same label can carry very different risk. Third, SAA is forward-looking, so historical return assumptions must be stress-tested against regimes that differ from the sample period.

This skill is for the judgment before the weights are set, not for executing the trades.

## Core Rules

### Anchor The Mix To Goals, Not To A Return Target

A target return is an output of constraints, not an input. Starting from "I need 8% so I will hold 90% equities" inverts the logic and often produces an allocation the investor cannot hold.

Gather first:

- goals and the dollar amounts behind them (retirement income, education, legacy, purchase);
- time horizon for each goal, and whether it is fixed or flexible;
- required real return after inflation, taxes, and fees;
- maximum drawdown and maximum underwater period the investor can actually tolerate;
- liquidity needs and reserves;
- human capital and income stability;
- existing concentrated exposures (employer stock, business, real estate).

A strong process sets the equity share from risk capacity and risk tolerance, then checks whether the implied return plausibly meets the goals. A weak process picks the equity share to hit a return number and ignores whether the investor can survive the path.

### Choose Asset Classes By Risk Role, Not By Label

Each sleeve should have a defined job: growth, stabilization, real-asset protection, or diversification. Two funds with the same label can do different jobs.

For every sleeve, define:

- the asset class and its core risk premium (equity, term, credit, liquidity, inflation);
- expected long-term real return and the uncertainty around it;
- expected volatility and drawdown profile;
- correlation to the other sleeves, including in stressed regimes;
- liquidity, cost, and tax efficiency;
- how it is measured and benchmarked.

A common strong choice is to separate global equities, high-quality government bonds, credit, real assets, and cash, each with a clear role. A common weak choice is to list many "diversifying" funds that are all equity beta in disguise.

### Make Return Assumptions Regime-Aware

Long-term expected returns are uncertain and regime-dependent. Using a single trailing average as the forward assumption is a frequent source of error.

Consider:

- current valuations (earnings yield, yield to maturity, spreads) as a starting point for forward returns;
- inflation and rate regime versus the historical sample;
- demographic, productivity, and globalization trends;
- whether credit or liquidity premia are currently compressed or wide;
- the cost of taxes, fees, and cash drag.

Express return assumptions as ranges or scenarios, not point estimates. The point of SAA is robustness across plausible futures, not precision about one.

### Set Tolerance Bands And A Review Cadence

A policy mix without rebalancing rules drifts and silently changes the risk profile. Define drift tolerance and review frequency up front.

Decide:

- absolute and relative tolerance bands per sleeve;
- whether rebalancing is calendar, threshold, or cash-flow driven;
- the strategic review cadence (for example annual for goals, every several years for the core mix);
- what would trigger an off-cycle review (goal change, capacity change, regime break).

Bands should reflect volatility, cost, and tax friction. A band that is too tight creates churn; a band that is too loose lets risk concentrate.

### Separate Strategic From Tactical

SAA is the policy. Tactical deviations are a separate decision with their own budget, discipline, and tracking. Mixing the two destroys both.

If the investor wants to express short-term views, define:

- a strategic core that does not move with views;
- a separate tactical sleeve with explicit size limits;
- a tracking-error budget;
- rules for entering and exiting tactical positions.

Do not relabel a tactical bet as a "strategic" change every time the market moves. A strategic change should be rare and driven by the investor's circumstances or a durable shift in capital market assumptions.

### Account For The Whole Balance Sheet

SAA is not only the investment account. Human capital, private business equity, real estate, pensions, and insurance all contribute to the household's effective allocation.

Map:

- the financial portfolio;
- human capital (its risk profile and duration);
- private and illiquid assets;
- liabilities and their rate sensitivity;
- insurance and option-like claims.

A young tenured worker with stable income has bond-like human capital and can hold more financial equity. A entrepreneur with concentrated business risk already holds a large equity position and may need more stabilization in the financial portfolio.

### Plan For Failure Modes

SAA should be designed for the bad scenarios, not optimized for the average.

Check:

- the worst historical drawdown of the mix and whether the investor could have held it;
- joint drawdowns where diversifiers fail (equity-credit, equity-bond in inflation shocks);
- the path of withdrawals during a drawdown (sequence-of-returns risk);
- liquidity gaps if illiquid sleeves cannot be sold.

A mix that looks good on average but is abandoned in the worst quarter is not a valid strategic allocation.

## Common Traps

### Solving For Return Instead Of Risk Capacity

Choosing the equity weight to hit a return goal, without testing whether the investor can tolerate the drawdown, is the most common SAA failure. The result is often a de-risking sale at the bottom.

### Trusting Backtests Of A Fixed Mix

A historical backtest of one fixed mix over one sample gives false confidence. It hides regime shifts, assumes the investor held through the worst points, and is sensitive to the start and end dates.

### Counting Correlated Sleeves As Diversification

Adding emerging-market equity, small-caps, sector equity, and private equity may all be equity beta. In a drawdown they fall together. Diversification that fails exactly when it is needed is not diversification.

### Ignoring Taxes And Account Location

The same SAA implemented in taxable and tax-advantaged accounts can have very different after-tax outcomes. Ignoring asset location turns a good policy mix into a poor after-tax portfolio.

### Changing The Policy Mix Under Stress

Selling equities after a crash and calling it a "strategic update" is usually capitulation relabeled. Strategic changes should be rare and tied to the investor's circumstances, not to recent market pain.

### Over-Optimizing The Mix

A 14-sleeve optimized mix that is sensitive to tiny input changes is fragile. Simpler, robust mixes often dominate complex ones after costs and estimation error.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The equity share is derived from risk capacity, risk tolerance, goals, and time horizon, not reverse-engineered from a required return.
- [ ] Each asset-class sleeve has a defined risk role, a benchmark, and an expected long-term risk-return profile.
- [ ] Forward return assumptions are regime-aware and expressed as ranges or scenarios, not a single trailing average.
- [ ] Rebalancing tolerance bands and a strategic review cadence are defined.
- [ ] Strategic policy is separated from any tactical sleeve, with size and tracking-error limits.
- [ ] The whole balance sheet, including human capital, private assets, and liabilities, was considered.
- [ ] The mix was stress-tested for worst-case drawdowns, joint failures, withdrawal paths, and liquidity gaps.
- [ ] The recommendation is not presented as suitable without investor-specific goals, horizon, risk tolerance, tax context, and a reminder that capital market assumptions are uncertain and that professional advice may be warranted for complex situations.
