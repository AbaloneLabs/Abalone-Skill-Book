# Tactical Asset Allocation

Tactical asset allocation (TAA) is the deliberate, bounded deviation from a strategic policy mix to express views about relative attractiveness across asset classes over a shorter horizon. Done well, it adds modest value with explicit risk control. Done poorly, it is undisciplined market timing that destroys the strategic plan, racks up costs and taxes, and leaves the investor with regret after the fact.

The judgment problem is that TAA feels productive because every view has a plausible story, but most tactical edges are small, noisy, and easily erased by costs, estimation error, and behavioral failure. Agents often overstate conviction, size tactical positions too large, and relabel emotion-driven trades as "tactical." The skill exists to force a discipline that most tactical activity lacks.

## Core Rules

### Start From A Strategic Core

TAA only makes sense relative to a defined policy mix. If there is no SAA, there is nothing to tactically deviate from, and the activity is just discretionary investing.

Establish first:

- the strategic policy weights;
- the tactical budget, expressed as a maximum deviation per sleeve and in total;
- a tracking-error budget relative to the policy portfolio.

A typical disciplined setup limits total tactical risk to a small fraction of portfolio risk. Without a budget, tactical positions expand until one bad call dominates the outcome.

### Require A Testable Thesis, Not A Narrative

Every tactical tilt should have a mechanism and an invalidation condition, not just a story.

For each tilt, state:

- the view (direction, asset class, horizon);
- the mechanism, what is mispriced and why the market should correct it;
- what is already priced in;
- the magnitude needed to overcome costs and taxes;
- what evidence would confirm or refute the view;
- the time frame after which the view is wrong if nothing happens.

Weak thesis: "Emerging markets look cheap, so overweight." Strong thesis: "EM equities trade at a wide valuation discount and earnings revisions have turned positive; overweight by a bounded amount, review if the discount narrows to X or if the dollar strengthens beyond Y."

### Size By Conviction, Edge, And Tracking Error

Tactical size should reflect the strength of the edge, the reliability of the signal, and the tracking-error budget, not enthusiasm.

Consider:

- the expected alpha net of costs;
- the probability the view is right and the payoff if right versus wrong;
- the correlation of the tilt to existing positions;
- how much tracking error the investor tolerates;
- the cost and tax drag of the trade.

Fractional sizing is usually wiser than all-in tilts. A view worth taking is worth sizing so that being wrong does not break the plan.

### Account For Costs, Taxes, And Friction

Tactical trading incurs transaction costs, bid-ask spreads, realized gains, and tax drag. These are not footnotes; they are the main reason most tactical activity underperforms.

Check:

- expected round-trip cost;
- tax impact of realizing gains;
- holding-period effects;
- fund-level costs and redemption fees;
- currency and settlement friction.

A tactical view whose expected edge is smaller than its round-trip cost is not worth taking. Many attractive-looking tilts fail this test.

### Respect The Regime And The Signal's Half-Life

Tactical signals decay. A momentum tilt that worked for months can reverse quickly. A valuation tilt can stay "early" for years.

Ask:

- what regime is the signal designed for;
- what is the typical holding period and signal half-life;
- does the current regime support or undermine the signal;
- are multiple signals aligned or conflicting.

Aligning several independent signals (valuation, momentum, macro, positioning) raises confidence. Acting on a single noisy signal invites whipsaw.

### Define Entry, Exit, And Reversion Rules

The most common tactical failure is entering on a view but never having an exit. Positions then drift into permanent overweights.

Define in advance:

- the target tactical weight;
- the trigger to add, trim, or close;
- the rule for reverting to policy;
- the maximum holding time if the thesis is unconfirmed.

Rules-based reversion prevents both premature abandonment and the "it will come back" trap.

### Separate Skill From Luck Over Time

TAA outcomes are noisy. A few wins do not prove skill; a few losses do not prove failure. Evaluate tactical activity over a full cycle, net of costs, against the policy portfolio.

Track:

- realized tracking error versus budget;
- hit rate and payoff of tilts;
- attribution of tactical value added;
- behavioral consistency, did the investor follow the rules.

If the investor cannot follow the discipline in practice, TAA is likely value-destructive regardless of the views.

## Common Traps

### TAA As Disguised Market Timing

Calling every impulse a "tactical tilt" without a budget, a thesis, or an exit rule is market timing. It usually underperforms the policy portfolio after costs.

### Sizing Tilts By Confidence Instead Of Edge

High conviction is not the same as a large expected edge. Large tilts on weak edges produce large tracking error with little expected reward.

### Ignoring The Cost Hurdle

Many tactical tilts have an expected edge smaller than their round-trip cost and tax drag. Net of friction, they lose money even when the view is directionally right.

### Never Reverting To Policy

Positions entered tactically become permanent overweights because there is no exit rule. The portfolio silently drifts away from the strategic plan.

### Chasing Recent Winners

Tilting toward what just outperformed feels prudent but is often late momentum. By the time the story is obvious, much of the move may be over.

### Relabeling Loss Aversion As Tactics

Cutting risk after a decline and calling it "tactical de-risking" is often fear, not analysis. Genuine tactical de-risking follows a pre-defined rule, not a reaction to pain.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A strategic policy mix and a tactical budget (per-sleeve and total, plus a tracking-error limit) exist before any tilt is recommended.
- [ ] Each tactical tilt has a testable thesis with a mechanism, what is priced in, an invalidation condition, and a time frame.
- [ ] Position sizes reflect conviction, expected edge net of costs, and tracking-error budget, not enthusiasm.
- [ ] Round-trip transaction costs, taxes, spreads, and fund-level fees were checked against the expected edge.
- [ ] The signal's regime, half-life, and alignment with other signals were considered.
- [ ] Entry, exit, and reversion-to-policy rules are defined in advance.
- [ ] The recommendation acknowledges that tactical value added is uncertain, that most tactical activity underperforms after costs, and that investor-specific goals, tax context, and professional advice matter for real decisions.
