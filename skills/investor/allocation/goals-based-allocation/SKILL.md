# Goals-Based Allocation

Goals-based allocation organizes a portfolio around distinct, named objectives rather than a single blended return target. Each goal gets its own sub-portfolio (often called a bucket or module) with a horizon, a required outcome, a risk profile, and an asset mix chosen to fund that specific liability or aspiration. The judgment problem is that goals-based allocation is frequently misused as a marketing label slapped onto a standard 60/40 portfolio, or implemented so literally that it fragments the household balance sheet into a dozen tiny, uncoordinated buckets that are costly and hard to manage. Done well, it aligns risk with the purpose of each dollar, improves investor discipline, and makes tradeoffs visible. Done poorly, it multiplies accounts, ignores correlations across buckets, and produces a worse aggregate outcome than a simple total-portfolio approach.

This skill is for structuring an allocation around goals with honest awareness of where the method helps and where it creates hidden costs.

## Core Rules

### Start From Goals, Not From A Return Number

The defining feature of goals-based allocation is that the goals define the portfolio, not the reverse. Begin by enumerating the investor's objectives in concrete terms.

For each goal, capture:

- the purpose (retirement spending, education, home, legacy, bequest, charitable, emergency);
- the time horizon and whether it is fixed or flexible;
- the required future cash flows, in real (inflation-adjusted) terms;
- the priority (essential needs versus aspirational wants);
- the funding status (fully funded, partially funded, unfunded);
- whether the goal is binary (must meet a target) or graduated (more is better).

A goal without a dollar amount and a date is not actionable. The strength of the method depends entirely on the quality of goal definition. Vague goals produce vague buckets.

### Match The Asset Mix To Each Goal's Horizon And Priority

Each goal warrants a different risk profile because the consequences of shortfall differ. Essential near-term needs cannot tolerate equity drawdown; aspirational long-term legacy goals can.

Use a risk hierarchy:

- essential needs with short horizons (one to three years): cash, short bonds, stable value;
- essential needs with medium horizons (three to ten years): a mix of bonds and moderate equity;
- essential needs with long horizons (ten-plus years): a growth-oriented mix, since inflation is the dominant risk;
- aspirational wants: higher equity and growth-asset tilt, because shortfall is tolerable.

The key insight is that the right allocation depends on when the money is needed and how badly a shortfall hurts, not on a universal risk-tolerance questionnaire applied once.

### Treat The Aggregate Portfolio Honestly

Goals-based buckets are a mental and operational structure, but the underlying assets still interact. The aggregate portfolio is what actually bears market risk.

Check:

- the combined exposure across all buckets (you may discover 90% aggregate equity hidden inside "conservative" long-horizon buckets);
- correlations between buckets, especially in drawdowns;
- whether the total risk is survivable for the investor;
- whether diversification is real or illusory when every long-term bucket holds the same equity index.

A goals-based structure that ignores the aggregate risk profile is just a relabeled total portfolio. Run the aggregate view alongside the bucket view.

### Fund Goals In Priority Order And Identify Gaps

Not every goal can be fully funded. Goals-based allocation forces the investor to confront scarcity and prioritize.

Sequence funding:

- emergency reserves and essential near-term needs first;
- essential long-term needs (retirement floor) next;
- aspirational goals last, accepting they may be underfunded.

When assets are insufficient, make the shortfall explicit: which goals are at risk, by how much, and what levers exist (save more, work longer, reduce the goal, take more risk on the surplus). Hiding an unfunded goal inside an optimistic return assumption is the most common failure.

### Account For Inflation And Real Versus Nominal Goals

Many goals are stated in nominal terms but are really real obligations. Retirement living costs, education, and medical costs inflate. A bucket that is "safe" in nominal bonds can fail in real terms over a long horizon.

Address:

- whether each goal is nominal (a fixed mortgage payoff) or real (retirement spending power);
- matching real goals with real-return assets (TIPS, equities, real estate) for long horizons;
- the erosion risk of cash and nominal bonds over decades;
- liability-matching where the inflation profile of assets should mirror the goal.

A 30-year retirement goal funded entirely in cash is not safe; it is almost certain to lose purchasing power.

### Decide How Literally To Bucket

There is a spectrum from conceptual bucketing to fully separated accounts. Each step toward separation adds clarity but also cost and complexity.

Options:

- conceptual buckets within one account (labels only, no separate holdings);
- separate sleeves within a portfolio (distinct holdings, coordinated rebalancing);
- separate accounts per goal (maximum clarity, maximum overhead).

Choose based on the investor's need for behavioral clarity versus operational cost. Most households benefit from a small number of meaningful buckets (two to four), not a dozen. Over-bucketing fragments assets, raises minimum-investment problems, and complicates rebalancing and tax management.

### Plan For Goal Interactions And Shared Resources

Goals are not independent. A job loss can threaten the education bucket and the retirement bucket simultaneously. A market crash hits every equity bucket at once.

Consider:

- shared emergency reserves that protect all goals;
- the sequence in which buckets draw down under stress;
- whether a flexible goal (retire later) can absorb a hit to protect a fixed goal (college tuition);
- human capital as a resource that funds multiple goals.

Design for the scenarios where multiple goals come under pressure together, because that is when the structure is actually tested.

### Revisit Goals And Horizons Over Time

A goal's horizon shrinks as the date approaches. A 20-year retirement bucket becomes a 5-year bucket fifteen years later, and its asset mix must de-risk. Goals also change: children, health, inheritance, divorce, career shifts.

Set a cadence to:

- roll each goal's horizon forward and de-risk as the date nears;
- re-prioritize when circumstances change;
- re-fund goals after large market moves;
- confirm the aggregate portfolio still matches total risk capacity.

A goals-based allocation set once and never updated drifts into the wrong risk profile for every bucket.

## Common Traps

### Labeling A Standard Portfolio As Goals-Based

Calling a 60/40 portfolio "goals-based" without actually decomposing it into funded objectives adds no value and obscures whether goals are met. The structure must be tied to defined goals with horizons and dollar targets.

### Ignoring Aggregate Risk Across Buckets

Five "diversified" buckets that each hold the same equity fund produce one concentrated equity bet in aggregate. The bucket view can hide risk that the aggregate view reveals.

### Over-Fragmenting Into Tiny Buckets

A dozen small buckets create minimum-investment problems, trading-cost drag, rebalancing complexity, and tax inefficiency. Most investors need a few meaningful buckets, not many.

### Funding Aspirational Goals Before Essential Needs

Allocating to aggressive growth for a legacy goal while the retirement floor is underfunded inverts priority. Essential needs should be secured first.

### Assuming Nominal Safety For Long-Horizon Goals

Funding a 30-year real goal with cash or short nominal bonds feels safe but guarantees purchasing-power loss. The relevant risk for long horizons is inflation, not short-term volatility.

### Hiding Unfunded Goals Behind Optimistic Returns

Assuming 9% equity returns to make an underfunded goal "work" on paper does not fund it. Use conservative assumptions and make the gap explicit.

### Treating Buckets As Independent Under Stress

In a crash, every equity bucket falls together and the investor's income may also be hit. Buckets that look separate in normal times collapse into one stressed portfolio.

### Forgetting Behavioral Discipline Cuts Both Ways

Bucketing can improve discipline by making each goal's purpose visible, but it can also encourage mental accounting errors, such as holding cash in a "safe" bucket while carrying high-interest debt that destroys wealth.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each goal is defined with a purpose, horizon, required real cash flows, priority, and funding status, not as a vague aspiration.
- [ ] The asset mix for each bucket reflects its horizon and the severity of shortfall, with essential near-term needs in low-volatility assets and long-horizon real goals protected against inflation.
- [ ] The aggregate portfolio across all buckets was examined for total risk, correlation, and survivability, not only the individual bucket views.
- [ ] Goals are funded in priority order (essential needs first), and any unfunded gap is made explicit rather than hidden behind optimistic return assumptions.
- [ ] The number of buckets is deliberate (typically a few meaningful modules), avoiding over-fragmentation that raises cost and complexity.
- [ ] Goal interactions and shared stress were planned for, including joint drawdowns, shared emergency reserves, and flexible goals absorbing shocks to protect fixed ones.
- [ ] A cadence exists to roll horizons forward, de-risk as dates approach, and revisit goals when circumstances change.
- [ ] The recommendation notes that goals-based allocation does not guarantee any goal will be met, that market outcomes are uncertain, and that for complex household situations professional financial advice may be warranted.
