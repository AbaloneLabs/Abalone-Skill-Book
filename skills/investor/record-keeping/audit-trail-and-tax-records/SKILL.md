# Audit Trail And Tax Records

Investment records are insurance against a future problem you cannot predict. The judgment problem is that the moment you need a record — to defend a cost basis in an audit, to prove a wash sale adjustment, to substantiate a long-term holding period, or to reconstruct a position after a broker migration — is years after the record was created, and by then it cannot be recreated. Agents and investors routinely assume the broker "keeps all that," discover too late that basis tracking has gaps, missing corporate-action adjustments, or orphaned lots from transferred accounts, and then face tax bills, penalties, or the loss of favorable long-term treatment. The skill is building a record system that survives broker changes, mergers, and decades, and that can reconstruct the truth when the default records are wrong.

This skill is for designing investment record retention that protects against tax and audit risk. It is not tax or legal advice; retention rules and tax treatment vary by jurisdiction and change over time, and a qualified professional should be consulted for specific situations.

## Core Rules

### Treat Cost Basis As The Investor's Responsibility, Not The Broker's

Brokers are required to track and report cost basis for many modern covered securities, but the legal responsibility for an accurate tax return remains with the investor. Broker-reported basis is frequently incomplete or wrong, and the gaps appear in exactly the cases that cost the most.

Take ownership of:

- the original purchase price, date, and lot identification for every position;
- pre-covered securities (older holdings, certain transfer cases) that the broker may not track at all;
- positions transferred between brokers, where basis can be mis-mapped or lost;
- reinvested dividends and dividend reinvestment plans, which create many small lots easily lost over time.

Do not treat a broker's 1099 as infallible. Reconcile it against your own records before filing.

### Reconstruct And Preserve The Full Cost Basis History

Cost basis is not a single number; it is a history of every event that adjusted it. Losing any link breaks the chain.

Events that adjust basis and must be recorded:

- original purchase price and date for each lot;
- reinvested dividends and capital gain distributions (each creates a new lot);
- corporate actions — splits, reverse splits, mergers, spinoffs, stock dividends, and reorganizations (each changes share counts and per-share basis);
- return of capital distributions (reduce basis rather than generating income);
- wash sale adjustments (disallowed losses add to the replacement basis);
- commission and fee adjustments embedded in historical trades.

When a corporate action occurs, record the adjustment immediately with the broker's explanation, because reconstructing a spinoff basis five years later from memory is often impossible.

### Retain Records Long Enough — And Define "Long Enough" Conservatively

The retention period is not the holding period of the investment; it is the period during which the tax authority can question the return that reported the sale, plus a safety margin.

Conservative practice:

- keep purchase and basis records for as long as the position is held, plus the full retention window after the sale is reported;
- keep records supporting any loss, wash sale, or special treatment for the extended period those items can be examined;
- keep records for positions that may have gift, inheritance, or transfer treatment indefinitely, since the relevant basis event may predate your own ownership;
- retain records for closed accounts and dissolved entities, because a tax question can arise long after the account is gone.

When in doubt, retain longer. Storage is cheap; a missing basis record is expensive.

### Keep The Primary Source Documents, Not Just Summaries

A spreadsheet of basis is a convenience, not a record. The defensible record is the underlying source.

Primary sources to retain:

- trade confirmations (or the electronic equivalent) showing price, date, fees, and lot;
- monthly and annual statements showing positions, corporate actions, and reinvestments;
- the broker's cost basis report and any 1099-B, including adjustments;
- Form 8937 or equivalent corporate-action explanations from issuers;
- records of gifts, inheritances, and transfers with their fair-market-value and basis carryover details.

A summary without sources cannot be defended in an audit. Keep both, but the sources are the record.

### Document Wash Sales, Substantial Compliance, And Special Situations Explicitly

Certain situations are audit magnets and need explicit documentation.

Document carefully:

- wash sales across accounts and substantially identical securities, including the replacement lot and adjusted basis;
- sales claimed as long-term near the one-year boundary, with holding-period proof;
- qualified small business, opportunity zone, conservation, or other special-treatment positions, with the eligibility records;
- gifts and inheritances, with the donor's basis or the date-of-death fair market value and valuation support;
- cryptocurrency transactions, where broker reporting is incomplete and the investor bears the full reconstruction burden.

These are the cases where a missing document converts a favorable outcome into an ordinary one.

### Plan For Broker Mergers, Account Transfers, And Platform Changes

The most common cause of lost basis is an account transfer or broker merger, after which the new platform shows a basis of zero, a wrong lot mapping, or "noncovered" status that forces the investor to prove the real number.

Protect against this:

- export a full position and lot report, including per-lot basis and acquisition dates, before any transfer or platform migration;
- keep the export in a format you control (a portable spreadsheet or PDF), not only inside the old platform;
- verify that basis imported correctly into the new platform immediately after the transfer, while the old records are still accessible;
- retain the pre-transfer records permanently, because the question can arise years later.

### Make Records Survivable And Accessible

Records locked in a format, device, or account you can no longer open are useless. Use portable formats, redundant storage, and a way for a fiduciary or executor to access them if you cannot.

## Common Traps

### Trusting The Broker's Reported Basis Without Reconciliation

Broker basis is often incomplete, especially for older, transferred, or noncovered lots. Reconcile before relying on it.

### Losing Basis In A Broker Transfer Or Merger

Account migrations routinely drop or mis-map basis. Export and verify before and after every transfer.

### Discarding Records After The Sale

The retention clock starts when the sale is reported, not when the position was bought. Records are needed long after the sale closes.

### Forgetting Reinvested Dividends As Separate Lots

Each reinvestment is a purchase with its own basis and date. Over decades these accumulate into hundreds of small lots that are easy to lose.

### Ignoring Corporate Action Adjustments

Splits, spinoffs, and mergers change basis. Without the adjustment record, the basis chain breaks and the holding period or gain is miscomputed.

### Treating A Spreadsheet As The Record

A summary is a convenience. The defensible record is the source confirmation, statement, and corporate-action notice.

### Assuming Cryptocurrency Or Foreign Assets Are Tracked For You

Broker reporting for crypto is incomplete, and foreign asset reporting has its own record and disclosure requirements. The investor bears the reconstruction burden.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Cost basis is treated as the investor's responsibility, with broker-reported basis reconciled against independent records before filing.
- [ ] The full basis history is preserved, including reinvested dividends, corporate actions, return of capital, wash sale adjustments, and fees.
- [ ] Retention periods are set conservatively, covering the holding period plus the full examination window after the sale, with indefinite retention for gift, inheritance, and special-treatment positions.
- [ ] Primary source documents (confirmations, statements, 1099-B, Form 8937, gift and inheritance valuations) are retained, not only summaries.
- [ ] Wash sales, long-term boundary sales, special-treatment positions, gifts, inheritances, and cryptocurrency transactions are documented explicitly as audit-prone cases.
- [ ] A full lot-and-basis export is made before any broker transfer or platform migration, and basis is verified after import.
- [ ] Records are stored in portable, redundant formats accessible to a fiduciary or executor.
- [ ] The conclusion flags that retention rules and tax treatment vary by jurisdiction and change over time, that this is not tax or legal advice, and that a qualified professional should be consulted for the investor's specific situation.
