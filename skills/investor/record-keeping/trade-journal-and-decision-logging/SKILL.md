# Trade Journal And Decision Logging

A trade journal is not a log of what was bought and sold — the brokerage already records that. It is a record of what the investor was thinking, what they expected, what they feared, and what would prove them wrong, captured at the moment of decision before outcomes rewrite memory. The judgment problem is that without a contemporaneous record, investors systematically misremember their own reasoning. Winners feel like skill, losses get rationalized as bad luck or external shocks, and the same decision errors repeat for decades because the feedback loop is corrupted. A journal's value is not in the writing; it is in forcing the investor to commit to a falsifiable thesis and then comparing it later to what actually happened.

This skill is for designing a decision-logging process that genuinely improves judgment, not one that becomes a chore abandoned after a month. It is not personalized financial advice.

## Core Rules

### Capture The Thesis Before The Outcome, Not After

The entire value of a journal depends on timing. A thesis written before the trade is evidence; a thesis reconstructed after the result is rationalization.

Record at the moment of decision:

- the specific investment thesis and the expected source of return;
- the time horizon over which the thesis should play out;
- the current price and what is believed to be priced in;
- the evidence or event that would prove the thesis wrong;
- the intended position size and how it fits the portfolio;
- the conditions for adding, trimming, or exiting.

If the journal entry cannot be written before the trade, the decision is probably under-defined. The discipline of writing it often surfaces gaps in the thesis itself.

### Make The Thesis Falsifiable

A thesis that cannot be wrong cannot be tested. Vague statements like "this is a great long-term company" or "AI will be big" provide no way to learn, because any outcome can be made to fit them retroactively.

A falsifiable thesis specifies:

- a concrete expectation (a metric, a milestone, a relative outcome, a price-implied scenario);
- a timeframe in which it should occur;
- the evidence that would invalidate it.

If the thesis survives every possible outcome, it is not a thesis — it is a feeling. Force specificity so that later review can actually judge whether the reasoning was sound.

### Log Emotion And Context, Not Just Numbers

Decision quality is degraded by emotional and circumstantial factors that investors prefer to ignore in hindsight. Recording them honestly is what makes later diagnosis possible.

Useful context to capture:

- the emotional state at decision time (fear, excitement, boredom, pressure to act);
- whether the trade was driven by a thesis, by a tip, by price movement, or by a need to "do something";
- external pressures (a falling market, peer behavior, a deadline, a recent loss);
- confidence level on a defined scale.

Patterns emerge only when emotion is logged consistently. Investors often discover that their worst trades cluster around specific emotional states, which is far more actionable than re-reading the numbers.

### Separate Decision Quality From Outcome Quality

A good decision can produce a bad outcome (luck), and a bad decision can produce a good outcome (also luck). Reviewing only outcomes teaches the wrong lessons.

When reviewing a past trade, judge:

- was the thesis well-reasoned given what was knowable at the time;
- was the position sized appropriately for the risk;
- was the process followed, or was it abandoned under pressure;
- did the outcome reflect skill, luck, or factors outside the thesis.

Rewarding lucky bad decisions and punishing unlucky good decisions corrupts future behavior. The journal should make it possible to judge the process, not just the result.

### Define A Review Cadence And Actually Perform It

A journal that is written but never reviewed is a diary, not a decision tool. Build a review into a routine.

Useful cadences:

- a quick check at each thesis milestone or earnings event;
- a quarterly review of open positions against their falsification conditions;
- an annual review of closed trades grouped by theme, sector, or emotional state to find repeating patterns.

The review is where learning happens. Without it, the journal is inert.

### Include Pre-Mortems And Post-Mortems

Before a trade, a pre-mortem asks: assuming this position has gone badly in a year, what most likely caused it? After a trade closes, a post-mortem asks: what did I believe, what actually happened, and what should I do differently?

These structured exercises counter the natural tendency to construct a flattering narrative. A pre-mortem forces downside imagination before capital is committed; a post-mortem forces honest comparison after the fact.

### Standardize The Format Enough To Be Searchable

A journal of free-form notes becomes unsearchable and unused. A light structure — fixed fields for thesis, horizon, falsification trigger, size, emotion, and confidence — lets the investor later filter by pattern (for example, all high-confidence trades that failed, or all trades made in a falling market).

Keep the structure light enough that the journal is actually maintained. An elaborate template abandoned after two weeks is worse than a simple one used for years.

## Common Traps

### Writing The Thesis After Knowing The Outcome

Reconstructing rationale after the result is rationalization. The journal only improves judgment if it captures thinking contemporaneously.

### Non-Falsifiable Theses

Theses that can explain any outcome teach nothing. "It's a long-term winner" cannot be wrong and therefore cannot be reviewed.

### Reviewing Only Outcomes

Judging trades by whether they made money rewards luck and punishes unlucky good reasoning. Process quality must be judged separately from outcome.

### Abandoning The Journal After Early Losses

Many investors stop journaling precisely when the feedback becomes uncomfortable. The journal is most valuable when it records and exposes errors, which means discomfort is a sign it is working.

### Logging Numbers But Not Emotion

Recording prices and returns without emotional and circumstantial context misses the patterns that drive repeated errors.

### Over-Engineering The Template

An elaborate journal structure that takes too long to fill is abandoned. Prefer a minimal, consistent format that survives routine use.

### Confusing Journaling With Overtrading

The act of journaling can create a bias toward action. A journal should also record deliberate non-decisions — the decision to do nothing is a decision worth logging.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Theses are captured before the trade, at the moment of decision, not reconstructed after the outcome.
- [ ] Each thesis is falsifiable, specifying a concrete expectation, timeframe, and invalidating evidence.
- [ ] Emotional state, confidence level, and circumstantial context are logged alongside the numbers.
- [ ] Decision quality is judged separately from outcome quality during reviews, accounting for luck.
- [ ] A defined review cadence (milestone, quarterly, annual) is built in and actually performed.
- [ ] Pre-mortems before trades and post-mortems after closure are part of the process.
- [ ] The journal format is structured enough to be searchable by pattern but light enough to be maintained.
- [ ] Deliberate non-decisions are logged, not only active trades.
- [ ] The conclusion flags that journaling supports but does not guarantee better outcomes, that past performance does not guarantee future results, and that this is not personalized financial advice.
