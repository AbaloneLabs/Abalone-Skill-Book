# Performance Tracking And Benchmarks

Measuring performance is easy to do badly. The judgment problem is that a return number in isolation is nearly meaningless — a 12 percent year could be excellent or disastrous depending on what was possible, what was risked, when money was added or withdrawn, and what the investor was trying to achieve. Agents and investors routinely compare a portfolio to the wrong index, use a return calculation that hides the impact of their own cash flows, or declare skill from a short winning streak that was mostly luck and market beta. The skill is constructing a comparison that actually answers whether the strategy added value, rather than one that flatters or misleads.

This skill is for designing performance measurement that supports honest decisions. It is not personalized financial advice; past performance does not guarantee future results.

## Core Rules

### Never Quote A Return Without A Benchmark And A Risk Measure

A return number means nothing without context. The same 10 percent is outstanding for a bond portfolio and poor for an aggressive equity portfolio in a bull market.

Always pair return with:

- an appropriate benchmark reflecting the portfolio's intended exposure;
- a risk measure (volatility, drawdown, beta) so return is judged against what was risked;
- the market environment over the period, since beta explains most short-term results.

A portfolio that returned 15 percent by holding the market is not skill; it is exposure. Separate the return earned by simply owning the asset class from any incremental return.

### Choose A Benchmark That Matches The Actual Exposure

The most common measurement error is comparing a portfolio to an index that does not reflect what it holds. A global portfolio compared to a US-only index, a value portfolio compared to a growth index, or a balanced portfolio compared to a pure equity index all produce misleading conclusions.

Test the benchmark:

- does it match the portfolio's asset mix, geography, market-cap range, and style tilt;
- does it reflect the currency and the after-cost, after-tax reality the investor actually experiences;
- is it investable and representative, or a cherry-picked comparison that makes the portfolio look good;
- for multi-asset portfolios, is a blended benchmark of the component indices used rather than a single equity index.

A benchmark chosen to make the portfolio look good is not a benchmark; it is marketing.

### Use The Right Return Calculation For The Question

The calculation method changes the answer, and each method answers a different question.

- **time-weighted return (TWR)** removes the effect of cash flows and measures the manager's or strategy's skill; appropriate for evaluating an investment process.
- **money-weighted return (IRR)** reflects the actual experience of the investor, including the timing and size of their contributions and withdrawals; appropriate for evaluating whether the investor met their personal goal.

A strategy can have excellent time-weighted returns while the investor experiences poor money-weighted returns because they added money at the top and sold at the bottom. Both numbers are true; they answer different questions. Use the one that matches the question being asked.

### Separate Skill From Luck And Beta Over Adequate Periods

Short-term performance is dominated by luck and by exposure to whatever factor happened to win. Distinguishing genuine skill requires enough time and the right decomposition.

Be skeptical of:

- track records shorter than a full market cycle (typically five to ten years), which mostly reflect the regime that prevailed;
- outperformance concentrated in a single period or a single winning position;
- returns explained entirely by a known factor tilt (size, value, momentum, sector, geography) rather than by selection;
- claims of skill without a credible, repeatable process behind the numbers.

Most apparent outperformance over one to three years is luck plus beta. Require a longer period and an attribution analysis before inferring skill.

### Perform Attribution, Not Just Totals

A total return hides where the return came from. Attribution breaks performance into allocation effect, selection effect, currency, and timing, revealing whether the value came from a deliberate edge or from an accidental concentration.

Ask:

- did outperformance come from one lucky position or a repeatable process;
- was it driven by a sector, factor, or currency bet rather than security selection;
- did the investor add value by rebalancing, or lose value by trading;
- how much of the return was simply the market rising.

Attribution turns a number into a diagnosis.

### Account For Survivorship And Selection Bias In Reported Records

Reported performance figures, especially for funds and managers, are biased upward because failures disappear and only survivors are counted. A composite of "managers who still exist" overstates what an investor would have actually experienced when choosing among them prospectively. Apply the same skepticism to an investor's own selective memory of their best calls while forgetting the losers.

### Reconcile Performance To Source Records

A performance figure that cannot be tied back to brokerage statements, custodian reports, and confirmed transactions is unreliable. Reconcile periodically to catch data errors, missed trades, and unbooked corporate actions that distort the numbers.

## Common Traps

### Comparing To The S&P 500 By Default

A global, balanced, or bond-containing portfolio compared to US large-cap equities will look terrible in a US bull market and great in a bear market, and tells you nothing either way. Match the benchmark to the exposure.

### Using IRR To Claim Manager Skill

Money-weighted returns conflate the investor's cash-flow timing with the strategy's quality. Use TWR to judge a process and IRR to judge personal experience.

### Declaring Skill From A Short Streak

Three good years in a rising market is not evidence of skill. Most such streaks are luck and beta.

### Cherry-Picking The Start Date

Starting a comparison at a market bottom or a portfolio trough manufactures flattering returns. Use full cycles and consistent periods.

### Ignoring Risk

Two strategies with identical returns but different drawdowns are not equally good. Return without risk is an incomplete picture.

### Forgetting Costs And Taxes

Gross returns overstate what the investor keeps. Compare on a net-of-fees and, where relevant, after-tax basis to reflect reality.

### Survivorship Bias In Manager Universes

Comparing against only the managers who survived flatters the apparent difficulty of beating them. The investor chose prospectively, not from the survivors.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Every quoted return is paired with an appropriate benchmark and a risk measure (volatility, drawdown, or beta).
- [ ] The benchmark matches the portfolio's actual asset mix, geography, style, currency, and after-cost reality, or a blended benchmark is used for multi-asset portfolios.
- [ ] The return calculation (TWR versus IRR) matches the question being asked — process skill versus personal experience.
- [ ] Apparent skill is tested over a full market cycle, not a short streak, with attribution separating selection from factor, sector, and currency bets.
- [ ] Attribution was performed to identify whether value came from a repeatable process or a single lucky position.
- [ ] Survivorship and selection bias were considered in any comparison to manager universes or to the investor's own record.
- [ ] Performance figures are reconciled to brokerage and custodian source records to catch data errors and unbooked corporate actions.
- [ ] Comparisons are made net of fees and, where relevant, after tax.
- [ ] The conclusion flags that past performance does not guarantee future results, that short-term outperformance is often luck, and that this is not personalized financial advice.
