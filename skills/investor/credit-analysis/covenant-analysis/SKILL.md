# Covenant Analysis

Covenants are the contractual terms in a bond or loan that restrict the issuer's behavior and protect the lender. They are the legal architecture of credit, and in distress they often matter more than the financials. An investing agent often ignores covenants entirely, or treats all bonds of an issuer as equally protected, missing that two bonds with identical ratings can have completely different covenant packages that determine whether the lender can block value-destructive actions, accelerate repayment, or improve their recovery in a restructuring. Covenants are where credit analysis becomes legal analysis, and they are the primary lever a creditor has beyond waiting to be paid.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how well is this bond protected", "what happens if the company breaches its covenants", "what are the restrictions on the issuer", or "how do covenants affect recovery". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating a bond as a pure financial instrument and to force it to read the covenant package, distinguish maintenance from incurrence tests, identify restrictions and exceptions, assess breach and workout dynamics, and understand cross-default and intercreditor arrangements.

Covenant analysis requires reading legal documents and understanding jurisdiction-specific restructuring law. It is complex and consequential. Conclusions should disclose limitations and recommend professional legal and financial advice for distressed or complex situations.

## Core Rules

### Read The Actual Covenant Package, Not Just The Rating

The rating reflects default and loss probability; the covenant package defines what the creditor can do about it. They are different and both matter.

Examine:

- Financial covenants: quantitative tests the issuer must maintain or meet.
- Restrictive covenants: limits on dividends, debt, asset sales, investments, and liens.
- Event-of-default and acceleration rights: what lets the creditor demand immediate repayment.
- Reporting and compliance requirements: what information the issuer must provide.
- Amendments, waivers, and consent mechanics: how the terms can be changed.

Two bonds of the same issuer can have radically different protections. A covenant-light loan and a fully covenanted bond of the same company offer very different control in distress. Read the indenture or credit agreement, not just the offering memorandum summary.

### Distinguish Maintenance From Incurrence Covenants

Financial covenants come in two fundamentally different types, and the difference determines how much protection they provide.

Types:

- Maintenance covenants: require the issuer to maintain a ratio, such as leverage or coverage, at all times. Breach triggers default regardless of whether new action is taken. These are stronger and more common in bank loans.
- Incurrence covenants: require the issuer to meet a test only when taking a specific action, such as paying a dividend or issuing debt. If no action is taken, the test does not apply. These are weaker and common in bonds.

A maintenance covenant with a tight leash can catch deterioration early and force a workout before value is destroyed. An incurrence covenant only bites when the issuer acts, allowing quiet deterioration in between. Know which type governs the instrument.

### Identify Key Restrictions And Their Exceptions

Restrictive covenants limit value-destructive actions, but their protection depends on the exceptions, the baskets and carve-outs that let the issuer act anyway.

Assess:

- Dividend and distribution restrictions: how much cash can leave the issuer, and what baskets permit excess payments.
- Additional-debt restrictions: whether the issuer can incur more debt ahead of or alongside the bond, and the capacity limits.
- Asset-sale restrictions: whether proceeds must be used to repay debt or can be reinvested or distributed.
- Lien restrictions: whether the issuer can grant security to other creditors, subordinating the bond.
- Investment and acquisition restrictions: limits on buying other businesses or assets.
- Restricted-payments and restricted-subsidiary definitions: how broadly the covenants capture the corporate group.

A restriction with a large basket or broad exception provides little real protection. Read the exceptions as carefully as the restrictions themselves.

### Assess Breach Dynamics And Remedies

What happens when a covenant is breached determines the creditor's leverage and the likely outcome.

Understand:

- Grace periods: time the issuer has to cure a breach before default is triggered.
- Covenant holidays and waivers: temporary relief that can be negotiated, often for a fee or amended terms.
- Acceleration rights: whether and when the creditor can demand immediate repayment of principal.
- Cross-default and cross-acceleration: whether a default on other debt triggers default on this bond, and whether acceleration on other debt triggers acceleration here.
- Standstill and forbearance: negotiated pauses that give the issuer time to restructure.

A covenant breach does not automatically produce repayment. The process is negotiated, and the creditor's leverage depends on the specific rights in the documents and the intercreditor arrangements.

### Understand Cross-Default And Intercreditor Arrangements

In complex capital structures, what happens to one instrument depends on its relationship to others.

Map:

- Cross-default clauses: whether a default on debt above a threshold triggers default on this bond.
- Cross-acceleration: whether acceleration of other debt must occur before this bond is affected.
- Intercreditor agreements: how value is shared among secured, senior unsecured, and subordinated creditors in a workout.
- Subordination provisions: contractual and structural subordination that affects which entity's creditors are paid first.

Cross-default can pull a bond into a restructuring triggered elsewhere in the capital structure. Intercreditor and subordination arrangements determine recovery allocation. These relationships are as important as the issuer's financials in distress.

### Evaluate The Workout And Restructuring Process

In distress, the covenant package and legal process determine the outcome, not the original yield.

Consider:

- Is the likely process a negotiated restructuring, a prepackaged bankruptcy, or a liquidation?
- What is the creditor's blocking position, the percentage needed to approve a restructuring plan?
- Can the issuer use debt-to-equity swaps, cramdown, or dilution to impose losses?
- What is the likely recovery given the collateral, seniority, covenants, and jurisdiction?
- How long will the process take, locking up capital?

Distressed investing is fundamentally a legal and negotiation exercise. The financial analysis establishes the value at stake; the covenants and process determine how it is divided.

### Compare Covenant Quality Across The Capital Structure

Covenant quality varies within an issuer, and comparing instruments reveals where protection is strongest and weakest.

Compare:

- Bank loans typically have maintenance covenants and security; bonds typically have incurrence covenants and no security.
- Newer cov-lite loans have weaker protections than traditional loans.
- Different bond tranches and vintages can have different covenant packages.
- Subordinated and hybrid instruments often have weaker covenants and worse recovery.

The strongest protection often sits with the most senior, secured creditors. Investing further down the structure requires demanding more yield to compensate for weaker covenants and worse recovery.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Covenants And Treating The Rating As The Whole Story

The rating reflects default and loss probability; covenants define creditor control. Ignoring covenants misses the legal protection that determines outcomes in distress.

### Confusing Maintenance And Incurrence Covenants

Maintenance covenants catch deterioration continuously; incurrence covenants only bite on action. The protection differs enormously.

### Reading Restrictions Without Reading The Exceptions

Restrictions with large baskets or broad carve-outs provide little real protection. The exceptions often matter more than the restrictions.

### Assuming A Breach Means Immediate Repayment

Covenant breaches trigger negotiated processes, grace periods, and waivers, not automatic repayment. Leverage depends on the specific rights.

### Overlooking Cross-Default And Intercreditor Terms

A bond can be pulled into a restructuring triggered elsewhere. Intercreditor and subordination arrangements determine recovery allocation.

### Treating Distressed Analysis As Pure Finance

Distressed outcomes are determined by legal process and negotiation, not just financials. The covenants and jurisdiction govern the result.

### Assuming All Instruments Of An Issuer Have Equal Protection

Covenant quality varies within the capital structure. Senior, secured instruments typically have stronger protection than subordinated or unsecured ones.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The actual covenant package, financial, restrictive, default, and reporting, is read, not just the rating.
- [ ] Maintenance and incurrence covenants are distinguished, and the type governing the instrument is identified.
- [ ] Key restrictions are assessed together with their exceptions, baskets, and carve-outs.
- [ ] Breach dynamics, grace periods, waivers, acceleration, and cross-default are understood.
- [ ] Cross-default, cross-acceleration, and intercreditor arrangements are mapped for the capital structure.
- [ ] The likely workout or restructuring process, blocking positions, and timeline are evaluated for distressed situations.
- [ ] Covenant quality is compared across the issuer's capital structure to locate the strongest and weakest protection.
- [ ] The analysis recognizes that distressed outcomes are legal and negotiated, not purely financial.
- [ ] Subordination, security, and jurisdiction are incorporated into recovery expectations.
- [ ] The conclusion flags the complexity, recommends reading the actual documents, and advises professional legal and financial counsel for distressed or covenant-sensitive investments.
