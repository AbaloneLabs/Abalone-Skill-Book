# Performance Presentation Standards

Performance presentation standards govern how investment returns are calculated and reported so that results are comparable, verifiable, and not misleading. The global benchmark is the GIPS standards (Global Investment Performance Standards), but the underlying principles, fair, consistent, complete presentation, apply to any honest performance report. The judgment problem is that performance presentation is where integrity is most tested, because the incentives to flatter are strong and the techniques to do so are subtle. Agents and managers can cherry-pick favorable periods, annualize short strong runs, exclude poor accounts, present gross-of-fees returns, or choose flattering benchmarks, all while staying technically within loose norms. The result is a track record that looks far better than the experience of a real investor. The skill is presenting and interpreting performance with the discipline that makes the numbers mean what they say, and spotting the distortions when they do not.

This skill is for presenting and evaluating performance with honesty and awareness of the common distortions.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Right Return Calculation For The Question

Different return calculations answer different questions, and using the wrong one misleads. The two families are time-weighted and money-weighted, and they can diverge sharply.

Time-weighted return (TWR):

- measures the manager's skill by neutralizing the effect of client cash flows;
- appropriate for evaluating a manager or strategy where the client controls contributions and withdrawals;
- required by GIPS for composites because it isolates investment performance.

Money-weighted return (MWR, or IRR):

- measures the investor's actual experience, including the timing and size of their cash flows;
- appropriate for evaluating the investor's outcome, where cash-flow timing materially affects results;
- sensitive to when money was added or withdrawn, which is often the largest driver of investor return.

Reporting TWR when the question is about the investor's experience, or MWR when the question is about manager skill, produces a number that answers the wrong question. Match the method to the question and disclose which is used.

### Neutralize Or Disclose Cash-Flow Effects

Cash-flow timing is one of the largest distortions in performance reporting, because returns are sensitive to when money enters or leaves. A manager can look brilliant or terrible based on client cash-flow luck.

Address:

- use TWR with proper geometric linking of sub-periods to neutralize cash-flow timing for manager evaluation;
- for MWR, disclose the cash-flow pattern and its effect, since it drives the investor's actual return;
- handle large external cash flows with sub-period breaks to keep TWR accurate;
- be explicit that investor returns (MWR) often lag manager returns (TWR) due to poor cash-flow timing.

The gap between time-weighted and money-weighted returns (the return gap or timing drag) is itself informative: it reveals whether investors added money at highs and withdrew at lows. Disclose it rather than hiding behind the more flattering number.

### Present Gross And Net Of Fees, And Disclose All Costs

Fees and costs materially reduce the return an investor actually receives. Presenting only gross returns overstates the real outcome.

Disclose:

- gross-of-fees returns (before management and advisory fees) and net-of-fees returns (after them);
- the impact of transaction costs, which are real and often excluded from headline returns;
- the effect of taxes for taxable investors, which can dwarf management fees;
- any performance fees or carried interest and how they are reflected.

A gross return that looks attractive can be mediocre net of fees, transaction costs, and taxes. Honest presentation shows the investor's likely realized return, not the theoretical gross. GIPS requires presentation of both gross and net where fees are deducted.

### Use Appropriate Benchmarks And Compare Fairly

Benchmark selection determines whether performance looks good or bad. A flattering or mismatched benchmark makes any strategy appear successful.

Ensure benchmarks are:

- representative of the strategy's risk and style (a large-cap growth manager benchmarked against a value index is meaningless);
- investable and replicable, not a constructed index that cannot be bought;
- total-return where the strategy reinvests, or price-only where appropriate, consistently applied;
- rebalanced and weighted consistently with the strategy;
- disclosed and stable over time, not switched to a weaker benchmark after poor performance.

Benchmarks that are changed after weak periods, or that are constructed to be easy to beat, are a red flag. The benchmark must be a fair, investable representation of the strategy's opportunity set.

### Avoid Survivorship, Selection, And Cherry-Picking Bias

The most damaging presentation distortions come from which results are shown and which are hidden. These biases can flatter a track record by 1-3% per year or more.

Guard against:

- survivorship bias: only surviving (successful) funds or accounts are included, hiding those that closed;
- selection bias: only the best-performing accounts or periods are presented (cherry-picking);
- backfill bias: a strong track record is backfilled into a composite only after it proves successful;
- inception-date selection: starting the track record at a favorable point.

GIPS requires composites that include all discretionary fee-paying accounts managed under the same strategy, with no exclusion of poor performers except for defined, non-performance reasons. Any presentation that selects only winners is misleading regardless of the technical accuracy of each shown return.

### Annualize Honestly And Respect Short Records

Annualizing returns lets short periods be compared to long ones, but it is easily abused. A strong quarter annualized becomes a fantastical year; a short track record annualized looks like a proven edge.

Annualize with discipline:

- do not annualize periods so short that the annualized figure is meaningless (a one-month return annualized is noise);
- disclose the actual period length alongside any annualized figure;
- avoid presenting annualized returns for track records too short to be meaningful (GIPS suggests at least a year before annualizing);
- show the range of annual or period returns, not just the compound average, to reveal volatility.

A 300% annualized return from a three-week record is not a credible indication of skill. Honest presentation shows the period, the volatility, and the limitations of short records.

### Disclose Methodology, Assumptions, And Limitations

Honest performance presentation makes its method visible so the reader can judge it. Opaque presentations invite suspicion and often hide distortion.

Disclose:

- the calculation method (TWR versus MWR), the frequency of valuation, and the geometric linking;
- the treatment of cash flows, fees, taxes, and transaction costs;
- the benchmark and any changes to it, with reasons;
- the composite definition and any account exclusions with non-performance rationale;
- the period covered and any gaps or breaks in the record.

A presentation that discloses its method invites trust; one that hides it invites scrutiny. The discipline of disclosure is itself a signal of integrity.

## Common Traps

### Using TWR When The Investor's Experience Is The Question

TWR neutralizes cash flows and measures manager skill. The investor's actual return is money-weighted and often lower due to poor timing.

### Presenting Only Gross Returns

Gross returns overstate the investor's realized outcome. Net-of-fees, net-of-costs, and after-tax returns are what the investor actually experiences.

### Choosing A Flattering Or Mismatched Benchmark

A weak or mismatched benchmark makes any strategy look good. The benchmark must represent the strategy's risk and opportunity set.

### Survivorship And Selection Bias

Including only surviving or successful accounts and periods flatters the record by 1-3% or more. Composites must include all qualifying accounts.

### Annualizing Short Or Cherry-Picked Periods

Annualizing a strong short run produces fantastical figures. Short records are not evidence of skill.

### Switching Benchmarks After Poor Performance

Changing to a weaker benchmark to restore a flattering comparison is a red flag. Benchmarks should be stable and disclosed.

### Hiding Methodology And Assumptions

Opaque calculation methods invite suspicion and often hide distortion. Disclosure of method is essential to honest presentation.

### Ignoring Taxes In Taxable Presentations

For taxable investors, taxes can dwarf fees. After-tax presentation is necessary for a fair view of the investor's outcome.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The return calculation method (time-weighted versus money-weighted) is matched to the question being asked (manager skill versus investor experience) and disclosed.
- [ ] Cash-flow effects are neutralized (for manager evaluation via TWR) or disclosed (for investor experience via MWR), and the return gap between them is acknowledged.
- [ ] Returns are presented both gross and net of fees, with transaction costs and (for taxable investors) taxes disclosed.
- [ ] The benchmark is representative, investable, consistently applied, and stable, with any changes disclosed and justified.
- [ ] Survivorship, selection, backfill, and inception-date biases are guarded against, with composites including all qualifying discretionary accounts.
- [ ] Annualization is applied honestly, with short records flagged and the range of returns shown alongside any average.
- [ ] The calculation methodology, cash-flow and fee treatment, benchmark basis, composite definition, and period coverage are fully disclosed.
- [ ] The recommendation states that past performance does not guarantee future results, that presentation standards reduce but do not eliminate bias, that returns are sensitive to method and period, and that this is not investment advice and professional performance-measurement or compliance expertise may be warranted for formal or regulated presentations.
