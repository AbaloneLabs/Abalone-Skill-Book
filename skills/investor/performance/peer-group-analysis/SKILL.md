# Peer Group Analysis

Peer group analysis compares a fund or manager's performance, risk, and characteristics against a universe of similar managers to judge whether the results are good or bad relative to alternatives an investor could have chosen. A percentile ranking, a quartile, or an average provides the context that absolute returns lack: a 10% return is excellent in one peer group and poor in another. The judgment problem is that peer-group analysis is only as valid as the peer universe, and universes are routinely biased, mismatched, or manipulated to flatter the subject. Agents tend to accept a provided peer group without scrutinizing its construction, ignore the survivorship and classification biases that make averages look better than reality, and compare a manager against weak or unlike peers to make a mediocre result look strong. The skill is constructing and interpreting peer groups with discipline, recognizing their biases, and never letting a flattering ranking substitute for genuine analysis.

This skill is for building and reading peer-group comparisons with awareness of their biases and limits.

## Core Rules

### Construct A Genuinely Comparable Peer Universe

The validity of peer analysis depends entirely on whether the peers are truly comparable. A mismatched universe makes any ranking meaningless.

Define peers by:

- the same strategy or style (large-cap value, high-yield bond, market-neutral equity);
- similar mandate, market cap, and geographic focus;
- similar risk profile and use of leverage;
- similar vehicle structure and fee level where relevant;
- consistent classification that does not drift over time.

A large-cap growth fund compared against a blend of all equity funds, or a leveraged strategy compared against unleveraged ones, is not a fair comparison. The peer universe must match the strategy's actual risk and style, or the ranking is decoration.

### Remove Survivorship And Classification Bias

Peer universes suffer from systematic biases that flatter the average and distort rankings. The two most damaging are survivorship and classification bias.

Address:

- survivorship bias: peer universes typically include only surviving funds, excluding those that closed or merged due to poor performance, which inflates the average by 1-3% per year or more;
- classification bias: funds are assigned to categories that may not reflect their actual holdings, and managers may self-select flattering categories;
- backfill bias: strong track records are added to the universe only after they prove successful;
- the fix: use universes that include defunct funds, verify classifications against holdings, and prefer independent classification over self-selected labels.

A peer average built on survivors overstates the achievable return and makes an average manager look worse than they are relative to the true opportunity set. Always ask whether the universe includes the dead and the reclassified.

### Interpret Percentiles And Quartiles With Context

Percentile rankings and quartiles are the common currency of peer comparison, but they are easy to misread without context.

Read rankings carefully:

- a top-quartile ranking over one year is weak evidence; over a full cycle across many peers it is meaningful;
- percentile rankings are sensitive to the size and quality of the universe (a top decile in a weak universe is less impressive than it sounds);
- rankings shift with the period chosen (a manager can be top quartile over three years and bottom quartile over five);
- consistency of ranking across periods matters more than a single strong period.

Present rankings over multiple periods (one, three, five, ten years and since inception) and note the consistency. A manager strong only in the period that flatters them is suspect.

### Compare On Risk And Consistency, Not Only Return

Return rankings without risk are incomplete. A manager can rank high on return by taking more risk than peers, which is not skill but risk-taking.

Compare across:

- risk-adjusted measures (Sharpe, information ratio, Sortino) alongside raw return;
- volatility, drawdown, and downside capture relative to peers;
- consistency of outperformance (hit rate, tracking error);
- the risk taken to achieve the ranking (beta, leverage, factor exposures).

A top-return manager who took twice the peer-group risk may be a poor risk-adjusted choice. Peer comparison must control for risk to distinguish skill from risk-taking.

### Check For Style Drift And Peer Migration

Managers drift over time, and their appropriate peer group changes with them. Comparing a manager against peers they no longer resemble distorts the ranking.

Examine:

- whether the manager's style, holdings, and risk profile have been stable or have drifted;
- whether the manager has been reclassified into a different (often easier) peer category over time;
- whether strong periods came from a style the manager no longer runs;
- the consistency of the peer universe itself over the comparison period.

A manager who drifted from small-cap growth to large-cap blend and is ranked against their old small-cap peer group is being compared against the wrong universe. Verify that both the manager and the peers have stayed true to the category.

### Use Peer Analysis As One Input, Not The Verdict

Peer rankings are useful context but they are not the whole analysis. A manager can rank well and still be a poor choice, or rank poorly and still be attractive.

Supplement peer analysis with:

- absolute return and the investor's own objectives (a top-quartile manager in a bad peer group may still not meet goals);
- the sustainability of the strategy, process, and team;
- fees, capacity, tax efficiency, and operational quality;
- forward-looking factors (valuation, positioning, factor exposures) that peer rankings ignore.

Peer analysis answers "how does this manager compare to alternatives?" It does not answer "is this a good investment for this investor?" Use it as one input among several, and never let a flattering ranking override fundamental analysis.

### Recognize The Gaming Of Peer Comparisons

Managers and marketers actively manipulate peer comparisons to look good, and the analyst must spot the manipulation.

Watch for:

- choosing a weak or mismatched peer group to improve the ranking;
- selecting the period that flatters (the strong three-year window rather than the weak five-year);
- presenting gross-of-fees returns against net-of-fees peers;
- comparing against an index or average rather than a true investable peer set;
- highlighting a single strong sub-strategy while hiding weaker ones.

Any peer comparison that is suspiciously favorable should be re-examined with a clean, independent universe and consistent methodology. The incentive to flatter is strong, and the techniques are subtle.

## Common Traps

### Accepting A Provided Peer Group Without Scrutiny

Peer universes are often biased, mismatched, or manipulated. The universe must be independently verified as genuinely comparable.

### Ignoring Survivorship Bias

Peer averages built on survivors overstate achievable returns by 1-3% or more. Universes should include defunct funds.

### Comparing Against Unlike Or Weak Peers

A mismatched or weak peer group makes any manager look good. Peers must match the strategy's risk and style.

### Ranking On Return Without Risk

A high return ranking achieved through higher risk is risk-taking, not skill. Compare on risk-adjusted measures.

### Cherry-Picking The Flattering Period

Rankings shift with the period chosen. A manager strong only in the period that flatters them is suspect; show multiple periods.

### Missing Style Drift

A manager who drifted to a different style, ranked against their old peers, is compared against the wrong universe. Verify consistency.

### Treating Peer Ranking As The Verdict

Peer analysis is context, not the whole decision. A well-ranked manager can be a poor choice for the investor's objectives.

### Falling For Manipulated Comparisons

Managers choose flattering universes, periods, and gross/net treatments. Re-examine suspiciously favorable comparisons independently.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The peer universe is constructed to be genuinely comparable (same strategy, style, risk, leverage, geography, structure), not accepted as provided.
- [ ] Survivorship, classification, and backfill biases are addressed, with preference for universes that include defunct funds and independently verified classifications.
- [ ] Percentile and quartile rankings are interpreted over multiple periods with attention to consistency, universe size and quality, and the period chosen.
- [ ] Comparison includes risk-adjusted measures, volatility, drawdown, and consistency, not only raw return, to distinguish skill from risk-taking.
- [ ] Style drift and peer migration are checked, ensuring the manager and the peers have remained true to the category over the comparison period.
- [ ] Peer analysis is used as one input alongside absolute return, objectives, process, fees, capacity, and forward-looking factors, not as the sole verdict.
- [ ] Manipulation of peer comparisons (weak universes, flattering periods, gross/net inconsistency) is actively checked, and suspiciously favorable rankings are re-examined independently.
- [ ] The recommendation states that peer rankings are sensitive to universe construction and period, that survivorship and classification bias affect all peer data, that past relative performance does not predict future results, and that this is not investment advice and professional performance-analysis expertise may be warranted for formal evaluations.
