# Bull And Bear Cycles

Bull and bear markets are not single states. They are multi-phase cycles driven by earnings, liquidity, valuation, leverage, and sentiment, and each phase rewards different strategies. An investing agent frequently labels the market "bullish" or "bearish" based on recent price action, then extrapolates. This ignores that bull markets end at valuation and leverage extremes, bear markets end at capitulation and despair, and the transition between them is where most capital is lost or made.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "are we in a bull or bear market", "how do I know when a bull market is ending", "what signals a market bottom", or "how should I invest differently across market phases". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating a label as a forecast and to force it to locate the current phase, the drivers, the embedded expectations, and the evidence that would confirm or refute a transition.

Cycle identification is probabilistic, not deterministic. Acting on it carries real risk of loss, and being early is indistinguishable from being wrong for long periods. Conclusions should disclose uncertainty and investor-specific constraints.

## Core Rules

### Locate The Phase, Do Not Just Label The Market

Bull and bear markets move through recognizable phases, and the right strategy depends on where in the cycle the market sits.

Common bull-market phases:

- Accumulation or despair: prices cheap, sentiment negative, smart money rebuilding positions after capitulation.
- Participation or early bull: improving fundamentals, earnings revisions turning up, broadening participation.
- Exuberance or blow-off: valuations stretched, leverage rising, retail frenzy, narratives justifying extreme prices.

Common bear-market phases:

- Distribution or topping: leadership narrows, valuations rich, leverage builds, early warning signs from credit or breadth.
- Panic or decline: earnings disappoint, forced selling, sentiment collapses, liquidity drains.
- Capitulation or despair: maximum pessimism, distressed valuations, volume spikes, sellers exhausted.

The same index level can mean very different things in accumulation versus blow-off. Locate the phase using multiple signals, not price alone.

### Separate Drivers: Earnings, Liquidity, Valuation, Sentiment

Markets move for different reasons in different phases. Identify the dominant driver.

Ask:

- Is the bull driven by earnings growth, multiple expansion, liquidity, or speculation?
- Is the bear driven by earnings recession, multiple compression, de-leveraging, or policy tightening?
- Are earnings revisions rising or falling, and is breadth improving or narrowing?
- Is liquidity expanding or contracting from central banks, credit growth, or fund flows?
- Is valuation a tailwind or a headwind at current levels?

A liquidity-driven bull can continue with weak fundamentals, and an earnings-driven bull can survive tighter policy. Knowing the driver tells you what would break the cycle.

### Use Valuation As A Gravity Force, Not A Timer

Valuation is a poor short-term timer but a strong long-term anchor. Expensive markets can get more expensive for years, and cheap markets can get cheaper, but starting valuation is one of the best predictors of long-term returns.

Apply valuation carefully:

- Compare to history, but adjust for the regime, interest rates, profit margins, and sector composition.
- Use multiple metrics, since P/E, CAPE, market-cap-to-GDP, and equity risk premium tell different stories.
- Distinguish headline index valuation from the median stock, since a few mega-caps can distort the index.
- Recognize that high valuations raise the risk of a bad long-term outcome but do not predict the timing of a top.

Do not declare a top because valuations are high, and do not call a bottom because valuations are low. Use valuation to set return expectations and risk budgets.

### Track Leverage And Forced Selling

Bull markets often end when leverage becomes fragile, and bear markets often end when forced selling is exhausted. Leverage accelerates moves in both directions.

Watch:

- margin debt and broker leverage;
- hedge fund gross and net exposure;
- derivatives positioning and dealer gamma;
- corporate leverage and debt maturity walls;
- fund redemption pressure and liquidity mismatches;
- collateral chains and repo stress.

De-leveraging is non-linear and can turn a moderate decline into a crash. The absence of leverage makes a market more resilient; its presence makes turning points sharper.

### Read Sentiment And Positioning For Extremes

Sentiment is a contrarian indicator mainly at extremes. Moderate optimism or pessimism is not actionable.

Useful sentiment inputs:

- investor surveys and allocation polls at multi-year extremes;
- fund flow data showing capitulation or euphoric inflows;
- options positioning, put-call ratios, and volatility term structure;
- media and retail-frenzy intensity;
- insider activity and corporate buyback patterns;
- IPO volume and quality as a risk-appetite gauge.

Extreme bearishness near capitulation can mark a bottom, and extreme bullishness near a top can mark the end of a bull. In the middle of a trend, sentiment is noise.

### Look For Breadth And Leadership Confirmation

Healthy bull markets have broad participation. Unhealthy late-stage bulls are carried by a shrinking group of leaders.

Check:

- advance-decline lines and percentage of stocks above moving averages;
- equal-weight versus cap-weight performance;
- sector and factor breadth;
- new highs versus new lows;
- small-cap and cyclical participation.

Narrow leadership, where a handful of mega-caps drive the index while most stocks decline, is a classic late-bull warning, though it can persist longer than expected.

### Respect That Cycles Rhyme But Do Not Repeat

Historical parallels are useful for pattern recognition but dangerous when applied mechanically. Each cycle has unique drivers, policy responses, structures, and narratives.

Ask:

- Which historical episode rhymes, and which structural differences break the analogy?
- Is the policy reaction function the same or different?
- Has market structure changed, such as ETF dominance, algorithmic trading, or zero-commission retail?
- Are the valuations, leverage, and inflation regimes comparable?

Use history to generate hypotheses and humility, not to forecast precise outcomes.

## Common Traps

### Calling A Top Or Bottom From A Single Indicator

No single signal, valuation, sentiment, or breadth, reliably times cycle turns. Tops and bottoms are processes confirmed by multiple signals diverging or aligning.

### Extrapolating Recent Price Action

Assuming a rising market will keep rising, or a falling market will keep falling, is the most common cycle error. Price action reflects current expectations, not the future path.

### Treating Sentiment As Always Contrarian

Sentiment is contrarian only at extremes. In the middle of a trend, optimism or pessimism can persist and confirm the move.

### Ignoring The Driver Of The Move

A bull driven by multiple expansion is more fragile than one driven by earnings. A bear driven by policy can end when policy turns, while one driven by balance-sheet repair takes longer.

### Using Historical Parallels Mechanically

"The 1970s", "the dot-com bust", or "2008" are templates, not scripts. Structural differences in rates, leverage, policy, and market structure can break the analogy.

### Confusing Cyclical With Secular

A cyclical bear within a secular bull can be a buying opportunity, while a cyclical bounce within a secular bear can be a trap. Distinguish the time frame of the cycle.

### Overconfidence In Timing

Even correct cycle identification can be early by months or years. Position sizing and time horizon must tolerate being early.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The current cycle phase is located using multiple signals, not labeled from price alone.
- [ ] The dominant driver, earnings, liquidity, valuation, or sentiment, is identified.
- [ ] Valuation is used to set return expectations and risk, not to time exact tops or bottoms.
- [ ] Leverage, derivatives positioning, and forced-selling risk are assessed for fragility.
- [ ] Sentiment and positioning are interpreted for extremes, not treated as always contrarian.
- [ ] Breadth and leadership are checked for confirmation or divergence from the index.
- [ ] Historical parallels are used with attention to structural differences, not mechanically.
- [ ] The time frame, cyclical versus secular, is explicit.
- [ ] The analysis acknowledges that timing is uncertain and being early can feel like being wrong.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids predicting a precise turning point and flags investor horizon, risk tolerance, and professional advice.
