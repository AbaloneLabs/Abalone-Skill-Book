# Insider Activity

Insider transactions, purchases and sales of a company's own stock by its executives, directors, and large shareholders, are one of the few data points grounded in the actions of people who know the business intimately and are putting their own money behind their view. Because of this, insider activity is widely cited as a signal. The problem is that it is also one of the most misread signals in investing. Agents routinely treat any insider purchase as bullish conviction and any sale as a warning, when in reality the vast majority of insider selling is driven by diversification, liquidity, tax, and compensation mechanics that say nothing about the company's prospects, while the most meaningful purchases are clustered, opportunistic, and made on the open market by insiders with no obligation to buy. Reading insider activity well means separating signal from noise, weighting transactions by who bought, how, how much, and in what context.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "what does this insider buying mean", "should I follow insider sales", "is insider selling a bad sign", or "how do I interpret executive stock transactions". The goal is to prevent the agent from mechanically mapping buys to bullish and sells to bearish, to force it to distinguish open-market from compensation-driven transactions, to assess the significance of cluster and pattern, and to weigh insider activity alongside, not instead of, fundamental and valuation analysis.

Insider activity is a secondary signal, not a strategy. Conclusions should be framed as one input among many and should reflect the investor's own objectives and risk tolerance.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Open-Market Purchases From Compensation-Driven Selling

The single most important distinction in insider analysis is the asymmetry between buying and selling. Insiders buy stock for essentially one reason, they expect it to go up, because there is almost never a non-conviction reason to deploy personal cash into the employer's shares. Insiders sell for many reasons, diversification, buying a house, paying taxes on vested stock, estate planning, and routine 10b5-1 plan liquidation, most of which carry no negative information.

Apply this asymmetry:

- Weight open-market, open-market purchases far more heavily than sales. A purchase is a stronger and rarer signal than a sale.
- Discount routine selling under pre-arranged 10b5-1 plans, which is scheduled and designed to avoid information advantages.
- Discount sales that immediately follow option exercises or vesting events, which are often tax or diversification driven.
- Treat large, unprompted, open-market sales by an insider with no history of selling more cautiously, though still less bearishly than a purchase is bullish.

An insider purchase, especially by a CEO or director using personal funds on the open market, is among the more credible management signals. A sale is usually noise unless it breaks a clear pattern.

### Weight Transactions By Insider Role And Information Access

Not all insiders have equal insight. The significance of a transaction depends heavily on who made it and what they are positioned to know.

Rank by information quality:

- C-suite and operating executives, CEO, CFO, COO, division heads, have the deepest view of current performance and near-term outlook.
- Directors, especially independent directors with financial or industry expertise, often buy to signal confidence or value.
- Founders and large shareholders, their transactions may reflect conviction, but also estate, control, or tax planning.
- General counsel, audit committee members, and accounting officers, their transactions can signal regulatory, legal, or accounting concerns.

A purchase by the CFO, who sees the cash flow and the books, may carry different information than a purchase by a non-executive director. Cluster buying, multiple insiders buying around the same time, is generally a stronger signal than a single purchase because it suggests broad, independent conviction.

### Assess Size Relative To Insider Wealth And Float

The dollar amount of a transaction matters, but its meaning depends on context. A $500,000 purchase is significant for an insider of modest means and trivial for a billionaire founder.

Normalize by:

- The transaction size relative to the insider's existing holdings and likely net worth, a purchase that materially increases their exposure is more meaningful than a token buy.
- The transaction relative to the company's trading float and market cap, very large purchases can themselves move the stock.
- Whether the insider is buying with new personal cash or exercising options and holding the shares, the latter is weaker than an outright open-market purchase.
- The frequency, a one-time purchase is a data point, a pattern of repeated purchases is a trend.

The most powerful insider signal is a large open-market purchase that meaningfully increases an already-significant holding, funded with personal cash, by an insider with deep operational knowledge.

### Account For 10b5-1 Plans And Pre-Arranged Trading

Many insider transactions occur under Rule 10b5-1 trading plans, which are pre-arranged schedules adopted when the insider claims to have no material non-public information. These plans exist precisely to allow insiders to trade without the appearance of using an information advantage.

Implications:

- Transactions under 10b5-1 plans should be heavily discounted as informational signals, they were scheduled in advance.
- However, the adoption, modification, or termination of a 10b5-1 plan can itself be informative, especially if done near significant events.
- Some plans are more flexible than others, and aggressive amendment patterns have drawn regulatory scrutiny.
- The presence of a plan does not make selling benign if the plan was adopted at a suspicious time or covers an unusually large amount.

Always check whether a transaction was plan-driven before interpreting it as a conviction signal.

### Consider Timing, Catalysts, And Context

Insider activity rarely exists in a vacuum. The context around a transaction often determines its meaning.

Ask:

- Is the purchase happening after a sharp price decline, suggesting the insider sees value in the dislocation?
- Is the purchase happening ahead of a known catalyst, earnings, a product launch, a regulatory decision, which may carry more information?
- Are purchases clustered across multiple insiders near a trough, a classic value signal?
- Is selling accelerating into strength after a long run, which can be routine profit-taking or an early warning?
- Are insiders buying while the company is buying back stock, reinforcing management confidence, or selling while the company buys back, a potential red flag?

Context turns a raw transaction into an interpretable signal. The same purchase after a 50% drop means something different than the same purchase at an all-time high.

### Use Insider Activity As Confirmation, Not Replacement

Insider activity is a secondary input. It can confirm or challenge a thesis built on fundamentals and valuation, but it should not generate a thesis on its own. Insiders can be wrong, can have horizons that differ from the investor's, and can be buying for reasons unrelated to the factors driving long-term value.

Combine insider signals with:

- Valuation, is the stock cheap or expensive regardless of who is buying?
- Business quality and competitive position.
- Balance sheet and capital allocation.
- The investor's own time horizon and risk tolerance.

Insider buying in a cheap, high-quality business is reinforcing. Insider buying in an expensive, deteriorating business is a weaker signal that may reflect insider optimism the fundamentals do not support.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Insider Selling As Bearish

Most insider selling is diversification, tax, or compensation driven and carries little negative information. Routine and plan-driven sales should be heavily discounted.

### Treating Option Exercise And Sale As A Negative Signal

Selling shares immediately after exercising options is often a tax-neutral or diversification move. The relevant signal is the net change in exposure, not the gross sale.

### Ignoring 10b5-1 Plan Transactions

Pre-arranged plan transactions are designed to remove information advantage and should not be read as conviction. Always check the plan status.

### Overweighting A Single Small Purchase

A token purchase, the minimum required, or a purchase tiny relative to insider wealth is a weak signal. Significance scales with size and wealth context.

### Assuming Insiders Are Always Right

Insiders have superior information but can still be wrong, have different horizons, or be buying for reasons unrelated to long-term value. Insider activity confirms a thesis, it does not replace one.

### Ignoring Cluster And Pattern

A single purchase is a data point; multiple insiders buying around the same time, or a pattern of repeated buying, is a much stronger signal. Isolated transactions are easily misread.

### Forgetting Legal And Window Restrictions

Insiders are restricted from trading on material non-public information and often limited to trading windows. The absence of buying is not necessarily bearish; insiders may simply be unable to trade.
