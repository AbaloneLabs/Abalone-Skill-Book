# Fund Structure And Terms

Real estate funds are pooled vehicles where limited partners (LPs) supply capital and a general partner (GP) makes investment decisions. The legal and economic terms — fees, carried interest, hurdle, term, call mechanics — determine how returns are split between the GP and the LPs. These terms are not boilerplate; they materially change the LP's net return and the GP's incentives. A fund with an attractive gross track record can deliver mediocre net returns because of aggressive fees, a high hurdle-free carry, or misaligned capital call timing. Reading the term sheet as a formality, rather than as the core economic contract, is one of the most expensive mistakes in private real estate.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "are these fund terms fair", "what is a good hurdle rate", "how does carried interest work", or "should I invest in this real estate fund". The goal is to prevent the agent from focusing on strategy and track record while glossing over the terms that determine how much of the return the LP actually keeps.

## Core Rules

### Understand The LP/GP Split And What Each Side Controls

The fund structure separates capital from control:

- Limited partners (LPs): provide most of the capital (often 90-95%), have limited liability, and generally do not participate in day-to-day decisions. They rely on the partnership agreement and the GP's fiduciary duty.
- General partner (GP / sponsor): manages the fund, sources deals, makes investment and exit decisions, and bears unlimited liability (often through a special GP entity). The GP typically contributes a small amount of capital (1-5%) to maintain alignment.
- Key-person provisions: if named key people leave or reduce involvement, LPs may have removal or suspension rights. These protect against the "ghost GP" where the brand remains but the talent leaves.

The LP's money is locked up and controlled by the GP for years. The terms govern that relationship. Read them as the operating constitution of the investment.

### Analyze The Full Fee Stack, Not Just The Headline Management Fee

Funds charge multiple layers of fees that compound:

- Management fee: typically 1-2% of committed capital (or invested capital) annually, regardless of performance. Note the base — committed versus invested — because committed-capital fees accrue even before capital is deployed.
- Acquisition fees: charged when the fund buys assets (often 1-2% of purchase price).
- Disposition fees: charged when the fund sells assets.
- Financing and refinancing fees: sometimes charged on debt placed.
- Asset management and construction management fees: ongoing or project-level fees.
- Fund expenses: legal, audit, administration passed through to the fund (and thus to LPs).

The headline management fee can be a fraction of total fees. Sum all fee layers and compute their impact on net return. A 2% management fee plus 2% acquisition plus asset management can consume a large share of the gross return.

### Model Carried Interest, Hurdle, And Catch-Up Carefully

Carried interest (carry) is the GP's share of profits, typically 20%, but the mechanics vary enormously:

- Hurdle rate (preferred return): the return LPs receive before the GP earns carry, often 8%. A higher hurdle favors LPs; a 0% hurdle lets the GP share profits from dollar one.
- Catch-up: after the hurdle is met, how quickly the GP "catches up" to its full carry percentage. A 100% catch-up means the GP takes all profits until the carry split is reached; this significantly reduces the LP's share of profits in the catch-up zone.
- Clawback: if the GP receives more carry than entitled over the fund's life (due to early wins then later losses), the clawback requires repayment. A strong, GP-funded clawback protects LPs; a weak or uncapped clawback leaves LPs exposed.
- Deal-by-deal versus whole-fund carry: deal-by-deal carry pays the GP on each profitable deal even if others lose; whole-fund carry computes carry only on aggregate fund profit. Whole-fund is more LP-favorable.

Compute net-of-fee, net-of-carry returns under realistic scenarios. The difference between a deal-by-deal carry with 100% catch-up and a whole-fund carry with no catch-up can be several percentage points of LP return.

### Assess Fund Term, Extensions, And Liquidation Mechanics

The fund term governs how long capital is locked:

- Initial term: typically 7-10 years for real estate (longer than buyout PE due to asset liquidity).
- Extensions: GP-discretionary extensions (often 1-2 years) can extend the lockup; LP-consent extensions are more LP-favorable.
- Harvest/liquidation period: the later years dedicated to selling assets and returning capital.
- Wind-down: how remaining hard-to-sell assets are handled (in-kind distributions, continuation vehicles, extensions).

A fund that cannot exit its assets at term can extend or distribute illiquid assets in specie, leaving LPs with unwanted direct holdings. Check the term, extension triggers, and liquidation mechanics.

### Understand Capital Calls, Deployment Pace, And Dry Powder

LPs commit capital but do not fund it all upfront; the GP "calls" it as deals are found:

- Capital call mechanics: LPs must fund calls on notice (often 10-14 days); failure to fund is a default with severe penalties (forfeiture, dilution, damages).
- Deployment pace: slow deployment leaves capital sitting in low-yield reserves while management fees accrue on commitments; fast deployment can signal discipline or rushed deals.
- Dry powder and vintage risk: large undeployed commitments create uncertainty about which vintage the LP is actually buying.
- Over-commitment: LPs often commit more than their liquid capital to manage deployment drag, but this creates funding risk if calls cluster.

Model the call schedule and the LP's liquidity to meet calls. A commitment is an obligation, not an option.

### Check Alignment, GP Commit, And Conflicts

Alignment between GP and LP determines whether incentives push the GP to act in LPs' interest:

- GP commitment (co-invest): the GP's own capital at risk, typically 1-5%; higher is better alignment.
- Fee offsets: management fees offset against carry, or vice versa, prevent double-charging.
- Concurrent funds: if the GP runs multiple funds in the same strategy, deal allocation conflicts arise (which fund gets which deal?).
- Related-party transactions: acquisitions from, or sales to, affiliate entities must be scrutinized and often require LP consent or independent valuation.
- Side letters: some LPs negotiate preferential terms (fee discounts, information, most-favored-nation) that disadvantage others.

Strong alignment is not just the GP commit percentage; it includes how the GP handles conflicts, deal allocation, and related-party deals. Read the conflict provisions.

### Evaluate Reporting, Valuation, And Governance

Private real estate is opaque; reporting and governance are the LP's window:

- Valuation frequency and methodology: quarterly NAV with appraised values; appraisals lag and smooth returns (see NAV skill).
- Reporting detail: property-level performance, lease rollover, capex, leverage, and realized versus unrealized gains.
- LP advisory committee (LPAC): reviews conflicts, related-party deals, key-person events, and extensions; its powers vary.
- Audit and administration: independent audit, fund administrator, custodian.

Strong governance and transparent reporting compensate somewhat for illiquidity. Weak reporting leaves the LP blind to deterioration until it is too late.

## Common Traps

### Focusing On Gross Track Record, Ignoring Net-Of-Fee Terms

A great gross track record can deliver poor net returns with aggressive fees and carry. Always model net-of-fee, net-of-carry returns.

### Accepting Deal-By-Deal Carry With Full Catch-Up

Deal-by-deal carry pays the GP on winners while losers reduce LP returns; full catch-up further skews profits to the GP. Whole-fund carry with limited catch-up is more LP-favorable.

### Underestimating The Full Fee Stack

Management fee is one layer. Acquisition, disposition, financing, asset management, and expense pass-throughs compound. Sum them.

### Overlooking GP-Discretionary Term Extensions

Extensions can lock capital up years beyond the stated term if the GP cannot exit. Check who controls extensions.

### Ignoring Capital Call Funding Risk

Commitments are obligations. LPs must hold liquid capital to meet calls; clustering or unexpected calls can force distressed sales of other assets.

### Missing Conflict And Side-Letter Provisions

Concurrent funds, related-party deals, and side letters can transfer value away from standard LPs. Read the conflict provisions and ask about side letters.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The LP/GP split, control, key-person provisions, and LP rights are understood as the operating constitution of the fund.
- [ ] The full fee stack (management, acquisition, disposition, financing, asset management, expenses) is summed and its net-return impact computed.
- [ ] Carry mechanics (hurdle, catch-up, clawback, deal-by-deal versus whole-fund) are modeled, and net-of-carry returns are computed under scenarios.
- [ ] Fund term, extensions, harvest, and liquidation mechanics are checked, including who controls extensions.
- [ ] Capital call mechanics, deployment pace, dry powder, and the LP's funding liquidity are modeled.
- [ ] GP commitment, fee offsets, concurrent funds, related-party transactions, and side letters are assessed for alignment and conflicts.
- [ ] Reporting, valuation methodology, LPAC powers, and audit/administration are evaluated for transparency and governance.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a fund on strategy alone and references the net-of-fee terms, investor-specific liquidity, and risk tolerance.
