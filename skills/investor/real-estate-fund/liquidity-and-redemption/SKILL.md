# Liquidity And Redemption

Many real estate funds are marketed as offering periodic liquidity — open-end (evergreen) funds, non-traded REITs, and interval funds quote NAV and allow redemptions. But the underlying assets are illiquid properties that take months to sell. This creates a fundamental liquidity mismatch: when many investors redeem at once, the fund cannot sell properties fast enough, and redemption mechanisms (gates, suspensions, side pockets) activate to protect the fund — at the cost of trapping the investor. Treating a redemption right as equivalent to liquidity, without understanding the gates and the mismatch, leads to being locked in exactly when exit matters most.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "can I get my money out of this fund", "how liquid are non-traded REITs", "what is a redemption gate", or "what happens if everyone redeems". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating periodic redemption rights as daily liquidity, and from ignoring the valuation, gate, and suspension mechanics that determine actual access to capital.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Open-End, Closed-End, Interval, And Non-Traded REIT Structures

Liquidity differs fundamentally by structure:

- Open-end (evergreen) funds: offer subscriptions and redemptions at NAV, typically monthly or quarterly, subject to limits and gates. The promise of liquidity is conditional.
- Closed-end funds: capital is locked for the fund term (7-10+ years); no periodic redemption. Liquidity only at fund wind-down or via secondary transfer.
- Interval funds: offer periodic repurchase offers (e.g., quarterly) for a limited percentage of NAV (e.g., 5%, with caps at 5/15/25% over quarters/years). Limited, conditional liquidity.
- Non-traded REITs: historically illiquid with long holding periods; newer versions offer limited repurchase programs (often quarterly, capped at a % of NAV, with discounts to NAV and suspensions permitted).

Identify the structure. "Redemptions allowed" means very different things in an open-end fund, an interval fund, and a non-traded REIT.

### Read The Redemption Mechanics: Notice, Caps, Gates, Suspensions

Redemption is governed by detailed mechanics that can block exit:

- Notice period: redemptions must be requested 30-120 days in advance; the investor is committed during the notice.
- Repurchase caps: per-quarter limits (e.g., 5% of NAV) mean large or clustered redemptions are prorated.
- Gates: if aggregate redemptions exceed a threshold (e.g., quarterly or annual), the fund can gate — limiting or halting redemptions to protect remaining investors.
- Suspensions: in extraordinary circumstances (market disruption, inability to value assets, liquidity crisis), the fund can suspend redemptions entirely, often at board discretion.
- Redemption fees and NAV discounts: some programs charge fees or redeem at a discount to NAV to discourage exit and reflect liquidity cost.

These mechanics exist precisely because the assets are illiquid. When they activate, the investor cannot exit regardless of the headline "redemption right." Read the offering documents' liquidity section in full.

### Understand The Liquidity Mismatch And Its Consequences

Open-end and interval funds hold illiquid properties but promise periodic redemptions. The mismatch creates structural fragility:

- Normal times: redemption requests are small and met from cash or credit lines; the mismatch is invisible.
- Stress times: redemptions cluster as investors flee; the fund cannot sell properties quickly without fire-sale prices; gates and suspensions activate.
- First-mover advantage: investors who redeem early get full NAV; later redeemers face gates, suspensions, or NAV marks that have not yet reflected forced sales. This creates a run dynamic.

The mismatch means redemption rights are best-case liquidity. In the scenarios where the investor most needs to exit (market stress, fund distress), the rights are most likely to be restricted. Plan for the restriction, not the headline.

### Assess Valuation And NAV-Based Redemption Pricing

Redemptions are priced at NAV, but NAV for illiquid real estate is appraisal-based and lags:

- Appraisal lag: property appraisals are quarterly or annual and smoothed; in a falling market, NAV overstates true value, so redeemers exit at an optimistic price (paid by remaining investors).
- Stale marks: NAV may not reflect current transaction evidence, especially in fast-moving markets.
- Fair-value adjustments: some funds apply liquidity discounts or fair-value haircuts during stress, but this is discretionary and varies.
- In-kind distributions: some funds can satisfy redemptions by distributing the underlying illiquid assets in specie, leaving the redeemer with direct property holdings.

A redemption at stale NAV can transfer value from remaining investors to redeemers (or vice versa). Understand the valuation policy and how it handles stress.

### Evaluate The Fund's Liquidity Management Tools

Funds manage the mismatch with tools that affect the investor:

- Cash and credit lines: buffer for normal redemptions; limited capacity for stress.
- Asset sales: selling properties to meet redemptions, but sales take months and fire-sale pricing harms remaining investors.
- Subscription pacing: slowing or halting new subscriptions when outflows dominate.
- Gates and suspensions: the ultimate backstop, blocking exit.
- Tender offers and continuation vehicles: occasional mechanisms to provide partial liquidity.

Assess the fund's stated liquidity management approach and its capacity. A fund relying on a small credit line to meet redemptions against a multi-billion illiquid portfolio has limited real liquidity.

### Consider Secondary Markets And Transferability

For closed-end funds and some open-end structures, secondary transfer may be the only exit:

- Secondary marketplaces: exist for private real estate fund interests, but discounts to NAV are common (10-30%+ in normal times, wider in stress).
- GP consent: transfers often require GP consent and the buyer must meet eligibility.
- Liquidity and timing: secondary sales take months and involve due diligence.
- Tax and structural constraints: some interests are difficult to transfer due to tax or fund restrictions.

Secondary liquidity is real but costly and slow. Do not assume a liquid secondary market; assume a discount and a delay.

### Match The Investment To The Investor's Liquidity Horizon

The central suitability question is whether the investor can tolerate the fund's actual liquidity profile:

- Capital that may be needed for emergencies, near-term spending, or rebalancing should not go into illiquid real estate funds.
- The investor must be able to hold through gates, suspensions, and the full fund term.
- "I can redeem if I need to" is a dangerous assumption; the redemption right is conditional and most restricted when most needed.

Illiquid real estate funds are suitable only for capital the investor can genuinely lock up for the full horizon, with no dependence on the redemption right in stress.

## Common Traps

### Treating Periodic Redemption Rights As Daily Liquidity

Redemption rights are conditional, capped, gated, and suspendable. They are not equivalent to liquid, daily-access capital.

### Ignoring Gates And Suspensions In Stress

Gates and suspensions activate precisely when redemptions cluster. The investor is most likely to be blocked when most in need of exit.

### Redeeming At Stale NAV In A Falling Market

Appraisal-based NAV lags; redeemers may exit at an optimistic price, or remaining investors may bear the lag. Understand the valuation policy.

### Assuming The Fund Can Sell Properties To Meet Redemptions

Property sales take months and harm remaining investors via fire-sale pricing. Funds gate rather than fire-sell.

### Overlooking The First-Mover Run Dynamic

Early redeemers get full NAV; later ones face restrictions. This creates a run incentive that can force the fund into crisis.

### Treating Secondary Transfer As Easy Liquidity

Secondary markets exist but at discounts, with delays and GP consent. It is costly, slow exit, not liquid access.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The fund structure (open-end, closed-end, interval, non-traded REIT) is identified, and the actual liquidity profile matches it.
- [ ] Redemption mechanics (notice, caps, gates, suspensions, fees, NAV discounts) are read from the offering documents.
- [ ] The liquidity mismatch between periodic redemption and illiquid holdings is understood, including the run dynamic and first-mover advantage.
- [ ] Valuation and NAV-based redemption pricing, including appraisal lag and in-kind distribution risk, are assessed.
- [ ] The fund's liquidity management tools (cash, credit lines, asset sales, gates) and their capacity are evaluated.
- [ ] Secondary market transferability, discounts, and GP consent are considered as a costly, slow fallback.
- [ ] The investment is matched to capital the investor can genuinely lock up for the full horizon, with no dependence on redemption in stress.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting redemption rights as liquidity and references investor-specific liquidity needs, horizon, and risk tolerance.
