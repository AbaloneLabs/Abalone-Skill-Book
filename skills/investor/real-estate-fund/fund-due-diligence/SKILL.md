# Fund Due Diligence

Real estate fund due diligence is the process of deciding whether to entrust locked-up capital to a sponsor for years. Unlike public securities, where mistakes can be reversed by selling, a private real estate fund commitment is a multi-year obligation with limited exit. The diligence must therefore cover not just the strategy and the track record, but the team, the portfolio the fund will build, the leverage and liquidity risks, the geographic and environmental exposure, and the operational backbone. Superficial diligence — reading the marketing deck and checking the gross IRR — is how LPs end up in funds whose returns were driven by a rising market rather than skill, or whose portfolios hide environmental and concentration risks.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do I evaluate a real estate fund", "is this sponsor any good", "what should I check before committing", or "what due diligence is needed for a private real estate fund". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating fund selection as a track-record-and-fee exercise, and from missing the operational, portfolio, and risk dimensions that determine outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Decompose The Track Record Into Skill, Market, And Luck

A sponsor's track record is the most-cited evidence, but it is also the most easily misread:

- Market versus skill: returns during a falling-rate, rising-valuation cycle reflect market beta, not sponsor alpha. Decompose how much of the IRR came from NOI growth versus cap-rate compression.
- Vintage effects: a sponsor's best vintage may reflect exceptional market conditions; later vintages may underperform as conditions normalize.
- Gross versus net: compare net-to-LP returns, not gross fund returns; fees and carry can consume a large share.
- Deal attribution: were the winning deals sourced and managed by the current team, or by people who have since left? Track record portability is a key-person risk.
- Benchmarking: compare to relevant indices and peer vintages, not in isolation.

Ask whether the track record is repeatable. A 20% IRR built on cap-rate compression in a single bull market is not evidence of skill in a rising-rate environment.

### Scrutinize The Strategy For Coherence And Cycle Fit

Strategy determines what the fund will own and how it will create value:

- Core, core-plus, value-add, opportunistic: each has different risk, leverage, and return targets. Match the strategy to the investor's risk tolerance.
- Thesis clarity: is the edge sourcing, operational improvement, development, distress, or financial engineering? A coherent edge is repeatable; "we buy good real estate" is not an edge.
- Cycle fit: a strategy that worked in a falling-rate, capital-abundant cycle may struggle when rates rise and liquidity tightens.
- Capacity: can the strategy absorb the fund size without diluting quality or pushing into riskier deals?
- Differentiation: what does this sponsor do that others cannot easily replicate?

A coherent, cycle-appropriate strategy with a defined edge is more repeatable than a generic "we buy real estate" approach in a late-cycle market.

### Assess The Team, Depth, And Key-Person Risk

The team executes the strategy; assess who they are and whether they will stay:

- Senior team experience: cycles worked through, deal leadership, continuity.
- Depth beyond founders: a team dependent on one or two people is key-person risk; depth and succession planning matter.
- Alignment and retention: equity in the firm, carry participation, vesting, and non-competes that retain talent.
- Turnover history: departures of senior dealmakers can signal problems and break track-record portability.

The team is the asset. A great strategy with a departing or overstretched team is a risk. Meet the people, not just the pitch.

### Analyze Portfolio Composition, Leverage, And Concentration

The fund's portfolio is where risk materializes:

- Property-type diversification: concentration in one sector (e.g., office, hospitality) amplifies sector risk.
- Geographic concentration: concentration in one metro or region exposes to local economic, regulatory, and climate risk.
- Lease and tenant concentration: reliance on a few large tenants or a single industry.
- Leverage: fund-level and asset-level leverage, fixed/floating mix, maturity ladder; higher leverage amplifies returns and losses.
- Development exposure: development adds construction, lease-up, and timing risk versus stabilized assets.
- Vintage and deployment: how quickly capital will be deployed and in what market conditions.

Stress the portfolio for sector, geographic, tenant, and rate concentration. A "diversified" fund that is 60% office in one region is not diversified.

### Evaluate Environmental, Climate, And ESG Risk

Real estate is physically exposed to environmental and climate risk, increasingly priced by the market:

- Physical climate risk: flood, wildfire, sea-level, heat, storm exposure of the portfolio's locations; insurance availability and cost.
- Transition risk: building energy efficiency, carbon regulation, retrofit cost; stranded assets as standards tighten.
- Regulatory and zoning: rent regulation, building codes, environmental remediation, permitting.
- ESG and tenant demand: tenant preferences for sustainable, healthy buildings; certification (LEED, WELL) affecting competitiveness.
- Disclosure and management: does the sponsor measure, manage, and report ESG and climate risk?

Climate and ESG risk is not optional diligence; it affects insurance, capex, tenant demand, and exit values. A portfolio of energy-inefficient assets in flood-prone locations carries real, growing risk.

### Review Operational, Legal, And Service Provider Infrastructure

Operational failures destroy value as surely as bad deals:

- Fund administrator, auditor, custodian: independent, reputable service providers reduce operational and fraud risk.
- Valuation policy: appraisal frequency, independent appraisers, fair-value process, conflict management.
- Reporting: frequency, detail, property-level transparency, fee disclosure.
- Compliance and regulatory history: prior regulatory actions, litigation, investor disputes.
- Technology and systems: portfolio management, accounting, investor reporting systems.

Weak operations — a related-party administrator, no independent audit, opaque reporting — are red flags. Strong infrastructure is necessary but not sufficient; its absence is a warning.

### Check References, Conflicts, And Concurrent Fund Activity

References and conflict checks reveal what marketing does not:

- LP references: prior fund investors on actual experience, communication, problem handling, and alignment in stress.
- Deal references: brokers, lenders, and partners on the sponsor's reputation and execution.
- Concurrent funds: multiple funds in the same strategy create deal-allocation conflicts; how does the sponsor decide which fund gets which deal?
- Related-party transactions: acquisitions from or sales to affiliate entities, managed properties, and brokered deals that generate fees to affiliates.
- Side letters and MFN: preferential terms for large LPs that may disadvantage others.

References and conflict checks are where hidden problems surface. Conduct them; do not rely on the pitch.

## Common Traps

### Treating Gross IRR As Evidence Of Skill

Gross IRR in a bull market reflects market beta, not alpha. Decompose skill from market and vintage effects.

### Ignoring Key-Person And Team Risk

Track records built by departed teams are not portable. Assess current team depth and retention.

### Overlooking Portfolio Concentration

A fund concentrated in one sector, region, or tenant is not diversified. Stress concentration in downturns.

### Skipping Environmental And Climate Diligence

Physical and transition climate risk affects insurance, capex, and values. It is not optional diligence.

### Accepting Weak Operational Infrastructure

Related-party administration, no independent audit, or opaque reporting are red flags for operational and fraud risk.

### Relying On The Pitch Instead Of References

Marketing decks show wins; references and conflict checks reveal problems. Conduct independent reference checks.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The track record is decomposed into skill, market beta, and vintage effects, and net-to-LP returns are benchmarked.
- [ ] The strategy is coherent, cycle-appropriate, differentiated, and capacity-tested.
- [ ] The team's experience, depth, key-person risk, turnover, and alignment are assessed.
- [ ] Portfolio composition — property type, geography, tenant, leverage, development, and vintage — is stress-tested for concentration.
- [ ] Environmental, climate, and ESG risk (physical, transition, regulatory) is evaluated for the portfolio.
- [ ] Operational, legal, and service-provider infrastructure (administrator, auditor, valuation, reporting, compliance) is reviewed.
- [ ] LP and deal references are conducted, and concurrent-fund and related-party conflicts are examined.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a fund on track record alone and references investor-specific horizon, liquidity, and risk tolerance.
