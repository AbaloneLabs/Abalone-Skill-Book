# Momentum And Low-Volatility Factor Investing

Momentum and low-volatility are two distinct equity factors with different risk and return profiles. Momentum captures the tendency of assets that have outperformed to continue outperforming over the medium term, exploiting slow information diffusion and behavioral underreaction. Low-volatility captures the counterintuitive finding that lower-volatility stocks have produced competitive or better risk-adjusted returns than higher-volatility stocks, challenging the textbook risk-return relationship. The judgment problem is that both factors have sharp edges. Momentum is prone to severe crashes during regime shifts, when trends reverse violently and the factor loses years of gains in months. Low-volatility can become crowded and expensive, concentrating in defensive sectors that underperform in sharp rallies, and it carries hidden interest-rate sensitivity. Investors who embrace these factors based on backtests without understanding their crash dynamics, crowding risk, and behavioral demands will abandon them at the worst moments.

This skill is for analyzing momentum and low-volatility exposures with attention to their distinctive risks and the discipline required to hold them.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Economic Rationale For Each Factor

Each factor has a rationale that explains why it might persist, and the rationale shapes expectations for its behavior and risks.

For momentum:

- behavioral underreaction, where investors slowly incorporate new information, causing trends to persist;
- herding and feedback, where rising prices attract buyers and falling prices attract sellers;
- the implication that momentum works until it does not, with crashes at regime shifts.

For low-volatility:

- the "low-volatility anomaly," where lower-risk stocks offer better risk-adjusted returns, possibly due to leverage constraints, lottery preferences, and benchmarking behavior;
- the implication that low-volatility is defensive and tends to outperform on a risk-adjusted basis, but may lag in strong bull markets;
- hidden risks, including rate sensitivity (low-volatility stocks often behave like long-duration bonds) and sector concentration.

The rationale is not just academic; it predicts how the factor behaves in different environments and what risks accompany the premium.

### Assess The Crash Risk Of Momentum

Momentum's most dangerous feature is its crash risk. Unlike value and quality, which grind out a premium over time, momentum can lose years of gains in a sharp reversal.

Understand momentum crashes:

- they occur at market regime shifts, when past losers rally sharply and past winners collapse;
- they are associated with market rebounds from oversold conditions, where the most beaten-down stocks surge;
- they can be severe and rapid, with the factor losing 20-40% or more in months;
- they are fat-tailed, meaning historical average returns understate the tail risk;
- mitigation strategies exist (e.g., momentum with volatility scaling, crash protection, or combining with value), but they add complexity and cost.

The investor in momentum must accept that crashes are a feature, not a bug, and must have the risk capacity and horizon to survive them. Position sizing should reflect the crash tail.

### Evaluate The Defensive Profile And Risks Of Low-Volatility

Low-volatility offers a smoother ride and better risk-adjusted returns, but it carries its own risks that are easy to overlook.

Assess:

- the defensive benefit, with lower drawdowns and beta, making the factor useful for risk-controlled portfolios;
- sector concentration, since low-volatility funds often overweight utilities, consumer staples, and regulated industries;
- interest-rate sensitivity, since low-volatility stocks can behave like duration assets and suffer in rising-rate environments;
- crowding risk, where popular low-volatility strategies become expensive and their defensive benefit erodes;
- underperformance in sharp rallies, where low-volatility lags a surging market.

Low-volatility is not a free lunch. It trades different risks (rate, sector, crowding) for lower volatility, and these risks manifest in specific environments.

### Account For Turnover And Implementation Cost

Momentum and low-volatility differ sharply in turnover and cost, which affects net returns.

For momentum:

- turnover is high, because the factor requires frequent rebalancing as rankings change;
- transaction costs, bid-ask spreads, and market impact erode the gross premium significantly;
- tax efficiency is poor in taxable accounts due to short-term gains;
- the gap between backtest and live fund returns is often large for momentum.

For low-volatility:

- turnover is moderate, lower than momentum but higher than market-cap indexing;
- costs are manageable but still above broad index funds;
- tax efficiency is better than momentum but worse than cap-weighted indexing.

Cost-adjusted realism is especially important for momentum, where the theoretical premium can be substantially reduced by implementation costs.

### Prepare For Long Periods Of Underperformance

Both factors underperform for extended periods, and the investor must be prepared to hold through them.

Understand:

- momentum's underperformance during choppy, mean-reverting markets and at crashes;
- low-volatility's underperformance during sharp bull markets and rising-rate environments;
- the tracking error relative to the market, which is psychologically painful;
- the need for a long horizon and pre-commitment to capture the premium;
- the behavioral risk of abandoning the factor at the worst time.

The investor who adds momentum after it has run up, or low-volatility after a market crash, is performance chasing and will likely capture the underperformance that follows.

### Consider Combining Factors To Diversify Factor Risk

Momentum and low-volatility have different risk profiles and can complement each other and other factors in a multi-factor portfolio.

Explore:

- momentum tends to do well in trending markets, while low-volatility defends in downturns;
- combining momentum with value can partially offset momentum's crash risk, since value tends to do well when momentum crashes;
- low-volatility pairs with quality and dividend factors, sharing some defensive characteristics;
- a multi-factor approach diversifies factor-specific risk but adds complexity and cost.

Factor combination is a way to reduce the sharp edges of each factor, but it requires understanding the interactions and not overpaying for complexity.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring Momentum Crash Risk

Momentum can lose years of gains in months at regime shifts. The crash tail must be sized and survived.

### Treating Low-Volatility As Risk-Free

Low-volatility trades volatility risk for rate, sector, and crowding risk. It is defensive, not risk-free.

### Underestimating Momentum Turnover Cost

Momentum's high turnover can consume much of the gross premium in transaction costs and taxes.

### Performance Chasing Factor Entry

Adding a factor after strong recent performance buys high and captures the subsequent underperformance.

### Neglecting Sector And Rate Concentration

Low-volatility funds often concentrate in rate-sensitive defensive sectors, creating hidden risks in rising-rate environments.

### Abandoning The Factor At The Trough

Both factors underperform for years. Investors who abandon at the worst time realize the pain and miss the recovery.

### Confusing Backtested Premia With Live Results

Backtests overstate net returns, especially for high-turnover momentum. Live fund performance after costs is the relevant benchmark.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The economic rationale for each factor (behavioral underreaction for momentum, leverage and lottery preferences for low-volatility) is understood and used to predict behavior and risks.
- [ ] Momentum's crash risk at regime shifts is assessed, with position sizing and risk capacity reflecting the fat-tailed downside, and mitigation (volatility scaling, value combination) considered.
- [ ] Low-volatility's defensive benefit is weighed against its sector concentration, interest-rate sensitivity, crowding risk, and rally underperformance.
- [ ] Turnover, transaction costs, tax efficiency, and the gap between backtest and live returns are evaluated, especially for momentum.
- [ ] The investor is prepared for long periods of underperformance, with a long horizon, pre-commitment, and a written rationale.
- [ ] Factor combination is considered to diversify factor-specific risk, with attention to interactions and cost.
- [ ] Entry is not based on recent performance chasing, and the factor exposure is maintained through disciplined rebalancing.
- [ ] The guidance flags that factor premia are uncertain, that momentum crashes are severe, that low-volatility carries hidden risks, that costs erode benefits, and that professional advice may be warranted for complex factor strategies.
