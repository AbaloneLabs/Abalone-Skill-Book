# Process Adherence And Checklists

An investment process is a repeatable methodology for making decisions: how opportunities are sourced, how they are analyzed, how positions are sized, how they are monitored, and how they are exited. Checklists are the concrete artifacts that enforce the process at each decision point. The judgment problem is that markets generate endless narrative pressure to deviate from process, to skip steps when excited, to bend criteria to fit a favored idea, or to drift the thesis after the purchase to justify holding. Without enforced process and checklists, decisions become ad hoc, unrepeatable, and impossible to improve, because there is no consistent method to evaluate.

The harm this skill prevents is buying on a story without running the analysis, drifting the thesis after purchase to avoid admitting error, skipping risk checks under enthusiasm, and producing a portfolio of decisions made by different standards that cannot be audited or learned from. The agent's job is to ensure each decision follows a stated process, that checklists are completed before action, and that the thesis at exit is compared to the thesis at entry. Process does not guarantee good outcomes, but it makes outcomes evaluable and improvable. Past performance does not guarantee future results, and even a sound process produces losing decisions sometimes.

## Core Rules

### Define The Process Before The Decision

A process must exist before it can be followed. The agent should ensure the investor has a defined methodology covering sourcing, analysis, sizing, monitoring, and exit, and that this methodology is written down. A process that exists only in the head is not a process; it is a feeling that retroactively justifies whatever was decided. The written process is the standard against which each decision is measured.

### Use A Pre-Trade Checklist That Must Be Completed

A checklist converts the process into a gate that must be passed before action. The agent should require a pre-trade checklist covering valuation, downside, portfolio fit, position size, time horizon, and falsification criteria, completed and recorded before the trade is executed. The checklist's value is in forcing each item to be considered even when enthusiasm wants to skip it. A checklist that is not required before execution is decoration; one that blocks the trade until complete changes behavior.

### Guard Against Thesis Creep After Purchase

Thesis creep is the gradual redefinition of the original reasons for owning a position after the purchase, usually to justify holding through adverse developments. The agent should record the entry thesis in writing and should compare the current rationale to it periodically; if the reasons have changed, that is either a legitimate update requiring re-underwriting or a drift that should trigger a sell. Without the written entry thesis, drift is invisible and positions are held on shifting rationales that were never actually validated.

### Separate Process From Outcome In Evaluation

A good process produces some bad outcomes, and a bad process produces some good outcomes, because luck is large in markets. The agent should evaluate decisions by whether they followed the process, not only by whether they made money, and should resist the temptation to judge a sound process by a single bad result or a flawed process by a lucky win. Outcome bias, judging process quality by result, destroys learning by rewarding luck and punishing skill.

### Make The Process Auditable And Repeatable

A process that cannot be audited cannot be improved. The agent should ensure decisions are recorded with their thesis, checklist, sizing logic, and eventual outcome, so that the record can be reviewed for patterns of strength and weakness. Repeatable means another decision under similar conditions would follow the same steps and reach a comparable conclusion. An auditable, repeatable process is the foundation of learning; an ad hoc one repeats the same mistakes under different justifications.

### Resist Bending Criteria To Fit A Favored Idea

When an investor wants to own something, there is strong pressure to relax the criteria that would screen it out. The agent should watch for post-hoc justification, where the conclusion is chosen first and the analysis is built to support it, and should require that criteria are set before the specific opportunity is evaluated. Pre-defined criteria applied honestly screen out favored ideas that do not qualify; bending them admits weak opportunities that the process was designed to reject.

### Review And Improve The Process Periodically

A process is not static. The agent should encourage periodic review of the decision record to identify which steps predict good outcomes and which fail, and should refine the checklist and methodology based on evidence. A process that never changes despite a record of failures is not being used for learning. Improvement requires honest review, which is uncomfortable but is the only way a process gets better over time.

## Common Traps

### Buying On A Story Without Running The Checklist

Enthusiasm wants to skip analysis. The agent should require the checklist before execution.

### Drifting The Thesis To Justify Holding

Without a written entry thesis, the rationale shifts invisibly. The agent should compare current reasons to the original.

### Judging Process Quality By Single Outcomes

Luck is large in markets. The agent should evaluate adherence, not just result.

### Bending Criteria To Admit A Favored Idea

Post-hoc justification admits weak opportunities. The agent should set criteria before evaluating the specific case.

### Keeping The Process In The Head

An unwritten process retroactively justifies any decision. The agent should write it down.

### Skipping Steps Under Time Pressure

Urgency is the enemy of process. The agent should require the steps even when fast action seems necessary.

### Never Reviewing Or Improving The Process

A static process despite failures is not learning. The agent should review the record periodically.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A written investment process exists covering sourcing, analysis, sizing, monitoring, and exit.
- [ ] A pre-trade checklist covering valuation, downside, fit, size, horizon, and falsification is completed and recorded before execution.
- [ ] The entry thesis is recorded in writing and compared to the current rationale to detect thesis creep.
- [ ] Decisions are evaluated by process adherence, not only by outcome, resisting outcome bias.
- [ ] The decision record is auditable and the process is repeatable across similar conditions.
- [ ] Criteria are set before the specific opportunity is evaluated to prevent post-hoc justification.
- [ ] The process is reviewed and refined periodically based on the decision record.
- [ ] No trade is recommended without the checklist being completed.
- [ ] The output acknowledges that a sound process produces some losses and that past performance does not guarantee future results.
- [ ] Process adherence is framed as the path to evaluable, improvable decisions, not as a guarantee of profit.
