# Pyramiding And Scaling

Pyramiding and scaling are the decisions about adding to an existing position over time: pyramiding into a winner as it confirms the thesis, scaling in on weakness to improve an average entry, or averaging down into a loser. Done with discipline, scaling can improve entries and let winners run. Done on emotion, it is the most common path to ruin, because adding to a position concentrates risk exactly where conviction is highest and objectivity is lowest. The judgment problem is that the moment an investor wants to add is usually the moment their judgment is most compromised.

This skill is for deciding when and how much to add, with risk limits that override conviction.

## Core Rules

### Separate Pyramiding From Averaging Down

The two are psychologically and risk-wise opposite, and confusing them is dangerous.

Pyramiding (adding to a winner):

- the market is confirming the thesis;
- the position is already profitable, adding from gains;
- risk is that the trend reverses after the add;
- requires the trend and thesis to remain intact.

Averaging down (adding to a loser):

- the position is moving against the thesis;
- adding increases loss if the thesis is wrong;
- risk is throwing good money after bad;
- requires the original thesis to still be valid and the price to be more attractive, not just cheaper.

Pyramiding rides confirmation; averaging down doubles down on a thesis the market is rejecting. Both can be right, but averaging down is far more often a path to large losses, because it feels like discipline while undermining risk control.

### Require The Thesis, Not Just The Price

Adding should be driven by the thesis, not by the price move alone. "It went up, so add" and "it went down, so it's cheaper" are not theses.

Before adding, restate:

- the original thesis and whether it still holds;
- what has changed since entry (fundamentals, price, sentiment);
- whether the add improves the expected risk-reward;
- the invalidation condition that says stop adding.

A price move without a thesis update is momentum or hope, not analysis. Adding without a thesis is how conviction replaces judgment.

### Cap Total Position Size And Risk Before Adding

Every add must respect pre-set size and risk limits. Conviction is not a license to concentrate.

Define in advance:

- the maximum total size for the position;
- the maximum risk if the full position is stopped out;
- the size of each incremental add;
- the rule that halts adding (size limit, risk limit, or thesis break).

Without pre-set limits, adding tends to continue until the position is too large to survive. The limit must be set when objective, not when the urge to add strikes.

### Pyramid With Decreasing Size And Rising Stops

Disciplined pyramiding adds smaller amounts as the position grows and raises stops to protect gains.

Apply:

- add a smaller fraction with each pyramid (first add largest, later adds smaller);
- raise the stop on earlier units so total risk does not grow with each add;
- only add when the trend and thesis remain intact;
- avoid pyramiding into exhaustion or parabolic moves where reversal risk is high.

The goal is to let the winner run without letting total risk balloon. Pyramiding that adds full size each time and never raises stops concentrates risk at the top.

### Treat Averaging Down With Extreme Caution

Averaging down is the most dangerous scaling behavior. It feels rational ("better average price") while increasing loss if wrong.

Before averaging down, require:

- the original thesis is intact and supported by new evidence, not just a lower price;
- the lower price reflects overreaction, not fundamental deterioration;
- the total size and risk after adding remain within limits;
- a hard stop on the full position if the thesis breaks;
- honest acknowledgment of the "value trap" risk where cheap gets cheaper.

Averaging down on a broken thesis is the classic path to a large permanent loss. If the thesis has changed, the right action is to exit, not add.

### Account For Correlation And Aggregate Exposure

Adding to one position also adds to factor and sector exposure. A series of "just one more add" decisions can concentrate the portfolio invisibly.

Check:

- the position's factor and sector exposure after the add;
- correlated positions that move together;
- aggregate exposure to the theme or factor;
- whether the add breaches portfolio-level concentration limits.

Five adds across five "different" names in the same theme are one large concentrated bet. Track aggregate theme risk, not just per-position size.

### Incorporate Cost, Tax, And Liquidity

Adding increases exposure to transaction costs, tax lots, and liquidity risk.

Consider:

- the cost and spread of each add, especially in less liquid names;
- the tax-lot structure created by multiple entries (use specific lots later);
- the liquidity of the now-larger position and the realistic exit;
- the effect of a larger position on your own market impact.

A position that was liquid at entry can become hard to exit after several adds have grown it.

### Plan The Psychological Discipline

Scaling is where emotion and conviction override discipline. Pre-commitment is essential.

Build in:

- pre-set size and risk limits set when objective;
- a rule to stop adding and to cut when the thesis breaks;
- a cooling-off period before large adds;
- a review of past scaling decisions, did adds help or hurt on average?

Honest review of past scaling outcomes often reveals that conviction-driven adds underperform a disciplined, limit-bound approach.

## Common Traps

### Averaging Down On A Broken Thesis

Adding to a loser whose thesis has deteriorated, justified by a lower price, is the most common scaling failure and a frequent cause of large permanent loss.

### Pyramiding Without Raising Stops

Adding full size each time without raising stops lets total risk balloon at the top, so a reversal wipes out the gains and more.

### Adding Based On Price Alone

"It's up, so add" or "it's cheaper, so add" without a thesis update is momentum or hope, not analysis.

### No Pre-Set Size Or Risk Limits

Without limits set in advance, adding continues until the position is unsupportable. Conviction fills the vacuum.

### Ignoring Aggregate Theme And Factor Risk

Multiple adds across correlated names concentrate the portfolio invisibly. Per-position limits miss the aggregate.

### Adding Into Illiquidity

Each add grows the position and reduces exit flexibility, so the eventual exit moves the market against you.

### Conviction Replacing Judgment

The urge to add is strongest when objectivity is weakest. Without pre-commitment and honest review, scaling becomes a behavioral failure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The decision distinguishes pyramiding (adding to confirmation) from averaging down (adding to a loser), with averaging down subject to stricter thesis and risk requirements.
- [ ] Each add is justified by an updated thesis, not by price movement alone.
- [ ] Total position size and risk limits are set in advance and respected, with a rule to halt adding.
- [ ] Pyramiding uses decreasing add sizes and rising stops so total risk does not grow with each add.
- [ ] Averaging down requires an intact thesis, evidence of overreaction, a hard stop, and value-trap awareness.
- [ ] Correlation and aggregate theme and factor exposure are tracked, not just per-position size.
- [ ] Transaction cost, tax-lot structure, liquidity, and market impact of the larger position are considered.
- [ ] A pre-commitment and honest review of past scaling outcomes support the discipline.
- [ ] The recommendation flags that scaling can concentrate risk and cause large losses, especially averaging down, and that professional advice may be warranted for concentrated or leveraged positions.
