# Human Capital And Financial Capital

A person's largest asset is usually not in their portfolio — it is their future earnings. The judgment problem is that investors allocate their financial assets as if their job did not exist, ignoring that the risk characteristics of their labor income should shape the risk they take with their investments. A tenured professor with bond-like, stable income has the human-capital capacity to hold an aggressive financial portfolio; a commission salesperson or founder with equity-like, volatile income tied to one company or sector is already heavily concentrated in risk and should hold a more conservative financial allocation to compensate. Agents and investors miss this because the human capital does not appear on any statement, so it is treated as irrelevant — when in fact it is the largest position on the household balance sheet and the one that determines what financial risk is prudent.

This skill is for integrating human capital into asset allocation decisions. It is not personalized financial advice; career risk, income stability, and financial capacity depend on individual circumstances.

## Core Rules

### Build The Total Wealth Balance Sheet, Not Just The Investment Portfolio

The household's true asset allocation includes both financial capital (savings, investments, retirement accounts) and human capital (the present value of expected future labor income). Decisions made on financial assets alone ignore the dominant asset.

Construct the full picture:

- estimate human capital as the present value of expected future earnings to retirement;
- compare its size to financial capital — for most working-age households, human capital is several times larger;
- recognize that the financial allocation is the adjustable part of a much larger, mostly fixed, total portfolio;
- judge risk taken in financial assets against the risk already embedded in human capital.

A young high-earner with stable income and modest savings is, in total-wealth terms, holding a large "bond" (their salary) and can afford equity risk in the small financial slice. A near-retiree has little human capital left and must carry risk in financial assets directly.

### Classify Human Capital As Bond-Like Or Equity-Like

Not all labor income has the same risk profile, and the classification drives the offsetting financial allocation.

Classify:

- **bond-like income** — stable, predictable, low correlation to equity markets, long duration (tenured, government, healthcare, tenured-academic, utility-style employment); supports a more aggressive financial allocation;
- **equity-like income** — volatile, cyclical, correlated to the market or a single sector (commission sales, finance, technology with stock compensation, entrepreneurship, cyclical-industry employment); argues for a more conservative financial allocation;
- **hybrid income** — most real jobs blend stability with some cyclicality; classify by the dominant risk.

The financial portfolio should diversify against, not duplicate, the risk already in human capital. A tech employee holding concentrated tech equity is doubling the same bet across both balance sheets.

### Diversify Financial Assets Against Career Concentration

The most common and damaging error is financial assets that reinforce, rather than offset, career risk. The household ends up concentrated in the same factor across both human and financial capital.

Check for overlap:

- holding significant employer stock or stock options on top of salary and bonus from the same employer;
- working in a sector and overweighting that sector in the portfolio;
- depending on a regional economy (employment and housing) and overweighting regional or country equity;
- earning income tied to a commodity (energy, mining, agriculture) and holding commodity-linked financial assets.

Where career and financial risks overlap, a downturn hits both income and portfolio simultaneously — the worst-case correlation. Diversify financial assets away from the source of labor income.

### Account For Wage Volatility And Unemployment Risk

Human capital is not guaranteed. Job loss, disability, industry decline, or obsolescence can sharply reduce or eliminate the expected income stream.

Build in resilience:

- a larger emergency reserve for volatile or specialized income that is hard to replace;
- disability and liability insurance to protect the human capital asset against interruption;
- skills and network investment that preserve employability and the value of human capital;
- a financial allocation that can sustain the household if human capital is impaired.

The more uncertain the human capital, the more conservative and liquid the financial side should be, all else equal.

### Treat Business Ownership And Entrepreneurship As Extreme Concentration

For business owners and founders, human capital and financial capital are often fused in a single highly concentrated, illiquid position. The business is simultaneously the job, the savings, and the investment.

Recognize:

- the household is extraordinarily concentrated in one asset;
- the business may be unprofitable or illiquid for years, so external liquidity and reserves matter more, not less;
- diversification requires eventually extracting value from the business (sale, partial sale, dividends) and reinvesting outside it;
- personal guarantees, loans, and leases often tie personal assets to business risk, deepening the concentration.

Entrepreneurs often need a more conservative portfolio outside the business precisely because the business is already an extreme risk concentration.

### Match Allocation To The Life Cycle Of Human Capital

Human capital depletes as retirement approaches. The role of financial capital shifts from being the diversifier of human capital to being the primary source of spending.

Sequence the shift:

- early career — large, bond-like human capital supports high financial equity risk and capacity to recover from drawdowns;
- mid-career — human capital still substantial but more specialized and harder to replace; begin diversifying and building defensive assets;
- late career and retirement — human capital largely depleted; financial capital must fund spending and carry sequence-of-returns risk; a more balanced allocation with a liquidity buffer is warranted.

The glide path is not mechanical age-based de-risking; it is the consequence of human capital running down and the need for funded spending rising.

### Incorporate The Value Of Benefits And Pensions

Defined-benefit pensions, annuitized income, and reliable government benefits are bond-like human capital that persists into retirement. An investor with a secure pension has, in effect, a large fixed-income position already, which can justify more equity risk in the remaining financial portfolio. Conversely, an investor relying solely on financial assets for retirement income has no such bond floor and may need more conservatism. Account for these sources explicitly on the total wealth balance sheet.

## Common Traps

### Allocating Financial Assets As If The Job Did Not Exist

Ignoring human capital treats the largest asset as irrelevant and leads to risk decisions that are wrong for the total household.

### Duplicating Career Risk In The Portfolio

Holding employer stock or overweighting one's sector doubles the same risk across both balance sheets, maximizing the chance of simultaneous income and portfolio loss.

### Treating All Income As Equally Stable

Bond-like and equity-like income warrant opposite financial offsets. Classifying volatile income as stable leads to excessive total risk.

### Underestimating Entrepreneurial Concentration

For founders, the business is job, savings, and investment fused. Failing to diversify outside it leaves the household with one extreme bet.

### Ignoring The Life-Cycle Depletion Of Human Capital

Human capital shrinks toward retirement. An allocation appropriate at thirty may be far too aggressive at sixty.

### Forgetting Pensions And Benefits As Bond-Like Capital

A secure pension is a large fixed-income position. Ignoring it can lead to double-counting conservatism or taking too little equity risk.

### Assuming Human Capital Is Guaranteed

Job loss, disability, and obsolescence can impair the income stream. Reserves and insurance protect the asset.
