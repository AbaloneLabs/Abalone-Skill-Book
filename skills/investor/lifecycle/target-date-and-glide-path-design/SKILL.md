# Target Date And Glide Path Design

A target-date fund bundles a lifetime allocation strategy into a single product, and its glide path — the schedule by which it shifts from growth to conservation — embodies a theory about how risk should evolve over a lifetime. The judgment problem is that investors buy target-date funds for the date in the name and rarely examine the glide path, assuming all funds with the same date are alike. They are not. Two funds with the same target date can hold dramatically different equity exposure at retirement, follow a "to" or "through" philosophy, and suit very different investors. Choosing or recommending a target-date fund without understanding its glide path is choosing a lifetime allocation strategy blind. The skill is evaluating whether the glide path's assumptions match the investor's actual horizon, spending needs, and risk capacity.

This skill is for evaluating and designing target-date glide paths. It is not personalized financial advice; appropriate glide paths depend on individual circumstances, and past performance does not guarantee future results.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That The Target Date Is A Label, Not A Specification

Funds sharing a target date can differ more than funds with different dates. The date tells you the approximate retirement year; it says almost nothing about the allocation.

Examine what actually differs:

- equity exposure at the target date (the "landing point"), which can range from roughly 20 percent to 70 percent or more across funds with the same date;
- the shape and steepness of the glide path in the decades before the target;
- whether the fund continues to de-risk after the target date (a "through" glide path) or stops at it (a "to" glide path);
- the composition of the equity and fixed-income sleeves, including international, real-asset, and credit allocations.

Never select a target-date fund by date alone. Compare the glide paths and landing points of the candidates.

### Decide Between "To" And "Through" Based On The Real Horizon

The choice between de-risking to a point (to) and continuing to de-risk through retirement (through) reflects a judgment about the investor's effective horizon.

Reason through the choice:

- **"to" glide paths** reach their most conservative allocation at the target date, assuming the investor needs capital preservation at retirement; suits investors who will spend down or move the assets soon after the date;
- **"through" glide paths** maintain higher equity exposure at and after the target date, treating retirement as the start of a multi-decade spending period that must outlast inflation; suits investors whose assets must fund twenty to forty years of spending and growth.

Because retirement assets often need to last decades, "through" glide paths with meaningful equity at the target date are common — but they carry more drawdown risk in the early retirement years. Match the philosophy to the investor's expected spending horizon and sequence-risk tolerance.

### Set The Landing-Point Equity Against Sequence Risk And Longevity

The equity allocation at the target date is the single most consequential number in the glide path, because it governs risk precisely when sequence-of-returns danger is highest.

Balance two forces:

- longevity and inflation argue for substantial equity at and through retirement, since a portfolio that is too conservative can be exhausted by decades of inflation and spending;
- sequence risk argues for enough conservatism that an early-retirement drawdown does not force distressed selling.

A landing point that is too low risks running out of real purchasing power late in retirement; one that is too high risks an unrecoverable early drawdown. The right level depends on the size of the portfolio relative to spending, other income sources, and the investor's flexibility to cut spending in bad years.

### Integrate Human Capital Into The Glide Path Logic

A glide path is implicitly a total-wealth allocation that treats young investors as having large, bond-like human capital justifying high equity risk, and older investors as having depleted human capital requiring more financial conservatism.

Check the assumptions:

- the glide path assumes the investor's income is stable and bond-like; investors with volatile, equity-like income may need a more conservative path than their age suggests;
- the glide path assumes steady accumulation; investors with lumpy savings (business sale, late inheritance) may be mis-served by a smooth path;
- the glide path does not know about the investor's pension, employer stock, or other balance-sheet items that change capacity.

A target-date fund is a default for a standardized investor. Where the investor deviates materially from the standard, the glide path may need to be overridden.

### Examine The Glide Path Shape, Not Just The Endpoints

The path matters as much as the endpoints. A glide path that de-risks sharply in the final decade exposes the investor to a different risk profile than one that de-risks gradually over decades.

Consider:

- how steep the de-risking is near retirement, when sequence risk peaks;
- whether the path is monotonic or has intentional tilts (for example, slight re-risking late in retirement to address longevity);
- the interaction with contributions: a steep path concentrates risk reduction exactly when the largest balances are at stake.

A smooth, well-justified path is easier to hold through stress than an erratic one whose logic the investor does not understand.

### Evaluate The Underlying Construction And Costs

A target-date fund is only as good as its underlying funds and its cost. Two funds with identical glide paths can deliver very different net returns based on construction.

Assess:

- whether the underlying funds are low-cost index funds, active funds, or a mix, and the total expense ratio of the composite;
- the diversification across geography, market cap, factors, and asset classes within each sleeve;
- the fixed-income construction (government, credit, inflation-linked, duration) and whether it matches the liability-like nature of near-term spending;
- whether the fund uses a single manager's funds or a multi-manager open architecture, which can affect diversification and conflicts.

A glide path built on expensive or poorly diversified underlying funds delivers the path's risk profile at a worse net return.

### Match The Fund To The Investor, Or Override It

A target-date fund is a reasonable default for an investor who fits the standardized profile and wants a single set-and-forget solution. For investors who deviate — different horizon, volatile income, concentrated employer stock, a pension, legacy goals, or strong views — the fund should be treated as one input, supplemented or overridden as needed. Do not let the convenience of a single fund substitute for matching the allocation to the actual balance sheet.

## Common Traps

### Selecting By Target Date Alone

Funds with the same date can have vastly different landing points and glide paths. The date is a label, not a specification.

### Ignoring The "To" Versus "Through" Philosophy

Whether the fund keeps de-risking after the target date changes the risk profile throughout retirement. Match it to the spending horizon.

### Setting Landing-Point Equity Too Low Or Too High

Too low risks inflation and longevity exhaustion; too high risks an unrecoverable early-retirement drawdown. Balance both forces.

### Assuming The Glide Path Knows The Investor's Balance Sheet

Target-date funds assume a standardized investor. Pensions, employer stock, volatile income, and legacy goals are invisible to the fund and may require overriding it.

### Focusing On Endpoints And Ignoring Path Shape

The steepness and timing of de-risking near retirement materially affects sequence risk when balances are largest.

### Overlooking Underlying Costs And Construction

Identical glide paths can deliver very different net returns depending on the expense ratios and diversification of the underlying funds.

### Treating Convenience As A Substitute For Fit

A single set-and-forget fund is convenient but may not match the investor's actual horizon, risk capacity, and balance sheet.
