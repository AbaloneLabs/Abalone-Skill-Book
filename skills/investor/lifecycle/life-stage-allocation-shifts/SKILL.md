# Life Stage Allocation Shifts

Asset allocation is not set once; it should evolve as the investor's capacity to bear risk changes. The judgment problem is that investors treat their allocation as a static identity ("I am an aggressive investor") when the right allocation depends on life-stage factors — age, dependents, income stability, proximity to goals, and the size of the portfolio relative to needs — that change continuously. Two errors follow: holding an aggressive allocation into a stage where a drawdown would be catastrophic (too much risk, too late), or de-risking mechanically by age into a stage where the investor has ample capacity and a long horizon (too little risk, too early). The skill is shifting allocation in response to the factors that actually change risk capacity, not in response to age alone or to market mood.

This skill is for deciding how allocation should shift across life stages. It is not personalized financial advice; risk capacity and tolerance are individual, and past performance does not guarantee future results.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Risk Capacity From Risk Tolerance, And Let Capacity Govern

Risk tolerance is psychological — how much loss the investor can endure without panic. Risk capacity is financial — how much loss the investor can afford without failing their goals. Capacity should govern, because an investor can have high tolerance but low capacity (and be one drawdown from ruin) or low tolerance but high capacity (and be needlessly conservative).

Assess capacity through:

- the time horizon to each goal and the ability to wait out drawdowns;
- the size of the portfolio relative to spending needs and human capital;
- the stability and diversification of income;
- the presence of fixed obligations and dependents;
- the availability of other resources (pension, insurance, partner income).

An investor with low capacity should hold less risk regardless of tolerance. An investor with high capacity but low tolerance may need to address the tolerance gap (education, framing, gradual exposure) rather than simply capitulating to a too-conservative allocation.

### Shift Allocation In Response To Specific Capacity Drivers, Not Age Alone

Age is a proxy for capacity drivers, and a crude one. Two people of the same age can have very different capacity. Shift allocation when the underlying drivers change.

Drivers that should trigger a reassessment:

- a shrinking time horizon to a goal (retirement, college funding, a planned purchase);
- the accumulation of enough assets that protecting them matters more than growing them;
- the addition or loss of dependents;
- a change in income stability (job change, self-employment, retirement);
- a change in health, longevity expectations, or insurance coverage;
- a shift from accumulation (adding savings) to decumulation (spending from the portfolio).

When a driver changes, reassess. Do not wait for a birthday to move an allocation that the situation already demands.

### Handle The Accumulation-To-Decumulation Transition Specially

The shift from adding to a portfolio to spending from it is the most consequential life-stage change, because it introduces sequence-of-returns risk: a severe drawdown in the first years of withdrawals can permanently impair a portfolio even if later returns are strong.

Manage the transition:

- build a liquidity buffer of one to several years of spending needs in cash and short-term bonds, so withdrawals do not force selling equities during a drawdown;
- maintain enough growth assets to fund the later years and offset inflation, but not so much that an early drawdown is unrecoverable;
- consider a bucket or liability-matching structure that separates near-term spending from long-term growth;
- plan flexible spending (reducing withdrawals in bad years) to reduce sequence risk.

The decumulation allocation is not simply "the accumulation allocation with less equity." It is a different problem with different risks.

### Account For Dependents And Obligations

Dependents raise the stakes of a drawdown and lower risk capacity. The allocation should reflect the number and dependence of people relying on the portfolio.

Consider:

- children's education funding with a defined horizon (de-risk as the funding date approaches);
- support for aging parents or family members;
- a non-working partner or dependents who rely on a single income;
- legacy or bequest intentions that extend the effective horizon beyond the investor's life.

More dependents and obligations argue for more resilience, larger reserves, and adequate insurance, even if the long horizon would otherwise support risk.

### Match Allocation To Goal Horizons, Not A Single Blended Number

A household usually has multiple goals with different horizons: near-term spending, medium-term education funding, long-term retirement, and legacy. A single allocation cannot be optimal for all of them.

Structure by goal:

- near-term goals (under roughly three years) funded with cash and short-dated safe assets;
- medium-term goals (three to ten years) with a balanced mix reflecting the horizon;
- long-term goals (ten-plus years) with a growth-tilted allocation that can endure volatility;
- legacy goals (beyond the investor's life) with the longest horizon and the most risk capacity.

A goals-based structure prevents near-term needs from forcing distressed sales of long-term growth assets.

### Reassess After Major Wealth Events

Windfalls and shocks change capacity abruptly and should trigger an allocation review, not just a portfolio deposit or withdrawal.

Events that demand reassessment:

- inheritance, business sale, or other windfall that sharply increases financial capital;
- job loss, divorce, or health crisis that reduces human capital or raises obligations;
- a large concentrated position (employer stock, single business) that changes the risk profile;
- a sharp market move that materially changes the portfolio's value relative to needs.

A windfall often argues for initially lower risk (to avoid immediate loss of new wealth) while a long-term plan is built; a shock often argues for more conservatism and larger reserves while the situation stabilizes.

### Avoid Mechanical Age Rules And Market-Timing Shifts

Two extremes fail. A rigid age-based rule (for example, equity equals one hundred minus age) ignores individual capacity and current conditions. Reactive shifts driven by market mood (becoming aggressive after rallies, conservative after declines) systematically buy high and sell low.

The right practice is principled, driver-based reassessment: shift when capacity drivers change, using a disciplined process, and resist shifting in response to recent market movements except through a pre-committed rebalancing policy.

## Common Traps

### Letting Risk Tolerance Override Risk Capacity

High tolerance does not create capacity. An investor who can stomach a 50 percent drawdown but cannot afford one is still one bad sequence from failing their goals.

### Shifting By Age Alone

Age is a crude proxy. Two same-age investors can have very different capacity based on horizon, obligations, income, and wealth.

### Carrying An Aggressive Allocation Too Long

Failing to de-risk as the horizon shortens and capacity falls leaves the portfolio exposed to a drawdown it cannot recover from.

### De-Risking Mechanically And Too Early

Over-conservatism in a high-capacity, long-horizon stage forgoes the growth needed to fund long-term goals and inflation.

### Ignoring Sequence Risk At Decumulation

The accumulation allocation with less equity is not the right decumulation allocation. Sequence-of-returns risk demands a liquidity buffer and flexible spending.

### Using One Allocation For Multiple Horizons

A single blended number cannot serve near-term spending, medium-term goals, and long-term growth simultaneously.

### Reacting To Market Mood

Becoming aggressive after rallies and conservative after declines is the systematic destruction of long-term returns.
