# Wash Sale Rules And Substantially Similar

The wash sale rule disallows a loss when a substantially identical security is bought within 30 days before or after the sale at a loss. The disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement, deferring rather than eliminating the benefit, except in one critical case: a wash sale triggered by a purchase in an IRA permanently disallows the loss with no basis adjustment, destroying the tax benefit entirely. The rule is mechanical, it reaches across all of the taxpayer's accounts, and it is the single most common way a tax-loss harvest is accidentally invalidated.

Agents tend to focus on the obvious repurchase (buying back the same stock) and miss the subtle triggers: dividend reinvestment, systematic purchase plans, purchases in a spouse's account, purchases of a different share class or an option on the security, and the cross-account aggregation that the IRS applies. The judgment problem is identifying everything that counts as substantially identical, auditing all accounts and automatic transactions across the full 61-day window, and choosing replacement securities that preserve exposure without triggering the rule.

This skill applies to wash sale compliance, substantially-identical determinations, cross-account management, and replacement security selection for harvesting. It is not tax or investment advice; wash sale rules vary by jurisdiction and the IRS interprets substantially identical by facts and circumstances, consult a qualified tax professional, and this is not legal or tax advice.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Window As 61 Days Spanning The Sale

The wash sale window is 30 days before and 30 days after the sale at a loss, a 61-day span. A repurchase of a substantially identical security on any day in that window triggers the rule. The "before" direction is commonly overlooked: if you bought the security in the last 30 days and then sell at a loss, that prior purchase can trigger a wash sale.

Audit the full 61-day window, not just the 30 days after. Check purchases in the 30 days leading up to a planned harvest, and ensure no purchases occur in the 30 days after. The bidirectional window is the most frequently missed element.

### Apply The Rule Across All Accounts And Spouses

The IRS applies the wash sale rule across all of the taxpayer's accounts, and for married couples filing jointly, purchases by one spouse can trigger a wash sale on the other's sale. A loss harvested in a taxable account is disallowed if a substantially identical security is bought in an IRA, a 401(k) brokerage window, a spouse's account, or any account under the taxpayer's control. Brokers increasingly aggregate across linked accounts.

Before harvesting, audit purchase activity in every account: taxable, IRA, Roth, HSA, spouse's accounts, and any automated plans. The cross-account reach is the mechanism by which most accidental wash sales occur, especially via dividend reinvestment in an IRA after a taxable harvest.

### Understand The IRA Permanent Disallowance Trap

A wash sale triggered by a purchase in an IRA (traditional or Roth) permanently disallows the loss with no basis adjustment to recover it later. This is the most damaging wash sale outcome: the loss is gone forever, and the IRA basis (which is irrelevant for Roth and rarely relevant for traditional) does not benefit. A single automatic dividend reinvestment in an IRA within 30 days of a taxable harvest can destroy a large loss.

Before harvesting a security held in both taxable and IRA accounts, ensure all dividend reinvestment and purchase activity in the IRA is halted for the full 61-day window. This specific trap is why many automated harvesting strategies fail for investors who hold the same fund in multiple account types.

### Distinguish Substantially Identical From Merely Similar

The wash sale rule applies to substantially identical securities, not merely similar ones. Substantially identical generally means the same security, the same mutual fund, different share classes of the same fund, or an option to buy the security. It does not generally include different funds tracking the same or a similar index, different stocks in the same sector, or ETFs from different providers tracking similar but not identical indexes, though the IRS evaluates facts and circumstances and the boundary is not always clear.

When selecting a replacement to preserve exposure, choose one that is clearly not substantially identical: a different index (S&P 500 vs total market), a different provider, or a related but distinct fund. Document the basis for the distinction. Avoid replacements that are economically identical but nominally different (same holdings, different wrapper), as these risk being deemed substantially identical.

### Halt Automatic Reinvestment And Systematic Purchases

Dividend reinvestment and systematic purchase plans are the most common accidental wash sale triggers. A dividend reinvestment in the 30 days after a harvest repurchases the same security and disallows the loss. Many investors discover this only at tax time.

Before harvesting, turn off dividend reinvestment on the security in all accounts for the full window. Review any systematic purchase plans (e.g., automatic monthly buys) that would touch the security. Resume reinvestment only after the 30-day post-sale window closes. This is a simple, high-value precaution.

### Preserve Exposure With A Non-Substantially-Identical Replacement

To maintain market exposure during the wash sale window without triggering the rule, replace the sold security with a correlated but not substantially identical one. For an S&P 500 fund, a total stock market fund or a Russell 1000 fund; for a sector fund, a related sector or broad market fund; for a Treasury bond, a different maturity or issuer. The replacement should track closely enough to preserve the intended exposure while differing enough to avoid the rule.

Evaluate the replacement's tracking difference and correlation to the original. Accept minor tracking error in exchange for wash sale safety. When the window closes, decide whether to switch back to the original (incurring another transaction) or hold the replacement; frequent switching adds cost.

### Document The Replacement Rationale

Because substantially identical is facts-and-circumstances, document why the replacement is not substantially identical: different index methodology, different provider, different holdings. This documentation supports the position if questioned. Avoid replacements whose only difference is the ticker while holdings are identical.

Maintain records of the harvest, the replacement chosen, the rationale, and the dates of the window. Good documentation is the defense against a wash sale challenge and the basis for confident harvesting.

## Common Traps

### Overlooking The 30-Day Before Window

A purchase in the 30 days before the sale triggers a wash sale. The trap is auditing only the days after. Check the full 61-day span.

### Triggering A Wash Sale Via An IRA Purchase Or Reinvestment

IRA-triggered wash sales permanently disallow the loss. The trap is harvesting a fund also held in an IRA without halting reinvestment. Audit IRAs especially.

### Counting Different Accounts As Independent

The IRS aggregates across the taxpayer's accounts and spouses. The trap is assuming a purchase in another account is safe. Audit all accounts.

### Using A Substantially Identical Replacement

Different share classes or identical-holding wrappers are substantially identical. The trap is thinking a different ticker avoids the rule. Choose clearly distinct replacements.

### Forgetting Dividend Reinvestment

Automatic reinvestment repurchases the same security. The trap is harvesting without disabling reinvestment. Turn it off for the window.

### Assuming Similar Index Funds Are Substantially Identical

Different funds tracking similar indexes are generally not substantially identical, but the boundary is unclear. The trap is being too aggressive (identical holdings) or too timid (refusing any related fund). Evaluate facts and circumstances.

### Losing The Loss Permanently By Not Recovering Basis

Outside IRAs, disallowed losses add to replacement basis, so the benefit is deferred, not lost. The trap is not tracking the basis adjustment and failing to recover it on the eventual sale. Maintain basis records.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The full 61-day window (30 days before and after the loss sale) is audited for repurchases, not only the days after.
- [ ] Purchase activity is checked across all accounts (taxable, IRA, Roth, HSA, spouse's accounts) and any automatic plans.
- [ ] IRA purchases and dividend reinvestments are halted for the window to avoid permanent loss disallowance.
- [ ] The replacement security is clearly not substantially identical (different index, provider, or holdings), with the distinction documented.
- [ ] Dividend reinvestment and systematic purchase plans on the harvested security are disabled for the full window.
- [ ] Exposure is preserved with a correlated replacement whose tracking difference is acceptable.
- [ ] The substantially-identical boundary is evaluated by facts and circumstances, not assumed; documentation supports the position.
- [ ] Basis adjustments from any disallowed losses (outside IRAs) are tracked to recover the deferred benefit on eventual sale.
- [ ] Replacement rationale and window dates are documented.
