# Gifted And Inherited Basis

When securities change hands by gift or inheritance, their tax basis does not simply reset to market value. Gifts generally carry over the donor's original basis (carryover basis), so the recipient inherits both the original cost and the unrealized gain or loss. Inherited securities, by contrast, generally receive a stepped-up (or stepped-down) basis to the fair market value at the date of death, eliminating the unrealized gain or loss. These two different rules create large planning consequences: gifting appreciated stock transfers a tax liability to the recipient, while holding it until death can eliminate the gain entirely for heirs.

Agents tend to treat transfers as basis-neutral or to confuse gift and inheritance rules. The judgment problem is determining the correct basis after a transfer, applying the special dual-basis rule for gifted stock that was at a loss when gifted, and planning whether to gift now or bequeath at death based on the tax consequences.

This skill applies to gifted and inherited basis determination, gift-versus-bequest planning, carryover and stepped-up basis, and holding-period continuation. It is not tax, legal, or investment advice; basis rules vary by jurisdiction and change, estate and gift tax rules are complex, and consult a qualified tax or legal professional.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Carryover Basis (Gifts) From Stepped-Up Basis (Inheritance)

The fundamental distinction: a gift generally carries over the donor's basis and holding period to the recipient, so the recipient takes the donor's original cost and the unrealized gain or loss travels with the asset. An inheritance generally steps the basis to fair market value at death, erasing the unrealized gain (step-up) or loss (step-down). This single difference drives much of estate and gift planning.

Confirm the transfer type before determining basis. A gift during life is carryover; a transfer at death is stepped-up. Confusing the two produces large basis errors. For gifts, obtain the donor's basis and holding period records, since the recipient will need them.

### Apply The Dual-Basis Rule For Gifted Loss Stock

When a security is gifted while trading below the donor's basis (at a loss), a special dual-basis rule applies: for determining a loss on a later sale by the recipient, the basis is the lower of the donor's basis or the fair market value at the time of the gift. For determining a gain, the basis is the donor's original basis. This means the recipient's basis depends on whether the eventual sale is at a gain or a loss relative to both reference points.

Compute both reference points when handling gifted loss stock: the donor's basis and the date-of-gift market value. If the recipient later sells above the donor's basis, use the donor's basis (a smaller gain). If the recipient sells below the date-of-gift value, use the date-of-gift value (a smaller loss). If the sale price falls between the two, no gain or loss is recognized. This dual-basis rule prevents transferring a loss to the recipient in a way that would create a deduction the donor could not use.

### Preserve And Transfer Holding Period Records

For gifts, the recipient's holding period includes the donor's holding period (tacks on), so a stock the donor held for 11 months and then gifted is treated as held long-term once the combined period exceeds one year. For inherited securities, the holding period is automatically long-term regardless of the decedent's actual holding period, a significant advantage. These rules affect whether a sale produces short-term or long-term character.

Maintain and transfer holding period records with gifted securities. For inherited securities, recognize the automatic long-term character, which makes inheritance especially tax-efficient for assets that would otherwise be short-term. Do not assume the recipient's holding period starts at zero for a gift.

### Plan Gifts Of Appreciated Versus Depreciated Stock Differently

Gifting appreciated stock transfers the embedded gain to the recipient, who will owe tax on sale based on the donor's carryover basis. If the recipient is in a lower bracket (e.g., a child or lower-income family member), this can reduce the tax on the eventual sale, but kiddie tax rules can limit the benefit for minor children. Gifting depreciated stock is generally disadvantageous because the dual-basis rule limits the recipient's ability to claim the loss.

Evaluate the recipient's tax bracket versus the donor's before gifting appreciated stock. For appreciated assets intended for charity, give directly to the charity (which pays no tax and the donor deducts market value) rather than to an individual who would inherit the gain. For loss stock, consider having the donor sell to realize the loss (if usable) rather than gifting it, since the recipient's loss basis is limited.

### Use Stepped-Up Basis At Death For Legacy Planning

Assets held until death and passed to heirs generally receive a stepped-up basis to the date-of-death fair market value, eliminating the unrealized gain. This makes long-term holding of appreciated assets especially powerful for legacy planning: the gain compounds tax-free during life and is erased at death. Conversely, assets that have declined may receive a stepped-down basis, so loss assets are often better sold before death to realize the loss.

For investors with appreciated assets and legacy intent, the step-up is a major reason to hold rather than gift during life, especially if the investor does not need the assets for spending. Coordinate this with estate tax considerations (the step-up applies to income tax; estate tax is separate) and with the overall estate plan. The step-up is among the most valuable provisions in the tax code for long-term investors.

### Coordinate With Annual Gift Exclusion And Estate Tax Rules

Gifting has its own tax regime: the annual gift exclusion (a per-recipient, per-year amount) allows gifts up to a threshold without gift tax or reporting; gifts above the annual exclusion use lifetime estate and gift tax exemption, requiring reporting. These rules interact with basis: a gift within the annual exclusion carries carryover basis but no gift tax reporting, while larger gifts use exemption. Inherited assets face estate tax rules separate from the income tax basis step-up.

Coordinate the income tax basis consequences (carryover vs step-up) with the gift and estate tax rules (annual exclusion, lifetime exemption, estate tax threshold). The decision to gift now versus bequeath at death depends on both the income tax basis outcome and the transfer tax outcome, which can pull in different directions. Do not analyze basis in isolation from the transfer tax framework.

### Maintain Basis Records For Carryover And Step-Up Transactions

Carryover basis requires the donor's original cost and holding period, which the recipient must obtain and retain. Stepped-up basis requires the date-of-death (or alternate valuation date) fair market value, documented from reliable sources. Poor recordkeeping is the most common cause of basis disputes and overpayment of tax.

For gifts, transfer and retain the donor's basis records. For inheritances, obtain and document the date-of-death values (from brokerage statements, estate records, or pricing services). Establish the basis contemporaneously; reconstructing it years later is difficult and often results in a zero or defaulted basis that overstates gain.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Carryover And Stepped-Up Basis

Treating a gift as stepped-up or an inheritance as carryover produces large errors. The trap is not confirming the transfer type. Verify gift versus inheritance.

### Ignoring The Dual-Basis Rule For Gifted Loss Stock

Using the donor's basis for both gain and loss on gifted loss stock overstates the deductible loss. The trap is applying a single basis. Use the lower of donor basis or date-of-gift value for losses.

### Assuming The Recipient's Holding Period Starts At Zero

Gifts carry over the holding period; inheritances are automatically long-term. The trap is resetting the period. Tack the donor's period for gifts; recognize long-term for inheritances.

### Gifting Appreciated Stock Without Considering The Recipient's Bracket

Carryover basis transfers the gain. The trap is gifting to a higher-bracket recipient or ignoring kiddie tax. Compare brackets and consider direct charitable gifts.

### Gifting Loss Stock Instead Of Selling It

The dual-basis rule limits the recipient's loss. The trap is gifting loss stock expecting the recipient to harvest the full loss. The donor may benefit more by selling.

### Overlooking Step-Up At Death For Legacy Planning

Holding appreciated assets until death erases the gain. The trap is gifting during life and transferring the gain unnecessarily. Weigh gift versus bequest for legacy assets.

### Losing Basis Records For Carryover Transactions

Without the donor's basis, the recipient may default to zero basis and overpay tax. The trap is not transferring records. Obtain and retain basis documentation.
