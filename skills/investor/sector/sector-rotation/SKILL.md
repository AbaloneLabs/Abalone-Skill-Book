# Sector Rotation

Sector rotation is the idea that different sectors lead at different stages of the business cycle, and that investors can add value by tilting toward the sectors best suited to the next phase. The concept is sound in principle but treacherous in practice. An investing agent often applies a rigid rotation map, defensive sectors in recession, cyclicals in recovery, and then trades on it as if the cycle phase were obvious and the map always held. Both assumptions fail. Cycle timing is uncertain, sector behavior shifts across regimes, and many apparent rotations are lagging reactions or false signals.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "which sectors perform best in a recession", "is it time to rotate into cyclicals", "how do I read sector rotation", or "should I move from growth to value". The goal is to prevent the agent from mechanically applying a textbook rotation map and to force it to verify the cycle phase, check regime-specific behavior, distinguish leading from lagging moves, and avoid chasing false signals.

Sector rotation is a tactical overlay, not a guaranteed source of return. It carries real risk of loss, whipsaw, and tax costs, and conclusions should disclose uncertainty and investor-specific constraints.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Rotation To A Verified Cycle Phase

Rotation only works if the current and next cycle phases are correctly identified. This is the hardest step and the one most often skipped.

Establish:

- Where is the economy in the cycle: early, mid, late, or recession?
- What do leading indicators, credit, yield curve, and earnings revisions imply about the next phase?
- Is the market already pricing a phase transition, making the rotation late?

A rotation call built on an assumed cycle phase is fragile. Confirm the phase with multiple leading indicators before rotating, and accept that phase identification is probabilistic.

### Know The Textbook Map, Then Question It

The classic rotation framework assigns sector leadership by phase:

- Early recovery: cyclicals, industrials, materials, consumer discretionary, financials lead as demand and credit recover.
- Mid-cycle expansion: technology, communication, broad growth lead as confidence and capex rise.
- Late-cycle: energy, commodities, industrials lead as capacity tightens and inflation rises.
- Recession and contraction: defensives, healthcare, consumer staples, utilities, and bonds outperform as earnings fall.

Use this map as a hypothesis, not a law. Then question it:

- Does the current inflation and rate regime match the historical period the map was built on?
- Has sector composition changed so that a sector means something different today?
- Are structural forces, such as deglobalization or AI, overriding the cyclical pattern?
- Is the policy reaction function the same as in the reference cycles?

### Distinguish Defensive, Cyclical, And Growth Characteristics

Sectors are categorized by their sensitivity to the cycle, but the categories blur and shift.

Clarify:

- Defensive sectors, staples, healthcare, utilities, have stable demand and lower earnings cyclicality, and tend to outperform late and in contraction.
- Cyclical sectors, industrials, materials, energy, consumer discretionary, have earnings that rise and fall with the cycle.
- Growth sectors, technology and communication, are sensitive to discount rates and long-duration earnings, and can behave cyclically or defensively depending on the driver.
- Financials are cyclical but also rate-sensitive through net interest margins.

A sector can shift category across regimes. Utilities can behave like bonds in a rate-shock, and technology can be defensive when its cash flows are stable.

### Check Whether The Rotation Has Already Happened

Markets discount the future. By the time a rotation looks obvious in the data, it is often largely complete.

Ask:

- Have the target sectors already outperformed, pricing in the expected phase?
- Are valuations in the target sectors stretched relative to history and the rest of the market?
- Is positioning crowded in the trade you are about to enter?
- What would have to happen from here for the rotation to continue?

Leading sectors at cycle turns often move before the fundamental confirmation. Entering after the move means buying the confirmation, which is when late money arrives and the trade can reverse.

### Use Relative Strength And Breadth, Not Absolute Price

Sector rotation is best read in relative terms, how a sector performs against the market, not in absolute price.

Use:

- relative strength ratios of sector to index over multiple time frames;
- sector breadth and participation;
- relative earnings revisions and estimate momentum;
- relative valuation changes, separating earnings-driven from multiple-driven moves.

A sector rising in absolute terms may still be losing relative ground if the market is rising faster. Rotation is about relative leadership.

### Separate Earnings-Driven From Multiple-Driven Rotation

Some rotations are supported by improving fundamentals; others are pure multiple expansion driven by flows and narrative. The former is more durable.

Check:

- Are earnings estimates rising in the leading sector, or is the move all valuation?
- Are margins and demand actually improving, or is it a sentiment re-rating?
- Is the rotation confirmed by credit, commodity, and rate signals relevant to that sector?

Multiple-driven rotations without earnings support are vulnerable to reversal when sentiment shifts.

### Account For Lags, False Signals, And Whipsaw

Sector rotations lag the cycle and often throw false signals. A sector can outperform on a lagging reaction to old data, or lead on a wrong anticipation of a phase that never arrives.

Manage:

- Use confirmation from multiple indicators rather than one sector move.
- Accept that rotations can reverse if the cycle phase call is wrong.
- Size rotation trades to tolerate being early or wrong.
- Consider staged or gradual rotation rather than abrupt all-in switches, which incur tax and transaction costs and raise whipsaw risk.

## Common Traps

### Applying The Textbook Map Mechanically

The classic rotation map is a hypothesis conditioned on a specific regime. Applying it without checking inflation, rates, and structural differences leads to trades that fail.

### Rotating After The Move Is Obvious

By the time a rotation is clear in the data, it is often priced. Chasing confirmed rotations is buying from those who anticipated them.

### Confusing A Lagging Reaction With A Leading Signal

A sector outperforming on old data is not predicting the next phase; it is catching up. Leading signals come from sectors and indicators that turn before the fundamentals confirm.

### Ignoring Valuation And Positioning

Rotating into a sector that is already expensive and crowded sets up a poor risk-reward even if the cycle call is right.

### Treating Sector Labels As Fixed

Sectors evolve. Today's technology sector differs from the dot-com era, and energy differs across shale and transition regimes. Do not assume historical sector behavior persists unchanged.

### Overtrading Rotations

Frequent rotation in response to every data point generates costs, taxes, and whipsaw. Rotation should be a deliberate, evidence-backed tilt, not a reaction to noise.

### Forgetting The Policy And Rate Driver

Sectors respond to rates and policy, not only the cycle. A rotation that ignores the rate and liquidity backdrop can fail even with the right cycle call.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The current and next cycle phases are established with leading indicators before rotating.
- [ ] The textbook rotation map is used as a hypothesis and questioned for regime fit.
- [ ] Sector categories, defensive, cyclical, growth, financial, are clarified and checked for regime-dependent behavior.
- [ ] The analysis checks whether the rotation has already been priced via relative performance, valuation, and positioning.
- [ ] Relative strength, breadth, and earnings revisions are used, not absolute price alone.
- [ ] Earnings-driven and multiple-driven rotations are distinguished.
- [ ] Lags, false signals, and whipsaw risk are managed with confirmation, sizing, and gradual implementation.
- [ ] Tax, transaction cost, and overtrading risks are considered.
- [ ] The rate, liquidity, and policy backdrop is incorporated, not only the cycle phase.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting rotation as a reliable timing tool and flags uncertainty, investor horizon, and professional advice.
