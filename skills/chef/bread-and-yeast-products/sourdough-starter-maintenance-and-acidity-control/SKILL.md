# Sourdough Starter Maintenance and Acidity Control

A sourdough starter is a symbiotic culture of yeast and lactic acid bacteria, and the judgment problem is that the culture's health — and therefore the bread's rise, flavor, and safety — depends on a balance that the cook cannot see and can only infer from behavior. The recurring failure is that cooks treat a starter as "flour, water, wait," without recognizing that the feeding ratio, the hydration, the temperature, and the flour determine which organisms dominate, and that a neglected or mismanaged starter shifts toward an acid-heavy, yeast-starved state that produces dense, sour, and sometimes unsafe bread. The discipline is to manage the starter as a living ecosystem, to read its signs (rise time, smell, acidity), and to intervene before the balance collapses.

The safety layer is specific and important: a sourdough starter's acidity (pH below 4.0) is the barrier that suppresses pathogens and unwanted organisms, and a starter that has lost its acidity — through neglect, contamination, or dilution — can harbor molds and, in rare but documented cases, conditions that allow harmful organisms. The agent's job is to maintain the acidity barrier, to recognize the signs of a compromised starter, and to discard a starter that has gone off rather than attempting to rescue a contaminated culture.

## Core Rules

### Feed The Starter On A Schedule Matched To Temperature And Use

A sourdough starter must be fed (refreshed with fresh flour and water) on a schedule that matches the fermentation speed, which depends on temperature: a warm starter (25-28°C) ferments fast and needs feeding every 8-12 hours; a room-temperature starter (20-22°C) needs feeding every 12-24 hours; a refrigerated starter needs weekly feeding. The feeding ratio (starter to fresh flour to water, by weight) controls how fast the culture consumes the new food and how acidic it becomes: a 1:1:1 feed ripens fast and is used for active maintenance; a 1:5:5 or 1:10:10 feed ripens slowly and is used to build a leaven overnight. Match the schedule and the ratio to the use: frequent small feeds for an active starter, a large-ratio build for an overnight leaven. A starter fed too infrequently starves and over-acidifies; fed too frequently with too little flour, it never ripens fully.

### Maintain The Acidity Barrier That Suppresses Unwanted Organisms

The starter's safety rests on its acidity: the lactic acid bacteria produce lactic and acetic acid, lowering the pH below 4.0, which suppresses molds, enteric pathogens, and spore-formers. A healthy ripe starter has a pH around 3.5-4.0 and a clean, sharp, yogurty-fruity aroma. The acidity barrier is maintained by regular feeding (which refreshes the flour's buffering capacity and the culture's acid production) and by avoiding dilution or neglect. A starter that has been unfed for a long time accumulates acid (the hooch layer, the sharp vinegar smell) and the yeast dies back, leaving an acid-heavy, weak culture; a starter that has been over-diluted or contaminated may lose its acidity and its protective barrier. Monitor the aroma and, if in doubt, the pH; a starter that smells putrid, cheesy-rotten, or moldy (rather than sharp and clean) has lost its balance and may be unsafe.

### Read The Starter's Signs To Judge Ripeness And Health

A ripe starter (ready to leaven bread or to be fed) has doubled in volume, is domed and aerated, smells clean-sharp-fruity, and passes the float test (a small dollop floats in water, indicating trapped gas). These signs, not the clock, indicate readiness. A starter that has risen and begun to fall (past peak) is over-ripe and more acidic; a starter that has not risen is under-ripe or sluggish. The smell indicates the balance: a balanced ripe starter smells of yogurt, fruit, and wheat; an acetic-heavy starter smells of vinegar or nail-polish remover (and produces a sharper, more sour bread); a contaminated starter smells putrid, musty, or of rot. Read these signs at every feeding and bake, and adjust the feeding ratio, temperature, or flour to shift the balance. A starter that consistently fails to rise or smells off needs intervention (more frequent feeding, warmer temperature, a different flour) or, if contaminated, discarding.

### Build The Leaven (Levain) For The Bake, Separate From The Maintenance Starter

For baking, build a separate leaven (levain) from a small amount of the maintenance starter, fed at a ratio and temperature chosen for the bake's schedule and flavor. A typical leaven is 1:5:5 or 1:10:10 (starter:flour:water), built 8-12 hours before the mix, ripened at the desired temperature, and used at peak rise. Building a separate leaven lets you control the leavening power and the acidity of the bake without disturbing the maintenance starter, and it lets you choose a flour (some bakers use a different flour, like rye, for the leaven) for flavor. The judgment point is that the leaven's ripeness and acidity directly determine the dough's fermentation speed, flavor, and structure, so the leaven build is a deliberate step, not an afterthought. Use the leaven at peak (doubled, domed, float-positive) for the strongest leavening.

### Choose The Flour To Shape The Starter's Ecology

The flour fed to the starter shapes its microbial ecology and its behavior. Whole-grain flours (whole wheat, rye) carry more fermentable sugars, more minerals, and more of the wild microorganisms, so they ferment faster and more vigorously and produce a more sour culture; white flour ferments more slowly and produces a milder culture. Rye is particularly active and is often used to build or revive a sluggish starter. Many bakers maintain a starter on a blend (e.g., mostly white with some whole wheat or rye) for balance. The judgment point is that changing the flour changes the starter, sometimes dramatically, so a starter moved from white to whole-grain will ferment faster and more sourly, and a starter moved from whole-grain to white may slow down. Document the maintenance flour and adjust the schedule when changing it.

### Recognize And Discard A Compromised Starter Rather Than Rescuing It

A starter can develop problems: contamination by mold (fuzzy growth on the surface, colored pink, orange, green, or black), putrid or rotten smell (indicating unwanted bacteria have taken over), or a complete loss of activity (no rise despite feeding). Surface mold can sometimes be salvaged if caught early (discard the top, take a clean core from below, rebuild with frequent feeds), but colored mold or a deeply contaminated, putrid starter should be discarded — the culture is compromised and the risk of carrying unwanted organisms into bread is real. A starter that has lost activity can often be revived with frequent small feeds of whole-grain flour at warm temperature, but if it refuses to revive after several days, discard and restart. The judgment is to value food safety over sentiment: a starter is replaceable, and a contaminated starter is not worth the risk.

## Common Traps

### Neglecting The Starter And Letting It Over-Acidify

A cook leaves the starter unfed for many days at room temperature, and a layer of dark liquid (hooch) forms and the starter smells sharply of vinegar or acetone. The mechanism is that the unfed culture exhausts its food, the yeast dies back, and the bacteria continue producing acid, shifting the balance toward an acid-heavy, yeast-starved state; the hooch is the alcohol and acid byproduct. The false signal is that the starter is "still alive" (it is, but weakened and over-acidic), so the cook uses it as-is. The harm is dense, overly sour bread with weak leavening, and a culture that takes several feeds to recover. The defense is to feed on a schedule matched to temperature, to refrigerate if not baking, and to revive a neglected starter with several frequent feeds before baking.

### Feeding By Volume And Inconsistent Ratios

A cook feeds the starter by scoops of flour and splashes of water, and the ratio varies every feeding, so the starter's consistency, fermentation speed, and behavior are unpredictable. The mechanism is that the feeding ratio controls ripening speed and acidity, and volume measurement is inaccurate enough that the ratio drifts, producing an inconsistent culture. The false signal is that the starter is "alive and rising," which reads as fine. The harm is inconsistent bread (one bake rises well, the next is dense) and an inability to diagnose problems. The defense is to feed by weight (starter:flour:water, e.g., 1:1:1 or 1:5:5), to document the ratio, and to maintain a consistent hydration (typically 100% for a liquid starter, 60-65% for a stiff starter).

### Using A Starter That Has Lost Its Acidity Barrier

A cook uses a starter that has been over-diluted (fed with too much water and flour relative to the culture) or that has been contaminated, and the starter's pH has risen above the protective 4.0, allowing unwanted organisms to grow. The mechanism is that the acidity barrier suppresses pathogens and molds, and a starter that has lost its acidity (through dilution, neglect, or contamination) can harbor organisms that a healthy starter would suppress. The false signal is that the starter rises (it may still rise, from residual yeast or from unwanted fermenters), so it reads as functional. The harm is bread carrying unwanted or potentially harmful organisms, and a culture that is progressively compromised. The defense is to maintain the acidity barrier through regular feeding, to monitor the aroma and (if in doubt) the pH, and to discard a starter that has lost its clean-sharp character or shows contamination.

### Judging Starter Readiness By The Clock

A cook builds a leaven and uses it after the recipe's stated 8 hours, regardless of the leaven's actual rise. The mechanism is that the leaven's ripening speed depends on temperature, ratio, and flour, so a stated time is correct only at the assumed conditions, and a cold leaven is under-ripe (weak leavening) while a warm one is over-ripe (over-acidic, collapsed) at the same clock time. The false signal is the elapsed time matching the recipe, which reads as "ready." The harm is under-leavened dense bread (cold/under-ripe leaven) or over-acidic collapsed bread (warm/over-ripe leaven). The defense is to judge the leaven by its signs (doubled, domed, float-positive) and to use it at peak, adjusting the build ratio or temperature to hit peak at the desired time.

### Changing The Maintenance Flour Without Adjusting The Schedule

A cook switches the starter from white flour to whole wheat or rye, and the starter suddenly ferments much faster and over-acidifies on the old schedule. The mechanism is that whole-grain flours carry more fermentables and microorganisms, so the culture ferments faster and more sourly, and the old feeding schedule is now too slow. The false signal is that "flour is flour," so the switch reads as neutral. The harm is an over-acidic, fast-fermenting starter that produces inconsistent bread. The defense is to recognize that the flour shapes the ecology, to adjust the feeding schedule and ratio when changing flour, and to document the maintenance flour.

### Rescuing A Moldy Or Putrid Starter Out Of Sentiment

A cook sees colored mold or smells putrid rot in a long-kept starter and, out of attachment, scoops off the top and continues using it. The mechanism is that colored mold (pink, orange, green, black) and putrid smell indicate a deeply compromised culture, and the unwanted organisms may have penetrated throughout; a surface scoop does not reliably remove them. The false signal is the cook's attachment and the partial recovery of rise, which read as "saved." The harm is bread carrying mold or unwanted organisms, and a culture that remains compromised. The defense is to value food safety over sentiment, to discard a starter with colored mold or putrid smell, and to restart from fresh flour and water (or a trusted dried starter), which takes only a week to re-establish.

## Self-Check

- Is the starter fed on a schedule matched to its temperature and use, with the feeding ratio (by weight) documented, rather than fed by volume and inconsistent scoops?
- Is the acidity barrier maintained through regular feeding, with the aroma (clean-sharp-fruity) and, if in doubt, the pH (below 4.0) monitored?
- Is starter and leaven readiness judged by signs (doubled, domed, float-positive, clean aroma) rather than by the clock?
- Is a separate leaven built for each bake at a deliberate ratio and temperature, and used at peak rise?
- Has the maintenance flour been chosen deliberately and the schedule adjusted when the flour changes, recognizing that whole-grain flours ferment faster and more sourly?
- Has a starter with colored mold or putrid smell been discarded rather than rescued, valuing food safety over sentiment?
- Have I avoided neglecting the starter to the point of hooch formation and over-acidification, refrigerating or feeding it appropriately when not baking?
- Is the reasoning documented so the feeding ratio, schedule, flour, and leaven build for each bake are verifiable?
