# Recipe Development and Testing Methodology

Recipe development is an experimental process, and the judgment problem is that a great dish is not born from a single attempt — it requires a structured methodology (a hypothesis, a test, a tasting, an iteration, repeated), disciplined documentation (so the iterations are tracked and the final recipe is reproducible), and a testing scope (testing for the one-off execution AND for the scaled production, the cost, the consistency, the shelf life) that matches how the dish will be used. The recurring failure is developing by feel (tweaking without tracking, so the iterations are lost and the final is irreproducible), or testing only for the one-off (the dish is great on the pass, but fails at scale, or is too costly, or is inconsistent across cooks), or not documenting the final (so the dish drifts as different cooks interpret it). The discipline is to develop with a methodology, to document the iterations, to test for the real-world use, and to document the final.

The agent's job is to develop recipes methodically, to test them rigorously, and to document them for reproducibility.

## Core Rules

### Develop With A Structured Methodology: Hypothesis, Test, Taste, Iterate

Recipe development should follow a structured methodology: a hypothesis (what are we trying to create, and why?), a test (cook the version), a tasting (evaluate against the hypothesis — flavor, texture, appearance, balance), and an iteration (adjust one variable, re-test), repeated until the dish meets the goal. The judgment point is that unstructured tweaking (changing multiple things at once, not tracking) does not converge (you can't tell what worked), and the structured methodology (one variable at a time, tracked) converges. Develop with the methodology (hypothesis, test, taste, iterate one variable), track each iteration, and converge on the goal. The scientific method applies to the kitchen.

### Document Each Iteration (The Variables And The Results)

Each iteration must be documented (the variables changed, the amounts, the method, the tasting results), so the iterations are tracked (what worked, what didn't) and the final is reproducible. The judgment point is that undocumented iterations are lost (you can't reproduce the success or avoid the failure), and the documentation (the development log) is the record. Document each iteration (the recipe version, the variables changed, the tasting notes), keep the development log, and use it to converge and to reproduce. The documentation is the development's memory.

### Test For The Real-World Use: Scale, Cost, Consistency, Shelf Life

A recipe must be tested for how it will be used, not just the one-off execution: the scale (does it work at 50 portions? — see the scaling skill), the cost (does the plate cost fit the target? — see the costing skill), the consistency (can different cooks produce the same result? — the documentation and the technique), and the shelf life (does it hold? for how long? — see the holding skill). The judgment point is that a dish that's great on the pass but fails at scale, or is too costly, or is inconsistent, or doesn't hold, is not a viable menu item; the real-world testing is required. Test the recipe for the scale, the cost, the consistency, and the shelf life, and adjust until it's viable for the real use.

### Test With A Structured Tasting Panel

The tasting should be structured (a panel of tasters — the chef, the sous, a cook, a server, and ideally a non-kitchen perspective), with a consistent evaluation (a scorecard — flavor, texture, appearance, balance, originality), and honest feedback (not just "it's good"). The judgment point is that a single taster (the chef) has biases, and a structured panel (multiple perspectives, a scorecard, honest feedback) gives a truer evaluation. Taste with a structured panel (multiple tasters, a scorecard), seek honest feedback (not polite approval), and use the evaluation to iterate. The non-kitchen perspective (a server, a manager) is valuable (it represents the guest).

### Document The Final Recipe (Reproducible And Scalable)

The final recipe must be documented (the exact ingredients, amounts, method, portion, presentation, with photos), so it's reproducible (any cook can make it to the standard) and scalable (the amounts and the method work at scale). The judgment point is that an undocumented final drifts (each cook interprets), and the documentation (the recipe book entry, with photos) is the standard. Document the final recipe (ingredients, amounts, method, portion, presentation, photos), enter it in the recipe book, train the staff, and use it as the single source of truth. See the training skill for the documentation and the training.

### Pilot The Dish Before The Full Launch

Before the full menu launch, pilot the dish (offer it as a special, for a limited time), to test it with the real guests (do they order it? do they like it? what's the feedback?), the real production (can the kitchen execute it consistently under service?), and the real cost (does the plate cost hit the target?). The judgment point is that a full launch without a pilot risks a failure (the dish doesn't sell, or the kitchen can't execute, or the cost is off), and the pilot (a low-risk test) reveals these. Pilot the dish (as a special), gather the guest and the operational feedback, adjust, and launch fully when it's proven.

## Common Traps

### Developing By Feel And Losing The Iterations

A chef develops by feel (tweaking without tracking), and the iterations are lost (can't tell what worked), and the final is irreproducible. The mechanism is that unstructured tweaking (multiple variables at once, no tracking) does not converge (can't identify the effective change), and the structured methodology (one variable, tracked) does; the documentation is required. The false signal is that the dish "is evolving," which reads as developing. The harm is an irreproducible, non-convergent dish. The defense is to develop with the methodology (one variable at a time, tracked), to document each iteration, and to converge.

### Testing Only The One-Off And Missing The Scale Or Cost Failure

A chef tests the dish only as a one-off (on the pass, for the chef), and it's great, but it fails at scale (the technique doesn't multiply), or the cost is too high, or it's inconsistent across cooks. The mechanism is that a dish must be tested for the real-world use (scale, cost, consistency, shelf life), and a one-off test misses these; the real-world testing is required. The false signal is that the dish "is great," which reads as ready. The harm is a dish that fails in production. The defense is to test for the scale, the cost, the consistency, and the shelf life, and to adjust until viable.

### Tasting Alone And Missing The Biases

A chef tastes the dish alone (the chef's own palate), and the biases (the chef's preferences, the familiarity) skew the evaluation. The mechanism is that a single taster has biases, and a structured panel (multiple perspectives, a scorecard, honest feedback) gives a truer evaluation; the panel is required. The false signal is that the chef's palate "is refined," which reads as sufficient. The harm is a biased evaluation and a dish that misses the guest. The defense is to taste with a structured panel (multiple tasters, a scorecard), to seek honest feedback, and to include a non-kitchen perspective.

### Not Documenting The Final And Letting It Drift

A chef develops the dish but does not document the final (the exact recipe), and the dish drifts as different cooks interpret it. The mechanism is that an undocumented final drifts (each cook interprets), and the documentation (the recipe book entry, with photos) is the standard; the documentation is required. The false signal is that the dish "is set," which reads as finalized. The harm is drifting execution. The defense is to document the final (ingredients, amounts, method, portion, presentation, photos), to enter it in the recipe book, to train the staff, and to use it as the single source.

### Launching Without A Pilot And Risking A Failure

A chef launches the dish on the full menu without a pilot, and it fails (the guests don't order it, or the kitchen can't execute, or the cost is off), wasting the development and the menu space. The mechanism is that a full launch without a pilot risks a failure (untested with the real guests and the real production), and the pilot (a low-risk test) reveals these; the pilot is required. The false signal is that the dish "is tested," which reads as ready. The harm is a failed launch. The defense is to pilot the dish (as a special), to gather the feedback, to adjust, and to launch fully when proven.

### Over-Iterating And Never Converging

A chef over-iterates (endlessly tweaking, never satisfied), and the dish never converges (never makes it to the menu). The mechanism is that the iteration must converge (toward a defined goal), and over-iteration (without a convergence criterion) never finishes; the goal and the convergence criterion are required. The false signal is that the dish "is improving," which reads as progressing. The harm is a dish that never launches. The defense is to define the goal and the convergence criterion (when is it "done"?), to iterate toward it, and to launch when the criterion is met.

## Self-Check

- Is the recipe developed with a structured methodology (hypothesis, test, taste, iterate one variable), tracked in a development log?
- Is each iteration documented (variables changed, amounts, method, tasting notes), so the iterations are tracked and the final is reproducible?
- Is the recipe tested for the real-world use (scale, cost, consistency, shelf life), not just the one-off execution?
- Is the tasting structured (a panel of tasters, a scorecard, honest feedback, a non-kitchen perspective)?
- Is the final recipe documented (ingredients, amounts, method, portion, presentation, photos), entered in the recipe book, and used as the single source of truth?
- Is the dish piloted (as a special) before the full launch, with guest and operational feedback gathered?
- Is there a convergence criterion (when is it "done"), and does the iteration converge and launch?
- Is the reasoning documented so the methodology, documentation, real-world testing, panel tasting, final documentation, piloting, and convergence for each recipe are verifiable?
