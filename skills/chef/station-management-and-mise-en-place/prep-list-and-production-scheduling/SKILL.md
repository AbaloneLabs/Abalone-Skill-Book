# Prep List and Production Scheduling

A prep list is a production plan disguised as a checklist, and the order, assignment, and timing of its items determine whether the kitchen opens ready or opens behind. This skill covers the judgment of how to sequence, assign, and time prep so that long-lead items finish in time, perishable items are prepped close to service, and the right cook does the right task at the right skill level. The harm this skill prevents is the chronic condition of kitchens that start every service behind because prep was listed but not scheduled, leaving the line to cook and catch up simultaneously, which produces slow tickets, inconsistent plates, and exhausted staff.

## Core Rules

### Build the prep list from the menu's component requirements, not from memory

The prep list must be derived from what every dish on the menu requires, calculated against expected covers, so that no component is missed and quantities are right-sized. Start from the menu, list every component, multiply by projected covers and by the recipe yield, and produce a quantified prep list. A prep list built from memory or from yesterday's list will miss new items, over- or under-produce based on stale assumptions, and leave gaps that surface during service. The menu is the source of truth for what must be prepped.

### Sequence prep by lead time, longest first

Items with long cooking, brining, fermenting, or chilling times must start first; items that are quick or perishable are prepped last. Sequence the list so that the longest-lead items are at the top and begin immediately, and so that the sequence naturally delivers each component to service-ready state at the right time. Sequencing by lead time prevents the common failure of discovering at service that a long-lead item was never started because it was buried mid-list.

### Assign tasks by skill level and by station ownership

Match each prep task to the cook whose skill can execute it correctly and who owns the station that uses it. A task assigned to a cook who lacks the skill produces defective prep that is discovered during service; a task assigned to a cook who does not own the station produces prep that does not match the station's actual needs. Assign critical and technique-sensitive tasks to the most skilled cooks, and assign repetitive tasks to less experienced cooks, but ensure that every cook owns and verifies the prep for their own station.

### Build in verification checkpoints before service

The prep list must include verification points where a lead checks that each component is complete, correct, and portioned before service. Verification catches the prep that was done incorrectly, the component that was missed, and the quantity that is wrong, before these become service failures. A prep list without checkpoints relies on the cook's self-assessment, which under pressure reads as done what is actually incomplete.

### Size prep quantities to projected covers with a defined buffer

Quantities should be calculated from projected covers and recipe yields, with a defined buffer for variability, typically ten to twenty percent depending on the component's perishability and the cost of running out. A buffer that is too small risks running out mid-service; a buffer that is too large creates waste. Define the buffer per component based on its cost, perishability, and the consequence of running out, and adjust the buffer based on actual usage over time.

### Account for shared equipment and bottleneck resources

Prep scheduling must account for shared resources: the oven, the braiser, the fryer, the walk-in space. If multiple tasks need the same oven, sequence them so they do not collide, or schedule them at different times. A prep list that ignores shared resources will create bottlenecks where tasks compete for the same equipment and one waits on another, delaying the whole list. Map shared resources and schedule around them.

### Update the prep list from actual service feedback

After each service, note what ran out, what was over-prepped, and what was prepped incorrectly, and feed that back into the next prep list. The prep list is a living document that should converge on accuracy over time. A static prep list repeated daily will drift from actual demand and accumulate waste or shortages.

## Common Traps

### Building the prep list from yesterday's list instead of from the menu

This is a trap because copying yesterday's list feels efficient but carries forward stale assumptions about covers, menu mix, and component needs, so the prep drifts from what the service actually demands. The mechanism is that the menu mix changes daily, specials rotate, and projected covers vary, so a list built from yesterday misses new components, over-preps items that are no longer moving, and under-preps items that are gaining popularity. The false signal is that yesterday's list mostly worked, which the cook reads as reliable. The harm is chronic over- and under-prep that creates waste and shortages simultaneously, and a kitchen that never converges on accurate production because it is always cooking to a stale plan. The correct response is to rebuild the prep list from the menu and projected covers each day, using yesterday's list only as a reference, not as the source.

### Listing tasks without sequencing by lead time

This trap operates because a prep list that is merely a checklist can be worked in any order, and a cook will naturally start with the easy or visible tasks, leaving long-lead items until too late to finish before service. The mechanism is that long-lead items, stocks, braises, doughs, brines, require hours and must start at the beginning of the shift, but an unsequenced list treats them as equal to a five-minute task, so they get deferred and are incomplete at service. The false signal is that all items are listed, which reads as planned. The harm is the discovery at service that a critical long-lead component is half-done, forcing the kitchen to either delay service or serve without the component. The correct response is to sequence the list by lead time, longest first, so that the items that need the most time begin immediately and the sequence naturally delivers each component to readiness at the right moment.

### Assigning critical tasks to the wrong skill level

This trap exists because prep assignment is often done by availability rather than by skill, so a technique-sensitive task like emulsifying a sauce or tempering chocolate is given to whoever is free, producing defective prep that is discovered during service when it is too late to fix. The mechanism is that critical tasks require specific technique and judgment, and a cook without that skill will execute the steps but produce a substandard result, a broken sauce, an over-whipped meringue, an uneven brunoise, that looks done but fails in service. The false signal is that the task was completed, which reads as progress. The harm is defective components that undermine dishes at service, requiring remakes that delay tickets and waste ingredients. The correct response is to assign critical and technique-sensitive tasks to cooks with the proven skill, to verify their output at a checkpoint, and to assign less experienced cooks to repetitive tasks with clear standards and supervision.

### Skipping verification checkpoints under time pressure

This is a trap because when the shift is behind, verification feels like a luxury, so the lead skips the checks and the kitchen opens with incomplete or incorrect prep that fails during service, costing far more time than the check would have. The mechanism is that verification catches errors before they compound, and skipping it lets defective or missing prep flow into service where each error becomes a remake, a delayed ticket, or a guest complaint. The false signal is that the cooks reported their prep as done, which the lead reads as reliable. The harm is service failures that are more expensive and more visible than the verification would have been, and a culture where done means claimed rather than verified. The correct response is to treat verification as non-negotiable, to build checkpoints into the timeline, and to verify by physical inspection and tasting, not by asking whether it is done.

### Sizing quantities by feel instead of by projected covers and yields

This trap operates because experienced cooks estimate quantities by intuition, and intuition is biased by recent memory and by the fear of running out, so quantities are consistently off in one direction or the other, creating waste or shortages. The mechanism is that feel-based sizing ignores the actual relationship between covers, recipe yield, and portion size, so the prep quantity is a guess that is sometimes too much and sometimes too little, with no feedback loop to correct it. The false signal is that the cook is experienced, which the kitchen reads as reliable estimation. The harm is chronic waste from over-prep and chronic crises from under-prep, neither of which is corrected because the estimation is never checked against calculation. The correct response is to calculate quantities from projected covers and recipe yields, to apply a defined buffer, and to adjust the calculation from actual usage data over time.

### Ignoring shared equipment bottlenecks in the schedule

This trap exists because a prep list schedules tasks as if each has its own resources, but shared equipment, the oven, the braiser, the fryer, the walk-in, can only serve one task at a time, so tasks collide and one waits on another, delaying the whole list. The mechanism is that without resource scheduling, multiple tasks are planned for the same oven at the same time, and the cook must choose which to delay, cascading the delay through the rest of the list. The false signal is that all tasks are scheduled, which reads as planned. The harm is that the list falls behind because of resource contention that was invisible at the planning stage, and the kitchen opens with incomplete prep. The correct response is to map shared resources, to schedule tasks around equipment availability, to stagger oven and braiser loads, and to treat equipment capacity as a scheduling constraint equal to labor.

### Repeating the same prep list without incorporating service feedback

This is a trap because a prep list that is never updated repeats the same errors, over-prepping the same items and under-prepping the same others, so the kitchen never converges on accurate production and the waste and shortages persist indefinitely. The mechanism is that without feedback from actual service, the prep list is a static document that encodes the cook's initial assumptions, which may be wrong, and those wrong assumptions are replicated every day. The false signal is that the list generally works, which the kitchen reads as good enough. The harm is chronic inefficiency, consistent waste from over-prep and consistent crises from under-prep, that erodes margins and morale because the kitchen is always fighting the same fires. The correct response is to capture after-service feedback on what ran out, what was over-prepped, and what was incorrect, and to adjust the next prep list accordingly, so the list converges on accuracy over time.

## Self-Check

- Did I build the prep list from the menu's component requirements and projected covers, rather than copying yesterday's list?
- Did I sequence the list by lead time, so the longest-lead items start first and finish before service?
- Did I assign critical and technique-sensitive tasks to cooks with proven skill, and verify their output?
- Did I build verification checkpoints where a lead physically inspects and tastes prep before service?
- Did I size quantities by calculation from covers and yields with a defined buffer, rather than by feel?
- Did I account for shared equipment and schedule tasks to avoid resource collisions?
- Did I capture after-service feedback and adjust the next prep list so it converges on accuracy?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
