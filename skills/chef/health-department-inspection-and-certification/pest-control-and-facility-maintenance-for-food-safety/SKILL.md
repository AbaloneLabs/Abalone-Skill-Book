# Pest Control and Facility Maintenance for Food Safety

Pests (rodents, cockroaches, flies, stored-product pests) are a critical food safety threat — they contaminate food and surfaces with pathogens and physical debris, they signal poor sanitation and facility disrepair to inspectors, and an infestation can close a kitchen — and the judgment problem is that pest control is not just reacting to sightings (setting traps when a mouse is seen) but an integrated system of exclusion (sealing entry points), sanitation (removing food and water sources), monitoring (traps and inspections), and professional treatment, supported by facility maintenance (cracked tiles, leaky pipes, gaps under doors are pest havens). The recurring failure is reacting to pests instead of preventing them, or treating the symptom (spraying cockroaches) without fixing the cause (the grease buildup, the crack in the wall). The discipline is integrated pest management (IPM): exclude, sanitize, monitor, treat, and maintain the facility.

The agent's job is to prevent pests through IPM, to maintain the facility, and to act immediately on signs.

## Core Rules

### Implement Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

Integrated Pest Management (IPM) is the professional approach: exclude the pests (seal entry points), remove the attractants (sanitation, no food or water sources), monitor (traps, inspections), and treat (professional, targeted, least-toxic) only as needed. The judgment point is that IPM prevents infestations (through exclusion and sanitation) rather than reacting to them, and the treatment is targeted (not blanket spraying, which is a chemical hazard). Implement IPM: seal the entry points (gaps under doors, cracks, pipes), maintain rigorous sanitation (no grease, no food debris, no standing water), set monitoring traps (checked regularly), and use a professional pest control service for targeted treatment.

### Exclude Pests By Sealing Entry Points

Pests enter through gaps and cracks (under doors, around pipes, through vents, in deliveries), and exclusion (sealing the entry points) is the first line of defense. The judgment point is that exclusion prevents entry, and a kitchen with open entry points will be infested regardless of treatment. Seal the gaps (door sweeps under doors, caulk around pipes, screens on vents and windows), inspect the deliveries (pests hitchhike in), and maintain the seals. The most common entry points are under doors (need sweeps), around pipes (need sealing), and in deliveries (need inspection).

### Remove Attractants Through Rigorous Sanitation

Pests are attracted to food, grease, and water, and rigorous sanitation (cleaning the grease, the food debris, the standing water) removes the attractants. The judgment point is that sanitation is pest control (a clean kitchen has few pests), and grease buildup, food debris, and standing water sustain infestations. Clean rigorously (daily cleaning of floors, drains, equipment; deep cleaning of grease traps, under equipment, behind ovens), eliminate standing water (fix leaks, dry the floors), and manage the trash (sealed containers, frequent removal). Drains (with grease and organic buildup) are a major cockroach and fruit-fly haven; clean them regularly.

### Monitor With Traps And Regular Inspections

Monitoring (glue boards, mechanical traps, pheromone traps, and regular inspections of the hotspots) detects pests early, before an infestation. The judgment point is that monitoring is the early warning, and a kitchen that does not monitor discovers the infestation late (when it's established). Set monitoring traps (glue boards for insects, mechanical traps for rodents) in the hotspots (along walls, near drains, near entry points), check them regularly (weekly), and inspect the hotspots (droppings, gnaw marks, egg cases) during cleaning. A pest log (sightings, trap catches, actions) tracks the activity.

### Use Professional Pest Control For Treatment

A professional pest control service (on a regular contract, not just emergency) provides the targeted treatment (insecticides, rodenticides, placed safely and legally) and the expertise (identifying the pest, locating the nest, recommending the structural fixes). The judgment point is that the treatment is professional (safe, legal, effective) and regular (preventive, not just emergency), and a kitchen that self-treats (over-the-counter sprays) risks chemical hazards and ineffective treatment. Contract a licensed pest control service (regular visits, emergency response), follow their recommendations (structural fixes, sanitation), and keep the service records (for inspection).

### Maintain The Facility To Prevent Pest Havens

Facility disrepair (cracked tiles, gaps in walls, leaky pipes, broken screens, cluttered storage) creates pest havens (hiding and breeding sites), and the maintenance (repairs, decluttering) is pest control. The judgment point is that the facility condition and the pest risk are linked, and a disrepaired kitchen is infested regardless of treatment. Maintain the facility (repair cracks, fix leaks, replace screens, declutter the storage), and address the disrepair promptly. Cracked tiles and coved-base gaps are cockroach havens; leaky pipes provide water; clutter provides hiding.

## Common Traps

### Reacting To Pests Instead Of Preventing (No IPM)

A chef reacts to pests (sets traps when a mouse is seen, sprays when cockroaches appear) instead of preventing them, and the infestations recur. The mechanism is that reactive pest control treats the symptom (the sighting) without fixing the cause (the entry point, the attractant), and the infestations recur; IPM (exclusion, sanitation, monitoring) prevents them. The false signal is that the pest "is gone," which reads as controlled. The harm is recurring infestations. The defense is to implement IPM (exclude, sanitize, monitor, treat), to prevent rather than react, and to fix the causes.

### Not Sealing Entry Points And Being Continuously Re-Infested

A kitchen treats the pests but does not seal the entry points (the gap under the door, the crack around the pipe), and the pests re-enter continuously. The mechanism is that the entry points allow continuous entry, and without exclusion (sealing), the treatment is overwhelmed by re-entry; exclusion is the first line of defense. The false signal is that the treatment "is working," which reads as controlled. The harm is continuous re-infestation. The defense is to seal the entry points (door sweeps, caulk, screens), to inspect deliveries, and to recognize exclusion as primary.

### Grease And Debris Buildup Sustaining An Infestation

A kitchen has grease buildup (under equipment, in drains, behind ovens) and food debris, and the pests are sustained by it despite treatment. The mechanism is that grease and food debris are pest attractants and food sources, and they sustain the infestation; rigorous sanitation (removing the attractants) is pest control. The false signal is that the kitchen "is clean," which reads as sanitary. The harm is a sustained infestation. The defense is to clean rigorously (grease, debris, drains), to eliminate standing water, and to recognize sanitation as pest control.

### Not Monitoring And Discovering The Infestation Late

A kitchen does not monitor (no traps, no inspections), and an infestation is discovered late (when it's established and visible), requiring extensive treatment. The mechanism is that monitoring (traps, inspections) detects pests early, and without it, the infestation is discovered late (when it's harder to eliminate); monitoring is the early warning. The false signal is that no pests "are seen," which reads as clear. The harm is a late-discovered, established infestation. The defense is to set monitoring traps (checked weekly), to inspect the hotspots during cleaning, and to keep a pest log.

### Self-Treating With Over-The-Counter Sprays And Causing Chemical Hazards

A chef self-treats with over-the-counter insecticide sprays (instead of a professional), and the sprays contaminate the food surfaces (chemical hazard) and are often ineffective. The mechanism is that the treatment must be professional (safe, legal, targeted) and over-the-counter sprays risk chemical contamination (of food surfaces) and are often misapplied; a licensed service is required. The false signal is that the spray "kills pests," which reads as effective. The harm is chemical contamination and ineffective treatment. The defense is to use a licensed pest control service (safe, targeted treatment), to not self-treat with over-the-counter sprays, and to keep the service records.

### Ignoring Facility Disrepair And Creating Pest Havens

A kitchen ignores the cracked tiles, the leaky pipe, the cluttered storage, and these create pest havens that sustain infestations despite treatment. The mechanism is that facility disrepair (cracks, leaks, clutter) provides hiding and breeding sites, and the pests persist in these havens; facility maintenance is pest control. The false signal is that the disrepair "is cosmetic," which reads as low-priority. The harm is sustained infestations. The defense is to maintain the facility (repair cracks, fix leaks, declutter), to address the disrepair promptly, and to recognize the facility-pest link.

## Self-Check

- Is Integrated Pest Management (IPM) implemented (exclude, sanitize, monitor, treat), preventing rather than reacting?
- Are the entry points sealed (door sweeps, caulk around pipes, screens on vents), and are deliveries inspected?
- Is the sanitation rigorous (grease, debris, drains, standing water removed), eliminating the attractants?
- Are monitoring traps set (glue boards, mechanical traps) in the hotspots, checked weekly, with a pest log kept?
- Is a licensed pest control service contracted (regular visits, emergency response, service records kept)?
- Is the facility maintained (cracks repaired, leaks fixed, screens replaced, storage decluttered), eliminating pest havens?
- Is the reasoning documented so the IPM, exclusion, sanitation, monitoring, professional treatment, and facility maintenance for the pest control are verifiable?
