# Coffee, Tea, And Non-Alcoholic Beverage Programs

Non-alcoholic beverage programs are treated as an afterthought — a capsule machine, a box of tea bags, a bottle of juice — and that is exactly where they fail. Coffee and tea are extraction-dependent products whose flavor is determined by grind, dose, water, temperature, time, and freshness, and a program that ignores these serves bitter, sour, or stale drinks that undercut the meal they accompany. But the failures that actually cost a business are the ones chefs rarely consider: an espresso dialed in once and never re-tuned as the beans age, so every shot after the first day is wrong; a "decaf" or "caffeine-free" claim that is wrong because the tea blend contains caffeinated ingredients; a specialty zero-proof drink whose nut-based syrup carries an undisclosed allergen; and dairy and plant milks held at unsafe temperature or steamed in a wand that is never cleaned, growing bacteria served hot to the guest. The judgment problem is that these drinks are technical extractions and food-safety surfaces disguised as simple service, and they must be designed and supervised with the same rigor as the kitchen.

Use this skill when building a coffee or espresso program, designing a tea service, creating a non-alcoholic beverage menu, writing brewing and extraction standards, selecting equipment, or training staff on specialty beverage service.

## Core Rules

### Control Extraction Through Dose, Grind, Water, Temperature, And Time

Every brewed coffee and tea is an extraction, and its flavor is the product of five controllable variables: the dose of coffee or tea, the grind or leaf size, the water quality and mineral content, the water temperature, and the contact time. For espresso, a shot is dialed in by adjusting grind to hit a target dose (e.g. 18 g) yielding a target mass (e.g. 36 g) in a target time (e.g. 25–30 seconds); a shot that runs too fast is under-extracted (sour, weak) and a shot that runs too slow is over-extracted (bitter, astringent), and the grind is the primary adjustment. For filter and immersion coffee, control the ratio (typically 1:15 to 1:17 coffee to water by weight), the grind matched to the method, and the water temperature (lighter roasts want hotter, around 93–96°C, to extract fully; darker roasts want slightly cooler to avoid bitterness). For tea, match water temperature to the tea type (delicate green and white teas want 70–80°C to avoid cooking the leaf into bitterness; oolongs 85–95°C; black and herbal 95–100°C) and time the steep (greens 1–3 minutes, oolongs and blacks 3–5 minutes, herbals longer). Treat extraction as a recipe with measured variables, not a pour-and-hope routine.

### Dial In Espresso Daily And Re-Tune As Beans Age

Espresso is the most extraction-sensitive coffee method and the most commonly mistuned, because the beans change as they age. Coffee beans off-gas CO2 for days to weeks after roasting, and as they degas the extraction speeds up, so a grind that produced a correct shot on Monday produces a fast, sour shot by Thursday. Dial in the espresso at the start of every service: pull a shot, taste it, adjust the grind finer to slow a sour shot or coarser to fix a bitter one, and re-pull until the shot is balanced, then re-check periodically through service as the beans, humidity, and machine temperature drift. Document the dial-in (dose, yield, time, grind setting) so the standard is reproducible, and train the bar to taste, not just to time. An espresso dialed in once is correct for one day; a program that does not re-tune serves wrong shots every day after.

### Use Water Quality Matched To The Beverage

Water is 98 percent of a brewed coffee and the entire medium of tea, and its mineral content determines both extraction and flavor. Coffee and tea want clean, filtered water with a balanced mineral content — total dissolved solids around 75–150 ppm, with some hardness (calcium, magnesium) to carry flavor and extract compounds, but not so much that it scales equipment or tastes mineral-heavy. Distilled or reverse-osmosis water without remineralization extracts poorly and tastes flat; very hard water scales espresso machines, restricts flow, and tastes chalky. Use a filtration system appropriate to the local water, remineralize reverse-osmosis water for coffee and tea, and descale and maintain equipment on a schedule. Serve tea and coffee brewed with water that is itself a quality ingredient, not tap water assumed to be fine.

### Manage Freshness Of Beans, Leaves, And Grounds

Coffee and tea are perishable products whose flavor degrades with time and exposure, and the degradation is invisible until tasted. Coffee beans are best from roughly one week to one month after roasting (too fresh and they are too gassy and uneven; too old and they are stale and flat), and should be stored sealed, away from light, heat, and oxygen; ground coffee stales within minutes, so grind to order, never pre-grind into a hopper for the day. Tea leaves degrade more slowly but lose aromatic volatiles over months, especially once opened, and should be stored sealed in a cool, dark, dry place away from strong odors (tea absorbs surrounding smells). Date and rotate stock, use beans and leaves within their peak window, and discard stale product rather than serving flat drinks. Freshness is an ingredient.

### Build Ratios And Recipes For Non-Alcoholic Drinks As Deliberately As Cocktails

Zero-proof and non-alcoholic beverages deserve the same recipe discipline as cocktails: measured ratios of acid, sweet, bitter, and aromatic, costed components, and balanced flavor. A common failure is to serve a "mocktail" that is simply juice and syrup — cloyingly sweet and unbalanced — instead of a constructed drink with acidity, length, and complexity (verjus or vinegar shrubs for acid, tea or infused water for body, herbs and bitters for aroma). Cost every component, standardize the recipe by weight or measure, and price from documented cost. Treat the non-alcoholic menu as a real program, not a courtesy, because a growing segment of guests order it and a weak program forfeits that revenue and goodwill.

### Disclose Caffeine Content And Verify Caffeine-Free Claims

Caffeine claims are a safety and trust issue, not a flavor note. A guest ordering "decaf" coffee expects minimal caffeine, and a guest ordering "caffeine-free" or "herbal" tea expects zero caffeine — but many teas sold as "herbal" or blended contain caffeinated ingredients (a "berry tea" that includes green or black tea base, a chai blend with black tea), and decaf coffee still contains a small amount of caffeine (decaf is not caffeine-free). Maintain accurate caffeine information for every beverage: label true caffeine-free options (tisanes like chamomile, peppermint, rooibos), distinguish low-caffeine (decaf coffee, which has 2–5 mg per cup) from caffeine-free, and verify tea blend ingredients against the supplier spec rather than assuming a blend is caffeine-free from its name. Train staff to answer caffeine questions accurately, because guests managing pregnancy, medication interactions, anxiety, or caffeine sensitivity rely on the answer. A wrong caffeine claim is a health claim served to a vulnerable guest.

### Hold Dairy, Plant Milks, And Ingredients To Food-Safety Standards

The coffee and beverage station is a food-prep surface with real perishable-food and cross-contamination risks that are rarely supervised like the kitchen. Dairy milk and cream are perishable and must be held at or below 5°C / 41°F, dated, and used within their open shelf life; plant milks have their own perishability and allergen profiles (soy, almond, oat, coconut are major allergens). Steaming wands that are not purged and wiped between uses accumulate milk protein and grow bacteria that are steamed into the next drink, so purge and wipe the wand after every use and deep-clean it on a schedule. Identify and disclose allergens in every specialty syrup and modifier (nut-based syrups, dairy, soy), use clean tools between allergen-containing and allergen-free drinks, and wash garnish produce. The beverage station is a kitchen and is held to kitchen food-safety and allergen standards.

### Match Equipment To Volume And Maintain It On Schedule

Specialty beverage quality depends on equipment that is matched to volume and maintained. An espresso machine must be sized to the peak volume so recovery time does not collapse temperature during a rush; grinders must be matched to the method (burr grinders for espresso and filter, never blade grinders that produce uneven extraction); tea needs accurate temperature-controlled water, not a boiling tap that scorches green tea; and water filtration must be sized and serviced. Maintain equipment on a schedule — backflush and clean espresso group heads and portafilters daily to prevent rancid coffee-oil buildup that taints shots, descale per water hardness, replace grinder burrs when worn, and calibrate thermometers. Equipment that is not maintained produces inconsistent or tainted drinks regardless of the bar's skill. The machine is part of the recipe.

## Common Traps

### Dialing In Espresso Once And Never Re-Tuning

A bar dials in the espresso when a new bag of beans arrives, sets the grind, and serves from that setting for the whole week, so by day three every shot is fast and sour because the beans have degassed and the extraction has sped up. The false signal is that the grind was set by a skilled barista and the shot time looks about right. The mechanism is that coffee beans off-gas CO2 continuously after roasting, and as they degas the puck offers less resistance and the shot runs faster, extracting less and tasting sour; humidity and machine temperature also shift extraction day to day, so a static grind is correct for one moment and wrong for every moment after. The harm is that the espresso program serves poor shots to every guest after the first day, undermining a program the business invested in. The defense is to dial in at the start of every service, to taste and adjust the grind through the shift, and to document the dial-in so the standard is reproducible. A shot is dialed in for today, not for the bag.

### Brewing All Teas At Boiling Water

A server fills a pot with boiling water and drops in every tea — green, white, oolong, black — the same way, and the delicate green tea comes out bitter and astringent because the boiling water cooked the leaf. The false signal is that boiling water is hot enough and "tea is tea." The mechanism is that delicate green and white teas are made of tender leaves whose amino acids and catechins extract bitterly at boiling temperature, so water at 100°C over-extracts the bitter compounds and destroys the sweet, umami notes the tea is valued for; only robust black teas and most herbals want a full boil. The harm is that the tea program serves flawed cups of its most delicate and often most expensive teas, and guests conclude the tea is bad when the service was wrong. The defense is to match water temperature to tea type (greens and whites 70–80°C, oolongs 85–95°C, blacks and herbals 95–100°C), to use a temperature-controlled kettle, and to time the steep. Boiling water is correct for one tea, not all of them.

### Calling A Blended Tea "Caffeine-Free" Without Checking Ingredients

A program labels a fruit and floral blend "caffeine-free herbal tea" based on its name and appearance, but the blend contains a green or black tea base for body, and serves it to a caffeine-sensitive or pregnant guest who has a reaction or medication interaction. The false signal is that the tea is fruity and herbal-looking, so it must be caffeine-free. The mechanism is that many commercial tea blends marketed as fruit, floral, or "wellness" include a caffeinated true-tea (Camellia sinensis) base for flavor and body, and the caffeine content is invisible in the cup and often buried in the ingredient list, so a caffeine-free claim based on the blend's name or appearance is unreliable. The harm is a health-claim failure served to a guest relying on it, with the same trust and liability weight as an allergen failure. The defense is to verify every tea blend's ingredients against the supplier spec, to distinguish true caffeine-free tisanes (chamomile, peppermint, rooibos, hibiscus) from blends that contain true tea, and to train staff to answer caffeine questions from the spec, not from the name. A fruit tea is not necessarily a caffeine-free tea.

### Serving Stale Coffee From Pre-Ground Hopper Or Old Beans

A cafe fills the grinder hopper with ground coffee at the start of the day, or keeps beans in an open hopper for weeks, and serves flat, stale coffee all shift because the grounds oxidized within minutes of grinding or the beans staled over weeks. The false signal is that the coffee was ground "fresh this morning" and the beans look fine. The mechanism is that ground coffee exposes vastly more surface area to oxygen and stales within minutes to hours, losing the volatile aromatics that make coffee taste alive, and whole beans in an open hopper stale over days to weeks as they off-gas and oxidize; the result is a cup that tastes flat, papery, or rancid regardless of the brew method. The harm is a coffee program that tastes stale even with good beans and equipment, wasting the investment. The defense is to grind to order for every cup, to store beans sealed and dated within their peak window (roughly one week to one month post-roast), and to discard stale product. Pre-ground coffee is stale coffee.

### Steaming Milk Without Purging And Cleaning The Wand

A barista steams milk drink after drink without purging or wiping the wand between uses, and the wand accumulates a crust of cooked milk protein that grows bacteria and is steamed into the next drink, tainting the flavor and posing a food-safety risk. The false signal is that the wand looks clean and the milk is heated hot enough to be safe. The mechanism is that milk protein cooks onto the steam wand at high temperature, forming a residue that harbors bacteria, and the next steam injects that residue and its bacterial load into fresh milk; the heating temperature of steamed milk (around 60–70°C) is not a sterilization step and does not kill all organisms, so the contamination is served into the drink. The harm is off-flavors in the milk drinks and a real food-safety risk from bacterial contamination. The defense is to purge the wand with steam and wipe it with a clean cloth after every single use, to deep-clean the wand and its tip on a schedule, and to hold the beverage station to kitchen food-safety standards. The steam wand is a food-contact surface.

### Holding Dairy And Plant Milks At Unsafe Temperature

A busy bar leaves milk jugs and plant-milk cartons out on the counter through service for speed, and the dairy warms into the danger zone, growing bacteria that are served steamed into lattes. The false signal is that the milk is used quickly and "seems fine." The mechanism is that dairy milk and many plant milks are perishable and support bacterial growth above 5°C / 41°F, and a jug held at room temperature through a long service enters the danger zone and accumulates bacteria that are not killed by steaming temperature; the speed of use does not override the time-temperature rule. The harm is foodborne illness from the beverage station, often misattributed to food. The defense is to hold dairy and plant milks refrigerated or in an ice bath at or below 5°C / 41°F, to date and use opened product within its shelf life, and to discard milk held too long at room temperature. Speed does not suspend the danger zone.

### Undisclosed Allergens In Specialty Syrups And Plant Milks

A bar builds a signature zero-proof drink with a nut-based syrup (macadamia, pistachio) or an oat-milk foam and does not disclose the allergen, serving it to a guest with a tree-nut or specific plant-milk allergy. The false signal is that the drink is "just a fancy juice" with no obvious allergens. The mechanism is that specialty non-alcoholic drinks increasingly use nut, seed, and legume-based syrups and milks (almond, cashew, macadamia, soy, coconut, oat) whose allergen content is not visible in the finished drink, and a program that does not maintain an allergen matrix for its beverage menu serves hidden allergens with the same risk as the kitchen. The harm is an allergic reaction from a drink the guest had no reason to suspect. The defense is to maintain an allergen matrix for every beverage including syrups and plant milks, to train staff on the allergens in every specialty ingredient, and to follow a clean-tools procedure for allergen-free beverage orders. A non-alcoholic drink is not an allergen-free drink.

### Using Tap Water Or Hard Water Without Filtration

A program brews all its coffee and tea with unfiltered tap water, and the chlorine, mineral hardness, or off-flavors of the water taint every cup and scale the equipment. The false signal is that the water is clear and drinkable. The mechanism is that tap water varies widely in chlorine, chloramine, hardness, and dissolved solids, all of which affect extraction and flavor — chlorine produces a chemical off-taste, excessive hardness scales espresso machines and restricts flow while tasting chalky, and very soft or distilled water extracts too little and tastes flat — so even good beans and leaves brewed in poor water taste mediocre. The harm is a beverage program that never reaches its potential and equipment that scales and fails prematurely. The defense is to filter water for coffee and tea, to remineralize reverse-osmosis water to a balanced TDS, and to descale equipment per the local hardness. Water is the largest ingredient and must be a quality ingredient.

## Self-Check

- Is every brewed coffee and tea treated as a measured extraction with controlled dose, grind or leaf size, water quality, temperature, and time, rather than a pour-and-hope routine?
- Is espresso dialed in at the start of every service and re-tuned through the shift as beans age, with the dial-in documented so the standard is reproducible?
- Are water temperature and steep time matched to each tea type (greens and whites cool, oolongs warm, blacks and herbals near boil), using a temperature-controlled kettle?
- Is water filtered and remineralized to a balanced mineral content for coffee and tea, and is equipment descaled per local hardness?
- Are coffee beans stored sealed and dated within their peak window, ground to order, and stale product discarded — never served from a pre-ground hopper or old beans?
- Are non-alcoholic and zero-proof drinks built to measured, balanced, costed recipes with the same discipline as cocktails, not served as juice-and-syrup afterthoughts?
- Are caffeine claims verified against supplier specs, with true caffeine-free tisanes distinguished from blends containing true tea, and decaf distinguished from caffeine-free?
- Are dairy and plant milks held at or below 5°C / 41°F and dated, steam wands purged and wiped after every use and deep-cleaned on schedule, and allergens in syrups and plant milks disclosed with a clean-tools procedure for allergen-free orders?
- Is equipment matched to peak volume and maintained on a schedule — group heads backflushed daily, grinders cleaned, burrs replaced, thermometers calibrated?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority on health claims, allergen disclosures, or equipment specification to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
