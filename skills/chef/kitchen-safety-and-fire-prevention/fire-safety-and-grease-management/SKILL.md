# Fire Safety and Grease Management

The dominant fire risk in a commercial kitchen is grease: grease accumulates in hoods, ducts, and on surfaces, ignites from a flare-up or an overheated pan, and spreads rapidly through the exhaust system where it cannot be reached by handheld extinguishers. This skill covers the judgment of how to manage grease as a fuel source and how to design fire prevention and response so that a flare-up does not become a structural fire. The harm this skill prevents is the kitchen fire that destroys a business and endangers lives because grease was managed to an appearance standard, the suppression system was unverified, or staff responded to a grease fire with water and spread it.

## Core Rules

### Treat grease accumulation as the primary fire load, not as a cleaning issue

Grease in the hood, ducts, and on surfaces above and behind equipment is the fuel that turns a small pan fire into a duct fire that propagates through the building. Manage grease removal as fire prevention, not as aesthetics, which means cleaning on a frequency determined by fuel load and cooking volume, not by appearance. A hood that looks acceptable can carry enough grease to sustain a duct fire. Set cleaning frequency by the type and volume of cooking, and verify by inspection, not by the calendar alone.

### Maintain and verify the suppression system as the critical defense

The hood suppression system is the primary defense against a grease fire that exceeds what a pan lid or extinguisher can handle, and it only works if it is charged, aimed, and serviced. Have the system inspected and serviced on the required schedule, confirm that nozzles are aimed at the current equipment layout, and never move equipment out from under a nozzle's coverage without re-engineering the system. A suppression system that was not updated when equipment moved provides no coverage where it is now needed.

### Match extinguisher type and placement to the actual fire risks

Different fire risks require different extinguishers: Class K for grease and cooking media, Class ABC for general areas, and CO2 or clean agents for electrical. Place the correct type within reach of each risk, and never use a Class ABC or water on a grease fire, which can spread the burning grease. Verify that staff know which extinguisher to use for which fire, because using the wrong type is worse than not using one. Extinguisher placement and type must match the kitchen's actual layout, reviewed whenever equipment moves.

### Train staff on grease fire response as a rehearsed behavior

A grease fire escalates in seconds, so the response must be automatic, not reasoned in the moment. Train and rehearse: for a pan fire, smother with a lid or Class K extinguisher and turn off the heat; never use water, which flashes to steam and aerosolizes the burning grease. For a larger fire, activate the suppression system, evacuate, and call emergency services. Rehearse the response so that the correct action is the first instinct under panic, because a wrong first action, water on a grease fire, spreads the fire and blocks evacuation.

### Control ignition sources through equipment maintenance and practice

Fires start from ignition sources meeting fuel: a flame touching grease buildup, an overheated pan, a gas leak, or a flare-up from fat dripping into an open flame. Control ignition by maintaining equipment, especially gas connections and fryers, by managing flare-ups through heat control and fat management, and by keeping combustibles away from heat sources. Ignition control is continuous, not periodic, because the fuel, grease, is always present and only the ignition source needs to be prevented.

### Design the kitchen layout to limit fire spread

Layout decisions affect fire spread: equipment under a properly covered hood, clearance around fryers and ranges, non-combustible materials near heat sources, and clear evacuation paths. When laying out or modifying the kitchen, evaluate fire spread paths and ensure that a fire at any station can be suppressed and that evacuation is not blocked. A layout that packs equipment without fire spread analysis turns a single flare-up into a multi-station fire.

### Conduct regular fire drills and post-incident review

Fire response degrades without practice, and the only way to know the response works is to drill it. Conduct regular drills covering pan fire response, suppression system activation, evacuation, and extinguisher use, and review any incident or near-miss to identify what failed. A kitchen that has never drilled will discover under real fire that staff do not know the suppression system location, the evacuation route, or which extinguisher to use.

## Common Traps

### Cleaning hoods to an appearance standard instead of to a fuel-load standard

This is a trap because a hood that looks acceptable can carry enough grease to sustain a duct fire, so cleaning to appearance leaves the primary fire load in place while creating confidence that the system is maintained. The mechanism is that grease accumulates in the ducts and on the plenum where it is not visible from the kitchen, so the visible hood may look clean while the hidden grease load continues to build, and a flare-up ignites the duct grease that no one was monitoring. The false signal is the clean appearance of the visible hood, which reads as maintained. The harm is a duct fire that propagates through the building because the hidden grease was never removed, destroying the kitchen and endangering lives despite the visible hood looking clean. The correct response is to set cleaning frequency by cooking type and volume, to inspect the ducts and plenum, not just the visible hood, and to use a certified cleaning service that documents the hidden zones, treating grease removal as fire prevention rather than as cleaning.

### Moving equipment without re-engineering the suppression coverage

This trap operates because the suppression system nozzles are aimed at a specific equipment layout, so when a fryer or range is moved, the nozzles no longer cover the actual fire risk, and the system provides false protection that fails when a fire occurs at the new location. The mechanism is that suppression nozzles have a defined discharge pattern aimed at the equipment beneath them, and moving equipment out of that pattern leaves the fire risk uncovered, so activating the system during a fire does not suppress the fire because the agent does not reach it. The false signal is that the suppression system exists and is serviced, which reads as coverage. The harm is a fire at the relocated equipment that the suppression system cannot reach, allowing the fire to spread because the kitchen believed it was protected. The correct response is to treat any equipment move as a trigger for suppression system re-engineering, to have the system re-aimed and recertified whenever the layout changes, and to never assume that a serviced system covers equipment it was not aimed at.

### Using the wrong extinguisher or water on a grease fire

This trap exists because the instinctive response to fire is water, and staff who have not been trained may grab the nearest extinguisher or water, but water on a grease fire flashes to steam and aerosolizes the burning grease, spreading the fire across the kitchen and onto the person, and a Class ABC extinguisher on a grease fire can react with the grease and spread it. The mechanism is that water is denser than grease, so it sinks into the burning grease, flashes to steam, and ejects the burning grease in all directions, while the wrong chemical extinguisher may not suppress a grease fire and can cause a violent reaction. The false signal is that an extinguisher is available, which reads as equipped. The harm is a small pan fire spread into a kitchen-wide fire by the wrong response, endangering everyone and destroying the kitchen, because the responder used water or the wrong extinguisher in the first critical seconds. The correct response is to train and rehearse the correct response, smother or Class K, never water, to place Class K extinguishers within reach of all cooking equipment, and to drill the response so the correct action is automatic under panic.

### Trusting that equipment is safe because it works

This is a trap because gas connections, fryer thermostats, and electrical connections degrade silently, so equipment that works can still be developing a gas leak, a thermostat that sticks and overheats the oil, or a connection that arcs, and the first sign is a fire. The mechanism is that safety-critical components fail without affecting function until the failure causes ignition, a gas leak that accumulates until a spark, a thermostat that fails open and superheats the oil to its flash point, so the equipment appears fine until it is not. The false signal is that the equipment is operating normally, which reads as safe. The harm is a fire or gas explosion from equipment that was believed safe because it functioned, causing injury and destruction that preventive maintenance would have prevented. The correct response is to maintain safety-critical components on a schedule regardless of function, to inspect gas connections with leak detection, to calibrate fryer thermostats, and to treat normal operation as insufficient evidence of safety.

### Storing combustibles near heat sources during service

This trap operates because the kitchen is busy and the convenient place to set cardboard boxes, paper towels, and dry goods is often near the range or fryer, but those combustibles provide the fuel that turns a flare-up into a spreading fire, and their presence is normalized by habit until a flare-up ignites them. The mechanism is that a flare-up or grease splatter reaches a combustible that has been stored too close, and the combustible ignites and spreads the fire beyond the pan to the surrounding area, overwhelming the suppression and extinguisher response. The false signal is that the combustibles have been there without incident, which reads as acceptable. The harm is a fire that spreads rapidly because combustibles were stored in the fire spread path, turning a controllable flare-up into a multi-station fire. The correct response is to enforce clearance around heat sources, to store combustibles in designated areas away from cooking equipment, and to treat any combustible within the fire spread path as a hazard to be removed, not as a convenience.

### Never drilling the fire response so staff discover it under real fire

This trap exists because fire response is trained once and then assumed, but without rehearsal the response degrades, and staff discover under a real fire that they do not know where the suppression activation is, which extinguisher to use, or the evacuation route, costing the critical first seconds that determine whether a flare-up is contained or spreads. The mechanism is that fire response knowledge that is not rehearsed is not retrievable under panic, so staff freeze or take wrong actions, and the response that was trained fails because it was never practiced to automaticity. The false signal is that staff were trained, which reads as prepared. The harm is a fire that spreads because the response was slow or wrong, causing more damage and danger than a rehearsed response would have, and a kitchen that believed it was prepared because training occurred once. The correct response is to conduct regular fire drills covering all response elements, to rehearse to automaticity, and to treat one-time training as insufficient for a response that must execute correctly in seconds under panic.

### Ignoring near-misses that signal an uncontrolled fire risk

This is a trap because a near-miss, a flare-up that was caught, a small fire that was smothered, a grease splash that almost ignited, is a signal that the conditions for a fire exist and that the next event may not be caught, but near-misses are often treated as successfully handled rather than as warnings. The mechanism is that a near-miss reveals an uncontrolled risk, grease accumulation, a combustible too close, a thermostat drifting, and treating it as handled rather than as a signal leaves the risk in place for the next event, which may be larger and faster than the response can contain. The false signal is that the near-miss was handled without damage, which reads as the system working. The harm is a subsequent fire that the kitchen was warned about by the near-miss but did not prevent, because the warning was treated as success rather than as a signal. The correct response is to treat every near-miss as an incident to investigate, to identify the condition that allowed it, and to correct the condition, recognizing that a near-miss is a fire that almost happened and that the next one may not be caught.

## Self-Check

- Did I set hood and duct cleaning frequency by cooking type and volume, and inspect hidden zones, not just the visible hood?
- Did I re-engineer and recertify the suppression system whenever equipment moved, so nozzles cover the actual fire risks?
- Are Class K extinguishers within reach of all cooking equipment, and are staff trained never to use water or the wrong extinguisher on a grease fire?
- Did I maintain safety-critical components, gas connections, fryer thermostats, electrical, on a schedule regardless of whether they function?
- Did I enforce clearance around heat sources and remove combustibles from fire spread paths?
- Did I conduct regular fire drills rehearsing pan fire response, suppression activation, evacuation, and extinguisher use to automaticity?
- Did I investigate every near-miss as a signal of uncontrolled risk and correct the underlying condition?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
