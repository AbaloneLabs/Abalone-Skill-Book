# Knife, Cut, and Burn Injury Prevention

Kitchen injuries, cuts and burns, are not random accidents; they are the predictable result of specific workflow conditions: dull knives that require excessive force, overcrowded cutting boards, unstable cutting surfaces, wet floors, unguarded hot surfaces, and rushed technique under service pressure. This skill covers the judgment of how to identify and eliminate the conditions that cause injuries, rather than treating each injury as an individual mistake. The harm this skill prevents is the recurring injuries that result from blaming the victim instead of fixing the workflow, which leaves the hazardous condition in place to injure the next person.

## Core Rules

### Maintain knives as the primary cut-prevention control

A sharp knife is safer than a dull one because it cuts where it is directed with controlled force, whereas a dull knife requires excessive force that slips and causes uncontrolled cuts. Maintain edges through regular honing and sharpening, and remove from service any knife with a damaged edge or loose handle that will behave unpredictably. Knife maintenance is injury prevention, not a quality concern, and a kitchen that does not maintain its knives is choosing to injure its staff through slippage and excessive force.

### Stabilize the cutting surface and the product

A cutting board that slides is a major cut risk because it moves under the knife and causes the blade to deviate. Secure every board with a damp towel or non-slip mat, and stabilize the product being cut by creating a flat surface before cutting round items, and by using the claw grip to hold the product steady. An unstable board or product is the condition for many lacerations, and stabilizing them is a simple, high-leverage control.

### Train and enforce safe cutting technique as a behavior

Safe technique, the claw grip with fingers curled back, cutting away from the body, knife down and visible when not in use, never catching a falling knife, is the behavioral control that prevents cuts. Train it, model it, and correct it in the moment, because technique that is not enforced degrades under pressure into unsafe shortcuts. A kitchen that trains technique once and does not enforce it will see injuries from the shortcuts that fill the gap.

### Manage burn risks through guarding, marking, and workflow

Hot surfaces, pan handles, oven doors, range tops, fryer edges, and steam from boiling pots are burn risks that are often unmarked and unguarded. Use handle guards and pan handle orientation to keep hot handles out of the traffic path, mark or train staff to assume any pan handle is hot, manage steam release to avoid scalding, and enforce the discipline of never reaching over an open flame or into a steam environment without protection. Burns are prevented by making the hazard visible and by designing the workflow to avoid contact.

### Control slips, trips, and falls through floor management

Wet and greasy floors cause slips that result in cuts from falling onto knives and burns from falling into hot equipment. Manage floors by immediate cleanup of spills, by anti-slip mats in wet zones, by requiring slip-resistant footwear, and by keeping walkways clear of boxes, cords, and obstacles. Floor management is injury prevention because a slip in a kitchen is a slip near knives and heat.

### Address fatigue and rushing as injury causes

Injuries cluster at the end of long shifts and during rushes, when fatigue and speed compromise technique and awareness. Manage fatigue through reasonable shift length and breaks, and manage rushing through realistic ticket pacing and adequate staffing, because a cook who is exhausted or overwhelmed will take shortcuts that cause injuries. Treating fatigue and rushing as individual discipline rather than as workflow conditions leaves the root cause in place.

### Investigate every injury as a workflow signal, not as individual fault

Every cut and burn signals a workflow condition that allowed it: a dull knife, an unstable board, an unguarded hot surface, a wet floor, an overwhelmed cook. Investigate each injury to identify and fix the condition, not to assign blame, because blame leaves the condition in place to injure the next person. An injury that is blamed on the victim and not investigated is a guarantee of recurrence.

## Common Traps

### Treating dull knives as a quality issue rather than a safety issue

This is a trap because dull knives are addressed for cutting quality but not for safety, so they remain in service and cause cuts through slippage and excessive force, and the kitchen treats the resulting cuts as individual mistakes rather than as the predictable consequence of the dull edge. The mechanism is that a dull knife does not bite into the product, so it requires more force and is more likely to slip off the product and into the hand, and the cook applies that force because the dull knife will not cut otherwise. The false signal is that the cook should just be more careful, which reads as the knife being acceptable. The harm is lacerations from slippage that are blamed on the cook while the dull knife that caused them remains in service, injuring the next cook who uses it. The correct response is to treat knife sharpness as a safety control, to maintain edges through regular sharpening, to remove damaged knives from service, and to recognize that a dull knife is a workplace hazard, not a preference.

### Letting cutting boards slide unstabilized

This trap operates because a board that slides a little feels normal and is ignored, but that movement under the knife causes the blade to deviate and the hand to compensate, which is the mechanism of many lacerations, and the instability is accepted because no one has connected it to injury. The mechanism is that an unstabilized board shifts when the knife enters the product, changing the blade's path and causing it to move toward the holding hand or to slip off the product, and the cook, expecting a stable surface, does not adjust in time. The false signal is that the board mostly stays still, which reads as stable enough. The harm is lacerations from blade deviation that are attributed to the cook's technique while the unstable board that caused the deviation remains in use. The correct response is to secure every board with a damp towel or non-slip mat before any cutting, to treat an unstabilized board as a hazard, and to enforce stabilization as a non-negotiable setup step.

### Allowing hot pan handles to extend into the traffic path

This trap exists because pan handles naturally extend beyond the range, and in a busy kitchen it is convenient to leave them where they are, but a handle extending into the path is grabbed, brushed, or fallen onto by passing staff, causing burns, and the hazard is normalized until someone is burned. The mechanism is that a hot handle in the traffic path is contacted by staff who are moving quickly and not looking at the range, and the contact causes a burn or a reflexive grab that can spill the hot pan. The false signal is that the handle has been there without incident, which reads as acceptable. The harm is burns to hands, arms, and bodies from contact with hot handles that were left in the path, injuries that are preventable by handle orientation. The correct response is to orient pan handles away from the traffic path and toward the range, to train staff to assume every handle is hot, and to treat a handle in the path as a hazard to be corrected immediately.

### Normalizing wet and greasy floors during service

This is a trap because spills during service feel unavoidable and cleanup feels like it can wait, but wet and greasy floors cause slips that result in cuts from falling onto knives and burns from falling into equipment, and the hazard is accepted because the kitchen is busy. The mechanism is that a wet or greasy floor reduces traction, and a cook moving quickly slips, and the slip occurs near knives and hot equipment, so the fall causes a secondary injury far more serious than the slip itself. The false signal is that everyone is managing the wet floor by walking carefully, which reads as controlled. The harm is a slip that results in a laceration or burn, causing serious injury that the wet floor caused but that is attributed to the individual's carelessness. The correct response is to clean spills immediately regardless of service pressure, to use anti-slip mats in wet zones, to require slip-resistant footwear, and to treat a wet or greasy floor as an immediate hazard, not as a normal service condition.

### Blaming injuries on individual carelessness instead of investigating the workflow

This trap operates because it is simpler to attribute an injury to the victim's mistake than to investigate the workflow condition that allowed it, so the condition remains in place and injures the next person, creating a cycle of repeated injuries that are all blamed on individuals. The mechanism is that every injury has a workflow cause, a dull knife, an unstable board, an unguarded surface, an overwhelmed cook, and attributing the injury to carelessness avoids the investigation that would reveal and fix the cause. The false signal is that the individual made a mistake, which reads as the explanation. The harm is recurrence, because the workflow condition that caused the injury is never addressed, and the next cook who encounters it is injured in the same way, producing a pattern of injuries that the kitchen treats as separate incidents. The correct response is to investigate every injury for its workflow cause, to fix the condition, and to treat blame as a barrier to prevention, recognizing that an injury is a signal from the workflow, not a failure of the individual.

### Allowing rushing and fatigue to compromise technique without intervention

This trap exists because service pressure and long shifts are accepted as normal, so the fatigue and rushing that compromise technique and cause injuries are treated as the cook's problem to manage, rather than as workflow conditions that the kitchen must address, and injuries cluster at the times of highest fatigue and pressure. The mechanism is that fatigue reduces attention and motor control, and rushing causes shortcuts in safe technique, so the end of a long shift and the peak of a rush are the highest-risk periods for cuts and burns, and accepting these conditions as normal ensures that injuries continue at those times. The false signal is that the cook should manage their own fatigue and speed, which reads as individual responsibility. The harm is injuries that occur at predictable high-risk times because the workflow conditions that caused them were not addressed, and the kitchen treats each as an individual failure while the pattern continues. The correct response is to manage shift length and breaks to control fatigue, to pace tickets and staff adequately to control rushing, and to treat fatigue and rushing as workflow hazards that cause injuries, not as individual shortcomings.

### Leaving knives in sinks or under debris where they cause hidden cuts

This is a trap because a knife in a sink or under debris is invisible, so the next person reaching into the sink or grabbing the debris is lacerated by a blade they could not see, and the hazard is created by the convenience of dropping the knife rather than placing it. The mechanism is that a knife dropped into a sink of water or under a pile of debris cannot be seen, and the next person reaches in blindly and contacts the blade, causing a severe laceration to the hand or arm. The false signal is that the knife is in the sink to be washed, which reads as normal. The harm is severe lacerations from hidden blades that are caused by the practice of dropping knives into sinks rather than placing them visibly, injuries that are preventable by a simple protocol. The correct response is to never leave knives in sinks or under debris, to place knives visibly on the side or in a designated rack, to wash knives immediately or set them clearly visible for washing, and to treat a knife in a sink as a serious hazard.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat knife sharpness as a safety control, maintaining edges and removing damaged knives from service?
- Did I secure every cutting board with a damp towel or non-slip mat before any cutting?
- Did I train and enforce safe cutting technique, claw grip, cutting away from body, never catching a falling knife, as a behavior?
- Did I orient hot pan handles away from the traffic path and train staff to assume every handle is hot?
- Did I clean spills immediately, use anti-slip mats, and require slip-resistant footwear to prevent slips near knives and heat?
- Did I manage shift length, breaks, ticket pacing, and staffing to control fatigue and rushing as injury causes?
- Did I investigate every injury for its workflow cause and fix the condition, rather than blaming the individual?
