# Mobile Kitchen Layout and Equipment

A food truck is a kitchen compressed into a vehicle, and every constraint of a stationary kitchen is amplified: space is measured in square feet, power is finite and must be generated or carried, water and waste are stored in tanks that fill and empty, and the whole assembly must be road-legal and balanced for driving. The harm this skill prevents is the common pattern of designing a mobile kitchen by listing desired equipment rather than by reasoning about the interlocking constraints of power, space, weight, water, and ventilation, which produces trucks that cannot run all their equipment simultaneously without tripping breakers, that run out of water or fill their waste tanks mid-service, that overheat because the hood cannot clear the cooking load, or that handle dangerously because the weight is poorly distributed. The agent's job is to reason about the mobile kitchen as an integrated system where every equipment choice commits power, space, weight, and water capacity that must be balanced against the menu and the service model.

## Core Rules

### Start from the menu and work backward to the equipment

The most common error is to choose equipment first and then try to fit a menu to it. The correct sequence is the reverse: define the menu and the per-hour volume, identify the equipment each dish requires, and then specify the minimum equipment set that can execute that menu at that volume. Every piece of equipment in a truck consumes power, propane, space, weight, and hood capacity, so each item must earn its place by directly supporting a menu item at the required volume. Equipment that does not support the menu is dead weight and wasted capacity. Build a load list that maps each menu item to the equipment and the power or fuel it needs, and use that list to drive the specification.

### Choose the power source based on total electrical load and runtime

Mobile kitchens power their electrical equipment either from a generator (onboard or towed) or from shore power (plug-in at a commissary or event), and cooking equipment is typically propane-fired to reduce the electrical load. The decision between generator and shore power depends on where the truck operates and the total electrical load: refrigeration, lights, point-of-sale, small appliances, and any electric cooking equipment. Size the generator to handle the simultaneous peak load with a margin, because undersized generators trip breakers and stall service. Consider runtime and fuel: a generator that runs out of fuel mid-service ends the day. Propane is preferred for cooking because it concentrates high heat in a compact, portable fuel, leaving the electrical system for refrigeration and ancillary loads. Understand the total power budget before specifying any electric cooking equipment.

### Size refrigeration to the menu and the service day, not to the truck

Refrigeration in a food truck must hold the entire day's perishable inventory at safe temperature, often in a hot vehicle, while being opened repeatedly during service. Undersized refrigeration fails on a hot day and endangers the food. Calculate the refrigeration capacity based on the volume of protein, dairy, produce, and prepared components the menu requires for a full service day, plus a margin for hot ambient conditions. Use commercial-grade, forced-air units designed for mobile use, not household refrigerators, which cannot maintain temperature in a hot truck. Include separate freezer capacity if the menu requires frozen storage. Verify that the refrigeration can run off the available power (generator or shore) for the full service day, and monitor temperatures during service, because mobile refrigeration is a common point of failure.

### Plan hood and ventilation capacity to match the cooking load

Cooking equipment under a hood produces heat, grease, smoke, and steam that must be cleared to keep the truck habitable and fire-safe. The hood must be sized to the total cooking surface area and the type of cooking: griddles, fryers, and charbroilers produce more grease and smoke than steamers and ovens, and require more exhaust capacity and more robust grease filtration. An undersized hood leaves the truck full of smoke, sets off alarms, and creates a grease-fire hazard; an oversized hood wastes power and conditioned air. Include a fire suppression system integrated with the hood, as required by code for most mobile cooking operations. Design the hood and the makeup air together, because exhausting air requires replacing it, and an imbalance makes the hood ineffective and the truck uncomfortable.

### Calculate water and waste capacity for the full service day

Mobile kitchens carry their potable water and their waste water in tanks, and both have finite capacity that must last the service day. Estimate the potable water demand from cooking, handwashing (a code requirement), cleaning, and any beverage or ice needs, and the waste water generated from each. Size the fresh water tank and the waste tank (which is always larger than the fresh tank, since waste includes the fresh water plus food debris) to handle the longest anticipated service day with a margin. Running out of fresh water ends service; filling the waste tank ends service and creates a sanitation problem. Include a handwashing sink with hot and cold water as required by health code, and plan the tank fills and waste disposal into the daily commissary routine.

### Manage weight distribution for safe handling and legal limits

Every piece of equipment, every tank of water and propane, and every stored inventory item adds weight to the truck, and the distribution of that weight determines whether the vehicle handles safely and stays within legal axle and gross weight limits. Heavy items like refrigeration, fryers, and full water tanks should be positioned to balance the load across the axles, not concentrated on one side or one end. A truck that is nose-heavy, tail-heavy, or side-heavy handles poorly and is dangerous. Calculate the loaded weight and the per-axle distribution against the vehicle's ratings, and remember that a full water tank adds substantial weight that must be in the calculation. Have the loaded truck weighed and adjust equipment placement if the distribution is off. Weight is a safety issue, not just an efficiency issue.

### Design the workflow for one or two people in a confined space

A food truck is typically operated by one or two people in a space of perhaps 50 to 100 square feet, and the layout must allow them to move efficiently without colliding. Design the workflow as a loop or a line: order taken, protein cooked, assembled, garnished, handed off, with each station within arm's reach and the path unobstructed. Equipment that is used sequentially should be adjacent; equipment that is used simultaneously by two people should not require them to cross. Test the layout by walking through the service of the top-selling items and identifying pinch points. A poor layout slows service and frustrates staff; a good layout lets a small team execute high volume. The layout is as much a part of the menu's viability as the recipe.

## Common Traps

### Specifying equipment without totaling the power load and tripping breakers in service

This is a trap because each piece of equipment seems reasonable on its own, but the cumulative electrical load exceeds what the generator or shore connection can deliver simultaneously. The mechanism is that a designer adds a griddle, a refrigerator, a freezer, a fryer, lights, a point-of-sale system, and a small oven, each within its own rating, but the sum of their running and startup loads exceeds the generator capacity, so when multiple pieces run together during a busy service, the breaker trips and the kitchen goes dark or the generator stalls. The false signal is that each piece of equipment fits the budget and the space, which the designer reads as feasible. The harm is a truck that cannot run its full kitchen at peak service, forcing the operator to shed load, slow service, or risk repeated outages. The correct response is to build a total load list with running and peak (startup) draws for every item, to size the generator or shore connection to the simultaneous peak with a margin, and to prefer propane for high-draw cooking equipment.

### Undersizing refrigeration for hot-weather service and endangering the food

This trap operates because refrigeration capacity is specified for normal conditions, but a food truck operates in a hot metal box, often in direct sun, with the doors opened repeatedly during service, all of which dramatically increase the cooling load. The mechanism is that an undersized or household-grade refrigerator cannot maintain safe cold-holding temperature (below 5 degrees Celsius) when the ambient temperature inside the truck reaches 35 degrees or more and the door is opened every few minutes, so the food inside drifts into the danger zone and grows bacteria, while the operator does not realize it because the unit is still running. The false signal is that the refrigerator is cold to the touch and running, which the operator reads as functioning. The harm is temperature abuse that can cause foodborne illness and that fails a health inspection. The correct response is to use commercial forced-air refrigeration rated for mobile use and high ambient conditions, to size capacity to the full day's inventory with a margin, and to monitor internal temperature with a thermometer during service.

### Filling the waste tank mid-service and ending the day early

This is a trap because waste tank capacity is easy to underestimate, and a full waste tank ends service immediately and creates a sanitation crisis. The mechanism is that the fresh water used for cooking, handwashing, and cleaning becomes waste water, and the waste tank fills faster than the operator expects, especially with handwashing (a code-required continuous use) and equipment cleaning, so a tank sized optimistically is full halfway through a busy service, forcing the truck to close and drive to a dump point. The false signal is that the fresh water tank still has water, which the operator reads as capacity remaining. The harm is lost sales, disappointed customers, and a stressful emergency disposal situation. The correct response is to size the waste tank larger than the fresh tank (since waste includes the fresh water plus debris), to estimate daily waste from all sources including handwashing, and to build tank management into the commissary routine so the truck starts each day empty.

### Concentrating heavy equipment on one side and making the truck handle poorly

This trap exists because equipment placement is often driven by workflow convenience rather than weight balance, and the result is a vehicle that is dangerous to drive. The mechanism is that heavy items like a full water tank, a fryer full of oil, refrigeration compressors, and a generator, placed on one side or at one end of the truck, create an unbalanced load that overloads one axle or one set of tires, causing poor braking, unstable cornering, and accelerated tire wear, and potentially exceeding legal weight limits per axle. The false signal is that the equipment fits the layout and the truck drives, which the operator reads as acceptable. The harm is a vehicle that handles dangerously, especially when fully loaded, and that may be illegal or unsafe to operate. The correct response is to calculate the weight and position of every heavy item, to distribute the load across the axles and side to side, to have the loaded truck weighed on each axle, and to reposition equipment if the distribution is unbalanced.

### Undersizing the hood and filling the truck with smoke during peak cooking

This is a trap because the hood capacity is specified for average cooking, but peak service produces a surge of grease, smoke, and steam that an undersized hood cannot clear. The mechanism is that a hood sized to the cooking surface but not to the intensity of peak cooking, or one with insufficient exhaust airflow and inadequate makeup air, cannot remove the effluent generated when the griddle and fryer run full during a rush, so smoke and grease fill the truck, obscuring vision, irritating the staff, setting off alarms, and creating a fire hazard as grease accumulates. The false signal is that the hood runs and clears smoke during slow periods, which the operator reads as adequate. The harm is an uninhabitable kitchen at peak, a fire safety risk, and potential code violations. The correct response is to size the hood and exhaust to the total cooking surface and the peak cooking intensity, to include adequate grease filtration and makeup air, and to integrate the required fire suppression system.

### Forgetting that water is heavy and exceeding payload with full tanks

This trap operates because water weight is not intuitive, and a full water tank adds hundreds of kilograms that pushes the truck over its payload rating. The mechanism is that water weighs one kilogram per liter, so a 200-liter fresh water tank adds 200 kilograms, and a matching waste tank adds similar weight, plus the inventory and equipment, and the cumulative load exceeds the vehicle's gross vehicle weight rating or payload capacity, making the truck illegal to drive and dangerous to operate with stressed brakes and suspension. The false signal is that the tanks fit the space and the truck feels driveable empty, which the operator reads as within limits. The harm is an overloaded vehicle that is unsafe and non-compliant, with potential fines or mechanical failure. The correct response is to include full tank weights in the payload calculation, to size tanks to the need rather than the space, and to consider traveling with partial tanks and filling at the commissary or site when possible.

### Designing a layout that works on paper but creates pinch points in service

This is a trap because a two-dimensional layout drawing does not capture the movement of one or two people working simultaneously in a confined space, and pinch points emerge only in real service. The mechanism is that equipment placed for logical sequence on paper may require two staff to reach across each other, may place the handwashing sink out of reach from the cook station (a code issue), or may create a single choke point through which every order must pass, so during a rush the staff collide, block each other, and slow to a crawl, and the layout that looked efficient becomes the bottleneck. The false signal is that the layout is logical on paper and each station is accessible, which the designer reads as workable. The harm is slow service, frustrated staff, and lower volume than the truck could otherwise handle. The correct response is to physically walk through the service of the top items in the actual or mocked-up space, to identify and eliminate pinch points, and to design for the simultaneous movement of the operating crew.

## Self-Check

- Has the equipment list been driven backward from the menu and per-hour volume, with each item justified by the dishes it supports, rather than chosen first?
- Has the total electrical load (running and peak startup) been calculated, and is the generator or shore connection sized to the simultaneous peak with a margin, with propane preferred for high-heat cooking?
- Is refrigeration commercial-grade, sized to the full day's inventory with a margin for hot ambient conditions, and temperature-monitored during service?
- Is the hood sized to the total cooking surface and peak cooking intensity, with adequate exhaust, grease filtration, makeup air, and an integrated fire suppression system?
- Are the fresh and waste water tanks sized to the full service day, with the waste tank larger than the fresh tank, and a handwashing sink with hot and cold water included as required?
- Has the weight of all equipment, full tanks, propane, and inventory been calculated and distributed across the axles, with the loaded truck weighed to confirm it is within legal and safe limits?
- Has the layout been physically walked through for the top menu items to identify and eliminate pinch points for the operating crew of one or two people?
- Does the output respect the agent's limits, deferring final engineering, code compliance, and vehicle certification to qualified mobile-kitchen builders and inspectors?
