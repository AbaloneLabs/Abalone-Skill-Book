# Mobile Vending Compliance and Permitting

Mobile vending is one of the most heavily regulated food-service formats, because it combines the food-safety requirements of a kitchen with the fire and fuel hazards of a propane-powered mobile unit and the public-space rules of vending on streets and at events. The harm this skill prevents is the common pattern of treating compliance as paperwork to file rather than as an integrated system of permits, equipment, and daily practices, which produces trucks that are shut down mid-service by an inspector, that fail a fire suppression check, that operate illegally because a vending permit lapsed, or that cause a propane incident because the safety systems were not maintained. The agent's job is to reason about the full compliance picture: health permits and their conditions, the commissary relationship that most jurisdictions require, fire suppression and propane safety, and the patchwork of local vending and parking rules, because a mobile operation is only as viable as its weakest compliance link.

## Core Rules

### Secure the full permit stack before operating, in the correct order

Mobile vending requires a stack of permits that must be obtained in sequence, and operating without any one of them is illegal. The typical stack includes a business license, a mobile food facility health permit from the county or city health department, a commissary agreement (most jurisdictions require a licensed commissary as a condition of the mobile permit), a fire department permit for the cooking and propane systems, and local vending or parking permits for each jurisdiction where the truck operates. Each permit has its own application, inspection, and fee, and some depend on others (the health permit often requires the commissary agreement first). Build a permit checklist for every jurisdiction of operation, start early because inspections take time, and never operate with an expired or missing permit, because a single inspection can shut the truck down and forfeit the day's revenue.

### Maintain the commissary relationship that the permit requires

Most jurisdictions require a mobile food facility to be based at a licensed commissary kitchen, where the truck returns daily to restock, clean, dump waste, and service the unit, and the commissary agreement is typically a condition of the health permit. The commissary is not optional; it is part of the legal operating model. Choose a commissary that can provide the services the operation needs: potable water, waste disposal, food storage, prep space, and grease disposal. Maintain the relationship and the paperwork, because an inspector may verify the commissary agreement and the services provided. Skipping the commissary return, or operating without a valid commissary, violates the permit and risks shutdown. Build the daily commissary routine into the operation as a non-negotiable.

### Install and maintain the required fire suppression system

A food truck with cooking equipment, particularly a fryer, griddle, or charbroiler, carries a significant grease-fire risk, and jurisdictions require an automatic fire suppression system over the cooking area, typically an Ansul or equivalent wet chemical system, integrated with the hood and the fuel shutoff. The system must be professionally installed, sized to the cooking equipment, and inspected and serviced on the required schedule (often semi-annually). The suppression system must automatically cut the fuel (gas or electric) to the cooking equipment when it activates. Maintain the inspection records, because an inspector will ask for them, and a system past its service date is a violation. Train staff on the system: how it activates, where the manual pull is, and what to do after a discharge. A fire suppression system that is not maintained is both a violation and a genuine safety hazard.

### Manage propane safety as a daily discipline

Propane is the primary cooking fuel for most food trucks, and it is also a significant hazard if handled carelessly. The propane system must be installed to code, with the tank secured, the lines protected from damage, a leak-tested connection, and an accessible shutoff. Inspect the system regularly for leaks (a soap solution at the connections, never an open flame), secure the tank during transit and service, and never store spare tanks inside the truck or near ignition sources. Train staff on propane safety: how to turn the system on and off, how to recognize a leak by smell, and what to do if a leak is suspected (shut off, evacuate, ventilate, do not ignite anything). Propane incidents are rare but severe, and they are almost always traceable to a lapse in the daily discipline.

### Follow the food-safety standards the health permit imposes

The mobile health permit imposes the same food-safety standards as a stationary kitchen: temperature control, handwashing, cross-contamination control, and source documentation. The truck must have a handwashing sink with hot and cold water, supplied from the potable tank, and staff must use it. Cold holding must be below 5 degrees Celsius and hot holding above 60 degrees Celsius, verified with a probe thermometer. Raw and ready-to-eat foods must be separated. Food must be sourced from approved suppliers, with invoices available. The truck must be cleaned and sanitized, and waste must be held and disposed of properly, not dumped on site. An inspector can arrive at any service location and check all of these, so the standards must be met every service, not just at inspection time. See the dedicated food-safety skills for the full framework.

### Navigate local vending and parking regulations for each location

Where a food truck can park and vend is governed by a patchwork of city, county, and sometimes property-owner rules, which vary by location and change frequently. Some cities require a specific vending permit for each location or zone, some restrict vending near brick-and-mortar restaurants or schools, some limit the duration a truck can stay in one spot, and event locations often have their own vendor agreements. Research the rules for every location where the truck operates, obtain the required permits, and respect the restrictions, because vending without the right permit or in a restricted zone leads to fines or towing. Build a location-permit matrix and keep it current, because the rules change. Public-space vending is a privilege governed by local rules, not a right.

### Prepare for inspection at any time and maintain records

A mobile food facility can be inspected at its commissary, at a service location, or at an event, often without warning, so the operation must be inspection-ready at all times. Maintain on the truck the documents an inspector will ask for: the health permit, the commissary agreement, the fire suppression inspection records, the propane system inspection, employee food-handler cards, temperature logs, and source invoices. Keep the truck clean, the systems functional, and the food-safety practices in place every service. Treat every service day as a potential inspection day, because the cost of a failed inspection, a shutdown, a fine, or a lost permit, is far higher than the cost of maintaining compliance continuously.

## Common Traps

### Operating with an expired or missing permit and being shut down mid-service

This is a trap because permits have expiration dates and renewals take time, and an operator who lets a permit lapse to save a fee or because they forgot discovers the lapse only when an inspector arrives. The mechanism is that each permit in the stack, health, fire, vending, business, has its own renewal cycle, and missing any one leaves the truck operating illegally, so when an inspector checks at a service location or an event, the truck is shut down on the spot, the day's revenue is lost, and the operator faces a fine and a reinstatement process. The false signal is that the truck has been operating fine, which the operator reads as compliant. The harm is sudden shutdown and financial loss. The correct response is to maintain a permit calendar with all expiration dates and renewal deadlines, to renew early, and never to operate with a lapsed permit.

### Skipping the commissary return and violating the health permit condition

This trap operates because the daily commissary return takes time and effort, and an operator under pressure may skip it to extend a service day or save fuel, but the commissary is a legal condition of the permit. The mechanism is that the health permit requires the truck to be based at a licensed commissary where it restocks, cleans, and disposes of waste, and skipping the return means the truck is operating without the required base, violating the permit, and accumulating waste and unsanitized conditions that build up over days, so an inspector who checks the commissary log or the truck's condition finds the violation. The false signal is that the truck still has supplies and looks clean, which the operator reads as fine without the return. The harm is a permit violation, potential shutdown, and deteriorating sanitation. The correct response is to treat the daily commissary return as non-negotiable, to build it into the schedule, and to maintain the commissary log as proof of compliance.

### Letting the fire suppression system inspection lapse and failing a fire check

This is a trap because the suppression system sits unused and looks fine, so its inspection is easy to defer, but a lapsed inspection is both a violation and a real safety failure waiting to happen. The mechanism is that the wet chemical suppression system over the cooking equipment must be professionally inspected and serviced on a required schedule (often semi-annually), and if the inspection lapses, the system may have low charge, blocked nozzles, or a faulty trigger that will not activate in a fire, so when a grease fire occurs the system fails to suppress it, and an inspector checking the service tag finds the lapsed inspection and shuts the truck down. The false signal is that the system is installed and has not activated, which the operator reads as functional. The harm is a fire that is not suppressed, endangering staff and the public, and a compliance shutdown. The correct response is to schedule the suppression inspection on the required cycle, to keep the service tag current, and to maintain the records on the truck.

### Mishandling propane and causing a leak or fire incident

This trap exists because propane is routine and familiar, so the daily safety discipline erodes, and a single lapse can cause a severe incident. The mechanism is that the propane system, with a secured tank, protected lines, and leak-tested connections, is safe only if maintained and operated correctly, and a careless practice like failing to secure the tank, neglecting a leak check, storing a spare tank improperly, or ignoring a smell of gas allows propane to accumulate, and any ignition source then causes a fire or explosion that can destroy the truck and injure or kill. The false signal is that the system has worked fine for months, which the operator reads as safe. The harm is a catastrophic incident that is rare but devastating. The correct response is to perform the daily propane safety checks, to secure the tank, to leak-test connections, to train staff on leak response, and never to ignore the smell of gas.

### Neglecting handwashing and cross-contamination on the truck and failing inspection

This is a trap because the truck is small and busy, and the handwashing sink and separation disciplines feel like obstacles to speed, so they slip, and an inspector catches the lapse. The mechanism is that the health permit requires a functional handwashing sink with hot and cold water and its routine use, plus separation of raw and ready-to-eat foods, and in a confined truck under service pressure these practices erode, so an inspector observing service, or checking the sink's water supply and the food storage, finds the violations and cites or shuts down the truck, and worse, the lapse can cause actual foodborne illness. The false signal is that the truck is busy and the food looks fine, which the operator reads as compliant. The harm is inspection failure, possible illness, and damage to the operation. The correct response is to keep the handwashing sink stocked and functional, to enforce its use, to maintain raw and ready-to-eat separation, and to treat food safety as non-negotiable every service.

### Vending in a restricted location and being fined or towed

This trap operates because vending locations are governed by rules that vary by block and change, and an operator who finds a good spot may not realize it is restricted until enforcement arrives. The mechanism is that cities restrict vending near restaurants, schools, or in certain zones, require specific location permits, and limit duration, so a truck that parks in a restricted or unpermitted location, even one that seems public and available, is operating illegally and subject to fines, towing, and impound, with the operator discovering the rule only when the citation is issued or the truck is towed. The false signal is that the location is public space and no one has objected, which the operator reads as allowed. The harm is fines, lost time, towing costs, and reputational damage. The correct response is to research the vending rules for every location, to obtain the required location permits, to respect the restrictions, and to maintain a current location-permit matrix.

### Not keeping records on the truck and being unable to prove compliance on inspection

This is a trap because the records exist somewhere, but if they are not on the truck when the inspector arrives, the truck cannot prove compliance and is treated as non-compliant. The mechanism is that an inspector checking a mobile facility at a service location asks for the health permit, the commissary agreement, the fire suppression and propane inspection records, employee food-handler cards, temperature logs, and source invoices, and if any are missing because they are filed at an office rather than carried on the truck, the inspector cites the violation or shuts the truck down, because absence of records is treated as absence of compliance. The false signal is that the records are maintained at the office, which the operator reads as sufficient. The harm is a citation or shutdown despite actual compliance, simply for lack of documentation on site. The correct response is to keep a complete compliance binder on the truck at all times, with current copies of every required document, and to update it at each renewal.

## Self-Check

- Is a complete permit stack secured for every jurisdiction of operation, with a calendar tracking all expiration dates and renewals done early so no permit is ever lapsed?
- Is the truck based at a licensed commissary with a valid agreement, and is the daily return for restocking, cleaning, and waste disposal treated as non-negotiable?
- Is the fire suppression system professionally installed, sized to the equipment, inspected on the required schedule, integrated with fuel shutoff, and its service records current and on the truck?
- Is propane handled with daily safety discipline: tank secured, lines protected, connections leak-tested, spares stored properly, and staff trained on leak response?
- Are food-safety standards met every service: handwashing sink stocked and used, cold holding below 5 degrees Celsius, hot holding above 60 degrees Celsius, raw and ready-to-eat separated, and sources documented?
- Has every vending location been researched for local rules, with required location permits obtained and restrictions respected, tracked in a current location-permit matrix?
- Is a complete compliance binder kept on the truck at all times, with current copies of the health permit, commissary agreement, fire and propane inspection records, food-handler cards, temperature logs, and source invoices?
- Does the output respect the agent's limits, deferring final legal, code, and regulatory determinations to qualified inspectors, fire marshals, and attorneys licensed in the jurisdiction?
