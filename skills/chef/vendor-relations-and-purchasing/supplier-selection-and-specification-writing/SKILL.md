# Supplier Selection and Specification Writing

A kitchen's quality is bounded by its ingredients, and the judgment problem is that supplier selection is not just about price (the cheapest supplier often delivers inconsistent quality, late deliveries, or substitution) but about a balance of quality (does the product meet the spec?), reliability (does the supplier deliver on time, every time?), consistency (is each delivery the same as the last?), and price (competitive, but not a race to the bottom) — and the foundation is the written specification (the exact product description, so the supplier knows what to deliver and the kitchen can verify it). The recurring failure is buying on price alone (and getting inconsistent quality), or buying without a spec (so the supplier delivers whatever they have, and it varies), or relying on a single supplier (and being vulnerable to their failure). The discipline is to write specs, to select on quality-reliability-consistency-price, to verify the deliveries, and to diversify.

The agent's job is to source for quality and reliability, to write the specs, and to manage the supplier relationships.

## Core Rules

### Write A Specification For Each Product

A specification (spec) is the exact product description (the item, the grade, the size, the variety, the pack, the brand or the equivalent, the quality criteria), so the supplier knows what to deliver and the kitchen can verify it on receiving. The judgment point is that a spec eliminates the ambiguity (the supplier cannot substitute a lower grade), and without a spec, the supplier delivers whatever they have (and it varies). Write a spec for each product (the specific grade, size, variety, pack, quality criteria), give it to the supplier, and verify each delivery against the spec. The spec is the purchasing contract.

### Select Suppliers On Quality, Reliability, Consistency, And Price

Supplier selection is a balance of quality (the product meets the spec), reliability (on-time, complete deliveries), consistency (each delivery is the same), and price (competitive, but quality and reliability often cost more). The judgment point is that price alone is a trap (the cheapest supplier cuts corners on quality or reliability), and the balance is the discipline. Evaluate the suppliers on all four criteria (sample the product, check the delivery record, test the consistency, compare the price), and select the best balance (not the cheapest). Quality and reliability often justify a higher price.

### Verify Each Delivery Against The Spec

Each delivery must be verified against the spec (the right product, the right grade, the right size, the right quantity, the right temperature) at receiving, and discrepancies (substitution, short weight, wrong temperature) rejected or documented. The judgment point is that unverified deliveries drift (the supplier substitutes, sends lower quality), and the receiving verification (against the spec) is the control. Verify each delivery at receiving (count, weigh, check the temp, check the quality against the spec), reject or document the discrepancies, and communicate with the supplier. See the receiving skill for the protocol.

### Diversify Suppliers To Avoid Single-Source Risk

Relying on a single supplier (for a key item) is a risk: if they fail (a crop failure, a delivery truck breakdown, a bankruptcy), the kitchen is stuck. The judgment point is that single-source is convenient but vulnerable, and diversification (a primary and a backup supplier for key items) is the defense. Diversify the suppliers for key items (a primary and a backup), maintain the relationships with both (order from the backup periodically to keep it active), and have a contingency plan for supply disruptions. For ultra-fresh items (seafood, produce), multiple local suppliers may be needed.

### Build Relationships, Not Just Transactions

A supplier relationship (built on communication, fairness, loyalty, and mutual benefit) yields better service (priority in shortages, early notice of market changes, flexibility in orders) than a purely transactional one (always chasing the lowest price). The judgment point is that the relationship is an asset (a good supplier goes the extra mile for a good customer), and treating suppliers as commodities (switching for a penny) loses the relationship benefits. Build the relationships (communicate clearly, pay on time, be loyal when the quality is good, give feedback), and recognize the relationship as an asset. Loyalty (when the quality is good) is rewarded.

### Negotiate Terms: Price, Delivery, Payment, Quality Guarantees

The supplier terms (the price, the delivery schedule and minimum, the payment terms, the quality guarantees and the return policy) should be negotiated (not just accepted), and reviewed periodically (as the market and the volume change). The judgment point is that the terms affect the cost and the reliability, and negotiation (based on the volume, the relationship, the market) optimizes them. Negotiate the terms (price, delivery frequency and minimum, payment terms, quality guarantees), review them periodically (annually, or on market changes), and document them (a written agreement). Volume and loyalty are leverage.

## Common Traps

### Buying On Price Alone And Getting Inconsistent Quality

A chef selects the cheapest supplier (to save on food cost), and the quality is inconsistent (lower grade, substitution) and the reliability is poor (late deliveries). The mechanism is that the cheapest supplier cuts corners (on quality, on reliability), and the savings are offset by the waste (lower quality) and the disruption (late deliveries); the balance (quality-reliability-consistency-price) is the discipline. The false signal is that the price "is low," which reads as saving. The harm is inconsistent quality and disruption. The defense is to select on the balance (quality, reliability, consistency, price), to sample the product, to check the delivery record, and to not buy on price alone.

### Buying Without A Spec And Getting Variable Product

A chef orders "chicken" (without a spec), and the supplier delivers whatever they have (different sizes, different grades each time), and the menu is inconsistent. The mechanism is that without a spec, the supplier delivers what they have (and it varies), and the spec (the exact product description) is the control; the spec is required. The false signal is that the product "is chicken," which reads as right. The harm is inconsistent menu quality. The defense is to write a spec for each product (grade, size, variety, pack, quality), to give it to the supplier, and to verify each delivery.

### Not Verifying Deliveries And Letting Quality Drift

A chef does not verify the deliveries (trusts the supplier), and the quality drifts (the supplier substitutes, sends lower quality over time). The mechanism is that unverified deliveries drift (the supplier tests the boundaries, substitutes), and the receiving verification (against the spec) is the control; the verification is required. The false signal is that the supplier "is trusted," which reads as reliable. The harm is drifting quality. The defense is to verify each delivery at receiving (count, weigh, check the temp and quality against the spec), to reject or document discrepancies, and to communicate with the supplier.

### Single-Sourcing A Key Item And Being Vulnerable

A chef relies on a single supplier for a key item (the only produce supplier), and when they fail (a delivery breakdown, a crop shortage), the kitchen is stuck (no produce). The mechanism is that single-source is vulnerable (the supplier's failure is the kitchen's failure), and diversification (a primary and a backup) is the defense. The false signal is that the supplier "is reliable," which reads as sufficient. The harm is a supply disruption. The defense is to diversify the suppliers for key items (a primary and a backup), to maintain both relationships, and to have a contingency plan.

### Treating Suppliers As Commodities And Losing Relationship Benefits

A chef switches suppliers constantly (chasing the lowest price each week), and the suppliers treat the kitchen as a transaction (no priority, no flexibility, no early notice), losing the relationship benefits. The mechanism is that the relationship (built on loyalty and communication) yields better service (priority, flexibility, notice), and treating suppliers as commodities (constant switching) loses these; the relationship is an asset. The false signal is that the price "is the best," which reads as optimal. The harm is lost relationship benefits (and often, higher effective cost from disruption). The defense is to build the relationships (be loyal when the quality is good, communicate, pay on time), and to recognize the relationship as an asset.

### Not Negotiating Or Reviewing The Terms And Overpaying

A chef accepts the supplier's initial terms (price, delivery, payment) and does not negotiate or review them, and over time overpays (as the market shifts or the volume grows). The mechanism is that the terms affect the cost and the reliability, and un-negotiated or un-reviewed terms drift (the supplier does not proactively lower the price), and negotiation and review optimize them. The false signal is that the terms "are set," which reads as standard. The harm is overpaying. The defense is to negotiate the terms (price, delivery, payment, guarantees), to review them periodically, and to use the volume and loyalty as leverage.

## Self-Check

- Is a specification written for each product (grade, size, variety, pack, quality criteria), given to the supplier, and verified at receiving?
- Are suppliers selected on the balance of quality, reliability, consistency, and price (sampled, delivery-record checked, not just the cheapest)?
- Is each delivery verified at receiving (count, weigh, temperature, quality against the spec), with discrepancies rejected or documented?
- Are the suppliers diversified for key items (a primary and a backup), with both relationships maintained and a contingency plan?
- Are the supplier relationships built (communication, fairness, loyalty, mutual benefit), recognized as assets?
- Are the terms (price, delivery, payment, guarantees) negotiated, documented, and reviewed periodically?
- Is the reasoning documented so the specs, selection criteria, verification, diversification, relationships, and terms for each supplier are verifiable?
