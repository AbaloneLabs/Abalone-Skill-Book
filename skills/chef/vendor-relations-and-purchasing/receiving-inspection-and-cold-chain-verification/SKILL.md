# Receiving Inspection and Cold Chain Verification

Receiving is the kitchen's first line of defense, and the judgment problem is that the quality and the safety of the food are established at the moment of delivery — and a delivery accepted without inspection (wrong temperature, short weight, poor quality, damaged packaging) brings those problems into the kitchen, where they become waste, food safety risk, or menu inconsistency. The recurring failure is trusting the delivery (signing without inspecting), not checking the temperatures (the cold chain may be broken), or accepting substitutes (the supplier sends a different brand or grade) because it's easier than rejecting. The discipline is to inspect every delivery (against the spec and the order), to verify the cold chain (temperatures), to reject the out-of-spec, and to document for credit.

The agent's job is to receive rigorously, to verify the cold chain, and to reject and document the failures.

## Core Rules

### Inspect Every Delivery Against The Order And The Spec

Every delivery must be inspected against the order (the right items, the right quantities) and the spec (the right grade, size, quality) at receiving, before it's accepted. The judgment point is that the inspection (not the invoice) confirms what's delivered, and unverified deliveries drift (short quantities, wrong items, lower quality). Check the delivery against the order and the spec (count, weigh, verify the items and the quality), and reject or document the discrepancies. Do not sign blindly.

### Verify The Cold Chain: Temperatures For Refrigerated And Frozen

Refrigerated and frozen deliveries must be temperature-checked at receiving (the cold chain — the unbroken refrigeration from the supplier to the kitchen — must be verified), because a broken cold chain (the food warmed during transport) is a food safety risk. The judgment point is that the cold chain is invisible without a thermometer, and a warm delivery (even if it looks fine) may be unsafe. Check the temperatures (refrigerated: below 4°C/40°F; frozen: below -18°C/0°F) with a calibrated thermometer (insert into the food, between packages, or use an infrared on the surface), and reject the out-of-temperature delivery. Log the temperatures.

### Check For Quality, Freshness, And Damage

The quality (freshness, appearance, smell, texture) and the packaging (no damage, no tears, no leaks, no pest evidence) must be checked at receiving, because damaged or poor-quality product is waste or contamination risk. The judgment point is that the quality at receiving is the best it will be (it only declines), and accepting poor quality guarantees waste. Check the quality (freshness — see the relevant protein and produce skills), check the packaging (no damage, leaks, tears, pest evidence, swollen cans), and reject the substandard. For seafood and meat, the freshness at receiving is critical.

### Reject Or Document The Out-Of-Spec, And Request Credit

Deliveries that are out-of-spec (wrong temperature, short weight, poor quality, damaged, substituted) must be rejected (returned to the supplier) or documented (accepted but noted, with a credit requested), not silently accepted. The judgment point is that silent acceptance (signing and putting it away) signals to the supplier that the poor quality is acceptable, and it recurs; rejection or documentation (with credit) corrects the supplier. Reject the out-of-spec (return it, note it on the invoice, do not accept), or document it (note the discrepancy, request a credit), and communicate with the supplier. The invoice is the legal document; note the discrepancies before signing.

### Rotate And Store Immediately After Receiving

After inspection, the delivery must be rotated (FIFO — the new stock behind the older) and stored immediately (refrigerated, frozen, or dry, per the product), not left at the loading dock (where it warms into the danger zone). The judgment point is that the cold chain continues through the storage, and leaving the delivery out (even for an hour) breaks it. Rotate and store the delivery immediately after inspection (FIFO, correct temperature zone), and do not leave it at the dock. See the inventory skill for FIFO and the storage.

### Log The Receiving (Items, Quantities, Temperatures, Discrepancies)

The receiving should be logged (the items, the quantities, the temperatures for cold items, any discrepancies), for inventory tracking, food safety documentation, and supplier management. The judgment point is that the log is the record (for inventory, for inspection, for supplier disputes), and unlogged receiving is uncontrolled. Log the receiving (items, quantities, temperatures, discrepancies), keep the logs, and use them for inventory and supplier management.

## Common Traps

### Signing Without Inspecting And Accepting Short Or Wrong Deliveries

A receiver signs the invoice without inspecting (trusting the supplier), and the delivery is short (fewer items) or wrong (different items), and the discrepancy is missed. The mechanism is that the invoice is not the verification (the inspection is), and signing without inspecting accepts the discrepancy; the inspection is required. The false signal is that the delivery "looks right," which reads as complete. The harm is short or wrong inventory. The defense is to inspect every delivery (count, weigh, verify), to note discrepancies before signing, and to not sign blindly.

### Not Checking Temperatures And Accepting A Broken Cold Chain

A receiver does not check the temperatures of refrigerated and frozen deliveries, and a broken cold chain (the food warmed in transit) is accepted, creating a food safety risk. The mechanism is that the cold chain is invisible without a thermometer, and a warm delivery (even if it looks fine) may be unsafe; the temperature check is required. The false signal is that the delivery "is cold," which reads as compliant. The harm is a food safety risk. The defense is to check the temperatures (refrigerated below 4°C, frozen below -18°C) with a calibrated thermometer, to reject the out-of-temperature, and to log.

### Accepting Poor Quality Or Damage And Creating Waste

A receiver accepts poor-quality or damaged product (it's easier than rejecting), and it becomes waste (or contamination risk). The mechanism is that the quality at receiving is the best it will be, and accepting poor quality guarantees waste; the quality check and the rejection are required. The false signal is that the product "is here," which reads as accepted. The harm is waste and contamination risk. The defense is to check the quality and the packaging, to reject the substandard, and to recognize that accepting poor quality guarantees waste.

### Silently Accepting Substitutes And Letting The Menu Drift

A receiver accepts a substitute (the supplier sends a different brand or grade) without noting it, and the menu drifts (the different product cooks or tastes different). The mechanism is that substitutes (un-noted) change the product, and the menu drifts; the substitution must be noted (and accepted or rejected deliberately). The false signal is that the product "is the same," which reads as equivalent. The harm is menu drift. The defense is to verify against the spec, to note the substitutes, to accept or reject deliberately, and to communicate with the supplier.

### Leaving The Delivery At The Dock And Breaking The Cold Chain

A receiver inspects the delivery but leaves it at the loading dock (to put away later), and it warms into the danger zone, breaking the cold chain. The mechanism is that the cold chain continues through the storage, and leaving the delivery out (even briefly) breaks it; the immediate storage is required. The false signal is that the delivery "is inspected," which reads as done. The harm is a broken cold chain and a food safety risk. The defense is to rotate and store the delivery immediately after inspection (FIFO, correct temperature zone), and to not leave it at the dock.

### Not Logging The Receiving And Losing Inventory Control

A receiver does not log the receiving (items, quantities, temperatures, discrepancies), and the inventory is uncontrolled (no record for inventory, inspection, or disputes). The mechanism is that the log is the record (for inventory, food safety, supplier management), and unlogged receiving is uncontrolled; the log is required. The false signal is that the delivery "is in," which reads as recorded. The harm is uncontrolled inventory and no documentation. The defense is to log the receiving (items, quantities, temperatures, discrepancies), to keep the logs, and to use them.

## Self-Check

- Is every delivery inspected against the order and the spec (count, weigh, verify items and quality) before acceptance?
- Are the temperatures of refrigerated (below 4°C/40°F) and frozen (below -18°C/0°F) deliveries checked with a calibrated thermometer, and out-of-temperature rejected?
- Are the quality (freshness, appearance, smell) and the packaging (no damage, leaks, pest evidence) checked, and substandard rejected?
- Are out-of-spec deliveries rejected or documented (noted on the invoice, credit requested), and the supplier communicated with?
- Is the delivery rotated (FIFO) and stored immediately (correct temperature zone), not left at the dock?
- Is the receiving logged (items, quantities, temperatures, discrepancies), and are the logs kept for inventory and documentation?
- Is the reasoning documented so the inspection, temperature verification, quality check, rejection/documentation, storage, and logging for each delivery are verifiable?
