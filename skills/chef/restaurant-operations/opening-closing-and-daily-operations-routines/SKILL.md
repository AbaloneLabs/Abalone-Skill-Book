# Opening, Closing, and Daily Operations Routines

A restaurant's consistency is built not in the drama of service but in the unglamorous routines that happen before and after: the opening checks that confirm the kitchen is ready, the closing tasks that leave it clean and safe overnight, the ordering and receiving that keep it supplied, and the pre-service line-up that aligns the team. These routines are the operating system of the kitchen. When they are reliable, the kitchen is consistent, safe, and resilient; when they are sloppy or absent, problems accumulate—food safety lapses, stockouts, equipment failures, inconsistent execution—and surface as crises during service. The chef's job is to design these routines, train the team to them, and verify them daily, because what is not verified drifts.

This skill covers the design of daily operational routines: opening and closing checklists, cleaning and maintenance schedules, ordering and receiving, and the pre-service line-up.

This guidance addresses operations routine design. It does not replace applicable food safety regulation (including HACCP), health department requirements, labor law, or institutional policy. Adapt routines to the specific operation and regulatory environment.

## Core Rules

### Design checklists as verification tools, not paperwork

Opening and closing checklists exist to ensure that critical tasks are done every day, not to generate paper. Design them as verification tools: specific, sequenced, with a verification (initial, time, or photo) that confirms completion. Focus on the safety-critical and consistency-critical items: temperature checks, equipment startup and shutdown, sanitation, labeling and dating, security. A checklist that is initialed without the task being done is worse than none, because it creates false assurance; verify by spot-check.

### Build the day around the critical control points

Food safety and operational readiness both have critical control points: receiving temperatures, cold holding temperatures, cooking temperatures, cooling and reheating, hot holding, and time-temperature control. Build the daily routine around checking these points at the right times, documenting them, and acting on deviations. A routine that does not surface a temperature deviation before it becomes a hazard has failed its purpose.

### Sequence ordering and receiving to protect quality and cost

Ordering and receiving determine what enters the kitchen and at what cost. Sequence ordering to forecast demand and lead times, specify quality and quantity standards, and receive against the order—checking count, quality, temperature, and condition—before accepting. Receiving that skips inspection lets short, spoiled, or substituted product into inventory, eroding both quality and cost. Train the receiver to reject non-conforming product and document it.

### Make the pre-service line-up non-negotiable

A short, structured line-up before service aligns the team: the menu and any changes, 86s and pars, specials and their allergens, reservations and expected volume, VIPs and special requests, and a safety or quality focus for the day. The line-up is the single most efficient tool for preventing the communication failures that break service. Keep it brief, consistent, and attended by kitchen and floor leadership.

### Schedule cleaning and maintenance as routines, not emergencies

Cleaning and maintenance that are left to "when there's time" never happen and accumulate into health code violations, equipment failure, and a degraded workplace. Schedule daily, weekly, and periodic cleaning and maintenance tasks, assign ownership, and verify completion. Deep cleaning (hoods, drains, behind equipment) follows a periodic schedule with documentation. Preventive maintenance on equipment follows manufacturer intervals. What is scheduled and verified gets done; what is left to initiative does not.

### Close to a standard that makes opening reliable

The closing routine determines how opening goes. A kitchen closed to a standard—clean, restocked, equipment secured, temperatures logged, prep started for the next day—opens smoothly. A kitchen closed in haste opens in chaos. Define the closing standard, assign and verify the tasks, and resist the pressure to cut closing short on busy nights, because the cost is paid the next day.

## Common Traps

### Checklists as paperwork, not verification

Initialed checklists that don't reflect completed tasks create false assurance. Verify by spot-check and focus on safety-critical items.

### Skipping critical control point checks

A routine that doesn't surface temperature and sanitation deviations before they become hazards has failed. Build the day around critical control points.

### Receiving without inspection

Accepting product without checking count, quality, temperature, and condition lets problems into inventory. Receive against the order and reject non-conformance.

### Treating the line-up as optional

Skipping the pre-service line-up to save ten minutes costs hours in service communication failures. Make it non-negotiable.

### Cleaning and maintenance "when there's time"

Unscheduled cleaning and maintenance never happen and accumulate into violations and failures. Schedule, assign, and verify.

### Closing in haste

A sloppy close makes for a chaotic open. Close to a standard that makes opening reliable, even on busy nights.

## Self-Check

- Are opening and closing checklists specific, sequenced, and verified—with safety-critical items emphasized and spot-checked?
- Are critical control points (receiving, cold holding, cooking, cooling, hot holding) checked at the right times, documented, and acted on?
- Is ordering sequenced to forecast and lead times, and is receiving inspected against the order for count, quality, temperature, and condition?
- Is a structured pre-service line-up held daily, attended by kitchen and floor leadership, covering menu changes, 86s, specials, allergens, and volume?
- Are daily, weekly, and periodic cleaning and maintenance scheduled, assigned, and verified, with documentation?
- Is the closing routine held to a standard that makes opening reliable, with pressure to cut it short resisted?
- Are routines adapted to the specific operation and to applicable food safety regulation and health department requirements?
- Are routines reviewed and refined based on audit findings, incidents, and team feedback?
