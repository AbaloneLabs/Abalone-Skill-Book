# Menu Execution and 86 Management

A menu on paper is a promise; a menu executed consistently across hundreds of covers, multiple stations, and shifting staff is an operational achievement. Execution breaks down at the seams: a component runs short, a station cook is absent, a delivery is short, a special sells faster than projected. How the kitchen handles these moments—whether an item quietly degrades, a guest is surprised by a sold-out dish, or the team communicates and adapts cleanly—defines the guest experience and the kitchen's reputation. The "86" (an item is sold out and must come off the menu) is the canonical test: a well-managed 86 is invisible to most guests; a poorly managed one becomes a series of disappointed orders and frustrated servers.

This skill covers the operational execution of the menu: maintaining consistency across stations, managing 86 situations, communicating changes to the front of house, and executing specials and limited items without disrupting the core menu.

This guidance addresses service operations judgment. It does not replace food safety regulation, allergen protocols, or institutional policy. Ensure all menu changes are communicated to affected staff, including allergen information.

## Core Rules

### Standardize execution without killing craft

Consistency requires standards: recipes, portions, plating, and cook times documented and trained. But standardization that becomes rigid kills the craft and the cook's judgment. Define the non-negotiable elements (portions, core technique, allergen control, plate components) precisely, and leave room for the cook's skill in execution. Document the standard, train to it, verify it, and refine it. A menu without standards is inconsistent; a menu with only standards is soulless.

### Anticipate and manage 86s before they surprise the floor

An item running short is predictable from prep levels and sell rate. Monitor par levels during service, project when an item will sell out, and communicate the 86 to the floor before servers continue selling it. The worst 86 is the one discovered after the order is fired and the guest is waiting. The best is communicated proactively, with a suggested alternative, so the server can guide the guest. Build the habit of scanning pars and projecting sell-out through service.

### Communicate menu changes to the floor clearly and in real time

Menu changes—86s, specials added or cut, substitutions, allergen information—must reach every server, in real time, in a form they can act on. Use a consistent channel (a line-up briefing at the start of service, a written board, a digital update) and confirm receipt. Ambiguity or delay leads to servers selling items that are not available or misinforming guests about allergens. Treat floor communication as a critical path, not a courtesy.

### Execute specials with the same rigor as the core menu

Specials and limited-run items often fail not because the dish is weak but because they are under-prepared, under-trained, or inconsistent. Apply the same rigor to specials as to the core menu: full prep, recipe and portion standards, station assignment, allergen identification, and a clear price and description for the floor. A special that is invented at the line and sold verbally will be inconsistent and error-prone.

### Manage the menu's complexity against the kitchen's capacity

Every item on the menu adds complexity: prep, storage, station load, and execution risk during service. A menu that exceeds the kitchen's capacity degrades execution across all items. Audit the menu's complexity against the crew, the equipment, and the space, and trim or simplify where complexity exceeds capacity. A smaller menu executed consistently beats a larger menu executed unevenly.

### Build redundancy and cross-training for key components

If a component is used across multiple dishes, its failure affects the whole menu. Build redundancy: cross-train cooks on key components, maintain backup prep, and identify which components can be subbed or simplified under pressure. A single cook who alone can produce a key component is a point of failure.

## Common Traps

### Inconsistent execution from undocumented standards

Without documented recipes, portions, and plating, execution varies by cook and by shift. Standardize the non-negotiables and train to them.

### Reactive 86 management

Discovering a sell-out when the order is fired leaves the guest waiting and the server embarrassed. Monitor pars and project sell-out, communicating proactively.

### Poor floor communication

Menu changes that don't reach every server, or reach them late, cause wrong orders and misinformed guests. Use a consistent, confirmed channel.

### Under-prepared specials

Specials invented at the line and sold verbally are inconsistent and error-prone. Apply core-menu rigor to specials.

### Menu complexity exceeding capacity

Too many items degrade execution across the menu. Audit complexity against capacity and trim where needed.

### Single points of failure on key components

A component only one cook can produce, with no backup, is an operational risk. Cross-train and build redundancy.

## Self-Check

- Are the non-negotiable execution elements (portions, technique, allergen control, components) documented, trained, and verified, with room for craft?
- Am I monitoring pars during service and projecting sell-outs, communicating 86s to the floor before they surprise guests?
- Is there a consistent, confirmed channel for menu changes, and does every server receive them in real time?
- Are specials prepared, trained, and communicated with the same rigor as the core menu, including allergen information?
- Have I audited menu complexity against the kitchen's capacity and trimmed where complexity exceeds capability?
- Are key components cross-trained and backed up, eliminating single points of failure?
- Am I reviewing execution consistency across shifts and refining standards?
- Are allergen protocols maintained across all menu changes and communicated to the floor?
