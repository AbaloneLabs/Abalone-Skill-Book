# Recipe Standardization And Documentation

A recipe that lives in a chef's head is not a recipe; it is a liability. When the dish depends on the person who invented it, the kitchen cannot scale, cannot train, cannot cost accurately, and cannot guarantee that the guest who loved the dish on Tuesday will get the same dish on Saturday. Standardization is the discipline of writing down exactly what goes into a dish, in what quantity, by what method, to what sensory standard, yielding what portion, at what cost — so that any trained cook can reproduce it and the business can rely on the result. The judgment problem is that "to taste" and "about that much" feel like craft, so cooks resist the precision that makes a dish repeatable, and the kitchen drifts into inconsistency that erodes guest trust and cost control. This skill covers how to write, test, cost, and maintain recipes as the operational backbone of the menu.

Use this skill when creating or revising recipes, training cooks to follow them, costing dishes, or diagnosing why a dish varies across cooks or shifts.

## Core Rules

### Write Recipes By Weight, Not Volume Or Guesswork

Professional recipes specify ingredients by weight (grams, kilograms) because weight is exact and reproducible, whereas volume (cups, spoons) varies with how the ingredient is packed, its grind, and who is measuring. "A cup of flour" can vary by 20 percent depending on scooping; "120 grams of flour" is the same every time. Use weight for everything that affects yield, cost, or chemistry — flours, sugars, fats, salts, liquids — and reserve volume only for minor seasonings where the variation is tolerable. Metric weights are easier to scale than imperial. Precision in measurement is the foundation of reproducibility.

### Specify Method, Time, Temperature, And Sensory Standard

A recipe is more than an ingredient list; it is a procedure. Specify the method (sweat the onions without browning, sear hard on one side), the time and temperature (180°C oven for 12 minutes, simmer 45 minutes), and the sensory standard for doneness (internal temperature 63°C, crust deep mahogany, sauce coats the back of a spoon). These cues together allow a cook to hit the target regardless of equipment variation, because they triangulate: if the oven runs hot, the visual cue catches it; if the visual is ambiguous, the temperature confirms. "Cook until done" is not a recipe; it is an invitation to inconsistency.

### Cost Every Recipe Accurately, Including Yield Loss

Standardized recipes are the basis of food costing, and accurate costing requires accounting for yield loss — the weight lost in trimming, peeling, cooking, and portioning. A kilogram of untrimmed fish does not yield a kilogram of portions; the as-served yield may be 60 percent, and the recipe cost must reflect the input cost divided by the usable yield. Document the yield percentage for each component, cost the recipe at the as-served portion, and compute both the food cost percentage and the contribution margin. Without yield accounting, costs are understated and prices are set too low. Costing is not bookkeeping; it is the information that makes pricing defensible.

### Document Yield, Portion Size, And Plating Spec

Every recipe must state the total yield (number of portions, weight of finished product), the portion size (weight or count per serving), and the plating specification (components, order, garnish, vessel). These three together let the kitchen predict how many covers a batch supports, control what each guest receives, and present the dish consistently. Without documented yield, the kitchen cannot order or prep accurately; without portion control, food cost drifts; without a plating spec, the dish looks different every time. Treat yield, portion, and plating as recipe fields as essential as the ingredient list.

### Test Recipes At Volume Before Standardizing

A recipe that works for four portions at a calm test may fail at forty portions during service, because scaling changes timing, equipment behavior, and the cook's capacity to execute each step. Test new recipes at realistic service volume, with the team that will cook them, on the equipment they will use, before declaring them standardized. Adjust the recipe based on what the test reveals — maybe the sear step needs to be batched, maybe the sauce breaks at volume, maybe the timing needs a rest built in. A recipe is only standardized after it has survived a real service.

### Version And Date Recipes, And Manage Changes Formally

Recipes evolve, and uncontrolled change destroys standardization. When a recipe is updated — a new supplier, a technique improvement, a cost adjustment — record the change, the date, the reason, and the person who made it, and distribute the new version while retiring the old. Versioning prevents the common failure where two cooks are using two different versions of "the same" recipe and producing two different dishes. Treat the recipe file as a controlled document, with a single current version accessible to all, and a change process that prevents silent drift.

### Train Cooks To Follow The Recipe, Then To Propose Changes Through Process

Cooks should follow the standardized recipe exactly, because that is what guarantees the guest the dish they expect. Improvements are welcome but must go through a formal change process — propose, test, document, distribute — rather than silent improvisation on the line. Silent improvisation is how a dish drifts away from its costed, tested standard without anyone noticing, until food cost rises or a guest complains that "it changed." Build a culture where following the recipe is the default and improving it is a structured activity, so that craft and consistency coexist.

## Common Traps

### "To Taste" And "About That Much" In A Standardized Recipe

A recipe specifies "salt to taste" or "a splash of wine" or "cook until it looks right," and every cook produces a different dish. The false signal is that the recipe exists on paper. The mechanism is that subjective quantities encode no standard, so each cook's palate, habit, and the day's ingredient variation produce a different result, and the dish the guest receives depends on who is on the station; the recipe provides no reproducibility, which is its entire purpose. The harm is inconsistent dishes, eroded guest trust, unpredictable food cost, and an inability to train new cooks because there is nothing fixed to teach. The defense is to specify quantities by weight, method by step, and doneness by measurable cues (temperature, time, sensory standard), reserving "to taste" only for final micro-adjustments that are documented as part of the procedure. A recipe that cannot be followed literally is not a recipe.

### Ignoring Yield Loss In Costing

A chef costs a dish based on the invoice weight of raw protein, ignoring that trimming and cooking remove 40 percent, and sets a price that loses money on every sale. The false signal is that the cost calculation "looks right" against the invoice. The mechanism is that yield loss — trim, peel, shrink, portioning waste — means the usable as-served portion costs far more per gram than the raw input, and costing on raw weight understates the true food cost, so the menu price is set too low and the margin evaporates on every plate sold. The harm is silent, structural unprofitability that survives even high sales volume. The defense is to document the yield percentage for each component through actual testing, cost the recipe on the as-served yield, and recompute the contribution margin to set defensible prices. The invoice is not the cost; the plated portion is the cost.

### Volume Failure Of A Quietly-Tested Recipe

A recipe tested at four portions is added to the menu, and at forty portions during service the timing collapses, the sauce breaks, and the dish goes out wrong. The false signal is that the recipe "worked" in testing. The mechanism is that scaling changes everything — heat recovery, batch timing, the cook's capacity to execute each step under volume — so a recipe that is calm and successful at low volume becomes impossible at peak, and the failure surfaces only when the menu launches and the guests arrive. The harm is a disastrous service, bad first reviews, and a dish that must be pulled or reworked under pressure. The defense is to test every new recipe at realistic service volume with the actual team and equipment before standardizing, to adjust for what the volume test reveals, and to treat a low-volume test as insufficient evidence. A recipe is standardized only after it survives a real rush.

### Two Cooks Using Two Versions Of The Same Recipe

A recipe is changed informally — a cook starts adding more acid, the sous chef adjusts the salt — and the change is never documented or distributed, so half the team uses the old version and half the new, and the dish varies by shift. The false signal is that everyone is "making the same dish." The mechanism is that without version control, recipe changes propagate by word of mouth or not at all, so the recipe file and the actual practice diverge, and the kitchen produces multiple versions of a dish that is supposed to be one thing. The harm is inconsistency that frustrates guests and defeats cost control, plus an inability to diagnose problems because nobody knows which version caused them. The defense is formal version control: dated, attributed, distributed changes with old versions retired, and a single current recipe accessible to all, so that "the recipe" is unambiguous. A recipe that exists in multiple forms does not exist.

### Portion Drift From Eye-Balling

Cooks portion by eye because weighing feels slow, and over weeks the portions drift larger or smaller, with no one noticing because each shift looks fine. The false signal is that the portions look about right. The mechanism is that eye-balling has no fixed reference, so each cook's "right" is slightly different and the average shifts as staff turn over or fatigue sets in, and the cumulative effect across thousands of covers is either significant cost overrun (larger portions) or guest complaints (smaller portions). The harm is silent margin erosion or reputation damage, both expensive and both invisible in daily operation. The defense is to portion by weight to a documented spec, with scales at every station and periodic audit of actual served portions against spec, treating eye-balling as a known source of drift to be controlled. The scale is the only honest portioner.

### Recipe Drift Through Silent Line Improvisation

A cook improves a dish on the fly — a different sear, an extra seasoning — and the improvement works, but it is never documented, so the next cook reverts to the old method and the dish is inconsistent until someone else rediscovers the improvement. The false signal is that the dish "got better." The mechanism is that unrecorded improvements are lost when the innovating cook is absent, and silent improvisation also goes the other way — a cook takes a shortcut that degrades the dish without anyone catching it — so the recipe drifts in both directions away from its tested standard. The harm is a dish that is sometimes better and sometimes worse than its standard, with no control over which. The defense is a culture where following the recipe is the default and improvements are proposed, tested, documented, and distributed through a formal process, so that good changes are captured and bad ones are caught. Craft and consistency coexist only through process.

## Self-Check

- Are all ingredients specified by weight (preferably metric), with volume reserved only for minor seasonings where variation is tolerable?
- Does each recipe specify method, time, temperature, and a measurable sensory standard for doneness, rather than "cook until done"?
- Is every recipe costed on as-served yield (accounting for trim and cooking loss), with food cost percentage and contribution margin computed?
- Does each recipe document total yield, portion size, and plating specification as essential fields?
- Was every recipe tested at realistic service volume with the actual team and equipment before being standardized?
- Are recipes version-controlled with dated, attributed, distributed changes and a single current version, with old versions retired?
- Are cooks trained to follow the recipe exactly and to propose improvements through a formal change process rather than silent line improvisation?
