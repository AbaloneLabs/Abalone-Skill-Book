# Traditional Technique Preservation and Documentation

Traditional culinary techniques (the regional methods, the oral recipes, the hand-skills passed through generations) are endangered, and the judgment problem is that they are transmitted orally and experientially (master to apprentice, parent to child), and when the chain breaks (the master dies, the apprentice does not continue, the community disperses), the technique is lost — and documenting it (in writing, in video, in archives) is the preservation, but the documentation must capture not just the recipe but the technique (the hand-feel, the visual cues, the timing, the judgment) that the written word alone cannot convey. The recurring failure is documenting only the recipe (the ingredients, the basic method) and losing the technique (the tacit knowledge — the feel of the dough, the smell of the readiness, the visual cue), or documenting too late (after the master is gone), or not archiving accessibly (the documentation is lost). The discipline is to document the technique (not just the recipe), to use video and the master's narration, to document while the master can teach, and to archive accessibly.

The agent's job is to preserve the techniques (document the tacit knowledge), to record the masters, and to archive for the future.

## Core Rules

### Document The Technique, Not Just The Recipe

A traditional technique is more than the recipe (the ingredients and the basic method) — it includes the tacit knowledge (the hand-feel — the texture of the dough, the pressure of the shaping; the sensory cues — the smell of the readiness, the sound of the cooking, the visual cue of the color; the timing — the pace, the rests; the judgment — the adjustments based on the conditions). The judgment point is that the recipe alone (the written ingredients and method) loses the tacit knowledge, and the technique documentation (the video, the master's narration, the sensory cues) captures it. Document the technique (not just the recipe) — use video to capture the hand-skills, record the master's narration (the cues, the judgment, the stories), and document the tacit knowledge (the feel, the smell, the visual cues).

### Use Video And The Master's Narration

Video is the most effective documentation for the technique (it captures the hand-skills, the pace, the visual cues that the written word cannot), and the master's narration (describing what they feel, smell, see, and decide) captures the tacit knowledge. The judgment point is that the written word alone cannot convey the hand-skill or the sensory cue, and the video (with the master's narration) is the medium that captures the technique. Document with video (multiple angles — the hands, the whole, the close-up), record the master's narration (what they feel, smell, see, decide), and capture the tacit knowledge. The video is the primary record; the writing is the supplement.

### Document While The Master Can Teach

The documentation must happen while the master is alive and able to teach (the technique is in their hands and their memory), and documenting too late (after the master is gone, or unable) loses the technique. The judgment point is that the technique is embodied (in the master), and the documentation must capture it while the master can demonstrate and narrate; timeliness is critical. Identify the masters (the elders, the tradition-holders), document while they can teach (prioritize the oldest, the most endangered), and do not delay. The loss of a master is the loss of a technique.

### Record The Context: History, Meaning, And Community

The technique's context (the history — how and why it developed; the meaning — what it signifies; the community — who makes it, for whom, when) is part of the technique, and documenting it (alongside the method) preserves the full tradition. The judgment point is that the technique without the context is a hollow skill (the how without the why), and the context (the history, the meaning, the community) gives it the significance. Record the context (the history, the meaning, the community — from the master and the community's own voices), and document it alongside the technique. See the cultural appropriation skill for the context.

### Archive Accessibly For The Future

The documentation must be archived accessibly (in a durable format, in a findable location, with the permissions for use), so it is preserved and usable for the future (the next generation, the researchers, the community). The judgment point is that unarchived or inaccessible documentation is lost (the video on an old phone, the writing in a drawer), and the accessible archive (a digital archive, a community library, a culinary heritage organization) preserves it. Archive accessibly (a durable digital format, a findable location — a culinary archive, a community library, a heritage organization), with the permissions for use (the community's consent), and ensure the preservation. The archive is the preservation.

### Build Master-Apprentice Relationships For The Living Transmission

The documentation (the video, the writing) is a record, but the living transmission (the master teaching the apprentice, hands-on) is the continuation, and building the master-apprentice relationships (supporting the apprentices, compensating the masters) keeps the tradition alive. The judgment point is that the documentation preserves the record, but the living transmission (the master-apprentice) continues the tradition, and both are needed. Build the master-apprentice relationships (support the apprentices — training, stipends; compensate the masters — teaching fees), and foster the living transmission alongside the documentation. The tradition lives in the hands of the next apprentice.

## Common Traps

### Documenting Only The Recipe And Losing The Tacit Knowledge

A documenter records the recipe (the ingredients, the basic method) but not the technique (the hand-feel, the sensory cues, the judgment), and the tacit knowledge is lost. The mechanism is that the technique is the tacit knowledge (the feel, the smell, the visual cue), and the recipe alone loses it; the technique documentation (video, narration) is required. The false signal is that the recipe "is recorded," which reads as preserved. The harm is the loss of the tacit knowledge. The defense is to document the technique (video, master's narration, sensory cues), not just the recipe, and to capture the tacit knowledge.

### Documenting Too Late And Losing The Technique

A documenter delays the documentation (other priorities), and the master dies (or becomes unable), and the technique is lost. The mechanism is that the technique is embodied (in the master), and the documentation must happen while the master can teach; delaying loses the technique. The false signal is that there "is time," which reads as deferrable. The harm is the permanent loss of the technique. The defense is to prioritize the documentation (the oldest, the most endangered masters), to document while they can teach, and to not delay.

### Using Only The Written Word And Missing The Hand-Skill

A documenter uses only the written word (a text description of the technique), and the hand-skill (the feel, the pace, the visual cue) is missed. The mechanism is that the written word cannot convey the hand-skill or the sensory cue, and the video (with the narration) is the medium that captures it; the video is required. The false signal is that the text "describes it," which reads as complete. The harm is the missed hand-skill. The defense is to use video (multiple angles, the master's narration), to capture the hand-skill and the sensory cues, and to use the writing as the supplement.

### Not Recording The Context And Creating A Hollow Record

A documenter records the technique (the method) but not the context (the history, the meaning, the community), and the record is hollow (the how without the why). The mechanism is that the context is part of the technique, and without it, the record is a hollow skill; the context is required. The false signal is that the technique "is recorded," which reads as preserved. The harm is a hollow record. The defense is to record the context (the history, the meaning, the community), from the master and the community's voices, and to document it alongside the technique.

### Not Archiving Accessibly And Losing The Documentation

A documenter creates the documentation but does not archive it accessibly (it sits on an old device, in a drawer), and it is lost. The mechanism is that the documentation must be archived accessibly (durable format, findable location, permissions), and unarchived documentation is lost; the archive is the preservation. The false signal is that the documentation "exists," which reads as preserved. The harm is the loss of the documentation. The defense is to archive accessibly (a durable digital format, a culinary archive, a community library, a heritage organization), with the permissions, and to ensure the preservation.

### No Living Transmission And The Tradition Dying With The Master

A documenter records the technique but does not build the master-apprentice relationships (the living transmission), and the tradition dies with the master (the documentation is a record, not a continuation). The mechanism is that the documentation preserves the record, but the living transmission (master-apprentice) continues the tradition, and both are needed; the relationships are required. The false signal is that the documentation "preserves it," which reads as sufficient. The harm is the tradition's end. The defense is to build the master-apprentice relationships (support the apprentices, compensate the masters), to foster the living transmission, and to continue the tradition.

## Self-Check

- Is the technique documented (not just the recipe), using video (multiple angles) and the master's narration (the feel, smell, visual cues, judgment)?
- Is the documentation done while the master can teach (prioritizing the oldest, the most endangered), not delayed?
- Is video used (not just the written word), to capture the hand-skill and the sensory cues?
- Is the context recorded (the history, the meaning, the community, from the culture's voices), alongside the technique?
- Is the documentation archived accessibly (durable format, findable location — a culinary archive, a community library, a heritage organization), with the permissions?
- Are the master-apprentice relationships built (apprentices supported, masters compensated), for the living transmission alongside the documentation?
- Is the reasoning documented so the technique capture, timeliness, video use, context, archiving, and living transmission for each traditional technique are verifiable?
