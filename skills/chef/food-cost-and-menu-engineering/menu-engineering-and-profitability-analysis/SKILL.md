# Menu Engineering and Profitability Analysis

A menu is not just a list of dishes; it is a financial instrument, and the judgment problem is that each item has a popularity (how often it sells) and a margin (the profit per sale), and the combination determines whether the item is a Star (high popularity, high margin — promote it), a Plowhorse (high popularity, low margin — a workhorse, keep but optimize), a Puzzle (low popularity, high margin — a puzzle, promote or reposition), or a Dog (low popularity, low margin — remove it), and the menu's overall profitability depends on this mix. The recurring failure is keeping items for sentiment or tradition (the Dog that drags the menu), or not promoting the Stars and Puzzles, or pricing for popularity without margin. The discipline is to classify the items, to act on the classification (promote, optimize, reposition, remove), and to design the menu to steer guests to the high-margin items.

The agent's job is to engineer the menu for profitability, to act on the data, and to design the menu to steer the guest.

## Core Rules

### Classify Items By Popularity And Margin (Stars, Plowhorses, Puzzles, Dogs)

Each menu item is classified by its popularity (sales count, compared to the average) and its margin (profit per sale, compared to the average), into four categories: Stars (high popularity, high margin — the best, promote and feature), Plowhorses (high popularity, low margin — popular but low-profit, keep but optimize the cost or the price), Puzzles (low popularity, high margin — profitable but slow-selling, promote or reposition), and Dogs (low popularity, low margin — the worst, remove or re-invent). The judgment point is that the classification drives the action, and sentiment (keeping a Dog because "it's a classic") undermines profitability. Classify each item, and act on the classification.

### Act On The Classification: Promote, Optimize, Reposition, Remove

The classification drives the action: promote the Stars and the Puzzles (menu placement, descriptions, photos, staff recommendations), optimize the Plowhorses (reduce the cost — cheaper ingredient, smaller portion — or raise the price), reposition the Puzzles (better menu placement, description, pairing), and remove the Dogs (or re-invent them). The judgment point is that the data must drive action, and an unacted-upon analysis is wasted. Act on each classification: promote Stars/Puzzles, optimize Plowhorses, reposition Puzzles, remove Dogs. Review the menu quarterly and act.

### Design The Menu To Steer To High-Margin Items

The menu design (the layout, the placement, the descriptions, the highlighting) steers the guest to the high-margin items: the "sweet spot" (the area the eye lands first, usually upper right), the callout boxes and the photos, the staff recommendations, and the menu descriptions (sensory, evocative language sells). The judgment point is that the menu is a marketing tool, and its design influences what sells; placing the Stars and Puzzles in the sweet spot, with compelling descriptions, increases their sales. Place high-margin items in the sweet spot, use callouts and descriptions to highlight them, train staff to recommend them, and avoid highlighting low-margin items.

### Price For Margin, Not Just For Popularity

Pricing for popularity (low prices to drive traffic) without margin erodes profitability, and each item must be priced to hit its target margin (see the recipe costing skill). The judgment point is that the margin (not the popularity) is the goal, and a popular item with no margin (a Plowhorse) drags the menu; the price must cover the cost and contribute the profit. Price each item to hit its target margin (from the true plate cost), recognize that Plowhorses may need a price increase (or a cost reduction), and that loss leaders (intentionally low-margin, like a cheap burger to draw traffic) must be balanced by high-margin items.

### Balance The Menu: Loss Leaders, Stars, Workhorses

A profitable menu has a balance: loss leaders (low-margin, high-popularity items that draw traffic — a cheap burger, a happy-hour special), Stars (high-margin, high-popularity — the profit engines), Plowhorses (the workhorses that sell well and cover costs), and a few Puzzles (high-margin, being tested for popularity). The judgment point is that the menu is a portfolio, and the balance (not every item is a Star) optimizes the overall profitability. Balance the menu (loss leaders to draw, Stars for profit, Plowhorses for volume, Puzzles for testing), and ensure the overall margin (the menu mix) hits the target.

### Review And Re-Engineer Regularly (Quarterly)

Menu engineering is not a one-time exercise; items shift (a Star may become a Plowhorse as costs rise, a Puzzle may become a Star with promotion), and the menu must be reviewed and re-engineered regularly (quarterly, with updated sales and cost data). The judgment point is that the menu is dynamic, and regular review keeps it profitable. Review the menu quarterly (update the popularity and margin data, re-classify, act), and do not let the menu stagnate.

## Common Traps

### Keeping A Dog For Sentiment And Dragging The Menu

A chef keeps a low-popularity, low-margin item (a Dog) because "it's a classic" or "a few regulars love it," and it drags the menu's profitability. The mechanism is that a Dog takes menu space, prep time, and inventory (often wasted), and contributes little profit; the data says remove it, but sentiment overrides. The false signal is that the item "has fans," which reads as worth keeping. The harm is dragged profitability. The defense is to classify by data, to act on the classification (remove or re-invent the Dog), and to not let sentiment override the numbers.

### Not Promoting The Stars And Leaving Profit On The Table

A chef has high-margin, high-popularity Stars but does not feature them (they're buried on the menu, no callout, no staff recommendation), and their sales are lower than they could be. The mechanism is that the menu design influences sales, and a buried Star sells less than a featured one; promoting the Stars (sweet spot, callout, staff recommendation) maximizes the profit. The false signal is that the Star "sells well," which reads as sufficient. The harm is left-on-the-table profit. The defense is to feature the Stars (sweet spot, callout, description, staff recommendation), to steer the guest to them, and to maximize the high-margin sales.

### Pricing For Popularity Without Margin And Eroding Profitability

A chef prices items low (to drive traffic and popularity) without ensuring the margin, and the menu is popular but unprofitable. The mechanism is that popularity without margin (low prices, high volume) erodes the profitability, and each item must be priced to hit its target margin; the price must cover the cost and contribute the profit. The false signal is that the menu "is busy," which reads as successful. The harm is an unprofitable menu. The defense is to price for margin (from the true plate cost and target %), to recognize that Plowhorses may need a price increase, and to balance loss leaders with high-margin items.

### Not Repositioning Puzzles And Missing The Opportunity

A chef has high-margin, low-popularity Puzzles but does not reposition them (better placement, description, pairing), and their slow sales persist, missing the profit opportunity. The mechanism is that a Puzzle is profitable but under-selling, and repositioning (sweet spot, compelling description, staff recommendation, pairing) can lift it to a Star; not repositioning leaves the profit unrealized. The false signal is that the Puzzle "doesn't sell," which reads as a failure to remove. The harm is missed profit. The defense is to reposition the Puzzles (placement, description, pairing, promotion), to test if they can become Stars, and to remove them only if repositioning fails.

### Over-Highlighting And Creating Choice Paralysis

A chef highlights too many items (callouts, photos, "chef's recommendations" on everything), and the guest experiences choice paralysis (too many highlighted options, no clear steer), reducing the steering effect. The mechanism is that highlighting works by contrast (a few items stand out), and over-highlighting (everything is highlighted) eliminates the contrast, causing paralysis; selective highlighting is required. The false signal is that "more is better," which reads as promotional. The harm is reduced steering and lower sales of the target items. The defense is to highlight selectively (a few Stars and Puzzles), to use the sweet spot for the top item, and to avoid over-highlighting.

### Not Reviewing The Menu Regularly And Letting It Stagnate

A chef engineers the menu once and does not review it, and items shift (a Star becomes a Plowhorse as costs rise, a Dog emerges), dragging the profitability. The mechanism is that the menu is dynamic (costs change, popularity shifts), and a one-time engineering is not enough; regular review keeps it profitable. The false signal is that the menu "was engineered," which reads as done. The harm is stagnating profitability. The defense is to review the menu quarterly (update the data, re-classify, act), and to keep the menu dynamic.

## Self-Check

- Is each menu item classified by popularity and margin (Star, Plowhorse, Puzzle, Dog), and is the classification acted on (promote, optimize, reposition, remove)?
- Are the Stars and Puzzles promoted (sweet spot, callout, description, staff recommendation), and are the Dogs removed or re-invented?
- Is the menu designed to steer to high-margin items (sweet spot placement, selective highlighting, compelling descriptions, staff recommendations)?
- Is each item priced for margin (from the true plate cost and target %), with Plowhorses optimized (cost or price)?
- Is the menu balanced (loss leaders to draw, Stars for profit, Plowhorses for volume, Puzzles for testing), with the overall margin hitting the target?
- Is the menu reviewed and re-engineered regularly (quarterly, with updated data), keeping it dynamic and profitable?
- Is the reasoning documented so the classification, actions, menu design, pricing, balance, and review cycle for the menu are verifiable?
