# Inventory Turnover and Spoilage Control

Inventory is tied-up cash and perishable risk, and the judgment problem is that too much inventory (over-ordering) leads to spoilage (perishables expire), cash tied up, and clutter, while too little (under-ordering) leads to stockouts (86'd items, lost sales) and emergency purchasing (higher prices, rush fees). The recurring failure is ordering by habit or fear (over-ordering "to be safe"), not tracking the usage (so the order is blind), or not rotating the stock (first-in, first-out), so the older stock spoils. The discipline is to track the usage, to set par levels, to order to the par (just-in-time), to rotate (FIFO), and to monitor the turnover and the spoilage.

The agent's job is to manage the inventory for cost and freshness, to order precisely, and to minimize spoilage.

## Core Rules

### Track Usage And Set Par Levels

Each item's usage (how much is used per week, on average) and par level (the maximum stock to hold, based on the usage and the delivery frequency) must be tracked and set, so the ordering is data-driven (order to the par, minus what's on hand). The judgment point is that ordering without usage data is guessing (and leads to over- or under-ordering), and the par level (based on the usage and the lead time) is the target. Track the usage (weekly inventory counts), set the par (usage x lead time + buffer), and order to the par (par minus on-hand). Adjust the pars as the usage changes.

### Order To The Par (Just-In-Time), Not By Habit

Ordering should be to the par (order the amount needed to reach the par, based on the usage and the lead time), not by habit ("order the same as last week") or fear ("order extra to be safe"). The judgment point is that habit and fear lead to over-ordering (and spoilage) or under-ordering (and stockouts), and the par-based order (just-in-time) is the control. Order to the par (par minus on-hand), adjust for events (a banquet, a holiday), and do not over-order "to be safe." Just-in-time ordering reduces spoilage and frees cash.

### Rotate Stock First-In, First-Out (FIFO)

Perishable stock must be rotated first-in, first-out (the older stock is used before the newer), so the older stock does not spoil in the back. The judgment point is that FIFO prevents spoilage (the older stock is used first), and it requires physical organization (the newer stock goes behind the older, or is labeled with the date). Store the newer stock behind the older (or label with the delivery date), train staff to pull from the front (the older), and date-label the perishables. FIFO is the core of spoilage control.

### Monitor Inventory Turnover And Target It

Inventory turnover (how often the inventory is sold/used and replaced in a period, calculated as the cost of goods sold / average inventory value) measures efficiency: a high turnover (fresh stock, less spoilage, less tied-up cash) is good, a low turnover (dead stock, spoilage, tied-up cash) is bad. The judgment point is that turnover is the efficiency metric, and a target (typically 4-8 times per month for perishables, lower for dry goods) drives the ordering. Calculate the turnover, target it (reduce ordering if too low, investigate stockouts if too high), and use it to assess the inventory management.

### Track And Minimize Spoilage

Spoilage (expired, rotten, or wasted stock) is a direct loss, and it must be tracked (a spoilage log: what, how much, why) and minimized (through precise ordering, FIFO, and using items before they expire). The judgment point is that spoilage is a controllable cost, and tracking it (the log) reveals the causes (over-ordering, poor rotation, a recipe change) and the fixes. Track the spoilage (log it), analyze the causes, and fix them (order less, rotate better, use the item in a special). Spoilage should be under 2% of the food cost.

### Manage Supplier Terms And Delivery Frequency

The supplier terms (delivery frequency, minimum order, payment terms) affect the inventory: frequent deliveries (daily or twice-weekly for produce, seafood) allow just-in-time ordering (low inventory, fresh stock), while infrequent deliveries (weekly) require higher inventory (and more spoilage risk). The judgment point is that the delivery frequency enables the inventory strategy, and frequent deliveries (for perishables) support just-in-time. Negotiate frequent deliveries for perishables (produce, seafood, dairy), use less-frequent for dry goods, and align the ordering with the delivery schedule.

## Common Traps

### Over-Ordering "To Be Safe" And Causing Spoilage

A chef over-orders perishables ("to be safe," fearing a stockout), and the excess spoils before it's used. The mechanism is that over-ordering (beyond the par, beyond the usage) leads to spoilage (perishables expire), and the fear of stockout causes the waste; par-based ordering (just-in-time) is the control. The false signal is that the stock "is full," which reads as secure. The harm is spoilage and tied-up cash. The defense is to order to the par (based on usage), to trust the data, and to not over-order out of fear.

### Not Tracking Usage And Ordering Blind

A chef orders by habit ("same as last week") without tracking the usage, and the order is wrong (too much or too little) as the usage changes. The mechanism is that ordering without usage data is guessing, and as the usage changes (seasonal, menu changes, trends), the habit-based order is wrong; tracking and par-based ordering are required. The false signal is that the order "is routine," which reads as reliable. The harm is over- or under-ordering. The defense is to track the usage (weekly counts), to set and adjust the pars, and to order to the par.

### Not Rotating (No FIFO) And Letting The Older Stock Spoil

Staff put the new stock in front (or do not label the dates), and the older stock spoils in the back. The mechanism is that without FIFO (first-in, first-out), the older stock is not used first and spoils, and the physical organization (new behind old, date labels) and the staff habit (pull from the front) are required. The false signal is that the stock "is organized," which reads as rotated. The harm is spoilage. The defense is to store new behind old (or date-label), to train staff to pull from the front, and to enforce FIFO.

### Holding Dead Stock And Tying Up Cash

A chef holds onto dead stock (items no longer on the menu, or over-ordered specialty items), and it ties up cash and clutters the storage. The mechanism is that dead stock (no longer used) ties up cash and space, and it should be used (in a special), donated, or written off; holding it is a loss. The false signal is that the stock "might be used," which reads as an asset. The harm is tied-up cash and clutter. The defense is to identify the dead stock, to use it (a special, a staff meal), to donate or write it off, and to avoid over-ordering specialties.

### Not Tracking Spoilage And Missing The Causes

A chef does not track the spoilage (no log), and the causes (over-ordering, poor rotation, a recipe change) are missed, so the spoilage persists. The mechanism is that spoilage must be tracked (the log) to reveal the causes and the fixes, and untracked spoilage persists; the log is the diagnostic. The false signal is that the spoilage "is normal," which reads as uncontrollable. The harm is persistent, unaddressed spoilage. The defense is to track the spoilage (log it: what, how much, why), to analyze the causes, and to fix them.

### Infrequent Deliveries And Holding Too Much Perishable Inventory

A chef uses weekly produce deliveries (to save on delivery fees), and holds a week's worth of perishables, leading to spoilage (produce expires before the week ends). The mechanism is that infrequent deliveries require higher inventory (a week's worth), and perishables (produce, seafood) do not last the week; frequent deliveries (daily or twice-weekly) support just-in-time and freshness. The false signal is that the delivery fee "is saved," which reads as efficient. The harm is spoilage that exceeds the delivery savings. The defense is to use frequent deliveries for perishables, to order just-in-time, and to recognize the spoilage-delivery tradeoff.

## Self-Check

- Is the usage tracked (weekly counts), are par levels set (usage x lead time + buffer), and is ordering to the par (par minus on-hand)?
- Is the ordering par-based (just-in-time), adjusted for events, not by habit or fear ("to be safe")?
- Is the stock rotated FIFO (new behind old, date-labeled, pull from the front), with staff trained?
- Is the inventory turnover calculated and targeted (4-8x/month for perishables), used to assess the inventory management?
- Is the spoilage tracked (log: what, how much, why), under 2% of food cost, with causes analyzed and fixed?
- Are the supplier terms managed (frequent deliveries for perishables, aligned ordering), supporting just-in-time?
- Is the dead stock identified and used, donated, or written off, avoiding tied-up cash?
- Is the reasoning documented so the usage tracking, par setting, ordering, rotation, turnover, spoilage, supplier terms, and dead stock for the inventory are verifiable?
