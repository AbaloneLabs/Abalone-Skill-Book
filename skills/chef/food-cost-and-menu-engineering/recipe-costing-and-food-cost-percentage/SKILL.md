# Recipe Costing and Food Cost Percentage

Recipe costing is the financial foundation of a menu, and the judgment problem is that the true cost of a plate is not the sum of the ingredient prices — it must account for the as-purchased (AP) vs. as-served (AS) yield (bones, trim, shrinkage), the waste (spoilage, mistakes, over-portioning), and the extras (garnishes, sauces, bread, oil for frying), and a costing that ignores these undercosts the plate and erodes the margin. The recurring failure is costing from the invoice price without the yield, ignoring the waste and the extras, or not updating the costing when prices change, so the menu is priced on stale or incomplete data. The discipline is to cost from the AS yield, to include all components, to update regularly, and to set the price from the true cost.

The agent's job is to cost recipes accurately, to set prices that protect the margin, and to monitor for cost variance.

## Core Rules

### Cost From The As-Served (AS) Yield, Not The As-Purchased (AP) Price

The true cost of an ingredient is based on the as-served (AS) yield — the amount that actually reaches the plate — not the as-purchased (AP) price, because trimming (bones, peels, fat), cooking shrinkage (moisture loss), and butchering reduce the usable amount. The judgment point is that the AP price per kg divided by the yield percentage gives the AS cost per kg, and costing from the AP price undercosts the plate. Calculate the AS yield (e.g., a whole chicken yields 65% usable meat, so the AS cost is the AP price divided by 0.65), and cost from the AS. Use yield tables (or test the yield) for each ingredient.

### Include All Components: Garnishes, Sauces, Oils, Bread, Condiments

The plate cost includes every component: the protein, the starch, the vegetable, the sauce, the garnish, the bread (if included), the butter, the condiments, and even the frying oil (a significant cost for fried items) and the salt and pepper (a small but real cost). The judgment point is that omitting the "small" components (the sauce, the garnish, the oil) undercosts the plate, and these add up. List every component, cost each one (even the small ones), and sum to the plate cost. Do not forget the frying oil (costed per use, as it degrades), the garnishes, and the condiments.

### Account For Waste: Spoilage, Mistakes, Over-Portioning

Waste (spoilage, prep mistakes, over-portioning, plate waste) is a real cost that must be accounted for, either in the costing (a waste factor) or in the margin (tracked as a variance). The judgment point is that waste erodes the margin, and ignoring it (costing only the perfect plate) hides the true cost. Track the waste (spoilage, mistakes, over-portioning), apply a waste factor to the costing (or track the variance), and work to reduce it. Over-portioning (a 6-oz portion instead of the costed 5-oz) is a common, invisible margin-killer.

### Set The Price From The True Cost And The Target Food Cost Percentage

The menu price is set from the true plate cost and the target food cost percentage (typically 25-35%, depending on the concept): Price = Plate Cost / Target Food Cost %. The judgment point is that the food cost percentage (the portion of the price that is food cost) is the key metric, and the price must be set to hit the target. Calculate the price (Plate Cost / Target %), round to a menu-appropriate number, and verify the food cost %. Recognize that different items have different targets (loss leaders at higher %, high-margin items at lower %).

### Update The Costing Regularly (At Least Monthly)

Ingredient prices change (seasonal fluctuation, supplier increases, market volatility), and the costing must be updated regularly (at least monthly, or when a key ingredient spikes) to reflect the current costs. The judgment point is that stale costing (based on last quarter's prices) misprices the menu and erodes the margin, especially during inflation or seasonal spikes. Update the costing monthly (or more often for volatile ingredients), re-price the menu as needed (or adjust portions or suppliers), and track the cost trends.

### Monitor Food Cost Variance And Investigate

The actual food cost (the cost of goods sold, calculated from inventory) should match the theoretical food cost (the sum of the costed recipes sold); the variance (the difference) indicates waste, theft, over-portioning, or costing errors. The judgment point is that the variance is the diagnostic, and a high variance (more than 2-3%) signals a problem. Calculate the actual food cost (from inventory and purchases), compare to the theoretical, investigate the variance, and fix the root cause (retrain portioning, reduce waste, update costing).

## Common Traps

### Costing From The AP Price And Undercosting The Plate

A chef costs the chicken plate from the AP price of the whole chicken, ignoring the 35% trim and bone loss, and undercosts the plate. The mechanism is that the AP price is for the whole bird, and only 65% is usable meat, so the AS cost is higher (AP / 0.65); costing from the AP undercosts the plate. The false signal is that the price "is on the invoice," which reads as the cost. The harm is an undercosted plate and an eroded margin. The defense is to cost from the AS yield (AP / yield %), to use yield tables, and to recognize the AP-AS difference.

### Omitting The Small Components And Undercosting

A chef costs the plate (protein, starch, vegetable) but omits the sauce, the garnish, and the frying oil, and undercosts the plate. The mechanism is that the small components (sauce, garnish, oil, condiments) are real costs that add up, and omitting them undercosts the plate; every component must be included. The false signal is that they "are small," which reads as negligible. The harm is an undercosted plate. The defense is to list and cost every component (including the oil, the garnish, the condiments), and to recognize the cumulative cost.

### Not Accounting For Over-Portioning And Eroding The Margin

Staff portion 6 oz instead of the costed 5 oz (a common over-portion), and the actual food cost is higher than the theoretical, eroding the margin. The mechanism is that over-portioning (inconsistent or generous portions) increases the actual cost above the costed amount, and the variance erodes the margin; consistent portioning (by weight or standard scoop) is required. The false signal is that the portion "looks right," which reads as correct. The harm is margin erosion. The defense is to portion by weight or standard scoop, to train staff, to monitor the variance, and to cost from the actual portion.

### Not Updating The Costing And Mispricing The Menu

A chef costs the menu at the start of the year and does not update it, and ingredient prices rise (seasonal or inflation), so the menu is underpriced. The mechanism is that ingredient prices change, and stale costing misprices the menu, eroding the margin; regular updates are required. The false signal is that the costing "was done," which reads as current. The harm is an underpriced menu. The defense is to update the costing monthly (or on price spikes), to re-price or adjust, and to track the cost trends.

### Setting The Price By Competitor Or Gut Instead Of Cost

A chef sets the price by matching a competitor or by gut feel, not from the plate cost and the target food cost %, and the margin is off (too low or unsustainably high). The mechanism is that the price must be set from the true cost and the target %, and competitor or gut pricing may not cover the cost or may leave money on the table; the cost-based price is the foundation. The false signal is that the price "is competitive," which reads as right. The harm is an eroded or unsustainable margin. The defense is to set the price from the plate cost and the target %, to use competitor pricing as a reference (not the basis), and to verify the food cost %.

### Ignoring The Food Cost Variance And Missing The Problem

A chef does not calculate the actual food cost or the variance, and a problem (waste, theft, over-portioning) goes unnoticed, eroding the margin. The mechanism is that the variance (actual vs. theoretical) is the diagnostic, and ignoring it misses the problems; the variance must be calculated and investigated. The false signal is that the costing "is done," which reads as controlled. The harm is an eroded margin from unaddressed problems. The defense is to calculate the actual food cost (from inventory), to compare to the theoretical, to investigate variances over 2-3%, and to fix the root causes.

## Self-Check

- Is the recipe costed from the as-served (AS) yield (AP price / yield %), accounting for trim, shrinkage, and butchering?
- Are all components included (protein, starch, vegetable, sauce, garnish, bread, oil, condiments), each costed?
- Is waste (spoilage, mistakes, over-portioning) accounted for, either as a waste factor or tracked as variance?
- Is the price set from the true plate cost and the target food cost % (Price = Plate Cost / Target %), rounded appropriately?
- Is the costing updated regularly (at least monthly, or on price spikes), and are cost trends tracked?
- Is the actual food cost calculated (from inventory), compared to the theoretical, and variances over 2-3% investigated and fixed?
- Is the reasoning documented so the AS costing, component inclusion, waste accounting, pricing method, updating, and variance monitoring for each menu item are verifiable?
