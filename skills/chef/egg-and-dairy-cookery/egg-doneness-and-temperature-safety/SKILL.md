# Egg Doneness and Temperature Safety

Eggs are one of the most common vectors for foodborne illness in professional kitchens, and the judgment problem is that eggs can be perfectly cooked for texture while remaining unsafe to eat. The recurring failure is that cooks treat egg doneness as a quality decision (runny yolk, set white, soft scramble) without recognizing that the same protein coagulation temperatures that determine texture also determine whether Salmonella Enteritidis survives. A poached egg with a set white and liquid yolk, a hollandaise held warm, a meringue kissed with a torch, a tiramisu built with raw yolks — each of these is a texture decision that is simultaneously a microbial safety decision, and the two are governed by different rules. The cook who masters texture but ignores the time-temperature kill has not cooked the egg safely; they have cooked it beautifully and dangerously.

The added complication is that eggs occupy a regulatory gray zone. Whole-muscle eggs have some protection (the shell and inner membranes), but once cracked, the interior is a nutrient-rich liquid that supports rapid pathogen growth. Many jurisdictions require eggs cooked to 63°C/145°F for general service and 74°C/165°F for vulnerable populations, yet classic technique demands temperatures well below that. The agent's job is to reconcile culinary tradition with the actual safety obligation, to know when pasteurized eggs are mandatory, and to recognize that "fresh eggs" is not a safety argument.

## Core Rules

### Understand That Egg Doneness Is A Protein Coagulation Curve, Not A Single Temperature

Egg proteins coagulate at different temperatures, and this is what creates the range of textures from runny to hard. Egg white begins to set around 62-65°C (144-149°F) and is fully set by 65-70°C; egg yolk begins to thicken around 65°C and is firm by 70°C. This is why a 63°C sous-vide egg has a custardy white and a barely-set yolk, and why a 65°C egg is fully tender-set throughout. The judgment point is that the temperatures that produce the textures chefs prize (soft, silky, runny) are precisely the temperatures at which Salmonella is not reliably destroyed. Salmonella is reduced by 7-log at 57°C/134.5°F held for 55 minutes, or instantly at around 71°C/160°F. A soft-poached egg at 63°C reaches texture but not a guaranteed kill in the brief cooking time. The cook must decide, for each service context, whether the texture target is compatible with the safety requirement, and when it is not, switch to pasteurized shell eggs or in-shell pasteurization.

### Use Pasteurized Eggs Whenever The Dish Will Not Reach A Lethal Temperature

Any egg dish that is served raw or barely cooked — mayonnaise, aioli, Caesar dressing, hollandaise and béarnaise, tiramisu, mousse, meringue, royal icing, egg-nog, Japanese-style tamago that stays soft — must use pasteurized eggs or a validated pasteurization step if the kitchen serves the general public or any vulnerable guest. Pasteurized shell eggs (heat-treated in-shell to kill Salmonella without cooking the egg) and pasteurized liquid egg products are the standard solutions. The judgment trap is assuming that fresh, high-quality, or farm-direct eggs are safer — they are not; Salmonella Enteritidis can infect the ovary and contaminate the yolk before the shell forms, so even a pristine egg from a trusted farm can be internally contaminated. Freshness affects quality and spoilage, not the presence of Salmonella. When a recipe cannot be adapted to a lethal cook, the egg is the variable that must change.

### Recognize The Vulnerable-Population Threshold And Adjust The Menu

Hospitals, nursing homes, day-care centers, and any operation serving the elderly, pregnant, immunocompromised, or young children must serve eggs fully cooked — no runny yolks, no raw-egg sauces, no soft-set custards unless made with pasteurized product and held safely. In many jurisdictions this is a hard regulatory requirement, not a recommendation. The agent reviewing a menu for such a venue must flag every egg item and verify the cook method and the egg source. A brunch menu with eggs Benedict and mimosas is fine for a hotel; the same menu in a care facility is a documented hazard. The judgment is to classify the service population first, then design the egg cookery to match, and to never assume "a little runny is fine."

### Hold Egg-Based Sauces And Custards Outside The Danger Zone Or Discard Them

Egg-thickened sauces (hollandaise, béarnaise, sabayon) and warm custards are held at temperatures that are both texturally ideal and microbiologically dangerous — typically 45-55°C, which sits squarely inside the danger zone. These sauces cannot be held hot enough to be safe (above 60°C) without breaking or scrambling, and cannot be held cold without seizing. The result is a high-risk product with a very short safe holding window. The working rule is to make egg sauces à la minute or in small batches, hold them no longer than two hours total at warm temperature, and discard any leftover. Never hold hollandaise in a warm water bath through a whole brunch service without a time limit. The same logic applies to custard fillings held warm for plating. If a sauce must be held longer, use a stabilized formulation (added acid, commercial base) or switch to a cornstarch-thickened equivalent that can be held safely hot.

### Cool And Store Cooked Egg Products Rapidly

Cooked egg dishes — quiche, frittata, custard, strata, deviled egg filling — must be cooled rapidly through the danger zone and held cold below 5°C/41°F. Custards and quiches are dense and insulate heat, so a whole deep quiche placed hot in the walk-in will spend hours in the danger zone at its center. Cool in shallow pans, cut large items into portions, use an ice bath for liquid custards, and verify the internal temperature drops from 60°C to 21°C within two hours and to 5°C within four more. Hard-cooked eggs spoil faster than raw eggs because the cooking removes the protective inner membrane, so peel and refrigerate them promptly and use within seven days. Deviled eggs and egg salads held at room temperature for a buffet are a classic outbreak vehicle; keep them on ice and limit total time out.

### Treat Egg Washes And Raw Egg In Breading As Contamination Risks

Egg wash used for breading, glazing, or binding is raw egg spread onto a surface and then partially cooked or left on a product that is handled. The trap is that egg wash sits in a pan at room temperature through a service, breeding bacteria, and then is applied to food that may not reach a lethal temperature (a breaded cutlet fried briefly, a pastry glazed and baked lightly). Change egg wash frequently, keep it cold when not in use, discard it after two hours at room temperature, and never reuse egg wash that has touched raw protein. Cross-contamination from egg-wash pans to hands, tongs, and adjacent surfaces is a documented pathway; treat the egg-wash station as you would a raw-poultry station.

## Common Traps

### Believing Fresh Or High-Quality Eggs Are Safe To Eat Raw

A cook sources beautiful farm-fresh eggs and serves a raw-yolk pasta or a fresh mayonnaise, reasoning that quality and freshness equal safety. The mechanism is a conflation of two unrelated properties: freshness governs spoilage (the egg degrades, the white thins, the yolk weakens) while Salmonella Enteritidis contamination is a function of the hen's health and can be present the moment the egg is laid. The false signal is the egg's pristine appearance, clean shell, rich yolk color, and trusted source, all of which signal quality but not microbial safety. The harm is that a raw-egg dish made from perfect eggs sickens a guest with salmonellosis, and the cook is blindsided because the eggs "looked fine." The defense is to treat all non-pasteurized eggs as potentially internally contaminated and to use pasteurized product for any raw or undercooked application regardless of source or quality.

### Holding Hollandaise Or Béarnaise Through A Long Service

A brunch cook makes a large batch of hollandaise at opening, sets it in a warm water bath at 50°C, and replenishes it through a four-hour service, believing the warm temperature keeps it safe. The mechanism is that 50°C is inside the danger zone and the sauce is an ideal growth medium (protein, moisture, near-neutral pH), so bacteria multiply steadily while the sauce appears stable. The false signal is the sauce's smooth texture and warm temperature, which read as "held properly." The harm is that by hour three or four the bacterial load is dangerous, and because the sauce is never reheated to a lethal temperature before plating, the pathogens reach the guest. The defense is to make egg sauces in small batches, enforce a strict two-hour discard rule, and never pool old sauce into new.

### Assuming A Set White Means A Safe Egg

A cook judges a fried or poached egg by the white being fully opaque and set, and serves it with a runny yolk believing the egg is cooked. The mechanism is that the white sets at a lower temperature than is required to guarantee a Salmonella kill, so a visually "done" egg can still harbor viable bacteria in the yolk and even in thicker white areas. The false signal is the opaque, coagulated white, which is the cook's standard doneness cue. The harm is that an egg served to a vulnerable guest — or in a jurisdiction requiring 74°C — is technically undercooked despite looking correct. The defense is to know the regulatory temperature for the service context, to use pasteurized eggs when a runny yolk is required for a general or vulnerable population, and to treat visual doneness as a quality check layered on top of a safety decision, not a substitute for it.

### Reusing Egg Wash Or Leaving It At Room Temperature

A line cook sets up a breading station with a pan of egg wash at the start of service and leaves it out for the entire shift, dipping raw chicken, veal, and fish through it, then uses the same wash for a final glaze. The mechanism is that the egg wash accumulates bacteria from every raw product dipped into it and multiplies them at room temperature, becoming a concentrated culture that is then spread onto products. The false signal is that the egg wash looks unchanged and "just egg," so it reads as a clean ingredient. The harm is cross-contamination and bacterial amplification: the wash transfers pathogens from one raw product to another and to the cook's hands and tools, and the glaze application may not be cooked to a lethal temperature. The defense is to change egg wash every two hours, keep it on ice, use separate washes for different proteins, and discard any wash that has contacted raw product.

### Peeling Hard-Cooked Eggs And Storing Them At Room Temperature

A kitchen hard-cooks a large batch of eggs, peels them all for convenience, and leaves them in a pan at room temperature for a buffet or prep station. The mechanism is that cooking destroys the inner membrane that protects the egg from bacterial invasion, so a peeled cooked egg is more perishable than a raw egg, and at room temperature bacteria on the surface multiply rapidly and can be drawn into the egg. The false signal is that cooked eggs "feel" preserved and stable compared to raw. The harm is rapid spoilage and potential foodborne illness from eggs that were safe when cooked but became unsafe in storage. The defense is to cool hard-cooked eggs rapidly, keep them refrigerated, peel them close to service, and discard any peeled egg held above 5°C for more than two hours.

## Self-Check

- For every egg dish on the menu, have I identified whether the cook reaches a lethal temperature (around 71°C/160°F instant, or a validated lower-temperature longer-time pasteurization), and if not, am I using pasteurized eggs?
- If the service population includes elderly, pregnant, immunocompromised, or young guests, are all eggs served fully cooked or made from pasteurized product, with no runny yolks or raw-egg sauces?
- Are egg-based sauces (hollandaise, béarnaise, sabayon) made in small batches and discarded within two hours of warm holding, with no pooling of old into new?
- Are cooked egg products (quiche, custard, strata) cooled rapidly in shallow containers and verified to pass through the danger zone within the required time window?
- Is egg wash changed every two hours, kept cold when not in use, and never reused after contacting raw protein?
- Are hard-cooked eggs cooled rapidly, refrigerated, peeled close to service, and never held at room temperature for extended periods?
- Have I avoided the assumption that fresh, high-quality, or farm-direct eggs are safe to serve raw, and instead based the safety decision on pasteurization and cook temperature?
- Is the reasoning documented so another practitioner can verify which egg dishes rely on cook-kill versus pasteurized product?
