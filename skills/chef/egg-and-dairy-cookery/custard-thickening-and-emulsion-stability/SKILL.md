# Custard Thickening and Emulsion Stability

Egg-yolk-thickened preparations and emulsified sauces are the technical heart of the classic kitchen, and the judgment problem is that the same proteins and lecithin that create silky texture also create fragile systems that fail in predictable, safety-relevant ways. The recurring failure is that cooks treat a broken hollandaise or a curdled custard as a purely cosmetic problem to be rescued with a technique, without recognizing that the rescue often involves holding the product in the danger zone longer, pooling batches, or masking a temperature-abused base. A custard that curdled because it was held too hot may have also spent too long in the danger zone; an emulsion that broke because the base was too warm may be carrying a bacterial load. The agent's job is to diagnose why a system failed before deciding whether to recover it, because the cause of failure often determines whether the product is still safe.

The added layer is that these preparations are almost never reheated to a lethal temperature before service. A crème anglaise is poured warm over dessert; a hollandaise is spooned onto a plate; a mayonnaise is never cooked at all. Whatever microbial history the base carries reaches the guest. The discipline is to build these systems with pasteurized or safely-cooked bases, to hold them for the minimum time, and to discard — not rescue — any batch whose failure was caused by temperature abuse.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Two Thickening Mechanisms: Yolk Coagulation And Emulsion

Egg yolks thicken by two distinct mechanisms, and confusing them causes most failures. In a custard (crème anglaise, pastry cream, quiche, ice cream base), the yolk proteins coagulate as they heat, forming a network that gels or thickens the liquid; this happens between about 65°C and 82°C, and above ~85°C the proteins over-coagulate, squeeze out water, and curdle into scrambled-egg texture (syneresis). In an emulsion (hollandaise, béarnaise, mayonnaise), the yolk lecithin stabilizes droplets of fat in water (or water in fat); the thickening is mechanical, not thermal, and the system breaks when the droplet size or the emulsifier ratio fails. The judgment point is that a curdled custard failed from too much heat, while a broken emulsion failed from adding fat too fast, too much fat, or temperature shock — different causes with different safety implications. Diagnose the mechanism before attempting recovery.

### Cook Custards To A Target Temperature, Not To A Visual Cue

Custard doneness is a temperature, not an appearance. Crème anglaise is done at 82-84°C (nappé, coats the back of a spoon); pastry cream at 85°C to fully hydrate the starch and cook the yolk; a baked custard or crème brûlée is set at around 80-82°C internal. The trap is cooking "until it looks right," which overshoots into curdling or undershoots into a thin, unsafe base. Use a thermometer for stovetop custards and verify baked custards by internal temperature or the gentle-set wobble, not by color. Cooking to temperature also ensures the yolk has reached a sufficient temperature to reduce microbial load in the base, which matters because many custards are then held warm or cooled without a further cook.

### Cool Custard Bases Rapidly And Immediately After Cooking

A cooked custard base (pastry cream, crème pâtissière, ice cream base, crème anglaise) is a warm, nutrient-rich liquid and a prime danger-zone product. The working rule is to cool it immediately: transfer to a shallow pan or a bowl over an ice bath, stir to release heat, bring it from 60°C to 21°C within two hours and to 5°C within four more, then cover and refrigerate with a date mark. The classic trap is leaving a pot of pastry cream on the stove to "cool down" before refrigerating, during which Bacillus cereus and other spore-formers multiply and may produce heat-stable toxins that survive any later use. Never cover hot custard and put it directly in the walk-in — the center stays in the danger zone for hours and the surface condenses moisture that encourages contamination. Cool actively, then store.

### Diagnose Emulsion Failure By Cause Before Deciding To Recover

A broken emulsion (hollandaise that has separated, mayonnaise that has thinned) can often be recovered by whisking the broken sauce into a fresh yolk or a spoonful of water, but the decision to recover must depend on why it broke. If it broke from mechanical causes — fat added too fast, slightly too much fat, a brief temperature shock — recovery is safe. If it broke because the base was held warm too long, because raw egg at room temperature multiplied bacteria, or because the batch has been pooled and held beyond its time limit, the break is a symptom of temperature abuse and the product should be discarded, not rescued. The false economy is rescuing a broken sauce to avoid waste while serving a product that has accumulated hours of danger-zone time. When in doubt about the cause, discard.

### Use Pasteurized Yolks For Any Emulsion That Will Not Be Cooked

Mayonnaise, aioli, Caesar dressing, and any yolk-stabilized emulsion served raw must use pasteurized yolks or a validated pasteurization step, because the yolk is the pathogen vector and the emulsion is never heated. Hollandaise and béarnaise are held warm but rarely reach a lethal internal temperature, so they should also use pasteurized yolks in any operation serving the general public. The judgment trap is using fresh shell eggs for a raw emulsion on the assumption that acidity (lemon juice, vinegar) makes it safe; while acid lowers pH, the contact time and concentration required for a reliable pathogen kill are far greater than a typical recipe provides, and acid does not inactivate pre-formed Staphylococcus enterotoxin. Pasteurization is the control; acid is a flavor and a marginal secondary hurdle, not a primary safety step.

### Hold Finished Custards And Emulsions For The Minimum Time At The Correct Temperature

Finished egg-thickened products have very short safe holding windows because they cannot be held hot enough to be safe without degrading. Warm custards and crème anglaise should be held briefly and served; leftover should be cooled and refrigerated, not held warm. Hollandaise and béarnaise should be made in small batches, held warm no longer than two hours total, and discarded after service. Cold emulsions (mayonnaise, dressings) should be held below 5°C and used within a few days. Never hold any of these products at a lukewarm "holding" temperature through a long service — that temperature is the danger zone, and the product is an ideal growth medium.

## Common Traps

### Rescuing A Curdled Custard By Re-Emulsifying Instead Of Discarding

A pastry cream or crème anglaise curdles from overheating, and the cook blends it smooth or strains it, reasoning that the texture is restored. The mechanism is that curdling indicates the yolk proteins over-coagulated from excessive heat, but the more important signal is often that the custard was held at high temperature long enough to curdle — which may also mean extended danger-zone time if the heat was in the microbial growth range. The false signal is the restored smooth texture after blending, which reads as "fixed." The harm is that a product that failed from temperature abuse is served, carrying whatever microbial load accumulated. The defense is to ask why it curdled: if the cause was a brief overshoot on the stove with immediate removal, a strain-and-use may be defensible; if the cause was prolonged warm holding, discard the batch.

### Holding Hollandaise In A Warm Water Bath Through An Entire Brunch

A brunch cook makes a large batch of hollandaise, holds it in a water bath at around 50°C, and replenishes it over four hours. The mechanism is that 50°C is inside the danger zone, the emulsion is a rich growth medium, and the long hold allows bacterial multiplication to infectious levels, while the sauce never receives a final lethal cook before plating. The false signal is the sauce's stable, smooth appearance and warm temperature, which read as properly held. The harm is foodborne illness from a sauce that was texturally perfect but microbiologically dangerous. The defense is to make egg sauces in small batches, enforce a two-hour discard rule, and never pool old sauce into new to extend the hold.

### Assuming Acid In Mayonnaise Or Dressing Makes Raw Egg Safe

A cook makes mayonnaise or Caesar dressing with raw egg yolk and lemon juice or vinegar and reasons that the acidity pasteurizes the egg. The mechanism is that while acid does inhibit and eventually kill some pathogens, the pH and contact time in a typical mayonnaise (final pH around 4.0, but the egg yolk is somewhat protected by its fat and the emulsion structure) are not a guaranteed instant kill, and acid does not destroy pre-formed enterotoxins from temperature abuse before mixing. The false signal is the acidic taste and the cultural belief that "mayo is safe because of the lemon." The harm is salmonellosis from a raw-egg emulsion served to a general or vulnerable population. The defense is to use pasteurized yolks for all raw emulsions and to treat acid as a flavor and secondary hurdle, not the primary safety control.

### Covering Hot Custard And Refrigerating It Directly

A cook makes a large batch of pastry cream, covers the pot, and places it in the walk-in to cool overnight. The mechanism is that a deep, covered, hot mass is an excellent insulator; the center remains in the danger zone for many hours while the walk-in struggles, during which spore-forming bacteria multiply and may produce heat-stable toxins. The false signal is that the pot is in the cooler and the outside feels cold, so the cook believes it is chilling. The harm is that the center of the custard has spent extended time in the danger zone and may carry toxins that survive later use in a tart or filling. The defense is to cool custard actively in a shallow pan or over an ice bath, stirring, before refrigerating, and to verify the internal temperature passes through the danger zone within the required window.

### Pooling A Broken Emulsion Into A Fresh Batch To Avoid Waste

A line cook has a broken béarnaise and, rather than discarding it, slowly whisks it into a fresh batch being made, recovering the texture and avoiding waste. The mechanism is sound for the texture, but if the original batch broke from temperature abuse or extended holding, the pooling transfers the microbial load of the old batch into the new one and resets the clock invisibly. The false signal is the smooth, recovered sauce, which reads as a fresh batch. The harm is that the combined batch carries the older product's accumulated danger-zone time and reaches a guest. The defense is to only pool batches whose failure cause is known to be mechanical, to never pool a batch of unknown or abusive history, and to enforce the holding clock on the oldest component, not the newest.

## Self-Check

- For each egg-thickened preparation, have I identified whether the thickening mechanism is yolk coagulation (custard) or emulsion (sauce), and am I diagnosing failures by the correct mechanism?
- Are stovetop custards cooked to a verified target temperature rather than to a visual cue, and are baked custards verified by internal temperature or set-wobble?
- Are cooked custard bases cooled actively in shallow pans or over ice baths and verified to pass through the danger zone within the required window before refrigeration?
- For any broken emulsion, have I established that the cause was mechanical (safe to recover) rather than temperature abuse (discard), and when in doubt, discarded the batch?
- Are all raw or barely-cooked emulsions (mayonnaise, Caesar, hollandaise) made with pasteurized yolks, with acid treated as a secondary hurdle rather than the primary safety control?
- Are warm egg sauces made in small batches and discarded within two hours, with no pooling of old into new to extend the hold?
- Have I avoided covering hot custard and refrigerating it directly, instead cooling actively before storage?
- Is the reasoning for each recovery or discard decision documented so the cause of failure and the safety basis are verifiable?
