# Guest Experience and Dietary Accommodation

Dietary accommodation is no longer an occasional exception; it is a daily reality of restaurant service. Allergies, intolerances, religious and cultural restrictions, health-driven diets, and personal preferences converge on the kitchen from every service. How the kitchen responds determines not only the guest's experience but, in the case of allergies, their safety. A kitchen that treats accommodation as an annoyance produces indifferent food and latent allergen risk; a kitchen that designs for it produces safe, delicious options that earn loyalty. The chef's job is to build the workflows, the menu strategy, and the staff training that let the kitchen accommodate graciously and safely, without compromising the menu's integrity or the kitchen's capacity.

This skill covers the kitchen's response to guest dietary needs: allergen-safe workflows, accommodation strategy, staff training, and the balance among accommodation, menu integrity, and capacity.

This guidance addresses accommodation judgment. It does not replace applicable allergen labeling and food safety regulation, the kitchen's duty of care to allergic guests, or institutional policy. Allergen accommodation carries serious safety obligations; when in doubt, decline to serve rather than risk harm.

## Core Rules

### Treat allergen safety as a non-negotiable system, not a one-off effort

Allergen accommodation is a safety system, not a matter of goodwill. For each major allergen the kitchen handles, define the workflow: ingredient sourcing and labeling, segregation in storage and prep, dedicated or sanitized equipment and surfaces, prevention of cross-contact during cooking and plating, and communication from order to plate. The system must be documented, trained, and verified. A single breakdown in the chain can send a guest to the hospital. When a guest declares a severe allergy, the kitchen must be able to execute the full chain reliably—or decline to serve.

### Distinguish allergy from preference and respond accordingly

Allergies (immune-mediated, potentially life-threatening), intolerances (non-life-threatening adverse reactions), religious and cultural restrictions, and personal preferences require different responses. An allergy demands the full allergen-safe workflow; a preference may require only a substitution. Train FOH to elicit the nature of the restriction ("Is this an allergy or a preference? How severe?") so the kitchen responds appropriately. Treating a severe allergy as a preference is dangerous; treating every preference as an allergy overwhelms the kitchen.

### Design the menu with accommodation in mind, not as an afterthought

A menu designed without thought to accommodation forces every restricted guest into a special request. A menu designed with accommodation in mind includes naturally flexible dishes, components that can be omitted or swapped, and one or two dishes that are naturally free of common allergens. This reduces the volume of special requests and gives restricted guests real options rather than a stripped-down version of a dish not designed for them. Accommodation designed in is graceful; accommodation bolted on is awkward.

### Build a clear protocol for special requests

When a special request arrives, the protocol must be clear: the server conveys the restriction through the agreed channel; the kitchen assesses what it can safely and quality-fully execute; the kitchen communicates the option back to the server; the server confirms with the guest; the order is fired with the modification clearly marked. The protocol must handle the "can you make..." requests by routing them to the kitchen for a decision, not having the server promise on the kitchen's behalf. A server who promises a modification the kitchen cannot execute safely sets up a failure.

### Train FOH on menu composition and allergens

FOH cannot represent the kitchen to guests without knowing the menu's composition: ingredients, allergens, and what can be modified. Train FOH on the components of each dish, the presence of major allergens, and the modification possibilities and limits, and update the training whenever the menu changes. FOH must know to check with the kitchen on any uncertain ingredient rather than guessing, because a guess about an allergen can be dangerous.

### Balance accommodation against menu integrity and kitchen capacity

Not every accommodation is possible or wise. A request that compromises the dish's integrity, that the kitchen cannot execute safely, or that overloads the kitchen during peak should be declined graciously with an alternative offered. The chef's judgment on what can be accommodated—and what cannot—must be clear to the team, so that FOH sets guest expectations accurately. Gracious refusal with a good alternative is better than a risky or compromised yes.

## Common Traps

### Treating allergen accommodation as goodwill rather than a system

Allergen safety is a non-negotiable system. A breakdown at any point in the chain can harm a guest. Document, train, and verify the full workflow.

### Conflating allergy with preference

A severe allergy demands the full workflow; a preference does not. Elicit the nature of the restriction and respond accordingly.

### Designing the menu without accommodation in mind

A rigid menu forces special requests for every restricted guest. Design in flexibility and naturally accommodating options.

### Servers promising modifications without kitchen confirmation

A promise the kitchen cannot keep sets up a failure—and an allergen failure can be dangerous. Route special requests to the kitchen for decision.

### FOH guessing about ingredients and allergens

A guess about an allergen can be dangerous. Train FOH on composition and modification limits, and require them to check with the kitchen on uncertainty.

### Risky accommodation to avoid a difficult conversation

Gracious refusal with an alternative is better than a risky yes. Make the boundaries of accommodation clear to the team.

## Self-Check

- Is there a documented, trained, and verified allergen-safe workflow for each major allergen, from sourcing through plating?
- Does the protocol distinguish allergy from preference and elicit the severity from the guest?
- Is the menu designed with accommodation in mind, including naturally flexible dishes and common-allergen-free options?
- Is there a clear protocol for special requests, routing them to the kitchen for decision before the server promises the guest?
- Is FOH trained on menu composition, allergens, and modification limits, and required to check with the kitchen on uncertainty?
- Are the boundaries of accommodation—what can and cannot be done—clear to the team, so expectations are set accurately?
- Where accommodation cannot be done safely, does the kitchen decline graciously with an alternative?
- Are allergen protocols and accommodation practices reviewed against incidents and guest feedback?
