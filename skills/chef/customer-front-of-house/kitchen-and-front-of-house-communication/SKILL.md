# Kitchen and Front of House Communication

The kitchen and the front of house (FOH) are two halves of one operation, yet they are separated by a wall, by different pressures, and often by mutual misunderstanding. The kitchen experiences FOH as a stream of tickets arriving at unpredictable rates, with modifications and special requests that complicate execution. FOH experiences the kitchen as a source of delays, 86s, and inflexibility that make their job with the guest harder. When communication is poor, the guest feels it: cold food, wrong orders, long waits, and a staff whose friction is visible. When communication is strong, the two halves operate as one team, the wall becomes a pass rather than a barrier, and service flows. Building this is the chef's job as much as the dining room manager's.

This skill covers the communication between kitchen and FOH: ticket and order flow, special requests and modifications, conflict resolution, and the culture and shared language that make the two teams operate as one.

This guidance addresses cross-team communication judgment. It does not replace institutional service standards, point-of-sale and order protocols, or applicable regulation. Adapt practices to the specific operation and service style.

## Core Rules

### Establish a single source of truth for orders, 86s, and modifications

Miscommunication thrives where information lives in multiple places or in people's heads. Establish a single source of truth: the point-of-sale system for orders, a consistent channel for 86s and menu changes, and a clear protocol for modifications and special requests. Every order, every 86, every allergen alert must flow through the agreed channel so that nothing is lost between floor and kitchen. Verbal side-channels ("hey, can you...") are the source of most errors.

### Standardize how modifications and special requests reach the kitchen

Modifications—no allergen, sauce on the side, a split plate, a well-done request—are where kitchen-floor communication most often fails. Standardize how modifications are entered, transmitted, and confirmed: the server enters the modification in the POS with clear, standard phrasing; the ticket reaches the kitchen with the modification legible; the kitchen acknowledges anything unusual. Resist ad-hoc verbal requests that bypass the system, because they are the ones that get missed. For complex or unusual requests, the server should confirm with the kitchen before promising the guest.

### Manage ticket pacing as a shared responsibility

The rate at which orders arrive determines whether the kitchen can execute them well. FOH controls the seating and ordering pace; the kitchen experiences the result. Treat pacing as a shared responsibility: FOH spaces seating to avoid dumping a wave of simultaneous orders, and the kitchen communicates when it is getting in the weeds so FOH can slow seating or manage guest expectations. A kitchen that suffers in silence and a floor that seats blindly produce cascading delays.

### Build shared language and mutual understanding

Friction between BOH and FOH often stems from ignorance of each other's work. Build shared language and understanding: FOH should know the menu's components, cook times, and constraints, so they can set guest expectations and avoid impossible requests; BOH should understand the guest experience and the server's pressures, so they can support rather than obstruct. Cross-training, shared tastings, and pre-service line-ups that include both teams build this understanding.

### Resolve conflicts at the pass, not in front of guests

When something goes wrong—a delayed ticket, a returned plate, a missed modification—the conflict must be resolved at the pass, between the expediter and the server, calmly and professionally, never in front of guests. Establish a norm that the pass is the place to solve problems, that blame is deferred to post-service debrief, and that the guest sees a unified team. A kitchen-floor argument visible to guests damages the experience far more than the original error.

### Debrief and improve the communication system

After service, debrief the communication: what broke down, what caused delays, what modifications were problematic, what 86s were poorly handled. Use the debrief to improve the system—adjust pacing norms, refine modification protocols, clarify menu constraints—rather than to assign blame. Communication is a system that improves iteratively.

## Common Traps

### Verbal side-channels for orders and modifications

Ad-hoc verbal requests bypass the system and get missed. Route everything through the agreed POS and communication channel.

### Dumping simultaneous orders on the kitchen

Seating a full section at once creates a wave of orders the kitchen cannot execute well. Pace seating and ordering.

### Suffering in silence

A kitchen that doesn't communicate when it's overwhelmed lets delays cascade. Communicate early so FOH can adjust.

### Ignorance of each other's work

FOH that doesn't know cook times sets impossible expectations; BOH that doesn't understand the guest experience obstructs. Build shared understanding.

### Arguing in front of guests

A visible kitchen-floor conflict damages the experience more than the original error. Resolve at the pass, debrief later.

### Blame instead of system improvement

Post-service blame doesn't fix the communication system. Debrief to improve the system iteratively.

## Self-Check

- Is there a single source of truth for orders, 86s, and modifications, with verbal side-channels eliminated?
- Are modifications and special requests standardized in entry, transmission, and acknowledgment, with complex requests confirmed before promising the guest?
- Is ticket pacing treated as a shared responsibility, with FOH spacing orders and the kitchen communicating when overwhelmed?
- Do FOH and BOH share language and understanding of each other's work, built through cross-training, tastings, and joint line-ups?
- Are conflicts resolved at the pass, calmly and out of guest view, with blame deferred to post-service debrief?
- Is there a post-service debrief that improves the communication system rather than assigning blame?
- Are allergen alerts and safety-critical modifications handled with redundant confirmation?
- Is the communication system reviewed and refined based on service experience?
