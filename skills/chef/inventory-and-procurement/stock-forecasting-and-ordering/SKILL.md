# Stock Forecasting And Ordering

Ordering is the link between what the kitchen will cook and what it will have on hand, and getting it wrong is expensive in both directions: under-order and the menu breaks mid-service with dishes 86'd and guests turned away; over-order and perishable inventory spoils, food cost rises, and the walk-in clogs. The judgment problem is that ordering is often done by habit or gut feel — "order what we usually get" — without tracing the order to projected demand, lead times, and pars, so the kitchen oscillates between shortages and waste. Disciplined forecasting converts ordering from a guessing game into a predictable process that keeps the kitchen supplied at minimum cost and waste. This skill covers how to forecast demand, build order guides, place orders against pars and lead times, and diagnose ordering failures.

Use this skill when building or revising order guides, training the person who orders, planning for events or seasonal shifts, or diagnosing why a kitchen chronically runs out or wastes.

## Core Rules

### Forecast Demand From Covers, Menu Mix, And History

Ordering begins with a demand forecast: how many covers are expected, what is the menu mix (what percentage of guests order each dish), and what does history say about variation. Build the forecast from reservations, day-of-week patterns, seasonality, events, weather, and recent trends, and translate it into component demand by multiplying covers by menu mix percentage by recipe component quantities. A forecast turns an order from a guess into a calculation, and it makes shortages and over-orders diagnosable rather than mysterious. Update the forecast as new information arrives (a big reservation, a storm) and adjust the order accordingly.

### Build And Maintain A Structured Order Guide

An order guide is the master list of what the kitchen buys, from whom, in what pack size, at what par, with what lead time. It is the tool that makes ordering consistent across staff and shifts, because it encodes the decisions (supplier, pack, par) so the orderer does not reinvent them each time. Maintain the guide with current prices, pars, and lead times, and review it when the menu, suppliers, or volume change. An order guide that is out of date produces wrong orders; keep it current and treat it as a living document.

### Order To Restore Pars, Accounting For Lead Time

The order quantity for each item is the par minus what is currently on hand, plus expected usage during the lead time (the days between ordering and delivery). Order to restore the par, not to a fixed quantity, because a fixed quantity ignores what is already in stock and produces over- or under-ordering. Account for lead time so that the delivery arrives before the par is exhausted, and adjust order timing for supplier delivery days and holidays. The par-minus-on-hand-plus-lead-time formula is the core of disciplined ordering.

### Account For Perishability And Shelf Life In Quantities

Perishable items with short shelf life should be ordered in smaller, more frequent quantities to minimize spoilage risk, even if the per-unit cost is slightly higher; shelf-stable items can be ordered in larger, less frequent quantities to capture volume pricing. Match the order size and frequency to the product's shelf life and the kitchen's usage rate, and never order a perishable in a quantity that exceeds what can be used before it spoils. A great price on a perishable that spoils is a loss, not a saving.

### Sequence Orders By Supplier Delivery Days And Cutoffs

Different suppliers deliver on different days with different order cutoffs, and the ordering schedule must respect these constraints so that product arrives when needed. Build a weekly ordering calendar that maps each supplier's delivery days and cutoffs, and place orders with enough lead time to hit the right delivery. Coordinate across suppliers so that a single missed cutoff does not strand the kitchen short for days. Sequencing is the logistics that makes the forecast actually arrive.

### Plan For Events, Seasonality, And Disruptions Separately

Routine ordering handles the routine week; events (a banquet, a holiday), seasonal shifts (a dish becoming popular in cold weather), and disruptions (a supplier shortage, a storm) require separate planning. Build event orders from the event's specific covers and menu, build seasonal adjustments into pars and menu mix, and maintain backup suppliers and emergency ordering channels for disruptions. Treat the exceptional as a distinct planning exercise, not as a routine order scaled up, because scaling a routine order for an event usually gets the components wrong.

### Review And Adjust Based On Shortages, Waste, And Variance

Ordering improves only with feedback. Track shortages (dishes 86'd, substitutions forced), waste (spoilage, trim loss), and inventory variance, and adjust pars, forecasts, and order quantities in response. A chronic shortage means the par or forecast is too low; chronic waste means it is too high. Use the data to continuously calibrate, because demand drifts and pars set once go stale. Ordering is a learning loop, not a static routine.

## Common Traps

### Ordering By Habit Or Gut Feel Instead Of Forecast

The orderer orders "what we usually get" each week, without reference to projected covers or menu mix, and the kitchen alternates between shortages and waste depending on whether the week was busier or slower than usual. The false signal is that the orderer is experienced and knows the amounts. The mechanism is that habit encodes a single demand level that does not adjust to variation, so on a busy week the order is short (dishes 86'd, guests turned away) and on a slow week it is excessive (spoilage, waste), and the kitchen accepts both as normal rather than as symptoms of unforecast ordering. The harm is lost sales on one side and eroded margin on the other, both invisible without a forecast to compare against. The defense is to forecast demand from covers, menu mix, and history, to order to restore pars accounting for lead time, and to treat gut feel as a starting hypothesis to be checked against the calculation. Experience informs the forecast; it does not replace it.

### Fixed Order Quantities Ignoring On-Hand Stock

The orderer orders a fixed quantity of each item every week regardless of what is already in stock, and the kitchen accumulates excess of slow items while running short of fast ones. The false signal is that the fixed quantity is "the right amount." The mechanism is that a fixed quantity ignores current inventory, so when stock is already high (a slow previous week) the order piles on top and spoils, and when stock is low (a busy previous week) the order is insufficient and the kitchen runs out; the same quantity produces opposite results depending on starting inventory. The harm is chronic waste and chronic shortages simultaneously, across different items. The defense is to count on-hand stock and order to restore the par (par minus on hand plus lead-time usage), so the order reflects the kitchen's actual position. The right order depends on what is already there.

### Ignoring Lead Time And Delivery Schedules

The orderer places orders without accounting for supplier lead times or delivery days, and product arrives a day late, stranding the kitchen short for a service. The false signal is that the order was placed "in time." The mechanism is that lead time — the days between order and delivery — and delivery schedules (some suppliers deliver only certain days) are hard constraints, and an order placed without reference to them arrives when the supplier can deliver, not when the kitchen needs the product, so a Monday order for a Tuesday delivery may be impossible if the supplier delivers Mondays and Thursdays. The harm is unexpected shortages that force menu changes or 86'd dishes. The defense is a weekly ordering calendar mapping each supplier's delivery days and cutoffs, with orders placed to hit the needed delivery day, and backup arrangements for gaps. Logistics, not just quantities, govern availability.

### Over-Ordering Perishables For A Price Break

The orderer buys a larger pack of a perishable to get a volume discount, and half of it spoils before the kitchen can use it. The false signal is that the per-unit price is lower, so the kitchen saved money. The mechanism is that a perishable's cost is not its purchase price but its purchase price divided by the portion actually used before it spoils, so a larger quantity at a lower unit price can cost more per usable portion if the excess spoils; the volume discount is real only if the kitchen can use the full quantity within shelf life. The harm is waste that exceeds the savings, plus walk-in congestion that complicates rotation. The defense is to match order size to usage rate and shelf life, to favor smaller more frequent orders for highly perishable items, and to compute cost on usable yield rather than invoice price. A saving that spoils is a loss.

### Pars And Forecasts Never Adjusted To Feedback

The kitchen sets pars and forecasts when the menu launches and never revisits them, so as demand drifts the ordering becomes systematically wrong — too much of items that slowed, too little of items that grew. The false signal is that the pars were correct once, so they remain correct. The mechanism is that demand is not static; menu items gain and lose popularity, seasons shift, and events change volume, so pars and forecasts set once drift away from reality, producing waste on declining items and shortages on growing ones, with no adjustment because the feedback (shortages, waste, variance) is not acted on. The harm is chronic mismatch between supply and demand. The defense is to track shortages, waste, and variance, and to adjust pars and forecasts in response on a regular cycle, treating ordering as a learning loop that improves with feedback. A forecast is a hypothesis to be updated, not a fact to be relied on.

### No Backup Plan For Supplier Disruption

The kitchen orders from its regular suppliers and has no plan for when a supplier cannot deliver — a shortage, a recall, a delivery failure — and the menu breaks when the disruption arrives. The false signal is that the suppliers have always delivered, so they always will. The mechanism is that supply chains fail, sometimes predictably (seasonal shortages, holidays) and sometimes not (recalls, weather, business failure), and a kitchen with no backup supplier or emergency channel has no recourse when its primary fails, forcing menu changes, 86'd dishes, or emergency purchases at premium prices. The harm is service disruption and damaged guest trust. The defense is to maintain qualified backup suppliers for critical items, to know emergency purchasing options (cash-and-carry, broader distributors), and to plan for disruption before it happens, not during it. Reliability is not permanence.

## Self-Check

- Is demand forecast from covers, menu mix, and history the basis for ordering, rather than habit or gut feel?
- Is there a current, maintained order guide encoding supplier, pack size, par, and lead time for each item?
- Are orders calculated to restore pars (par minus on hand plus lead-time usage), not placed as fixed quantities ignoring stock?
- Are perishables ordered in sizes and frequencies matched to shelf life and usage rate, with cost computed on usable yield rather than invoice price?
- Is there a weekly ordering calendar respecting each supplier's delivery days and cutoffs, so product arrives when needed?
- Are events, seasonal shifts, and disruptions planned separately, with backup suppliers and emergency channels identified in advance?
- Are pars and forecasts adjusted regularly based on shortage, waste, and variance feedback, so ordering improves over time?
