# Kitchen Brigade Structure and Role Definition

The kitchen brigade (the hierarchical organization of roles, from the executive chef down) is the operational structure of the kitchen, and the judgment problem is that each role must have clearly defined responsibilities (what the role does, what it owns, what it reports), a clear chain of command (who they report to, who reports to them), and the right number of roles for the kitchen's size (a 20-seat cafe does not need a full Escoffier brigade; a 200-seat hotel might). The recurring failure is vague roles (the sous and the lead cook overlap, or a gap exists), an inappropriate structure (a tiny kitchen with a top-heavy hierarchy, or a large kitchen with no middle management), or unclear accountability (no one knows who owns what, so nothing is owned). The discipline is to define the roles clearly, to match the structure to the size, and to clarify the accountability.

The agent's job is to design the brigade for the kitchen's needs, to define the roles, and to clarify the accountability and the communication.

## Core Rules

### Define Each Role's Responsibilities, Authority, And Accountability

Each role (executive chef, chef de cuisine, sous chef, lead cook, line cook, prep cook, pastry chef, dishwasher) must have clearly defined responsibilities (what they do), authority (what they decide), and accountability (what they own and report). The judgment point is that vague roles (overlap or gaps) cause confusion and conflict, and clear definitions (in writing, in a job description) prevent this. Define each role in writing (responsibilities, authority, accountability), communicate the definitions to the staff, and review them as the kitchen evolves. The job description is the tool.

### Match The Structure To The Kitchen's Size And Concept

The brigade structure must match the kitchen's size and concept: a small kitchen (a cafe, a bistro) needs a flat structure (a chef-owner, a cook or two, a dishwasher), a medium kitchen (a full-service restaurant) needs a moderate structure (a chef, a sous, line cooks, prep, dish), a large kitchen (a hotel, a high-volume operation) needs a full brigade (executive chef, chef de cuisine, sous chefs, station chefs, prep team, dish team). The judgment point is that an over-structured small kitchen (top-heavy, too many chiefs) wastes labor, and an under-structured large kitchen (no middle management) loses control; the structure must fit. Match the structure to the size (flat for small, moderate for medium, full for large), and adjust as the operation grows or shrinks.

### Establish A Clear Chain Of Command And Communication

The chain of command (who reports to whom) and the communication paths (how information flows, up and down) must be clear, so the staff know who to report to, who to ask, and how decisions are made. The judgment point is that an unclear chain (multiple bosses, conflicting instructions) causes confusion and conflict, and a clear chain (one direct supervisor per role) streamlines the communication. Establish the chain of command (an org chart, one direct supervisor per role), define the communication paths (daily line-ups, pre-service meetings, the escalation path for issues), and communicate them to the staff.

### Assign Stations Based On Skill And Development

Station assignments (who works sauté, who works grill, who works garde manger) should be based on the skill (the strongest cook on the hardest station) and the development (developing cooks on stations that stretch them, with support). The judgment point is that the station assignment affects the service quality (a weak cook on a hard station fails), and the assignment is both a coverage and a development decision. Assign the stations based on the skill and the development (strongest on the hardest, developing cooks stretched with support), rotate periodically (for cross-training and development), and have a deep station guide (the recipes, the setup, the standards) for each station.

### Create Middle Management For Larger Kitchens

In a larger kitchen (a full brigade), the middle management (the sous chefs, the chef de cuisine) is the critical layer: they translate the executive chef's vision into the daily execution, they supervise the line, they manage the prep, and they are the communication bridge. The judgment point is that a large kitchen without middle management (the executive chef directly supervising all the line) loses control, and the middle layer is required. In a large kitchen, create the middle management (sous chefs, chef de cuisine), define their roles (supervision, translation, communication), and empower them to run the daily operation.

### Clarify Accountability For Each Domain

Each domain (the menu, the ordering, the scheduling, the training, the sanitation, the quality) must have a clear owner (a role accountable for it), so nothing falls through the cracks. The judgment point is that unowned domains (everyone assumes someone else is doing it) are neglected, and a clear owner (with the accountability) ensures it's done. Assign each domain to a role (the executive chef owns the menu, the sous owns the scheduling, the lead cook owns the station quality, etc.), communicate the ownership, and hold the owner accountable. The RACI (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) framework is a tool.

## Common Traps

### Vague Roles And Causing Overlap Or Gaps

A chef runs the kitchen with vague roles (the sous and the lead cook overlap, or no one owns the ordering), and the overlap causes conflict (both do it, differently) or the gap causes neglect (no one does it). The mechanism is that vague roles (no written definition) create overlap and gaps, and clear definitions (in writing) prevent this; the job description is the tool. The false signal is that the staff "know their jobs," which reads as clear. The harm is conflict, neglect, and confusion. The defense is to define each role in writing (responsibilities, authority, accountability), to communicate the definitions, and to review them.

### Over-Structuring A Small Kitchen And Wasting Labor

A chef imposes a full brigade (executive chef, chef de cuisine, sous, station chefs) on a 20-seat cafe, and the top-heavy hierarchy wastes labor (too many chiefs, not enough cooks). The mechanism is that the structure must match the size, and an over-structured small kitchen (top-heavy) wastes labor (the chiefs are under-utilized, the labor cost is high); a flat structure is appropriate for small. The false signal is that the brigade "is professional," which reads as serious. The harm is wasted labor cost. The defense is to match the structure to the size (flat for small), to avoid over-structuring, and to recognize the size-structure fit.

### Under-Structuring A Large Kitchen And Losing Control

A chef runs a 200-seat hotel kitchen with no middle management (the executive chef directly supervising all the line), and the control is lost (the executive chef cannot oversee everything). The mechanism is that a large kitchen needs middle management (sous chefs, chef de cuisine) to translate and supervise, and without it, the executive chef is overwhelmed and the control is lost; the middle layer is required. The false signal is that the chef "is hands-on," which reads as in control. The harm is lost control and inconsistent execution. The defense is to create the middle management (sous chefs, chef de cuisine), to define their roles, and to empower them.

### Unclear Chain Of Command And Conflicting Instructions

A kitchen has an unclear chain (a line cook reports to both the sous and the chef de cuisine, who give conflicting instructions), and the cook is confused and the work is inconsistent. The mechanism is that the chain of command must be clear (one direct supervisor per role), and an unclear chain (multiple bosses) causes conflicting instructions and confusion; the org chart is the tool. The false signal is that the staff "are flexible," which reads as adaptive. The harm is confusion and inconsistency. The defense is to establish a clear chain (org chart, one supervisor per role), to define the communication paths, and to communicate them.

### Assigning Stations Without Regard To Skill And Failing The Service

A chef assigns stations by rotation (without regard to skill), and a weak cook is on the hardest station, and the service fails. The mechanism is that the station assignment affects the service quality, and a weak cook on a hard station fails; the assignment must be skill-based (strongest on the hardest, developing with support). The false signal is that the rotation "is fair," which reads as equitable. The harm is a service failure. The defense is to assign based on skill and development, to provide support (a deep guide, a stronger cook nearby), and to rotate for cross-training when appropriate.

### Unowned Domains And Things Falling Through The Cracks

A kitchen has unowned domains (no one owns the sanitation, or the ordering, or the training), and these are neglected (everyone assumes someone else is doing it). The mechanism is that unowned domains are neglected, and a clear owner (with the accountability) ensures they're done; the domain ownership must be assigned. The false signal is that the team "handles it," which reads as covered. The harm is neglected domains (sanitation failures, stockouts, untrained staff). The defense is to assign each domain to a role, to communicate the ownership, to hold the owner accountable, and to use a framework like RACI.

## Self-Check

- Is each role defined in writing (responsibilities, authority, accountability), communicated to the staff, and reviewed?
- Is the brigade structure matched to the kitchen's size and concept (flat for small, moderate for medium, full for large)?
- Is the chain of command clear (org chart, one direct supervisor per role), with defined communication paths (line-ups, pre-service, escalation)?
- Are the stations assigned based on skill and development (strongest on the hardest, developing with support), with rotation for cross-training?
- Is there middle management (sous chefs, chef de cuisine) in larger kitchens, with defined roles and empowerment?
- Is each domain (menu, ordering, scheduling, training, sanitation, quality) owned by a role, with the ownership communicated and the owner held accountable?
- Is the reasoning documented so the role definitions, structure fit, chain of command, station assignments, middle management, and domain ownership for the brigade are verifiable?
