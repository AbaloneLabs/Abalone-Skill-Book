# Large Quantity Recipe Scaling and Yield Calculation

Scaling a recipe from 4 servings to 400 is not simple multiplication, and the judgment problem is that cooking dynamics change with volume: heat transfer is slower in large pots (the center takes longer, the edges scorch), evaporation is proportionally different (a large pot loses less moisture per volume), seasoning does not scale linearly (spices and salt often need less per portion at scale, or they become harsh), and leavening and emulsification behave differently in bulk. The recurring failure is multiplying every ingredient by 100 and expecting the same result, producing an over-seasoned, scorched, undercooked, or broken dish. The discipline is to scale in tested increments, to adjust for the volume dynamics, to calculate yield with moisture loss, and to test the scaled recipe before full production.

The agent's job is to scale recipes safely, to calculate yield and portions accurately, and to recognize the limits of linear scaling.

## Core Rules

### Scale In Tested Increments, Not By Blind Multiplication

Recipe scaling should be done in tested increments (test at 10, then 25, then 50, then 100, then 400), not by blind multiplication (4 to 400 in one step), because the cooking dynamics change with volume and the adjustments (heat, time, seasoning, liquid) emerge through testing. The judgment point is that a recipe tested at 4 servings may not work at 400 without adjustment, and incremental testing reveals the adjustments. Scale in increments (test at each step), document the adjustments at each level, and do not jump from a small recipe to a large production without testing.

### Adjust Seasoning Non-Linearly At Scale

Seasoning (salt, spices, acids) often does not scale linearly: at large volumes, salt and spices can become harsh or overwhelming if multiplied directly, and the correct amount may be 70-90% of the linear scale. The judgment point is that palate fatigue (the cook tasting a large batch), spice extraction (more efficient in large volumes), and concentration (less evaporation per volume) change the seasoning needs, and a linear scale often over-seasons. Scale seasoning to 70-90% of the linear amount initially, taste and adjust, and document the correct amount. Herbs and spices may extract differently in bulk; test.

### Account For Different Heat Transfer And Evaporation In Large Volumes

Large pots and pans have different heat transfer (slower to the center, hotter at the edges, risk of scorching) and different evaporation (less moisture loss per volume in a large pot) than small ones, and the cooking time, the heat level, and the liquid amount must be adjusted. The judgment point is that a large batch cooks differently (longer to reach temperature, risk of scorching, less reduction), and the recipe must be adjusted. Use lower heat (to avoid scorching), stir more frequently (for even cooking), allow more time (to reach temperature), and adjust the liquid (less needed, as evaporation is less). Use wide, shallow pans for even heating over deep, narrow pots.

### Calculate Yield With Moisture Loss And Shrinkage

The yield (the final servable amount) is less than the input weight due to moisture loss (cooking reduces water weight) and shrinkage (trimming, fat rendering), and the yield calculation must account for this. The judgment point is that the raw weight is not the servable weight, and the yield (often 70-80% for meats, 50-60% for some vegetables) must be calculated to portion correctly. Calculate the yield (using known shrinkage factors — meats lose 20-30%, vegetables vary), and portion from the yield, not the raw weight. Document the yield factors for each recipe.

### Portion Consistently For The Count

Institutional feeding requires consistent portions (each guest gets the same amount), and the portioning (by weight, by volume, or by count) must be controlled. The judgment point is that consistent portioning controls food cost (over-portioning wastes money) and ensures the count (the calculated portions) is met. Portion by weight (the most accurate) or by volume (with standard scoops or ladles), calculate the portions from the yield, and verify the count (does the batch yield the expected portions?). Train staff on consistent portioning.

### Test The Scaled Recipe Before Full Production

Before full production (cooking for 400), test the scaled recipe (at a smaller scale, or a test batch), to verify the yield, the seasoning, the texture, and the timing. The judgment point is that a full production run is not the time to discover a scaling failure, and testing prevents waste and failure. Test the scaled recipe (at 25 or 50), verify the yield and the quality, adjust as needed, and document the final scaled recipe before full production.

## Common Traps

### Multiplying Every Ingredient Linearly And Over-Seasoning

A cook multiplies every ingredient by 100, and the dish is over-seasoned (harsh, salty, spicy). The mechanism is that seasoning (salt, spices) often does not scale linearly, and at large volumes, the linear amount is too much (spice extraction is more efficient, concentration is higher); the seasoning must be scaled non-linearly (70-90%). The false signal is that the recipe "is scaled," which reads as correct. The harm is an over-seasoned, unservable dish. The defense is to scale seasoning to 70-90% initially, to taste and adjust, and to document the correct amount.

### Using The Small-Recipe Heat And Time And Scorching Or Undercooking

A cook uses the small recipe's heat level and cooking time for a large batch, and it scorches (high heat on a large pot) or undercooks (insufficient time to reach temperature). The mechanism is that large volumes have different heat transfer (slower center, hotter edges, scorch risk) and require lower heat and more time; the small-recipe settings do not transfer. The false signal is that the recipe "is the same," which reads as applicable. The harm is a scorched or undercooked batch. The defense is to use lower heat, to stir frequently, to allow more time, and to adjust for the large-volume dynamics.

### Not Accounting For Moisture Loss And Over-Portioning Or Under-Portioning

A cook portions from the raw weight (not the yield), and the portions are too large (the raw weight is more than the servable weight) or the count is off. The mechanism is that cooking reduces the weight (moisture loss, shrinkage), and the yield (not the raw weight) is the servable amount; portioning from the raw weight over-portions or miscounts. The false signal is that the raw weight "is the amount," which reads as the portion. The harm is over-portioning (food cost loss) or a short count (not enough portions). The defense is to calculate the yield (with shrinkage factors), to portion from the yield, and to verify the count.

### Jumping From 4 Servings To 400 Without Testing And Failing

A cook scales from 4 servings to 400 without testing, and the full production fails (wrong texture, wrong seasoning, wrong yield). The mechanism is that scaling dynamics change with volume, and an untested large-scale recipe is likely to fail; incremental testing is required. The false signal is that the recipe "is simple," which reads as scalable. The harm is a full-production failure (waste, unservable food). The defense is to scale in tested increments, to test before full production, and to document the scaled recipe.

### Inconsistent Portioning And Blowing The Food Cost Or The Count

Staff portion inconsistently (some large, some small), and the food cost is off (over-portioning) or the count is short (under-portioning). The mechanism is that institutional feeding requires consistent portions (for cost and count), and inconsistent portioning (eyeballing, varying scoops) wastes food or short the count. The false signal is that the portions "look right," which reads as consistent. The harm is food cost loss or a short count. The defense is to portion by weight or standard scoop, to train staff, and to verify the count.

### Using Deep Narrow Pots And Scorching The Bottom

A cook uses a deep, narrow pot for a large batch, and the bottom scorches while the top is undercooked. The mechanism is that deep, narrow pots have uneven heat (the bottom is hot, the top is cool), and stirring is difficult, leading to scorching; wide, shallow pans heat more evenly. The false signal is that the pot "is big enough," which reads as suitable. The harm is a scorched batch. The defense is to use wide, shallow pans for even heating, to stir frequently, and to recognize the pot-shape effect.

## Self-Check

- Is the recipe scaled in tested increments (10, 25, 50, 100, 400), with adjustments documented at each level, not by blind multiplication?
- Is the seasoning scaled non-linearly (70-90% of linear for salt and spices), tasted and adjusted, and documented?
- Are the heat transfer and evaporation differences accounted for (lower heat, more time, less liquid, frequent stirring, wide shallow pans)?
- Is the yield calculated with moisture loss and shrinkage (meats 70-80%, vegetables variable), and are portions calculated from the yield?
- Is the portioning consistent (by weight or standard scoop), and is the count verified (does the batch yield the expected portions)?
- Is the scaled recipe tested before full production (at a smaller scale), with yield, seasoning, texture, and timing verified?
- Is the reasoning documented so the scaling method, seasoning adjustment, heat/evaporation, yield calculation, portioning, and testing for each scaled recipe are verifiable?
