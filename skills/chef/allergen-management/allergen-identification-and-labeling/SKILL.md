# Allergen Identification And Labeling

Allergen management begins with knowing exactly what is in the food, and that knowledge is harder to maintain than it looks. Ingredients change, suppliers substitute, processed components carry hidden allergens, and a single misread label can send a guest into anaphylaxis. The judgment problem is that allergens are not always obvious — milk powder in a spice blend, sesame in a tahini substitute, mustard in a "house" dressing, tree nuts in a liqueur — and the cost of an undeclared allergen is severe and immediate. This skill covers the identification and communication side: building an accurate allergen map of the menu, keeping it current, and presenting it truthfully to guests so they can make safe choices.

Use this skill when building or revising recipes, onboarding new ingredients, designing menu labels, training service staff on allergen claims, or auditing whether declared allergen information matches what is actually being served.

## Core Rules

### Map Every Allergen In Every Dish, From Raw To Plate

Build a documented allergen matrix that lists each menu item against the regulated allergens (commonly peanut, tree nut, milk, egg, wheat/gluten, soy, sesame, fish, shellfish, mustard, celery, lupin, sulphites, and others per jurisdiction). The matrix must trace from the finished dish down through every sub-recipe, sauce, marinade, garnish, stock, and processed ingredient to the supplier specification sheet. A dish is only "free of" an allergen if every component, including the oil it was fried in and the bun it sits on, is free of it. Update the matrix whenever a recipe, supplier, or ingredient changes, and date every revision.

### Read Supplier Specifications, Not Just Ingredient Labels

The retail ingredient label is not sufficient for professional allergen control. Request full allergen specifications from every supplier, including "may contain" or "made in a facility that also processes" statements, because shared equipment introduces real cross-contact risk for sensitive guests. Confirm whether a supplier reformulated a product, because reformulations silently change the allergen profile of dishes that have not changed on the menu. Maintain a file of current specifications and review them on a set schedule, not only when a problem appears.

### Recognize Hidden And Unexpected Allergen Sources

Allergens hide in places cooks do not expect: wheat in soy sauce, milk in dark chocolate and charcuterie, egg in pasta and some bread glazes, sesame in hamburger buns and hummus, tree nuts in pesto and amaretto, peanut in satay and some chili powders, sulphites in wine and dried fruit, mustard in many condiments, celery in stock bases, lupin in some flours. Train the team to assume nothing and verify everything, because a "simple" vinaigrette or stock can carry an undeclared allergen. When a guest asks, the only safe answer comes from the documented matrix, not from memory or assumption.

### Declare Allergens Truthfully And Accessibly

Allergen information must reach the guest before they order, in a form they can act on. This may be symbols on the menu, a separate allergen guide, or trained staff who consult the matrix in real time. The critical rule is accuracy over convenience: never claim a dish is "allergen-free" unless every component and the entire preparation path is verified free, including fryer oil and shared equipment. A vague "probably fine" or "I think so" from untrained staff is dangerous. If information is uncertain, say so and escalate to someone who can verify, rather than guessing.

### Handle "Free-From" Claims With Strict Verification

Marketing a dish as gluten-free, nut-free, dairy-free, or vegan carries legal and medical weight. Such a claim requires that the dish be prepared with dedicated equipment, separate fryers, segregated ingredients, and verified supplier documentation — not merely that the recipe omits the allergen. A "gluten-free" item fried in the same oil as breaded chicken is not gluten-free. Reserve free-from claims for dishes that can be defended end to end, and document the controls that support the claim. If full segregation is not possible, do not make the claim; describe the dish accurately instead.

### Communicate Allergen Changes Immediately

When a supplier reformulates, a recipe changes, or a substitution occurs during service, the allergen matrix and the front-of-house information must update the same day. The trap is a silent change: the kitchen switches soy sauce brands, the new one contains wheat, and the menu still says the dish is gluten-free. Build a process where any ingredient change triggers an allergen review and a notification to service staff before the next service. Stale allergen information is worse than no information because it creates false confidence.

### Train Service Staff To Route, Not Guess

Servers are usually the first point of contact for an allergen question, and they must be trained never to answer from memory. The protocol is: listen to the specific allergen, check the documented matrix, confirm with the kitchen if anything is uncertain, and communicate the controls (dedicated prep, clean equipment) to the guest. Empower staff to refuse a request they cannot safely fulfill rather than reassuring a guest into danger. A clear escalation path protects both the guest and the business.

## Common Traps

### Trusting Memory And Recipe Assumptions Over Documentation

Cooks and servers frequently answer allergen questions from memory, assuming a dish "obviously" contains or lacks an ingredient. The false signal is confidence born of familiarity with the dish. The mechanism is that memory omits the secondary components — the marinade, the finishing oil, the garnish, the stock base — and that suppliers change formulations without obvious notice, so a dish that was safe last month may not be safe today. The harm is that a guest receives a confident "yes, it's nut-free" that is actually wrong, and the first indication of error is a medical emergency. The defense is a documented, dated allergen matrix that is the single source of truth, consulted every time, with no exceptions for "obvious" dishes. Familiarity is exactly when errors happen.

### Ignoring "May Contain" And Shared-Equipment Statements

Kitchens often treat "may contain traces of" labels as legal boilerplate and serve the product to allergic guests anyway. The false signal is that the label seems precautionary and the product looks clean. The mechanism is that shared production lines introduce real cross-contact, and for a severely allergic guest, trace amounts below the detection threshold of the label can still trigger a reaction. The harm is a reaction to a product the kitchen believed was safe. The defense is to take precautionary labeling seriously, to obtain supplier specifications that state the actual risk, and to communicate the "may contain" status to the guest so they can decide. Dismissing the label is making the guest's medical decision for them.

### Treating Free-From Claims As Recipe-Level Only

A kitchen removes nuts from a dessert recipe and labels it "nut-free," but the dessert is still baked on a sheet pan that toasted nuts yesterday, garnished with a knife that sliced almond cake, and stored next to nut items. The false signal is that the recipe no longer lists nuts. The mechanism is that cross-contact, not recipe composition, is what exposes the guest, and shared equipment carries allergen proteins far longer than cooks assume. The harm is that a verified-safe recipe still delivers allergen protein to the guest. The defense is to treat any free-from claim as a full-chain commitment requiring dedicated or rigorously cleaned equipment, segregated storage, separate fryers, and documented procedures — and to decline the claim entirely when such controls are not feasible.

### Letting Supplier Reformulations Pass Unnoticed

A supplier ships a reformulated stock base that now contains celery, but the kitchen never re-reads the spec and the menu still lists the soup as celery-free. The false signal is that the dish on the plate looks identical to before. The mechanism is that ingredient changes happen upstream, invisible to the line, and the allergen matrix becomes silently false. The harm is that a guest relying on outdated information is exposed. The defense is a scheduled review of supplier specifications, a requirement that suppliers notify the kitchen of reformulation, and a process that triggers an allergen-matrix update the moment any ingredient changes. Assume the matrix is stale until confirmed current.

### Vague Or Untrained Front-Of-House Communication

A server, unsure and pressed for time, tells a guest "I think it's fine, let me check" but then forgets, or guesses to avoid looking uninformed. The false signal is the reassuring tone. The mechanism is that the guest makes an ordering decision based on incomplete or fabricated information, and the kitchen never learns an allergic guest is in the restaurant. The harm is exposure plus a lost opportunity to apply dedicated controls. The defense is a hard protocol: never answer from memory, always consult the matrix, always notify the kitchen of an allergic guest, and always escalate uncertainty rather than guessing. Staff must feel safe saying "I don't know, let me verify."

### Assuming Cooking Destroys Allergens

A persistent belief is that heat denatures allergen proteins and makes them safe. It does not reliably do so for most major allergens; many proteins are heat-stable and retain their ability to trigger reactions, including anaphylaxis, after cooking. The false signal is that cooked food "looks different" and feels safer. The mechanism is that the cook reasons a grilled sandwich or baked good no longer poses a risk, and serves it to an allergic guest. The harm is a severe reaction to food the kitchen believed was rendered safe. The defense is to treat allergen protein as persistent through cooking and to rely on avoidance and segregation, never on heat, as the control.

## Self-Check

- Is there a documented, dated allergen matrix tracing every menu item through every sub-recipe, garnish, stock, and processed ingredient to supplier specifications?
- Are full supplier allergen specifications (including "may contain" statements) on file and reviewed on a set schedule, not only when a problem arises?
- Does the team recognize hidden and unexpected allergen sources (soy sauce, dark chocolate, pesto, liqueurs, stock bases) and verify rather than assume?
- Are all "free-from" claims supported by dedicated or rigorously cleaned equipment, segregated storage, separate fryers, and documented controls end to end?
- Does any ingredient or supplier change trigger an immediate allergen-matrix update and a same-day notification to service staff?
- Are service staff trained never to answer allergen questions from memory, to consult the matrix, notify the kitchen, and escalate uncertainty rather than guess?
- Is the team aware that cooking does not reliably destroy allergens, so avoidance and segregation — not heat — are the real controls?
