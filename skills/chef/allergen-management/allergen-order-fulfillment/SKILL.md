# Allergen Order Fulfillment

An allergen order is a chain of communication and action that is only as strong as its weakest link. A guest states an allergy to a server, the server writes it on a ticket, the kitchen reads the ticket, a cook prepares the dish, the expediter passes the plate, the server delivers it — and at any link a misunderstanding, an abbreviation, or a forgotten step can deliver a contaminated meal. The judgment problem is that the protocol must work under service pressure, with rotating staff, partial information, and guests who may not fully describe their allergy's severity. Designing the protocol so it is simple, repeatable, and verifiable is what separates a kitchen that handles allergies safely from one that gets lucky until it does not.

Use this skill when designing the front-to-back allergen order protocol, training staff, handling a live allergen request during service, or auditing how orders actually flow from guest to plate.

## Core Rules

### Capture The Specific Allergen And Severity At Order

The protocol begins with the server capturing exactly what the guest is allergic to, not a vague "allergy" or "intolerance." Different allergies demand different controls: a peanut allergy requires total avoidance and dedicated handling, while a sulphite sensitivity may require only avoiding certain wines. Ask whether the allergy is severe or anaphylactic, because that raises the bar for segregation and may warrant declining dishes that cannot be made safe. Record the allergen in the guest's own words on the order ticket and in the point-of-sale system so it travels with the dish. Never paraphrase an allergy into something vague like "special diet."

### Route The Request Through A Single, Named Channel

Every allergen order must reach the kitchen through a defined channel that cannot be missed: a specific ticket flag, a verbal call to the expediter, and a physical marker on the plate. Do not rely on a server's memory or a casual "table four has an allergy" shouted across the line. The expediter or chef-on-duty should personally acknowledge the allergen order, confirm the dish can be made safely, and assign it to a specific cook. A single, named channel means there is exactly one path the information takes, and a break in that path is visible immediately.

### Confirm The Dish Can Be Made Safe Before Promising It

Before telling a guest a dish can be adapted, the kitchen must verify that every component, every shared surface, and every step can be controlled. If the dish relies on a shared fryer, a marinade with the allergen, or a station that cannot be segregated at that moment, the honest answer is to decline or suggest an alternative. Promising a safe version that the kitchen cannot actually deliver is the most dangerous failure mode. Build a short list of dishes that are reliably safe to adapt and a clear policy for declining requests that cannot be met.

### Assign One Cook To Own The Dish End To End

The allergen dish should be handled by a single cook from start to finish, who is responsible for the separate workflow: clean station, dedicated tools, fresh gloves, segregated ingredients, and a clear plating path. Splitting the dish across multiple cooks multiplies the chance that one assumes another handled the segregation. The owning cook should call out the allergen status at the pass and hand the plate directly to the expediter or server, not leave it on a shared pass.

### Use A Physical Marker And Verbal Handoff At The Pass

The finished plate must carry a physical marker — a colored skewer, a flagged pick, a distinct plate rim, a sticker — that identifies it as the allergen order, so it is not confused with standard plates during pickup. The cook hands it off with a verbal confirmation of the allergen avoided and the controls applied, and the server repeats back the allergen before taking the plate. This closed-loop communication catches mismatches at the last moment, when they are still fixable.

### Document And Review Allergen Incidents And Near-Misses

When an allergen error occurs or is narrowly caught, document what happened: which link failed, what information was lost, what control broke. Review these incidents in staff meetings to improve the protocol, because near-misses reveal where the chain is fragile. A kitchen that never examines its near-misses is relying on luck; one that treats each as a system signal gets safer over time. Keep a log and act on patterns.

### Train And Rehearse The Protocol Regularly

A protocol written in a binder but never practiced will fail under pressure. Run regular drills: a server takes a mock allergen order, routes it through the system, the kitchen produces it, the handoff is checked. New staff must be trained on the protocol before their first service, and existing staff must be retrained when the menu or the system changes. Verify competence by observation, not by signing a sheet. The protocol is a skill, and skills need repetition.

## Common Traps

### Vague Allergen Capture Leading To Wrong Controls

A server writes "allergy" or "special diet" on a ticket without specifying the allergen or severity, and the kitchen applies generic caution instead of targeted controls. The false signal is that the ticket is flagged, so it looks handled. The mechanism is that "allergy" could mean anything from a mild intolerance to an anaphylactic peanut allergy, and the appropriate controls differ enormously — a generic response is either dangerously inadequate for a severe allergy or wastefully broad for a minor one. The harm is that a severe allergy receives insufficient control, or that the kitchen modifies a dish in a way that still exposes the guest. The defense is a mandatory capture of the specific allergen and severity in the guest's words, repeated back for confirmation, recorded on the ticket and in the POS. Vague flags are not information.

### Relying On Shouted Or Unrecorded Communication

During a rush, a server calls out "table six, nut allergy" to the line and trusts it was heard, with nothing on the ticket. The false signal is the verbal acknowledgment. The mechanism is that the line is noisy, multiple orders are in flight, and a verbal call is forgotten the moment the next order arrives; the cook plates the dish without ever knowing it was an allergen order. The harm is a contaminated plate delivered to a guest the kitchen never properly registered. The defense is a hard rule that every allergen order is recorded in writing on the ticket and in the POS, with a physical marker, and that verbal calls are only a supplement to — never a substitute for — the written record. If it is not written, it did not happen.

### Promising Adaptations The Kitchen Cannot Safely Deliver

A server, eager to please, tells a guest "we can make that gluten-free" without checking whether the kitchen can segregate the station at that moment. The false signal is the confident promise. The mechanism is that the promise creates an obligation the kitchen cannot meet — the fryer is shared, the marinade contains soy sauce, the only available cook is also handling breaded items — and the kitchen either declines too late or improvises inadequate controls. The harm is a contaminated dish served to a guest who was explicitly promised safety. The defense is a policy that servers never promise adaptations without kitchen confirmation, a short list of reliably safe dishes, and a clear, practiced script for declining requests that cannot be met safely. A declined order is far cheaper than an allergic reaction.

### Splitting The Dish Across Multiple Cooks

To speed service, the allergen dish is started by one cook, finished by another, and plated by a third, with each assuming the others applied the segregation controls. The false signal is that more hands means faster service. The mechanism is that distributed responsibility means no single person owns the safety of the dish, and the assumption that "someone else handled it" lets each cook skip the controls they would have applied if they owned it. The harm is a dish that slips through with no consistent segregation. The defense is to assign one cook to own the allergen dish from start to finish, with clear handoff only at the pass and a verbal confirmation of controls. Ownership, not parallelism, produces safety.

### No Physical Marker Or Closed-Loop Handoff

The allergen plate looks identical to the standard plates and is set on the pass among them; a server grabs the wrong one. The false signal is that the expediter "knows" which plate is the allergy order. The mechanism is that under volume, identical plates are indistinguishable, and a server in a hurry picks up the nearest matching dish. The harm is a guest with a severe allergy receives the wrong plate. The defense is a physical marker on every allergen plate — a colored pick, a flagged garnish, a distinct rim — plus a verbal handoff where the cook states the allergen and the server repeats it back. The marker and the closed loop together make a mix-up nearly impossible.

### Never Reviewing Near-Misses

A cook catches a wrong-garnish error just in time, tosses it, and says nothing; the same fragile link fails again next week and reaches a guest. The false signal is that the catch means the system worked. The mechanism is that a near-miss is a free signal about a weak link, and ignoring it guarantees the failure recurs until it causes harm. The harm is a preventable exposure that the kitchen had the information to prevent. The defense is a no-blame log of near-misses, regular review in staff meetings, and concrete protocol changes in response. Near-misses are data, not embarrassments.

## Self-Check

- Does the server capture the specific allergen and its severity in the guest's words, recorded on the ticket and in the POS, rather than a vague "allergy" flag?
- Does every allergen order travel a single named channel — ticket flag, verbal call, physical marker — that cannot be silently missed?
- Does the kitchen confirm a dish can be made safe before the server promises it, with a clear policy for declining requests that cannot be met?
- Is each allergen dish owned by a single cook from start to finish, responsible for the full segregation workflow?
- Does the finished plate carry a physical marker and undergo a verbal closed-loop handoff where cook and server confirm the allergen?
- Are allergen errors and near-misses documented and reviewed to improve the protocol, not hidden?
- Is the full protocol rehearsed regularly with all staff, with new hires trained before their first service and competence verified by observation?
- Are assumptions, uncertainties, and confidence levels stated explicitly rather than buried in a confident-sounding conclusion?
