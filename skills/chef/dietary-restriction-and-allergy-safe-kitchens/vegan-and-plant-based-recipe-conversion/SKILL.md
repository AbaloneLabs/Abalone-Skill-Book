# Vegan and Plant-Based Recipe Conversion

Vegan conversion is a functional substitution problem, and the judgment problem is that each animal ingredient (egg, milk, butter, cream, honey) performs specific functions (binding, leavening, emulsifying, fat, moisture, sweetness, browning), and a successful vegan version must replace each function — not just swap the ingredient. The recurring failure is a one-to-one swap (a banana for an egg) that ignores the function (the egg was a leavener, the banana is not), producing a dense, flat, or weepy result. The discipline is to identify the function of each animal ingredient, to select a substitute that performs that function, and to recognize that vegan baking and cooking are different systems that require testing.

The agent's job is to convert recipes by function, to test the conversions, and to recognize the limits and the cross-contact risk (vegan for a vegan guest also means no animal cross-contact).

## Core Rules

### Identify The Function Of Each Animal Ingredient

Each animal ingredient performs one or more functions in the recipe, and the conversion must replace each function. Eggs bind (hold the structure), leaven (trap gas for rise), emulsify (hold oil and water together), add moisture, and add fat (yolks); milk adds moisture, fat, and protein; butter adds fat, moisture, and flavor; cream adds fat and emulsification; honey adds sweetness and moisture. The judgment point is that the conversion is by function, not by ingredient, and identifying the function (is the egg a binder or a leavener?) determines the substitute. Identify each animal ingredient's function in the specific recipe, then select the substitute that performs that function.

### Replace Eggs By Function

Eggs are the hardest to replace because they perform multiple functions. For binding (cookies, brownies): flax egg (ground flax + water) or chia egg. For leavening (cakes, muffins): baking soda + acid (vinegar or lemon juice), or aquafaba (chickpea brine, whipped like egg whites). For structure in rich baked goods: silken tofu or yogurt. For moisture: plant milk or water. The judgment point is that the egg's function determines the substitute, and a single substitute (like a banana) rarely replaces all functions. Match the substitute to the function (flax for binding, aquafaba for leavening, tofu for structure), and use a combination if the egg performed multiple functions.

### Replace Dairy By Function

Milk is replaced by plant milk (soy, oat, almond) for moisture and protein — choose an unsweetened, unflavored milk for neutral taste. Butter is replaced by plant butter (vegan block, for fat and moisture) or oil (for fat, with hydration adjusted) — plant butter is the closest swap. Cream is replaced by coconut cream (full-fat, for fat and emulsification) or cashew cream (soaked and blended cashews, for richness). Honey is replaced by maple syrup or agave (for sweetness and moisture). The judgment point is that the dairy's function (moisture, fat, emulsification) determines the substitute, and the substitute should match the function and the flavor. Match the substitute to the function, adjust the hydration if needed, and consider the flavor (coconut cream adds coconut flavor).

### Recognize Vegan Baking As A Different System

Vegan baking is a different system from traditional baking: without eggs and dairy, the structure (no egg protein network), the leavening (no egg foam), the emulsification (no egg lecithin), and the browning (no milk sugars) are different, and the results are often denser, less tender, and less browned. The judgment point is that treating vegan as "traditional without the animal" leads to failure, and recognizing it as a different system (with its own techniques and expectations) leads to success. Learn the vegan system (the binders, the leaveners, the emulsifiers), test recipes, and set appropriate expectations.

### Prevent Cross-Contact For Strict Vegans

For strict vegans (ethical vegans, or those with dairy/egg allergies), even trace animal products matter, and the cross-contact prevention (dedicated tools, sanitized surfaces, verified ingredients) applies. The judgment point is that a vegan dish must be truly vegan (no animal cross-contact), and shared equipment (a grill that cooked meat, a pan that cooked butter) recontaminates it. Use dedicated or sanitized tools, verify ingredients (some sugars are processed with bone char, some wines are fined with animal products), and prepare vegan dishes separately. See the allergen cross-contact skill for the protocol.

### Test And Document The Conversion

Vegan conversions require testing (the substitutes vary, and a conversion that works with one brand may fail with another), and documenting the conversion (the exact substitutes, the amounts, the adjustments) ensures reproducibility. The judgment point is that vegan baking is less forgiving than traditional, and testing and documentation are essential. Test the conversion with the specific substitutes, document the substitutes and amounts, and note the adjustments for reproducibility.

## Common Traps

### Doing A One-To-One Swap And Ignoring The Function

A baker swaps a banana for an egg (a common one-to-one swap), and the baked good is dense and flat. The mechanism is that the egg was a leavener (trapping gas for rise), and the banana is not a leavener (it adds moisture and binding, but not rise), so the result is dense and flat; the function must be matched. The false signal is that a banana "replaces an egg," which reads as a swap. The harm is a dense, flat result. The defense is to identify the egg's function (binder, leavener, emulsifier), to select the substitute that performs that function, and to use a combination if needed.

### Using A Neutral Plant Milk Where Fat And Emulsification Are Needed

A baker replaces cream with a low-fat plant milk (almond milk), and the sauce breaks or the baked good is dry. The mechanism is that cream provides fat and emulsification, and a low-fat plant milk (almond milk is mostly water) does not, so the emulsion breaks or the baked good is dry; a high-fat substitute (coconut cream, cashew cream) is needed for fat and emulsification. The false signal is that it's a "milk," which reads as a swap. The harm is a broken sauce or a dry baked good. The defense is to match the substitute's fat and emulsification to the original (coconut cream for cream, plant butter for butter), and to recognize the function.

### Expecting The Same Structure And Browning As Traditional Baking

A baker expects the vegan baked good to have the same structure and browning as traditional, and is disappointed by the denser, less browned result. The mechanism is that without egg protein (structure) and milk sugars (browning), vegan baked goods are denser and less browned, and the system is different; appropriate expectations are needed. The false signal is that it's "the same recipe, vegan," which reads as equivalent. The harm is disappointment and perceived failure. The defense is to recognize vegan as a different system, to set appropriate expectations, and to optimize the substitutes and technique for the best vegan result.

### Not Verifying Ingredients For Hidden Animal Products

A baker uses white sugar (which may be processed with bone char) or wine (which may be fined with animal products), and the "vegan" dish is not truly vegan. The mechanism is that some ingredients have hidden animal products (bone-char sugar, animal-fined wine, some food dyes), and verification requires reading labels and sourcing carefully; a strict vegan (or an allergic guest) cares about these. The false signal is that the ingredient "is plant-based," which reads as vegan. The harm is a non-vegan dish served as vegan. The defense is to verify ingredients (source vegan sugar, vegan wine), to read labels, and to recognize the hidden animal products.

### Cross-Contacting The Vegan Dish With Shared Equipment

A cook grills the vegan burger on the same grill as the meat burgers, and the vegan dish is contaminated with animal fat. The mechanism is that shared equipment (a grill, a pan) carries animal residues, and cooking the vegan dish on it recontaminates it; dedicated or sanitized equipment is required for strict vegans. The false signal is that the grill "is hot," which reads as clean. The harm is a contaminated vegan dish. The defense is to use dedicated or sanitized equipment, to cook vegan dishes separately, and to recognize the cross-contact risk.

### Not Testing The Conversion And Having It Fail In Production

A baker converts a recipe without testing, and it fails in production (dense, flat, weepy). The mechanism is that vegan conversions are less forgiving and brand-dependent, and an untested conversion is likely to fail; testing is essential. The false signal is that the conversion "looks right," which reads as ready. The harm is a production failure. The defense is to test the conversion with the specific substitutes, to document the substitutes and amounts, and to note the adjustments for reproducibility.

## Self-Check

- Is each animal ingredient's function identified (binding, leavening, emulsifying, fat, moisture, sweetness, browning) before the substitute is selected?
- Are eggs replaced by function (flax/chia for binding, aquafaba/baking soda + acid for leavening, silken tofu for structure)?
- Is dairy replaced by function (plant milk for moisture, plant butter or oil for fat, coconut/cashew cream for fat and emulsification, maple/agave for honey)?
- Is vegan baking recognized as a different system (different structure, leavening, emulsification, browning), with appropriate techniques and expectations?
- Is cross-contact prevented for strict vegans (dedicated or sanitized tools, verified ingredients like vegan sugar and wine, separate cooking)?
- Is the conversion tested with the specific substitutes and documented (substitutes, amounts, adjustments) for reproducibility?
- Is the reasoning documented so the function identification, egg replacement, dairy replacement, system recognition, cross-contact prevention, and testing for each vegan conversion are verifiable?
