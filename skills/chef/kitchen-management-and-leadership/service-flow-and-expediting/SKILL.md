# Service Flow And Expediting

Expediting is the conductor's role of a kitchen: reading tickets, calling orders, timing courses, coordinating stations, and ensuring that each table's food leaves the pass complete, correct, and at the right moment. The judgment problem is that expediting looks like simply reading tickets and shouting them, when it is actually a real-time orchestration of multiple stations with different cook times, varying dish complexity, and the constraint that a table's courses must land together. A poor expediter stacks tickets, calls orders without timing, and lets the kitchen drown; a skilled expediter sequences work so that each station peaks smoothly and every table is served well. This skill covers the expediting decisions that determine whether service flows or collapses.

Use this skill when training an expediter, designing the pass and ticket system, running a service, or diagnosing why a kitchen cannot keep up at peak.

## Core Rules

### Read And Sequence Tickets By Table And Timing

The expediter's first job is to read each ticket and sequence it against the other tickets in flight: which table, how many guests, which courses, which stations, and what are the cook times. Sequence so that a table's courses arrive in order with appropriate pacing and so that all plates for a table's course leave together, even if they come from different stations with different cook times. Do not call tickets in the order they arrive without timing; call them so the kitchen's work and the guests' experience both flow. Sequencing is the core expediting skill, and it requires constant mental tracking of what is in flight.

### Coordinate Stations By Cook Time So Courses Land Together

Different dishes have different cook times — a steak takes longer than a salad, a fried item faster than a braised one — and the expediter must call orders so that all plates for a table's course finish at the same moment. Call the long-cook items first, then time the quick items to land with them, communicating the plan to the stations so each knows when to start. A table where one guest's plate sits under the heat lamp while another's is still cooking is an expediting failure, not a station failure. Build the timing into how orders are called.

### Call Orders Clearly, Completely, And With Confirmation

Every call must be clear, complete (all items, all modifications, all allergens), and confirmed by the receiving station. Use a consistent call structure, state modifications and allergens explicitly (never assume the station remembers), and require a verbal confirmation so nothing is missed in noise. Ambiguous or partial calls produce wrong dishes, missed modifications, and allergen errors. The call is the communication backbone of service, and its clarity determines whether the kitchen executes the right food.

### Manage The Pass: Inspect, Complete, And Release

The pass is where dishes are inspected, completed (garnish, wipe, sauce), grouped by table, and released to the runner or server. The expediter inspects every plate against the standard before it leaves — correct protein, correct doneness, correct components, clean rim — because a plate that leaves wrong is a guest's whole impression of the kitchen. Group all plates for a table's course and release them together, with a verbal handoff for allergen and special plates. The pass is the last control point, and skipping inspection lets errors reach the guest.

### Pace The Dining Room To The Kitchen's Capacity

The expediter (often with the floor manager) controls the pace at which tables are seated and orders are fired, because a kitchen has finite capacity and seating everyone at once overwhelms it. Pace seating and firing so that the kitchen works at a sustainable peak rather than collapsing under a wave. This is sometimes called "the gate," and managing it well is the difference between a smooth Saturday and a disastrous one. Communicate with the floor about timing, and do not let the dining room push the kitchen past its capacity.

### Communicate Shortages And 86s Immediately And Completely

When a dish runs out (86), a component is delayed, or a substitution is forced, the expediter must communicate it immediately and completely to the floor, so servers can guide guests before they order. An 86 that reaches the floor after a guest has ordered is a service failure. Maintain a running 86 board, call 86s the moment they are known, and confirm the floor has heard. The expediter is the bridge between kitchen and floor for everything that affects what guests can order.

### Stay Calm And Triage Under Pressure

At peak, tickets stack and the kitchen falls behind; the expediter's job is to stay calm, triage, and sequence recovery rather than panic. Identify which tables are most delayed, which can be prioritized, and communicate honestly with the floor about realistic timing. Panic transmits to the team and degrades execution; calm triage recovers the service. Build the skill of staying composed and decisive when the rail is full, because that is when expediting matters most.

## Common Traps

### Calling Tickets In Arrival Order Without Timing

An expediter calls tickets in the exact order they print, with no sequencing, and a table's appetizers come out after their entrees because a long-cook steak on a later ticket was fired at the same time as a quick salad on an earlier one. The false signal is that the expediter is "calling everything in order." The mechanism is that arrival order ignores cook time and course structure, so dishes finish in the order they were called, not in the order the guests need them, producing courses out of sequence, plates sitting under heat lamps waiting for slower companions, and a dining experience that feels chaotic. The harm is poor guest experience, remade dishes, and a kitchen that looks incompetent when the failure was expediting. The defense is to read and sequence each ticket by table, course, and cook time, calling long items first and timing quick items to land together, so courses arrive in order and a table's plates leave the pass simultaneously. Sequence, not arrival order, governs service flow.

### Failing To Coordinate Cross-Station Courses

A table orders dishes from three different stations — a sauté appetizer, a grill entrée, a fryer side — and the expediter calls them without coordinating start times, so they finish minutes apart and the table's food leaves piecemeal. The false signal is that each station is doing its job. The mechanism is that stations work independently when not coordinated, each starting when called and finishing when done, so without explicit timing the plates land at different times and cannot be coursed together; the expediter has effectively delegated coordination to chance. The harm is a table receiving plates one at a time, food cooling under lamps, and a guest experience that feels disorganized. The defense is for the expediter to call cross-station orders with explicit timing — fire the steak now, hold the sauté to fire in six minutes so both finish together — and to confirm each station understands the plan. Courses land together only when they are orchestrated to.

### Ambiguous Or Incomplete Calls

An expediter calls "two steaks, one chicken, allergy table four" without specifying the doneness, the cut, or which allergen, trusting the stations to remember or infer, and the table receives wrong or unsafe food. The false signal is that the call was made and the kitchen is experienced. The mechanism is that ambiguous calls transfer the responsibility for accuracy from the expediter to each station's memory, and under volume memory fails — the wrong doneness gets fired, the allergen is forgotten, the modification is missed — because the call did not encode the information; the noise and pace of service guarantee that inference will be wrong sometimes. The harm is wrong dishes, missed modifications, and allergen errors that can be medically serious. The defense is a consistent, complete call structure: every item, every modification, every allergen, stated explicitly, with a required verbal confirmation from each receiving station, treating the call as the single source of truth that travels with the dish. If it was not clearly called and confirmed, it was not communicated.

### Skipping Pass Inspection Under Pressure

At peak, the expediter stops inspecting plates and just waves them through to clear the rail, and wrong or substandard dishes reach guests. The false signal is that the kitchen is busy and the expediter is "keeping up." The mechanism is that the pass is the last control point, and skipping inspection to speed flow eliminates the check that catches wrong proteins, missed components, dirty rims, and incorrect doneness, so errors that the stations made propagate directly to the guest; the few seconds saved cost the table their impression of the kitchen and often require a remade dish that adds more load. The harm is inconsistent, wrong, or substandard food reaching guests, exactly when the kitchen is most visible. The defense is to treat pass inspection as non-negotiable regardless of pressure: every plate checked against the standard before release, because the pass is where the kitchen's work becomes the guest's experience, and a plate that leaves wrong can never be taken back. Speed at the pass comes from efficiency, not from skipping the check.

### Letting The Floor Overwhelm The Kitchen

The floor seats and fires all tables at once to keep guests happy, the kitchen drowns under a wave of simultaneous tickets, and every table's food is late. The false signal is that the floor is serving guests promptly. The mechanism is that a kitchen has finite capacity, and seating or firing everyone at once pushes it past its peak into collapse — tickets stack, cook times extend, quality drops, and every table is late rather than a few being paced; the attempt to serve everyone immediately results in serving everyone poorly. The harm is uniformly slow service, stressed kitchen, and bad reviews across the board. The defense is for the expediter (with the floor manager) to control the gate: pace seating and firing to the kitchen's sustainable capacity, hold and stagger orders so the kitchen works at peak without collapsing, and communicate realistic timing to the floor so expectations align. The dining room must breathe at the kitchen's pace, not the reverse.

### Late Or Incomplete 86 Communication

A dish runs out and the kitchen knows but does not tell the floor, a server continues selling it, and a guest orders a dish the kitchen cannot make. The false signal is that the kitchen is busy and will mention it later. The mechanism is that an 86 communicated late reaches the floor after guests have ordered, forcing an awkward re-order, a delayed table, and a guest who feels misled; an 86 communicated incompletely (the dish is out but the server is not told the substitution options) produces a server who cannot guide the guest. The harm is service failure and guest disappointment that was entirely preventable. The defense is to call 86s the moment they are known, to maintain a visible 86 board, to confirm the floor has heard, and to provide substitution guidance so servers can redirect guests proactively. The 86 is kitchen-floor information that has a deadline; missing it breaks service.

## Self-Check

- Are tickets read and sequenced by table, course, and cook time, rather than called in arrival order?
- Are cross-station orders called with explicit timing so a table's course plates finish and leave together?
- Is every call clear, complete (items, modifications, allergens), and verbally confirmed by receiving stations?
- Is every plate inspected against the standard at the pass before release, even under peak pressure?
- Is seating and firing paced to the kitchen's sustainable capacity, with the gate managed by the expediter and floor manager?
- Are 86s, shortages, and substitutions communicated to the floor immediately, completely, and confirmed, with a visible 86 board?
- Does the expediter stay calm and triage under pressure, sequencing recovery and communicating honest timing rather than panicking?
- Is the reasoning documented clearly enough that another practitioner could review the basis and reproduce the conclusion?
