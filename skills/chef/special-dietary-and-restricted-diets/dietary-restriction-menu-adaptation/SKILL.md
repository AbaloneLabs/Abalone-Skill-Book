# Dietary Restriction Menu Adaptation

Adapting a dish for a restricted diet is not a matter of swapping one ingredient for another; each restriction removes a structural, flavor, or safety component that the rest of the dish depended on, and a naive substitution produces a dish that is technically compliant but inferior in texture, balance, or appeal. This skill covers the judgment of how to adapt dishes so that the result honors both the restriction and the quality of the original. The harm this skill prevents is the common pattern of producing compliant-but-poor dishes that disappoint guests, erode trust in the kitchen's ability to accommodate, and in the worst case introduce safety failures when substitutions carry hidden allergens or cross-contact.

## Core Rules

### Identify the exact boundary of the restriction before adapting

Dietary labels are not self-explanatory. Vegan excludes all animal products including honey and some refining agents; gluten-free excludes wheat, barley, rye, and their derivatives including soy sauce; kosher and halal have specific slaughter, ingredient, and combination rules; low-FODMAP excludes specific fermentable carbohydrates that hide in onion, garlic, wheat, and dairy. Before adapting, define exactly what is excluded and what is permitted, including hidden sources and derivative ingredients. A dish adapted to a vague understanding of the restriction will fail on a technicality or on a hidden ingredient.

### Adapt the dish's structure, not just its ingredients

Most restrictions remove an ingredient that carried a structural role: gluten provides elasticity and gas retention; eggs bind, leaven, and emulsify; dairy fat tenderizes and carries flavor; gelatin sets. A direct substitution that matches the missing ingredient in name rarely matches its function. For each removed ingredient, identify its structural role and select a substitute or combination of substitutes that reproduces that function, such as a binder plus a leavener to replace egg, or a gum plus a protein blend to replace gluten. Structural adaptation, not ingredient swapping, is what produces a compliant dish that still works.

### Rebuild flavor balance after removing a flavor-carrying ingredient

Many restricted ingredients are major flavor carriers: dairy fat, bacon and cured meats, fish sauce, soy sauce, cheese. Removing them often leaves a dish that is structurally intact but flavor-flat. After adapting structure, audit the flavor balance and rebuild umami, salt, acid, and fat using permitted ingredients such as mushroom, seaweed, miso (where permitted), smoked paprika, nutritional yeast, tamari, or aged vinegars. A compliant dish that tastes like a penalty will not be ordered twice, so flavor rebuilding is part of the adaptation, not optional.

### Verify compliance of every component and garnish, not just the main

A dish is only as compliant as its least compliant component. A vegan entree served with a butter-finished sauce, a gluten-free pasta topped with soy-sauce-based dressing, or a dairy-free dessert garnished with white chocolate that contains milk fat all fail the restriction through a component the kitchen overlooked. Audit every component, including sauces, garnishes, marinades, stocks, and the cooking medium, against the restriction. The hidden component is where most compliance failures occur.

### Source certified ingredients when the restriction is safety-critical

For gluten-free and allergen-free restrictions, the risk is not only the ingredient itself but cross-contact in the supply chain. Oats are often processed on wheat equipment; nuts share facilities with other allergens; spices and flours can carry trace contaminants. For safety-critical restrictions, source certified ingredients and verify supplier documentation rather than assuming a generic product is safe. The cost of certified ingredients is part of serving restricted diets responsibly.

### Test the adapted dish against the original for quality, not just compliance

An adapted dish should be evaluated on whether it is a good dish in its own right, not merely whether it is compliant. Plate the adapted version alongside the original and compare texture, flavor, appearance, and satisfaction. If the adapted version is clearly inferior, iterate the adaptation rather than serving a penalty dish. Guests ordering restricted options are paying the same price and deserve equivalent quality.

### Document the adaptation so it is reproducible and auditable

Each adapted dish should have a documented formula, ingredient list with brands and certifications, and component breakdown so that the kitchen can reproduce it consistently and so that front-of-house can answer guest questions accurately. Undocumented adaptations drift, lose compliance through staff turnover, and cannot be verified when a guest asks about a specific ingredient.

## Common Traps

### Swapping a restricted ingredient for a same-name substitute without matching function

This is a trap because same-name substitutes, such as gluten-free flour for wheat flour or a single egg replacer for eggs, rarely replicate the multiple functions of the original, so the dish fails structurally even though it is compliant. The mechanism is that the original ingredient performed several roles at once, binding, leavening, tenderizing, emulsifying, and a one-to-one substitute covers only one role, leaving the others unsupported. The false signal is that the substitute exists and is marketed as a replacement, which implies equivalence. The harm is a compliant dish with a gummy, crumbly, or flat texture that disappoints the guest and damages the kitchen's reputation for accommodation. The correct response is to decompose the original ingredient's functions and assemble a combination of substitutes that covers all of them, then test the result structurally before service.

### Treating the restriction as a penalty and serving an inferior dish

This trap operates because restricted dishes are often treated as afterthoughts, so the kitchen invests less effort in their flavor and presentation, producing a compliant but unappealing plate that signals to the guest that their needs are a burden. The mechanism is that the team focuses on compliance, removing the restricted ingredient, and stops there, without rebuilding the flavor, texture, and visual appeal that the removed ingredient supported. The false signal is that the dish passes the compliance check, which the kitchen reads as complete. The harm is that guests with restrictions receive a worse experience for the same price, do not return, and share negative reviews, and the kitchen never learns that the adaptation was the problem. The correct response is to evaluate every adapted dish against the original for quality and to iterate until the restricted version is a dish worth ordering on its own merits.

### Overlooking hidden restricted ingredients in sauces, stocks, and garnishes

This is a trap because the main protein or vegetable is obviously compliant while the sauce, stock, or garnish carries the restricted ingredient in a concentrated, less visible form, so the failure is hidden until the guest reacts or asks. The mechanism is that stocks contain gluten via soy sauce or roux, sauces contain dairy via butter or cream finish, marinades contain honey, and garnishes contain nuts or sesame, none of which are obvious from the plate. The false signal is that the main component is compliant, which anchors the kitchen's confidence. The harm is a compliance failure that can be a medical emergency for an allergic guest or a religious violation, and a loss of trust. The correct response is to audit every component, including stocks, sauces, marinades, garnishes, and cooking mediums, against the restriction, and to maintain a component-level ingredient map for each adapted dish.

### Assuming a generic ingredient is free of the restricted substance

This trap exists because many ingredients carry the restricted substance as a contaminant or derivative that is not obvious from the label, so the kitchen assumes safety based on the ingredient's name. The mechanism is that oats carry wheat cross-contact, soy sauce contains wheat, some vanilla extracts and sugars are processed with animal bone char, spices share facilities with allergens, and processed foods contain hidden dairy or gluten derivatives. The false signal is that the ingredient name does not mention the restricted substance. The harm is a compliance or safety failure from an ingredient the kitchen believed was safe. The correct response is to source certified ingredients for safety-critical restrictions, to read full ingredient lists and allergen statements, and to verify supplier documentation rather than relying on the ingredient name.

### Failing to rebuild umami and salt after removing a flavor carrier

This is a trap because removing a flavor-carrying ingredient, bacon, cheese, fish sauce, soy sauce, also removes the umami and salt that anchored the dish's balance, and a compliant dish without rebuilt flavor tastes flat and incomplete. The mechanism is that the restricted ingredient contributed savory depth and seasoning that the remaining ingredients cannot supply, so the adapted dish is structurally compliant but flavor-deficient. The false signal is that the dish is seasoned correctly on paper, because the removed ingredient's salt is still accounted for. The harm is a dish that guests find bland and disappointing, which they attribute to the kitchen's lack of skill rather than to the missing umami. The correct response is to audit the adapted dish for umami and salt separately from compliance, and to rebuild depth with permitted ingredients such as mushroom, seaweed, smoked spices, nutritional yeast, or aged vinegars.

### Cross-contacting the adapted dish through shared equipment and hands

This trap operates because the adapted dish can be contaminated after it is correctly formulated, through shared fryers, cutting boards, knives, tongs, and unwashed hands that carry the restricted substance from other dishes. The mechanism is that cross-contact is invisible and occurs in the normal flow of service, so a perfectly compliant recipe is ruined by a shared tool. The false signal is that the recipe is compliant, which the kitchen reads as sufficient. The harm is a medical emergency for an allergic guest or a religious violation, and legal liability. The correct response is to designate separate equipment and utensils for restricted dishes, to train staff on cross-contact protocols, to prepare restricted dishes in a separate area or at a separate time, and to treat cross-contact prevention as part of the recipe, not as a separate concern.

### Documenting the adaptation loosely so it drifts out of compliance

This is a trap because undocumented or loosely documented adaptations change as staff turn over and memory fades, so a dish that was compliant at launch silently loses compliance over time. The mechanism is that without a precise, written formula and ingredient list, each cook interprets the adaptation differently, substitutes available ingredients, and the dish drifts away from its verified state. The false signal is that the dish has been served without incident, which the kitchen reads as ongoing compliance. The harm is a compliance failure that surfaces only when a guest has a reaction or asks a detailed question, at which point the kitchen cannot verify what was actually served. The correct response is to document each adapted dish with a full formula, brand-level ingredient list, and component map, to train staff on the documented version, and to audit periodically against the documentation.

- Did I rebuild umami, salt, acid, and fat after removing a flavor-carrying ingredient, so the dish is appealing and not merely compliant?
- Did I audit every component, including sauces, stocks, marinades, garnishes, and cooking medium, against the restriction?
- Did I source certified ingredients and verify supplier documentation for safety-critical restrictions, rather than assuming generic products are safe?
- Did I test the adapted dish against the original for quality and iterate until the restricted version is worth ordering on its own?
- Did I document the adaptation with a full formula, brand-level ingredient list, and component map, and train staff on the documented version?
- Does the output stay within the agent's scope, deferring final authority, licensed judgment, or specialist sign-off to the qualified person where the question exceeds the agent's competence?
