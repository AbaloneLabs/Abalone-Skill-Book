# Food Cost Calculation and Menu Pricing

Food cost and pricing are the arithmetic that determines whether a dish contributes to the business or drains it, and they are consistently miscalculated because cooks estimate costs from memory and price from the competition rather than from the actual yield and cost of the documented recipe. This skill covers the judgment of how to calculate true food cost, how to set prices that protect margin, and how to evaluate menu items for profitability. The harm this skill prevents is the slow erosion of profitability caused by dishes that appear successful by volume but lose money on every plate because their true cost was never calculated and their price was set by feel.

## Core Rules

### Calculate food cost from documented recipe costs, not from memory

Every recipe's food cost must be calculated from the documented cost of each ingredient at the documented quantity, including the cost of trim, marinades, and finishing components that are easy to overlook. Memory-based costing consistently underestimates because it forgets the small components and uses stale prices. Build a costing sheet for every dish that lists every component, its as-purchased cost, its yield, and its edible portion cost, and recalculate when prices or yields change. The costing sheet is the only reliable basis for price.

### Account for yield and trim, not just as-purchased weight

The cost of a dish is based on what is actually served, which is the edible portion, not what is purchased. A protein purchased at a price per kilogram yields less after trimming, boning, and cooking, and the edible portion cost is higher than the as-purchased cost. Calculate yield for every ingredient that is trimmed or cooked, and cost the dish on the edible portion cost. Ignoring yield understates the true cost and produces a dish that loses money on every plate.

### Price to a target food cost percentage with a floor and ceiling

Set menu prices based on a target food cost percentage, typically 25 to 35 percent depending on the concept, but enforce a floor below which price cannot fall and a ceiling above which the guest will not pay. The floor protects margin against rising costs; the ceiling protects sales against price resistance. A single target without a floor and ceiling produces prices that either erode margin when costs rise or suppress sales when the ceiling is exceeded. Review prices against actual cost regularly and adjust within the floor and ceiling.

### Include all plate components in the cost, not just the center-of-plate

The center-of-plate protein is the most visible cost, but the garnish, sauce, starch, vegetable, and even the finishing salt and cooking oil contribute to the true plate cost. A costing sheet that includes only the protein understates the cost and overstates the margin. Include every component, measured at the served quantity, in the plate cost. The small components, summed across the menu, are a significant cost that is invisible when only the protein is costed.

### Evaluate menu items by both popularity and profitability

A dish's contribution to the business is the product of its margin per plate and its volume, so a high-margin dish that no one orders contributes nothing, and a popular dish with a thin margin contributes little per plate but may contribute in volume. Plot every menu item on a matrix of popularity and profitability, and manage each quadrant differently: high popularity and high profitability are stars to promote; low profitability and high popularity are candidates for cost reduction or repricing; high profitability and low popularity are candidates for repositioning; low on both are candidates for removal. Pricing decisions are made on the matrix, not on individual dishes.

### Recalculate cost and margin when ingredient prices change

Ingredient prices fluctuate, especially proteins and produce, and a dish that was profitable at last quarter's prices may be unprofitable at current prices. Monitor key ingredient prices and recalculate affected dish costs when prices move significantly. A static costing sheet that is not updated against current prices produces dishes that silently lose money as costs rise. Recalculation is a recurring task, not a one-time exercise.

### Separate food cost from labor and overhead when evaluating dishes

Food cost percentage is only one component of total cost; labor and overhead are the others. A dish with a low food cost percentage but high labor, a hand-assembled plate that takes extra minutes, may be less profitable than a dish with a higher food cost percentage that is fast to produce. Evaluate dishes on contribution margin, the price minus the food cost, and on the labor required, not on food cost percentage alone. Food cost percentage is a useful signal but not the complete picture of profitability.

## Common Traps

### Costing from memory or from the last calculation rather than from current documented costs

This is a trap because memory and stale calculations consistently understate cost, so the kitchen believes a dish is profitable when rising prices and changing yields have made it unprofitable, and the loss is invisible until the period-end P&L reveals it. The mechanism is that ingredient prices rise, yields change with different suppliers or product specs, and small components are forgotten, so the remembered cost drifts below the true cost while the price stays fixed, eroding margin on every plate. The false signal is that the dish has always been profitable, which reads as still profitable. The harm is dishes that lose money on every plate for months because the cost was never recalculated, and the loss is discovered only at period end when it has already damaged the business. The correct response is to maintain a costing sheet for every dish, to recalculate when prices or yields change, and to treat memory and stale calculations as unreliable for pricing decisions.

### Ignoring yield and costing on as-purchased weight

This trap operates because as-purchased weight is the number on the invoice and is easy to use, but the served portion is the edible portion after trim and cooking loss, which is smaller and therefore more expensive per serving, so costing on as-purchased weight understates the true cost. The mechanism is that a protein purchased at a price per kilogram loses 20 to 40 percent to trim, bone, and cooking shrink, so the edible portion cost is significantly higher than the as-purchased cost, and costing the dish on the as-purchased weight assigns too low a cost to the serving and overstates the margin. The false signal is that the invoice price is known and applied, which reads as accurate. The harm is dishes priced on an understated cost, so the true margin is lower than calculated and the dish may be unprofitable at its current price, a loss that is invisible because the costing method systematically understates. The correct response is to calculate yield for every trimmed or cooked ingredient, to cost the dish on the edible portion cost, and to update the yield when suppliers or specs change.

### Including only the center-of-plate in the cost

This is a trap because the protein is the largest and most visible cost, so the costing focuses on it and omits the garnish, sauce, starch, and finishing components, which understates the plate cost and overstates the margin, especially for dishes with expensive sauces or garnishes. The mechanism is that the small components each contribute a small cost, but summed across the plate and across the volume of service they represent a significant cost that is invisible when only the protein is costed, so the dish appears more profitable than it is. The false signal is that the protein cost is calculated, which reads as the cost known. The harm is dishes that appear profitable but are margin-thin or unprofitable because the uncosted components consume the margin, a loss that accumulates across volume and is discovered only at period end. The correct response is to include every component at its served quantity in the plate cost, to cost sauces and garnishes as deliberately as the protein, and to recognize that the small components are where margin quietly leaks.

### Pricing by matching the competition instead of from the documented cost

This trap exists because competitive pricing feels safe and market-driven, but it ignores the dish's actual cost, so a dish priced to match a competitor may be profitable or may lose money depending on whether the competitor's cost structure matches, and the kitchen has priced blind to its own economics. The mechanism is that competitors may have different ingredient costs, portion sizes, and labor structures, so matching their price does not guarantee the same margin, and a dish priced to the market may have a food cost percentage far above target, eroding profitability while appearing competitive. The false signal is that the price is in line with the market, which reads as appropriate. The harm is dishes that are priced competitively but unprofitably because the cost was never the basis for the price, and the loss is invisible because the competitive price feels correct. The correct response is to price from the documented cost to the target food cost percentage, to use competitive pricing only as a ceiling check, and to treat a competitive price that falls below the cost-based floor as a signal to reformulate the dish, not to accept a loss.

### Keeping a popular low-margin dish because it sells well

This trap operates because a popular dish feels successful by volume, so the kitchen keeps it, but if its margin per plate is thin, its contribution to profit is small despite the volume, and it occupies menu space and kitchen capacity that a more profitable dish could use, so the popularity masks the poor economics. The mechanism is that a high-volume, low-margin dish generates revenue but little profit, and it consumes ingredients, labor, and station capacity that could produce more profit if allocated to a higher-margin dish, so the opportunity cost of keeping it is the profit foregone from the better use of that capacity. The false signal is the high sales number, which reads as success. The harm is reduced overall profitability because the kitchen's capacity is tied up in a dish that generates volume but not profit, and the dish is never reformedulated or replaced because its popularity protects it from scrutiny. The correct response is to evaluate every dish on the popularity-profitability matrix, to reformulate or reprice low-margin high-popularity dishes to improve margin, and to be willing to replace a popular dish that cannot be made profitable, recognizing that volume without margin is opportunity cost.

### Failing to recalculate when ingredient prices change

This is a trap because ingredient prices fluctuate, especially proteins and seasonal produce, and a dish that was profitable at last quarter's prices may be unprofitable at current prices, but the price is not adjusted because the cost was not recalculated, so the dish silently loses money until the period-end review. The mechanism is that key ingredient prices rise over a quarter, the plate cost increases, but the menu price stays fixed because no one recalculated, so the food cost percentage rises above target and the margin erodes on every plate sold during the period. The false signal is that the dish was profitable when priced, which reads as still profitable. The harm is margin erosion across the menu as costs rise and prices stay fixed, a loss that accumulates across volume and is discovered only at period end, by which time significant profit has been foregone. The correct response is to monitor key ingredient prices, to recalculate affected dish costs when prices move significantly, and to adjust prices within the floor and ceiling to maintain the target margin.

### Judging dishes on food cost percentage alone without considering labor

This is a trap because food cost percentage is the most visible metric and is easy to calculate, so dishes are judged on it alone, but a dish with a low food cost percentage that requires extensive hand-assembly may be less profitable than a dish with a higher food cost percentage that is fast to produce, because the labor cost of the low-percentage dish consumes the margin it appears to save. The mechanism is that labor is a significant cost, and a dish that takes extra minutes per plate accumulates labor cost that the food cost percentage does not capture, so a dish that looks profitable on food cost percentage may be unprofitable on total cost when labor is included. The false signal is the low food cost percentage, which reads as profitable. The harm is dishes that are retained and promoted because they look profitable on food cost percentage but are actually margin-thin or unprofitable when labor is considered, tying up capacity in dishes that contribute less than they appear to. The correct response is to evaluate dishes on contribution margin, price minus food cost, and on the labor required per plate, and to treat food cost percentage as one signal among several, not as the complete profitability picture.

## Self-Check

- Did I calculate the food cost from a documented costing sheet with current ingredient prices, rather than from memory?
- Did I account for yield and trim, costing the dish on edible portion cost rather than as-purchased weight?
- Did I include every plate component, garnish, sauce, starch, and finishing, in the cost at served quantity?
- Did I price from the documented cost to a target food cost percentage, with a floor and a ceiling, using competitive pricing only as a check?
- Did I evaluate menu items on the popularity-profitability matrix, and manage each quadrant differently?
- Did I monitor key ingredient prices and recalculate affected dish costs when prices moved significantly?
- Did I evaluate dishes on contribution margin and labor required, not on food cost percentage alone?
