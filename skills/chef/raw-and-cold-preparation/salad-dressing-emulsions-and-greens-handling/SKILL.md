# Salad Dressing Emulsions and Greens Handling

A great salad depends on two technical foundations — a stable, balanced vinaigrette and properly washed, dried greens — and the judgment problem is that both are often executed casually: a vinaigrette whisked without an emulsifier breaks into greasy pools, and greens dressed while wet repel the oil-based dressing and wilt. The recurring failure is a broken vinaigrette (the oil and acid separated, producing a greasy mouthfeel) or wet greens (the water dilutes the dressing and prevents adhesion, and the greens wilt faster). The discipline is to balance the vinaigrette with an emulsifier, to wash and thoroughly dry the greens, and to dress the salad at the last minute.

The safety layer is that salads are raw produce (which can carry pathogens and pesticides, requiring washing), and the dressings often contain raw egg (mayonnaise, Caesar), which is a Salmonella risk. The agent's job is to handle the greens and the dressings with produce-washing and egg-safety discipline.

## Core Rules

### Balance The Vinaigrette: Oil, Acid, Emulsifier, Seasoning

A balanced vinaigrette has components with functions: oil (the base, for body and richness, typically 3 parts to 1 part acid), acid (vinegar, lemon juice, for brightness and to cut the oil), an emulsifier (mustard, honey, egg yolk, mayonnaise, mashed garlic — to bind the oil and the acid into a stable emulsion), and seasoning (salt, pepper, herbs). The classic ratio is 3 parts oil to 1 part acid, but this varies with the acid's strength and the dressing's purpose (a lemon vinaigrette may be 2:1 for brightness). The judgment point is that the balance and the emulsifier are what make the vinaigrette pleasant (coating, glossy, integrated) rather than greasy (separated oil). Taste the vinaigrette and adjust the balance before dressing the salad.

### Emulsify The Vinaigrette For Stability

The emulsion (the oil and the acid bound into a stable, glossy mixture) is achieved by whisking the acid with the emulsifier first, then drizzling in the oil while whisking vigorously (or blending with an immersion blender for a very stable emulsion). The emulsifier (mustard is the classic) coats the oil droplets and prevents them from coalescing and separating. The judgment point is that the emulsion is the technique that gives the vinaigrette its stability and its pleasant mouthfeel, and a vinaigrette without an emulsifier (or whisked incorrectly) breaks into greasy pools. Whisk the acid and the emulsifier, drizzle the oil slowly while whisking, and the vinaigrette will emulsify and hold.

### Wash And Thoroughly Dry The Greens

Greens must be washed (to remove soil, grit, insects, and pesticide residue) and thoroughly dried (water repels the oil-based dressing, dilutes the flavor, and causes the greens to wilt faster). Wash the greens in cold water (a deep sink or a salad spinner), lifting them out of the water (leaving the grit behind), and dry them in a salad spinner (the most effective method) or on clean towels. The judgment point is that the drying is as important as the washing, and wet greens produce a diluted, poorly dressed salad that wilts quickly. Spin the greens thoroughly, and if needed, roll them in a towel to absorb the residual moisture. Store the dried greens (refrigerated, in a bag or a container with a towel) until dressing.

### Dress The Greens At The Last Minute

The dressed salad degrades quickly: the acid in the dressing breaks down the greens' cell walls (causing wilting), and the salt draws out moisture (causing the salad to weep). The judgment point is that the salad should be dressed at the last minute (just before serving), and holding a dressed salad (even for 15 minutes) produces a wilted, weepy result. Dress the greens in a large bowl (toss to coat evenly with a small amount of dressing — overdressing is a common flaw), taste and adjust, and plate immediately. For a buffet or a held service, dress the salad in batches as needed, and hold the greens and the dressing separately until the last minute.

### Match The Dressing To The Greens

Different greens suit different dressings: delicate greens (mesclun, butter lettuce, mâche) for a light vinaigrette (a lemon or a mild vinegar vinaigrette, not overpowering); robust greens (arugula, radicchio, kale) for a bolder dressing (a garlic vinaigrette, a creamy dressing, a blue cheese); hearty greens (romaine, iceberg) for a creamy dressing (ranch, blue cheese, Caesar). The judgment point is that the dressing's intensity should match the green's flavor and texture, and a delicate green with a heavy dressing is overpowered (while a robust green with a light dressing is underdressed). Match the dressing to the green, and consider the salad's other components (nuts, cheese, fruit, protein) for balance.

### Handle Egg-Based Dressings With Safety Discipline

Dressings with raw egg (Caesar, mayonnaise-based dressings) are a Salmonella risk, and the egg must be handled safely: use pasteurized eggs or egg yolks (the safest option, especially for vulnerable guests), or coddle the egg (briefly boil, to reduce the surface bacteria), and hold the dressing refrigerated. The judgment point is that raw egg is a pathogen risk, and the dressing must be made with pasteurized eggs (or an equivalent safety step) and held cold. For a Caesar or a mayonnaise dressing served to vulnerable guests (pregnant, elderly, immunocompromised), pasteurized eggs are essential. Hold the dressing refrigerated, and discard it after a limited time.

## Common Traps

### Making A Vinaigrette Without An Emulsifier And Having It Break

A cook whiskes oil and vinegar together without an emulsifier, and the vinaigrette separates into greasy pools. The mechanism is that oil and acid do not naturally combine, and without an emulsifier (mustard, honey, egg yolk) to coat the oil droplets and prevent coalescence, they separate; the broken vinaigrette has a greasy mouthfeel. The false signal is that the vinaigrette "combined while whisking," which reads as emulsified. The harm is a greasy, separated dressing. The defense is to use an emulsifier (mustard is classic), to whisk the acid and the emulsifier first, then drizzle the oil while whisking, and to recognize the emulsion as the stability technique.

### Dressing Wet Greens And Diluting The Dressing

A cook dresses the greens straight from washing without drying, and the dressing slides off and the salad is diluted and wet. The mechanism is that the water on the greens' surface repels the oil-based dressing (oil and water do not mix), and the dressing slides off, leaving the greens underdressed; the residual water also dilutes the dressing and causes faster wilting. The false signal is that the greens "are washed," which reads as ready. The harm is a diluted, poorly dressed, fast-wilting salad. The defense is to dry the greens thoroughly (salad spinner, towels), to dress only dried greens, and to recognize the drying as a critical step.

### Dressing The Salad Early And Letting It Wilt

A cook dresses the salad 30 minutes before service "to let the flavors meld," and the greens are wilted and weepy. The mechanism is that the dressing's acid and salt break down the greens' cell walls and draw out moisture, and a dressed salad held for 30 minutes is wilted and watery; the salad must be dressed at the last minute. The false signal is that "the flavors are melding," which reads as beneficial. The harm is a wilted, unappealing salad. The defense is to dress the salad just before serving, to hold the greens and the dressing separately until the last minute, and to dress in batches for a held service.

### Overdressing The Greens

A cook pours the vinaigrette over the greens and tosses, using too much, and the salad is swimming and greasy. The mechanism is that the greens need only a light coating of dressing (enough to glisten, not to pool), and overdressing produces a heavy, greasy salad that masks the greens' flavor; the dressing should be added gradually and tossed to assess. The false signal is that "more dressing is more flavor," which reads as generous. The harm is a heavy, greasy salad. The defense is to dress gradually (add a small amount, toss, taste, adjust), to use less than seems necessary (the greens need only a light coating), and to recognize overdressing as a common flaw.

### Using Raw Egg In A Caesar Without Pasteurization

A cook makes a Caesar dressing with raw egg yolk, serves it to a pregnant guest, and the guest is exposed to Salmonella. The mechanism is that raw egg carries Salmonella risk, and the dressing transmits it directly (no cook step); vulnerable guests (pregnant, elderly, immunocompromised) are at higher risk. The false signal is that "Caesar has raw egg traditionally," which reads as acceptable. The harm is potential Salmonella illness. The defense is to use pasteurized eggs or yolks for raw-egg dressings (especially for vulnerable guests), to coddle the egg as a partial reduction, and to hold the dressing refrigerated.

### Storing Greens In A Sealed Bag With Moisture And Rotting Them

A cook stores the washed greens in a sealed plastic bag with residual moisture, and they rot quickly. The mechanism is that the residual moisture and the sealed environment promote decay (bacterial and fungal growth), and the greens rot; the greens should be dried, stored with a towel to absorb moisture, and in a container that allows some breathability. The false signal is that "the bag keeps them fresh," which reads as correct storage. The harm is spoiled greens. The defense is to dry the greens thoroughly, to store them with a towel (to absorb moisture) in a bag or container (with some air), and to use them within a few days.

## Self-Check

- Is the vinaigrette balanced (oil, acid, emulsifier, seasoning), with the oil-to-acid ratio (typically 3:1) adjusted for the acid's strength and the dressing's purpose?
- Is the vinaigrette emulsified (acid and emulsifier whisked first, oil drizzled while whisking, or blended), so it is stable and glossy, not separated?
- Are the greens washed (in cold water, lifted out) and thoroughly dried (salad spinner, towels), with no residual moisture?
- Is the salad dressed at the last minute (just before serving), in a large bowl, with a small amount of dressing tossed to coat evenly?
- Is the dressing matched to the greens (light for delicate, bold for robust, creamy for hearty), and balanced with the salad's other components?
- Are egg-based dressings made with pasteurized eggs (or an equivalent safety step), held refrigerated, and discarded after a limited time?
- Are the greens stored dried, with a towel, in a container with some air, and used within a few days?
- Is the reasoning documented so the vinaigrette balance, emulsion, greens washing and drying, dressing timing, dressing-to-green match, egg safety, and storage for each salad are verifiable?
