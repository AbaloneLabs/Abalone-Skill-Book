# Kitchen Equipment Selection and Specification

Equipment selection is where menu, volume, budget, and operations converge—and where short-term thinking creates long-term pain. The wrong equipment constrains the menu, slows service, breaks under load, or costs a fortune to run and repair. The right equipment disappears into the workflow, doing its job reliably for years. The decisions are technical and consequential: capacity matched to peak demand, energy source matched to the kitchen's infrastructure and the cuisine, durability matched to the duty cycle, and serviceability matched to the reality that everything breaks eventually. A chef who specifies equipment on price or brand familiarity alone is mortgaging years of service to save a week of deliberation.

This skill covers the selection and specification of kitchen equipment: matching capacity to demand, choosing energy source, balancing capital and lifecycle cost, and specifying the installation requirements that determine whether the equipment can actually be used.

This guidance addresses equipment selection judgment. It does not replace local building, fire, gas, electrical, and health codes; equipment manufacturers' specifications; or licensed trades. Coordinate specifications with engineers, contractors, and code officials, and verify utility capacity and installation requirements before purchase.

## Core Rules

### Match capacity to peak demand, not average or hoped-for volume

Equipment must handle the peak: the busiest service, the largest batch, the simultaneous demand from multiple stations. Size ovens, fryers, ranges, combi ovens, and refrigeration to peak demand with realistic recovery time, not to average covers. Undersized equipment bottlenecks peak service and never recovers; oversized equipment wastes capital, energy, and space. Calculate capacity from the menu's cookery methods, batch sizes, and timing, not from generic rules of thumb.

### Choose energy source deliberately: gas, electric, induction

Each energy source has trade-offs. Gas offers responsive heat and lower operating cost in many markets but requires ventilation, gas supply, and combustion safety. Electric is flexible and cleaner at the point of use but may strain electrical capacity and cost more to run. Induction offers efficiency, speed, safety, and a cooler kitchen but requires compatible cookware and adequate electrical capacity. Match the source to the cuisine, the kitchen's infrastructure, the climate, and the operating cost structure, not to habit.

### Balance capital cost against durability, serviceability, and lifecycle cost

First cost is a fraction of lifecycle cost. A cheaper piece of equipment that fails often, is hard to service, or consumes more energy can cost more over its life than a premium unit. Evaluate durability (build quality, materials, duty rating), serviceability (are parts and qualified technicians available locally?), energy efficiency, and warranty. For high-duty equipment, premium construction pays back; for low-duty or occasional use, it may not. Match the investment to the duty cycle.

### Specify utilities, clearances, and installation before purchase

Equipment that cannot be installed—because the gas line is the wrong size, the electrical circuit is inadequate, the ventilation hood does not cover it, or the clearances to combustibles are not met—is an expensive mistake discovered at installation. Specify and verify utility requirements (gas pressure and volume, electrical voltage/phase/amperage, water supply and drainage, ventilation), required clearances, and floor and wall preparation before purchase. Coordinate with the trades who will install it.

### Consider the service and parts reality

Every piece of equipment will eventually need service. Before selecting, confirm that qualified service technicians and parts are available in your area for the brand and model. Premium equipment with no local service support can sit idle for weeks waiting for a part or a technician. The service network is as important as the equipment specification.

### Plan for redundancy and failure for critical equipment

Identify the equipment whose failure would halt service—the primary cooking line, the primary refrigeration, the combi oven—and plan redundancy or contingency. This may mean a backup unit, a shared resource, or a menu contingency. Total dependence on a single critical piece of equipment with no backup is an operational risk.

## Common Traps

### Sizing to average rather than peak

Equipment sized to average covers fails at peak and never catches up. Size to peak demand with realistic recovery time.

### Choosing energy source by habit

Defaulting to gas or electric without considering infrastructure, cuisine, operating cost, and the cooler, safer profile of induction misses opportunities and creates constraints.

### Optimizing for first cost alone

The cheapest equipment often costs more over its life through failure, inefficiency, and service difficulty. Evaluate lifecycle cost, not only purchase price.

### Forgetting installation requirements

Utility capacity, clearances, ventilation, and floor preparation discovered at installation are expensive to fix. Verify before purchase.

### Ignoring the service network

Premium equipment without local parts and service support can be down for weeks. Confirm service availability before selecting.

### No redundancy for critical equipment

A single point of failure on the cooking line or refrigeration can halt service. Plan redundancy or contingency for critical equipment.

## Self-Check

- Have I sized each piece of equipment to peak demand with realistic recovery time, based on the menu's cookery methods and timing?
- Have I chosen the energy source deliberately, considering infrastructure, cuisine, operating cost, and the induction option?
- Am I balancing capital cost against durability, serviceability, energy efficiency, and warranty, matched to the duty cycle?
- Have I specified and verified utility requirements, clearances, ventilation, and installation needs before purchase?
- Have I confirmed that qualified service technicians and parts are available locally for the selected brand and model?
- Have I identified critical equipment and planned redundancy or contingency for its failure?
- Have I coordinated specifications with engineers, contractors, and code officials, and verified code compliance?
