# Kitchen Ergonomics, Safety, and Ventilation Design

Kitchens are among the most physically punishing work environments: sustained heat, noise, humidity, wet and slippery floors, repetitive motion, heavy lifting, sharp tools, open flame, and long hours on hard surfaces. Over a career, these conditions produce cumulative injury—burns, cuts, musculoskeletal damage, hearing loss, heat illness—and they drive staff turnover. A kitchen designed without regard for ergonomics and safety extracts this cost from the people who work in it, every shift, for years. Designing for the human body and for a survivable environment is not a luxury; it is a core determinant of staff retention, consistency, and legal compliance. Ventilation is the single most consequential environmental system: too little and the kitchen becomes unbearable and unsafe; wrong and it fails to capture grease, smoke, and heat.

This skill covers the design of kitchen ergonomics, safety, and ventilation: workstation height and reach, movement and lifting, heat and noise control, slip and fire safety, surface materials, and ventilation system design.

This guidance addresses design judgment. It does not replace local occupational health and safety regulations, building and fire codes, mechanical engineering standards, or the specifications of licensed design professionals. Coordinate ventilation and fire suppression design with qualified engineers and code officials.

## Core Rules

### Design workstations to the body, not to a standard counter height

Sustained work at the wrong height produces back, shoulder, and neck injury. Size workstation height to the task and the crew (prep surfaces lower for knife work, cooking surfaces at a comfortable reach), and provide anti-fatigue matting at standing stations. Place frequently used items within the primary reach zone to reduce repetitive stretching. Adjustability, where feasible, accommodates a diverse crew. The cost of ergonomic design is trivial compared to the cost of injury and turnover.

### Engineer heat, smoke, and grease capture at the source

Cooking generates heat, steam, smoke, and grease-laden vapor. The ventilation system—hood, exhaust, makeup air, and filtration—must capture these at the source before they enter the workspace. Undersized or poorly placed hoods allow heat and grease to escape into the kitchen, raising temperature, coating surfaces, and creating fire and air quality hazards. Size hoods to the cooking load and equipment footprint, match exhaust to makeup air, and specify grease filtration appropriate to the cooking type. Ventilation is not an afterthought; it is the dominant environmental system.

### Match makeup air to exhaust, or the system fails

Exhaust without balanced makeup air creates negative pressure that pulls conditioned air from the dining room, hampers hood capture, and makes doors hard to operate. Makeup air must be matched in volume, tempered (heated or cooled) as required, and distributed to avoid drafts. A ventilation system with unbalanced makeup air underperforms regardless of hood quality. Engage a mechanical engineer to balance the system.

### Control slips, the most common kitchen injury

Wet, greasy floors drive the most frequent kitchen injuries: slips and falls. Specify flooring with high slip resistance even when wet and greasy (e.g., quarry tile with proper grout, or commercial anti-slip surfaces, not polished finishes), slope floors to drains in wet areas, maintain the flooring (greasy tile must be cleaned with degreaser, not just water), and require slip-resistant footwear. Coving at floor-wall junctions enables sanitation. Flooring is a safety and sanitation decision, not only an aesthetic one.

### Design for fire safety as a system

Fire safety in a kitchen is a system: hood and duct construction, grease filtration, automatic fire suppression (typically wet chemical over hoods and ducts), manual extinguishers and fire blankets, gas and electrical shutoff interlocks, clear egress, and trained staff. Each element must be specified to code and to the cooking load, and the system must be inspected and maintained. Do not treat fire suppression as a checkbox; a poorly maintained or undersized system fails when needed.

### Manage noise to protect hearing and communication

Commercial kitchens are loud: fans, dishwashers, blenders, and music combine to levels that risk hearing damage and impair communication. Specify quieter equipment where feasible, isolate noisy equipment (e.g., remote dish room, isolated blender stations), absorb sound with finishes where possible, and provide hearing protection for sustained exposure. Balance the desire for kitchen energy and communication against the cumulative hearing risk.

## Common Traps

### Treating ventilation as an afterthought

Ventilation is the dominant environmental system. Undersized or unbalanced ventilation makes the kitchen unbearable, unsafe, and non-compliant, and no amount of equipment or skill compensates.

### Standard-height workstations for all tasks and all crew

Sustained work at wrong heights causes cumulative injury. Size stations to the task and crew, and provide anti-fatigue matting.

### Polished or inadequately maintained flooring

Slippery floors are the most common injury source. Specify slip-resistant flooring, slope to drains, and maintain with degreaser. Require slip-resistant footwear.

### Unbalanced makeup air

Exhaust without matched makeup air creates negative pressure, impairs capture, and wastes conditioned air. Balance the system with a mechanical engineer.

### Fire suppression as a checkbox

An undersized or unmaintained fire suppression system fails when needed. Specify to the cooking load, interlock shutoffs, and inspect and maintain.

### Ignoring cumulative noise exposure

Sustained loud environments risk hearing damage and impair communication. Specify quieter equipment, isolate noise sources, and provide hearing protection.

## Self-Check

- Are workstation heights sized to the task and crew, with frequently used items in the primary reach zone and anti-fatigue matting at standing stations?
- Is the ventilation system—hood, exhaust, makeup air, filtration—sized to the cooking load and equipment footprint, capturing heat and grease at the source?
- Is makeup air matched in volume, tempered, and distributed, with the system balanced by a mechanical engineer?
- Is flooring slip-resistant even when wet and greasy, sloped to drains, coved at junctions, and maintained with degreaser, with slip-resistant footwear required?
- Is fire suppression specified to the cooking load, with hood and duct construction, grease filtration, automatic suppression, shutoff interlocks, clear egress, and a maintenance and inspection plan?
- Has noise been addressed through quieter equipment, isolation, absorption, and hearing protection?
- Have I coordinated ergonomics, safety, and ventilation design with qualified engineers and code officials, and verified code compliance?
