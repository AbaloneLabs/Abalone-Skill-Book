# Recipe Costing and Yield Analysis

The price of an ingredient is not the cost of the dish. Between the as-purchased price and the plate that reaches the guest lies a cascade of transformations: trimming, butchering, peeling, cooking loss, breakage, and plate waste. A chef who prices a dish on invoice price without accounting for yield overstates margin and misleads the business. Accurate recipe costing requires yield analysis: understanding how much of what you buy actually becomes what you serve, and what is lost along the way. This is the foundation of food cost management—without it, menu pricing, portion decisions, and waste reduction all rest on false numbers. The chef who masters yield analysis knows the true cost of every plate and can defend pricing, spot waste, and protect margin with evidence.

This skill covers the costing and yield judgments: building accurate recipe cost cards, performing yield analysis, accounting for trim and shrinkage, and determining true plate cost.

This guidance addresses costing judgment. It does not replace the operation's accounting system, inventory valuation method, or institutional financial controls. Coordinate costing with ownership and accounting, and reconcile to inventory and purchasing records.

## Core Rules

### Cost from the as-served yield, not the as-purchased price

The invoice price is for the as-purchased (AP) quantity; the dish uses the as-served (AS) quantity after trimming, butchering, peeling, and cooking. Convert AP cost to AS cost using the yield percentage: AS cost = AP cost / yield %. A fillet purchased whole and portioned in-house has a yield determined by the cutting test; a vegetable trimmed and peeled has a yield determined by the trim test. Costing from AP price without the yield conversion understates the true cost of the served portion and overstates margin.

### Perform and document cutting tests for proteins

Proteins are the highest-cost and highest-variability ingredients, and their yield depends on the cut, the butcher's skill, and the specification. Perform a cutting test: buy a representative unit, break it down, weigh every component (primary use, usable trim, fat, bone, waste), and calculate the yield and the true cost of the primary portion. Document the test and repeat it when suppliers, specs, or cuts change. A cutting test reveals that a "cheaper" cut with low yield may cost more per served portion than a pricier cut with high yield.

### Account for cooking loss and shrinkage

Cooking changes weight and volume: proteins shrink, vegetables lose water, baked goods lose moisture. The raw portion cost is not the cooked portion cost. Account for cooking loss by testing the yield from raw to cooked, and cost the cooked portion that reaches the plate. For batch-cooked items (stocks, braises, roasts), the yield includes evaporation and trim. Ignoring cooking loss understates cost, especially for high-shrinkage proteins.

### Build complete recipe cost cards, including all components

A recipe cost card must include every component on the plate: the protein, the starch, the vegetable, the sauce, the garnish, the finishing oil or salt, and any accompaniment. Omitting "minor" components—the sauce, the garnish, the finishing butter—understates cost, and these components often sum to a material fraction of plate cost. Include the cost of each component at its AS yield cost, sum to plate cost, and update the card when recipes, portions, or prices change.

### Distinguish edible portion from plate waste

Even the served portion is not all consumed; plate waste—the food left on the plate—is a real cost that yields no guest value. Track plate waste for dishes with high returns, and adjust portions or presentation to reduce it. A portion sized larger than guests want to eat wastes cost and diminishes the experience. Right-sizing portions to guest consumption protects both margin and satisfaction.

### Reconcile costing to inventory and purchasing

Recipe costing is a model; inventory and purchasing are reality. Periodically reconcile the modeled food cost (plate cost x covers) to the actual food cost (purchases adjusted for inventory change). Persistent gaps indicate unaccounted waste, theft, over-portioning, or costing errors. Use the reconciliation to find and fix the gap between the model and reality.

## Common Traps

### Costing from as-purchased price without yield conversion

This is the most common costing error and understates true cost. Convert AP to AS using the yield percentage.

### Skipping cutting tests for proteins

Proteins are too costly and variable to cost without a cutting test. A "cheaper" cut with low yield may cost more per portion.

### Ignoring cooking loss

Raw and cooked portions cost differently. Account for shrinkage in cooking, especially for proteins.

### Omitting minor components from the cost card

Sauces, garnishes, and finishing ingredients sum to a material fraction of plate cost. Include every component.

### Portioning larger than guests consume

Plate waste is real cost with no guest value. Right-size portions to consumption.

### Never reconciling the model to reality

A costing model untested against actual food cost hides waste, theft, and errors. Reconcile periodically.

## Self-Check

- Am I costing each component from its as-served yield, converting AP to AS using the yield percentage?
- Have I performed and documented cutting tests for proteins, and repeated them when suppliers, specs, or cuts change?
- Have I accounted for cooking loss and shrinkage in costing cooked portions?
- Does each recipe cost card include every plate component—protein, starch, vegetable, sauce, garnish, finishing—at AS yield cost?
- Have I tracked plate waste and right-sized portions to guest consumption?
- Do I periodically reconcile modeled food cost (plate cost x covers) to actual food cost (purchases adjusted for inventory)?
- Are cost cards updated when recipes, portions, or prices change?
- Have I coordinated costing with accounting and reconciled to inventory and purchasing records?
