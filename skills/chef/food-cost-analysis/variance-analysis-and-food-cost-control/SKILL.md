# Variance Analysis and Food Cost Control

Theoretical food cost is what the menu should cost; actual food cost is what the business pays. The gap between them—variance—is where money leaks out of a restaurant, quietly and continuously, through over-portioning, waste, spoilage, theft, over-ordering, and unrecorded comps. A chef who reads only the period-end food cost percentage sees the leak too late and too aggregated to fix. Variance analysis is the practice of reconciling actual to theoretical, diagnosing the drivers of the gap, and implementing controls that close it. Done well, it finds the specific, fixable causes of cost overrun—far more actionable than a vague directive to "control food cost." A restaurant that does not analyze variance is managing food cost by hope.

This skill covers the analysis and control of food cost variance: reconciling actual to theoretical, diagnosing drivers, and implementing controls.

This guidance addresses variance analysis judgment. It does not replace the operation's accounting system, inventory method, or institutional financial controls. Coordinate analysis with ownership and accounting, and investigate material variances promptly.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile actual to theoretical food cost every period

Theoretical food cost is the sum of (plate cost x covers sold), from the recipe cost cards. Actual food cost is purchases adjusted for inventory change (beginning inventory + purchases - ending inventory). The variance is the gap. Calculate both every period—weekly is far more actionable than monthly—and track the variance over time. A variance that is within tolerance is fine; a variance that spikes or trends up demands investigation. Without the reconciliation, food cost is managed by the period-end percentage, which hides the causes.

### Diagnose variance by category, not in aggregate

An aggregated variance ("food cost is 2% over") is unactionable. Decompose the variance into categories: over-portioning (portions served larger than cost card), waste (trim, burn, drop, expire), spoilage (out-of-date, mishandled), theft or unrecorded consumption, over-ordering (purchases exceeding use), and comps and staff meals not recorded. Each category has a different fix. Investigate the largest categories first, with evidence (waste logs, portion checks, inventory counts, comp records).

### Use waste logs to quantify and target waste

Waste is often the largest and least visible variance driver. Implement a waste log that records every discarded item, the reason, the quantity, and the cost. Patterns emerge: repeated spoilage of an over-ordered item, repeated burning of a tricky dish, repeated trim waste that could be repurposed. Quantify the cost of the waste and target the highest-cost patterns. A waste log converts invisible leakage into visible, fixable data.

### Verify portioning against cost cards

Over-portioning is a common and insidious variance driver, because it happens a little at a time, every plate. Verify portions against the cost cards: weigh portions on the line periodically, check that scoops and ladles match the specified sizes, and confirm that cooks are not "being generous" in ways that erode margin. Portion drift is natural; periodic verification brings it back. A line cook using a 6-oz ladle where a 5-oz is specified raises food cost silently.

### Investigate inventory shrinkage

Inventory shrinkage—ending inventory lower than beginning plus purchases minus use—signals waste, spoilage, theft, or recording error. Count inventory accurately and consistently (same method, same staff, same timing), and investigate material shrinkage. Shrinkage that cannot be explained by waste or spoilage may indicate theft or unrecorded consumption, which requires process controls, not just exhortation.

### Close the loop with targeted controls

Diagnosis without action is pointless. For each variance driver identified, implement a targeted control: adjust pars to reduce over-ordering, retrain on portioning, repurpose trim, tighten receiving inspection, record comps and staff meals consistently, or add process controls for shrinkage. Verify that the control reduces the variance in the next period. Variance analysis is a closed loop: measure, diagnose, control, verify.

## Common Traps

### Managing by period-end food cost percentage alone

The aggregate percentage hides the causes. Reconcile actual to theoretical and decompose the variance.

### Investigating variance in aggregate

"Food cost is over" is unactionable. Decompose by category and investigate the largest with evidence.

### Ignoring waste because it's invisible

Waste is often the largest variance driver and the least visible. Use a waste log to quantify and target it.

### Assuming portions match the cost cards

Portion drift is natural and erodes margin silently. Verify portions periodically against cost cards.

### Counting inventory inconsistently

Inconsistent counts obscure shrinkage. Count with the same method, staff, and timing, and investigate material shrinkage.

### Diagnosing without implementing and verifying controls

Diagnosis without action is pointless. Implement targeted controls and verify they reduce the variance.

## Self-Check

- Do I reconcile actual to theoretical food cost every period (preferably weekly), and track the variance over time?
- Do I decompose the variance by category (portioning, waste, spoilage, theft, over-ordering, comps) and investigate the largest with evidence?
- Is there a waste log recording item, reason, quantity, and cost, and do I target the highest-cost waste patterns?
- Do I verify portions against cost cards periodically, checking ladles, scoops, and line portions?
- Is inventory counted accurately and consistently, and is material shrinkage investigated?
- For each variance driver, have I implemented a targeted control and verified it reduces the variance in the next period?
- Are comps, staff meals, and other non-revenue consumption recorded consistently?
- Have I coordinated the analysis with accounting and investigated material variances promptly?
