# Literary Device And Figurative Language

Literary devices are not ornamental flourishes an author added on top of the meaning; in serious literature they often are the meaning. A metaphor establishes how a character sees the world. A recurring image carries theme. A pattern of sound gives a sentence its weight and memorability. When a translator renders the literal sense but strips or mangles the device, the translation may be "accurate" in a dictionary sense while being profoundly unfaithful to the work, because what made the passage literature has been deleted. The difficulty is that figurative and sonic devices are built out of the specific resources of one language, its sound system, its idioms, its cultural associations, and they rarely transfer by direct equivalence. A metaphor that is vivid in one language may be dead, comic, or offensive in another. A rhyme that structures a line may have no target equivalent. The translator's task is to recognize the device, understand the function it performs, and rebuild that function in target-language resources, while knowing when a device genuinely cannot be rebuilt and must be flagged or compensated elsewhere. The harm this prevents is the production of literary translations that read like competent paraphrases of plot with all the art sanded off.

Use this skill when translating poetry, literary prose, or any text where metaphor, imagery, sound patterning, wordplay, or rhetorical figures are doing deliberate artistic work. The goal is to carry the function and effect of each device into the target, or to compensate honestly when direct transfer is impossible.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Device And Its Function Before Rendering

Every figurative or sonic element should be diagnosed before it is translated. A metaphor may be doing one of several jobs: establishing atmosphere, revealing character perception, carrying theme, creating shock, or simply pleasing the ear. The same surface device can serve different functions in different passages, and the function determines the strategy.

Ask what the device achieves in its context. A simile that compares a character's hands to birds may be telling us about delicacy, about nervous movement, or about the character's own way of seeing the world through nature. If you know the function, you can judge whether a target rendering carries it. If you translate the device without understanding its job, you risk producing an image that is literally equivalent but functionally inert. Build the habit of naming the device and its purpose for any passage where artistry is present, because devices that go unnamed go untranslated in any meaningful sense.

### Transfer Function Over Literal Vehicle

The vehicle of a metaphor, the concrete image used to carry the comparison, often cannot transfer directly because its associations are culture-bound. What must transfer is the tenor, the underlying meaning, and the effect on the reader.

A source metaphor drawn from a sport, a folk practice, or a landscape unknown to the target reader may carry the right feeling but the wrong reference. Options include finding a target-culture vehicle with equivalent associations, retaining the source vehicle when its strangeness is itself valuable, or restructuring into non-figurative language when no equivalent exists. The choice depends on the function. If the metaphor's job is to evoke a specific source-culture world, retain it and let the foreignness work. If its job is purely to convey speed or delicacy, a target-culture vehicle may serve better. Never assume the literal vehicle is sacred; never assume it is disposable. Each case turns on what the device is doing.

### Recreate Sound Devices In Target Resources

Alliteration, assonance, consonance, onomatopoeia, and rhyme are built from the sound system of the source language and almost never survive literal transfer. They must be recreated, not translated.

This requires working at the level of target-language phonetics. If a source line uses sibilance to evoke stealth or sea, find target words whose sounds perform the same evocation, even if they are different words. If a line rhymes and the rhyme is structurally important, as in poetry or in prose that quotes verse, build a target rhyme, accepting that the rhyming words will differ. Where exact recreation is impossible, compensate by introducing sound patterning elsewhere in the passage so the sonic density is roughly preserved. A translation that silently drops all sound devices produces text that is meaning-correct but aesthetically impoverished, and for poetry that is a failure of the translation, not a limitation.

### Handle Wordplay And Ambiguity Deliberately

Puns, double meanings, and deliberate ambiguity exploit features specific to one language and are among the hardest devices to carry. The translator must make a conscious decision rather than letting the wordplay vanish.

First determine whether the wordplay is load-bearing. A pun that is central to a scene's meaning or a character's wit demands more effort than a throwaway flourish. Strategies include finding a target-language pun on a related meaning, relocating the wordplay to a nearby point where the target language permits it, or, where the pun is structurally essential and untransferable, adding a brief translator's note. For deliberate ambiguity, where a sentence is meant to sustain two readings, seek target syntax that sustains both if possible; where impossible, choose the reading the context favors and note the loss. Never silently collapse a deliberate double meaning into a single reading without recognizing that you have reduced the text.

### Preserve Symbolism And Recurring Imagery Consistently

Symbols and recurring images accumulate meaning across a work. A color, an object, or a natural image that returns chapter after chapter builds thematic resonance, and its rendering must be consistent or the resonance breaks.

Identify symbolic and recurring images early and fix their target renderings. If a novel returns to the image of water as death, the water vocabulary must be handled so the recurrence is recognizable; scattering different synonyms across chapters dissolves the symbol. Watch for images that are symbolic in the source culture but carry different symbolism in the target culture, because a color or animal that signifies mourning in one tradition may signify joy in another, and a literal transfer can invert the theme. Where source and target symbolism conflict, decide whether to retain the source symbol with its foreign associations or adapt to a target symbol, and apply the decision consistently.

### Respect The Author's Restraint As Much As Their Excess

Literary craft includes knowing when not to use a device. An author's plainness, austerity, or deliberate repetition of simple words is itself a device, and a translator who "improves" the prose by adding elegance or variety betrays the author's intention.

Do not intensify imagery the author kept restrained. Do not add metaphors where the author used literal language. Do not vary a deliberately repeated plain word for the sake of elegance. Restraint transfers as restraint; if the source is spare, the target should be spare. Recognize that adding artistry the author withheld is as much a distortion as removing artistry the author included.

### Compensate When A Device Cannot Transfer

Some devices genuinely cannot be rebuilt at their original location, and good literary translation uses compensation: introducing an equivalent effect nearby to keep the overall artistic density balanced.

If a pun is lost in one sentence, a wordplay opportunity elsewhere in the paragraph can restore the playful texture. If sound patterning drops from one line, heightened sound craft in an adjacent line can compensate. Compensation is not invention for its own sake; it is a disciplined effort to preserve the total artistic experience when perfect local fidelity is impossible. Use it judiciously, and never let compensation become an excuse to add devices the author did not have anywhere.

## Common Traps

### Translating The Literal Vehicle And Losing The Effect

Rendering a metaphor word for word when its associations do not carry produces an image that is "faithful" on the surface but dead, comic, or confusing in effect. Function, not vehicle, is what must transfer.

### Dropping Sound Devices Silently

Letting alliteration, rhyme, and rhythm disappear because they are hard to recreate leaves poetry and lyrical prose aesthetically hollow. Sound devices must be recreated or compensated, not abandoned.

### Collapsing Deliberate Ambiguity Into One Reading

Choosing the more obvious meaning of a deliberately double-edged sentence reduces the text. Ambiguity is a device and must be recognized before it is resolved.

### Adding Elegance The Author Withheld

Embellishing plain or austere prose with metaphors and varied diction betrays authorial restraint. Literary translation can be guilty of over-translating as much as under-translating.

### Breaking Recurring Symbols Through Variation

Translating a recurring image with different words each time dissolves the symbolic pattern. Symbolic and recurring images need fixed, enforced renderings.

### Forcing Source Symbolism Onto Conflicting Target Associations

A literal transfer of a symbol whose meaning inverts across cultures can turn mourning into celebration or vice versa. Check cultural symbolism before assuming transfer.

### Treating Wordplay As Expendable

Silently dropping puns and wit because they are difficult removes a layer of artistry the author intended. Wordplay deserves a deliberate strategy, not quiet omission.

## Self-Check

Before approving a translation of figurative or sonically crafted text, verify:

- Each significant device was identified and its function understood before rendering, not translated blind.
- The function and effect of metaphors and similes transfer, with vehicle decisions, retain, adapt, or restructure, made deliberately based on what the device does.
- Sound devices such as alliteration, assonance, rhyme, and rhythm were recreated in target-language phonetics or compensated nearby, not silently dropped.
- Wordplay and deliberate ambiguity were handled with a conscious strategy, not collapsed into a single reading or quietly omitted.
- Symbolic and recurring images use consistent renderings so thematic resonance survives across the work.
- Cultural symbolism was checked for conflicts, and source symbols that invert in the target were adapted or retained with awareness.
- The author's restraint and plainness were respected, with no elegance, metaphor, or variation added that the source did not have.
- Where devices could not transfer locally, compensation was used judiciously to preserve overall artistic density.
- Read aloud and cold, the target passage carries approximately the imagistic, sonic, and figurative density the source carried for its reader.
