# Poetry Translation And Verse Craft

Poetry is the form of literature where form and meaning are most inseparable, and therefore the form of translation where rendering the sense without the form fails most completely. A poem is not its paraphrase. What makes a poem a poem is the convergence of sound, rhythm, line, structure, density, and silence with meaning, and that convergence is built from the specific resources of one language: its stress patterns, its syllable weights, its rhyme inventory, its line-break possibilities, its traditions of meter and free verse. When a translator renders a poem's meaning in smooth prose paragraphs, the result may be accurate as a semantic summary, but it is not a translation of the poem; it is a translation of what the poem is about, which is a different and lesser thing. The central problem of poetry translation is that the formal architecture almost never transfers directly, because meter, rhyme, and sound are language-specific, and the translator must decide how to rebuild an equivalent architecture in the target language's own resources, or how to compensate when exact equivalence is impossible. These decisions are not minor stylistic preferences; they determine whether the target text functions as poetry at all. The harm this skill prevents is the production of poetry translations that read as competent prose explanations of poems, with all the verse craft excised, which is the most common failure mode and the one that most betrays the source poet's work.

Use this skill when translating poetry, verse, or any text where meter, rhyme, line structure, or sonic patterning is the primary artistic medium. The goal is to rebuild, in the target language, a poem that functions as poetry, carrying the source's formal architecture and sonic experience through target-language verse craft rather than reducing the poem to its semantic content.

## Core Rules

### Decide The Formal Strategy Before Translating

Before translating a single line, decide the formal strategy, because every line depends on it and changing strategy mid-work produces incoherence. The strategy is a commitment about how the poem's form will be handled, and it must be made deliberately.

The main strategies each carry tradeoffs. Formal equivalence seeks to recreate the source's formal features, meter, rhyme scheme, stanza structure, in target-language equivalents, accepting that the specific words will differ and that some semantic content may shift to serve the form. This strategy honors the poem's architecture but risks strain, because forcing a target rhyme or meter can distort meaning. Free verse translation carries the poem into target-language free verse, preserving line integrity, rhythm, and density without imposing meter or rhyme, accepting the loss of formal pattern in exchange for greater semantic and sonic freedom. This strategy honors meaning and line but abandons the source's formal specificity. Prose paraphrase renders the poem as prose and is almost never acceptable as poetry translation, though it may serve as a crib alongside verse. Hybrid approaches combine elements, such as preserving meter without rhyme or preserving a key rhyme without full scheme. Choose based on the poem, the poet, the target tradition, and the intended reader, and state the choice, because a strategy left implicit drifts line by line.

### Rebuild Meter And Rhythm In Target-Language Resources

Meter and rhythm are the pulse of verse, and they are built from the prosodic features of a specific language. They almost never survive literal transfer and must be rebuilt, not assumed to carry over.

English stress-timed meter, French syllabic meter, classical quantitative meter, Japanese mora-based rhythm, and Chinese tonal prosody are fundamentally different systems, and a meter in one cannot be mapped mechanically onto another. A translator must understand both the source's prosodic system and the target's, and rebuild an equivalent rhythm using the target's resources. An English iambic pentameter poem may become a target-language verse line of equivalent weight and movement built from the target's stress or syllable conventions, not a literal copy of five iambs in a language where iambs carry no traditional weight. Free verse rhythm, though unmetered, is still rhythm, built from line length, phrasing, caesura, and enjambment, and it must be felt and rebuilt, not flattened into prose cadence. Read target verse aloud throughout, because rhythm that looks acceptable on the page often fails in the ear, and poetry lives in the ear.

### Handle Rhyme As A Deliberate Structural Decision

Rhyme is one of the most visible and most difficult features to translate, because a rhyme in one language rarely has a target equivalent using the same words, and forcing a target rhyme can distort meaning. Treat rhyme as a structural decision, not a decoration to be preserved or dropped casually.

If the source rhyme is structurally essential, as in a sonnet, a ballad, or a ghazal where rhyme organizes the form, then the target should recreate a rhyme scheme that performs the same organizing function, using target-language rhyming resources and accepting that the rhyming words will differ. If the source rhyme is occasional or ornamental, a lighter approach may serve, such as sporadic rhyme or sound echo. If forcing rhyme would require distorting meaning beyond what the poem can bear, a deliberate decision to drop rhyme while compensating through other formal features, such as heightened rhythm, alliteration, or line integrity, may be more faithful than a strained rhyme that mangles the sense. Never drop rhyme silently as if it did not matter, and never force rhyme as if meaning were secondary. Each poem's rhyme deserves a conscious, documented choice about its role and its target rendering.

### Preserve Line Integrity And The Meaning Of Line Breaks

In poetry, where a line breaks is a meaning-bearing decision. The line break controls pacing, emphasis, breath, ambiguity, and the relationship between syntactic unit and verse unit. Translation must preserve the function of line breaks, not just their positions.

Enjambment, where a syntactic unit runs over the line break, creates tension, suspense, or surprise, and the translator must rebuild that effect in the target, which may require different line breaks because syntax differs between languages. End-stopped lines, where the syntactic unit completes at the line break, create closure and regularity. A translator who regularizes all enjambment into end-stopped lines, or who breaks lines arbitrarily to match source positions without regard to target syntax, loses the poem's pacing and emphasis. Preserve the number of lines per stanza and the overall line count where the form requires it, but within each line, rebuild the break so it performs the same function in the target syntax. Do not collapse verse into prose or rewrap lines without thought, because line integrity is part of what makes the text a poem.

### Carry Density And Compression Without Padding Or Sprawling

Poetry is characteristically denser than prose: more meaning per word, more sound per syllable, more implication per line. Translation must carry that density, neither padding the target with explanatory words the source did not need nor sprawling into loose, prolix lines that lose the compression.

Different languages pack meaning at different rates, and a concise source poem may require more target words to express the same content, or fewer. The translator's task is to achieve equivalent density, the same weight of meaning and sound per line, not equivalent word count. If the target language is more analytic and requires more words, find compression through other means: tighter syntax, denser diction, sound patterning. If the source is highly compressed and the target tradition favors expansion, resist the urge to explain or fill out, because poetic compression often depends on implication and ellipsis that explanation destroys. A translated poem that is twice as long as the source, full of connective tissue the source omitted, has lost the compression that made the source a poem. A translated poem that is artificially terse, missing content the source carried, has lost meaning. Aim for the same felt density.

### Respect Silence, White Space, And The Unsaid

Poetry communicates as much through what is omitted as through what is said. White space, stanza gaps, abrupt transitions, and deliberate ellipsis are part of the poem's architecture, and translation must respect them rather than filling them in.

A translator who finds a poem's leaps confusing may be tempted to add connective words that smooth the transitions, but those leaps are often the point, carrying implication, juxtaposition, or emotional truth that explicit connection would flatten. A translator who finds white space or stanza breaks excessive may be tempted to regularize, but the spacing is a pacing and emphasis decision. Preserve the source's omissions, leaps, and spatial architecture, and trust the target reader to do the work the source reader did. If a poem is difficult or elliptical in the source, the translation should be difficult or elliptical in an equivalent way, not clarified into plainness. Filling in the unsaid is a form of untranslating the poem, because the unsaid is part of what was said.

### Translate Within A Poetic Tradition, Not In A Vacuum

Every poem exists within a poetic tradition, and a translation that ignores tradition produces verse that feels rootless or wrong to target-language readers who know their own poetry. Translate into the target tradition.

This means knowing the target language's verse forms, metrical conventions, diction registers, and the contemporary practice of poetry in that language. A sonnet translated into a language with a strong sonnet tradition should engage that tradition's conventions. A free verse poem translated into a language where free verse has its own established practice should sound like that practice, not like imported prose chopped into lines. Diction matters: poetic register in the target, whether high, colloquial, or hybrid, should match the source's relationship to its own tradition. A poet who wrote against their tradition, using anti-poetic diction, should be translated into target-language anti-poetic diction, not into conventional poetic register. Knowing the target tradition lets the translator make the translated poem sound like it belongs, while preserving its foreign origin, rather than sounding like verse in no one's tradition.

### Acknowledge Loss And Use Compensation Honestly

Some features of a source poem genuinely cannot be recreated in the target, and honest poetry translation acknowledges loss rather than pretending every feature transferred. Compensation, introducing an equivalent effect elsewhere, is the disciplined response to unavoidable loss.

If a rhyme cannot be rebuilt at its original location, a rhyme or sound echo nearby can compensate. If a pun is untransferable, wordplay elsewhere can restore the playful density. If a metrical effect is lost, heightened rhythm or sound craft in an adjacent line can compensate. Compensation is not invention for its own sake; it is a measured effort to keep the total poetic experience in balance when local fidelity is impossible. Use it judiciously, and where loss is significant and cannot be compensated, a brief translator's note may serve for scholarly or annotated editions. The principle is honesty: do not claim full transfer where loss occurred, and do not let loss accumulate silently until the translation is a diminished version of the poem.

## Common Traps

### Reducing The Poem To A Prose Paraphrase

Rendering meaning in smooth prose strips the verse craft and produces a summary of what the poem is about, not a translation of the poem. Form and meaning are inseparable in poetry.

### Letting Strategy Drift Line By Line

An implicit formal strategy drifts as the translator shifts between approaches. Decide the strategy deliberately and commit to it across the work.

### Assuming Meter Transfers Literally

Meter is built from language-specific prosody. A source meter must be rebuilt in target-language resources, not copied as if iambs or syllable counts were universal.

### Dropping Or Forcing Rhyme Without Decision

Rhyme is structural. Dropping it silently as if it did not matter, or forcing it at the expense of meaning, both betray the poem. Make a conscious choice about rhyme's role.

### Ignoring Line Breaks And Enjambment

Where a line breaks is meaning-bearing. Rewrapping lines arbitrarily or collapsing verse into prose loses the pacing, emphasis, and tension the line breaks create.

### Padding Compression Into Prolix Lines

Poetry is dense. Adding explanatory words the source omitted, or sprawling to match word count rather than density, loses the compression that makes verse verse.

### Filling In Silences And Leaps

The unsaid is part of the poem. Smoothing transitions, regularizing white space, or explaining ellipsis flattens implication that the source reader was trusted to supply.

### Translating Outside Any Poetic Tradition

Verse that ignores the target language's poetic conventions feels rootless. Translate into the target tradition's forms, diction, and practice while preserving the source's origin.

## Self-Check

Before approving a poetry translation, verify:

- A formal strategy was chosen deliberately before translating and committed to consistently, whether formal equivalence, free verse, or a documented hybrid.
- Meter and rhythm were rebuilt in target-language prosodic resources, not assumed to transfer literally, and the target verse reads aloud with equivalent movement.
- Rhyme was handled as a structural decision, with structurally essential rhyme recreated in target resources, occasional rhyme treated lightly, and any dropping of rhyme documented and compensated.
- Line integrity and the function of line breaks, including enjambment and end-stopping, are preserved by rebuilding breaks in target syntax rather than copying positions or collapsing into prose.
- Density and compression are carried without padding the target with explanatory words or sprawling into prolix lines, achieving equivalent felt weight per line.
- Silence, white space, leaps, and ellipsis are respected, with no smoothing of transitions or filling in of the unsaid that the source trusted the reader to supply.
- The translation engages the target language's poetic tradition in forms, diction, and register, so the verse sounds like it belongs rather than rootless.
- Loss is acknowledged honestly, with compensation used judiciously to keep the total poetic experience in balance, and significant loss noted where appropriate.
- No poem was reduced to a prose paraphrase, and no formal feature was dropped silently as if it did not matter.
- Read aloud, the target text functions as poetry in the target language, carrying the source's sonic, rhythmic, and structural experience rather than only its semantic content.
