# Competitive Context Localization

Competitive content, comparisons, benchmarks, "us versus them" claims, feature parity tables, and market rankings, is among the hardest content to localize because it sits at the intersection of accuracy, law, and local market reality. A comparison that is true and legal in the home market may be false, irrelevant, or illegal in the target market. The competitor named at home may be absent abroad, while a different competitor dominates. The feature that differentiates at home may be table stakes abroad, making the comparison meaningless. The benchmark that impresses at home may be unknown or distrusted abroad. The claim phrasing that passes advertising law in one country violates comparative advertising rules in another. Translators and localizers who handle competitive content as ordinary text, swapping languages while keeping the names and claims, produce content that is inaccurate, non-compliant, or simply ineffective. Competitive context localization is the work of re-grounding comparisons, claims, and benchmarks in the target market's actual competitive landscape, while preserving accuracy and respecting local advertising law. The judgment problem is that this work often requires information the translator does not have, who the real competitors are, what claims are legal, whether a benchmark is recognized, and the right response is structured escalation and verification, not confident guessing. This skill covers how to localize competitive content responsibly, what must be verified, and how to avoid the legal and credibility traps that competitive content concentrates.

Use this skill when localizing competitive comparisons, feature parity tables, benchmark claims, rankings, or "us versus them" content, when adapting competitive references to a target market, or when ensuring comparative claims are accurate and legally compliant locally. The goal is competitive content that is true, legal, and relevant in the target market.

## Core Rules

### Map The Target Market's Actual Competitive Landscape

Competitive content only works if it references competitors the target audience actually recognizes. Begin by mapping the real landscape, do not inherit the home market's.

The competitors a product faces abroad are often entirely different from those at home. A rival that dominates the home market may be a minor player or absent abroad, while a local champion the home team has never heard of may be the primary alternative. Naming a home-market competitor unknown to the target audience makes the comparison pointless and signals that the content was not localized. Research, with local market or product teams, who the actual alternatives are in the target market, what they claim, and where the credible differentiation lies. Where the source names a competitor irrelevant to the target, flag for replacement or removal rather than translating the irrelevant name.

### Verify Every Comparative Claim For Accuracy Locally

Comparative claims are factual assertions, and a claim true at home may be false abroad because the comparison target or the facts differ. Verify each claim for the target market.

A claim that a product is faster, cheaper, more reliable, or more feature-rich than a competitor depends on evidence that may not hold in the target market: prices differ, feature sets differ by region, and the competitor's offering may differ. A claim verified against the home-market version of a competitor may be inaccurate against that competitor's target-market offering. For every comparative claim, confirm the comparison is still valid in the target context, with current evidence. If the claim cannot be substantiated locally, it must be weakened, removed, or flagged for legal and product review. Shipping an unsubstantiated comparative claim is both a credibility risk and a legal one.

### Respect Local Comparative Advertising Law

Comparative advertising is regulated, and the rules differ sharply by country. Treat every comparative claim as a legal risk until confirmed compliant locally.

Some markets permit direct comparative advertising with conditions; others restrict or prohibit it. Rules differ on whether competitors can be named, whether claims must be objectively substantiated, whether comparisons must be between products meeting the same need, and whether denigration is allowed. A comparison legal in the home market may be prohibited or require disclaimers abroad. Health, financial, and claims about regulated products carry additional restrictions. When localizing comparative content, do not assume home-market legality transfers. Route comparative claims through local legal or compliance review, and adjust phrasing, add disclaimers, or remove claims as required. The cost of a non-compliant comparison, fines, takedowns, or competitor lawsuits, far exceeds the cost of a review.

### Re-Ground Benchmarks And Rankings In Local Recognition

Benchmarks, awards, rankings, and third-party endorsements only persuade if the target audience recognizes and trusts the source. Re-ground them in local recognition.

A benchmark famous in the home market may be unknown or distrusted in the target. An award from a home-market body may carry no weight abroad. A ranking that impresses one audience may be irrelevant to another. Where a benchmark or endorsement is not recognized locally, it fails to persuade and may even raise suspicion. Replace unrecognized third-party references with ones the target audience trusts, where credible local equivalents exist, or remove them and lead with different proof. Never invent a local benchmark or award; if no recognized local endorsement exists, find other evidence. Verify that any retained third-party reference is current and that the product's standing in it has not changed.

### Localize Feature Parity And Comparison Tables Carefully

Comparison and parity tables concentrate competitive claims and accuracy risk. Localize them element by element, with verification.

For each row and cell, confirm the competitor named is relevant to the target market, the feature comparison is accurate for the target-market offerings, and the claim is legally compliant. Watch for checkmarks or ratings that imply superiority not substantiated locally, for features that differ by region, and for competitor product names that change by market. Confirm the table's overall framing, that it compares products meeting the same need, complies with local rules. A table that is accurate at home but references the wrong competitors or unsubstantiated feature claims abroad is defective and risky. Where the table cannot be made accurate and compliant locally, flag for revision rather than translating it as-is.

### Handle Competitor Names And Trademarks Correctly

Competitor names, product names, and trademarks have legal and usage considerations that vary by market. Handle them correctly.

Confirm the correct local name of each competitor and product, because names sometimes differ by market. Use trademarks correctly according to local convention and the owner's usage rules; misuse can create trademark problems. Do not alter a competitor's name or mark in a way that could be seen as denigration, which some markets prohibit. Where a competitor's name is subject to local trademark restrictions or where naming competitors is restricted by law, adjust the comparison to comply. When in doubt about name or trademark usage, verify with legal rather than guessing.

### Escalate Competitive Content Beyond The Translator's Authority

Much competitive content requires decisions, which competitors to name, which claims to substantiate, which benchmarks to use, that exceed the translator's authority. Escalate rather than decide silently.

Competitive localization often needs input from product, marketing, and legal teams who know the target market and the claim evidence. The translator's role is to surface the issues, propose options, and flag for decision, not to unilaterally rewrite competitive claims. Build a review path for competitive content that includes local market and legal input, and document each significant decision so the rationale is clear. Silent rewriting of competitive content by a translator, however well-intentioned, can introduce inaccuracy or non-compliance that no one else will catch.

## Common Traps

### Inheriting Home-Market Competitors

Naming competitors the target audience does not recognize makes comparisons pointless and signals un-localized content.

### Assuming Claims Stay True Across Markets

A claim accurate at home may be false abroad; verify each comparative claim against the target-market offerings and current evidence.

### Treating Comparative Claims As Ordinary Text

Comparative advertising is legally regulated; translating claims without legal review risks prohibition, fines, or lawsuits.

### Retaining Unrecognized Benchmarks And Awards

Third-party references unknown or distrusted locally fail to persuade; replace with recognized equivalents or remove.

### Localizing Comparison Tables Cell By Cell Without Verification

Tables concentrate accuracy and legal risk; verify each competitor, feature, and claim for the target market.

### Misusing Competitor Names And Trademarks

Names and marks differ by market and carry usage rules; verify correct local usage to avoid legal problems.

### Silently Rewriting Competitive Content

Competitive decisions exceed the translator's authority; escalate to market and legal teams and document decisions.

## Self-Check

Before approving competitive context localization, verify:

- The target market's actual competitive landscape was mapped, and named competitors are ones the target audience recognizes.
- Every comparative claim was verified for accuracy against target-market offerings and current evidence, with unsubstantiated claims weakened, removed, or flagged.
- Comparative claims were routed through local legal or compliance review, with phrasing, disclaimers, or removal applied to meet local advertising law.
- Benchmarks, awards, and rankings were re-grounded in local recognition, with unrecognized references replaced or removed and none invented.
- Comparison and parity tables were localized element by element, with competitors, features, and claims verified for accuracy and compliance.
- Competitor names and trademarks use correct local forms and respect usage rules and trademark restrictions.
- Competitive content beyond the translator's authority was escalated to product, market, and legal teams, with decisions documented.
- No comparative claim was shipped that is inaccurate, non-compliant, or irrelevant to the target market's competitive reality.
- The localized competitive content would be true, legal, and persuasive to the target audience without exposing the brand to liability.
