# Medical Device Localization

Medical device localization is the adaptation of device documentation, software interfaces, and labeling for target markets under regulatory constraints that govern almost every word. Instructions for use, software UI, hardware labels, packaging, and promotional materials must all be localized, and each carries safety and regulatory meaning. Devices are approved by regulators such as the FDA, EMA, or MDR authorities on the basis of specific labeling, and the localized versions must preserve the approved meaning, including intended use, contraindications, warnings, and specifications. Localization also interacts with usability: a device whose instructions or interface confuse users in a target market creates use error that can harm patients. The translator in this domain works at the intersection of language, regulation, engineering, and human factors, and errors can cause regulatory rejection, product recall, or patient harm.

Use this skill when localizing medical device content, translating instructions for use or device software, handling regulatory labeling, or ensuring localized device content meets target-market approval and usability requirements. The goal is to produce localized device content that is regulatorily compliant, safe, and usable in the target market.

## Core Rules

### Preserve Intended Use And Regulatory Claims Exactly

The intended use statement and regulatory claims define what the device is approved for. They must be translated exactly, because they determine the device's regulatory status.

The intended use, indications for use, contraindications, warnings, and precautions are reviewed and approved by regulators. Translating them in a way that expands or narrows the approved use can make the localized device non-compliant or off-label. Preserve the exact scope. Do not add claims the source does not make, even if marketing wants them, and do not soften contraindications or warnings. Use the target regulator's required terminology where it is prescribed.

Flag any source content that appears to make claims beyond the approved labeling, because this may be a compliance issue requiring resolution before localization.

### Handle Instructions For Use As Safety Content

Instructions for use, IFUs, are how users operate the device safely. Translate them as safety content, with usability as a core requirement.

IFUs must be accurate, complete, and understandable to the target user, who may be a clinician, a patient, or a caregiver. Preserve every step, caution, and warning. Match the structure and numbering so steps align with the device's actual operation. Ensure figures, callouts, and references are consistent with the translated text. Verify that the instructions a target user would follow lead to correct and safe device use.

An IFU that is accurate but unusable causes use error; one that is usable but inaccurate causes harm. Both must be prevented.

### Localize Software UI For Usability And Safety

Device software interfaces must be localized for both usability and safety. The UI is part of how users operate the device, and confusing labels can cause error.

Preserve terminology consistency between the UI and the documentation, because users move between them. Respect character limits and layout constraints, because a truncated or overflowing label can obscure a critical function. Handle alarms, error messages, and status indicators with special care, because these communicate safety information. Ensure that the localized UI supports the device's workflow without introducing ambiguity.

Coordinate with engineering to test the localized UI in context, because text that works in isolation may fail in the actual interface.

### Preserve Safety Symbols And Standardized Wording

Medical devices use standardized symbols and signal words that carry regulatory and safety meaning. Preserve them according to standard.

Many safety symbols are defined by standards such as ISO and are not translated but must be used correctly and consistently. Signal words such as danger, warning, caution have defined meanings and must map to the target language's equivalent signal words as the standard prescribes. Color coding, if regulated, must be preserved. Do not substitute alternative symbols or wording that the standard does not allow.

Where the target market requires additional or different safety markings, flag this, because it may require design changes, not just translation.

### Manage Regulatory Labeling Requirements Per Market

Each market has its own medical device labeling requirements. The localized labeling must satisfy the target market's rules, which may differ from the source's.

Requirements can include language rules, such as mandatory languages; content rules, such as required elements like CE marks, UDI, or FDA-mandated statements; format rules, such as label layout and symbol use; and submission rules, such as the need to submit localized labeling for approval. Determine the target market's requirements before localizing, and adapt the labeling to satisfy them.

Labeling that meets the source market's rules but not the target's cannot be sold in the target market.

### Handle Units Measurements And Specifications

Device specifications, measurements, and units must be localized accurately, with attention to systems that differ across markets.

Preserve exact values and units, converting only where the target market requires or where the device is calibrated for a different system. Watch for locale-specific decimal and thousands separators that can change a value's meaning. Ensure that specifications in the documentation match the device's actual labeling and software, because inconsistency causes confusion and regulatory issues.

Verify all numeric content against the source and against the device's approved specifications.

### Coordinate Usability Testing In The Target Market

For devices where use error could cause harm, localized content should be usability-tested with target-market users. Coordinate or recommend this testing.

Usability testing reveals whether target users can understand and follow the localized IFU and UI safely. It catches issues that linguistic review misses, such as culturally confusing instructions or UI labels that mislead. Regulators increasingly expect evidence that localized content supports safe use. Flag where usability testing is needed and support it by refining content based on results.

### Maintain Traceability And Version Control

Medical device content is versioned and traceable for regulatory reasons. Localization must maintain this traceability.

Track which source version each translation is based on, because devices and their labeling are updated and the translations must stay aligned. Maintain a translation memory and termbase specific to the device and its product family. Record changes and their rationale, because regulators may require evidence of how localized content was produced and maintained.

Loss of traceability can cause a device's localized versions to fall out of regulatory alignment.

### Coordinate Regulatory And Engineering Review

Medical device localization requires review by regulatory affairs and by engineering or human factors. Coordinate both.

Regulatory review verifies that localized labeling meets target-market requirements and preserves approved claims. Engineering and human factors review verifies that the localized IFU and UI support correct and safe device use. Provide reviewers with source, translation, termbase, and context such as screenshots and device access where possible. Incorporate review feedback carefully.

The translator localizes content; regulatory and engineering confirm compliance and usability. All are required for safe device localization.

## Common Traps

### Expanding Or Narrowing Intended Use In Translation

Altering the approved scope of the device's use in localization causes non-compliance or off-label promotion.

### Producing Accurate But Unusable IFUs

Instructions that users cannot follow cause use error and patient harm, even when medically accurate.

### UI Labels That Truncate Or Overflow

Layout failures in localized UI can obscure critical functions and cause unsafe operation.

### Substituting Non-Standard Symbols Or Signal Words

Safety symbols and signal words are standardized; substitutions can violate standards and confuse users.

### Ignoring Target-Market Labeling Requirements

Labeling that meets source rules but not target rules cannot be sold in the target market.

### Numeric Or Unit Inconsistency Across Labeling And Software

Inconsistent specifications across documentation, labeling, and software cause confusion and regulatory issues.

### Skipping Usability Testing

Linguistic review alone cannot confirm that target users can operate the device safely from localized content.

### Losing Version Traceability

Untracked localization can cause device versions to fall out of regulatory alignment during updates.

## Self-Check

Before approving medical device localization, verify:

- Intended use, indications, contraindications, warnings, and regulatory claims are translated exactly, preserving approved scope.
- Instructions for use are accurate, complete, usable, and aligned with the device's actual operation and figures.
- Software UI is localized for usability and safety, with terminology consistent with documentation and layout constraints respected.
- Safety symbols, signal words, color coding, and standardized wording are preserved according to the relevant standards.
- Target-market labeling requirements for language, content, format, and submission are identified and satisfied.
- Units, measurements, and specifications are accurate and consistent across documentation, labeling, and software.
- Usability testing with target-market users is recommended or coordinated for devices where use error could harm.
- Version traceability is maintained, with source versions, translation memory, termbase, and change records tracked.
- Regulatory affairs and engineering or human factors review are coordinated for compliance and usability.
- No claim, warning, instruction, or specification has been altered in a way that affects regulatory status, safety, or usability.
