# Condensation And Segmentation

Subtitles almost never have room for everything the speaker said. Speech is fast, redundant, and syntactically complex, while subtitles are bounded by characters per line, lines per cue, and seconds on screen. The subtitle translator is therefore constantly deciding what to keep, what to compress, and how to break text across lines and cues so the viewer can read, understand, and stay immersed. This is the craft of condensation and segmentation, and it is where amateur and professional subtitles diverge most sharply. Bad condensation strips meaning the viewer needed or flattens tone that carried the scene. Bad segmentation breaks a sentence at a point that confuses the grammar, splits a thought across a cut, or creates line breaks that fight the eye. Agents miss the difficulty because condensation looks like simple shortening and segmentation looks like hitting return, but both are governed by linguistic and cognitive principles: you must cut without losing function, and you must break without breaking sense. The harm this prevents is subtitles that are either overstuffed and unreadable or hollowed-out and unfaithful, and line breaks that make viewers stumble or misread.

Use this skill when condensing subtitle content to fit reading-speed and character budgets, segmenting text into lines and cues, or deciding what to prioritize when not everything can fit. The goal is condensed, well-segmented subtitles that preserve the meaning and tone the viewer needs while remaining effortless to read.

## Core Rules

### Condense By Priority, Not By Truncation

Condensation is the art of reducing text while preserving what matters, and it must be guided by priority, not by chopping the end of a sentence. Decide what the viewer most needs before cutting.

Rank what the speech carries: plot-critical information comes first, then character-revealing tone, then atmosphere and texture, then redundant filler. Cut from the bottom of that ranking. Hesitations, repetitions, discourse markers, and pleasantries that do no work can usually go. Condense syntax by simplifying clause structure without losing the proposition: convert a long circumlocution into a direct statement, merge redundant clauses, drop qualifiers that do not change meaning. The test is whether a viewer who reads only the subtitle still understands the scene and feels the speaker's intent. If condensation removes something the viewer needed to follow plot or character, it has failed, however clean the resulting line looks. Condense toward readability, but never past the point of fidelity to function.

### Preserve Tone And Function Under Compression

Condensation that saves characters but flattens tone corrupts the scene. A sarcastic line condensed into sincerity, a tender line condensed into coldness, or a threatening line softened for brevity all fail even if the propositional meaning survives.

Identify the speech's function and tone before condensing, and protect them as invariants. Tone is carried by word choice, register, and structure, so when you simplify structure, choose replacements that carry the same attitude. Function, whether the line warns, promises, jokes, deflects, or commands, must survive compression. Where a literal condensation would kill tone, accept a freer reformulation that preserves it, because in subtitles the effect on the viewer matters as much as the proposition. This is where subtitling approaches transcreation: the line that fits and carries the tone may look quite different from the source words, and that is correct if it does the source's work in the time available.

### Segment At Linguistic Boundaries

Line breaks and cue splits must occur at natural linguistic boundaries so each line and cue reads as a coherent unit. Breaking mid-phrase or mid-thought forces the viewer to re-parse and slows reading.

Break lines at syntactic joints: between clauses, before conjunctions, after punctuation, or at points where a natural pause falls. Each line should ideally contain a complete thought or a self-contained phrase, so the eye can process it as a unit. Avoid orphaning short function words at the end of a line, leaving a conjunction or preposition dangling, and avoid splitting a noun phrase across lines in a way that obscures it. The goal is that a viewer reading line by line never has to mentally recombine awkwardly. Good segmentation is invisible; the viewer simply reads smoothly. Bad segmentation is felt as a stumble even when the words are all correct.

### Balance Line Length And Visual Symmetry

Subtitles occupy a visual space, and line length affects readability. The two lines of a two-line subtitle should be balanced, with the upper line often slightly shorter or equal, and neither line exceeding the character-per-line limit.

Determine the character-per-line limit for the target language and platform, and keep every line within it. Aim for visual balance: a top line of two words over a bottom line of twelve looks unstable and is harder to read. Where possible, distribute content so the two lines are roughly even, with the break at a natural boundary. Avoid full-width bottom lines under short top lines, which can look bottom-heavy. These are not mere aesthetics; balanced lines are genuinely easier to read at speed, and the eye tracks them more reliably. Treat the subtitle as a designed object, not just a string of text.

### Split Across Cues At Logical Points

When a long utterance must span multiple cues, the split between cues should fall at a logical boundary, ideally at a sentence end, clause boundary, or natural pause, and each cue should stand as a readable unit.

Each cue should make sense on its own as much as possible, because the viewer reads it as a discrete event. Avoid splitting a sentence so that the first cue ends mid-clause and the second cue begins with a dependent fragment that only makes sense with the prior cue already gone. Where a split is unavoidable, prefer ending the first cue at a point of partial closure so the viewer is not left hanging awkwardly. Time each cue to its portion of the speech, and ensure the reading-speed budget holds per cue. Coordinated multi-cue handling is essential for monologues, explanations, and any dense passage.

### Coordinate Segmentation With Timing And Shot Changes

Segmentation is not independent of timing and editing; line breaks and cue splits should cooperate with reading speed, speech rhythm, and shot changes. The three disciplines must be solved together.

A line break that creates two balanced lines may still produce a cue that exceeds reading speed, requiring further condensation. A cue split that reads well on paper may cross a shot change and need re-timing. A natural pause in speech may offer an ideal cue split point that pure text segmentation would miss. Always solve condensation, segmentation, and timing as an integrated problem, iterating until all three constraints are satisfied. Treating them in isolation produces work that passes each individual check but fails as a whole.

### Maintain Consistency Across A Title

Condensation and segmentation choices should be consistent across a film or series, because viewers adapt to patterns and inconsistency is distracting. Establish conventions and apply them throughout.

If you condense filler discourse markers in one scene, do so throughout. If you adopt a line-balancing convention, hold to it. If character names or terms get abbreviated under pressure, abbreviate them the same way each time. Maintain a running note of condensation and segmentation decisions, especially for recurring phrases, character speech patterns, and difficult constructions. Consistency is what makes a body of subtitles feel professionally authored rather than assembled, and it reduces cognitive load on the viewer, who comes to trust the subtitle's conventions.

## Common Traps

### Truncating The End Instead Of Condensing By Priority

Chopping the tail of a sentence to fit a limit drops whatever came last, which may be the most important part. Condense by ranking importance, not by position.

### Flattening Tone To Save Characters

Compression that strips a line's sarcasm, warmth, or menace leaves the meaning but kills the scene. Tone and function are invariants of condensation.

### Breaking Lines Mid-Phrase

Line breaks inside phrases or clauses force re-parsing and slow reading. Break at linguistic boundaries so each line is a coherent unit.

### Unbalanced Or Overlong Lines

A short line over a full-width line, or lines exceeding the character limit, are harder to read and look unstable. Balance line length within limits.

### Splitting Cues Into Dependent Fragments

A cue that ends mid-thought and a following cue that begins with a dangling fragment confuse the viewer across the gap. Split at points of closure.

### Solving Condensation, Segmentation, And Timing Separately

Treating the three disciplines in isolation produces work that fails as a whole despite passing individual checks. Solve them together.

### Inconsistent Conventions Across A Title

Varying condensation and segmentation choices scene to scene distracts viewers who have adapted to a pattern. Establish and hold conventions.

## Self-Check

Before approving condensed and segmented subtitles, verify:

- Condensation was guided by priority, cutting redundancy and filler before meaning, so the viewer can still follow plot and character.
- Tone and function survived compression, with no line flattened into a different attitude or stripped of its purpose.
- Line breaks fall at linguistic boundaries, with each line a coherent unit and no mid-phrase splits or orphaned function words.
- Lines are balanced in length and within the character-per-line limit, without unstable short-over-long layouts.
- Multi-cue splits fall at logical boundaries, with each cue as self-contained as possible and no dangling dependent fragments.
- Condensation, segmentation, and timing were solved together, with each constraint satisfied in the final result.
- Conventions for condensation, abbreviation, and segmentation are consistent across the title.
- No meaning the viewer needed was dropped, no tone was flattened, and no line break forces the viewer to re-parse.
- Read at subtitle speed by a target-language viewer, each line and cue is effortless to read and faithfully carries the speech's meaning and tone.
