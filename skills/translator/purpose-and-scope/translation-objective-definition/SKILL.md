# Translation Objective Definition

A translation is not a language swap. It is a piece of communication that must perform a job in a new language for a new reader, and that job is rarely self-evident from the source text alone. The same contract can be translated so a party can understand their obligations, so a court can enforce them, or so a negotiator can compare wording across language versions. Each objective demands different fidelity, different terminology choices, and a different tolerance for adaptation. When the objective is never defined, the translator defaults to a generic "accurate and fluent" target that is, in fact, aimed at no particular reader and satisfies no particular function. The result reads acceptably but fails the moment someone relies on it for a specific purpose: the marketing headline that was translated for comprehension but needed to convert, the warning that was translated for elegance but needed to prevent harm, the clause that was translated for readability but changed a legal obligation.

Agents miss objective definition because the source gives the illusion of self-sufficiency. The words are there, the meaning seems clear, and the instinct is to begin converting. But meaning without purpose is underspecified. Two equally accurate renderings can be correct for one objective and wrong for another. Defining the objective first is the work of forcing the translation's function into the open so that every later decision, from term choice to register to how much ambiguity to preserve, has a criterion to test against.

Use this skill before drafting begins, when the purpose of the translation is unclear or contested, when the same source could serve several functions, or when a requester has given text without explaining what the output must achieve. The goal is to establish a single, stated communicative objective that the deliverable can be evaluated against.

## Core Rules

### Ask What The Target Text Must Do, Not Just What It Must Say

The objective is a function, not a topic. A source about a drug's dosage can be translated to inform a patient, to instruct a clinician, to satisfy a regulator, to support a patent claim, or to advertise a benefit. Each function changes the translation.

Formulate the objective as a verb the reader will be able to perform after reading: understand their rights, operate the device safely, decide whether to buy, comply with a regulation, prove ownership, follow a procedure, feel reassured, compare two language versions for equivalence. If you cannot state the reader's post-reading capability in one sentence, the objective is not yet defined. A topic such as "translate the user manual" is not an objective; "enable a non-expert user to assemble and operate the product without injury or error" is.

### Separate The Source Author's Intent From The Target Text's Function

The reason the source was written and the reason the translation is needed are often different. A press release written to manage a crisis in one market may be translated to inform investors in another, where the persuasive framing is irrelevant or even counterproductive. An internal memo written to persuade management may be translated as evidence in litigation, where the persuasive tone must be preserved as evidence rather than smoothed.

Identify both intents explicitly. Note where they diverge, because divergence changes the translation strategy. When the target function differs from the source intent, the translator is not merely reproducing the source but producing a new communicative act, and that shift must be authorized by the requester.

### Classify The Objective By Communicative Function

Objectives cluster into families that imply different translation behaviors. Identify which family the job belongs to.

Informative objectives, such as manuals, reports, and news, prioritize accurate transfer of content and logical clarity; the reader needs facts and procedures. Persuasive objectives, such as marketing, fundraising, and political messaging, prioritize effect on the reader; literal accuracy may be secondary to emotional or rhetorical impact. Legally binding or evidentiary objectives, such as contracts, certificates, and court documents, prioritize equivalence of obligation and effect; wording carries legal force and must be conservative. Preservational objectives, such as literary, historical, or archival translation, prioritize fidelity to voice, form, and cultural specificity. Access objectives, such as plain-language or accessibility adaptation, prioritize comprehension by a defined underserved reader. A translation that applies the rules of one family to a job in another will fail its objective even when it is technically accurate.

### Make The Objective Testable

An objective that cannot be tested is a wish. Convert the objective into a concrete question the deliverable must answer or a behavior it must enable.

For an informative objective, the test might be whether a target reader can complete the procedure without error. For a persuasive objective, whether the target carries the intended emotional or rhetorical effect. For a legal objective, whether the target creates the same obligations and liabilities as the source. For an access objective, whether the defined reader can understand it at the intended reading level. State the test before drafting, because it becomes the acceptance criterion and the defense against vague feedback such as "make it sound better."

### Resolve Competing Objectives By Priority

A single translation often serves multiple objectives that conflict. A patient leaflet must inform accurately and reassure without alarming. A consumer contract must be legally precise and readable by a non-lawyer. A product warning must be terse for a label and complete for safety.

Do not attempt to satisfy all objectives equally; that produces a compromise that serves none well. Rank the objectives by stakes and by the requester's actual priority. Safety and legal obligation usually outrank elegance. For high-stakes content, preserve the binding or protective function first and accept reduced fluency. Record the priority order so that when objectives collide during drafting, the decision is already made rather than relitigated segment by segment.

### Confirm The Objective With The Requester

The objective is a hypothesis until the requester confirms it. Many translation failures originate in an objective the translator inferred but never checked.

State your understanding of the objective in plain terms and ask the requester to confirm or correct it. If the requester cannot articulate the objective, propose two or three plausible ones and ask which applies. A requester who says "just translate it" has not declined to set an objective; they have delegated the guess to you, and the guess should be made explicit so it can be challenged before work is invested.

### Revisit The Objective When The Source Reveals A Different Job

Objectives defined upfront sometimes prove wrong once the source is read in full. A document presented as marketing may contain regulatory claims that make it compliance-critical. A text presented as internal may contain clauses with external legal effect.

When the source reveals that the actual stakes or function differ from the stated objective, stop and renegotiate the objective rather than proceeding under a false one. Translating under a wrong objective produces a deliverable that is internally consistent but aimed at the wrong target.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Generic Accuracy

"Accurate and fluent" is not an objective. It is the absence of one. A translation optimized for no specific function tends to satisfy none of them when tested.

### Inferring The Objective From The Source Alone

The source reveals the author's intent, not necessarily the target text's function. Inferring the objective without confirming it with the requester leads to a deliverable aimed at the wrong reader.

### Applying One Function's Rules To Another

Treating marketing like a manual, or a contract like a brochure, applies the wrong fidelity rules. Persuasive freedom applied to legal text changes obligations; legal conservatism applied to marketing kills the effect.

### Confusing Topic With Objective

"Translate the annual report" names a topic. The objective is what the reader must gain: assess financial health, meet a disclosure requirement, or compare performance. Topic without function leaves the translation unmoored.

### Trying To Satisfy All Objectives Equally

When objectives conflict, averaging them produces a compromise that serves none. Rank by stakes and priority, and accept that a lower-ranked objective may be partially sacrificed.

### Keeping The Objective Implicit

An objective kept in the translator's head cannot be challenged, tested, or inherited by reviewers. Implicit objectives become silent assumptions that surface only as delivery disputes.

## Self-Check

Before drafting or approving a translation plan, verify:

- The objective is stated as a function the target reader must be able to perform, not merely as a topic or a word count.
- The source author's intent and the target text's function have both been identified, and any divergence is noted and authorized.
- The objective has been classified into a communicative family whose fidelity rules match the job.
- A concrete, testable acceptance criterion exists for the objective, not a vague quality aspiration.
- Competing objectives have been ranked by priority, with the ranking recorded.
- The requester has confirmed the objective, or a stated assumption has been put to them for correction.
- The objective was revisited after reading the full source, and any mismatch between stated and actual stakes was renegotiated.
- No objective-critical decision, such as how much ambiguity to preserve or how free the adaptation may be, has been left implicit.
