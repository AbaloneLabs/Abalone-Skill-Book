# Success Criteria Establishment

"Is the translation good?" is a question that cannot be answered, because good has no threshold. Without explicit success criteria, a translation is judged by whatever standard happens to be in the reviewer's head, and that standard shifts with mood, seniority, and hindsight. A requester who never stated criteria will, at delivery, find the translation too literal, too free, too formal, or too long, none of which are failures the translator could have anticipated. The translator, having no criterion to defend against, is forced into endless revision that converges on nothing. Success criteria are the mechanism that makes done mean something. They convert quality from a feeling into a set of checks the deliverable either passes or does not, and they protect both sides: the requester gets a deliverable that meets an agreed standard, and the translator gets a defensible stopping point beyond which further change is preference, not correction.

Agents skip criteria establishment because it feels like overhead before the real work, and because fluency seems self-evidently the goal. But fluency is one criterion among many, and it often conflicts with the criteria that actually matter: legal equivalence, term consistency, character limits, safety completeness, brand voice. Establishing criteria forces these competing standards into the open, ranks them, and turns them into acceptance tests. The cost of skipping this step is paid in revision cycles, delivery disputes, and deliverables that satisfy no one because no one agreed on what satisfaction meant.

Use this skill when defining what a finished translation must achieve, when agreeing acceptance standards with a requester, when setting review thresholds, or when a project needs a defensible definition of done. The goal is to make completion a verifiable state rather than a negotiation.

## Core Rules

### Define Acceptance As Tests, Not As Adjectives

Criteria stated as adjectives, accurate, fluent, natural, professional, are untestable because every reviewer applies them differently. Convert each criterion into a test that produces a yes or no.

A testable criterion names a checkable property: every term in the glossary is applied consistently; no sentence exceeds the character limit; every safety warning present in the source appears in the target; the target creates no obligation not present in the source; the reading level matches the agreed grade; every translator query is resolved or documented with a default. Tests like these can be verified by a reviewer in minutes and defended by the translator. Adjectives cannot. When a requester offers an adjective, translate it back into a test before accepting it.

### Separate Mandatory From Desirable Criteria

Not every quality dimension is equally important, and treating them all as mandatory produces a deliverable that can never ship. Distinguish the criteria that block delivery from those that improve it.

Mandatory criteria are the ones whose failure makes the deliverable unfit: a wrong dose in a medical text, a missing obligation in a contract, a broken UI string, an untranslated safety warning. Desirable criteria improve quality but do not block: a more elegant phrasing, a smoother register, a tighter headline. Mark each criterion as mandatory or desirable, and agree that desirable items may be deferred or declined without blocking delivery. This prevents a reviewer's stylistic preference from being treated as a correctness failure and stalling sign-off.

### Anchor Criteria To The Translation Objective

Criteria are not universal; they follow from the objective the translation must serve. A criterion set built for one objective will misjudge a translation built for another.

If the objective is legal equivalence, criteria center on obligation preservation, defined-term consistency, and completeness of clauses. If the objective is marketing conversion, criteria center on effect, brand voice, and character limits, with literal accuracy demoted. If the objective is safe operation, criteria center on completeness and clarity of warnings and procedures. Build the criterion set from the objective, and where the objective ranks objectives against each other, rank the criteria the same way. A criterion that contradicts the objective is a sign the criterion, not the translation, is wrong.

### Set Quantitative Thresholds Where They Exist

Many translation quality dimensions have established quantitative measures, and using them removes subjectivity from acceptance.

Define thresholds where applicable: a maximum error density under an error-weighted model, a maximum percentage of segments flagged by automated quality checks, a required consistency score against the termbase, a maximum character or line count for constrained formats, a required reading-level score for plain-language targets. State the threshold and the measurement method, because a threshold without a measurement method is still an adjective. Where industry models such as error-category severity frameworks apply, reference them so reviewers apply the same yardstick.

### Agree The Review And Sign-Off Process

Success criteria are meaningless without a process that applies them. Define how acceptance is determined.

Specify who reviews, against which criteria, in how many cycles, and what counts as a revision versus a new request. Distinguish linguistic review, subject-matter review, brand review, and legal review, and decide whether they run in parallel or sequence, because parallel reviews with conflicting criteria produce gridlock. Define what constitutes sign-off: a named authority confirming the mandatory criteria are met. Without a defined sign-off authority, the deliverable is never accepted; it merely stops being revised when everyone is tired. A reviewer who joins late and applies unstated criteria should be routed through the agreed process, not allowed to reopen acceptance.

### Define How Criteria Handle Source Limitations

A translation cannot meet criteria the source does not support. A source with contradictions, gaps, or ambiguity cannot yield a target that is simultaneously complete, consistent, and faithful, because the source is none of those things.

Build criteria that account for source reality: where the source is ambiguous, the criterion may be that ambiguity is preserved or flagged rather than resolved; where the source has gaps, the criterion may be that gaps are queried or noted rather than filled. Criteria that demand qualities the source lacks set the translator up to fail at no fault of their own. State explicitly how source limitations convert into target criteria, so the deliverable is judged against what was possible, not against an idealized source.

### Make Done A Single, Documented State

Completion should be a state the deliverable reaches, not a feeling the parties converge on. Define done as the conjunction of all mandatory criteria met, all queries resolved or defaulted, and sign-off received from the named authority.

Record the criteria, the measurement results, and the sign-off. This record is the translator's protection against reopened scope and the requester's assurance that the standard was applied. A project with no documented done state is a project that can be revised indefinitely at the requester's whim.

## Common Traps

### Using Adjectives As Criteria

Accurate, fluent, and natural mean different things to each reviewer and cannot be verified. Convert every adjective into a testable property before treating it as a criterion.

### Treating All Quality Dimensions As Mandatory

When every dimension blocks delivery, nothing ships. Separate mandatory from desirable, or a reviewer's preference will stall sign-off indefinitely.

### Borrowing Criteria From The Wrong Objective

Legal-equivalence criteria applied to marketing, or marketing criteria applied to a contract, misjudge a translation that is correct for its actual purpose. Build the criterion set from the objective.

### Setting Thresholds Without Measurement Methods

A maximum error rate means nothing if reviewers count errors differently. State the threshold and the method, or the threshold is decorative.

### Leaving Sign-Off Authority Undefined

Without a named sign-off authority, acceptance never formally occurs; it just fades out. Define who accepts and on what basis.

### Demanding Qualities The Source Lacks

Criteria that require completeness, consistency, or unambiguity from a source that has none guarantee failure. Align criteria with what the source can support.

### Letting Late Reviewers Apply Unstated Criteria

A reviewer who arrives late with their own standard reopens acceptance. Route them through the agreed criteria and authority rather than allowing silent re-scoping of done.

## Self-Check

Before approving a translation or declaring it deliverable, verify:

- Every acceptance criterion is stated as a testable property, not as an adjective.
- Criteria are separated into mandatory, which block delivery, and desirable, which do not.
- The criterion set is anchored to the translation objective and ranked consistently with it.
- Quantitative thresholds, where used, are paired with a defined measurement method or industry model.
- The review and sign-off process names reviewers, cycles, criteria, and a sign-off authority.
- Criteria account for source limitations, judging the target against what the source supports rather than an ideal.
- Done is defined as a single documented state: mandatory criteria met, queries resolved or defaulted, sign-off received.
- No criterion contradicts the objective or demands a quality the source cannot provide.
- A late reviewer applying unstated criteria would be routed through the agreed process, not allowed to reopen acceptance.
- The criteria, measurement results, and sign-off are recorded so completion is defensible.
