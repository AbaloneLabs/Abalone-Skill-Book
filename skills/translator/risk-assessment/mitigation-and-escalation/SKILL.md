# Mitigation And Escalation

Identifying risk is only half the work. Once a translator knows that a segment carries elevated stakes, an ambiguity, or a compliance exposure, they must decide what to do about it, and the available responses are not interchangeable. The wrong response can amplify the risk instead of reducing it: silently resolving a high-stakes ambiguity converts doubt into confident error; escalating every minor stylistic question swamps the review process until real risks are ignored; a disclaimer tacked onto a delivery does not undo a mistranslated dose. Mitigation and escalation are the discipline of matching the response to the risk, so that genuine hazards are reduced or routed upward while trivial issues are handled efficiently and do not consume the attention that hazards deserve.

Agents struggle here because escalation feels like admitting weakness and mitigation feels like extra work. The temptation is to resolve everything silently and present a clean deliverable, but a clean deliverable that has absorbed unresolved risk is the most dangerous kind, because its smoothness hides the hazards from everyone, including the relying party. Conversely, escalating indiscriminately trains requesters to ignore queries, so that when a genuinely critical query arrives it is treated as noise. Effective mitigation and escalation require judgment about which risks can be safely reduced in-house, which must be queried, which must be escalated to qualified authority, and which must be disclosed rather than resolved. The goal is a deliverable whose residual risk is conscious and documented, not unconscious and hidden.

Use this skill when deciding how to handle identified translation risks, when designing escalation pathways, when choosing between conservative handling and queries, or when determining what must be routed to qualified review before delivery. The goal is to ensure risk is reduced, routed, or disclosed rather than absorbed silently.

## Core Rules

### Match The Response To The Risk Level

Risk responses exist on a spectrum, and applying the wrong strength of response is itself an error. Calibrate the response to the consequence of being wrong.

Low-risk issues, such as obvious source typos in non-critical text, can be mitigated in-house with a silent or noted correction. Medium-risk issues, such as terminology gaps or register questions, warrant a documented decision plus a note for the reviewer. High-risk issues, such as ambiguity in a binding clause, a missing unit on a dose, or a contradiction affecting compliance, must be escalated to qualified authority before delivery. Critical issues, such as a safety warning whose meaning cannot be determined, must halt delivery until resolved. The calibration principle is that the cost and rigor of the response must scale with the consequence of the unresolved risk. Over-handling low-risk issues wastes effort; under-handling high-risk issues creates harm.

### Escalate When The Question Exceeds The Translator's Authority

Not every question a translator encounters is a translation question. Some are legal, medical, financial, or compliance questions wearing translation clothing, and resolving them as translation decisions exceeds authority.

When the real question is which reading of an ambiguous clause is legally correct, what a clinical instruction actually requires, or whether a regulatory claim is permitted, the translator must escalate rather than decide. Escalation routes the question to the authority that owns it: legal counsel for legal effect, a subject-matter expert for domain correctness, a compliance owner for regulatory wording, the requester for intent. Resolving an authority question under the guise of translation transfers a decision to someone unqualified to make it, and the transfer is the liability. When in doubt about whether a question is yours to answer, escalate it.

### Query For Intent, Escalate For Correctness

Queries and escalations serve different purposes and should not be confused. Use each for what it does.

A query asks the requester or source owner to supply missing intent or context: which of two readings did you mean, is this term the same as that one, should this product name be localized. Queries resolve information gaps the translator cannot fill. An escalation routes a correctness or authority question to a qualified reviewer: is this clause legally equivalent, is this dose instruction safe, does this wording satisfy the regulator. Escalations resolve judgment gaps the translator is not authorized to fill. Mixing them up wastes effort: querying a legal reviewer about intent they do not own, or escalating a terminology preference to counsel who cannot judge it. Route each issue to the party that can actually resolve it.

### Make Queries Specific And Answerable

An escalation or query that cannot be answered efficiently is a stalled project. Craft each one to be resolved quickly.

State the segment, the issue, the options considered, your best guess, and the specific decision you need. Offer a recommended resolution and ask for confirmation, because a yes or no is faster than an open-ended question. Batch related queries so the responder can answer efficiently rather than context-switching per item. A vague query such as "this is unclear" wastes a round-trip and returns an equally vague answer. A precise query such as "the pronoun here could refer to the company or the product; I chose the company because the next sentence continues that topic; please confirm" lets the responder resolve it in seconds.

### Track Every Escalated Item To Resolution

An escalated item that drifts unanswered becomes a silent assumption at delivery, and silent assumptions are where risk hides. Tracking is non-optional.

Maintain a query and escalation log with owner, status, and resolution for every item. Define a default rule for items that remain unanswered as the deadline approaches: proceed with a documented conservative assumption, extend the deadline, or withhold the affected segment. The default must be agreed in advance, not invented under pressure. An unresolved escalation that ships as a silent guess converts a managed risk into an unmanaged one, and the log is the only defense against that conversion.

### Use Conservative Handling When Escalation Is Unavailable

Sometimes escalation is not possible within the timeframe or the authority is unreachable. In those cases, mitigate through conservative handling and disclose the residual risk.

Conservative handling means choosing the rendering most likely to preserve safety, legal effect, or compliance, erring toward the stricter or more protective reading when stakes are high. It does not mean guessing freely; it means selecting the option that minimizes harm if wrong. Pair conservative handling with explicit disclosure: a translator's note stating that the segment was ambiguous, that the conservative reading was chosen, and that qualified review is recommended. Disclosure does not eliminate the risk, but it prevents the relying party from assuming the segment was unambiguous and authoritative.

### Disclose Rather Than Conceal Residual Risk

Some risks cannot be fully resolved before delivery, and the correct response is to disclose them rather than to hide them behind a smooth deliverable.

Where a segment remains ambiguous after query, where review was incomplete, where jurisdictional equivalence is uncertain, or where the source itself is defective, state the limitation in the deliverable. Disclosure takes forms such as translator's notes, bracketed uncertainty markers, a limitations section, or a delivery cover note. The principle is that the relying party must not be led to believe the translation is more authoritative or complete than it is. Concealing residual risk to make the deliverable look clean transfers the risk to an unsuspecting reader, which is the opposite of mitigation.

### Design The Escalation Pathway Before It Is Needed

Escalation under deadline pressure without a defined pathway produces ad-hoc decisions and lost items. Define the pathway when the project starts.

For each risk category, name the escalation owner, the channel, the target response time, and the default if no response arrives. Legal questions go to counsel; domain questions go to the subject-matter expert; regulatory questions go to the compliance owner; intent questions go to the requester. A pre-defined pathway means that when a high-risk segment appears mid-project, the response is a routing decision, not a panic.

## Common Traps

### Resolving High-Risk Issues Silently

Silent resolution of a high-stakes ambiguity or authority question converts managed risk into hidden error. Escalate or disclose instead.

### Escalating Indiscriminately

Escalating every minor issue trains requesters to ignore queries, so critical escalations are treated as noise. Reserve escalation for issues that genuinely exceed in-house authority.

### Confusing Queries With Escalations

Querying the wrong party, such as asking counsel about intent or the requester about legal correctness, wastes round-trips. Route each issue to the authority that can resolve it.

### Letting Escalations Drift Unanswered

An unresolved escalation that ships as a silent guess is an unmanaged risk. Track every item and apply a pre-agreed default for late answers.

### Using Disclaimers To Avoid Work

A disclaimer does not excuse a mistranslation or substitute for review that was feasible. Disclose residual risk, but do the mitigation and escalation work first.

### Guessing Freely Under Time Pressure

When escalation is unavailable, conservative handling means the most protective reading, not the most convenient one. Pair it with disclosure.

### Concealing Risk For A Cleaner Deliverable

Hiding residual risk behind smooth output transfers the hazard to an unsuspecting reader. Disclosure is a mitigation; concealment is the opposite.

## Self-Check

Before delivering a translation that involved identified risks, verify:

- Each identified risk received a response calibrated to its level, with response rigor scaling to consequence.
- Issues that exceeded the translator's authority were escalated to qualified authority rather than resolved as translation decisions.
- Queries were used for intent and context gaps, escalations for correctness and authority gaps, and each was routed to the party that could resolve it.
- Every query and escalation is specific, answerable, and tracked in a log to resolution or to a documented default.
- A default rule for unresolved items was agreed in advance and applied rather than invented under pressure.
- Where escalation was unavailable, conservative handling was paired with explicit disclosure of the residual risk.
- Residual risks that could not be fully resolved are disclosed in the deliverable, not concealed.
- The escalation pathway was defined at project start, with owners, channels, response times, and defaults per risk category.
- No high-risk segment ships as a silent guess, and the relying party would understand the deliverable's residual limitations.
