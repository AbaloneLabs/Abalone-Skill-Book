# Content Completeness Verification

Completeness is the most basic obligation of a translation, and one of the easiest to violate without noticing. A translation can be fluent, accurate in every word it contains, and still defective because something is missing: a sentence skipped, a list item dropped, a footnote forgotten, a caveat that vanished, a parenthetical that was absorbed into the next clause. Omissions happen for many reasons. Some are deliberate, where a translator judged a passage redundant or hard and quietly removed it. Most are accidental, produced by fatigue, by segmentation that hid a sentence, by copy-paste errors, or by the natural drift of attention across a long document. The reason completeness is so often missed is that a target-only read feels fine: the translation reads smoothly because the missing piece is not there to complain. Only a structured comparison against the source reveals the gap. Verification of completeness is therefore not a glance but a discipline, a systematic confirmation that every unit of source meaning has a counterpart in the target. This skill covers how to verify completeness at every level, from the whole document down to the element, and how to do it without relying on the unreliable feeling that nothing is missing.

Use this skill when performing a completeness check before delivery, when a translation must contain all source content, when verifying that nothing was omitted or merged, or when confirming coverage of sentences, clauses, list items, footnotes, and sections. The goal is to prove, not assume, that no source content was lost.

## Core Rules

### Treat Completeness As A Separate Pass From Fluency

Do not check completeness while reading for fluency, because the two modes compete. Fluency reading asks whether the target flows; completeness reading asks whether everything is there. A translator in fluency mode will glide over a gap because the surrounding text reads well.

Run completeness as its own pass, with a different question in mind: for each unit of source, where is its counterpart in the target? This separation is what catches omissions that a combined read misses. In high-stakes content, completeness verification should be performed by someone other than the translator, because the translator's familiarity with the text makes them fill gaps from memory.

### Verify At Every Level Of Granularity

Completeness fails at every level, and each level needs its own check. Do not assume that sentence-level completeness implies document-level completeness, or vice versa.

At the document level, confirm all sections, chapters, appendices, front matter, and back matter are present. At the section level, confirm every heading, subheading, paragraph, and block is translated. At the paragraph level, confirm every sentence is present. At the sentence level, confirm every clause is present, because a translator may merge two clauses into one and lose nuance. At the element level, confirm list items, table cells, footnotes, captions, callouts, and cross-references. Each level has its own failure mode: documents lose appendices, sections lose headings, paragraphs lose sentences, and lists lose items. Check each deliberately.

### Pay Special Attention To Omission-Prone Content

Some content is omitted far more often than prose. Know the high-risk categories and check them explicitly.

Footnotes and endnotes are frequently forgotten, because they sit outside the main flow and segmentation may separate them. List items get dropped when a translator works quickly or when formatting obscures the count. Table cells are misaligned or left empty, especially in complex tables. Captions and figure titles vanish because they look like decoration. Cross-references and internal links break or point to the wrong place. Parentheticals and asides are absorbed into the main clause. Caveats, exceptions, and conditions are dropped because they complicate the sentence. Defined terms and definitions are lost when they appear in a list. Numbers, units, dates, and names are transposed or omitted. Treat each of these as a category to verify, not as content that will obviously be present.

### Distinguish Acceptable Condensation From Unacceptable Omission

Not every reduction is an error. Some translation briefs allow condensation, summarization, or selective translation. Distinguish permitted reduction from loss.

Acceptable condensation occurs when the brief explicitly allows it, such as a summary translation, an abstract, or selective translation of only certain sections. It also occurs when the source contains genuinely redundant repetition that the target convention collapses, though even here the choice should be deliberate. Unacceptable omission occurs when content that carries meaning, obligation, or necessary information is dropped without authorization. The test is whether the missing content carries meaning the target reader needs. If it does, its absence is an error regardless of how smoothly the rest reads. When condensation is permitted, document its scope so reviewers do not flag permitted reductions as omissions.

### Check That Nothing Was Added Either

Completeness has an inverse: the target should contain no content the source did not. Added content is a completeness defect too, though it is often overlooked because additions usually improve fluency.

Additions happen when a translator supplies explanatory material, fills perceived gaps, or editorializes. Some explicitation is legitimate and even necessary, where the target reader needs information the source left implicit; this should be deliberate and, where extensive, noted. Illegitimate addition invents content the source did not imply, such as adding a claim, a caveat, or an example. Verify that target content traces to source meaning, and flag additions that go beyond legitimate explicitation. A translation that reads better than the source because it says more than the source is not faithful.

### Use Structural Mapping To Prove Coverage

For complex documents, use structural mapping to prove completeness rather than relying on impression. Map source structure to target structure and confirm each node has a counterpart.

This is especially valuable for manuals, legal documents, specifications, and any structured content. Confirm that source section 3.2.1 has a target 3.2.1, that list item seven has a target item seven, that table row four has a target row four. Structural mismatches reveal omissions, additions, and misalignments that prose reading misses. For tables and nested lists, verify cell by cell, because misalignment places correct content in the wrong location, which is a completeness defect even though no content is strictly missing.

### Verify Completeness After Every Revision

Completeness is not established once and done. Every revision, every round of correction, every post-editing pass can introduce new omissions. Re-verify after changes.

When a reviewer asks for changes, the correction can accidentally drop a clause or merge a list item. When machine translation is post-edited, an editor focused on fluency may delete a sentence that seemed redundant but carried meaning. Treat the completeness check as the final gate before delivery, run after all other revisions, so that late changes did not reopen the gap the earlier check closed.

## Common Traps

### Checking Completeness During The Fluency Read

Reading for flow and for coverage at the same time lets gaps slip through because the surrounding text reads well.

### Assuming Sentence Completeness Implies Document Completeness

Sentences can all be present while an appendix, footnote, or section is missing. Check every level.

### Forgetting Footnotes Captions And List Items

Content outside the main flow is omitted most often; check these categories explicitly.

### Treating Permitted Condensation As An Error

Some briefs allow summarization or selective translation; do not flag authorized reductions as omissions.

### Overlooking Added Content

Completeness includes absence of additions; target content that invents meaning the source lacked is a defect.

### Trusting Alignment In Tables And Lists

Misalignment places correct words in wrong cells; verify cell by cell, not by impression.

### Skipping Re-Verification After Revisions

Late corrections and post-editing can reintroduce omissions; run the completeness check last.

## Self-Check

Before approving a translation on completeness grounds, verify:

- Completeness was checked as a separate pass from fluency, ideally by a reviewer other than the translator.
- Every level of granularity was verified: document, section, paragraph, sentence, clause, and element.
- Omission-prone content, footnotes, list items, table cells, captions, cross-references, parentheticals, caveats, and defined terms, was checked explicitly.
- Any condensation is authorized by the brief and documented, and no meaning-carrying content was dropped without permission.
- No illegitimate content was added; explicitation is deliberate and traces to source meaning.
- Structural mapping confirmed that every source section, list item, and table cell has a correctly placed target counterpart.
- Completeness was re-verified after the final revision, so late changes did not reintroduce gaps.
- The verification proves coverage rather than assuming it from the feeling that nothing is missing.
