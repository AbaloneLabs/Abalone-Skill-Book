# Source Domain And Register Identification

Every text belongs to a domain, a register, and a genre, and these three dimensions determine how it should be translated. A medical journal article, a patient-facing leaflet, a marketing brochure, and a regulatory filing may all discuss the same drug, but they demand completely different terminology, tone, syntax, and adaptation freedom. The domain determines what specialized terms mean and which equivalents are valid. The register determines the formality, complexity, and voice the target must match. The genre determines the conventions, structure, and reader expectations that shape the translation. A translator who does not identify these dimensions before translating applies a single undifferentiated strategy, and the result reads wrong even when the words are individually correct: a patient leaflet translated in journal register is incomprehensible to patients, a marketing text translated in technical register loses all persuasive force, and a regulatory clause translated in casual register loses its legal authority. Domain and register identification is the act of reading the source as a communicative act with a specific purpose, audience, and convention, and choosing a translation strategy that serves the same act in the target language.

Use this skill when identifying the domain, register, or genre of a source before translation, recognizing the audience and formality, detecting mixed domains or register shifts, or aligning translation strategy with the source's communicative purpose. The goal is to ensure the translation serves the same function for the same kind of reader, with terminology, tone, and conventions matched to the source's communicative intent.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Domain And Specialization

The domain is the subject field the text belongs to, and it determines terminology, conceptual precision, and the equivalents that are valid. Identify it precisely.

Determine whether the text is medical, legal, financial, technical, scientific, marketing, literary, journalistic, administrative, or another field, and identify the specialization within it. Medical text may be clinical, pharmaceutical, regulatory, or patient-facing, and each uses different terminology. Legal text may be contractual, statutory, judicial, or regulatory, each with different conventions. The more precisely the domain and specialization are identified, the more accurately terminology can be researched and the better the translator can anticipate the concepts and conventions involved.

A vague domain identification, such as just medical or just legal, misses the specialization that determines which terminology is correct.

### Determine The Register And Formality

Register is the level of formality and stylistic complexity the text uses, shaped by the relationship between writer and reader. Determine it and plan to match it.

Registers range from highly formal and specialized, such as academic and statutory text, through neutral professional registers like journalism and standard documentation, to informal and conversational registers like marketing and social content. Register affects vocabulary choice, sentence complexity, use of jargon, directness, and tone. A formal register uses precise terminology, complex syntax, and impersonal voice; an informal register uses accessible vocabulary, shorter sentences, and direct address. Identify the source register and plan the target to match, because register mismatch makes a translation feel wrong even when accurate.

Translating a formal source into casual register, or vice versa, violates the communicative relationship the text establishes.

### Recognize The Genre And Its Conventions

Genre is the category of text with its own conventions, structure, and reader expectations. Recognize it, because genre shapes how the translation should read.

Genres include contracts, manuals, journal articles, patents, news reports, marketing copy, literary fiction, social media posts, and official notices. Each genre has conventions: a contract uses defined terms and operative language; a manual uses imperative procedures and warnings; a journal article uses passive voice and citations; marketing uses persuasive devices and brand voice. The target must follow the conventions of the same genre in the target language, because readers expect a contract to read like a contract and a manual to read like a manual.

Ignoring genre produces translations that are individually accurate but fail to function as their genre requires.

### Identify The Intended Audience

Domain, register, and genre all serve an audience, and identifying that audience sharpens every translation decision. Identify who the text is for.

Determine whether the reader is an expert, a professional, a layperson, a patient, a consumer, a regulator, a child, or a general public audience. Expert audiences expect precise terminology and can handle complexity; lay audiences need accessible language and explanation. The audience determines how much jargon is acceptable, how much explanation is needed, and what cultural and background knowledge can be assumed. A text written for experts may need adaptation when its audience becomes laypeople in the target, or it may need to preserve its expert register if the target audience matches the source.

Translating for the wrong audience produces text that is either patronizingly simple or impossibly dense for the actual reader.

### Detect Mixed Domains And Register Shifts

Many texts are not uniform. A single document may shift domains or registers, and the translator must detect and handle each shift. Do not assume uniformity.

A product document may combine technical specifications, marketing language, and legal disclaimers, each requiring different treatment. A medical article may have a clinical body and a patient-facing abstract. A contract may have formal operative clauses and more readable schedules. Detect where the domain or register shifts, and adjust the translation strategy accordingly, so each section is rendered in the register and terminology appropriate to its function. Treating a mixed text as uniform flattens the shifts that carry communicative meaning.

### Align Translation Strategy With Communicative Purpose

Domain, register, genre, and audience together define the text's communicative purpose, and the translation strategy must serve that purpose. Align them.

A text whose purpose is to persuade, like marketing, may warrant transcreation rather than literal translation. A text whose purpose is to inform accurately, like a manual, warrants precise procedural translation. A text whose purpose is to create legal obligation, like a contract, warrants conservative literal translation preserving every operative element. A text whose purpose is aesthetic, like literature, warrants literary translation preserving voice and effect. Choose the strategy, the degree of freedom, the terminology approach, and the register target that serve the same communicative purpose for the target reader.

### Verify Domain And Register Against Evidence

Domain and register identification should be grounded in evidence from the text, not assumption. Verify against what the source actually shows.

Check the terminology used, which signals the domain and specialization. Check the syntax and vocabulary complexity, which signal register. Check the structure and conventions, which signal genre. Check paratextual cues like the publication venue, author affiliation, and stated audience. Where the text's signals conflict with the stated or assumed domain, investigate, because a text labeled technical may prove to be marketing in technical clothing, requiring a different strategy.

Assumed domain and register that are not verified against the text produce strategies that fit the label but not the actual content.

## Common Traps

### Vague Domain Identification

Identifying the field without the specialization misses the terminology precision that determines correct equivalents.

### Register Mismatch

Translating formal source into casual register, or vice versa, violates the writer-reader relationship the text establishes.

### Ignoring Genre Conventions

Individually accurate translation that ignores genre conventions fails to function as a contract, manual, or article should.

### Translating For The Wrong Audience

Text translated for experts when the audience is lay, or for laypeople when the audience is expert, misses the reader entirely.

### Treating Mixed Texts As Uniform

Flattening domain and register shifts in a mixed document removes the communicative distinctions the shifts carry.

### Applying One Strategy Regardless Of Purpose

Using literal translation for persuasive text or transcreation for legal text serves neither text's communicative purpose.

### Assuming Domain Without Verifying

Domain and register assumed from a label rather than verified against the text produce a strategy that fits the label, not the content.

## Self-Check

Before approving a translation strategy based on source analysis, verify:

- The domain and specialization are identified precisely, determining the terminology research and conceptual framework.
- The register and formality are determined, with the target planned to match the writer-reader relationship.
- The genre is recognized, with target conventions planned to match how that genre reads in the target language.
- The intended audience is identified, with jargon, explanation, and assumed knowledge calibrated to the actual reader.
- Mixed domains and register shifts within the document are detected, with strategy adjusted per section.
- The translation strategy, degree of freedom, terminology approach, and register target align with the text's communicative purpose.
- Domain and register are verified against textual evidence, not assumed from a label.
- No text is treated as uniform when it shifts domain or register.
- No formal source is casualized, no expert text is simplified for the wrong audience, and no genre convention is ignored.
- The translation will serve the same communicative purpose for the same kind of reader as the source.
