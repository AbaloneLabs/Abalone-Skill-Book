# Source Comprehension And Context

Translation begins with comprehension, and comprehension is where most translation errors originate. A translator who has not fully understood the source will produce target text that reads fluently but says the wrong thing. Fluency masks the error, because the target reader, who cannot see the source, has no signal that the meaning drifted. The danger is greatest when the source looks easy. Apparent simplicity hides implicit context, genre conventions, cultural assumptions, and references that the source reader took for granted but the target reader does not share. Thorough source comprehension is the non-negotiable first stage of any translation, and it must be done deliberately, not assumed.

Use this skill when reading a source before translating, when the source contains implicit or incomplete meaning, when references or pronouns are ambiguous, when the genre shapes interpretation, or when the agent must confirm full understanding before drafting. The goal is to prevent confident mistranslation, where fluent output conceals a comprehension failure.

## Core Rules

### Understand Before Translating

Do not begin drafting until the source meaning is understood at the level required to defend every choice. Reading the source once and starting to translate is how comprehension errors enter silently.

Work in stages. First, read the whole document to grasp purpose, audience, genre, and structure. Second, read each section to understand argument, narrative, or procedure. Third, read each sentence closely before translating it, resolving references and ambiguities. If a sentence is not fully understood, stop and investigate rather than translating the words and hoping.

A sentence-level translation without document-level comprehension produces locally correct but globally incoherent text.

### Reconstruct Implicit Context

Source texts rely on context the source reader shares but the translator and target reader may not. Identify what is implicit and decide whether to make it explicit.

Implicit context includes the assumed knowledge of the reader, the institutional or cultural setting, prior events referenced indirectly, shared legal or regulatory frameworks, and conventions of the genre. A business letter assumes a commercial relationship. A legal judgment assumes a procedural history. A marketing text assumes brand awareness. A technical manual assumes a product context.

List the implicit assumptions that affect meaning. For each, decide whether the target reader needs it made explicit, whether a brief gloss suffices, or whether leaving it implicit preserves the source effect.

### Resolve References And Pronouns

References and pronouns are a frequent source of comprehension error. The source may use pronouns, demonstratives, ellipsis, or relative clauses whose antecedent is not obvious, especially across sentence boundaries or in languages that drop subjects.

For each ambiguous reference, identify the antecedent explicitly before translating. In languages with grammatical gender or formal and informal address, confirm who or what a pronoun refers to. Watch for zero anaphora in languages that omit subjects, where the translator must supply the subject in a language that requires it.

Record resolved references so they stay consistent. A pronoun resolved one way in paragraph one and another way in paragraph three breaks coherence.

### Identify Genre And Its Conventions

Genre shapes how meaning is constructed. A clause in a contract means something different from the same words in a marketing brochure or a news report. Genre determines what is literal, what is conventional, what is performative, and what is persuasive.

Identify the genre before translating. Legal documents use defined terms and performative language. Technical documents use procedural precision. Marketing uses persuasion and emotion. Literature uses imagery and voice. News uses attribution and neutrality. Each genre has conventions that the translation should reflect.

Misreading genre produces translations that are word-accurate but functionally wrong. A contract clause translated with marketing freedom changes obligations. A poem translated with legal literalism loses the poem.

### Check Domain Knowledge

Many comprehension failures are not linguistic but domain-related. The translator may parse every word and still misunderstand because the subject is unfamiliar.

For specialized domains such as law, medicine, finance, engineering, and theology, confirm understanding of the concepts, not just the words. Use domain references, parallel texts in the target language, and subject-matter expert queries. A term may have a precise technical meaning that differs from its everyday meaning, and translating the everyday meaning is an error even though the words match.

When domain knowledge is insufficient, flag the limitation and recommend expert review rather than translating from partial understanding.

### Watch For Intentional Ambiguity And Wordplay

Sometimes ambiguity is intentional. Poetry, advertising, diplomacy, and humor often rely on double meaning, puns, or strategic vagueness. Translating such text as if it had a single clear meaning destroys the source effect.

Identify intentional ambiguity before choosing a strategy. Options include preserving the ambiguity in the target, choosing the primary meaning and losing the secondary, recreating a parallel effect with different target-language resources, or adding a note. The choice depends on purpose and audience.

Do not resolve intentional ambiguity silently. That is a comprehension success that becomes a translation failure.

### Verify Numbers Dates Names And Quoted Material

Factual elements carry meaning and risk. Verify them, do not assume them.

Numbers, dates, quantities, units, names, titles, citations, and quoted material should be checked against the source and against reality where possible. A misread number in a dose, a deadline, a price, or a measurement can cause harm. A misspelled name or wrong title can cause offense or legal issue. Quoted material may need to match an existing target-language version rather than be retranslated.

Treat factual elements as high-stakes even when the surrounding text is casual.

### Use Parallel Texts To Confirm Understanding

Parallel texts, existing target-language documents on the same topic, help confirm both meaning and conventional phrasing. Reading how target-language professionals write about the same subject reveals whether your understanding matches the domain.

Use parallel texts to verify terminology, to see how concepts are conventionally expressed, and to detect when your literal rendering would sound foreign to the target reader. Parallel texts are references, not sources to copy. Use them to inform judgment, not to replace it.

## Common Traps

### Translating Word By Word Without Sentence Understanding

Rendering each word as it comes produces grammatical target text that misses the meaning of the sentence as a whole.

### Assuming The Obvious Reading

The obvious reading is often right, but when it is wrong, the error is invisible to the target reader. Verify the non-obvious cases.

### Ignoring Genre

The same words mean different things in different genres. Reading everything as neutral prose misreads legal, literary, and persuasive text.

### Resolving Ambiguity Silently

When the source is deliberately ambiguous, forcing a single meaning loses the source's effect and may change its function.

### Trusting Everyday Meaning For Technical Terms

A word with a precise domain meaning translated by its everyday meaning is wrong even though the dictionary supports the everyday sense.

### Skipping Factual Verification

Numbers, dates, names, and citations are easy to skim past and easy to get wrong. Verify them as content, not as formatting.

### Translating Without Document-Level Understanding

Sentence-level correctness without grasp of the whole produces incoherent text where later segments contradict earlier ones.

## Self-Check

Before drafting or approving a translation, verify:

- The whole source document has been read for purpose, audience, genre, and structure before sentence-level work.
- Each sentence is fully understood before it is translated, not translated in hope of emerging meaning.
- Implicit context affecting meaning has been identified, with decisions on what to make explicit for the target reader.
- References, pronouns, ellipsis, and zero anaphora have been resolved and recorded for consistency.
- Genre has been identified and its conventions inform the translation strategy.
- Domain knowledge is sufficient for the subject, or the limitation is flagged with a recommendation for expert review.
- Intentional ambiguity and wordplay have been identified and handled deliberately, not resolved silently.
- Numbers, dates, names, titles, citations, and quoted material have been verified against source and reality.
- Parallel texts have been consulted where domain or conventional phrasing is uncertain.
- No segment has been translated from partial comprehension, especially in specialized or high-stakes content.
