# Client And Stakeholder Context

A source text does not arrive in a vacuum. It arrives from a client, for an audience, inside an organization, alongside existing assets, under constraints the translator cannot see. Two translations of the same source can both be linguistically correct and one can be wrong, because it ignores the context the client lives in. A brand's voice, accumulated across years of marketing, demands consistency the source alone cannot reveal. A prior glossary, built for this product line, fixes terminology that the translator would otherwise choose fresh. A stakeholder hierarchy determines whose feedback is authoritative and whose is preference. A regulatory history explains why certain wording must not change. Client and stakeholder context is the situational information that surrounds the source and shapes what a correct translation means for this client, this audience, this engagement. Without it, the translator optimizes for a generic reader and produces a target that is accurate in the abstract and wrong in the specific.

Agents skip client context gathering because the source feels self-sufficient and asking questions feels like friction. But the source is only the visible tip of an engagement that includes history, assets, constraints, and politics. The translator who translates the source without gathering that surrounding context is guessing at variables the client could have supplied, and the guesses surface as rework: terminology that conflicts with the existing termbase, tone that breaks the brand voice, formatting that ignores established conventions, feedback that contradicts because the stakeholders were never aligned. Context gathering is the work of surfacing the situational variables before they become delivery disputes. The goal is not to interrogate the client but to collect the context that lets the translation fit the engagement rather than merely fit the source.

Use this skill when starting an engagement, when a translation must fit a brand or organization, when prior assets and constraints may exist, or when mapping who the work is for and who decides. The goal is to ground translation choices in the client's actual situation rather than in assumptions about it.

## Core Rules

### Gather Brand Voice And Tone Conventions

A client's brand voice is an asset built over time, and a translation that breaks it feels off even when the words are correct. Gather the voice before translating brand-sensitive content.

Ask for or locate the brand voice guidelines, tone-of-voice documents, and examples of previously published target-language content. Brand voice includes formality level, sentence rhythm, pronoun choice, humor tolerance, and emotional register, and these vary by locale even within one brand. Where guidelines do not exist, request examples of content the client considers on-brand in the target language, and derive the voice conventions from them. A translation that is linguistically accurate but tonally wrong fails the brand objective, and the failure is invisible until the brand owner reads it. Voice is context the source does not carry, because the source is one instance of a voice defined across many.

### Locate Prior Assets: Glossaries, Style Guides, Translation Memory

Most clients who translate regularly have accumulated assets that the current translation must align with. Failing to use them produces inconsistency the client will catch at review.

Ask whether a termbase, style guide, translation memory, or previously translated reference material exists for this product, domain, or locale. A termbase fixes terminology; ignoring it creates conflicts the reviewer must resolve. A style guide fixes conventions such as date formats, capitalization, and register; ignoring it produces a target that looks unfamiliar. Translation memory provides prior approved renderings that should be reused for consistency and efficiency. Prior assets are the client's accumulated decisions, and aligning with them is how a translation fits the organization rather than standing alone. Where assets exist, request them before drafting; where they do not, offer to help create them, because their absence is itself a risk.

### Map The Audience Beyond The Abstract Language

A translation for an abstract language is a translation for no one. The audience is specific, and its specifics shape the translation.

Identify who will actually read the target: their locale, expertise, age, accessibility needs, reading situation, and cultural assumptions. The same source translated for clinicians differs from translation for patients; for regulators differs from translation for consumers; for experts differs from translation for laypeople. Audience specifics drive register, terminology depth, explanation level, and cultural adaptation. Ask the client who the reader is and what they must be able to do after reading. An audience described only as Spanish speakers or English readers is underspecified; push for the reader's actual context, because translating for an underspecified audience produces a target aimed at no real reader.

### Identify The Decision Authority And Feedback Structure

Knowing who decides is as important as knowing who reads, because the wrong authority applying feedback derails the deliverable. Map the decision structure.

Identify who has authority over terminology, brand voice, legal wording, technical accuracy, and final sign-off. Identify who reviews and in what sequence, and how conflicting feedback is reconciled. A client with no clear decision authority produces endless revision, because each reviewer's feedback feels mandatory. Establishing authority upfront means that when feedback conflicts, the resolution is structural rather than a guess. Also identify in-country reviewers, who are valuable but often lack the brief and constraints; give them the context they need to review against the agreed standards rather than their personal preferences. Decision structure is context that prevents the translator from becoming the battleground for unresolved stakeholder conflict.

### Understand Organizational And Regulatory Constraints

Clients operate under constraints that shape what the translation may and may not do, and these constraints are rarely visible in the source. Surface them.

Ask about regulatory requirements that mandate wording, legal review gates that must be passed, compliance constraints on claims or disclosures, accessibility requirements, and organizational policies on naming, trademarks, or sensitive content. A translation that ignores a regulatory mandate is non-compliant however accurate. A translation that changes a trademarked term violates brand policy. Constraints are the boundaries within which correctness is defined, and a translation correct in the abstract can be wrong because it crossed a constraint the client never mentioned but assumed. Constraint gathering is how the translator learns the rules of the field they are playing on.

### Capture The Engagement's History And Prior Feedback

The current translation is often one episode in an ongoing relationship, and prior feedback carries forward. Capture the history.

Ask whether this client or product has been translated before, what feedback arose, what issues recurred, and what decisions were made. Prior feedback reveals preferences and pitfalls that the source cannot: a client who repeatedly rejects formal register, a reviewer who insists on a particular term, a locale where a certain phrasing caused offense. History is context that prevents repeating resolved mistakes. Where the engagement is ongoing, maintain a decisions log that carries forward, so each project inherits the prior one's lessons rather than rediscovering them.

### Distinguish What The Client Stated From What You Assumed

Context gathering produces both confirmed facts and assumptions, and conflating them causes errors. Keep them separate.

Record what the client explicitly stated, what you inferred from examples or assets, and what you assumed by default. Confirm inferences and assumptions with the client before treating them as fact, because an unconfirmed assumption treated as fact becomes a silent error. A context brief that distinguishes confirmed from assumed lets the client correct misunderstandings cheaply before drafting, rather than expensively at review.

## Common Traps

### Translating The Source Without Gathering Surrounding Context

The source is one instance of a larger engagement. Translating it in isolation ignores brand, assets, audience, and constraints the client could have supplied.

### Ignoring Prior Assets

Termbases, style guides, and translation memory encode accumulated decisions. Ignoring them creates inconsistencies the reviewer must fix.

### Translating For An Abstract Language

A translation for Spanish speakers or English readers is aimed at no real reader. Specify the audience's locale, expertise, and reading situation.

### Leaving Decision Authority Undefined

Without clear authority, every reviewer's feedback feels mandatory and revision never converges. Map who decides what before drafting.

### Missing Regulatory And Organizational Constraints

Constraints define the boundaries of correctness. A translation correct in the abstract can be wrong because it crossed a constraint the client assumed.

### Discarding Prior Feedback

Prior feedback reveals recurring preferences and pitfalls. An engagement that ignores history repeats resolved mistakes.

### Treating Assumptions As Confirmed Facts

An unconfirmed assumption treated as fact becomes a silent error. Distinguish what the client stated from what you inferred or assumed.

## Self-Check

Before drafting or approving a translation for a specific client, verify:

- Brand voice and tone conventions were gathered from guidelines or prior on-brand examples for the target locale.
- Prior assets, including termbase, style guide, translation memory, and reference material, were requested and reviewed where they exist.
- The audience is specified beyond an abstract language, including locale, expertise, reading situation, and accessibility needs.
- Decision authority is mapped for terminology, brand, legal, technical, and final sign-off, with a reconciliation path for conflicting feedback.
- Regulatory, legal, compliance, accessibility, and organizational constraints were surfaced and recorded.
- Engagement history and prior feedback were captured, with a decisions log carrying forward for ongoing work.
- The context brief distinguishes what the client stated from what was inferred or assumed, with assumptions flagged for confirmation.
- In-country reviewers, if present, have the brief and constraints needed to review against agreed standards.
- No translation choice rests on an unconfirmed assumption about brand, audience, or constraint.
- The translation fits the client's situation, not merely the source text in isolation.
