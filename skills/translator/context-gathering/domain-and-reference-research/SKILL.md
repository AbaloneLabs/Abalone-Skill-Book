# Domain And Reference Research

Translation is not a word-level activity; it is a meaning-level activity, and meaning in specialized domains cannot be guessed. A translator who encounters a clinical term, a financial instrument, an engineering specification, or a legal doctrine and renders it from general knowledge is producing a target that sounds plausible and may be wrong in ways that matter. Domains have their own terminology, their own conceptual architecture, and their own conventions, and these are not recoverable from fluency alone. Domain and reference research is the work of building a verified understanding of the source's subject matter before and during translation, gathering authoritative references, and grounding terminology and concept choices in evidence rather than assumption. Without it, the translator is improvising in a domain they do not command, and the improvisation is invisible until a domain expert reads the result and finds it wrong.

Agents skip domain research because the source often reads comprehensibly. A medical report can be read and roughly understood by a fluent layperson; the danger is that rough understanding is not enough to translate safely. A term that has a precise clinical meaning may resemble an everyday word, and translating it as the everyday word produces a target that is fluent and clinically wrong. Research is what closes the gap between lay comprehension and domain command. It is not optional decoration before translation; it is the foundation that determines whether the translation is correct. The goal of research is not to become a domain expert but to verify, for each domain-critical term and concept, that the chosen rendering matches the domain's actual usage.

Use this skill when translating specialized content, when a source belongs to a domain the translator does not fully command, when validating terminology choices, or when building a reference base to defend translation decisions. The goal is to ensure domain-sensitive choices rest on authoritative evidence rather than on general-language intuition.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Domain And Its Sub-Domain Precisely

Research begins by identifying exactly what domain the source belongs to, because domains have sub-domains with different terminology. A generic identification is not enough.

A source is not merely medical; it is cardiology, or pediatrics, or pharmacovigilance. Not merely legal; it is contract law, or data privacy regulation, or criminal procedure. Not merely financial; it is derivatives, or retail banking, or tax accounting. The sub-domain determines which terminology applies, because the same word can mean different things across sub-domains. Identify the sub-domain from the source's content, its stated purpose, and its intended audience, and let that identification guide which references are authoritative. Researching the wrong sub-domain produces confidently wrong terminology.

### Gather Authoritative References Before Translating Domain-Critical Terms

Domain terminology must be grounded in authoritative references, not in general dictionaries or memory. Gather the references before rendering domain-critical terms.

Authoritative references vary by domain: for medical content, terminologies such as ICD, MeSH, or domain-specific glossaries; for legal content, jurisdiction-specific legal dictionaries, statutes, and defined-term registers; for technical content, standards documents, manufacturer glossaries, and industry specifications; for financial content, regulatory definitions and accounting standards. Build a reference set for the engagement, prioritizing sources that are authoritative in the target locale, because a term authoritative in one locale may differ in another. General bilingual dictionaries are a starting point, not an authority; they frequently omit domain-specific senses. Rendering a domain-critical term without an authoritative reference is a guess, and guesses in specialized domains are how fluent errors are made.

### Verify Concept Equivalence, Not Just Word Equivalence

A correct domain translation matches the concept, not just the word, and concept equivalence is harder to verify than word equivalence. Check it deliberately.

A source term and a candidate target term may be dictionary equivalents while referring to different concepts. A legal term in one jurisdiction may have no exact equivalent in another, because the underlying institution does not exist. A medical term may name a condition defined differently across classification systems. A technical term may refer to a component that differs across standards. For each domain-critical term, verify that the candidate target refers to the same concept, not merely that it is a common translation. Where no equivalent exists, the choice is among explanation, transliteration with gloss, or preserving the source term, and that choice should be deliberate and referenced, not a default word swap.

### Research The Source Author's Usage And Conventions

Beyond the domain's general terminology, the specific source may use terms in particular ways, and matching the source's conventions is part of fidelity. Research the source's own usage.

Check whether the source uses terms consistently, whether it defines terms, and whether it follows a particular style or standard. A source that references a specific standard should be translated to match that standard's target-language terminology, where it exists. A source that uses a term idiosyncratically should have that idiosyncrasy recognized, so the translation does not normalize it into a different concept. Author usage research also catches cases where the source itself deviates from domain convention, which is a source quality issue to flag rather than a usage to propagate.

### Build A Reusable Domain Evidence Base

Research done for one engagement is valuable for the next, and discarding it wastes effort. Capture research in a reusable form.

Record verified term equivalences, with their references, in a termbase or glossary that can be reused across projects in the same domain. Annotate each entry with the source, the target, the domain and sub-domain, the reference that supports it, and any conditions on its use. A reusable evidence base accelerates future work, enforces consistency, and provides a defensible record of why each term was chosen. Research performed but not captured is research that must be repeated, and the repetition introduces inconsistency as much as it wastes time.

### Recognize The Limits Of Research And Escalate

Research has limits, and some domain questions exceed what research can resolve. Recognize when a question requires a domain expert rather than more searching.

When a term is genuinely ambiguous, when a concept has no clear equivalent, when the source's meaning depends on domain knowledge research cannot verify, or when the stakes are high, escalate to a subject-matter expert rather than forcing a choice. Research reduces uncertainty; it does not eliminate it, and in high-stakes domains the residual uncertainty must be routed to someone with domain authority. Treating research as a substitute for expert review in critical content is overconfidence, and overconfidence in specialized domains causes harm.

### Time-Box Research Against Stakes And Deadline

Research can expand indefinitely, and a translator can disappear into investigation while the deadline approaches. Manage research effort against the stakes.

Prioritize research toward the highest-stakes and most domain-critical terms and concepts, where error is most consequential. For lower-stakes content, accept reasonable, referenced choices without exhaustive investigation. Set a research budget proportionate to the engagement, and when the budget is exhausted, move to translation with the evidence gathered, escalating the remaining uncertainties. Unbounded research delays delivery without proportionate quality gain; the discipline is to research enough to be correct where it matters and to move on where it does not.

## Common Traps

### Trusting Lay Comprehension As Domain Command

A source that reads comprehensibly is not necessarily understood at the level needed to translate. Lay comprehension produces fluent but domain-wrong renderings.

### Using General Dictionaries For Domain Terms

General bilingual dictionaries omit domain-specific senses and are a starting point, not an authority. Use domain-specific references for domain-critical terms.

### Equating Words Instead Of Concepts

Dictionary-equivalent words can refer to different concepts across domains and jurisdictions. Verify concept equivalence, especially where institutions or standards differ.

### Ignoring Sub-Domain Differences

The same word can mean different things across sub-domains. Identify the precise sub-domain before researching terminology.

### Discarding Research After One Project

Research not captured in a reusable termbase must be repeated, introducing inconsistency. Record verified equivalences with references for reuse.

### Forcing A Choice When Research Cannot Resolve

Some domain questions need an expert, not more searching. Escalate high-stakes ambiguities rather than overconfidently resolving them.

### Letting Research Consume The Deadline

Unbounded research delays delivery. Prioritize toward high-stakes terms, set a budget, and move on with documented residual uncertainties.

## Self-Check

Before approving domain-sensitive translation choices, verify:

- The source's domain and precise sub-domain were identified before terminology research began.
- Authoritative, domain-specific references were gathered for domain-critical terms, prioritizing sources authoritative in the target locale.
- Concept equivalence was verified, not just word equivalence, especially where institutions, standards, or classifications differ.
- The source author's own usage and conventions were researched, including adherence to specific standards.
- Verified equivalences are recorded in a reusable termbase with source, target, domain, reference, and conditions.
- Questions exceeding research limits were escalated to a subject-matter expert rather than forced.
- Research effort was time-boxed against stakes and deadline, prioritizing high-consequence terms.
- No domain-critical term rests on general-language intuition or an unverified dictionary equivalent.
- Where no equivalent exists, the handling choice, explanation, transliteration, or preservation, is deliberate and referenced.
- A domain expert reviewing the target would find the terminology consistent with domain usage, not merely fluent.
