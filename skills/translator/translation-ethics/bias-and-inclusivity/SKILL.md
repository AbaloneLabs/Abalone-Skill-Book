# Bias And Inclusivity

Translation is not a neutral transfer of words; it carries and can amplify the biases embedded in source content, and it introduces biases of its own through the choices the translator makes. Gendered language that the source uses generically may become exclusionary when rendered in a language with grammatical gender. Terms that are neutral in one culture carry derogatory or loaded meanings in another. Identity-related language around race, disability, age, religion, or sexuality varies widely across locales, and a direct translation can misrepresent, offend, or erase the people it describes. Inclusive language in one language may have no direct equivalent in another, forcing creative solutions. The translator stands at a decision point with every such term: render it literally and import the bias, adapt it to the target locale's inclusive norms, or flag it for the client. These decisions affect whether the translation welcomes or alienates its audience, represents people accurately or stereotypically, and respects or violates the evolving standards of the communities it addresses. Bias and inclusivity in translation require deliberate attention, because the default of literal transfer often perpetuates harm that a thoughtful adaptation could avoid.

Use this skill when translating or reviewing content for bias and inclusivity, handling identity-related language, or deciding how to render terms that carry bias across locales. The goal is translations that are accurate, respectful, and inclusive of the audiences they address, without importing or amplifying bias.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Translation Carries And Amplifies Bias

Translation is not neutral regarding bias. Recognize your role in carrying or challenging it.

Source content may contain biases: gendered generics, stereotypes, ableist or ageist language, culturally loaded terms, or outdated identity language. A literal translation imports these biases into the target, where they may land even more heavily due to linguistic or cultural differences. Additionally, the translator's own choices, such as defaulting to masculine forms in a gendered language, introduce bias. Recognize that every choice around biased or identity-related language has consequences for the audience, and approach these choices deliberately rather than by default. Neutrality is not achieved by ignoring bias; it is achieved by thoughtful handling.

### Handle Gendered Language Deliberately

Gendered language is a core inclusivity challenge, especially in grammatically gendered languages. Handle it deliberately.

Many languages mark gender on nouns, pronouns, adjectives, and articles, and the source may use masculine forms as generics, which can exclude women and non-binary people. Where the target language allows, use neutral or inclusive alternatives: epicene forms, restructured phrasing, or inclusive conventions where accepted. Where the source specifies a person's gender, render it accurately. Where the source uses a generic and the target requires a gendered form, consider the audience and the locale's evolving norms; some locales accept inclusive forms, others find them non-standard, and the choice should serve the audience. Do not default to masculine without considering the exclusionary effect.

Follow the locale's accepted inclusive conventions, and stay current, as these evolve.

### Adapt Identity-Related Language To The Locale

Identity language around race, ethnicity, disability, age, religion, and sexuality varies by locale. Adapt accordingly.

Terms for racial or ethnic groups, disability, age, or sexuality that are current and respectful in one locale may be unknown, outdated, or offensive in another. The respectful term in the source's culture may not translate directly; the target locale has its own terminology, history, and sensitivities. Research the target locale's accepted terminology, ideally with input from the community or native reviewers. Do not assume that a term neutral in the source is neutral in the target, or that a term respectful in one locale is respectful in another. Where the source uses outdated or loaded language, consider whether to render it faithfully, adapt it to current respectful usage, or flag it.

Identity language is not fixed; stay current with the communities' preferred terminology.

### Address Culturally Loaded And Stereotyping Language

Terms and portrayals can carry cultural loading or stereotypes. Address them.

A term neutral in the source culture may be derogatory or stereotyping in the target, or a source portrayal may rely on stereotypes that the target audience finds offensive. Assess culturally loaded terms for their effect in the target locale, and adapt or flag them. Watch for stereotypes in descriptions of people, places, or cultures, and consider whether a literal translation reinforces them. For content that portrays the target's own culture, ensure the portrayal is respectful and accurate, and flag concerns to the client. Cultural loading is often invisible to outsiders, so native review is valuable.

### Do Not Import Bias Absent From The Source

The translator can introduce bias not present in the source. Avoid importing it.

Even when the source is inclusive, translator choices can introduce bias: defaulting to masculine forms, using loaded terms the source avoided, or applying stereotypes in examples and illustrations. Match the source's level of inclusivity, and do not regress. If the source uses gender-neutral or inclusive language, find the equivalent in the target rather than reverting to exclusionary defaults. The translator's responsibility is to preserve and, where possible, enhance inclusivity, not to erode it through careless choices.

### Flag Source Bias For The Client

When the source contains bias that cannot be resolved in translation, flag it for the client. Do not silently absorb.

Some bias cannot be addressed by the translator alone: a source term may be integral to the content, or a portrayal may require client-level decisions. In such cases, flag the issue to the client with an explanation and options, rather than silently rendering it literally and perpetuating harm, or silently changing it and exceeding the translator's role. Flagging respects the client's authority over content while ensuring they are aware of the issue. Document the flag and the client's decision.

### Respect Evolving Standards And Community Preferences

Inclusive language evolves, and communities define their preferred terms. Respect and follow them.

Inclusive terminology is not fixed; communities advocate for and adopt preferred language over time, and what was acceptable may become outdated. Stay current with the target locale's evolving standards, using resources from the communities, style guides, and native reviewers. Recognize that there may not be universal agreement within a community, and that the best choice serves the specific audience and context. Imposing terminology from another locale, or from an earlier time, disrespects the community's agency in defining how it is described.

### Balance Inclusivity With Accuracy And Audience

Inclusivity must be balanced with accuracy and the audience's expectations. Balance deliberately.

Inclusive adaptation should not distort the source's meaning or alienate the audience. A translation that is inclusive but inaccurate fails its primary purpose; one that is inclusive but incomprehensible to the audience fails to communicate. Consider the audience's familiarity with inclusive forms, the content's purpose, and the locale's norms, and choose adaptations that are both inclusive and effective. Where inclusive forms are non-standard, weigh the benefit of inclusivity against the risk of distraction, and consider the client's and audience's preferences. Balance is judgment, not compromise.

## Common Traps

### Literal Transfer Of Biased Language

Rendering biased terms literally imports and often amplifies harm in the target locale.

### Defaulting To Masculine Forms

Using masculine as generic in gendered languages excludes women and non-binary people; use inclusive alternatives.

### Assuming Source-Neutral Means Target-Neutral

A term neutral in the source may be loaded or offensive in the target; research locale terminology.

### Importing Bias Not In The Source

Translator choices can introduce exclusionary language the source avoided; match the source's inclusivity.

### Silently Changing Or Silently Perpetuating Bias

Both extremes fail; flag unresolvable bias to the client with options rather than acting unilaterally.

### Using Outdated Identity Language

Inclusive terminology evolves; using outdated terms disrespects the communities' current preferences.

### Ignoring Cultural Loading And Stereotypes

Terms and portrayals neutral to outsiders may offend the target; use native review to assess.

### Prioritizing Inclusivity Over Comprehension

Inclusive forms that the audience cannot understand fail to communicate; balance inclusivity with accuracy and audience.

## Self-Check

Before delivering or approving content for bias and inclusivity, verify:

- The role of translation in carrying or challenging bias is recognized, and biased or identity-related language is handled deliberately, not by default.
- Gendered language is handled with inclusive or neutral alternatives where the target allows, without defaulting to exclusionary forms.
- Identity-related language around race, disability, age, religion, and sexuality is adapted to the target locale's accepted and current terminology.
- Culturally loaded terms and stereotyping portrayals are assessed for target-locale effect and adapted or flagged.
- No bias absent from the source is introduced through translator choices; the source's inclusivity is preserved or enhanced.
- Source bias that cannot be resolved in translation is flagged to the client with explanation and options, not silently absorbed.
- Evolving community standards and preferences are followed, with current terminology used rather than outdated forms.
- Inclusivity is balanced with accuracy and audience comprehension, serving the specific audience and context.
- No literal transfer perpetuates harm that thoughtful adaptation could avoid.
- The translation is accurate, respectful, and inclusive of the audiences it addresses.
