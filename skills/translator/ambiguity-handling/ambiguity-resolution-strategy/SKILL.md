# Ambiguity Resolution Strategy

Once an ambiguity has been detected and classified as accidental, the translator faces a different problem: which reading to commit to, and how to do it defensibly. Resolution is not guessing. Guessing picks the first plausible reading and moves on; resolution gathers evidence, weighs it, chooses the reading the evidence best supports, and records enough that a reviewer can follow the reasoning and overturn it if better evidence appears. The danger in resolution is overconfidence: translators often resolve on thin evidence because the source "feels" clear, or because they have already started drafting and do not want to backtrack. The other danger is under-confidence masquerading as caution, where every minor ambiguity becomes a query and the work stalls. Good resolution sits between these, applying more rigor where stakes are higher and less where a wrong reading is harmless, but always leaving a trace. This skill covers the strategy of resolving accidental ambiguity: how to generate candidate readings, how to rank them by evidence, when resolution is appropriate versus querying, and how to document the choice.

Use this skill when you have identified an accidental ambiguity and must choose a reading to translate, when weighing evidence for competing interpretations, or when deciding whether to resolve, note, or query. The goal is to resolve defensibly, not to guess, and to match the rigor of resolution to the stakes.

## Core Rules

### Generate The Full Set Of Candidate Readings First

Resolution begins by enumerating, not selecting. If you jump to the most likely reading without listing the others, you cannot weigh them and you will miss the case where a less obvious reading is actually correct.

State every defensible reading, including ones that seem unlikely. A reading is a candidate if a competent reader of the source could defend it, not only if it is probable. For a pronoun with two possible antecedents, both are candidates. For a polysemous term, every sense the context does not rule out is a candidate. For scope ambiguity, each parse is a candidate. Generating the full set protects against anchoring on the first reading that comes to mind, which is the most common resolution error.

### Rank Readings By Evidence, Not By Preference

Once the candidates exist, rank them by the weight of evidence each one accumulates. Evidence, not the translator's stylistic taste, should decide.

Categories of evidence include immediate linguistic context, the words and grammar surrounding the ambiguous point; broader discourse context, what comes before and after and what the document is about; terminological evidence, how the term is used elsewhere in the text or termbase; domain evidence, what the reading means in the subject matter; and external evidence, authoritative references, parallel texts, or confirmation from the requester. Each category can support or undermine a reading. A reading supported by immediate context and domain knowledge outranks one supported only by preference. Record which evidence supports the leading reading so the ranking is transparent and reviewable.

### Let Stakes Govern The Threshold For Resolution Versus Query

Not every ambiguity should be resolved by the translator. The decision to resolve, resolve with a note, or query depends on the stakes and on the strength of evidence.

For low-stakes ambiguity where the leading reading is well-supported, resolve and move on; a note is optional. For moderate-stakes ambiguity, resolve toward the leading reading but add a note stating the alternatives and the evidence, so a reviewer can confirm. For high-stakes ambiguity, where a wrong reading changes a legal obligation, medical dose, safety instruction, or financial figure, do not resolve on your own evidence however strong it seems; query the requester, because the translator is not the authority on what the source meant to commit. The threshold rises with stakes. Resolving a high-stakes ambiguity internally, even correctly, removes the requester's chance to confirm and can create liability if the reading is later disputed.

### Use The Smallest Departure That Disambiguates

When you render the chosen reading in the target, use the smallest departure from a neutral translation that makes the meaning unambiguous. Over-disambiguation, adding explanatory material the source did not have, can distort tone and over-commit.

If the target language naturally distinguishes the chosen reading through grammar or a precise term, use that and nothing more. If natural wording leaves the ambiguity alive, add the minimal explicitation needed to select the reading, and note that you did so. The goal is a target that conveys the resolved meaning without lecturing the reader or padding the text. In literary or stylistically sensitive content, weigh explicitation against flow, because heavy disambiguation can flatten voice.

### Resolve Consistently Where The Same Ambiguity Recurs

Accidental ambiguity often repeats, because the same vague term or unclear reference appears throughout a document. Resolve recurring ambiguity consistently.

If you determine that a pronoun refers to the company in section one, carry that resolution wherever the same reference appears, unless context genuinely shifts. If a term is resolved to a specific sense, use that sense consistently and record it in the termbase. Inconsistent resolution, where the same ambiguity is read differently in different places, creates contradictions that confuse readers and undermine credibility. Track resolutions in a log so later segments follow earlier decisions, and revisit the log if a later passage reveals an earlier resolution was wrong.

### Document The Resolution So It Can Be Overturned

A resolution that cannot be reviewed is a silent guess. Document each meaningful resolution so a reviewer can confirm or overturn it with new evidence.

A useful resolution record states the ambiguous point, the candidate readings, the leading reading chosen, the evidence that supported it, and any explicitation added in the target. This lets a reviewer who brings better evidence overturn the choice quickly, and it lets the requester see where interpretation was applied. In high-stakes content, the record is part of the deliverable, not an internal convenience. Without it, the translator's interpretation is locked into the target with no way to challenge it.

### Revisit Earlier Resolutions When New Context Arrives

Translation is sequential, and early segments are often resolved with incomplete context. When later context reveals that an early resolution was wrong, go back and fix it.

This is why documentation matters: the log tells you where to look. A resolution made in paragraph two on partial evidence may be contradicted by paragraph twenty. Translators who do not revisit leave latent errors in the text. Build a habit of reviewing the resolutions log against the full document before final delivery, and correct any resolution the complete context overturns.

## Common Traps

### Anchoring On The First Plausible Reading

Jumping to the most obvious reading without enumerating alternatives misses the case where a less obvious reading is correct.

### Resolving On Preference Rather Than Evidence

Choosing the reading that sounds better, rather than the one the evidence supports, produces defensible-sounding but wrong translations.

### Resolving High-Stakes Ambiguity Internally

Even a correct internal resolution of legal, medical, or safety ambiguity removes the requester's authority to confirm and can create liability. Query instead.

### Over-Disambiguating In The Target

Adding heavy explanatory material to force a reading distorts tone and over-commits; use the smallest explicitation that selects the meaning.

### Inconsistent Resolution Of Recurring Ambiguity

Reading the same vague term or reference differently across a document creates contradictions and signals carelessness.

### Leaving Resolutions Undocumented

A resolution with no record cannot be reviewed or overturned; it becomes a locked-in silent guess.

### Failing To Revisit Early Resolutions

Early segments resolved on partial evidence may be wrong once the full document is known; review the log before delivery.

## Self-Check

Before approving a translation where accidental ambiguity was resolved, verify:

- The full set of defensible candidate readings was enumerated before any selection, not just the first plausible one.
- Readings were ranked by evidence, including linguistic, discourse, terminological, domain, and external evidence, not by translator preference.
- The decision to resolve, note, or query matched the stakes, with high-stakes legal, medical, or safety ambiguity queried rather than resolved internally.
- The target uses the smallest departure from neutral wording needed to convey the resolved meaning, without over-disambiguation.
- Recurring ambiguity was resolved consistently, with resolutions recorded in the termbase or a decisions log.
- Each meaningful resolution is documented with the point, candidates, chosen reading, supporting evidence, and any explicitation added.
- Early resolutions were revisited against the full document and corrected where later context overturned them.
- No resolution rests on evidence too thin for its stakes, and none was made silently where a note or query was required.
