# Effort And Timeline Estimation

Translation estimates are consistently wrong in one direction: they are too optimistic. Translators and project managers underestimate because they count words and divide by an ideal productivity rate, ignoring everything that makes real translation slower than the arithmetic suggests. They ignore source complexity, terminology research, ambiguity resolution, formatting and tag handling, review cycles, context gathering, and the overhead of coordination across locales and vendors. They treat the translation stage as the whole project, when review, QA, and delivery preparation often take as long as production. The consequence is predictable: deadlines slip, work is rushed to recover schedule, quality drops under pressure, and the translator or vendor absorbs the cost of the underestimate. Honest effort and timeline estimation is a planning discipline that protects quality, because a realistic schedule is what allows thorough work, and an unrealistic schedule is what forces the shortcuts that produce defects.

Use this skill when estimating translation effort, cost, or timeline, calculating productivity, accounting for complexity and review overhead, or setting deadlines. The goal is to produce estimates realistic enough to allow quality work, with explicit assumptions and contingency for the factors that make translation slower than naive word-count arithmetic.

## Core Rules

### Start From Accurate Word Counts And Content Analysis

Every estimate begins with the volume of work, and inaccurate volume produces inaccurate estimates. Start from real counts.

Obtain accurate word or segment counts for the source content, broken down by content type, because different types translate at different rates. Analyze the content for repetition and leverage, because translation memory matches reduce effective volume. Analyze for complexity: dense technical, legal, or specialized content takes longer per word than general prose. A word count without complexity analysis treats all words as equal, which they are not. Confirm the count reflects the final source version, because estimating against a draft that later grows invalidates the estimate.

A precise count of the wrong content, or a count that ignores complexity, produces an estimate that looks rigorous and is wrong.

### Use Realistic Productivity Rates, Not Ideal Ones

Productivity rates determine how volume maps to effort, and ideal rates produce optimistic estimates. Use realistic rates grounded in actual performance.

Base rates on historical data for the content type, language pair, and production method, not on theoretical maximums. Human translation rates vary by complexity: general content may move faster, while specialized or creative content moves slower. Post-editing rates depend on MT quality and the post-editing level. Review rates differ from translation rates. Account for the fact that no translator sustains peak productivity for an entire workday without interruption. Where no historical data exists, use conservative rates and note the assumption.

Ideal rates produce estimates that assume the translator works at full speed without friction, which never reflects reality.

### Account For Complexity And Non-Production Effort

Translation effort is more than rendering words. Account for the work that surrounds production.

Include terminology research, especially for specialized or emerging domains where equivalents must be validated. Include ambiguity resolution and query handling, because waiting for answers consumes schedule even when it does not consume translator hours. Include formatting, tag and placeholder handling, and layout work, which can be time-intensive in technical and UI content. Include context gathering, reference review, and asset preparation. For each, estimate the overhead or it will be absorbed invisibly into the production stage and surface as delay.

Estimates that count only production hours miss the majority of real effort in complex projects.

### Include Review, QA, And Delivery Stages

The translation stage is often less than half the project timeline. Include the stages that follow.

Include bilingual review, which can take a third to half of production effort depending on quality requirements. Include in-country or client review, with its own turnaround that often exceeds the translation stage. Include QA and quality measurement, formatting and assembly, and final delivery preparation. Include revision cycles, because content rarely passes review without changes, and revisions consume additional effort and schedule. A timeline that ends at translation completion ignores the stages where most schedule slip occurs.

### Factor In Language Pair And Locale Differences

Language pairs differ in difficulty, availability of qualified resources, and turnaround. Factor these differences into multi-locale estimates.

Some language pairs have abundant qualified translators and fast turnaround; others have scarce resources and longer lead times. Right-to-left, CJK, and complex-script languages may require additional layout and engineering time. Locales with in-country review add a stage and turnaround. Do not assume all locales move at the same speed or cost the same per word; estimate per locale based on its realities.

Uniform per-word estimates across all locales hide the differences that cause some locales to miss the shared deadline.

### Build Contingency For Risk And Unknowns

Every project encounters unknowns, and an estimate without contingency is an estimate that will be exceeded. Build in contingency proportional to risk.

Add contingency for source instability, where the source may change; for complexity uncertainty, where the content may prove harder than analyzed; for resource risk, where a translator or vendor may become unavailable; and for first-time locales or domains where historical data is weak. Higher-risk projects warrant more contingency. Contingency is not padding to inflate the estimate; it is honest acknowledgment that unknowns exist and will consume schedule.

An estimate with no contingency assumes a frictionless project, which guarantees a missed deadline when friction appears.

### Sequence Tasks And Identify The Critical Path

Effort and timeline are not the same. Timeline depends on how tasks sequence, and the critical path determines the minimum duration. Identify it.

Determine which tasks must be sequential, such as translation before review, and which can run in parallel, such as multiple locales translating simultaneously. Identify the critical path, the longest chain of dependent tasks, because it sets the minimum timeline and any slip on it delays the project. Where tasks can parallelize, the timeline can be shorter than the total effort would suggest; where they must sequence, the timeline extends. Estimate the timeline from the task structure, not by dividing total effort by team size.

Ignoring the critical path produces timelines that are impossible because dependent tasks were assumed parallel.

### Communicate Assumptions And Update Estimates

An estimate is a prediction based on assumptions, and assumptions that are hidden cannot be validated or updated. Make them explicit.

State the word count, complexity analysis, productivity rates, included stages, contingency, and sequencing assumptions. When conditions change, the source grows, scope expands, a locale is added, or MT quality proves lower than expected, update the estimate rather than holding the original deadline and forcing rushed work. Communicate changes to stakeholders early, because late notification of a slip eliminates the chance to adjust.

Estimates treated as commitments rather than predictions force quality sacrifice when reality diverges from the assumption.

## Common Traps

### Estimating From Word Count Alone

Words divided by ideal rate ignores complexity, leverage, and non-production effort, producing optimistic estimates.

### Using Ideal Productivity Rates

Theoretical maximums assume frictionless sustained work; real rates are lower and must be based on data.

### Ignoring Non-Production Effort

Terminology research, ambiguity, formatting, and context gathering consume schedule but are often uncounted.

### Ending The Timeline At Translation Completion

Review, QA, in-country review, revision, and delivery often exceed the translation stage in duration.

### Uniform Estimates Across Locales

Language pairs differ in difficulty, resource availability, and turnaround; uniform estimates hide the locales that will slip.

### No Contingency For Unknowns

Estimates without contingency assume a frictionless project and guarantee a missed deadline when friction appears.

### Ignoring The Critical Path

Assuming dependent tasks can run in parallel produces impossible timelines that cannot be met.

### Treating Estimates As Fixed Commitments

Holding original deadlines when conditions change forces rushed work and quality loss instead of honest re-estimation.

## Self-Check

Before approving a translation effort or timeline estimate, verify:

- The estimate starts from accurate word or segment counts broken down by content type, with complexity and leverage analyzed.
- Productivity rates are realistic, based on historical data for the content type, language pair, and method, not ideal maximums.
- Non-production effort, terminology research, ambiguity, formatting, context, is included, not absorbed invisibly.
- Review, QA, in-country review, revision, and delivery stages are included in the timeline, not cut off at translation completion.
- Language pair and locale differences in difficulty, resource availability, and turnaround are factored into multi-locale estimates.
- Contingency proportional to risk is built in for source instability, complexity uncertainty, resource risk, and weak historical data.
- The critical path is identified, with sequential and parallel tasks mapped, so the timeline reflects real task dependencies.
- Assumptions are stated explicitly and the estimate is updated when conditions change, with changes communicated early.
- No estimate assumes frictionless sustained work, and no deadline is held fixed when scope or conditions have changed.
- The timeline is realistic enough to allow quality work rather than forcing the shortcuts that produce defects.
