# Continuous Improvement Cycle

A translation program does not improve by fixing defects one at a time; it improves by closing the loop from defect to cause to systemic change to verified result, repeatedly, over time. The continuous improvement cycle is the discipline that turns review findings, error data, root causes, and client feedback into permanent quality gains. Agents often confuse activity with improvement: they hold review meetings, they fix segments, they respond to complaints, and yet quality does not get better, because none of that activity changed the conditions that produced the defects. The harm is that effort is spent without return, the same error classes recur project after project, translators feel judged but not supported, and the program's quality plateaus or decays. A real improvement cycle is a structured loop: measure, analyze, prioritize, act, verify, and embed. Each turn of the loop raises the baseline so that next year's common errors are different from this year's, which is the only honest sign that improvement is happening. Without the cycle, a program is busy but not learning.

Use this skill when running a quality improvement program, analyzing error and feedback data for patterns, prioritizing actions, updating references and processes, measuring effectiveness, or sustaining gains. The goal is a repeatable loop that converts quality data into permanent, verified improvements.

## Core Rules

### Treat Improvement As A Loop, Not A Project

Improvement is not a one-time initiative that ends; it is a recurring loop that must be sustained. Each cycle measures current quality, analyzes the data for the dominant error classes and their causes, prioritizes the actions with the highest leverage, implements the changes, verifies that they worked, and embeds the successful changes into the standard references and process. Then the loop starts again on the new dominant classes. Treating improvement as a finite project produces a burst of activity followed by decay back to baseline, because the conditions that generated the old defects were only partially changed. Budget the loop as ongoing operational work with named owners, not as an exceptional effort triggered only by a crisis.

### Measure Before Acting

Action without measurement is guessing. Before choosing what to improve, assemble the quality data: error types and severities from tracking, root cause categories from analysis, client feedback themes, reviewer calibration indicators, and trend lines over time. Identify the dominant error classes, the ones that account for most weighted defects or most client complaints, because those offer the highest leverage. Acting on a visible but minor issue while a dominant class goes unaddressed wastes effort and leaves quality unchanged. Measurement also establishes the baseline against which improvement is verified; without a baseline, you cannot tell whether a change worked.

### Prioritize By Leverage, Not By Visibility

Not all improvements are equal. Prioritize actions by leverage: the reduction in weighted defects or client complaints per unit of effort. A termbase expansion that eliminates a recurring terminology class across all projects has high leverage; rewriting one translator's segments has low leverage. A process change that prevents a whole defect class, such as adding a mandatory completeness check, has higher leverage than re-fixing individual omissions. Consider feasibility alongside leverage: the highest-leverage change that the program cannot actually implement is worth less than a slightly lower-leverage change that will be adopted. Rank candidate actions on a leverage-versus-feasibility basis and attack the top of the list, rather than scattering effort across many small fronts.

### Act At The Cause Level, Not The Symptom Level

Improvement actions must change the conditions that produce defects, not just the defects themselves. If terminology errors dominate, the action is termbase expansion, terminology research process, and enforcement, not re-correcting individual terms. If omissions dominate, the action is a completeness check in the process, not re-reading more files. If a translator's accuracy errors dominate, the action is targeted training or mentoring, not silent rework. Acting at the symptom level, fixing instances, feels productive but changes nothing systemic, and the same defects return on the next project. Map each prioritized class to its root cause and design the action at that level.

### Verify That The Change Worked

A change is not an improvement until it is verified. After implementing an action, measure the same metric over the same population and compare to baseline. If terminology errors dropped after the termbase expansion, the action worked and should be embedded; if they persisted, the cause was mis-assigned or the action was inadequate, and the loop reopens. Verification requires that the metric definition stayed stable, which is why metric versioning matters. Declaring victory without verification is how programs accumulate actions that look right but do not work, and it is why busy programs fail to improve. Build verification into every cycle as a mandatory step with a defined follow-up window.

### Embed Successful Changes Into Standards

A verified improvement must be embedded into the standard references and process so it persists after the improvement team moves on. Update the termbase, the style guide, the briefing template, the QA configuration, the training, or the workflow so that the change becomes the new default rather than a one-time fix. Changes that live only in a meeting minute or an individual's memory decay rapidly. Embedding is what turns a cycle's output into a permanent baseline raise, and it is the step most often skipped because the team moves to the next issue before consolidating the last one.

### Close The Loop Across All Feedback Sources

The richest improvement signals come from combining sources: internal review data shows defect classes, root cause analysis shows why, client feedback shows what matters in context, and reviewer calibration data shows whether the measurements are trustworthy. A cycle that uses only one source is blind to the others. A program that improves internal error scores while client complaints rise has improved the wrong thing, usually by optimizing what is measured rather than what matters. Integrate the sources so that improvement targets the defects that affect real outcomes, not just the ones that are easy to count.

### Assign Ownership And Time-box The Cycle

A cycle without ownership and cadence stalls. Assign an owner for the cycle, a quality lead or a small team, responsible for measurement, analysis, prioritization, action, verification, and embedding. Set a cadence, monthly or quarterly depending on volume, so the loop turns at a rhythm that matches the program's throughput. Time-box each cycle so that analysis does not become an open-ended study and actions are actually implemented within the period. Ownership and cadence are what distinguish a living improvement cycle from a one-off analysis that sits in a deck.

## Common Traps

### Treating Improvement As A Finite Project

A burst of activity followed by no sustaining loop decays back to baseline and changes nothing permanently.

### Acting Without Measuring

Choosing what to improve from impression rather than data wastes effort on visible but minor issues while dominant classes persist.

### Prioritizing Visibility Over Leverage

Fixing what is noticeable rather than what reduces the most defects leaves quality unchanged despite busy effort.

### Acting At The Symptom Level

Re-correcting instances without changing the producing condition guarantees the same defects return on the next project.

### Declaring Victory Without Verification

Assuming a change worked, without measuring against baseline, lets ineffective actions accumulate as activity without improvement.

### Forgetting To Embed Changes

Improvements that stay in meeting minutes rather than standards decay rapidly and never become a baseline raise.

### Using Only One Feedback Source

Optimizing internal error scores while ignoring client feedback improves what is measured, not what matters.

### No Owner Or Cadence

A cycle without ownership and rhythm stalls into a one-off analysis and stops turning.

## Self-Check

Before declaring an improvement cycle complete or a quality gain sustained, verify:

- Improvement is operated as a recurring loop with named owners and a set cadence, not as a finite project or a crisis response.
- The cycle began with measurement of current quality, establishing a baseline of dominant error classes, root causes, and client feedback themes.
- Candidate actions were prioritized by leverage and feasibility, attacking the dominant classes rather than scattering across minor issues.
- Actions were designed at the cause level, changing the conditions that produce defects rather than re-fixing individual instances.
- Each action was verified by measuring the same metric against baseline over a defined window, with the loop reopened if the change failed.
- Verified improvements were embedded into standards such as the termbase, style guide, briefing, QA configuration, training, or workflow.
- The cycle integrated all feedback sources, internal review, root cause, client feedback, and calibration, so improvement targets real outcomes.
- The cycle is time-boxed so analysis and action happen within the period rather than becoming open-ended study.
- No action was declared effective without verification, and no verified change was left unembedded.
- The loop raises the baseline so that next cycle's dominant errors are different from this cycle's, the honest sign that the program is learning.
