# Client Feedback Processing

Client feedback arrives after delivery, when the client or their in-country reviewers read the translation in its real context and respond. This feedback is different in kind from internal reviewer feedback. It is less structured, often emotional, sometimes vague, frequently mixed with scope and preference issues, and always carries commercial weight because it affects the relationship, payment, and future work. Agents often mishandle client feedback by treating it either as a quality verdict to be obeyed without analysis or as a nuisance to be deflected. Both are wrong. Client feedback is evidence about how the translation performs in the wild, and it must be triaged, classified, and responded to with discipline. Some of it reveals real defects the internal process missed; some of it reveals preferences that should update the style guide; some of it is out of scope and must be negotiated as additional work; some of it reflects client-side conditions, such as an in-country reviewer with a different dialect preference, that are not defects at all. The harm of poor processing is that valid quality signals are lost, scope creep is absorbed silently and erodes margin, the provider either capitulates or fights in ways that damage the relationship, and the same client complaints recur because the underlying issue was never correctly classified.

Use this skill when receiving client or end-user feedback, triaging complaints and change requests, deciding what is a defect versus a preference versus a scope change, negotiating revisions, or handling in-country review. The goal is to turn client feedback into correctly classified, commercially fair, relationship-preserving action that also improves future quality.

## Core Rules

### Triage Before Reacting

Client feedback, especially a complaint, triggers a defensive impulse to explain or a conciliatory impulse to agree. Resist both and triage first. Read all the feedback, group related points, and separate quality issues from preference issues, scope requests, misunderstandings, and client-side conditions. Triage produces a structured picture before any response, so the reply addresses the real content rather than reacting to tone or to the first point raised. A reactive response to a partial reading almost always misclassifies the feedback and escalates the situation. Triage is the step that makes the rest possible.

### Classify Each Point As Defect, Preference, Scope, Or Condition

Apply a classification to every point. A defect is a real quality failure the internal process missed: a mistranslation, an omission, a terminology error against the agreed termbase. A preference is an alternative the client prefers that is not more correct: a different register, a dialectal choice, a stylistic taste. A scope change is content or change the client wants that was not in the original brief: added segments, a new locale, a format change. A condition is a client-side factor that produced the feedback but is not a translation defect: an in-country reviewer with a different dialect, a stakeholder who was not consulted during briefing, a brand voice that was never documented. Classification determines the response: defects are fixed at provider cost, preferences are negotiated and may update the style guide, scope changes are quoted as additional work, conditions are clarified and often require a briefing change rather than a text change. Without classification, everything blurs into one bucket and the response is either over-delivering on preferences and scope or under-delivering on real defects.

### Validate Defect Claims Against The Brief And References

When the client reports a defect, validate it before fixing. Compare the reported issue against the source, the agreed termbase, the style guide, and the brief. Some client-reported defects are real and reveal an internal process gap to close. Some are not defects at all: the client's preferred term conflicts with the termbase they approved, or the client objects to a faithful rendering of ambiguous source. Validating protects the provider from fixing things that were not wrong and from undermining the agreed references, and it gives the response an evidence base rather than a capitulation. When a reported defect is valid, own it, fix it, and feed the root cause back into the process. When it is not, explain the basis respectfully and offer the change as a preference if the client still wants it.

### Negotiate Scope And Preference Changes Commercially

Preferences and scope changes are not free, and absorbing them silently erodes margin and sets a precedent that all change is included. Quote preference and scope work as additional services with a price and timeline, while making clear which points are being handled as goodwill to preserve the relationship. Distinguish clearly between what the provider is fixing because it was a defect, and what the client is choosing to add or change. This protects the commercial relationship and also helps the client understand the boundary, which reduces future scope creep. The tone is collaborative, not transactional: the goal is a fair exchange, not a dispute, but fairness requires that the boundary be visible.

### Handle In-Country Review Feedback Specifically

In-country review, where the client's local reviewers assess the translation, produces a distinctive feedback pattern: often highly dialectal, frequently preference-laden, sometimes contradictory across reviewers, and rarely aligned with the original brief. Handle it specifically. Recognize that in-country reviewers judge as readers, not as bilingual reviewers, so their feedback is strongest on fluency and locale fit and weakest on source fidelity. Aggregate the reviewers' feedback to find the consistent signals rather than implementing every individual suggestion, which would produce a text pulled in several directions. Where reviewers disagree with the agreed termbase or style, decide whether to update the references for future work or to hold the line, and document the decision. Treat in-country review as a calibration input that improves the brief for the next project, not as an adversarial override.

### Respond With Evidence And A Clear Plan

The client response should pair every classification with evidence and a plan. For defects, acknowledge, cite the fix, and state the root-cause action so it will not recur. For preferences, state whether they are accepted as goodwill or quoted, and what reference update they imply. For scope, quote the addition. For conditions, clarify the factor and propose a briefing change. A response that is only apologies, or only a list of changes without classification, leaves the client uncertain what was actually wrong and what they are paying for. Evidence and a plan build confidence that the provider understands the feedback and controls the quality, which is often more valuable to the relationship than the fixes themselves.

### Capture Feedback To Improve The Brief And Process

Client feedback is a leading indicator of how the translation performs in context, and it should update the brief, termbase, and style guide for future work. Recurring preference feedback suggests the style guide needs a rule; recurring defect feedback suggests a process or resource gap. Capture the validated learnings so the next project starts from a better baseline. A provider that fixes the current complaint but never updates its references guarantees the same client feedback on the next project, which is the fastest way to lose the client. The feedback loop's value is cumulative improvement, and that requires capture.

### Preserve The Relationship While Protecting Boundaries

Client feedback processing is relationship work as much as quality work. The client needs to feel heard, respected, and confident in the provider, even when the feedback is partly invalid or commercially charged. At the same time, the provider must protect its boundaries on scope, on agreed references, and on fair payment, because a relationship built on silent over-delivery is not sustainable. Hold both: respond with respect and evidence, deliver on real defects, negotiate preferences and scope fairly, and make the boundary visible without making it adversarial. The strongest client relationships are the ones where both sides understand the boundary and trust the other to honor it.

## Common Traps

### Reacting Before Triaging

A defensive or conciliatory response to a partial reading misclassifies the feedback and often escalates the situation.

### Everything In One Bucket

Mixing defects, preferences, scope, and conditions prevents correct response and leads to over- or under-delivering.

### Fixing Reported Defects Without Validation

Capitulating to client-reported defects that conflict with the agreed termbase or brief undermines the references and fixes things that were not wrong.

### Absorbing Scope And Preference Silently

Treating all client change as included erodes margin and sets a precedent that guarantees future scope creep.

### Implementing Every In-Country Suggestion

Acting on each individual reviewer comment pulls the text in several directions and produces inconsistency.

### Apologies Or Changes Without Classification

A response without evidence and a plan leaves the client uncertain what was wrong and what they are paying for.

### Fixing Complaints Without Updating References

Correcting the current file without capturing the learning guarantees the same client feedback on the next project.

### Over-Delivering To Avoid Conflict

A relationship built on silent over-delivery is not sustainable; preserving it requires visible, fair boundaries.

## Self-Check

Before responding to client feedback or closing a post-delivery loop, verify:

- All feedback was triaged and grouped before any response, rather than reacted to piecemeal.
- Each point is classified as defect, preference, scope change, or client-side condition, with the response matched to the classification.
- Reported defects were validated against source, termbase, style guide, and brief before fixing, with non-defects explained respectfully.
- Preference and scope changes are quoted commercially where appropriate, with goodwill clearly distinguished from billable work.
- In-country review feedback was aggregated for consistent signals rather than implemented suggestion by suggestion, with reference updates documented.
- The client response pairs each classification with evidence and a plan, including root-cause action for real defects.
- Validated learnings are captured in the brief, termbase, or style guide so the next project starts from a better baseline.
- The response preserves the relationship through respect and evidence while protecting boundaries on scope, references, and payment.
- No reported defect was fixed without validation, and no scope or preference was absorbed silently as if included.
- The loop turns client feedback into correctly classified, commercially fair, relationship-preserving action that improves future quality.
