# Quality Evidence And Reporting

Translation quality reporting is where measurement meets decision-making, and it is where honesty matters most. A quality report tells a client whether to accept or reject a translation, tells a vendor what to improve, and tells a program whether its quality is trending up or down. When the report is vague, unsupported, or overstated, it fails at all of these. A report that says the translation has quality issues without specifying where, what kind, or how severe helps no one improve. A report that asserts a translation is excellent without evidence asks the client to trust an opinion. A report that presents subjective stylistic preferences as objective errors misleads vendors into chasing the wrong fixes. And a report that overstates certainty, declaring a translation correct or fit for purpose when the reviewer has not verified the high-risk segments, creates false confidence that can cause harm. Quality evidence and reporting is the discipline of substantiating every claim, separating objective defect from subjective preference, and presenting findings so they drive the right decisions.

Use this skill when reporting translation quality findings, writing review or LQA reports, substantiating quality claims, documenting errors for feedback, or presenting quality data for acceptance decisions. The goal is to produce reports that are accurate, evidence-based, actionable, and honest about their limits.

## Core Rules

### Substantiate Every Quality Claim With Evidence

A quality claim without evidence is an opinion, and opinions do not drive improvement or defensible decisions. Substantiate every claim.

When reporting an error, provide the segment, the source text, the target text, the error category, the severity, and the rationale explaining why it is an error and what the consequence is. When reporting that a translation is acceptable, state what was checked, how deeply, and against what reference, so the claim is scoped rather than absolute. Evidence transforms a claim from assertion into something a client or vendor can verify, act on, and trust.

Reports full of claims but empty of evidence are dismissed as subjective and drive no improvement.

### Separate Objective Defect From Subjective Preference

The most damaging reporting error is treating stylistic preference as objective defect. Distinguish them rigorously.

Objective defects are errors against an external standard: mistranslation, omission, wrong number, broken placeholder, termbase violation, grammar error, or format violation. These can be verified against the source, the termbase, or the style guide, and two competent reviewers should agree they are errors. Subjective preferences are choices the reviewer would have made differently: a phrasing that is valid but not the reviewer's favorite, a tone that is acceptable but not preferred. Reporting subjective preferences as errors floods vendors with non-actionable feedback, erodes trust in the report, and masks the real defects.

Label preferences as suggestions, not errors, and reserve error status for verifiable defects.

### Categorize And Severity-Tag Each Finding

Findings that are not categorized and severity-tagged cannot be aggregated, compared, or acted on systematically. Structure every finding.

Use the project's quality model categories, such as accuracy, fluency, terminology, style, locale, and technical, and assign severity according to consequence. A categorized, severity-tagged finding lets the client see not just that there are issues but what kind and how serious. It lets the vendor identify patterns, such as recurring terminology deviations, and improve systematically. It lets the program track quality over time. Findings without structure are a list of complaints; findings with structure are data.

### Scope Acceptance Claims Honestly

When reporting that a translation is acceptable or fit for purpose, scope the claim to what was actually verified. Do not overstate.

State what was reviewed: which segments, which content types, what depth of review, and what was sampled versus fully checked. State what was not reviewed, so the client understands the limits. State the standard applied, whether full publication quality, light post-editing acceptance, or gisting adequacy. A report that declares a translation correct without scoping implies a verification that may not have occurred, and if a defect later surfaces in an unreviewed high-risk segment, the unscoped claim is exposed as overreach.

Honest scoping protects the reviewer and the client from false confidence.

### Make Reports Actionable For The Recipient

A report's value is measured by whether the recipient can act on it. Make findings actionable.

For vendors and translators, provide enough detail that they can fix the error and, more importantly, avoid repeating it. Group findings by type so patterns are visible. Provide the correct rendering or the principle behind it where possible. For clients, translate findings into decisions: what is acceptable, what requires rework, what the risk implications are, and what the recommendation is. A report that lists problems without enabling action is overhead.

Actionable reporting turns quality measurement into quality improvement.

### Distinguish Translation Defects From Source Problems

Not every quality issue originates in the translation. Some findings are source problems that the translator handled as well as possible. Distinguish them.

Source ambiguity, source errors, source terminology inconsistency, and missing source context produce target issues that are not the translator's fault. Reporting these as translation defects misattributes blame and misleads the client about where remediation is needed. Label source-origin issues as source problems, and recommend source remediation, because fixing the translation without fixing the source reproduces the problem in every future translation.

Conflating source and target defects hides the real root cause and wastes remediation effort.

### Report Quality Trends, Not Just Snapshots

A single report is a snapshot. Over time, reports should reveal trends that guide program decisions.

Track quality metrics across projects, vendors, locales, and content types. Report whether quality is improving, stable, or deteriorating. Identify systemic issues, such as a locale consistently underperforming or a terminology area with persistent deviation, that single reports miss. Trend reporting lets the program allocate resources, adjust vendor mix, and target improvement where it is needed, rather than reacting to each project in isolation.

Snapshots inform one decision; trends inform a strategy.

### Avoid Overstating Certainty In High-Stakes Domains

In legal, medical, financial, and safety content, quality reports carry consequence, and overstating certainty is dangerous. Be honest about residual risk.

Do not declare a high-stakes translation certified, authoritative, or free of error unless the verification supports that and the appropriate authority has confirmed it. Recommend qualified expert review where domain expertise is required. State that the review verifies translation quality, not legal, medical, or technical validity, which requires domain expertise the translator may not have. Overstated certainty in high-stakes domains can lead clients to rely on a translation beyond what was verified, causing harm and liability.

## Common Traps

### Making Claims Without Evidence

Quality claims without supporting segments, categories, and rationale are dismissed as subjective opinion.

### Reporting Preference As Error

Treating stylistic preferences as objective defects floods vendors with non-actionable feedback and masks real defects.

### Findings Without Category Or Severity

Uncategorized, untagged findings are a list of complaints, not data that can drive improvement.

### Unscoped Acceptance Claims

Declaring a translation correct without stating what was verified implies a verification that may not have occurred.

### Reports That Do Not Enable Action

Findings without enough detail to fix or avoid the error are overhead, not improvement.

### Conflating Source And Target Defects

Attributing source-origin issues to the translator misdirects blame and hides the real remediation need.

### Reporting Only Snapshots

Single-project reports miss systemic patterns; without trend reporting, the program reacts rather than strategizes.

### Overstating Certainty In High-Stakes Domains

Declaring high-stakes content authoritative beyond verification leads to over-reliance and harm.

## Self-Check

Before approving or submitting a translation quality report, verify:

- Every quality claim is substantiated with segment, source, target, category, severity, and rationale.
- Objective defects are distinguished from subjective preferences, with preferences labeled as suggestions, not errors.
- Each finding is categorized and severity-tagged according to the project's quality model.
- Acceptance claims are scoped to what was actually reviewed, including depth, sampling, and the standard applied.
- The report is actionable, with findings grouped by type and enough detail for the recipient to fix and avoid errors.
- Source-origin problems are distinguished from translation defects, with source remediation recommended where needed.
- Quality trends are reported across projects, vendors, and locales, not just single-project snapshots.
- In high-stakes domains, certainty is not overstated, with qualified expert review recommended where required.
- No defect is reported without evidence, and no preference is reported as an error.
- The report would let a client make an informed acceptance decision and a vendor improve systematically.
