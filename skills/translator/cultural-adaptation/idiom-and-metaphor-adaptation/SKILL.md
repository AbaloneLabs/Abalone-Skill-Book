# Idiom And Metaphor Adaptation

Idioms and metaphors are where languages diverge most visibly. An idiom's meaning is not the sum of its words, so translating it word for word produces nonsense or, worse, an unintended meaning in the target. Metaphors draw on bodily, cultural, and environmental imagery that may not transfer, so a metaphor that is vivid in the source can be baffling, comic, or offensive in the target. Yet figurative language carries much of a text's force, voice, and concision. The translator cannot simply strip it out and paraphrase everything, because that flattens the text. The work is to recognize figurative language, understand its function, and choose a strategy that preserves the function in target-language resources, whether by an equivalent idiom, a recreated metaphor, or a controlled paraphrase.

Use this skill when a source contains idioms, metaphors, similes, proverbs, or fixed expressions, when deciding among literal translation, functional equivalent, paraphrase, and replacement, or when figurative meaning must be carried across a language boundary. The goal is to preserve the source's expressive function without producing target text that is opaque, misleading, or absurd.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Figurative Language Before Translating

The first task is detection. Translators sometimes render an idiom literally because they failed to notice it was figurative, especially when the idiom is common in their own variety.

Watch for fixed expressions whose meaning is not compositional, metaphors that map a source domain onto a target domain, similes and comparisons, proverbs and sayings, phrasal verbs with idiomatic meaning, collocations that are conventional rather than logical, and culture-bound imagery such as sports, weather, animals, food, and body parts used figuratively. In some languages, large portions of everyday vocabulary are idiomatic, and the translator must stay alert throughout.

When you detect figurative language, state its literal meaning and its intended meaning separately. If you cannot separate them, you have not fully understood it.

### Preserve Function Over Words

The goal is to carry the function of the figurative language, not its literal imagery. Function includes the meaning conveyed, the emotional tone, the rhetorical effect, the register, and the concision.

A metaphor that makes an abstract idea vivid should remain vivid in the target, but it may need different imagery. An idiom that conveys a concept concisely should remain concise, whether by a target idiom or a tight paraphrase. A proverb that carries cultural authority should carry equivalent authority, whether by a target proverb or a dignified rendering.

Preserving the literal words while losing the function is a common failure. The reader gets imagery that no longer signifies.

### Choose Among The Core Strategies

The core strategies for figurative language are literal translation with explanation, functional equivalent idiom, recreated metaphor, paraphrase, and omission. Choose based on function, audience, and text type.

Literal translation with explanation keeps the source imagery and adds a note or gloss. It suits literature and contexts where the source imagery is itself content, but it interrupts flow and is unsuitable for most user-facing or time-constrained text. Functional equivalent idiom replaces the source idiom with a target idiom that conveys the same meaning and tone. It is the strongest choice when a genuine equivalent exists, because it preserves concision and naturalness. Recreated metaphor builds a new metaphor in the target using target-culture imagery that performs the same function. It suits marketing, literature, and persuasive text where vividness matters. Paraphrase states the meaning directly without figurative language. It is reliable but loses concision and flavor, and should be a fallback, not a default. Omission drops the figurative element entirely and is appropriate only when it is decorative and non-essential.

Each strategy has a cost. Match the cost to the text.

### Verify That A Functional Equivalent Is Genuine

A common error is choosing a target idiom that looks similar but differs in meaning, register, or connotation. False friends among idioms are dangerous because they feel right.

Verify that the candidate equivalent idiom actually conveys the same meaning, not just similar imagery. Two idioms may share an image but differ in tone, one casual and one vulgar, or in scope, one general and one restricted to a specific situation. Check the equivalent against target-language corpus or native intuition, and confirm register and connotation match.

When no genuine equivalent exists, do not force one. Recreate or paraphrase instead.

### Handle Culture-Bound Imagery Deliberately

Metaphors and idioms often draw on imagery that is culture-bound. Sports metaphors, weather metaphors, animal symbolism, food imagery, and body-part idioms vary enormously across cultures.

A sports metaphor from a sport unknown in the target culture will baffle readers. An animal symbol that is positive in the source culture may be negative in the target. A weather metaphor tied to a source climate may not resonate. Decide whether to retain the imagery with explanation, substitute target-culture imagery that performs the same function, or paraphrase away the imagery.

In literature and culture-specific content, retain and explain. In functional content such as software, marketing, and training, substitute or paraphrase for clarity.

### Preserve Register And Tone

Figurative language carries register. An idiom may be slang, colloquial, formal, archaic, or vulgar. The translation should match the register, not just the meaning.

Translating a colloquial idiom into formal target prose changes the voice. Translating a vulgar idiom into polite language softens the source's force. Translating an archaic proverb into modern idiom loses its authority. Match the register deliberately, and where the target language lacks an equivalent at the same register, note the compromise.

### Watch For Mixed And Extended Metaphors

Source texts sometimes mix metaphors or extend a metaphor across a passage. Handle these as systems, not as isolated phrases.

A mixed metaphor in the source may be intentional for comic effect or accidental from careless writing. Preserve intentional mixing; smooth accidental mixing only with a note. An extended metaphor that runs through a paragraph or section should be carried by a consistent target metaphor, not rendered with different imagery in each sentence, which fragments the effect.

Map the metaphor system before translating, then apply it consistently.

### Respect Character And Narrative Voice

In fiction and dialogue, figurative language is part of character voice. A character who speaks in colorful idioms should speak in colorful target idioms, not in neutral paraphrase.

Match figurative language to the speaker. Different characters may use different registers and imagery, and the translation should preserve those distinctions. Flattening all characters to the same neutral voice destroys characterization.

## Common Traps

### Translating Idioms Literally Without Noticing

The most basic error. If you did not consciously identify the idiom as figurative, you may have rendered it word for word.

### Choosing A Look-Alike Idiom That Differs In Meaning

Similar imagery across languages often masks different meaning or connotation. Verify equivalents genuinely.

### Paraphrasing Everything To Be Safe

Defaulting to paraphrase flattens the text and loses the source's voice and concision. Use paraphrase as a fallback, not a habit.

### Ignoring Register In Figurative Language

Matching meaning while losing register changes the voice. Match tone and formality, not just sense.

### Fragmenting Extended Metaphors

Rendering each occurrence of an extended metaphor with different imagery breaks the passage's coherence. Map and apply consistently.

### Forcing Equivalents Where None Exist

Inventing a target idiom that is not actually idiomatic produces unnatural text. Recreate or paraphrase instead.

### Flattening Character Voice

Neutralizing figurative language in dialogue removes the distinctions between characters. Preserve voice.

## Self-Check

Before approving a translation of figurative language, verify:

- Figurative language was recognized before translation, and literal and intended meanings were separated.
- The function of each idiom or metaphor, meaning, tone, effect, register, concision, is stated and targeted.
- A strategy of literal-with-explanation, functional equivalent, recreated metaphor, paraphrase, or omission was chosen deliberately per instance.
- Functional equivalent idioms were verified for genuine match of meaning, register, and connotation, not just similar imagery.
- Culture-bound imagery was handled deliberately, with retention and explanation or substitution suited to text type and audience.
- Register and tone of figurative language are matched in the target, not flattened.
- Extended and mixed metaphors are handled as systems and applied consistently.
- Character and narrative voice in fiction and dialogue is preserved through figurative language choices.
- Paraphrase is a deliberate fallback, not a default that strips the text of figurative force.
- No idiom was translated literally without a conscious decision, especially common idioms that are easy to overlook.
