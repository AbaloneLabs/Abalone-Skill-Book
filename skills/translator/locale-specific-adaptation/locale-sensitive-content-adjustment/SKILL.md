# Locale Sensitive Content Adjustment

Some content is not wrong in the source but wrong, offensive, illegal, or ineffective in the target locale. A perfectly accurate translation of a marketing example, a training scenario, a holiday reference, a color choice, a gesture, or a historical analogy can land badly because what it assumes or depicts does not fit the target culture, its laws, its religion, or its social norms. Locale-sensitive content is the category where literal fidelity produces harm: a hand gesture that is friendly in one culture is an insult in another, a color that signals celebration in one signals mourning in another, an example name that is ordinary in one locale belongs to a sensitive figure in another, a regulatory claim that is legal in one country is prohibited in another, a historical reference that is neutral in one region is raw trauma in another. The translator's job is not to translate this content verbatim but to recognize its sensitivity, judge whether to retain, adjust, substitute, or flag it, and ensure the target locale receives content that is appropriate, compliant, and respectful. This is delicate work because the line between respectful adaptation and unwarranted censorship is real, and because mishandling sensitive content can damage brands, violate laws, or cause genuine offense. This skill covers how to identify locale-sensitive content, how to choose an adjustment strategy, and how to handle the hardest cases without either erasing meaning or importing harm.

Use this skill when content carries cultural, legal, religious, political, or social sensitivity for a target locale, when deciding whether to retain adjust substitute or flag examples imagery references and claims, or when ensuring localized content is appropriate and compliant. The goal is to deliver content that fits the locale without silently distorting the source or amplifying harm.

## Core Rules

### Identify Sensitivity Before Translating, Not After

Locale-sensitive content is easiest to handle when recognized early, before the translation commits to a rendering that is hard to undo. Build sensitivity detection into the analysis pass.

Scan for content categories that frequently carry locale sensitivity: examples and scenarios using names, foods, holidays, or family structures; imagery and color references; gestures and body language described in text; humor involving religion, family, status, gender, or disability; historical, political, and geopolitical references; religious content; regulatory and legal claims; health and medical claims; content touching race, ethnicity, and indigenous peoples; and content about contested territories, borders, or flags. For each, ask whether it carries different meaning, connotation, or legal status in the target locale. Recognizing sensitivity is a judgment, and it benefits from native or in-country input, especially for locales that are not the translator's own.

### Choose Among Retain, Adjust, Substitute, And Flag

Once sensitivity is identified, choose a strategy deliberately rather than defaulting to literal translation or to deletion.

Retain the content when it is appropriate for the locale and its sensitivity is not a problem, or when the content's specificity is the point, as in literature or journalism. Adjust the content when a small change removes the problem while preserving function, such as softening a gesture description or rewording a claim to comply with local regulation. Substitute the content when a target-locale equivalent carries the same function without the sensitivity, such as replacing a holiday example with a local holiday. Flag the content when the sensitivity is real but the right handling is unclear or beyond the translator's authority, such as a geopolitical reference that may violate local law. The choice depends on the content type, the audience, the stakes, and the degree of freedom the brief allows. Record the rationale for each significant decision so reviewers can evaluate it.

### Handle Legal And Regulatory Sensitivity Conservatively

Legal and regulatory sensitivity is the highest-stakes category, because mishandling it can cause non-compliance, fines, or content takedowns. Handle it conservatively.

Advertising and marketing claims are regulated differently by country: health and medical claims, comparative advertising, environmental claims, pricing and offer rules, and claims targeting children all have locale-specific rules. A claim legal in the source country may be prohibited in the target. Financial and investment content may require disclaimers that the source did not have. Data privacy references must match the target jurisdiction's requirements. Content about regulated products, alcohol, tobacco, pharmaceuticals, gambling, may be restricted or banned. When a claim or content type is regulated in the target locale, do not ship it on the translator's authority; flag for legal or compliance review, and adjust or remove only with authorization. The cost of a compliance breach far exceeds the cost of a query.

### Navigate Political And Geopolitical Sensitivity Carefully

Political and geopolitical references can be neutral in one locale and deeply sensitive, even illegal, in another. Navigate them with care and without taking sides.

Contested territories, borders, country names, flags, and references to conflicts carry political weight that varies by locale. A place name that is standard in one country is disputed in another. A historical event that is commemorated in one region is contested or traumatic in another. References to governments, leaders, and political movements can trigger censorship or offense. Where the source contains geopolitical content, preserve the source's meaning without amplifying a stance the source did not take, and flag content that may violate target-locale law or provoke harm. Do not silently substitute a politically convenient rendering, because that distorts the source, and do not force through content that is illegal or harmful in the target. Where the stakes are high, escalate to the requester with the options and their implications.

### Respect Religious And Social Norms

Religion and social norms shape what content is acceptable, and insensitivity here causes deep offense. Respect the locale's norms while preserving the source's intent where possible.

Religious references, depictions, and terminology require care, because what is casual in one tradition is sacred or forbidden in another. Content about gender, family structure, sexuality, disability, and social status is read through the locale's norms, and a progressive framing in one culture may be inappropriate or illegal in another, just as a conservative framing may be unacceptable to a different audience. Humor touching these areas is especially risky. The aim is not to impose either the source's or the translator's values, but to deliver content appropriate to the target audience and consistent with the brand and purpose. Where source content genuinely conflicts with the locale's norms, flag it and propose adjustments rather than forcing it or silently deleting it.

### Adjust Examples And Imagery To Fit The Locale

Examples, scenarios, names, foods, holidays, and imagery that work in the source culture may be unrecognizable or inappropriate in the target. Adjust them to fit.

A training example built around a source-culture holiday or sport may confuse target readers; substituting a local equivalent preserves the learning function. An example name that is ordinary in the source may belong to a sensitive figure or carry an unwanted connotation in the target. Imagery described in text, colors, gestures, clothing, may signal something different or offensive in the target. Coordinate text adjustments with visual assets, because substituting text while leaving a source-culture image creates a visible mismatch. The goal is content that functions for the target reader, not content that advertises its foreign origin unless foreignness is the point.

### Preserve Cultural Specificity Where It Is The Content

Not all sensitive content should be adjusted. In literature, memoir, history, travel writing, and journalism, cultural specificity is the content, and domesticating it falsifies the work.

Know the difference between content where the function matters more than the specific referent, which warrants substitution, and content where the specific referent is the point, which warrants retention with explanation. A novel set in a specific culture loses its texture if every reference is replaced with a target-culture analog. A historical account must retain its references, even sensitive ones, with neutral explanation where the reader needs it. Adjusting such content to avoid all difficulty is a form of distortion. Match the strategy to the text type and the purpose.

## Common Traps

### Translating Sensitive Content Verbatim

Literal fidelity to content that is offensive, illegal, or ineffective in the target locale causes harm the source never intended.

### Defaulting To Deletion As Safe

Silent deletion looks like censorship and loses content; choose retain, adjust, substitute, or flag deliberately.

### Shipping Regulated Claims Without Review

Marketing, health, financial, and privacy claims are locale-regulated; shipping without compliance review risks legal breach.

### Taking A Political Side Through Substitution

Substituting a politically convenient rendering distorts the source; preserve meaning and flag genuinely sensitive geopolitical content.

### Forcing Source Social Norms On The Target

Imposing source-culture norms on a locale with different expectations can offend; respect the target audience's norms.

### Substituting Text But Not Imagery

Text-image mismatch is a visible defect; coordinate adjustments across modalities.

### Domesticating Content Whose Specificity Is The Point

Replacing cultural references in literature or history falsifies the work; retain and explain where specificity is content.

## Self-Check

Before approving locale-sensitive content adjustment, verify:

- Sensitivity was identified during analysis, covering legal, political, religious, social, and cultural categories, with native or in-country input where needed.
- A strategy of retain, adjust, substitute, or flag was chosen deliberately for each sensitive item, with rationale recorded.
- Legal and regulatory claims were handled conservatively, with regulated content flagged for compliance review rather than shipped on the translator's authority.
- Political and geopolitical content was handled without taking sides, preserving source meaning and flagging content that may violate target-locale law.
- Religious and social norms were respected, with conflicts between source content and target norms flagged and proposed rather than forced or deleted.
- Examples, names, imagery, and scenarios were adjusted to fit the locale, with text and visual changes coordinated.
- Cultural specificity was preserved where it is the content, as in literature and history, with explanation rather than domestication.
- No sensitive content was translated verbatim where it would cause harm, and none was silently deleted where it carried meaning.
- The localized content is appropriate, compliant, and respectful for the target locale without distorting the source's intent.
