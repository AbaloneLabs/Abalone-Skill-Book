# Stakeholder Alignment

Translation projects rarely have a single owner. A marketing team wants brand impact, a legal team wants liability protection, a regulatory team wants compliance wording, a product team wants feature accuracy, a localization manager wants consistency, and end users want clarity. Each stakeholder measures success differently. When these expectations are not aligned before work begins, the translator becomes the battleground, and the deliverable is revised until it satisfies no one fully. Stakeholder alignment is the work of making authority, priorities, and escalation paths explicit so that conflicts are resolved through structure rather than through the translator guessing.

Use this skill when a translation involves more than one approving party, when instructions conflict, when terminology authority is unclear, when reviewers disagree, or when the agent must set up a query and approval workflow before drafting. The goal is to prevent late, contradictory feedback from derailing a deliverable that was otherwise on track.

## Core Rules

### Map The Stakeholders Before Translating

Identify every party with an interest in the output. A stakeholder map should name who requests, who decides, who reviews, who is consulted, and who is informed.

Typical stakeholders include the commissioning requester, the budget owner, the brand or marketing owner, the legal or compliance owner, the regulatory owner, the product or engineering owner, the subject-matter expert, the in-country reviewer, the localization project manager, and the end-user representative. Not every project has all of these, but each role that exists should be named.

For each stakeholder, record what they care about. Legal cares about liability and exact wording. Brand cares about voice and emotional effect. Regulatory cares about mandated phrasing. Product cares about feature accuracy. Users care about clarity. These cares overlap and conflict.

### Establish Decision Authority

Stakeholders give input, but someone must decide. Ambiguous authority produces endless revision cycles.

For each decision category, name the authority. Common categories include terminology, brand voice, legal wording, regulatory wording, technical accuracy, tone, and final sign-off. The authority for terminology may be the terminologist or the subject-matter expert. The authority for brand voice may be the marketing owner. The authority for final sign-off may be the commissioning requester.

When two stakeholders both claim authority over the same category, escalate before work begins. Do not wait for a conflict to surface during review.

### Resolve Priority Conflicts Explicitly

Stakeholder cares conflict in predictable ways. Brand wants short punchy headlines; legal wants complete disclaimers. Regulatory wants mandated phrasing; marketing wants friendly tone. Product wants feature completeness; UX wants brevity.

Make the priority order explicit. A useful default for high-stakes content is regulatory and safety first, then legal, then technical accuracy, then user clarity, then brand voice, then style preference. For marketing content the order may invert. The point is not the default but that the order is agreed and recorded.

When a conflict arises, refer to the priority order rather than relitigating each time.

### Set Up A Query Workflow

Translators generate queries when the source is ambiguous, terminology is missing, or instructions conflict. Without a query workflow, queries pile up unanswered and stall delivery.

Define how queries are raised, who answers them, the target response time, and what happens when an answer is late. A query log with owner, status, and resolution prevents lost questions. Specify a default rule for late answers, such as proceed with a documented assumption after a stated deadline.

Queries are not a sign of weakness. A project with no queries usually means queries are being silently resolved by guessing.

### Agree Review Cycles In Advance

Review scope and cycle count should be agreed before delivery, not negotiated after. Unbounded review produces churn.

Define how many review cycles are included, who reviews in each cycle, what feedback format is expected, what counts as a revision versus a new request, and how conflicting reviewer feedback is reconciled. Distinguish linguistic review, subject-matter review, brand review, and legal review, and decide whether they run in parallel or sequence.

A reviewer who joins late and contradicts an earlier approved decision should be routed through the authority structure, not allowed to override silently.

### Manage In-Country Reviewers Carefully

In-country reviewers are valuable but dangerous. They know the locale and the audience, but they often have no brief, no glossary, and no awareness of constraints already accepted. Their feedback can range from essential corrections to personal stylistic preferences presented as errors.

Give in-country reviewers the brief, the glossary, the style guide, and the agreed priority order. Ask them to classify feedback as error, improvement, or preference. Route preference feedback through the authority rather than applying it automatically.

### Document Accepted Decisions

Every resolved conflict, accepted term, and approved deviation should be recorded in a living decisions log. The log prevents the same argument recurring in the next cycle or the next project.

The log should capture the decision, the rationale, the authority that approved it, and the date. When a stakeholder later challenges the choice, the log shows that the decision was made deliberately and by the right person.

### Communicate Scope And Authority To Reviewers

Reviewers who do not know the scope and their own authority cause the most damage. A legal reviewer who rewrites marketing tone, or a marketing reviewer who softens regulatory warnings, breaks the priority order.

Brief reviewers on what is in their lane and what is not. A short reviewer charter that states the review focus, the constraints already accepted, and the feedback format prevents most overreach.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Feedback As Equal

A stylistic preference from a senior stakeholder can feel mandatory but may conflict with the brief. Weight feedback by authority and by the agreed priority order, not by seniority or volume.

### Letting The Loudest Reviewer Win

In-country and senior reviewers often dominate because they speak with confidence. A quieter subject-matter expert may hold the actual authority on terminology. Confirm authority before applying feedback.

### Skipping The Stakeholder Map

When the translator assumes the requester is the only stakeholder, late input from legal, brand, or regulatory forces rework. Map stakeholders even for small projects, because small projects still have hidden owners.

### Allowing Unlimited Review Cycles

Without a cycle limit, review never converges. Each cycle introduces new preferences. Agree the cycle count and the feedback scope before delivery.

### Resolving Conflicts By Averaging

When two stakeholders disagree, splitting the difference often satisfies neither and introduces a third option no one wanted. Refer conflicts to the authority and the priority order instead.

### Letting Queries Drift

Unanswered queries become silent assumptions, and silent assumptions become delivery disputes. Track queries to resolution or to a documented default.

### Forgetting The End User

Stakeholders argue among themselves and lose sight of the reader. Periodically test decisions against the actual target reader's needs, especially for clarity, safety, and accessibility.

## Self-Check

Before starting a multi-stakeholder project or finalizing its governance, verify:

- A stakeholder map exists naming requesters, decision authorities, reviewers, consultants, and informed parties.
- Decision authority is assigned for each category such as terminology, brand, legal, regulatory, technical, and final sign-off.
- A priority order is agreed and recorded for resolving conflicts among stakeholder cares.
- A query workflow exists with owners, response targets, and a default rule for late answers.
- Review cycles, reviewer scope, and feedback format are agreed before delivery.
- In-country reviewers receive the brief, glossary, style guide, and priority order, and are asked to classify feedback.
- A decisions log captures resolved conflicts, accepted terms, and approved deviations with rationale and authority.
- Reviewers are briefed on their lane and on constraints already accepted.
- No stakeholder has implicit veto authority that was never explicitly granted.
- The end user's needs remain visible in decisions, not only internal stakeholder preferences.
