# Brief Template And Checklist

A translation brief is the contract between intent and output. When it is missing, the translator guesses at purpose, audience, tone, constraints, and risk, and the guesses rarely match what the requester actually needed. A brief is not a formality. It is the instrument that turns a vague request such as translate this into a job with measurable success criteria. Agents often skip the brief because the source text looks translatable on the surface, then discover late that the locale was wrong, the tone was wrong, the deadline implied triage, or a glossary existed that nobody mentioned.

Use this skill when assembling, reviewing, or completing a translation brief, when a requester has provided only partial instructions, or when the agent must reconstruct missing brief information before starting work. The goal is to prevent silent assumptions that surface as rework, complaints, or unusable deliverables.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Brief As A Decision Document

A brief captures decisions that constrain everything downstream. Do not treat it as a header to fill in quickly. Each field changes the correct translation. Record the decisions explicitly so they can be challenged, confirmed, or revised.

A complete brief should resolve at minimum the following before drafting begins:

- source language and locale of the source;
- target language and specific target locale;
- task type, such as translation, localization, transcreation, certified translation, subtitle, summary, or post-editing;
- purpose and success criteria;
- target reader and reading context;
- tone, register, and brand voice requirements;
- degree of freedom allowed;
- glossary, termbase, style guide, and translation memory references;
- deliverable format and structure;
- deadlines, volume, and any triage rules;
- risk level and review obligations;
- confidentiality and data handling constraints.

If a field cannot be answered, mark it as an open question rather than leaving it blank and guessing later.

### Distinguish Hard Constraints From Preferences

Some brief elements are hard constraints that cannot be negotiated silently. Others are preferences that the translator may adjust. Conflating the two causes both over-cautious and over-creative output.

Hard constraints typically include legal or regulatory wording, certified translation requirements, fixed terminology, character limits, file formats, locale, and non-disclosure obligations. Preferences typically include style flourishes, paragraph ordering, and optional explanatory notes.

When in doubt, ask the requester to classify an element. Record the classification in the brief so reviewers see which choices were mandated and which were judgment calls.

### Capture Reference Materials Explicitly

A brief without references is fragile. List every artifact the translator is allowed or required to use, and every artifact that is forbidden.

Common reference materials include prior translations, approved glossaries, termbases, style guides, brand guidelines, competitor examples, screenshots, design files, source author contacts, regulatory text, and translation memory exports. Note version numbers and dates where relevant, because a stale glossary can contradict a current one.

If no references exist, state that explicitly. Do not invent references or assume prior translations are authoritative without confirmation.

### Define Locale Precisely

Language is not enough. Spanish for Mexico differs from Spanish for Spain in vocabulary, politeness, legal terms, and date formats. English for the United States, the United Kingdom, India, and Singapore differ in spelling, idiom, and reader expectation. Arabic, French, Portuguese, German, and Chinese each have significant regional variation.

The brief must specify the target locale, not just the language. When a requester says Spanish without a locale, flag the gap and propose the most likely locale based on audience, then confirm.

### Record Success Criteria In Verifiable Terms

Vague success criteria such as good quality or natural sounding cannot be checked. Translate criteria into verifiable statements.

Examples of verifiable criteria include the target must be understandable to a non-specialist reader, terminology must match the approved glossary, warnings must remain in the same position and emphasis as the source, character count must not exceed the UI field limit, and the deliverable must pass a bilingual review with no major errors. Verifiable criteria let the translator and reviewer agree on done.

### Plan For Missing Information

Briefs are often incomplete. The agent should have a default recovery path rather than improvising.

For each missing field, decide whether to query the requester, make a stated assumption, or refuse to proceed. High-stakes fields such as locale, task type, risk level, and certification status should almost always be queried before work begins. Lower-stakes fields such as optional notes can be assumed with a recorded caveat.

Document every assumption in the brief so reviewers can see what was decided in the absence of instruction.

### Align The Brief With The Deliverable Format

The deliverable format shapes how the brief should be written. A subtitle job needs timing and character-per-line constraints in the brief. A software localization job needs context screenshots, string IDs, and concatenation rules. A certified translation needs a statement of accuracy and a defined layout. A marketing transcreation needs brand voice references and back-translation expectations.

Do not copy a generic brief template into a specialized job. Adapt the checklist to the deliverable.

## Common Traps

### Filling The Brief From Memory Of A Similar Job

Two jobs that look similar can have different locales, glossaries, or risk levels. Copying a previous brief without verifying each field imports stale constraints and produces output that fits the wrong job.

### Treating Locale As Optional

Omitting locale forces the translator to pick a default, which may not match the audience. The error often surfaces only after delivery, when readers complain about unfamiliar terms or spelling.

### Confusing Purpose With Topic

The topic is what the text is about. The purpose is what the translation must achieve. A patient leaflet and a clinical study report may share a topic but have opposite purposes, audiences, and acceptable freedom. The brief must capture purpose, not just topic.

### Leaving Degree Of Freedom Undefined

Without a stated degree of freedom, translators default either to literal or to free, and neither default is always right. A legal clause translated freely can change an obligation. A marketing headline translated literally can lose all effect. State the allowed freedom explicitly.

### Ignoring Deadline Implications

A short deadline may require triage, partial delivery, or post-editing of machine output rather than full human translation. If the brief does not acknowledge the deadline, the translator may promise full quality that cannot be delivered, or cut corners silently. Make triage rules explicit.

### Assuming Confidentiality Is Handled Elsewhere

Translation often exposes personal, legal, medical, financial, or unreleased material. If the brief does not state handling rules, the translator may paste content into external tools, share examples, or store data insecurely. Record the confidentiality classification and permitted tools.

## Self-Check

Before approving a brief or starting work under one, verify:

- Source and target languages are recorded, and the target locale is specified, not just the language.
- Task type is explicit and matches the work being requested.
- Purpose and success criteria are written in verifiable terms, not as vague quality goals.
- Target reader and reading context are defined.
- Tone, register, and brand voice requirements are captured or marked as open.
- Degree of freedom is stated, distinguishing hard constraints from preferences.
- Reference materials are listed with versions, or their absence is recorded.
- Deliverable format, structure, and any character or layout limits are defined.
- Deadline, volume, and triage rules are acknowledged if they constrain quality.
- Risk level is assessed and review obligations are stated for high-stakes content.
- Confidentiality classification and permitted tools are recorded.
- Every unanswerable field is marked as an open question rather than left blank for silent guessing.
