# Locale And Register Matching

A language is not a single uniform system. Spanish, Arabic, French, English, Chinese, German, and Portuguese each split into regional and social varieties that differ in spelling, vocabulary, grammar, politeness, date and number formats, and reader expectation. Register, the level of formality and social distance, varies independently and interacts with locale. A translation that is grammatically correct in the language can still be wrong for the locale, wrong for the register, or wrong for the institutional voice the reader expects. These mismatches feel jarring or even offensive to the target reader, and in regulated contexts they can cause non-compliance.

Use this skill when selecting among regional variants, when deciding formal versus informal address, when aligning tone to a brand or institution, or when resolving locale-dependent conventions such as dates, numbers, units, and names. The goal is to make the translation belong to a specific place and social context rather than to a generic language.

## Core Rules

### Specify Locale, Not Just Language

Never accept a language without a locale. The same language varies enough across regions that a wrong locale produces text the reader recognizes as foreign.

Spanish for Mexico differs from Spain and Argentina in second-person pronouns, vocabulary, and politeness norms. French for France differs from Canadian French in terminology, especially in government and technology. Portuguese for Brazil differs from Portugal in grammar, spelling, and address. Arabic dialects differ enough that a Modern Standard Arabic text reads formally across regions but a colloquial text does not travel. English for the United States, United Kingdom, India, and Singapore differ in spelling, vocabulary, idiom, and reader expectation. Simplified and Traditional Chinese differ in character set, vocabulary, and sometimes grammar.

When a requester gives only a language, flag the gap, propose the most likely locale from the audience, and confirm before drafting.

### Match Register To Relationship And Institution

Register encodes the social relationship between writer and reader. Choose it deliberately.

Dimensions of register include formality, politeness, intimacy, technicality, and institutional voice. A patient-facing health notice, a legal contract, a marketing email, a chatbot reply, and an internal engineering memo each call for a different register even within the same locale.

For languages with grammaticalized politeness such as Japanese, Korean, and many European languages, the formal or informal address choice is not optional and not personal. It signals the relationship. Choosing informal address where formal is expected can insult; choosing formal where intimate is expected can feel cold.

### Align With Brand Or Institutional Voice

Organizations develop a voice. The translation should match it, not impose the translator's preferred register.

Brand voice may be warm and conversational, authoritative and clinical, playful and irreverent, or formal and respectful. The same source sentence rendered in different voices produces different target sentences. A brand style guide, when available, should govern these choices. When no guide exists, request reference samples of approved target-language content and match them.

Institutional voice for governments, courts, regulators, and universities often has conventional phrasings that readers expect. Departing from them can reduce trust even when the wording is grammatically correct.

### Resolve Locale-Dependent Formats Consistently

Dates, times, numbers, currency, units of measurement, names, addresses, and telephone formats are locale-dependent. Handle them by rule, not by whim.

Date formats differ as day-month-year, month-day-year, or year-month-day, and the same numeric date can be misread across locales. Decimal and thousands separators differ, with some locales using comma for decimal. Currency symbols and placement differ. Measurement units may need conversion between metric and imperial depending on the target locale. Name order differs as given-surname or surname-given, and some locales use patronymics or middle names that affect forms of address.

Decide the rule for each category and apply it consistently. Inconsistent formatting signals carelessness to the reader.

### Handle Politeness And Honorifics Systematically

In languages with honorific systems, politeness is structural, not stylistic. It appears in pronouns, verb endings, titles, and name forms.

Japanese and Korean encode relative status and distance throughout the sentence. Choosing the wrong level can make text sound presumptuous or excessively deferential. Many European languages encode a formal or informal you distinction that carries social meaning. Arabic, Chinese, and others have title and name conventions that signal respect.

Do not default to one level for the whole document without considering the relationship. A customer service reply, a legal notice, and a peer-to-peer message in the same language may require different politeness levels.

### Watch For False Friends Across Locales

Words that are acceptable in one locale may be wrong or offensive in another. A term that is neutral in Spain may be vulgar in Mexico. A marketing phrase that is friendly in the United States may read as overly direct in the United Kingdom. A product name that works in one market may have an unfortunate meaning in another.

When translating for a locale that is not the translator's native variety, verify locale-sensitive terms with a native reviewer or a locale-specific reference.

### Keep Locale And Register Consistent Within A Deliverable

A single deliverable should not shift locale or register mid-text. Switching from formal to informal address, or from one regional term to another, breaks the reader's trust.

Maintain a locale and register decisions log so that choices made early govern later segments. When the source itself shifts register for effect, mirror the shift deliberately rather than letting it happen accidentally.

## Common Traps

### Accepting A Language Without A Locale

Defaulting to the translator's own variety produces text that may not match the audience. Confirm locale first.

### Defaulting To A Familiar Register

Translators often default to the register they are comfortable with, which may not match the brand, institution, or relationship. Match the required voice, not the personal preference.

### Mixing Formal And Informal Address

Inconsistent politeness, especially in languages with a formal or informal you, reads as erratic or disrespectful. Decide the level and hold it.

### Forgetting Format Conversion

Leaving source dates, numbers, or units unchanged can confuse or mislead the target reader. Convert deliberately and consistently.

### Assuming One Spanish, Arabic, Or Chinese

Treating a language as monolithic ignores real variation. Specify the variety and verify sensitive terms.

### Ignoring Brand Voice In Favor Of Linguistic Preference

A grammatically elegant rendering that contradicts the brand voice is wrong for that deliverable. Voice governs style.

### Trusting Cross-Locale Cognates

Words shared across locales of the same language can carry different meanings or connotations. Verify rather than assume.

## Self-Check

Before approving locale and register choices, verify:

- The target locale is specified, not just the target language, and the requester confirmed it.
- Register matches the relationship, institution, and brand voice required by the brief.
- Formal or informal address and honorific choices are deliberate and consistent throughout.
- Dates, times, numbers, currency, units, names, and addresses follow locale rules applied consistently.
- Locale-sensitive terms have been verified for the target variety, especially when the translator is not native to that variety.
- Brand or institutional voice is matched, not overridden by personal style preference.
- Locale and register do not shift unintentionally within the deliverable.
- A decisions log captures locale and register choices so reviewers apply the same standards.
- No term has been used that is neutral in one variety but wrong or offensive in the target locale.
- Where the requester did not specify locale or register, a stated assumption replaces silent guessing.
