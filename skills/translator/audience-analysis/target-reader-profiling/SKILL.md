# Target Reader Profiling

The same source text can be translated many ways depending on who reads it. A clinical trial protocol read by a regulatory authority is not the same translation problem as the same study summarized for patients. A software release note read by engineers is not the same as one read by non-technical business users. A legal warning read by a judge is not the same as one read by a consumer skimming a package insert. When the translator does not profile the target reader, the output drifts toward an abstract ideal of correct language that serves no actual reader well. It may be too technical for lay users, too simple for experts, too formal for customers, or too casual for officials.

Use this skill when defining who the translation is for, when the requester has not specified the reader, when the source and target readers differ in expertise, or when the agent must decide reading level, vocabulary density, and explanatory depth. The goal is to translate for a concrete reader in a concrete situation rather than for a language in general.

## Core Rules

### Profile The Reader On Multiple Dimensions

A reader is not just a language speaker. Profile the reader across dimensions that change the correct translation.

Relevant dimensions include native or non-native speaker of the target language, expertise in the subject, education level, age range, role such as consumer patient professional official or student, motivation such as deciding learning complying buying or warning, reading situation such as skimming studying or acting under pressure, prior exposure to the topic, and whether the reader will compare the translation with the source.

Each dimension shifts choices. A non-native reader needs simpler syntax and fewer idioms. An expert reader expects precise terminology and tolerates density. A reader acting under pressure needs the critical information first and unambiguously.

### Distinguish Source Reader From Target Reader

The source was written for a source reader. The translation is for a target reader. They are often not the same.

A source written by specialists for specialists may need to become a translation for patients or customers. A source written for a domestic consumer may need to become a translation for a foreign regulator. A source written for trained staff may need to become a translation for the general public. When the readers differ, fidelity to the source wording can betray fidelity to the source purpose.

Identify where the readers diverge and adjust the translation strategy accordingly. This is not license to rewrite freely. It is recognition that serving the same function for a different reader may require different choices.

### Match Vocabulary Density To Expertise

Vocabulary density is the proportion of specialized terms and compact syntax in a passage. Match it to the reader.

For expert readers, use precise terminology even when dense, because paraphrasing reads as condescending or imprecise. For lay readers, unpack terms, add brief explanations, and prefer common words where they do not lose meaning. For mixed audiences, layer the text so the core message is accessible and the detail is available without forcing every reader through the same density.

Do not flatten all content to the lowest reading level. That insults experts and bloats the text. Do not keep expert density for lay readers. That excludes them.

### Decide Explanatory Depth Deliberately

How much background to add is a translation decision, not an afterthought. The source assumes the source reader's background. The target reader may lack it.

For cultural references, institutions, laws, units, and brands, decide whether to translate directly, translate with a brief gloss, substitute a local equivalent, or explain. The choice depends on the reader. An expert reader may need no gloss. A lay reader may need a short explanation. A reader in a different legal system may need context that the source reader took for granted.

Record the explanatory depth rule so it stays consistent across the document. Inconsistent explanation reads as erratic.

### Account For Reading Situation And Pressure

A reader skimming a warning label under stress needs different text than a reader studying a textbook chapter. Reading situation changes sentence length, information order, and emphasis.

For high-pressure reading, front-load the critical action or warning, keep sentences short, avoid subordinate clauses that delay the main point, and repeat key safety information where appropriate. For study reading, density and nuance are acceptable because the reader will reread. For skimming, structure with headings and bullets so the reader can find what matters.

### Consider Non-Native And Accessibility Needs

Many target readers are not native speakers of the target language, and some have accessibility needs. Both affect translation.

For non-native readers, prefer common vocabulary, active voice, literal idioms or none, and shorter sentences. Avoid culture-bound metaphors that require native intuition. For readers using screen readers or with cognitive load constraints, avoid ambiguous punctuation, avoid information buried in images, and ensure structure is navigable.

Accessibility is part of audience profiling, not a separate afterthought.

### Validate The Profile Against Real Samples

A profile is a hypothesis. Test it when possible. If representative target readers can review a sample, their reaction reveals whether the profiled choices work. If not, at least re-read the translation while imagining the profiled reader in the profiled situation, and ask whether that reader would understand, trust, and act on the text.

A translation that passes a linguistic check but fails the imagined reader is not done.

## Common Traps

### Translating For An Abstract Language

Choosing correct target-language words without a concrete reader produces text that is grammatically fine but functionally wrong for whoever actually reads it.

### Assuming Target Reader Equals Source Reader

When the readers differ, source-faithful wording can miss the target reader entirely. Confirm the readers match before inheriting the source's assumptions.

### Over-Explaining For Experts

Adding background that experts already know bloats the text and signals disrespect. Match explanatory depth to the actual reader.

### Under-Explaining For Lay Readers

Translating dense source terms directly for lay readers produces text they cannot use. Unpack where the reader needs it.

### Ignoring Reading Pressure

Text written for study reading fails when the reader is acting under stress. Front-load critical information for high-pressure situations.

### Using Idioms A Non-Native Reader Will Miss

Idioms that feel natural to a native translator may be opaque to a non-native target reader. Choose with the reader in mind.

### Confusing Reading Level With Dumbing Down

Adjusting reading level is not removing content. It is presenting the same content in syntax and vocabulary the reader can process.

## Self-Check

Before finalizing audience choices or approving a translation against its reader profile, verify:

- The target reader is profiled on expertise, education, age, role, motivation, reading situation, and native or non-native status.
- Source reader and target reader are compared, and divergences are identified.
- Vocabulary density matches the reader's expertise, neither over-dense for lay readers nor over-simplified for experts.
- Explanatory depth for cultural, legal, institutional, and brand references is decided and consistent.
- Reading situation and pressure are reflected in sentence length, information order, and emphasis.
- Non-native and accessibility needs are considered in syntax, idiom, and structure choices.
- The translation has been re-read while imagining the profiled reader in the profiled situation.
- No choice serves grammatical correctness at the expense of the actual reader's understanding or action.
- The profile is recorded so reviewers evaluate the translation against the same reader, not their own preferences.
- Where the requester did not specify the reader, a stated assumption replaces silent guessing.
