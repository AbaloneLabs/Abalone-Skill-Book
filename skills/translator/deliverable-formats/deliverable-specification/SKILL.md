# Deliverable Specification

A deliverable specification is the contract that defines what the client will receive, in what form, by when, and judged against what criteria. It exists to remove ambiguity from the most contested moment of a translation project: handoff, when the client opens the deliverable and decides whether it meets their expectation. Without a specification, that expectation is unspoken, and the translator guesses at format, scope, naming, language variant, and completeness, then discovers at delivery that the guess was wrong. With a specification, both parties agreed in advance on what "done" means, and handoff becomes a verification against a shared standard rather than a negotiation under deadline pressure. Agents miss the importance of specification because the linguistic work feels like the real task and the packaging feels like an afterthought, because clients often cannot articulate their requirements until they see the wrong deliverable, and because the cost of an unspecified deliverable is paid entirely at the end when rework is most expensive. The harm this skill prevents is deliverables that are linguistically sound but rejected because they are the wrong format, the wrong language variant, incompletely scoped, or judged against criteria that were never agreed.

Use this skill when defining what a translation deliverable will contain and look like, specifying output formats and structure, agreeing acceptance criteria and turnaround, distinguishing in-scope from out-of-scope content, or establishing the deliverable contract before work begins. The goal is a written, agreed specification that makes "done" unambiguous and verifiable.

## Core Rules

### Specify Output Format And Structure Explicitly

The deliverable format is the first thing the client encounters and the most common source of rejection when it is wrong. Specify, in writing and agreed before work begins, the exact output format or formats: the file type, the version, the structure, and any format-specific requirements such as encoding, byte order mark presence, naming convention, and folder organization. If the client needs multiple formats, such as an editable working file plus a final formatted file plus a bilingual review file, specify each and what it contains. If the deliverable must integrate into a downstream system, such as a content management system import or a software build, specify the integration requirements and confirm compatibility. A format specified as "Word document" is insufficient because it leaves version, encoding, and structure to assumption; a format specified as "DOCX, UTF-8, named per the source with locale suffix, in a folder per language" is verifiable. Vague format specifications produce deliverables that are technically translations but practically unusable by the client.

### Define Scope Boundaries And Exclusions Precisely

Scope is what is in and what is out of the translation, and imprecise scope is the primary cause of deliverable disputes. Specify what content is in scope: the main text, headings, captions, table content, UI strings, metadata, and any hidden or embedded content, with an explicit decision per type. Specify what is out of scope: code, identifiers, URLs, legal disclaimers that must remain in the source language, content under separate contract, or content the client will handle internally. Specify partial-scope cases: content that is translated but not reviewed, content that is transcreated rather than translated, or content that requires subject-matter expert validation before delivery. Scope must be defined at a granularity that lets the translator decide any borderline case without guessing, because every undocumented borderline case becomes a potential rejection point. A scope statement of "translate the document" leaves dozens of decisions unresolved; a scope statement that enumerates content types and their treatment resolves them in advance.

### Specify The Language Variant And Locale Precisely

"Spanish," "Chinese," "French," and "Portuguese" are not single languages for translation purposes; they are families with significant regional and lexical differences. A deliverable in European Spanish may be wrong for a Mexican audience; Simplified Chinese is wrong for a Taiwan market; Canadian French differs from European French in vocabulary and register. Specify the exact language variant and locale, including region and script, and confirm it matches the target audience. Where a client requests a "generic" or "international" variant, explain the tradeoffs: a neutral variant may be broadly acceptable but optimal for no single market, and it may still carry the translator's regional bias. Document the chosen variant so that the deliverable's intended audience is unambiguous and so that future updates target the same variant. A deliverable in the wrong variant is linguistically a translation but commercially wrong for its market.

### Agree Acceptance Criteria And Turnaround Before Starting

Acceptance criteria define what makes the deliverable acceptable, and they must be agreed before work begins, not invented at delivery. Criteria should cover the quality dimensions that matter for the content, the threshold or standard applied, the review and revision process, the handling of queries and rework, and the conditions under which the deliverable is deemed complete. Tie the criteria to the content's risk tier: high-risk content such as medical or legal material demands stricter criteria and defined review by qualified parties; lower-risk content may accept a lighter standard. Agree the turnaround, including milestones for large projects, and build in the time that the agreed criteria require. Criteria and turnaround are linked: strict criteria need more time, and a tight turnaround may force a lighter criteria, and that tradeoff must be explicit rather than hidden. An unagreed standard lets the client apply any expectation at delivery, and an unagreed turnaround lets either party claim the other missed it.

### Document The Specification In Writing And Confirm Agreement

A specification that exists only in conversation or in the translator's notes is not a specification; it is an assumption. Document the deliverable specification in writing, share it with the client, and obtain explicit confirmation before starting work. The document should capture format, scope, language variant, acceptance criteria, turnaround, and any assumptions or dependencies. Written confirmation protects both parties: the translator has a defensible standard to deliver against, and the client has a reference for what they agreed to. Where the client's requirements are vague, the written specification forces clarification, which is exactly its value. A specification confirmed in writing eliminates the "I thought you meant" disputes that destroy trust at handoff.

### Manage Changes To The Specification Through A Defined Process

Specifications change during projects: the client adds scope, requests a different format, changes the target variant, or tightens the turnaround. These changes must be handled through a defined process, not absorbed silently. When a change is requested, assess its impact on schedule, cost, and quality, communicate the impact to the client, obtain agreement to the revised specification, and update the written document. Silent absorption of changes, where the translator simply does the additional work without revising the basis, leads to a deliverable judged against a moving target and to effort that is never accounted for. A defined change process keeps the specification live and accurate and ensures that both parties agree to the basis under which the work is actually delivered.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Output Format To Assumption

Specifying the deliverable as "the translated document" leaves format, version, encoding, and structure to guesswork. This is a trap because the translator delivers a reasonable format that may not match the client's integration need. Specify format explicitly and confirm compatibility with downstream systems.

### Vague Scope That Leaves Borderline Cases Unresolved

A scope of "translate the document" leaves decisions about hidden text, metadata, captions, and embedded content to the moment of work, where each becomes a guess. This is a trap because each undocumented case is a potential rejection. Enumerate content types and their treatment in advance.

### Requesting Or Delivering A Generic Language Variant Without Discussing Tradeoffs

A "generic" or "international" variant carries regional bias and is optimal for no single market. This is a trap because it sounds efficient but under-serves every audience. Specify the exact variant for the target market and document the choice.

### Agreeing Criteria And Turnaround Only Verbally

A verbal agreement on standard and schedule cannot be referenced or defended when expectations diverge. This is a trap because both parties remember it differently. Document criteria and turnaround in writing and confirm before starting.

### Absorbing Scope Or Format Changes Silently

Doing additional work or changing format without revising the specification basis leads to a deliverable judged against a moving target. This is a trap because the extra effort is invisible and the basis is unclear. Manage changes through a defined process with documented impact and agreement.

### Starting Work Before The Specification Is Confirmed

Beginning translation before the client confirms the written specification risks delivering against an unagreed standard. This is a trap because the pressure to start discourages the pause needed for confirmation. Obtain explicit written agreement before starting.

## Self-Check

Before starting work and before delivering, verify:

- The output format or formats are specified explicitly, including file type, version, encoding, byte order mark, naming convention, and folder structure, and compatibility with any downstream system is confirmed.
- Scope boundaries are defined at content-type granularity, with every borderline case, hidden text, metadata, captions, embedded content, resolved, and exclusions and partial-scope cases explicitly documented.
- The exact language variant and locale, including region and script, are specified to match the target audience, and any "generic" variant request has had its tradeoffs discussed and documented.
- Acceptance criteria and turnaround are agreed in writing before work begins, tied to the content's risk tier, with the link between strictness and time made explicit.
- The full deliverable specification is documented in writing and explicitly confirmed by the client before translation starts, not carried as a verbal or internal assumption.
- Any changes to scope, format, variant, criteria, or turnaround during the project are handled through a defined process with impact assessed, communicated, agreed, and recorded.
- No work proceeds against an unconfirmed specification, and no change is absorbed without updating the written basis.
- At handoff, the deliverable can be verified against the agreed specification rather than judged against an unspoken expectation.
