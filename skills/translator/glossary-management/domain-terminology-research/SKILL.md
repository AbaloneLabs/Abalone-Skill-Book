# Domain Terminology Research

Specialized domains have their own terminology, and getting it wrong is one of the fastest ways to lose credibility or cause harm. A medical translator who uses the everyday word where a clinical term is required misleads clinicians. A legal translator who invents an equivalent for a jurisdiction-specific concept creates a false impression of equivalence. A technical translator who guesses at engineering terms produces documentation that professionals distrust. Domain terminology research is the disciplined work of finding the right target-language term for a specialized concept, evaluating whether a source is authoritative, and deciding what to do when no established equivalent exists. It is not a quick dictionary lookup. It is a research task that draws on corpora, parallel texts, standards, expert input, and judgment, and the quality of the research directly determines the quality of the translation.

Use this skill when translating specialized content and needing to establish terminology, when evaluating terminology sources, when a term has no clear target equivalent, or when building domain glossaries for a project. The goal is to use terms that domain professionals recognize as correct, and to handle gaps honestly rather than guessing.

## Core Rules

### Research Before Translating Specialized Content

For specialized domains, establish key terminology before drafting, not ad hoc as you go. Researching term by term during translation produces inconsistent choices and interrupts comprehension.

Start by identifying the domain and subdomain precisely. Medical translation splits into clinical, pharmaceutical, device, public health, and patient-facing, each with different terminology. Legal translation splits by jurisdiction and by area of law. Technical translation splits by engineering discipline. The subdomain determines which sources are relevant.

Build a working glossary for the project before drafting, and refine it as research continues. A pre-built glossary keeps terminology consistent from the first segment.

### Use Authoritative Sources In A Hierarchy

Not all terminology sources are equal. Use them in a hierarchy of authority, and know the limits of each.

At the top are official standards, regulatory terminology databases, and authoritative institutional glossaries, such as those from regulators, standards bodies, and professional associations. Next are specialized domain dictionaries and terminological resources compiled by experts. Next are parallel texts, documents in the target language written by domain professionals on the same topic. Next are corpus evidence showing how terms are actually used. At the bottom, used with caution, are general bilingual dictionaries and machine translation suggestions, which often give everyday rather than domain meanings.

Prefer higher-authority sources. When sources conflict, the authoritative domain source wins over the general dictionary.

### Evaluate Source Reliability Critically

Before trusting a source for a term, evaluate its reliability. A term found online is not automatically correct.

Assess who produced the source, whether it is an authoritative institution, a domain expert, or an anonymous contributor. Assess its currency, because domain terminology evolves, especially in technology and medicine. Assess its provenance, whether it is a curated resource or user-generated. Assess its consistency with other authoritative sources. A single blog post using a term does not establish it as standard, even if the term sounds plausible.

Record the source for each term decision so it can be verified and so the basis is transparent.

### Use Parallel Texts To Confirm Usage

Parallel texts, target-language documents written by domain professionals on the same subject, are among the most reliable evidence for how terms are actually used.

Find parallel texts from authoritative producers such as regulators, professional bodies, peer-reviewed publications, and established companies in the domain. Read them to see how concepts are expressed, which terms are standard, and how they appear in context. Parallel texts reveal conventions that dictionaries miss, such as collocations, register, and how a term fits into a sentence.

Parallel texts are references to inform judgment, not sources to copy. Use them to confirm that your chosen term is what domain professionals actually write.

### Handle Terms With No Established Equivalent

Sometimes no established target-language equivalent exists, especially for new technology, source-culture-specific legal concepts, or emerging medical terms. Handle these deliberately rather than inventing silently.

Options include borrowing the source term with explanation, calquing or loan-translating the term if the structure transfers, coinage following target-language word-formation patterns, descriptive paraphrase, or retaining the source term if it is used as-is in the target domain. The choice depends on domain convention, audience, and whether the term needs to be memorable, precise, or official.

For official or regulated domains, do not coin terms unilaterally. Flag the gap and recommend that the authority establish an equivalent. Document the decision and rationale.

### Verify Numbers Units And Classifications

Domain terminology includes not only words but numeric, unit, and classification systems that vary across jurisdictions and standards.

Medical dosages use different units and conventions. Legal classifications such as crime categories or corporate forms may not map across jurisdictions. Financial terms may carry different regulatory meanings. Technical specifications use standards that differ by region. Verify these against authoritative sources, and convert or annotate where the systems differ, rather than assuming direct equivalence.

A term that is correct in one system can be wrong in another, even when the words match.

### Consult Subject-Matter Experts

For high-stakes or genuinely uncertain terminology, consult a subject-matter expert. Do not rely on research alone when the cost of error is high.

Frame queries precisely. Give the expert the source term, the concept definition, the context, the options you have considered, and what you need to decide. A well-framed query gets a useful answer; a vague query wastes the expert's time. Record the expert's input as the basis for the decision.

Expert consultation is especially important in medicine, law, and regulated technical domains where terminology errors can cause harm or non-compliance.

### Document Research For Reuse

Terminology research is expensive, and the results should be reusable. Document decisions so they feed the termbase and future projects.

For each significant term, record the source term, the chosen equivalent, the concept definition, the context, the source of the decision, and the date. Feed accepted decisions into the termbase with appropriate status. This turns one-time research into organizational asset and prevents the same research being redone on the next project.

## Common Traps

### Using General Dictionaries For Domain Terms

General dictionaries give everyday meanings that are often wrong in specialized domains. Use domain-authoritative sources.

### Trusting Any Online Source

A term found in a random search result is not established. Evaluate source authority before trusting.

### Inventing Equivalents Silently

Coining a term without documentation or authority, especially in regulated domains, creates risk and inconsistency.

### Ignoring Jurisdictional And System Differences

Terms, units, and classifications that match in words may differ in meaning across systems. Verify against the target system.

### Researching Ad Hoc During Translation

Term-by-term decisions during drafting produce inconsistency and interrupt comprehension. Research key terms first.

### Skipping Expert Consultation For High-Stakes Terms

Relying on research alone for uncertain high-stakes terminology risks harm and non-compliance. Consult experts.

### Not Recording Sources And Decisions

Undocumented research is lost and must be redone. Record sources and feed decisions into the termbase.

## Self-Check

Before approving specialized terminology, verify:

- Key domain terminology was researched before drafting, with a working project glossary established.
- Sources were used in a hierarchy of authority, with domain-authoritative sources preferred over general dictionaries.
- Source reliability was evaluated for producer, currency, provenance, and consistency before trusting.
- Parallel texts from authoritative domain professionals were used to confirm actual usage and conventions.
- Terms with no established equivalent were handled deliberately, with the decision documented and authority consulted for regulated domains.
- Numbers, units, and classifications were verified against the target system, with conversion or annotation where systems differ.
- Subject-matter experts were consulted for high-stakes or genuinely uncertain terminology, with precisely framed queries.
- Research decisions are documented with source, definition, context, and date, and fed into the termbase.
- No domain term was translated from an everyday meaning without checking the domain-specific sense.
- The chosen terminology is what domain professionals in the target language would recognize as correct.
