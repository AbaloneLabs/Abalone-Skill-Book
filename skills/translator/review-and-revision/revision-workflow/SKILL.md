# Revision Workflow

Revision is where translation quality is consolidated, but only if the workflow is designed for it. A revision workflow defines who revises what, in what order, against which criteria, and how feedback flows back to the translator. Without a workflow, revision becomes ad hoc: one reviser checks everything while another checks nothing, bilingual comparison is skipped or done without fluency review, feedback is delivered too late to help, and sign-off is a formality rather than a quality gate. With a structured workflow, revision is staged, the right reviewer checks the right aspects, feedback is timely and actionable, and sign-off confirms that the defined quality bar has been met. Revision is not a single act of "checking the translation." It is a sequence of checks, each with a purpose, that together close the gap between a first draft and a deliverable. A weak workflow wastes revision effort and lets errors through; a strong workflow turns revision into the reliable quality control stage it should be.

Use this skill when designing, following, or improving a revision workflow, deciding how revision is staged and assigned, or managing the feedback and sign-off loop between translator and reviser. The goal is a workflow where revision is staged, assigned, and fed back so that it reliably improves the translation before delivery.

## Core Rules

### Separate Self-Revision From Peer Revision

Self-revision and peer revision serve different purposes and must both happen.

Self-revision is the translator's own review of their draft before delivery, ideally after a break so they see the text fresh. Self-revision catches the obvious errors, polishes fluency, and ensures completeness. It is mandatory because no translator produces a final draft on the first pass. Peer revision, or bilingual review by another linguist, catches errors the translator is blind to, brings a second perspective, and provides the quality gate. Peer revision is not a substitute for self-revision; it assumes a self-revised draft. Skipping self-revision dumps avoidable errors on the reviser and wastes revision time.

Define both stages in the workflow and require self-revision before peer revision begins.

### Stage Revision By Purpose

Revision is more effective when staged by purpose rather than done as one undifferentiated pass. Stage the checks.

A common staging is: a bilingual pass comparing source and target for accuracy, completeness, and terminology; a monolingual pass reading the target alone for fluency, style, and readability; and a conformance pass checking formatting, locale conventions, and project requirements. Staging prevents one concern from crowding out another: a reviewer doing accuracy and fluency at once often sacrifices fluency to accuracy, or misses accuracy while polishing. Separate passes let each concern get full attention.

Define the passes, their criteria, and their order in the workflow.

### Assign The Right Reviewer For Each Pass

Different passes need different reviewers. Assign by purpose.

The bilingual pass requires a translator-level linguist fluent in both languages. The monolingual fluency pass can be done by a native target-language reviewer who may not know the source, which is often an advantage because they judge the target as a standalone text. The conformance pass may be done by a project reviewer checking against requirements. A client or subject-matter expert may review terminology and accuracy in their domain. Assigning the wrong reviewer to a pass wastes their effort or misses the pass's purpose.

Match reviewer expertise to pass purpose, and document who is responsible for each.

### Define Revision Criteria And Scope

Revision is effective only when criteria and scope are defined. Define them before revision begins.

Criteria include what the reviser should check: accuracy against source, terminology against the termbase, style against the guide, fluency in the target, formatting and locale conventions, and any project-specific requirements. Scope includes whether the reviser should fix issues directly or only flag them, whether they should rewrite or only suggest, and whether they cover the whole text or a sample. Undefined criteria lead to inconsistent revision; undefined scope leads to revisers either over-editing or under-checking.

Provide the reviser with a brief covering criteria, scope, and the reference materials.

### Close The Feedback Loop

Revision produces feedback that must flow back to the translator. Close the loop.

Feedback should be specific, classified by error type and severity, and delivered in time for the translator to act before delivery. The translator should have the opportunity to accept, reject with reason, or discuss suggestions, because the translator may have context the reviser lacks. Rejected suggestions should be discussed, not silently overridden, to avoid errors being re-introduced. The loop is closed when translator and reviser agree on the final text, not when the reviser unilaterally imposes changes.

A feedback loop that is one-directional, with the reviser imposing edits without translator input, loses context and demoralizes the translator.

### Define Sign-Off Criteria

Sign-off is the quality gate that confirms the translation meets the defined bar. Define what sign-off requires.

Sign-off should confirm that all revision passes are complete, that critical and major errors are resolved, that the termbase and style guide are conformed to, that formatting and locale requirements are met, and that the translator and reviser agree on the final text. Sign-off is not a formality; it is the checkpoint where the team attests that the defined quality has been achieved. Define who can sign off and what they must verify.

Without defined sign-off criteria, delivery happens by default rather than by confirmed quality.

### Match Revision Depth To Risk And Budget

Revision depth should match the content's risk and the project's budget. Not all content needs full revision.

A life-critical medical document warrants full bilingual and monolingual revision by qualified reviewers. Internal marketing draft may need only a sample revision. A high-volume knowledge base may use automated checks plus targeted human review. Define revision depth by content tier: full revision for high-stakes content, sample or targeted revision for medium-stakes, and automated or self-revision only for low-stakes. Applying full revision everywhere wastes budget; applying minimal revision to high-stakes content risks harm.

Document the revision depth for each content tier and apply it consistently.

### Track And Improve The Workflow

A revision workflow should improve over time. Track its effectiveness.

Track metrics such as error density found in revision, revision turnaround time, feedback rejection rate, and post-delivery issues. Identify bottlenecks, such as revision taking too long, and quality gaps, such as certain error types recurring. Adjust the workflow based on the data: add a pass, change an assignment, refine criteria. A workflow that is never reviewed and improved ossifies and stops serving quality.

## Common Traps

### Skipping Self-Revision

Dumping an un-revised draft on the reviser wastes revision time on avoidable errors.

### One Undifferentiated Pass

Doing accuracy and fluency in one pass causes one concern to crowd out the other.

### Wrong Reviewer For The Pass

Assigning a source-ignorant reviewer to bilingual review, or a non-native to fluency review, defeats the pass's purpose.

### Undefined Criteria And Scope

Revision without defined criteria and scope leads to inconsistent checking and over- or under-editing.

### One-Directional Feedback

Imposing edits without translator input loses context and demoralizes the translator.

### Sign-Off As Formality

Signing off without verifying defined criteria lets unconfirmed quality ship.

### Uniform Revision Depth

Applying full revision to low-stakes content wastes budget; minimal revision to high-stakes content risks harm.

### Never Improving The Workflow

A workflow that is never tracked and adjusted stops serving quality and ossifies.

## Self-Check

Before approving a revision workflow or signing off a revised translation, verify:

- Self-revision by the translator is required before peer revision begins.
- Revision is staged by purpose, with separate bilingual, monolingual, and conformance passes defined.
- Each pass is assigned to a reviewer with the right expertise for that pass's purpose.
- Revision criteria and scope are defined in a brief provided to the reviser before revision begins.
- Feedback is specific, classified by type and severity, and flows back to the translator with opportunity for response.
- Sign-off criteria are defined and confirmed, including resolution of critical and major errors and agreement on the final text.
- Revision depth matches content risk and budget, with tiers defined and applied consistently.
- The workflow is tracked for effectiveness and improved based on error, time, and feedback data.
- No pass is skipped or assigned to the wrong reviewer due to time pressure.
- The workflow reliably consolidates quality before delivery rather than serving as a formality.
