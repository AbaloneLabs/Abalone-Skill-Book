# Bilingual Review Methodology

Bilingual review is the comparison of a translation against its source to verify that the target conveys the source meaning accurately, completely, and without unintended additions. It is the review mode that catches the errors that matter most: mistranslations, omissions, additions, and shifts in meaning. A target-only review, reading only the translation, catches fluency and style problems but is blind to meaning errors, because the reviewer cannot see what the source said. Bilingual review is therefore essential for any content where meaning accuracy is critical, and it is demanding work. The reviewer must hold source and target in mind together, segment by segment, resist the fatigue that leads to skimming, and distinguish real errors from acceptable translation choices. Done well, bilingual review is the last line of defense against meaning errors; done poorly, it becomes a rubber stamp that misses what it was meant to catch.

Use this skill when conducting or structuring bilingual review, when comparing source and target for accuracy, or when ensuring a review catches the meaning errors a target-only read misses. The goal is to verify that the translation faithfully conveys the source meaning and to catch the specific defect types that only source-target comparison reveals.

## Core Rules

### Compare Source And Target Segment By Segment

The core of bilingual review is systematic segment-by-segment comparison. Do not skim or sample when accuracy matters.

For each segment, read the source, then read the target, then judge whether the target conveys the source meaning accurately and completely. Check that nothing in the source is omitted from the target, nothing in the target is added that is not in the source, and the relationship between parts is preserved. Move through the document systematically, maintaining attention, because fatigue causes reviewers to skim and miss errors in later segments.

A bilingual review that skips segments or reads in bulk catches only the errors visible in the target alone, which defeats the purpose.

### Check For The Core Meaning Defect Types

Bilingual review targets specific defect types that target-only review cannot detect. Check for each deliberately.

Mistranslations render the source meaning incorrectly, such as a wrong term, a shifted condition, or a reversed negation. Omissions drop source content from the target, from a word to a whole sentence. Additions insert target content not present in the source. Shifts change the emphasis, scope, or relationship of meaning, such as a condition becoming an obligation or a possibility becoming a certainty. Register and tone shifts change the source's effect. Each defect type requires a different scan, so review with all of them in mind.

### Verify Completeness And Correspondence

Completeness means all source content appears in the target. Correspondence means the right target content aligns with the right source content.

Check that every sentence, clause, list item, number, name, reference, and visible element in the source has a counterpart in the target. Watch for content that was dropped because it was hard to translate, or because segmentation split it awkwardly. Check that target content aligns with the correct source, because misalignment, especially in tables, lists, and formatted content, can place the right words in the wrong place. Verify cross-references, numbers, and names specifically, because these are easy to drop or alter.

A translation that is fluent but missing a sentence, or that has content misaligned, is defective even if the visible prose reads well.

### Distinguish Errors From Acceptable Choices

Not every difference between source and target is an error. Distinguish genuine errors from acceptable translation choices.

Translation often requires departing from literal wording to produce natural, accurate target text. A restructuring, a different but equivalent idiom, or an explicitation that aids the target reader may be correct, not an error. Judge differences against the source meaning and the brief's allowed freedom, not against literal correspondence. A difference that preserves meaning and serves the purpose is acceptable; one that changes meaning, drops content, or adds content is an error.

Recording the distinction helps avoid over-flagging, which burdens the translator with false corrections, and under-flagging, which misses real errors.

### Check Terminology Against The Termbase

Bilingual review is where terminology consistency is verified against the agreed termbase. Check it deliberately.

For each source term with an approved target equivalent, verify the target uses the approved equivalent. Flag deviations as errors to correct or as deliberate exceptions to document. Check that the same source term is rendered consistently across the document, because inconsistency may indicate the translator missed termbase guidance. Check that defined terms, especially in legal and technical content, are used consistently.

Terminology errors are common and consequential; bilingual review is often where they are caught.

### Verify Numbers Names Dates And References

Factual elements carry meaning and risk. Verify them specifically during bilingual review.

Check every number, date, name, unit, citation, and reference against the source. A transposed digit, a misspelled name, a wrong date, or a broken reference can cause harm, confusion, or liability. These elements are easy to skim past because they look like formatting, but they are content. Verify them as deliberately as the prose.

A bilingual review that does not specifically check numbers and references misses a high-risk defect category.

### Structure The Review For Sustained Attention

Bilingual review is fatiguing, and fatigue causes missed errors. Structure the review to sustain attention.

Break long documents into review sessions with breaks. Use review tools that present source and target side by side clearly. Track progress so no segment is skipped. Where possible, have a second reviewer sample-check, because a fresh reader catches errors a fatigued one missed. For very long or high-stakes content, consider splitting the review across reviewers with overlap zones to catch boundary errors.

A review structured around attention management catches more than one driven through by endurance.

### Provide Actionable Review Feedback

Review findings must be actionable. Provide feedback that the translator or corrector can use.

For each finding, state the segment, the issue, the source meaning, and the suggested correction or the question. Classify findings by severity so the corrector knows what must be fixed and what is optional. Distinguish errors that must be corrected from preferences and suggestions. Clear, specific feedback makes correction efficient and prevents the corrector from misreading the reviewer's intent.

Vague feedback such as check this slows correction and often results in the issue persisting.

## Common Traps

### Reviewing The Target Only

A target-only read cannot catch mistranslations, omissions, or additions, which are the highest-impact defects.

### Skimming Due To Fatigue

Bilingual review is fatiguing, and skimming in later segments misses errors; structure for attention.

### Over-Flagging Acceptable Choices

Treating every departure from literal as an error burdens the translator and erodes trust in the review.

### Missing Numbers And References

Factual elements are easy to skim past but carry high risk; verify them specifically.

### Ignoring Completeness And Alignment

Fluent text that is missing a sentence or has misaligned content is defective despite reading well.

### Inconsistent Terminology Checks

Not verifying terms against the termbase leaves consistency errors uncaught.

### Vague Or Unseverity-Ranked Feedback

Feedback without severity or specifics slows correction and lets issues persist.

## Self-Check

Before approving a bilingual review, verify:

- Source and target were compared segment by segment, systematically, without skipping or skimming.
- Core meaning defect types, mistranslations, omissions, additions, shifts, and register changes, were checked deliberately.
- Completeness and correspondence were verified, with every source element confirmed to have a correct target counterpart.
- Errors were distinguished from acceptable translation choices, with differences judged against meaning and the brief's freedom.
- Terminology was checked against the termbase, with deviations flagged and consistency verified across the document.
- Numbers, names, dates, units, citations, and references were verified specifically against the source.
- The review was structured for sustained attention, with breaks, tools, and second-reviewer sampling where needed.
- Review feedback is actionable, with segment, issue, source meaning, suggested correction, and severity for each finding.
- No segment was reviewed target-only where meaning accuracy was required.
- The review would catch the meaning errors a target-only read cannot, which is the purpose of bilingual review.
