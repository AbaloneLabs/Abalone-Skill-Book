# Scientific And Research Translation

Scientific and research translation covers peer-reviewed papers, preprints, conference proceedings, research abstracts, methods and protocols, grant proposals, and review articles. The readers are researchers, reviewers, clinicians, and regulators who use this content to reproduce experiments, cite findings, assess validity, and build on prior work. The translation must preserve scientific meaning with precision, because a mistranslated method step, a converted concentration done wrong, a misrendered statistical result, or a garbled taxonomic name can make research unreproducible or misleading. Scientific translation sits at the intersection of language expertise and domain literacy: the translator must render meaning accurately, follow the conventions of scientific writing in the target language, and respect the conventions of the discipline. Unlike marketing or literary work, scientific translation rewards clarity, precision, and convention over creativity, and deviations from established terminology or reporting style are read as errors, not flair.

Use this skill when translating scientific papers, research articles, abstracts, methods and experimental protocols, figures and tables captions, or literature reviews, or when ensuring translated research content is accurate and credible to expert readers. The goal is to produce target-language research text that a domain expert can read, trust, cite, and act on with the same confidence as the source.

## Core Rules

### Preserve Scientific Terminology With Authoritative Equivalents

Scientific terminology is the backbone of research translation. Use established, authoritative target-language terms, not everyday approximations or literal renderings.

Build and maintain a domain termbase from authoritative sources before and during translation: disciplinary dictionaries, IUPAC nomenclature for chemistry, ICZN and ICBN rules for organism names, MeSH for biomedical terms, ISO terminology standards, and established target-language scientific corpora. Recognize that many scientific terms have precise, standardized equivalents that differ from their everyday-language cognates. A term that looks like a straightforward translation may carry a different technical sense in the target discipline. Where a term has no established equivalent, use the source term with a gloss on first use, or follow the discipline's convention for borrowing. Never coin a term silently without flagging it for reviewer or client confirmation.

Imprecise or non-standard terminology makes the research read as amateurish and can obscure the scientific meaning.

### Handle Nomenclature And Naming Conventions Exactly

Scientific disciplines have strict naming conventions that must be preserved exactly across languages.

Chemical names follow IUPAC rules; organism names follow binomial nomenclature with genus capitalized and species lowercase, always in italics or underlined; genes and proteins follow organism-specific casing conventions that differ between species. Preserve these conventions exactly, because they carry scientific meaning. Gene names in human genetics use all-uppercase conventions while the same gene in mouse uses different casing, and confusing them changes the meaning. Preserve strain designations, accession numbers, and catalog identifiers exactly as written. Do not translate or transliterate proper names of cell lines, antibodies, reagents, or commercial products unless there is an official localized name, because researchers order them by the original designation.

Mishandled nomenclature makes the research unreproducible, because readers cannot identify the exact materials or organisms.

### Preserve Quantitative Data And Statistical Reporting

Scientific research reports data, statistics, and measurements that must be carried without alteration.

Preserve every value, unit, confidence interval, p-value, effect size, sample size, and standard deviation exactly as in the source. Watch for locale-specific decimal and thousands separators that can change a value's magnitude if mishandled. Preserve the form of statistical reporting, including the number of decimal places, because the precision implies the measurement's resolution. Where the target language uses different conventions for statistical notation, follow the target discipline's convention while preserving the value. Verify every numeric value against the source through a separate check, because transcription errors in data are among the most damaging defects in scientific translation. Never round, truncate, or "clean up" values.

A statistic shifted by a decimal place or a sample size transcribed wrong can invalidate the reported finding.

### Handle Methods And Protocols With Reproducibility In Mind

Methods and experimental protocols describe how research was performed, and they must be translated so that a reader could reproduce the work.

Translate methods with attention to sequence, conditions, reagents, equipment, and parameters. Preserve the order of steps, because procedural sequence matters. Preserve concentrations, temperatures, durations, and instrument settings exactly. Translate equipment and reagent names to their established target-language terms or retain source designations where researchers use them. Recognize that methods sections use a condensed, procedural register, often in passive voice or imperative, and follow the target language's convention for procedural writing. Where a protocol references a standard method or kit, preserve the reference rather than retranslating it, because researchers locate these by designation.

A methods section that loses a step, a condition, or a reagent makes the experiment unreproducible and undermines the paper's credibility.

### Handle Citations References And Academic Conventions

Scientific writing is dense with citations, references, and academic conventions that must be handled consistently.

Preserve in-text citations in their original form, because they refer to entries in the reference list that the translator typically does not alter. Do not translate author names, journal titles, or article titles in the reference list unless the target publication requires it, because researchers locate sources by their original bibliographic data. Where the target language or publication style requires translated titles, provide the translation alongside the original, not in place of it. Follow the target language's convention for academic hedging, attribution verbs, and citation phrasing, because these conventions differ and signal scholarly register. Handle DOIs, accession numbers, and persistent identifiers exactly.

Mishandled citations break the chain of scholarly reference and make the translated paper difficult to verify.

### Match Academic Register And Discipline Conventions

Scientific writing follows discipline-specific register and style that the translation must match.

Recognize that biomedical writing differs from physical sciences writing, which differs from social sciences writing, in terminology, hedging, voice, and structure. Match the target language's established conventions for abstract structure, section headings, figure and table captions, and acknowledgment phrasing. Preserve the impersonal, evidence-oriented register typical of scientific writing, and avoid injecting the more rhetorical or persuasive register of other text types. Follow the target language's convention for units, abbreviations, and symbols, which may differ from the source. Where the source uses a structure mandated by a journal, preserve that structure unless the target publication specifies otherwise.

Register that drifts toward journalistic or promotional tone undermines the research's credibility with expert readers.

### Handle Figures Tables And Captions Coherently

Scientific papers convey much of their meaning through figures, tables, and their captions, which must be translated and aligned coherently.

Translate figure and table captions fully, preserving the relationship between caption text and the labeled elements in the figure or table. Translate axis labels, legends, and annotations, ensuring they match the caption and the body text terminology. Preserve table structures and ensure values, units, and headings align correctly. Where figures contain embedded text, coordinate localization so the text matches the caption and body. Ensure that statistical notation, abbreviations, and symbols in figures and tables are consistent with the rest of the paper.

Misaligned or untranslated figure elements make the data unreadable and the paper unusable.

### Coordinate Domain Expert Review

Scientific translation should be reviewed by a target-language domain expert, especially for high-stakes or specialized content. Coordinate this review.

Provide the reviewer with the source, translation, termbase, and context such as referenced methods and related literature. Frame the review as verification of scientific accuracy, terminology, and reproducibility, not stylistic preference. Domain expert review catches errors in terminology, methodology, data, and meaning that linguistic review alone misses. Incorporate review feedback carefully, because a researcher's correction may be scientifically necessary even if it differs from the translator's rendering.

The translator renders meaning and convention; the domain expert confirms scientific validity. Both are needed for credible research translation.

## Common Traps

### Using Everyday Terms For Scientific Concepts

Dictionary or everyday meanings where precise scientific senses are required mislead expert readers and obscure the research meaning.

### Mishandling Nomenclature And Naming Conventions

Translating or altering chemical names, organism names, gene symbols, or reagent designations makes the research unreproducible and can change the scientific meaning.

### Altering Data Statistics Or Units

Changing a value, unit, p-value, confidence interval, or sample size produces research that is numerically wrong and potentially misleading.

### Losing Steps Or Conditions In Methods

A methods or protocol section that drops a step, condition, reagent, or parameter makes the experiment unreproducible.

### Translating Citations Or Reference Entries

Translating author names, journal titles, or bibliographic data breaks the chain of scholarly reference and makes sources impossible to locate.

### Drifting Toward Promotional Or Journalistic Register

Scientific writing that reads as promotional or journalistic undermines credibility with expert readers who expect evidence-oriented register.

### Misaligning Figures Tables And Captions

Untranslated or misaligned figure labels, axis labels, legends, and captions make the data unreadable and the paper unusable.

### Skipping Domain Expert Review

Linguistic review alone cannot confirm scientific accuracy or reproducibility; domain expert review is needed for credible research translation.

## Self-Check

Before approving a scientific or research translation, verify:

- Scientific terminology uses authoritative, established target-language equivalents drawn from a domain termbase, with non-standard terms flagged rather than coined silently.
- Nomenclature, including chemical names, organism names, gene and protein symbols, strain designations, and reagent identifiers, is preserved exactly according to discipline convention.
- Every value, unit, confidence interval, p-value, effect size, sample size, and statistical notation is preserved exactly, verified against the source through a separate check.
- Methods and protocols preserve every step, condition, reagent, equipment reference, and parameter needed for reproducibility.
- Citations and reference entries preserve original bibliographic data, with translated titles provided alongside originals only where publication style requires.
- Academic register matches the discipline and target-language scientific writing conventions, without drift toward promotional or journalistic tone.
- Figures, tables, captions, axis labels, legends, and annotations are translated and aligned coherently with the body text.
- Domain expert review by a target-language specialist is coordinated for accuracy, terminology, and reproducibility.
- No term, value, nomenclature element, citation, or method detail has been altered in a way that changes the scientific meaning.
- The translated research, read by a target-language domain expert, would support accurate understanding, citation, and reproduction of the work.
