# Engineering Specifications

Engineering specifications are documents that define what a product, system, or component must be: its dimensions, materials, performance, tolerances, test methods, and compliance with standards. They are written for engineers and technicians who use them to design, build, verify, and maintain. The translation must be technically precise, because the readers act on exact values and conditions. A mistranslated tolerance, a converted unit done wrong, a misread standard reference, or a garbled formula can cause a part to be manufactured out of spec, a test to be performed incorrectly, or a system to fail compliance. Engineering translation is unforgiving: the readers are experts who expect precision, and they detect imprecision as unreliability. The translator must preserve every value, unit, reference, and formula exactly, understand the engineering meaning well enough to render it correctly, and know when a specification refers to a standard or system that requires specialized handling.

Use this skill when translating engineering specifications, technical data sheets, standards excerpts, test procedures, or design documentation, or when ensuring translated engineering content is precise and usable. The goal is to produce target specifications that engineers can use with the same confidence as the source, with every technical element preserved exactly.

## Core Rules

### Preserve Exact Values Tolerances And Ranges

Engineering specifications live or die on exact values. Preserve every number, unit, tolerance, and range exactly as in the source.

Dimensions, pressures, temperatures, voltages, frequencies, forces, speeds, and tolerances must be carried without alteration. Preserve the form of expression, including decimal places, because the precision implied by the number of decimals carries meaning. Preserve ranges and limits, such as minimum, maximum, nominal, and typical values. Watch for locale-specific decimal and thousands separators that can change a value's magnitude if mishandled. Verify every numeric value against the source through a separate check, not just while reading.

A tolerance shifted by a decimal place can make a part unusable or unsafe. Numeric precision is the core of engineering translation.

### Handle Units And Conversion Deliberately

Units of measurement are central to engineering specifications and vary across systems and regions. Handle them deliberately.

Determine whether the target requires unit conversion or whether the source units must be preserved, because engineering often uses standardized systems regardless of locale. Where conversion is required, convert accurately using authoritative conversion factors, and document the conversion. Watch for units that look similar but differ, such as short tons versus metric tons, or US gallons versus imperial gallons. Preserve compound units such as newton-meters or kilometers per hour in their correct target-language form. Never convert silently where the specification is tied to a standard that mandates specific units.

Unit errors are among the most damaging engineering translation defects, because they produce values that are numerically plausible but wrong.

### Preserve Formulas Equations And Symbols

Engineering specifications contain formulas, equations, and mathematical symbols that must be preserved exactly.

Reproduce formulas with the same variables, operators, and structure. Variable names that follow a convention, such as single-letter symbols, should usually be preserved, not translated, because engineers recognize them by convention. Ensure mathematical notation renders correctly in the target, including subscripts, superscripts, Greek letters, and special operators. Where a formula references defined terms, preserve the definitions and their relationship to the formula. Do not translate or alter symbols that carry mathematical meaning.

A formula with a translated variable or altered operator is wrong, even if the surrounding text is correct.

### Handle Standards References Correctly

Engineering specifications reference standards such as ISO, IEC, ASTM, DIN, and national standards. Handle these references correctly.

Standards have official designations and often official titles in multiple languages. Preserve the standard's designation exactly, such as ISO 9001, because the designation is how engineers find it. Where the standard has an official target-language title, use it. Where it does not, preserve the original title and designation, possibly with a gloss. Do not retranslate a standard's title into a non-official rendering, because that creates a reference engineers cannot locate. Where a source standard has a target-market equivalent, note the equivalence rather than silently substituting, because the standards may differ in detail.

Standards references are how specifications establish compliance; mishandling them undermines the specification's authority.

### Preserve Technical Terminology Consistently

Engineering terminology must be precise and consistent. Use established target-language engineering terms, not everyday approximations.

Build a termbase from authoritative engineering sources before translating. Component names, process terms, material designations, and test method names must match target-language engineering usage. Maintain consistency across the specification and with related documents such as drawings and manuals. Where the source uses an abbreviation or acronym standard in the field, use the target-language equivalent or retain the source abbreviation if that is field convention.

Imprecise or inconsistent terminology makes engineers distrust the translation and can cause misunderstanding of the specification.

### Understand The Engineering Meaning

Engineering translation requires understanding what the specification means, not just rendering the words. Translate from comprehension.

If you do not understand a specification's technical meaning, research it using parallel texts, standards, and domain references, or consult a subject-matter expert. Do not translate terms by their everyday dictionary meaning where the engineering meaning differs. Recognize that some ordinary words have precise engineering senses, and use the engineering sense. Where the specification's meaning is genuinely unclear, flag it rather than producing a plausible-sounding but possibly wrong rendering.

Translating words without understanding the engineering produces text that looks correct but misleads the expert reader.

### Handle Drawings Diagrams And Tables

Engineering specifications include drawings, diagrams, and tables that carry technical meaning. Translate and align them carefully.

Translate labels, callouts, and annotations on drawings, ensuring they match the text. Preserve table structures and ensure values, units, and headings align correctly. Handle drawing revisions and title block information consistently. Where drawings contain embedded text, coordinate localization so text and drawing match. Ensure that symbols on drawings, such as welding symbols or surface finish symbols, are preserved according to standard, not translated.

Misaligned or mistranslated drawing labels make the specification unusable, because engineers work from the drawings.

### Coordinate Engineering Review

Engineering specifications should be reviewed by a target-language engineer or technical specialist. Coordinate this review.

Provide the reviewer with the source, translation, termbase, and context such as drawings and referenced standards. Frame the review as verification of technical accuracy and usability, not stylistic preference. Engineering review catches errors in values, units, terminology, and meaning that linguistic review misses. Incorporate review feedback carefully, because an engineer's correction may be technically necessary even if stylistically different.

The translator renders meaning; the engineer confirms technical validity. Both are needed for usable specifications.

## Common Traps

### Altering Values Tolerances Or Precision

Changing a number, tolerance, or decimal precision produces a specification that is technically wrong.

### Silent Or Wrong Unit Conversion

Converting units incorrectly, or converting where the standard mandates source units, produces plausible but wrong values.

### Translating Formulas Or Variables

Altering variables, operators, or symbols in formulas changes the engineering meaning.

### Retranslating Standards Titles

Non-official renderings of standards titles create references engineers cannot locate.

### Using Everyday Terms For Engineering Concepts

Dictionary meanings where engineering senses are required mislead expert readers.

### Translating Without Engineering Comprehension

Word-level translation without understanding produces text that looks correct but is technically wrong.

### Misaligning Drawing And Table Labels

Labels that do not match the text or the drawing make the specification unusable.

### Skipping Engineering Review

Linguistic review alone cannot confirm technical accuracy; engineering review is needed for usable specifications.

## Self-Check

Before approving an engineering specification translation, verify:

- Every value, tolerance, range, and decimal precision is preserved exactly, verified against the source through a separate check.
- Units are handled deliberately, with conversion accurate and documented, and source units preserved where standards mandate.
- Formulas, equations, variables, operators, and mathematical symbols are reproduced exactly without translation of meaningful symbols.
- Standards references preserve official designations and use official target-language titles where they exist, noting equivalences rather than substituting silently.
- Technical terminology is precise, consistent, and drawn from authoritative engineering sources in a project termbase.
- The engineering meaning is understood before rendering, with research or expert consultation where needed, and unclear meaning flagged.
- Drawings, diagrams, tables, callouts, and symbols are translated and aligned correctly according to standard.
- Engineering review by a target-language technical specialist is coordinated for accuracy and usability.
- No value, unit, formula, standard reference, or term has been altered in a way that changes the engineering meaning.
- The translated specification, used by a target-language engineer, would support correct design, manufacture, testing, and compliance.
