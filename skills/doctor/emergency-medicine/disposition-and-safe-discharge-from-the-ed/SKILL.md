# Disposition and Safe Discharge from the ED

The disposition decision is where the emergency encounter is won or lost. The right diagnosis and the right treatment still fail the patient if they are sent somewhere they cannot be monitored, sent home before they are stable, or admitted when observation would have sufficed. Disposition is a judgment under uncertainty that integrates clinical trajectory, physiologic stability, social and functional context, access to follow-up, and the reliability of return precautions. The recurring failure modes are discharging patients who are still unstable or whose social situation cannot support safe recovery, admitting patients out of defensive habit rather than need, writing vague discharge instructions that the patient cannot act on, and failing to arrange follow-up so that deterioration after discharge has nowhere to go. This skill covers disposition decision factors, safe discharge criteria, the role of observation, social and functional assessment, return precautions in plain language, follow-up arrangement, and escalation when disposition is uncertain.

## Core Rules

### Base Disposition on Trajectory, Not Just the Snapshot

The single most important disposition variable is the patient's trajectory over the emergency stay, not their state at one moment. A patient who arrived hypotensive and is now normotensive after fluids but still tachycardic, with rising lactate, is not stable; they are improving but unproven. A patient who arrived septic and has normalized vital signs, mental status, and lactate over several hours of observation is a safer discharge candidate. Explicitly compare the arrival state, the current state, and the trend before deciding. When the trajectory is flat or worsening, discharge is unsafe regardless of how the patient looks at the moment of decision. Build observation time into the disposition logic for borderline patients rather than forcing a binary admit-or-discharge call at the first reassessment.

### Apply Explicit Stability Criteria Before Discharge

Discharge should meet concrete physiologic and functional criteria: stable and acceptable vital signs sustained over a meaningful observation period, no ongoing or anticipated airway or breathing threat, controlled pain that can be managed with oral analgesia, ability to tolerate oral intake where relevant, preserved or baseline mental status, and hemoglobin, electrolytes, and other labs that are safe for outpatient monitoring. For specific conditions, add syndrome-specific criteria: normal or near-normal perfusion after sepsis resuscitation, no ongoing chest pain and a non-high-risk electrocardiogram and troponin for chest pain, and demonstrated ability to ambulate and void for selected presentations. If a criterion is not met, either continue observation, admit, or arrange a higher level of monitoring; do not rationalize the gap.

### Use Observation for Patients Who Need Time, Not Admission

Emergency department observation or a clinical decision unit is the right disposition for a defined subset of patients: those who need short-term monitoring to prove stability, who require a time-limited intervention such as a repeat troponin or a diuretic infusion, or who are awaiting test results that will determine safety. Observation reduces unnecessary admissions while providing a safety net that discharge cannot. Use it deliberately with a clear expected duration and explicit discharge criteria, not as a holding pen for indecision. When observation criteria are not met or the patient fails to improve within the expected window, convert to admission rather than extending observation indefinitely.

### Weigh Social and Functional Factors as Heavily as Physiologic Ones

A clinically stable patient may be unsafe to discharge because of their context. Assess the home environment: who is present, whether the patient can perform activities of daily living, whether they can obtain and administer medications, whether they have reliable transport and a phone, whether they have substance use or mental health factors that affect adherence, and whether there is a caregiver who understands the plan. Elderly patients, those with new disability, those living alone, and those with limited health literacy or language barriers need explicit accommodation: home health, caregiver involvement, social work, or short-term rehabilitation. Discharging a medically stable patient into a socially unstable situation is an unsafe discharge. When social factors are complex, involve social work and case management before the disposition decision, not after.

### Write Return Precautions in Plain, Actionable Language

Discharge instructions are a safety device, not a formality. Return precautions must be specific, concrete, and written at a literacy level the patient can use, ideally in their preferred language. Instead of "return if symptoms worsen," specify the signs that should trigger return: new or worsening chest pain, shortness of breath at rest, fainting, fever above a stated threshold, inability to keep fluids down, increased swelling or redness spreading from a wound, or weakness that prevents walking. Tell the patient what to do and where to go if these occur. Confirm comprehension by teach-back, asking the patient to restate the warnings in their own words. Vague or unread instructions fail the central purpose of the discharge document.

### Arrange Concrete Follow-Up Before the Patient Leaves

Safe discharge depends on a defined follow-up pathway. Specify who the patient should see, within what timeframe, and how they will get the appointment. For high-risk discharges such as new diagnoses of atrial fibrillation, heart failure, deep vein thrombosis, or asthma exacerbation, arrange the appointment before discharge and confirm it with the patient. Ensure that discharge summaries, test results pending at discharge, and medication lists reach the follow-up provider, because breakdowns in information transfer are a leading cause of post-discharge adverse events. When timely follow-up cannot be arranged, reconsider the disposition; a patient who cannot be seen promptly may need observation or admission.

### Reconcile Medications and Communicate Clearly at Transitions

At every disposition, reconcile medications explicitly: what to continue, what to stop, what to start, and what has changed, and communicate this to the patient, the caregiver, and the receiving team. For admissions and transfers, give a structured handoff with the working diagnosis, interventions done, ongoing concerns, and pending results. For discharges, provide a written medication list and confirm the patient understands each change. Medication errors at transitions are common and harmful, and they are preventable with disciplined reconciliation.

### Escalate When Disposition Is Uncertain

When the disposition is genuinely unclear, escalate to a senior emergency physician, the admitting service, or the relevant specialist rather than defaulting to discharge. Ambivalence about safety is itself a signal; patients about whom the team is uncomfortable are higher-risk, and that discomfort should drive observation, admission, or consultation. Involve the patient and family in the decision where possible, and document the reasoning so the next clinician understands the plan and its rationale.

## Common Traps

### Discharging on a Snapshot Rather Than a Trajectory

Sending a patient home who looks acceptable at the moment but whose vital signs or labs were abnormal an hour earlier and have not been rechecked. Counter by comparing arrival, current, and trend, and observing borderline patients longer.

### Rationalizing an Unmet Stability Criterion

Talking oneself past a persistent tachycardia, elevated lactate, or uncontrolled pain to justify discharge. Counter by applying explicit stability criteria and choosing observation or admission when a criterion is not met.

### Defaulting to Admission Out of Defensive Habit

Admitting every uncertain case regardless of need, which strains resources and exposes patients to hospital-acquired harm. Counter by using observation pathways with clear criteria and duration.

### Ignoring Social and Functional Barriers

Discharging a medically stable patient who cannot manage at home, leading to rapid representation. Counter by assessing the home context and involving social work and case management before disposition.

### Vague or Unreadable Return Precautions

Instructions that say "return if worse" without specifics, or that exceed the patient's literacy or language. Counter by writing concrete warnings in plain language and confirming comprehension by teach-back.

### Discharging Without Arranged Follow-Up

Sending a high-risk patient home with a vague instruction to "see your doctor" and no appointment. Counter by arranging and confirming follow-up before discharge and reconsidering disposition when timely follow-up is unavailable.

### Inadequate Medication Reconciliation at Transition

Failing to reconcile and communicate medication changes, leading to duplication, omission, or adverse interactions. Counter by performing structured reconciliation at every disposition and communicating changes to patient, caregiver, and receiving team.

### Discharging Despite Team Discomfort

Overriding a sense that the patient is unsafe because no single objective finding is abnormal. Counter by treating ambivalence as a signal and escalating to observation, admission, or senior input.

## Self-Check

- Have I based the disposition on the patient's trajectory over the stay, comparing arrival, current, and trend, rather than on a single snapshot?
- Does the patient meet explicit physiologic and functional stability criteria, including syndrome-specific criteria for this presentation?
- Have I used observation for patients who need short-term monitoring or time-limited intervention, with clear criteria and expected duration?
- Have I assessed social and functional factors, including home support, activities of daily living, medication access, and health literacy, and involved social work or case management when complex?
- Are return precautions specific, concrete, written in plain language at the patient's literacy level, and confirmed by teach-back?
- Have I arranged and confirmed concrete follow-up before discharge, with information transferred to the follow-up provider?
- Have I reconciled medications and communicated changes clearly to the patient, caregiver, and receiving team?
- Have I escalated to senior or specialist input when the disposition is uncertain or the team is uncomfortable about safety?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
