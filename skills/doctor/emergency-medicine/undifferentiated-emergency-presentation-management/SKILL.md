# Undifferentiated Emergency Presentation Management

The defining feature of emergency medicine is that the patient arrives before the diagnosis is known, and often before it is even safe to pursue one. Chest pain, dyspnea, abdominal pain, altered mental status, and syncope are not diagnoses; they are entry points into large differential sets that contain both trivial and immediately lethal conditions. The discipline is to resuscitate first, diagnose in parallel, and never let the search for a precise diagnosis delay the interventions that keep the patient alive. The recurring failure mode is sequential thinking: waiting to identify the disease before treating, or becoming absorbed in a single diagnostic track while the airway, breathing, or circulation quietly deteriorates. This skill covers resuscitation-first management, parallel processing of resuscitation and diagnosis, building a broad initial differential, time-critical interventions, and knowing when to escalate to senior support or critical care.

## Core Rules

### Resuscitate First: The ABCDE Sequence Is Non-Negotiable

Before any diagnostic reasoning, assess and stabilize the threats to life in order: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure. A patient who appears to be describing chest pain but has a threatened airway, a respiratory rate of 35, or a systolic pressure of 70 is not primarily a diagnostic problem; they are a resuscitation problem. Intervene on the ABCDE threats as you find them, in parallel with history and examination, and re-evaluate continuously. The trap is to be drawn into the chief complaint, order an electrocardiogram and troponin, and miss that the patient is about to lose their airway. Resuscitation is not a phase that finishes before diagnosis begins; it is a continuous obligation that takes priority whenever a new threat appears.

### Run Resuscitation and Diagnosis in Parallel, Not in Sequence

Emergency care succeeds by concurrent processing. While intravenous access is placed and oxygen given, the clinician is taking a focused history, examining the patient, ordering the first diagnostic studies, and activating time-critical pathways. Assign tasks to the team explicitly: one clinician leads and thinks, others perform interventions, and a nurse documents times and interventions. Use the first minutes to send the studies that will most rapidly separate life threats: electrocardiogram for chest pain and dyspnea, glucose and point-of-care testing for altered mental status, hemoglobin and lactate for shock, and a focused bedside ultrasound where available. Sequential thinking, where each step waits for the previous to finish, is the most common cause of delayed intervention in time-critical presentations.

### Build a Broad Initial Differential Organized by Danger

For every undifferentiated presentation, generate a differential that explicitly separates the lethal must-not-miss diagnoses from the common and the less likely. For chest pain, the must-not-miss set includes acute coronary syndrome, pulmonary embolism, aortic dissection, tension pneumothorax, and esophageal rupture. For dyspnea, it includes pulmonary embolism, heart failure, pneumonia with sepsis, tension pneumothorax, and metabolic acidosis. For abdominal pain, it includes ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm, mesenteric ischemia, perforated viscus, ectopic pregnancy, and sepsis from any source. For altered mental status, it includes hypoglycemia and other metabolic causes, intracranial hemorrhage, meningitis or encephalitis, toxidromes, and postictal states. For syncope, it includes arrhythmia, pulmonary embolism, aortic stenosis, subarachnoid hemorrhage, and gastrointestinal hemorrhage. State the must-not-miss list out loud or in the record and decide how each will be ruled in or out.

### Apply Validated Risk Tools to Avoid Under- and Over-Triage

Use structured risk scores to calibrate the probability of dangerous diagnoses and to guide imaging and admission. The HEART score stratifies chest pain for major adverse cardiac events. The Wells score and revised Geneva score support pulmonary embolism probability alongside PERC and age-adjusted D-dimer. CURB-65 and pneumonia severity index grade pneumonia severity. The National Early Warning Score tracks physiologic deterioration. These tools do not replace judgment, but they discipline it: they reduce the variability that leads to both missed lethal diagnoses and unnecessary admissions. Document the score and use it to justify the disposition and imaging plan.

### Deliver Time-Critical Interventions Within Their Windows

Many lethal conditions have therapies whose benefit decays sharply with time, and the window is measured in minutes. Acute ischemic stroke requires thrombolysis or thrombectomy within defined door-to-needle and door-to-puncture targets. ST-elevation myocardial infarction requires reperfusion through primary percutaneous coronary intervention or fibrinolysis within door-to-balloon and door-to-needle targets. Sepsis and septic shock require the hour-one bundle of lactate, cultures, broad antibiotics, and fluids and vasopressors. Cardiac arrest requires immediate high-quality cardiopulmonary resuscitation and defibrillation. Tension pneumothorax requires immediate needle decompression. Recognize the time-critical pattern early, activate the appropriate pathway, and document the time zero precisely, because every downstream metric depends on it.

### Reassess Continuously and Re-formulate When the Course Deviates

Emergency patients change. The chest pain patient who develops ventricular fibrillation, the dyspnea patient whose saturation drops after positioning, the abdominal pain patient whose blood pressure falls: each demands immediate reassessment and re-formulation of the differential. Build forced re-evaluation into the workflow at defined intervals for undifferentiated or unstable patients, and never accept a static plan in a dynamic patient. If the working diagnosis does not explain the trajectory, return to the must-not-miss list and consider what has been missed.

### Escalate to Senior Support and Critical Care Early

Know the thresholds for calling for help. Senior emergency physician involvement is indicated for any unstable patient, any case requiring time-critical intervention, any diagnostic uncertainty in a high-risk presentation, and any procedure beyond the current clinician's scope. Critical care or ICU consultation is indicated for persistent or worsening hemodynamic or respiratory instability, for patients likely to need airway intervention or vasopressor support, and for those requiring post-resuscitation monitoring. Early escalation is a marker of good judgment, not failure; delayed escalation is the failure. State explicitly what you are worried about and what help you need.

## Common Traps

### Sequential Diagnostic-Then-Treat Thinking

Pursuing a diagnosis before stabilizing the patient, allowing reversible threats to progress. Counter by applying ABCDE first and running resuscitation and diagnosis in parallel.

### Anchoring on the Chief Complaint or Referral Impression

Accepting the patient's framing, the paramedic's report, or the triage label as the diagnosis and stopping the differential. Counter by independently forming a broad differential with an explicit must-not-miss list.

### Missing the Must-Not-Miss Diagnosis

Focusing on the most likely explanation and failing to rule out the lethal one. Counter by naming the must-not-miss diagnoses for each presentation and stating how each is excluded.

### Cognitive Tunneling on One Diagnostic Track

Becoming absorbed in a single workup, such as ruling out myocardial infarction, while another lethal process such as dissection or embolism progresses. Counter by maintaining parallel consideration and reassessing when data conflict with the leading diagnosis.

### Delaying Time-Critical Interventions

Waiting for complete data before activating stroke, STEMI, or sepsis pathways. Counter by recognizing the time-critical pattern early, activating the pathway on reasonable suspicion, and documenting time zero precisely.

### Failing to Reassess an Unstable Patient

Setting a plan and not returning to the bedside as the patient changes. Counter by building forced re-evaluation at defined intervals for any unstable or undifferentiated patient.

### Late Escalation to Senior or Critical Care

Trying to manage an unstable patient alone past the point where help would have changed the outcome. Counter by escalating early for instability, time-critical intervention, uncertainty, or scope limits.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed and stabilized ABCDE threats before, and continuously alongside, diagnostic reasoning?
- Am I running resuscitation and diagnosis in parallel with explicit team roles, rather than sequentially?
- Have I generated a broad initial differential with an explicit must-not-miss list for this presentation, and stated how each lethal diagnosis is ruled in or out?
- Have I applied validated risk tools to calibrate probability and guide imaging and disposition?
- Have I recognized time-critical presentations and delivered or activated interventions within their windows, with time zero documented?
- Have I reassessed the unstable or undifferentiated patient at defined intervals and re-formulated when the course deviated?
- Have I escalated to senior emergency physician or critical care support at the right thresholds for instability, time-critical need, uncertainty, or scope?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
