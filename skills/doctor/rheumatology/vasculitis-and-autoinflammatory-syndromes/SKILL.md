# Vasculitis and Autoinflammatory Syndromes

Vasculitis is the diagnosis where hesitation costs organs and lives. The blood vessel wall, once inflamed, narrows, ruptures, or thromboses, and the territory it supplies infarcts — a temporal artery left untreated causes irreversible blindness, an ANCA-associated vasculitis left untreated destroys kidneys and lungs within days. The judgment problem is that vasculitis mimics common disease (headache, sinusitis, fatigue, neuropathy, "atypical infection") and presents with non-specific inflammation, so it is repeatedly misattributed to infection, malignancy, or functional illness while the window for salvage closes. Agents tend to err by waiting for biopsy before treating, by accepting a single negative antibody as exclusion, and by under-recognizing the pulmonary-renal syndrome as an emergency. This skill covers the disciplined recognition of large, medium, and small-vessel vasculitis, the autoinflammatory periodic fever syndromes, the workup and biopsy logic, and the urgent escalation that must not be delayed.

## Core Rules

### Stratify by Vessel Size to Organize the Differential

Vessel size predicts the clinical syndrome and guides investigation. Large-vessel vasculitis (giant cell arteritis, Takayasu) presents with limb or jaw claudication, absent or asymmetric pulses, bruits, new headache, scalp tenderness, and visual symptoms; think cranial and aortic branch ischemia. Medium-vessel disease (polyarteritis nodosa) causes mesenteric ischemia, renal infarction, mononeuritis multiplex, and livedo; think organ and nerve infarcts. Small-vessel vasculitis divides into ANCA-associated (granulomatosis with polyangiitis, microscopic polyangiitis, eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis) and immune-complex types (IgA vasculitis, cryoglobulinemia, anti-GBM), presenting with palpable purpura, glomerulonephritis, alveolar hemorrhage, and upper-respiratory or sinus involvement. Name the likely vessel size first — it determines which antibodies, which imaging, and which biopsy site are appropriate, and it frames the urgency.

### Treat Giant Cell Arteritis as a Threat to Sight

New headache, scalp or jaw claudication, visual disturbance (amaurosis fugax, diplopia, unilateral vision loss), or polymyalgia rheumatica symptoms in a patient over fifty is giant cell arteritis until proven otherwise. Visual loss, once it occurs, is usually permanent, and the second eye can be lost within days. Do not wait for biopsy or specialist review to start high-dose glucocorticoids when the clinical suspicion is high — treatment should begin immediately, ideally the same day, because the biopsy remains useful for up to two weeks after steroids are started. Arrange temporal artery biopsy within that window, consider temporal artery ultrasound for the halo sign where available, and escalate urgently to rheumatology and ophthalmology. Document the pre-test probability reasoning that justified immediate steroids; the risk of a short course of high-dose steroids in an older adult is far lower than the risk of irreversible blindness.

### Recognize the Pulmonary-Renal Syndrome as an Emergency

The combination of alveolar hemorrhage (hemoptysis, falling hemoglobin, diffuse infiltrates, dyspnea) and rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (rising creatinine, active urinary sediment with red cell casts, oliguria) is a pulmonary-renal syndrome — most often ANCA-associated vasculitis or anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture). This is a same-day emergency requiring simultaneous resuscitation, urgent serology (ANCA, anti-GBM), and immediate rheumatology, nephrology, and often critical-care involvement. Do not delay high-dose steroids and immunosuppression while awaiting biopsy confirmation in a deteriorating patient; the kidney can be biopsied after stabilization, and treatment decisions are made on clinical and serologic grounds when the syndrome is florid. Plasma exchange is considered in anti-GBM disease and severe ANCA vasculitis — this decision belongs to the specialist team, but the agent's job is to recognize the syndrome and escalate immediately.

### Interpret the Vasculitis Workup in Context

ANCA testing (MPO-PR3 antibodies and immunofluorescence pattern) supports ANCA-associated vasculitis but is neither perfectly sensitive nor specific — a negative ANCA does not exclude small-vessel vasculitis, and a positive ANA or infection can cause atypical patterns. Complement levels (low in cryoglobulinemia, immune-complex disease), cryoglobulins (drawn and transported warm), hepatitis B and C serology (polyarteritis nodosa, cryoglobulinemia), immunoglobulins and IgA, and renal function with urinalysis form the targeted workup. ESR and CRP are non-specific but track inflammatory burden. Imaging — CT of sinuses, chest, and mesenteric vessels, MR or CT angiography for large-vessel wall thickening — supplements but does not replace biopsy where tissue is accessible. State the pre-test probability that drove each test, and never exclude vasculitis on the basis of a single negative result when the clinical picture is compelling.

### Biopsy the Accessible Involved Tissue When Possible

Tissue confirmation is valuable because vasculitis treatment is toxic and prolonged, and because mimics (infection, malignancy, cholesterol emboli, thrombotic microangiopathy) must be excluded. Biopsy the involved tissue: skin lesion in palpable purpura, sural or other nerve in mononeuritis multiplex (combined nerve-muscle biopsy increases yield), kidney in glomerulonephritis, temporal artery in suspected GCA, and lung nodule or sinus mass in granulomatosis with polyangiitis. The biopsy should ideally be of actively involved tissue, not end-stage scarred tissue. Do not delay treatment for biopsy in sight-threatening, renal-deteriorating, or pulmonary-hemorrhagic presentations — biopsy after stabilization, within the window where it remains informative. When biopsy is not feasible or is non-diagnostic, treat on clinical and serologic grounds with multidisciplinary input.

### Recognize Autoinflammatory and Periodic Fever Syndromes

Recurrent fevers with stereotyped episodes, a family history, childhood or young-adult onset, and inflammation between episodes raise autoinflammatory syndromes: familial Mediterranean fever (peritonitis, pleuritis, arthritis, erysipelas-like rash), TNF receptor-associated periodic syndrome (TRAPS), cryopyrin-associated periodic syndromes (CAPS), and mevalonate kinase deficiency. These are not infections and not classic autoimmune disease; they are disorders of innate immunity, and they require genetic testing and rheumatology or immunology subspecialty input. Untreated familial Mediterranean fever leads to amyloidosis and renal failure, so recognition matters. Ask about episode duration, triggers, family origin, and response to colchicine, and refer for confirmatory genetic testing rather than repeatedly treating episodes as infections.

### Escalate Before Organ Damage, Not After

The recurring failure in vasculitis is late escalation. Any suspected medium or small-vessel vasculitis with organ involvement (renal, pulmonary, neurologic, mesenteric) requires same-day rheumatology and relevant organ-specialist input; suspected giant cell arteritis with visual symptoms requires same-day ophthalmology and steroids; suspected pulmonary-renal syndrome requires immediate critical-care, nephrology, and rheumatology coordination. When in doubt, treat and refer — a brief course of high-dose steroids while awaiting definitive workup is safer than watching a kidney or an eye be lost. Document the red flags that drove escalation and follow institutional protocols for urgent vasculitis pathways.

## Common Traps

### Waiting for Biopsy Before Treating Sight- or Organ-Threatening Vasculitis

Delaying steroids for temporal artery biopsy in a patient with visual symptoms risks permanent blindness; biopsy remains useful for up to two weeks after steroids. Counter by starting high-dose steroids immediately on high clinical suspicion and arranging biopsy within the informative window.

### Accepting a Negative ANCA as Exclusion

ANCA sensitivity is imperfect; a negative result does not rule out small-vessel vasculitis in a compelling clinical picture. Counter by treating on clinical grounds with multidisciplinary input when the syndrome is convincing, and pursuing biopsy of involved tissue.

### Misattributing Pulmonary-Renal Presentation to Infection

Hemoptysis with renal failure is often labeled pneumonia or atypical infection while glomerulonephritis progresses. Counter by checking urinalysis and ANCA/anti-GBM early in any pulmonary-renal presentation and escalating as a same-day emergency.

### Treating Periodic Fevers as Recurrent Infection

Recurrent stereotyped fevers with a family history are more likely autoinflammatory than infectious; repeated antibiotics delay diagnosis and amyloidosis prevention. Counter by asking about episode pattern, triggers, and family origin, and referring for genetic and rheumatology evaluation.

### Under-Dosing or Prematurely Tapering Immunosuppression

Vasculitis relapse is common and damaging when steroids are tapered too quickly or induction is inadequate. Counter by following specialist-led tapering protocols, monitoring inflammatory markers and organ function, and escalating relapse features promptly.

### Forgetting Infection and Malignancy as Vasculitis Mimics

Disseminated infection, endocarditis, cholesterol emboli, and paraneoplastic states can mimic vasculitis and are worsened by immunosuppression. Counter by excluding infection (blood cultures, echocardiography, hepatitis serology) and considering malignancy before committing to intensive immunosuppression, in consultation with specialists.

## Self-Check

- Have I stratified the presentation by vessel size (large, medium, small-vessel ANCA or immune-complex) to guide investigation and urgency?
- For any patient over fifty with new headache, jaw claudication, or visual symptoms, have I treated giant cell arteritis as a sight-threatening emergency and started high-dose steroids without waiting for biopsy?
- Have I recognized pulmonary-renal syndromes (hemoptysis with rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis) as same-day emergencies requiring immediate rheumatology, nephrology, and critical-care escalation?
- Have I interpreted ANCA and other serology by pre-test probability, and avoided excluding vasculitis on a single negative result when the clinical picture is compelling?
- Have I arranged biopsy of accessible involved tissue where feasible, without delaying treatment for organ- or sight-threatening presentations?
- Have I considered autoinflammatory periodic fever syndromes in recurrent stereotyped fevers, and referred for genetic testing rather than repeated antibiotic courses?
- Have I excluded infection and malignancy as mimics before committing to intensive immunosuppression, in consultation with specialists?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
