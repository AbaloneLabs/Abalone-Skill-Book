# Musculoskeletal Pain and Soft Tissue Disorders

Musculoskeletal pain is the most common reason patients seek care, and it is where over-investigation and under-diagnosis coexist. The judgment problem is not recalling a list of named tendinopathies — it is triaging pain into mechanical, inflammatory, neurologic, referred, or systemic categories, finding the small fraction with serious underlying disease (infection, fracture, malignancy, neurologic compromise), and resisting the pull to image and inject every painful region. Agents tend to err in two directions: chasing every ache with MRI and generating incidental findings that cascade into unnecessary intervention, and dismissing chronic widespread pain or atypical presentations as non-organic while missing red-flag disease. This skill covers the disciplined assessment of musculoskeletal pain, the management of common soft-tissue disorders, the fibromyalgia approach, and the decision points for imaging, injection, and referral — with constant attention to the must-not-miss diagnoses that demand escalation.

## Core Rules

### Triage Pain by Pattern, Not by Anatomic Label

Before naming a structure, classify the pain. Mechanical pain is activity-related, relieved by rest, and follows predictable aggravating and easing factors; inflammatory pain has prolonged morning stiffness, nocturnal symptoms, and systemic features; neurologic pain is burning, electric, or lancinating with dermatomal or nerve-territory distribution; referred pain is felt distant from its source (shoulder from diaphragmatic irritation, knee from hip pathology, back from visceral organs) and lacks local tenderness or movement reproduction at the symptomatic site; fibromyalgia-type pain is widespread, persistent, accompanied by fatigue, sleep disturbance, and multiple tender regions without objective inflammation. The pattern reshapes the entire investigation strategy — a patient with night pain, weight loss, and systemic features is not a soft-tissue problem regardless of where it hurts. Document the pain pattern explicitly before reaching for imaging.

### Actively Screen for Red Flags on Every Assessment

Every musculoskeletal assessment must explicitly address red flags, because missing them causes irreversible harm. For back pain, ask about age over fifty, cancer history, unexplained weight loss, night pain, fever, immunosuppression, intravenous drug use, recent significant trauma, corticosteroid use, and neurologic symptoms (saddle anesthesia, bladder or bowel dysfunction, progressive leg weakness — the cauda equina syndrome). For limb pain, consider septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, deep vein thrombosis, acute limb ischemia, compartment syndrome, and malignancy (especially night pain, persistent non-mechanical pain, or a palpable mass). For any regional pain, consider referred cardiac, abdominal, or pelvic visceral disease. State the red flags considered and ruled out in the assessment; if any are present, escalate to imaging, bloods, or specialist evaluation immediately rather than treating as routine soft-tissue pain.

### Manage Tendinopathy as a Degenerative-Adaptive Process, Not Acute Inflammation

Most chronic tendinopathies (Achilles, patellar, lateral elbow, rotator cuff) are degenerative tendinosis with failed healing, not active inflammation, which is why repeated rest and anti-inflammatives alone fail and why corticosteroid injection gives short-term relief but worse medium-term outcomes. The evidence-based core is load management and progressive strengthening — heavy slow resistance or eccentric loading protocols over weeks to months, activity modification to remove aggravating load, and patient education that tendons take months to adapt. Reserve corticosteroid injection for clear co-existing bursitis or when pain prevents engagement with rehabilitation, and counsel that injection without rehab leads to recurrence. Image only when diagnosis is unclear, symptoms are atypical, or red flags exist; avoid imaging asymptomatic regions because incidental tendinopathy is common and misleading.

### Approach Fibromyalgia with Validation, Not Dismissal

Fibromyalgia is a central sensitization syndrome producing widespread pain, fatigue, unrefreshing sleep, cognitive symptoms, and often comorbid mood disorder, irritable bowel syndrome, and headaches. It is real and disabling, and patients are often harmed by being told nothing is wrong. The correct approach is to make the diagnosis positively (widespread pain index and symptom severity, exclusion of mimics), validate the experience, explain central sensitization without implying psychogenesis, and build a multimodal plan: graded aerobic exercise, sleep and mood management, cognitive behavioral therapy, and judicious pharmacotherapy (low-dose neuromodulators such as amitriptyline, duloxetine, or pregabalin). Avoid high-dose opioids, which worsen outcomes. Screen for and treat comorbid mood disorder, and recognize that fibromyalgia can coexist with — not exclude — genuine inflammatory or mechanical disease. Do not over-investigate, but do not miss concurrent pathology either.

### Decide on Imaging by What It Will Change

Imaging should be ordered when it will change management: to evaluate a red flag, to confirm a diagnosis before injection or procedural intervention, when the course deviates from expectation, or when conservative management fails over an appropriate trial period. Avoid imaging acute non-specific back pain in the first six weeks without red flags, because incidental disc degeneration and bulges are common in asymptomatic people and drive unnecessary intervention. Match the modality to the question: plain radiograph for fracture and gross structural change, ultrasound for dynamic soft-tissue and tendon assessment and injection guidance, MRI for occult fracture, spinal cord or nerve compression, infection, and malignancy, CT for complex fracture. State the specific question the imaging is intended to answer; if the answer would not change management, reconsider ordering it.

### Use Injection Selectively and with Clear Intent

Corticosteroid injection has a role but is overused. It is most effective for inflamed structures (bursitis, synovitis, intra-articular flare) and can break a pain cycle to enable rehabilitation, but repeated injections into the same site carry risk of tendon weakening, cartilage damage, skin atrophy, and systemic effects such as hyperglycemia. Limit injections per site per year, ensure accurate placement (ultrasound guidance where precision matters), counsel on transient flare and on the need to combine injection with rehabilitation, and never inject a possibly infected joint or bursa without first aspirating to exclude sepsis. For osteoarthritis, injection offers time-limited symptomatic benefit and should be framed as such, not as disease-modifying. Escalate to orthopedics or rheumatology when injection fails, when mechanical derangement dominates, or when red flags emerge.

### Know When to Refer and to Whom

Most soft-tissue and mechanical pain is managed in primary care with conservative measures, but specific triggers require referral. Suspected inflammatory arthritis or connective tissue disease goes to rheumatology early (see the inflammatory arthritis skill). Neurologic compromise, suspected fracture requiring intervention, mechanical joint instability, or failed conservative management over an adequate trial go to orthopedics or the relevant surgical specialty. Persistent diagnostic uncertainty, suspected malignancy, or atypical non-resolving pain requires targeted imaging and the appropriate specialist. Fibromyalgia with complex comorbidity may benefit from pain medicine or rheumatology input. Communicate the question clearly in referrals and document the red flags considered. When red flags appear, escalate immediately and follow institutional protocols.

## Common Traps

### Imaging Asymptomatic or Incidental Findings

Incidental disc bulges, meniscal tears, and tendinopathy are common in pain-free people and drive unnecessary intervention when imaged without a clear question. Counter by imaging only when the result will change management and by correlating findings strictly with symptoms.

### Treating Tendinopathy as Acute Inflammation

Repeated rest and anti-inflammatives, or repeated corticosteroid injection, fail chronic tendinopathy because the pathology is degenerative tendinosis. Counter by prioritizing progressive load-based rehabilitation over months and reserving injection for specific indications.

### Missing Cauda Equina and Other Neurologic Red Flags

Bilateral leg symptoms, saddle anesthesia, bladder or bowel dysfunction, or progressive weakness must not be attributed to routine back pain. Counter by explicitly screening red flags at every assessment and escalating suspected cord or cauda equina compression as an emergency.

### Dismissing Fibromyalgia as Non-Organic

Patients with fibromyalgia are often invalidated, leading to harm, over-investigation, and poor outcomes. Counter by making the diagnosis positively, validating the experience, and building a multimodal non-opioid plan while remaining vigilant for concurrent pathology.

### Over-Investigating Widespread Pain Without Red Flags

Extensive autoimmune and imaging panels in widespread pain with normal examination and no red flags generate false positives and anxiety. Counter by reserving serology for objective synovitis or systemic features and by managing typical fibromyalgia on clinical grounds.

### Injecting Without Excluding Infection

Injecting a septic joint or bursa with corticosteroid worsens infection and can be catastrophic. Counter by aspirating and excluding sepsis before any injection when there is any suspicion of infection.

## Self-Check

- Have I classified the pain as mechanical, inflammatory, neurologic, referred, or fibromyalgia-type using pattern features before naming a structure or ordering imaging?
- Have I explicitly screened for and documented red flags (infection, fracture, malignancy, neurologic compromise, visceral referred pain) on this assessment?
- For chronic tendinopathy, have I prioritized progressive load-based rehabilitation over repeated rest, anti-inflammatories, and injection?
- For suspected fibromyalgia, have I made the diagnosis positively, validated the patient, and built a multimodal non-opioid plan while remaining vigilant for concurrent disease?
- Have I ordered imaging only when the result will change management, with a specific clinical question stated?
- Have I used corticosteroid injection selectively, with limits on repeat injections, and excluded infection before injecting?
- Have I referred appropriately and urgently — rheumatology for inflammatory disease, orthopedics for neurologic compromise or mechanical derangement, and specialist input for diagnostic uncertainty or red flags?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
