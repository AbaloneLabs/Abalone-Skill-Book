# Fitness for Work and Rehabilitation

Fitness-for-work assessment is not a binary "fit or unfit" verdict; it is a structured judgment about the match between a person's functional capacity and the real demands of a specific job, in a specific environment, with specific hazards. The recurring failures are predictable: clinicians sign patients off work indefinitely without understanding the job or the rehabilitative value of work itself; they issue vague restrictions ("light duties") that the employer cannot implement; they fail to distinguish capacity from risk; or they allow the medico-legal or compensation frame to override the clinical and rehabilitative frame. Good work is itself a determinant of health, and prolonged absence from work is associated with worse physical, mental, social, and economic outcomes. This skill covers fitness-for-work assessment, restricted and modified duty, return-to-work planning, functional capacity evaluation, reasonable accommodation, and the recognition of when to escalate to occupational medicine, physiotherapy, or occupational therapy.

## Core Rules

### Frame Work as a Health Outcome, Not a Risk to Be Avoided

The strongest single predictor of return to work after illness or injury is early, structured, sustained contact with the workplace; the strongest predictor of long-term work disability is prolonged absence. Work is generally good for physical and mental health, and the default clinical posture should be to support a timely, safe return to appropriate work rather than to certify indefinite absence. Reframe "when can the patient return to work?" as "what can the patient do now, and what modifications enable safe work?" This is both clinically and ethically superior to reflex certification and aligns with modern occupational health evidence.

### Assess the Demands of the Specific Job, Not a Generic "Work"

Fitness-for-work assessment is meaningless without a job description. Obtain the actual physical, cognitive, sensory, and psychosocial demands of the role: lifting and carrying loads, postures, repetition, standing and walking, vision and hearing requirements, driving or safety-critical tasks, shift pattern, cognitive load, interpersonal demands, and exposure to hazards. Standardized job demand classifications (such as physical demand levels from sedentary to very heavy) help structure this. A patient may be unfit for one role and fully fit for another in the same workplace; the assessment is job-specific, not person-generic.

### Distinguish Capacity from Risk, and Both from Symptoms

Three distinct questions must be separated: does the person have the capacity to do the task (strength, range, cognition, stamina), is there a risk to the person or to others if they do it (e.g., seizures in a safety-critical role, infectivity, medication effects on operation of machinery), and are symptoms limiting function (pain, fatigue, anxiety). Capacity and risk are the determinants of fitness; symptoms inform the plan but do not by themselves define unfitness. A patient may have ongoing symptoms and still be fit for modified work, and conversely may be asymptomatic but unfit because of risk (e.g., a newly diagnosed seizure disorder in a commercial driver). Conflating these leads to both over- and under-certification.

### Define Restrictions Precisely and Implementably

Vague restrictions — "light duties," "avoid heavy lifting," "as tolerated" — are unimplementable and lead to conflict, prolonged absence, and re-injury. Specify restrictions numerically and operationally: maximum weight to be lifted, maximum time standing or sitting, permitted postures and movements, hours per day or shift, cognitive or sensory requirements, and the anticipated duration and review date. Frame the assessment in terms of capacity ("can lift up to 5 kg, can sit up to 30 minutes") rather than only prohibitions, because capacity guides the employer toward possible modified duties. Provide a clear review date so that the plan is time-limited and progressive, not open-ended.

### Plan Return to Work as a Graded, Time-Limited Process

Return to work after significant illness or injury is best planned as a graded process: modified hours, modified duties, or both, with a defined progression to full duties over days to weeks. Coordinate with the employer, the occupational health service, the insurer where relevant, and the patient to agree the plan, the milestones, and the review points. Early, structured return to modified work is associated with better outcomes than waiting for full recovery before any return. The plan should be written, shared (with consent), and reviewed at each milestone. Anticipate setbacks and build review into the plan rather than treating them as failures.

### Use Functional Capacity Evaluation When Self-Report Is Insufficient

When the gap between self-reported capacity and job demands is uncertain, or when objective assessment is needed for medico-legal or rehabilitation planning, refer for functional capacity evaluation (FCE) by a physiotherapist or occupational therapist. FCE provides structured, objective measurement of lifting, carrying, postural tolerance, mobility, and sometimes cognitive or hand function, calibrated to job demands. It is particularly valuable in complex or contested cases, in planning graded return, and in determining maximum medical improvement. Do not rely on self-report alone when the stakes are high or the trajectory is unclear.

### Apply Reasonable Accommodation and Know the Legal Frame

Many workers with illness, injury, or disability are entitled to reasonable accommodation under disability discrimination law: modification of tasks, hours, equipment, or environment that enables them to perform the inherent requirements of the job without imposing unjustifiable hardship on the employer. Know the local legal framework and the clinician's role in identifying accommodation needs and certifying them. Accommodation is often the difference between sustained work and job loss, and the clinician's input is frequently decisive. Involve occupational health and the employer early.

### Coordinate the Multidisciplinary Rehabilitation Team

Complex return-to-work — after major injury, mental illness, cancer, cardiac or neurological events — requires coordinated multidisciplinary input: occupational medicine physician, treating specialists, general practitioner, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, psychology, vocational rehabilitation, and the employer. The clinician often acts as the coordinator and the consistent point of contact. Define roles, share the plan (with consent), and review progress against milestones. Fragmented care is a major driver of failed return to work.

### Escalate to Occupational Medicine and Allied Health Appropriately

Refer to occupational medicine for complex or contested fitness assessments, safety-critical work, independent medico-legal opinions, and coordination of complex return-to-work. Refer to physiotherapy for musculoskeletal rehabilitation and FCE, to occupational therapy for functional and workplace assessment and accommodation, to psychology or psychiatry for work-related mental ill-health, and to vocational rehabilitation services for workers struggling to return to their previous role. Do not delay escalation; early multidisciplinary input is the strongest predictor of successful return.

## Common Traps

### Certifying Indefinite Absence Without Job Knowledge

Signing patients off work without understanding the job denies them the rehabilitative benefit of work and predicts long-term disability. Counter by obtaining the job demands and planning modified return.

### Issuing Vague Restrictions

"Light duties" and "as tolerated" are unimplementable and lead to conflict and re-injury. Counter by specifying restrictions numerically, operationally, and with a review date.

### Conflating Symptoms with Unfitness

Pain, fatigue, or anxiety do not by themselves define unfitness; capacity and risk do. Counter by separating capacity, risk, and symptom questions explicitly.

### Ignoring Safety-Critical Risk

Focusing on capacity and missing risk (seizures, infectivity, substance effects, cardiac events) in safety-critical roles endangers the worker and others. Counter by asking explicitly about safety-critical tasks and relevant risks.

### Waiting for Full Recovery Before Any Return

Prolonged absence awaiting complete recovery worsens outcomes. Counter by planning graded, modified, time-limited return to work early.

### Failing to Consider Reasonable Accommodation

Missing the opportunity for accommodation causes avoidable job loss. Counter by identifying accommodation needs, knowing the legal frame, and involving the employer and occupational health.

### Fragmented Care Without Coordination

Multiple uncoordinated clinicians and an absent return-to-work plan predict failure. Counter by designating a coordinator and sharing a written plan with consent.

## Self-Check

- Have I framed the assessment around a timely, safe return to appropriate work rather than reflex certification of absence?
- Have I obtained the specific physical, cognitive, sensory, and psychosocial demands of the actual job?
- Have I distinguished capacity from risk and from symptoms, and assessed all three?
- Have I specified restrictions numerically, operationally, and with a clear review date, framing capacity as well as prohibition?
- Have I planned a graded, time-limited return to work with the employer, occupational health, and patient, with milestones and review?
- Have I referred for functional capacity evaluation when self-report is insufficient or the case is complex or contested?
- Have I considered reasonable accommodation under the relevant disability discrimination framework and involved the employer?
- Have I coordinated the multidisciplinary team and escalated to occupational medicine or allied health for complex, contested, or safety-critical cases?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
