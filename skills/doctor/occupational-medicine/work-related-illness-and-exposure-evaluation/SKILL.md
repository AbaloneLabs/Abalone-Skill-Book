# Work-Related Illness and Exposure Evaluation

Occupational illness is systematically under-recognized. Clinicians attribute work-related disease to non-occupational causes because they do not ask about work, do not know the hazards of common jobs, or assume that someone else — the employer, the occupational health service, the insurer — is responsible for the assessment. The result is delayed or missed diagnosis, ongoing exposure, preventable progression, and lost opportunities for compensation and workplace hazard control. A second, equally important, function of recognizing work-relatedness is population health: a single case of occupational asthma or lead toxicity often signals a workplace hazard affecting many workers. This skill covers taking an occupational exposure history, assessing work-relatedness, recognizing common occupational diseases, coordinating workplace assessment and reporting, and knowing when to escalate to occupational medicine or a relevant specialist.

## Core Rules

### Take a Systematic Occupational Exposure History for Every Potentially Work-Related Presentation

The occupational history is the single most under-performed part of the clinical assessment and the most common reason work-related illness is missed. For any presentation that could be work-related — respiratory, dermatological, musculoskeletal, neurological, audiovestibular, hematological, psychiatric, or a new malignancy — take a structured history: current and past jobs (job title not job label, materials and processes used, ventilation and protective equipment), temporal relationship of symptoms to work (improvement on days off, on holidays, on change of job), specific exposures (chemicals, dusts, fumes, metals, biological agents, physical agents such as noise, vibration, radiation, and ergonomic stressors), and co-workers with similar symptoms. The pattern of symptom improvement away from exposure is one of the strongest clues to occupational causation and must be asked about explicitly.

### Apply a Structured Causation Assessment

Determining work-relatedness is a clinical judgment, not a bureaucratic label, and it should be reasoned explicitly. Consider temporality (does the symptom relate temporally to exposure?), dose-response (more exposure, more symptoms), biological plausibility (is this a known effect of this agent?), consistency with the literature, absence of a more likely non-occupational explanation, and supporting evidence such as co-worker illness, workplace monitoring, or biological monitoring. For chronic diseases with long latency (mesothelioma, bladder cancer, noise-induced hearing loss), the relevant exposure may be decades in the past, which makes the lifetime job history essential. Document the reasoning behind the causation assessment, including the level of certainty, because this drives reporting, compensation, and workplace intervention.

### Recognize the Common Occupational Diseases and Their Triggers

Know the high-yield associations. Occupational asthma and hypersensitivity pneumonitis (isocyanates, flour, animal proteins, metalworking fluids, wood dusts); contact and allergic dermatitis (wet work, rubber accelerators, chromates, nickel, epoxy resins); noise-induced hearing loss (chronic noise exposure, with the classic 4 kHz notch); hand-arm vibration syndrome and carpal tunnel (vibrating tools, repetitive motion); lead, mercury, manganese, and solvent neurotoxicity; pesticide illness (organophosphates, carbamates); asbestos-related disease (pleural plaques, asbestosis, mesothelioma); silica-related disease (silicosis, TB risk, autoimmune associations, lung cancer); work-related musculoskeletal disorders (back, neck, upper limb from repetitive or heavy work); and work-aggravated mental ill-health and cardiovascular effects of shift work. A new diagnosis in a working-age adult should always prompt the question "could this be work-related?"

### Identify Acute and Chronic Exposure Patterns Differently

Acute exposure (a chemical spill, a single noise trauma, a solvent gassing, an organophosphate exposure) presents with recognizable acute toxicity and requires immediate decontamination, removal from exposure, symptom-directed treatment, and reporting. Chronic low-dose exposure (slowly progressive dyspnea, gradual hearing loss, insidious neuropathy, slowly rising blood lead) presents insidiously and is far easier to miss; the diagnosis depends on a high index of suspicion, a careful exposure history, and sometimes biological monitoring (blood lead, urinary metals, cholinesterase activity). Do not wait for severe disease before considering chronic exposure — early removal from exposure prevents irreversible progression in most occupational conditions.

### Coordinate Workplace Assessment and Use Biological and Environmental Monitoring

Work-relatedness is rarely confirmed by the clinical encounter alone. Use workplace exposure data, industrial hygiene reports, safety data sheets, and biological monitoring (where validated) to support the assessment. With the worker's consent, communicate with the employer's occupational health service, the workplace safety officer, or the relevant regulator to initiate workplace assessment and hazard control. A clinician's report can be the trigger that converts a hidden workplace hazard into a controlled one. Be explicit about consent, confidentiality, and the distinction between a clinical report and a medico-legal report.

### Report and Document Work-Relatedness Appropriately

Many jurisdictions require reporting of occupational disease to a regulator, insurer, or compensation authority, and completion of compensation certificates. Understand the local statutory requirements and the forms used. Accurate, timely reporting is not only a legal duty but the mechanism by which workplace hazards are identified at population level and by which workers access compensation, treatment, and rehabilitation. Failure to report is a common and avoidable failure. Document the diagnosis, the causation reasoning, the exposure, the functional impact, and the reporting actions taken.

### Remove from Exposure and Prevent Recurrence

Definitive treatment for most occupational disease is removal from exposure. Continuing to work in the causative environment while receiving symptomatic treatment guarantees progression. Coordinate modified duties, relocation, or — where necessary — cessation of the causative work, and ensure the workplace hazard is controlled (substitution, enclosure, ventilation, personal protective equipment) so that other workers are protected. The clinician's role extends beyond the individual patient to the workforce.

### Escalate to Occupational Medicine and Relevant Specialists

Refer to occupational medicine for complex causation assessment, surveillance programs, compensation and medico-legal reports, and coordination with employers and regulators. Refer to relevant organ specialists (respiratory, dermatology, neurology, audiovestibular) for diagnostic confirmation and management. Refer to toxicology for unusual or severe poisoning. Do not delay escalation while awaiting certainty; early specialist input often determines whether the diagnosis is made at all.

## Common Traps

### Not Asking About Work

The single most common error is omitting the occupational history, which guarantees that work-related illness is missed. Counter by asking about job, tasks, exposures, and temporal patterns for any potentially work-related presentation.

### Attributing Symptoms to Non-Occupational Causes by Default

Occupational asthma labeled as intrinsic asthma, contact dermatitis as eczema, or hand-arm vibration syndrome as Raynaud's leads to ongoing exposure and progression. Counter by considering occupational causation explicitly in the differential.

### Missing Chronic Low-Dose Exposure

Insidious chronic disease (hearing loss, neuropathy, pneumoconiosis) is missed because it lacks an acute event. Counter by taking a lifetime job history and using biological monitoring where validated.

### Failing to Use Temporal Improvement Away from Work

Symptom improvement on days off or holidays is a powerful clue that is often not asked about. Counter by explicitly inquiring about symptom patterns relative to work and rest.

### Treating Without Removing from Exposure

Symptomatic treatment while the worker remains exposed guarantees progression. Counter by coordinating removal or modification of exposure as part of the treatment plan.

### Failing to Report and Document

Omitting statutory reporting and compensation certification denies the worker access to compensation and rehabilitation and prevents population-level hazard identification. Counter by knowing local requirements and completing documentation contemporaneously.

### Ignoring Co-Worker Illness

A single case often indicates a workplace-wide hazard; failing to act on co-worker illness leaves others exposed. Counter by asking about co-workers and communicating with the workplace or regulator.

## Self-Check

- Have I taken a structured occupational exposure history, including current and past jobs, tasks, materials, protective equipment, and temporal patterns relative to work and rest?
- Have I reasoned explicitly about causation using temporality, dose-response, plausibility, and supporting evidence, and documented the level of certainty?
- Have I considered the common occupational diseases for this presentation rather than defaulting to a non-occupational diagnosis?
- Have I distinguished acute from chronic exposure and used biological or environmental monitoring where validated?
- Have I coordinated workplace assessment with the employer, occupational health, or regulator, with appropriate consent and confidentiality?
- Have I reported and documented work-relatedness according to local statutory requirements and completed compensation documentation?
- Have I arranged removal from or modification of exposure as part of the treatment plan, and considered protection of co-workers?
- Have I escalated to occupational medicine or the relevant specialist for complex causation, surveillance, or medico-legal reports?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
