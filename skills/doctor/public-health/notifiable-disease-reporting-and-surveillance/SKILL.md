# Notifiable Disease Reporting and Surveillance

Disease reporting is a legal and ethical duty that sits uneasily alongside the clinician's instinct to focus on the individual patient, and it is one of the most commonly missed obligations in routine care. The central judgment problem is that the moment of diagnosis is also the moment a public health obligation begins, and the clinician must recognize, within the press of clinical work, that this case is not just a patient but a signal in a population. A single case of tuberculosis, meningococcal meningitis, salmonellosis, or measles triggers reporting, contact tracing, and sometimes prophylaxis of others — and a delay of hours to days can convert a containable case into a cluster. The second judgment problem is recognizing outbreaks from the clinical frontline: the clinician is often the first sensor in the surveillance system, and the pattern (two similar cases in a week, an unusual pathogen, a cluster in a school or workplace) may be invisible to public health until a clinician connects the dots and escalates. This skill covers the duty to report, what and when to report, outbreak recognition, and collaboration with public health authorities.

## Core Rules

### Treat Reporting as a Mandatory Concurrent Duty, Not a Deferred Task

Reporting a notifiable disease is a legal requirement in virtually every jurisdiction, and it is the responsibility of the diagnosing clinician (and often the laboratory) — it cannot be delegated upward to a consultant or deferred to a future visit. The obligation attaches the moment the diagnosis is suspected or confirmed, depending on the disease and local rules. Some diseases require immediate phone notification (for example, suspected meningococcal disease, measles, pulmonary TB, polio, viral hemorrhagic fevers, botulism, anthrax), because public health action — contact prophylaxis, isolation orders, source investigation — must begin within hours. Others require notification within days via electronic or paper systems. Know the reportable disease list and the timeline for your jurisdiction, and treat the report as part of the clinical plan, documented in the chart alongside the treatment plan. Do not assume "the lab will report it" — laboratory reporting and clinical reporting are often separate duties, and both may be required.

### Know the Reportable Diseases and the Local Reporting Pathway

Notifiable disease lists are jurisdiction-specific and are updated as new threats emerge. Maintain working knowledge of the diseases that are reportable in your setting and the mechanism (telephone hotline, electronic system, fax, form). The list typically includes vaccine-preventable diseases (measles, mumps, rubella, pertussis, polio, diphtheria, Haemophilus influenzae type b, invasive pneumococcal disease), serious bacterial infections (TB, meningococcal disease, invasive group A streptococcus, legionellosis, shigellosis, salmonellosis, E. coli O157 and Shiga-toxin producing E. coli, syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia, HIV), viral infections of public health concern (hepatitis A, B, C; influenza with novel strains; COVID-19 per local rules; arboviruses such as dengue, Zika, West Nile), zoonoses (rabies exposures, brucellosis, Q fever, Lyme and other tickborne diseases), and high-consequence or bioterrorism-associated agents (anthrax, botulism, plague, tularemia, smallpox, viral hemorrhagic fevers). When you diagnose or strongly suspect a reportable condition, verify the reporting pathway and timeline the same day; if you cannot reach the usual contact, use the after-hours public health emergency number.

### Report on Suspicion for Immediately Notifiable Diseases, Not Only on Confirmation

For immediately notifiable diseases, report on clinical suspicion, before laboratory confirmation, because public health action cannot wait. Suspected meningococcal disease, suspected measles, suspected pulmonary TB, suspected botulism, and suspected viral hemorrhagic fever are all reportable the moment they are clinically suspected. Provide the public health team with the information they need to act: the clinical picture, the specimen results available, the patient's demographics and contacts, travel and exposure history, and the infection control measures already in place. Reporting on suspicion is not "crying wolf" — public health teams expect and prefer early notification of possible cases, and a negative workup is far less costly than a delayed response to a true case. Update the report as results return.

### Recognize Outbreaks and Clusters From the Frontline

The clinician is often the first to detect an outbreak. Maintain situational awareness for patterns: two or more cases of an unusual illness in a short period; a cluster linked by location (school, dormitory, workplace, long-term care facility, neighborhood), by food source, or by social network; an unusual pathogen or unusual resistance pattern; a rise in a common syndrome (gastroenteritis, respiratory illness, rash illness) above the seasonal baseline; or a single case of a rare or eradicated disease (measles, polio, diphtheria). When you suspect a cluster, gather the minimum dataset (who, when, where, common exposures, severity, outcomes) and notify public health immediately. Do not attempt to run a full outbreak investigation alone — that is a public health function requiring epidemiologic expertise, but your early signal is what triggers it. Document the cluster suspicion in the chart and the report.

### Collaborate With Public Health on Contact Tracing and Prophylaxis

For many reportable diseases, public health action extends beyond the patient to contacts: identification, screening, prophylaxis, or exclusion. Meningococcal disease requires chemoprophylaxis of close contacts within a tight window (commonly within 24 hours of the index case's onset); measles requires identification and vaccination or immunoglobulin of susceptible contacts within days; TB requires contact investigation that can extend over weeks; hepatitis A requires post-exposure prophylaxis of contacts within two weeks; pertussis requires prophylaxis and exclusion guidance for household and close contacts. Provide public health with accurate contact information and exposure details, and follow institutional protocols for patient isolation and staff protection during the investigation. Recognize that contact tracing is resource-intensive and that public health teams must prioritize — your job is to provide complete, accurate information so they can prioritize effectively.

### Interpret Surveillance Data With an Understanding of Its Limits

When using surveillance data to assess population risk, understand the limitations. Surveillance is subject to underreporting (many reportable diseases are substantially under-ascertained), reporting delay (data lag behind real-time transmission), changes in case definitions and testing capacity (which can create artificial rises or falls), and surveillance bias (populations that test more are counted more). A rise in reported cases may reflect a true increase in incidence, increased testing, a change in case definition, an outbreak, or improved reporting — distinguish these before drawing conclusions. Compare rates, not raw counts, and stratify by age, geography, and time to detect true signals. Use surveillance data to inform clinical suspicion (e.g., when influenza or COVID-19 is circulating, adjust your differential for febrile respiratory illness), but do not treat it as a complete picture of transmission.

### Protect Confidentiality and Apply Public Health Ethics

Reporting and contact tracing involve sharing identifiable patient information with public health authorities, which is permitted — and required — under public health law, but it must be done with the minimum information necessary for the public health purpose. Share information through secure channels, share only what is needed for the investigation, and limit disclosure within your own team to those who need to know. For stigmatized conditions (HIV, sexually transmitted infections, tuberculosis), be especially attentive to confidentiality and to the risk that disclosure causes harm. Balance the duty to protect population health with respect for individual autonomy and privacy, and when these conflict, consult public health and ethics resources. Recognize that for some patients, the fear of reporting and contact tracing may deter them from seeking care; address this with clear communication about what will and will not be shared and why.

## Common Traps

### Assuming the Laboratory Will Handle the Report

Laboratory and clinical reporting are often separate duties, and both may be required. Counter by confirming that the clinical report is filed regardless of laboratory reporting.

### Deferring Reporting Until Confirmation

For immediately notifiable diseases, confirmation-based delay can cost lives. Counter by reporting on clinical suspicion and updating as results return.

### Not Knowing the Local Reporting Pathway or Timeline

Each jurisdiction has its own list and timeline; assuming a generic standard leads to missed or late reports. Counter by knowing your local reportable disease list and reporting mechanism, including the after-hours number.

### Missing an Outbreak Because Each Case Looks Isolated

A clinician seeing one case of an unusual illness may not recognize it as part of a cluster. Counter by maintaining situational awareness for patterns and reporting clusters on suspicion.

### Providing Incomplete Information to Public Health

Incomplete or inaccurate contact and exposure data cripples the investigation. Counter by gathering the minimum dataset (who, when, where, exposures, contacts, severity) before reporting.

### Over-Interpreting Surveillance Data Without Considering Bias

A rise in reported cases may reflect testing, definitions, or reporting rather than true incidence. Counter by examining rates, stratifying, and considering alternative explanations before concluding.

### Breaching Confidentiality Beyond the Public Health Need

Sharing identifiable information beyond what the investigation requires violates ethics and law. Counter by sharing the minimum necessary through secure channels and limiting internal disclosure.

### Failing to Report Because the Patient Is Embarrassed or Asks for Privacy

Patient preference does not override the legal duty to report. Counter by reporting as required while protecting confidentiality to the extent possible and supporting the patient through the process.

## Self-Check

- Have I treated reporting as a concurrent clinical duty, documented alongside the treatment plan, rather than deferring it?
- Do I know the reportable disease list and the reporting pathway and timeline for my jurisdiction, including the after-hours emergency number?
- For any immediately notifiable disease, did I report on clinical suspicion before confirmation, and update as results returned?
- Have I maintained situational awareness for clusters and reported any suspected outbreak with the minimum dataset (who, when, where, exposures, severity)?
- For diseases requiring contact action, did I provide public health with complete and accurate contact and exposure information to enable timely prophylaxis or exclusion?
- When interpreting surveillance data, did I account for underreporting, reporting delay, case-definition changes, and surveillance bias before drawing conclusions?
- Have I protected patient confidentiality by sharing only the minimum necessary information through secure channels and limiting internal disclosure?
- Have I escalated to public health authorities for any case of unusual severity, unusual pathogen, or suspected outbreak, and sought ethics or public health guidance when confidentiality and population health duties conflict?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
