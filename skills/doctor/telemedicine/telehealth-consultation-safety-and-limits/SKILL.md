# Telehealth Consultation Safety and Limits

Telehealth extends care beyond the clinic walls, but distance is also a constraint. A video visit is not simply an in-person visit delivered through a screen — the examination is partial, the patient may be in an uncontrolled environment, the technology can fail at the worst moment, and the clinician cannot lay hands, run point-of-care tests, or summon a crash team. The central judgment problem in telehealth is not "can I see this complaint remotely?" but "is remote care safe *enough* for this patient, this presentation, this moment, and this fallback plan?" Clinicians get into trouble when they treat telehealth as a faster, lighter version of a clinic visit rather than a different modality with a different risk envelope. This skill covers how to triage appropriateness, recognize red flags that demand in-person or emergency care, modify the examination for the virtual setting, handle technology failure gracefully, and document the reasoning that justified a remote encounter — including the reasoning that ended one.

## Core Rules

### Decide Appropriateness Before the Visit, Not During It

Telehealth appropriateness is a triage decision and should be made deliberately, ideally before the encounter begins. Appropriate presentations tend to be low-acuity, follow-up oriented, chronic-disease monitoring, medication management, results review, behavioral health, or minor self-limited complaints where the physical examination is unlikely to change management. Inappropriate presentations are those where physical examination, vital signs, point-of-care testing, or procedural intervention are likely to be decisive — acute abdominal pain, chest pain, dyspnea, suspected sepsis, new neurological deficits, severe trauma, obstetric emergencies, and acutely unstable patients of any kind. When in doubt, default to in-person. Build an explicit appropriateness check into intake, and document why telehealth was chosen for borderline cases. Do not let patient preference or scheduling convenience override a clinical safety judgment; if the presentation is wrong for telehealth, redirect to clinic or emergency department regardless of how the visit was booked.

### Maintain an Explicit Red-Flag List and Act on It

Telehealth demands a lower threshold to escalate because the safety net is thinner. Maintain a working list of red flags that mandate immediate escalation to in-person evaluation or emergency services: chest pain or pressure, syncope, severe dyspnea, signs of shock, focal neurological deficits, severe or worsening abdominal pain, uncontrolled bleeding, suicidal or homicidal ideation with intent, obstetric bleeding or decreased fetal movement, anaphylaxis, suspected sepsis, and any patient who appears unwell on camera. The rule is not "consider escalation" — it is "escalate." When a red flag appears, stop the diagnostic deliberation, direct the patient to emergency services (call emergency services, go to the nearest emergency department), give clear written and verbal instructions, and document the escalation including time, advice given, and the patient's acknowledged plan. Never end a telehealth visit on a red flag without a concrete safety plan.

### Modify the Examination for the Virtual Setting Deliberately

You cannot replicate an in-person examination remotely, but you can perform a structured modified examination if you plan it. Use patient-guided self-examination: have the patient or a caregiver point to pain, press on the abdomen and describe tenderness or guarding, demonstrate range of motion, show a rash under good lighting, or use a home device (blood pressure cuff, pulse oximeter, thermometer, glucometer) when available. Inspect general appearance, work of breathing, speech, mental status, gait, skin color, and distress level — these are observable on camera and carry real diagnostic weight. Be explicit about what you can and cannot assess, and document the modified examination as such ("abdomen: patient self-palpated, RLQ, no rebound described; cannot assess for guarding reliably"). When the modified examination is insufficient to make a safe decision, that itself is the indication for in-person care.

### Plan the Fallback Before You Need It

Every telehealth encounter should begin with a clear fallback plan that the clinician and patient share: what symptoms or changes would require escalation, where the patient should go, and within what timeframe. This is the telehealth equivalent of safety-netting in primary care. Specify return precautions in concrete, observable terms ("if the fever rises above 39°C, if breathing becomes labored, if you cannot keep fluids down"). Confirm the patient's location at the start of every visit — if a patient becomes unstable you need to know where to send help. Verify a call-back number in case the connection drops. A telehealth visit without a fallback plan is unsafe by default.

### Handle Technology Failure Without Losing the Patient

Connections drop, audio fails, video freezes, and patients may be in areas with poor signal. Have a protocol: if video fails, downgrade to telephone with an explicit acknowledgment of the further reduced examination; if telephone also fails, call back on a verified number; if contact cannot be re-established and the patient has any concerning features, escalate to emergency services using the patient's verified location. Never silently abandon a telehealth encounter. Document any technology failure, the mitigation attempted, and the final disposition. If a telephone-only encounter leaves you unable to make a safe decision, convert to in-person rather than guessing.

### Confirm Identity, Location, and Consent Every Time

At the start of each visit, verify patient identity, confirm the patient's current physical location (not just their registered address), and confirm informed consent for telehealth, including its limitations. These are not bureaucratic formalities — identity confirmation prevents errors, location confirmation enables emergency response, and consent ensures the patient understands that telehealth is not equivalent to in-person care. Document all three.

### Document Telehealth Specifically

The note should reflect that this was a telehealth encounter, the modality (video, telephone, asynchronous), the modified examination performed and its limitations, technology issues and mitigations, the patient's location, consent, the clinical reasoning, and the fallback plan with return precautions. A note that reads identically to an in-person visit, without acknowledging the virtual setting and its constraints, is clinically and medicolegally weaker.

## Common Traps

### Treating Telehealth as a Faster, Lighter Clinic Visit

Assuming telehealth is appropriate because it is convenient or booked. Counter by applying an explicit appropriateness triage before the encounter and being willing to redirect to in-person care.

### Continuing a Visit Despite a Red Flag

Continuing diagnostic deliberation when a red flag appears because the visit is "already underway." Counter by treating any red flag as an immediate stop-and-escalate signal.

### Overstating the Virtual Examination

Documenting examination findings as if performed in person when they were patient-guided or observational only. Counter by documenting the modified examination honestly, including what could not be assessed.

### No Fallback Plan

Ending a visit with advice but no concrete return precautions or escalation pathway. Counter by stating specific, observable triggers for re-presentation and confirming the patient understands them.

### Silent Abandonment on Technology Failure

Ending a visit without re-establishing contact or escalating when the connection drops on a concerning patient. Counter by having and following a technology-failure protocol, including escalation using the verified location.

### Assuming the Patient's Registered Address Is Their Current Location

Failing to confirm current location, leaving no way to send help in an emergency. Counter by verifying physical location at the start of every visit.

### Practicing Across Jurisdictions Without Checking Licensure

Assuming telehealth licensure follows the clinician rather than the patient's location. Counter by confirming the patient is in a jurisdiction where you are licensed before proceeding; escalate to compliance or legal when uncertain.

## Self-Check

- Did I make an explicit appropriateness decision for this presentation before or at the start of the visit, and document the reasoning for borderline cases?
- Did I screen for and act on red flags, escalating immediately rather than continuing when any were present?
- Did I perform and document a modified virtual examination honestly, including what I could and could not assess?
- Did I confirm the patient's current physical location, identity, and telehealth consent at the start of the visit?
- Did I provide a concrete, observable fallback plan and return precautions, and confirm the patient understood them?
- Did I handle any technology failure with a defined protocol, without abandoning the patient?
- Does my documentation clearly reflect the telehealth modality, its limitations, and the reasoning behind the disposition?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
