# Cancer and Palliative Pain Management

Cancer and end-of-life pain differ fundamentally from chronic non-cancer pain: the prognosis is limited, the pain is often severe and mixed (nociceptive, neuropathic, visceral, incident), and the balance shifts toward relief of suffering as a primary goal. Yet this is not a domain without judgment — opioids can relieve agony or cause sedation, delirium, and respiratory depression that robs a dying patient of their last conscious hours with family. The judgment problem is titrating analgesia aggressively enough to control severe pain while preserving alertness and dignity, anticipating and managing the side effects that drive non-adherence, recognizing neuropathic and breakthrough components that simple opioid escalation will not solve, and knowing when to escalate to specialist palliative care or consider palliative sedation for refractory suffering. This skill covers the cancer pain ladder, opioid titration and rotation, breakthrough pain, neuropathic components, side-effect management, sedation reasoning, and specialist escalation.

## Core Rules

### Apply the Cancer Pain Ladder and Combine Modalities

The World Health Organization analgesic ladder — non-opioids for mild pain, weak opioids or low-dose strong opioids for moderate pain, strong opioids for severe pain, with adjuvants at every step — remains a useful framework for cancer pain, but in practice most patients with significant cancer pain need strong opioids early and in combination with non-opioids and adjuvants. Combine scheduled acetaminophen and NSAIDs (where not contraindicated by renal function, thrombocytopenia, or bleeding) with the opioid to reduce opioid requirement. Add corticosteroids for inflammatory, edematous, or raised intracranial pressure pain (bone metastases, nerve compression, visceral capsular stretch), and add anticonvulsants or antidepressants for neuropathic components. The ladder is a framework for combining modalities, not a rigid sequence; severe cancer pain warrants strong opioids without delay, and under-treatment at the end of life is a serious failure of care.

### Titrate Opioids Aggressively but Safely for Severe Pain

For severe cancer pain, titrate rapidly to adequate analgesia. In opioid-naive patients, start with low doses (for example, oral morphine 5 to 10 mg every 4 hours, or 2 to 4 mg IV) and titrate using breakthrough doses; in opioid-tolerant patients, calculate the total daily dose and provide a baseline-plus-breakthrough regimen, with breakthrough doses typically 10 to 20 percent of the total daily dose available every hour as needed. Reassess frequently during titration — every 4 to 24 hours depending on route and acuity — and adjust the baseline based on total breakthrough use over the prior 24 hours. Renal and hepatic impairment modify choice and dose: avoid morphine and codeine in significant renal impairment (prefer fentanyl, hydromorphone, or oxycodone with caution), and reduce doses in hepatic impairment. Aggressive titration does not mean reckless titration — monitor sedation, respiratory rate, and side effects, and remember that opioid-tolerant patients are still at risk of respiratory depression from dose stacking.

### Rotate Opioids Using Equianalgesic Dosing With Cross-Tolerance Reduction

Opioid rotation — switching from one opioid to another — is indicated when the current opioid produces unmanageable side effects, when pain is uncontrolled despite titration, when renal or hepatic function changes, or to exploit incomplete cross-tolerance. Rotation requires three steps: calculate the 24-hour total of the current opioid, convert to an equianalgesic dose of the new opioid using published tables, then reduce the calculated dose by 25 to 50 percent for incomplete cross-tolerance (with greater reduction in frail, elderly, or highly tolerant patients). Methadone rotation is complex because of its variable and long half-life and should be done only with specialist input. After rotation, provide adequate breakthrough medication and reassess frequently, because equianalgesic tables are approximations and individual variation is large. Document the calculation and rationale, and escalate to palliative care for any complex rotation, especially involving methadone or high-dose conversion.

### Identify and Treat Breakthrough Pain Separately From Background Pain

Breakthrough pain — transient exacerbations on a background of controlled pain — affects most cancer patients and requires separate management. Characterize each breakthrough episode: is it incident (predictable, movement- or procedure-related), idiopathic (unpredictable), or end-of-dose failure (predictably before the next scheduled dose)? End-of-dose failure is managed by shortening the dosing interval or increasing the baseline dose. Incident pain is managed with preemptive breakthrough doses before the trigger and with non-pharmacologic strategies (positioning, splinting). Idiopathic pain requires rapid-onset rescue analgesia. Use rapid-onset opioid formulations (transmucosal, intranasal, buccal) for predictable breakthrough episodes because they match the time course of breakthrough pain better than oral immediate-release opioids; titrate the rescue dose individually rather than assuming a fixed proportion. Do not manage breakthrough pain solely by escalating the baseline opioid, because this causes toxicity between episodes. Distinguish breakthrough pain from poorly controlled background pain — if the patient needs rescue doses repeatedly around the clock, the baseline regimen needs revision.

### Address Neuropathic and Mixed Pain Components Explicitly

Cancer pain is often mixed, and a neuropathic component — from nerve infiltration, plexopathy, radiculopathy, or chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy — responds poorly to pure opioid escalation and signals the need for adjuvants. Recognize neuropathic features by the pain quality (burning, shooting, electric, lancinating) and distribution (dermatomal, stocking-glove), and add gabapentinoids, tricyclic antidepressants, or serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitors, titrating to effect. Corticosteroids help inflammatory and edematous neuropathic pain (nerve compression). For localized neuropathic pain, consider regional or neuraxial techniques in consultation with pain or palliative specialists. Continuing to escalate opioids in the face of untreated neuropathic pain causes sedation and toxicity without relief; naming the neuropathic component and adding targeted therapy is essential. Escalate to specialist palliative care for refractory or complex neuropathic cancer pain.

### Anticipate and Manage Opioid Side Effects Proactively

Opioid side effects are a leading cause of undertreatment, because patients stop or reduce their analgesia to escape them. Anticipate and manage them proactively rather than reactively. Constipation is near-universal, does not resolve with tolerance, and must be prevented from the first opioid dose with a stimulant laxative (often combined with an osmotic agent); opioid antagonists (naloxegol, methylnaltrexone) are options for refractory cases. Nausea and vomiting often occur at initiation and may resolve with tolerance; manage with antiemetics and by addressing the cause (gastric stasis, constipation, raised intracranial pressure). Sedation and cognitive effects often improve over days but warrant dose review, opioid rotation, or psychostimulant use if persistent and problematic. Myoclonus and neurotoxicity (especially with high-dose morphine metabolite accumulation in renal impairment) signal the need for rotation. Proactive side-effect management sustains adherence and prevents the cycle of untreated pain and avoidable suffering.

### Balance Pain Control With Sedation and Consider Palliative Sedation for Refractory Suffering

At the end of life, the balance between analgesia and alertness becomes central. The goal is adequate pain control with preserved consciousness when possible, but when pain or other refractory symptoms (delirium, dyspnea, existential distress) cannot be controlled without sedation, palliative sedation is an ethically accepted intervention of last resort. Palliative sedation is distinct from euthanasia: the intent is relief of refractory suffering, the drugs are titrated to symptom control, and the sedation is proportionate to the symptom. It requires informed consent from the patient or surrogate, multidisciplinary agreement that symptoms are truly refractory, and careful titration of sedatives (midazolam is common). Do not confuse palliative sedation with terminal restlessness from untreated pain or delirium — these require diagnosis and treatment first. Escalate to specialist palliative care for any case being considered for palliative sedation, and never undertake it without appropriate expertise and ethics input.

## Common Traps

### Under-Treating Severe Cancer Pain Out of Opioid Fear

Withholding strong opioids from a patient with severe cancer pain causes needless suffering and is a failure of care. Counter by titrating strong opioids early and aggressively, with monitoring.

### Escalating Opioids Indefinitely for Neuropathic or Mixed Pain

Pure opioid escalation for neuropathic pain causes sedation and toxicity without relief. Counter by recognizing neuropathic features and adding targeted adjuvants.

### Managing Breakthrough Pain Solely by Raising the Baseline Dose

Increasing the baseline opioid for breakthrough episodes causes between-episode toxicity. Counter by characterizing each episode, using rapid-onset rescue analgesia, and revising the baseline only for end-of-dose failure.

### Failing to Anticipate Constipation

Constipation is near-universal with opioids and does not resolve with tolerance; unmanaged constipation drives non-adherence and suffering. Counter by starting a stimulant laxative with the first opioid dose.

### Opioid Rotation Without Cross-Tolerance Reduction

Switching opioids at full equianalgesic dose causes overdose. Counter by reducing the calculated dose by 25 to 50 percent, providing rescue doses, and reassessing frequently.

### Confusing Palliative Sedation With Untreated Delirium or Pain

Terminal restlessness from untreated pain or delirium requires diagnosis and treatment, not sedation. Counter by assessing and treating reversible causes before considering palliative sedation.

### Undertaking Complex Rotations or Palliative Sedation Without Specialist Input

Methadone rotation, high-dose conversions, and palliative sedation carry significant risk and require expertise. Counter by escalating to specialist palliative care.

## Self-Check

- Have I combined modalities (non-opioids, opioids, adjuvants, corticosteroids) rather than relying on opioid escalation alone?
- For severe cancer pain, have I titrated strong opioids aggressively but safely, accounting for renal and hepatic function and monitoring sedation and respiratory rate?
- For any opioid rotation, have I calculated the equianalgesic dose, reduced it by 25 to 50 percent for incomplete cross-tolerance, provided rescue doses, and documented the calculation?
- Have I characterized breakthrough pain as incident, idiopathic, or end-of-dose failure and managed each appropriately, rather than only raising the baseline dose?
- Have I identified neuropathic or mixed pain components and added targeted adjuvants instead of escalating opioids indefinitely?
- Have I anticipated and proactively managed opioid side effects, especially constipation from the first dose?
- When balancing analgesia and alertness, have I distinguished palliative sedation for refractory suffering from untreated pain or delirium requiring diagnosis and treatment?
- For complex opioid rotation (especially methadone), refractory neuropathic pain, or any case being considered for palliative sedation, have I escalated to specialist palliative care and sought ethics input where appropriate?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
