# Acute Pain Assessment and Management

Acute pain is both a symptom and a harm in itself: untreated pain drives sympathetic surge, atelectasis, immobility, thromboembolism, delirium, and progression to chronic pain, while overtreatment causes respiratory depression, sedation, hypotension, and falls. The judgment problem in acute pain is not simply choosing a drug — it is matching analgesic intensity to pain severity and patient risk, layering multimodal agents so that opioid exposure is minimized, and detecting respiratory depression before it becomes arrest. The twin failures are under-treatment (dismissing pain, withholding opioids from patients who need them, or relying on PRN orders that are never given) and over-treatment (escalating opioids without monitoring, stacking sedating agents, missing the deteriorating patient). This skill covers structured pain assessment, multimodal analgesia, opioid dosing and titration, patient-controlled analgesia, regional options, respiratory monitoring, and when to escalate to an acute pain service.

## Core Rules

### Assess Pain With a Validated, Patient-Appropriate Tool

Pain is what the patient says it is, but it must be measured consistently to be managed. Use a validated scale appropriate to the patient: the 0-to-10 numeric rating scale for most adults, the visual analog scale, the verbal rating scale for quick bedside use, and the Faces or FLACC scale for patients who cannot use numbers (children, cognitively impaired adults). For sedated or critically ill patients, use behavioral pain tools such as the Behavioral Pain Scale or Critical-Care Pain Observation Tool, because vital signs alone are neither sensitive nor specific for pain. Assess pain at rest and on movement, because movement-evoked pain better predicts functional recovery and is the pain that drives complications. Reassess after every intervention at a time consistent with the drug's onset — within 15 to 30 minutes for IV opioids, 60 minutes for oral. A pain score that is not reassessed is not being managed.

### Build a Multimodal Regimen to Reduce Opioid Burden

Multimodal analgesia combines agents with different mechanisms so that each contributes and opioid dose is minimized. The foundation is scheduled acetaminophen (respecting the daily maximum, typically 3 to 4 grams per day in adults, lower in hepatic impairment, frailty, or chronic use) and an NSAID where not contraindicated (renal impairment, active bleeding, peptic ulcer, cardiovascular risk, third-trimester pregnancy). Add adjuvants targeted to pain type: gabapentinoids for neuropathic or postoperative pain (with dose reduction in renal impairment and caution for sedation, especially in older adults), ketamine infusions for severe or opioid-tolerant postoperative pain, alpha-2 agonists such as dexmedetomidine in critical care, and regional or neuraxial blockade where feasible. Multimodal regimens reduce opioid consumption, opioid-related side effects, and pain scores, but they also stack sedation risk — monitor accordingly, especially when gabapentinoids and opioids are combined in older or renally impaired patients.

### Apply the Analgesic Ladder and Match Intensity to Severity

Match analgesic intensity to pain severity and to the clinical context. For mild pain, scheduled non-opioids plus a non-opioid adjuvant often suffice. For moderate pain, add a weak opioid or low-dose strong opioid, continuing scheduled non-opioids. For severe pain (postoperative, traumatic, sickle crisis, renal colic, burns), use strong opioids titrated to effect, typically by the IV route for rapid control, alongside non-opioids and regional options. Do not treat severe acute pain with oral PRN codeine or insufficient doses — under-dosing severe pain is a failure of care. Conversely, do not escalate to strong opioids for pain that non-opioids and adjuvants can control. The ladder is a framework for matching intensity, not a rigid sequence to climb regardless of context.

### Dose and Titrate Opioids Correctly and Individually

There is no safe fixed opioid dose that applies to all patients. Opioid-naive adults require conservative starting doses (for example, morphine 2 to 4 mg IV every 2 to 4 hours, or 5 to 10 mg orally every 4 hours) with careful reassessment. Opioid-tolerant patients — those taking 60 mg or more oral morphine equivalents per day for a week or more — require higher baseline dosing and often a baseline-plus-breakthrough regimen; do not under-dose opioid-tolerant patients or assume their tolerance eliminates respiratory risk. Convert between opioids using equianalgesic tables with a reduction for incomplete cross-tolerance (typically 25 to 50 percent reduction when switching opioids), and adjust for renal or hepatic impairment (avoid morphine and codeine in significant renal impairment; prefer fentanyl or hydromorphone). Titrate to adequate analgesia with acceptable side effects, reassessing after each change. When pain is uncontrolled despite reasonable titration, escalate to the acute pain service rather than escalating opioids indefinitely.

### Use Patient-Controlled Analgesia Safely

Patient-controlled analgesia (PCA) allows self-titration within clinician-set limits and generally provides better pain control with lower total opioid use. Safe PCA requires correct programming of the drug, concentration, demand dose, lockout interval, and continuous (basal) rate. Basal infusion in opioid-naive patients increases respiratory depression and is generally avoided; basal rates may be appropriate in opioid-tolerant patients. Ensure the patient, not family or staff, presses the button (no proxy dosing), that the patient can understand and physically operate the device, and that monitoring is in place (sedation scoring, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and capnography where high-risk). PCA errors are usually programming errors — wrong concentration, wrong drug, or basal rate left on — so an independent double-check of the pump settings is mandatory at setup and after any change.

### Add Regional and Neuraxial Techniques Where Appropriate

Regional analgesia — peripheral nerve blocks, continuous catheter techniques, epidural and spinal analgesia — can provide superior pain control with minimal systemic opioid, and is especially valuable for thoracic, abdominal, and orthopedic surgery and for rib fractures. Regional techniques reduce opioid use, respiratory complications, ileus, and chronic postsurgical pain, but they carry their own risks (local anesthetic systemic toxicity, nerve injury, infection, hematoma, and, for neuraxial techniques, epidural hematoma and abscess). Recognize the signs of local anesthetic systemic toxicity (CNS excitation followed by seizure, then cardiovascular collapse) and have intralipid rescue available wherever major blocks are performed. Neuraxial techniques require attention to anticoagulation timing to avoid compressive hematoma. Involve the acute pain service early for patients who would benefit, and escalate any new neurological deficit or severe back pain after neuraxial block as a potential emergency.

### Monitor for and Prevent Opioid-Induced Respiratory Depression

Respiratory depression is the most feared opioid complication and is often preceded by sedation rather than by a low respiratory rate. Use a sedation scale (such as the Pasero or Richmond scale) and treat increasing sedation as the earliest warning — a sedated patient is at risk even if the respiratory rate appears acceptable. Risk factors include opioid-naive status, high opioid dose, concurrent benzodiazepines or other sedatives, sleep apnea, obesity, age, renal or hepatic impairment, and PCA basal infusion. Layered sedating agents (opioid plus gabapentinoid plus benzodiazepine) are particularly dangerous. Monitor respiratory rate, sedation, and oxygen saturation; consider capnography for high-risk patients. Naloxone reverses opioid respiratory depression but must be titrated to respiratory effort, not to full alertness, to avoid precipitating acute withdrawal and severe pain. Any patient requiring naloxone requires continued observation for renarcotization, and escalation to a higher level of monitoring.

## Common Traps

### Under-Treating Acute Pain Out of Fear of Opioids

Withholding adequate opioids from a patient with severe acute pain causes suffering, complications, and progression to chronic pain, and is a failure of care. Counter by matching analgesic intensity to pain severity and reassessing, not by defaulting to weak agents.

### Treating Pain Scores Without Reassessment or Functional Context

A single pain score drives poor decisions; the trend and the functional impact matter. Counter by reassessing after every intervention and using movement-evoked pain and function as the real targets.

### Stacking Sedating Agents Without Adjusted Monitoring

Combining opioids, gabapentinoids, and benzodiazepines multiplies respiratory-depression risk, especially in older or renally impaired patients. Counter by reducing doses when combining agents and escalating monitoring, including capnography for high-risk patients.

### Using PCA Basal Infusion in Opioid-Naive Patients

Basal infusion in opioid-naive patients increases respiratory depression without improving analgesia. Counter by reserving basal rates for opioid-tolerant patients and performing an independent double-check of pump settings.

### Missing Local Anesthetic Systemic Toxicity

CNS excitation, perioral tingling, or sudden cardiovascular collapse after a regional block are often misattributed to other causes. Counter by recognizing the signs early and having intralipid rescue immediately available.

### Ignoring New Neurological Deficit After Neuraxial Block

New weakness, sensory loss, or severe back pain after epidural or spinal analgesia may indicate compressive hematoma or abscess and is an emergency. Counter by urgent imaging and escalation to the acute pain or neurosurgical team.

### Relying on Respiratory Rate Alone to Detect Opioid Toxicity

Respiratory depression is often preceded by sedation, and a "normal" respiratory rate can coexist with dangerous opioid effect. Counter by using a sedation scale and treating rising sedation as the earliest warning sign.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed pain with a validated, patient-appropriate tool at rest and on movement, and reassessed after every intervention at the correct interval?
- Have I built a multimodal regimen with scheduled non-opioids and targeted adjuvants so that opioid burden is minimized rather than relying on opioids alone?
- Have I matched analgesic intensity to pain severity and clinical context, neither under-treating severe pain nor escalating opioids unnecessarily?
- For opioid dosing, have I accounted for opioid tolerance, renal and hepatic function, and incomplete cross-tolerance when switching opioids?
- For PCA, have I performed an independent double-check of drug, concentration, demand dose, lockout, and basal rate, and ensured no proxy dosing?
- Where regional or neuraxial techniques are used, have I planned anticoagulation timing, recognized local anesthetic systemic toxicity signs, and have intralipid available?
- Am I monitoring sedation (not just respiratory rate) for opioid-induced respiratory depression, and have I identified high-risk patients for capnography?
- For pain uncontrolled despite reasonable titration, or for any new neurological deficit after neuraxial block, have I escalated to the acute pain service or relevant specialist?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
