# Chronic Non-Cancer Pain Management

Chronic non-cancer pain is one of the most clinically and ethically difficult domains in medicine, because the treatments that relieve pain in the short term — especially opioids — can cause grave long-term harm, and because pain itself is inseparable from mood, function, sleep, and social circumstance. The judgment problem is not choosing a drug but deciding what the goal of treatment is, whether opioids are ever appropriate for this patient, how to limit harm if they are used, and when to redirect care toward non-pharmacologic and multidisciplinary approaches. The dominant failure of the past two decades was reflexive long-term opioid prescribing for chronic non-cancer pain, which produced dependence, diversion, overdose death, and hypogonadism without sustained functional improvement. The mirror failure is abandoning patients in pain without offering alternatives. This skill covers the biopsychosocial model, opioid risk stratification, the limited role of chronic opioids, adjuvant and non-pharmacologic therapy, and when to escalate to a pain management program or specialist.

## Core Rules

### Adopt the Biopsychosocial Model and Treat Function, Not Just Pain Scores

Chronic pain is not a simple nociceptive signal; it is a complex interaction of tissue injury, central sensitization, mood, beliefs, sleep, and social context. Adopting the biopsychosocial model means assessing not only the pain location and quality but also the patient's mood (depression and anxiety amplify pain and reduce coping), sleep (poor sleep worsens pain and vice versa), beliefs and catastrophizing, functional limitations, and social and occupational circumstances. Set functional goals — improved walking distance, return to work, better sleep, participation in family life — rather than chasing a pain score of zero, because complete pain elimination is usually unrealistic and pursuing it drives escalating and harmful medication use. Measure function with validated tools such as the Pain Disability Assessment Scale or brief functional questions. A treatment that lowers a pain score but does not improve function is not succeeding; a treatment that improves function despite persistent pain is succeeding.

### Stratify Opioid Risk Before Initiating or Continuing Chronic Opioids

Before starting or continuing long-term opioids for chronic non-cancer pain, assess the patient's risk of harm using a validated tool such as the Opioid Risk Tool (ORT). The ORT stratifies risk based on personal and family history of substance use, psychiatric history, age, and history of preadolescent sexual abuse, and predicts the likelihood of aberrant medication-taking behaviors. High-risk patients are poor candidates for chronic opioids and should be redirected to non-opioid and multidisciplinary care; low-to-moderate-risk patients may be considered for a careful opioid trial only if non-opioid approaches have failed and clear functional goals are defined. Risk stratification is not a gatekeeping ritual — it determines whether opioids are appropriate at all. Screen for depression and suicide risk, because chronic pain and opioid use both elevate these. Document the risk assessment and the decision rationale; prescribing chronic opioids without risk stratification is poor practice and medico-legally exposed.

### Recognize the Limited and Conditional Role of Chronic Opioids

The evidence for long-term opioids in chronic non-cancer pain is weak: benefits for pain and function are modest, attenuate over time, and are offset by rising harms (dependence, tolerance, endocrine dysfunction, overdose, falls in older adults, all-cause mortality). Chronic opioids are not first-line therapy and are rarely the best long-term strategy. If they are used at all, it should be after a structured trial with defined functional goals, the lowest effective dose, regular reassessment of benefit and harm, monitoring for aberrant behaviors, and a clear exit strategy. Avoid dose escalation beyond established thresholds (commonly 50 and especially 90 morphine milligram equivalents per day) without specialist input, because harm rises steeply with dose. Tapering is appropriate when opioids are not achieving functional goals, when harms emerge, or when high-risk behaviors appear — and tapering itself requires skill, empathy, and a structured plan to avoid withdrawal and relapse. Do not abandon patients during taper; offer ongoing non-opioid and multidisciplinary care.

### Use Adjuvant Analgesics Targeted to Pain Mechanism

Adjuvant analgesics — gabapentinoids (gabapentin, pregabalin), tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline), and serotonin-noradrenaline reuptake inhibitors (duloxetine, venlafaxine) — are first-line for neuropathic and centralized chronic pain and are often more effective than opioids with lower dependence risk. Match the agent to the pain and the patient: gabapentinoids and duloxetine for neuropathic pain and fibromyalgia; tricyclics for neuropathic pain and where sleep benefit is desired (but with caution in older adults and cardiac disease); duloxetine also for chronic musculoskeletal pain. Start low and titrate slowly, especially in older adults and renal impairment, and reassess for benefit over 4 to 8 weeks — discontinue if ineffective rather than escalating indefinitely. Be aware of misuse potential with gabapentinoids (especially with opioids) and of the sedation and fall risk in older adults. Avoid combining multiple sedating adjuvants and opioids without clear justification and adjusted monitoring.

### Prioritize Non-Pharmacologic and Psychological Therapies

Non-pharmacologic therapies are the evidence-based backbone of chronic non-cancer pain management and are too often underused. Cognitive behavioral therapy for chronic pain changes pain-related beliefs, catastrophizing, and avoidance, and has durable benefit on function and mood. Graded exercise and activity programs reverse deconditioning and fear-avoidance. Mindfulness-based stress reduction, acceptance and commitment therapy, sleep optimization, and sleep-focused cognitive behavioral therapy address the central sensitization and mood components that drive chronic pain. Physical therapies, occupational therapy, and vocational rehabilitation restore function. These interventions require referral, time, and patient engagement, and they are most effective when delivered as a coordinated program rather than as isolated referrals. Frame these as the core treatment, not as adjuncts or "lifestyle advice," and set expectations that improvement is gradual and functional rather than a quick fix.

### Refer to a Multidisciplinary Pain Management Program When Appropriate

Multidisciplinary pain management programs (PMPs), which combine physical reconditioning, psychological therapy, education, and medication review, have the strongest evidence for improving function and mood in chronic non-cancer pain and for supporting opioid taper. Refer patients with persistent pain-related disability, significant psychological comorbidity, failed single-modality treatments, or problematic opioid use. Recognize that referral is a positive therapeutic step, not an admission of failure. For complex neuropathic pain, consider pain specialist input for interventional options (nerve blocks, neuromodulation) where appropriate, but maintain realistic expectations and ensure interventions are embedded in a broader functional plan. Escalate urgently to mental health services for suicidal ideation, and to addiction services for substance use disorder or severe opioid use disorder, because these are common and dangerous comorbidities in this population.

## Common Traps

### Initiating Chronic Opioids Without Risk Stratification or Defined Goals

Prescribing long-term opioids without an ORT, functional goals, and an exit strategy leads to harm without sustained benefit. Counter by stratifying risk, defining functional targets, and planning the trial and review schedule before the first prescription.

### Chasing a Pain Score of Zero With Escalating Doses

Pursuing complete pain elimination drives dose escalation beyond safe thresholds without functional gain. Counter by setting functional goals and treating improvement in function, not a lower pain score, as success.

### Using Adjuvants Indiscriminately or Without Reassessment

Stacking gabapentinoids, tricyclics, and opioids without mechanism targeting or scheduled review causes sedation, falls, and misuse. Counter by matching adjuvants to pain mechanism, starting low, titrating slowly, and discontinuing ineffective agents.

### Dismissing Non-Pharmacologic Therapies as Adjuncts

Framing cognitive behavioral therapy, exercise, and sleep work as optional add-ons underuses the most effective interventions. Counter by presenting them as the core treatment and coordinating referral.

### Abandoning Patients During Opioid Taper

Abrupt taper or discharge leaves patients in withdrawal and drives relapse and overdose. Counter by tapering with empathy and structure, and offering ongoing multidisciplinary care.

### Missing Depression, Suicide Risk, or Substance Use Disorder

Chronic pain patients have elevated rates of mood disorder, suicidality, and substance use, which are often overlooked. Counter by screening for these and escalating to mental health or addiction services when identified.

### Failing to Refer to a Pain Management Program

Single-modality, repeated interventions without functional progress indicate the need for multidisciplinary care. Counter by recognizing referral as a positive step and not a failure.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed the patient biopsychosocially — mood, sleep, beliefs, function, and social context — and set functional goals rather than chasing a pain score of zero?
- Before initiating or continuing chronic opioids, have I stratified risk with a validated tool (ORT), screened for depression and suicidality, and documented the decision rationale?
- Have I recognized that chronic opioids are not first-line, used the lowest effective dose, avoided escalation beyond established thresholds without specialist input, and maintained a clear exit strategy?
- Have I selected adjuvant analgesics by pain mechanism, started low and titrated slowly, and planned discontinuation if ineffective?
- Have I prioritized and coordinated non-pharmacologic therapies (cognitive behavioral therapy, graded exercise, sleep work) as the core treatment, not adjuncts?
- For persistent disability, psychological comorbidity, failed single-modality treatment, or problematic opioid use, have I referred to a multidisciplinary pain management program?
- Have I screened for and escalated depression, suicide risk, and substance use disorder to mental health or addiction services?
- When tapering opioids, have I planned a structured, empathetic taper and offered ongoing non-opioid and multidisciplinary care rather than abandoning the patient?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
