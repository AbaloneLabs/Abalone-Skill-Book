# Functional and Disability Assessment

Functional assessment asks a deceptively simple question: what can this person actually do, and what has their illness taken from them? The answer drives decisions far beyond the bedside — admission versus discharge, rehabilitation candidacy, return to work, eligibility for benefits, placement, and the goals of care themselves. Yet functional status is routinely assessed casually, by guessing from the diagnosis or by asking a single vague question, and the resulting errors cut both ways. Over-assessment labels a capable person as dependent, hastening institutionalization and stripping autonomy; under-assessment sends a fragile patient home to fail, or denies rehabilitation to someone who would have benefited. Functional assessment is also freighted with stakes that clinical assessment is not: its conclusions become legal and financial documents, entered into insurance files, occupational records, and care plans that follow the patient for years. This skill covers the disciplined use of functional and frailty instruments, the avoidance of over- and under-assessment, and the escalation pathways when capacity and rehabilitation questions exceed the assessment. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when assessment is complex, contested, or high-stakes, escalate to the appropriate specialist and follow institutional protocols.

## Core Rules

### Measure Function Directly and Separately From Diagnosis

Do not infer function from diagnosis. Two patients with identical diagnoses may have vastly different capacities, and two patients with identical capacities may carry different diagnoses. Measure function directly, using validated instruments, and report it as its own domain. The core distinction is between Basic Activities of Daily Living (BADL/ADL — bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, feeding), which capture basic self-care, and Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADL — shopping, cooking, housekeeping, finances, medications, transport, telephone), which capture independent community living. Use named scales (Katz Index for ADL, Lawton-Brody for IADL) rather than free-text impressions, so the assessment is reproducible and comparable across time and clinicians. State which instrument was used and the resulting score; a note that says "independent" without a scale is neither auditable nor useful to the next clinician.

### Anchor Assessment in the Patient's Baseline and Observe Performance

A single snapshot of function is easily misread. Anchor every assessment to the patient's baseline — what they could do a month or a year ago, before this illness — because the gap between baseline and current is often more clinically meaningful than the absolute level. A patient who has fallen from fully independent to needing help with dressing is in trouble even if they still appear "moderate" on a scale. Wherever possible, assess performance rather than report: watch the patient walk, transfer, dress, or manage their medications rather than asking whether they can. Self-report overstates capacity, particularly in cognitive impairment and in patients eager to appear well for discharge. Collateral from family and caregivers is essential when self-report is unreliable, and should be sought and documented with its source.

### Assess Frailty Explicitly in Older and Vulnerable Patients

Frailty is a distinct biological syndrome of reduced reserve, and it predicts outcomes (mortality, complications, institutionalization, length of stay) more powerfully than age or comorbidity alone. Use a validated frailty instrument — the Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) is widely used, quick, and prognostically robust; other options include the Edmonton Frail Scale and the FRAIL scale. Do not substitute "elderly" or "frail-looking" for a scored assessment; subjective frailty judgments are inconsistent and biased. Frailty changes the interpretation of severity scores, the threshold for aggressive intervention, and the rehabilitation plan, so it must be measured and recorded, not assumed. In surgical and critical-care contexts, frailty is increasingly used to risk-stratify; an unmeasured frail patient is an under-informed plan.

### Distinguish Capacity From Function, and Assess Both

Functional capacity (what the body can do) and decision-making capacity (what the mind can authorize) are different and must be assessed separately. A patient may be physically capable yet lack capacity to consent, or may have full capacity yet be physically dependent. Capacity is decision-specific and time-specific: assess it for the actual decision at hand, using a structured capacity assessment (understanding, appreciation, reasoning, expressing a choice), and document the assessment rather than asserting "capacity intact." Never equate cognitive impairment, age, or unpopular decisions with incapacity. Where capacity is contested or fluctuating, escalate to a specialist and follow the legal framework for substitute decision-making in your jurisdiction.

### Avoid Both Over-Assessment and Under-Assessment

The two failure modes are mirror images. Over-assessment occurs when a patient is scored as more dependent than they are — often because of a single observed difficulty, because of ageism, or because of pressure to justify placement or services. This strips autonomy, accelerates institutionalization, and can become self-fulfilling as the patient stops doing what they are no longer asked to do. Under-assessment occurs when function is overestimated from a good day, from self-report, or from a desire to discharge — sending a patient home to fall, decompensate, or fail at tasks they cannot manage. Counter both by measuring directly, observing performance, seeking collateral, anchoring to baseline, and using validated instruments rather than impression. When in doubt, assess over more than one encounter; function fluctuates, especially in delirium, fatigue, and chronic disease.

### Link the Assessment to a Concrete Plan and Rehabilitation Candidacy

A functional assessment without a plan is a measurement without a purpose. Translate the findings into action: what does the patient need to go home safely (equipment, carers, modifications, follow-up), are they a candidate for rehabilitation (and which level — inpatient, subacute, outpatient), what are the rehabilitation goals and timeframe, and what is the expected trajectory. Rehabilitation candidacy depends not only on current function but on rehab tolerance, learning potential, premorbid status, and goals; a low current score in a motivated patient with good premorbid function may still warrant aggressive rehab, while a higher score in a patient who cannot participate may not. Escalate to physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and rehabilitation medicine for formal assessment when the question is complex or the stakes are high. This is educational not medical advice; for contested insurance or occupational determinations, follow the specific assessment framework required by the requesting body.

### Recognize the Legal and Financial Weight of the Documentation

Functional and disability assessments for insurance, occupational, or legal purposes carry consequences that ordinary clinical notes do not. Be precise, evidence-based, and defensible; distinguish observed findings from reported history, state the instruments used, and avoid speculation beyond the evidence. Do not conflate clinical advocacy with the assessor role; in contested determinations, your job is an accurate assessment, not a favorable one. Where the assessment is outside your expertise or the stakes are high, defer to the appropriate specialist assessor.

## Common Traps

### Inferring Function From Diagnosis

Assuming capacity from the diagnosis rather than measuring it. Counter by assessing ADL, IADL, and frailty directly with validated instruments, independent of the medical diagnosis.

### Trusting Self-Report Over Performance

Recording what the patient says they can do without observing it. Counter by observing transfers, gait, dressing, and medication management, and seeking collateral.

### Single-Snapshot Assessment Without Baseline

Judging function from one encounter without reference to premorbid baseline. Counter by anchoring current function to the patient's prior level and treating the gap as the key signal.

### Equating Age or Cognitive Impairment With Incapacity

Conflating function or cognition with decision-making capacity. Counter by assessing capacity decision-specifically and documenting the structured assessment.

### Over-Assessing to Justify Placement

Scoring a patient as more dependent than they are to qualify them for services or placement. Counter by measuring directly and resisting pressure to inflate dependence.

### Under-Assessing to Justify Discharge

Overestimating function from a good day or self-report to send a patient home. Counter by observing performance, seeking collateral, and assessing over multiple encounters when function fluctuates.

### Assessment Without a Plan

Measuring function without translating it into equipment, carers, rehab candidacy, or goals. Counter by linking every assessment to a concrete plan and escalating to PT/OT or rehab medicine when complex.

## Self-Check

- Did I measure function directly with validated instruments (e.g., Katz ADL, Lawton IADL), and state the instrument and score, rather than inferring from diagnosis?
- Did I anchor the assessment to the patient's premorbid baseline and treat the gap as a key clinical signal?
- Did I observe performance and seek collateral, rather than relying on self-report alone?
- Did I assess frailty explicitly (e.g., Clinical Frailty Scale) in older or vulnerable patients, rather than substituting age or appearance?
- Did I assess decision-making capacity separately and decision-specifically, documenting the structured assessment rather than asserting it?
- Did I guard against both over-assessment (stripping autonomy) and under-assessment (unsafe discharge), by measuring directly and over multiple encounters when needed?
- Did I translate the assessment into a concrete plan — equipment, carers, rehabilitation candidacy, goals — and escalate to PT/OT or rehab medicine when complex?
- For insurance, occupational, or legal assessments, did I keep the documentation precise, evidence-based, and free of speculation beyond the evidence?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
