# Symptom Evaluation and Red-Flag Recognition

Most symptoms that bring patients to care are benign and self-limiting; a small minority signal life-threatening disease. The central task of symptom evaluation is not to diagnose every complaint but to reliably separate the dangerous few from the benign many — and to do so without either missing serious disease or subjecting every patient to exhaustive workup. This is harder than it sounds. Dangerous presentations often begin as ordinary symptoms, and the features that distinguish them are subtle, temporal, and easy to under-weight in a busy environment. The clinician's failure mode is rarely ignorance of the disease; it is the assumption that a familiar symptom must have a familiar, benign cause. Reassurance given to the wrong patient is a serious harm, as is the cascade of over-investigation given to the right patient handled poorly. This skill covers the disciplined evaluation of undifferentiated symptoms, the systematic screening for red flags, and the threshold decisions that separate urgent workup from safe observation. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when red flags are present or the diagnosis is uncertain, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.

## Core Rules

### Characterize the Symptom Along the Axes That Separate Dangerous From Benign

Most symptoms are distinguished not by their presence but by their features. Use a structured characterization framework (OLDCARTS or SOCRATES) and weight the axes that carry prognostic signal: onset (sudden versus gradual — sudden onset of severe headache, chest pain, or dyspnea raises the pre-test probability of vascular catastrophe), progression, character (tearing, pressure, pleuritic, cramping each point differently), radiation, precipitants, and relieving factors. Age, comorbidity, and baseline function act as modifiers that shift the threshold for concern — the same chest pain in a twenty-five-year-old athlete and a seventy-year-old diabetic smoker means very different things. Build the characterization before deciding on workup; a symptom described only as "chest pain" cannot be triaged safely.

### Apply a Known Red-Flag Set for Each Common Presentation

Red flags are not generic warnings; they are specific, evidence-anchored features that meaningfully raise the probability of a dangerous diagnosis for a given complaint. Know them by system and ask for them explicitly. For chest pain: sudden severe onset, tearing pain, pain with diaphoresis or syncope, radiation to the back, exertional pattern, associated dyspnea, unequal pulses. For dyspnea: sudden onset, pleuritic pain, hypoxia, unilateral leg swelling, history of immobility or malignancy. For headache: sudden thunderclap onset, worst-ever, neck stiffness, focal neurology, immunocompromise, new headache after age fifty, headache with systemic features. For abdominal pain: severe pain out of proportion to exam, hematemesis or melena, rigid abdomen, jaundice, aneurysm history, peritoneal signs. For weight loss: unintentional loss exceeding a meaningful threshold, night sweats, dysphagia, hemoptysis, change in bowel habit. Document that each red flag was asked and answered; absence of a red flag is clinically meaningful only if you confirmed its absence.

### Use Pre-Test Probability and Risk Stratification, Not Gut Feeling

The decision to investigate urgently should be driven by an explicit estimate of risk, not by a feeling that the patient is "probably fine." Use validated risk stratification tools where they exist (HEART score for chest pain, Wells for pulmonary embolism, CURB-65 or pneumonia severity for respiratory infection, Ottawa rules for ankle/knee, Canadian C-spine and CT head rules). These tools force the relevant variables into the decision and reduce the influence of availability bias and anchoring. State the pre-test probability category before ordering tests or discharging; a patient sent home from a moderate- or high-risk stratum without a plan is a patient at risk. When no validated tool exists, name the must-not-miss diagnoses and state what would rule each in or out.

### Define the Threshold for Urgent Workup Explicitly

For each undifferentiated symptom, decide and document whether the presentation is below the testing threshold (safe to observe or reassure), in the testing zone (workup warranted), or above the treatment threshold (treat empirically while confirming). The threshold is a function of pre-test probability, the danger of the conditions being considered, and the harms of the workup itself. A low-probability but high-danger condition (suspected aortic dissection, subarachnoid hemorrhage, pulmonary embolism) often demands urgent imaging even when a benign diagnosis seems more likely, because the cost of missing it is catastrophic. A high-probability but low-danger condition may warrant reassurance and safety-netting instead. Make the threshold reasoning explicit so it can be reviewed and so the patient understands why workup is or is not being done.

### Provide a Safety-Net That Makes Reassurance Safe

Reassurance without a safety-net is a common and dangerous error. If a patient is discharged without urgent workup, they must leave with a clear understanding of what symptoms should bring them back, when, and to whom. Specify the red-flag features that would change the decision (new dyspnea, syncope, neurological deficit, worsening pain, fever with stiff neck), the timeframe for expected improvement, and the route of return. A safety-net converts a disposition from a gamble into a monitored decision. Document the safety-net advice given; it is part of the clinical plan, not a courtesy.

### Reassess When the Symptom Does Not Follow the Expected Course

Benign symptoms follow predictable courses; dangerous ones often deviate. Build in a defined reassessment point for any undifferentiated symptom sent home without a firm diagnosis, and treat failure to improve as a signal to widen the differential and raise the workup threshold. A symptom that persists beyond its expected benign course, changes character, or acquires new associated features is a new presentation requiring fresh evaluation, not a continuation of the old one.

### Recognize When Reassurance Is Unsafe

Some presentations should never be reassured without investigation regardless of how low the probability seems: any feature on the must-not-miss list, any immunocompromised or frail patient with systemic symptoms, any post-operative patient with new chest pain or dyspnea, any pregnant patient with atypical symptoms, and any patient whose account does not fit a coherent benign pattern. When in doubt, investigate or observe rather than reassure; the downside of an unnecessary test is usually smaller than the downside of a missed catastrophe.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring a Familiar Symptom Without Characterizing It

Treating "chest pain" or "headache" as benign because most are, without characterizing onset, character, and associated features. Counter by completing structured characterization before deciding on disposition.

### Failing to Ask for Specific Red Flags

Knowing the red flags exist but not asking for them, then recording a benign impression. Counter by asking each red-flag question explicitly and documenting the answers.

### Relying on Gut Feeling for Risk Stratification

Estimating risk by intuition rather than validated tools, allowing bias to dominate. Counter by using risk scores and stating the pre-test probability category before disposition.

### Discharging Without a Safety-Net

Reassuring the patient without specifying what should bring them back. Counter by giving explicit return precautions, timeframe, and route of return, and documenting them.

### Ignoring Age, Comorbidity, and Baseline as Modifiers

Applying the same threshold to a young fit patient and an elderly diabetic one. Counter by weighting age, comorbidity, and baseline into the risk estimate.

### Treating a Persistent or Changing Symptom as the Original Benign One

Re-evaluating a non-resolving symptom as more of the same rather than a new presentation. Counter by widening the differential and raising the workup threshold when the course deviates.

### Anchoring on a Benign Explanation

Accepting the first plausible benign diagnosis and discounting features that do not fit. Counter by maintaining a must-not-miss list and explaining — not dismissing — inconsistent features.

## Self-Check

- Did I characterize the symptom along the prognostically meaningful axes (onset, character, progression, radiation, modifiers) before deciding on workup?
- Did I ask and document the specific red-flag features for this presentation, rather than relying on a general impression?
- Did I use a validated risk-stratification tool or explicit pre-test probability estimate, rather than gut feeling, to guide the workup decision?
- Did I state whether the presentation is below testing threshold, in the testing zone, or above treatment threshold, with reasoning?
- For any discharge without urgent workup, did I provide and document a specific safety-net — return precautions, timeframe, and route?
- Did I plan a reassessment point for undifferentiated symptoms, and treat deviation from the expected course as a trigger to widen the differential?
- Did I avoid reassuring any presentation that belongs on the "never reassure without investigation" list?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
