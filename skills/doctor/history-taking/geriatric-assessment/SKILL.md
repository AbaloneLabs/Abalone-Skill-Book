# Geriatric Assessment

Older adults are the population in which the standard adult history and examination most reliably fails, and the failure is not a minor calibration error — it is the difference between a correct diagnosis and a missed one, between independence and institutionalization, between recovery and irreversible decline. The geriatric patient differs from the textbook adult in several structural ways that change the meaning of every symptom and every finding. Disease presents atypically: the myocardial infarction without chest pain, the infection without fever, the depression that looks like dementia, the urinary tract infection that looks like a fall or acute confusion. Multimorbidity is the norm rather than the exception, so the patient has five or six chronic conditions whose treatments interact and whose symptoms overlap, and a single-disease framework cannot capture the person. Frailty, functional decline, cognitive impairment, polypharmacy, and social vulnerability are not background details but central determinants of outcome that the standard history omits. The recurring failure is to apply the organ-by-organ adult framework to a patient whose problems are syndromic and interdependent — to chase the presenting complaint (a fall, confusion, a "off legs" presentation) toward a single discrete diagnosis while missing the cascade of contributors (delirium on dementia, polypharmacy, dehydration, infection, environmental hazard) that actually caused it. This skill covers the structured geriatric assessment: history-taking that accounts for atypical presentation and multimorbidity, the evaluation of function cognition and frailty, medication reconciliation, screening for the high-yield geriatric syndromes (falls, delirium, malnutrition, incontinence, elder mistreatment), and the integration of these domains into a coherent care plan. The discipline is to assess the whole older person, not just the presenting organ, and to recognize that the goal of care is often function and quality of life rather than disease eradication.

## Core Rules

### Expect Atypical Presentation and Build the Differential Around It

In older adults, disease routinely presents in ways that diverge from the classic textbook description, and expecting the classic presentation is a direct cause of missed diagnosis and delayed treatment. The mechanisms are physiological blunting (impaired fever response, reduced pain perception, blunted sympathetic response) and the masking effect of comorbidity and polypharmacy. Build the geriatric differential around atypical presentations: infection may present without fever, as delirium, a fall, functional decline, or "off legs"; myocardial infarction may present as dyspnea, syncope, confusion, or epigastric discomfort rather than crushing chest pain; acute abdomen may present without guarding or rebound; depression may present as cognitive impairment, somatic complaints, or withdrawal; pulmonary embolism may present as isolated confusion or syncope; and thyroid disease may present as atrial fibrillation, depression, or dementia. The practical implication is that any acute change in an older adult — new confusion, a fall, a decline in function or mobility, new incontinence, or simply "not their usual self" — must trigger a search for an underlying acute illness (infection, metabolic disturbance, drug effect, cardiovascular event, or neurological event) even when the vital signs and the presenting symptom seem minor. Never attribute an acute decline to "age" or to a chronic condition without excluding a treatable acute cause.

### Take a Function-Centered History, Because Function Is the Outcome That Matters

For older adults, functional status — the ability to perform basic and instrumental activities of daily living — is often a more meaningful outcome than disease markers, and functional decline is both a key symptom and a key prognostic indicator. Take a structured functional history using validated frameworks: basic activities of daily living (bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, feeding) and instrumental activities of daily living (managing medications, finances, transportation, shopping, cooking, housekeeping). Establish the patient's baseline function and compare the current state to it, because an acute functional decline is a red flag for acute illness even when the patient cannot articulate a specific complaint. Ask what the patient could do a month ago that they cannot do now, and pursue the cause of the change. Functional history also reveals the supports the patient relies on (family, paid caregivers, community services) and the gaps that put them at risk, which is essential for any safe discharge or care plan. Treat function as a vital sign: measure it, trend it, and act on its decline.

### Screen for Cognitive Impairment and Distinguish It From Delirium and Depression

Cognitive impairment is common in older adults, frequently unrecognized, and profoundly affects history reliability, treatment adherence, safety, and prognosis. Screen cognition with a validated brief tool (such as the Mini-Cog, Montreal Cognitive Assessment, or similar) as a routine part of the geriatric assessment, because bedside impression alone misses mild and even moderate impairment. When cognitive impairment is detected, the critical first step is to distinguish delirium (acute, fluctuating, inattention, often reversible) from dementia (chronic, progressive) and from depression (which can mimic dementia as "pseudodementia"). Delirium is a medical emergency: it is acutely caused by an underlying illness or drug, it carries a high mortality, and it demands immediate investigation for the cause (infection, metabolic disturbance, drug effect, pain, urinary retention, fecal impaction, hypoxia). Never attribute acute confusion to dementia or to "being elderly" without excluding delirium. Use collateral history from family or caregivers to establish the baseline cognitive state and the acuity of change, because the patient themselves may not be a reliable historian for their own cognitive decline. Assess mood explicitly, because depression is common, treatable, and frequently presents atypically in older adults as somatic complaints, cognitive complaints, or withdrawal.

### Evaluate Frailty and Incorporate It Into Prognosis and Decision-Making

Frailty is a state of reduced physiological reserve and increased vulnerability to stressors, and it is one of the most powerful predictors of outcome in older adults — more predictive than chronological age or individual comorbidities for mortality, complications, functional decline, and institutionalization. Assess frailty using a validated tool or clinical assessment (unintentional weight loss, exhaustion, weakness, slow gait speed, low physical activity; or a cumulative deficit approach). Incorporate frailty into prognosis and treatment decisions: a frail older adult may not survive or may be harmed by an intervention that would benefit a robust patient of the same age, and conversely may benefit from a less aggressive approach focused on function and comfort. Frailty is not fixed — it can be modified by treating reversible contributors (malnutrition, depression, polypharmacy, deconditioning) and by targeted exercise and nutrition — so identifying it opens therapeutic opportunities, not just prognostic information. Avoid both errors: do not withhold beneficial treatment solely on the basis of frailty or age (ageism), and do not subject a frail patient to high-burden interventions unlikely to benefit them (overtreatment).

### Reconcile Polypharmacy and Assess Each Drug for Appropriateness

Polypharmacy is near-universal in multimorbid older adults and is a leading cause of adverse events, falls, delirium, hospitalization, and death. Take a complete and accurate medication history — including prescribed, over-the-counter, herbal, and as-needed medications — and reconcile it against every source (patient, caregiver, pharmacy records, prior records), because discrepancies are common and dangerous. Assess each medication for appropriateness using a structured approach (such as deprescribing principles and inappropriate-medication lists for older adults): Is there a current indication? Does the benefit outweigh the harm in this individual, given their frailty, life expectancy, and goals? Is the dose adjusted for renal and hepatic function? Is there a duplication, an interaction, or a drug contributing to a geriatric syndrome (falls, delirium, incontinence, bleeding)? Be especially vigilant for high-risk drug classes in older adults: sedatives and hypnotics, anticholinergics, opioids, anticoagulants, hypoglycemics, and non-steroidal anti-inflammatories. Deprescribing is an active, positive intervention, not failure to treat — schedule reduction, monitor for withdrawal and recurrence, and document the rationale.

### Screen for the High-Yield Geriatric Syndromes and Social Vulnerabilities

The geriatric assessment is incomplete without screening for the syndromes that drive harm and that the standard history omits. Falls: ask about falls and near-falls in the past year, because a fall history predicts future falls and serious injury, and pursue multifactorial assessment (balance, gait, vision, medications, orthostatic hypotension, environmental hazards, foot problems). Delirium: screen in any acutely unwell or hospitalized older adult using a validated tool. Malnutrition: assess weight, appetite, and weight loss, because malnutrition predicts poor outcomes and is often unrecognized. Incontinence: ask directly, because it is common, treatable, and rarely volunteered, and it drives falls, isolation, and institutionalization. Sensory impairment: assess vision and hearing, because impairment drives falls, isolation, delirium, and misdiagnosis of cognitive impairment. Elder mistreatment: screen sensitively for financial, physical, psychological, and neglect abuse, because it is common, under-recognized, and carries high morbidity and mortality, and follow mandatory reporting obligations. Each of these syndromes is multifactorial and requires a multifactorial response, not a single diagnosis.

## Common Traps

### Attributing Acute Decline to Age or Chronic Disease

Dismissing new confusion, a fall, or functional decline as "just getting old" or "their dementia" without excluding a treatable acute cause. Counter by treating any acute change as a red flag and investigating for infection, metabolic disturbance, drug effect, and cardiovascular or neurological events.

### Expecting Classic Disease Presentations

Missing myocardial infarction without chest pain, infection without fever, or abdomen without guarding because the presentation is atypical. Counter by building the differential around atypical presentations and pursuing unexplained acute changes.

### Missing Delirium by Attributing Confusion to Dementia

Labeling acute fluctuating confusion as baseline dementia and failing to investigate the reversible cause. Counter by establishing the baseline with collateral history and treating acute confusion as delirium until proven otherwise.

### Omitting Functional, Cognitive, and Frailty Assessment

Focusing on the presenting organ and missing the function, cognition, and frailty that determine outcome. Counter by making structured functional, cognitive, and frailty assessment routine in every geriatric encounter.

### Failing to Reconcile and Deprescribe Polypharmacy

Accepting a long medication list without assessing each drug for ongoing indication, harm, and appropriateness. Counter by reconciling against all sources and actively deprescribing high-risk and inappropriate medications.

### Neglecting Falls, Incontinence, Sensory Impairment, and Elder Mistreatment

Treating high-yield geriatric syndromes as optional or social issues rather than central clinical risks. Counter by screening routinely for falls, delirium, malnutrition, incontinence, sensory impairment, and elder mistreatment.

## Self-Check

- Did I build the differential around atypical presentation, and did I treat any acute change (confusion, fall, functional decline, "not their usual self") as a red flag requiring investigation for a treatable acute cause?
- Did I take a structured functional history (basic and instrumental activities of daily living), establish the baseline, and pursue the cause of any functional decline?
- Did I screen cognition with a validated tool, distinguish delirium from dementia and depression, and investigate delirium as a medical emergency using collateral history for the baseline?
- Did I assess frailty with a validated approach and incorporate it into prognosis and the risk-benefit of treatment decisions, avoiding both ageism and overtreatment?
- Did I reconcile the complete medication history against all sources and assess each drug for ongoing indication, appropriateness, dose adjustment, and contribution to geriatric syndromes, with active deprescribing?
- Did I screen for the high-yield geriatric syndromes — falls, delirium, malnutrition, incontinence, sensory impairment — and for elder mistreatment, with multifactorial assessment and response?
- Did I integrate these domains into a coherent care plan centered on the patient's function, goals, and quality of life, rather than on a single disease?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
