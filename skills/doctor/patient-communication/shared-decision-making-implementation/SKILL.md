# Shared Decision Making Implementation

Knowing that shared decision making (SDM) matters is not the same as being able to do it in a fifteen-minute visit with the next patient already in the waiting room. The principles skill covers why SDM exists and what good SDM looks like; this skill covers the operational layer that determines whether SDM actually happens at the point of care or quietly collapses under time pressure. The recurring failure is that SDM is taught as an attitude and then expected to emerge spontaneously in encounters that have no structure, no tools, no team distribution, and no documentation. Clinicians who endorse SDM in principle routinely default to recommendation-and-consent in practice, not because they are hypocrites but because the workflow gives them no scaffold. A second failure is to assume that a single clinician must perform the entire SDM conversation alone and in one sitting, when in reality the highest-functioning systems distribute the work across the team, the visit, and the patient's own time at home. This skill covers the concrete mechanics: the three-talk model as a sequence that fits real visits, option grids and encounter decision aids as instruments that compress the conversation, the deliberate carving of time, documentation that both protects legally and demonstrates genuine shared decision, the delegation of SDM roles to nurses and pharmacists, and the measurement of decision quality and decisional conflict so that SDM can be improved rather than merely asserted. The discipline is to treat SDM as a workflow you engineer, not a virtue you hope to display.

## Core Rules

### Use the Three-Talk Model to Sequence the Conversation

The three-talk model (team talk, option talk, decision talk) gives SDM a defensible sequence that maps onto how real visits actually unfold, and it prevents the common error of jumping straight to a recommendation before the patient is even oriented to the fact that a decision exists. Team talk is the step most often skipped: before any options are presented, you explicitly tell the patient that a decision needs to be made, that there is more than one reasonable way forward, that you want their input, and that you will support whatever they choose. This reframes the encounter from "the doctor will tell me what to do" to "we have a choice to make together," and without it the rest of SDM collapses into informed consent theater. Option talk is where you lay out the reasonable options with their benefits and harms in plain, quantified terms, ideally using a visual aid. Decision talk is where you elicit the patient's preference, check for decisional conflict, and confirm the choice — and it is the step most often rushed, because by the time you reach it the clock is pressing. Treat the three talks as a scaffold, not a rigid script; a patient who interrupts option talk to state a clear preference has effectively collapsed the sequence, and forcing them back through it is its own failure.

### Deploy Option Grids and Encounter Decision Aids as Compression Tools

A well-designed option grid — a one-page table comparing the reasonable options across the questions patients actually ask (what is involved, the main benefits, the main harms, recovery, effect on daily life) — does more to enable genuine SDM in a short visit than any amount of verbal explanation, because it externalizes the comparison so the patient can see it, point to it, and take it home. The recurring error is to treat decision aids as optional luxuries for the unhurried clinic; in fact they are most valuable exactly where time is shortest, because they compress the option talk into a shared visual artifact that both parties can reference. Use validated, up-to-date option grids and encounter decision aids where they exist (many specialty societies and the Option Grid Collaborative maintain them), and check that the probabilities shown reflect current evidence and the relevant population. When no validated grid exists, construct a simple hand-drawn or printed comparison covering the two to four reasonable options and the patient-relevant outcomes, using absolute numbers rather than relative risk reductions. Hand the grid to the patient, let them read it, and watch where their attention lands — that is often where the real decision lives. Do not let the aid replace the conversation; use it to anchor one.

### Engineer Time for SDM Rather Than Hoping It Fits

SDM fails operationally when it is treated as something that should happen if there is time, because there is never time. Decide in advance which decisions in your practice are preference-sensitive enough to warrant a structured SDM conversation (screening with contested balance, surgery versus conservative management, anticoagulation in the frail elderly, treatment intensity at the end of life) and pre-load the workflow: send the decision aid home with the patient or through the portal before the visit, schedule a longer appointment slot for the decision visit, and use the team to front-load information so the clinician's time goes to the values conversation rather than the data dump. The single most effective operational move is to separate the information-giving from the decision-making across two contacts — the patient receives and absorbs the options at one point, and the decision is made at the next, after reflection. When a genuinely preference-sensitive decision surfaces unexpectedly in a time-pressured visit, name it explicitly ("This is a decision where what matters to you should drive the choice, and we should not rush it"), provide the decision aid, and book a dedicated follow-up rather than forcing a decision under time pressure that the patient will later regret or reverse.

### Document SDM So It Is Both Legally Defensible and Clinically Honest

Documentation is where SDM either becomes real or evaporates, because what is not written is assumed not to have happened, both in malpractice review and in quality measurement. A defensible SDM note records four things: that a preference-sensitive decision was identified, that the reasonable options and their benefits and harms were discussed (ideally with the decision aid used noted by name), that the patient's values and preferences were elicited and how they weighed, and the decision reached and the patient's agreement to it. Avoid the two failure modes: the checkbox note ("risks, benefits, and alternatives discussed, patient agrees") that documents nothing and satisfies no one, and the defensive note that lists every conceivable harm to the point of obscuring the actual conversation. Document decisional conflict if present and how it was addressed, and document any deferral of the decision and the plan to revisit it. Where the patient chose against the evidence-based recommendation, record their reasoning specifically, because this is the scenario most likely to be scrutinized later and the one where a clear values-based rationale protects both patient and clinician. Link the note to consent processes where the institution requires it, but do not mistake the consent form for the SDM record — they are distinct artifacts serving distinct purposes.

### Distribute SDM Across the Team Rather Than Concentrating It in the Physician

In high-functioning SDM, the conversation is not owned by a single clinician but distributed across roles, each contributing at the point where they add the most value. Nurses and medical assistants can introduce the decision and distribute the decision aid during rooming, plant the idea that a choice exists, and screen for decisional conflict using a brief tool. Pharmacists can conduct the option talk for medication decisions — anticoagulation choice, diabetes regimen selection, statin intensity — with a depth of benefit-harm detail and adherence counseling that the physician cannot match in a short visit. Care coordinators and patient navigators can support patients who need more time or who face barriers to participating. The physician's role then becomes the decision talk: eliciting values, offering a recommendation grounded in those values, and confirming the choice. For this distribution to work, the team must be aligned on which decisions trigger SDM, which tools to use, and how to hand off — otherwise the patient receives fragmented or contradictory information. Build the SDM pathway into standing orders, protocols, and EHR templates so the team acts consistently rather than improvising.

### Measure Decision Quality and Decisional Conflict to Know Whether SDM Worked

You cannot improve SDM you do not measure, and the absence of measurement is why so many SDM programs produce no demonstrable change. The two outcomes that matter most are decision quality — the extent to which the decision is informed and concordant with the patient's values — and decisional conflict, the patient's uncertainty about their choice. Use a brief validated instrument such as the Decisional Conflict Scale or the SURE test (a four-item screen) to detect patients who remain uncertain after the conversation, because unresolved decisional conflict predicts decision regret, decision reversal, and non-adherence. Track concordance between the chosen option and the patient's stated values as a marker of whether the SDM was genuine or performative. At the practice or system level, measure the proportion of preference-sensitive decisions accompanied by a documented SDM conversation and decision aid use, and audit a sample of notes for the four documentation elements. Treat patient-reported decision quality as the primary outcome, not process metrics, because it is possible to hit every process target while patients still leave uncertain and uninformed. Use the data to target the decisions, teams, or visit types where SDM is failing, rather than declaring victory based on adoption alone.

## Common Traps

### Skipping Team Talk and Jumping to a Recommendation

Launching into options or a recommendation before orienting the patient that a decision exists and that their input matters. Counter by opening every preference-sensitive encounter with an explicit team-talk statement that names the decision and invites participation.

### Treating Decision Aids as Optional Rather Than as Compression Tools

Reserving decision aids for unhurried visits, when they are most valuable precisely when time is short. Counter by making validated option grids a default, pre-visit handout for known preference-sensitive decisions and referencing them in the note.

### Forcing a Decision in a Time-Pressured Visit

Pressing the patient to choose during the same visit the options were first presented, which produces rushed and often regretted decisions. Counter by separating information-giving from decision-making across two contacts whenever a preference-sensitive decision surfaces unexpectedly.

### Documenting SDM as a Checkbox

Writing "risks, benefits, and alternatives discussed" without recording the options, the values, or the reasoning. Counter by documenting the four elements — decision identified, options and harms discussed with the aid named, values elicited, and the choice and agreement.

### Concentrating the Whole Conversation in the Physician

Assuming the physician must personally deliver all of team talk, option talk, and decision talk, which makes SDM impossible under time pressure. Counter by distributing roles across nursing, pharmacy, and navigation with aligned protocols and tools.

### Measuring Process Without Measuring Decision Quality

Tracking decision aid distribution and note checkboxes while never asking patients whether they feel informed and decided. Counter by using a brief decisional conflict screen on every preference-sensitive decision and tracking value-concordance as the primary outcome.

## Self-Check

- Did I open the encounter with a team-talk statement that named the decision, signaled that more than one reasonable option exists, and invited the patient's input before presenting any option?
- Did I use a validated option grid or encounter decision aid (or a constructed equivalent) as a shared visual artifact during option talk, and did I note which aid was used?
- For preference-sensitive decisions, did I engineer time by pre-loading information, scheduling a dedicated decision visit, or separating information-giving from the decision rather than forcing a choice under time pressure?
- Does my documentation record the four elements — the preference-sensitive decision identified, the options and benefits/harms discussed with the aid named, the patient's values and how they weighed, and the decision and the patient's agreement?
- Where the patient chose against the evidence-based option, did I record their specific values-based reasoning rather than a generic consent statement?
- Did I distribute the SDM work across the team — nursing for introduction and conflict screening, pharmacy for medication option talk, navigation for support — with aligned protocols rather than improvising?
- Did I screen for decisional conflict (e.g., SURE test or Decisional Conflict Scale) after the conversation, and do I track decision quality and value-concordance at the practice level rather than only process metrics?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
