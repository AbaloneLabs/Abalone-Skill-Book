# Health Coaching

Health coaching is the structured support of a patient's own movement toward health-related behavior change. It is not telling patients what to do — that is advice, and advice alone rarely changes behavior. The recurring clinical error is to conflate giving information with producing change: a patient who understands that smoking harms them, that exercise helps their diabetes, or that they must take their statin has not thereby changed. Behavior change is governed by motivation, confidence, perceived capability, competing demands, environment, and habit, none of which are moved by a stronger lecture. A second error is to set goals that belong to the clinician rather than the patient — "you need to lose twenty pounds" — which produces nominal agreement followed by no change and mutual frustration. This skill covers the disciplined use of motivational interviewing, collaborative goal-setting, confidence scaling, action planning, and follow-up to build genuine patient activation. The discipline is to meet the patient where they are, to evoke their own reasons for change rather than installing yours, to make goals small and specific and patient-owned, and to treat relapse as expected data rather than failure. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when complex psychological, psychiatric, or social barriers dominate, refer to qualified behavioral health professionals and multidisciplinary resources.

## Core Rules

### Separate Information From Behavior Change

Information is necessary but not sufficient. Before investing in more education, assess whether the barrier is knowledge, motivation, confidence, environment, or a clinical problem (depression, untreated pain, cognitive impairment). A patient with uncontrolled diabetes who knows the targets but cannot afford insulin does not need more teaching; they need cost relief. Match the intervention to the actual barrier. Resist the reflex to repeat instructions louder when adherence does not improve — that reflex signals the wrong diagnosis of the problem.

### Assess Readiness Before Pushing Change

Use a stage-of-change or readiness framework to locate the patient before selecting your strategy. A patient in precontemplation needs information and exploration of ambivalence, not an action plan; a patient in preparation needs help building a concrete plan; a patient in action needs reinforcement and troubleshooting; a patient in relapse needs normalization and re-engagement, not judgment. Pushing an action plan on someone not ready produces resistance and a fractured relationship. Ask directly: "On a scale of zero to ten, how important is this change to you right now?" and "How confident are you that you could do it if you decided to?" The answers tell you whether to work on importance, confidence, or planning.

### Evoke the Patient's Own Reasons (Motivational Interviewing)

Lasting change is built on the patient's own motivations, not the clinician's arguments. Use open questions, affirmations, reflective listening, and summaries to draw out what matters to this person — their grandchildren, their work, their dignity, their fear — and connect the change to those values. Avoid the "righting reflex," the urge to correct and persuade, which predictably generates sustain talk (arguments against change) and entrenches resistance. When a patient voices both change talk and sustain talk, amplify the change talk by reflecting it. Permission-based advice ("Would it be okay if I shared what has helped other patients?") preserves autonomy and reduces resistance.

### Set Goals That Are Small, Specific, and Patient-Owned

Vague clinician goals ("eat better," "exercise more," "lose weight") are not actionable and are not owned by the patient. Collaboratively set SMART goals — specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound — that the patient chooses and that fit their life. A goal of "walk for fifteen minutes after dinner on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday" is more powerful than "get more active." Start small enough to succeed; success builds confidence (self-efficacy), which predicts sustained change more than initial motivation does. Write the goal down, give the patient a copy, and schedule a specific follow-up to review it.

### Build an Action Plan With Implementation Intentions

Translate goals into concrete when-where-how plans. "I will take my metformin with breakfast at the kitchen table" is more likely to be executed than "I will remember my pills." Help the patient identify cues, anticipate barriers, and pre-commit to a specific response: "If it is raining and I cannot walk outside, I will do the video in the living room." Implementation intentions reduce the cognitive load of behavior change and substantially improve follow-through. Include a specific measure of progress the patient can track themselves.

### Use Confidence Scaling to Predict Success

Ask: "On a scale of zero to ten, how confident are you that you can do this?" A confidence below seven predicts failure; rather than proceeding, explore what would raise it. Lower the goal, remove a barrier, add support, or address a specific worry. Confidence scaling surfaces hidden obstacles the patient would not otherwise volunteer and prevents the false agreement of a plan destined to fail.

### Plan for Relapse as Expected, Not Exceptional

Behavior change is non-linear. Most patients who quit smoking, start exercising, or improve their diet relapse, often repeatedly, before sustained change. Frame relapse in advance as normal and as data: "Most people slip — when you do, it doesn't erase your progress; let's figure out what triggered it." A patient who expects relapse and has a recovery plan is far more likely to re-engage than one who interprets a slip as proof of failure. Avoid expressing disappointment or moralizing; the clinician's reaction to relapse strongly shapes whether the patient returns or hides.

### Structure Follow-Up to Sustain Change

A single coaching conversation rarely produces durable change. Build in follow-up — a phone call, a portal message, a scheduled visit, a peer or community health worker — to reinforce progress, troubleshoot barriers, and revise the plan. Accountability and continued contact are active ingredients. Connect the patient to resources beyond the visit: disease-specific programs, peer support, apps, community resources, and family or social networks. Sustained behavior change is a population of small contacts over time, not one heroic encounter.

### Address the Social and Environmental Determinants

Behavior change fails when the environment opposes it. A patient cannot eat well if they live in a food desert, exercise if their neighborhood is unsafe, or adhere to medications they cannot afford. Assess and address social determinants — income, housing, food security, transportation, social support, literacy — and refer to social work, community resources, or care coordination. Coaching a patient to change behavior they lack the resources to change is both ineffective and demoralizing.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Education With Change

Assuming that better understanding will produce behavior change, and repeating instructions when it does not. Counter by diagnosing the real barrier — motivation, confidence, cost, environment, mental health — and matching the intervention to it.

### Setting Clinician Goals the Patient Does Not Own

Prescribing goals the patient agrees to politely but has no intention or ability to pursue. Counter by using readiness and confidence scaling, eliciting the patient's own goals, and starting small enough to succeed.

### Inducing Sustain Talk by Arguing

Triggering the patient to defend their current behavior by confronting or persuading. Counter by using reflective listening, affirmations, and evoking the patient's own change talk rather than installing the clinician's reasons.

### Abandoning the Patient at Relapse

Treating a slip as failure or betrayal, which drives the patient away and confirms their worst self-assessment. Counter by normalizing relapse, exploring triggers, and revising the plan.

### Coaching Without Follow-Up

Delivering a powerful conversation and then leaving change unsupported. Counter by scheduling specific follow-up and connecting the patient to ongoing resources and accountability.

### Ignoring Social Determinants

Pushing behavior change on a patient whose environment makes it impossible. Counter by assessing and addressing cost, food, safety, transportation, and social support, and referring to appropriate resources.

## Self-Check

- Did I distinguish whether the barrier was knowledge, motivation, confidence, environment, or a clinical problem, and match the intervention to it?
- Did I assess the patient's readiness and confidence before pushing an action plan, using scaling questions?
- Did I use motivational interviewing to evoke the patient's own reasons for change rather than arguing for mine?
- Is the goal specific, measurable, achievable, patient-owned, and small enough to build confidence?
- Did I build an implementation intention (when, where, how) and a plan for anticipated barriers?
- Did I frame relapse as expected, with a recovery plan, and schedule structured follow-up?
- Did I assess and address social and environmental determinants, and refer to behavioral health or community resources where the barrier exceeds the scope of coaching?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when complex psychological, psychiatric, or social barriers dominate, seek qualified multidisciplinary support.
