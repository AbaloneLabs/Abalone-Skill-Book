# Shared Decision Making and Health Literacy

A treatment recommendation is not a decision. The decision belongs to the patient, and it is made at the intersection of the clinical evidence, the patient's values, and the patient's capacity to understand the first two. The recurring failure is to mistake informed consent — a signature on a form — for genuine shared decision making, or to assume that a patient who nods and signs has actually understood. Most patients leave medical encounters unable to recall the key risks and alternatives they were told, and a large minority have limited health literacy that they will hide out of shame. Clinicians consistently overestimate how much their patients understand, and they consistently underestimate how often their own jargon is incomprehensible. This skill covers the disciplined practice of shared decision making, the universal precautions approach to health literacy, the non-negotiable use of teach-back, the proper use of interpreters and decision aids, and knowing when a patient's comprehension, language, or support needs exceed what the encounter can provide and warrant patient navigation, education resources, or surrogate involvement. The discipline is to assume misunderstanding until proven otherwise, to verify understanding explicitly, and to treat the patient's values as the deciding vote on any preference-sensitive choice.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Preference-Sensitive from Effective Care, and Share the Decision Accordingly

Not every decision is a shared decision. Effective care — interventions where evidence shows clear benefit and little downside (antibiotics for pneumonia, insulin in DKA) — warrants a clear recommendation and consent, not a lengthy deliberation. Preference-sensitive care — where two or more reasonable options exist with different tradeoffs (screening thresholds, surgery versus watchful waiting, anticoagulation in frail elderly, end-of-life treatment intensity) — is where shared decision making is mandatory. Name the decision explicitly as a choice, present the reasonable options, and be honest that there is no single right answer. Avoid presenting your preferred option as the only one, and avoid the opposite failure of dumping an unfiltered list of options on a patient who wants your recommendation. Offer a recommendation grounded in the evidence while making clear the patient may choose differently based on their values, and ask what matters most to them in the decision.

### Practice Health Literacy Universal Precautions

You cannot reliably tell which patients have limited health literacy by appearance, education, or demeanor, and patients hide their difficulty out of shame. Therefore treat every patient as if they may have difficulty understanding — universal precautions. Use plain language: short sentences, common words, no medical jargon or acronyms (say "sugar" not "glucose," "blood clot" not "thromboembolism," "medicine by mouth" not "oral agent"). Organize information into the two or three most important points, because patients cannot retain a ten-item list. Speak slowly, pause frequently, and present information in small chunks. Use written and visual materials at a fifth- to sixth-grade reading level, and offer them as support, not as a substitute for verbal explanation. Do not equate a nod, a smile, or a signed form with understanding — these are the most common false reassurances in medicine. When you suspect limited literacy, do not single the patient out; the universal precautions approach protects their dignity while serving their need.

### Use Teach-Back to Verify Understanding Every Time

Teach-back is the single most effective tool against the illusion of comprehension, and it is underused because clinicians fear it sounds like a test. Frame it as checking your own performance, not the patient's: "I want to make sure I explained this clearly — can you tell me in your own words what we just discussed?" Ask the patient to explain their condition, the plan, the medications, and especially the warning signs that should bring them back. If they cannot teach it back accurately, do not blame them — re-explain in simpler terms and teach-back again until it lands, because the failure was your explanation, not their intelligence. Use the show-me method for devices and techniques (inhalers, injections, wound care) by having the patient demonstrate. Document that teach-back was used and what the patient understood. Teach-back is not optional for high-stakes instructions (discharge medications, warning signs, procedure prep); when understanding cannot be confirmed despite repeated attempts, escalate to nursing education, pharmacy counseling, or care coordination.

### Use Professional Interpreters and Decision Aids Correctly

For any patient with limited language proficiency, use a trained medical interpreter — in person, by video, or by phone — for every clinically meaningful encounter, and document it. Do not use family members, especially children, as interpreters: they omit, soften, mistranslate, and are traumatized by the content. Brief the interpreter before the conversation on the goals, and speak to the patient, not the interpreter, maintaining eye contact with the patient. Recognize that interpreter-mediated encounters take longer and require patience and explicit verification. For preference-sensitive decisions, use validated decision aids — structured tools that present the options, the probabilities of benefits and harms, and values clarification exercises. Decision aids improve knowledge, reduce decisional conflict, and produce choices more aligned with patient values, but they do not replace the conversation; they support it. Offer the aid, review it with the patient, and use it as a scaffold for discussion. When no validated aid exists, construct an analogous conversation: options, outcomes with realistic numbers, and what matters to the patient.

### Elicit Values and Goals Before Recommending an Option

A shared decision made without understanding the patient's values is not shared — it is a clinician's preference wearing a costume. Before presenting options in detail, ask what matters most to the patient: living as long as possible regardless of burden, avoiding suffering, preserving independence, being home, being there for a specific event, not being a burden to family. These values determine which option fits. A patient whose overriding goal is to remain independent at home will weigh a treatment that requires institutional rehabilitation very differently from one whose goal is maximum longevity. Ask about prior experiences — their own or family members' — with serious illness and medical care, because these powerfully shape preferences. Revisit values over time and across decisions, because goals change as illness progresses. When a patient cannot articulate values (cognitive impairment, acute illness), involve the surrogate or legally authorized representative and ground the decision in substituted judgment — what the patient would have wanted — not what the surrogate wants.

### Adapt for Sensory and Cognitive Impairment Without Infantilizing

Hearing impairment, vision impairment, and cognitive impairment each require specific communication adaptation, and each carries the risk of the clinician infantilizing the patient or assuming incapacity. For hearing impairment, face the patient, ensure working hearing aids, reduce background noise, and use written supplements or amplification devices; do not shout. For vision impairment, narrate what you are doing, orient the patient to the room, and provide materials in accessible formats. For cognitive impairment, simplify, repeat, involve caregivers, assess capacity specifically for the decision at hand, and recognize that capacity is decision-specific and fluctuating — a patient may lack capacity for a complex financial decision while retaining it for a simpler treatment choice. Never assume incapacity from age, diagnosis, or an uncooperative demeanor alone; assess it formally when in doubt. When capacity is in question or impaired, escalate to capacity assessment, ethics, or surrogate processes rather than proceeding on assumption.

## Common Traps

### Mistaking Consent for Shared Decision Making

Treating a signed form as evidence the patient understood and chose freely. Counter by using teach-back and explicit option-and-values discussion for preference-sensitive decisions.

### Assuming Understanding From a Nod or Smile

Accepting social cues of agreement as proof of comprehension. Counter by using teach-back on every high-stakes instruction and re-explaining until it is accurate.

### Using Family Members as Interpreters

Delegating interpretation to relatives, which distorts content and harms the family member. Counter by using a professional interpreter for every encounter with a language barrier and documenting it.

### Overwhelming the Patient With Information

Delivering a long list of risks and instructions that exceeds working memory. Counter by limiting to two or three key points, using plain language, and chunking information.

### Recommending Without Eliciting Values First

Presenting your preferred option before understanding what matters to the patient. Counter by asking about goals and prior experiences before walking through options.

### Equating Cognitive Diagnosis With Incapacity

Assuming a patient with dementia or delirium cannot make any decision. Counter by assessing capacity specifically for the decision at hand and escalating for formal assessment when uncertain.

## Self-Check

- For preference-sensitive decisions, did I name the decision as a choice, present the reasonable options, and offer a recommendation while inviting the patient to choose based on their values?
- Did I practice health literacy universal precautions — plain language, short sentences, no jargon, two to three key points, materials at an appropriate reading level?
- Did I use teach-back to verify understanding of the diagnosis, plan, medications, and warning signs, and re-explain until accurate?
- For any language barrier, did I use a professional interpreter (not a family member), speak to the patient, and document the interpreter's use?
- Did I use a validated decision aid or an equivalent structured conversation (options, realistic probabilities, values clarification) for preference-sensitive choices?
- Did I elicit the patient's goals and values before recommending, and revisit them across decisions as illness progressed?
- Did I adapt for sensory and cognitive impairment appropriately, and assess capacity decision-specifically rather than assuming it from diagnosis?
- When understanding, language, literacy, or support needs exceeded the encounter, did I escalate to patient navigation, education resources, pharmacy counseling, or surrogate involvement?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
