# End-of-Life Decisions: Advance Care Planning, Documentation, and Family Support

The operational work of end-of-life care — the conversations, the documents, the referrals, and the bedside support of a dying patient's family — is where good intentions most often fail to become good care. Advance care planning is widely endorsed and widely neglected; when it is done, it is frequently reduced to a checkbox ("Do you have an advance directive?") that produces a form no one can later find or interpret. Goals of care drift silently as illness progresses, because no one revisits the plan at the points where the trajectory changes. Hospice referral happens too late, after weeks of hospitalization that hospice was designed to prevent, because clinicians misread eligibility or fear the conversation. And families arrive at the bedside of a dying loved one unprepared, unsupported, and burdened with guilt that compounds their grief for years. This skill covers the operational layer of end-of-life care: how to conduct and document advance care planning so it actually travels with the patient, how to use the right instrument for the right purpose (advance directives versus physician orders like POLST or MOLST), how to align goals of care across the illness trajectory rather than only at crisis, how to make timely and well-framed hospice and palliative referrals, and how to support families through the active dying process. It complements — and does not replace — the ethical frameworks for withholding and withdrawing treatment, futility, and resource allocation; the focus here is the planning, documentation, and human support that turn those frameworks into practice. Because advance-directive and POLST law is jurisdiction-specific, confirm local statute and institutional policy, and involve palliative care and ethics early when the plan is contested or the family is in conflict.

## Core Rules

### Treat Advance Care Planning as a Process, Not a Document

Advance care planning is an ongoing series of conversations that help a person understand their likely future health trajectory, reflect on what matters to them, and record preferences so that care can align with their values if they lose the ability to speak for themselves. The recurring failure is to equate the planning with the completion of a form, which produces a document that is legally present but clinically useless. Begin early, in the context of serious illness, advancing age, or before high-risk surgery — not at the moment of crisis. Structure the conversation: explore the patient's understanding of their illness and prognosis, their values and what makes life worth living, their priorities if they became seriously ill (prolonging life at all costs, avoiding suffering, being at home, not burdening family), and whom they trust to make decisions. Use validated frameworks and conversation guides rather than improvising. Revisit the plan at defined trigger points — a new diagnosis, a hospitalization, functional decline, a change in prognosis — because preferences shift as the illness trajectory changes. Document not only the resulting orders and directives but the values and reasoning behind them, because a surrogate and a future clinical team will need to interpret the plan in circumstances the patient did not specifically anticipate.

### Distinguish Advance Directives From Physician Orders, and Use Each Correctly

The most common operational error in end-of-life documentation is confusing two categories of instruments that have different legal force, different scopes, and different purposes. An advance directive (living will, healthcare power of attorney, personal directive) is completed by the patient while they have capacity; it appoints a surrogate and expresses the patient's preferences for future care, and it guides decisions when the patient loses capacity. It is not a medical order and does not itself direct treatment in real time. A physician order for life-sustaining treatment (POLST, MOLST, POST, or similar, named by jurisdiction) is a medical order, signed by a clinician (and often the patient or surrogate), that translates the patient's goals into concrete, immediately actionable orders: resuscitation status (full code or DNAR), level of medical intervention (full treatment, selective treatment, comfort-focused only), and sometimes artificial nutrition and antibiotics. POLST travels with the patient across settings — home, ambulance, emergency department, hospital, nursing facility — and is meant to be honored as a current order, unlike an advance directive which must be interpreted. Use both: the advance directive captures values and appoints the surrogate; the POLST captures the actionable orders. Keep the POLST current, because it reflects the patient's goals at a point in time and must be revised as the trajectory changes. Know which instrument your jurisdiction recognizes and its exact legal requirements, because these vary and a form that is invalid in one state may be honored in another.

### Align Goals of Care at Every Inflection of the Illness Trajectory

Goals of care are not set once; they must be realigned at each point where the clinical reality changes, because a plan made at diagnosis rarely fits the patient at the stage of progressive decline. Identify the trajectory the patient is on — a terminal decline, a steady worsening of chronic illness, a sudden acute event with uncertain recovery — and time the conversation to the trajectory. At each inflection (a new metastasis, a third hospitalization in a month, a transfer to the ICU, loss of the ability to perform basic activities, a failed time-limited trial), pause and ask whether the current plan still matches the patient's goals and the realistic prognosis. Share prognostic information honestly and with empathy, using ranges rather than false precision, and acknowledge uncertainty. Align treatment options with the stated goals: if the overriding goal is to be home and avoid suffering, then a plan of repeated hospitalizations and ICU care is not goal-concordant and should be named as such. When the goals and the prognosis diverge, make the divergence explicit and help the patient and family choose a coherent path rather than drifting into default aggressive care by inertia. Document the realignment and update the POLST and care plan accordingly.

### Make Hospice and Palliative Referrals Early and Frame Them Honestly

Hospice and palliative care are underused because they are referred too late, misframed as "giving up," or withheld until the patient is imminently dying — which denies them the benefit they were designed to provide. Understand the eligibility and purpose of each. Palliative care is appropriate at any stage of serious illness alongside disease-directed treatment and focuses on symptom relief, quality of life, and alignment of care with goals; it is not contingent on a prognosis. Hospice is a benefit for patients whose prognosis is limited (commonly six months or less, if the disease runs its expected course) who elect a focus on comfort rather than curative treatment. Refer to hospice when the patient meets prognostic eligibility and shares a comfort-focused goal — not only in the final days, when there is little time for hospice to help. Frame the referral honestly: hospice is a model of added support, not abandonment, and it does not mean stopping all care — it means redirecting care toward comfort, dignity, and support at home or in a homelike setting wherever possible. Address the common fears (that hospice hastens death, that it means the team has given up, that it requires giving up the doctor) directly, because these fears drive late referral. When prognosis is uncertain, use a hospice consult to help assess eligibility rather than assuming the patient is "not ready."

### Support the Family Through the Active Dying Process

The dying process is a clinical event for the patient and a defining life event for the family, and the family's experience shapes their bereavement for years. Prepare the family for what active dying looks like: decreased oral intake, changes in breathing pattern (including the death rattle and Cheyne-Stokes respirations), skin changes, decreased urine output, restlessness, and the typical timeline. Reassure them that these are expected features of the dying process, not emergencies or signs of suffering, and explain what comfort measures are being provided. Manage symptoms proactively — pain, dyspnea, agitation, secretions, nausea — and explain each intervention so the family sees comfort being prioritized. Create an environment that allows presence: flexible visiting, room for family, attention to cultural and religious practices, and opportunities for rituals and farewells. Give the family meaningful roles (holding a hand, moistening the mouth, playing music) because action reduces helplessness. Anticipate and name the family's grief and guilt, and offer bereavement support and follow-up. Avoid both false reassurance and brutal honesty delivered without compassion; the family needs truth delivered with care and repeated as needed, because they will not retain everything in distress.

### Ensure the Plan Travels With the Patient Across Settings

Advance care plans fail most often at transitions — the patient with a DNAR at home who is resuscitated by EMS who never saw the order, the POLST that does not transfer with the nursing-home resident, the advance directive locked in a primary-care chart while the patient lies intubated in an ICU. Make the plan portable and visible: ensure the POLST is in the chart, at the bedside, and — for home patients — physically accessible to first responders; register it in any available electronic or state registry; and communicate it explicitly at every handoff and transfer. Verify that receiving facilities and EMS systems in the jurisdiction recognize the form being used. When the patient moves between settings, confirm that the orders and directives transfer and are reconfirmed, not assumed. A plan that cannot be found at the moment it matters is, in practice, no plan at all.

## Common Traps

### Reducing Advance Care Planning to a Checkbox

Asking only whether a directive exists, without exploring values, prognosis, and goals. Counter by conducting a structured values conversation and documenting the reasoning, not merely the resulting form.

### Confusing Advance Directives With Physician Orders

Treating a living will as an order that directs real-time care, or expecting a POLST to appoint a surrogate. Counter by using each instrument for its purpose: directive for values and surrogate, POLST for actionable orders.

### Failing to Realign Goals as the Trajectory Changes

Letting an old plan govern a changed clinical reality through inertia. Counter by revisiting goals at every inflection and updating the POLST and care plan to match.

### Referring to Hospice Only in the Final Days

Denying patients weeks to months of hospice benefit by waiting until death is imminent. Counter by assessing prognostic eligibility early, using hospice consults when prognosis is uncertain, and framing hospice as added support rather than giving up.

### Framing Comfort Care as Abandonment

Letting the family equate a shift to comfort-focused care with the team giving up. Counter by explaining that comfort care is active, symptom-focused care that prioritizes dignity and presence.

### Losing the Plan at Transitions of Care

Allowing a DNAR or POLST to vanish during transfer so EMS or the receiving team acts without it. Counter by making the plan portable and visible, registering it where possible, and explicitly reconfirming it at every handoff.

### Leaving the Family Unprepared at the Bedside

Letting the family witness the dying process without explanation, compounding grief with shock and guilt. Counter by preparing them for what to expect, giving them roles, managing symptoms visibly, and offering bereavement support.

## Self-Check

- Have I treated advance care planning as an ongoing process of conversation and documentation, not merely a form, and recorded the patient's values and reasoning alongside any directives?
- Have I distinguished advance directives (values and surrogate) from physician orders (POLST or MOLST, actionable across settings), used each for its correct purpose, and confirmed my jurisdiction's recognized instruments and requirements?
- Have I realigned goals of care at each inflection of the illness trajectory, shared honest prognostic information, and updated the POLST and care plan when goals and prognosis diverged?
- Have I assessed hospice and palliative eligibility early, referred before the final days when possible, and framed the referral honestly as added comfort-focused support rather than abandonment?
- Have I prepared the family for the active dying process, managed symptoms proactively, given them meaningful roles, and offered bereavement support?
- Have I ensured the plan is portable and visible — in the chart, at the bedside, accessible to first responders, registered where available, and explicitly reconfirmed at every transition?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
