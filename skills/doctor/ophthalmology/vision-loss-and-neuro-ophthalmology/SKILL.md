# Vision Loss and Neuro-Ophthalmology

Vision loss spans an unusually wide differential, from trivial refractive change to an intracranial mass or an impending stroke, and the cost of misclassification is high: a missed giant cell arteritis blinds the second eye within days, a missed amaurosis fugax foretells a completed stroke, and a missed intracranial lesion allows a treatable mass to grow. The recurring failure in vision-loss evaluation is treating it as a single symptom to be referred for routine eye testing rather than localizing the lesion along the visual pathway and asking whether the timeline is an emergency. This skill covers the localization of vision loss, transient visual loss and amaurosis fugax, diplopia, optic disc swelling versus pseudopapilledema, visual field defects, and giant cell arteritis as a must-not-miss cause.

## Core Rules

### Localize Vision Loss Along the Visual Pathway Before Ordering Tests

Approach every vision-loss complaint by localizing the lesion, because localization drives both the differential and the urgency. Determine first whether the loss is monocular or binocular, and whether it is transient or persistent. Monocular vision loss localizes to the eye or optic nerve; binocular vision loss that respects the vertical midline localizes to the chiasm or retrochiasmal pathway. A bitemporal hemianopia points to a chiasmal lesion, classically a pituitary adenoma; a homonymous hemianopia points to a lesion behind the chiasm in the optic tract, radiation, or occipital cortex. Central scotoma suggests optic nerve or macular disease; altitudinal defects suggest optic nerve or vascular causes. Forcing this localization before imaging prevents ordering the wrong test and missing the right diagnosis.

### Treat Sudden Painless Persistent Vision Loss as an Emergency

Sudden, persistent vision loss in one eye is an emergency until proven otherwise. The major causes are retinal artery occlusion (central retinal artery occlusion), retinal vein occlusion, optic neuropathy including non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy, and vitreous hemorrhage. Central retinal artery occlusion is the ocular equivalent of a stroke: a pale retina with a cherry-red spot at the macula, and a narrow window of hours for intervention. Any patient with sudden profound monocular vision loss needs immediate ophthalmology assessment and evaluation for giant cell arteritis and embolic sources; sending them for a routine outpatient appointment sacrifices irrecoverable retina or optic nerve.

### Evaluate Transient Monocular Vision Loss (Amaurosis Fugax) as a Stroke Warning

Amaurosis fugax — a transient, painless monocular vision loss often described as a curtain descending — is retinal transient ischemic attack and carries the same stroke risk as a hemispheric TIA. It is most often embolic from carotid disease or a cardiac source. The patient must be evaluated urgently with carotid imaging (duplex ultrasound, with CT or MR angiography as needed), cardiac workup including ECG and echocardiography, and stroke-risk reduction. Do not reassure the patient that the episode resolved; the resolution is the point, because it identifies a high-risk vascular event that demands secondary prevention to avoid a completed stroke.

### Differentiate Diplopia by Monocular Versus Binocular and Cranial Nerve Versus Restrictive

For double vision, first establish whether it persists when one eye is closed (monocular diplopia, usually refractive, lens, or retinal) or resolves when one eye is closed (binocular diplopia, a misalignment). Binocular diplopia localizes to cranial nerve palsies (third, fourth, or sixth), restrictive disease (thyroid eye disease, trauma), myasthenia gravis, or internuclear ophthalmoplegia. A third-nerve palsy with pupillary involvement is a compressive lesion, classically a posterior communicating artery aneurysm, until proven otherwise and is a neurologic emergency. Painful third-nerve palsy raises aneurysm or microvascular ischemia; sixth-nerve palsy can be benign or reflect raised intracranial pressure. Variability and fatigability through the day raises myasthenia gravis, which can mimic any ocular motor palsy. Always check the pupils and assess fatigability before attributing diplopia to a "simple" nerve palsy.

### Separate True Papilledema From Pseudopapilledema and Grade It Correctly

A swollen optic disc has a broad differential, and overdiagnosing papilledema exposes patients to unnecessary imaging and procedures, while underdiagnosing it misses raised intracranial pressure. Pseudopapilledema (optic disc drusen, anomalous discs) lacks the hyperemia, vessel obscuration, hemorrhages, and exudates of true edema and is best confirmed on ultrasound or OCT. True papilledema is bilateral disc swelling from raised intracranial pressure and demands urgent neuroimaging and consideration of venous sinus thrombosis, mass lesion, or idiopathic intracranial hypertension. Unilateral disc swelling points instead to optic neuritis, ischemic optic neuropathy, or compressive lesion. Distinguish optic neuritis (young patient, pain on eye movement, recovery over weeks, possible multiple sclerosis) from ischemic optic neuropathy (older patient, painless, altitudinal defect, poor recovery, association with giant cell arteritis).

### Consider Giant Cell Arteritis in Every Older Patient With Vision Change or Headache

Giant cell arteritis is the diagnosis that must not be missed in any patient over 50 with new headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, visual change, or unexplained elevated inflammatory markers, because anterior ischemic optic neuropathy from arteritis can blind the second eye within days. Ask specifically about jaw claudication (pain on prolonged chewing), new headache, polymyalgia rheumatica symptoms, and visual symptoms. Check an erythrocyte sedimentation rate and C-reactive protein immediately; a markedly elevated CRP supports the diagnosis. If giant cell arteritis is suspected, start high-dose glucocorticoids immediately — do not wait for temporal artery biopsy, which remains useful for up to two weeks after starting steroids — and arrange urgent ophthalmology and rheumatology input and biopsy within days.

### Use Visual Field Testing to Localize and to Detect Chronic Disease

Formal visual field testing (confrontation at the bedside, formal perimetry in clinic) confirms and localizes defects and detects asymptomatic disease such as early glaucomatous field loss or a slowly enlarging pituitary lesion. A new homonymous defect requires neuroimaging; a bitemporal defect requires imaging of the sella; arcuate and nasal step defects suggest glaucoma. Do not rely on confrontation alone for subtle defects, and arrange formal perimetry when the complaint or examination suggests field loss.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring a Patient With Transient Vision Loss Because It Resolved

Amaurosis fugax that resolved fully is still a retinal TIA and a stroke warning, not a benign event. Counter by evaluating for carotid and cardiac embolic sources urgently and initiating stroke-risk reduction.

### Treating Sudden Painless Vision Loss as a Routine Referral

Central retinal artery occlusion and optic neuropathies have narrow treatment windows; a routine outpatient appointment loses irrecoverable tissue. Counter by triaging sudden persistent vision loss as an emergency and arranging same-day ophthalmology assessment.

### Missing a Pupil-Involving Third-Nerve Palsy as a Compressive Aneurysm

A third-nerve palsy with a dilated pupil, ptosis, and diplopia is a posterior communicating artery aneurysm until proven otherwise. Counter by checking the pupil in every diplopia patient and obtaining urgent neuroimaging (CT angiography) when the pupil is involved.

### Overdiagnosing Papilledema From Disc Drusen

Calling pseudopapilledema papilledema leads to unnecessary MRI, lumbar puncture, and anxiety. Counter by looking for the features of true edema (hyperemia, vessel obscuration, hemorrhages) and confirming drusen with ultrasound or OCT before escalating.

### Missing Giant Cell Arteritis in an Older Patient With Nonspecific Symptoms

Attributing headache, fatigue, or transient blur to tension, infection, or aging in a patient over 50 can blind the second eye within days. Counter by asking about jaw claudication and polymyalgia, checking ESR and CRP, and starting steroids immediately when giant cell arteritis is suspected.

### Attributing Progressive Vision Loss to Cataract Without Examination

Cataract is common and easily blamed, but progressive vision loss may be a chiasmal lesion, glaucoma, or macular disease. Counter by examining the patient and localizing the deficit before assuming a benign cause.

### Forgetting Myasthenia Gravis in Variable Diplopia or Ptosis

Diplopia or ptosis that varies through the day, fatigues, or spares the pupil is myasthenia until excluded, and can mimic any ocular motor palsy. Counter by testing fatigability and considering edrophonium or serologic and electromyographic evaluation with neurology.

## Self-Check

- Did I localize the vision loss (monocular versus binocular, central versus field, pathway level) before ordering tests or imaging?
- For sudden persistent monocular vision loss, did I arrange same-day ophthalmology assessment and consider retinal vascular occlusion and optic neuropathy?
- For transient monocular vision loss, did I evaluate for amaurosis fugax as a TIA with carotid and cardiac workup?
- In any diplopia, did I distinguish monocular from binocular, check the pupils, and consider a pupil-involving third-nerve palsy as an aneurysm emergency?
- For disc swelling, did I distinguish papilledema from pseudopapilledema and unilateral from bilateral, and arrange appropriate imaging?
- For any patient over 50 with new headache, jaw claudication, or visual change, did I check ESR and CRP and start steroids for suspected giant cell arteritis without waiting for biopsy?
- Did I use formal visual field testing to localize defects and detect chronic disease such as glaucoma or chiasmal lesions?
- Did I consider myasthenia gravis in variable or fatigable diplopia or ptosis with a normal pupil?
