# Ocular Trauma and Foreign Body Management

Eye trauma is frightening for patients and deceptively dangerous for clinicians because the visible injury often underestimates the damage, and the wrong maneuver (pressing, patching, or probing) can convert a salvageable globe into a blind eye. The recurring failure is treating an injured eye as a soft-tissue wound rather than as a potentially ruptured globe, and applying generic wound principles — pressure, exploration, irrigation — that are catastrophic inside the orbit. This skill covers trauma triage, foreign body removal, hyphema, suspected globe rupture, orbital blow-out fracture, lid laceration, the imaging decision, and the mandatory ophthalmology referral criteria.

## Core Rules

### Triage Every Eye Injury for Globe Rupture First, Before Any Manipulation

The first question in any eye trauma is whether the globe is intact, because a ruptured globe changes everything. Suspect globe rupture with high-velocity injury (metalworking, hammering), sharp penetrating injury, a history of the eye "giving way," significant periorbital injury, an irregular or teardrop pupil, a shallow or flat anterior chamber, hyphema, prolapsed dark tissue (uvea) at the wound, a soft globe, or severe vision loss. If globe rupture is suspected, do not press, palpate, manipulate, probe, or patch the eye with pressure; place a rigid shield (a plastic or metal shield that does not touch the globe) to protect it, keep the patient nil by mouth in case of surgery, give antiemetics and analgesia to prevent vomiting and agitation that raise intraocular pressure, and arrange immediate ophthalmology referral. The single worst action is applying a pressure patch to a suspected rupture, which extrudes intraocular contents.

### Remove Superficial Foreign Bodies Safely and Know When to Stop

For a suspected corneal or conjunctival foreign body, take a precise history of the mechanism (grinding, hammering, outdoor wind), check visual acuity, and examine with good light and magnification, everting the upper eyelid to inspect the tarsal conjunctiva where foreign bodies often hide. A superficial conjunctival foreign body can be removed with a moistened cotton swab or fine forceps under topical anesthesia; a corneal foreign body may be removed with a needle or spud at the slit lamp by a clinician trained in the technique. Stop and refer if the object is deeply embedded, if it is a likely intraocular foreign body (high-velocity metal), if there is a rust ring, a large epithelial defect, or any suspicion of globe penetration. After removal, treat the abrasion, check tetanus status, prescribe antibiotic prophylaxis, and arrange follow-up for healing; never patch a contact lens-related abrasion because of infection risk.

### Manage Hyphema With Rest, Shielding, and Strict Activity Restriction

Hyphema — blood in the anterior chamber — ranges from microscopic (seen only on slit lamp) to a total "eight-ball" hyphema. It results from blunt trauma tearing iris or ciliary body vessels. Management aims to prevent rebleeding (which usually occurs within the first several days and is worse than the initial bleed) and secondary glaucoma, and to protect the eye. Keep the patient at rest with the head elevated, place a protective shield, restrict activity, avoid aspirin and NSAIDs, control nausea and vomiting, and monitor intraocular pressure daily. Indications for urgent ophthalmology referral and often admission include large hyphema, sickle cell trait or disease (high risk of optic nerve ischemia from elevated pressure), elevated intraocular pressure, a child, or risk of amblyopia. Remind the patient that rebleeding can worsen vision dramatically and that activity restriction is mandatory.

### Recognize Orbital Blow-Out Fracture and Its Functional Consequences

A blow to the orbit can fracture the thin floor or medial wall, often without fracturing the orbital rim, entrapping orbital fat or the inferior or medial rectus muscle. Suspect a blow-out fracture with periorbital ecchymosis, subcutaneous emphysema (worsened by nose blowing), enophthalmos, diplopia on upgaze or lateral gaze from muscle entrapment, infraorbital nerve numbness, and restricted eye movements. Obtain CT of the orbits for suspected fracture, muscle entrapment, or significant diplopia. Surgical repair, when indicated, is typically delayed one to two weeks to allow swelling to settle, except in children with a "trapdoor" fracture and the oculocardiac reflex (bradycardia, nausea), which is a surgical emergency. Advise the patient not to blow the nose to avoid forcing air into the orbit.

### Evaluate Lid Lacerations for Canalicular, Deep, and Penetrating Injury

Not all lid lacerations are simple skin wounds. Examine every lid laceration for involvement of the lid margin (which needs precise repair to avoid notching), the medial canthus and canalicular system (lacerations medial to the punctum can tear the canaliculus and need specialist repair within days to preserve tear drainage), the levator aponeurosis (causing ptosis), the orbital septum, or the globe itself. Penetrating lid injuries can hide an underlying globe or intracranial injury. Refer lid margin, canalicular, deep, or complex lacerations to ophthalmology or oculoplastics; do not attempt primary closure of a canalicular laceration without specialist input, because delayed repair compromises outcome.

### Use Imaging Selectively and Choose the Right Modality

Imaging is indicated when globe rupture, intraocular foreign body, orbital fracture, muscle entrapment, or intracranial injury is suspected, and when examination is limited by swelling. CT is the modality of choice for trauma: it detects fractures, radiopaque foreign bodies, and acute blood, and is fast. MRI is contraindicated when a metallic intraocular foreign body is possible, because it can move the metal and blind the eye; exclude a metallic foreign body with CT or a careful history before MRI. Ultrasound is generally avoided when globe rupture is suspected because pressure on the globe can extrude contents. Match the imaging to the suspected injury and never let imaging delay the protection of a suspected ruptured globe.

### Define Mandatory Ophthalmology Referral Criteria and Act on Them

Certain findings mandate urgent ophthalmology referral rather than outpatient follow-up: suspected globe rupture or intraocular foreign body, hyphema (especially large or with sickle cell disease), significant vision loss, an afferent pupillary defect, a teardrop or irregular pupil, shallow anterior chamber, laceration involving the lid margin or canaliculus, suspected orbital fracture with diplopia or entrapment, chemical injury (after irrigation), and any injury where examination is limited and serious injury cannot be excluded. State the referral threshold explicitly, document the time, and transfer the patient with the eye shielded and protected.

## Common Traps

### Applying a Pressure Patch or Palpating a Suspected Ruptured Globe

Pressure on a ruptured globe extrudes intraocular contents and converts a salvageable eye into a blind one. Counter by shielding without pressure and avoiding palpation whenever rupture is suspected, and by using a rigid shield that does not touch the globe.

### Missing an Intraocular Foreign Body Behind a Small Entry Wound

A high-velocity metal fragment can enter through a tiny, self-sealing wound with minimal external signs and devastating internal damage. Counter by taking a careful mechanism history (grinding, hammering, metal-on-metal), checking for a teardrop pupil or shallow chamber, and obtaining CT when an intraocular foreign body is possible.

### Removing a Deeply Embedded Foreign Body at the Bedside

Attempting to dig out a deeply embedded corneal foreign body or rust ring without slit-lamp control can scar the cornea or perforate the globe. Counter by removing only clearly superficial foreign bodies and referring embedded or rust-ring cases to ophthalmology.

### Sending a Hyphema Patient Home With Activity Advice Only

Hyphema can rebleed and cause secondary glaucoma and optic nerve damage, especially in children and sickle cell patients. Counter by enforcing rest, head elevation, shielding, activity restriction, avoidance of NSAIDs and aspirin, and close follow-up or admission for high-risk cases.

### Missing a Canalicular or Lid Margin Laceration

A laceration medial to the punctum can tear the canaliculus and cause lifelong tearing if not repaired within days; a lid margin laceration causes notching if not aligned precisely. Counter by inspecting the medial canthus and lid margin in every lid laceration and referring canalicular and lid margin injuries for specialist repair.

### Forgetting the Trapdoor Fracture With Oculocardiac Reflex in Children

A pediatric trapdoor orbital fracture with muscle entrapment can cause the oculocardiac reflex (bradycardia, nausea, syncope) and is a surgical emergency, not a delayed repair. Counter by recognizing diplopia with restricted gaze and bradycardia in a child after trauma and arranging urgent surgical repair.

### Choosing MRI Before Excluding a Metallic Foreign Body

MRI can move a retained metallic intraocular foreign body and blind the eye. Counter by excluding a metallic foreign body with CT or a careful mechanism history before any MRI of the head or orbits.

## Self-Check

- For every eye injury, did I first assess for globe rupture and, if suspected, shield without pressure, keep the patient nil by mouth, and arrange immediate ophthalmology referral?
- Did I take a mechanism history for high-velocity injury and consider an intraocular foreign body, using CT rather than MRI when metal is possible?
- For a foreign body, did I evert the upper lid, remove only superficial objects, and refer embedded, rust-ring, or penetrating cases?
- For hyphema, did I enforce rest, head elevation, shielding, activity restriction, avoidance of NSAIDs and aspirin, and arrange close follow-up or admission for high-risk patients?
- Did I obtain CT orbits for suspected blow-out fracture, muscle entrapment, or significant diplopia, and recognize the trapdoor fracture with oculocardiac reflex in children as an emergency?
- Did I inspect every lid laceration for lid margin and canalicular involvement and refer these for specialist repair?
- Did I apply the mandatory ophthalmology referral criteria (rupture, intraocular foreign body, hyphema, vision loss, afferent pupillary defect, complex lid injury) and document the referral time?
- Did I avoid pressure patching a contact lens-related abrasion because of infection risk?
- This skill provides educational information only and does not constitute medical advice. Always consult qualified healthcare professionals for clinical decisions.
