# Red Eye and Ocular Emergencies

The red eye is one of the most common presentations in primary care and emergency settings, and it is also one of the most dangerous to triage casually. Most red eyes are benign viral or allergic conjunctivitis that resolve without intervention, and clinicians who see these daily can be lulled into reassuring the next patient who actually has acute angle-closure glaucoma, a corneal ulcer, or scleritis with imminent vision loss. The recurring failure is not ignorance that these diagnoses exist — it is skipping the few discriminating questions and examinations (pain, vision, photophobia, pupil, intraocular pressure) that separate a trivial condition from an emergency. This skill covers the structured red-eye evaluation, the must-not-miss sight-threatening causes, chemical injury management, and the disciplined thresholds for urgent ophthalmology referral.

## Core Rules

### Always Separate the Red Eye by Pain, Vision, and Pupil Before Reaching for a Label

Before diagnosing conjunctivitis, force an explicit triage on three axes: is there true eye pain (not just grittiness), is vision reduced or distorted, and is the pupil abnormal? Benign conjunctivitis causes grittiness or itch, normal vision, a normal pupil, and a diffuse conjunctival injection that is typically bilateral. The presence of any of the following moves the case out of the conjunctivitis bucket and mandates escalation or further evaluation: severe or deep aching pain, true photophobia, reduced visual acuity, a unilateral red eye, a fixed or mid-dilated pupil, a hypopyon, or a cloudy cornea. Writing these findings down before naming the diagnosis prevents the most common error — labeling a dangerous unilateral red eye as "conjunctivitis" because it is red and watery.

### Recognize Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma as a Hours-Critical Emergency

Acute angle-closure glaucoma presents with severe eye pain, headache, nausea and vomiting, blurred vision with haloes around lights, a mid-dilated and fixed pupil, a steamy or cloudy cornea, and a shallow anterior chamber, often in a hyperopic older patient triggered by dim light or mydriatics. The intraocular pressure is markedly elevated. This is an emergency where hours matter: untreated, the optic nerve is permanently damaged. Do not send the patient home with analgesia and antiemetics as a migraine or gastroenteritis — the eye examination (mid-dilated fixed pupil, corneal edema, hard eye to palpation) is the discriminator. Measure intraocular pressure if available, give the appropriate acute medications (topical and systemic aqueous suppressants, hyperosmotic agent as needed), and arrange immediate ophthalmology referral.

### Distinguish Anterior Uveitis (Iritis) From Conjunctivitis by Pain, Photophobia, and Pupil

Anterior uveitis causes a unilateral red eye with deep aching pain, marked photophobia, blurred vision, ciliary flush (injection most intense at the limbus), and often a small, irregular pupil from synechiae; cells and flare are present in the anterior chamber on slit lamp. It is frequently mislabeled as conjunctivitis because the eye is red. The keys are the pain and photophobia out of proportion to the redness, the ciliary rather than diffuse pattern of injection, and the reduced vision. Ask about associated systemic disease (spondyloarthropathies, inflammatory bowel disease, sarcoidosis, infections such as tuberculosis or syphilis, and HLA-B27 conditions). Do not prescribe topical steroids without ophthalmology assessment, because an undiagnosed herpetic keratitis will be made dramatically worse by steroids.

### Treat Infectious Keratitis and Contact Lens Red Eye as an Emergency

A red eye in a contact lens wearer, especially with pain, photophobia, discharge, or a corneal opacity, is a corneal ulcer until proven otherwise and is a sight-threatening emergency. Pseudomonas and other pathogens can perforate the cornea within 24 hours. Stop contact lens wear immediately, do not patch, remove the lens if still in place, and arrange same-day ophthalmology slit-lamp evaluation with corneal scraping for culture before starting intensive topical antibiotics. Never prescribe topical steroids for a contact lens red eye. Any patient with a corneal epithelial defect and infiltrate, a hypopyon, or a history of trauma with a dirty foreign body is at risk of infectious keratitis and needs urgent specialist evaluation.

### Recognize Scleritis and Episcleritis and the Systemic Implications of Scleritis

Episcleritis is mild, self-limited, and nontender; scleritis is severe, boring pain that radiates and wakes the patient, with deep red or violaceous injection that does not blanch with phenylephrine, tenderness to palpation, and often reduced vision. Scleritis is commonly associated with systemic autoimmune disease — rheumatoid arthritis, granulomatosis with polyangiitis, and other vasculitides — and can cause permanent structural damage to the eye. Differentiate the two by the pain quality, tenderness, blanching response, and involvement of the deeper vascular plexus. Episcleritis can be managed conservatively; scleritis mandates urgent ophthalmology assessment and systemic workup for vasculitis, because the eye finding may be the first manifestation of a life-threatening systemic disease.

### Manage Chemical Injuries by Immediate, Prolonged Irrigation Before Anything Else

A chemical exposure to the eye is the one ocular emergency where treatment must begin before history or examination: irrigate immediately and copiously. The severity of an alkali burn (lye, ammonia, cement) is greater than acid because alkali penetrates and saponifies tissues, and the prognosis depends on the time to irrigation, not the time to the emergency department. Irrigate with saline or Ringer's lactate for at least 15 to 30 minutes, everting the eyelids and sweeping the fornices to remove particulate matter, and continue until the pH (tested with litmus at the fornix after stopping) is neutral and stable. Only after irrigation and pain control should you assess visual acuity, perform a full examination, and refer urgently to ophthalmology. Do not attempt neutralization with weak acids or bases — irrigation with plain solution is what works.

### Use the History of Trauma, Surgery, and Systemic Infection to Risk-Stratify for Endophthalmitis

Endophthalmitis — severe intraocular infection — presents with pain, marked vision loss, hypopyon, and severe inflammation, classically after recent eye surgery, trauma, or bacteremia. Any patient with a red eye and vision loss in the days to weeks after cataract or intraocular surgery, after a penetrating injury, or in the setting of systemic sepsis has endophthalmitis until proven otherwise and needs immediate ophthalmology referral for intraocular sampling and intravitreal antibiotics. Time is vision; delay for outpatient workup is inappropriate.

## Common Traps

### Diagnosing Conjunctivitis on a Unilateral Red Eye Without Checking Vision

The single most dangerous reflex is to label a red, watery eye as conjunctivitis. Unilateral redness, especially with pain or reduced vision, is almost never simple conjunctivitis and should prompt evaluation for keratitis, uveitis, glaucoma, or foreign body. Counter by checking visual acuity and pupil on every red eye and by treating any unilateral red eye with pain or vision loss as not-conjunctivitis until proven otherwise.

### Missing Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma Behind Vomiting and Headache

Because angle-closure glaucoma causes nausea, vomiting, and headache, it is misdiagnosed as migraine, gastroenteritis, or even a stroke, especially when the eye is not examined. Counter by examining the pupils and palpating the globe in any older patient with headache and vomiting, and by measuring intraocular pressure when available.

### Prescribing Topical Steroids for an Undiagnosed Red Eye

Topical steroids relieve inflammation but can catastrophically worsen herpetic keratitis, fungal keratitis, and undiagnosed infection, and can precipitate glaucoma and cataract. Counter by never prescribing topical steroids without a slit-lamp diagnosis and ophthalmology input, and by always considering herpes simplex in any unilateral red eye with a dendritic or epithelial defect.

### Reassuring a Contact Lens Wearer With a Red Eye

A contact lens wearer with a red, painful eye may have a corneal ulcer that can perforate within a day. Counter by treating every contact lens red eye as infectious keratitis until slit-lamp examination excludes an ulcer, removing the lens, and arranging same-day ophthalmology review.

### Sending a Chemical Injury for Evaluation Before Irrigating

Waiting to irrigate until arrival at a clinic, or pausing to take a history, multiplies tissue damage in alkali burns. Counter by starting copious irrigation at the scene, before transport, and continuing until pH is neutral.

### Dismissing Scleritis as Episcleritis

Scleritis causes deep boring pain, tenderness, and reduced vision, and it signals systemic vasculitis; episcleritis is mild and self-limited. Counter by testing for tenderness, blanching with phenylephrine, and visual change, and by escalating scleritis for systemic autoimmune workup.

### Giving Antibiotic Drops and "Review if Not Better" for a Dangerous Eye

A delayed review plan is appropriate for low-risk conjunctivitis but dangerous for uveitis, keratitis, glaucoma, or endophthalmitis, where hours to days determine outcome. Counter by setting a same-day or next-day ophthalmology review for any red eye with pain, vision loss, pupil abnormality, or systemic red flags, and by not delegating the review to a phone call.

## Self-Check

- For every red eye, did I explicitly record pain, photophobia, visual acuity, pupil size and reactivity, pattern of injection, and presence of discharge or hypopyon before naming a diagnosis?
- Did I treat a unilateral red eye with pain or vision loss as not-conjunctivitis and arrange slit-lamp or ophthalmology evaluation?
- In the older patient with headache, vomiting, and a mid-dilated fixed pupil, did I consider and test for acute angle-closure glaucoma with intraocular pressure?
- For any contact lens wearer with a red, painful eye, did I stop lens wear, avoid steroids and patching, and arrange same-day ophthalmology review for possible keratitis?
- For a chemical exposure, did I begin copious irrigation immediately and continue until the fornix pH was neutral before detailed examination?
- Did I avoid prescribing topical steroids in any undiagnosed red eye, particularly when herpetic keratitis is possible?
- For suspected scleritis, did I assess for systemic autoimmune or vasculitic disease and arrange urgent ophthalmology review?
- For postoperative, post-traumatic, or septic patients with a painful eye and vision loss, did I consider endophthalmitis and arrange immediate ophthalmology referral?
