# Polypharmacy and Deprescribing

Polypharmacy is the accumulation of medications beyond what is clinically justified, and it is one of the most underrecognized sources of harm in modern medicine, especially in older adults and those with chronic disease. The recurring failure is the assumption that more drugs equal better care. Each additional medication raises the probability of adverse effects, interactions, falls, cognitive impairment, hospitalization, and non-adherence, and the marginal benefit of the fifth or tenth drug is often far smaller than its marginal harm. Deprescribing — the planned, supervised reduction or cessation of inappropriate medications — is a clinical skill in its own right, and it is where agents most often default to inertia, adding drugs indefinitely and never asking whether any should stop. This skill covers recognizing problematic polypharmacy, structured deprescribing, tapering high-risk drugs safely, and the judgment to slow down when withdrawal risk is real.

## Core Rules

### Quantify and Characterize the Medication Burden

Polypharmacy cannot be managed if it is not measured. At every encounter, build the complete medication list — prescription, over-the-counter, herbal, and as-needed — and count both the total number and the number of high-risk classes (anticholinergics, sedatives, opioids, anticoagulants, antihypertensives, hypoglycemics, NSAIDs, PPIs). Apply validated tools: the Beers Criteria and STOPP/START for potentially inappropriate medications in older adults, anticholinergic burden scales, and drug-burden indices. Characterize each drug by whether it has an active indication, whether the indication is still present, whether the benefit exceeds the harm given the patient's current goals and life expectancy, and whether it is part of a prescribing cascade. A list that is never interrogated is a list that only grows.

### Identify and Interrupt Prescribing Cascades

A prescribing cascade occurs when an adverse effect of one drug is misinterpreted as a new condition and treated with another drug, which then causes its own effects. Classic cascades include NSAID-induced hypertension treated with antihypertensives, diuretic-induced gout treated with allopurinol, antipsychotic-induced parkinsonism treated with antiparkinsonian drugs, PPI-induced hypomagnesemia or B12 deficiency treated with supplements, and statin-related muscle symptoms masked rather than addressed. For every new symptom in a heavily medicated patient, ask first: could this be a drug effect? Interrupting a cascade by reducing or stopping the offending drug is almost always safer and more effective than adding another prescription to suppress its symptom.

### Apply Explicit Criteria to Find Inappropriate Therapy

Use Beers, STOPP/START, and local guidance as systematic screens for drugs that are rarely appropriate in older adults or in specific clinical contexts: long-acting benzodiazepines and Z-drugs for sleep, first-generation antihistamines, certain muscle relaxants, anticholinergic antimuscarinics for overactive bladder in cognitively vulnerable patients, long-term NSAIDs in those with renal, cardiac, or gastrointestinal risk, long-term PPIs without an ongoing indication, and tight glycemic control targets in frail patients where hypoglycemia is the greater danger. These tools do not replace clinical judgment, but they force a structured review that catches drugs inertia would otherwise keep. For each flagged drug, decide: stop, taper, substitute, or continue with justification — and document the decision.

### Prioritize Deprescribing by Risk, Benefit, and Patient Goal

Not every inappropriate drug should be stopped at once. Prioritize using three axes: how much harm the drug is causing now, how little benefit it provides given current goals and prognosis, and how easy or risky it is to stop. Drugs causing active harm (falls from sedatives, hypoglycemia from overtight glycemic control, bleeding from unnecessary anticoagulants, renal decline from NSAIDs) take precedence. Drugs with marginal benefit and meaningful risk in limited life expectancy (statins for primary prevention in advanced frailty, tight blood pressure targets in the bedbound) follow. Layer the patient's goals: a drug that prevents a long-term event may be deprioritized when the patient's priority is function and cognition today. Sequence the plan so the highest-value changes happen first and the patient is not overwhelmed.

### Taper High-Risk Drugs Structurally

Some drugs cannot be stopped abruptly without serious withdrawal or rebound. Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs require gradual tapering to avoid withdrawal seizures, rebound insomnia, and anxiety; opioids require slow reduction to avoid withdrawal and uncontrolled pain; PPIs require stepping down to avoid rebound hyperacidity; certain antidepressants, antipsychotics, anticonvulsants, clonidine, and corticosteroids require scheduled reductions with monitoring. Use a documented taper plan with dose steps, intervals, and review points, and adjust based on the patient's response. For drugs where abrupt cessation is dangerous, never stop without a taper plan. Engage pharmacy for complex tapers, especially in patients on multiple high-risk agents.

### Monitor for Withdrawal and Rebound After Each Change

Deprescribing is an active intervention with its own adverse-effect profile. After each reduction, monitor for withdrawal symptoms (anxiety, insomnia, autonomic signs, pain recurrence, rebound hyperacidity, mood change) and for return of the original condition the drug was treating. Schedule a defined review point after each step, give the patient clear guidance on what to expect and what to report, and have a plan to pause or reverse the taper if tolerance fails. A deprescribing plan without monitoring is as dangerous as no plan at all, because unmonitored withdrawal erodes trust and leads to relapse and re-initiation at higher doses.

### Involve the Patient and Align With Goals of Care

Deprescribing succeeds only with the patient's understanding and agreement. Explain in plain language why a drug is being reduced, what the expected benefits are, what to expect during the taper, and what the alternatives are — including the alternative of continuing if the patient prefers. Address fears (fear of returning symptoms, fear of "giving up," misunderstanding that stopping a drug means giving up on care). Frame deprescribing as active, positive care — reducing harm and improving function — rather than as withdrawal of treatment. In patients with changing goals of care (advanced age, progressive disease, transition to comfort-focused care), align the medication regimen explicitly with the new goals, which often means substantial deprescribing of preventive therapies.

### Know When to Slow Down or Not Deprescribe

Not every candidate drug should be deprescribed, and not every moment is the right one. Slow down when the drug has a strong ongoing indication with clear benefit (anticoagulation for a high-CHADS-VASc patient, disease-modifying therapy for an active autoimmune condition, antiretrovirals, antiepileptics controlling seizures, immunosuppressants protecting a transplant), when the patient is acutely unstable, when withdrawal risk is high and support is limited, or when the patient does not want to change. Deprescribing is a judgment, not a mandate to minimize drugs at all costs. The goal is the right medications for this patient at this time — sometimes that is many drugs, sometimes few.

## Common Traps

### Assuming More Drugs Mean Better Care

Never questioning the growing list because each drug was "indicated" when started. Counter by quantifying burden and interrogating every drug for current indication and benefit-harm balance.

### Missing the Prescribing Cascade

Treating a drug adverse effect as a new disease and adding another prescription. Counter by asking, for every new symptom in a medicated patient, whether it could be a drug effect, and stopping the cause rather than suppressing the symptom.

### Skipping Structured Criteria

Reviewing the list by gestalt instead of applying Beers, STOPP/START, and anticholinergic burden tools. Counter by running an explicit criteria screen at every medication review.

### Stopping High-Risk Drugs Abruptly

Ceasing benzodiazepines, opioids, PPIs, antidepressants, or corticosteroids without a taper. Counter by using a documented taper plan with steps, intervals, and review points, and engaging pharmacy for complex cases.

### Deprescribing Without Monitoring

Reducing a drug and not scheduling review for withdrawal or recurrence. Counter by defining a review point after each step and giving the patient clear guidance on what to report.

### Imposing Deprescribing Against Patient Goals

Reducing drugs the patient values without explanation or agreement. Counter by framing deprescribing as positive care, addressing fears, and aligning the regimen with the patient's goals.

### Deprescribing Indiscriminately in Unstable Patients

Cutting drugs during acute illness or in patients with strong ongoing indications. Counter by recognizing when not to deprescribe and prioritizing stability over minimization.

## Self-Check

- Have I built the complete medication list and quantified both total burden and high-risk class burden using validated tools?
- For each new symptom, have I asked whether it could be a drug effect and considered interrupting a prescribing cascade rather than adding another prescription?
- Have I applied Beers, STOPP/START, and anticholinergic burden criteria systematically to identify inappropriate therapy?
- Have I prioritized deprescribing by current harm, marginal benefit, goals of care, and ease of stopping, sequencing the highest-value changes first?
- For high-risk drugs (benzodiazepines, opioids, PPIs, antidepressants, corticosteroids), have I written a documented taper plan with steps, intervals, and review points?
- Have I scheduled monitoring for withdrawal and rebound after each change and given the patient clear guidance on what to report?
- Have I involved the patient, framed deprescribing as positive care, and aligned the regimen with current goals of care?
- Have I recognized when to slow down or not deprescribe — strong ongoing indications, acute instability, high withdrawal risk, or patient preference?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
