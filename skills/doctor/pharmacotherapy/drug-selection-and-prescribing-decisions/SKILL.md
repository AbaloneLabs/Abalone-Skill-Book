# Drug Selection and Prescribing Decisions

Prescribing is the most common intervention in medicine and one of the most common sources of preventable harm. The recurring failure is not ignorance of drug names; it is casual selection — reaching for a familiar drug without checking whether it is the right first-line agent for this condition, whether it is appropriate for this patient's physiology, whether it interacts with what they already take, and whether the prescription is clear enough to be carried out safely. A prescription is a high-stakes instruction with downstream consequences that can extend for years, and it deserves the same rigor as any other clinical decision. This skill covers disciplined drug selection, individualization, the practical constraints that determine whether a therapy actually works, and the judgment to consult a pharmacist or specialist when the choice is non-routine.

## Core Rules

### Start With the Evidence-Based First-Line Therapy

Begin every prescribing decision from current guideline-endorsed first-line therapy for the specific condition and patient profile, not from habit or the last drug used. Guidelines (disease-specific society guidelines, national formularies, local protocols) encode the evidence on efficacy, safety, and comparative effectiveness and should be the default starting point. Document the diagnosis that justifies the drug and the guideline source. Deviate from first-line only with a clear reason — prior intolerance, contraindication, drug interaction, pregnancy, severe renal or hepatic impairment, formulary restriction, or patient preference — and record that reason. Reaching for a second- or third-line agent by reflex exposes patients to more harm, more cost, and less evidence without justification.

### Individualize to the Patient's Physiology

A correct drug at a wrong or un-individualized dose is a prescribing error. Before finalizing any prescription, gather the parameters that change the choice or dose: renal function (eGFR and, where relevant, estimated creatinine clearance), hepatic function and synthetic function, age and frailty, weight (and where relevant, ideal or adjusted body weight), volume status, concomitant organ failure, and genotype or phenotype where it matters (for example, CYP2C19 or CYP2D6 for clopidogrel and certain antidepressants, HLA-B*5701 for abacavir, TPMT for thiopurines). State explicitly how each parameter modified the choice or dose. In the elderly, the frail, and the renally or hepatically impaired, the default is to start lower and titrate more slowly — see the dose-adjustment skill for the detailed mechanics.

### Screen Systematically for Drug Interactions and Contraindications

Every new prescription must be checked against the patient's complete medication list — prescription, over-the-counter, herbal, and recreational — for interactions and duplications. Look for the high-yield categories: QT-prolonging combinations, serotonergic combinations risking serotonin syndrome, anticholinergic burden in the elderly, anticoagulant or antiplatelet combinations raising bleeding risk, CYP inducers and inhibitors that change levels of narrow-therapeutic-index drugs (warfarin, tacrolimus, phenytoin, digoxin), and renally cleared drug combinations that compound nephrotoxicity (NSAIDs with ACE inhibitors and diuretics). Use a drug interaction checker as a tool, not a substitute for clinical judgment — most flagged interactions are minor; the task is to recognize the serious ones. Verify absolute contraindications (pregnancy for teratogens, severe renal impairment for renally cleared drugs with no alternative, prior life-threatening allergy) and document allergy and prior adverse drug reaction history before prescribing.

### Consider Formulary, Cost, and Adherence From the Outset

A perfectly chosen drug the patient cannot obtain or will not take is a failed prescription. Check formulary status, coverage, and out-of-pocket cost; a generic first-line agent is almost always preferable to a branded equivalent when efficacy is similar. Choose the simplest regimen that achieves the goal — once-daily over twice-daily, combination tablets where they reduce pill burden, routes of administration the patient can actually use. Ask about adherence barriers (cost, side effects, complexity, beliefs, memory, dexterity) and design around them. A drug that requires rigid timing, frequent monitoring, or lifestyle restrictions the patient cannot meet will fail regardless of its pharmacology. When cost or access is a barrier, involve pharmacy or social resources before the patient leaves.

### Write an Unambiguous, Complete Prescription

A prescription must be clear enough to be carried out exactly as intended. Specify the drug (preferably by generic name), indication, dose, route, frequency, duration or stop condition, quantity to dispense, and any monitoring or review date. Avoid error-prone abbreviations, trailing zeros, and vague instructions such as "take as directed." For high-risk drugs (anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, chemotherapy, immunosuppressants), specify the indication, the monitoring plan, the patient education provided, and the review point. State the planned review or de-prescribing trigger explicitly so that the drug does not become a permanent prescription by inertia. Every prescription should answer: what, why, how much, how often, for how long, how monitored, and when reviewed.

### Reconcile and De-Prescribe at Every Transition

Prescribing is not a one-time act; the cumulative medication list grows over time and across encounters unless actively managed. At every transition (admission, discharge, clinic visit), reconcile the medication list: confirm what the patient actually takes, identify duplications, interactions, drugs no longer indicated, and drugs added to treat side effects of other drugs (the prescribing cascade). Actively de-prescribe drugs that have completed their course, have no ongoing indication, are causing harm, or whose risks now exceed benefits in a changed clinical context (especially in the elderly and those with changing goals of care). Document the rationale for each change and communicate it to the patient and the next clinician.

### Know When to Consult a Pharmacist or Specialist

Many prescribing decisions are routine; some are not. Involve clinical pharmacy for complex regimens, renal or hepatic dosing uncertainty, narrow-therapeutic-index drugs requiring monitoring, significant interactions, transitions of care, and patient education on high-risk drugs. Involve the relevant specialist when first-line therapy has failed or is contraindicated, when the diagnosis is uncertain, when initiation of a high-risk or specialized drug (biologic, chemotherapy, immunosuppressant) is being considered, or when the patient has multi-system disease that complicates the choice. Asking early is cheaper and safer than managing an adverse event.

## Common Traps

### Reaching for a Familiar Drug Without Checking First-Line Status

Prescribing from habit rather than current evidence. Counter by starting from guideline-endorsed first-line therapy and documenting any deviation.

### Failing to Individualize the Dose

Using standard dosing in renal, hepatic, elderly, low-weight, or obese patients. Counter by gathering the relevant parameters before finalizing and adjusting dose and interval explicitly.

### Missing Serious Drug Interactions

Adding a drug without checking the full medication list for QT, serotonergic, anticholinergic, bleeding, or CYP-mediated interactions. Counter by running a focused interaction check on every new prescription and prioritizing the serious categories.

### Ignoring Cost, Formulary, and Adherence

Choosing a regimen the patient cannot afford or sustain. Counter by checking cost and formulary up front and designing the simplest regimen the patient can actually follow.

### Writing Vague or Error-Prone Prescriptions

Using ambiguous instructions, dangerous abbreviations, or omitting duration and monitoring. Counter by specifying drug, indication, dose, route, frequency, duration, quantity, and monitoring on every prescription.

### Letting the Medication List Grow by Inertia

Never reconciling or de-prescribing, so drugs accumulate and interact over time. Counter by reconciling at every transition and actively de-prescribing drugs without ongoing indication.

### Prescribing in Isolation for Complex Cases

Failing to consult pharmacy or specialist for non-routine choices. Counter by escalating early when dosing, interactions, diagnosis, or high-risk drug initiation is involved.

## Self-Check

- Have I started from guideline-endorsed first-line therapy for this condition and patient, and documented any deviation and its reason?
- Have I gathered and explicitly applied renal function, hepatic function, age, frailty, weight, and relevant genotype or phenotype to the choice and dose?
- Have I checked the complete medication list for serious interactions and contraindications, prioritizing QT, serotonergic, anticholinergic, bleeding, and CYP categories?
- Have I considered formulary status, cost, regimen complexity, and adherence barriers, and chosen the simplest effective regimen the patient can sustain?
- Is the prescription unambiguous — drug, indication, dose, route, frequency, duration or stop condition, quantity, and monitoring — and free of error-prone abbreviations?
- Have I reconciled the full medication list at this transition and actively de-prescribed drugs without ongoing indication?
- Have I involved a pharmacist or specialist where the choice is non-routine, high-risk, or complicated by multi-system disease?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
