# Dose Adjustment for Special Populations

Standard adult dosing is calibrated for a typical adult with normal organ function, and applying it unchanged to patients at the extremes of physiology is a leading cause of preventable drug harm. The recurring failure is not ignorance that dose adjustment exists; it is overconfidence in the wrong estimate. Clinicians reach for a single eGFR number, or assume "normal for age," and miss that the estimate is unreliable in the elderly, in low muscle mass, in acute kidney injury, in dialysis, and in critical illness where volume of distribution, protein binding, and clearance all shift unpredictably. Dose adjustment in special populations is a judgment task that combines the right estimate, the right pharmacology, and the right monitoring — and the humility to escalate to pharmacy when the situation is non-routine. This skill covers the mechanics and the judgment of dosing across the populations where standard dosing fails.

## Core Rules

### Use the Right Renal Estimate for the Right Drug

Renal dose adjustment depends on which estimate the drug's labeling and pharmacokinetics were built on. Most older drug labels and many references use estimated creatinine clearance from the Cockcroft-Gault equation, while modern lab systems report eGFR from MDRD or CKD-EPI equations normalized to body surface area. The two are not interchangeable, especially at the extremes of body weight, in the elderly, and in acute kidney injury. For drugs with narrow therapeutic index and renally cleared kinetics (vancomycin, many beta-lactams, digoxin, gabapentin, pregabalin, DOACs in some ranges, metformin), use the estimate the labeling and your local guidance specify, and where they diverge or the patient is atypical, treat the estimate as uncertain and rely on therapeutic drug monitoring. Never dose a high-risk renally cleared drug from a single static eGFR in an unstable patient.

### Recognize the Limits of Renal Estimates in Acute and Extremes States

Creatinine-based estimates fail precisely when the stakes are highest. In acute kidney injury, serum creatinine lags the actual GFR, so the current value reflects yesterday's function, not today's — dose for the trajectory, not the number, and re-check frequently. In the elderly and in low-muscle-mass patients (frail, cirrhotic, amputee, cachectic), creatinine underestimates renal impairment because less creatinine is produced, so a "normal" creatinine can mask serious dysfunction. In dialysis patients, residual renal function, dialysis modality, and timing relative to the session all change dosing. In critical illness, capillary leak, fluid resuscitation, and changing hemodynamics alter volume of distribution and clearance hour to hour. In all of these, treat the estimate as a starting point, monitor drug effect and levels, and adjust dynamically.

### Adjust for Hepatic Impairment by Function, Not Just Diagnosis

Hepatic dose adjustment is less formulaic than renal because liver function is not captured by a single number. Use synthetic function markers (albumin, INR, bilirubin) and validated severity scores (Child-Pugh for chronic disease, and where relevant, scores for acute liver failure) to stratify impairment, and consult drug-specific labeling for the recommended adjustment. Drugs that are heavily hepatically cleared, highly protein bound, or active as parent compounds (many benzodiazepines, opioids, statins, some anticoagulants) accumulate and precipitate encephalopathy, bleeding, or hypotension in impaired livers. Prefer drugs that are renally cleared or have safer hepatic profiles when available. Avoid sedatives and those that precipitate encephalopathy in cirrhosis. When hepatic function is borderline or changing, start low, titrate slowly, and monitor.

### Apply Geriatric Pharmacology Principles: Start Low, Go Slow

Aging changes pharmacokinetics (reduced renal clearance, increased volume of distribution for lipophilic drugs, altered protein binding) and pharmacodynamics (increased sensitivity to sedatives, anticoagulants, antihypertensives, anticholinergics). Apply the Beers Criteria and STOPP/START frameworks to identify potentially inappropriate medications in older adults. Default to "start low, go slow" — begin at the low end of the adult range, titrate in small increments with adequate observation, and reassess at every change. Watch for anticholinergic and sedative burden, orthostatic hypotension, hypoglycemia, falls, and bleeding. Frailty amplifies risk beyond chronological age, so dose the patient in front of you, not the age on the chart.

### Use Weight-Based Dosing Correctly in Pediatrics and Obesity

In pediatrics, dosing is weight-based (mg/kg) and sometimes age- or surface-area-based, and the wrong weight or unit is a common and dangerous error. Confirm the weight in the correct units, use weight-based dosing within age-appropriate ranges, and cap doses at adult maxima where relevant. In obesity, total body weight, ideal body weight, and adjusted body weight give different results for different drugs depending on lipophilicity; use drug-specific guidance (for example, certain antibiotics and anticoagulants have obesity-specific dosing) rather than assuming. Verify pediatric doses against a current reference and have a second check for high-risk drugs. Never use household spoons for liquid medications — specify oral dosing syringes in milliliters.

### Assess Pregnancy and Lactation Safety Deliberately

In pregnancy, weigh teratogenic and fetal risk against the consequence of untreated maternal disease — both undertreatment and overtreatment harm mother and fetus. Use current pregnancy and lactation safety resources rather than outdated letter categories, prefer drugs with the longest safety track record for the condition, and avoid known teratogens (for example, ACE inhibitors and ARBs, warfarin, isotretinoin, valproate where avoidable, certain antiepileptics and immunosuppressants) unless no alternative exists and the indication is compelling. In lactation, most drugs are compatible with breastfeeding; assess infant dose, age, and clinical status, and prefer drugs with established lactation safety data. Document the risk-benefit discussion and the patient's preference.

### Use Therapeutic Drug Monitoring for Narrow-Index Drugs

For narrow-therapeutic-index drugs (vancomycin, aminoglycosides, digoxin, warfarin via INR, antiepileptics, tacrolimus and other immunosuppressants, lithium), dose is only the starting point; the target is the level or effect. Draw levels at the correct time relative to the dose (trough, peak, or random per the assay), interpret against the clinical context and the target range, and adjust using validated methods or pharmacy support. Recognize that targets shift with indication (for example, different vancomycin targets for different infections) and that monitoring frequency must increase in unstable renal function, dialysis, critical illness, and at the start of therapy or after dose changes. Unmonitored narrow-index drugs in unstable patients are a predictable source of toxicity and failure.

### Escalate to Pharmacy in Complex or Unstable Situations

When the patient has multi-organ failure, acute kidney injury with changing creatinine, dialysis with residual function, critical illness with shifting volume of distribution, hepatic impairment with borderline synthetic function, or a regimen of narrow-index drugs, the right dose is not a number you can safely derive alone. Involve clinical pharmacy early and explicitly. State the clinical question (target level, dose for this clearance, interaction management, transition between routes), document the recommendation, and re-evaluate as the patient changes. Escalation is a sign of rigor, not uncertainty.

## Common Traps

### Dosing From a Single eGFR in an Unstable Patient

Treating a static eGFR as truth in acute kidney injury, critical illness, or dialysis. Counter by dosing for the trajectory, re-checking frequently, and relying on levels for narrow-index drugs.

### Confusing Cockcroft-Gault and MDRD/CKD-EPI Estimates

Using whichever estimate the lab reports regardless of what the drug labeling requires. Counter by using the estimate the labeling and local guidance specify and treating divergence as uncertainty.

### Missing Renal Impairment in the Elderly and Low-Muscle-Mass Patients

Accepting a "normal" creatinine in a frail or cachectic patient. Counter by recognizing that low muscle mass underestimates creatinine and adjusting dose for the likely true clearance.

### Overdosing Sedatives and Anticholinergics in Older Adults

Applying standard adult dosing of benzodiazepines, opioids, antihistamines, or antimuscarinics to the elderly. Counter by applying Beers and STOPP/START and starting low and going slow.

### Using the Wrong Weight or Unit in Pediatrics and Obesity

Dosing on the wrong weight (total versus ideal versus adjusted) or the wrong unit. Counter by confirming weight and units, using drug-specific obesity guidance, and applying a second check for high-risk drugs.

### Defaulting to Drug Letter Categories in Pregnancy and Lactation

Relying on outdated pregnancy letter categories instead of current resources. Counter by using current safety references, weighing untreated disease, and documenting the discussion.

### Skipping Therapeutic Drug Monitoring for Narrow-Index Drugs

Initiating vancomycin, aminoglycosides, antiepileptics, or immunosuppressants without levels at the right time. Counter by drawing levels correctly, interpreting against target, and increasing frequency in unstable patients.

### Prescribing Complex Dosing Alone

Deriving doses for multi-organ failure, dialysis, or critical illness without pharmacy input. Counter by escalating to clinical pharmacy early and documenting the recommendation.

## Self-Check

- Have I used the renal estimate the drug labeling and local guidance specify, and treated divergence between Cockcroft-Gault and MDRD/CKD-EPI as uncertainty?
- In acute kidney injury, dialysis, critical illness, or low-muscle-mass states, have I dosed for the trajectory rather than a single static number, and increased monitoring frequency?
- For hepatically cleared or highly protein-bound drugs, have I stratified impairment with synthetic markers and severity scores and preferred safer alternatives where possible?
- For older adults, have I applied Beers and STOPP/START, started low and titrated slowly, and assessed anticholinergic, sedative, and fall risk?
- For pediatric and obese patients, have I confirmed the correct weight and units, used weight- or surface-area-based dosing within age-appropriate ranges, and applied drug-specific obesity guidance?
- In pregnancy and lactation, have I used current safety resources rather than outdated categories, weighed untreated disease, and documented the risk-benefit discussion?
- For narrow-therapeutic-index drugs, have I drawn levels at the correct time, interpreted against indication-specific targets, and increased monitoring in unstable patients?
- For complex or unstable situations, have I escalated to clinical pharmacy, documented the recommendation, and planned re-evaluation as the patient changes?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
