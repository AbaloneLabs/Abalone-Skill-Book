# Falls and Immobility in Older Adults

A fall in an older adult is too often dismissed as an inevitable consequence of aging, and that dismissal is where the harm begins. A fall is a symptom with causes, a powerful predictor of further falls, fracture, loss of independence, and death, and — when assessed properly — a preventable event in a large proportion of cases. The recurring failure is the single-cause workup: attributing the fall to "the rug," "low blood pressure," or "a funny turn," treating that one factor, and discharging the patient to fall again. Immobility, the downstream consequence of fear of falling and deconditioning, then completes a spiral toward dependency. The judgment problem is to treat every fall as a geriatric syndrome requiring a multifactorial assessment, to distinguish syncope from other falls, to address the modifiable contributors in combination, to decide on rehabilitation honestly, and to balance injury prevention against the harms of over-restriction. This skill covers multifactorial falls assessment, syncope workup, orthostatic hypotension, medication review, rehabilitation candidacy, injury prevention, and escalation thresholds.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Fall as a Multifactorial Syndrome

A fall in an older adult is rarely caused by one thing. It is the product of interacting intrinsic factors (comorbidity, balance, strength, cognition, vision, continence, footwear), extrinsic factors (medications, environment), and circulatory factors (orthostatic hypotension, arrhythmia, syncope). Conduct a multifactorial falls assessment that covers all of these, not a single-cause workup. Take a detailed history of the fall itself — the circumstances, position, prodrome, witnesses, injuries, and any loss of consciousness — because the description is the single most useful diagnostic clue. A fall with a clear prodrome of lightheadedness on standing points to orthostatic hypotension; a sudden loss of consciousness without warning points to arrhythmia or seizure; a mechanical trip points to balance, vision, and environment. Document the history verbatim where possible and let it direct the workup.

### Distinguish Syncope from Mechanical Falls

The most important diagnostic fork in falls assessment is whether there was loss of consciousness. Syncope — transient loss of consciousness with loss of postural tone and spontaneous recovery — is a circulatory or neurological event with a specific differential (orthostatic hypotension, vasovagal, carotid sinus hypersensitivity, arrhythmia, structural cardiac disease, seizure mimicking syncope) and a higher risk of recurrence and death than a mechanical fall. Establish from the patient and witnesses whether consciousness was lost, whether there was a prodrome, the duration, the recovery, and any injury (facial injury, in particular, suggests syncope with collapse forward). When syncope is suspected, escalate the cardiac and neurological workup accordingly and consider cardiology input, especially for exertional syncope, palpitation-associated syncope, an abnormal ECG, or a family history of sudden death — each is a red flag for serious cardiac causes.

### Assess and Manage Orthostatic Hypotension Actively

Orthostatic hypotension is one of the most common and most treatable causes of falls in older adults, and it is frequently both under-diagnosed and mismanaged. Measure lying and standing blood pressure routinely in any older patient who falls — wait at least one minute lying, then measure at one and three minutes standing, and record the symptoms. A drop of 20 mmHg systolic or 10 mmHg diastolic, with or without symptoms, is significant; symptoms with any drop warrant attention. Review medications first — antihypertensives, diuretics, alpha-blockers, dopaminergic agents, and tricyclics are common culprits — and deprescribe where the benefit no longer justifies the fall risk. Educate the patient on standing slowly, adequate hydration, and avoiding large meals and hot environments, and consider compression stockings and, in selected cases, midodrine under specialist guidance. Do not chase a "normal" blood pressure number at the cost of falls.

### Conduct a Structured Medication Review Targeted at Fall Risk

Medications are among the most modifiable fall risk factors, and the medication review is a core intervention, not an afterthought. Use a structured approach (for example, identifying fall-risk-increasing drugs) and focus on psychoactive drugs (benzodiazepines, antipsychotics, sedating antidepressants, anticonvulsants), antihypertensives (especially when over-treated), diuretics, opioids, and anticholinergics. Each drug should be assessed for ongoing indication, benefit versus fall risk, and the possibility of tapering or stopping. Deprescribe one change at a time with monitoring. The aim is the lowest effective regimen consistent with the patient's goals — not the tightest disease control, which in frail older adults often increases fall risk without proportionate benefit.

### Assess Gait, Balance, and Function Directly

Do not infer mobility from the history alone — observe it. Perform a structured gait and balance assessment (for example a Timed Up and Go test, and observation of gait for pace, symmetry, and assistive device use). Assess muscle strength, joint range, feet and footwear, vision, and proprioception. These observations identify the modifiable impairments that physiotherapy and targeted exercise can address. A patient who cannot rise from a chair unaided, who has an unsteady turning gait, or whose footwear is unsafe has identifiable, treatable contributors to falls. Refer to physiotherapy for individualized strength and balance exercise, which is among the most effective single interventions for fall prevention.

### Decide on Rehabilitation Honestly and Goal-Concordantly

Not every older patient who falls or becomes immobile is a candidate for intensive rehabilitation, and the decision should be honest and goal-concordant rather than default. Assess rehabilitation candidacy by the patient's premorbid function, current potential, motivation, cognitive capacity to participate, and the goals they value. For a patient with reasonable reserve and a clear goal (returning home, regaining mobility), multidisciplinary rehabilitation can be transformative. For a patient with very advanced frailty, terminal illness, or goals centered on comfort, aggressive rehabilitation may impose burden without benefit, and a supportive or palliative approach is more appropriate. Discuss the options openly with the patient and family, set realistic goals, and arrange the right level of input — inpatient rehabilitation, community therapy, or a supported discharge — rather than a one-size-fits-all referral.

### Prevent Injury and Recurrence, but Avoid Harmful Over-Restriction

Fall prevention has two goals: reduce falls and reduce injury from the falls that still occur. Address bone health — assess osteoporosis risk, ensure adequate calcium and vitamin D, and treat osteoporosis where indicated, because fracture is the most consequential injury. Optimize vision, footwear, and the home environment (hazards, lighting, grab rails). Consider hip protectors in selected high-risk patients. But balance injury prevention against the harms of over-restriction: bed rest, restraints, and excessive caution cause deconditioning, pressure injury, thromboembolism, depression, and paradoxically more falls. The goal is safe mobility, not immobility. Frame the plan around enabling activity and independence, with risk reduction, rather than around confinement.

### Escalate at Defined Thresholds

Escalate to geriatric medicine for recurrent falls, falls with syncope of unclear cause, falls in the context of significant frailty requiring comprehensive geriatric assessment, and falls where the multifactorial contributors exceed what single-discipline care can address. Refer to cardiology for syncope with red flags (exertional, palpitation, abnormal ECG, family history of sudden death, structural heart disease). Refer to physiotherapy and occupational therapy for gait, balance, strength, and environmental assessment in nearly every older patient who falls. Refer to neurology for suspected seizure or Parkinsonian gait disorder. Escalation is appropriate whenever the cause is unclear, the falls are recurrent, or the patient is not responding to initial intervention.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing the Fall as "Just Old Age"

A fall is a symptom with causes and a predictor of serious outcomes, not an inevitable part of aging. Investigate every fall with a multifactorial assessment rather than reassuring the patient and discharging.

### Stopping at a Single Cause

Attributing the fall to one factor (the rug, low blood pressure, a trip) and addressing only that factor leaves the other contributors in place and the patient at risk of recurrence. Always complete the multifactorial assessment.

### Failing to Establish Whether Consciousness Was Lost

The single most important diagnostic fork — syncope versus mechanical fall — depends on whether consciousness was lost. Establish this from the patient and witnesses every time; missing syncope misses a higher-risk and differently managed problem.

### Over-Treating Blood Pressure and Causing Falls

Tight blood pressure control in frail older adults, especially with multiple antihypertensives, is a leading and iatrogenic cause of falls. Measure orthostatic vital signs and deprescribe rather than chasing numbers.

### Neglecting the Medication Review

Fall-risk-increasing drugs are among the most modifiable contributors and are often overlooked. Conduct a structured review and deprescribe where appropriate, one drug at a time.

### Over-Restricting to "Prevent" Falls

Bed rest, restraints, and excessive caution cause deconditioning and harm and can increase falls. The goal is safe mobility and independence, not immobility.

### Missing Cardiac Red Flags in Syncope

Exertional syncope, palpitation, an abnormal ECG, structural heart disease, or a family history of sudden death suggest serious cardiac causes and warrant urgent cardiology escalation. Do not attribute such syncope to "vasovagal" without excluding these.

## Self-Check

- Have I treated this fall as a multifactorial geriatric syndrome and completed a multifactorial assessment, rather than a single-cause workup?
- Have I taken a detailed, witnessed history of the fall and established whether consciousness was lost, distinguishing syncope from a mechanical fall?
- Have I measured orthostatic vital signs and reviewed fall-risk-increasing medications with a plan to deprescribe where appropriate?
- Have I directly observed gait and balance (for example Timed Up and Go) and referred to physiotherapy for targeted strength and balance exercise?
- For suspected syncope, have I checked for cardiac red flags (exertional, palpitation, abnormal ECG, family history) and escalated to cardiology where present?
- Have I assessed bone health and addressed osteoporosis, vision, footwear, and home environment to reduce injury from any future fall?
- Have I decided on rehabilitation honestly and goal-concordantly, matching the level of input to the patient's reserve, motivation, and goals rather than a default referral?
- Have I balanced injury prevention against the harms of over-restriction, framing the plan around safe mobility rather than immobility?
- Have I escalated to geriatric medicine, cardiology, neurology, or therapy at the defined thresholds (recurrent falls, unexplained syncope, red flags, complex contributors)?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
