# Frailty and Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment

Older patients are where the single-disease, organ-system model of medicine breaks down most visibly. An older adult who presents with a fall, delirium, or functional decline is rarely suffering from one discrete problem; they are usually experiencing the interaction of multiple chronic conditions, acute illness, medications, sensory loss, social circumstances, and physiological reserve that has eroded quietly. The recurring failure is to chase the presenting complaint in isolation — investigating a fall as if it were only a mechanical event, treating delirium as if it were only confusion — while the underlying frailty and its drivers go unaddressed and the patient returns, again, with something else. The judgment problem is to recognize frailty as a clinical entity in its own right, to perform a comprehensive geriatric assessment that captures the domains that actually determine outcomes, to align interventions with what the patient values, and to resist both therapeutic nihilism (ageism) and aggressive over-intervention that ignores quality of life. This skill covers frailty assessment, comprehensive geriatric assessment, falls and functional decline, polypharmacy, goal-setting, and the thresholds for geriatric specialty escalation.

## Core Rules

### Recognize and Grade Frailty Explicitly

Frailty is a state of reduced physiological reserve such that minor stressors cause disproportionate loss of function, and it predicts outcomes — mortality, length of stay, institutionalization, complications — far better than chronological age alone. Do not substitute age for frailty; a 90-year-old can be robust and an 80-year-old severely frail. Use a validated tool to grade frailty explicitly: the Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS, 1 to 9) is widely used and quick; the Edmonton Frail Scale or a formal Fried phenotype assessment are alternatives. State the CFS score in the assessment. Recognize that moderate-to-severe frailty (typically CFS 5 and above) changes the risk-benefit calculus for invasive procedures, aggressive investigations, and intensive treatments, and should prompt a geriatric medicine consultation for high-stakes decisions.

### Perform Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment, Not Single-Issue Medicine

Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) is a multidimensional, interdisciplinary process and it is the single intervention with the strongest evidence base in geriatric medicine. Do not collapse the older patient into the presenting complaint. Assess the core domains: medical (comorbidities, acute issues), functional (activities of daily living, mobility), cognitive (screen for delirium and dementia), psychological (mood), medications (polypharmacy review), nutrition, continence, sensory function (vision, hearing), social and environmental circumstances, and the patient's own goals and values. Involve the multidisciplinary team — physiotherapy, occupational therapy, pharmacy, social work — rather than attempting the assessment alone. CGA changes outcomes precisely because it surfaces the interacting problems that single-issue care misses.

### Investigate the Fall, Functional Decline, or Delirium as a Geriatric Syndrome

Falls, functional decline, and delirium in older adults are geriatric syndromes — multifactorial presentations in which the final common pathway (a fall, confusion, loss of independence) has many contributors. Resist the temptation to assign a single cause. For a fall, assess gait and balance, orthostatic vital signs, vision, feet and footwear, environmental hazards, medications, and comorbidities (the multifactorial falls assessment). For functional decline, ask what changed, when, and against what baseline — obtain a collateral history, because the patient may lack insight or have delirium. For delirium, search systematically for reversible causes. The presenting syndrome is the entry point; the assessment must be broader. When the syndrome is recurrent or severe, escalate to geriatric medicine.

### Conduct a Structured Polypharmacy Review

Polypharmacy (typically five or more medications) is near-universal in frail older adults and is a leading cause of falls, delirium, acute kidney injury, bleeding, and hospital admission. Do not add medications reflexively or assume that every guideline-directed drug still applies to a frail patient with limited life expectancy. Use a structured deprescribing approach: list all medications with indications, identify high-risk drugs (anticholinergics, sedatives, antihypertensics, anticoagulants, NSAIDs), assess whether each still serves a goal consistent with the patient's current state and wishes, and discontinue those that do not, one at a time with monitoring. Apply the principles of medication appropriateness — benefit, harm, burden, and alignment with goals — rather than mechanically following disease-specific guidelines written for younger, robust patients. Involve a pharmacist for complex regimens.

### Set Goals with the Patient, Not for the Patient

The goals of care in geriatric medicine are defined by the patient's values and priorities, not by disease-specific targets alone. A frail 85-year-old may prioritize independence and remaining at home over aggressive life-extension; another may want everything done. Ask explicitly what matters most to them, what they would and would not want, and what trade-offs they accept. Incorporate advance care planning and, where appropriate, ceilings of treatment. Align investigations and interventions with these goals — a test whose result would not change the plan aligned with the patient's wishes should not be done. Document the goals and the conversation, and revisit them as circumstances change. Goal-concordant care is the measure of quality in geriatrics, not disease control alone.

### Obtain Collateral History Routinely

Older patients with cognitive impairment, delirium, or functional decline often cannot give a complete or reliable history. Collateral history from family, caregivers, or care home staff is not optional — it is essential to establish baseline function, the onset and trajectory of decline, medication adherence, and recent changes. Ask specifically about the patient's usual cognitive state, mobility, and self-care, and what is different now. The gap between baseline and current state is often the key diagnostic clue. Document the collateral source and content.

### Avoid Ageism in Both Directions

Two equal and opposite errors threaten geriatric care. The first is therapeutic nihilism — assuming an older patient is "too old" for investigation, rehabilitation, or intervention, and under-treating reversible conditions. The second is aggressive over-intervention — pursuing every disease-specific treatment regardless of frailty, burden, and goals, causing harm without proportionate benefit. Both are ageism in effect. Counter nihilism by assessing physiological reserve (frailty) and offering what is appropriate to that reserve; counter over-intervention by grounding decisions in goals and evidence applied to the individual. When the right balance is unclear, seek a geriatric medicine opinion.

### Escalate to Geriatric Medicine at Defined Thresholds

Involve geriatric medicine for moderate-to-severe frailty facing high-stakes decisions, complex or recurrent geriatric syndromes (falls, delirium, polypharmacy), patients needing comprehensive geriatric assessment and multidisciplinary rehabilitation, and any older patient whose function and goals are not being captured by the current care plan. For perioperative care of frail older patients, request a geriatric or perioperative medicine review. Escalation is appropriate whenever the complexity exceeds what single-disease care can address.

## Common Traps

### Substituting Chronological Age for Frailty

Treating "old" as a diagnosis leads to both under-treatment (of robust older patients who could benefit) and over-treatment (of frail patients who would be harmed). Always grade frailty with a validated tool and use it, not age, to inform decisions.

### Chasing the Presenting Complaint in Isolation

A fall investigated only for fracture, delirium treated only with sedation, functional decline attributed to "getting old" — each misses the interacting causes that the geriatric syndrome model is designed to capture. Always assess the broader domains.

### Adding Medications Without Reviewing the Existing Burden

Adding a new guideline-directed drug to an already-polypharmacy regimen without reviewing the whole list compounds harm. Conduct a structured review and deprescribe before and alongside any new prescription.

### Assuming Disease-Specific Guidelines Apply Unchanged

Most guidelines are derived from trials excluding frail, multi-morbid older adults. Applying them mechanically can cause net harm. Interpret guidelines through the lens of frailty, life expectancy, and patient goals.

### Failing to Obtain Collateral History

Without collateral history, baseline function, onset of decline, and medication adherence are guesses. Routinely seek collateral from family or caregivers, especially when cognition or delirium is in question.

### Therapeutic Nihilism Disguised as "Comfort Care"

Withholding reversible, goal-concordant treatment under the guise of "keeping the patient comfortable" is ageism and causes avoidable harm. Distinguish appropriate goal-concordant care from under-treatment, and seek a geriatric opinion when unsure.

### Ignoring Goals and Defaulting to Disease Targets

Optimizing numbers (blood pressure, glucose, lipids) in a frail patient with limited life expectancy can cause harm without meaningful benefit. Anchor care in the patient's stated goals and revisit them as the situation evolves.

## Self-Check

- Have I graded frailty with a validated tool (for example Clinical Frailty Scale) and documented the score, rather than relying on age?
- Have I performed or initiated a comprehensive geriatric assessment across the core domains, involving the multidisciplinary team?
- Have I investigated the presenting syndrome (fall, functional decline, delirium) as a multifactorial geriatric syndrome rather than a single cause?
- Have I conducted a structured polypharmacy review and deprescribed where medications no longer serve the patient's goals?
- Have I elicited and documented the patient's goals and aligned investigations and interventions with them, including advance care planning?
- Have I obtained collateral history to establish baseline function and the trajectory of change?
- Have I avoided both therapeutic nihilism and aggressive over-intervention, seeking a geriatric opinion when the balance is unclear?
- Have I escalated to geriatric medicine at the defined thresholds (moderate-to-severe frailty, complex syndromes, perioperative risk, unmet goals)?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
