# Delirium and Cognitive Disorders

Cognitive change in an older adult is one of the most commonly mismanaged presentations in medicine. The recurring and dangerous error is to treat confusion as a single entity — labeling it "dementia," "confusion," or "UTI" — and to reach for sedation or antibiotics before establishing what is actually happening. Delirium is a medical emergency with a substantial mortality, frequently the only sign of life-threatening underlying illness, and it is missed or misattributed in a large fraction of cases. Dementia is a chronic syndrome that is neither homogeneous nor untreatable, and mild cognitive impairment sits on a boundary that demands careful characterization. The judgment problem is to distinguish these states accurately, to hunt reversible causes with discipline, to manage behavioral symptoms without defaulting to antipsychotics, and to support the caregivers whose wellbeing determines whether the patient can remain at home. This skill covers delirium recognition, the delirium-versus-dementia distinction, reversible cause workup, behavioral management, and the thresholds for escalation.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Older and Hospitalized Patient for Delirium

Delirium is common, dangerous, and under-recognized, and it cannot be reliably identified by unstructured observation. Screen every older adult on admission, every hospitalized older patient daily, and whenever there is an acute change in mental status, using a validated tool — the 4AT (Alertness, Attention, Abbreviated Mental Test, Acute change) or the CAM (Confusion Assessment Method). A positive screen is not a diagnosis in itself but a mandate to investigate. Document the screen result and the specific abnormalities (inattention is the cardinal feature of delirium). Involve family or staff to establish whether the change is acute — the acute onset and fluctuating course are what separate delirium from chronic dementia, and only collateral history can establish them reliably.

### Distinguish Delirium from Dementia Deliberately

Delirium and dementia are not interchangeable labels, and conflating them changes management entirely. Delirium is acute, fluctuating, characterized by inattention and often altered consciousness, and is a medical emergency requiring a search for a cause. Dementia is chronic, progressive, with preserved consciousness (until late) and intact attention early on, and is managed with cognitive support, planning, and disease-specific treatment where available. Critically, delirium frequently supervenes on pre-existing dementia, and the patient with known dementia who acutely worsens has delirium until proven otherwise — do not attribute acute change to "their dementia." Use the time course (collateral history is essential), the attention deficit, and the level of consciousness to make the distinction, and document it.

### Hunt Reversible Causes Systematically, Not by Reflex

Delirium is a syndrome with a cause, and finding and treating the cause is the treatment — sedation does not treat delirium. Search systematically using a structured framework (a common mnemonic covers drugs, electrolytes and metabolic causes, lack of drugs or withdrawal, infection, reduced sensory input, intracranial causes, urinary and fecal problems, sleep and cardiovascular or respiratory compromise). Investigate with targeted blood tests, urinalysis and culture, and where indicated chest imaging, ECG, and infection markers. Do not reflexively blame the urinary tract — asymptomatic bacteriuria is common in older adults and is not a cause of delirium in the absence of true UTI symptoms or systemic signs; treating it does not resolve delirium and causes harm. Review the medication list with particular attention to newly added psychoactive drugs, anticholinergics, opioids, benzodiazepines, and corticosteroids, any of which can precipitate delirium.

### Treat Delirium with Non-Pharmacological Measures First

The first-line treatment of delirium is not a drug. It is the identification and reversal of the cause, combined with supportive measures: reorientation, a calm and familiar environment, sensory aids (glasses and hearing aids returned and used), sleep promotion, adequate hydration and nutrition, mobility where possible, and the presence of family or familiar caregivers. These measures reduce the duration and severity of delirium and should be instituted for every delirious patient. Address pain, constipation, urinary retention, and hypoxia — each can drive delirium and each is treatable. Reserve pharmacological treatment for the patient in whom non-pharmacological measures have failed and whose agitation poses a direct danger to themselves or to essential treatment, and even then use the lowest effective dose for the shortest possible time.

### Reserve Antipsychotics for Danger, and Use Them Cautiously

Antipsychotics carry a black-box-level risk in older adults with dementia — increased mortality and stroke — and they can worsen delirium. They are not a treatment for delirium per se. Reserve low-dose antipsychotics for the specific situation of severe agitation or distress that endangers the patient or prevents essential care, after non-pharmacological measures and cause-directed treatment have failed. Use the lowest effective dose, review daily, and stop as soon as possible. Avoid benzodiazepines except for alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal and specific indications, as they prolong delirium. Document the indication, the dose, the review plan, and the rationale clearly. Never prescribe antipsychotics as a routine standing order for "agitation" without a defined review.

### Characterize Cognitive Disorders Accurately When Chronic

When cognitive impairment is chronic rather than acute, characterize it rather than labeling it generically. Distinguish mild cognitive impairment (objective cognitive decline with preserved function) from dementia (cognitive decline impairing function). Among dementias, recognize the major subtypes because they differ in treatment, prognosis, and behavioral features: Alzheimer disease (memory-predominant, progressive), vascular dementia (stepwise, associated with vascular risk and imaging changes), dementia with Lewy bodies (fluctuating cognition, visual hallucinations, parkinsonism, severe antipsychotic sensitivity), and frontotemporal dementia (behavioral or language-predominant, younger onset). Mislabeling dementia with Lewy bodies as Alzheimer disease and giving an antipsychotic can be catastrophic. Use cognitive screening tools (for example a validated test such as MoCA or MMSE), assess function, and arrange formal assessment where the diagnosis is unclear.

### Support the Caregivers as Part of the Treatment Plan

In cognitive disorders, the caregiver is part of the unit of care. Caregiver strain predicts institutionalization, elder mistreatment, and worse patient outcomes. Assess caregiver burden, provide education about the diagnosis and its trajectory, connect them to support services and respite, and address reversible stressors. Discuss advance care planning and future planning (finances, driving, safety) early, while the patient retains capacity. Document the caregiver situation and the support offered. Neglecting the caregiver is not a minor omission; it is a failure of care that often determines outcome.

### Escalate at Defined Thresholds

Escalate to geriatric medicine for complex or recurrent delirium, delirium not resolving as expected, diagnostic uncertainty between delirium and dementia, and severe behavioral symptoms not responding to standard management. Escalate to psychiatry (ideally liaison or old-age psychiatry) for suspected dementia with Lewy bodies or frontotemporal dementia requiring specialist diagnosis, for severe behavioral and psychological symptoms of dementia unresponsive to non-pharmacological measures, and for comorbid depression or anxiety complicating the cognitive disorder. For any acute, life-threatening cause of delirium (sepsis, hypoxia, metabolic emergency), manage the emergency first and escalate within the institution as the cause dictates.

## Common Traps

### Labeling Acute Confusion as "Dementia"

Acute change in a patient with or without prior dementia is delirium until proven otherwise. Attributing it to chronic dementia leads to missed life-threatening causes. Always establish the time course with collateral history.

### Reflexively Blaming the Urinary Tract

Asymptomatic bacteriuria is common in older adults and does not cause delirium. Treating it does not help and causes antibiotic harm. Diagnose UTI only with true urinary symptoms or systemic signs of infection, and keep searching for other causes.

### Reaching for Antipsychotics First

Antipsychotics are not first-line for delirium and carry serious risks in dementia. Use non-pharmacological measures and cause-directed treatment first, reserving low-dose antipsychotics for dangerous agitation reviewed daily.

### Giving Benzodiazepines for Delirium (Except Withdrawal)

Benzodiazepines prolong delirium except in alcohol or benzodiazepine withdrawal and specific indications. Avoid them as routine sedation for the delirious older adult.

### Missing Dementia with Lewy Bodies

Dementia with Lewy bodies has visual hallucinations, fluctuating cognition, and parkinsonism, and patients are exquisitely sensitive to antipsychotics, which can cause severe, irreversible harm. Recognize the subtype before prescribing.

### Failing to Establish the Baseline from Collateral History

Without knowing the patient's usual cognitive state and the onset of change, delirium cannot be distinguished from dementia. Routinely obtain collateral history from family or staff.

### Neglecting the Caregiver

Caregiver strain drives institutionalization and poor outcomes. Assess and support the caregiver as a core part of the plan, not an afterthought.

## Self-Check

- Have I screened for delirium with a validated tool (4AT or CAM) in every older or hospitalized patient and after any acute mental status change?
- Have I deliberately distinguished delirium from dementia using the time course (from collateral history), attention, and consciousness level, rather than labeling confusion generically?
- Have I searched systematically for reversible causes using a structured framework, and avoided reflexively attributing delirium to asymptomatic bacteriuria?
- Have I instituted non-pharmacological measures (reorientation, environment, sensory aids, hydration, mobility, family) as first-line treatment?
- Have I reserved low-dose antipsychotics strictly for dangerous agitation unresponsive to other measures, used the lowest dose, and planned daily review?
- For chronic cognitive impairment, have I characterized the subtype (MCI versus dementia; Alzheimer, vascular, Lewy body, frontotemporal) rather than labeling generically, given the treatment and safety implications?
- Have I assessed and supported the caregiver, and initiated advance and future planning while the patient retains capacity?
- Have I escalated to geriatric medicine or old-age psychiatry at the defined thresholds (complex delirium, diagnostic uncertainty, severe behavioral symptoms, suspected Lewy body or frontotemporal dementia)?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
