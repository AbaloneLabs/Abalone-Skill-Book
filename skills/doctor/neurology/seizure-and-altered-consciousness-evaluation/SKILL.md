# Seizure and Altered Consciousness Evaluation

Seizures and altered consciousness are presentations where the wrong first assumption can be fatal. The patient who is convulsing needs a protocol, not deliberation; the patient who is confused or comatose needs a systematic hunt for a reversible cause, because most altered mental status is toxic-metabolic and treatable if found. The two failure modes are opposite in nature: treating a non-seizure mimic with escalating antiepileptics (the psychogenic or syncopal patient who gets intubated and anesthetized), and missing ongoing non-convulsive status epilepticus in a patient written off as "postictal" or "encephalopathic." Status epilepticus is a neurological emergency with mortality that rises minute by minute, and it has a defined escalating algorithm — benzodiazepine, then a second-line antiseizure medication, then anesthetic induction — that must be applied without hesitation. Coma demands a structured approach that does not stop at "sepsis" or "intoxication" but excludes life-threatening structural and metabolic causes. This skill covers the status epilepticus algorithm, seizure mimics, coma etiology, reversible causes, EEG use, and when to escalate to neurology or the ICU.

## Core Rules

### Treat Status Epilepticus as a Time-Critical Protocol

Status epilepticus is defined as a seizure lasting more than 5 minutes, or recurrent seizures without recovery of consciousness between them, and it is a true emergency — neuronal injury and mortality rise with duration, and the longer it lasts the harder it is to terminate. Operate by a defined escalating algorithm rather than ad hoc dosing. First-line: a benzodiazepine at adequate dose — intravenous lorazepam (4 mg, repeatable) is preferred if access exists; intramuscular midazolam (10 mg) if no IV access, and it works as fast as IV in prehospital data; buccal or intranasal midazolam where appropriate. The common error is under-dosing out of fear of respiratory depression; give an adequate dose and repeat if the seizure continues. Second-line, if seizures persist after benzodiazepines: a loading dose of a non-sedating antiseizure medication — levetiracetam, fosphenytoin (preferred over phenytoin for safer infusion and lower tissue necrosis risk if extravasated), or valproate — chosen by patient factors, allergy, and likely etiology. Third-line, if seizures continue (refractory status): induce coma with an anesthetic — propofol, midazolam infusion, pentobarbital, or ketamine — which requires intubation, ICU admission, and continuous EEG monitoring. Do not skip steps or stall between them; each step has a defined time window and the goal is termination as fast as possible. Throughout, protect the airway, give oxygen, establish monitoring and access, check glucose immediately (hypoglycemia is a rapidly reversible cause and a mimic), and send labs for reversible triggers (electrolytes including sodium and calcium, tox screen, infection workup).

### Distinguish Seizure from Its Mimics Before Committing to Antiepileptic Therapy

Not everything that shakes is a seizure, and over-treating a mimic causes real harm. The common mimics are syncope (brief loss of tone and consciousness, sometimes with myoclonic jerks from cerebral hypoperfusion — the "convulsive syncope" that is not epilepsy), psychogenic non-epileptic spells (psychogenic seizures, which can look indistinguishable clinically but have features like preserved awareness during bilateral motor activity, closed eyes, resistance to eye opening, asynchronous thrashing, and no postictal confusion), movement disorders, breath-holding spells in children, and rigors or shivering. Features favoring true seizure include a clear aura, tonic-clonic activity with stereotyped progression, tongue biting (especially lateral), incontinence, postictal confusion and somnolence, and postictal Todd's paralysis. Cyanosis and a stereotyped evolution favor seizure; immediate recovery and eyes-open unresponsiveness favor psychogenic. The definitive discriminator for recurrent unclear spells is video-EEG monitoring, which captures the events with electrical correlation — pursue this for any patient with diagnostic uncertainty rather than committing to or withdrawing antiepileptic drugs on clinical guess alone. A patient mislabeled as having refractory epilepsy who actually has psychogenic spells can accumulate years of unnecessary, harmful treatment; a patient with true non-convulsive status mislabeled as psychogenic can die. When uncertain, image and monitor.

### Evaluate Coma with a Structured Etiology Framework

Coma and profound altered consciousness require a systematic search for cause, because most causes are reversible if identified and many are fatal if missed. Use a mnemonic framework — one common is the "T" list or the WHHHAT/WHHHIMP variants covering: Trauma, infection (meningitis, encephalitis), cardiac arrest and hypoxia, hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia, hepatic and renal encephalopathy, toxins and drug overdose, intracranial hemorrhage and mass, and postictal states. The immediate bedside priorities are airway, breathing, circulation, a fingerstick glucose (give dextrose if low — and give thiamine before or with dextrose in any suspected alcoholic or malnourished patient to avoid precipitating Wernicke encephalopathy), and naloxone if opioid overdose is plausible. Examine for signs of lateralization (asymmetric pupils, reflexes, or motor response) that suggest a structural lesion — herniation, hemorrhage, mass — requiring immediate imaging. Check for meningismus and fever suggesting CNS infection, where lumbar puncture (after imaging if focal signs or depressed consciousness) is urgent. Send comprehensive labs: electrolytes including sodium and calcium, renal and hepatic function, ammonia, thyroid, toxicology, blood cultures if infection suspected, and a coagulation and platelet check before any LP. Get a head CT early in any comatose or focal patient. Do not anchor on the first plausible cause (the intoxicated patient may also have a subdural hematoma; the septic patient may also be hyponatremic) — work through the full differential and treat each reversible cause found.

### Aggressively Seek and Reverse the Reversible Causes

Most altered mental status is reversible, and the clinician's highest-yield move is to run through the reversible causes and address each. The classic reversible categories include hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia, hypoxia and hypercapnia, hypotension and shock, electrolyte disturbances (especially hyponatremia, hypernatremia, hypercalcemia), toxins and drug effects (including prescription sedatives and anticholinergics in the elderly), infections (urinary tract, pneumonia, meningitis, encephalitis), hepatic and renal encephalopathy, endocrine emergencies (myxedema coma, thyroid storm, adrenal crisis), Wernicke encephalopathy (give thiamine to any at-risk patient empirically — it is harmless and the cost of missing it is irreversible), hypertensive encephalopathy, seizures and postictal states, and intracranial mass or hemorrhage. In the elderly especially, infection and medication effects are the leading culprits of delirium and must be sought actively. Treat the cause, not the agitation — avoid sedating a delirious patient into a deeper coma unless required for safety or ventilation. Reassess after each intervention; persistent altered consciousness despite correction of obvious metabolic causes warrants EEG to exclude non-convulsive status epilepticus and neuroimaging to exclude structural disease.

### Use EEG to Detect Non-convulsive Status and Clarify Unclear Spells

Non-convulsive status epilepticus — ongoing electrographic seizure without overt motor activity — is under-recognized and presents as unexplained altered consciousness, coma, or subtle features (eye deviation, nystagmus, facial twitching). Any patient with unexplained persistent altered consciousness after a convulsive seizure, or coma of unclear cause, should have urgent EEG, because non-convulsive status will not resolve without antiepileptic treatment and prolongs neuronal injury. Continuous EEG is the standard in the ICU for patients in pharmacologically induced coma for refractory status epilepticus, where the goal is burst suppression or seizure suppression titrated to the EEG. For recurrent unclear spells in ambulatory patients, video-EEG monitoring is the gold standard to distinguish epilepsy from mimics and to classify seizure type, guiding therapy. Do not assume a patient who has stopped convulsing has stopped seizing — confirm with EEG when concern persists, especially in the critically ill.

### Know When to Escalate to Neurology and the ICU

Escalate early rather than late. Status epilepticus that has not terminated after first-line benzodiazepine warrants neurology involvement and ICU-level monitoring; refractory status requiring anesthetic coma demands ICU admission, intubation, continuous EEG, and neurology or neurocritical care. New-onset status epilepticus, especially in a young person without prior epilepsy, warrants urgent workup for structural, infectious, autoimmune, and toxic causes, often including MRI, lumbar puncture with autoimmune and infectious encephalitis panels, and toxicology. Coma of unclear cause, coma with lateralizing signs, or any suspected non-convulsive status should involve neurology. Patients with airway compromise, respiratory depression from treatment, hemodynamic instability, or need for anesthetic coma belong in the ICU. Do not manage escalating status epilepticus on a general ward — the patient needs the monitoring, airway expertise, and EEG access of a higher level of care.

## Common Traps

### Under-dosing Benzodiazepines in Status Epilepticus

Giving a token dose out of fear of respiratory depression and watching the seizure continue. Counter by giving an adequate benzodiazepine dose and repeating if the seizure persists.

### Over-treating Psychogenic Non-epileptic Spells as Epilepsy

Committing a patient with psychogenic spells to escalating antiepileptics or even intubation for "refractory status." Counter by recognizing mimic features and pursuing video-EEG confirmation for unclear spells.

### Missing Non-convulsive Status in the "Postictal" or "Encephalopathic" Patient

Assuming persistent altered consciousness after a seizure is postictal when it is ongoing non-convulsive status. Counter by obtaining urgent EEG for unexplained persistent altered consciousness.

### Anchoring on a Single Cause in Coma

Stopping the workup at "intoxication" or "sepsis" and missing a coexisting intracranial hemorrhage, infection, or metabolic derangement. Counter by running through the full reversible-cause differential and imaging when lateralizing signs or persistent depression of consciousness exist.

### Giving Dextrose Without Thiamine in At-risk Patients

Precipitating Wernicke encephalopathy by giving dextrose to a thiamine-deficient patient. Counter by giving thiamine before or alongside dextrose in any alcoholic or malnourished patient.

### Sedating Delirium Instead of Treating the Cause

Using sedatives to quiet an agitated delirious patient, deepening coma and obscuring exam. Counter by identifying and treating the underlying cause and using sedation only for safety or ventilation.

### Delaying ICU Escalation in Refractory Status

Managing escalating status epilepticus on a general ward beyond second-line therapy. Counter by escalating to ICU and neurology at refractory status, before cardiovascular or airway collapse.

## Self-Check

- For status epilepticus, did I apply the escalating algorithm (adequate benzodiazepine, then second-line antiseizure medication, then anesthetic induction) without skipping steps or stalling between them?
- Did I distinguish seizure from mimics (syncope, psychogenic spells) using clinical features and pursue video-EEG for diagnostic uncertainty rather than committing to therapy on guess?
- For coma or profound altered consciousness, did I use a structured etiology framework and not anchor on the first plausible cause?
- Did I aggressively seek and treat reversible causes (glucose, oxygen, electrolytes, toxins, infection, hepatic/renal, endocrine, Wernicke, mass) and reassess after each intervention?
- Did I obtain urgent EEG for unexplained persistent altered consciousness to exclude non-convulsive status epilepticus?
- Did I escalate to neurology and the ICU at the right thresholds — refractory status, unclear coma, lateralizing signs, airway or hemodynamic compromise?
- Did I give thiamine before or with dextrose in any at-risk (alcoholic, malnourished) patient?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
