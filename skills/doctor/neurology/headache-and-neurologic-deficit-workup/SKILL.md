# Headache and Neurologic Deficit Workup

Headache is one of the most common presentations in medicine and one of the most dangerous to evaluate casually, because the vast majority are benign primary headaches while a small minority are secondary headaches caused by lethal, time-sensitive pathology. The clinician's central task is not to treat the headache but to separate the dangerous few from the benign many — and the cost of a wrong reassurance is catastrophic. A subarachnoid hemorrhage misdiagnosed as migraine, a temporal arteritis dismissed as tension headache, a meningitis attributed to a viral syndrome, or a mass lesion overlooked because the exam was normal — each is a preventable disaster that hinges on whether red flags were sought and acted upon. Focal neurologic deficit accompanying headache shifts the calculus entirely: it demands localization, imaging, and usually specialist involvement. This skill covers red-flag screening (SNNOOP10), subarachnoid hemorrhage workup, primary versus secondary headache distinction, focal deficit localization, lumbar puncture indications, and when to escalate to neurology or neurosurgery.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every Headache for Red Flags with a Structured Framework

Do not rely on gestalt to decide which headache is dangerous — use a structured red-flag screen on every patient. The SNNOOP10 mnemonic (and similar frameworks) organizes the red flags: Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss), Neoplasia history, Neurologic deficit (focal weakness, numbness, visual change), Onset that is sudden or thunderclap, Older age of new-onset headache (especially over 50), Pattern change (new or different from prior headaches), Positional (worse on standing or lying), Precipitated by Valsalva or exertion, Papilledema on fundoscopy, Progressive or atypical presentation, Pregnancy or puerperium, Painful eye with vision change, Post-traumatic, Pathology of the immune system (HIV, immunosuppression), and Painkiller overuse. The presence of any red flag mandates investigation proportional to the risk; the absence of all red flags in a headache that fits a primary pattern supports a primary diagnosis. Fundoscopy for papilledema, vital signs for fever and hypertension, and a focused neurologic exam are part of every headache evaluation, not optional add-ons. Document the red-flag screen explicitly — it is both clinically and medicolegally essential.

### Treat Thunderclap Headache as Subarachnoid Hemorrhage Until Proven Otherwise

A thunderclap headache — reaching maximum intensity within seconds to one minute — is subarachnoid hemorrhage from a ruptured aneurysm until excluded, and the mortality of a missed SAH is severe. The classic "worst headache of my life" is a red flag, but the absence of that phrasing does not exclude SAH; any sudden severe headache warrants workup. The workup sequence is non-contrast CT of the head first, ideally within 6 hours of onset, where modern CT sensitivity for SAH approaches 100%. A negative CT within 6 hours in a neurologically intact patient effectively excludes SAH. If the CT is negative and the presentation was beyond 6 hours, or if clinical suspicion remains high, perform a lumbar puncture looking for xanthochromia (yellowish supernatant from bilirubin, which is not present in a traumatic tap and persists for up to two weeks) and a stable red cell count across sequential tubes (a falling count suggests traumatic tap). Do not accept a CT-only workup in a patient with high clinical suspicion and a negative scan beyond the high-sensitivity window — the LP is essential. Once SAH is confirmed or strongly suspected, involve neurosurgery immediately for angiography and definitive management, control blood pressure to prevent rebleeding, and manage complications including vasospasm, hydrocephalus, and hyponatremia.

### Distinguish Primary from Secondary Headache by Features and Red Flags

Primary headaches (migraine, tension-type, cluster, and the trigeminal autonomic cephalalgias) are diagnoses of exclusion made only after secondary causes are reasonably excluded. Migraine has defined criteria (moderate to severe, often unilateral, pulsating, with photophobia, phonophobia, nausea, and sometimes aura, lasting hours to days) and a pattern that recurs over time. Tension-type headache is bilateral, pressing, mild to moderate, without the migrainous accompaniments. Cluster headache is severe unilateral orbital/temporal pain with ipsilateral autonomic features (lacrimation, conjunctival injection, nasal congestion, ptosis) in attacks lasting 15 minutes to 3 hours, often in clusters over weeks. A headache that is new, first-and-worst, changing in character, or accompanied by any red flag cannot be safely labeled primary without investigation. Beware the patient with a known primary headache disorder who presents with a new or different headache — they can still have a secondary cause, and the prior diagnosis creates dangerous anchoring. Treat the primary headache once secondary causes are excluded, and match acute and preventive therapy to the specific primary diagnosis rather than using nonspecific analgesia that causes medication-overuse headache.

### Recognize and Treat Temporal Arteritis Before Vision Is Lost

Giant cell arteritis (temporal arteritis) in patients over 50 is a reversible cause of catastrophic bilateral blindness and stroke, and the window to prevent visual loss is short. Suspect it in any older patient with new headache (often temporal, sometimes occipital or generalized), scalp tenderness, jaw claudication (pain in jaw muscles with chewing that resolves with rest — a highly specific feature), visual symptoms (amaurosis fugax, diplopia, visual loss), polymyalgia rheumatica symptoms (shoulder and hip girdle aching and stiffness), constitutional symptoms, or an elevated ESR or CRP. Jaw claudication and visual symptoms are especially ominous. Do not wait for biopsy to start treatment when clinical suspicion is high — start high-dose glucocorticoids immediately, because the risk of irreversible visual loss outweighs the downside of a short steroid course in a patient who turns out not to have GCA. Arrange temporal artery biopsy within days (changes regress with prolonged steroids, but do not delay treatment for the biopsy). The ESR and CRP are usually markedly elevated but can be normal; treat on clinical grounds when suspicion is high. Aspirin is often added for stroke risk reduction. This is one of the few headaches where immediate empiric treatment is mandatory before confirmatory testing.

### Work Up Focal Neurologic Deficit by Localizing and Imaging

Any headache accompanied by focal neurologic deficit demands immediate attention, because the deficit localizes the lesion and narrows the differential. Perform a focused neurologic exam and reason about localization: unilateral weakness or sensory loss suggests a contralateral hemispheric lesion; aphasia suggests dominant hemisphere; visual field cuts suggest optic pathway or occipital involvement; cranial nerve deficits suggest brainstem or skull base; ataxia suggests cerebellum or posterior column. The differential includes stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic), mass lesion (tumor, abscess, hematoma), demyelinating disease, venous sinus thrombosis, and arterial dissection. Obtain neuroimaging — typically MRI for definitive characterization, but CT first if acute hemorrhage or emergency is suspected. Recognize that headache with focal deficit can be migraine with aura (hemiplegic migraine), but this is a diagnosis of exclusion in a first presentation and in any atypical case. Carotid or vertebral artery dissection causes headache (often neck, face, or head pain) with focal deficit (Horner's syndrome, stroke) and is a recognized cause of stroke in young patients — ask about trauma, neck manipulation, and connective tissue disease. Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis presents with headache, sometimes with focal deficit, seizures, or papilledema, and risk factors include hypercoagulable states, oral contraceptives, pregnancy, and infection. Escalate to neurology for any unexplained focal deficit and to neurosurgery for mass effect, hemorrhage, or hydrocephalus.

### Use Lumbar Puncture Correctly and Image Before Puncturing When Indicated

Lumbar puncture is diagnostic for subarachnoid hemorrhage (xanthochromia, stable red cells), meningitis and encephalitis (cells, protein, glucose, organisms, PCR), idiopathic intracranial hypertension (elevated opening pressure with normal composition), and leptomeningeal disease (cytology), and it can be therapeutic (CSF drainage in idiopathic intracranial hypertension). But LP is not without risk — herniation in a patient with raised intracranial pressure from a mass lesion is catastrophic. Image the brain with CT before LP in any patient with focal neurologic deficit, altered consciousness, papilledema, new-onset seizure, immunocompromise, or a suspicious history, to exclude a mass or shift. Measure opening pressure whenever possible, especially when idiopathic intracranial hypertension is in the differential. Interpret CSF in context: a traumatic tap shows falling red cells across tubes and no xanthochromia; bacterial meningitis shows neutrophilic pleocytosis, low glucose, high protein; viral meningitis and encephalitis show lymphocytic pleocytosis with near-normal glucose; send PCR for herpes simplex virus in any suspected encephalitis and start empiric acyclovir immediately while awaiting results, because untreated HSV encephalitis is devastating and the treatment is time-sensitive. Do not delay antibiotics or acyclovir for the LP if meningitis or encephalitis is suspected — treat first, then puncture.

### Never Reassure in the Presence of Red Flags

The most dangerous action in headache evaluation is reassurance without excluding dangerous causes. A patient sent home with "it's just a migraine" who has a subarachnoid hemorrhage, meningitis, temporal arteritis, or a mass lesion may not survive the error. Reassurance is appropriate only after a structured red-flag screen is negative, the headache fits a primary pattern, the neurologic exam is normal, and appropriate investigation (if any red flag is present) is complete. When any red flag is present, investigate proportionally and follow up — a single negative test does not always close the case if clinical suspicion remains high. Document the reasoning that supports the primary diagnosis and the plan if symptoms evolve. The default in uncertainty is to investigate and to involve neurology, not to reassure.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring a Thunderclap Headache as Migraine

Discharging a sudden severe headache without CT and LP, missing subarachnoid hemorrhage. Counter by treating thunderclap headache as SAH until excluded with CT within 6 hours or CT plus LP.

### Accepting a CT-Only Workup Beyond the High-Sensitivity Window

Declaring SAH excluded by a negative CT done at 12 or 24 hours without LP. Counter by adding LP for xanthochromia when CT is beyond 6 hours or suspicion remains high.

### Anchoring on a Prior Primary Headache Diagnosis

Labeling a new or different headache as the patient's "usual migraine," missing a secondary cause. Counter by investigating any change in pattern, especially with red flags, regardless of prior diagnosis.

### Delaying Steroids in Suspected Temporal Arteritis

Waiting for biopsy or ESR before treating, allowing irreversible visual loss. Counter by starting high-dose glucocorticoids immediately on clinical suspicion and arranging biopsy within days.

### Dismissing Focal Deficit as Hemiplegic Migraine

Attributing new focal deficit with headache to migraine without imaging, missing stroke, mass, or dissection. Counter by imaging any new focal deficit and treating hemiplegic migraine as a diagnosis of exclusion.

### Performing LP Without Imaging When Herniation Risk Exists

Puncturing a patient with papilledema, focal signs, or depressed consciousness without prior CT, causing herniation. Counter by imaging before LP whenever red flags for raised pressure or shift are present.

### Delaying Antibiotics or Acyclovir for the LP

Withholding empiric treatment while waiting for CSF results in suspected meningitis or encephalitis. Counter by treating first and puncturing after — do not let diagnostic sequencing delay life-saving therapy.

## Self-Check

- Did I screen every headache with a structured red-flag framework (SNNOOP10) and document the findings, rather than relying on gestalt?
- Did I treat thunderclap headache as subarachnoid hemorrhage and exclude it with CT within 6 hours or CT plus LP for xanthochromia?
- Did I diagnose a primary headache only after reasonably excluding secondary causes, and investigate any change in pattern in a patient with a known primary disorder?
- For suspected temporal arteritis, did I start high-dose glucocorticoids immediately on clinical suspicion and arrange biopsy within days, without waiting for ESR or biopsy?
- For headache with focal neurologic deficit, did I localize the lesion, image (CT or MRI as appropriate), consider dissection and venous thrombosis, and escalate to neurology?
- Did I image before lumbar puncture when herniation risk factors (focal signs, papilledema, depressed consciousness, immunocompromise) were present, and measure opening pressure?
- Did I start empiric antibiotics and acyclovir before LP in suspected meningitis or encephalitis rather than delaying treatment for the procedure?
- Did I avoid reassurance until the red-flag screen was negative, the exam was normal, and appropriate investigation was complete?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
