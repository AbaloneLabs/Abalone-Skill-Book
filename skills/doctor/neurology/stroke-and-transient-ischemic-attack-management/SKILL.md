# Stroke and Transient Ischemic Attack Management

Stroke is the prototype of a time-critical disease where every minute of delay kills neurons and where the difference between a good and a catastrophic outcome is measured in decisions made within the first hour. "Time is brain" is not a slogan — roughly 1.9 million neurons die per minute of untreated large-vessel occlusion. The clinician's task is to move fast but not carelessly: recognize stroke and its mimics, quantify deficit with the NIHSS, distinguish ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke with immediate imaging, and apply time-window and eligibility criteria for intravenous thrombolysis and endovascular thrombectomy. The danger is twofold — missing a treatable stroke by attributing it to a mimic, or causing catastrophic hemorrhage by giving thrombolysis to a patient with a contraindication or an unrecognized bleed. TIA is not a consolation diagnosis; it is a warning that a completed stroke may follow within days and demands urgent workup and secondary prevention. This skill covers stroke recognition, NIHSS, thrombolysis and thrombectomy decisions, ischemic versus hemorrhagic distinction, blood pressure management, secondary prevention, and when to escalate to the stroke team.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Stroke Fast and Distinguish It from Mimics

Use a structured recognition tool — FAST (Face, Arm, Speech, Time) or BEFAST (adding Balance, Eyes for posterior circulation symptoms) — to screen, but recognize that stroke presentation is broader than the classic triad. Sudden onset is the cardinal feature: stroke happens in seconds to minutes, not hours to days, and a deficit that gradually worsens over hours suggests a different pathology. Posterior circulation strokes are commonly missed because they present with vertigo, diplopia, ataxia, or isolated vertigo without the classic hemiparesis — any acute vertigo with other neurologic symptoms, nystagmus, or gait instability is a stroke until proven otherwise, and the HINTS exam (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) helps distinguish central from peripheral causes in skilled hands. Stroke mimics include hypoglycemia (always check a fingerstick glucose first — it is both a mimic and a contraindication to thrombolysis if low), seizure with postictal Todd's paralysis, complex migraine, conversion disorder, and toxic-metabolic encephalopathy. Do not diagnose a mimic until you have imaging excluding stroke, and do not let a plausible mimic (especially postictal paresis or migraine) delay imaging in a patient with sudden focal deficit. When the diagnosis is uncertain, image first and reason after.

### Quantify Deficit with the NIHSS and Use It to Guide Therapy

The NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS) is not optional documentation — it is the standardized language of stroke severity and the gatekeeper for therapy. Perform and record an NIHSS on every suspected stroke; it stratifies severity, guides thrombectomy decisions (higher scores suggest large-vessel occlusion), tracks evolution, and communicates severity to the stroke team. Learn the scoring precisely, because over- or under-scoring changes management — for example, an NIHSS that under-counts because the patient is intubated or poorly cooperative still informs prognosis. Be aware that the NIHSS under-weights posterior circulation and right-hemisphere strokes (neglect is scored lightly, cortical signs may be subtle), so a low NIHSS does not exclude a devastating posterior circulation occlusion. Use the NIHSS together with vessel imaging (CT angiography) when large-vessel occlusion is suspected, because clinical severity plus imaging occlusion determines thrombectomy candidacy.

### Decide Thrombolysis Eligibility by Strict Criteria, Not Impression

Intravenous alteplase (tissue plasminogen activator, tPA) for acute ischemic stroke is time-dependent and benefit-fraught, and eligibility is determined by explicit criteria, not clinical gestalt. The treatment window is up to 4.5 hours from last known well, with the greatest benefit in the first 90 minutes and declining benefit to the window edge. Work through the inclusion and exclusion criteria systematically: time of symptom onset (last known well, not time of discovery, is what matters — a patient who woke with symptoms is a wake-up stroke with onset at last-seen-normal), age, baseline deficit severity, blood glucose, blood pressure, prior stroke with diabetes (relative exclusion within window), anticoagulant use (absolute if therapeutic), recent surgery or bleeding, intracranial hemorrhage history, and current imaging showing hemorrhage. Do not let fear of complications exclude patients who qualify — the number needed to treat for benefit is favorable, and the absolute contraindications are well-defined. Conversely, do not bend criteria for a patient with a relative contraindication without weighing the bleeding risk explicitly and documenting the reasoning. The decision is best made with the stroke team by phone early in the workup, not after all results are in.

### Identify Thrombectomy Candidates and Image the Vessels

Endovascular thrombectomy for large-vessel occlusion in the anterior circulation is one of the most effective interventions in medicine, with benefit extending to 24 hours from onset in selected patients using perfusion imaging (DAWN and DEFUSE 3 criteria). Any patient with suspected anterior circulation stroke and a measurable deficit should undergo CT angiography of the head and neck during the initial imaging, in parallel with non-contrast CT to exclude hemorrhage — do not sequence imaging serially and lose time. Thrombectomy candidacy is determined by the presence of a large-vessel occlusion (internal carotid, M1, M2, basilar in selected cases), the deficit severity, the ASPECTS score on CT (a measure of early ischemic change; lower scores predict worse outcome), and the time window or perfusion mismatch. Notify the neurointerventional team the moment a large-vessel occlusion is identified; "drip and ship" (thrombolysis then transfer to a thrombectomy-capable center) is appropriate when the patient is at a non-thrombectomy hospital. Basilar artery occlusion causing brainstem stroke is a thrombectomy emergency with its own mortality — recognize acute coma, quadriparesis, or locked-in syndrome and escalate immediately.

### Distinguish Ischemic from Hemorrhagic Stroke Before Any Treatment

The single most dangerous error is treating a hemorrhagic stroke with antithrombotic or thrombolytic therapy. Non-contrast CT of the head is the immediate discriminator and must be done before any antithrombotic decision; it is highly sensitive for acute hemorrhage though insensitive for early ischemia. If CT shows hemorrhage, the path diverges entirely: reverse any anticoagulation, control blood pressure aggressively (target systolic <140 mmHg in many patients), consult neurosurgery for possible hematoma evacuation or external ventricular drainage especially with cerebellar hemorrhage or hydrocephalus, and manage intracranial pressure. If CT is negative for hemorrhage, proceed with ischemic stroke pathway. When clinical presentation and CT are discordant, or when early ischemic changes are subtle, MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging is more sensitive but should not delay acute treatment decisions. Always confirm the stroke type definitively before giving any agent that could worsen bleeding.

### Manage Blood Pressure Deliberately by Stroke Phase and Treatment

Blood pressure management in acute stroke is phase- and treatment-dependent, and getting it wrong causes harm in both directions. For ischemic stroke in a patient eligible for thrombolysis, lower blood pressure to below 185/110 mmHg before giving tPA and maintain below 180/105 mmHg for 24 hours after, using labetalol or nicardipine. For ischemic stroke not receiving thrombolysis, permissive hypertension is the rule — do not lower blood pressure unless it exceeds 220/120 mmHg, because the ischemic penumbra depends on collateral perfusion pressure and dropping pressure can convert salvageable tissue into infarct. For intracerebral hemorrhage, lower systolic blood pressure to 140 mmHg rapidly but smoothly, because ongoing hypertension expands the hematoma. For subarachnoid hemorrhage, control hypertension to prevent rebleeding before the aneurysm is secured, then maintain euvolemia and consider induced hypertension for delayed cerebral ischemia after securing. Know which phase you are in and manage to that phase's target; the same blood pressure number can be therapeutic in one phase and lethal in another.

### Treat TIA as an Emergency, Not a Reassurance

Transient ischemic attack is defined by transient symptoms without infarct on imaging, and it carries a substantial risk of completed stroke in the following days — the risk is highest in the first 48 hours. A TIA is not a diagnosis to reassure and discharge; it demands urgent workup and secondary prevention. Risk-stratify with the ABCD2 score but do not let a low score provide false reassurance, because it does not capture all high-risk features (e.g., dual TIA, anticoagulation status, vessel imaging). Initiate urgent workup: brain imaging, vessel imaging of the head and neck (carotid ultrasound, CTA, or MRA) to find a surgically treatable stenosis, cardiac evaluation for embolic source (ECG, echocardiogram, and prolonged cardiac monitoring for paroxysmal atrial fibrillation), and laboratory workup for modifiable risk factors. Start antiplatelet therapy (typically aspirin, with dual antiplatelet therapy for a short period in selected high-risk TIA per current guidance), and address risk factors aggressively (blood pressure, lipids with statin, diabetes, smoking). Refer urgently for carotid endarterectomy or stenting if symptomatic significant carotid stenosis is found, because the benefit is time-dependent.

### Apply Secondary Prevention Systematically and Sustain It

Secondary prevention after stroke or TIA is a bundle, not a single prescription, and must be applied comprehensively and sustained lifelong. Antiplatelet therapy for non-cardioembolic stroke (aspirin, clopidogrel, or aspirin-dipyridamole), anticoagulation for cardioembolic stroke from atrial fibrillation (initiated at the right time after stroke to avoid hemorrhagic transformation — earlier for small strokes, delayed for large ones), high-intensity statin therapy regardless of baseline LDL for atherosclerotic stroke, rigorous blood pressure control (the single most effective secondary prevention), glycemic control, smoking cessation, and lifestyle modification. For patients with carotid stenosis, address the vessel. For patients with atrial fibrillation, ensure anticoagulation is not forgotten at discharge or post-acute transitions. Each component reduces recurrent stroke risk independently; prescribing one and declaring the work done leaves most of the protection unrealized. Review and reinforce the regimen at every follow-up.

## Common Traps

### Attributing Focal Deficit to a Mimic Without Imaging

Diagnosing hypoglycemia, postictal paresis, or migraine and delaying imaging, missing a treatable stroke. Counter by imaging first when the diagnosis is uncertain and never letting a mimic delay CT in sudden focal deficit.

### Missing Posterior Circulation Stroke

Dismissing isolated vertigo or ataxia as peripheral vestibulopathy. Counter by treating acute vertigo with neurologic signs, nystagmus, or gait ataxia as stroke until proven otherwise and using HINTS in skilled hands.

### Using Time of Discovery Instead of Last Known Well

Clocking the window from when the patient was found rather than last-seen-normal, excluding wake-up strokes from treatment. Counter by establishing last-known-well time rigorously and treating wake-up strokes by appropriate protocols.

### Excluding Thrombolysis Out of Unfounded Fear

Declining tPA in eligible patients due to overcautious reading of relative contraindications. Counter by applying explicit criteria and involving the stroke team early to weigh benefit and risk.

### Sequencing Imaging Serially and Losing Time

Doing non-contrast CT, then waiting, then CTA, costing precious minutes. Counter by acquiring CTA in parallel with non-contrast CT and notifying neurointerventional early.

### Lowering Blood Pressure in Ischemic Stroke Not Receiving Thrombolysis

Treating permissive hypertension as a problem and dropping pressure below 220/120, worsening the penumbra. Counter by allowing permissive hypertension in non-thrombolysis ischemic stroke unless the threshold is exceeded.

### Reassuring After TIA Instead of Investigating Urgently

Discharging a TIA patient with reassurance rather than urgent vessel and cardiac workup. Counter by treating TIA as an emergency with urgent imaging, carotid and cardiac evaluation, and immediate secondary prevention.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize stroke using FAST/BEFAST, check glucose immediately, and image before attributing the deficit to a mimic?
- Did I perform and record a standardized NIHSS and use it together with vessel imaging to guide thrombectomy decisions?
- For ischemic stroke, did I establish last-known-well time, apply thrombolysis eligibility criteria systematically, and involve the stroke team early?
- Did I obtain CT angiography in parallel with non-contrast CT for suspected anterior circulation stroke and notify neurointerventional when a large-vessel occlusion was found?
- Did I definitively exclude hemorrhage on CT before any antithrombotic or thrombolytic therapy, and diverge to hemorrhage management if bleeding was present?
- Is my blood pressure target matched to the stroke phase and treatment (thrombolysis, non-thrombolysis ischemic, hemorrhage)?
- For TIA, did I initiate urgent vessel and cardiac workup, start secondary prevention, and refer urgently for carotid intervention if significant symptomatic stenosis was found?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
