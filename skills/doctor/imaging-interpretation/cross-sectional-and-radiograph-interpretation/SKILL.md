# Cross-Sectional and Radiograph Interpretation

Imaging interpretation is high-stakes pattern recognition performed under time pressure, and it is where missed diagnoses become missed patients. The recurring failure is not lack of anatomical knowledge; it is unsystematic looking. A clinician who glances at the most obvious abnormality, declares the study "read," and stops searching will reliably miss the second, subtler, and often more dangerous finding — the small pneumothorax on a film dominated by a fracture, the intracranial hemorrhage behind a more prominent infarct, the subtle free air under the diaphragm. A disciplined, reproducible search pattern, awareness of windowing and technique, and tight correlation with the clinical question are what separate a reliable read from a dangerous one. This skill covers systematic reading, critical-look sequences, when to trust versus re-examine a radiology report, and escalation for findings that cannot wait.

## Core Rules

### Always Use a Reproducible Systematic Search Pattern

Never read by free gaze. Use a fixed sequence so that no region is skipped. For chest radiographs, an ABCS approach — Airway (trachea, carina, main bronchi), Breathing (lungs, pleura, costophrenic angles, pneumothorax, effusion, consolidation), Cardiac (heart size, mediastinum, aortic knob), Soft tissues and bones, and Supportive hardware (lines, tubes, catheters) — is a robust default. For abdominal films and CT, survey gas pattern, bowel wall, free air, calcifications, solid organs, and bony structures in a fixed order. For cross-sectional studies, scroll through every slice in two planes, then re-scroll with attention to a single organ system across the whole volume. The discipline is not optional: a fixed sequence is the only protection against satisfaction of search, the cognitive error of stopping after the first finding.

### Check the Patient, the Technique, and the Comparison First

Before interpreting, confirm the study belongs to the right patient, that it is the right study and the right date, and that the technique is adequate. A rotated, under-inspired, or portable film mimics cardiomegaly, mediastinal widening, and consolidation; an under-dosed CT hides low-contrast lesions; motion artifact on MRI simulates pathology. Always read against prior imaging when available — many "new" findings are old, and many "stable" findings are not. State the comparison explicitly in your interpretation. If the technique is inadequate to answer the question, say so and request repeat or alternative imaging rather than over-reading a limited study.

### Master Windowing for CT and MRI

On CT and MRI, the same dataset tells different stories depending on window and level. A pulmonary embolism is missed on soft tissue windows that hide clot in the contrast-filled vessels; a lung nodule is invisible on mediastinal windows; an acute hemorrhage looks different on brain windows versus subdural windows; bone destruction is obvious on bone windows and invisible on soft tissue windows. Read every CT in at least three windows (lung, mediastinum, bone) and every brain CT in brain, subdural, and bone windows. On MRI, review all relevant sequences (T1, T2, FLAIR, diffusion, susceptibility, post-contrast) — diffusion restriction identifies acute stroke, susceptibility identifies hemorrhage, FLAIR highlights edema. A single-window, single-sequence read is a guaranteed way to miss findings.

### Build a Critical-Look Sequence for Imaging Emergencies

Before settling into the systematic read, run an explicit critical-look pass for the findings that change management within minutes. On chest imaging: tension pneumothorax, large mediastinal widening suggesting aortic dissection or traumatic aortic injury, massive hemothorax, and endotracheal tube malposition. On abdominal CT: free air from perforation, mesenteric ischemia signs, ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm, and high-grade bowel obstruction with ischemia. On brain CT: acute intracranial hemorrhage (intraparenchymal, subdural, epidural, subarachnoid), midline shift, and loss of gray-white differentiation in large vessel stroke. State each critical finding as present, absent, or indeterminate, and define the action threshold for each. A finding that is "indeterminate" for a surgical emergency requires immediate escalation, not a wait-and-see approach.

### Correlate Imaging With the Clinical Context

Imaging is one data stream and must be interpreted with the clinical picture, not in isolation. A lung nodule in a 70-year-old smoker and the same nodule in a 25-year-old non-smoker carry radically different implications; a small amount of free fluid means something different in trauma versus cirrhosis; white matter changes on MRI are pathological in one context and incidental in another. Always ask whether the imaging explains the presentation. When the imaging does not fit the clinical picture, distrust the imaging — re-examine the study, request a different modality, or seek a second read. A radiology report that contradicts a high clinical suspicion is a prompt for further action, not reassurance.

### Know When to Trust the Radiology Report and When to Re-Examine

Formal radiology reports are usually more reliable than a bedside clinician's read, but they are not infallible, and they are produced under time and volume pressure. Trust the report for detailed measurements, comparison with priors, and nuanced characterization. Re-examine the images yourself when the stakes are high, when the report is non-diagnostic or hedged, when the report contradicts the clinical picture, when a critical finding is at stake, or when you suspect satisfaction of search. Document your own read in the chart when it differs from the report and escalate to the reporting radiologist directly. Never let a reassuring report override a deteriorating patient.

### Recognize and Avoid the Classic Missed-Finding Pitfalls

Certain findings are missed repeatedly because of anatomy, overlap, or cognitive bias. On chest radiograph: small pneumothoraces (especially apical, supine, or in young patients), retrocardiac consolidation, nodules behind the hila or clavicle, and subtle pleural effusions blunting the costophrenic angle. On abdominal CT: mesenteric ischemia, closed-loop obstruction, small amounts of free air, and mesenteric hematoma in trauma. On brain CT: early ischemic changes (loss of insular ribbon, effacement of sulci), small subdural hematomas along the tentorium or falx, and subarachnoid blood in the interpeduncular cistern. Build a personal checklist of the high-yield missed findings for each study type and explicitly look for each on every read.

### Escalate Critical Findings Immediately and Close the Loop

When a critical finding is identified — tension pneumothorax, large intracranial hemorrhage with shift, aortic dissection, perforation, or malpositioned life-support hardware — escalate immediately to the treating team and the responsible consultant, document the time and the recipient, and confirm that action has been taken. Verbal handoff is not sufficient; verify the loop is closed. For findings that are urgent but not immediately life-threatening (suspected malignancy, non-obstructing stone, incidental adrenal mass), establish a defined follow-up pathway rather than leaving the finding to be rediscovered. A finding communicated but not acted upon is as dangerous as one never seen.

## Common Traps

### Satisfaction of Search

Stopping after the first obvious finding and missing the second, often more dangerous one. Counter by completing the full systematic sequence on every study, regardless of what was found first.

### Reading Without Adequate Technique or Comparison

Over-reading a rotated, under-inspired, or motion-degraded study, or missing change by not comparing with prior imaging. Counter by checking technique and priors before interpreting and requesting repeat imaging when inadequate.

### Single-Window or Single-Sequence Reading

Drawing conclusions from one window or one MRI sequence. Counter by reviewing CT in multiple windows and MRI in all relevant sequences, and by knowing which finding each is optimized to show.

### Skipping the Critical-Look Pass

Settling into the systematic read before explicitly checking for the surgical and vascular emergencies. Counter by running a fixed critical-look sequence first on every emergency study.

### Treating the Radiology Report as Infallible

Accepting a reassuring report that contradicts the clinical picture. Counter by re-examining the images yourself in high-stakes or discordant cases and by contacting the radiologist directly.

### Failing to Escalate or Close the Loop

Communicating a critical finding verbally but not confirming action. Counter by documenting time, recipient, and action taken, and by verifying the loop is closed before moving on.

## Self-Check

- Did I use a fixed, reproducible systematic search pattern across the entire study rather than free-gaze reading?
- Did I confirm correct patient, study, date, adequate technique, and review of prior imaging before interpreting?
- For CT and MRI, did I review multiple windows and all relevant sequences rather than relying on a single view?
- Did I run an explicit critical-look pass for the surgical and vascular emergencies relevant to this study type, and define the action threshold for each?
- Have I correlated the imaging with the clinical context, and when they conflict, planned further action rather than accepting reassurance?
- For high-stakes, non-diagnostic, or clinically discordant reports, have I re-examined the images and contacted the radiologist directly?
- For any critical finding, have I escalated immediately, documented the time and recipient, and confirmed the loop is closed?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
