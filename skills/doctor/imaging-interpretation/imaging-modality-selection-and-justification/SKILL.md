# Imaging Modality Selection and Justification

Choosing the right imaging test is a clinical decision, not a reflex. The wrong choice wastes time and money, exposes the patient to radiation or contrast risk, and — most dangerously — produces a result that cannot answer the clinical question and delays the correct diagnosis. The recurring failure is not ordering too little imaging; it is ordering the wrong imaging, ordering without a clear question, or ordering when no imaging is needed at all. Every imaging request must begin with a specific, answerable clinical question and a plan for how the result will change management. This skill covers disciplined modality selection, radiation and contrast risk, appropriateness criteria, and the frequent situations where the best imaging decision is to image later, image differently, or not image at all.

## Core Rules

### Start With the Clinical Question, Not the Modality

Before requesting any study, articulate the precise question the imaging must answer. "Chest pain" is not a question; "is there aortic dissection or pulmonary embolism given this presentation" is. The modality follows the question: CT angiography answers vascular emergencies, ultrasound answers biliary and pelvic questions at the bedside, MRI answers soft tissue and neuroanatomy questions, plain radiography answers simple fracture and line-position questions. If you cannot state the question in one sentence, you are not ready to order. Equally, state what decision the result will drive — if a positive or negative result would not change your next step, the study is unlikely to be worth its risks or cost.

### Match Modality to Tissue and Acuity

Different tissues and time pressures favor different modalities. Bone and air are best seen on plain film and CT; soft tissue, ligament, and marrow pathology favor MRI; hollow viscus and fluid collections favor ultrasound; perfusion and metabolic activity favor nuclear medicine or PET. Acuity compresses choice: in trauma, stroke, or suspected dissection, CT is preferred for speed and availability; MRI is reserved for when its superior tissue contrast is essential and the patient is stable enough for a longer study. Recognize tradeoffs explicitly — ultrasound is operator-dependent and limited by body habitus and bowel gas; MRI is degraded by motion and metallic implants; CT sacrifices soft tissue contrast for speed.

### Apply Appropriateness Criteria and the ALARA Principle

Use validated appropriateness criteria (ACR Appropriateness Criteria, Choosing Wisely, NICE, or local pathways) as the default arbiter of whether imaging is indicated and which modality is "usually appropriate." These criteria encode evidence on diagnostic yield and harm and should override habit. When imaging is indicated, apply the ALARA principle — As Low As Reasonably Achievable — for radiation-based studies. Prefer non-ionizing modalities (ultrasound, MRI) when they answer the question equally well, especially in children, pregnant patients, and patients likely to need repeated imaging over time. Justify any CT, nuclear study, or fluoroscopy by explaining why a lower-radiation alternative cannot answer the question.

### Weigh Contrast Risk Before Choosing Contrast-Enhanced Protocols

Contrast changes both the diagnostic value and the risk profile of a study, and the decision must be deliberate. For iodinated contrast in CT, assess renal function (eGFR, and where critical, recent creatinine trend) and risk of contrast-associated nephropathy, hydrate appropriately, and consider metformin hold and alternative imaging in high-risk patients. For gadolinium in MRI, screen renal function for nephrogenic systemic fibrosis risk and document prior allergy. For contrast allergy, ascertain prior reaction severity and plan premedication or a non-contrast alternative. Never default to "with contrast" without confirming renal function, allergy history, and pregnancy status; never default to "without contrast" when the clinical question requires enhancement, as this may render the study non-diagnostic and force repeat imaging and repeat radiation.

### Screen Pregnancy and Lactation Explicitly

For any patient of reproductive potential undergoing ionizing imaging, document pregnancy status before ordering. When imaging is necessary in pregnancy, prefer ultrasound or MRI without gadolinium; reserve CT for situations where the maternal benefit clearly outweighs fetal risk (for example, suspected pulmonary embolism or trauma), and use dose-reduction protocols. Document the justification and the pregnancy-specific risk-benefit discussion in the record. For lactation, most contrast studies are compatible with continued breastfeeding, but the rationale and any expressed-discard interval should be documented rather than assumed.

### Avoid Low-Yield and Defensive Imaging

A large fraction of imaging adds no diagnostic value: routine imaging for uncomplicated low back pain, plain films for non-traumatic soft tissue complaints, CT head for uncomplicated syncope without red flags, and "pan-scans" for low-risk presentations. Such imaging finds incidentalomas that trigger cascades, exposes patients to radiation, and rarely changes management. Identify the trigger for ordering — if it is reassurance, patient expectation, or defensive habit rather than a clinical question, reconsider. Use shared decision-making to explain why imaging is not indicated, what the natural history is, and what symptoms should prompt return. When in doubt, consult radiology before ordering rather than after.

### Engage Radiology as a Consultant, Not a Technician

The most underused resource in modality selection is the radiologist. For any non-routine question — uncertain indication, complex allergy or renal status, possible need for protocol modification, or disagreement with prior imaging — call radiology to discuss the question and the patient. Protocol decisions (contrast phase, slice thickness, specific sequences, point-of-care versus departmental) materially change what the study can answer. Document the discussion and the agreed protocol. For high-risk or unusual requests, radiology consultation is mandatory before ordering, not an afterthought.

### Know When No Imaging Is the Correct Answer

The absence of imaging can be the correct, evidence-based decision. Many conditions are clinical diagnoses (uncomplicated migraine, classic Bell palsy, low-risk chest pain with negative high-sensitivity troponin and low-risk score, nonspecific low back pain without red flags). Imaging these patients does not reduce risk; it increases it through false positives, incidental findings, radiation, and delayed reassurance. State explicitly why imaging is withheld, what red flags would prompt imaging, and the safety-net plan — this is a defensible clinical decision, not an omission.

## Common Traps

### Ordering Without a Defined Clinical Question

Defaulting to a study because "we should image" without stating what the result must answer. Counter by writing the question on the request; if you cannot, do not order.

### Choosing the Wrong Modality for the Tissue or Acuity

Requesting MRI for an unstable trauma patient, or plain film for suspected soft tissue injury where MRI is needed. Counter by matching modality to tissue and acuity and by consulting radiology when uncertain.

### Ignoring Appropriateness Criteria and Choosing Wisely

Ordering out of habit or patient pressure against evidence-based guidance. Counter by checking appropriateness criteria as the default and documenting justification when deviating.

### Defaulting to Contrast Without Renal and Allergy Screening

Adding "with contrast" reflexively, exposing patients to nephropathy, allergy, or nephrogenic systemic fibrosis risk. Counter by confirming renal function, allergy history, and pregnancy before any contrast study, and escalate to nephrology or radiology for borderline cases.

### Failing to Screen for Pregnancy

Ordering ionizing imaging in a pregnant patient without documentation of pregnancy status and risk-benefit discussion. Counter by making pregnancy screening a hard pre-order check for all patients of reproductive potential.

### Defensive Imaging That Cascades Into Harm

Ordering "to be safe" or to satisfy expectation, generating incidentalomas and false positives that trigger further radiation, biopsies, and anxiety. Counter by recognizing the trigger and using shared decision-making and safety-netting instead of imaging.

### Treating the Radiologist as a Technician

Submitting a request with no clinical context and expecting the right answer. Counter by providing a focused clinical question and relevant history, and by calling for protocol discussion on non-routine cases.

## Self-Check

- Have I stated, in one sentence, the specific clinical question the imaging must answer and the management decision it will drive?
- Is the chosen modality the most appropriate for the tissue, acuity, and availability, and have I consulted appropriateness criteria rather than habit?
- For any ionizing study, have I justified it against non-ionizing alternatives and applied ALARA, especially in children, pregnant patients, and those needing serial imaging?
- For any contrast study, have I confirmed renal function, contrast allergy history, and pregnancy status, and planned hydration, premedication, or alternatives where needed?
- Have I explicitly screened and documented pregnancy status for any patient of reproductive potential undergoing ionizing imaging?
- Have I considered whether no imaging is the correct, evidence-based decision, and if so documented the rationale, red flags, and safety-net plan?
- Have I engaged radiology as a consultant for non-routine questions and documented the agreed protocol?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
