# Fall Prevention

Falls are among the most common and most underestimated causes of harm in both hospital and community settings, and they sit at the center of a genuine clinical tension: the interventions that prevent falls can also cause harm by restricting mobility, accelerating deconditioning, and violating patient autonomy. The recurring failure is to treat fall prevention as a matter of putting up a bed alarm or applying a restraint, when in reality the most effective prevention is multifactorial and the single most powerful modifiable risk factor is often iatrogenic — the medications the clinician prescribed. Clinicians consistently miss that a fall is rarely a random accident; it is usually the predictable product of accumulated risks (deconditioning, sedating drugs, sensory impairment, environmental hazards, postural hypotension) that were present and unaddressed. They also consistently overestimate the value of restraints and alarms, which can prevent the visible fall while causing the invisible harms of immobility, pressure injury, delirium, and loss of dignity. This skill covers the disciplined practice of fall prevention: structured risk assessment, multifactorial intervention, the careful review and reduction of fall-risk-increasing drugs, the deliberate balance between protection and mobility, environmental modification, and the post-fall huddle as a learning mechanism rather than a blame exercise. The discipline is to prevent the fallable patient from falling while preserving their function, to attack the modifiable risks rather than the symptom, and to treat every fall as a signal to be understood rather than an accident to be forgotten.

## Core Rules

### Assess Fall Risk Systematically, Not by Impression

Fall risk is not reliably detected by a clinician's impression of whether a patient looks steady; it must be assessed with a structured tool and a targeted history. Use a validated risk assessment instrument appropriate to the setting (for example, the Morse Fall Scale in hospitals, the Timed Up and Go test or STEADI toolkit in the community) as a starting point, and supplement it with the specific risk factors the tools may underweight. Assess for prior falls, because a history of falls is the strongest single predictor of future falls — ask about the circumstances, frequency, and any injuries. Evaluate gait and balance directly, because observing how a patient rises, turns, and walks reveals instability that questioning will miss. Identify the modifiable contributors: muscle weakness, deconditioning from bed rest, sensory impairment (vision, hearing, proprioception, neuropathy), cognitive impairment and delirium, orthostatic hypotension, urinary urgency and nocturia, foot problems and inappropriate footwear, and environmental hazards. In hospitalized patients, recognize that admission itself is a fall risk — the unfamiliar environment, the tethering to lines and monitors, the sedating medications, and the bed rest that causes rapid deconditioning all converge to elevate risk within hours.

### Design Multifactorial Interventions, Not Single Fixes

Because falls arise from the accumulation of multiple risks, effective prevention requires multifactorial intervention that addresses several contributors simultaneously, not a single fix. Combine strength and balance training (the intervention with the strongest evidence for reducing falls in the community), medication review and reduction of fall-risk-increasing drugs, management of postural hypotension and cardiac causes, correction of vision and hearing deficits, optimization of foot care and footwear, home hazard assessment and modification (grab bars, lighting, removal of rugs and clutter), and patient and family education. In the hospital, add structured mobility programs that get patients up and moving early, because bed rest accelerates the deconditioning that causes falls — the paradox is that the safest patient is often the one who is mobilized, not the one who is kept in bed. Tailor the bundle to the individual's specific risk factors rather than applying the same generic plan, because a patient whose primary risk is sedating medication needs a different intervention than one whose primary risk is untreated cataracts. Set the intervention as a coordinated plan, assign owners to each component, and reassess effectiveness over time.

### Review and Reduce Fall-Risk-Increasing Medications

The most impactful and most neglected fall-prevention intervention is the systematic review and reduction of medications that increase fall risk. Fall-risk-increasing drugs include sedatives and hypnotics (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, antihistamines), antipsychotics, opioids, anticholinergics, antihypertensives that cause orthostasis (especially alpha-blockers and diuretics leading to volume depletion), and some antiepileptics and antidepressants. Perform a structured medication review with explicit attention to fall risk, and deprescribe where the risk now exceeds the benefit — this is especially important in older adults, where polypharmacy multiplies the cumulative sedative and hypotensive burden. Deprescribe gradually to avoid withdrawal, and substitute non-pharmacologic alternatives where possible (sleep hygiene instead of hypnotics, physical therapy and scheduling instead of sedatives for agitation). Recognize that newly started sedating medications in the hospital are a frequent precipitant of inpatient falls, and that the medication added last night is often the cause of the fall this morning. Document the review, communicate changes to the patient and the next provider, and re-review at every transition.

### Balance Fall Prevention Against Mobility and Autonomy

The central tension in fall prevention is that the measures that most reliably prevent falls — bed rest, physical restraints, sedation, and constant supervision — are also the measures that cause the most harm through deconditioning, pressure injury, delirium, loss of dignity, and psychological distress. The goal is not zero falls at any cost; it is the lowest achievable fall rate compatible with preserving function and autonomy. Avoid physical restraints, which do not reliably prevent falls, can cause serious injury, and are associated with worse outcomes; use the least restrictive alternatives and follow institutional and legal restraint policies rigorously when restraint is unavoidable. Be skeptical of bed and chair alarms, which alert staff after the patient is already moving and have not consistently shown to reduce falls while contributing to alarm fatigue. Instead, invest in supervised, assisted mobility — the presence of staff who help the patient move safely — which prevents falls while preserving and building the function that ultimately reduces risk. Engage the patient and family in the plan, explain the reasoning, and respect the competent patient's informed choice to accept some fall risk in order to maintain mobility and independence, because zero-risk immobility is itself a harm.

### Modify the Environment to Reduce Fall Harm

Environmental hazards are a modifiable contributor to both the occurrence and the severity of falls, and environmental modification is a core component of prevention. In the hospital, ensure the call bell is within reach, the bed is in the low position with brakes engaged, the floor is dry and uncluttered, lighting is adequate especially at night, and the path to the bathroom is clear and short — because the most common inpatient falls occur during toileting at night. Use nonslip footwear, keep mobility aids within reach, and minimize the tethers (catheters, IV lines, monitors) that trip patients. In the community, conduct a home hazard assessment and recommend grab bars in the bathroom, improved lighting especially on stairs and at night, removal of loose rugs and clutter, secure handrails, and adjustment of furniture height. Recognize that environmental modification is most effective when combined with the other multifactorial components, not deployed alone, because a safe environment does not protect a patient whose medications, weakness, and vision remain unaddressed.

### Conduct Post-Fall Huddles to Learn, Not to Blame

When a fall occurs, the immediate priorities are clinical assessment of the patient for injury and the longer-term priority is learning from the event to prevent recurrence. Conduct a post-fall huddle promptly, bringing together the staff involved to reconstruct what happened without assigning individual blame, because falls are almost always the product of system factors rather than a single person's negligence. Examine the antecedents: was the call bell in reach, was the bed low, were the medications reviewed, was the patient's risk recognized and the plan followed, were the sensory aids in place, was the toileting need anticipated. Identify what defenses failed and what can be changed — a new medication, an adjusted mobility plan, an environmental fix, a staffing or supervision change. Document the fall, the assessment, and the corrective actions, and report through the institutional incident system so that patterns across units and patients can be detected. The post-fall huddle is a just-culture mechanism: it distinguishes human error from system design failure, and it converts each fall into improved prevention rather than repeated harm.

## Common Traps

### Equating Restraints and Alarms With Prevention

Believing that bed rails, physical restraints, or bed alarms reliably prevent falls, when they often cause harm and shift rather than reduce risk. Counter by prioritizing supervised assisted mobility and the least restrictive alternatives, and reserving restraint for unavoidable cases under strict policy.

### Keeping the Patient in Bed to Prevent Falls

Restricting mobility to avoid falls, which accelerates deconditioning and paradoxically increases future fall risk. Counter by mobilizing patients early and often with assistance, because function is the best long-term fall prevention.

### Neglecting the Medication Review

Failing to identify and deprescribe fall-risk-increasing drugs, especially sedatives, anticholinergics, and hypotensive agents added during the hospital stay. Counter by performing a structured medication review with explicit fall-risk attention at admission and at every transition.

### Assessing Risk Once and Never Reassessing

Treating the initial fall-risk score as static, when risk changes rapidly with new medications, deconditioning, delirium, and clinical change. Counter by reassessing fall risk after any change in condition and after any fall.

### Blaming the Patient or Staff for a Fall

Attributing the fall to carelessness or noncompliance and missing the system factors that allowed it. Counter by conducting a blame-free post-fall huddle that reconstructs the antecedents and designs corrective changes.

### Applying a Generic Bundle to Every Patient

Deploying the same fall-prevention plan regardless of the individual's specific risk factors. Counter by tailoring the multifactorial intervention to the patient's identified contributors, such as vision, strength, medications, and environment.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess fall risk with a validated tool and a targeted history including prior falls, gait and balance observation, and the specific modifiable contributors?
- Did I design a multifactorial intervention bundle addressing several contributors simultaneously, including strength and balance training, medication review, sensory correction, and environmental modification?
- Did I review and reduce fall-risk-increasing medications, deprescribing where risk exceeds benefit and substituting non-pharmacologic alternatives?
- Did I balance fall prevention against mobility and autonomy, avoiding restraints and alarms as primary prevention and prioritizing supervised assisted mobility?
- Did I modify the environment — call bell reach, low bed position, clear path to bathroom, adequate lighting, nonslip footwear, and home hazard reduction — as part of the bundle?
- Did I reassess fall risk after any clinical change or fall, rather than treating the initial score as static?
- After a fall, did I conduct a prompt post-fall huddle to learn from antecedents without assigning blame, document the event, and design corrective changes?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
