# Medication Safety

Medication is the most common medical intervention, and medication error is one of the most common causes of preventable patient harm. The recurring failure is to treat prescribing as a routine clerical act rather than a high-risk decision: a dose is chosen from memory, a allergy is not checked, a kidney function is assumed rather than measured, and a look-alike drug is selected from a dropdown. Most medication errors are not caused by careless individuals but by fragile systems — ambiguous labeling, interruptions during order entry, absent reconciliation at transitions, and a culture that punishes reporting. Clinicians consistently underestimate how often the "standard dose" is dangerous for the elderly or renally impaired, and they consistently miss that the highest-risk moments are not initiation but handoff, when a medication list is rebuilt from memory or from an outdated record. This skill covers the disciplined practice of medication safety: the five rights as a minimum mental check, the special handling of high-alert medications, reconciliation at every transition of care, dosing adjustment for organ impairment, the recognition of look-alike and sound-alike drugs, and the reporting of errors and near-misses in a just culture. The discipline is to assume that every prescription can harm, to build redundant verification for the drugs that most often do, and to treat the medication list as a living, reconciled document rather than a static record.

## Core Rules

### Treat the Five Rights as a Minimum, Not a Guarantee

The five rights — right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time — are the baseline mental checklist for every medication transaction, but they are necessary and not sufficient. They are a final defense, not a design. Verify the patient with at least two identifiers (name and date of birth, or medical record number), not by bed number or room. Confirm the drug name against the indication, because the most dangerous error is a correct drug for the wrong problem. Check the dose against a current reference, especially for weight-based pediatric dosing and high-alert medications where the therapeutic window is narrow. Confirm the route explicitly, because the wrong route (intrathecal vincristine, intravenous instead of subcutaneous) is a classic fatal error. Recognize that the five rights protect against slips but not against knowledge gaps — a clinician who does not know the correct dose will confidently administer the wrong one and pass every rights check. Pair the five rights with independent double checks for high-alert medications, where a second qualified person verifies the drug, dose, and calculation separately before administration.

### Apply Heightened Vigilance to High-Alert Medications

A small number of medication classes cause a disproportionate share of serious harm: anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, chemotherapy, concentrated electrolytes (potassium chloride, hypertonic saline, magnesium), neuromuscular blockers, and sedatives. These are high-alert because the harm from error is severe and the therapeutic margin is narrow. For each, apply layered safeguards: standardized concentrations and doses, limits on the number of available strengths, smart-pump dosing libraries with hard maximum limits, independent double checks, and restricted ordering (for example, no verbal orders for chemotherapy except in emergencies). Anticoagulants require dose adjustment for renal function and weight, scheduled platelet and coagulation monitoring, and explicit reversal plans. Insulin is one of the most error-prone drugs because of the proliferation of formulations and the use of abbreviations (the abbreviation U has caused tenfold overdoses when misread); always write units in full. Opioids require assessment of opioid tolerance, equianalgesic conversion done carefully and usually in consultation for high doses, co-prescription of naloxone, and avoidance of sedative stacking with benzodiazepines. Chemotherapy requires protocol-driven ordering, dose calculation verification by pharmacy, and a clear distinction between curative and palliative intent.

### Reconcile Medications at Every Transition of Care

Medication reconciliation is not a paperwork exercise; it is the process that prevents the most harmful transition errors — unintended duplication, omitted critical therapy, and wrong doses carried forward. Reconcile at every transition: admission, transfer between units or facilities, and discharge. Obtain the best possible medication history by using at least two sources (the patient or caregiver, the pharmacy fill record, the prior discharge summary, the outpatient chart) and asking specifically about over-the-counter drugs, supplements, herbal products, inhalers, eye drops, topical agents, and PRN medications that patients forget. Compare each medication against the new plan and categorize as continue, modify, hold, or stop, with an explicit reason for each change. At discharge, provide the patient with a written, updated medication list, explain what changed and why, and use teach-back to confirm they understand which medications to take, which to stop, and the warning signs. Transitions are where therapeutic anticoagulation is accidentally continued into bleeding, where home beta-blockers are omitted and rebound occurs, and where duplicate opioids are prescribed from two prescribers — reconciliation is the defense.

### Adjust Dosing for Renal and Hepatic Impairment

Many serious medication errors are dosing errors in patients with reduced kidney or liver function, because the "standard" adult dose accumulates to toxic levels. Renal function must be measured, not assumed, before prescribing renally cleared drugs — obtain a current creatinine and estimated glomerular filtration rate, and recognize that eGFR formulas can overestimate function in the elderly, the cachectic, and those with low muscle mass. Apply renal dosing adjustments to the common offenders: gabapentin, pregabalin, many antibiotics (especially nitrofurantoin, which is contraindicated below a threshold), metformin, direct oral anticoagulants, lithium, digoxin, and low-molecular-weight heparin. For hepatic impairment, be cautious with drugs that are hepatically metabolized or that can precipitate encephalopathy (opioids, sedatives, diuretics causing dehydration and electrolyte shifts). In the elderly, apply the principle of start low and go slow, recognize that polypharmacy multiplies interaction risk, and periodically perform deprescribing reviews to stop medications whose risks now exceed their benefits. Never prescribe a standard dose on autopilot in a patient whose clearance may be impaired.

### Recognize and Prevent Look-Alike and Sound-Alike Errors

Look-alike and sound-alike (LASA) drugs are pairs whose names or packaging are easily confused: hydralazine and hydroxyzine, clonidine and colchicine, epinephrine and ephedrine, cisplatin and carboplatin, and many more. These errors slip through because they are not knowledge errors but perceptual errors — the eye reads what it expects. Use tall man lettering where adopted (hydrOXYzine versus hydrALAZine) to highlight the distinguishing portion of the name. When prescribing or administering, read the indication alongside the drug name, because a mismatch (hydroxyzine ordered for hypertension) is a red flag. Avoid storing look-alike products adjacent to each other, and use barcode verification at administration. For verbal and telephone orders, require read-back and spelling of the drug name, and avoid abbreviations that are easily misread. Be especially vigilant with LASA drugs during order entry autocomplete, where selecting the wrong entry from a dropdown is a single click away from harm.

### Report Errors and Near-Misses in a Just Culture

Medication safety depends on learning from error, and learning depends on reporting, and reporting depends on a culture that does not punish honest mistakes. Report every error and every near-miss, because the near-miss that harmed no one today is the fatal error tomorrow when one more defense fails. Distinguish human error (an inadvertent slip) from at-risk behavior (a risky shortcut that seemed reasonable) from reckless behavior (conscious disregard of safety) — a just culture responds to each differently, with support and system redesign for the first two and accountability for the third. Do not blame the last person in the chain (often the nurse who administered the error) for a failure that was designed into the system hours earlier. Participate in root cause analysis to identify the latent conditions (interruptions, staffing, confusing interfaces, missing allergy alerts) that allowed the error to reach the patient. The goal is not to find who to punish but to find what to fix, and the most dangerous organizations are those where errors are hidden out of fear.

## Common Traps

### Trusting Memory Instead of a Current Reference

Prescribing a dose or interaction from recall without verifying against an up-to-date source, especially for unfamiliar or high-alert drugs. Counter by looking up the dose, the renal adjustment, and the interaction profile every time for high-alert medications and whenever uncertainty exists.

### Skipping Reconciliation at Transfer

Rebuilding the medication list from memory or an outdated record when a patient moves between units or facilities, causing omissions and duplications. Counter by performing structured reconciliation using at least two sources at every transition and documenting each continue, modify, hold, or stop decision with a reason.

### Assuming Renal Function Is Normal

Prescribing a standard adult dose without checking current creatinine and eGFR, leading to accumulation and toxicity in renally cleared drugs. Counter by measuring renal function before prescribing renally cleared drugs and applying documented dose adjustments.

### Stacking Sedatives

Co-prescribing opioids with benzodiazepines or other central nervous system depressants without recognizing the synergistic respiratory depression risk. Counter by reviewing the full sedative load, avoiding the combination where possible, and co-prescribing naloxone when opioids are continued.

### Selecting the Wrong Dropdown Entry

Choosing a look-alike drug from an autocomplete list or picking the wrong product from adjacent storage. Counter by reading the indication with the drug name, using tall man lettering and barcode verification, and not storing confusable products together.

### Blaming the Nurse Instead of the System

Focusing the investigation on the last person who touched the medication rather than the latent system failures that allowed the error to reach them. Counter by conducting root cause analysis that looks for design flaws and by fostering a just culture that reports rather than hides error.

## Self-Check

- Did I verify the patient with two identifiers and apply the five rights as a minimum mental check, recognizing they do not protect against knowledge gaps?
- For high-alert medications (anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, chemotherapy, concentrated electrolytes), did I apply layered safeguards such as independent double checks, smart-pump limits, and protocol-driven dosing?
- Did I perform structured medication reconciliation at admission, transfer, and discharge using at least two sources, and did I document each change with a reason?
- Did I measure renal and hepatic function before prescribing cleared or metabolized drugs, and apply documented dose adjustments rather than assuming normal clearance?
- Did I check for look-alike and sound-alike confusion, read the indication alongside the drug name, and avoid dangerous abbreviations such as U for units?
- Did I review the patient's full medication list for sedative stacking, duplicate therapy, and polypharmacy, and did I consider deprescribing where risk exceeds benefit?
- Did I report every error and near-miss, participate in root cause analysis, and contribute to a just culture rather than blaming individuals for system failures?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
