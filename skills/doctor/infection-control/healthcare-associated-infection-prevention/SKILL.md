# Healthcare-Associated Infection Prevention

Healthcare-associated infections (HAIs) are largely preventable, yet they remain among the most common causes of patient harm. The central judgment problem is that the decisions that create HAI risk — placing a device, choosing a prophylactic antibiotic, delaying a dressing change, leaving a catheter in "just one more day" — feel individually trivial and are made moment to moment by many clinicians, while the harm they cause is delayed, diffuse, and often attributed to bad luck. Prevention therefore depends on bundles: small sets of evidence-based practices applied together reliably, on relentless device minimization, and on the discipline to remove a device the moment it is no longer essential. The agent's role is not to recite the bundle but to recognize the moments where risk is being created and to insist on the bundle, on prompt removal, and on escalation when patterns suggest an outbreak. This skill covers the major device- and procedure-related HAIs, prevention bundles, antimicrobial stewardship linkage, outbreak recognition, and when to escalate to infection prevention and control.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Device as a Time-Limited Liability, Not a Convenience

Central lines, urinary catheters, endotracheal tubes, surgical drains, and feeding tubes each carry a daily infection risk that accumulates from the moment of insertion. The single most effective prevention action is removing the device as soon as it is no longer strictly needed, ideally the same day. Build daily review of device necessity into rounds for every patient with a line, catheter, or tube; the question is not "should we remove it?" but "is there a specific, active indication for it today?" Document the indication daily. Default to removal when the original indication has resolved, even if removal causes mild short-term inconvenience. Devices left in place "just in case" or for nursing convenience are a leading source of preventable infection. When a device must remain, apply the full prevention bundle and review it daily.

### Apply the Central Line Bundle at Insertion and Throughout the Line's Life

Central line-associated bloodstream infection (CLABSI) prevention rests on a bundle: maximal sterile barrier precautions (cap, mask, sterile gown, sterile gloves, full-body sterile drape) for the operator and all assistants during insertion; chlorhexidine skin antisepsis with appropriate contact time; optimal catheter and site selection (avoiding femoral in adults where possible, preferring subclavian or jugular based on thrombosis and mechanical tradeoffs); hand hygiene before the procedure; and daily review of line necessity with prompt removal. Use antimicrobial-impregnated catheters for higher-risk contexts (long dwell times, known high CLABSI rates, limited barrier resources). Scrub the hub with chlorhexidine or alcohol before every access; contamination at the hub is a major late-life CLABSI source. Replace administration sets at recommended intervals and immediately after blood or lipid contamination. When a CLABSI is suspected, draw paired blood cultures from the line and a peripheral vein before antibiotics, remove the line if it is the likely source, and escalate to infection control if rates exceed benchmarks.

### Apply the Urinary Catheter Bundle and Resist Placement Pressure

Catheter-associated urinary tract infection (CAUTI) prevention hinges on avoiding insertion and on prompt removal. The only strong indications are: acute urinary retention or obstruction, need for strict input-output monitoring in critically ill patients, perioperative use for selected surgeries, management of open sacral or perineal wounds in incontinent patients, end-of-life comfort, and prolonged immobilization. Incontinence alone, convenience, and "to get an accurate urine output" in a stable patient are not indications. Use the smallest gauge catheter that drains adequately, maintain a sterile closed drainage system, keep the bag below the bladder without touching the floor, do not break the system to specimen-sample (use the sampling port), and remove the catheter as soon as the indication ends. Consider nurse-driven or protocol-driven removal protocols to overcome the inertia of retained catheters. Do not treat asymptomatic bacteriuria in catheterized patients; it is not CAUTI and treating it drives resistance.

### Apply the Ventilator Bundle and Recognize That VAP Prevention Begins Before Intubation

Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) prevention is a bundle of small, reliable practices: elevation of the head of the bed to 30–45 degrees unless contraindicated; daily sedation interruption and assessment of readiness to extubate (the "wake up and breathe" pairing); peptic ulcer disease prophylaxis; deep vein thrombosis prophylaxis; oral care with chlorhexidine where recommended (noting evolving guidance); and subglottic secretion drainage for patients expected to be intubated more than 48–72 hours. Prefer non-invasive ventilation where feasible to avoid intubation entirely. Minimize pooling of secretions above the cuff. Recognize that the highest-risk period is the first few days, so bundle elements should be in place from the start, not added later. Reassess daily for readiness to liberate from the ventilator; each additional day intubated increases VAP risk.

### Apply SSI Prevention Across the Entire Perioperative Arc

Surgical site infection (SSI) prevention is not just a preoperative antibiotic. The bundle spans the entire arc: appropriate hair removal (clipping, not shaving, and only when needed); preoperative bathing with chlorhexidine where indicated; normothermia during and after surgery; glycemic control in diabetic and hyperglycemic patients (perioperative glucose targets matter); oxygenation with adequate inspired fraction in the immediate postoperative period for selected procedures; appropriate preoperative skin preparation; and correct choice, timing, dose, and redosing of prophylactic antibiotics. Give the antibiotic within the window that ensures tissue levels at incision (typically within 60 minutes before incision, with specific agents like vancomycin and fluoroquinolones allowed up to 120 minutes), redose during long procedures based on half-life, and stop prophylaxis within 24 hours after surgery (48 hours for some cardiac surgery guidance) to limit resistance and C. diff risk. Avoid unnecessary prolongation of prophylaxis — it does not prevent SSI and causes harm.

### Integrate Antimicrobial Stewardship as an Infection Prevention Lever

Antimicrobial exposure is itself a risk factor for HAIs, most prominently Clostridioides difficile infection, and for selection of resistant organisms. Every unnecessary antibiotic day increases the patient's risk of C. diff and colonization with resistant flora that can spread to others. Link stewardship to HAI prevention: narrow empiric therapy once cultures and susceptibilities return, set automatic stop dates, avoid treating asymptomatic bacteriuria and colonization, and reserve broad-spectrum agents for confirmed indications. Suspect C. diff in any patient with new or unexplained diarrhea during or after antibiotic exposure, and test with a sensitive and specific algorithm (e.g., GDH/toxin/NAAT or a multistep algorithm) rather than a single low-sensitivity test. Initiate contact precautions plus soap-and-water hand hygiene (alcohol gel does not kill spores) for suspected or confirmed C. diff while continuing the diagnostic workup.

### Recognize Outbreaks Early and Escalate Immediately

An outbreak is often first visible as a cluster of the same organism, the same infection type, or infections in the same unit over a short period. Two or more related cases in a unit within a narrow window warrant a look. Watch for: a rise in CLABSI or CAUTI rates above the unit's baseline; a cluster of C. diff cases; appearance of a resistant organism (CRE, VRE, MRSA) in patients who did not have it on admission; surgical site infections clustered by surgeon, procedure, or operating room; or any unusual pathogen ( Legionella, non-tuberculous mycobacteria in surgical sites, group A strep in postpartum patients). Escalate to infection prevention and control and the institutional outbreak response team the moment a cluster is suspected — do not wait for confirmation. Early escalation enables environmental sampling, cohorting, enhanced surveillance, and root-cause analysis before the cluster expands. For organisms or patterns with public health significance, infection control will engage public health authorities.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Devices in Place for Convenience or "Just in Case"

A catheter or line retained for convenience accrues daily infection risk. Counter by reviewing device necessity daily and removing the moment the active indication resolves.

### Treating Asymptomatic Bacteriuria or Colonization as Infection

Treating asymptomatic bacteriuria in catheterized patients or colonization with resistant organisms drives resistance and C. diff without preventing infection. Counter by treating only symptomatic infection based on clinical criteria plus laboratory evidence.

### Prolonging Surgical Prophylaxis

Continuing prophylactic antibiotics beyond 24 hours does not reduce SSI and increases resistance and C. diff risk. Counter by stopping at the guideline-recommended time and using automatic stop orders.

### Treating Bundle Elements as Optional or One-Time

Bundle efficacy depends on all elements applied together reliably; partial application loses most of the benefit. Counter by auditing bundle compliance, not just infection rates, and treating each missed element as a defect.

### Using Alcohol Hand Rub for C. diff and Ignoring Sporicidal Cleaning

Alcohol does not kill C. diff spores, and standard cleaning may not remove them. Counter by using soap and water for hand hygiene and EPA-registered sporicidal disinfectants for environmental cleaning in C. diff rooms.

### Attributing a Single HAI to Bad Luck Instead of a Process Failure

A single CLABSI, CAUTI, or SSI often reflects a bundle gap that, if unaddressed, will cause more. Counter by reviewing each HAI as a potential system defect and escalating patterns to infection control.

### Delaying Outbreak Escalation While Gathering More Data

By the time a cluster is "certain," it has usually spread. Counter by escalating to infection control on suspicion, not on confirmation.

## Self-Check

- For every device in this patient, have I documented a specific active indication today and planned removal the moment that indication resolves?
- Did I apply the full prevention bundle at central line insertion (maximal barriers, chlorhexidine, optimal site, hub scrub) and review line necessity daily?
- Did I avoid placing a urinary catheter for a weak indication (incontinence, convenience, stable-patient monitoring) and maintain the closed system correctly?
- For the intubated patient, are all ventilator bundle elements in place (head elevation, sedation interruption, ulcer and DVT prophylaxis, secretion management)?
- For surgical prophylaxis, did I choose the right agent, time it to incision, redose for long procedures, and stop within the recommended window?
- Am I integrating antimicrobial stewardship — narrowing therapy, stopping unnecessary antibiotics, and not treating asymptomatic bacteriuria or colonization?
- For suspected C. diff, am I using contact precautions plus soap-and-water hand hygiene and an appropriate diagnostic algorithm?
- Have I escalated any cluster of infections, resistant organism, or unusual pathogen to infection prevention and control on suspicion rather than waiting for confirmation?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
