# Occupational Exposure and Staff Protection

Occupational exposure management is a domain where minutes matter and where hesitation, incomplete history, or rote application of a protocol can lead to either unnecessary toxic prophylaxis or a preventable, lifelong infection. The central judgment problem is rapid, time-critical risk stratification: assessing the source's infectivity, the exposure's severity, and the exposed person's immune and physiological status, then deciding whether post-exposure prophylaxis is indicated within a window measured in hours. The second judgment problem is the broader protection of staff through immunization, fitness-for-work decisions, and work restrictions that prevent both staff-acquired illness and transmission from staff to vulnerable patients. Errors here are irreversible — seroconversion after a missed HIV PEP indication cannot be undone — so the agent must act quickly, escalate appropriately, and never let uncertainty or inconvenience delay the time-critical steps. This skill covers exposure assessment, PEP decisions, follow-up, staff immunization, and work-restriction judgment.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Exposure as Time-Critical Until Proven Otherwise

The first action after any percutaneous, mucous membrane, or non-intact skin exposure to blood or potentially infectious body fluid is immediate first aid: wash the wound with soap and water (do not squeeze or apply caustic agents), flush mucous membranes copiously with water or saline, and remove contaminated clothing. Then immediately assess for HIV, hepatitis B, and hepatitis C risk. HIV post-exposure prophylaxis is most effective when started within hours — ideally within 1–2 hours and generally not beneficial after 72 hours — so the decision must be made fast. Do not wait for source testing results to start PEP if the source is high-risk or unknown; start a three-drug regimen and discontinue if the source is subsequently proven negative. Triage the exposure as you would a clinical emergency: rapid assessment, immediate action, and escalation to occupational health or the emergency department in parallel, not in sequence.

### Stratify HIV PEP by Source Risk and Exposure Severity

HIV PEP is indicated when there is a significant exposure and the source is HIV-positive or likely to be (unknown source with risk factors, or a source in a high-prevalence setting where status cannot be rapidly determined). Significant exposures include percutaneous injury with a hollow-bore needle or deep injury, mucous membrane exposure, and non-intact skin exposure to blood, genital secretions, or other potentially infectious material. Assess the source's viral load if known — a source with an undetectable viral load on antiretroviral therapy carries substantially lower transmission risk, though PEP may still be considered based on guidelines and shared decision-making. For a known HIV-positive source, consider resistance history when selecting the regimen in consultation with an infectious disease or PEP specialist. For unknown sources, weigh the epidemiologic likelihood. When in doubt within the window, start PEP and reassess; the cost of a few days of medication is far lower than the cost of seroconversion. A baseline HIV test of the exposed person is required, with follow-up testing at defined intervals (commonly 6 weeks, 12 weeks, and sometimes 6 months depending on the algorithm and whether fourth-generation testing is used).

### Manage Hepatitis B by Source Status and Exposed Person Immunity

Hepatitis B post-exposure management depends on both the source's HBsAg status and the exposed person's documented hepatitis B antibody response. For an exposed person with documented protective anti-HBs levels (typically 10 mIU/mL or above after vaccination), no treatment is needed regardless of source status. For an unvaccinated or non-responder exposed to an HBsAg-positive source, give hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG) plus initiate or accelerate the vaccine series, with timing ideally within 24 hours and HBIG generally of limited value after 7 days for percutaneous exposure and after 14 days for sexual exposure. For an exposed person with unknown antibody status, test anti-HBs urgently and manage based on the result, giving HBIG and vaccine if the source is positive and the exposed person is unprotected. Confirm the source's HBsAg status rapidly. Document the exposed person's full vaccination history — many adults have incomplete or undocumented series, which materially changes management.

### Manage Hepatitis C by Recognition and Early Treatment, Not Prophylaxis

There is no approved post-exposure prophylaxis for hepatitis C. Management rests on early detection of infection and prompt treatment, which is curative. For a source known or suspected to be HCV-RNA positive, test the exposed person for HCV RNA at 3–6 weeks and for anti-HCV at 3–6 months. If HCV RNA becomes positive, refer immediately for direct-acting antiviral therapy, which is highly curative, especially when treated early in acute infection. Do not administer antivirals as PEP outside of a specialist-guided protocol. Counsel the exposed person about signs of acute hepatitis and the importance of follow-up testing. Because HCV and HIV co-exposure can occur, manage both pathways concurrently.

### Base PEP Decisions on the Source's Actual Status, Not Assumptions

A common and dangerous error is assuming the source is "low-risk" based on appearance, stated occupation, or absence of obvious risk factors. Obtain consent and test the source for HIV (preferably with a rapid antigen/antibody test), HBsAg, and HCV antibody (with reflex RNA) as rapidly as possible. If the source is a known patient, review their recent results from the record. If the source is unknown (a discarded needle, an unidentified patient), assess the epidemiologic context and lean toward PEP if the exposure is significant and the setting suggests risk. Never delay PEP pending source results if the exposure is significant and the source is plausibly infected; start empirically and stop if results are negative. Document the source assessment, including any refusal or inability to test.

### Maintain Staff Immunization as the Primary Prevention Layer

The strongest protection against occupational infection is pre-existing immunity. Ensure every healthcare worker has documented immunity to hepatitis B (post-vaccination anti-HBs titers), measles, mumps, rubella (MMR), and varicella (VZV), and receives annual influenza vaccination per institutional and public health guidance. Verify immunity rather than accepting verbal history: serology for HBV, MMR, and VZV where records are incomplete. Offer vaccination to non-immune staff and manage those who decline according to institutional policy, recognizing that non-immune staff may face work restrictions during outbreaks of vaccine-preventable diseases. Consider additional immunizations for specific roles: tuberculosis screening (IGRA or symptom screen, with chest imaging if positive), meningococcal vaccination for microbiology staff handling Neisseria meningitidis, and rabies pre-exposure for some laboratory roles. Maintain the immunization record as a living document, not a one-time onboarding check.

### Apply Work Restrictions to Protect Patients and Colleagues

Fitness-for-work decisions protect both the staff member and the patients they care for, especially vulnerable patients such as neonates, transplant recipients, and immunocompromised individuals. Staff with acute gastroenteritis should not work until symptom-free for the period specified by guidance (commonly 48 hours after last symptom for norovirus). Staff with undiagnosed respiratory illness should be evaluated and may need exclusion, particularly during respiratory virus season or when caring for high-risk patients. Staff with active conjunctivitis, draining skin lesions that cannot be covered, or known communicable infections (e.g., active TB, group A streptococcal infection, varicella, measles, mumps, rubella, pertussis) must be restricted until no longer infectious. Non-immune staff exposed to a vaccine-preventable disease may need exclusion during the incubation period. When uncertain, consult occupational health and infection control before allowing the staff member to work.

### Escalate to Occupational Health and Infectious Disease Appropriately

Occupational exposure and staff protection sit at the intersection of clinical care, occupational health, infection control, and sometimes public health. Escalate to occupational health for exposure registration, follow-up scheduling, and documentation; to infectious disease or a designated PEP expert for complex PEP decisions (resistance history, pregnancy, unknown source, regimen selection); to infection control for patterns of exposure that suggest a system defect (repeated needlestick injuries from a faulty device, a sharps disposal problem, a cluster of staff exposures); and to public health for exposures involving reportable diseases or sources of public health concern. For any exposure involving a source with HIV, HBV, or HCV, ensure formal follow-up is arranged and documented, including psychological support, because the anxiety after an exposure is substantial and can affect adherence and retention.

## Common Traps

### Delaying PEP While Waiting for Source Test Results

HIV PEP efficacy drops sharply with time; waiting hours for source results can push the decision past the useful window. Counter by starting empiric PEP for significant exposures with a plausibly infected source and stopping if the source tests negative.

### Assuming the Source Is Low-Risk Based on Appearance

Appearance and stated occupation are poor predictors of bloodborne virus status. Counter by testing the source (with consent) and basing PEP on actual results plus epidemiologic context, not assumptions.

### Squeezing or Applying Caustic Agents to a Needlestick Wound

Squeezing, bleaching, or applying antiseptics to the wound does not reduce transmission and causes tissue injury. Counter by washing with soap and water only.

### Forgetting the Exposed Person's Own HBV Immunity Status

PEP decisions for hepatitis B depend entirely on the exposed person's documented anti-HBs response. Counter by checking the exposed person's serology urgently rather than assuming immunity from a prior vaccine series.

### Treating HCV Exposure as "Nothing to Do Until Symptoms"

There is no HCV PEP, but early detection and treatment of acute HCV is curative. Counter by scheduling HCV RNA testing at 3–6 weeks and referring immediately if positive.

### Allowing Non-Immune or Symptomatic Staff to Work Out of Loyalty or Short-Staffing

Presenteeism by infectious or non-immune staff causes outbreaks among patients and colleagues. Counter by enforcing work restrictions and consulting occupational health when uncertain.

### Failing to Register and Follow Up Exposures

An exposure not registered is an exposure not followed; seroconversion may be missed until late. Counter by registering every exposure with occupational health and completing the full follow-up testing schedule.

### Underestimating the Psychological Impact

The weeks after a significant exposure are marked by substantial anxiety that can affect adherence and work. Counter by offering counseling and clear follow-up plans, and escalating to occupational health for support.

## Self-Check

- After an exposure, did I provide immediate first aid (soap and water wash, mucous membrane flush) and assess HIV, HBV, and HCV risk within minutes?
- For a significant HIV exposure, did I start PEP within hours (ideally under 1–2 hours) rather than waiting for source results, and arrange baseline and follow-up HIV testing?
- Did I base hepatitis B management on both the source's HBsAg status and the exposed person's documented anti-HBs level, giving HBIG and vaccine when indicated and within the effective window?
- For an HCV exposure, did I schedule HCV RNA testing at 3–6 weeks and arrange immediate referral if infection is detected?
- Did I obtain consent and test the source rapidly, and avoid assuming low risk based on appearance or occupation?
- For staff protection, is documented immunity to HBV, MMR, VZV, and influenza verified and maintained, with additional role-specific immunizations as needed?
- Are work restrictions applied for symptomatic, infectious, or non-immune-exposed staff, with occupational health consulted when uncertain?
- Have I escalated to occupational health, infectious disease, infection control, or public health as appropriate, and ensured the exposure is registered with full follow-up arranged?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
