# Shared Decision-Making

Shared decision-making is the disciplined practice of making clinical decisions with patients rather than for them, and it applies specifically to the large and growing class of decisions where there is no single right answer — where the best choice depends on the patient's values, preferences, and circumstances as much as on the medical evidence. These are the preference-sensitive decisions: whether to start a statin or an anticoagulant in a patient with competing risks, whether to pursue cancer screening at an age where harms begin to outweigh benefits, whether to choose surgery or conservative management for a condition that is not limb- or life-threatening, and how aggressively to pursue treatment near the end of life. In these decisions, the clinician who simply recommends a course of action without exploring the patient's values is making a value judgment on the patient's behalf, often without realizing it. The recurring failure is to collapse shared decision-making into informed consent at the end of a recommendation — presenting the chosen treatment, listing its risks, and asking the patient to sign — rather than engaging the patient as a genuine partner in defining the problem, weighing the options, and choosing the path that fits their life. Equally common is the opposite failure: presenting options so vaguely or neutrally that the patient is abandoned to choose among things they do not understand, leaving them more anxious and less supported than if the clinician had simply made a recommendation. This skill covers the structure of an effective shared decision: defining the decision and its preference-sensitive nature, presenting the options and their tradeoffs in understandable terms, eliciting the patient's values and preferences, integrating evidence with values to reach a preference-concordant decision, and documenting the reasoning. The discipline is to be clear about where the evidence ends and the values begin, to communicate tradeoffs honestly and understandably, and to support the patient in making a decision that is truly their own.

## Core Rules

### Recognize When a Decision Is Preference-Sensitive and Requires Shared Decision-Making

Not every clinical decision requires shared decision-making, and applying it indiscriminately wastes time and burdens patients. Shared decision-making is essential for preference-sensitive decisions — those where the best choice depends on the patient's values because the options have different benefit-harm profiles, because the evidence is uncertain, or because the outcomes that matter differ between patients. These include decisions about starting or stopping preventive medications (statins, anticoagulants, hormone therapy), cancer screening at the margins of age or life expectancy, surgical versus conservative management of stable conditions, end-of-life and goals-of-care decisions, and any decision where the patient's quality of life, risk tolerance, or personal circumstances are decisive. In contrast, decisions where the evidence clearly favors one option and the harms of deviation are high (a fractured femur needing repair, bacterial meningitis needing antibiotics) are not preference-sensitive and require informed consent rather than shared deliberation. The first step is to recognize which type of decision you are facing, and to deploy shared decision-making where the values genuinely determine the answer.

### Define the Decision and Frame the Options Neutrally Without Biasing the Patient

The way options are framed powerfully influences the decision the patient reaches, and the disciplined clinician frames options in a balanced way that does not steer the patient toward the clinician's preferred outcome. Begin by stating clearly that a decision needs to be made and that there is more than one reasonable option — this signals to the patient that their input matters and that there is no single right answer. Present the options in balanced terms, giving each its rationale, its benefits, and its harms, and avoiding loaded language ("the sensible choice," "the risky option"). Be aware of framing effects: presenting benefits in relative terms ("this halves your risk") makes a treatment seem more attractive than presenting the same benefit in absolute terms ("this prevents one event per two hundred people treated"), and presenting harms as rare makes them seem less significant than presenting them as "one in twenty patients experiences this." Use absolute terms, natural frequencies ("out of one hundred people like you"), and consistent timeframes to allow the patient to compare options honestly. Offer a recommendation if the patient wants one, but make clear that it is a recommendation informed by your judgment of their values, not a directive, and that they may choose differently.

### Communicate Risks, Benefits, and Tradeoffs in Understandable Terms

Effective shared decision-making requires that the patient actually understands the options and their tradeoffs, and this demands specific communication skills that go beyond reciting statistics. Use plain language, avoiding jargon and defining necessary terms. Use absolute risks and natural frequencies rather than relative risks, because relative risks exaggerate benefit ("halves your risk" versus "prevents one event per two hundred"). Use consistent denominators ("out of one hundred people like you") so that options can be compared directly. Use visual aids — pictographs, decision aids, risk charts — because visual representation of risk is understood far more accurately than verbal or numerical description alone. Be honest about uncertainty: state where the evidence is strong and where it is weak, and acknowledge the limits of prediction for the individual. Use the teach-back method to confirm understanding: ask the patient to explain the options and the tradeoffs in their own words, and re-explain rather than moving on if their understanding is incomplete. Recognize that health literacy, language, numeracy, and emotional state all affect understanding, and adapt the communication to the patient.

### Elicit the Patient's Values, Preferences, and Goals Before Recommending

The heart of shared decision-making is understanding what matters to this patient, because the right decision depends on their values and goals. Elicit the patient's values explicitly: what outcomes matter most to them (length of life, quality of life, independence, avoiding specific harms), what their tolerance is for risk and for treatment burden, what their life circumstances and supports are, and what they hope to achieve or avoid. Ask open questions ("What matters most to you in this decision?" "What would a good outcome look like for you?") and listen to the answer rather than imposing your own assumptions about what they should want. Recognize that patients often have values and goals that differ from the clinician's — a patient may value avoiding hospitalization more than extending life, or may accept a higher risk of a serious event to avoid the burden of a medication — and that these values are legitimate even when they differ from the clinician's recommendation. Integrate the patient's values with the evidence to shape a recommendation that fits them, rather than presenting the evidence and then asking the patient to choose in a vacuum.

### Integrate Evidence and Values Into a Preference-Concordant Decision

The shared decision is the integration of the best available evidence with the patient's values and the clinician's expertise, producing a decision that is concordant with the patient's preferences. After presenting the options and eliciting the values, work with the patient to identify which option best fits their goals, and offer a recommendation that is explicitly grounded in their stated values. Check that the decision is consistent with the values the patient expressed: if they prioritized avoiding harm, does the chosen option minimize harm? If they prioritized extending life, does it maximize that outcome? Recognize that some patients want the clinician to make the decision for them, and that this is a legitimate preference — but even then, the decision should be made in light of the patient's values, with their assent, and with the option to revisit it. Some patients need time to decide, and offering a deferred decision with a defined follow-up is often better than forcing a choice in the moment. Avoid both paternalism (deciding for the patient without their input) and abandonment (presenting options without support or recommendation) — the goal is a guided, values-concordant decision.

### Use Decision Aids and Structured Tools to Support the Conversation

Decision aids — structured tools that present the options, the probabilities, and the value questions in a standardized format — improve the quality of shared decisions by ensuring that patients receive complete and balanced information and that the conversation covers the key elements. Use validated decision aids where they exist for the decision at hand (statins, anticoagulants, cancer screening, prostate and breast cancer treatment, joint replacement), because they have been shown to improve knowledge, to reduce decisional conflict, and to produce decisions more concordant with patient values. Adapt the use of the aid to the patient and the setting — some patients benefit from reviewing it before the visit, others during it. Even without a formal aid, structure the conversation: define the decision, present the options and tradeoffs, elicit values, integrate, and decide. Document the conversation and the reasoning, including the options discussed, the patient's expressed values, and the basis for the chosen decision.

### Manage Disagreement, Family Dynamics, and the Patient Who Cannot Decide

Shared decisions are complicated by disagreement — between the patient and the family, between the patient and the clinician, or within the care team — and by the patient who lacks the capacity to decide. When the patient and family disagree, recognize that the patient's autonomous decision takes precedence (where they have capacity), and work to understand and mediate the family's concerns without overriding the patient. When the patient and clinician disagree, explore the reasons, ensure the patient understands the tradeoffs, and accept a decision that is informed and values-concordant even if it differs from the recommendation. When the patient lacks capacity, use the principles of surrogate decision-making: identify the legally authorized surrogate, use the patient's known wishes (advance directives, prior statements) to guide a substituted judgment, and where these are unknown, decide in the patient's best interests through a structured best-interests process. Assess and document capacity where it is in question. In all cases, document the decision, the reasoning, and the values that informed it.

## Common Traps

### Collapsing Shared Decision-Making Into End-of-Process Informed Consent

Presenting the chosen treatment and asking the patient to sign, rather than engaging them in defining the problem and weighing options. Counter by recognizing preference-sensitive decisions early and engaging the patient from the start.

### Framing Options in a Way That Biases the Decision

Using relative risks, loaded language, or unbalanced presentation to steer the patient toward the clinician's preferred option. Counter by using absolute risks, natural frequencies, consistent denominators, and neutral language.

### Presenting Options Without Eliciting or Integrating Values

Giving the patient a list of options and asking them to choose without understanding what matters to them. Counter by eliciting values explicitly and integrating them into a values-concordant recommendation.

### Abandoning the Patient to Choose Among Things They Do Not Understand

Presenting options so neutrally that the patient is left unsupported and anxious. Counter by checking understanding with teach-back, using visual aids, and offering a recommendation grounded in the patient's values.

### Assuming the Patient's Values Match the Clinician's

Recommending based on what the clinician would want, without discovering what the patient actually values. Counter by asking open questions about what matters most and listening to the answer.

### Failing to Document the Decision and Its Reasoning

Recording only the chosen plan without the options discussed, the values elicited, and the basis for the decision. Counter by documenting the conversation, the options, the values, and the preference-concordant reasoning.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize this as a preference-sensitive decision requiring shared decision-making, rather than a decision with a single evidence-based answer?
- Did I frame the decision and the options neutrally, using absolute risks, natural frequencies, and consistent denominators, avoiding language that biases the patient?
- Did I communicate the risks, benefits, and tradeoffs in plain language, using visual aids and teach-back to confirm understanding?
- Did I elicit the patient's values, goals, risk tolerance, and life circumstances with open questions before recommending?
- Did I integrate the evidence with the patient's values to offer a recommendation that is explicitly grounded in what matters to them?
- Did I use a validated decision aid or a structured conversation that covered the key elements of the decision?
- Did I manage disagreement (with family, within the team, or with the patient) by exploring reasons and respecting the patient's autonomous, informed choice?
- Did I document the options discussed, the values elicited, and the reasoning behind the preference-concordant decision?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
