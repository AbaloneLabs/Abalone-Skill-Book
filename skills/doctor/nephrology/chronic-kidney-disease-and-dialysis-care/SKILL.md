# Chronic Kidney Disease and Dialysis Care

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) management is where long-term judgment separates good care from harm. The temptation is to treat CKD as a static number to monitor, when in fact it is a progressive disease with modifiable trajectory and a cascade of complications that each require their own management. Patients lose renal function silently for years, present late, and then face either a well-prepared, planned transition to dialysis or transplant — or an emergent, catheter-based, higher-mortality start that was entirely preventable. The clinician's job is twofold: slow progression aggressively so that dialysis is delayed or avoided, and prepare the patient so that if dialysis becomes necessary it begins safely with mature access and informed choice of modality. For patients already on dialysis, the work shifts to access preservation, complication recognition, and transplant readiness. This skill covers CKD staging, progression slowing, complication management, dialysis preparation and modality choice, dialysis access problems, and transplant referral timing.

## Core Rules

### Stage CKD by eGFR and Albuminuria, and Act on Both Axes

CKD is staged by eGFR (G1 ≥90, G2 60–89, G3a 45–59, G3b 30–44, G4 15–29, G5 <15 mL/min/1.73m²) and categorized by albuminuria (A1 <30, A2 30–300, A3 >300 mg/g). Both axes independently predict progression and mortality, and a patient with G2 and A3 albuminuria is at far higher risk than a patient with G3a and A1. Do not classify risk by eGFR alone. The KDIGO heat map combining the two guides monitoring frequency and referral intensity. Confirm chronicity (abnormality present for >3 months) before labeling CKD, using imaging, prior labs, and chronicity markers. Reassess eGFR with the trend over months, not a single value — a single fluctuation may reflect AKI on CKD and warrants investigation, not dismissal. Patients with rapidly progressive decline (>5 mL/min/year, or sustained decline despite therapy) warrant urgent nephrology referral.

### Slow Progression with a Bundle, Not a Single Intervention

CKD progression slows only when multiple interventions stack. The evidence-based bundle is: rigorous blood pressure control (target <120/80 mmHg systolic per SPRINT-style data in many patients, individualized for frailty and orthostasis), maximally tolerated RAAS blockade with an ACE inhibitor or ARB (these reduce proteinuria and intraglomerular pressure and are first-line for albuminuric CKD, especially diabetic), SGLT2 inhibitors in appropriate patients (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin now indicated for CKD regardless of diabetes given their proven renoprotection), glycemic control in diabetes, smoking cessation, and avoidance of nephrotoxins including chronic NSAID use. Do not abandon RAAS blockade at the first creatinine bump — a rise of up to 30% after initiation is acceptable and expected; stop only if hyperkalemia is refractory or rise exceeds this. Combine interventions deliberately and titrate to targets, rechecking renal function and potassium 1–2 weeks after each change. Each component adds incremental benefit; prescribing one and declaring the work done leaves most of the protection on the table.

### Manage Complications Proactively, Not Reactively

CKD complications appear in a predictable sequence and should be screened for and treated before they cause symptoms. Anemia of CKD (typically when eGFR <60) presents with fatigue and, if severe, worsens cardiac output; investigate iron studies first (iron deficiency is common and treatable), then use erythropoiesis-stimulating agents when iron-replete with Hb typically below 100–110 g/L, avoiding over-correction above 115 g/L which increases thromboembolic and cardiovascular risk. Chronic kidney disease–mineral and bone disorder (CKD-MBD) emerges as eGFR falls: monitor phosphate, calcium, and parathyroid hormone (PTH) starting at G3, control phosphate with dietary restriction and phosphate binders (prefer non-calcium binders in hypercalcemic or vascular-calcification-prone patients to avoid calcium loading), and treat secondary hyperparathyroidism with calcitriol or vitamin D analogs guided by PTH trends. Metabolic acidosis (typically when bicarbonate <22 mmol/L) accelerates progression and muscle wasting; treat with oral sodium bicarbonate to maintain bicarbonate ≥22. Hypertension and volume overload worsen as GFR falls and often require loop diuretics. Each complication has a threshold and a target — manage to those targets, not to symptoms, because by the time symptoms appear the damage is advanced.

### Prepare for Dialysis Early: Access Matures Over Months

The single most modifiable determinant of early dialysis survival is starting with mature arteriovenous access rather than a tunneled catheter. Arteriovenous fistulas take 2–6 months to mature and may fail, requiring a secondary procedure; grafts take weeks; catheters work immediately but carry far higher infection, thrombosis, and mortality risk. Therefore, plan access when eGFR approaches 20–25 mL/min: refer for vascular surgery evaluation, map veins with duplex ultrasound, and place the fistula early enough to mature before it is needed. "Late referral" — first nephrology contact within 3–6 months of dialysis start — is associated with catheter starts, higher early mortality, and lost modality choice, and is largely preventable. Do not wait for symptoms to start preparation; by the time the patient is uremic, it is too late for planned access.

### Guide Modality Choice with Education, Not Assumption

Dialysis is not one treatment — it is a choice among hemodialysis (in-center or home), peritoneal dialysis, conservative (non-dialytic) management, and transplant. Each has different demands, outcomes, and fit. Provide structured dialysis education early (ideally at eGFR <30) so the patient can make an informed choice rather than defaulting to in-center hemodialysis. Home modalities (peritoneal dialysis, home hemodialysis) offer better quality of life and, in many patients, comparable or better survival, and should be offered to all medically suitable patients, not only those who "seem motivated." Conservative management is a legitimate choice for frail elderly patients with limited life expectancy where dialysis may not extend meaningful life. Present options honestly, including the burden and prognosis of each, and respect informed refusal. Document the decision and revisit it as circumstances change.

### Preserve and Protect Dialysis Access

For patients already on dialysis, the access is the lifeline and the most common source of complications. For arteriovenous fistulas and grafts, protect them zealously: no blood pressures, no venipuncture, no peripheral IVs in the access arm; assess the access regularly for thrill, bruit, and quality; and recognize signs of dysfunction — loss of thrill, prolonged bleeding after needle removal, arm swelling (suggesting central venous stenosis), or difficulty cannulating — as urgent problems requiring vascular access review. Catheters carry high infection risk: any dialysis patient with fever or rigors must be evaluated for catheter-related bloodstream infection, with blood cultures drawn and empiric antibiotics covering gram-positive and gram-negative organisms without delay. Monitor for access-related steal syndrome (hand ischemia), aneurysms, and infection. Access failure is a major cause of hospitalization and mortality; do not treat access problems casually.

### Refer for Transplant Early; Listing Takes Time

Transplantation offers superior survival and quality of life compared with dialysis for most suitable candidates, but evaluation and listing take months to years, and waiting lists are long. Refer for transplant evaluation when eGFR is below 20 mL/min, before dialysis start, so that the patient can be listed for preemptive transplant if possible. Evaluate candidacy honestly: absolute and relative contraindications (active malignancy, uncontrolled infection, severe cardiovascular disease, non-adherence, limited life expectancy) must be weighed, but do not exclude patients reflexively based on age alone — physiologic frailty matters more than chronologic age. Address modifiable barriers (smoking cessation, weight loss, infection clearance, cardiac optimization) as part of the referral. Living donor transplant should be explored for every candidate, as it offers the best outcomes and shortest wait; facilitate donor education proactively.

## Common Traps

### Staging by eGFR Alone and Ignoring Albuminuria

Classifying risk by GFR category while missing high-risk A3 albuminuria. Counter by always combining the two axes using the KDIGO heat map to guide intensity.

### Abandoning RAAS Blockade at the First Creatinine Rise

Stopping ACE inhibitors or ARBs because creatinine bumped 15%, losing the most protective therapy. Counter by accepting up to 30% rise and stopping only for refractory hyperkalemia or excessive rise.

### Forgetting SGLT2 Inhibitors in Non-diabetic CKD

Assuming SGLT2 inhibitors are diabetes drugs only. Counter by prescribing them for appropriate CKD patients regardless of diabetes, given proven renoprotection.

### Late Dialysis Referral and Catheter Starts

Delaying nephrology referral until the patient is symptomatic, forcing emergent catheter-based dialysis. Counter by referring for access planning at eGFR 20–25 and starting education at eGFR <30.

### Defaulting to In-center Hemodialysis Without Offering Home Modalities

Assuming the patient wants or needs in-center hemodialysis. Counter by providing structured modality education including peritoneal dialysis and home hemodialysis for all suitable patients.

### Treating Access Problems Casually

Dismissing loss of thrill, arm swelling, or fever in a dialysis patient as minor. Counter by treating access dysfunction and suspected catheter infection as urgent and escalating to the dialysis unit or vascular access team.

### Calcium Overload from Calcium-Based Phosphate Binders

Prescribing calcium-based binders in patients with hypercalcemia or vascular calcification risk, worsening calcific burden. Counter by preferring non-calcium binders (sevelamer, lanthanum) in those patients.

## Self-Check

- Have I staged CKD using both eGFR (G1–G5) and albuminuria (A1–A3) categories and confirmed chronicity, rather than relying on a single value?
- Am I applying the full progression-slowing bundle — BP target, RAAS blockade, SGLT2 inhibitor where appropriate, glycemic control, nephrotoxin avoidance — rather than a single intervention?
- Am I screening for and treating complications (anemia, CKD-MBD, acidosis, hypertension) to defined targets before symptoms appear?
- Have I initiated dialysis access planning at eGFR 20–25 and provided structured modality education at eGFR <30 to avoid late referral and catheter starts?
- For dialysis patients, am I protecting access (no BP or IVs in access arm), monitoring for dysfunction, and treating suspected catheter infection as an emergency?
- Have I referred appropriate candidates for transplant evaluation at eGFR <20 and explored living donor options proactively?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
