# Electrolyte and Acid-Base Management in Kidney Disease

Electrolyte and acid-base disturbances are where kidney disease becomes immediately dangerous and where well-intentioned treatment causes the most iatrogenic harm. The kidney regulates potassium, sodium, acid-base balance, phosphate, and calcium; when renal function fails, each of these can derange, and each has a treatment that can itself cause harm if applied too fast, too slow, or without understanding the underlying disturbance. The two cardinal errors are opposite in nature but equally lethal: under-treating a disturbance that is about to cause arrhythmia or seizure, and over-correcting sodium so rapidly that the brain demyelinates. The clinician must know not only the target value but the safe rate of change, the order of interventions, and when the disturbance has crossed from "manage medically" to "needs dialysis." This skill covers stepwise hyperkalemia management, CKD-related metabolic acidosis, hyponatremia correction limits, phosphate-calcium balance, and when to escalate to nephrology.

## Core Rules

### Treat Hyperkalemia in a Fixed Stepwise Sequence

Hyperkalemia is the electrolyte emergency most likely to cause sudden death in renal patients, and it must be managed by protocol, not improvisation. First, assess threat: get an ECG immediately. Peaked T waves, PR prolongation, QRS widening, or a sine-wave pattern indicate imminent arrhythmic risk and demand immediate myocardial stabilization with intravenous calcium (calcium gluconate is safer peripherally; calcium chloride via central line if arrest is imminent). Calcium does not lower potassium — it buys time by stabilizing the myocardial membrane. Second, shift potassium into cells: intravenous insulin with dextrose (the most reliable shifter; ensure dextrose to prevent hypoglycemia and recheck glucose), nebulized or intravenous beta-2 agonist (salbutamol), and sodium bicarbonate if the patient is acidotic (bicarbonate shifts potassium and corrects acidosis, but works poorly when pH is normal). Third, remove potassium from the body: loop diuretics if the patient makes urine, gastrointestinal cation exchange resins (patiromer, sodium zirconium) for subacute removal, and dialysis for definitive removal in refractory, severe, or anuric cases. Fourth, recheck potassium within 1–2 hours of treatment and monitor on telemetry until stable — shifting agents wear off and rebound is real. Identify and remove the precipitant (missed dialysis, potassium supplements, potassium-sparing diuretics, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, digitalis toxicity, hemolysis, tissue breakdown). Never declare hyperkalemia resolved after a single intervention without a repeat level.

### Correct Metabolic Acidosis to a Bicarbonate Target, Not to Normal

CKD causes a high-anion-gap metabolic acidosis from reduced acid excretion, and chronic acidosis accelerates CKD progression, causes muscle wasting, worsens bone disease, and contributes to hyperkalemia by shifting potassium out of cells. Treat chronic metabolic acidosis when serum bicarbonate is below 22 mmol/L, using oral sodium bicarbonate (typically 0.6–1.2 g three times daily, titrated) to maintain bicarbonate at or above 22. Do not chase a normal bicarbonate of 24–26, which risks volume overload from the sodium load and is not clearly beneficial. In acute severe acidosis (pH <7.1–7.2, often with concurrent hyperkalemia or shock), intravenous bicarbonate has a role but is hazardous: it can cause hypernatremia, volume overload, hypocalcemia (worsening tetany), and overshoot alkalosis, and it must be given with close monitoring. Always interpret acid-base status with a venous or arterial blood gas including the anion gap and, when ambiguous, the delta-delta ratio to detect mixed disturbances. Severe or refractory acidosis, especially with volume overload or hyperkalemia, is an indication for dialysis — escalate to nephrology rather than escalating bicarbonate indefinitely.

### Respect Sodium Correction Limits to Avoid Osmotic Demyelination

Hyponatremia correction is the single most dangerous electrolyte treatment to get wrong, because correcting too fast causes osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis) — a devastating, often irreversible brain injury. The safe correction limit is generally an increase in serum sodium of no more than 8–10 mmol/L in any 24-hour period, and the risk is highest in patients at elevated risk (severe malnutrition, alcohol use disorder, liver disease, hypokalemia, burns). The key insight: in chronic hyponatremia (present >48 hours, which is the default assumption in CKD), the brain has adapted, and rapid correction is catastrophic; in acute hyponatremia (<48 hours, e.g., postoperative, ecstasy use), the brain has not adapted and faster correction is tolerated. Use hypertonic saline only for severe symptomatic hyponatremia (seizure, coma) and in small boluses (100–150 mL of 3% saline) with frequent sodium rechecks (every 2–4 hours), not as a continuous infusion left to run. Stop hypertonic saline once symptoms resolve or the correction limit is approached. In CKD, hyponatremia is often dilutional (volume overload with heart failure or cirrhosis) or from sodium wasting; treat the underlying state — fluid restriction for hypervolemic hyponatremia, address volume depletion for hypovolemic — rather than reflexively giving saline. If correction exceeds the safe limit, relower sodium with desmopressin and free water and involve nephrology urgently. Desmopressin can also be used proactively to prevent over-correction during hypertonic saline therapy.

### Manage Phosphate and Calcium as an Interconnected System

In CKD, phosphate retention, hypocalcemia, and secondary hyperparathyroidism are intertwined as chronic kidney disease–mineral and bone disorder (CKD-MBD), and treating one axis in isolation unbalances the others. Monitor phosphate, calcium, and parathyroid hormone (PTH) starting at CKD G3 and manage to targets. Control phosphate first with dietary restriction and phosphate binders taken with meals; choose the binder by calcium status — non-calcium binders (sevelamer, lanthanum, iron-based) for patients who are hypercalcemic, calcified, or at vascular risk, to avoid calcium loading that worsens vascular calcification; calcium-based binders (calcium acetate, carbonate) for patients who are hypocalcemic. Treat secondary hyperparathyroidism with calcitriol or vitamin D analogs guided by PTH trends and calcium/phosphate, because oversuppression of PTH causes adynamic bone disease. Beware the calcium-phosphate product: when calcium × phosphate (both in mg/dL) exceeds roughly 55, the risk of metastatic calcification and calciphylaxis rises sharply. Severe hyperphosphatemia (e.g., tumor lysis, rhabdomyolysis) is a dialysis indication. Do not correct hypocalcemia aggressively in the setting of high phosphate, because calcium-phosphate precipitation worsens harm — lower phosphate first.

### Know When Electrolyte Disturbances Cross the Line to Dialysis

Medical management buys time, but some disturbances require renal replacement therapy for definitive correction. Dialysis is indicated for hyperkalemia that is severe (typically >6.5 mmol/L with ECG changes), refractory to medical therapy, or occurring in an oliguric/anuric patient; for severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1–7.2) refractory to bicarbonate, especially with concurrent volume overload or hyperkalemia; for severe hyperphosphatemia (as in tumor lysis syndrome) causing symptomatic hypocalcemia or AKI; and for volume overload refractory to diuretics. The decision weighs the disturbance severity, the trajectory, the response to medical therapy, and the patient's overall status and goals of care. Involve nephrology at the first sign that a disturbance is trending toward dialysis, not after the patient is in extremis — access placement and planning take time. In a patient already on dialysis, missed sessions are the most common cause of hyperkalemia and volume overload; the fix is urgent dialysis, not escalating medical therapy.

## Common Traps

### Treating Hyperkalemia Without Rechecking

Giving insulin-dextrose and assuming the problem is solved, then missing rebound. Counter by rechecking potassium within 1–2 hours and monitoring on telemetry until stable.

### Using Bicarbonate in Non-acidotic Hyperkalemia

Relying on sodium bicarbonate to shift potassium when the patient is not acidotic, where it works poorly. Counter by using insulin-dextrose and beta-agonists as primary shifters, reserving bicarbonate for the acidotic patient.

### Over-correcting Hyponatremia

Giving hypertonic saline as an unmonitored continuous infusion and exceeding the 8–10 mmol/L per 24-hour limit, causing osmotic demyelination. Counter by using small boluses with frequent sodium rechecks and stopping at symptom resolution or limit approach.

### Chasing a Normal Bicarbonate in CKD

Overtreating chronic metabolic acidosis to bicarbonate 24–26, causing sodium and volume overload. Counter by targeting bicarbonate ≥22 and titrating to the lowest effective dose.

### Giving Calcium-Based Binders to Hypercalcemic Patients

Worsening vascular calcification by adding calcium load. Counter by preferring non-calcium binders in hypercalcemic or calcification-prone patients.

### Correcting Hypocalcemia Before Lowering Phosphate

Causing calcium-phosphate precipitation and metastatic calcification by giving calcium in a high-phosphate state. Counter by lowering phosphate first, then addressing calcium.

### Delaying Nephrology Referral Until Disturbances Are Emergent

Managing escalating hyperkalemia or acidosis to the brink before consulting, leaving no time for dialysis planning. Counter by involving nephrology at the first trend toward dialysis-requiring disturbance.

## Self-Check

- For hyperkalemia, did I get an ECG, stabilize the myocardium with calcium if indicated, shift then remove potassium, and recheck the level within 1–2 hours?
- Am I treating CKD metabolic acidosis to a bicarbonate target of ≥22 rather than chasing normal, and recognizing severe acute acidosis as a dialysis indication?
- For hyponatremia, am I respecting the 8–10 mmol/L per 24-hour correction limit, using small hypertonic saline boluses with frequent rechecks, and prepared to relower with desmopressin if over-correcting?
- Am I managing phosphate and calcium as an interconnected CKD-MBD system, choosing binders by calcium status, and watching the calcium-phosphate product?
- Have I recognized when a disturbance (refractory hyperkalemia, severe acidosis, hyperphosphatemia, volume overload) has crossed the line to dialysis and involved nephrology early?
- Have I identified and addressed the precipitant of the disturbance (missed dialysis, offending drug, underlying volume or acid-base state), not just the number?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
