# Acute Kidney Injury and Fluid Management

Acute kidney injury (AKI) is one of the most common and most mishandled inpatient problems. The harm comes less from missing the diagnosis than from misjudging it: giving fluids to a volume-overloaded patient because "AKI needs fluids," withholding fluids from a genuinely volume-depleted patient out of fear of overload, missing a post-renal obstruction because no one did a bladder scan, or chasing the creatinine instead of the cause. AKI is rarely a primary disease — it is usually the kidney's response to an insult upstream (sepsis, hypovolemia, nephrotoxins, obstruction) or downstream (heart failure, abdominal compartment). The clinician's job is not to "treat the creatinine" but to find and fix the insult while protecting the kidney from further harm and recognizing when the kidneys can no longer sustain life on their own. This skill covers disciplined AKI staging, the pre/intrinsic/post-renal workup, fluid responsiveness, nephrotoxin avoidance, hyperkalemia management, and the indications for renal replacement therapy (RRT).

## Core Rules

### Stage AKI Objectively with KDIGO, Then Treat the Cause

Use KDIGO criteria to stage AKI by serum creatinine rise and urine output, not by gestalt. Stage 1 is a rise of ≥26.5 µmol/L (0.3 mg/dL) in 48 hours or 1.5–1.9× baseline; Stage 2 is 2.0–2.9× baseline; Stage 3 is 3× baseline, creatinine ≥353.6 µmol/L (4.0 mg/dL) with an acute rise, initiation of dialysis, or urine output <0.3 mL/kg/h for ≥24 hours (anuria ≥12 hours). Staging matters because higher stages carry higher mortality and dictate monitoring intensity. But staging is descriptive, not therapeutic — it tells you severity, not etiology. Once staged, pivot immediately to cause. AKI is categorized as pre-renal (hypoperfusion), intrinsic (intrinsic parenchymal disease: acute tubular necrosis, acute interstitial nephritis, glomerulonephritis, vasculitis), and post-renal (obstruction). Most inpatient AKI is a mix; do not force a single bucket. Always establish a baseline creatinine — without it you cannot stage AKI or distinguish it from undiagnosed chronic kidney disease (CKD). If no baseline exists, use the MDRD equation back-calculated to an eGFR of 75 to estimate a presumed baseline, and revisit as data accrues.

### Work Up All Three Compartments; Do Not Stop at Pre-renal

Pre-renal is the most common label and the most over-applied. A patient who is hypovolemic, septic, and on an NSAID has elements of all three compartments. For pre-renal, assess volume status clinically (jugular venous pressure, orthostatics, fluid balance, weight), consider a fluid challenge if genuinely depleted, and identify the source of loss (hemorrhage, GI losses, third-spacing, diuretics, septic vasodilation). For intrinsic, review the medication list and recent exposures for nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, contrast, aminoglycosides, vancomycin, tenofovir, proton pump inhibitors causing acute interstitial nephritis), send a urinalysis with microscopy early — muddy brown granular casts suggest acute tubular necrosis, white blood cells and eosinophils suggest interstitial nephritis, dysmorphic red cells and red cell casts suggest glomerulonephritis — and consider ANA, ANCA, complement, anti-GBM, and hepatitis serologies when glomerular disease is plausible. For post-renal, do not rely on history; perform a bladder scan or bedside ultrasound in any unexplained AKI, especially in older men, patients with pelvic malignancy, or anyone with a single functioning kidney. A post-renal cause that is relieved early is among the most reversible forms of AKI — missing it is indefensible.

### Manage Fluids by Responsiveness, Not by Diagnosis

The single most dangerous reflex in AKI is "give fluids." Fluid is a drug with a narrow therapeutic window: too little perpetuates hypoperfusion and worsens pre-renal injury; too much causes pulmonary edema, worsens abdominal compartment pressure, and can itself prolong AKI. Assess fluid responsiveness before bolusing repeatedly — passive leg raise, pulse pressure or stroke volume variation in ventilated patients, or a structured small bolus (250–500 mL crystalloid) with reassessment of mean arterial pressure, heart rate, and urine output within 30 minutes. Do not continue bolusing because "the creatinine is still rising"; if the patient is no longer responsive or is now congested, further fluid harms. In septic or vasodilatory shock, pair fluids with vasopressors early to restore mean arterial pressure rather than chasing pressure with volume. Track cumulative fluid balance daily; a positive balance of several liters is a warning sign that fluid is now contributing to harm. When in doubt about volume status in a complex patient, point-of-care ultrasound (IVC, B-lines, cardiac) and, in the ICU, advanced hemodynamics provide better guidance than clinical exam alone — escalate to critical care or nephrology for this assessment rather than guessing.

### Eliminate Nephrotoxins Aggressively and Proactively

Nephrotoxic injury is cumulative and iatrogenic, and it is the most preventable cause of AKI. On any AKI, perform a hard stop review of every medication: hold NSAIDs entirely (including over-the-counter and PRN orders), review ACE inhibitors and ARBs (hold in acute prerenal states, severe AKI, or hyperkalemia), avoid or minimize iodinated contrast (use alternative imaging or, if essential, use lowest dose with pre-procedure hydration), audit aminoglycoside and vancomycin dosing against levels, avoid tenofovir and other direct tubular toxins where possible, and stop suspected culprit drugs in interstitial nephritis (commonly PPIs, antibiotics, NSAIDs). Use contrast only when the benefit clearly outweighs the risk, and document that judgment. Do not restart nephrotoxins reflexively as the patient improves — confirm renal recovery first. Medication reconciliation at every transition of care is where nephrotoxins sneak back in; own that step.

### Treat Hyperkalemia in a Defined Stepwise Order

Hyperkalemia in AKI can kill within minutes through cardiac arrhythmia, so treat it as a protocol, not an afterthought. First, get an ECG immediately — peaked T waves, PR prolongation, QRS widening, or a sine wave demand emergent action. Stabilize the myocardium with intravenous calcium (calcium gluconate or calcium chloride) in any patient with ECG changes; this does not lower potassium but prevents arrhythmic death. Then shift potassium into cells with insulin-dextrose, beta-2 agonists (nebulized salbutamol), and consider sodium bicarbonate if the patient is acidotic. Then remove potassium from the body with loop diuretics (if the patient still makes urine) and, definitively, with dialysis if hyperkalemia is severe, refractory, or the patient is oliguric/anuric. Recheck potassium within 1–2 hours of treatment, because the effect of shifting agents is transient and rebound occurs. Never declare hyperkalemia "fixed" after a single insulin-dextrose dose without a repeat level. If potassium is ≥6.5 mmol/L with ECG changes, or refractory, or accompanied by severe acidosis or volume overload, escalate to nephrology for RRT without delay.

### Recognize Dialysis Indications Early: AEIOU

Do not wait for the patient to crash before involving nephrology. The classic indications for emergent RRT are summarized as AEIOU: Acidosis (severe metabolic acidosis refractory to bicarbonate, pH <7.1 or worsening), Electrolytes (refractory hyperkalemia or other severe disturbance), Ingestion/toxin (dialyzable poison such as lithium, methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates), Overload (volume overload refractory to diuretics with pulmonary edema), and Uremia (symptomatic uremia — pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding). These are triggers for consultation and planning, not absolute thresholds; the decision to start RRT also weighs trajectory, reversibility of the AKI, and the patient's goals of care. Involve nephrology the moment a patient is trending toward any of these, not after the patient meets all criteria. In a critically ill patient with multi-organ failure and rising potassium, acidosis, and oliguria, the dialysis discussion should begin hours before it becomes emergent.

### Distinguish AKI from CKD and from AKI on CKD

AKI and CKD are not opposites — they coexist and interconvert. AKI on CKD is common and dangerous: a patient with baseline eGFR 30 who develops AKI has less renal reserve and may not recover. Establish baseline renal function from old records whenever possible. Findings suggesting chronicity include small echogenic kidneys on ultrasound, long-standing anemia, hyperphosphatemia with hypocalcemia, and secondary hyperparathyroidism. A single creatinine value tells you almost nothing without a baseline and a trend. When AKI and CKD coexist, manage both: treat the acute insult aggressively to maximize recovery, and address chronic progression (see the CKD skill). After an AKI episode, plan follow-up renal function checks for weeks to months, because AKI increases the long-term risk of progression to CKD even when creatinine "normalizes" at discharge.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Creatinine Instead of the Cause

Chasing the number with fluids or dopamine analogs without identifying and fixing the underlying insult. Counter by treating AKI as a symptom: find the sepsis, the obstruction, the nephrotoxin, the heart failure.

### Reflexive Fluids in Every AKI

Assuming all AKI is pre-renal and bolusing repeatedly, causing pulmonary edema and abdominal compartment syndrome. Counter by assessing fluid responsiveness before each bolus and tracking cumulative balance.

### Missing Post-renal Obstruction

Attributing AKI to pre-renal or sepsis without a bladder scan or ultrasound, especially in elderly men or pelvic malignancy patients. Counter by imaging the urinary tract in every unexplained AKI.

### Restarting Nephrotoxins at Transitions of Care

Holding an NSAID or ACE inhibitor during the AKI admission, then having it silently restarted at discharge or transfer. Counter by owning medication reconciliation and documenting hold/avoid orders explicitly.

### Declaring Hyperkalemia Fixed After One Treatment

Accepting a post-treatment clinical improvement without a repeat potassium, then being surprised by rebound arrhythmia. Counter by rechecking potassium within 1–2 hours and monitoring until stable.

### Waiting Too Long to Call Nephrology

Treating an escalating patient to the brink of arrest before consulting, leaving no time for access placement and planning. Counter by involving nephrology at the first sign of an AEIOU trajectory.

### Confusing AKI with CKD

Labeling an elevated creatinine "CKD" without a baseline, or "AKI" in a patient with small kidneys and chronic metabolic bone disease. Counter by establishing baseline and chronicity markers in every renal evaluation.

## Self-Check

- Have I staged the AKI objectively with KDIGO criteria using a confirmed baseline creatinine and urine output?
- Have I actively worked up all three compartments (pre-renal, intrinsic, post-renal) rather than defaulting to a single label?
- Have I assessed fluid responsiveness before each fluid bolus and tracked cumulative fluid balance for overload?
- Have I performed a hard-stop nephrotoxin review and held all non-essential nephrotoxic medications, with explicit hold orders at transitions?
- For hyperkalemia, did I get an ECG, stabilize the myocardium with calcium if indicated, shift then remove potassium, and recheck the level within 1–2 hours?
- Have I reviewed the AEIOU indications and involved nephrology early when the patient is trending toward dialysis, not after they meet all criteria?
- Have I distinguished AKI from CKD and from AKI on CKD using baseline data and chronicity markers, and planned post-discharge renal follow-up?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
