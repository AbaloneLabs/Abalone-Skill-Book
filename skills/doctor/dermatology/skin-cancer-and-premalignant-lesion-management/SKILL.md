# Skin Cancer and Premalignant Lesion Management

Skin cancer is the most common malignancy in many populations, and melanoma is among the most lethal if not caught early — yet the window for cure is wide when lesions are recognized at thin stages. The recurring failure in skin cancer care is not lack of awareness; it is the casual dismissal of a changing lesion as benign, the use of a biopsy technique that compromises melanoma staging, the misclassification of a keratinocyte carcinoma as a benign growth, and the absence of a structured surveillance plan for high-risk patients. This skill covers the structured evaluation of suspicious lesions, the clinical distinction among melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, and squamous cell carcinoma, the biopsy decision and technique, and the surveillance and referral pathways that prevent delay.

## Core Rules

### Apply ABCDE and the Ugly Duckling Sign to Every Pigmented Lesion

For any pigmented lesion, apply the ABCDE criteria explicitly: Asymmetry (one half unlike the other), Border irregularity (notched, blurred, or ragged), Color variegation (multiple shades of brown, black, blue, red, or white), Diameter (traditionally over 6 mm, though smaller melanomas occur), and Evolution (any change in size, shape, color, elevation, or new symptoms such as itching or bleeding). Evolution is the single most powerful criterion because melanoma is, by definition, a dynamic lesion. Pair ABCDE with the ugly duckling sign: each person's nevi tend to share a common pattern, so the lesion that looks different from the patient's other moles is the one to biopsy, even if it does not meet every ABCDE threshold. Patient-reported change is a strong signal — take it seriously and do not reassure on appearance alone.

### Distinguish Melanoma From Basal and Squamous Cell Carcinoma by Morphology

The three common skin cancers have characteristic morphologies that guide the biopsy decision. Melanoma presents as an asymmetric, variably colored, enlarging pigmented lesion with irregular borders; nodular melanoma may be uniformly dark or even amelanotic and is especially dangerous because it grows vertically and is easily dismissed. Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) classically appears as a pearly, translucent papule or nodule with a rolled border, telangiectasias, and central ulceration, most often on sun-exposed face; it is locally destructive but rarely metastasizes. Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) presents as a scaly, crusted, keratotic, or ulcerated plaque or nodule, often arising in actinic keratoses or scars, and carries a real, if lower than melanoma, risk of metastasis, especially on the lip, ear, scalp, and in immunosuppressed patients. Recognize that amelanotic and nodular variants break the textbook pattern, so any solitary, growing, bleeding, or non-healing lesion warrants biopsy regardless of classic features.

### Match Biopsy Technique to the Suspected Diagnosis

The biopsy technique must preserve the information needed for diagnosis and staging. For suspected melanoma, perform an excisional biopsy — full-thickness, complete removal of the lesion with a narrow margin of normal skin — because Breslow depth, measured from the granular layer to the deepest tumor cell, is the dominant prognostic factor and must not be compromised. A shave biopsy that transects a melanoma can understate depth and upstage the patient at definitive surgery; a punch biopsy is acceptable only for large lesions where a full excision is impractical, sampling the most suspicious area at full thickness. For suspected BCC or SCC, a shave or punch biopsy of the representative area is usually sufficient for diagnosis, because depth and margins are reassessed at definitive treatment. When the diagnosis is uncertain and melanoma cannot be excluded, default to an excisional approach or refer to dermatology rather than risk a partial-thickness sample.

### Treat Actinic Keratoses as Premalignant and Stratify Risk

Actinic keratoses are premalignant keratinocyte lesions on chronically sun-exposed skin, with a small per-lesion annual risk of progression to squamous cell carcinoma. Treat them, but also use them as a marker of field cancerization and cumulative ultraviolet damage. Field therapy (such as topical 5-fluorouracil, imiquimod, or photodynamic therapy) addresses subclinical lesions and reduces new SCC development. Identify high-risk features that lower the threshold for biopsy rather than empiric treatment: induration, tenderness, rapid growth, bleeding, ulceration, or a thick hypertrophic base — any of these suggests evolution to invasive SCC and warrants biopsy. Counsel patients on sun protection and self-examination, because field cancerization implies ongoing risk across exposed skin.

### Stratify Patients for Surveillance Intensity

Surveillance must be matched to risk. High-risk patients include those with a personal history of melanoma, multiple or atypical (dysplastic) nevi, family history of melanoma in a first-degree relative, marked cumulative sun exposure, history of prior keratinocyte carcinoma, fair skin with tendency to burn, and immunosuppression (solid organ transplant, chronic immunosuppressive therapy, hematologic malignancy). For melanoma survivors, total-body skin examinations are typically recommended at intervals that shorten with higher-stage disease; for transplant recipients, the risk of aggressive SCC is markedly elevated and warrants frequent examination. Document a personalized surveillance plan with intervals, self-examination guidance, and clear criteria for earlier return. Surveillance is not a one-time event; it is a longitudinal program, and patients should leave every visit knowing when to return and what changes should prompt an earlier visit.

### Escalate to Dermatology, Surgery, or Oncology by Diagnosis and Stage

Define escalation pathways in advance. Suspected melanoma warrants urgent dermatology or surgical referral and an excisional biopsy, with subsequent management guided by Breslow depth — thin melanomas may need only wide local excision, while thicker lesions require sentinel lymph node biopsy and multidisciplinary planning. Suspected BCC and low-risk SCC can often be managed in primary care or dermatology with biopsy and definitive treatment; high-risk SCC (large size, deep invasion, immunosuppression, recurrent, or on a high-risk anatomic site) requires specialist management and consideration of nodal staging. Any lesion with diagnostic uncertainty, any pigmented lesion with concerning features, and any patient with a personal history of skin cancer plus a new suspicious lesion should be referred rather than observed. Do not let reassurance or scheduling delay shorten the window for curative treatment.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring a Patient About a Changing Pigmented Lesion

Patient-reported change — growth, color shift, itching, bleeding — is a strong predictor of melanoma and must not be dismissed because the lesion "looks benign." Counter by treating evolution as decisive and biopsying changing lesions, ideally with an excisional technique.

### Using a Shave Biopsy on a Lesion That Could Be Melanoma

Transection of a melanoma by shave biopsy compromises Breslow depth measurement and can worsen staging and outcome. Counter by defaulting to excisional or full-thickness punch biopsy whenever melanoma is plausible, and referring when unsure.

### Calling a Pearly Papule a "Cyst" or "Age Spot"

A pearly, telangiectatic, slowly enlarging facial papule is often basal cell carcinoma, not a benign cyst or sebaceous hyperplasia. Counter by biopsying any persistent, translucent, ulcerated, or slowly growing lesion on sun-exposed skin.

### Treating Actinic Keratoses Empirically When High-Risk Features Are Present

Induration, tenderness, bleeding, or rapid growth in a presumed actinic keratosis suggests progression to invasive SCC. Counter by biopsying rather than applying field therapy whenever high-risk features appear.

### Underestimating Squamous Cell Carcinoma Risk in Immunosuppressed Patients

Solid organ transplant recipients and chronically immunosuppressed patients have markedly elevated SCC risk, and their SCCs behave more aggressively. Counter by intensifying surveillance, lowering the biopsy threshold, and coordinating with transplant or dermatology services.

### Failing to Set a Surveillance Plan After a First Skin Cancer

Patients with one skin cancer are at high risk of subsequent primaries, and absence of a documented surveillance plan leads to delayed detection of recurrences and new lesions. Counter by documenting a personalized interval, self-examination guidance, and return precautions at every visit.

### Ignoring Atypical or Dysplastic Nevi as a Field Risk

Patients with many atypical nevi have a field risk of melanoma, not just risk at the biopsied lesion. Counter by using the ugly duckling sign across all nevi and scheduling total-body examinations rather than focusing only on the lesion of the day.

## Self-Check

- For every pigmented lesion, have I applied ABCDE and the ugly duckling sign, and treated patient-reported evolution as a decisive reason to biopsy?
- Have I distinguished melanoma from basal and squamous cell carcinoma by morphology, and recognized that amelanotic and nodular variants may break the textbook pattern?
- Have I chosen a biopsy technique that preserves melanoma staging — excisional or full-thickness — rather than a shave biopsy when melanoma is plausible?
- For actinic keratoses, have I biopsied rather than empirically treated when induration, tenderness, bleeding, or rapid growth suggests progression to SCC?
- Have I stratified the patient's surveillance intensity by personal and family history, sun exposure, atypical nevi, and immunosuppression, and documented a return interval?
- Have I escalated to dermatology, surgery, or oncology for suspected melanoma, high-risk SCC, diagnostic uncertainty, or new lesions in patients with prior skin cancer?
- Have I counseled the patient on sun protection and self-examination, and given clear return precautions for concerning changes?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
