# Common Skin Lesions and Rash Evaluation

Skin complaints are among the most common reasons patients seek care, and they are also among the most commonly misread. A rash is a visual diagnosis that depends on precise description, but many clinicians describe lesions imprecisely, jump to a label ("eczema," "drug rash") before fully characterizing what they see, or treat symptomatically without considering infection, malignancy, or life-threatening dermatoses. The recurring failure is not lack of dermatology knowledge in the abstract — it is skipping the disciplined morphologic description, underweighting distribution and evolution, and missing the small number of rashes where delay causes death or permanent scarring. This skill covers the structured evaluation of a rash or lesion, the inflammatory-versus-infectious-versus-neoplastic decision, the biopsy decision, and the must-not-miss rashes that require immediate escalation.

## Core Rules

### Describe Morphology Before Naming the Diagnosis

Resist the urge to label a rash before you have described it. Use precise primary lesion terminology — macule, patch, papule, plaque, nodule, vesicle, bulla, pustule, wheal, ulcer, atrophy — and then describe secondary features: scale, crust, erosion, excoriation, lichenification, fissure, eschar. State the color (erythematous, violaceous, hyperpigmented, hypopigmented, purpuric), the shape (annular, linear, targetoid, reticular, serpiginous, nummular), and the border quality (well-demarcated, ill-defined, raised, rolled). The single most common diagnostic error is naming a diagnosis from a glance and then rationalizing the morphology to fit. A faithful description, written before the diagnosis, lets you reconsider the case when the first guess is wrong, and it communicates reliably to a dermatologist when you escalate.

### Define Distribution and Symmetry Explicitly

Distribution often narrows the differential more than morphology alone. Specify whether the eruption is localized or generalized, flexural (atopic dermatitis) or extensor (psoriasis), acral, palms and soles, photo-distributed, dermatomal, linear, or intertriginous. Note symmetry: atopic dermatitis, psoriasis, and drug eruptions are typically bilateral and symmetric; tinea, contact dermatitis, and herpes simplex are often asymmetric or geometric. Distinguish a true dermatomal pattern (herpes zoster) from a linear pattern (phytophotodermatitis, contact). Consider what is spared — sun-protected areas in a photosensitive eruption, scalp and nails in psoriasis involvement — because spared regions are as informative as affected ones.

### Determine Whether the Lesion Is Inflammatory, Infectious, or Neoplastic

Force an explicit category judgment for every persistent or atypical lesion. Inflammatory dermatoses (eczema, psoriasis, lichen planus) tend to be symmetric, chronic, and pruritic, with characteristic morphology and distribution. Infectious processes (bacterial, fungal, viral, parasitic) are often asymmetric, may have a leading edge or focal origin, and may show pustules, honey-crusted erosions, or kerion. Neoplastic lesions — and this is where delay is most harmful — are typically solitary, progressive, non-resolving, and may show the ABCDE features of melanoma or the pearly, translucent, telangiectatic border of basal cell carcinoma. A lesion that does not behave like a benign inflammatory process (not resolving with appropriate therapy, growing, bleeding, ulcerating without trauma) must be reclassified toward neoplastic until proven otherwise and biopsied.

### Apply KOH, Culture, and Wood's Lamp to Confirm Infectious and Inflammatory Calls

When the diagnosis is uncertain, use the cheap, fast confirmatory tools before committing to empiric treatment. A potassium hydroxide (KOH) preparation distinguishes dermatophyte (tinea) from inflammatory annular plaques — tinea is routinely misdiagnosed as "ringworm treated with steroid" or, conversely, eczema treated with antifungal. Bacterial and viral cultures, Gram stain, and PCR support impetigo, staphylococcal infection, and herpes. Wood's lamp can highlight erythrasma, tinea capitis (certain species), and vitiligo. Do not prescribe oral antifungals or prolonged topical steroids on appearance alone when a bedside test could confirm or refute the diagnosis; steroid-treated tinea (tinea incognito) worsens and obscures the diagnosis.

### Recognize the Dangerous Rashes That Demand Immediate Escalation

Certain cutaneous presentations are emergencies where hours matter, and they must be named explicitly so they are not treated as ordinary rashes. Purpura and petechiae that do not blanch suggest meningococcemia, vasculitis, or disseminated intravascular coagulation; non-blanching purpura with fever is a medical emergency. A rapidly spreading, exquisitely tender erythematous area with systemic toxicity, bullae, or crepitus suggests necrotizing fasciitis and demands surgical evaluation, not dermatology. Mucosal involvement plus target or dusky macules raises Stevens-Johnson syndrome or toxic epidermal necrolysis. Diffuse erythroderma with skin pain and systemic symptoms raises staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome, toxic shock, or a severe drug reaction. For any of these, stop the suspect drug, obtain urgent dermatology or surgical consultation, and arrange transfer to a higher level of care — do not wait for a confirmatory biopsy if the patient is deteriorating.

### Decide on Biopsy Deliberately, and Choose the Right Technique

Biopsy is not a reflex; it is a decision with a right technique. Biopsy lesions that are solitary and progressive, that fail appropriate therapy, that show concerning pigment or vascular features, or where the differential includes malignancy or a serious inflammatory disorder requiring histology (pemphigus, pemphigoid, vasculitis). Match the technique to the question: shave biopsy is acceptable for raised, epidermal lesions like suspected basal or squamous cell carcinoma but is inappropriate for a lesion suspicious for melanoma, where a partial-thickness sample can compromise microstaging and worsen prognosis. Punch biopsy provides dermis and subcutis and is preferred for inflammatory rashes and smaller pigmented lesions; excisional biopsy is required for any lesion where melanoma is a genuine concern, to preserve Breslow depth measurement. When the diagnosis could change management dramatically, biopsy before prolonged empiric therapy.

### Take a Directed History: Onset, Evolution, Drugs, Exposures, and Itch

The morphology is only half the evaluation. Establish onset (acute over hours versus chronic over months), evolution (fixed, spreading, recurrent, migratory), provoking factors, and associated systemic symptoms (fever, arthralgia, mucosal involvement). A careful drug history — including new medications, antibiotics, anticonvulsants, NSAIDs, and over-the-counter and herbal products — is essential because drug eruptions mimic nearly every other rash. Ask about sick contacts, travel, animal and outdoor exposures, occupational exposures, sun exposure, and personal or family history of atopy, psoriasis, or skin cancer. Itch quality and intensity, and whether the lesion is painful rather than itchy, often shifts the differential (painful nodules suggest glomus, leiomyoma, or infection; painless ulceration suggests malignancy or vascular cause).

## Common Traps

### Calling Everything "Eczema" or "Drug Rash"

Eczema and drug rash are wastebasket labels that close the differential prematurely. Many tinea infections, scabies, contact dermatitis, early mycosis fungoides, and even pemphigoid are mislabeled as eczema and treated with steroids that mask or worsen the true process. Counter by completing the morphologic description and confirmatory tests before settling on a label.

### Treating Presumed Fungal Infection Without KOH Confirmation

Empiric topical or oral antifungals for an annular plaque often treat steroid-modified eczema or granuloma annulare, while true tinea is missed or undertreated. Counter with a KOH preparation for any scaly annular lesion, and reconsider the diagnosis if there is no response to appropriate antifungal therapy.

### Missing the Pigmented Lesion That Is Actually Melanoma

A changing, dark, or "ugly duckling" lesion dismissed as a benign nevus or "age spot" is a recurring cause of delayed melanoma diagnosis. Counter by applying the ABCDE and ugly duckling criteria to every pigmented lesion, and biopsy — with an excisional technique — any lesion that meets criteria or that the patient reports is changing.

### Using a Shave Biopsy on a Lesion Suspicious for Melanoma

A shave or partial-thickness biopsy of a possible melanoma can transect the lesion and prevent accurate Breslow depth measurement, upstaging risk and complicating management. Counter by using an excisional or full-thickness punch biopsy whenever melanoma is in the differential, and consult dermatology or surgery if unsure.

### Dismissing Non-Blanching Purpura or Rapidly Spreading Erythema as Benign

Petechiae, purpura, or a tender spreading erythematous plaque with systemic toxicity may be meningococcemia, vasculitis, or necrotizing fasciitis. Counter by checking for blanching, assessing hemodynamics and mental status, and escalating to urgent evaluation when systemic signs accompany cutaneous findings — do not reassure on appearance alone.

### Ignoring Mucosal Involvement

Mucosal erosions alongside a cutaneous eruption strongly suggest Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, pemphigus vulgaris, or a reactive erythema. Counter by explicitly inspecting oral, conjunctival, and genital mucosa in any widespread or atypical rash and escalating when mucosae are involved.

### Reassuring a Patient About a Lesion That Is Not Healing

A non-healing ulcer or nodule, especially on sun-exposed skin, may be basal or squamous cell carcinoma or a less common malignancy. Counter by setting a clear timeframe for expected healing and biopsy any lesion that persists, grows, or ulcerates beyond that window.

## Self-Check

- Have I described the primary lesion, secondary features, color, shape, and distribution in writing before committing to a diagnosis?
- Have I explicitly categorized the process as inflammatory, infectious, or neoplastic, and justified the call with morphology and confirmatory tests?
- Did I use KOH, culture, Gram stain, Wood's lamp, or PCR where the diagnosis was uncertain rather than treating empirically on appearance?
- For any pigmented lesion, have I applied ABCDE and ugly duckling criteria and chosen an excisional biopsy if melanoma is plausible?
- Have I actively screened for the dangerous rashes — non-blanching purpura, necrotizing fasciitis, SJS/TEN, staphylococcal scalded skin, purpura fulminans — and escalated immediately when any is suspected?
- Did I inspect the mucosa and ask about drug history, onset, evolution, and systemic symptoms as part of the evaluation?
- Have I matched the biopsy technique to the diagnostic question, avoiding shave biopsy where melanoma staging could be compromised?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
