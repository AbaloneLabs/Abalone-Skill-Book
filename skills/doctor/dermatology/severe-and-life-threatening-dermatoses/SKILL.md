# Severe and Life-Threatening Dermatoses

A small set of dermatologic presentations are true emergencies in which hours — sometimes minutes — determine survival, permanent scarring, blindness, or limb loss. The danger in these conditions is not diagnostic complexity; it is the ease with which they are initially mistaken for ordinary, benign rashes. A clinician who treats Stevens-Johnson syndrome as "a mild drug rash," necrotizing fasciitis as "cellulitis," or purpura fulminans as "bruising" loses the narrow window in which stopping a drug, debriding tissue, or supporting the patient prevents catastrophe. This skill covers the rapid recognition of the major severe cutaneous adverse reactions and dermatologic emergencies, body surface area and severity scoring, the immediate management that must precede definitive diagnosis, and the escalation pathways that must be triggered without delay.

## Core Rules

### Treat Severe Cutaneous Adverse Reactions as Distinct Entities, Not a Single "Drug Rash"

The severe cutaneous adverse reactions — Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS), toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN), drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS), and acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis (AGEP) — overlap in cause (often a drug) but differ profoundly in mechanism, timeline, severity, and management. SJS and TEN are mucocutaneous reactions with full-thickness epidermal necrosis, detachment, and a delayed onset of one to three weeks after drug exposure; they are medical emergencies with significant mortality. DRESS is a systemic hypersensitivity with visceral organ involvement (hepatitis, nephritis, pneumonitis, eosinophilia) that emerges two to eight weeks after exposure and can relapse. AGEP is an acute sterile pustular eruption with rapid onset and favorable prognosis but can mimic infection. Identify which entity is present, because the workup, monitoring, and prognosis differ, and because conflating them leads to wrong management — for example, treating DRESS as a self-limited exanthem and missing evolving liver failure.

### Quantify Body Surface Area Involvement to Separate SJS From TEN

The distinction between SJS and TEN rests on the percentage of detached or detachable body surface area (BSA). SJS involves less than 10% BSA detachment, SJS-TEN overlap involves 10% to 30%, and TEN involves more than 30%. Estimate detachment carefully, including skin that sloughs with gentle lateral pressure (Nikolsky sign) even if not yet detached. Mucosal involvement of at least two sites is characteristic. The BSA estimate is not academic — it drives prognosis, triage to a burn unit, and severity scoring. Document the estimate explicitly and recheck it as the disease evolves, because progression over hours changes the level of care required.

### Apply SCORTEN to Stratify Risk in SJS/TEN

Use the SCORTEN severity score within the first days to estimate mortality risk and to justify intensive care or burn unit transfer. The components — age over 40, malignancy, heart rate over 120, initial detached BSA over 10%, serum urea over 10 mmol/L, serum bicarbonate below 20 mmol/L, and serum glucose over 14 mmol/L — are each independent predictors. A rising SCORTEN over the first days is a stronger predictor than a single value, so recalculate. Do not let a reassuring initial score delay escalation if the patient is clinically deteriorating; the score supports judgment, it does not replace it.

### Identify and Stop the Culprit Drug Immediately

For all severe cutaneous adverse reactions, identifying and withdrawing the suspected causative drug is the single most important early intervention. The most commonly implicated drugs in SJS/TEN are allopurinol, anticonvulsants (lamotrigine, carbamazepine, phenytoin), sulfonamide antibiotics, nevirapine, and NSAIDs (especially oxicams); DRESS is also associated with anticonvulsants, sulfonamides, allopurinol, and minocycline. Take a precise timeline of every drug, including those started or dose-changed in the preceding one to eight weeks, and stop all non-essential agents. Document the suspected culprit clearly and ensure the patient is flagged for future avoidance, because re-exposure can be fatal. This action precedes and outranks every diagnostic refinement.

### Distinguish Necrotizing Fasciitis From Cellulitis Using Clinical and Laboratory Signals

Necrotizing fasciitis is a surgical emergency misdiagnosed as cellulitis with lethal frequency. Suspect it when pain is severe and disproportionate to the visible erythema, when the skin is indurated or woody, when there are bullae, ecchymosis, skin necrosis, crepitus, or rapid spread despite antibiotics, and when systemic toxicity (tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status) is present. Use the LRINEC score — C-reactive protein, white cell count, hemoglobin, sodium, creatinine, glucose — to support the diagnosis, but recognize its modest sensitivity; a low score does not exclude necrotizing fasciitis if clinical findings are convincing. Imaging should not delay surgical exploration in an unstable patient. The definitive diagnostic and therapeutic step is surgical exploration and debridement; when necrotizing fasciitis is genuinely suspected, escalate to surgery immediately.

### Recognize Purpura Fulminans and Non-Blanching Purpura as Emergencies

Purpura fulminans — rapidly progressive cutaneous hemorrhage and necrosis with disseminated intravascular coagulation — presents as sharply demarcated, painful, non-blanching purpuric patches that evolve to bullae and necrosis, often on the distal extremities. It may follow meningococcemia, pneumococcal or other severe infection, or inherited protein C or S deficiency, particularly in children. Any non-blanching purpura accompanied by fever, hypotension, or altered mental status must be treated as overwhelming sepsis with meningococcemia until proven otherwise: blood cultures, empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics, fluid resuscitation, and intensive care without waiting for dermatology or biopsy. Delay for confirmatory testing in this scenario is a recurring cause of preventable death.

### Triage to the Right Level of Care: Burn Unit, ICU, Surgery, or Dermatology

Match the escalation to the entity. Extensive SJS/TEN, particularly with high BSA detachment, requires burn unit or intensive care for fluid, electrolyte, nutritional, and wound management, plus ophthalmology to prevent corneal scarring and symblepharon. DRESS with visceral involvement requires hospitalization and monitoring of organ function. Necrotizing fasciitis requires immediate surgical consultation and operative debridement. Purpura fulminans and septic shock require intensive care and infectious disease input. Urgent dermatology consultation helps confirm the diagnosis, but it should not delay the above; obtain dermatology input in parallel with stabilization, not sequentially. When in doubt about the diagnosis or the patient is deteriorating, escalate first and refine the diagnosis afterward.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Early SJS/TEN as a Mild Drug Rash

Early SJS/TEN may show only targetoid macules and mild mucosal involvement that resembles a benign exanthem. Counter by inspecting mucosa in every drug rash, applying Nikolsky testing, and escalating when there is mucosal involvement, skin pain, or any detachment.

### Failing to Stop the Suspected Drug Quickly

Continuing a culprit anticonvulsant or allopurinol while awaiting diagnostic certainty worsens outcome. Counter by withdrawing all non-essential drugs started in the relevant window immediately and documenting the likely culprit for permanent avoidance.

### Treating Necrotizing Fasciitis as Cellulitis and Awaiting Imaging

Pain out of proportion, induration, bullae, crepitus, or systemic toxicity with a spreading erythema demands surgical exploration, not further observation or a CT scan. Counter by escalating to surgery immediately and treating imaging as adjunctive, not gating.

### Relying on LRINEC to Exclude Necrotizing Fasciitis

A low or intermediate LRINEC score does not exclude necrotizing fasciitis, and the score has limited sensitivity. Counter by treating the clinical picture as decisive and using LRINEC only as supportive evidence.

### Underestimating BSA Detachment in SJS/TEN

Detachment is often underestimated because detachable skin (positive Nikolsky) is not counted. Counter by estimating both detached and detachable skin and using the higher figure for SJS-TEN classification and burn unit triage.

### Missing Ophthalmologic Involvement in SJS/TEN

Ocular involvement in SJS/TEN can cause permanent corneal scarring and blindness if untreated. Counter by obtaining urgent ophthalmology consultation for every SJS/TEN patient, even when eye symptoms seem mild.

### Delaying Antibiotics and Resuscitation for Purpura Fulminans While Awaiting Biopsy

In suspected meningococcemia or purpura fulminans with sepsis, antibiotics and resuscitation must not wait for cultures or dermatology. Counter by giving empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics within the first hour and escalating to intensive care.

### Forgetting DRESS Can Relapse or Involve Viscera Late

DRESS may worsen, relapse, or show evolving hepatitis and renal injury even after the drug is stopped. Counter by monitoring organ function and blood counts over weeks and consulting the appropriate specialists when visceral involvement appears.

## Self-Check

- Have I classified the reaction as SJS, TEN, SJS-TEN overlap, DRESS, or AGEP rather than labeling it a generic drug rash?
- Have I estimated both detached and detachable body surface area and used it to guide triage to a burn unit or intensive care?
- Have I calculated and trended SCORTEN for SJS/TEN and used it to support, not replace, clinical judgment?
- Have I identified and immediately stopped the suspected culprit drug, and documented it for permanent avoidance?
- For any tender, rapidly spreading erythema with systemic toxicity, have I escalated to surgery for necrotizing fasciitis rather than treating it as cellulitis and waiting for imaging?
- For non-blanching purpura with fever or hypotension, have I given empiric antibiotics and resuscitation within the first hour without waiting for confirmatory tests?
- Have I obtained urgent ophthalmology consultation for SJS/TEN and arranged dermatology consultation in parallel with stabilization?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
