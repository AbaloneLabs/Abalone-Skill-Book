# Pediatric Emergencies and Weight-Based Management

Pediatric emergencies are unforgiving. Children differ from adults in airway anatomy, physiology, drug handling, and the speed at which they decompensate, and the single most dangerous assumption a clinician can bring to a sick child is that adult reasoning scales down. Most pediatric resuscitation errors are dosing errors — a wrong weight estimate, a decimal point misplaced, a drug given in adult concentration — and these errors are frequently lethal. The judgment problem is to recognize deterioration early when the signs are subtle, to obtain an accurate weight by the best available method, to calculate every dose in mg/kg and have it independently checked, and to escalate to pediatric intensive care at the right moment rather than after arrest. This skill covers weight-based dosing discipline, use of resuscitation aids, the key pediatric emergencies, recognition of deterioration, and the thresholds for escalation to PICU and pediatrics.

## Core Rules

### Establish Weight Accurately and Independently Verify Every Dose

Weight is the foundation of all pediatric drug dosing and equipment sizing, and a wrong weight propagates into every subsequent decision. Obtain an actual weight whenever possible — a recent documented weight is far better than an estimate. When the child cannot be weighed, use a validated method: the Broselow or equivalent length-based tape for emergencies (which gives both weight estimate and equipment/drug guidance), or an age-based formula such as (age in years + 4) x 2 kg for children aged one to ten, recognizing these are estimates with wide error. State the weight and its source explicitly. Calculate every drug dose in mg/kg, write it out, and have a second clinician independently recalculate and verify before administration — this single practice prevents the majority of catastrophic dosing errors. Never use an adult pre-filled concentration without confirming it is appropriate for the weight-based dose.

### Use Resuscitation Aids and Algorithms Rather Than Memory

In a pediatric arrest or peri-arrest, memory is unreliable and the stakes are absolute. Use the structured aids available: the Broselow tape for immediate weight, equipment size, and drug doses; the PALS (Pediatric Advanced Life Support) algorithm for the rhythm and the sequence; local pediatric resuscitation drug charts. Follow the algorithm rather than improvising. Post the algorithm where the team can see it and call the steps aloud. If your institution has a pediatric resuscitation trolley organized by weight or color, use it as designed. The algorithms encode decades of evidence; deviating from them without a compelling reason is a mistake.

### Know the Pediatric Airway Differences and Manage Accordingly

The pediatric airway is not a small adult airway. The larynx is higher and more anterior, the tongue is relatively large, the epiglottis is angled and floppy, the narrowest point is the cricoid (not the glottis as in adults), and the occiput is prominent. These differences change technique: position with mild head extension (or a shoulder roll in infants), use a straight-blade laryngoscope for infants, select an uncuffed tube for small children in many settings (though cuffed tubes are increasingly used with careful cuff pressure), and size all airway equipment by age or length using a formula or tape. Pre-oxygenate diligently because children desaturate rapidly due to a high metabolic rate and small functional residual capacity. Have a difficult-airway plan and call pediatric or anesthesia help early when intubation is not straightforward.

### Recognize Deterioration from Subtle Signs, Not Just Arrest

Children compensate physiologically until they decompensate abruptly — the "sick but stable" child is often neither. Learn the early signs of deterioration: tachycardia out of proportion to fever or distress, increasing work of breathing (recession, grunting, nasal flaring, head bobbing), altered mental status or reduced interaction, prolonged capillary refill, cool peripheries, and falling urine output. Hypotension is a late and pre-terminal sign in children; do not wait for it to recognize shock. Apply a pediatric early warning score (PEWS) serially and act on upward trends, not just threshold values. The trajectory — a child getting worse over an hour — is more alarming than any single observation.

### Manage Specific Emergencies by Protocol, Then Escalate

Each major pediatric emergency has a protocol-driven first response. For status epilepticus, give a benzodiazepine at the correct weight-based dose (and repeat) and prepare second-line agents; prolonged seizure is a medical emergency requiring escalation. For anaphylaxis, give intramuscular adrenaline at the weight-based dose, lie the patient flat, give oxygen and fluids, and repeat adrenaline as needed. For septic shock, follow the sepsis bundle — oxygen, fluid boluses in 10 mL/kg aliquots with reassessment, early antibiotics, and vasopressors/inotropes for fluid-refractory shock. For severe dehydration from gastroenteritis or DKA, calculate fluid deficits and manage with controlled rehydration to avoid cerebral edema in DKA. For upper airway obstruction (croup, epiglottitis, foreign body), keep the child calm, avoid distressing procedures, and summon senior airway help. In every case, begin the protocol-driven first steps while escalating to senior pediatric and intensive care colleagues.

### Avoid the Fluid and Electrolyte Errors That Harm Children

Fluid management is a frequent source of serious pediatric harm. Use isotonic maintenance fluids (for example sodium chloride with glucose and potassium) in most children — hypotonic fluids cause hyponatremia and seizures and are now broadly discouraged for maintenance. Calculate maintenance by a validated formula (for example the Holliday-Segar 4-2-1 rule), assess deficit separately for dehydration, and rehydrate in stages with reassessment. In DKA, fluid must be given cautiously with close monitoring to reduce cerebral edema risk. Document fluid balance meticulously. When a child is not improving on fluids, reassess rather than simply continuing — occult ongoing losses, wrong deficit estimate, or a different diagnosis may be at play.

### Escalate to PICU and Pediatrics at Defined Triggers

Know and respect the triggers for higher-level care. These include any child requiring ventilatory or circulatory support, fluid-refractory septic shock, status epilepticus not terminating, severe DKA, severe respiratory distress with impending failure, and any sustained abnormal physiology you cannot stabilize. Pediatric intensive care involvement should be early and by phone, not delayed until arrest. When you call PICU, give a structured handover: age, weight, presenting problem, current observations, interventions given and response, and your specific concern. The decision to transfer is made jointly but the threshold to involve PICU should be low.

## Common Traps

### Estimating Weight Poorly and Dosing Off the Bad Estimate

A guessed weight that is 30 percent off produces a 30 percent dosing error, which for drugs like adrenaline or sedatives can be fatal. Always obtain a real weight or use a length-based tape, and never dose from a guess without stating it is an estimate and verifying.

### Decimal-Point and Tenfold Dosing Errors

Pediatric doses are small and a misplaced decimal creates a tenfold error. Write doses in full (0.5 mg, not .5 mg), calculate in mg/kg, and have a second person independently verify every high-risk drug. This is the most preventable lethal error in pediatric care.

### Waiting for Hypotension to Diagnose Shock

Children maintain blood pressure until late. Tachycardia, poor perfusion, and altered mentation are the early signs of shock; waiting for hypotension means waiting until the child is nearly arrested. Treat tachycardia and poor perfusion as potential shock.

### Using Hypotonic Maintenance Fluids

Hypotonic fluids (such as 0.18 percent sodium chloride) for maintenance cause hyponatremia, seizures, and death in children and are now broadly avoided. Use isotonic maintenance fluids unless there is a specific indication.

### Treating DKA Dehydration Aggressively

Rapid rehydration in DKA precipitates cerebral edema, a leading cause of DKA mortality. Rehydrate cautiously, monitor neurologically, and follow the DKA protocol rather than aggressive boluses.

### Delaying Airway Help Because the Child "Looks Okay"

A child with impending airway obstruction can crash with minimal provocation. Summon senior pediatric and anesthesia or ENT help early for upper airway obstruction, and keep the child calm — agitating the child can precipitate complete obstruction.

### Calling PICU Too Late

The threshold to involve pediatric intensive care should be low and early. Delaying the call until arrest forfeits the preparation and stabilization time PICU provides. Call when the trajectory is worsening, not only when the child has arrested.

## Self-Check

- Have I established the child's weight by the most accurate available method and stated its source?
- Have I calculated every drug dose in mg/kg, written it in full without ambiguous decimals, and had it independently verified by a second clinician?
- Am I following a validated resuscitation aid or algorithm (Broselow, PALS) rather than relying on memory?
- Have I accounted for pediatric airway differences in equipment sizing and technique, and called senior airway help early?
- Am I recognizing deterioration from early signs (tachycardia, work of breathing, perfusion, mentation) rather than waiting for hypotension or arrest?
- For each specific emergency (seizure, anaphylaxis, septic shock, DKA, airway obstruction), am I following the protocol-driven first steps while escalating?
- Have I used isotonic maintenance fluids and avoided the hypotonic-fluid and aggressive-DKA-rehydration errors?
- Have I escalated to PICU and pediatrics at the defined triggers, with a structured handover, rather than delaying until arrest?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
