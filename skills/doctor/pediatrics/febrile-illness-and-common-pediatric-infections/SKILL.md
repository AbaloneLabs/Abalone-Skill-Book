# Febrile Illness and Common Pediatric Infections

Fever in a child is one of the most common presentations in clinical medicine and one of the most dangerous to evaluate casually. The central judgment problem is not "what is the temperature" but "where on the spectrum from benign viral illness to life-threatening sepsis does this child sit, and is that assessment stable or changing." Children deteriorate faster than adults, they communicate poorly (especially under three months), and the signs of serious illness are subtle until they are not. The recurring failure mode is binary thinking: either treating every fever as viral reassurance, or over-investigating every well-looking child. Neither serves the patient. This skill covers disciplined risk stratification, age-specific assessment, recognition of serious bacterial infection and sepsis, management of the common childhood infections, and the thresholds for escalation to pediatric specialty care.

## Core Rules

### Stratify Risk by Age First, Then by Appearance

Age is the single most powerful risk modifier in pediatric fever. The younger the child, the less reliable the clinical examination and the higher the risk of occult serious bacterial infection. Any febrile infant under one month (some guidelines extend to three months) with a temperature of 38C or higher is high-risk until proven otherwise and generally requires a full septic workup (blood, urine, cerebrospinal fluid) and admission for parenteral antibiotics — do not be reassured by a well-looking neonate. Between one and three months, use a structured tool (for example the NICE "traffic light" system) and have a low threshold to investigate. Beyond three months, clinical appearance and focused testing become more reliable, but never abandon vigilance. State the child's age and the age-specific risk threshold explicitly in every assessment.

### Use a Validated Risk-Stratification Framework

Do not rely on gestalt alone for a febrile child. Apply a validated framework such as the NICE traffic-light system (green, amber, red) or the Pediatric Early Warning Score appropriate to your setting. These tools force you to assess the domains that actually predict serious illness: color, activity, respiratory effort, hydration, and response. A child in the "red" column (for example reduced responsiveness, grunting, capillary refill over three seconds, bilious vomiting) is not a discharge candidate regardless of how the fever came on. Document the traffic-light category and the specific findings that placed the child there. If your local institution mandates a specific tool, use that one and document accordingly.

### Recognize Pediatric Sepsis Early and Act on It

Sepsis is the must-not-miss diagnosis in pediatric fever, and it is missed most often because early signs are attributed to the fever itself. Pediatric sepsis presents differently from adult sepsis: tachycardia may be the only early sign, hypotension is a late and pre-terminal finding, and the child may not look shockingly ill until they crash. Know the age-specific normal ranges for heart rate, respiratory rate, and blood pressure, and treat any unexplained tachycardia, prolonged capillary refill, mottled skin, altered mental status, or reduced urine output as potential sepsis. Apply a pediatric sepsis screening tool, obtain lactate and blood gas where available, and give antibiotics, fluids, and oxygen per protocol without delay. When sepsis is on the differential, escalate to senior pediatric or emergency colleagues immediately — time to antibiotics is the variable that changes outcome.

### Interpret Vital Signs Against Age-Specific Norms

A heart rate of 150 is normal in a two-month-old and concerning in a six-year-old. Using adult normal ranges for children is a serious error. Keep age-specific pediatric vital sign reference ranges at hand and interpret every set of observations against them. Fever itself raises heart rate and respiratory rate roughly ten and four beats/breaths per degree Celsius respectively, so account for the temperature when judging whether tachycardia or tachypnea is appropriate. Inconsolable crying can transiently elevate vital signs; recheck when the child is settled. A heart rate or respiratory rate that is inappropriately low for the degree of fever (relative bradycardia) is a red flag for serious illness.

### Assess Hydration Systematically in Gastroenteritis

Dehydration is the principal danger in pediatric gastroenteritis and is frequently both under- and over-estimated. Use a structured hydration assessment: alertness, thirst, mucous membranes, tears, capillary refill, skin turgor, and urine output. Mild dehydration can usually be managed with oral rehydration; moderate dehydration often requires supervised oral or nasogastric rehydration; severe dehydration with shock requires intravenous access, fluid boluses, and admission. Do not rely on a single sign — combine them. Weight loss, if a pre-illness weight is known, is the most objective measure. Reassess hydration status frequently because the trajectory matters as much as the current state.

### Diagnose and Manage Common Infections with Restraint

The common childhood infections — otitis media, urinary tract infection, pneumonia, viral upper respiratory infection, gastroenteritis — account for the majority of febrile presentations. Diagnose them on focused clinical grounds and treat according to current guidelines, but resist the reflex to prescribe antibiotics for every febrile child. Many of these are viral and self-limiting. For otitis media, distinguish acute otitis media (requiring consideration of antibiotics, especially under two years or with systemic features) from otitis media with effusion (which does not). For suspected UTI, obtain an appropriate urine sample (clean catch in the older child, catheter or suprapubic aspirate in the infant) before starting antibiotics. Always ask whether the diagnosis you are making explains the whole picture, or whether it is a partial explanation masking something more serious.

### Give Explicit Safety-Netting Advice

Most febrile children are managed as outpatients, and the safety of that decision depends entirely on the quality of safety-netting. Do not simply say "return if worse." Give the caregivers concrete, written instructions: return immediately if the child has a non-blanching rash, becomes difficult to rouse, stops drinking or passing urine, develops labored breathing, has a seizure, or the fever persists beyond a defined period (often 48 hours for an undifferentiated fever, or five days if Kawasaki disease is being considered). Document that this advice was given. Provide a named route for review. Safety-netting is a clinical intervention, not a disclaimer — it is what converts a borderline discharge into a safe one.

### Escalate to Pediatrics at Defined Thresholds

Define the thresholds at which you will involve pediatric specialty care and do not cross them alone. These include any febrile neonate, any child in the red traffic-light category, suspected sepsis or meningitis, persistent fever without a source, any safeguarding concern, and any child whose trajectory you cannot confidently explain to a colleague. Escalation is not failure; in pediatric care it is the standard of care for the high-risk presentations. When you call, give a structured handover including age, duration of fever, key observations, your working diagnosis, and your specific concern.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring on a Well-Looking Neonate

A febrile infant under one to three months may look well and still have occult bacteremia, UTI, or meningitis. Never be reassured by appearance alone in this age group — investigate fully and escalate. This is the single most dangerous trap in pediatric fever.

### Treating Sepsis Signs as "Just the Fever"

Tachycardia, tachypnea, and reduced perfusion are frequently attributed to fever when they are actually early sepsis. Any vital sign abnormality out of proportion to the fever, or any sign of poor perfusion, warrants sepsis screening and escalation — not antipyretics and observation alone.

### Using Adult Vital Sign Ranges

Applying adult normal values to children masks serious illness (an inappropriately "normal" heart rate for a febrile infant is itself abnormal) and causes false alarms in older children. Always interpret observations against age-specific reference ranges.

### Over-Prescribing Antibiotics for Viral Illness

The pressure to "do something" for a febrile child leads to unnecessary antibiotics for viral upper respiratory infections, contributing to resistance and adverse effects. Diagnose specifically, reserve antibiotics for bacterial infection or clear high-risk scenarios, and document the reasoning.

### Accepting a Contaminated Urine Sample

A bag urine in an infant is frequently contaminated and leads to both over-diagnosis of UTI and missed diagnoses when falsely negative. Obtain a catheter or suprapubic specimen before committing to a UTI diagnosis and before starting antibiotics whenever feasible.

### Missing Kawasaki Disease Behind Prolonged Fever

Fever persisting beyond five days, especially with conjunctivitis, rash, mucous membrane changes, and extremity changes, is Kawasaki disease until proven otherwise. Delayed diagnosis risks coronary artery aneurysms. Build in a forced re-evaluation at the five-day mark for any unexplained fever.

### Discharging Without Concrete Safety-Netting

Vague advice to "return if concerned" is not adequate. Caregivers need specific, written return precautions, and the clinician needs to document that they were given. A discharge without safety-netting is an unsafe discharge.

## Self-Check

- Have I stratified this child's risk by age first, and applied age-specific thresholds for investigation and admission?
- Have I applied a validated risk-stratification tool (for example NICE traffic light) and documented the category and the findings driving it?
- Have I explicitly screened for and addressed pediatric sepsis, using age-specific vital signs and a low threshold to escalate?
- Have I interpreted every vital sign against age-specific normal ranges, accounting for the expected rise with fever?
- Have I assessed hydration systematically and chosen the correct route of rehydration based on severity?
- Is my working diagnosis (viral illness, otitis, UTI, pneumonia, gastroenteritis) specific, and does it explain the whole picture?
- Have I given concrete, written safety-netting advice with specific return precautions, and documented it?
- Have I escalated to pediatrics at the defined thresholds (febrile neonate, red-category child, suspected sepsis or meningitis, unexplained persistent fever, safeguarding concern)?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
