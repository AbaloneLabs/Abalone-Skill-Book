# Bedside Procedure Safety and Consent

Bedside procedures are where clinical benefit and patient harm sit only millimeters apart. A central venous catheter, a thoracentesis, or a lumbar puncture can be decisive, life-saving interventions — and they can also cause pneumothorax, hemorrhage, nerve injury, infection, or death. The danger is not usually ignorance of the technique; it is the failure to treat each procedure as a high-stakes event that demands the same discipline as an operating room: the right patient, the right indication, true informed consent, a deliberate pause to verify, sterile barriers, real-time imaging, and a plan for what will go wrong. Most procedural harm is preventable and traces back to skipped steps — a consent that was implied rather than obtained, a timeout that was a formality, a coagulopathy that was never checked, an ultrasound that was available but not used. This skill covers the disciplined preparation, execution, and post-procedure care of common bedside procedures, with the constant recognition that any deviation from safety standards demands immediate correction and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Confirm a Genuine Indication Before Setting Up

Every procedure must begin with an explicit, defensible indication, and an equally explicit consideration of whether the same goal can be achieved less invasively. Ask: what decision or therapy depends on this procedure, and is there a lower-risk alternative? A central line placed "for access" in a patient who could be managed with a peripheral IV is a line exposed to all risk and no benefit. Document the indication concretely (hemodynamic monitoring, vasopressor infusion, dialysis, diagnostic sampling, therapeutic drainage). Equally important is recognizing contraindications and relative contraindications: coagulopathy, thrombocytopenia, overlying cellulitis, an uncooperative patient, lack of safe ultrasound windows, or an operator without sufficient experience. If the indication is weak or the contraindication significant, pause and reconsider, and involve a more experienced operator or a different modality rather than proceeding out of momentum.

### Obtain Real Informed Consent, Not Implied Assent

Informed consent is a conversation, not a signature. The patient (or surrogate) must understand the indication, the proposed procedure, the major risks specific to that procedure (pneumothorax for central lines and thoracentesis, bleeding for any vascular or puncture procedure, infection, nerve injury, failure, and the need for a repeat or alternate approach), the alternatives including the option of not doing the procedure, and the consequences of refusal. Document this conversation in the record, not just the signed form. For emergent procedures where consent cannot be obtained, document the clinical justification for proceeding under implied consent and notify the team. Never back-date or fabricate consent, and never present a procedure as routine or risk-free — a patient who consents without understanding pneumothorax risk has not consented to pneumothorax risk. If the patient cannot consent and no surrogate is available, follow institutional policy and document the urgency and the attempts to reach a decision-maker.

### Check and Optimize Bleeding Risk Before Any Puncture

Bleeding is one of the most common and most serious procedural complications, and it is frequently preventable. Before any procedure, check platelet count, INR, and any anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy, and assess renal function for patients on direct oral anticoagulants whose effect may be prolonged. Apply procedure-specific thresholds: deep or non-compressible procedures (lumbar puncture, solid organ biopsy, deep muscle injection) demand stricter correction than superficial, compressible ones (peripheral IV, superficial joint injection). For central lines, modern evidence supports many internal jugular approaches under ultrasound without correcting mild coagulopathy, but this is an operator-and-institution-specific judgment, not a blanket license — femoral and especially subclavian routes are far less compressible and far more dangerous in coagulopathic patients. Plan anticoagulant holding and bridging with pharmacy or the primary team, and document the plan. Never proceed with a non-compressible procedure in a severely coagulopathic patient without explicit correction or specialist involvement.

### Run a Genuine Timeout Immediately Before Incision

The WHO surgical safety checklist principle applies to bedside procedures: a formal pause immediately before starting, in which the team verbally confirms the correct patient (two identifiers), the correct site and side, the correct procedure, the indication, consent, allergies, relevant imaging, anticoagulation status, and the availability of all equipment and assistance. This is not a box to tick silently; it is a designed stop moment that catches wrong-patient, wrong-site, and wrong-procedure errors, which are devastating and almost always preventable. If any element is uncertain, stop and resolve it before proceeding. Empower every team member, including nurses and technicians, to speak up if something seems wrong — a culture of silence is how wrong-site procedures happen. If you are the operator, state the plan aloud and invite challenge.

### Use Ultrasound Guidance for Imageable Procedures

Real-time ultrasound guidance dramatically reduces mechanical complications for central venous access (arterial puncture, pneumothorax, multiple attempts) and is now the standard of care for internal jugular and femoral cannulation wherever equipment is available. Use it not only to find the vessel before puncture but to visualize the needle tip in real time during the puncture, and to confirm intravascular placement and rule out pneumothorax afterward. For thoracentesis and paracentesis, ultrasound identifies the fluid pocket, marks a safe entry site, and shows intervening structures; for arthrocentesis it confirms the effusion. Do not let familiarity breed complacency — even ultrasound-guided procedures cause harm when the operator loses the needle tip, confuses vein for artery, or proceeds after a difficult stick. If you cannot maintain needle-tip visualization, stop and reposition rather than advancing blind.

### Maintain Strict Sterile Technique Throughout

Procedural infections — line-associated bloodstream infections, meningitis after lumbar puncture, septic arthritis after injection — are catastrophic and largely preventable. Use full sterile barriers for central lines (cap, mask, sterile gown and gloves, full-body drape), chlorhexidine skin preparation, and sterile technique for all invasive procedures. Prepare a wide field, not just the puncture site. Once gloved and gowned, maintain sterile discipline: do not contaminate gloves by touching non-sterile surfaces, do not reach across the field carelessly, and re-gown and re-glove if contamination occurs. For lumbar puncture and joint injection, mask and use sterile drapes. A single contamination event can seed a bloodstream infection; treat sterile breaks seriously and restart rather than rationalize.

### Anticipate, Recognize, and Plan for Complications

Before starting, mentally rehearse the worst-case complication and your immediate response. For central lines: arterial puncture (withdraw needle, hold compressible pressure for at least 10 minutes, assess for expanding hematoma), pneumothorax (post-procedure assessment, chest x-ray, chest tube if symptomatic or large). For thoracentesis: pneumothorax, re-expansion pulmonary edema (limit single drainage to roughly 1.5 liters), vasovagal reaction. For lumbar puncture: post-dural puncture headache, epidural hematoma in coagulopathic patients (a neurologic emergency), cerebral herniation in raised intracranial pressure (check for papilledema or image first when indicated). Have monitoring in place, have the equipment to respond ready, and know exactly when and whom to call. Monitor the patient after the procedure — most complications declare themselves in the minutes to hours afterward, not during the procedure itself.

### Document the Procedure Completely and Honestly

A complete procedure note records the indication, consent, timeout, operator and assistants, anticoagulation status, sterile preparation, ultrasound use, technique, findings (e.g., fluid character, CSF opening pressure), estimated blood loss, immediate complications and their management, post-procedure patient status, and the follow-up plan (e.g., post-line chest x-ray, post-thoracentesis lung re-expansion). If a complication occurred, document it factually and completely, including the recognition, the response, who was notified, and the plan — never minimize, hide, or omit a complication. Honest, contemporaneous documentation protects the patient, enables safe handoffs, and is the correct medicolegal response to an adverse event. If a complication has occurred, disclose it to the patient per institutional policy.

## Common Traps

### Proceeding Without a Defensible Indication

Placing a line or draining fluid because it "seemed like a good idea" or out of habit, without a concrete decision or therapy depending on it. Counter by stating the specific indication and the alternative before setting up.

### Treating Consent as a Signature, Not a Conversation

Obtaining a signed form without ensuring the patient understands the major risks and alternatives. Counter by having the risk conversation explicitly and documenting it, not just the signature.

### Ignoring Anticoagulation or Coagulopathy

Proceeding with a non-compressible procedure in a patient with an elevated INR, low platelets, or active anticoagulation without assessment or correction. Counter by checking coagulation status before every procedure and applying procedure-specific thresholds, escalating to hematology or anesthesia when uncertain.

### Skipping or Rushing the Timeout

Treating the timeout as a formality or skipping it for "quick" procedures. Counter by running a verbal, team-confirmed stop moment before every invasive procedure and empowering staff to halt if anything is unclear.

### Abandoning Ultrasound Because "You Know the Anatomy"

Cannulating without real-time guidance out of confidence, especially for subclavian or repeat lines. Counter by treating ultrasound guidance as the default for imageable vascular access and never advancing without needle-tip visualization.

### Draining Too Much, Too Fast

Removing large volumes of fluid (more than roughly 1.5 liters of pleural fluid in one thoracentesis) and precipitating re-expansion pulmonary edema or hypotension. Counter by limiting single-session drainage and stopping for chest tightness, cough, or vasovagal signs.

### Performing Lumbar Puncture Without Considering Raised Intracranial Pressure

Performing LP in a patient with papilledema, focal neurologic signs, immunocompromise, or a space-occupying lesion without first imaging, risking herniation. Counter by assessing for red flags and imaging before LP when any concern exists, and escalating to neurology or the ED if unsure.

### Hiding or Minimizing a Complication

Failing to document, disclose, or escalate a complication out of discomfort or fear. Counter by documenting complications factually and completely, disclosing to the patient, notifying the team, and following institutional adverse-event protocols.

## Self-Check

- Have I confirmed a concrete, defensible indication and considered a less invasive alternative before setting up this procedure?
- Did I obtain real informed consent — a conversation covering indication, major risks, alternatives, and refusal consequences — and document it, not just the signature?
- Have I checked and addressed coagulation status, platelets, and anticoagulants using procedure-specific thresholds, and escalated when uncertain?
- Did I run a genuine, team-confirmed timeout immediately before starting, verifying correct patient, site, procedure, consent, and equipment?
- Am I using real-time ultrasound guidance for imageable vascular access and fluid drainage, and maintaining needle-tip visualization throughout?
- Have I maintained full sterile barriers and sterile discipline, and prepared a wide field rather than a minimal one?
- Have I rehearsed the worst-case complication and my immediate response, and is monitoring and rescue equipment available?
- Does my procedure note capture the indication, consent, timeout, technique, findings, complications, and follow-up — and have I disclosed any complication honestly?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
