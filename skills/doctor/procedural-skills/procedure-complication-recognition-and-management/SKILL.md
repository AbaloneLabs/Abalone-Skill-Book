# Procedure Complication Recognition and Management

A procedure does not end when the needle or catheter is withdrawn — it ends when the patient has safely passed the window in which complications declare themselves. Most procedural harm is recognized afterward, in the minutes to hours following the intervention: the pneumothorax that develops as the patient turns in bed, the bleed that slowly expands into the neck, the line infection that smolders for days, the nerve injury the patient reports the next morning. The clinician's task in this window is active surveillance, not passive waiting — knowing which complications to expect for each procedure, what signs herald them, when to image, when to intervene, and when to escalate. The two great failure modes are denial and concealment: dismissing a patient's new symptom as expected discomfort until it becomes a catastrophe, and failing to document, disclose, and escalate a complication once it occurs. This skill covers the disciplined recognition and management of procedural complications, with the constant recognition that an unexplained post-procedure symptom is a complication until proven otherwise and that early escalation and honest disclosure are professional obligations, not admissions of defeat.

## Core Rules

### Treat Any New Post-Procedure Symptom as a Complication Until Proven Otherwise

The single most important rule is to give weight to new symptoms after a procedure. New dyspnea after a central line or thoracentesis is pneumothorax until ruled out. New pain, swelling, or expanding hematoma at a puncture site is bleeding until assessed. New fever, erythema, or purulence at a line site is infection until evaluated. New numbness, weakness, or radiating pain after a procedure near a nerve is nerve injury until examined. New headache after lumbar puncture may be post-dural puncture headache, but new neurologic deficit or severe headache with signs of raised pressure is epidural hematoma or hemorrhage — an emergency. The default posture must be skeptical: assume the procedure caused it, prove otherwise, and never dismiss a symptom as routine without a specific assessment. Patients who report that "something is wrong" after a procedure are often right.

### Know the Expected Complications for Each Procedure and Monitor for Them

Different procedures have different risk profiles, and the clinician must know which complications to anticipate. Central venous catheterization: pneumothorax (especially subclavian and internal jugular), arterial puncture and bleeding, catheter malposition, arrhythmia, and late line-related bloodstream infection. Thoracentesis: pneumothorax, re-expansion pulmonary edema, vasovagal reaction, and bleeding. Paracentesis: bleeding (especially from mesenteric or abdominal wall vessels), bowel injury, and ascitic fluid leak. Lumbar puncture: post-dural puncture headache, epidural hematoma or abscess (especially in coagulopathic or bacteremic patients), and cerebral herniation in raised intracranial pressure. Arthrocentesis and joint injection: septic arthritis, bleeding into the joint, and cartilage injury. For each procedure performed, document the specific complications to watch for and set the monitoring and follow-up accordingly. A patient who had a thoracentesis this morning needs lung re-expansion assessment; a patient with a new central line needs a chest x-ray and line site checks.

### Image Promptly When a Structural Complication Is Suspected

Imaging is the definitive tool for most structural complications, and it should be obtained promptly when there is clinical concern, not delayed while "watching" the patient. Suspected pneumothorax after central line or thoracentesis: upright chest x-ray, or point-of-care ultrasound or CT if the patient is unstable or the x-ray is equivocal; a tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis treated immediately with needle decompression before imaging. Suspected bleeding after a vascular procedure: assess the site, check hemodynamics and hemoglobin, and image (ultrasound or CT) if there is an expanding hematoma, hemodynamic instability, or a deep non-compressible concern. Suspected epidural hematoma after lumbar puncture or neuraxial anesthesia with new neurologic deficit: emergent MRI of the spine and urgent neurosurgical consultation — delay here causes permanent paralysis. Suspected line infection: blood cultures and evaluation, with echocardiography if endocarditis is a concern. Match the imaging to the question and the urgency, and escalate to radiology or the relevant specialist when the diagnosis is unclear or the patient is deteriorating.

### Manage Bleeding Systematically and Correct Reversible Factors

Post-procedure bleeding ranges from trivial oozing to life-threatening hemorrhage. For external, compressible sites, apply sustained direct pressure for at least 10 to 20 minutes and reassess; do not peek repeatedly, which disrupts clot. For deeper or expanding hematomas, assess the airway (critical in neck procedures where an expanding hematoma can obstruct the airway — a surgical emergency), monitor hemodynamics and hemoglobin, correct coagulopathy (reverse anticoagulants per protocols, transfuse platelets or plasma as indicated, consult hematology or pharmacy for reversal agents), and image to define the extent. For intra-abdominal bleeding after paracentesis or liver biopsy, monitor for peritonitis, hemodynamic instability, and dropping hemoglobin, and obtain urgent CT and surgical or interventional consultation. For retroperitoneal or non-compressible bleeding, the patient can deteriorate rapidly; secure large-bore access, resuscitate, reverse coagulopathy, and involve surgery or interventional radiology early. Document the bleeding, the response, and the notifications.

### Recognize and Respond to Line-Related Infections

Catheter-related bloodstream infection is a leading cause of nosocomial sepsis and is largely preventable, but when it occurs it demands prompt, structured management. Suspect it in any patient with a central or PICC line and new fever, leukocytosis, rigors, or signs of sepsis, with or without local exit-site or tunnel-tract findings. Obtain blood cultures (preferably paired peripheral and catheter-drawn, with differential time to positivity considered), examine the site, and assess for metastatic infection (endocarditis, septic emboli, discitis). Start empiric antibiotics based on local resistance patterns and severity, and in most cases of confirmed catheter-related bloodstream infection, remove the catheter — particularly with Staphylococcus aureus, Pseudomonas, Candida, or any organism with persistent bacteremia, tunnel infection, or septic thrombophlebitis. Involve infectious diseases for complex or persistent infections, and reassess the need for ongoing central access rather than reflexively replacing the line.

### Evaluate Nerve Injury Promptly and Escalate

Nerve injury after a procedure presents as new pain, paresthesia, weakness, or sensory loss in a nerve distribution. Common scenarios include radial or median nerve injury from arterial or venous lines, femoral or sciatic nerve injury from pelvic or groin procedures, and spinal nerve injury or epidural hematoma after neuraxial procedures. Examine the patient, document the neurologic deficit precisely, and distinguish a transient neuropraxia from a more serious injury. For new, severe, or progressive deficits after neuraxial anesthesia or lumbar puncture, suspect epidural hematoma or abscess and obtain emergent MRI and neurosurgical consultation — permanent paralysis can result from delayed decompression. For peripheral nerve injury, consult neurology or the relevant specialist, arrange electrodiagnostic studies as appropriate, and follow the patient. Never dismiss a new neurologic deficit as "expected."

### Document, Disclose, and Escalate Every Complication Honestly

When a complication occurs, the professional response is recognition, documentation, disclosure, and escalation — not minimization or concealment. Document the complication factually and contemporaneously: what happened, when it was recognized, what was done, who was notified, the patient's status, and the plan. Disclose the complication to the patient or surrogate honestly and with empathy, explaining what occurred, the implications, and the plan; disclosure is an ethical and, in most jurisdictions, a regulatory expectation, and it supports trust and safety. Escalate to the appropriate specialist (anesthesia, ENT, vascular or general surgery, neurosurgery, infectious diseases, hematology) and to senior colleagues per institutional policy. Report the event through the institutional incident-reporting system so that systems improvements can follow. Never alter the record retrospectively to obscure what occurred; honest documentation is both the right and the safest course.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing New Symptoms as Expected Discomfort

Attributing post-procedure dyspnea, pain, or neurologic change to routine recovery and missing a pneumothorax, bleed, or nerve injury. Counter by treating any new symptom as a complication until assessed and proven otherwise.

### Delaying Imaging to "Watch and Wait"

Observing a deteriorating patient instead of imaging promptly. Counter by imaging as soon as a structural complication is suspected and treating tension pneumothorax clinically before imaging when unstable.

### Inadequate Pressure for Compressible Bleeding

Peeking repeatedly at a bleeding site and disrupting clot formation. Counter by applying sustained pressure for at least 10 to 20 minutes before reassessing.

### Missing an Expanding Neck Hematoma

Failing to recognize that an expanding hematoma after a neck procedure can obstruct the airway. Counter by monitoring the airway closely after neck vascular procedures and treating airway compromise as a surgical emergency.

### Keeping an Infected Catheter in Place

Deferring removal of a central line with confirmed bloodstream infection, especially with high-virulence organisms. Counter by removing the catheter in most cases of catheter-related bloodstream infection and involving infectious diseases.

### Delaying MRI for Post-Neuraxial Neurologic Deficit

Watching a new deficit after epidural or spinal anesthesia instead of imaging immediately. Counter by obtaining emergent MRI and neurosurgical consultation for any new or progressive neurologic deficit after a neuraxial procedure.

### Concealing or Minimizing a Complication in the Record

Failing to document, disclose, or escalate an adverse event. Counter by documenting factually and contemporaneously, disclosing to the patient, escalating to specialists and seniors, and reporting through the incident system.

### Failing to Reassess the Need for the Device

Leaving a central line, catheter, or drain in place past its indication and exposing the patient to ongoing infection and thrombosis risk. Counter by reviewing line and device necessity daily and removing as soon as no longer needed.

## Self-Check

- Am I treating every new post-procedure symptom as a possible complication until assessed, rather than dismissing it as expected discomfort?
- Do I know the expected complications for the procedure performed, and have I set the monitoring and follow-up to detect them?
- Have I imaged promptly when a structural complication (pneumothorax, bleeding, epidural hematoma) is suspected, and treated tension pneumothorax clinically before imaging when unstable?
- For bleeding, am I applying sustained pressure for compressible sites, monitoring the airway after neck procedures, correcting coagulopathy, and escalating to surgery or interventional radiology for non-compressible hemorrhage?
- For suspected line infection, have I drawn cultures, examined the site, started empiric antibiotics, and planned catheter removal for confirmed or high-virulence infections?
- For any new neurologic deficit after a procedure, especially neuraxial, have I examined, documented, and obtained emergent MRI and neurosurgical consultation without delay?
- Have I documented the complication factually and contemporaneously, disclosed it to the patient, escalated to the appropriate specialist and senior colleague, and reported it through the incident system?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
