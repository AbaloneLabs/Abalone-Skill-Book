# Airway and Vascular Access Procedures

Airway and vascular access are the two procedural domains where failure is measured in minutes, not hours. Loss of an airway kills within minutes; misplacement of a central or arterial line seeds infection, hemorrhage, or ischemia that may not surface for days. Both demand a disciplined, planned approach rather than improvisation under pressure: assess difficulty before you start, prepare equipment and rescue options, use real-time imaging, verify placement rigorously, and have a clear escalation pathway to anesthesia, ENT, or interventional radiology before the situation becomes unsalvageable. The recurring failure mode in both domains is overconfidence — proceeding with a single plan, ignoring predictors of difficulty, making repeated traumatic attempts, and calling for help only after the situation has deteriorated. This skill covers the judgment for securing an airway and establishing vascular access, with the constant recognition that these are high-stakes procedures where early escalation is a sign of competence, not weakness.

## Core Rules

### Predict the Difficult Airway Before You Induce

Airway disasters almost always involve an airway that was predictable but unprepared for. Before any intubation, assess for predictors of difficulty using a structured tool such as LEMON (Look for external features, Evaluate mouth opening and thyromental distance, Mallampati score, Obstruction, Neck mobility) and consider the history of prior difficult intubation, obesity, beard, pregnancy, trauma, or airway pathology. Combine this with an assessment of physiologic difficulty: hypoxemia, hypotension, acidosis, and shock make every intubation harder and less forgiving, because the patient has no reserve for a prolonged attempt. Classify the airway as anticipated easy, anticipated difficult, or anticipated impossible, and match your preparation to that assessment. For a predicted difficult airway, do not induce with a paralytic unless you are confident of ventilation and have a rescue plan; consider awake fiberoptic intubation, video laryngoscopy as the first device, or an alternative such as a supraglottic airway. Have a difficult-airway cart and an experienced second operator present. When physiologic difficulty is high, resuscitate first (fluids, vasopressors, preoxygenation) whenever the clinical situation permits, because inducing a shocked patient can cause cardiovascular collapse on laryngoscopy.

### Prepare and Execute Rapid Sequence Intubation Systematically

For the patient at risk of aspiration (full stomach, pregnancy, ileus, upper GI bleed, sepsis), rapid sequence intubation (RSI) is the standard: preoxygenate, administer an induction agent and a fast-onset paralytic in rapid succession, apply cricoid pressure (its benefit is debated, but it is still widely used), and intubate without mask ventilation. Prepare everything before induction — drugs drawn up and labeled, suction on and under the pillow, endotracheal tubes with tested cuffs and available stylets, video laryngoscope with a backup direct laryngoscope, supraglottic airway, bougie, and a surgical airway kit. Choose the induction agent and dose for the patient's hemodynamics (ketamine or etomidate for the unstable; propofol may cause dangerous hypotension in shock). Preoxygenate to denitrogenate the functional residual capacity and maximize apnea time, and use apneic oxygenation (nasal cannula or nasal high flow) during the attempt. Confirm tube placement immediately with waveform capnography — the absence of a sustained CO2 trace means esophageal intubation until proven otherwise, regardless of how the chest sounds or moves. Never rely on auscultation or chest rise alone.

### Define and Rehearse the Can't-Intubate, Can't-Oxygenate Pathway

The most lethal airway scenario is the failure of both intubation and oxygenation. This must be anticipated, not improvised. Define, before induction, the plan A (primary technique), plan B (secondary technique such as video laryngoscopy or supraglottic airway), and plan C (rescue oxygenation and surgical airway). If oxygenation cannot be maintained after the best attempts at intubation and supraglottic rescue, perform a surgical airway — cricothyrotomy in adults — without delay. The single most common fatal error is waiting too long to commit to the surgical airway while making repeated failed attempts. Know the technique, have the equipment, and declare the decision aloud. For children, needle cricothyrotomy with jet ventilation may be preferred per pediatric protocols; know your institutional guidance. Call for anesthesia and ENT help at the first sign of a difficult airway, not after multiple failures.

### Choose Vascular Access Site by Indication, Risk, and Anatomy

Central venous access is not one procedure but several, each with a distinct risk profile. The internal jugular vein is compressible, ultrasound-friendly, and the preferred site for most inpatients; the femoral vein is compressible and useful in emergencies or coagulopathic patients but carries higher infection and deep vein thrombosis risk; the subclavian vein has the lowest infection rate but is non-compressible and carries the highest pneumothorax risk, making it dangerous in coagulopathic patients and in patients who cannot tolerate a pneumothorax. Match the site to the patient: avoid subclavian in coagulopathy or severe respiratory compromise, avoid femoral for long-term access or in the obese where infection risk is high, and prefer ultrasound-guided internal jugular when feasible. For peripherally inserted central catheters (PICCs), consider the indication (longer-term access, antibiotics, chemotherapy), the patient's arm vasculature, and the contraindications (renal failure where arm veins may be needed for fistula, history of mastectomy with axillary dissection). For arterial lines, the radial artery is the default because of collateral circulation from the ulnar; confirm collateral flow when feasible, and reserve femoral or axillary arterial lines for specific indications given their higher complication profile.

### Use Ultrasound and Verify Placement Rigorously

Real-time ultrasound guidance is the standard for internal jugular and femoral central access and reduces arterial puncture, pneumothorax, and multiple attempts. Visualize the vessel in transverse and longitudinal planes, distinguish vein from artery by compressibility and color or pulsed Doppler, and maintain needle-tip visualization throughout. After cannulation, confirm venous placement before dilation and large-bore insertion — transduce the pressure or aspirate venous blood — because inadvertent arterial dilation is a serious complication. After central line placement, obtain a post-procedure chest x-ray to confirm position (tip at the cavoatrial junction) and to exclude pneumothorax, especially after internal jugular or subclavian attempts. For arterial lines, confirm waveform and blood return, and watch for distal ischemia. Never assume placement is correct because the line "flushes well."

### Recognize and Manage Immediate Complications Without Delay

Immediate complications include arterial puncture (withdraw the needle, hold compressible pressure for at least 10 minutes, assess for expanding hematoma, and if a dilator or catheter was placed in an artery, leave it and call vascular or interventional surgery before removal in most cases), pneumothorax (assess for dyspnea and chest pain, obtain a chest x-ray, and place a chest tube if symptomatic or under tension), hematoma and bleeding (apply pressure, correct coagulopathy, and monitor the airway in neck procedures where expanding hematoma can obstruct), catheter malposition (reposition or replace), and arrhythmia from wire or catheter stimulation of the right heart (withdraw the wire to the appropriate depth). For airway procedures, immediate complications include esophageal intubation (recognized by absent capnography — remove and reintubate), mainstem bronchus intubation (recognized by asymmetric breath sounds and confirmed by depth and chest x-ray — withdraw), hypotension after induction (treat with fluids, vasopressors, and reduced anesthetic depth), and laryngospasm or bronchospasm. Have the monitoring, drugs, and personnel to respond before you begin.

### Escalate Early to the Right Specialist

The boundary of your competence is a clinical judgment, not a point of pride. Escalate to anesthesia for any predicted or realized difficult airway, for hemodynamically unstable patients needing intubation, and for repeated failed attempts. Escalate to ENT for surgical airway support, airway obstruction, or trauma involving the airway. Escalate to vascular surgery or interventional radiology for inadvertent arterial catheter placement, large hematomas, suspected vascular injury, or access in patients with difficult anatomy or coagulopathy. Call for help at the first sign of difficulty, communicate with a structured format (the patient, the problem, the attempts made, and what you need), and document the escalation. Waiting until the situation is desperate converts a manageable problem into a catastrophe.

## Common Traps

### Inducing a Paralytic Without a Rescue Plan

Paralyzing a patient whose airway you cannot secure, converting a breathing patient into one who cannot breathe. Counter by assessing difficulty before induction and having plan A, B, and C and rescue devices ready; when difficulty is predicted, consider awake techniques.

### Repeated Laryngoscopy After Failed Attempts

Making multiple traumatic attempts that cause bleeding and edema and collapse the airway. Counter by limiting attempts, switching devices (video laryngoscope, bougie), and calling anesthesia or ENT early.

### Relying on Chest Rise or Auscultation for Tube Confirmation

Accepting tube placement without waveform capnography. Counter by confirming every intubation with sustained end-tidal CO2 and treating absent capnography as esophageal intubation until proven otherwise.

### Delaying the Surgical Airway

Waiting too long in the can't-intubate, can't-oxygenate situation. Counter by defining the threshold for cricothyrotomy before induction and committing without delay when oxygenation fails.

### Placing a Subclavian Line in a Coagulopathic Patient

Choosing a non-compressible site in a patient who cannot bleed. Counter by matching site to risk — prefer compressible internal jugular or femoral sites when coagulopathy is present.

### Dilating an Artery Because It Was Misidentified as a Vein

Advancing a dilator or large-bore catheter into an artery before confirming venous pressure or blood gas. Counter by transducing or sampling the vessel before dilation, and if an arterial catheter is in place, consult vascular or interventional surgery before removal.

### Skipping the Post-Procedure Chest X-Ray

Failing to confirm line position and exclude pneumothorax. Counter by obtaining a chest x-ray after every central line and treating unexpected pneumothorax or malposition promptly.

### Calling for Help Too Late

Escalating only after multiple failures when the patient is already compromised. Counter by calling anesthesia, ENT, or vascular help at the first sign of difficulty and communicating the problem and needs clearly.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed the airway for difficulty (LEMON, Mallampati, prior history) and for physiologic difficulty (hypoxemia, hypotension, acidosis) before inducing?
- For RSI, are all drugs, suction, tubes, laryngoscope, supraglottic airway, bougie, and surgical airway kit prepared, and is capnography ready to confirm placement?
- Have I defined plan A, B, and C and the threshold for a surgical airway before induction, and am I prepared to commit without delay if oxygenation fails?
- For vascular access, have I chosen the site by indication, compressibility, infection risk, and coagulation status, and avoided subclavian in coagulopathy?
- Am I using real-time ultrasound guidance, confirming venous placement before dilation, and obtaining a post-procedure chest x-ray?
- Do I have a plan and the equipment to manage immediate complications (arterial puncture, pneumothorax, bleeding, malposition, esophageal intubation, post-induction hypotension)?
- Have I escalated to anesthesia, ENT, or vascular or interventional surgery at the first sign of difficulty, and communicated the problem and needs clearly?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
