# Clinical Handover and Care Transitions

Transitions of care are the most dangerous moments in a patient's journey, and they are dangerous precisely because they feel routine. Every handover — shift change, ward transfer, ICU step-down, hospital discharge, referral to a new service — is a point where critical information can be lost, tasks can be dropped, medications can be inadvertently changed, and the mental model of the patient can fail to transfer from one clinician to the next. Studies of preventable harm after discharge and transfer consistently show that the failures are rarely dramatic; they are the missed pending test result, the medication that was stopped or started without reconciliation, the code status that was assumed rather than confirmed, the "if this happens, do that" contingency that was never spoken. Clinicians most often fail at handovers not because they do not care but because they rely on memory and unstructured narrative instead of a closed-loop, structured transfer of both data and judgment. This skill covers the disciplined practice of handover and transition: structured communication, medication reconciliation, contingency planning, closed-loop verification, and the recognition of when a transition is unsafe.

## Core Rules

### Use a Structured Handover Framework Every Time

An unstructured verbal handover is a memory test, and memory fails predictably under fatigue, interruption, and time pressure. Use a validated structure — SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) for focused communication, or I-PASS (Illness severity, Patient summary, Action list, Situation awareness and contingency planning, Synthesis by receiver) for full shift handover — every time, not only when something feels complicated. The structure exists to force completeness: illness severity so the receiver knows who is unstable, a concise patient summary so the clinical picture transfers, an explicit action list with owners and timing so tasks do not fall, contingency planning so the receiver knows what to watch for and what to do, and synthesis by the receiver so understanding is confirmed rather than assumed. If your handover does not include each of these elements, it is incomplete by definition, regardless of how thorough it felt.

### Transfer the Mental Model, Not Just the Data

The goal of handover is not to recite facts but to transfer the receiving clinician's situational awareness — the working diagnoses, the active problems, the anticipated trajectory, the things that worry you, and the contingencies you have already thought through. State explicitly what you are watching for, what would make you concerned, and what the receiver should do if that concern materializes. A handover that lists problems without conveying clinical judgment leaves the receiver to reconstruct the model from scratch, which is exactly when instability is missed. Include the patient's goals, code status, and any limitations of treatment, because these shape every downstream decision and are frequently assumed rather than confirmed at transition.

### Reconcile Medications at Every Transition

Medication discrepancy is the single most common and most harmful transition failure, and it occurs at every boundary: admission, transfer between units, and discharge. Perform formal medication reconciliation at each transition: compile the pre-transition list from all sources (patient, pharmacy, prior records, family), compare it against the current orders, and resolve every discrepancy intentionally, documenting the rationale for each change. Pay particular attention to high-risk medications — anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, immunosuppressants, antibiotics — where a missed or duplicated dose causes rapid harm. At discharge, provide the patient with an explicit, reconciled medication list, identify which medications are new, changed, or stopped, and confirm the patient understands. A discharge without reconciled medications is a predictable cause of readmission and adverse drug events.

### Make the Action List Explicit With Owners and Timing

Tasks that are implied but not assigned are tasks that do not happen. At every handover, produce an explicit action list: each pending task stated, with the responsible person named and the timeframe specified ("recheck potassium in four hours, covering nurse to call if greater than 5.5," "MRI today, covering doctor to call radiology if not scheduled by noon"). Distinguish tasks that the outgoing clinician will complete before leaving from tasks that transfer to the receiver. Read back critical orders and confirm understanding. The action list is the operational core of the handover; without it, the receiver inherits a patient but not a plan.

### Plan Contingencies and Define Escalation Triggers

A receiving clinician who does not know what to watch for will recognize deterioration late. For each patient, state the contingencies: if this happens, do that, and call me if this. Define explicit escalation triggers — vital sign thresholds, symptom onset, lab values, or clinical changes — that should prompt the receiver to act or to call for help. Specify who to call and how, especially out of hours and across service boundaries. Contingency planning is particularly important for unstable patients, for patients with evolving pictures, and for handovers to less familiar teams, where the receiver may not share your intuition for early warning signs.

### Close the Loop With Synthesis by the Receiver

Handover is not complete when the sender finishes speaking; it is complete when the receiver demonstrates understanding. Require the receiver to synthesize back: the ill patients, the active issues, the action list, and the contingencies. This is not a formality — it is the verification step that catches the majority of transfer failures, because misunderstandings surface here rather than at the bedside. Use read-back for critical information, and treat a handover without receiver synthesis as incomplete. If the receiver cannot synthesize, the handover has not succeeded regardless of how clearly it was delivered.

### Manage the Written and Verbal Handover Together

The verbal handover and the written record must agree. Keep the written handover document current and aligned with the verbal exchange, because the next clinician may rely on either. Avoid contradictory information between the two — a verbal plan that differs from the written plan is a setup for error. Ensure that pending results, follow-up commitments, and code status are captured in writing, not only spoken, because verbal-only information is lost within a shift or two.

### Recognize and Escalate Unsafe Transitions

Not every handover can or should proceed as planned. A handover is unsafe when the receiver lacks the competence, capacity, or information to accept care safely — for example, a critically unstable patient handed to a team without the skills or staffing to manage them, a transfer to a setting that cannot provide the required monitoring, or a discharge to a home situation that cannot support the care plan. When you recognize an unsafe transition, do not complete it silently: escalate to the charge nurse, the responsible consultant, the bed manager, or the receiving institution, and document the concern. Accepting an unsafe handover is itself a safety failure. Equally, if you are the receiver and you cannot safely accept, say so and escalate rather than absorb risk you cannot manage.

### Pay Specific Attention to Discharge as a Transition

Hospital discharge is a transition to a setting with far less monitoring, and it is where many preventable readmissions originate. Before discharge, confirm the patient is clinically stable, that the working diagnosis and plan are clear, that medications are reconciled and understood, that pending results have a named owner who will follow them up, that follow-up appointments are booked and the patient knows how to get there, that the patient and family understand warning signs and what to do, and that the primary care provider receives a timely discharge summary. A discharge that meets these criteria is a safe transition; one that does not is a deferred crisis.

## Common Traps

### Unstructured Narrative Handover

Telling the patient's story without a framework, which omits action items and contingencies. Counter by using SBAR or I-PASS every time, regardless of perceived complexity.

### Transferring Data Without Judgment

Reciting problems without conveying what to watch for and what to do. Counter by explicitly stating contingencies, escalation triggers, and the things that worry you.

### Skipping or Superficial Medication Reconciliation

Assuming the medication list is correct, especially at discharge. Counter by performing formal reconciliation at every transition and resolving every discrepancy intentionally.

### Implied but Unassigned Tasks

Saying "we need to recheck the potassium" without naming who and when. Counter by making every task explicit with an owner and a timeframe.

### No Receiver Synthesis

Treating handover as complete when the sender finishes. Counter by requiring the receiver to synthesize back the ill patients, the action list, and the contingencies.

### Verbal-Only Information

Communicating code status, pending results, or follow-up only verbally. Counter by capturing every commitment in the written record.

### Completing an Unsafe Handover Silently

Accepting a patient you cannot safely manage, or transferring to a setting that cannot support the care. Counter by escalating and documenting when a transition is unsafe.

### Discharge Without Follow-Up Ownership

Discharging with pending results and no named owner to receive and act on them. Counter by assigning explicit follow-up responsibility before discharge.

## Self-Check

- Did I use a structured framework (SBAR or I-PASS) for the handover rather than unstructured narrative?
- Did I transfer clinical judgment and contingencies, not just a list of problems?
- Did I perform formal medication reconciliation at this transition and resolve every discrepancy?
- Is every pending task on an explicit action list with a named owner and a timeframe?
- Did I define escalation triggers and specify who to call and how if the patient deteriorates?
- Did the receiver synthesize the handover back, and did I use read-back for critical information?
- Do the verbal and written handover agree, with no contradictions?
- If the transition was unsafe, did I escalate and document rather than complete it silently?
- At discharge, are medications reconciled, pending results owned, follow-up booked, and warning signs communicated?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
