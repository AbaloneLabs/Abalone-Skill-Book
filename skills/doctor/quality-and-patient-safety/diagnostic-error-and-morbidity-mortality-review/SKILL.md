# Diagnostic Error and Morbidity and Mortality Review

Diagnostic error is the largest and most underexamined category of preventable harm in medicine, and it is also the hardest to confront because it implicates the clinician's own thinking. A diagnostic error is not simply a wrong answer; it is a failure to establish an accurate and timely explanation of the patient's problem, whether by missed diagnosis, wrong diagnosis, or delayed diagnosis. Morbidity and mortality (M&M) review exists to make these failures visible, to extract learning from them, and to convert individual cases into system improvements. The danger in M&M is not only that the case is misdiagnosed again in the discussion — it is that the review collapses into blame, into "the doctor should have known," or into ritualistic case presentation that produces no change. Clinicians most often fail to learn from harm not because they are indifferent but because they treat the review as a performance rather than an analysis, and because they stop at the individual error instead of reaching the system conditions that produced it. This skill covers the disciplined approach to diagnostic error analysis and M&M participation: classifying the error, applying systems thinking, conducting a blameless review, and designing corrective actions that actually close the loop.

## Core Rules

### Classify the Error Before Judging It

Diagnostic errors arise from different sources, and the response depends on the source. Use a recognized taxonomy — no-fault errors (atypical or masked presentations, patient non-adherence), system errors (equipment failure, staffing, process breakdown, lost results), and cognitive errors (anchoring, premature closure, bias, knowledge gaps). Within cognitive errors, distinguish knowledge deficits from application failures and from reasoning failures. The classification matters because the corrective action differs: a system error calls for process redesign, a knowledge deficit calls for education and decision support, and a reasoning failure calls for cognitive forcing strategies and structured re-evaluation. Misclassifying a system error as a cognitive one leads to "re-educate the clinician," which will not prevent recurrence; misclassifying a cognitive error as a system one leads to a new checklist that no one follows. Spend the time to classify accurately, and revisit the classification as the analysis deepens.

### Reconstruct the Diagnostic Trajectory, Not Just the Outcome

A diagnostic error is a process that unfolds over time, not a single wrong decision at a single moment. Reconstruct the trajectory: what was known at each encounter, what differential was considered, what data was sought, what data was available but not accessed, how probability was revised, what thresholds were set for action, and where the trajectory diverged from the path that would have reached the diagnosis. Map the points where disconfirming evidence was discounted, where the differential narrowed prematurely, where follow-up was assumed but not closed, and where handovers lost critical information. The trajectory view reveals the leverage points for intervention in a way that the outcome view never can, because the outcome is already known and biases the reconstruction.

### Apply Systems Thinking: Error Is the Symptom, Not the Cause

Human error is the starting point of an investigation, not the conclusion. When a clinician makes a cognitive error, ask why the system permitted that error to occur and propagate: was the critical result buried in a long report, was the patient handed over without the key uncertainty communicated, was there no decision support for a high-risk drug interaction, was the workload such that deliberate Type 2 reasoning was impossible, was there a culture that discouraged asking for a second opinion? Each "why" should move the analysis from the individual toward the latent conditions — design, environment, supervision, culture, regulation — that set the conditions for failure. A corrective action that ends at the individual (re-education, warning, discipline) addresses the symptom and leaves the disease intact. Keep asking why until you reach a system condition you can change.

### Conduct the Review as Blameless Analysis

The purpose of M&M is learning, not adjudication, and a blameful review destroys learning. Establish and protect a blameless posture: describe behavior in neutral terms, avoid naming and shaming, assume the clinician was trying to do the right thing, and examine the conditions that made the wrong path seem reasonable at the time. This does not mean excusing recklessness or misconduct — those are handled through separate just-culture and disciplinary channels — but it does mean that honest error is met with analysis, not punishment. A blameless review encourages clinicians to bring forward their own errors and near misses, which is the only way the system sees its real failure rate. If the room senses that blame is in play, future cases will be hidden, and the next preventable death will occur without warning.

### Examine Cognitive Biases by Name

Within the cognitive contribution to the error, name the specific biases that operated. Anchoring on the first impression, premature closure on a plausible diagnosis, availability bias from a recent memorable case, framing bias from accepting a referral diagnosis, diagnostic momentum from accepting prior clinicians' labels, search-satisficing from stopping the search once one finding is made. Naming the bias is diagnostic in itself: it tells you which cognitive forcing strategy would have countered it — for example, mandated differential breadth for anchoring, explicit must-not-miss ruling-out for premature closure, structured re-evaluation triggers for diagnostic momentum. A review that concludes "the clinician made an error" without naming the cognitive mechanism produces no actionable insight.

### Close the Loop With Measurable Corrective Actions

Every case reviewed should produce at least one corrective action with an owner, a deadline, and a measurable target — and the loop must be closed by verifying that the action was implemented and that it reduced risk. Vague intentions ("we will improve communication," "staff will be more vigilant") are not corrective actions. Strong corrective actions redesign the process or the environment (forcing functions, checklists embedded in workflow, decision support, redundancy); weaker ones rely on memory and exhortation (posters, education, reminders). Prefer strong actions, and schedule a follow-up review to confirm the change took hold and the failure mode did not recur or migrate. An M&M program that generates recommendations but never verifies their effect is a ritual, not a safety system.

### Feed Learning Back Into Practice and Into the Diagnostic Process

The learning from a case must reach the clinicians who were not in the room. Distribute anonymized case learning through grand rounds, safety newsletters, guideline updates, and decision-support changes. Where a recurring error pattern emerges across cases, escalate to the quality and patient safety committee for a system-level intervention rather than case-by-case fixes. Build feedback loops into the diagnostic process itself: when a diagnosis is revised or made later, ensure the original clinician is notified, so that their future calibration is improved. Diagnostic reasoning without feedback calcifies; diagnostic reasoning with feedback improves.

### Recognize When to Escalate Beyond the Department

Some cases exceed what a single department can fix: cases involving multiple services, systemic communication failures across settings, recurring patterns, or potential criminal, regulatory, or credentialing concerns. Escalate these to the institutional quality and patient safety committee, risk management, or the appropriate regulatory body. Do not allow a case to be quietly absorbed at the department level when its implications are institutional. Equally, recognize when a case indicates a clinician who may be impaired, unwell, or practicing outside competence, and escalate to the appropriate wellness, credentialing, or supervisory channel rather than handling it informally.

## Common Traps

### Stopping at "The Clinician Made an Error"

Ending the analysis at the individual cognitive failure. Counter by continuing to ask why the system permitted the error until an actionable system condition is reached.

### Hindsight Bias in Reconstruction

Judging the clinician's reasoning with knowledge of the final diagnosis, which makes the error seem obvious in retrospect. Counter by reconstructing what was known at each moment and what was reasonable given that information.

### Blameful Language That Silences Future Reporting

Naming, shaming, or moralizing about the involved clinician. Counter by describing behavior neutrally and protecting a blameless analytic posture.

### Vague Corrective Actions

"We will be more careful" or "education will be provided" with no owner, deadline, or measure. Counter by redesigning the process and scheduling verification that the change reduced risk.

### Misclassifying System Errors as Cognitive Ones

Treating a staffing, equipment, or process failure as if it were a thinking failure. Counter by mapping the system contributions before assigning cognitive contribution.

### Failing to Close the Loop

Generating recommendations but never verifying implementation or effect. Counter by scheduling follow-up review of each corrective action and tracking recurrence.

### Case Review Without Feedback to the Original Clinician

Reaching a revised diagnosis without informing the clinician who initially missed it. Counter by building notification into the diagnostic feedback loop so calibration improves.

### Department-Level Absorption of Institutional Problems

Quietly resolving cases that have system-wide implications. Counter by escalating recurring or multi-service patterns to the institutional safety committee.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify the diagnostic error as no-fault, system, or cognitive, and did I revisit that classification as the analysis deepened?
- Did I reconstruct the diagnostic trajectory over time rather than judging only the outcome?
- Have I identified the latent system conditions that permitted the error, not only the individual cognitive contribution?
- Did I name the specific cognitive biases that operated, and the forcing strategies that would have countered them?
- Did I protect a blameless analytic posture that encourages future reporting rather than silencing it?
- Did I avoid hindsight bias by reasoning from what was known at each moment?
- Does each corrective action have an owner, a deadline, a measurable target, and a scheduled verification?
- Have I escalated cases with institutional, multi-service, recurring, or potential misconduct implications to the appropriate authority?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
