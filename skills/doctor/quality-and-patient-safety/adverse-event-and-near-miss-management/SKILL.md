# Adverse Event and Near Miss Management

Adverse events and near misses are the moments when the safety of a healthcare system is tested most directly. An adverse event is harm that reached the patient; a near miss is a failure that was caught before it reached the patient. Both carry the same diagnostic value for the system, but only one carries visible harm, and that asymmetry shapes how clinicians respond. The danger in the immediate aftermath of harm is not only the clinical deterioration of the patient — it is the cascade of defensive, evasive, or blame-focused reactions that destroy the opportunity to learn. Clinicians most often compound harm not through malice but through delayed disclosure, premature fault-finding, undocumented facts, and failure to secure the environment and the evidence. This skill covers the disciplined response to incidents: stabilizing the patient, disclosing honestly, preserving information, analyzing root causes without scapegoating, and feeding learning back into practice.

## Core Rules

### Stabilize the Patient First, Then Secure the Scene

The first duty after any adverse event is to the patient, not to the paperwork. Address clinical deterioration before anything else — escalate acuity, call for help, and treat the harm. Once the patient is stable, secure the scene and the evidence before it degrades or is altered. This means preserving the involved devices, medications, packaging, infusion lines, and equipment in their post-event state; sequestering any suspected faulty item for biomedical engineering or the manufacturer; and not discarding, "cleaning up," or returning items to circulation. Failing to preserve evidence is one of the most common and most damaging errors, because it permanently destroys the ability to determine what happened. If a device or medication may have contributed, treat it as evidence until risk management instructs otherwise.

### Notify and Escalate Promptly

Reporting is time-sensitive and is a professional obligation, not an optional courtesy. Notify the responsible senior clinician, the charge nurse, and the unit leader immediately for any event involving harm, unexpected death, wrong-site procedure, retained foreign object, medication error with harm, or any sentinel event as defined by your institution. Escalate to risk management, patient safety, and hospital leadership according to the institutional severity matrix — most organizations require notification within hours, not days, for serious harm. Understand your local definition of a reportable event and err on the side of reporting; an unreported event cannot be learned from, and underreporting is itself a safety failure. If you are uncertain whether an event meets the threshold, report it and let the safety system triage.

### Fulfill the Duty of Candor Through Open Disclosure

Patients and families who have been harmed have a right to a timely, honest, and compassionate explanation of what happened, what is known, what is not yet known, and what will be done. Disclosure is not optional and it is not conditional on certainty — you disclose what you know, acknowledge what you do not, and commit to follow up as the investigation proceeds. Do not speculate, do not assign blame to colleagues in front of the patient, and do not minimize or euphemize harm. Use trained disclosure conversations, often led by the attending physician with risk management support, and document only what was disclosed and agreed in the record — not opinion, blame, or speculation. In most jurisdictions, apology and expression of sympathy are protected and encouraged; do not let fear of litigation silence you, but coordinate the legal framing with risk management before any detailed discussion of fault or compensation.

### Preserve Information Without Speculation in the Record

The medical record after an event must be factual, contemporaneous, and focused on the patient's clinical condition and the actions taken. Document the clinical facts: what was observed, when, what was done, who was notified, and the patient's response. Do not document blame, opinion, "should have," or speculation about cause — those belong in the incident report, not the patient chart. Do not alter, backdate, or delete prior entries; if an error in the record needs correction, follow the formal amendment process with date, time, signature, and a clear mark that the original is not erased. Recognize that the chart is a legal document and will be reviewed; a clean, factual, timely record protects both the patient and the clinician, while a record that has been edited or loaded with blame damages everyone.

### Complete an Incident Report Through the Designated Channel

The incident reporting system is the mechanism by which the organization learns. File the report as soon as practical after the patient is stable and the immediate facts are recorded, ideally within the same shift. Describe what happened factually and sequentially, identify contributing factors as you understand them, and avoid assigning individual blame in the report — the purpose is system learning, not personnel discipline. Distinguish clearly between the patient medical record (clinical care) and the incident report (safety analysis); they serve different purposes and have different legal protections. Understand that near misses are as important to report as events that caused harm, because they reveal the same latent failures without the cost of injury.

### Conduct Root Cause Analysis, Not Root Person Blame

When analyzing an event, the default question must be "why did the system allow this to happen," not "who is at fault." Use a structured method — root cause analysis (RCA), apparent cause analysis, or the five-whys technique — to trace the chain of events back to the latent system conditions that made the error possible: lookalike packaging, ambiguous orders, staffing pressure, missing alerts, workarounds that became normal, equipment failures, communication gaps. Human error is almost never the root cause; it is the symptom of a system that set a person up to fail. Identify actionable corrective actions that address system conditions, not merely reminders to "be more careful," which are not corrective actions at all. Validate that each corrective action has an owner, a deadline, and a measurable effect, and close the loop by confirming the change actually reduced risk.

### Apply a Just Culture to Distinguish Error From Misconduct

A just culture separates blameless human error and at-risk behavior from reckless or intentionally harmful conduct, and responds to each appropriately. Blameless error calls for console and system redesign; at-risk behavior calls for coaching and removal of the incentives that made the risky path attractive; reckless conduct calls for accountability through the disciplinary process. Do not reflexively punish clinicians for honest errors, because a punitive response drives reporting underground and guarantees the next event will also be hidden. Equally, do not use "system thinking" as a shield to avoid addressing willful disregard of safety rules. When the behavior may cross into recklessness or impairment, escalate to the appropriate authority rather than adjudicating it yourself.

### Support the Second Victim

The clinician involved in harm is a second victim, and their distress is real, predictable, and consequential. Acknowledge it explicitly, offer peer support, and connect the involved clinician to the institution's second-victim or peer-support program. Recognize that unsupported second victims are more likely to leave practice, make further errors, or suffer burnout, and that supporting them is itself a patient-safety intervention. Do not isolate the involved clinician or treat them as if they are guilty before the analysis is complete.

## Common Traps

### Delaying or Avoiding Disclosure

Waiting for certainty, waiting for the investigation to close, or hoping the family will not ask. Counter by disclosing promptly what is known and committing to follow up; certainty is not a prerequisite for honesty.

### Speculating About Cause in the Medical Record

Writing opinion, blame, or "the nurse failed to" into the chart. Counter by recording only clinical facts and actions in the chart and reserving analysis for the incident report.

### Discarding or Altering the Scene

Returning a suspected device to use, cleaning up packaging, or editing prior chart entries. Counter by sequestering evidence and using the formal amendment process for any record correction.

### Underreporting Near Misses

Treating "nothing happened" as "nothing to report." Counter by reporting every near miss, because the latent failure is identical to the one that will eventually cause harm.

### Individual Blame Masquerading as Root Cause

Stopping the analysis at "the doctor made an error." Counter by continuing to ask why the system permitted, enabled, or failed to catch the error until an actionable system condition is identified.

### Using "Be More Careful" As a Corrective Action

Issuing reminders or education as the only response to a system failure. Counter by redesigning the process, the environment, or the technology so that the safer path is the default.

### Punishing Honest Error

Reflexively pursuing discipline for blameless mistakes, which silences future reporting. Counter by applying just-culture principles and reserving discipline for reckless or intentional conduct.

### Ignoring the Second Victim

Leaving the involved clinician isolated and unsupported. Counter by proactively offering peer support and recognizing that clinician recovery is part of the system response.

## Self-Check

- Did I stabilize the patient and secure the scene before turning to documentation and reporting?
- Have I preserved all relevant devices, medications, packaging, and equipment as evidence rather than discarding or returning them?
- Have I notified the responsible senior clinician, charge nurse, and risk management within the institutional timeframe for an event of this severity?
- Did I disclose to the patient and family what is known, what is not known, and the plan to follow up, without speculation or blame?
- Is the medical record factual, contemporaneous, and free of opinion, blame, or backdated alterations?
- Have I filed an incident report through the designated channel, including near misses?
- Did I conduct a root cause analysis that reached an actionable system condition rather than stopping at individual error?
- Did I apply just-culture principles to distinguish blameless error from at-risk behavior from reckless conduct?
- Have I supported the involved clinician as a second victim and connected them to peer support?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
