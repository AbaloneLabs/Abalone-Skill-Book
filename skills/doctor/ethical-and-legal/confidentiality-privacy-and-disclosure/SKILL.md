# Confidentiality, Privacy, and Disclosure

Confidentiality is a core, not a peripheral, obligation of medicine: it is the condition under which patients reveal the information that makes diagnosis and treatment possible, and breaching it wrongly damages both the individual and the trust on which the whole system runs. Yet confidentiality is not absolute, and the central judgment problem is deciding when the duty to protect privacy yields to a competing duty — to the patient's own safety, to identifiable third parties, to public health, or to the courts. The danger is two-sided. Clinicians either over-disclose, treating confidentiality casually and exposing patients to harm, stigma, and legal liability; or they under-disclose, hiding behind "confidentiality" to avoid mandatory reporting, duty-to-warn obligations, or safeguarding duties that the law and ethics actually require. This skill covers the scope of the duty, the recognized exceptions, the special protections for sensitive data and vulnerable groups, and the disciplined process for deciding whether and how to disclose. Because privacy and reporting law are jurisdiction-specific and breaches carry serious consequences, escalate to legal counsel, ethics, or administration whenever the disclosure decision is contested, novel, or high-stakes.

## Core Rules

### Treat Confidentiality as the Default, and Justify Every Exception Explicitly

The default is non-disclosure: patient information is shared only with those directly involved in the patient's care, only to the extent necessary, and only with a legitimate basis. Before any disclosure, ask four questions: is there an exception that permits or requires this disclosure, is the recipient authorized, is the scope of disclosure limited to what is necessary, and have I documented the justification? Casual disclosure — discussing patients in lifts, sharing identifiable details with colleagues not involved in care, gossiping with family — is a breach even when no malice is intended. Apply the minimum-necessary principle: disclose the least information needed to achieve the legitimate purpose. When in doubt, do not disclose; seek guidance first. The asymmetry of harm matters: a wrongful disclosure cannot be undone, while a briefly delayed permissible disclosure usually can be corrected.

### Recognize and Act on Mandatory Reporting Duties

Some disclosures are not optional — they are legally mandated, and failure to report carries legal and professional consequences. Know your jurisdiction's mandatory reporting obligations, which commonly include suspected child abuse and neglect, vulnerable-adult (elder) abuse, certain infectious and notifiable diseases, gunshot and certain other wounds, and impaired drivers in some jurisdictions. Mandatory reporting overrides confidentiality by law; the duty is to report to the designated authority, not to investigate or adjudicate. When you have a reasonable suspicion that meets the reporting threshold, report — you do not need certainty, and you are generally protected when reporting in good faith. Document the suspicion, the threshold met, the authority notified, and the time. Do not promise a patient you will keep abuse or a notifiable disease secret; you cannot. Conversely, do not report where the legal threshold is not met — over-reporting harms patients and wastes safeguarding resources.

### Apply the Duty to Warn and Protect Identifiable Third Parties

Where a patient poses a serious risk of harm to an identifiable person or persons, confidentiality may yield to a duty to warn or protect, derived in many jurisdictions from landmark case law. The duty is triggered by a credible, serious threat to an identifiable victim, and the response is calibrated: warn the potential victim, notify law enforcement, take reasonable steps to contain the threat, and document the reasoning. The disclosure should be limited to what is necessary to avert the harm. This is a high-stakes, fact-specific judgment — assess the seriousness and credibility of the threat, the identifiability of the victim, and the imminence, and act where the threshold is met. When uncertain, escalate immediately to risk assessment, legal counsel, and security; do not sit on a credible threat in the name of confidentiality.

### Protect Sensitive Data and Vulnerable-Group Confidentiality Extra Carefully

Certain information and certain patients require heightened protection. Adolescent confidentiality: where the law permits minors to consent to specific care (contraception, sexually transmitted infection treatment, mental health, substance use, pregnancy care in some jurisdictions), that care is confidential from parents and others — breaching it drives adolescents away from care and causes measurable harm. Reproductive, sexual, mental health, substance use, gender, and HIV status are highly sensitive; share only with explicit basis and minimum necessary. Domestic violence situations carry particular danger — disclosure to a partner or family can endanger the patient, so verify who is authorized to receive information and screen for safety before sharing. Be alert to coercion: a family member demanding information may not be authorized, and the patient may not be free to consent. When handling records requests, verify identity and authority, release only what is authorized, and exclude third-party or specially protected information where required.

### Secure Data in Practice, Not Just in Policy

Confidentiality is operational, not theoretical. Apply it in daily practice: lock screens, do not leave records visible, use secure channels for electronic communication, avoid identifiable details in messages and emails to unauthorized recipients, limit verbal discussions to private spaces, and follow institutional data-protection and breach-reporting protocols. Recognize that de-identification is harder than it looks — combinations of rare diagnoses, demographics, and dates can re-identify patients, so apply true de-identification standards before any secondary use. Report breaches promptly when they occur; concealment compounds the harm and the liability. Treat patient data as you would your own most sensitive information.

### Manage Records Requests Through Verified Authority

Requests for patient information — from the patient, family, insurers, employers, lawyers, or other clinicians — must be processed through verified authority and proper scope. Confirm the patient's consent or the legal basis for release, verify the identity of the requester, release only the information authorized and necessary, and apply the special protections to sensitive content. Do not release records to a family member on their say-so, and do not release an entire record when a specific note or summary is requested. When a request is legally complex (subpoena, court order, third-party records), escalate to legal counsel or health information management before releasing.

## Common Traps

### Hiding Behind Confidentiality to Avoid Mandatory Reporting

Confidentiality does not override legally mandated reporting of abuse or notifiable disease. Counter by knowing your reporting duties and acting on reasonable suspicion.

### Casual Disclosure in Seemingly Harmless Settings

Lifts, corridors, messages, and social settings breed identifiable disclosures. Counter by applying the minimum-necessary principle and confining clinical discussion to private, authorized settings.

### Over-Disclosing in a Duty-to-Warn Situation

The duty permits necessary disclosure, not a data dump. Counter by limiting disclosure to what is required to avert the harm to the identifiable victim.

### Breaching Adolescent or Sensitive-Data Confidentiality

Revealing confidential adolescent care or sensitive diagnoses to family drives patients from care and may be unlawful. Counter by knowing when minors' care is confidential and protecting sensitive data with heightened rigor.

### Releasing Records Without Verifying Authority

Handing records to family or on informal request is a breach. Counter by verifying identity, consent, and legal basis before any release.

### Assuming De-Identified Data Cannot Be Re-Identified

Rare diagnoses plus demographics re-identify patients. Counter by applying true de-identification standards before any secondary use or publication.

### Sitting on a Credible Threat

Delay under the banner of confidentiality can cost a life. Counter by escalating credible threats immediately for risk assessment, warning, and law-enforcement notification.

## Self-Check

- Have I treated confidentiality as the default and justified every exception explicitly, applying the minimum-necessary principle?
- For any suspected abuse, notifiable disease, or other mandatory-report situation, have I recognized the legal duty and reported to the proper authority on reasonable suspicion?
- Where a credible threat to an identifiable third party exists, have I escalated for duty-to-warn assessment and limited disclosure to what is necessary to avert harm?
- Have I applied heightened protection to adolescent, reproductive, mental health, substance use, and other sensitive data, and screened for coercion before sharing?
- For any records request, have I verified the requester's identity and legal authority and released only the authorized, necessary information?
- Am I securing data operationally — screens, channels, discussions, de-identification — and reporting breaches promptly when they occur?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical or legal judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as contested disclosure, credible threats, subpoenas, or safeguarding concerns appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
