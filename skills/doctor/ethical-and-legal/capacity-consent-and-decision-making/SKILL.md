# Capacity, Consent, and Decision-Making

Consent and capacity are not paperwork — they are the legal and ethical foundation of every medical intervention, and they are where well-intentioned clinicians most often cause harm through shortcuts. The central judgment problems are fourfold. First, capacity is frequently assumed rather than assessed, and clinicians confuse capacity with the outcome they want (a patient who "consents" is deemed capable; one who refuses is deemed incapable). Second, informed consent is reduced to a signature, stripping out the disclosure, comprehension, and voluntariness that make consent valid. Third, refusals of treatment — especially life-saving treatment — trigger anxiety that leads to coercion, mislabeling, or paternalistic override. Fourth, when capacity is genuinely lacking, clinicians reach for vague "best interest" intuitions instead of the structured, legally grounded frameworks that govern substitute decision-making. This skill covers functional capacity assessment, the elements of valid consent, the disciplined handling of refusal, and the proper use of advance directives and substitute decision-makers. Because capacity law varies by jurisdiction and the stakes include both patient harm and legal liability, escalate to ethics, legal counsel, or administration whenever the case is contested, irreversible, or unclear.

## Core Rules

### Assess Capacity Functionally, Decision by Decision

Capacity is not a global trait and not a label; it is decision-specific, time-specific, and situation-specific. A patient may have capacity to consent to a blood draw but not to refuse life-sustaining treatment; capacity may fluctuate with delirium, intoxication, pain, or medication. Assess capacity functionally using the four-part test: does the person understand the relevant information (the condition, the proposed treatment, alternatives including no treatment, and the consequences), appreciate the situation and its consequences for themselves specifically, reason with the information in a logical way, and communicate a choice (by any means, including non-verbal). Each element must be tested, not assumed. Do not equate capacity with "making the right choice" — a capacitated person may make a decision you judge unwise, and disagreement with medical advice is not, by itself, evidence of incapacity. Document the functional assessment, including the specific questions asked and the responses, not merely a conclusion.

### Obtain Valid Informed Consent Through Disclosure, Comprehension, and Voluntariness

A signature is the endpoint of consent, not its substance. Valid informed consent requires three things working together. Disclosure: the patient must receive the information a reasonable person would want — the nature of the condition, the proposed intervention, material risks and benefits, reasonable alternatives, and the consequences of declining, in language they understand. Comprehension: you must verify, by asking the patient to teach back, that they actually understood — disclosure without comprehension is not consent. Voluntariness: the decision must be free of coercion, undue influence, or manipulation, including from family, clinicians, or institutional pressure. Assess each element explicitly. Consent obtained under duress, after inadequate disclosure, or without checking comprehension is not valid, and proceeding on it is both ethically and legally a battery. Document who disclosed what, the patient's demonstrated understanding, and the voluntariness of the decision.

### Treat Refusal of Treatment With the Same Rigor as Consent

Refusal is the mirror image of consent and demands the same functional capacity assessment and the same rigor — no more, no less. The danger is asymmetry: clinicians scrutinize refusals for incapacity far harder than consents, effectively requiring patients to "prove" capacity to decline. Apply the same four-part functional test. A capacitated adult may refuse any treatment, including life-saving treatment, even if the refusal appears unwise, self-destructive, or contrary to medical recommendation — autonomy is the right to make a decision others consider wrong. Explore the reasons for refusal (fear, misunderstanding, religious belief, past trauma, financial concerns), address reversible barriers, offer alternatives, and give time where possible — but if capacity is intact and the refusal persists, respect it and document thoroughly. Where refusal of life-sustaining treatment is at stake, seek a second clinical opinion on capacity and involve ethics early; these cases are high-risk for both the patient and the clinician.

### Use Advance Directives and Substitute Decision-Makers Correctly

When a patient lacks capacity, decision-making authority passes by a defined legal hierarchy, not by clinical convenience. First, check for a valid advance directive or advance care plan made when the patient had capacity — these carry legal weight and must be honored within their scope. Second, identify the legally recognized substitute decision-maker (healthcare proxy, power of attorney, guardian, or statutory hierarchy of family), and understand that the substitute decides using the patient's known wishes and values where known, not their own preferences. Third, where no directive or surrogate exists, apply the best-interest standard: weigh the benefits, burdens, and risks of each option from the patient's perspective, considering their known values, and choose the option a reasonable person in their situation would choose. Best interest is not "what the family wants" and not "what the clinician would choose" — it is a structured, patient-centered analysis. Document the legal basis for the surrogate's authority and the reasoning behind the best-interest determination.

### Reassess Capacity and Remove Barriers Before Concluding Incapacity

Capacity can be impaired by reversible factors — delirium, infection, hypoxia, pain, medication, intoxication, psychiatric crisis — and concluding incapacity in an acutely unwell patient without treating the reversible cause is a common and serious error. Treat the delirium, relieve the pain, allow intoxication to clear, optimize the environment, use communication aids and interpreters, and reassess. Transient incapacity is not the same as enduring incapacity. Equally, support capacity before declaring it absent: provide information in accessible form, involve family or trusted others the patient wants present, and allow time. Only after reversible barriers are addressed and support is provided should you conclude capacity is genuinely lacking. Document what was tried.

### Escalate Contested, Irreversible, or High-Stakes Cases

Capacity and consent disputes are a leading source of ethics consultation and legal action. Escalate whenever the decision is irreversible (sterilization, amputation, withdrawal of life support), whenever capacity is genuinely unclear or contested, whenever there is conflict between family members or between clinicians and family, whenever the patient is a vulnerable adult at risk of coercion, and whenever restraint or compulsion is being considered. Involve the ethics committee, seek legal counsel, and in some jurisdictions the courts, before acting. Do not resolve a high-stakes ethical-legal question alone.

## Common Traps

### Equating Capacity With Agreeing With the Clinician

Patients who consent are assumed capable; those who refuse are scrutinized for incapacity. Counter by applying the same functional test to consent and refusal alike.

### Treating the Signature as the Consent

A signed form without disclosure, verified comprehension, and voluntariness is not valid consent. Counter by assessing and documenting each element, including teach-back.

### Concluding Incapacity in an Acutely Unwell Patient Without Treating Reversible Causes

Delirium, pain, hypoxia, and intoxication can mimic incapacity. Counter by treating reversible factors, supporting the patient, and reassessing before concluding.

### Letting Family Decide Using Their Own Preferences

Substitute decision-makers must use the patient's known wishes and values, not their own. Counter by orienting the surrogate to the patient's perspective and applying the best-interest standard.

### Overriding a Valid Refusal of Life-Saving Treatment Out of Anxiety

A capacitated refusal, even of life-saving care, must be respected. Counter by rigorous capacity assessment, second opinion, ethics involvement, and thorough documentation — but respect autonomy if capacity is intact.

### Ignoring or Misapplying Advance Directives

A valid advance directive made with capacity governs care within its scope and must be honored. Counter by actively checking for directives and applying them correctly rather than defaulting to family wishes.

### Acting Alone on Irreversible or Contested Decisions

High-stakes, irreversible, or disputed capacity cases carry legal and ethical risk. Counter by escalating to ethics, legal counsel, or the courts before acting.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed capacity functionally and decision-specifically using understand, appreciate, reason, and communicate — and documented the questions and responses, not just a conclusion?
- For consent, have I verified disclosure, comprehension (teach-back), and voluntariness, rather than relying on a signature?
- For any refusal, have I applied the same rigor as for consent, explored reasons, addressed barriers, and respected a capacitated refusal even of life-saving treatment?
- Where capacity is lacking, have I checked for a valid advance directive and identified the legally authorized substitute decision-maker, orienting them to the patient's wishes and values?
- Have I treated reversible causes of incapacity and supported the patient before concluding capacity is absent?
- For irreversible, unclear, or contested decisions, have I escalated to the ethics committee, legal counsel, or the courts before acting?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical or legal judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as contested capacity, suspected coercion, or irreversible decisions appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
