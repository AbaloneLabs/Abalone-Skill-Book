# Pediatric and Maternal Resuscitation

Resuscitating a child, a newborn, or a pregnant patient is where standard adult algorithms fail most dangerously, because the physiology, the drug doses, the equipment, and even the ethical framing differ fundamentally from the adult default. The danger is applying adult logic to a non-adult body: an infant's airway is not a small adult airway, a child's weight-based dose cannot be estimated by eye, a newborn's transition requires a sequence unrelated to adult ACLS, and a pregnant patient in arrest carries two patients whose physiology has been remodeled by gestation. A second danger is the emotional charge itself — pediatric and maternal arrests are rare, terrifying, and prone to both freezing and chaotic improvisation. This skill covers the disciplined adaptation of resuscitation to pediatric, neonatal, and maternal contexts — always with the understanding that these are high-stakes, low-frequency events demanding early escalation to specialists (pediatrics, neonatology, obstetrics, anesthesia) and meticulous preparation.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Weight and Drug Doses Explicitly, Never Estimate

In pediatric resuscitation, every drug dose, defibrillation energy, fluid bolus, and equipment size is weight-based, and errors of estimation are a leading cause of preventable harm. Use a validated length-based tape (e.g., Broselow) at the bedside to estimate weight when actual weight is unknown, and use pre-calculated dosing references or institutional emergency drug sheets rather than mental arithmetic under stress. State the weight, the dose, and the concentration aloud, and use closed-loop communication for every medication. For epinephrine in cardiac arrest, the dose is 0.01 mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000) IV/IO every 3-5 minutes; energy for defibrillation is 2 J/kg initially, then 4 J/kg for subsequent shocks; fluid bolus in shock is typically 10 mL/kg in children and 20 mL/kg in infants, reassessed after each. Have a second provider verify calculations whenever possible. When the weight is uncertain, err toward using the tape rather than guessing, and document the basis for dosing.

### Adapt Airway and Ventilation to Pediatric Anatomy

The pediatric airway is anatomically distinct, not merely smaller. Infants have a large occiput (position the head in the sniffing position, avoid overextension), a large tongue relative to the oral cavity, a larynx that is higher and more anterior, and the narrowest point is at the cricoid cartilage rather than the vocal cords. Use uncuffed endotracheal tubes for the youngest infants per local practice, or cuffed tubes with careful cuff pressure for older children. Choose tube size by age-based formulas or length-based tape, and have a size above and below ready. For bag-valve-mask ventilation, use an appropriately sized mask and avoid excessive ventilation — children arrest primarily from respiratory failure, so effective oxygenation and ventilation are the foundation of pediatric resuscitation. Avoid hyperoxia and hyperventilation once circulation is restored. If intubation is difficult, do not persist — use a supraglottic airway or bag-mask ventilation and call for experienced pediatric or anesthesia help early.

### Follow PALS Pathways: Respiratory and Circulatory Failure Precede Most Pediatric Arrests

Unlike adults, most pediatric cardiac arrests are asphyxial, not primary cardiac, and the majority are preceded by progressive respiratory failure or shock. Recognize and treat respiratory failure and shock aggressively before arrest occurs — this is where pediatric resuscitation saves lives. In shock, give fluid boluses (10-20 mL/kg isotonic crystalloid) and reassess; if shock persists after 60 mL/kg total or signs of overload appear, start vasoactive support and escalate. In the arrest itself, follow PALS: high-quality CPR (compression depth about one-third the anteroposterior chest diameter, rate 100-120, full recoil, minimal interruptions), epinephrine every 3-5 minutes, and defibrillation for VF/pulseless VT at 2 J/kg then 4 J/kg. Reversible causes (the Hs and Ts) apply, with particular attention to hypoxia, hypovolemia, and hypoglycemia in children. Reassess rhythm and pulses every two minutes with compressor rotation.

### Apply NRP for Newborn Resuscitation as a Distinct Sequence

Neonatal resuscitation follows the Neonatal Resuscitation Program (NRP), which is a different sequence from pediatric or adult resuscitation. Most newborns transition without intervention; the 30 seconds immediately after birth are decisive. Assess tone, breathing, and heart rate; provide warmth, dry, stimulate, and position the airway. If the baby is not breathing or the heart rate is low, initiate positive-pressure ventilation (PPV) within the first minute — ventilation is the most important intervention in neonatal resuscitation. Assess heart rate by auscultation at the apex or by pulse oximetry, and escalate PPV, then chest compressions (3:1 ratio with ventilation) and epinephrine if the heart rate remains below 60 despite effective ventilation. Use blended oxygen, starting at 21% for term infants and titrating up by oximetry, rather than defaulting to 100%. Volume expansion with isotonic fluid or O-negative blood is reserved for suspected blood loss or shock. Delay cord clamping when feasible. Call for neonatal or pediatric specialist help early for any non-vigorous newborn.

### Manage Maternal Arrest with the Physiology of Pregnancy in Mind

Pregnancy remodels physiology: increased oxygen consumption, decreased functional residual capacity, aortocaval compression by the gravid uterus in the supine position, elevated resting heart rate, dilutional anemia, and a hypercoagulable state. In maternal arrest, three adaptations are critical. First, manually displace the uterus to the left (or tilt the patient left-lateral) to relieve aortocaval compression from about 20 weeks gestation — supine positioning alone compromises venous return and must be corrected immediately. Second, prepare for difficult airway: the pregnant airway is edematous and friable, oxygen desaturation is rapid, and aspiration risk is high; have an experienced laryngoscopist and suction ready. Third, consider perimortem cesarean delivery: if there is no ROSC by approximately 4 minutes of resuscitation in a pregnant patient at roughly 20 weeks or beyond, perform a resuscitative hysterotomy to relieve aortocaval compression and improve maternal venous return and to allow neonatal resuscitation — the procedure is intended to save the mother first, and time from arrest is the key variable. Continue CPR throughout and after the procedure. Use standard adult ACLS drugs and doses (do not reduce doses for pregnancy), and identify and treat reversible causes including hemorrhage, embolism, eclampsia, and anesthetic complications.

### Prepare Equipment and Team Before the Event, Not During It

Pediatric, neonatal, and maternal arrests are low-frequency and high-acuity, and improvisation during the event costs lives. Maintain age- and weight-based equipment carts, pre-printed drug dosing references or apps, and length-based tapes in every resuscitation area. Run mock codes and simulations to build team familiarity. Identify, in advance, who will lead, who will manage the airway, who will access (IO if IV is not rapidly obtained), and who will call for specialist help. For maternal events, know the protocol for activating the obstetric and neonatal teams and the location of the cesarean tray. Preparation converts chaos into coordinated action.

### Communicate with and Support the Family

Family-witnessed resuscitation is increasingly standard and is associated with better bereavement outcomes; offer it when feasible with a designated family support person. Communicate honestly and compassionately, avoiding false reassurance and false finality. In pediatric and maternal arrest, the emotional weight on the team is extreme — debrief after every event, address team welfare, and follow institutional support pathways. Document the resuscitation, the reasoning, and the discussions with family, including goals of care for the mother, the child, and (in maternal arrest) the fetus.

### Escalate to Specialists Early

These are events where specialist involvement changes outcomes. Call pediatrics, neonatology, obstetrics, anesthesia, or a maternal-fetal medicine team at the earliest recognition of a critical pediatric, neonatal, or pregnant patient — do not wait for full arrest. For maternal arrest, activate the obstetric and neonatal teams simultaneously. For refractory pediatric arrest, consider ECPR at a capable center. Arrange transfer to a higher level of care early when local pediatric, neonatal, or obstetric capability is limited.

## Common Traps

### Estimating Weight and Doses by Eye

Guessing a child's weight or dose under stress, leading to under- or over-dosing. Counter by using a length-based tape and pre-calculated references, with verification by a second provider.

### Applying Adult Airway Logic to Children

Treating the pediatric airway as a scaled-down adult airway, leading to poor positioning, wrong tube size, or failed intubation. Counter by using pediatric airway anatomy and age-based equipment selection, and calling for experienced help early.

### Missing Respiratory Failure as the Precursor to Pediatric Arrest

Focusing on cardiac causes when most pediatric arrests are asphyxial. Counter by recognizing and aggressively treating respiratory failure and shock before arrest.

### Using 100% Oxygen and Adult ACLS Defaults in Newborns

Defaulting to high oxygen and adult-style sequences in neonatal resuscitation. Counter by following NRP, starting with air for term infants and titrating oxygen by saturation targets.

### Failing to Relieve Aortocaval Compression in Maternal Arrest

Resuscitating a pregnant patient flat on her back. Counter by manually displacing the uterus or tilting the patient left-lateral from the start.

### Delaying Perimortem Cesarean

Waiting too long to perform resuscitative hysterotomy in maternal arrest. Counter by initiating the procedure at approximately 4 minutes if no ROSC, recognizing it benefits the mother first.

### Improvising Equipment During the Event

Searching for the right tube, dose, or tray mid-arrest. Counter by maintaining prepared pediatric, neonatal, and obstetric equipment and running simulations.

## Self-Check

- Have I determined the child's weight using a length-based tape or actual weight, and calculated all drug doses, defibrillation energy, and equipment sizes explicitly with verification?
- Have I adapted airway management to pediatric anatomy (positioning, tube size, alternative airways) and called for experienced help for difficulty?
- In pediatric arrest, am I following PALS with high-quality CPR, and have I recognized and treated respiratory failure and shock as the usual precursors?
- For newborn resuscitation, am I following the NRP sequence (warm, dry, stimulate, positive-pressure ventilation, then compressions and epinephrine) with blended oxygen titrated to saturation targets?
- In maternal arrest, have I relieved aortocaval compression by manual uterine displacement or left tilt, prepared for a difficult airway, and considered perimortem cesarean at approximately 4 minutes if no ROSC?
- Have I prepared equipment, references, and team roles in advance, and offered family-witnessed resuscitation with a support person?
- Have I escalated early to pediatrics, neonatology, obstetrics, anesthesia, or a higher-level center?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
