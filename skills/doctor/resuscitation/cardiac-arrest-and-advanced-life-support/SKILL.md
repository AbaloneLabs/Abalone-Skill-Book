# Cardiac Arrest and Advanced Life Support

Cardiac arrest is the most time-compressed emergency in medicine: outcomes degrade minute by minute, and the quality of the first ten minutes of resuscitation often determines whether the patient survives neurologically intact. The danger in arrest management is rarely a lack of algorithm knowledge — it is the failure to execute under pressure: interruptions in compressions, delayed defibrillation, disorganized teams without a clear leader, and the neglect of reversible causes. A second danger is treating the algorithm as a rigid script rather than a framework that must be adapted to the individual patient and the evolving picture. This skill covers the disciplined execution of BLS and ACLS, the coordination of the resuscitation team, the systematic search for reversible causes, and the optimization of post-arrest care — always with the understanding that arrest is a high-stakes, high-mortality event demanding clear leadership, immediate defibrillation when indicated, and early escalation to specialty care.

## Core Rules

### Prioritize High-Quality CPR and Early Defibrillation Above All Else

The two interventions with the greatest impact on survival are uninterrupted, high-quality chest compressions and early defibrillation of shockable rhythms. Compress at a rate of 100-120 per minute, depth of 5-6 cm (2 inches) in adults, allowing full chest recoil, and minimize interruptions — every pause drops coronary and cerebral perfusion and erodes survival. Rotate compressors every two minutes to prevent fatigue, and use a metronome or feedback device to maintain rate and depth. For shockable rhythms (ventricular fibrillation, pulseless ventricular tachycardia), defibrillate as soon as the device is available; the probability of successful defibrillation falls with every minute of delay. Charge the defibrillator during compressions to minimize the pre-shock pause, and resume compressions immediately after the shock without pausing to check a pulse. Interruptions and shallow compressions are the most common preventable errors in real arrests — guard against them actively.

### Run the Team with Explicit Leadership and Roles

A resuscitation without a designated leader is a resuscitation that will be performed poorly. Appoint a single team leader early whose only job is to direct, not to perform procedures. Assign explicit roles: compressor, airway, defibrillator operator, medications, timer/recorder, and access. Use closed-loop communication: the leader gives an instruction, the receiver repeats it back, the receiver confirms when it is done. Call out time markers every two minutes for rhythm checks and compressor rotation. The leader must maintain situational awareness — tracking the rhythm, the drugs given, the time elapsed, the reversible causes considered, and the overall trajectory — and must be willing to direct even senior team members. A clear, calm leader who enforces quality compressions and minimizes interruptions is the single biggest controllable factor in arrest outcomes.

### Distinguish Shockable from Non-Shockable Rhythms and Manage Each Correctly

The rhythm determines the pathway. Shockable rhythms (VF, pulseless VT) are treated with immediate defibrillation plus CPR, epinephrine after the second shock, and an antiarrhythmic (amiodarone or lidocaine) after the third shock; defibrillation is the definitive therapy and must not be delayed. Non-shockable rhythms (asystole, PEA) are treated with CPR and epinephrine, with defibrillation playing no role. For PEA, the heart is electrically organized but mechanically failing — the key is not the rhythm but the reversible cause. Reassess the rhythm briefly every two minutes during compressor rotation, but do not stop compressions for prolonged rhythm checks. When the rhythm changes, adapt the pathway immediately. Refractory VF may benefit from double sequential defibrillation or early ECMO (ECPR) in centers with capability — escalate rather than cycling indefinitely.

### Systematically Identify and Treat Reversible Causes (the Hs and Ts)

In every arrest, especially PEA and refractory rhythms, actively search for and treat the reversible causes: the Hs — Hypovolemia (give fluids, check for hemorrhage), Hypoxia (optimize oxygenation and ventilation, check tube placement), Hydrogen ion (severe acidosis), Hypo-/Hyperkalemia (check, treat empirically if suspected), Hypothermia (warm), and the Ts — Tension pneumothorax (decompress), Tamponade (cardiac, pericardiocentesis), Thrombosis (pulmonary embolism or coronary — consider thrombolytics or percutaneous intervention), Toxins (overdose, tailor antidotes), Trauma (identify and control bleeding). Use bedside ultrasound early to identify tamponade, pneumothorax, pulmonary embolism, and profound hypovolemia — but do not let ultrasound prolong pauses in compressions. The most common missed reversible cause is a treatable one that was never considered; name the Hs and Ts explicitly during the arrest.

### Optimize Post-ROSC Care to Protect the Brain and the Heart

Return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) is not the end of the resuscitation — it is the start of post-arrest care, which determines neurologic outcome. Optimize oxygenation (target normoxia, avoid both hypoxia and hyperoxia), ventilation (target normocapnia, as both hypocapnia and hypercapnia harm the injured brain), hemodynamics (target MAP 65 mmHg or higher, treat shock and arrhythmias), and perform early coronary angiography for suspected cardiac cause with ST-elevation. Apply targeted temperature management (TTM) for comatose post-arrest patients, typically 32-36°C for 24 hours, avoiding fever. Control seizures, maintain glucose in a normal range, and avoid hypotension. The post-arrest patient is critically ill and requires ICU-level care; deterioration after ROSC is common and must be anticipated. Reassess the cause of the arrest and address it definitively to prevent rearrest.

### Know When to Terminate and How to Communicate It

Termination is a clinical decision based on the totality of circumstances: the presenting rhythm, the downtime before CPR, the quality and duration of resuscitation, the reversibility of the cause, the patient's baseline and expressed wishes, and local protocols. Asystole throughout, unwitnessed arrest with prolonged downtime, non-reversible cause, and failure to respond to a full ACLS effort with reversible causes addressed all support termination. Prolonged resuscitation may be appropriate in special circumstances (hypothermia, toxin, PE, young patients with reversible causes). Involve the team in the decision, document the reasoning, and communicate with the family with clarity and compassion. Do not terminate prematurely in potentially reversible cases, and do not prolong futile resuscitation — both are errors. When appropriate, discuss organ donation per local policy.

### Escalate and Use Advanced Modalities When Indicated

For refractory arrest with a reversible cause, escalate to advanced modalities early rather than late: extracorporeal membrane oxygenation for ECPR in selected patients (witnessed, shockable, reversible, within time window), percutaneous coronary intervention for STEMI-related arrest, thrombolysis for proven or highly suspected pulmonary embolism, and double-sequential defibrillation for refractory VF. These require system-level readiness and early activation. Do not wait until the patient has arrested for 30 minutes to consider them — by then the window has often closed. Communicate clearly with receiving teams and activate pathways early.

## Common Traps

### Interrupting Compressions for Procedures or Rhythm Checks

Prolonged pauses for intubation, line placement, or rhythm checks destroy perfusion. Counter by performing procedures during continuous compressions where possible, charging the defibrillator during compressions, and limiting rhythm checks to brief pauses every two minutes.

### Delaying Defibrillation in Shockable Rhythms

Waiting for IV access, medications, or a "perfect" setup before shocking VF or pulseless VT. Counter by defibrillating as soon as the rhythm is recognized and the device is ready.

### Lack of a Clear Team Leader

Multiple people giving orders, or the leader also performing procedures. Counter by appointing a single leader who directs but does not perform, and by using closed-loop communication.

### Forgetting the Reversible Causes

Cycling the algorithm without actively considering the Hs and Ts. Counter by explicitly naming and addressing each reversible cause during the arrest, especially in PEA and refractory rhythms.

### Hyperoxia and Hypocapnia After ROSC

Leaving the patient on 100% oxygen or hyperventilating after ROSC, which worsens neurologic injury. Counter by titrating to normoxia and normocapnia immediately after ROSC.

### Failing to Optimize Post-Arrest Care

Treating ROSC as the finish line and neglecting TTM, hemodynamics, coronary angiography, and cause-directed therapy. Counter by treating the post-arrest patient as critically ill and applying post-arrest bundles.

### Premature or Prolonged Resuscitation

Terminating without addressing reversible causes, or continuing futile resuscitation past reasonable limits. Counter by integrating reversibility, downtime, and patient context into the termination decision, and by following local protocols.

## Self-Check

- Am I delivering high-quality CPR (rate 100-120, depth 5-6 cm, full recoil, minimal interruptions) and defibrillating shockable rhythms immediately?
- Have I appointed a single team leader who directs without performing procedures, and assigned explicit roles with closed-loop communication?
- Am I managing the rhythm correctly — shockable pathway (defibrillation, epinephrine, antiarrhythmic) versus non-shockable pathway (CPR, epinephrine, cause-focused)?
- Have I systematically considered and treated the reversible causes (Hs and Ts) using bedside ultrasound without prolonging pauses?
- After ROSC, am I optimizing oxygenation, ventilation, hemodynamics, targeted temperature management, and cause-directed therapy such as coronary angiography?
- Have I made a reasoned termination decision based on reversibility, downtime, response, and patient context, and communicated it clearly?
- Have I escalated to advanced modalities (ECPR, PCI, thrombolysis, double-sequential defibrillation) early in appropriate refractory cases?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
