# Mass Casualty and Disaster Triage

Mass-casualty and disaster triage inverts the foundational ethic of individual medicine. In routine care, the clinician maximizes the outcome of each patient; in a mass-casualty incident (MCI), demand exceeds resources, and the clinician must maximize the number of survivors across the population — the greatest good for the greatest number. The danger is not unfamiliarity with triage categories; it is the failure to actually shift mental gears, clinging to one-patient-at-a-time logic while patients die waiting, or conversely, abandoning triage discipline under emotional pressure and over-investing in a single unsalvageable patient. A second danger is treating triage as a static label rather than a dynamic reassessment as resources and physiology change. This skill covers the disciplined application of mass-casualty triage, resource allocation under scarcity, and the coordination of emergency response — always with the understanding that disaster care carries profound ethical weight, that protocols must be applied with compassion, and that decisions should be made within an established incident command framework with senior leadership and ethics support.

## Core Rules

### Shift to Population-Based Triage Logic the Moment Demand Exceeds Resources

The defining feature of an MCI is that resources (staff, supplies, space, transport) cannot meet the needs of all casualties using standard care. The instant this is recognized, the operating ethic shifts from individual optimization to population optimization — greatest good for the greatest number. This is not abandonment; it is a different and disciplined mode of care. Failing to make this shift is the cardinal error: continuing to pour resources into the first critically injured patient while dozens of salvageable patients go untreated. Recognize the threshold early (number of casualties, severity, available staff and beds, transport times) and declare an MCI through incident command. Communicate the shift explicitly to the team so that everyone operates under the same framework. Reassess the resource-to-demand ratio continuously; if resources arrive (reinforcements, supplies, transport), the triage posture can relax toward standard care, and vice versa.

### Apply a Validated Triage Algorithm Rapidly and Consistently

Use a validated triage system rather than ad hoc judgment. START (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) for adults and JumpSTART for children categorize patients using ambulation, respiration, perfusion, and mental status: Immediate (red) — life-threatening but salvageable with rapid intervention; Delayed (yellow) — serious but can wait; Minor (green) — walking wounded; Expectant/Deceased (black) — unsalvageable or dead given current resources. SALT (Sort, Assess, Lifesaving interventions, Treatment/Transport) is a more modern mass-gathering and MCI framework that adds simple lifesaving interventions (e.g., controlling catastrophic hemorrhage, opening the airway, needle decompression) at the point of triage. Apply the algorithm in under a minute per patient, label visibly, and move on. The speed and consistency of the algorithm matter more than perfect accuracy — the goal is to sort a chaotic scene into actionable categories quickly. Document categories and reassess, because a patient's status changes and a mis-triaged patient must be caught on re-sort.

### Re-Triage Continuously as Physiology and Resources Change

Triage categories are not final diagnoses; they are snapshots. A "Delayed" patient who deteriorates becomes "Immediate"; an "Immediate" patient who stabilizes becomes "Delayed"; an "Expectant" patient whose resources become available may become treatable. Re-triage at defined intervals and whenever resources shift (more ambulances arrive, a surgeon becomes available, a ward opens). Build re-triage into the incident action plan with assigned roles. Static triage is a recurring failure in prolonged incidents — patients labeled early and never reassessed deteriorate and die unnoticed. The converse error is also real: re-triaging so frequently that triage teams never stabilize the scene. Balance speed with periodic structured reassessment.

### Allocate Resources to Maximize Survivors, Not to Treat the Most Dramatic Injury

Resource allocation in an MCI follows the principle of maximizing total survival, which sometimes means withholding or withdrawing treatment from patients with very low probability of survival given current resources (the "expectant" category). This is ethically agonizing but is the core of disaster ethics. Treat expectant patients with palliation (analgesia, comfort) and revisit them if resources become available. Do not, under emotional pressure, convert an expectant patient into a massive resource sink that costs several salvageable patients their lives — this is the failure mode disaster triage is designed to prevent. Conversely, do not label salvageable patients expectant prematurely; use objective triage criteria and, where possible, a second clinician's input for expectant designation. Document the criteria used and the resource context for every expectant decision, because these decisions are reviewed ethically and legally.

### Activate and Work Within Incident Command

Disaster response is not a collection of individual clinicians improvising; it is a structured system. Activate the Hospital Incident Command System (HICS) or the field Incident Command System (ICS) early, with a designated incident commander, operations, logistics, planning, and finance sections. The triage officer reports into operations; clinicians do not freelance outside the structure. Clear roles prevent duplication, gaps, and conflicting orders. Communicate status, resource needs, and casualty flow up the chain and receive direction down the chain. If you are the first clinician on scene, you may need to assume initial command and then hand off as the structure forms — state explicitly when you transfer command. Failure to use incident command produces fragmented, uncoordinated response and is a leading contributor to poor disaster outcomes.

### Plan for Surge Capacity, Decontamination, and Special Populations

Disaster response depends on surge capacity — the ability to expand staff, supplies, space, and systems beyond normal operations. Know your facility's surge plan: how wards convert, how staff are recalled, how supplies are stockpiled and released, and how non-critical services are curtailed. For chemical, radiological, or biological incidents, decontamination precedes entry to the facility — contaminated casualties must not contaminate the hospital and shut it down. Plan for special populations: children (weight-based everything, separate dosing), pregnant patients, the elderly, the disabled, and those with limited language or mobility, all of whom are easily under-served in a chaotic MCI. Coordinate with public health, EMS, and regional partners for mutual aid and patient distribution across facilities to avoid overwhelming a single site.

### Maintain Ethical Discipline, Documentation, and Compassion

Disaster triage decisions are ethically weighty and are subject to later review. Make decisions using objective criteria within an established framework, document the criteria and the resource context, and involve a second clinician or ethics liaison for difficult expectant decisions where feasible. Treat every patient, including the expectant and the deceased, with dignity — palliate suffering, communicate honestly with patients and families where possible, and respect cultural and religious practices around death. Support the team: MCI participation is a known cause of moral injury and post-traumatic stress, and debriefing and psychological support should be built into the response. Do not abandon ethical standards under pressure; rather, apply a different ethical framework (population-based) with the same rigor.

### Escalate and Coordinate Across the System

An MCI is bigger than any single clinician or facility. Escalate early through incident command to regional and state systems for mutual aid, transport, and resource allocation. Coordinate patient distribution so that no single facility is overwhelmed while others have capacity. Communicate with public health for infectious or chemical incidents, with law enforcement for security and scene safety, and with EMS for field triage and transport. If your facility exceeds its capability, declare an internal disaster and request external support and diversion of patients. Do not wait until collapse to ask for help — early escalation is the mechanism by which systems survive surges.

## Common Traps

### Failing to Shift from Individual to Population Logic

Continuing one-patient-at-a-time care in an MCI, over-investing in the first critical patient. Counter by declaring an MCI early and explicitly shifting to population-based triage with the team.

### Triage by Drama Rather Than by Algorithm

Allocating resources to the most visually dramatic injury rather than by objective triage criteria. Counter by applying START/SALT consistently and rapidly, and labeling before treating.

### Static Triage Without Reassessment

Labeling patients once and never re-triaging as they deteriorate or as resources change. Counter by building periodic re-triage into the incident action plan with assigned roles.

### Over-Investing in Expectant Patients

Pouring scarce resources into unsalvageable patients at the cost of salvageable ones. Counter by applying objective expectant criteria, providing palliation, and revisiting if resources arrive.

### Freelancing Outside Incident Command

Individual clinicians improvising without coordination, producing duplication and gaps. Counter by activating HICS/ICS early and working within the structure with clear roles.

### Hospital Contamination from Untreated Casualties

Allowing chemically or radiologically contaminated patients to enter and shut down the facility. Counter by enforcing decontamination before entry and coordinating with hazmat and public health.

### Under-Serving Special Populations

Overlooking children, pregnant patients, the elderly, and the disabled in a chaotic scene. Counter by building special-population considerations into the surge plan and triage process.

## Self-Check

- Have I recognized that demand exceeds resources and explicitly shifted to population-based (greatest-good) triage logic, declaring an MCI through incident command?
- Am I applying a validated triage algorithm (START/JumpSTART or SALT) rapidly and consistently, with visible category labels?
- Am I re-triaging at defined intervals and whenever physiology or resources change, rather than treating categories as final?
- Am I allocating resources to maximize total survivors, using objective criteria for expectant designation and providing palliation, with documentation of the resource context?
- Have I activated and am I working within the incident command structure (HICS/ICS), with a clear incident commander and defined roles?
- Have I planned for surge capacity, decontamination, special populations, and regional coordination across facilities and EMS?
- Am I maintaining ethical discipline, documentation, and compassion, including team debriefing and psychological support?
- Have I escalated early for mutual aid, transport, and external support rather than waiting until collapse?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
