# Electrolyte and Acid-Base Disorder Analysis

Electrolyte and acid-base disturbances are among the few areas in medicine where the rate of correction matters as much as the direction, and where well-intentioned treatment causes harm as often as the underlying disorder. Correcting sodium too rapidly causes central pontine myelinolysis; correcting potassium too rapidly causes lethal arrhythmia; missing a potassium of 7.0 with ECG changes causes cardiac arrest. The recurring failure is treating these as arithmetic problems to be solved fast, when they are physiology problems to be solved carefully. The disciplined skill is to work each disturbance systematically, to distinguish competing causes (SIADH versus cerebral salt-wasting versus diuretics), to calculate corrected values before interpreting, to respect correction-rate limits that exist to prevent iatrogenic injury, and to know exactly when to escalate. This skill covers sodium, potassium, calcium, and magnesium disturbances, systematic blood gas interpretation, the anion gap and delta-delta, and the correction-rate discipline that separates safe practice from dangerous practice.

## Core Rules

### Work Every Disturbance Systematically, Never by Pattern Matching

Electrolyte and acid-base disorders reward a systematic, stepwise approach and punish shortcuts. For sodium, begin with volume status and osmolality to classify hypotonic, isotonic, or hypertonic hyponatremia, then use urine sodium and osmolality to separate renal from extrarenal losses and to distinguish SIADH from cerebral salt-wasting and from adrenal insufficiency. For acid-base, follow a fixed sequence: pH to determine acidemia or alkalemia, primary respiratory versus metabolic disturbance via PaCO2 and bicarbonate, anion gap to detect unmeasured anions, delta-delta to detect a mixed disorder, and compensation to check whether the response is appropriate. Skipping steps produces a single-diagnosis answer to what is often a mixed disorder. Write the steps out; the discipline of the sequence is what catches the second disorder hiding behind the first.

### Apply Correction Formulas Before Interpreting

Several key values must be corrected before they can be interpreted, and failing to correct leads to misdiagnosis. Correct calcium for albumin: add 0.02 mmol/L (or 0.8 mg/dL) to the measured calcium for every 1 g/L that albumin is below 40 g/L. Correct sodium for hyperglycemia: sodium falls by approximately 2 mmol/L for every 5.5 mmol/L (100 mg/dL) of glucose above normal. Calculate the anion gap (sodium minus chloride minus bicarbonate) and the albumin-corrected anion gap, because a falling albumin narrows the normal gap and can mask a high-gap acidosis. Calculate the delta ratio (delta gap divided by delta anion gap) to detect mixed metabolic acidosis and alkalosis. These corrections are not optional refinements; they change the diagnosis and the management. State the corrected value explicitly in your interpretation.

### Respect Correction-Rate Limits to Prevent Iatrogenic Harm

The rate at which you correct an electrolyte disturbance can be more dangerous than the disturbance itself, and these limits exist because correction beyond them causes irreversible harm. For chronic hyponatremia, correct by no more than 8 to 10 mmol/L in the first 24 hours, because faster correction risks osmotic demyelination (central pontine myelinolysis), a devastating and often permanent neurologic injury. For hypernatremia, lower sodium by no more than 10 mmol/L per 24 hours. For severe hyperkalemia, the danger is the opposite: correction must be immediate (calcium for membrane stabilization, insulin and glucose to shift potassium intracellularly) because the risk is cardiac arrest, but ongoing potassium removal (resins, dialysis) must follow the initial stabilization. Know the rate limits for each disturbance, monitor the correcting value frequently during active correction, and stop or slow correction as the limit is approached. Over-correction is a never-event in this domain.

### Recognize the Potassium and Calcium Emergencies and Act Immediately

Some electrolyte disturbances are immediate threats to life and demand action before the full workup is complete. Severe hyperkalemia with ECG changes (peaked T waves, widened QRS) requires intravenous calcium gluconate within minutes to stabilize the myocardium, followed by insulin-glucose and other measures to shift and remove potassium; waiting for a repeat result is waiting too long. Severe hypokalemia below 2.5 mmol/L, especially with arrhythmia or digoxin therapy, requires careful intravenous replacement. Symptomatic severe hypocalcemia (tetany, seizures, prolonged QT) requires intravenous calcium. Hypercalcemia above 3.5 mmol/L with altered consciousness or dehydration requires aggressive saline and bisphosphonate escalation. In each of these, the immediate action precedes and does not wait for the complete diagnostic workup. Identify the emergency, stabilize, then investigate the cause.

### Distinguish Competing Causes Before Committing to Treatment

The treatment of an electrolyte disorder depends entirely on its cause, and several causes produce superficially similar numbers. Hyponatremia with concentrated urine could be SIADH, cerebral salt-wasting, hypothyroidism, or glucocorticoid deficiency; treating SIADH with fluid restriction when the patient has cerebral salt-wasting (volume-depleted) worsens them. Hyperkalemia could be renal failure, adrenal insufficiency, potassium-sparing diuretics, or a hemolyzed sample (pseudohyperkalemia). A high anion gap acidosis requires the MUDPILES or similar differential, and the treatment of lactic acidosis from sepsis differs entirely from the treatment of toxic alcohol ingestion. Before committing to treatment, gather the discriminating data: volume status, urine electrolytes and osmolality, concurrent medications, renal function, cortisol, TSH, lactate, toxicology. When the cause is ambiguous and the disturbance is severe, treat the immediate threat while pursuing the diagnosis in parallel, and seek specialist input early.

### Interpret Blood Gases in Full Context, Including the Clinical Story

A blood gas is not interpreted in isolation. The pH, PaCO2, bicarbonate, base excess, lactate, and electrolytes must be read against the clinical picture: a respiratory acidosis in a patient with opiate overdose is expected and treated with ventilation, while the same numbers in a COPD patient on home oxygen may indicate chronic compensated respiratory failure with an acute exacerbation. Distinguish arterial from venous samples (venous PaCO2 runs higher and pH slightly lower), recognize whether the sample was drawn on oxygen and at what concentration, and check the internal consistency of the values. A lactate elevated out of proportion to the clinical picture prompts a search for tissue hypoperfusion, bowel ischemia, or toxin. Always correlate the gas with the patient, the oxygen delivery, and the trend.

## Common Traps

### Correcting Sodium Too Fast and Causing Osmotic Demyelination

Exceeding the 8 to 10 mmol/L per 24 hours correction limit in chronic hyponatremia. Counter by setting the correction target explicitly, monitoring sodium frequently during active correction, and stopping hypertonic saline or desmopressin as the limit approaches.

### Pattern-Matching a Single Diagnosis to a Mixed Disorder

Stopping the systematic workup at the first identified disturbance and missing the coexisting one. Counter by completing every step, including the anion gap and delta-delta, to detect mixed acid-base disorders.

### Failing to Correct Before Interpreting

Reading an uncorrected calcium, sodium, or anion gap and misdiagnosing. Counter by applying the albumin, glucose, and albumin-anion-gap corrections before concluding.

### Treating the Number Without the Cause

Committing to fluid restriction for hyponatremia before distinguishing SIADH from cerebral salt-wasting, or treating hyperkalemia without excluding pseudohyperkalemia from hemolysis. Counter by gathering discriminating data and treating the cause, not just the value.

### Delaying Action on a Potassium or Calcium Emergency

Waiting for a repeat result in severe hyperkalemia with ECG changes. Counter by giving calcium gluconate immediately for membrane stabilization, then pursuing confirmation and removal.

### Ignoring the Correction Rate in the Opposite Direction

Under-correcting severe hyperkalemia because of fear of over-correction, or over-correcting chronic disturbances because of impatience. Counter by matching the urgency to the specific disturbance: immediate for hyperkalemia with ECG changes, deliberately slow for chronic sodium disorders.

## Self-Check

- Did I work the disturbance systematically through every step (volume status, urine indices, anion gap, delta-delta, compensation) rather than stopping at the first diagnosis?
- Did I apply the necessary corrections (calcium for albumin, sodium for glucose, anion gap for albumin) before interpreting the values?
- Did I set and respect correction-rate limits, especially the 8 to 10 mmol/L per 24 hours limit for chronic hyponatremia, with frequent monitoring during active correction?
- Did I recognize and act immediately on the potassium and calcium emergencies (calcium for hyperkalemia with ECG changes, intravenous calcium for symptomatic hypocalcemia) before completing the workup?
- Did I distinguish competing causes (SIADH versus cerebral salt-wasting, true versus pseudohyperkalemia) with discriminating data before committing to cause-specific treatment?
- Did I interpret the blood gas in full context, including sample type, oxygen delivery, internal consistency, lactate, and the clinical story?
- Did I escalate to nephrology, endocrinology, or intensive care early when the disturbance was severe, the cause was ambiguous, or correction was not proceeding as planned?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
