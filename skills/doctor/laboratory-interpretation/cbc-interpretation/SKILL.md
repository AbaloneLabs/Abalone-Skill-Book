# CBC Interpretation

The complete blood count is the most frequently ordered test in medicine, and its very familiarity is the hazard. Clinicians glance at the flagged values, label a low hemoglobin "anemia," chase a mildly low platelet count with unnecessary panels, or reassure a patient with a normal hemoglobin that is actually a 30 g/L drop from their baseline. The recurring failure is reading each line of the CBC in isolation rather than building a mechanistic story from the counts, the indices, the differential, and the patient together. Equally dangerous is the opposite reflex: treating the numbers as the patient. A hemoglobin of 70 g/L means nothing without the context of symptoms, speed of fall, and comorbidity; a platelet count of 25 means different things in a stable outpatient and a febrile inpatient with active bleeding. This skill covers the disciplined interpretation of the CBC: classifying anemia by mean corpuscular volume and mechanism, reading the white cell differential for pattern rather than total, working up thrombocytopenia and spurious counts, using the reticulocyte count and peripheral smear as decisive tools, and respecting the trend over any single value. The discipline is to interpret mechanistically, to question every surprising value for artifact, and to treat the patient rather than the number.

## Core Rules

### Classify Anemia by MCV, Then Pursue the Mechanism

A low hemoglobin is a finding, not a diagnosis. Begin classification with the mean corpuscular volume (MCV). Microcytic anemia (low MCV) points to iron deficiency, thalassemia trait, anemia of chronic inflammation, sideroblastic anemia, or lead poisoning; the discriminating tests are ferritin, transferrin saturation, iron studies, and — when inflammation confounds the ferritin — soluble transferrin receptor or a trial of iron. Normocytic anemia points to acute blood loss, anemia of chronic disease, early iron deficiency, bone marrow disorder, renal failure, or hemolysis; the reticulocyte count is the decisive branch point here, separating an underproductive marrow (low reticulocyte) from a compensating or hemolyzing marrow (high reticulocyte). Macrocytic anemia (high MCV) points to B12 or folate deficiency, alcohol, hypothyroidism, liver disease, myelodysplasia, or drug effect; check B12, folate, and review the smear for macro-ovalocytes and hypersegmented neutrophils. Never prescribe iron empirically without iron studies, because treating thalassemia trait or anemia of chronic inflammation with iron causes iron overload without correcting the anemia.

### Read the White Cell Differential for Pattern, Not Just the Total

The total white cell count is a poor summary of a rich differential. Leukocytosis demands decomposition: a neutrophilic predominance with a left shift and toxic granulation suggests bacterial infection or inflammation, while lymphocytosis with atypical cells suggests viral infection or, if persistent and clonal, a lymphoproliferative disorder. Eosinophilia prompts a search for allergy, parasitic infection, drug reaction, or hypereosinophilic syndrome. Basophilia, monocytosis, or the presence of blasts on the differential are red flags for marrow pathology and warrant urgent smear and hematology review. Leukopenia, especially neutropenia (absolute neutrophil count below 1.5, severe below 0.5), narrows toward viral suppression, severe sepsis, drug effect, autoimmune destruction, or marrow failure. A markedly elevated or depressed white count with abnormal forms on the smear should never be dismissed as reactive without a clear cause; persistent unexplained leukocytosis or leukopenia needs a defined workup and hematology referral.

### Work Up Thrombocytopenia Systematically and Recognize the Emergencies

Thrombocytopenia has a broad differential, and the workup must distinguish decreased production, increased destruction, sequestration, and dilution. Decreased production is suggested by a low reticulocyte, other cytopenias, or marrow pathology; increased destruction by an isolated thrombocytopenia with a normal or high reticulocyte and large platelets on the smear. Key diagnoses include immune thrombocytopenia (isolated thrombocytopenia, otherwise well), thrombotic microangiopathy (thrombocytopenia with schistocytes, elevated LDH, and a falling hemoglobin — a medical emergency needing urgent plasma exchange), heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (platelet fall after heparin exposure, paradoxical thrombosis), sequestration from hypersplenism, and pregnancy-related causes. Recognize the emergencies: a platelet count below 20 with active bleeding, or below 10 in any patient, risks spontaneous intracranial hemorrhage and needs urgent assessment and often transfusion. Always examine the smear for clumps — EDTA-dependent platelet clumping causes spurious thrombocytopenia, and treating it as real exposes the patient to unnecessary transfusion and procedures.

### Question Every Surprising Value for Artifact and Spurious Counts

The automated hematology analyzer is reliable in most cases but fails in specific, recognizable ways, and acting on a spurious count causes real harm. EDTA-dependent clumping produces a falsely low platelet count; redraw in a citrate or alternative anticoagulant tube to confirm. Cold agglutinins cause red cells to clump, producing a falsely high MCV, a falsely low hemoglobin and hematocrit, and a spurious elevation of the mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration; warming the sample resolves it. Hemolysis in vitro falsely lowers hemoglobin and hematocrit and elevates potassium. Marked leukocytosis can falsely elevate hemoglobin by increasing plasma turbidity. A dramatically discordant value, or a sudden change implausible for the time elapsed, should prompt a repeat on a fresh sample and a smear before any intervention. Treat the analyzer's flags (e.g., nucleated red cells, blast flag, platelet clumps) as prompts for smear review, not as diagnoses.

### Use the Reticulocyte Count and Peripheral Smear as Decisive Tools

Two investigations transform CBC interpretation from guesswork into mechanism, and both are underused. The reticulocyte count answers the single most important question in anemia: is the marrow responding appropriately? A low reticulocyte in the face of anemia means underproduction (iron, B12, folate, marrow failure, chronic disease, renal failure); a high reticulocyte means the marrow is working and the anemia is from loss or destruction (bleeding, hemolysis). The peripheral smear visualizes mechanism directly: schistocytes signal microangiopathic hemolysis and demand urgent action, spherocytes signal autoimmune hemolysis, target cells and basophilic stippling suggest hemoglobinopathy or thalassemia, sickled cells confirm sickle disease, and blasts or dysplastic forms point to marrow pathology. Request and review the smear whenever the cytopenia, cytosis, or morphology is unexplained, whenever hemolysis is suspected, and whenever the analyzer flags abnormal cells. The smear is where the diagnosis becomes visible.

### Interpret the Trend Over Any Single Value, and the Patient Over the Number

A single CBC is a snapshot; the trend is the story. Always compare the current result to the patient's prior values using delta checks. A hemoglobin of 110 g/L that has been stable for years means something very different from 110 g/L that fell from 150 g/L over a week — the latter demands an explanation for the drop. Conversely, a flagged value that has been stable for years is far less alarming than the same value appearing for the first time. More importantly, interpret the CBC against the clinical context and the patient's symptoms, not against the reference range alone. A hemoglobin of 70 g/L in an asymptomatic young patient may be tolerated; the same value in an older patient with cardiac disease and exertional angina may need transfusion. Treat the patient, not the number: the decision to transfuse, to investigate aggressively, or to observe is driven by symptoms, comorbidity, rate of change, and risk — not by the absolute count alone.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Low Hemoglobin as "Anemia" Without Mechanism

Prescribing iron for any anemia without iron studies, missing hemolysis or marrow failure. Counter by classifying with the MCV and pursuing the mechanism with ferritin, reticulocyte count, B12, folate, and a hemolysis screen.

### Dismissing a Normal Hemoglobin That Is a Major Delta

Reassuring a patient whose hemoglobin sits in the reference range but has dropped 30 g/L from their baseline. Counter by always comparing to prior values and explaining any significant delta.

### Chasing Spurious Thrombocytopenia From Platelet Clumping

Investigating or transfusing for a low platelet count caused by EDTA-dependent clumping. Counter by reviewing the smear for clumps and redrawing in a citrate tube to confirm.

### Ignoring the Reticulocyte Count in Anemia Workup

Ordering a battery of anemia tests without the single value that separates underproduction from loss or destruction. Counter by making the reticulocyte count the first branch point in any unexplained anemia.

### Reading the White Cell Total Without the Differential

Concluding "infection" from a high white count without examining the differential for left shift, toxic changes, eosinophilia, or blasts. Counter by decomposing the differential and reviewing the smear for abnormal forms.

### Missing Thrombotic Microangiopathy Behind "Low Platelets"

Treating isolated thrombocytopenia as immune thrombocytopenia without checking for schistocytes, LDH, and hemolysis. Counter by examining the smear and checking the hemolysis screen whenever thrombocytopenia is unexplained.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify the anemia by MCV (microcytic, normocytic, macrocytic) and pursue the mechanism with targeted tests rather than labeling "anemia"?
- Did I use the reticulocyte count as the branch point to separate marrow underproduction from loss or hemolysis?
- Did I decompose the white cell differential for pattern (left shift, toxic changes, eosinophilia, blasts, lymphocytosis) rather than reading only the total?
- Did I work up thrombocytopenia systematically and recognize emergencies such as thrombotic microangiopathy, severe thrombocytopenia below 10 to 20, and heparin-induced thrombocytopenia?
- Did I question every surprising value for artifact — EDTA clumping, cold agglutinins, hemolysis, analyzer flags — and repeat on a fresh sample before acting?
- Did I request and review the peripheral smear whenever the cytopenia, cytosis, or morphology was unexplained, and act on schistocytes or blasts as emergencies?
- Did I compare the current CBC to prior values using delta checks, and interpret the result against the patient's symptoms and comorbidity rather than the reference range alone?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
