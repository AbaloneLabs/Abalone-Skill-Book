# Metabolic Panel Interpretation

The basic and comprehensive metabolic panel is ordered on nearly every patient, and its very ubiquity breeds shallow reading. Clinicians scan the flagged values, treat a mildly elevated creatinine as "kidney injury," chase a borderline glucose with a diabetes workup, or correct a sodium number without first asking whether the value is even real. The recurring failure is twofold: reading each analyte in isolation rather than as an interconnected metabolic picture, and treating the number as the patient. A potassium of 6.0 mmol/L demands a different response depending on whether the sample was hemolyzed, whether there are ECG changes, and whether the patient has renal failure; a creatinine of 150 micromol/L means different things in a young athlete with high muscle mass and an elderly woman with low muscle mass. This skill covers the disciplined interpretation of the metabolic panel: reading the electrolytes, renal markers, glucose, and calcium together, calculating the anion gap, distinguishing acute from chronic kidney disease, recognizing the emergencies that hide among routine values, and guarding against the pre-analytical errors that distort every analyte. The discipline is to correlate the panel with the clinical context, to correct values before interpreting, and to never act on a discordant result without first questioning whether it is real.

## Core Rules

### Read the Panel as an Interconnected Picture, Not Isolated Analytes

The metabolic panel is a single metabolic story told across several lines, and reading each value independently misses the connections. Sodium and glucose must be read together, because marked hyperglycemia lowers measured sodium and the value must be corrected before interpretation. Chloride and bicarbonate must be read together with sodium to calculate the anion gap, which reveals a high-gap metabolic acidosis that the individual values conceal. BUN and creatinine must be read together as a ratio, because a high BUN-to-creatinine ratio suggests a pre-renal or gastrointestinal bleeding component while a low ratio suggests liver disease or malnutrition. Calcium must be corrected for albumin before interpretation, because hypoalbuminemia makes a normal total calcium falsely reassuring while the ionized calcium is low. Always calculate the corrected values and the anion gap explicitly, because these derived measures frequently change the diagnosis that the raw numbers suggest.

### Correct Values Before Interpreting, and Calculate the Anion Gap

Several analytes on the metabolic panel must be corrected before they can be interpreted, and failing to correct leads to misdiagnosis. Correct calcium for albumin: add approximately 0.02 mmol/L (0.8 mg/dL) to the measured calcium for every 1 g/L that albumin is below 40 g/L, because roughly half of circulating calcium is albumin-bound and a low albumin underestimates the physiologically active calcium. Correct sodium for hyperglycemia: sodium falls by roughly 2 mmol/L for every 5.5 mmol/L (100 mg/dL) of glucose above normal, so a sodium of 130 in a patient with glucose of 30 mmol/L is not true hyponatremia. Calculate the anion gap (sodium minus chloride minus bicarbonate) and correct it for albumin, because a falling albumin narrows the normal gap and can mask a high-gap acidosis. Use the delta-delta to detect mixed metabolic disorders hiding behind a single apparent disturbance. State the corrected value explicitly in your interpretation; an uncorrected calcium or sodium routinely leads to the wrong diagnosis and the wrong treatment.

### Distinguish Acute Kidney Injury From Chronic Kidney Disease

An elevated creatinine is not a diagnosis; it is a finding that demands classification of acute, chronic, or acute-on-chronic. Compare the current creatinine to the patient's baseline — the single most important step. A creatinine that has risen over hours to days is acute kidney injury, and the workup separates pre-renal (volume depletion, heart failure, sepsis), intrinsic (acute tubular necrosis, glomerulonephritis, interstitial nephritis, nephrotoxins), and post-renal (obstruction) causes using history, volume status, urine indices, urinalysis, and imaging. A chronically elevated creatinine with a small kidney on imaging, anemia of chronic disease, and a long-standing abnormality indicates chronic kidney disease, which is managed by staging and slowing progression rather than reversing it. Acute-on-chronic kidney injury requires identifying the acute insult superimposed on chronic disease. Do not label a single elevated creatinine "renal failure" and stop; classify the temporal pattern and pursue the mechanism, because acute kidney injury is often reversible if the cause is addressed promptly.

### Recognize the Emergencies Hidden in Routine Values

Several metabolic panel abnormalities are immediate threats to life and demand action before the full workup is complete. Severe hyperkalemia with ECG changes (peaked T waves, widened QRS) requires intravenous calcium for membrane stabilization within minutes, followed by insulin-glucose to shift potassium intracellularly; waiting for a repeat result in this setting is waiting too long. Severe hypokalemia below 2.5 mmol/L, especially with arrhythmia or digoxin therapy, requires careful intravenous replacement. Symptomatic severe hypoglycemia with altered consciousness requires immediate dextrose. Hypercalcemia above 3.5 mmol/L with altered consciousness or dehydration requires aggressive saline and escalation to bisphosphonate therapy. Severe hyponatremia with seizures requires hypertonic saline, but always within correction-rate limits. Identify the emergency, stabilize the immediate threat, then pursue the diagnostic workup in parallel. The metabolic panel is where life-threatening electrolyte and metabolic emergencies first reveal themselves.

### Guard Against Pre-Analytical Error: Pseudohyponatremia and Hemolyzed Samples

A surprising proportion of abnormal metabolic panel values are artifacts of sample handling, not of patient physiology, and acting on an artifact causes real harm. Hemolysis is the most common offender: it falsely elevates potassium, lactate dehydrogenase, and aspartate aminotransferase, and can mask or distort other analytes; a hemolyzed sample with a potassium of 6.5 mmol/L may be pseudohyperkalemia, and treating it as real exposes the patient to unnecessary and dangerous intervention. Prolonged tourniquet time elevates potassium and lactate. Drawing from a line above a glucose or heparin infusion contaminates the sample and produces a falsely elevated glucose or prolonged clotting. Pseudohyponatremia occurs when marked hyperlipidemia or hyperproteinemia displaces plasma water, producing a falsely low sodium measured by indirect ion-selective electrode; the true osmolality is normal and the patient is not hyponatremic. A result that is dramatically discordant with the clinical picture, or that appears suddenly in an otherwise stable patient, should prompt the question: is this real, or is it a pre-analytical artifact? Check sample integrity, redraw if needed, and confirm before acting.

### Correlate the Panel With Clinical Context and Trend the Values

No metabolic panel value should be interpreted in isolation from the patient. A glucose of 8 mmol/L means different things in a fasting outpatient and a stressed, steroid-treated inpatient with sepsis. A creatinine of 120 micromol/L is normal in a muscular young man and significantly abnormal in a frail elderly woman with low muscle mass. Always interpret against the patient's own baseline and the clinical question the test was ordered to answer. Use the trend: a creatinine that has doubled since yesterday is an emergency regardless of the absolute value, while a stable mild elevation over years is a chronic finding to be managed, not panicked over. A result that contradicts a strong clinical impression should prompt re-checking the sample, reconsidering the diagnosis, and seeking corroborating data rather than blindly accepting or dismissing the number. The panel informs the clinical picture; it never replaces it.

## Common Traps

### Treating Hemolyzed Potassium as True Hyperkalemia

Acting on a potassium of 6.5 mmol/L from a hemolyzed sample, exposing the patient to unnecessary insulin-glucose and admission. Counter by checking the sample for hemolysis and redrawing before treating a discordant or asymptomatic elevation.

### Correcting Sodium Without Recognizing Pseudohyponatremia

Restricting fluids or giving hypertonic saline for a sodium that is falsely low due to hyperlipidemia or hyperproteinemia. Counter by measuring serum osmolality and confirming true hypotonic hyponatremia before treating.

### Labeling a Single Creatinine as Renal Failure Without a Baseline

Diagnosing acute kidney injury without comparing to prior creatinine, missing chronic disease or over-diagnosing acute injury. Counter by always obtaining the baseline and classifying the temporal pattern as acute, chronic, or acute-on-chronic.

### Interpreting Calcium Without Correcting for Albumin

Missing hypocalcemia because a low albumin makes the total calcium look normal, or over-treating a normal ionized calcium. Counter by correcting calcium for albumin before interpreting, and checking ionized calcium when albumin is very low.

### Missing a High-Anion-Gap Acidosis Behind Normal-Looking Electrolytes

Reading sodium, chloride, and bicarbonate individually without calculating the gap. Counter by always calculating the anion gap and delta-delta, and correcting the gap for albumin.

### Delaying Action on a Potassium or Glucose Emergency

Waiting for a repeat result in severe hyperkalemia with ECG changes or symptomatic severe hypoglycemia. Counter by acting immediately on the emergency while pursuing confirmation in parallel.

## Self-Check

- Did I read the panel as an interconnected picture, calculating the anion gap, delta-delta, and BUN-to-creatinine ratio rather than each value in isolation?
- Did I correct calcium for albumin and sodium for glucose before interpreting, and state the corrected values explicitly?
- Did I distinguish acute kidney injury from chronic kidney disease by comparing the creatinine to the patient's baseline and classifying the temporal pattern?
- Did I recognize and act immediately on the emergencies — severe hyperkalemia with ECG changes, severe hypokalemia, symptomatic hypoglycemia, hypercalcemia above 3.5 mmol/L, and hyponatremia with seizures?
- Did I rule out pre-analytical error (hemolysis, tourniquet time, line contamination, pseudohyponatremia from hyperlipidemia or hyperproteinemia) before acting on a discordant or surprising result?
- Did I interpret the panel against the patient's own baseline, clinical context, and trend rather than against the reference range alone?
- Did I escalate to nephrology, endocrinology, or intensive care early when the disturbance was severe, the cause was ambiguous, or correction was not proceeding as planned?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
