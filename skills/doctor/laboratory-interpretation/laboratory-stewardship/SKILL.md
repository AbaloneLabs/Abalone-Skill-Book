# Laboratory Stewardship

Laboratory testing is one of the most powerful diagnostic tools in medicine, and it is also one of the most overused, misused, and harmful-when-misused. The recurring failure is not the failure to order tests but the failure to order them rationally — to order tests that will not change management, to order panels reflexively when a single targeted test would answer the question, to repeat tests without reason, to order tests without considering the pre-test probability that gives the result its meaning, and to pursue every minor abnormality that the testing itself generates. Over-testing is not harmless. Every test carries a risk of false positives that trigger cascades of further investigation, patient anxiety, and invasive procedures; every incidental finding demands decisions that may cause more harm than the finding ever would; every unnecessary test consumes resources that are then unavailable for the patients who need them; and every test panel ordered without thought fragments the clinician's reasoning into a list of numbers rather than a coherent diagnostic question. The disciplined clinician treats every test order as a decision with benefits, harms, and alternatives, and asks the same question before every test: will the result of this test, in this patient, at this time, change what I do? This skill covers the rational ordering of laboratory tests, the choice of the right test for the clinical question, the integration of pre-test probability, the avoidance of over-testing and its downstream harms, the design of diagnostic pathways, and the principles of laboratory stewardship that protect patients from the harms of both under- and over-investigation. The discipline is to test with intent — to define the question, choose the test that answers it, interpret the result in context, and stop testing when the question is answered.

## Core Rules

### Define the Clinical Question Before Ordering Any Test

The single most important act of laboratory stewardship is to define, before ordering, the clinical question the test is meant to answer. A test ordered without a clear question is a test whose result cannot be interpreted, because the meaning of a result depends entirely on the question it was ordered to address. The question should be specific and actionable: not "check the bloods" but "is this patient's fatigue due to anemia or thyroid disease?"; not "liver panel" but "is this jaundice cholestatic or hepatocellular?"; not "bleeding screen" but "is this bruising due to thrombocytopenia or coagulopathy, and does it change whether I proceed with the procedure?". Once the question is defined, choose the test that answers it most directly and ask whether the answer will change management. If the result will not change what you do — if you will treat the patient the same way regardless of the result, or if the test is being ordered for reassurance that will not be achieved — then the test is unlikely to be worth its harms and costs. Document the question and the expected action in the order, so that the result is interpreted against its original purpose rather than in isolation.

### Integrate Pre-Test Probability Into Every Test Decision

The value of a test result depends on the pre-test probability of the condition it is testing for, and ordering a test without considering the pre-test probability is a common source of error. A positive test in a patient with a very low pre-test probability is more likely to be a false positive than a true positive, and pursuing it generates harm; a negative test in a patient with a high pre-test probability is more likely to be a false negative, and reassuring on it misses disease. Before ordering a test, estimate the pre-test probability from the clinical picture, the prevalence, and the patient's risk factors, and use it to judge whether the test will be informative. In a patient with a very low probability of disease, consider not testing at all, or testing only if the result would decisively change management. In a patient with a high probability, recognize that a negative test may not exclude disease and that further testing or treatment may be warranted regardless. Use likelihood ratios, where available, to understand how much a given result will shift the probability, and recognize that tests are most informative when the pre-test probability is intermediate (the zone of uncertainty where the result will actually change the decision). Avoid the reflexive ordering of tests for conditions that are clinically very unlikely, because the false positives will outnumber the true positives and generate cascades of harm.

### Choose the Right Test for the Question and Avoid Reflexive Panels

Different tests answer different questions, and choosing the right test — rather than a reflexive panel — improves diagnostic accuracy and reduces harm. A targeted test with high sensitivity is used to rule out disease when the probability is low; a targeted test with high specificity is used to rule in disease when the probability is intermediate or high. Understand the characteristics of the tests you order: the difference between a screening test (high sensitivity, may sacrifice specificity) and a confirmatory test (high specificity); the difference between a total measurement and a fractionated or specific measurement (total testosterone versus free, total calcium versus ionized); and the limitations of each test (interference, cross-reactivity, the hook effect, the effect of timing and the patient's state). Avoid reflexive panel ordering — the "comprehensive metabolic panel plus complete blood count plus thyroid" ordered on every patient — because panels generate results that were not asked for and that then demand explanation. Prefer the single test that answers the question, and add tests only as the question evolves. When a panel is appropriate (such as a structured septic workup or a defined diagnostic pathway), use it deliberately as a pathway, not as a reflex.

### Avoid Duplicate, Repetitive, and Standing Tests Without Justification

A substantial fraction of laboratory testing is repetitive — the same test ordered daily on a stable inpatient, or repeated at every clinic visit without reason — and the repetition generates cost, phlebotomy, and harm without changing management. Apply the principle of the minimum testing interval: how often does this test need to be repeated to detect a clinically meaningful change? A stable chronic value does not need daily or weekly repetition; an acutely changing value (potassium in hyperkalemia treatment, hemoglobin in active bleeding) may need frequent repetition. Challenge standing orders and daily panels, and ask whether each repeat will change management. Recognize that repeated testing in a stable patient generates more abnormal results purely by chance (the more you test, the more outliers you find), and that pursuing these chance abnormalities causes harm. Define the indication for each repeat test, and stop testing when the question is answered or the patient is stable.

### Recognize and Limit the Downstream Harms of Over-Testing

Over-testing generates harms that extend far beyond the immediate test, and the disciplined clinician anticipates and limits these downstream harms. Every test has a false positive rate, and in a low-probability patient the false positives outnumber the true positives, triggering cascades of further investigation, imaging, biopsies, and procedures — each with its own risks. Incidental findings (a pulmonary nodule on a scan ordered for another reason, a mildly abnormal liver test in a well patient) demand decisions that may cause more harm than the finding ever would have, through surveillance, biopsy, and anxiety. The patient who is investigated extensively for minor abnormalities suffers anxiety, loss of time, financial cost, and the risks of invasive follow-up, and may be labeled with a "disease" that would never have caused them harm. Anticipate these harms before ordering: what will I do with a positive result, and what will I do with an incidental finding? If the answer is "more investigation that the patient does not need," reconsider the test. Discuss the potential for incidental findings and false positives with the patient when shared decision-making is appropriate, and set clear boundaries on follow-up before the cascade begins.

### Build Diagnostic Pathways That Sequence Tests Rationally

Rational laboratory use is built on diagnostic pathways that sequence tests in order of yield, cost, and harm, rather than ordering everything at once. A pathway begins with the highest-yield, lowest-harm tests (the first-line tests that answer the question for most patients) and reserves more invasive, costly, or specialized tests for patients in whom the first-line tests are inconclusive or the probability warrants. A pathway for fatigue begins with a focused set (complete blood count, thyroid, iron, basic biochemistry) rather than a battery of esoteric tests; a pathway for chest pain begins with an ECG and troponin before advanced imaging; a pathway for abnormal liver tests begins with a structured panel and a history before autoimmune, metabolic, and imaging workup. Pathways reduce variation, prevent both under- and over-testing, and ensure that each test builds on the last rather than duplicating it. Use local and evidence-based pathways where they exist, and develop them within your team for common presentations. A pathway is not a rigid protocol — it is a rational default that the clinician departs from with reason and documentation.

### Close the Loop: Follow Up on Every Result and Act on It

Laboratory stewardship does not end with the order; it ends with the result being seen, interpreted, acted upon, and communicated. A test ordered but not followed up is worse than a test never ordered, because it generates a false sense of security that the question has been addressed. Build a system that ensures every result is reviewed by the responsible clinician within a defined timeframe, that critical results are actioned immediately, that the patient is informed of the result (especially abnormal results and especially in the outpatient setting), and that the result is integrated into the ongoing plan. A significant fraction of diagnostic error and malpractice claims arise from results that were generated but not followed up. Treat the follow-up as a non-negotiable part of the test order, and document that the loop was closed.

## Common Traps

### Ordering Tests Without a Defined Clinical Question

Ordering a panel reflexively without specifying what question it answers or how the result will change management. Counter by defining the question and the expected action before every order.

### Testing for Conditions With Very Low Pre-Test Probability

Ordering tests for unlikely conditions, generating false positives that outnumber true positives. Counter by estimating pre-test probability and testing only when the result will be informative.

### Reflexive Panel Ordering Instead of Targeted Tests

Ordering comprehensive panels that generate unasked-for results demanding explanation. Counter by choosing the single test that answers the question and adding tests only as the question evolves.

### Repeating Tests Without Justification

Daily panels on stable inpatients or repeats at every visit without a defined minimum testing interval. Counter by defining the indication for each repeat and stopping when the question is answered.

### Pursuing Every Minor Abnormality and Incidental Finding

Investigating chance outliers and incidental findings that would never have caused harm, generating cascades of further testing and harm. Counter by anticipating downstream harms, distinguishing clinical from statistical abnormality, and setting boundaries on follow-up.

### Failing to Follow Up on Ordered Results

Assuming a result will be seen or that the patient will return, leaving significant abnormalities unactioned. Counter by building a system that ensures every result is reviewed, actioned, and communicated.

## Self-Check

- Did I define the specific clinical question before ordering the test, and will the result change what I do?
- Did I estimate the pre-test probability and judge whether the test will be informative, avoiding tests for very unlikely conditions?
- Did I choose the targeted test that answers the question, with the right sensitivity and specificity, rather than a reflexive panel?
- Did I avoid duplicate and repetitive testing by defining the minimum testing interval and the indication for each repeat?
- Did I anticipate the downstream harms of over-testing — false positives, incidental findings, cascades, anxiety — and set boundaries on follow-up before the cascade began?
- Did I use or build a rational diagnostic pathway that sequences tests by yield, cost, and harm?
- Did I ensure that every result was reviewed, actioned, and communicated to the patient, closing the loop on every test ordered?
