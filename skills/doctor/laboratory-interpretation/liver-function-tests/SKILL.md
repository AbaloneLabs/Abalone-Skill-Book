# Liver Function Tests Interpretation

Liver tests are misnamed, and the misnomer causes harm. Most of what is called a "liver function test" — AST, ALT, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin — is a marker of liver injury or cholestasis, not of liver function, while the true markers of hepatic synthetic function (albumin, INR, and glucose) are often ignored or buried at the bottom of the panel. The recurring failure is to chase an elevated AST or ALT as "liver disease" without classifying the pattern, to dismiss an isolated alkaline phosphatase as insignificant when it may signal cholestasis or malignancy, and to miss decompensated liver failure because the transaminases look only mildly abnormal while the INR is climbing. Equally dangerous is attributing an elevated alkaline phosphatase to the liver when it originates in bone, placenta, or intestine, or attributing a transaminase elevation to the liver when muscle is the source. This skill covers the disciplined interpretation of liver tests: classifying the pattern as hepatocellular, cholestatic, or mixed, working up isolated enzyme elevations and jaundice, distinguishing true hepatic dysfunction from non-hepatic sources, recognizing drug-induced liver injury, and treating albumin and INR as the decisive markers of synthetic function. The discipline is to read the whole pattern, to localize the source of each abnormal enzyme, and to escalate when synthetic failure appears.

## Core Rules

### Classify the Pattern: Hepatocellular, Cholestatic, or Mixed

The first step in interpreting abnormal liver tests is to classify the dominant pattern, because the differential and the workup diverge completely. A hepatocellular pattern is defined by a predominant elevation of AST and ALT relative to alkaline phosphatase; the differential includes viral hepatitis, alcoholic and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, drug-induced liver injury, autoimmune hepatitis, ischemic hepatitis (shock liver), hemochromatosis, Wilson disease, and alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency. A cholestatic pattern is defined by a predominant elevation of alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin relative to the transaminases; the differential splits into extrahepatic obstruction (gallstones, strictures, tumors, primary sclerosing cholangitis) and intrahepatic cholestasis (primary biliary cholangitis, drug-induced, infiltrative disease, sepsis, pregnancy). A mixed pattern shows elevation of both. Use the ratio of ALT to alkaline phosphatase to refine the classification, but recognize that ratios are guides, not rules. Always classify before ordering the workup, because chasing a viral hepatitis panel in a patient with extrahepatic biliary obstruction delays the imaging and intervention that patient needs.

### Treat Albumin and INR as the True Markers of Synthetic Function

The most clinically consequential liver test results are often the ones clinicians skim past: albumin and INR. These reflect the liver's synthetic capacity, and their derangement signals serious hepatic dysfunction even when the transaminases are only mildly elevated. A low albumin (excluding nephrotic syndrome, protein-losing enteropathy, malnutrition, and inflammation) indicates impaired protein synthesis in chronic liver disease. A prolonged INR not due to warfarin, vitamin K deficiency, or DIC indicates impaired synthesis of clotting factors and is a hallmark of acute liver failure and decompensated cirrhosis. An elevated INR in acute liver injury is a red flag for acute liver failure and mandates urgent hepatology consultation and evaluation for transplant. Never conclude "mild liver dysfunction" from near-normal transaminases while ignoring an INR of 2.5 or an albumin of 25 g/L; the synthetic markers are where the severity and the prognosis live.

### Work Up Isolated Alkaline Phosphatase Elevation by Localizing the Source

An isolated elevated alkaline phosphatase is a common and often mishandled finding. The first step is to localize the source, because alkaline phosphatase isoenzymes exist in liver, bone, placenta, and intestine, and treating a bone-source elevation as liver disease leads to a wrong and costly workup. Use gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) or 5'-nucleotidase to confirm hepatic origin: if the GGT is also elevated, the alkaline phosphatase is hepatic; if the GGT is normal, suspect bone or another source. Once hepatic origin is confirmed, the cholestatic workup follows: imaging (ultrasound first, then MRCP or CT) to evaluate for biliary dilation and obstruction, and serology for primary biliary cholangitis (anti-mitochondrial antibody) and primary sclerosing cholangitis. If the source is bone, pursue the bone differential (Paget disease, malignancy, vitamin D deficiency, healing fractures, hyperparathyroidism). Do not ignore a persistently elevated alkaline phosphatase, and do not attribute it to the liver without confirming the isoenzyme source.

### Work Up Jaundice and Hyperbilirubinemia Systematically

An elevated bilirubin demands classification into unconjugated (indirect) and conjugated (direct) fractions, because the differential splits at that branch. Unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia results from increased production (hemolysis, ineffective erythropoiesis, resorption of hematomas) or impaired conjugation (Gilbert syndrome, Crigler-Najjar, neonatal jaundice); the workup centers on hemolysis (LDH, haptoglobin, reticulocyte count, smear) and hereditary conjugation defects. Conjugated hyperbilirubinemia reflects impaired excretion and points to hepatocellular disease or biliary obstruction; pair it with the AST, ALT, and alkaline phosphatase pattern to classify, and image the biliary tree. Jaundice visible on examination is usually a bilirubin above roughly 40 to 50 micromol/L, and scleral icterus appears slightly earlier. Always interpret bilirubin with the transaminases and alkaline phosphatase, and image the biliary tree when a cholestatic or obstructive pattern is present, because missing a resectable obstructing tumor or a draining cholangitis is a high-stakes error.

### Recognize Drug-Induced Liver Injury and Take a Complete Medication and Supplement History

Drug-induced liver injury is one of the most common causes of transaminase elevation and one of the most frequently missed, because clinicians underweight medications, herbal supplements, and recently stopped drugs. Take a complete history including prescription drugs, over-the-counter medications (especially acetaminophen, the leading cause of acute liver failure in many regions), herbal and dietary supplements, recreational substances, and recently discontinued agents, because liver injury can persist or emerge after a drug is stopped. Recognize the patterns: acetaminophen produces a steep transaminase rise with relatively preserved synthetic function early on; amoxicillin-clavulanate produces a cholestatic or mixed pattern that can appear weeks after the course; isoniazid, statins, methotrexate, and many others produce characteristic patterns. Apply a causality assessment framework (such as RUCAM) when the cause is unclear, and withdraw the suspected agent. Always consider acetaminophen toxicity in any unexplained acute transaminase elevation, because it is treatable with N-acetylcysteine and delay is dangerous.

### Correlate the Pattern With Clinical Context and Image When Obstruction Is Possible

Liver tests must be interpreted with the clinical picture, the tempo of change, and the presence of symptoms. Acute transaminase elevations into the thousands in a hypotensive patient suggest ischemic hepatitis; a gradual cholestatic rise with painless jaundice and weight loss raises concern for pancreatic or biliary malignancy; fever, right upper quadrant pain, and jaundice suggest cholangitis and demand urgent imaging and antibiotics. When a cholestatic pattern or jaundice is present, image the biliary tree early — ultrasound first for stones and dilation, then MRCP or CT for finer detail, and ERCP for both diagnosis and therapeutic intervention. Do not delay imaging in a patient with possible biliary obstruction while awaiting extensive serology, because obstruction is mechanical and time-sensitive. Correlate the labs with the history, examination, and imaging, and escalate to gastroenterology or hepatology when the pattern is severe, the cause is unclear, or synthetic function is impaired.

## Common Traps

### Chasing Transaminases While Ignoring INR and Albumin

Concluding "mild liver disease" from near-normal AST and ALT while the INR is elevated and the albumin is low. Counter by always reading albumin and INR as synthetic-function markers and escalating when they are deranged.

### Attributing Alkaline Phosphatase to the Liver Without Confirming the Source

Pursuing a liver workup for an alkaline phosphatase of bone origin. Counter by confirming hepatic origin with GGT or 5'-nucleotidase before initiating the cholestatic workup.

### Missing Extrahepatic Biliary Obstruction Behind a "Hepatitis" Workup

Ordering viral and autoimmune panels while delaying imaging in a patient with a cholestatic pattern and possible obstruction. Counter by imaging the biliary tree early when the pattern is cholestatic or jaundice is present.

### Forgetting Drug-Induced Liver Injury, Including Supplements

Attributing transaminase elevation to chronic liver disease while the patient is taking a hepatotoxic supplement or recently took acetaminophen. Counter by taking a complete medication, supplement, and recent-discontinuation history and considering acetaminophen in any unexplained acute rise.

### Not Classifying Bilirubin Into Conjugated and Unconjugated Fractions

Working up hyperbilirubinemia without splitting the fraction, leading to a hemolysis workup in conjugated disease or an obstruction workup in Gilbert syndrome. Counter by always classifying bilirubin into direct and indirect fractions before pursuing the differential.

### Dismissing an Isolated or Persistent Enzyme Elevation

Ignoring a chronically elevated alkaline phosphatase or transaminase because it is "mild." Counter by localizing the source, classifying the pattern, and pursuing a defined workup for persistent abnormalities.

## Self-Check

- Did I classify the liver test abnormality as hepatocellular, cholestatic, or mixed using the relative elevation of transaminases versus alkaline phosphatase before ordering the workup?
- Did I read albumin and INR as markers of hepatic synthetic function and escalate urgently when they signaled acute liver failure or decompensation?
- Did I localize the source of an isolated alkaline phosphatase elevation using GGT or 5'-nucleotidase before initiating a hepatic workup?
- Did I split bilirubin into conjugated and unconjugated fractions and pursue the appropriate differential for each?
- Did I take a complete medication, supplement, and recent-discontinuation history and consider acetaminophen and drug-induced liver injury in any unexplained transaminase elevation?
- Did I image the biliary tree early when the pattern was cholestatic or jaundice was present, rather than delaying while awaiting serology?
- Did I correlate the liver tests with the clinical context, tempo, and symptoms, and escalate to gastroenterology or hepatology when the pattern was severe, unclear, or associated with synthetic dysfunction?
