# Hematology and Coagulation Interpretation

Hematology and coagulation results are deceptively familiar — every clinician reads a CBC and an INR — and that familiarity breeds shallow interpretation. A low hemoglobin is treated as "anemia" rather than decomposed into its mechanism; a prolonged aPTT is labeled "coagulopathy" without asking whether it reflects a factor deficiency, an inhibitor, heparin contamination, or lupus anticoagulant; a high platelet count is dismissed as reactive when it may herald a myeloproliferative disorder. The recurring failure is reading numbers in isolation rather than building a mechanistic picture from the CBC, the indices, the smear, and the coagulation panel together. The disciplined skill is to interpret cytopenias and coagulopathies mechanistically, to recognize the patterns that demand urgent hematology input, to monitor anticoagulation against the correct targets, and to apply transfusion thresholds rather than transfusing by habit. This skill covers the structured interpretation of CBC and smear, coagulation panels, cytopenia and pancytopenia workup, anticoagulation monitoring, and the escalation and transfusion decisions that protect patients with hematologic disease.

## Core Rules

### Decompose the CBC Into Mechanism, Not Just Severity

An abnormal CBC is a starting point for mechanistic reasoning, not a conclusion. For anemia, use the mean corpuscular volume to classify microcytic, normocytic, or macrocytic, then pursue the mechanism: iron studies and ferritin for iron deficiency (low ferritin is diagnostic; a normal ferritin does not exclude it in inflammation), reticulocyte count and hemolysis screen (LDH, haptoglobin, bilirubin, direct antiglobulin test) for hemolysis, B12 and folate for macrocytosis, and bone marrow consideration for pancytopenia or unexplained cytopenias. For thrombocytosis, distinguish reactive (infection, inflammation, iron deficiency, post-splenectomy) from clonal (essential thrombocythemia, other myeloproliferative neoplasms) using history, smear, and persistent elevation over time. For leukocytosis, distinguish a left shift and toxic changes suggesting infection from the presence of blasts or abnormal forms suggesting marrow pathology. Always read the indices and the smear alongside the absolute counts; the indices and morphology often reveal the mechanism that the count alone conceals.

### Use the Peripheral Smear as a Diagnostic Tool, Not an Afterthought

The peripheral smear is one of the highest-yield, lowest-cost investigations in hematology, and it is underused. Request and review it whenever the cytopenia, cytosis, or morphology is unexplained, when hemolysis is suspected (schistocytes in microangiopathic hemolysis, spherocytes in autoimmune hemolysis), when a thalassemia or hemoglobinopathy is possible (target cells, basophilic stippling), when a marrow disorder is suspected (blasts, dysplastic forms), or when an infection such as malaria is possible (parasites within erythrocytes). The smear can convert a vague cytopenia into a specific diagnosis and can flag emergencies such as thrombotic microangiopathy (schistocytes, thrombocytopenia, elevated LDH) that demand immediate plasma exchange. Do not accept a CBC interpretation without morphology when the picture is unclear; the smear is where mechanism becomes visible.

### Interpret Coagulation Panels Mechanistically and in Combination

PT/INR and aPTT must be interpreted together and mechanistically. An isolated prolonged PT/INR with normal aPTT suggests factor VII deficiency (the shortest half-life factor), vitamin K deficiency, early liver dysfunction, or warfarin effect. An isolated prolonged aPTT suggests intrinsic pathway factor deficiency (factor VIII, IX, XI, XII), an inhibitor (factor VIII inhibitor, lupus anticoagulant), or heparin contamination of the sample. A prolongation of both suggests severe liver disease, disseminated intravascular coagulation, vitamin K deficiency, or combined factor deficiency. Always perform mixing studies to distinguish a factor deficiency (corrects) from an inhibitor (does not correct) when the cause is unclear. A prolonged aPTT that does not correct, in a patient with thrombosis rather than bleeding, suggests lupus anticoagulant or antiphospholipid syndrome. Never label a prolonged clotting time "coagulopathy" and stop; identify the mechanism because the treatment diverges completely.

### Recognize Pancytopenia and Cytopenic Emergencies That Demand Urgent Hematology Input

Pancytopenia — concurrent anemia, leukopenia, and thrombocytopenia — indicates marrow failure, marrow infiltration, severe B12 or folate deficiency, or hypersplenic sequestration, and it warrants urgent hematology review, a smear, and usually a bone marrow examination. Within cytopenias, recognize the emergencies: severe thrombocytopenia below 20 with bleeding or below 10 in any patient risks spontaneous intracranial hemorrhage and needs urgent assessment and often transfusion; neutropenic sepsis in a patient with an absolute neutrophil count below 0.5 is a medical emergency requiring empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics within the first hour; acute leukemia presenting with cytopenias and blasts requires rapid hematology referral and tumor lysis precautions. Do not allow an unexplained cytopenia to be observed indefinitely; persistent or severe cytopenias need a defined workup and a hematology referral pathway.

### Monitor Anticoagulation Against the Correct Target and Recognize Bleeding and Reversal Needs

Each anticoagulant has a specific monitoring target and a specific reversal strategy, and confusing them causes harm. Warfarin is monitored by INR, with target ranges that depend on the indication (e.g., 2.0 to 3.0 for atrial fibrillation and deep vein thrombosis, higher for mechanical mitral valves); a supratherapeutic INR with bleeding requires vitamin K and, for major bleeding, prothrombin complex concentrate. Heparin is monitored by aPTT or anti-Xa, unfractionated heparin requiring frequent aPTT checks and dose adjustment. Direct oral anticoagulants (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) generally do not require routine monitoring, but their effect must be accounted for before procedures or in bleeding, and specific reversal agents (idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors) exist for life-threatening bleeding. Know the target for each agent, the expected half-life and renal clearance, and the reversal pathway, and escalate to hematology or the major bleeding protocol early in significant hemorrhage.

### Apply Transfusion Thresholds, Not Habitual Transfusion

Transfusion carries real risks (transfusion reactions, transfusion-associated circulatory overload, transfusion-related acute lung injury, alloimmunization, infection), and transfusing by habit rather than by threshold harms patients. Apply the evidence-based thresholds: transfuse red cells for hemoglobin below 70 g/L in most stable inpatients, with a higher threshold around 80 g/L in those with cardiovascular disease or acute coronary syndromes, and transfuse for symptomatic anemia regardless of the number. Transfuse platelets prophylactically below 10 (below 20 in fever or sepsis, below 50 before procedures with bleeding risk, and maintain above 50 to 100 for active major bleeding or neurosurgery). Transfuse fresh frozen plasma for multiple factor deficiency with bleeding or before urgent procedures, and cryoprecipitate for fibrinogen below 1.5 to 2.0 g/L with bleeding. Transfuse one unit at a time in stable patients and reassess rather than automatically giving two units. In massive hemorrhage, activate the massive transfusion protocol and follow the institutional ratio-based or goal-directed approach.

## Common Traps

### Labeling Anemia Without Determining Mechanism

Treating a low hemoglobin as "anemia" and prescribing iron without iron studies, or missing hemolysis or marrow failure. Counter by classifying with the MCV and pursuing the mechanism with iron studies, reticulocyte count, and a hemolysis screen.

### Dismissing the Peripheral Smear

Accepting a CBC interpretation without morphology when the picture is unexplained, missing schistocytes, spherocytes, or blasts. Counter by requesting and reviewing the smear whenever the cytopenia or cytosis is unexplained.

### Calling a Prolonged Clotting Time "Coagulopathy" and Stopping

Failing to perform mixing studies or identify the mechanism, missing an inhibitor or lupus anticoagulant. Counter by interpreting PT and aPTT together and using mixing studies to separate factor deficiency from inhibitor.

### Under-Reacting to Pancytopenia or Severe Cytopenias

Observing an unexplained pancytopenia or severe thrombocytopenia without urgent hematology referral and marrow evaluation. Counter by recognizing pancytopenia as a marrow-level problem requiring rapid workup and escalation.

### Treating Neutropenic Fever as Ordinary Sepsis

Delaying antibiotics in a neutropenic patient while awaiting cultures. Counter by giving empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics within the first hour and following neutropenic sepsis protocols.

### Confusing Anticoagulant Targets and Reversal Strategies

Applying warfarin monitoring to a direct oral anticoagulant, or using the wrong reversal agent in major bleeding. Counter by knowing each agent's target, half-life, and reversal pathway, and escalating to the major bleeding protocol early.

### Transfusing by Habit Rather Than Threshold

Giving two units of red cells automatically, or transfusing platelets above evidence-based thresholds. Counter by applying the thresholds, transfusing one unit at a time in stable patients, and reassessing after each unit.

## Self-Check

- Did I decompose the CBC into mechanism using indices, reticulocyte count, iron studies, and hemolysis screen rather than labeling "anemia"?
- Did I request and review the peripheral smear when the cytopenia, cytosis, or morphology was unexplained?
- Did I interpret the PT/INR and aPTT together and use mixing studies to distinguish factor deficiency from inhibitor when the cause was unclear?
- Did I recognize pancytopenia and severe cytopenias (platelets below 20, neutropenic fever) as conditions requiring urgent hematology input and specific protocols?
- Did I monitor anticoagulation against the correct target for the specific agent and know the reversal strategy for major bleeding?
- Did I apply evidence-based transfusion thresholds rather than habitual transfusion, transfusing one unit at a time and reassessing?
- Did I escalate to hematology early for unexplained or severe cytopenias, suspected marrow disorders, thrombotic microangiopathy, or major bleeding on anticoagulation?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
