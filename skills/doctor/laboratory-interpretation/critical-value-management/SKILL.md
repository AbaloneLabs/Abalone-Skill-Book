# Critical Value Management

A critical laboratory value is a result so far outside the normal range that it poses an immediate threat to the patient's life and demands action within minutes, not the next clinic visit. The defining feature of a critical value is not its statistical abnormality but its urgency: a potassium of 6.8 mmol per liter can stop the heart within the hour; a glucose of 1.5 mmol per liter can cause irreversible brain injury; a hemoglobin of 45 g per liter can precipitate cardiac ischemia; an international normalized ratio above 5 carries a major bleeding risk; and a positive blood culture or a markedly abnormal troponin changes management immediately. The recurring failure in critical value management is not the failure to recognize the number — the laboratory flags it — but the failure in the chain of communication and action that connects the result to the patient. The result is generated, but not seen; seen, but not communicated to the responsible clinician; communicated, but without confirmation that it was received; received, but not acted upon; or acted upon, but not documented and followed up. Each break in this chain has killed patients, and each is preventable with a disciplined closed-loop process. A second, equally dangerous failure is over-reacting to a spurious critical value — a hemolyzed sample producing a falsely elevated potassium, a poorly taken sample producing a falsely low glucose, or a clotted sample producing a spurious abnormality — and subjecting the patient to dangerous treatment for a number that does not reflect their physiology. This skill covers the recognition of the high-yield critical values, the closed-loop communication and action process, the immediate clinical responses, the exclusion of spurious results before treatment, and the documentation and follow-up that close the loop. The discipline is to treat every critical value as a time-critical clinical event with a defined process, to verify before treating when possible, and to never let a critical result sit unactioned.

## Core Rules

### Treat Critical Values as Time-Critical Clinical Events, Not Laboratory Anomalies

A critical value is a clinical emergency that happens to be detected by the laboratory, and it must be managed with the same urgency as any other emergency. The first principle is that the clock starts when the result is available, not when the clinician happens to see it, and the target is action within a defined, short timeframe (often 30 to 60 minutes for the most dangerous values). Establish who is responsible for receiving and acting on critical values in your setting, and ensure that the result reaches them directly — through a phone call, an active alert, or a dedicated notification system — rather than sitting in an inbox or a printout. Treat the receipt of a critical value as an interruption that takes priority over routine work. Recognize that the most dangerous values — severe hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, severe metabolic acidosis or alkalosis, critical sodium derangement, severe anemia or thrombocytopenia, marked coagulopathy, and dangerously abnormal drug levels — can cause death or irreversible harm within hours, and that delay is the most common contributor to adverse outcomes.

### Know the High-Yield Critical Values and Their Immediate Threats

Effective response requires knowing which values are critical and what each one threatens, so that the immediate action is automatic rather than improvised. Severe hyperkalemia (typically above 6.5 mmol per liter, or lower with ECG changes) threatens cardiac arrhythmia and arrest; the response is an immediate ECG, stabilization of the myocardium with intravenous calcium, and measures to shift potassium into cells and remove it from the body. Severe hypoglycemia (typically below 2.5 mmol per liter, or any level with symptoms) threatens brain injury; the response is immediate glucose, orally if conscious and intravenously if not. Severe hyperglycemia with ketosis (diabetic ketoacidosis or hyperosmolar state) threatens dehydration, acidosis, and coma; the response is intravenous fluids, insulin, and electrolyte management. Critical sodium derangements (typically below 120 or above 155 mmol per liter) threaten cerebral edema or hemorrhage and seizures; the response is careful, guided correction at a rate that avoids osmotic demyelination or cerebral edema. Severe metabolic acidosis (pH below 7.1) threatens cardiovascular collapse; the response addresses the cause and supports the circulation. Severe anemia (hemoglobin below 60 to 70 g per liter, or higher with symptoms or ischemia) threatens tissue hypoxia; the response is transfusion if symptomatic or unstable. Severe thrombocytopenia (below 20 to 10) threatens spontaneous hemorrhage; the response is assessment and often transfusion. Marked coagulopathy (INR above 5, or with bleeding) threatens major hemorrhage; the response is reversal and bleeding assessment. Critical troponin or cardiac markers indicate myocardial injury and demand cardiac assessment. Positive blood cultures or critical microbiology results indicate serious infection and demand antimicrobial review. Knowing these by heart allows the immediate, correct response.

### Verify the Result Before Dangerous Treatment When Clinically Possible

Not every critical value reflects the patient's true physiology, and treating a spurious value can be as dangerous as missing a real one. Before initiating aggressive treatment for a critical value, ask whether the result could be an artifact, and verify it when the patient's clinical state allows the brief delay. The classic spurious critical values include: hyperkalemia from a hemolyzed sample, a delayed sample, or a sample taken from a limb with a tourniquet or an infusion running; falsely low glucose from a sample left unprocessed or taken without fluoride tube; falsely abnormal potassium, phosphate, and other cell-derived analytes from a sample taken from a line or with poor technique; spuriously high or low hemoglobin from a clotted or diluted sample; and spuriously abnormal coagulation from a under-filled or heparin-contaminated sample. The verification step is to check the sample integrity and the collection details, to correlate the result with the patient's clinical state (a patient with a "critical" potassium of 7.0 who is asymptomatic with a normal ECG and no renal failure may have a spurious result), and to repeat the test on a fresh, well-taken sample before committing to dangerous treatment. The exception is the patient who is unstable or symptomatic, in whom treatment must begin immediately on the basis of the result while verification proceeds in parallel. Use judgment: verify when safe, treat immediately when unstable.

### Use Closed-Loop Communication From Result to Action to Documentation

The single most important process in critical value management is the closed loop: the result is generated, communicated to a named responsible clinician, confirmed as received, acted upon, and documented — with each step verified. The laboratory must have a protocol that requires direct communication of critical values to a clinician (a phone call, not just an electronic flag), with read-back of the value and the patient identifier to confirm accurate receipt. The receiving clinician must acknowledge receipt and record the action taken. The action must be timely (within the defined target), appropriate to the value and the patient, and documented in the record with the time of the result, the time of action, and the clinical reasoning. The loop is closed by follow-up: a repeat test to confirm the response, a reassessment of the patient, and a plan for the underlying cause. Each break in this chain — result not communicated, communication not confirmed, action not taken, action not documented, follow-up not arranged — is a point at which patients are harmed, and disciplined closed-loop management is the defense. Design and follow the protocol in your setting, and never assume that a result has been seen or acted upon without confirmation.

### Tailor the Response to the Patient, Not Just the Number

A critical value is interpreted in the context of the patient, and the same number demands different responses in different patients. A potassium of 6.5 in a patient with renal failure and ECG changes is an immediate emergency; the same value in a stable outpatient with a normal ECG and a known baseline allows verification and a more measured approach. A hemoglobin of 65 in a young, asymptomatic patient may be tolerated; the same value in an older patient with cardiac disease and chest pain demands transfusion. A glucose of 2.0 in an unconscious patient demands intravenous dextrose immediately; the same value in an awake, asymptomatic patient allows oral treatment and investigation of the cause. A sodium of 118 developing over two days demands urgent correction; the same value developing over two months demands slow correction to avoid osmotic demyelination. Tailor the urgency, the setting (ward, intensive care, outpatient), the treatment, and the monitoring to the patient's symptoms, comorbidities, the rate of change, and the underlying cause. Treat the patient, not the number — but never use this principle to delay action on a genuinely critical value in an unstable patient.

### Investigate and Address the Underlying Cause, Not Just the Number

Correcting a critical value without addressing its cause guarantees recurrence. After the immediate stabilization, investigate why the value was critical: severe hyperkalemia demands identification of the cause (renal failure, potassium-sparing drugs, adrenal insufficiency, tissue breakdown, dietary or iatrogenic load); hypoglycemia demands identification of the cause (diabetic medication, insulinoma, adrenal insufficiency, liver failure, sepsis); severe anemia demands identification of the cause (bleeding, hemolysis, underproduction); and coagulopathy demands identification of the cause (medication, liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, disseminated intravascular coagulation). The critical value is the signal; the cause is the disease. Build the investigation and management of the cause into the plan from the outset, and arrange the follow-up (repeat testing, specialist referral, medication review) that prevents recurrence. Document the cause and the plan so that the next clinician understands the whole picture.

## Common Traps

### The Result That Is Generated but Never Seen or Acted Upon

A critical value sitting in an inbox or a printout without reaching the responsible clinician. Counter by ensuring direct, confirmed communication of critical values through a closed-loop protocol.

### Treating a Spurious Value as Real

Initiating dangerous treatment for a hemolyzed-sample potassium or a poorly-taken glucose without verification. Counter by checking sample integrity, correlating with the clinical state, and repeating on a fresh sample when the patient is stable.

### Delaying Treatment in the Unstable Patient While Verifying

Waiting for a repeat result in a patient who is symptomatic or deteriorating. Counter by treating immediately on the result while verifying in parallel, when the patient is unstable.

### Correcting a Derangement Too Quickly or Too Slowly

Over-correcting sodium and causing osmotic demyelination, or under-correcting hyperkalemia and allowing arrhythmia. Counter by following evidence-based correction rates and monitoring serially.

### Treating the Number Without Addressing the Cause

Correcting the critical value and discharging without investigating why it occurred, guaranteeing recurrence. Counter by building cause investigation and follow-up into every critical value response.

### Failing to Document the Communication, Action, and Follow-Up

Acting on a critical value but leaving no record of the time, the reasoning, or the plan. Counter by documenting the closed loop — receipt, action, reasoning, and follow-up — for every critical value.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat the critical value as a time-critical clinical event and ensure it reached the responsible clinician through a confirmed, closed-loop communication?
- Did I recognize the specific value as critical and know its immediate threat (arrhythmia, brain injury, hemorrhage, hypoxia, acidosis) and the correct first-line response?
- Did I verify the result by checking sample integrity and correlating with the clinical state, repeating on a fresh sample when the patient was stable, while treating immediately when unstable?
- Did I use closed-loop communication — read-back confirmation, documented receipt, action within the target timeframe, and documented reasoning?
- Did I tailor the urgency, setting, and treatment to the patient's symptoms, comorbidities, rate of change, and baseline, treating the patient rather than the number?
- Did I investigate and address the underlying cause of the critical value, and arrange follow-up (repeat testing, referral, medication review) to prevent recurrence?
- Did I document the time of the result, the time of action, the reasoning, and the follow-up plan so the loop is visibly closed?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
