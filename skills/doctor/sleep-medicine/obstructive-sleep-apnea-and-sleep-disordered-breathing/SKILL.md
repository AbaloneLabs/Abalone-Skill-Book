# Obstructive Sleep Apnea and Sleep-Disordered Breathing

Obstructive sleep apnea is one of the most underdiagnosed and undertreated conditions in medicine, and the gap between its prevalence and its recognition drives enormous preventable harm. It is not simply snoring or poor sleep; it is a recurrent, nightly cycle of upper-airway collapse, oxygen desaturation, sympathetic surge, arousal, and fragmented sleep that, over years, contributes to resistant hypertension, atrial fibrillation, heart failure, stroke, type 2 diabetes, cognitive impairment, depression, and a markedly elevated risk of motor vehicle and occupational accidents. The central failures in its care are failing to suspect it in the many patients whose presentation is fatigue, cardiovascular disease, or obesity rather than a sleep complaint; over-relying on a single screening score; misinterpreting or under-ordering sleep studies; abandoning positive airway pressure therapy because early adherence is difficult; and underestimating perioperative risk. The clinician's discipline is to screen systematically, to interpret sleep studies in clinical context, to initiate positive airway pressure with structured adherence support, to distinguish obstructive from central breathing disorders, and to manage the high-risk perioperative patient deliberately. This skill covers screening, sleep study interpretation, positive airway pressure initiation and adherence, perioperative risk, central versus obstructive disease, and escalation to sleep medicine.

## Core Rules

### Screen Systematically but Treat Screening Scores as Decision Aids, Not Diagnoses

Use validated tools to structure suspicion, but understand their limits. The STOP-BANG questionnaire captures the key risk factors of snoring, tiredness, observed apnea, blood pressure, body mass index, age, neck circumference, and gender, and stratifies patients into low, intermediate, and high probability of moderate-to-severe disease. The Epworth Sleepiness Scale quantifies the propensity to doze across situations and is useful for tracking symptoms and response to therapy over time. Neither tool diagnoses sleep apnea, and both can mislead: a thin, non-snorning woman can have severe disease, particularly after menopause, and a high Epworth score can reflect sleep deprivation, depression, or another sleep disorder rather than apnea. Use the scores to decide who needs a sleep study, not to decide who has the disease, and never withhold investigation because a score is borderline in a patient with a compelling clinical picture such as resistant hypertension, atrial fibrillation, or witnessed apneas.

### Interpret Sleep Studies in Clinical Context, Not by the Numbers Alone

Polysomnography and home sleep apnea testing quantify the apnea-hypopnea index, oxygen desaturation patterns, sleep architecture, and positional and stage dependence of events, but the numbers require clinical interpretation. The apnea-hypopnea index is a per-hour average that can obscure long, severe clusters of events and significant oxygen desaturation, so always review the oxygen nadir, time below threshold, and the distribution of events across the night. Home testing tends to underestimate severity relative to in-laboratory polysomnography because it captures fewer signals and fewer hours, so a negative home study in a high-suspicion patient warrants in-laboratory testing rather than reassurance. Distinguish obstructive events, where respiratory effort continues against a closed airway, from central events, where effort ceases, because the treatments diverge sharply. When the study is ambiguous or the clinical picture does not fit the result, repeat or escalate rather than forcing a mismatched plan.

### Initiate Positive Airway Pressure With Structured Adherence Support, Not Just a Prescription

Continuous positive airway pressure is highly effective when used, but adherence is the central challenge, and abandonment in the first months is the most common reason for treatment failure. Initiate therapy with attention to mask fit and interface choice, because an uncomfortable or ill-fitting mask is the leading driver of discontinuation; offer nasal, oronasal, and pillow options and re-evaluate at follow-up. Address pressure-related side effects proactively: nasal congestion and dryness with heated humidification, mouth leak with chin straps or full-face masks, difficulty exhaling against pressure with pressure-relief features or ramp settings, and aerophagia with pressure adjustment. Set realistic expectations that benefit accrues with consistent nightly use and that the first weeks require active problem-solving, and schedule early follow-up to review data downloads, address side effects, and reinforce adherence. Do not declare therapy failed before these steps are exhausted; most adherence problems are solvable with interface and pressure optimization, and alternative therapies exist for the minority who cannot tolerate positive airway pressure.

### Distinguish Obstructive From Central Sleep Apnea Because the Treatments Diverge

Obstructive and central sleep-disordered breathing can coexist and can mimic each other symptomatically, but they require different management. Obstructive disease responds to positive airway pressure that splints the airway, oral appliances, weight loss, positional therapy, and selected upper-airway surgery. Central disease, including Cheyne-Stokes respiration in heart failure and treatment-emergent central apnea, may worsen with standard continuous positive airway pressure and often requires adaptive servo-ventilation or, in heart failure, optimization of cardiac therapy. Treatment-emergent central apnea appears in a subset of obstructive patients after positive airway pressure is started and should be suspected when symptoms persist or oxygen desaturation continues despite apparently adequate pressure; it requires sleep specialist reassessment rather than simple pressure escalation. Misclassifying central disease as obstructive, or vice versa, leads to ineffective or harmful therapy, so review the event types and treatment response whenever the clinical course is unexpected.

### Manage Perioperative Risk Deliberately, Not as an Afterthought

Patients with obstructive sleep apnea are at substantially elevated risk of perioperative airway compromise, hypoxemia, respiratory depression from opioids and sedatives, and cardiac events, and this risk is often unrecognized at the moment it matters most. Screen every preoperative patient for sleep apnea risk, including those with a known diagnosis and those with undiagnosed but high-risk features. For diagnosed patients, confirm that positive airway pressure is being used and instruct them to bring their device to the hospital; for high-risk undiagnosed patients, consider delaying elective surgery for evaluation when feasible. Plan anesthesia and postoperative analgesia to minimize opioids, favoring regional and multimodal non-opioid techniques, and arrange appropriate postoperative monitoring for patients at elevated risk. Do not assume that a patient who tolerates surgery in the operating room is safe on the ward at night, because sedation, recumbency, and opioids combine to produce late respiratory events in the obstructive sleep apnea population.

### Address Comorbidities and Lifestyle as Part of the Treatment Plan

Obstructive sleep apnea is both a cause and a consequence of cardiometabolic disease, and effective care addresses the whole patient, not just the airway. Weight loss in overweight patients can substantially reduce severity and, in some, resolve the disorder, so refer for structured weight management, including pharmacotherapy or bariatric surgery where appropriate. Optimize comorbid hypertension, atrial fibrillation, heart failure, and glycemic control, because untreated apnea worsens all of these and treating apnea improves their management. Counsel on the driving and occupational safety implications of untreated sleepiness, because the medicolegal and personal consequences of a fatigue-related crash are severe. Avoid alcohol and sedatives at night, which worsen upper-airway collapse, and address nasal obstruction where it contributes.

### Escalate to Sleep Medicine for Complex, Refractory, or Atypical Cases

Refer to sleep specialists when the diagnosis is uncertain after testing, when central or mixed disease is suspected, when positive airway pressure is poorly tolerated despite optimization, when symptoms persist despite apparently adequate therapy, or when alternative therapies such as oral appliances, hypoglossal nerve stimulation, or surgery are being considered. Refer urgently when sleepiness is severe enough to endanger the patient or others through driving or safety-critical work, and impose clear driving restrictions in the interim. Involve sleep medicine early for patients with significant cardiopulmonary comorbidity, neuromuscular disease, or complex sleep symptomatology that extends beyond simple obstructive disease.

## Common Traps

### Relying on a Single Screening Score to Rule In or Rule Out Disease

Withholding investigation because STOP-BANG is borderline, or diagnosing based on the score alone. Counter by using scores to decide on testing and by investigating any patient with a compelling clinical picture regardless of score.

### Accepting a Negative Home Study in a High-Suspicion Patient

Dismissing suspected sleep apnea because a home test was negative, when home testing underestimates severity. Counter by escalating to in-laboratory polysomnography when clinical suspicion remains high.

### Declaring Positive Airway Pressure Failed Before Optimizing Interface and Pressure

Abandoning therapy after early side effects without addressing mask fit, humidification, pressure-relief, or follow-up data review. Counter by treating the first months as an active problem-solving period and exhausting interface and pressure optimization before considering alternatives.

### Treating Central Apnea as Obstructive

Escalating continuous positive airway pressure in a patient with central or treatment-emergent disease, which can worsen events. Counter by reviewing event types and treatment response and referring for adaptive servo-ventilation assessment when central disease is present.

### Underestimating Perioperative Risk

Sending a high-risk patient to surgery without screening, device planning, opioid minimization, or postoperative monitoring. Counter by screening every preoperative patient and planning anesthesia and monitoring around sleep apnea risk.

### Treating the Airway in Isolation From Comorbidities

Prescribing positive airway pressure without addressing weight, cardiometabolic disease, alcohol and sedative use, and safety. Counter by integrating lifestyle, comorbidity, and safety management into every treatment plan.

## Self-Check

- Did I use validated screening tools to decide who needs a sleep study, while investigating any patient with a compelling clinical picture regardless of score?
- Did I interpret the sleep study in clinical context, reviewing oxygen desaturation and event types rather than relying on the apnea-hypopnea index alone, and escalating to in-laboratory testing when home testing is negative but suspicion is high?
- Did I initiate positive airway pressure with interface selection, proactive side-effect management, and early data-driven follow-up to support adherence?
- Did I distinguish obstructive from central or treatment-emergent disease and refer for adaptive servo-ventilation assessment when appropriate?
- Did I screen and plan for perioperative risk, including device availability, opioid minimization, and postoperative monitoring?
- Did I address weight, comorbid cardiometabolic disease, substance use, and driving and occupational safety as part of the treatment plan?
- Did I escalate to sleep medicine for uncertain diagnosis, refractory symptoms, poor therapy tolerance, and consideration of alternative therapies?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
