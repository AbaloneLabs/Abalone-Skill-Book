# Insomnia and Circadian Rhythm Disorders

Insomnia is one of the most common complaints in medicine and also one of the most poorly managed, because the easy path, prescribing a hypnotic, is often the wrong one. Chronic insomnia is not a benzodiazepine deficiency; it is a self-perpetuating disorder of hyperarousal, conditioned arousal, and dysregulated sleep-wake scheduling that responds best to behavioral reconditioning, not to nightly sedation. The same is true for the circadian rhythm disorders, where the problem is a misalignment between the patient's internal clock and the sleep timing they need, and the treatment is timed light, melatonin, and schedule engineering rather than sedatives. The clinician's failures in this area are predictable: reaching for a hypnotic before exploring the behavioral and cognitive drivers, underestimating the risks of sedatives especially in older adults, treating insomnia as primary when it is secondary to depression, sleep apnea, or substance use, dismissing shift-work and jet-lag complaints as unsolvable, and failing to deprescribe hypnotics once dependence has developed. The clinician's discipline is to treat cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia as first-line, to use hypnotics sparingly and with clear exit strategies, to recognize and address the circadian disorders, and to know when insomnia or circadian disruption signals a deeper psychiatric, medical, or sleep-disordered cause that requires escalation. This skill covers cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia as first-line, the limits of sleep hygiene, hypnotic risks and deprescribing, circadian disorder management, and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia as First-Line, Not a Last Resort

Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia is the recommended first-line treatment for chronic insomnia because it targets the maintaining mechanisms directly and produces durable benefit without the risks of medication. Its core components are stimulus control, which re-associates the bed with sleep by restricting bed use to sleep and intimacy and by moving out of bed when unable to sleep; sleep restriction, which consolidates fragmented sleep by limiting time in bed to actual sleep time and then expanding it as efficiency improves; cognitive restructuring, which addresses the catastrophic and performative thinking that fuels sleep anxiety; and sleep hygiene, which addresses the basic environmental and behavioral conditions for sleep. Recommend and facilitate a structured course, whether through a trained therapist, a validated digital program, or a guided self-help resource, because the benefit depends on adherence to the full protocol over weeks rather than isolated tips. Set the expectation that sleep often worsens transiently during stimulus control and sleep restriction before it improves, because this is the active mechanism, and abandoning the protocol at that point forfeits the benefit.

### Understand the Limits of Sleep Hygiene and Avoid Stopping There

Sleep hygiene advice, avoiding caffeine late, limiting alcohol, keeping a cool dark room, exercising regularly, and maintaining a regular schedule, is necessary but rarely sufficient for chronic insomnia. Patients with chronic insomnia have usually already tried these measures, and offering hygiene alone communicates that the clinician has not grasped the problem. The maintaining drivers in chronic insomnia are conditioned arousal, in which the bed itself becomes a cue for wakefulness and anxiety, and hyperarousal, in which the nervous system is chronically activated; hygiene does not address either. Use hygiene as a foundation but layer stimulus control, sleep restriction, and cognitive work on top of it. Reserve hygiene-only advice for acute or mild situational insomnia where no conditioned arousal pattern has yet developed.

### Use Hypnotics Sparingly, With a Clear Indication and a Clear Exit Strategy

Pharmacotherapy has a limited but real role, and the discipline is to use it deliberately rather than reflexively. Short-term use, typically two to four weeks, can be appropriate for acute insomnia, situational stress, or as an adjunct while cognitive behavioral therapy takes effect, but it should be prescribed with a planned stop date and a documented exit strategy. Prefer agents with favorable safety profiles, use the lowest effective dose, and avoid escalating over time. Be explicit with the patient that hypnotics are a short-term bridge, not a long-term solution, because the expectation set at initiation strongly shapes long-term use. Avoid benzodiazepines and benzodiazepine-receptor agonists in older adults whenever possible, because the risks, detailed below, generally outweigh the benefits in this group. Never initiate chronic nightly hypnotic therapy for insomnia without a structured review plan, because open-ended prescribing is how dependence develops.

### Recognize and Mitigate Hypnotic Risks, Especially in Older Adults

The risks of sedative-hypnotics are substantial and rise sharply with age. Falls and fractures, cognitive impairment and delirium, motor vehicle accidents, daytime sedation and rebound insomnia, tolerance and dependence, and, in observational data, increased mortality and dementia risk are all associated with chronic benzodiazepine and benzodiazepine-receptor agonist use, particularly in older adults. Apply a Beers-type lens to prescribing in this group: if a sedative is being considered, justify it explicitly against these risks, prefer non-pharmacological therapy, and if a drug is truly necessary, choose the lowest-risk agent at the lowest dose for the shortest time. Screen every older patient for current hypnotic use at every encounter, because these prescriptions accumulate silently over years and are a leading contributor to preventable harm in geriatric care.

### Deprescribe Established Hypnotics Actively and Gradually

Long-term hypnotic users are common in primary care, and deprescribing is one of the highest-value interventions available, but it must be done deliberately because abrupt cessation risks withdrawal, seizures with high-dose benzodiazepines, and rebound insomnia that reinforces dependence. Use a structured taper, reducing the dose gradually over weeks to months, combined with cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia to address the underlying sleep disorder and to support adherence through the taper. Engage the patient in shared decision-making, explain that sleep may transiently worsen, and schedule close follow-up during the taper. Address the psychological dependence directly, because many long-term users fear they cannot sleep without the drug even when the drug is no longer providing real benefit. Do not abandon long-term users to indefinite prescribing out of therapeutic nihilism; a planned, supported taper succeeds far more often than is assumed.

### Manage Circadian Rhythm Disorders With Timed Light, Melatonin, and Schedule Engineering

Circadian disorders are disorders of timing, not of sleep quality, and they require timing-based treatment. In delayed sleep-wake phase disorder, common in adolescents, the internal clock runs late; treatment uses morning bright light and low-dose melatonin in the early evening to advance the rhythm. In advanced sleep-wake phase disorder, more common in older adults, the rhythm runs early and may need evening bright light to delay it. In shift-work disorder, the treatment is structured napping before night shifts, bright light during the night shift, light-blocking glasses and morning darkness after the shift, and melatonin before daytime sleep. In jet lag, treatment uses timed light exposure and melatonin aligned to the destination time zone, with eastward and westward travel requiring opposite strategies. The key principle is that light, darkness, and melatonin shift the clock only when timed correctly relative to the body's current phase; mistimed interventions can shift the clock the wrong way or do nothing. Counsel patients on the specific timing rather than offering generic advice, because a light box used at the wrong time is useless or harmful.

### Identify and Address Secondary Causes Before Treating Insomnia as Primary

Insomnia is frequently secondary to another condition, and treating it as primary while ignoring the cause dooms the therapy. Screen systematically for depression and anxiety, which present with early-morning waking and rumination; for obstructive sleep apnea, which presents with fragmented unrefreshing sleep rather than classic snoring in many patients; for restless legs syndrome and periodic limb movements; for chronic pain, nocturia, dyspnea, and reflux; for medication and substance effects, including caffeine, alcohol, stimulants, and withdrawal states; and for the sleep disruption of major medical illness. Treating the underlying condition, or addressing the offending substance, often resolves the insomnia without any sleep-specific therapy. When insomnia persists after the secondary cause is treated, layer cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia on top.

### Escalate to Sleep Medicine or Psychiatry for Refractory or Complex Cases

Refer to sleep medicine when insomnia is refractory to a structured behavioral and pharmacological approach, when a circadian disorder is severe or atypical, when a parasomnia or hypersomnia is suspected alongside the insomnia, or when specialist-delivered cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia is needed and unavailable locally. Refer to psychiatry or mental health services when insomnia is embedded in significant depression, anxiety, trauma, or substance use disorder, because the sleep complaint will not resolve until the psychiatric condition is addressed. Escalate urgently when insomnia is accompanied by suicidal ideation, severe functional impairment, or signs of a serious underlying disorder, because insomnia can be both a marker and a driver of acute psychiatric risk.

## Common Traps

### Reaching for a Hypnotic Before Behavioral Therapy

Prescribing a sedative at the first visit without exploring drivers or offering cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia. Counter by treating behavioral therapy as first-line and reserving medication for clear short-term indications with an exit strategy.

### Stopping at Sleep Hygiene for Chronic Insomnia

Offering caffeine and screen-time advice to a patient with conditioned arousal and hyperarousal. Counter by layering stimulus control, sleep restriction, and cognitive work on top of basic hygiene.

### Initiating Chronic Nightly Hypnotics Without a Review Plan

Open-ended prescribing that converts acute insomnia into long-term dependence. Counter by setting a planned stop date at initiation and scheduling structured review.

### Prescribing Sedatives to Older Adults Without Justifying the Risk

Adding a benzodiazepine or benzodiazepine-receptor agonist to an older patient's regimen without weighing falls, cognition, and delirium. Counter by applying a Beers-type risk lens and preferring non-pharmacological therapy.

### Leaving Long-Term Users on Hypnotics Indefinitely

Accepting chronic prescribing out of therapeutic nihilism rather than attempting a supported taper. Counter by initiating structured, gradual deprescribing combined with behavioral therapy and close follow-up.

### Treating Insomnia as Primary While Missing Secondary Causes

Pursuing sleep therapy while ignoring depression, sleep apnea, restless legs, pain, or substance effects. Counter by screening systematically for secondary causes and addressing them first.

### Giving Generic Circadian Advice Without Specific Timing

Advising light and melatonin without aligning timing to the patient's current circadian phase. Counter by prescribing the specific timing for light, darkness, and melatonin based on the disorder and travel direction.

## Self-Check

- Did I recommend and facilitate cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia as first-line, rather than defaulting to medication?
- Did I layer stimulus control, sleep restriction, and cognitive work on top of basic sleep hygiene rather than stopping at hygiene alone?
- If I used pharmacotherapy, did I choose a low-risk agent at the lowest dose for a defined short period with a documented exit strategy?
- Did I screen for and mitigate hypnotic risks, particularly in older adults, applying a Beers-type lens before prescribing?
- Did I identify long-term hypnotic users and offer a structured, gradual deprescribing plan with behavioral support?
- Did I recognize and manage circadian disorders with correctly timed light, melatonin, and schedule engineering rather than generic advice?
- Did I screen for and address secondary causes such as depression, sleep apnea, restless legs, pain, and substance use before treating insomnia as primary?
- Did I escalate to sleep medicine or psychiatry for refractory insomnia, severe circadian disorders, or insomnia embedded in significant psychiatric illness?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
