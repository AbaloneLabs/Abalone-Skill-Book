# Hypersomnia and Parasomnia Evaluation

Excessive daytime sleepiness and abnormal nocturnal behaviors are among the most diagnostically rich and most frequently mishandled problems in sleep medicine, because they are easy to dismiss and easy to misattribute. The tired patient is labeled lazy, depressed, or sleep-deprived; the patient who acts out dreams is told it is stress; the patient with nocturnal wandering is assured it is benign sleepwalking. In reality, these symptoms can be the presenting features of narcolepsy, idiopathic hypersomnia, REM behavior disorder as a prodrome to neurodegenerative disease, nocturnal frontal lobe epilepsy masquerading as a parasomnia, or restless legs syndrome with severe sleep fragmentation. The cost of error is measured in years of untreated sleepiness, injury from dream enactment, missed synucleinopathy at the stage where neuroprotective trials matter, and seizures misdiagnosed as behavioral events. The clinician's discipline is to quantify sleepiness objectively rather than accept vague descriptors, to distinguish sleepiness from fatigue, to perform a structured evaluation that includes polysomnography followed by multiple sleep latency testing where indicated, to recognize the parasomnias and their red flags, to manage restless legs rationally, and to escalate to sleep or neurology for confirmation and long-term management. This skill covers narcolepsy and idiopathic hypersomnia workup, REM behavior disorder as prodromal neurodegeneration, parasomnia safety and differentiation from epilepsy, restless legs management, and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Sleepiness From Fatigue and Quantify It Objectively

The first and most common error is conflating sleepiness, the propensity to fall asleep, with fatigue, the sense of exhaustion or low energy without the ability to nap. They have different causes and different evaluations. Sleepiness points toward insufficient sleep, sleep apnea, narcolepsy, idiopathic hypersomnia, or medication effects; fatigue points toward depression, chronic illness, anemia, thyroid disease, or post-viral states. Ask explicitly whether the patient dozes off in passive situations, such as reading, watching television, as a passenger, or in meetings, and quantify with the Epworth Sleepiness Scale as a baseline and tracking tool. Accept that patients often underestimate their sleepiness, so corroborate with a bed partner or family member whenever possible, and treat a mismatch between the patient's self-report and observed behavior as a red flag warranting objective testing. Do not attribute sleepiness to depression or laziness without excluding primary sleep disorders first.

### Evaluate Suspected Narcolepsy and Idiopathic Hypersomnia With the Correct Sequenced Testing

Narcolepsy and idiopathic hypersomnia are central disorders of hypersomnolence whose diagnosis depends on objective testing, not on history alone. The evaluation begins with a thorough history and a sleep diary or actigraphy to exclude chronic sleep deprivation, followed by in-laboratory polysomnography to exclude other causes of sleepiness such as sleep apnea and to record sleep architecture, and then, on the following day, the multiple sleep latency test, which measures how quickly the patient falls asleep during structured naps and whether sleep-onset rapid eye movement periods occur. A mean sleep latency of eight minutes or fewer with two or more sleep-onset REM periods supports narcolepsy type 2; narcolepsy type 1 additionally features cataplexy and low cerebrospinal fluid hypocretin. Idiopathic hypersomnia is characterized by hypersomnolence, long unrefreshing naps, prolonged sleep, and severe sleep inertia without the REM features of narcolepsy. Ensure the polysomnography demonstrates adequate total sleep time the night before the latency test, because insufficient prior sleep invalidates the result, and ensure stimulating medications have been washed out appropriately, because they suppress sleep-onset REM and produce false negatives. Misordered or misinterpreted testing is a leading cause of delayed diagnosis in this population.

### Recognize REM Behavior Disorder as a Prodromal Neurodegenerative Signal

REM behavior disorder, the loss of normal skeletal-muscle atonia during REM sleep leading to dream enactment, is clinically distinctive and neurologically important. Patients, typically older men, describe acting out vivid and often violent dreams, with shouting, punching, kicking, or flailing that can injure themselves or their bed partner, and they usually recall the dream content. Polysomnography confirms the loss of REM atonia. The significance is that the majority of patients with idiopathic REM behavior disorder will eventually develop a synucleinopathy, most commonly Parkinson disease, dementia with Lewy bodies, or multiple system atrophy, often years to decades later. This makes REM behavior disorder both a safety problem requiring dream-enactment suppression and a window for neuroprotective and early-disease surveillance. Refer every confirmed or suspected case to neurology or sleep medicine for long-term monitoring, counsel the patient and family honestly about the neurodegenerative risk without inducing paralysis, and consider enrollment in prodromal research where available. Do not dismiss dream enactment as a curiosity or a stress response in an older adult.

### Differentiate Non-REM Parasomnias From Nocturnal Epilepsy Using Red Flags

Non-REM parasomnias, such as sleepwalking, sleep terrors, and confusional arousals, arise from deep sleep and are common, particularly in children, but they can be difficult to distinguish from nocturnal frontal lobe epilepsy and other seizure disorders. The features that favor epilepsy include stereotyped episodes that are identical each time, brief duration, occurrence from any sleep stage, abnormal posturing or dystonia, tongue biting, incontinence, postictal confusion, and a family history of epilepsy. The features that favor parasomnia include variable semiology, longer duration, eyes open with glazed appearance, occurrence in the first third of the night during deep sleep, childhood onset, and triggering by sleep deprivation, fever, or alcohol. When the distinction is unclear, particularly in adults with new-onset nocturnal events, refer for video-polysomnography or prolonged electroencephalographic monitoring, because misdiagnosing epilepsy as parasomnia leaves seizures untreated and misdiagnosing parasomnia as epilepsy exposes the patient to unnecessary antiseizure medication. Safety, detailed below, is paramount for both.

### Make Parasomnia Safety the Immediate Priority

Whatever the eventual diagnosis, nocturnal events that involve wandering, agitation, or motor activity carry immediate injury risk, and safety must be addressed at the first encounter before the workup is complete. Counsel on environmental safety: securing windows and doors, removing firearms and sharp objects from the bedroom, placing the mattress on the floor or padding furniture, using stair gates, and protecting the bed partner. For REM behavior disorder, melatonin and clonazepam are effective in suppressing dream enactment and reducing injury and should be initiated under specialist guidance. For non-REM parasomnias, ensure adequate total sleep time, because sleep deprivation is the leading trigger, and address febrile illness, alcohol, and sedative triggers. Document the safety advice and the injury risk, because these events have medicolegal and driving implications in addition to their clinical importance.

### Manage Restless Legs Syndrome Rationally, Including Iron Status

Restless legs syndrome is common, underdiagnosed, and highly treatable, but it is also frequently mismanaged. The diagnosis is clinical, based on the urge to move the legs, worse at rest and in the evening, relieved by movement, and accompanied by an unpleasant sensation. Always check iron status with a ferritin level, because low or low-normal brain iron is a key driver, and iron replacement when ferritin is below threshold can substantially improve symptoms without the need for dopaminergic drugs. Avoid dopamine agonists as first-line for moderate-to-severe chronic disease, because they carry a high risk of augmentation, the worsening and spread of symptoms over time that is one of the most troublesome complications of restless legs treatment; prefer alpha-2-delta ligands such as gabapentinoids for most patients. Screen for and address exacerbating factors including sleep deprivation, caffeine, alcohol, antidepressants particularly selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, and underlying renal disease or neuropathy. Refer to sleep or neurology when symptoms are refractory, when augmentation has developed on dopaminergic therapy, or when the diagnosis is uncertain.

### Escalate to Sleep Medicine or Neurology for Confirmation and Long-Term Management

Refer to sleep medicine for confirmation of central hypersomnia disorders through polysomnography and multiple sleep latency testing, for management of refractory hypersomnia requiring stimulant and wake-promoting therapy, and for complex parasomnia evaluation including video-polysomnography. Refer to neurology for any suspected REM behavior disorder given its neurodegenerative implications, for nocturnal events where epilepsy is in the differential, and for refractory or augmented restless legs syndrome. Escalate urgently when sleepiness endangers the patient or others through driving or safety-critical work, impose clear driving restrictions in the interim, and arrange rapid specialist assessment for severe, recent-onset, or progressive symptoms.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Sleepiness With Fatigue and Misattributing the Cause

Labeling sleepiness as depression or laziness while missing narcolepsy, sleep apnea, or insufficient sleep. Counter by distinguishing sleepiness from fatigue through targeted questioning and objective scales, and excluding primary sleep disorders before psychiatric attribution.

### Invalidating Multiple Sleep Latency Testing Through Poor Preparation

Performing the test without adequate prior-night sleep, without excluding sleep apnea first, or without washing out REM-suppressing medications, producing false negatives. Counter by ensuring adequate polysomnography sleep time, treating apnea first, and reviewing medications before testing.

### Dismissing REM Behavior Disorder as Stress or Vivid Dreaming

Failing to recognize dream enactment in an older adult as a synucleinopathy prodrome. Counter by referring every suspected case for polysomnographic confirmation and neurologic surveillance.

### Misdiagnosing Nocturnal Epilepsy as Parasomnia, or Vice Versa

Treating seizures as sleepwalking, or parasomnia as epilepsy, based on history alone. Counter by using red-flag features to triage and by referring ambiguous cases for video-polysomnography or prolonged electroencephalographic monitoring.

### Neglecting Safety While Pursuing Diagnosis

Focusing on investigation while leaving the patient and bed partner at ongoing injury risk. Counter by addressing environmental safety and, for REM behavior disorder, initiating dream-enactment suppression at the first encounter.

### Using Dopamine Agonists First-Line for Restless Legs and Ignoring Iron

Reaching for dopaminergic drugs without checking ferritin, inviting augmentation. Counter by checking iron status first and preferring alpha-2-delta ligands for chronic moderate-to-severe disease.

### Failing to Restrict Driving in the Dangerously Sleepy Patient

Allowing a patient with severe untreated hypersomnia to continue driving while awaiting workup. Counter by imposing clear driving restrictions and arranging rapid specialist assessment.

## Self-Check

- Did I distinguish sleepiness from fatigue, quantify sleepiness with a validated scale, and corroborate the history with an informant?
- Did I evaluate suspected narcolepsy and idiopathic hypersomnia with polysomnography followed by multiple sleep latency testing, ensuring adequate prior sleep, exclusion of apnea, and medication washout?
- Did I recognize REM behavior disorder as a prodromal synucleinopathy signal and refer for polysomnographic confirmation and neurologic surveillance?
- Did I use red-flag features to differentiate non-REM parasomnias from nocturnal epilepsy, and refer ambiguous cases for video-polysomnography or electroencephalographic monitoring?
- Did I address parasomnia safety, including environmental modification and dream-enactment suppression, at the first encounter?
- Did I check ferritin and prefer non-dopaminergic therapy for restless legs, screening for exacerbating factors and referring for refractory or augmented disease?
- Did I escalate to sleep medicine or neurology for confirmation and long-term management, and did I impose driving restrictions for the dangerously sleepy patient?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
