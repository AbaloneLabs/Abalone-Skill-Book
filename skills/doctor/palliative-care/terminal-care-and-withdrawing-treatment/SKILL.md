# Terminal Care and Withdrawing Life-Sustaining Treatment

Terminal care — the care of the patient who is expected to die within days — is one of the highest-stakes, most emotionally charged, and most ethically freighted areas of clinical practice. Errors here are irreversible and are remembered by families for the rest of their lives. The recurring failures are recognizable: failing to recognize that the patient is dying and continuing aggressive investigation and treatment; treating withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment as euthanasia or as giving up; under-treating terminal symptoms from misplaced fear of hastening death; and leaving families uninformed, unprepared, and unsupported. This skill covers recognizing the dying patient, the ethics and process of withdrawing and withholding life-sustaining treatment, symptom control in the last days, family support, documentation, and the recognition of when complexity or conflict requires escalation to specialist palliative care or an ethics committee.

## Core Rules

### Recognize the Dying Patient and Communicate It Explicitly

The first and most consequential step is recognizing that the patient is dying. The signs are well-described: progressive weakness, reduced oral intake, increasing drowsiness, reduced consciousness, peripheral cooling and mottling, noisy secretions, Cheyne-Stokes breathing, and loss of interest in surroundings. Diagnosing dying is a clinical judgment based on trajectory and the totality of signs, and it should be made and communicated explicitly to the team, the patient where conscious, and the family. The most common error is not making the diagnosis at all — continuing to investigate, treat, and escalate while the patient is actively dying. Once dying is recognized, the focus of care shifts: non-essential medications are stopped, investigations are discontinued, and the plan becomes comfort, dignity, and family support.

### Distinguish Withholding, Withdrawing, and Euthanasia

Withholding and withdrawing life-sustaining treatment are ethically equivalent — both are decisions not to initiate or continue a treatment whose burden exceeds its expected benefit — and both are standard, lawful, good medical practice when aligned with the patient's goals and best interests. They are not euthanasia or physician-assisted suicide, which involve an act or substance intended to cause death. The ethical distinction is critical and must be explained to staff, families, and the patient where conscious: stopping a treatment that is not benefiting the patient allows the underlying disease to take its natural course; it does not cause death. Failing to make this distinction causes moral distress in staff, refusal by families, and inappropriate continuation of burdensome treatment.

### Ground Decisions in the Patient's Values and Best Interests

Decisions to limit, withdraw, or withhold treatment must be grounded in the patient's known wishes, advance directives, and, where the patient lacks capacity, the substituted judgment of a legally authorized decision-maker. Where prior wishes are unknown, decide on a best-interests standard weighing the likely benefit, the burden, the reversibility, and the patient's values as far as they can be known. Document the basis for every decision, the people consulted, and the reasoning. Never make unilateral "futility" declarations without a structured process; conflict about futility should trigger ethics consultation rather than clinician fiat. Capacity is decision-specific and may fluctuate — reassess at each major decision and involve the patient to the maximum extent possible.

### Follow a Structured Process for Withdrawing Treatment

When a decision is made to withdraw life-sustaining treatment (mechanical ventilation, dialysis, vasopressors, artificial nutrition and hydration, antibiotics), follow a structured process: confirm the decision and its basis, communicate with the family and team, plan the timing and setting, anticipate and pre-prescribe symptom relief, withdraw in a manner that minimizes distress, and remain present. Pre-prescribe opioids and benzodiazepines for breathlessness, pain, and agitation, and antimuscarinics for secretions, so that symptoms are anticipated rather than chased. The family should be invited to be present and prepared for what they will see. After withdrawal, do not abandon the patient — remain available, observe, and treat symptoms as they arise.

### Control Symptoms in the Last Days Proactively

The actively dying patient develops a predictable set of symptoms: pain, breathlessness, agitation and restlessness, terminal secretions ("death rattle"), nausea, and sometimes seizures or myoclonus. Manage them proactively using the least invasive effective route (subcutaneous is preferred when oral is not possible): opioids for pain and breathlessness, midazolam or other benzodiazepines for agitation and anxiety, antimuscarinics (hyoscine, glycopyrronium) for secretions, antiemetics and sometimes levomepromazine for nausea and broad terminal sedation. Titrate to effect; the principle is symptom relief, not the absence of consciousness. Continue regular baseline analgesia for patients already on opioids, converting to an equivalent subcutaneous regimen. Avoid suddenly stopping drugs that prevent symptoms (analgesics, anticonvulsants, anxiolytics) — withdrawal of these can itself cause suffering.

### Address the "Double Effect" Honestly and Without Misuse

The doctrine of double effect — that treatment given with the intent of relieving symptoms may be given even if it risks hastening death — is sometimes invoked defensively and sometimes misused. The correct principle is simpler: treatments given to relieve symptoms in the dying patient, titrated to effect, are appropriate and lawful; the intent is symptom relief, and any life-shortening effect is a foreseen but unintended consequence. Document the indication, the symptom being treated, and the titration. Do not use the double effect to justify disproportionate dosing for the purpose of hastening death, which crosses into euthanasia; equally, do not under-treat symptoms from misplaced fear of causing death, which is itself a harm.

### Support the Family Before, During, and After Death

The family is also the patient in terminal care. Prepare them for what dying looks like, explain the changes they will observe, invite their presence and participation as they wish, and provide clear, honest, compassionate communication throughout. Acknowledge uncertainty about timing. After death, offer condolences, explain what happens next, support bereavement, and complete documentation (death certificate, notification of relevant authorities). Poor communication and lack of preparation are the most common drivers of complaints and complicated bereavement. Cultural and religious practices around death should be respected and accommodated wherever possible.

### Document Thoroughly and Defensibly

Terminal care decisions are scrutinized and must be documented contemporaneously: the recognition of dying, the basis for treatment limitation or withdrawal, the people consulted, the goals-of-care discussion, the symptom management plan and its titration, and the family communication. Good documentation protects the patient, the family, the clinicians, and the institution, and it is the single best defense against later complaint. Vague or absent documentation is a recurring and avoidable failure.

### Escalate to Specialist Palliative Care and Ethics Appropriately

Specialist palliative care should be involved for complex symptom control, difficult goals-of-care conflict, family disagreement, or when the team is uncertain. Ethics committee consultation is indicated for conflict about futility, disagreement among decision-makers, or perceived conflict between institutional obligations and patient/family wishes. Do not delay escalation when conflict is escalating or symptoms are uncontrolled.

## Common Traps

### Failing to Recognize That the Patient Is Dying

Continuing aggressive investigation, treatment, and escalation in a patient who is actively dying causes suffering, wastes resources, and denies the patient and family a good death. Counter by diagnosing dying explicitly and shifting the focus of care.

### Equating Withdrawal with Euthanasia or Giving Up

Staff, families, and sometimes clinicians conflate withdrawal of futile treatment with causing death or abandoning the patient. Counter by explaining the ethical equivalence of withholding and withdrawing and the distinction from euthanasia, and by emphasizing continued presence and care.

### Under-Treating Symptoms from Fear of Hastening Death

Misplaced fear of opioids and sedatives leads to untreated pain, breathlessness, and agitation in the dying. Counter by pre-prescribing symptom relief, titrating to effect, and documenting indication and intent.

### Making Unilateral Futility Decisions

Declaring treatment "futile" without a structured process and without engaging the family generates conflict and ethical harm. Counter by grounding decisions in patient values and best interests, documenting reasoning, and triggering ethics consultation for conflict.

### Stopping Symptom-Preventing Drugs at the End of Life

Suddenly stopping analgesics, anticonvulsants, or anxiolytics in the dying patient causes withdrawal suffering. Counter by continuing and converting these to appropriate routes.

### Poor Family Communication and Preparation

Failing to prepare families for dying, withdrawal, and death drives complaints, complicated bereavement, and requests for inappropriate escalation. Counter by honest, timely, compassionate communication throughout and after.

### Inadequate Documentation

Vague or absent documentation of decisions, reasoning, and symptom management exposes patients, families, and clinicians to harm. Counter by contemporaneous, thorough documentation of every decision and its basis.

## Self-Check

- Have I explicitly recognized and communicated that the patient is dying, and shifted the focus of care accordingly?
- Have I grounded decisions to limit, withdraw, or withhold treatment in the patient's wishes, advance directives, or best interests, and documented the basis?
- Have I distinguished withdrawal and withholding from euthanasia and explained this to staff and family?
- Have I followed a structured withdrawal process with pre-prescribed symptom relief and family preparation?
- Have I proactively managed the predictable terminal symptoms (pain, breathlessness, agitation, secretions, nausea) using appropriate routes and titration?
- Have I supported the family before, during, and after death, and respected cultural and religious practices?
- Have I documented decisions, reasoning, consultations, and symptom management contemporaneously and defensibly?
- Have I escalated to specialist palliative care or ethics consultation for refractory symptoms, conflict, or uncertainty?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
