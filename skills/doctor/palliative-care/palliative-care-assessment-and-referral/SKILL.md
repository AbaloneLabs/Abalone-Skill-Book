# Palliative Care Assessment and Referral

Palliative care is widely misunderstood as the care that begins when disease-directed treatment ends. In reality, it is an approach focused on quality of life, symptom relief, and alignment of treatment with patient values, and it is most effective when introduced early alongside active disease management. The recurring failure is one of timing and framing: clinicians wait for a "palliative phase" that never announces itself, miss the predictable trajectories of non-cancer life-limiting illness, or trigger specialist referral only at the very end of life when its value is reduced. This skill covers how to recognize palliative needs, when to integrate a palliative approach, when to escalate to specialist palliative care, and how to apply the same logic to non-malignant disease where the dying trajectory is far less obvious.

## Core Rules

### Treat Palliative Care as Concurrent, Not Sequential

Palliative care is not an alternative to disease-directed therapy; it runs in parallel with it. The evidence for early integration, particularly in advanced cancer, COPD, and heart failure, shows improved symptom control, better quality of life, reduced aggressive treatment near death, and in some populations survival benefit. The practical rule is to introduce a palliative approach at the point of diagnosis of any advanced, life-limiting, or symptomatic illness — not at the point of treatment failure. Asking "is this patient palliative?" is the wrong question; the right question is "what are this patient's palliative needs, and who should meet them?" Disease-directed and palliative goals are then triaged together at every decision point.

### Identify Life-Limiting Illness Beyond Cancer

Cancer is the prototype, but most palliative need now lies in non-cancer life-limiting illness: advanced heart failure, end-stage COPD, end-stage renal disease on or declining dialysis, decompensated cirrhosis, advanced dementia, motor neuron disease, Parkinson's disease, and progressive neurological conditions. These illnesses have less predictable trajectories than cancer — the organ-failure trajectory is one of gradual decline punctuated by acute exacerbations, and the frailty/dementia trajectory is one of slow functional loss. Use the "surprise question" ("would I be surprised if this patient died in the next 12 months?") as a screening prompt, not a diagnostic rule, and combine it with disease-specific triggers (e.g., repeated heart-failure admissions, oxygen-dependent COPD with hypercapnia, recurrent falls and infections in advanced dementia, declining MND respiratory function). When the answer is no, or when disease-specific triggers are met, begin the palliative assessment.

### Perform a Structured Palliative Needs Assessment

Palliative need is multi-dimensional and must be assessed explicitly rather than inferred. Cover physical symptoms (pain, breathlessness, nausea, fatigue, anorexia, constipation, sleep), psychological distress (depression, anxiety, fear, demoralization), social and practical concerns (family burden, finances, caregiving capacity), spiritual or existential distress (meaning, loss, fear of dying), and information and decision-making preferences. Use validated tools where available (ESAS, IPOS, APCA POS) to make the assessment reproducible rather than impressionistic. The output is a problem list, not a label — a patient may have high palliative need while still receiving curative-intent disease therapy, and conversely a patient with a terminal diagnosis may have few unmet needs at a given moment.

### Clarify Prognosis Honestly and Share It Carefully

Patients and families consistently want honest prognostic information, yet clinicians frequently avoid it from fear of causing distress or destroying hope. Withholding prognosis is itself a harm: it prevents planning, blocks advance care planning, and leaves families unprepared for deterioration. Provide prognostic information in a way that is honest, calibrated to the patient's desire for detail, and paired with explicit commitment to non-abandonment and symptom control. Use ranges rather than precise predictions ("days to weeks," "weeks to months," "months to a year"), acknowledge uncertainty explicitly, and revisit as the trajectory evolves. Avoid the false hope of certainty in either direction; never promise recovery that cannot be delivered, and never deliver a blunt prognosis without a plan for ongoing care.

### Align Treatment with Documented Goals of Care

Palliative assessment must culminate in a goals-of-care conversation and, where appropriate, advance care planning and advance directives. Explore what matters most to the patient — longevity, function, being at home, avoiding specific interventions, being with family — and document the resulting priorities so they guide future decisions, including resuscitation status, hospital vs home care, escalation thresholds, and the use of life-sustaining treatments. Goals of care are not a one-time event; they are revisited at every change in trajectory. Where the patient lacks capacity, work with the legally authorized decision-maker using a substituted-judgment standard, and document the basis for decisions clearly.

### Recognize Triggers for Specialist Palliative Care Referral

Generalist palliative care is the responsibility of every clinician caring for life-limiting illness; specialist palliative care is for complex need. Refer to specialist palliative services when symptoms are refractory to first-line management (intractable pain, refractory breathlessness, complex nausea, terminal agitation), when goals-of-care decisions are conflicted or ethically difficult, when psychosocial or existential distress exceeds the team's capacity, when discharge planning is complicated by rapid decline, or when the family system is struggling. Referral is not a sign of failure; it is the correct escalation when complexity exceeds local capability. In many systems, early specialist referral for newly diagnosed metastatic cancer or advanced organ failure is now standard.

### Escalate According to Acuity and Trajectory

Recognize the deteriorating patient and escalate appropriately. Sudden deterioration, new uncontrolled symptoms, hyperacute delirium, suspected spinal cord compression, hypercalcemia, or major hemorrhage in advanced disease require urgent specialist input and often inpatient or hospice-level care. Do not delay escalation while waiting for certainty; in palliative care, the cost of late escalation is a bad death. When in doubt, contact specialist palliative care or the local hospice team directly.

## Common Traps

### Waiting for the "Palliative Phase"

The most common error is deferring palliative involvement until all disease-directed options are exhausted, by which point the patient is often too unwell, too close to death, or too distressed to benefit fully. Counter by introducing the palliative approach at diagnosis of advanced illness and re-evaluating at every transition.

### Applying Cancer Logic to Non-Cancer Disease

Non-cancer trajectories are less linear, and the absence of a clear "terminal phase" leads to under-recognition of palliative need in heart failure, COPD, dementia, and frailty. Counter by using disease-specific triggers and the surprise question rather than waiting for an unambiguous decline.

### Equating Palliative with Terminal or with "Giving Up"

Patients, families, and clinicians still conflate palliative care with end-of-life care and with withdrawal of active treatment, leading to refusal, delayed referral, and distress. Counter by framing palliative care explicitly as additive, concurrent, and focused on living well, and by correcting the misconception that referral means stopping treatment.

### Failing to Conduct a Structured Needs Assessment

Inferring palliative need from the diagnosis alone misses the dimensions that most affect quality of life — psychological, social, spiritual, and practical concerns. Counter by using a structured multi-dimensional assessment and a validated symptom tool at defined intervals.

### Withholding Prognosis to "Protect" the Patient

Avoiding prognostic conversation deprives patients of the chance to plan, say what needs saying, and avoid unwanted aggressive treatment. Counter by asking how much the patient wants to know, then providing honest, calibrated, hopeful-in-the-right-way information with a clear care plan.

### Referring Too Late to Specialist Palliative Care

Treating specialist referral as a last resort means complex symptoms, family conflict, and ethical dilemmas arrive at the service in crisis. Counter by referring early when complexity is foreseeable, not only when it has become unmanageable.

### Documenting Goals of Care as a One-Time Event

Advance directives completed once and never revisited often no longer reflect the patient's wishes as the illness progresses. Counter by revisiting goals at every transition, hospitalization, or functional change.

## Self-Check

- Have I introduced a palliative approach at diagnosis of advanced illness rather than waiting for a "palliative phase"?
- For non-cancer life-limiting illness, have I applied disease-specific triggers and the surprise question rather than waiting for obvious decline?
- Have I performed a structured multi-dimensional needs assessment (physical, psychological, social, spiritual, informational) using a validated tool?
- Have I offered honest, calibrated prognostic information matched to the patient's desired level of detail, paired with a clear care plan?
- Have I documented goals of care, advance directives, and escalation preferences, and scheduled a revisit at the next transition?
- Have I referred to specialist palliative care when symptoms, psychosocial complexity, or ethical difficulty exceed local capability, rather than as a last resort?
- Have I escalated urgently for acute deterioration, refractory symptoms, or oncologic/palliative emergencies without waiting for certainty?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
