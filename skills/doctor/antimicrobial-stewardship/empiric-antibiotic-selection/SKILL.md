# Empiric Antibiotic Selection

Empiric antibiotic therapy is prescribed before definitive microbiological data are available, and the decisions made in the first hours determine both patient survival and downstream resistance pressure. The central tension is that coverage must be broad enough to cover the probable pathogens within the time window that matters, yet narrow enough to avoid collateral ecological harm, toxicity, and selection of multidrug-resistant organisms. The recurring failure mode is not ignorance of drug names; it is defaulting to the broadest regimen out of discomfort with uncertainty, treating "sepsis" rather than a syndrome at a specific site, and then forgetting to narrow. This skill covers disciplined syndrome-based empiric selection, the sepsis hour-one bundle, host and allergy adjustments, and the explicit de-escalation plan that must accompany every empiric order.

## Core Rules

### Anchor the Regimen to a Syndrome and Site, Not to "Infection"

Empiric therapy should be chosen by suspected source, because the likely pathogens, the severity threshold, and the evidence-based regimens all differ by syndrome. State the working syndrome explicitly before selecting drugs: community-acquired pneumonia, healthcare-associated pneumonia, uncomplicated vs complicated urinary tract infection, intra-abdominal infection, skin and soft tissue infection, meningitis, neutropenic fever, or sepsis of unknown source. Each syndrome has guideline-endorsed regimens whose spectrum is calibrated to its typical flora. Treating "infection" generically and reaching for a catch-all broad regimen is how patients get unnecessary double anaerobic and Pseudomonas coverage for years. If the syndrome is genuinely unclear, say so, broaden within reason, and commit to revisiting within 24 to 48 hours once the source is identified.

### Cover the Probable Pathogens at the Right Severity Tier

Severity changes the required coverage. For sepsis or septic shock, the Surviving Sepsis hour-one bundle calls for immediate broad-spectrum therapy covering all likely pathogens, typically a high-dose antipseudomonal beta-lactam plus coverage for methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus when risk factors or hemodynamic instability are present, and consideration of an agent with activity against resistant gram-negatives or anaerobes depending on source. For non-severe community infections, narrower targeted therapy is usually adequate and is better stewardship. Match the regimen to the tier: low-severity community syndromes get narrow oral therapy; moderate inpatient infections get a single targeted IV agent; septic shock gets robust empiric combination therapy that is de-escalated as soon as data return. Document the severity reasoning so the team can later narrow confidently.

### Use the Local Antibiogram, Not National Averages

Resistance patterns are intensely local. A hospital where 25% of Pseudomonas is resistant to cefepide demands different empiric choices than one where it is 5%. Before finalizing empiric therapy, consult the institution's current antibiogram and, where relevant, the unit-specific data (ICU, oncology, transplant). Also weight patient-level risk factors for resistant organisms: prior resistant culture or colonization, healthcare exposure, recent antibiotics, admission from a long-term care facility, and known immunocompromise. When local resistance is high or the patient carries a resistant organism, escalate empirically and consult infectious diseases early rather than waiting for failure. National guidelines are a starting point; the local antibiogram is the actual decision tool.

### Adjust for Host Factors: Allergy, Renal Function, Weight, Pregnancy, Drug Interactions

Every empiric order must be filtered through the patient. Clarify the allergy history specifically: a reported "penicillin allergy" is often a childhood rash or non-immune symptom, and inappropriate beta-lactam avoidance drives use of inferior alternatives. When the reaction was non-severe, a beta-lactam with or without graded challenge may be appropriate; for true IgE-mediated anaphylaxis, choose an alternative and involve allergy or infectious diseases. Dose-adjust for renal and hepatic function using actual or adjusted body weight for weight-based agents, and remember that severe sepsis increases volume of distribution and may require higher loading doses. Check pregnancy status and interactions with warfarin, calcineurin inhibitors, and antiepileptics. A correctly chosen drug at the wrong dose fails.

### Plan De-escalation at the Moment of Prescribing

Empiric breadth is justified only by diagnostic uncertainty, and that justification expires when cultures and data return. Write an explicit de-escalation plan in the order: the antibiotics are empiric, cultures have been sent, and therapy will be narrowed or stopped based on day-two to day-three results. Set a review date. The single most powerful stewardship intervention is a forced antibiotic time-out at 48 to 72 hours asking four questions: Is there still evidence of infection? What is the source? Can the regimen be narrowed? Can the route be switched to oral? Empiric therapy without a planned stop or narrow is not empiric; it is indefinite by default.

### Choose the Right Route and Dose for the Site

Route matters pharmacodynamically. Meningitis requires agents that achieve cerebrospinal fluid levels; high-dose IV therapy is mandatory and oral agents are inadequate. Bacteremia, severe sepsis, neutropenic fever, and infections with poor oral absorption or compromised gut require IV therapy. For many other infections, oral therapy with good bioavailability is equivalent to IV once the patient can tolerate it. Dosing should reflect the site: higher doses or extended infusions for beta-lactams in critically ill patients, and awareness that standard doses under-treat deep-seated or high-inoculum infections.

### Know When to Consult Infectious Diseases

Empiric selection is a team task in several scenarios: septic shock with an unclear source, suspected resistant organisms outside your unit's usual patterns, complex immunocompromised hosts, severe beta-lactam allergy requiring desensitization, endocarditis, central nervous system infection, and any case where the empiric regimen is failing at 48 to 72 hours. Consult early rather than after multiple escalations; early ID involvement shortens duration, narrows spectrum, and improves outcomes. When escalating empirically for clinical deterioration, reassess the source and source control, not just the drug list.

## Common Traps

### Reaching for the Broadest Regimen by Default

Defaulting to maximum-spectrum combination therapy for any infection to "be safe" selects resistance, increases Clostridioides difficile risk, and exposes patients to toxicity. Counter by anchoring to syndrome and severity, and by committing to a de-escalation plan at the time of the order.

### Treating "Sepsis" Without Identifying a Source

Ordering broad antibiotics for sepsis but never localizing the infection delays source control, the intervention that most changes outcome. Counter by pursuing imaging and cultures aggressively in parallel and revisiting the source daily; if no source is found, question whether infection is the true process.

### Ignoring the Local Antibiogram

Choosing regimens from textbooks or memory while the local resistance rate makes that drug inadequate. Counter by checking the current institutional and unit antibiogram before finalizing empiric therapy, and escalating empirically when local rates are high.

### Accepting "Penicillin Allergy" Without Clarification

Taking a vague allergy label at face value and avoiding all beta-lactams, which leads to inferior therapy and resistance. Counter by characterizing the reaction specifically and involving allergy or ID when beta-lactams are the best option.

### Forgetting Renal or Weight Dose Adjustment

Prescribing standard doses in renal failure or under-dosing obese or septic patients with expanded volume of distribution. Counter by recalculating dosing for each patient and checking high-dose or loading requirements in severe infection.

### Never Revisiting the Empiric Regimen

Letting broad empiric therapy continue indefinitely because no one owned the de-escalation decision. Counter by scheduling an antibiotic time-out at 48 to 72 hours and writing the de-escalation plan in the original order.

### Delaying Infectious Diseases Consult in Complex Cases

Escalating empirically through multiple agents before calling ID in septic shock with unclear source, resistant organisms, or immunocompromise. Counter by consulting ID early when the case is complex or failing rather than after serial failures.

## Self-Check

- Have I named the suspected syndrome and source explicitly, rather than treating "infection" generically?
- Is the empiric spectrum matched to severity, with sepsis or septic shock receiving the hour-one bundle and non-severe infections receiving narrower therapy?
- Did I consult the current local and unit antibiogram and factor in patient-level risk factors for resistant organisms?
- Have I adjusted for allergy (with clarification or desensitization when appropriate), renal and hepatic function, weight, pregnancy, and drug interactions?
- Did I write an explicit de-escalation plan and schedule a 48-to-72-hour antibiotic time-out with the four standard questions?
- Is the route and dose appropriate for the site of infection, including high-dose IV therapy for meningitis and bacteremia?
- Have I consulted or arranged infectious diseases input for septic shock with unclear source, resistant organisms, immunocompromise, severe allergy, or failure at 48 to 72 hours?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
