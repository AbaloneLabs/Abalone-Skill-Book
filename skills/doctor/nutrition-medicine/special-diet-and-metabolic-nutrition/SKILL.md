# Special Diet and Metabolic Nutrition

Therapeutic diets are prescribed like drugs and carry the same obligations: right indication, right dose, right monitoring, and awareness of harm. The central judgment problems are threefold. First, clinicians hand out blanket dietary advice ("eat healthy," "cut carbs," "go gluten-free") without tailoring it to the disease, the patient's culture and means, or the evidence — and then are surprised when it fails or causes new problems. Second, micronutrient deficiency is under-worked: clinicians either dismiss vague symptoms or, conversely, prescribe supplements empirically without confirming the deficiency or chasing its cause. Third, supplements are widely assumed safe, yet they interact with medications, mask serious disease, and occasionally cause direct toxicity. This skill covers disease-specific diet prescription, the disciplined workup of micronutrient deficiency, supplement risk assessment, and the avoidance of restrictive-diet and fad-diet harm. When the dietary problem is complex, restrictive, or tied to a metabolic diagnosis, escalate to a registered dietitian and the relevant specialist.

## Core Rules

### Prescribe Disease-Specific Diets by Indication, Not by Slogan

A therapeutic diet is a prescription, so name the disease, the dietary mechanism, and the target. Diabetic nutrition is individualized carbohydrate management and cardiometabolic risk reduction — not "no sugar." Renal nutrition restricts sodium, phosphorus, potassium, and fluid according to the stage and labs, and is markedly different in dialysis versus CKD; never give a generic "renal diet" without the labs. Low-FODMAP is a staged intervention for irritable bowel syndrome — elimination, then structured reintroduction — not a lifetime ban, and it requires dietitian supervision to avoid nutritional inadequacy and microbiome disruption. Gluten-free is a strict, lifelong, medically necessary prescription for celiac disease and non-celiac gluten sensitivity, with a high cost of error; it is not a wellness choice. Low-sodium targets heart failure and hypertension and must specify a gram target, not "less salt." For each diet, state the indication, the specific restriction, the monitoring plan, and the expected endpoint; a diet without these is advice, not treatment.

### Work Up Micronutrient Deficiency Systematically, Never Empirically Treat and Walk Away

A suspected micronutrient deficiency demands a workup, not a reflex supplement. Take a directed history (intake, absorption, loss, risk factors such as bariatric surgery, vegan diet, malabsorption, alcohol, pregnancy, chronic disease) and confirm biochemically where possible: B12 with methylmalonic acid or homocysteine when borderline, iron with ferritin and transferrin saturation plus a search for the source of loss (GI, menstrual), vitamin D with 25-OH-D and attention to the high prevalence of insufficiency, folate, thiamine in alcohol use or hyperemesis. Critically, identify and treat the cause: iron deficiency in an adult male or postmenopausal woman mandates investigation for GI blood loss including malignancy until proven otherwise; B12 deficiency demands a search for pernicious anemia, malabsorption, or terminal ileal disease. Do not simply prescribe the vitamin and discharge the patient — you will mask the underlying disease and delay a cancer or autoimmune diagnosis. Replace according to severity and route (oral for mild, parenteral for severe, malabsorptive, or symptomatic deficiency), and recheck to confirm response.

### Evaluate Supplements for Risk, Interaction, and Evidence Before Endorsing

Supplements are not automatically safe. Before recommending or permitting a supplement, assess four things: is there a deficiency or indication (most supplements taken without indication confer no benefit and some harm), what is the evidence for the claimed effect, what are the interaction risks (vitamin K with warfarin, St. John's wort with numerous drugs including antidepressants and contraceptives, high-dose vitamin C interfering with some tests and worsening oxalate stones, calcium impairing certain antibiotic and thyroid medication absorption), and what is the toxicity profile (fat-soluble vitamins A and D, iron, selenium, and beta-carotene in smokers carry real toxicity). Recognize that supplement regulation is weaker than drug regulation — purity, dose, and contamination vary — so prefer pharmaceutical-grade products and credible sources. Document the supplement list as part of the medication reconciliation; patients rarely volunteer supplements unless asked, and interactions are common. When in doubt about an interaction, consult a pharmacist.

### Avoid Restrictive-Diet Harm and Decline to Endorse Fads

Restrictive diets, even medically indicated ones, carry risk: nutritional inadequacy, disordered eating, social isolation, microbiome disruption, and cost. Each restriction must be justified by diagnosis and periodically reassessed — do not let patients accumulate lifelong bans without re-evaluation. Be especially cautious with elimination diets in children, where over-restriction impairs growth. Actively decline to endorse fad diets presented as medical treatment — extreme restriction, "detox" regimens, unproven supplement stacks, and disease-cure claims — and explain the evidence and the harm. Where a patient is committed to a non-standard eating pattern (vegan, keto, intermittent fasting), do not dismiss it; assess it for adequacy, supplement what is missing (B12 in vegan diets, for example), and counsel on realistic expectations. The clinician's role is evidence-based guidance and harm reduction, not reflex endorsement or dismissal.

### Individualize to Culture, Means, Access, and Capability

A diet the patient cannot follow is not a treatment. Assess food culture, cooking ability, dentition, swallowing, income, food access, and family context before prescribing. A low-sodium, fresh-food diet is unattainable for a patient dependent on cheap processed food or without cooking facilities; a gluten-free prescription must account for cost and availability. Adapt the prescription to reality, prioritize the highest-yield changes, and refer to social or community resources where food security is the barrier. A dietitian is essential for translating disease-specific nutrition into an individualized, feasible plan.

### Reassess and Escalate to Specialist Dietitians

Therapeutic diets are not set-and-forget. Reassess adherence, tolerance, labs, and clinical endpoints at intervals; adjust the prescription as the disease changes (e.g., advancing CKD requiring tighter restrictions, or dialysis relaxing some). Escalate to a dietitian for any complex, restrictive, pediatric, pregnancy, or multi-disease diet, and to the relevant specialist when the underlying metabolic disease is unstable. Document the dietary prescription and the rationale in the record.

## Common Traps

### Prescribing Generic Dietary Slogans Instead of Disease-Specific Plans

"Eat healthy" or "cut carbs" is not a prescription. Counter by naming the indication, the specific restriction, the monitoring, and the endpoint for each diet.

### Treating and Walking Away from a Deficiency

Prescribing a supplement without investigating the cause masks serious disease. Counter by always searching for the source of deficiency (GI blood loss, malabsorption, pernicious anemia) before or alongside replacement.

### Assuming Supplements Are Harmless

Supplements interact with medications and can be toxic. Counter by evaluating indication, evidence, interactions, and toxicity before endorsing, and reconciling supplements as medications.

### Endorsing or Remaining Silent on Fad Diets

Silence reads as endorsement, and fad diets cause real harm. Counter by explicitly declining to endorse unproven regimens and offering evidence-based alternatives.

### Applying a One-Size-Fits-All Restriction Without Labs or Stage

A generic "renal diet" without potassium, phosphorus, and stage data is dangerous. Counter by tailoring restrictions to current labs and disease stage and reassessing over time.

### Ignoring Food Security and Culture

A diet the patient cannot afford or cook fails. Counter by assessing means, access, and culture, and adapting the plan to reality.

### Letting Elimination Diets Become Lifelong Without Reassessment

Unnecessary restriction causes nutritional inadequacy and disordered eating. Counter by staging reintroduction (as in low-FODMAP) and periodically re-evaluating every restriction.

## Self-Check

- For each therapeutic diet I recommend, have I specified the indication, the precise restriction, the monitoring plan, and the expected endpoint, rather than a generic slogan?
- For every suspected micronutrient deficiency, have I confirmed it biochemically and investigated the underlying cause, not just prescribed a supplement?
- Have I evaluated any supplement for indication, evidence, drug interactions, and toxicity, and reconciled the patient's supplement list as part of their medications?
- Have I declined to endorse fad or unproven diets and offered evidence-based alternatives and harm reduction?
- Have I individualized the diet to the patient's culture, means, food access, cooking ability, and comorbidities so it is actually achievable?
- Have I built in reassessment of adherence, tolerance, labs, and endpoints, and escalated to a dietitian or specialist for complex or restrictive diets?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as severe deficiency, suspected malabsorption or malignancy, or supplement toxicity appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
