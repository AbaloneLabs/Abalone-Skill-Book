# Malnutrition Assessment and Nutritional Support

Nutritional status is not a footnote to medical care; it is an independent predictor of mortality, infection, wound failure, length of stay, and functional decline. Yet malnutrition is routinely under-recognized, and nutritional support is often started too late, given by the wrong route, or escalated too quickly into harm. The central judgment problem is twofold. First, clinicians treat nutrition as a "lifestyle" issue rather than a treatable diagnosis with its own screening tools, severity grading, and route-specific indications. Second, when support is initiated, the dangerous phase — refeeding syndrome — is invisible until electrolytes collapse and the patient decompensates. This skill covers disciplined nutritional assessment, the route-selection ladder, requirement calculation, and the specific precautions that prevent refeeding-related death. When in doubt, escalate early to a dietitian or the nutrition support team; under-treatment and over-treatment both carry serious risk.

## Core Rules

### Screen Every At-Risk Patient, Then Confirm with a Validated Assessment

Malnutrition must be actively screened, not inferred from weight alone. Screen every inpatient and every high-risk outpatient (elderly, oncology, post-operative, chronic GI disease, alcohol use disorder, critical care, prolonged NPO) using a validated tool such as MUST (Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool), NRS-2002 for inpatients, MNA for older adults, or STRONGkids for pediatrics. A positive screen is not the diagnosis — it triggers a formal assessment. Confirm malnutrition severity using the GLIM criteria, which require both phenotypic criteria (weight loss, low BMI, reduced muscle mass) and etiologic criteria (reduced intake, disease burden, inflammation). Severity is graded mild, moderate, or severe, and it directly drives the aggressiveness of support. Document the tool used, the score, and the severity; an undocumented "looks malnourished" is clinically and legally inadequate.

### Use the Least Invasive Route That Meets the Goal

Nutritional support follows a strict ladder: oral first, then enteral, then parenteral — and each step up must be justified. Oral nutrition with food modification, fortified meals, and oral nutritional supplements (ONS) is always preferred when the gut works and intake can be adequate. Enteral nutrition (tube feeding) is indicated when oral intake is unsafe (dysphagia, impaired consciousness), inadequate despite ONS, or expected to be so for a meaningful period — typically more than a few days. Parenteral nutrition (PN) is reserved for patients with a non-functioning, inaccessible, or resected gut (short bowel, prolonged ileus, severe malabsorption, high-output fistula), or when enteral feeding fails to meet needs. PN is never the default for "convenience," and the gut-should-always-be-used principle holds: even a small volume of enteral feeding preserves mucosal integrity and reduces infection risk compared with exclusive PN. State explicitly why each step up the ladder is necessary.

### Calculate Requirements Deliberately, Not by Guess

Energy and protein targets must be calculated and documented, not estimated by eye. Estimate energy using predictive equations (e.g., weight-based 25–30 kcal/kg/day for stable adults, adjusted for stress) or, in critical care, indirect calorimetry when available; avoid unadjusted Harris-Benedict in obesity, where it overestimates. For obese patients, use adjusted or ideal body weight and consider hypocaloric high-protein feeding. Protein targets are typically 1.0–1.5 g/kg/day in general illness, 1.5–2.0 g/kg/day in catabolic states (burns, sepsis, critical care, wounds), and require adjustment in renal and hepatic failure. Fluid and electrolyte requirements must be specified alongside macronutrients. Overfeeding — delivering full targets immediately to a starved patient — is a direct cause of refeeding complications and hyperglycemia. Calculate first, then titrate up.

### Anticipate and Prevent Refeeding Syndrome Before the First Feed

Refeeding syndrome is the defining danger of nutritional resumption in a malnourished patient, and it is preventable only when anticipated. Identify high-risk patients before feeding starts: those with negligible intake for several days, marked weight loss, low baseline potassium/magnesium/phosphate, alcohol misuse, or pre-existing low BMI. In these patients, begin feeding at low energy (10–20 kcal/kg/day, up to half of estimated needs), correct electrolytes (potassium, magnesium, phosphate) before and during feeding, and give high-dose thiamine before the first feed and daily during the high-risk period — thiamine deficiency is precipitated by the carbohydrate load and can cause Wernicke encephalopathy. Monitor electrolytes and fluid closely (typically daily initially, sometimes more often), titrate feeding upward over several days, and supplement micronutrients broadly. Refeeding risk is not a reason to withhold nutrition, but it is a reason to start slowly and monitor intensively. If you cannot monitor electrolytes, escalate before feeding.

### Reassess Continuously and Adjust to the Clinical Course

Nutrition is a therapy with a dose, a response, and side effects — manage it like any other. Reassess tolerance (aspiration, diarrhea, abdominal distension, hyperglycemia), intake versus targets, weight trend, and functional status at defined intervals. Enteral feeding intolerance is common and has correctable causes (positioning, prokinetics, formula osmolality, rate); do not abandon the enteral route for a problem that can be fixed. Reassess the route itself: a patient who recovers swallow or gut function should step down from PN to enteral, or enteral to oral. Conversely, a patient who fails to meet targets orally or enterally may need escalation. Build in forced re-evaluation; nutrition plans written on admission and never revisited are a common source of both under- and over-feeding.

### Escalate to Specialist Nutrition Support Early

Nutrition support is a specialist field. Involve a dietitian for any patient with confirmed malnutrition, complex requirements, or non-standard feeding; involve the nutrition support team or gastroenterology for PN, complex enteral access, fistula/short-gut management, or refractory intolerance. Escalate before complications, not after. For refractory refeeding physiology, electrolyte chaos, or fluid overload, seek intensive monitoring. In pediatric, geriatric, renal, hepatic, and oncologic contexts, specialist input changes the plan. Document who was consulted and what was recommended.

## Common Traps

### Equating Normal Weight with Normal Nutrition

Patients with normal or high BMI can be functionally malnourished (sarcopenic obesity, acute weight loss masked by edema, low intake despite size). BMI alone misses sarcopenia and acute depletion. Counter by screening with validated tools and assessing weight trajectory, intake, and muscle mass, not just current weight.

### Jumping Straight to Parenteral Nutrition

PN feels definitive and is overused when enteral access is "difficult" or feeding is delayed. Counter by exhausting oral and enteral options first and documenting the gut-related reason PN is required; remember PN carries higher infection, metabolic, and hepatic risk.

### Overfeeding the Starved Patient

Delivering full energy targets on day one to a depleted patient precipitates refeeding syndrome. Counter by starting at reduced energy, giving thiamine, correcting electrolytes, and titrating upward over days.

### Forgetting Thiamine Before the Carbohydrate Load

Thiamine is the single most often omitted refeeding precaution. Counter by prescribing high-dose thiamine before the first feed in every high-risk patient, regardless of how "well" they appear.

### Treating Enteral Intolerance as a Reason to Stop

Diarrhea, high gastric residuals, or aspiration are often correctable rather than reasons to abandon the gut. Counter by reviewing rate, osmolality, position, prokinetics, and concurrent medications before stepping up to PN.

### Ignoring Aspiration Risk in Enteral Feeding

Aspiration pneumonia is a leading complication of tube feeding, especially in obtunded or elderly patients. Counter by elevating the head of the bed, assessing gastric residuals per protocol, and considering post-pyloric placement in high-risk patients.

### Failing to Reassess and Step Down

Patients left on PN or tube feeding long after they could eat orally incur avoidable line infections and deconditioning. Counter by building scheduled reassessment and step-down into every nutrition plan.

## Self-Check

- Have I screened the patient with a validated tool and confirmed malnutrition severity with GLIM or equivalent criteria, not just eyeballed their weight?
- Have I justified the route using the oral-then-enteral-then-parenteral ladder, stating explicitly why each step up is necessary?
- Have I calculated and documented energy, protein, fluid, and electrolyte targets appropriate to the patient's weight, stress state, and organ function?
- For any high-risk or depleted patient, have I anticipated refeeding syndrome — low starting energy, electrolyte correction, high-dose thiamine before the first feed, and close monitoring?
- Am I reassessing tolerance, intake, weight, and route at defined intervals and stepping up or down as the clinical course changes?
- Have I involved a dietitian or nutrition support team for confirmed malnutrition, PN, complex access, or refractory intolerance?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as electrolyte collapse, fluid overload, or neurological signs appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
