# Obesity and Weight Management

Obesity is a chronic, relapsing, neuro-endocrine disease, not a failure of willpower, and the way clinicians frame it determines whether care helps or harms. The central judgment problems are threefold. First, clinicians still treat obesity as a lifestyle complaint, offering generic advice ("eat less, move more") that is known to fail and that erodes trust. Second, they under-use the full ladder of effective therapy — intensive behavioral intervention, anti-obesity pharmacotherapy, and metabolic surgery — leaving patients undertreated for a disease with proven, guideline-backed options. Third, weight stigma is itself a clinical hazard: it drives avoidance of care, worsens cardiometabolic outcomes, and is often perpetrated unconsciously by the very clinician trying to help. This skill covers staging obesity beyond BMI, the stepped-care ladder, comorbidity management, and the communication discipline that makes obesity care both effective and humane. When the case is complex, refractory, or surgical, escalate to an obesity medicine specialist or bariatric service.

## Core Rules

### Stage Obesity Beyond BMI Alone

BMI is a screening number, not a severity grade. A patient with BMI 32 and type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and functional limitation has far more advanced disease than a patient with BMI 38 who is metabolically healthy and active. Use a staging system such as EOSS (Edmonton Obesity Staging System), which grades 0–3 by metabolic, physical, and psychological comorbidity, to capture the disease that BMI misses. Assess waist circumference for central adiposity, screen for comorbidities (type 2 diabetes or prediabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia, sleep apnea, NAFLD/MASH, osteoarthritis, depression, PCOS), and document functional impact. Stage the disease, define the health goals (not just a target weight), and let the stage — not the BMI number — drive the intensity of therapy. A patient at EOSS 2–3 with a "normal-high" BMI may warrant pharmacotherapy or surgical referral; a patient at EOSS 0 with a high BMI may not.

### Use a Stepped-Care Ladder, Not a Single Intervention

Obesity care is a ladder, and most clinicians stop on the bottom rung. Step one is intensive behavioral therapy: structured dietary change, physical activity, sleep, and behavioral support delivered with enough intensity to work (frequent contact, realistic goals, sustained follow-up), not a one-off pamphlet. Step two is anti-obesity pharmacotherapy, indicated when lifestyle alone is insufficient, when BMI thresholds are met, or when comorbidity is present — options include GLP-1 receptor agonists and other approved agents, chosen by efficacy, tolerability, contraindications, and access. Step three is metabolic (bariatric) surgery, the most effective durable therapy for severe obesity. Move up the ladder when the current step fails to reach the agreed health goals, and never present surgery or pharmacotherapy as a "last resort for the weak" — they are standard, evidence-based treatments. Combine steps: pharmacotherapy and lifestyle together outperform either alone.

### Counsel Without Stigma, Using Person-First Language

The language of obesity care shapes outcomes. Use person-first language ("a person with obesity," not "an obese patient"), ask permission before discussing weight ("would it be alright if we talked about your weight and health?"), and frame obesity as a medical condition with biological drivers, not a moral failing. Avoid blaming, surveillance, or unsolicited commentary on bodies. Recognize that weight stigma — from clinicians, society, or the patient themselves — causes harm: delayed care seeking, disordered eating, depression, and physiologic stress that worsens the disease. Examine your own assumptions; bias is often invisible to the holder. A patient who feels judged will not return, and a patient who does not return cannot be treated.

### Manage Comorbidity as Part of the Same Plan

Obesity and its comorbidities are mechanistically linked, and weight loss often improves or resolves type 2 diabetes, hypertension, sleep apnea, NAFLD/MASH, and osteoarthritis pain. Coordinate the plan: weight management is not separate from diabetes or hypertension management — it is often the most effective treatment for them. Anticipate medication adjustments as weight falls (insulin, antihypertensives, diuretics may need down-titration to avoid hypoglycemia and hypotension). Screen for and treat the psychological comorbidity (depression, anxiety, binge eating) that both accompanies and sabotages obesity care. Do not silo the patient into a "weight clinic" disconnected from their medical home.

### Set Realistic Goals and Anticipate Relapse

Obesity is chronic and relapsing; the goal is sustained health improvement, not a number on a chart. A 5–10% weight loss produces clinically meaningful improvements in cardiometabolic risk, and presenting this as a success (rather than a failure to reach "ideal weight") sustains motivation. Set health-based goals (HbA1c, blood pressure, function, sleep) alongside weight goals. Anticipate the weight plateau and relapse that follow most interventions: pharmacotherapy works only while taken, surgery requires lifelong follow-up, and lifestyle change erodes without reinforcement. Build in long-term follow-up and a relapse plan. A patient who regains weight is not a failure of character; they are experiencing the natural history of a chronic disease, and the response is to re-engage therapy, not to abandon the patient.

### Know the Surgical Thresholds and Refer Early

Metabolic surgery is the most effective treatment for severe obesity, yet it is systematically under-referred. Know the indications: typically BMI ≥ 40, or BMI ≥ 35 with significant comorbidity (type 2 diabetes, hypertension, sleep apnea), with some guidelines extending to lower BMI in specific populations with poorly controlled diabetes. Refer early in the disease course — surgery works best before end-organ damage and before the patient has "failed everything." Do not require patients to cycle through years of ineffective therapy before referral. Refer to a multidisciplinary bariatric service for assessment; the decision to operate is theirs, but the decision to refer is yours. Counsel that surgery requires lifelong nutritional surveillance (vitamin deficiency, hypoglycemia, revisional needs).

## Common Traps

### Offering Generic "Eat Less, Move More" as Treatment

This advice is known to be ineffective as sole therapy and conveys blame. Counter by delivering structured, intensive behavioral intervention and moving up the ladder when it is insufficient.

### Using BMI as the Sole Arbiter of Severity and Treatment

BMI misses metabolically unhealthy normal-weight and sarcopenic obesity and over-treats the metabolically healthy. Counter by staging with EOSS and assessing comorbidity and function.

### Withholding Pharmacotherapy or Surgery as "Last Resorts"

Framing effective therapy as a punishment for failure delays treatment and worsens outcomes. Counter by presenting the full ladder as standard, evidence-based options to be used when indicated.

### Perpetrating Weight Stigma Unconsciously

Stigma drives patients out of care and worsens disease. Counter by using person-first language, asking permission, and examining personal bias.

### Failing to Adjust Comorbidity Medications During Weight Loss

Insulin and antihypertensives cause hypoglycemia and hypotension as weight falls. Counter by anticipating and proactively down-titrating during active weight loss.

### Treating Regain as Patient Failure

Relapse is the natural history of a chronic disease. Counter by planning for relapse, re-engaging therapy, and maintaining long-term follow-up rather than discharging the patient.

### Ignoring Psychological Comorbidity

Depression, anxiety, and binge eating both accompany and sabotage obesity care. Counter by screening and treating mental health as part of the integrated plan.

## Self-Check

- Have I staged the patient's obesity beyond BMI — using EOSS or equivalent, with comorbidity and functional assessment — rather than treating the BMI number as the whole picture?
- Am I using the full stepped-care ladder (intensive behavioral therapy, pharmacotherapy, metabolic surgery) and moving up when the current step fails, rather than stopping at generic advice?
- Am I using person-first language, asking permission before discussing weight, and actively countering weight stigma in my own communication?
- Have I integrated comorbidity management (diabetes, hypertension, sleep apnea, mental health) into the same plan and anticipated medication adjustments during weight loss?
- Have I set realistic, health-based goals (5–10% loss, cardiometabolic targets) and built in a relapse plan and long-term follow-up?
- For patients meeting surgical thresholds, have I referred early to a multidisciplinary bariatric service rather than requiring years of failed therapy first?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as severe comorbidity, eating-disorder features, or surgical complications appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
