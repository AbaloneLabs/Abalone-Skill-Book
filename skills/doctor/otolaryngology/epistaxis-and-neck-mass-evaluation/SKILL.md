# Epistaxis and Neck Mass Evaluation

Epistaxis and a neck mass are common presentations that hide opposite failure modes. With epistaxis, the danger is underestimating posterior bleeding, missing a bleeding disorder or medication cause, and discharging a patient who rebleeds catastrophically. With a neck mass, the danger is the opposite: reassuring a patient with a painless persistent lump as a "reactive node" when it is a head and neck or thyroid malignancy, where delay reduces cure. The recurring failure is treating both as routine — epistaxis as a nosebleed to pack and send home, and a neck mass as a node to watch — rather than asking the discriminating questions about source, duration, age, and risk. This skill covers anterior versus posterior epistaxis and packing, the neck mass workup by age and location, thyroid nodule risk stratification, and the threshold for biopsy, imaging, and ENT referral.

## Core Rules

### Locate the Epistaxis Source as Anterior or Posterior and Manage Accordingly

Most epistaxis is anterior, from Kiesselbach's plexus, and is controlled with sustained direct pressure (pinching the soft nose against the septum for 15 to 20 minutes without letting go), posture upright and leaning forward, and topical vasoconstrictor. If anterior pressure fails, identify the bleeding point and apply chemical or electrocautery or an anterior nasal pack or balloon. Posterior epistaxis, from the sphenopalatine artery, presents with bleeding despite an adequate anterior pack, blood running down the posterior pharynx, bilateral bleeding, and hemodynamic compromise; it requires a posterior pack or balloon and admission, because posterior bleeds are harder to control, more likely in older hypertensive patients, and carry airway and aspiration risk. Always establish the source, hemodynamic status, and airway before deciding on disposition.

### Check for and Correct Bleeding Contributors Before Discharge

Epistaxis is frequently worsened by anticoagulants (warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants), antiplatelet agents, underlying coagulopathy (liver disease, thrombocytopenia, von Willebrand disease), and uncontrolled hypertension. Check a full blood count, coagulation studies, and blood group and hold in significant bleeds. Address reversible contributors: control blood pressure, discuss temporary anticoagulant adjustment with the prescribing team (weighing thrombotic risk), and treat iron-deficiency anemia from chronic epistaxis. Discharging a patient on anticoagulants with uncontrolled hypertension and an inadequately packed posterior bleed invites a return with airway compromise. Give clear advice on nasal care (petrolatum, saline, humidification), avoidance of nose-picking and nose-blowing, and when to return.

### Work Up the Neck Mass Systematically by Age, Location, and Duration

The neck mass differential is driven by three variables: patient age, location, and duration. In children and young adults, most neck masses are inflammatory (reactive lymphadenopathy) or congenital (branchial cleft cyst, thyroglossal duct cyst, dermoid). In adults over 40, a persistent painless neck mass is malignant until proven otherwise — metastatic lymphadenopathy from a head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (oral cavity, oropharynx, larynx, nasopharynx), thyroid cancer, or a lymphoma. The location matters: midline masses suggest thyroid or thyroglossal duct origin; lateral neck masses suggest lymph nodes, branchial cysts, or metastases; the posterior triangle and supraclavicular regions have specific differentials. Duration is critical: a mass persisting beyond two weeks despite a trial of observation or antibiotics in an adult demands definitive workup, not further watching.

### Examine the Full Upper Aerodigestive Tract in Any Adult Neck Mass

For an adult with a persistent neck mass, the mass is often a metastatic node from an occult primary in the upper aerodigestive tract. Examine the oral cavity, oropharynx (including the base of tongue and tonsils), larynx, and nasopharynx; palpate the neck systematically and assess all nodal levels. Do not assume a lateral neck mass is a branchial cyst in an older adult, because a cystic metastatic node (for example from a papillary thyroid cancer or human papillomavirus-related oropharyngeal cancer) can mimic a benign cyst. Flexible nasendoscopy, imaging (contrast CT or MRI of the neck), and ultimately examination under anesthesia with biopsy of the primary and the node are needed. Refer to ENT for any adult neck mass persisting beyond two weeks.

### Risk-Stratify Thyroid Nodules With Ultrasound and Avoid Reflex Biopsy

Most thyroid nodules are benign, and the goal is to identify the minority that are malignant without over-investigating. Assess risk with thyroid ultrasound using standardized criteria (such as TI-RADS), considering size, composition, echogenicity, margins, calcifications, and shape. Fine-needle aspiration biopsy is reserved for nodules meeting size and feature thresholds, not performed on every nodule. Check thyroid function, because a hyperfunctioning nodule is rarely malignant and is assessed with scintigraphy. Evaluate for features of malignancy (rapid growth, hoarseness from recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement, cervical adenopathy, fixation, age extremes) and refer to endocrinology or ENT or a thyroid nodule clinic for structured risk stratification and biopsy when indicated.

### Define the Mandatory Biopsy, Imaging, and ENT Referral Thresholds

Mandatory ENT referral with imaging and tissue diagnosis applies to: any adult neck mass persisting beyond two weeks despite observation or antibiotics; a neck mass with red flags (hard, fixed, supraclavicular, rapidly enlarging, over 1.5 cm, associated voice change, dysphagia, otalgia, weight loss, night sweats, smoking or alcohol history); a thyroid nodule meeting biopsy criteria; and any pediatric mass that is not clearly inflammatory or congenital. Avoid empiric antibiotics for a neck mass without a clear infectious source, because they can mask a malignancy and delay diagnosis. Biopsy should be by fine-needle aspiration for a thyroid nodule or suspected node (cutting biopsy or excisional biopsy as appropriate and per protocol), and a suspected metastatic node should not be openly excised without ENT planning, because open excision can compromise later neck dissection.

### Recognize Epistaxis and Neck Mass Emergencies Requiring Immediate Action

Massive epistaxis with hemodynamic instability, airway compromise, or failure of packing is an emergency requiring otolaryngology, anesthesia, and interventional radiology (embolization) or surgical ligation. A neck mass causing airway compromise, stridor, or superior vena cava syndrome (large mediastinal mass) is an emergency. A rapidly enlarging, painful, or fluctuant neck mass with systemic toxicity suggests an abscess or deep neck infection needing imaging and drainage. Recognize these and escalate immediately rather than arranging routine referral.

## Common Traps

### Discharging a Posterior Epistaxis With Only an Anterior Pack

A posterior bleed that continues behind an anterior pack can compromise the airway and cause hemodynamic collapse. Counter by recognizing posterior bleeding (posterior pharyngeal blood, failure of anterior packing) and placing a posterior pack or balloon and admitting for observation.

### Ignoring Anticoagulants and Hypertension in Epistaxis

Rebleeding is common when contributors are uncorrected. Counter by checking and addressing coagulation, platelets, blood pressure, and anticoagulant management before discharge.

### Watching an Adult Neck Mass Beyond Two Weeks

A persistent painless neck mass in an adult is malignant until proven otherwise; prolonged observation or repeated antibiotics waste the window for early cure. Counter by initiating definitive workup and ENT referral at the two-week threshold.

### Assuming a Cystic Lateral Neck Mass Is a Branchial Cyst in an Older Adult

Cystic metastatic nodes from papillary thyroid or HPV-related oropharyngeal cancer mimic branchial cysts. Counter by imaging and obtaining cytology rather than assuming a benign congenital cause in adults.

### Reflexively Biopsying Every Thyroid Nodule

Over-biopsy of low-risk nodules causes anxiety, nondiagnostic samples, and unnecessary surgery. Counter by risk-stratifying with ultrasound criteria and reserving fine-needle aspiration for nodules meeting thresholds.

### Empirically Prescribing Antibiotics for a Neck Mass Without an Infectious Source

Antibiotics without a clear infection can partially suppress a malignancy and delay diagnosis. Counter by reserving antibiotics for clear infectious lymphadenopathy and referring non-responsive masses for definitive workup.

### Openly Excising a Suspected Metastatic Node Without ENT Planning

Open excision of a metastatic node can compromise subsequent neck dissection and staging. Counter by using fine-needle aspiration for diagnosis and involving ENT before any open procedure.

## Self-Check

- For epistaxis, did I establish anterior versus posterior source, hemodynamic status, and airway before deciding on packing and disposition?
- Did I check full blood count, coagulation, and blood group in significant bleeds, and address anticoagulants, antiplatelets, and hypertension before discharge?
- For a neck mass, did I interpret the differential by age, location, and duration, treating an adult mass persisting beyond two weeks as malignant until proven otherwise?
- Did I examine the full upper aerodigestive tract (oral cavity, oropharynx, larynx, nasopharynx) and arrange nasendoscopy and imaging for an adult neck mass?
- For a thyroid nodule, did I risk-stratify with ultrasound criteria and reserve fine-needle aspiration for nodules meeting thresholds, checking thyroid function?
- Did I avoid empiric antibiotics for a neck mass without an infectious source, and avoid open excision of a suspected metastatic node without ENT planning?
- Did I recognize epistaxis and neck mass emergencies (massive uncontrolled bleeding, airway compromise, superior vena cava syndrome, deep neck abscess) and escalate immediately?
- Did I document the ENT referral threshold and timeline so time-critical malignancies are not lost in routine queues?
