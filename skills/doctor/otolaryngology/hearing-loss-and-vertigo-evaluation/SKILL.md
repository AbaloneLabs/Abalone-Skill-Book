# Hearing Loss and Vertigo Evaluation

Hearing loss and dizziness are among the most common complaints in adult primary care, and they are also among the most frequently misclassified. The danger is twofold: missing sudden sensorineural hearing loss, which is reversible only if treated within a narrow window, and labeling a posterior circulation stroke as "peripheral vertigo" because the patient is dizzy. The recurring failure is treating dizziness or hearing change as a symptom to reassure rather than localizing it with focused bedside tests (tuning fork, head impulse, nystagmus, gait) that separate dangerous central causes from benign peripheral ones. This skill covers sudden sensorineural hearing loss, the conductive-versus-sensorineural distinction, the vertigo differential, central red flags, and the imaging decision.

## Core Rules

### Treat Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss as an Emergency

Sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) is defined as a loss of 30 decibels or more across three contiguous frequencies occurring within 72 hours, in one or both ears, and it is an emergency where early treatment (typically high-dose oral or intratemporal corticosteroids) improves the chance of recovery. The window for benefit narrows quickly, and treatment started beyond two to four weeks has little effect. Any patient reporting sudden hearing loss must be assessed urgently with tuning fork tests and audiometry to confirm the loss is sensorineural rather than conductive, and referred to otolaryngology within days for confirmation and treatment. Do not attribute sudden unilateral hearing loss to wax or fluid without examining and testing; the cost of delay is permanent deafness.

### Differentiate Conductive From Sensorineural Loss With Weber and Rinne

Use a 512 Hz tuning fork to localize the type of loss. In conductive loss (wax, otosclerosis, middle ear effusion, ossicular disruption), Rinne is negative (bone conduction greater than air conduction) on the affected side, and Weber lateralizes to the affected ear. In sensorineural loss (cochlear or retrocochlear), Rinne is positive (air greater than bone) bilaterally but reduced on the affected side, and Weber lateralizes to the unaffected ear. Confirm with formal audiometry, but the bedside tests guide the urgent referral. A conductive loss with an abnormal exam suggests a middle ear problem; a sensorineural loss demands urgent otolaryngology referral to define the cause (idiopathic, infectious, vascular, autoimmune, or a vestibular schwannoma in asymmetric loss).

### Distinguish the Common Peripheral Causes of Vertigo by Trigger and Duration

Most vertigo is peripheral and benign, and the history distinguishes the cause. Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) causes brief (under a minute), position-triggered episodes (rolling over in bed, looking up) with a latency and fatigability on the Dix-Hallpike, and is treated with repositioning maneuvers. Vestibular neuritis causes a single prolonged episode of severe vertigo lasting days, with nausea and vomiting, spontaneous horizontal nystagmus, and no hearing loss, often after a viral illness; management is supportive with short courses of vestibular sedatives and early vestibular rehabilitation. Meniere disease causes recurrent episodes of vertigo lasting minutes to hours with fluctuating low-frequency hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness. Matching the history to the pattern prevents both over-investigation of BPPV and under-treatment of these conditions.

### Actively Screen for Central Causes and Stroke Red Flags in Dizziness

The most dangerous error in dizziness is attributing a posterior circulation stroke or cerebellar hemorrhage to peripheral vertigo. Use the HINTS examination (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) in the acute vestibular syndrome: a normal head impulse test, direction-changing or vertical nystagmus, or a skew deviation on alternate cover test suggests a central cause and is more sensitive than early MRI for stroke. Central red flags include any new neurologic symptom or sign (diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, weakness, sensory change, ataxia, vertical nystagmus), severe headache (especially occipital or with neck pain, suggesting vertebral dissection), a pattern inconsistent with peripheral vertigo, vascular risk factors, older age, and inability to stand (truncal ataxia). Any of these mandates urgent neuroimaging and neurology input.

### Recognize When Vertigo or Hearing Loss Needs Imaging

Imaging is not needed for classic BPPV or uncomplicated vestibular neuritis. Obtain MRI (with attention to the internal auditory canals and posterior circulation) for sudden sensorineural hearing loss to exclude a vestibular schwannoma and to define the cause, for asymmetric or progressive hearing loss, for dizziness with central signs or HINTS suggesting stroke, for persistent or atypical vertigo, and for trauma. CT is appropriate when acute hemorrhage or bony injury is suspected and MRI is unavailable. Match imaging to the suspected pathology, and do not delay urgent stroke evaluation waiting for an MRI in a patient with central signs.

### Take a Directed Otologic and Vestibular History

The history is the most powerful diagnostic tool. Establish onset (sudden versus gradual), duration of episodes (seconds, minutes, hours, days), triggers (position, sound, stress, head movement), associated symptoms (hearing loss, tinnitus, aural fullness, ear pain, discharge, neurologic symptoms), and the patient's description (true spinning vertigo versus presyncope, disequilibrium, or lightheadedness, which point elsewhere). Ask about ototoxic medications (aminoglycosides, high-dose loop diuretics, some chemotherapy), recent viral illness, head trauma, noise exposure, and vascular risk factors. Distinguishing true vertigo from presyncope or imbalance prevents misdirected workup.

### Define When to Refer to Otolaryngology, Neurology, or the Emergency Department

Refer urgently to otolaryngology for sudden or progressive sensorineural hearing loss, asymmetric hearing loss, recurrent vertigo with hearing loss, and chronic or atypical dizziness. Refer to the emergency department or neurology immediately for dizziness with central signs, suspected stroke, severe headache with neck pain, or new neurologic deficits. Refer for audiology for hearing aids and rehabilitation in chronic loss. State the referral pathway and timeline explicitly so that time-critical conditions are not lost in routine outpatient queues.

## Common Traps

### Missing Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss Behind "Wax" or "Fluid"

Attributing sudden hearing loss to wax or congestion without tuning fork tests and otoscopy sacrifices the narrow treatment window. Counter by examining the ear and performing Weber and Rinne in any sudden hearing loss, and referring urgently when sensorineural loss is confirmed.

### Labeling Posterior Circulation Stroke as Peripheral Vertigo

Dizziness with any neurologic symptom, vertical or direction-changing nystagmus, inability to walk, or vascular risk factors may be a stroke. Counter by applying the HINTS examination and central red-flag screen, and obtaining urgent neuroimaging when central signs are present.

### Overusing Vestibular Sedatives for Chronic Dizziness

Prolonged use of vestibular sedatives suppresses central compensation and worsens chronic dizziness. Counter by limiting vestibular sedatives to the acute phase of vestibular neuritis (a few days) and emphasizing early vestibular rehabilitation.

### Treating BPPV With Medications Instead of Repositioning

BPPV responds to canalith repositioning maneuvers, not to chronic vestibular sedatives. Counter by performing the Dix-Hallpike and repositioning maneuvers when the history fits, and reserving medication for symptomatic nausea.

### Forgetting Ototoxic Medications and Bilateral Loss

Aminoglycosides, high-dose loop diuretics, and certain chemotherapy can cause bilateral sensorineural loss, which may be missed if only one ear is assessed. Counter by reviewing medications and testing both ears.

### Dismissing Asymmetric Hearing Loss as Normal Aging

Age-related loss is symmetric; asymmetric loss raises a vestibular schwannoma or other retrocochlear lesion until excluded. Counter by obtaining audiometry and MRI of the internal auditory canals for asymmetric loss.

### Reassuring a Patient With Vertigo and Severe Occipital Headache

Vertigo with severe headache, especially with neck pain, suggests vertebral artery dissection or posterior circulation hemorrhage. Counter by treating this as a neurologic emergency and imaging urgently.

## Self-Check

- For any sudden hearing loss, did I examine the ear and perform Weber and Rinne to confirm sensorineural loss, and refer urgently to otolaryngology within the treatment window?
- Did I distinguish conductive from sensorineural loss using tuning fork tests and plan formal audiometry?
- For vertigo, did I match the episode duration, trigger, and associated symptoms to BPPV, vestibular neuritis, or Meniere disease rather than treating all dizziness the same?
- Did I actively screen for central red flags and perform HINTS in the acute vestibular syndrome, treating central findings as suspected stroke?
- Did I obtain MRI for sudden sensorineural hearing loss, asymmetric or progressive loss, and dizziness with central signs, rather than imaging every case of BPPV?
- Did I take a directed history distinguishing true vertigo from presyncope or imbalance, and ask about ototoxic medications and noise exposure?
- Did I limit vestibular sedatives to the acute phase and arrange vestibular rehabilitation for chronic dizziness?
- Did I define the correct referral pathway (otolaryngology, neurology, emergency department) and timeline for time-critical conditions?
