# Acute Otolaryngologic Infections and Airway

Ear, nose, throat, and neck infections are common and mostly benign, but the same anatomy that makes them routine also makes them lethal when they spread: the deep neck spaces connect to the mediastinum, the sinus and orbital veins drain to the cavernous sinus, and the upper airway narrows to a critical point where a few millimeters of swelling obstruct breathing in a child. The recurring failure is treating these infections as a generic "infection requiring antibiotics" rather than asking the two questions that matter: is the airway safe, and is there a complication that has spread beyond the local site? This skill covers otitis, rhinosinusitis and complications, deep neck infections, the sore throat with abscess, Ludwig angina, and the recognition and escalation of airway compromise.

## Core Rules

### Secure the Airway First in Any Upper Airway or Deep Neck Infection

In any infection involving the pharynx, epiglottis, retropharyngeal or parapharyngeal spaces, or the floor of the mouth, the first assessment is airway patency, not antibiotic choice. Stridor, tripod positioning, drooling, inability to swallow secretions, trismus, a muffled "hot potato" voice, and agitation or fatigue are signs of impending airway loss and demand immediate senior airway support — anesthesiology and otolaryngology — in a setting equipped for a surgical airway. Do not lay a child with epiglottitis flat, do not instrument the throat of a patient with suspected epiglottitis or deep abscess (which can precipitate complete obstruction), and keep the patient in the position they find most comfortable until expert airway help arrives. A patient who is sitting forward, drooling, and struggling to breathe is a surgical airway candidate, not a routine assessment.

### Distinguish Acute Otitis Media From Otitis Externa and Reserve Antibiotics Appropriately

Acute otitis media presents with ear pain, hearing loss, and systemic symptoms, with a bulging, opaque, erythematous tympanic membrane that may perforate with discharge; otalgia in a young child who is febrile and irritable should prompt pneumatic otoscopy to assess for a non-mobile, bulging drum. Otitis externa ("swimmer's ear") causes pain (especially on tragal manipulation), fullness, and a tender, swollen ear canal with debris, and a normal tympanic membrane when visible; management is topical. The trap is overdiagnosing acute otitis media from simple redness, which is often viral, and prescribing antibiotics unnecessarily; reserve antibiotics for a bulging drum with acute signs, particularly in young children or severe cases. In otitis externa, clean the canal, use topical antimicrobial and steroid drops, and place a wick if the canal is swollen shut. Suspect malignant otitis externa — an invasive skull base osteomyelitis — in older diabetic or immunocompromised patients with severe unrelenting otalgia and granulation at the canal floor; this requires systemic antipseudomonal therapy and urgent otolaryngology referral.

### Distinguish Bacterial From Viral Rhinosinusitis and Watch for Orbital and Intracranial Complications

Most acute rhinosinusitis is viral and resolves without antibiotics; bacterial rhinosinusitis is suggested by duration beyond ten days without improvement, double worsening (worsening after initial improvement), purulent nasal discharge with unilateral facial or dental pain, and fever. When antibiotics are indicated, choose an agent active against the common pathogens. The dangerous step is missing the complications: orbital cellulitis or abscess presents with eyelid swelling, erythema, proptosis, restricted or painful eye movement, and visual change, and is an emergency requiring imaging, intravenous antibiotics, and otolaryngology and ophthalmology input. Cavernous sinus thrombosis presents with cranial nerve palsies, chemosis, proptosis, and signs of systemic sepsis. Intracranial spread causes meningitis, subdural empyema, or brain abscess with headache, fever, and neurologic signs. Any patient with facial or periorbital cellulitis from sinusitis, visual change, proptosis, or neurologic signs needs urgent imaging and escalation.

### Separate Peritonsillar Abscess From Severe Tonsillitis and From Deeper Infections

Peritonsillar abscess presents with severe unilateral sore throat, fever, trismus, a muffled voice, drooling, and uvular deviation away from the affected side with a swollen, anterior tonsillar pillar; it is the most common deep neck abscess and is usually amenable to needle drainage in trained hands. Differentiate it from uncomplicated tonsillitis (bilateral, no trismus, no uvular deviation) and from deeper infections. Retropharyngeal abscess (especially in young children) presents with fever, neck stiffness, dysphagia, and respiratory symptoms; lateral neck imaging or CT confirms, and it can obstruct the airway. Epiglottitis in adults presents with sudden sore throat out of proportion to oropharyngeal findings, drooling, and airway compromise; in vaccinated children it is rare but still occurs. For any abscess with airway threat, systemic toxicity, or failure of drainage, involve otolaryngology urgently and image before manipulation.

### Recognize Ludwig Angina and Deep Neck Space Infections as Surgical Emergencies

Ludwig angina is a rapidly spreading bilateral cellulitis of the submandibular, sublingual, and submental spaces, usually from an odontogenic source, presenting with firm floor-of-mouth swelling, tongue elevation and displacement, drooling, trismus, and rapidly progressing airway compromise. It is a surgical emergency: the airway can obstruct within hours, and definitive airway control is difficult, often requiring awake tracheostomy rather than standard intubation. Give broad-spectrum antibiotics early, but do not let medical therapy delay airway securing and surgical decompression. Other deep neck space infections (parapharyngeal, retropharyngeal) similarly threaten the airway and mediastinum (descending necrotizing mediastinitis) and require imaging, intravenous antibiotics, and surgical drainage.

### Use Imaging and Cultures Deliberately in Complicated or Atypical Infection

For uncomplicated otitis or sinusitis, imaging is unnecessary. Imaging (contrast CT of the neck and chest as indicated) is essential when you suspect deep neck abscess, Ludwig angina, malignant otitis externa, orbital or intracranial complication, or when examination is limited by trismus or swelling. Obtain blood cultures and inflammatory markers in systemically unwell patients, and tailor antibiotics to culture results. Do not delay antibiotics for cultures in septic patients, but do obtain cultures before de-escalation.

### Define the Threshold for Otolaryngology Referral and Inpatient Care

Refer urgently to otolaryngology for any airway threat, suspected deep neck abscess, Ludwig angina, peritonsillar abscess needing drainage or with airway compromise, malignant otitis externa, orbital or intracranial complication of sinusitis, and any infection with systemic toxicity or immunocompromise. Admit for intravenous antibiotics and observation when the airway is at risk, when pain or swallowing prevent oral intake, or when complications are suspected. State the escalation threshold explicitly.

## Common Traps

### Treating Airway Infections as Routine Antibiotic Cases

Focusing on antibiotic choice while the patient drools, sits forward, and tires risks losing the airway. Counter by assessing airway first in every throat and neck infection and calling senior airway help before instrumentation.

### Overdiagnosing Acute Otitis Media From a Red Drum

Viral upper respiratory infections cause a red eardrum without acute otitis media. Counter by using pneumatic otoscopy and reserving antibiotics for bulging, opaque drums with acute systemic signs.

### Missing Orbital Cellulitis Behind Periorbital Swelling

Periorbital swelling after sinusitis can be preseptal cellulitis (mild) or postseptal orbital cellulitis or abscess (sight- and life-threatening). Counter by assessing proptosis, eye movement, vision, and systemic signs, and imaging when postseptal involvement is suspected.

### Instrumenting the Throat in Suspected Epiglottitis or Deep Abscess

Tongue depression or endoscopy in an unstable epiglottitis or deep abscess can precipitate complete obstruction. Counter by avoiding throat instrumentation in the at-risk patient and securing the airway first in a controlled setting.

### Treating Ludwig Angina With Antibiotics Alone

Ludwig angina obstructs the airway faster than antibiotics can work. Counter by recognizing the firm floor-of-mouth swelling and tongue elevation early and securing the airway surgically while giving antibiotics.

### Missing Malignant Otitis Externa in the Diabetic Older Patient

Severe unrelenting otalgia with granulation at the canal floor in a diabetic or immunocompromised patient is skull base osteomyelitis, not simple otitis externa. Counter by checking for cranial nerve involvement, obtaining imaging, and starting systemic antipseudomonal therapy with urgent otolaryngology referral.

### Discharging a Patient With a Peritonsillar Abscess Without Drainage or Follow-Up

Antibiotics alone often fail for an established peritonsillar abscess. Counter by arranging drainage (in trained hands) or urgent otolaryngology review, and clear safety-net advice for airway symptoms.

## Self-Check

- For any throat or neck infection, did I assess airway patency first and call senior airway help before instrumentation when stridor, drooling, or tripod positioning is present?
- Did I distinguish acute otitis media from otitis externa using pneumatic otoscopy and canal findings, and reserve oral antibiotics appropriately?
- For rhinosinusitis, did I distinguish bacterial from viral, and did I actively screen for orbital and intracranial complications (proptosis, eye movement, vision, neurologic signs)?
- For a severe sore throat, did I differentiate peritonsillar abscess from tonsillitis and from retropharyngeal abscess or epiglottitis, and image or refer when deeper infection is suspected?
- Did I recognize Ludwig angina (firm floor-of-mouth swelling, tongue elevation) as a surgical airway emergency rather than a medical case?
- Did I consider malignant otitis externa in the diabetic or immunocompromised patient with severe otalgia and arrange imaging and systemic antipseudomonal therapy?
- Did I use contrast CT neck/chest for suspected deep neck abscess, Ludwig angina, or complications, and obtain cultures in septic patients without delaying antibiotics?
- Did I define and document the otolaryngology referral and admission threshold for airway threat, systemic toxicity, or complication?
- This skill provides educational information only and does not constitute medical advice. Always consult qualified healthcare professionals for clinical decisions.
