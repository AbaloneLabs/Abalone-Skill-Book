# GI Bleeding and Hepatic Disorders

Gastrointestinal bleeding and liver decompensation are twin domains where the difference between routine and catastrophic is measured in hours, and where the wrong first move — delaying endoscopy, withholding vasoactive drugs in suspected variceal bleeding, or under-resuscitating a cirrhotic — costs lives. The recurring error is treating the bleeding or the abnormal liver panel as a single problem rather than recognizing that the patient's hemodynamics, comorbidity, and underlying portal pressure determine the entire trajectory. This skill covers the threat-first assessment of GI bleeding, the structured use of risk scores, the divergent management of variceal versus non-variceal hemorrhage, the recognition and stepwise treatment of cirrhotic decompensation, the must-not-miss diagnosis of acute liver failure, and the judgment of when to escalate to gastroenterology, hepatology, endoscopy, or transplant. It is written for the agent deciding who needs the endoscopy suite tonight and who can wait until morning.

## Core Rules

### Resuscitate and Risk-Stratify Before Anything Else in GI Bleeding

Every patient with significant GI bleeding needs simultaneous resuscitation and risk assessment, not a workup in series. Establish two large-bore IVs, restore circulating volume with crystalloid then blood, correct coagulopathy and platelets pragmatically rather than chasing arbitrary targets, and avoid over-transfusion, which can paradoxically worsen variceal bleeding by raising portal pressure. Apply a validated risk score at the bedside: the Glasgow-Blatchford score identifies who can be safely managed as an outpatient (a score near zero) versus who needs admission and urgent endoscopy, while the Rockall score (full version after endoscopy) estimates mortality and guides intensity of care. Hemodynamic instability, ongoing hematemesis, hematochezia (suggesting a brisk upper source), severe comorbidity, and known portal hypertension all push toward urgent upper endoscopy within hours. Do not let a normal hemoglobin early in the bleed reassure you — hemodilution lags behind blood loss.

### Treat Variceal and Non-Variceal Bleeding Differently From the Outset

The management forks the moment portal hypertension is suspected. For suspected variceal bleeding, start a vasoactive drug (terlipressin, octreotide, somatostatin) early and continue for several days, give prophylactic broad-spectrum antibiotics (which reduce mortality and rebleeding in cirrhotics), and arrange urgent endoscopy for band ligation; have a plan for refractory bleeding including balloon tamponade as a bridge, and transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS) for failure or high-risk patients. For non-variceal upper bleeding, the priority is endoscopic hemostasis with clips, thermal therapy, or injection — start a proton pump inhibitor infusion before endoscopy in high-risk cases and continue after hemostasis. Never assume the source is non-variceal in a cirrhotic; manage as variceal until endoscopy proves otherwise. Massive, uncontrolled hematochezia suggests a brisk upper source and warrants upper endoscopy before colonoscopy.

### Evaluate Lower GI Bleeding Methodically but Recognize the Brisk Upper Source

Lower GI bleeding — typically hematochezia — is most often diverticular, followed by colonic polyps or cancer, ischemic colitis, inflammatory bowel disease, hemorrhoids, and angiodysplasia. Most lower bleeds stop spontaneously; the task is to resuscitate, risk-stratify, and localize. A brisk upper GI bleed can present with hematochezia and hemodynamic instability — perform nasogastric aspiration or upper endoscopy if there is any doubt. For ongoing unstable lower bleeding, urgent colonoscopy after rapid purge, CT angiography, or tagged red cell scan can localize the source; angiography with embolization is both diagnostic and therapeutic. Avoid premature colonoscopy in an unprepped, unstable patient when upper endoscopy or angiography would be more informative.

### Recognize and Treat Cirrhotic Decompensation Systematically

Cirrhosis decompensates through ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, jaundice, and infection, and each has specific therapy — but the underlying driver is failing liver function and portal hypertension. Manage ascites with sodium restriction and diuretics (spironolactone with furosemide), and reserve large-volume paracentesis for tense or refractory ascites; give albumin after large-volume drainage. Suspect spontaneous bacterial peritonitis in any cirrhotic with ascites and new abdominal pain, fever, encephalopathy, or renal decline, and perform diagnostic paracentesis immediately — a neutroph count above threshold warrants empiric antibiotics and albumin to prevent hepatorenal syndrome. Treat hepatic encephalopathy by correcting precipitants (infection, bleeding, constipation, electrolyte disturbance, sedatives, dehydration) alongside lactulose, adding rifaximin for recurrence. Always look for a precipitant, because decompensation is rarely spontaneous.

### Identify Acute Liver Failure and Escalate to a Transplant Center

Acute liver failure — coagulopathy and encephalopathy in a patient without known chronic liver disease, developing over weeks — is a true emergency with a narrow window for intervention. Common causes include acetaminophen toxicity (the most common in many regions, often unintentional and staggered), drug-induced liver injury, viral hepatitis, ischemic hepatopathy, autoimmune hepatitis, Wilson disease, and mushroom poisoning. Give N-acetylcysteine early when acetaminophen is possible or even if the cause is unclear, since it can improve outcomes beyond acetaminophen; correct the reversible causes; monitor for cerebral edema, sepsis, renal failure, and coagulopathy; and transfer to a liver transplant center as soon as the diagnosis is made, because transplant decisions depend on dynamic King's College or similar criteria and time is critical. Do not manage acute liver failure in a center without transplant capability.

### Avoid Harm in Liver Disease: Nephrotoxins, Sedatives, and Premature Procedures

Cirrhotic patients are exquisitely sensitive to renal injury and sedation. Avoid NSAIDs and other nephrotoxins, use lactulose and rifaximin rather than sedatives for agitation when possible, and reserve invasive procedures for clear indications with correction of coagulopathy as needed. Vaccinate against hepatitis A and B, screen for hepatocellular carcinoma with ultrasound and alpha-fetoprotein every six months in cirrhotics, and screen for varices at diagnosis. Coordinate care with hepatology early, because transplant referral should occur before the patient is too sick to benefit.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring on an Early Normal Hemoglobin

Hemodilution lags behind acute blood loss. A normal hemoglobin in the first hours of a GI bleed does not exclude significant hemorrhage; trend it and resuscitate based on hemodynamics.

### Over-Transfusing in Suspected Variceal Bleeding

Restrictive transfusion (e.g., to around 7 g/dL in stable patients without cardiovascular disease, higher thresholds in those with comorbidity) lowers portal pressure and rebleeding. Avoid liberal transfusion targets reflexively.

### Withholding Vasoactive Drugs and Antibiotics in Suspected Variceal Bleeding

Terlipressin or octreotide and prophylactic antibiotics reduce mortality and rebleeding in cirrhotics. Start them empirically when portal hypertension is suspected, before endoscopy.

### Assuming Hematochezia Is Always Lower GI

Brisk upper bleeding can present as hematochezia with instability. Perform nasogastric aspiration or upper endoscopy if hemodynamics suggest an upper source.

### Missing Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis

Any cirrhotic with ascites and new encephalopathy, fever, pain, or renal decline needs diagnostic paracentesis now, not after observation. A delay in antibiotics worsens survival and hepatorenal risk.

### Treating Hepatic Encephalopathy Without Looking for a Precipitant

Lactulose helps, but encephalopathy is almost always precipitated by infection, bleeding, constipation, dehydration, electrolyte disturbance, or sedatives. Find and fix the trigger.

### Managing Acute Liver Failure Outside a Transplant Center

Acute liver failure can deteriorate within hours and transplant criteria are dynamic. Transfer to a transplant center as soon as the diagnosis is suspected.

## Self-Check

- Have I resuscitated and risk-stratified simultaneously using Glasgow-Blatchford (and Rockall after endoscopy), and avoided false reassurance from an early normal hemoglobin?
- For suspected variceal bleeding, have I started a vasoactive drug, prophylactic antibiotics, and arranged urgent endoscopy with a TIPS or balloon-tamponade plan for failure?
- For non-variceal upper bleeding, have I given a proton pump inhibitor and prioritized early endoscopic hemostasis?
- For lower GI bleeding, have I resuscitated, considered an upper source in unstable hematochezia, and chosen colonoscopy, CT angiography, or embolization appropriately?
- For cirrhotic decompensation, have I managed ascites, performed diagnostic paracentesis when SBP is possible, and treated encephalopathy by correcting precipitants alongside lactulose?
- Have I recognized acute liver failure (coagulopathy plus encephalopathy without chronic disease), given N-acetylcysteine early, and arranged transfer to a transplant center?
- Have I avoided nephrotoxins and unnecessary sedatives, screened for hepatocellular carcinoma and varices, and escalated to GI, hepatology, endoscopy, or transplant early?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
