# Inflammatory and Functional Bowel Disorders

Chronic bowel symptoms sit on a spectrum from entirely functional and benign to life-altering inflammation with real cancer risk, and the central clinical task is separating the two without over-investigating everyone or under-investigating the few who need urgent care. The recurring failure comes in pairs: missing inflammatory bowel disease because the symptoms were dismissed as irritable bowel syndrome, and conversely subjecting every patient with functional symptoms to repeated endoscopies, scans, and restrictive diets that reinforce illness behavior without relieving it. This skill covers the disciplined separation of organic from functional disease, the acute and maintenance management of Crohn disease and ulcerative colitis, the recognition of severe flares and colorectal cancer risk, the judicious use of endoscopy and biomarkers, and the decision of when to escalate to gastroenterology for biologic therapy. It is written for the agent who must avoid both the error of dismissal and the error of over-medicalization.

## Core Rules

### Use Alarm Features to Separate Organic From Functional Disease

The single most important judgment in chronic bowel symptoms is whether this is likely functional or likely organic, because it determines everything downstream. Irritable bowel syndrome is a positive diagnosis based on symptom criteria (recurrent abdominal pain related to defecation, change in stool frequency or form) in the absence of alarm features, and it can be diagnosed without exhaustive testing when the pattern is classic and there are no red flags. The alarm features that mandate further investigation are weight loss, nocturnal symptoms waking the patient from sleep, overt or occult gastrointestinal bleeding, anemia, fever, family history of colorectal cancer or inflammatory bowel disease, new onset in older age, recent antibiotic use, persistent diarrhea, and abnormal screening bloods or fecal markers. In their absence, a focused workup — CBC, CRP or ESR, celiac serology, and a fecal calprotectin — is usually sufficient, and a confident functional diagnosis with explanation and reassurance is therapeutic. In their presence, escalate to endoscopy and structured evaluation.

### Use Fecal Calprotectin and Fecal Immunochemical Testing Deliberately

Fecal calprotectin is the best non-invasive discriminator of intestinal inflammation and should be used to triage chronic diarrhea before reaching for colonoscopy: a normal value makes significant inflammatory bowel disease unlikely, while an elevated value mandates endoscopic evaluation. Do not order it indiscriminately for every abdominal complaint, and interpret it knowing that it can rise with NSAID use, infection, and colorectal cancer. Fecal immunochemical testing (FIT) screens for occult blood and is part of colorectal cancer surveillance, but it is not a substitute for colonoscopy when alarm features are present. Use these tools to decide who avoids endoscopy (low-risk functional symptoms with normal markers) and who proceeds (alarm features or elevated markers), not as a substitute for clinical judgment.

### Manage IBD Flares by Severity and Do Not Underestimate the Severe Flare

Inflammatory bowel disease flares range from mild exacerbations manageable with topical or oral therapy to severe attacks that threaten colectomy or life. For ulcerative colitis, a severe flare — frequent bloody diarrhea, fever, tachycardia, anemia, elevated inflammatory markers, and systemic toxicity — warrants hospital admission, IV steroids, stool studies to exclude infection (especially C. difficile, which can mimic and worsen flare), and early surgical and gastroenterology input; consider the Truelove and Witts criteria and monitor for toxic megacolon with daily abdominal films and examination. Crohn disease flares may involve pain, obstruction, fistula, or abscess, and the key distinction is inflammatory from fibrostenotic disease, because steroids help the former and worsen or fail the latter. Always exclude infection (C. difficile, CMV) before attributing worsening to a flare, and image for abscess before intensifying immunosuppression, because steroids and biologics in the presence of an undrained abscess cause harm.

### Choose Maintenance Therapy by Disease Severity, Location, and Risk

Mild ulcerative colitis confined to the rectum or left colon often responds to mesalamine or topical steroids; extensive or steroid-dependent disease generally requires immunomodulators (thiopurines, methotrexate) or biologics (anti-TNF agents, anti-integrins, IL-12/23 inhibitors) or small molecules. Crohn disease frequently requires biologics earlier because mesalamine has limited efficacy, and the choice of agent depends on disease location (ileal versus colonic), behavior (inflammatory, stricturing, penetrating), extraintestinal manifestations, prior failures, infection and tuberculosis screening, and patient preference. Steroids are for induction only — they are not maintenance therapy, and chronic use causes real harm; every patient needing repeated steroids should be escalated to steroid-sparing therapy. Monitor patients on biologics and immunomodulators for infection, screen for latent tuberculosis, hepatitis, and other infections before starting, and follow therapeutic drug monitoring where appropriate.

### Define and Pursue Mucosal Healing, Not Just Symptom Control

Modern IBD care targets mucosal healing and biochemical remission (normal CRP and calprotectin), not merely symptom relief, because healing predicts fewer hospitalizations, surgeries, and cancers. Reassess disease activity objectively with endoscopy, biomarkers, and imaging rather than relying on the patient's report alone, because symptoms can be functional even in established IBD, and IBD patients can have irritable bowel syndrome-like symptoms overlaid on quiescent disease. This distinction matters: escalating biologics for functional symptoms in a patient with healed mucosa exposes them to immunosuppression without benefit. Coordinate objective reassessment with gastroenterology.

### Organize Colorectal Cancer Surveillance and Acknowledge Functional Symptoms Seriously

Patients with long-standing ulcerative colitis or Crohn colitis carry a rising colorectal cancer risk that demands structured surveillance colonoscopy, typically beginning around eight years after diagnosis (or earlier with primary sclerosing cholangitis or extensive family history) and repeated at intervals guided by risk. Do not let a young patient's age reassure you away from surveillance. For functional disease, take the symptoms seriously: a validated diagnosis, explanation of the gut-brain axis, dietary strategies, symptom-directed pharmacotherapy, and cognitive-behavioral or gut-directed psychological therapies all help. Avoid the twin failures of dismissing functional symptoms as "just stress" and of chasing them with escalating invasive tests once organic disease has been excluded.

## Common Traps

### Labeling IBD as IBS and Missing the Diagnosis

Diagnosing irritable bowel syndrome without checking for alarm features, anemia, inflammatory markers, celiac serology, or fecal calprotectin misses inflammatory bowel disease. Apply alarm-feature screening before settling on a functional diagnosis.

### Over-Investigating Classic Functional Symptoms

Once organic disease is reasonably excluded, repeated endoscopies and scans reinforce illness behavior without benefit. Make a confident functional diagnosis and treat it.

### Treating IBD Flares With Steroids Without Excluding Infection

C. difficile and CMV can mimic or worsen a flare, and steroids in their presence cause harm. Send stool studies and image for abscess before intensifying immunosuppression.

### Using Steroids as Maintenance Therapy

Corticosteroids induce remission but do not maintain it and cause cumulative harm. Any patient needing repeated steroids should be escalated to steroid-sparing therapy.

### Escalating Biologics for Functional Symptoms in Quiescent IBD

IBD patients can have irritable-bowel-like symptoms despite healed mucosa. Confirm objective disease activity (endoscopy, calprotectin, CRP) before intensifying immunosuppression.

### Assuming Young Age Excludes Colorectal Cancer Risk

Long-standing colitis carries a real cancer risk regardless of age, and primary sclerosing cholangitis accelerates it. Follow structured surveillance beginning around eight years after diagnosis.

### Failing to Distinguish Inflammatory From Fibrostenotic Crohn Disease

Steroids and biologics help inflammatory disease but not fibrostenotic strictures, which may need endoscopic or surgical management. Image and characterize the disease behavior before choosing therapy.

## Self-Check

- Have I screened for alarm features before settling on a functional diagnosis, and used CBC, inflammatory markers, celiac serology, and fecal calprotectin to triage?
- Have I used fecal calprotectin and FIT deliberately to decide who needs endoscopy, and avoided over-investigating classic functional symptoms once organic disease is excluded?
- For an IBD flare, have I assessed severity, excluded infection (C. difficile, CMV) and abscess before intensifying immunosuppression, and recognized the severe colitis that needs admission and surgical input?
- Have I used steroids only for induction and escalated any steroid-dependent patient to steroid-sparing maintenance therapy?
- Have I selected biologic or small-molecule therapy based on disease location, behavior, prior failures, and infection screening, with therapeutic drug monitoring where appropriate?
- Have I targeted objective mucosal healing and biochemical remission, and confirmed active inflammation before escalating therapy for symptoms?
- Have I organized colorectal cancer surveillance for long-standing colitis (around eight years, earlier with primary sclerosing cholangitis) and treated functional symptoms seriously rather than dismissing them?
- Have I escalated to gastroenterology for diagnosis, severe flares, biologic initiation, refractory disease, or uncertainty about inflammatory versus functional symptoms?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
