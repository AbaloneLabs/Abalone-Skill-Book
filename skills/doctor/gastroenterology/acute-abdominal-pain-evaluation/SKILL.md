# Acute Abdominal Pain Evaluation

Acute abdominal pain is the archetypal high-stakes, time-pressured evaluation: the same symptom — pain — spans trivial gastroenteritis and a leaking abdominal aortic aneurysm, and the window to intervene in a surgical catastrophe is measured in hours. The recurring failure is not ignorance of the diagnoses but misreading the severity — anchoring on a benign explanation, over-relying on a single normal lab or radiograph, or under-appreciating that the very old, pregnant, immunocompromised, and diabetic present atypically with muted signs. This skill covers the disciplined, threat-first assessment of the acute abdomen: identifying the surgical abdomen, applying location-based and must-not-miss differentials, choosing imaging deliberately, and knowing exactly when to call surgery before completing the workup. It is written for the agent who must decide, under uncertainty, who can be observed and who must be in the operating room.

## Core Rules

### Identify the Surgical Abdomen First, Before Refining the Diagnosis

The first question in any acute abdomen is not "what is it?" but "does this patient need a surgeon, now?" Look systematically for peritonitis: generalized tenderness with involuntary guarding or rigidity, rebound, marked worsening on movement or cough, a rigid or board-like abdomen, and signs of systemic toxicity such as hypotension, tachycardia, fever, or altered mental status. These findings suggest perforation (perforated ulcer, appendicitis, diverticulitis, ischemic bowel with perforation) or intra-abdominal catastrophe requiring urgent surgical input — do not wait for imaging confirmation before involving surgery when peritonitis is present. Other surgical triggers include suspected obstruction with strangulation or ischemia, a tender pulsatile abdominal mass suggesting a ruptured aneurysm, and hemodynamic instability with abdominal signs. When in doubt, call surgery early; the cost of an unnecessary consult is trivial compared with a delayed laparotomy.

### Build the Differential by Location, but Never Abandon the Must-Not-Miss List

Location is a powerful organizing principle — right lower quadrant points to appendicitis, mesenteric adenitis, ovarian pathology, ureteric colic, or Crohn disease; right upper quadrant to biliary disease, hepatitis, or perforated ulcer; epigastrium to pancreatitis, ulcer disease, or an aortic catastrophe masquerading as a benign pain; left lower quadrant to diverticulitis, ischemic colitis, or ovarian pathology. But location-based reasoning fails when applied rigidly, because the elderly, diabetic, pregnant, and immunosuppressed present atypically, and because referred pain is common. Always run a parallel must-not-miss list independent of location: mesenteric ischemia, leaking or ruptured abdominal aortic aneurysm, perforated viscus, ectopic pregnancy in any woman of reproductive age, and occult sepsis from a urinary, biliary, or pelvic source. For each, state explicitly what would rule it in or out and what threshold mandates imaging or consultation.

### Recognize Mesenteric Ischemia Before It Is Too Late

Acute mesenteric ischemia is the classic must-not-miss diagnosis because it presents with pain out of proportion to examination and the window for salvage is short. Suspect it in any patient with severe acute abdominal pain and minimal tenderness on palpation, especially older patients with atrial fibrillation, heart failure, recent MI, vascular disease, or on vasoactive drugs, and in dialysis patients. Lactate rises late but is a red flag when elevated; metabolic acidosis, bloody diarrhea, and progression to peritonitis signal infarction. CT angiography is the diagnostic test of choice — do not settle for a standard portal-venous CT, which can miss the vascular problem. Treat this as a surgical and vascular emergency: involve surgery and interventional radiology early, resuscitate aggressively, give broad-spectrum antibiotics, and anticoagulate as appropriate. Delay for further tests once peritonitis develops is fatal.

### Distinguish True Obstruction from Ileus and Identify Strangulation

Bowel obstruction presents with colicky pain, distension, vomiting, and constipation, most often from adhesions, hernias, or malignancy. The key judgment is identifying the obstructed bowel that is strangulating or ischemic — continuous severe pain rather than colicky, marked tachycardia, fever, peritonitis, a tender irreducible hernia, or rising lactate mandate urgent surgery rather than conservative management. Plain films may show dilated loops and air-fluid levels but miss closed-loop obstruction and ischemia; CT is the workhorse for defining the level, cause, and signs of compromise. Postoperative or metabolic ileus can mimic obstruction but is managed conservatively with nasogastric decompression, fluid and electrolyte correction (especially potassium), and treating the precipitant — but never assume ileus in a patient with signs of strangulation.

### Choose Imaging Deliberately and Match It to the Question

Do not reflexively order a CT for every abdominal pain. Ultrasound is first-line for suspected biliary disease and is the preferred initial modality in pregnancy and many pediatric presentations; it is operator-dependent but avoids radiation. CT with intravenous contrast is the most informative single test for most undifferentiated acute abdomens in adults and is essential when mesenteric ischemia, perforation, or complicated obstruction is suspected — request the appropriate phase (arterial/CTA for vascular questions). Plain radiographs have a limited but real role (free air under the diaphragm, obstruction pattern, renal stones) but should not be the stopping point when the picture is serious. MRI is reserved for pregnancy when ultrasound is nondiagnostic. Always ask: what question am I trying to answer, and which modality answers it fastest given this patient's stability and pregnancy status? An unstable patient may need to go to the operating room with no imaging at all.

### Adjust for Special Populations Explicitly

The elderly frequently present with atypical or muted appendicitis, perforation, cholecystitis, and mesenteric ischemia, and can deteriorate rapidly with minimal signs — a low threshold for imaging and surgical involvement is mandatory. Pregnant patients require pregnancy-adjusted considerations: ectopic pregnancy must be excluded in any woman of reproductive age with abdominal pain, appendicitis is the most common non-obstetric surgical emergency and can present with right upper or flank pain as the uterus displaces the appendix, and imaging should prefer ultrasound then MRI over CT. Immunocompromised patients — neutropenic, transplant, on biologics or steroids — may have muted signs and unusual pathogens (neutropenic enterocolitis, CMV, fungal); maintain a low threshold for broad workup and infectious disease and surgical input. Diabetic and renal failure patients may not mount fever or typical pain. Never let a "well-looking" impression override risk factors.

## Common Traps

### Anchoring on a Benign Explanation

Attributing severe pain to gastroenteritis, constipation, or "gas" without ruling out a surgical cause. Counter by running the must-not-miss list regardless of how benign the picture first appears.

### Waiting for Imaging Before Calling Surgery in Frank Peritonitis

Peritonitis with instability is a surgical emergency. Involve surgery before or alongside imaging; do not let CT delay laparotomy.

### Missing Mesenteric Ischemia Because the Exam Is "Soft"

Pain out of proportion to examination is the hallmark, not a reassuring sign. Suspect ischemia in vascular and elderly patients and order CT angiography early.

### Accepting a Normal Plain Film as Reassurance

Plain radiographs miss closed-loop obstruction, early perforation, and ischemia. If the clinical picture is serious, escalate to CT with appropriate contrast phase.

### Forgetting Ectopic Pregnancy

Any woman of reproductive age with abdominal pain needs a pregnancy test, and a positive result with pain or instability is ectopic until proven otherwise. Do not attribute pain to a benign cause first.

### Underestimating the Muted Presentation in Elderly, Diabetic, or Immunosuppressed Patients

These groups may have perforation or infarction with minimal tenderness or no fever. Lower the threshold for imaging and surgical consultation.

### Treating Suspected Obstruction Conservatively Despite Signs of Strangulation

Continuous pain, tachycardia, fever, peritonitis, or rising lactate indicate ischemia and mandate surgery, not further observation.

## Self-Check

- Have I explicitly assessed for peritonitis and decided whether surgery is needed now, before refining the diagnosis?
- Have I built a location-based differential while running a parallel must-not-miss list (mesenteric ischemia, ruptured aneurysm, perforation, ectopic pregnancy, sepsis)?
- For any severe pain out of proportion to examination, especially in an elderly or vascular patient, have I considered mesenteric ischemia and ordered CT angiography early?
- For suspected obstruction, have I actively looked for signs of strangulation or ischemia that shift management from conservative to surgical?
- Have I chosen imaging deliberately — ultrasound for biliary and pregnancy, CT with the right contrast phase for most adults, and recognized when an unstable patient needs the operating room rather than a scanner?
- Have I adjusted my threshold for special populations — elderly, pregnant, immunocompromised, diabetic, renal failure — where presentations are muted and atypical?
- Have I excluded pregnancy in every woman of reproductive age before attributing pain to a benign cause?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
