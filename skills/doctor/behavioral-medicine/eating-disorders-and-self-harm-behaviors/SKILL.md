# Eating Disorders and Self-Harm Behaviors

Eating disorders sit at the dangerous intersection of psychiatric illness and life-threatening medical instability, and they are among the highest-mortality conditions in psychiatry. Anorexia nervosa carries a substantial mortality rate from medical complications and suicide, and the clinician who treats these patients as "just a mental health problem" will miss the cardiac, electrolyte, and metabolic crises that kill them. The recurring failure mode is not ignorance of the diagnosis — it is underestimating medical severity, dismissing weight loss as a lifestyle choice, delaying nutritional rehabilitation, and precipitating refeeding syndrome through incautious refeeding. A parallel danger exists in non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI): clinicians may either panic and over-manage, or minimize and miss the suicide risk that travels alongside it. This skill covers the disciplined medical evaluation of eating disorders, the recognition and prevention of refeeding syndrome, the criteria for escalation to inpatient or specialist eating-disorder care, and the calibrated assessment of self-harm. The clinician's role is to keep the patient medically safe, to avoid iatrogenic harm from over-rapid refeeding or punitive management, and to know when the problem has outgrown primary care and specialist psychiatric and nutritional teams must lead.

## Core Rules

### Medically Risk-Stratify Every Patient at Every Encounter

Eating disorders fluctuate, and a patient who was stable last week may be medically unstable today. At every contact, screen for the markers of medical instability: abnormal vital signs (bradycardia, hypotension, orthostatic changes, hypothermia), significant weight loss or percent of ideal body weight, syncope or near-syncope, chest pain or palpitations, severe electrolyte derangement (especially hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia, hypomagnesemia), prolonged QTc on ECG, evidence of dehydration, and acute medical complications such as hematemesis from Mallory-Weiss tears or esophageal rupture. Any one of these can be the herald of a fatal decompensation. Do not reassure yourself or the patient on appearance alone — patients with eating disorders can look deceptively well while being electrically and metabolically precarious. Document vital signs and weight trends, not just the patient's report.

### Order the Right Medical Workup, Not Everything

The medical evaluation of an eating disorder should be targeted: full blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel (electrolytes, renal function, hepatic function, glucose), magnesium, phosphate, calcium, thyroid function, ECG (for QTc and arrhythmia), and where indicated bone density (DEXA for chronic low weight) and amylase/lipase. The purpose is to detect complications and to establish a baseline for safe refeeding. Resist the reflex to order an exhaustive panel in search of an alternative explanation for weight loss — if the eating disorder is clear, over-investigation delays treatment and reinforces the patient's somatic focus. However, when the diagnosis is uncertain or weight loss is atypical, do investigate alternate causes (malignancy, hyperthyroidism, inflammatory bowel disease, celiac disease, infection) before attributing it to an eating disorder, because misattribution in either direction is dangerous.

### Recognize and Prevent Refeeding Syndrome

Refeeding syndrome is the most feared iatrogenic complication of eating disorder treatment, and it is preventable. It occurs when nutrition is reintroduced too rapidly after a period of starvation or significant weight loss: insulin surges drive intracellular shifts of phosphate, potassium, and magnesium, producing potentially fatal hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, fluid overload, thiamine depletion, and cardiac and neurological collapse. Identify high-risk patients before refeeding begins: those with very low body mass index, rapid or profound weight loss, minimal recent intake, pre-existing electrolyte abnormalities, or significant comorbidity. Start refeeding at a low caloric rate (commonly 15-20 kcal/kg/day, lower in highest-risk patients) and advance gradually; give thiamine before and during early refeeding; supplement phosphate, potassium, and magnesium prophylactically or promptly as levels fall; and monitor electrolytes frequently (often daily) during the first one to two weeks. Never assume a normal initial phosphate means the patient is safe — phosphate typically crashes two to three days into refeeding. When in doubt, refeed in a supervised inpatient setting with protocol-driven monitoring.

### Use Clear Criteria for Inpatient and Specialist Escalation

Know the thresholds for admission and specialist referral and apply them without delay. Indications for medical admission include severe malnutrition (commonly below 70-75% of ideal body weight, though thresholds vary by guideline), rapid weight loss, physiological instability (bradycardia, hypotension, orthostasis, hypothermia), significant electrolyte abnormalities, prolonged QTc, failure of outpatient management, refeeding risk requiring supervised nutrition, acute medical complications, and suicidality. Indications for psychiatric admission include severe psychiatric comorbidity, suicide risk, and inability to engage with outpatient care. Refer early to specialist eating-disorder services — these conditions are difficult to manage in nonspecialist settings, and outcomes improve with multidisciplinary care involving psychiatry, dietetics, psychology, and medicine. Do not let a patient deteriorate while you attempt yet another outpatient adjustment.

### Assess Self-Harm With Calibrated Risk Stratification

Non-suicidal self-injury is common, particularly in adolescents and young adults, and it requires a structured rather than reactive assessment. Distinguish NSSI (intentional self-injury without suicidal intent, often a coping mechanism) from suicide attempts, while recognizing that NSSI is itself a risk factor for future suicidal behavior and must never be dismissed. Assess the frequency, methods, triggers, and functions of the self-harm; the presence and severity of suicidal ideation and any planning or past attempts; access to lethal means; co-occurring mental health disorders (depression, anxiety, borderline personality features, PTSD); protective factors; and recent psychosocial stressors. Use validated tools where available. Remove or secure lethal means as a concrete risk-reduction step. Arrange appropriate follow-up — brief intervention may suffice for low-risk, isolated NSSI, while recurrent self-harm, suicidality, or underlying psychiatric illness requires mental health referral and often a safety plan.

### Engage the Family and Support System Safely

For adolescents and dependent adults, the family or support system is central to both safety and recovery. Involve them in monitoring meals, recognizing warning signs, securing means of self-harm, and supporting treatment — while respecting confidentiality and autonomy appropriate to the patient's age and circumstances. For adults, assess capacity and the role of supports with the patient's consent. Psychoeducation for families reduces the blame and conflict that worsen outcomes. Recognize that families are often frightened, exhausted, and in need of their own support; direct them to appropriate resources rather than leaving them to manage crises alone.

### Avoid the Lifestyle-Choice Framing

Eating disorders are not choices, vanity, or phases. Framing them as such — by the clinician, the family, or the patient — delays treatment and deepens shame. Use biopsychosocial language that acknowledges the serious, treatable nature of the illness. At the same time, do not collude with the patient's minimization ("I'm fine," "I just have a fast metabolism") when objective signs indicate instability. Hold both truths: the illness is real and serious, and the patient retains dignity and the potential for recovery.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Weight Loss as a Lifestyle Choice

Attributing significant weight loss to dieting, exercise, or stress without investigating an eating disorder or medical cause. This delays diagnosis until the patient is medically unstable. Counter by taking unexplained or rapid weight loss seriously and assessing for eating disorder features directly and without judgment.

### Underestimating Medical Severity by Appearance

Reassuring oneself because the patient "looks okay" while ignoring bradycardia, orthostasis, electrolyte derangement, or prolonged QTc. Patients can be electrically and metabolically unstable while appearing deceptively well. Counter by relying on objective vital signs, ECG, and labs, not appearance.

### Precipitating Refeeding Syndrome Through Incautious Refeeding

Starting full nutrition abruptly in a starved patient, or assuming an initially normal phosphate means the patient is safe. Refeeding syndrome is preventable and potentially fatal. Counter by identifying high-risk patients, starting low and advancing gradually, supplementing thiamine and electrolytes, and monitoring closely — refeed inpatient when risk is high.

### Delaying Admission While Adjusting Outpatient Care

Repeatedly tweaking the outpatient plan as the patient deteriorates, hoping to avoid hospitalization. This costs time the patient may not have. Counter by applying admission criteria early and escalating to specialist eating-disorder services without waiting for a crisis.

### Missing Suicide Risk Behind Eating Disorder or Self-Harm

Focusing on the eating disorder or the self-injury and failing to assess suicidality directly. Eating disorders carry high suicide rates, and NSSI is a risk factor for future suicidal behavior. Counter by explicitly assessing suicidal ideation, planning, past attempts, and access to means, and by securing lethal means.

### Over-Managing or Under-Managing Self-Harm

Either reacting to every instance of NSSI with emergency-level intervention that overwhelms services and alienates the patient, or dismissing it as attention-seeking and missing escalating risk. Counter by using structured risk assessment to calibrate the response to the actual risk level.

### Investigating Exhaustively for Alternate Diagnoses When the Eating Disorder Is Clear

Ordering scan after scan in a patient with a clear eating disorder, delaying nutritional treatment and reinforcing somatic preoccupation. Counter by performing a focused workup and, when the diagnosis is clear, moving to treatment while remaining alert to atypical features that warrant reconsideration.

## Self-Check

- Have I medically risk-stratified this patient at this encounter using objective vital signs, weight trend, ECG, and electrolytes, rather than relying on appearance or the patient's self-report?
- Have I ordered a focused, appropriate workup (metabolic panel, magnesium, phosphate, ECG, and where indicated thyroid and DEXA) and avoided both under-investigation of atypical presentations and exhaustive over-investigation when the diagnosis is clear?
- For any patient being refed, have I assessed refeeding risk, planned a low starting caloric rate with gradual advancement, arranged thiamine and electrolyte supplementation, and scheduled close monitoring of phosphate, potassium, and magnesium?
- Have I applied admission and specialist referral criteria and escalated to inpatient or eating-disorder specialist care without delay when physiological instability, severe malnutrition, or failed outpatient management is present?
- For self-harm, have I distinguished NSSI from suicidal behavior, assessed frequency, method, triggers, suicidal ideation, planning, past attempts, access to means, comorbidity, and protective factors, and secured lethal means?
- Have I involved the family or support system appropriately, provided psychoeducation, and connected them to support resources while respecting confidentiality and autonomy?
- Have I framed the illness as a serious, treatable condition rather than a lifestyle choice or character flaw, and avoided colluding with the patient's minimization?
- Have I explicitly assessed suicidality and not allowed the eating disorder or self-injury to mask the suicide risk that travels with it?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as physiological instability, severe electrolyte derangement, prolonged QTc, refeeding risk, or suicidality appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
