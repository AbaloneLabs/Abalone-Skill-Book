# Multidisciplinary Rounds

Multidisciplinary rounds are the central coordinating mechanism for complex inpatient and team-based care, and when done well they reduce length of stay, readmissions, errors, and harm. When done poorly — which is the default in many institutions — they become a ritual that consumes time, generates no shared plan, leaves disciplines siloed, and produces a chart note rather than coordinated care. The recurring failure is to let rounds be physician-centered: the doctor presents, the team listens, the plan is pronounced, and the disciplines that hold critical information — nursing, pharmacy, physical therapy, social work, nutrition, care coordination — are reduced to spectators or never speak. A second failure is to round without structure: the team wanders through the patient list, plans are inconsistent, discharge criteria are never stated, and the same issues recur day after day without resolution. A third failure is to round without follow-through: a plan is discussed but never documented, never assigned to an owner, and never executed, so rounds become theater. This skill covers the disciplined practice of effective multidisciplinary rounds: structuring the round, ensuring every discipline contributes, driving toward an explicit daily plan and discharge criteria, managing disagreement, documenting and assigning ownership, and using rounds as the engine of coordinated care. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; adapt to institutional structure and engage the full team for complex patients.

## Core Rules

### Structure the Round Around a Shared Framework

An effective round follows a consistent structure so that every team member knows what to expect and what to contribute. A useful structure moves through patient identification, the clinical summary, the active issues, the plan for each issue, the anticipated discharge and its barriers, and the specific actions assigned to owners. Use a structured tool — a daily goals sheet, a standardized rounding template, or a structured handover format — so that the round produces a shared mental model rather than a series of disconnected statements. The structure should be brief enough to fit the patient load and complete enough to cover the critical questions. Avoid the two failure modes: rounds so unstructured that nothing is decided, and rounds so rigidly templated that clinical judgment and team input are suffocated.

### Ensure Every Discipline Contributes Information

The core premise of multidisciplinary rounds is that each discipline holds information the others lack, and that integrating it produces better care. Nursing knows what happened overnight, what the patient actually did, and what the barriers to mobility and self-care are. Pharmacy knows the medication regimen, the interactions, the renal dosing, and the adherence issues. Physical and occupational therapy know the functional trajectory and the discharge barriers. Social work and care coordination know the home situation, the insurance constraints, and the placement options. Nutrition knows the intake and the deficits. Specialists know their domain. Deliberately invite each discipline to contribute — by name if necessary — and create a culture where speaking up is expected, not optional. A round in which only the physician speaks has forfeited the reason for the round.

### Drive Toward an Explicit Daily Plan and Discharge Criteria

The two questions every round must answer for every patient are: what is the plan for today, and what will it take to discharge this patient safely? State the daily plan explicitly — what tests, what consultations, what medications, what level of activity, what education — and assign each element to an owner. State the discharge criteria explicitly — what physiologic, functional, social, and logistical milestones must be met — so that the entire team works toward the same goal rather than each discipline assuming someone else is handling the barrier. Anticipate discharge from the day of admission, identify the rate-limiting step (a procedure, a placement, a functional milestone, a family meeting), and attack that step rather than waiting for it to resolve itself. Patients languish in hospital because no one named the discharge barrier and assigned someone to solve it.

### Manage Disagreement Openly and Constructively

When disciplines disagree — about readiness for discharge, about the medication plan, about goals of care, about whether the patient is deteriorating — surface the disagreement in the round rather than suppressing it. Disagreement is data; it usually reflects that different team members see different facets of the patient, and resolving it produces a better plan than any single perspective. Avoid both the failure of the physician overriding by hierarchy without engaging the dissent, and the failure of chronic unresolved disagreement that paralyzes the plan. Use the structured round to make the disagreement explicit, gather the relevant information, decide, and document the reasoning. Where the disagreement reflects a genuine values or goals-of-care question, bring it to the patient and family rather than resolving it by clinician preference.

### Document the Plan and Assign Ownership

A plan discussed but not documented and not assigned is a plan that will not be executed. Document the daily plan, the discharge criteria, the anticipated barriers, and the specific actions with named owners and timeframes. Use a shared, visible artifact — a daily goals sheet, a rounding note, a shared board — so that the entire team can see and execute the plan. Close the loop on prior days' actions: was the consult obtained, the test done, the family meeting held, the medication changed? Rounds that do not review prior actions repeat the same unresolved issues day after day.

### Protect Time, Focus, and Psychological Safety

Effective rounds require protected time (no interruptions, no competing demands), focused attention (devices down, eyes on the patient and team), and psychological safety (every team member can speak, ask questions, and raise concerns without penalty). The senior clinician sets the tone: invite input, thank contributors, ask for dissent, and never punish a team member for raising a concern. Rounds conducted at speed, with the senior clinician distracted or dismissive, generate poor plans and suppress the very input that would prevent harm. Where rounds are conducted at the bedside, introduce the team, involve the patient and family in the plan, and respect dignity and confidentiality.

### Use Rounds to Drive Coordination, Not Just Reporting

The purpose of rounds is not to report status but to coordinate action. Every round should answer: what needs to happen today, who will do it, by when, and what could go wrong. Use rounds to sequence the day (the imaging before the consult, the procedure before the transfer, the family meeting before the goals-of-care decision), to identify and mitigate risks (the patient at risk of deterioration, the medication at risk of error, the discharge at risk of failure), and to align the team on priorities. Rounds that merely report what already happened are a missed opportunity; rounds that drive what happens next are the engine of coordinated care.

### Adapt the Round to the Patient and the Setting

Not every patient needs the same depth of rounding. Tier the intensity: complex, unstable, or discharge-barrier-heavy patients get full multidisciplinary discussion; stable patients awaiting a single milestone get a focused update. Adapt the round to the setting — ICU rounds emphasize physiology and the daily plan; ward rounds emphasize discharge and function; rehabilitation rounds emphasize goals and therapy; outpatient team huddles emphasize coordination and panel management. The principles are constant, but the structure should fit the work.

## Common Traps

### Physician-Centered Rounds

The doctor presents and pronounces, and the disciplines that hold critical information never speak. Counter by deliberately inviting each discipline to contribute and building a culture of expected input.

### Unstructured Wandering

Rounds without a framework, producing inconsistent plans and unresolved recurring issues. Counter by using a consistent structure that covers the summary, the issues, the plan, the discharge criteria, and the assigned actions.

### No Stated Discharge Criteria

Rounds that never name what it will take to discharge the patient, so barriers accumulate unaddressed. Counter by stating discharge criteria explicitly and attacking the rate-limiting barrier.

### Discussed but Undocumented Plans

Plans that are talked about but never written or assigned, so they are not executed. Counter by documenting the plan, assigning owners and timeframes, and using a shared visible artifact.

### Hierarchy Overriding Dissent

The senior clinician overriding a dissenting discipline without engaging the concern. Counter by surfacing disagreement openly, gathering information, deciding with reasoning, and bringing values questions to the patient.

### Rounds as Reporting, Not Coordination

Rounds that report what happened rather than driving what happens next. Counter by sequencing the day, identifying risks, and aligning priorities for coordinated action.

### Rushed, Distracted, or Unsafe Rounds

Rounds at speed with distracted or dismissive leadership, suppressing input and generating poor plans. Counter by protecting time, focusing attention, and building psychological safety.
