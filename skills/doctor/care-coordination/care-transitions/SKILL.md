# Care Transitions

The transition between care settings — hospital discharge, transfer to a skilled nursing facility, emergency department discharge, ICU step-down, or referral between clinicians — is the single most dangerous moment in a patient's encounter with the healthcare system. The recurring failure is to treat transition as an administrative event rather than a clinical one: the patient is "discharged," paperwork is generated, and the assumption is that care continues seamlessly, when in reality information is lost, medications are changed without reconciliation, pending results are orphaned, follow-up is assumed but not arranged, and the patient goes home without understanding what to do. The result is a predictable cluster of harms: adverse drug events, readmissions, missed dangerous results, abandoned workups, and patients lost to follow-up who return sicker or do not return at all. A second failure is to discharge to the next setting without a genuine handover — the receiving clinician inherits a patient they do not know, with a problem list and medication regimen they did not design, and often with critical context missing. A third failure is to ignore the patient's actual capacity to manage at home: the frail elder sent home alone with twelve medications and no follow-up, the patient who cannot afford the discharge prescription, the family caregiver who was never taught the wound care. This skill covers the disciplined practice of safe care transitions: structured handover, medication reconciliation, closing the loop on pending results, arranging concrete follow-up, educating the patient and caregiver, and addressing the social and practical conditions that determine whether the transition succeeds. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; engage case management, social work, pharmacy, and the receiving team for complex transitions.

## Core Rules

### Treat Transition as a Clinical Event, Not an Administrative One

A transition is a high-risk clinical moment that demands the same rigor as admission. Plan the transition from the beginning of the encounter, not at the moment of discharge: what will the patient need at home, what follow-up must be arranged, what results are pending, who will own the ongoing care, what education does the patient and caregiver need. Build the transition into the daily plan, revisit it at every handover, and do not let discharge be driven by bed pressure or administrative timing rather than clinical readiness. A transition made under pressure, without preparation, is the prototype of an unsafe one.

### Reconcile Medications at Every Transition

Medication discrepancy is the most common and most preventable source of post-transition harm. Reconcile medications at admission, at every transfer, and at discharge: compare what the patient was actually taking (not what the chart says — verify with the patient, family, pharmacy, and prior records) against what is now prescribed, identify and resolve every discrepancy, and communicate the final list explicitly to the patient and the next clinician. Pay particular attention to high-risk medications — anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, sedatives, immunosuppressants — where errors cause serious harm. Provide the patient with a written, plain-language medication list, explain what to take, what has changed, and why, and confirm understanding with teach-back. Ensure the patient can obtain the medications — a prescription the patient cannot afford or cannot fill is a transition failure.

### Close the Loop on Pending Results and the Workup

A patient discharged with pending lab results, cultures, biopsies, or imaging is a patient at risk of a missed dangerous finding, and the failure is reliably systemic: the result returns to a clinician who no longer owns the patient, or to no one. At every transition, explicitly identify every pending result, assign a named responsible clinician to follow it up, define the action thresholds, document the handover of responsibility, and communicate the plan to the patient. Do not assume the receiving team will check; make the transfer explicit. Build and use tracking systems so that no pending result is lost. A culture that grew after discharge, a biopsy that returns malignant, an imaging finding that requires follow-up — these are the classic transition failures, and they are entirely preventable with closed-loop design.

### Arrange Concrete, Actionable Follow-Up

"Follow up with your primary care provider" is not a follow-up plan; it is a hope. Specify who the patient should see, when, for what purpose, and how the appointment will be made. For high-risk patients — recent heart failure exacerbation, new anticoagulation, complex medication changes, fragile social situation — arrange an early, often within seven days, follow-up, and consider a bridging contact (a phone call, a home visit, a pharmacist review) in the interval. Ensure the receiving clinician receives a timely, structured summary of the encounter, the discharge medications, the pending results, and the follow-up plan. Verify that the patient has the means to attend the appointment — transportation, insurance, an accessible location. A follow-up plan the patient cannot execute is no plan.

### Communicate a Structured Handover to the Receiving Team

The receiving clinician needs a structured, accurate, timely summary — not a copy of the entire chart. Use a consistent framework (such as I-PASS: Illness severity, Patient summary, Action list, Situation awareness, Synthesis by receiver) that conveys the diagnosis, the course, the current status, the active issues, the medications, the pending results, the anticipated problems, and the plan. Communicate the handover directly where possible — a conversation is far more reliable than a note alone. Ensure the receiving team has the opportunity to ask questions and to confirm understanding. A handover that is one-way, unstructured, or buried in a long note is a handover that will fail at the moment it matters.

### Educate the Patient and Caregiver in Plain Language

The patient and their caregiver are the constant across the transition, and their understanding determines whether the plan is executed. Teach, in plain language and with teach-back confirmation, the diagnosis, the warning signs that should prompt return, the medication regimen, the follow-up plan, and the self-care tasks (wound care, injections, monitoring). Provide written instructions at an appropriate literacy level and in the patient's language, using a professional interpreter where needed. Involve the family caregiver directly — they are often the one who will actually manage the care at home. A patient who does not understand the plan, or who cannot articulate when to seek help, is at high risk of a failed transition.

### Address the Social and Practical Conditions for Success

The clinical plan succeeds or fails in the patient's actual living conditions. Assess and address, before discharge, whether the patient has a safe place to go, the ability to perform activities of daily living, the support of family or paid caregivers, the financial means to obtain medications and equipment, transportation to follow-up, and access to food and shelter. Engage case management, social work, home health, and community resources early, not at the moment of discharge. A clinically perfect plan sent into an unsupported home is a transition that will fail. For patients with complex needs, structured transitional care management — with a dedicated coordinator, home visits, and close follow-up — reduces readmissions and harm substantially.

### Use Structured Transition Tools and Checklists

Transition safety improves with structure. Use discharge checklists, standardized handover templates, medication reconciliation forms, teach-back protocols, and transitional care management programs. These tools compensate for the cognitive limits of clinicians working under pressure and ensure that critical steps are not omitted. Institutions with rigorous transition protocols have measurably lower readmission and adverse event rates. Use the tools available, and advocate for their improvement where they are weak.

## Common Traps

### Treating Discharge as Administrative

Rushing discharge under bed pressure without preparing the transition. Counter by planning the transition from admission and discharging on clinical readiness.

### Medication Discrepancy

Failing to reconcile medications against what the patient actually takes, causing duplication, omission, or interaction. Counter by reconciling at every transition, verifying with the patient and pharmacy, and teaching with teach-back.

### Orphaned Pending Results

Discharging with pending labs, cultures, or biopsies and no named owner. Counter by identifying pending results, assigning a responsible clinician, defining action thresholds, and documenting the handover.

### Vague Follow-Up Instructions

"Follow up with your doctor" without a specific who, when, and how. Counter by arranging concrete appointments, verifying the patient can attend, and providing bridging contact for high-risk patients.

### One-Way, Unstructured Handover

Dumping a long note on the receiving team without a structured summary or direct communication. Counter by using a consistent handover framework and direct conversation with teach-back.

### Patient Who Does Not Understand the Plan

Discharging without plain-language education and teach-back, leaving the patient unable to execute the plan or recognize warning signs. Counter by teaching in the patient's language, involving caregivers, and confirming understanding.

### Ignoring Social Determinants

Discharging into an unsupported home the plan cannot survive. Counter by assessing housing, support, finances, and transportation, and engaging case management and community resources.

## Self-Check

- Did I plan the transition from the start of the encounter and discharge on clinical readiness, not administrative pressure?
- Did I reconcile medications at every transition, verify with the patient and pharmacy, resolve discrepancies, and teach the final list with teach-back?
- For every pending result, did I identify it, assign a named responsible clinician, define action thresholds, and document the handover?
- Did I arrange concrete follow-up — who, when, for what, how — and verify the patient can attend, with bridging contact for high-risk patients?
- Did I communicate a structured handover to the receiving team using a consistent framework, with direct conversation and confirmation of understanding?
- Did I educate the patient and caregiver in plain language, with teach-back, written instructions in their language, and clear warning signs for return?
- Did I assess and address housing, activities of daily living, caregiver support, finances, transportation, and access to medications and food?
- Did I use structured transition tools and checklists, and engage case management, social work, pharmacy, and home health for complex transitions?
