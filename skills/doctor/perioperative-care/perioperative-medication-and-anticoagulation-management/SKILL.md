# Perioperative Medication and Anticoagulation Management

Perioperative medication management is the discipline of weighing, for every chronic drug, the risk of continuing it against the risk of holding it — and getting that balance wrong is a leading cause of preventable perioperative death. The two catastrophic failures are mirror images of each other: holding an anticoagulant in a patient with a mechanical mitral valve or a recent coronary stent and causing a fatal thrombosis, or continuing an anticoagulant through a high-bleeding-risk procedure and causing a fatal hemorrhage. Between these extremes lies a set of decisions that look routine — when to hold metformin, how to manage basal insulin, whether to give stress-dose steroids — but each carries its own failure mode. The disciplined clinician treats every perioperative medication decision as an explicit risk-benefit calculation anchored to the indication, the pharmacology, and the procedural bleeding risk, documents the plan, and escalates to cardiology, hematology, or endocrinology whenever the thrombotic or bleeding stakes are high. This skill covers anticoagulant and antiplatelet bridging, diabetes and insulin management, steroid and immunosuppressant timing, and the resumption decisions that complete the perioperative arc.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Every Decision to the Indication, Not the Drug

The first question for any perioperative medication is not "should I hold this drug?" but "what happens to the underlying disease if I hold it?" A patient on warfarin for a remote deep vein thrombosis is in a very different risk category from a patient on warfarin for a mechanical mitral valve, even though the drug is identical. Establish the indication, the recency of the event (a VTE within three months carries far higher recurrence risk than one five years ago), the consequence of thrombosis (catastrophic for a mechanical valve or recent stroke), and the consequence of bleeding for the planned procedure. Only after the indication is clear can the holding or bridging decision be made. A medication plan that does not state the indication is incomplete and dangerous.

### Stratify Thrombotic Risk to Decide Whether to Bridge Anticoagulation

For patients on warfarin, the decision to bridge with heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin during the perioperative interruption depends on thrombotic risk. High-risk patients — mechanical mitral valves, any mechanical valve with prior stroke or TIA, atrial fibrillation with high CHA2DS2-VASc (generally greater than 6, or prior stroke), or VTE within three months — usually require bridging. Moderate-risk patients require individualized decisions, often with cardiology or hematology input. Low-risk patients (atrial fibrillation with low CHA2DS2-VASc, remote VTE) generally do not require bridging, and bridging them exposes them to bleeding without thrombotic benefit. Stop warfarin approximately five days before surgery, check the INR near the time of surgery to confirm it has normalized, resume warfarin the evening of surgery or the next day if hemostasis is adequate, and bridge only those who truly need it. Bridging is not a default — it is a calculated risk for a calculated benefit.

### Manage Direct Oral Anticoagulants by Renal Function and Bleeding Risk

Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs — dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban) differ from warfarin in that they do not require bridging, because of their rapid onset and offset. The holding interval depends on the drug, the renal function, and the bleeding risk of the procedure: typically one to two days for low-bleeding-risk procedures and two to four days for high-bleeding-risk procedures, with longer holds for dabigatran in renal impairment because it is predominantly renally cleared. Do not bridge DOACs in most patients — bridging adds bleeding without preventing thrombosis. Resume DOACs after surgery only when hemostasis is secure, typically the evening of or the day after low-bleeding-risk procedures, and delayed by 48 to 72 hours after high-bleeding-risk procedures. Renal function must be checked within weeks of surgery to set the correct interval, and the absence of a reliable reversal agent for some DOACs (outside idarucizumab for dabigatran and andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors) raises the stakes of premature resumption.

### Never Stop Antiplatelets Prematurely After Coronary Stenting

The most dangerous antiplatelet error is stopping dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) within the period of stent thrombosis risk. For drug-eluting stents, the highest-risk window is generally the first six months, and elective non-cardiac surgery should be deferred for at least six months (and ideally twelve) after placement; for bare-metal stents, the window is at least 30 days. If surgery cannot be deferred within these windows, continue aspirin throughout the perioperative period whenever possible and resume the second agent as soon as hemostasis allows. For patients on DAPT for a recent acute coronary syndrome, defer elective surgery for at least two to three months and involve cardiology for any earlier operation. For patients on single antiplatelet therapy for primary or secondary prevention, the decision to hold aspirin depends on the indication and procedural bleeding risk — aspirin can often be continued for many procedures, but is held for high-bleeding-risk surgery such as neurosurgery or posterior chamber ophthalmic surgery. Cardiology involvement is mandatory whenever stent thrombosis is a possibility.

### Manage Perioperative Diabetes by Procedure Type and Glycemic Target

Perioperative diabetes management aims to avoid hypoglycemia (more dangerous than transient hyperglycemia) and severe hyperglycemia (which impairs wound healing and immunity). For patients on oral agents, hold metformin on the morning of surgery and resume when renal function is stable and oral intake resumes (concern for lactic acidosis with contrast or perioperative renal injury drives this hold), and hold sulfonylureas on the morning of surgery to avoid hypoglycemia. For insulin-treated patients, reduce the basal insulin dose the night before or morning of surgery (typically to 75 to 80 percent of the usual basal dose) to avoid hypoglycemia while fasting, hold short-acting or rapid-acting insulin while the patient is nil by mouth, and use a variable-rate intravenous insulin infusion (insulin drip) for patients with type 1 diabetes undergoing major surgery, patients with poorly controlled diabetes, or prolonged fasting. Type 1 diabetes must never be without insulin — basal insulin or an infusion must continue to prevent diabetic ketoacidosis. Target glucose is generally 140 to 180 mg/dL perioperatively; tighter control increases hypoglycemia risk without outcome benefit.

### Give Stress-Dose Steroids for Patients on Chronic Glucocorticoids

Patients on chronic glucocorticoids have suppression of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis and may develop adrenal insufficiency under surgical stress. The historical practice of large intravenous stress doses for all patients has been refined: patients on the equivalent of prednisone 20 mg or more per day for three weeks or longer within the past year, and patients with confirmed adrenal insufficiency, require perioperative stress-dose glucocorticoids. For moderate surgery, a single intravenous dose of hydrocortisone (for example 50 mg at induction) followed by resumption of the usual dose is often sufficient; for major surgery, a higher dose on the day of surgery tapering over one to two days is typical. Patients on lower doses for shorter durations usually do not require stress doses and can continue their usual dose. Do not abruptly stop chronic steroids perioperatively under any circumstances, as this precipitates adrenal crisis.

### Time Immunosuppressants to the Surgery and the Indication

For transplant recipients and patients on immunosuppressants for autoimmune disease, the perioperative plan depends on the regimen, the stability of the underlying condition, and the type of surgery. Most maintenance immunosuppressants (calcineurin inhibitors, mycophenolate, azathioprine) are continued through surgery to prevent rejection or flare, with dose adjustment only for specific toxicity. Biologic agents are typically held around surgery to reduce wound-healing and infection risk, with timing guided by the dosing interval and the activity of the disease — involve the transplant or rheumatology team for any decision to hold or delay. Methotrexate is often held for one to two weeks around major surgery in patients with rheumatoid disease, particularly in the setting of renal dysfunction or infection, but continued for many minor procedures. Never independently stop a transplant patient's immunosuppression — rejection risk is real, and the specialist team must be involved.

### Plan Resumption Explicitly and Document It

The perioperative medication plan is incomplete without a documented resumption plan. For each held medication, specify when it will be resumed, the condition for resumption (typically adequate hemostasis and oral intake), and any dose adjustment. Anticoagulants resumed too early cause bleeding; resumed too late cause thrombosis. Metformin resumed before renal function is confirmed risks lactic acidosis. Insulin regimens must be adjusted back to the outpatient plan only when the patient is eating normally and recovering. Write the resumption plan in the orders and in the discharge summary, communicate it to the patient, and ensure the outpatient prescriber is informed — the perioperative interruption is a common point of medication error after discharge.

### Escalate to Specialists When the Stakes Are High

Perioperative medication decisions that involve mechanical valves, recent coronary stents, recent VTE, severe renal impairment, complex insulin regimens, transplant immunosuppression, or high-bleeding-risk surgery in a patient who cannot safely stop anticoagulation require specialist input. Cardiology for stent and antiplatelet decisions, hematology or anticoagulation clinics for complex bridging, endocrinology for brittle diabetes, nephrology for renal-dependent dosing, and the transplant or rheumatology team for immunosuppression. Do not make these decisions in isolation, and document the consultation and the agreed plan. When in doubt, an anesthesia or perioperative medicine consultation brings the team together.

## Common Traps

### Bridging Every Patient on Warfarin

Defaulting to bridging out of caution. Counter by stratifying thrombotic risk and bridging only high-risk patients, because bridging low-risk patients causes bleeding without preventing thrombosis.

### Using the Same Hold Interval for Every DOAC

Applying a single holding rule regardless of drug or renal function. Counter by setting the interval by drug, renal function, and procedural bleeding risk, with longer holds for dabigatran in renal impairment.

### Stopping DAPT Within the Stent Thrombosis Window

Electively stopping dual antiplatelet therapy within six months of a drug-eluting stent. Counter by deferring elective surgery, continuing aspirin if surgery is unavoidable, and involving cardiology.

### Forgetting Type 1 Diabetes Needs Continuous Insulin

Withholding all insulin in a fasting type 1 diabetic. Counter by continuing basal insulin or an infusion to prevent diabetic ketoacidosis.

### Abruptly Stopping Chronic Steroids

Discontinuing glucocorticoids perioperatively. Counter by continuing the usual dose and giving stress-dose hydrocortisone for eligible patients.

### Independently Stopping Transplant Immunosuppression

Holding calcineurin inhibitors or mycophenolate without consulting the transplant team. Counter by involving the specialist team for any immunosuppression change.

### Resuming Anticoagulants Without Confirming Hemostasis

Restarting warfarin or DOACs on a fixed schedule regardless of bleeding. Counter by making resumption conditional on adequate hemostasis and documenting the trigger.

### Failing to Document the Resumption Plan

Leaving the postoperative team without a clear plan for restarting held medications. Counter by writing the resumption plan in orders, discharge summary, and outpatient communication.

## Self-Check

- For every chronic medication, have I stated the indication and the consequence of holding it, rather than deciding by drug name alone?
- For warfarin, have I stratified thrombotic risk and bridged only high-risk patients, with an INR check near the time of surgery?
- For DOACs, have I set the holding interval by drug, renal function, and procedural bleeding risk, and avoided routine bridging?
- For antiplatelets, have I confirmed no elective surgery within the stent thrombosis window, continued aspirin where appropriate, and involved cardiology for any decision involving recent stents or acute coronary syndrome?
- For diabetes, have I avoided hypoglycemia, continued basal insulin or an infusion in type 1 diabetes, and set a perioperative glucose target of roughly 140 to 180 mg/dL?
- For chronic steroids, have I continued the usual dose and given stress-dose hydrocortisone to eligible patients rather than stopping abruptly?
- For immunosuppressants, have I involved the transplant or rheumatology team before any change and continued maintenance therapy unless the specialist advises otherwise?
- Have I documented an explicit resumption plan for every held medication, with the trigger condition and the outpatient follow-up?
- Have I escalated to cardiology, hematology, endocrinology, nephrology, or the transplant team for high-stakes decisions rather than managing them in isolation?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as mechanical valves, recent stents, recent VTE, severe renal impairment, or high-bleeding-risk surgery appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
