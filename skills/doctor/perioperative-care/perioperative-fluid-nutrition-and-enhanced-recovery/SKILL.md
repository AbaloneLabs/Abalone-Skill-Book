# Perioperative Fluid, Nutrition, and Enhanced Recovery

Perioperative fluid and nutrition management has been transformed by enhanced recovery after surgery (ERAS) pathways, and the central tension is no longer "aggressive resuscitation versus dehydration" but "evidence-based protocolization versus blind tradition." The danger now cuts both ways. Clinicians who cling to outdated practices — prolonged fasting, liberal crystalloid, routine nasogastric tubes, days of bed rest — cause harm through ileus, edema, infection, and catabolism. Clinicians who apply ERAS rigidly to patients for whom it was never designed — the malnourished, the septic, the patient with a major leak or hemorrhage — cause harm by starving a patient who cannot afford it or by withholding fluid from a hypovolemic patient. The disciplined clinician understands the physiology behind each ERAS element, applies the protocol to the patient it fits, recognizes immediately when the patient deviates from the expected recovery, and knows when to abandon the pathway and escalate. This skill covers goal-directed fluid therapy, ERAS principles, early feeding, drain and tube management, malnutrition, and the decision points for deviation and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Understand the Physiology Behind Each ERAS Element

Enhanced recovery pathways are not a checklist of preferences — each element counters a specific harm of traditional surgical care. Avoidance of prolonged fasting and carbohydrate loading reduces insulin resistance and catabolism. Goal-directed fluid therapy prevents both hypovolemia (which causes hypoperfusion and acute kidney injury) and fluid overload (which causes tissue edema, delayed wound healing, and ileus). Early mobilization prevents DVT, atelectasis, deconditioning, and insulin resistance. Early feeding supports gut mucosal integrity, reduces bacterial translocation, and shortens ileus. Avoidance of routine nasogastric tubes reduces pneumonia and length of stay. When you understand the why, you can recognize when a patient should not receive an element (the malnourished patient who needs more, the patient with a leak who must be nil by mouth) rather than applying or abandoning the pathway reflexively. Never treat an ERAS pathway as a rigid script; treat it as a default that must be modified for the patient in front of you.

### Use Goal-Directed Fluid Therapy Rather Than Fixed Boluses

The single most important fluid principle is that perioperative fluid must be matched to the patient's physiology, not given by formula. Both hypovolemia and fluid overload cause harm: hypovolemia leads to hypoperfusion, acute kidney injury, and organ dysfunction, while overload causes pulmonary and tissue edema, prolonged ileus, and impaired wound healing. Goal-directed fluid therapy uses dynamic markers — stroke volume variation, pulse pressure variation, cardiac output where available, and clinical markers such as urine output, lactate, and capillary refill — to titrate fluid to a euvolemic state. In the operating room this means small boluses guided by responsiveness rather than large fixed infusions; on the ward it means assessing volume status clinically and giving fluid only when the patient is hypovolemic, rather than maintaining "maintenance" fluid that accumulates insensibly. A patient who is hypotensive after surgery is not always hypovolemic — assess before bolusing, because fluid overload in a patient with myocardial dysfunction or septic capillary leak is its own disaster.

### Avoid Both Prolonged Fasting and Premature Feeding in the Wrong Patient

ERAS pathways favor clear fluids until two hours before anesthesia and solids until six hours, with early feeding (often within hours of surgery) for patients undergoing elective colorectal and other gastrointestinal surgery. Early feeding reduces ileus and length of stay in appropriately selected patients. But early feeding is not universal: patients with anastomotic leak, prolonged ileus, bowel obstruction, hemodynamic instability, or postoperative nausea and vomiting that prevents intake should not be force-fed. The principle is to feed when the gut works and the patient is stable, and to provide nutrition support — enteral first, parenteral when enteral fails or is contraindicated — when oral intake is not feasible within several days. Starving a stable ERAS patient out of tradition is harmful; force-feeding a patient with ileus or leak is equally harmful.

### Identify and Optimize Malnutrition Before Elective Surgery

Malnutrition is one of the strongest predictors of postoperative complications — infection, leak, wound dehiscence, prolonged length of stay, and mortality — and it is routinely under-recognized. Screen every surgical patient for malnutrition using validated tools (Subjective Global Assessment, MUST, or the GLIM criteria), looking for recent unintentional weight loss (5 percent or more in one to three months, or 10 percent over six months), low body mass index, reduced intake, disease-related catabolism, and low serum albumin or prealbumin in context. For moderately to severely malnourished patients undergoing elective major surgery, provide 7 to 14 days of preoperative nutrition support (oral supplements, enteral, or parenteral as needed) when the delay is acceptable — this reduces complications measurably. For severely malnourished patients, the case for delaying elective surgery to refeed is strong. Do not dismiss weight loss as expected or assume a high body mass index rules out malnutrition; sarcenic obesity is common and dangerous.

### Manage Drains and Tubes With Intent, Not Routine

Drains, nasogastric tubes, and urinary catheters should be placed for specific indications and removed as soon as those indications resolve. Routine nasogastric tubes after gastrointestinal surgery increase pulmonary complications and prolong ileus and should not be used prophylactically; reserve them for obstruction, prolonged ileus, or specific operative indications, and remove them early. Surgical drains are placed to manage expected fluid, blood, or air, or to detect early leak; they should be removed when output falls below a threshold, character normalizes, or the indication resolves — prolonged drainage increases infection and impairs mobilization. Urinary catheters should be removed within 24 to 48 hours for most patients unless there is a specific indication (epidural, urethral surgery, urinary retention, strict intake and output monitoring), because catheter-associated urinary tract infection rises with each day of catheterization. Write the removal criteria in the orders so the tube does not linger by inertia.

### Recognize When to Deviate From the ERAS Pathway

ERAS pathways are designed for elective, uncomplicated patients and must be abandoned when the patient deviates from the expected course. Deviate when there is hemodynamic instability (suspected hemorrhage, sepsis, anastomotic leak), prolonged or high-output ileus, signs of bowel ischemia or peritonitis, uncontrolled pain or nausea that prevents mobilization and feeding, acute kidney injury requiring careful fluid management, or any deterioration without explanation. Deviation is not failure — it is the correct response to a patient who no longer fits the pathway. Document the reason for deviation, the alternative plan, and the criteria for returning to the pathway if applicable. A pathway applied to a deteriorating patient causes harm; a pathway correctly abandoned and re-engaged when the patient recovers is the mark of disciplined care.

### Prevent Complications Through Protocolized Bundle Care

ERAS is a bundle, and its benefit comes from the bundle, not from any single element. Apply the elements together: avoid prolonged fasting, use carbohydrate drinks, multimodal analgesia with opioid sparing, regional anesthesia where appropriate, goal-directed fluid, normothermia, VTE prophylaxis, early mobilization, early feeding, and avoidance of routine drains and tubes. Each element multiplies the others — opioid sparing reduces nausea and ileus, which enables early feeding, which enables mobilization, which reduces VTE and atelectasis. Applying one or two elements in isolation captures little of the benefit. When implementing ERAS, audit adherence to each element, because bundle performance predicts outcomes more than any single intervention.

### Escalate to Surgery, Nutrition, and Anesthesia at the Right Thresholds

Escalation is integral to perioperative care. Escalate to surgery for any sign of leak, ischemia, hemorrhage, or unexplained deterioration — see the postoperative complications skill for thresholds. Escalate to a dietitian or nutrition support team for malnourished patients, patients unable to meet nutritional needs orally within five to seven days, and any patient in whom enteral or parenteral nutrition is being considered. Escalate to anesthesia or acute pain service for pain or nausea that blocks mobilization and feeding. Escalate to nephrology for acute kidney injury or complex fluid and electrolyte management. Perioperative fluid, nutrition, and recovery decisions are rarely solo decisions for the deteriorating patient — bring the team in early.

## Common Traps

### Applying ERAS Rigidly to the Wrong Patient

Force-feeding or withholding fluid from a malnourished, septic, or deteriorating patient because "the pathway says so." Counter by understanding the physiology behind each element and deviating when the patient no longer fits.

### Liberal Crystalloid Resuscitation by Default

Bolusing fluid for any hypotension without assessing volume status. Counter by using goal-directed fluid therapy and dynamic markers to distinguish hypovolemia from other causes of hypotension.

### Prolonged Fasting Out of Tradition

Keeping patients nil by mouth overnight or longer before elective surgery. Counter by allowing clear fluids until two hours and solids until six hours before anesthesia, and feeding early in appropriate patients.

### Missing Malnutrition Because the Patient Is Overweight

Assuming a high body mass index excludes malnutrition. Counter by screening all patients with validated tools and recognizing sarcenic obesity and disease-related malnutrition.

### Leaving Drains and Catheters in Place by Inertia

Forgetting to remove tubes once the indication resolves. Counter by writing explicit removal criteria and reviewing each tube daily.

### Withdrawing the Whole Bundle When One Element Fails

Abandoning all ERAS elements when one is contraindicated. Counter by maintaining the elements that still apply and documenting the reason for each deviation.

### Treating Postoperative Hypotension as Hypovolemia Alone

Bolusing fluid reflexively for hypotension that may be cardiogenic, septic, or anesthetic. Counter by assessing the cause before treating and escalating to anesthesia or nephrology for complex cases.

### Delaying Nutrition Support in a Patient Who Cannot Eat

Waiting weeks before starting enteral or parenteral nutrition in a patient with prolonged ileus or leak. Counter by escalating to the nutrition team and starting support when oral intake is not feasible within five to seven days.

## Self-Check

- Do I understand the physiology behind each ERAS element, and am I modifying the pathway for the patient in front of me rather than applying it rigidly?
- Am I using goal-directed fluid therapy with dynamic markers rather than fixed boluses or routine maintenance fluid?
- Have I avoided both prolonged fasting and premature feeding, and provided nutrition support when oral intake is not feasible within five to seven days?
- Have I screened every patient for malnutrition with a validated tool and provided preoperative nutrition support for the malnourished when surgery can be delayed?
- Have I placed drains, nasogastric tubes, and urinary catheters only for specific indications and written explicit removal criteria?
- Have I recognized and documented when to deviate from the pathway for deterioration, leak, ischemia, or instability, with an alternative plan?
- Am I applying the ERAS bundle as a bundle rather than as isolated elements, and auditing adherence?
- Have I escalated to surgery, nutrition, anesthesia, or nephrology at the right thresholds for the deteriorating or complex patient?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as suspected leak, hemorrhage, sepsis, acute kidney injury, or unexplained deterioration appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
