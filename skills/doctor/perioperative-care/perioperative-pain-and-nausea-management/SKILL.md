# Perioperative Pain and Nausea Management

Postoperative pain and nausea are often dismissed as discomforts to be endured, but poorly controlled they are direct causes of complications, prolonged length of stay, readmission, persistent opioid use, and chronic post-surgical pain. The central failure mode is not undertreatment — it is reliance on opioids as the default, which causes the very nausea, ileus, sedation, delirium, and respiratory depression that block recovery. The disciplined approach is multimodal and opioid-sparing: combine regional anesthesia, acetaminophen, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, and adjuvants to cover pain through several mechanisms, add opioids only as rescue for breakthrough pain, and treat nausea prophylactically in high-risk patients rather than reactively. This skill covers multimodal analgesia, regional and neuraxial options, opioid-sparing pathways, PONV risk stratification and prophylaxis, prevention of chronic post-surgical pain, and the escalation points to an acute pain service.

## Core Rules

### Build Analgesia as a Multimodal, Opioid-Sparing Stack

The foundation of postoperative analgesia is multimodal therapy — combining agents with different mechanisms to achieve analgesia while minimizing opioid dose and its downstream effects. The standard stack for most patients is scheduled acetaminophen (the backbone of multimodal analgesia, given on a fixed schedule), an NSAID where not contraindicated (renal function, bleeding risk, peptic ulcer, and cardiovascular risk must be weighed — NSAIDs reduce opioid requirements and pain but require patient selection), and a regional or neuraxial block where appropriate. Adjuvants — gabapentinoids, ketamine, lidocaine infusions, alpha-2 agonists such as dexmedetomidine — add analgesia for specific situations such as opioid-tolerant patients, major surgery, or chronic pain. Opioids are rescue therapy for breakthrough pain, not the scheduled backbone. The goal is not zero opioids but the lowest effective opioid dose, because every milligram of opioid carries dose-dependent risk of nausea, ileus, sedation, delirium, respiratory depression, and persistent use.

### Use Regional and Neuraxial Anesthesia Aggressively Where Appropriate

Regional anesthesia — peripheral nerve blocks, fascial plane blocks, neuraxial (epidural and spinal) techniques — is among the most effective opioid-sparing strategies and should be considered for any patient undergoing a procedure amenable to a block. Single-shot peripheral nerve blocks (transversus abdominis plane, erector spinae, serratus anterior, interscalene, adductor canal, and others) provide hours of profound analgesia; continuous catheter techniques extend this for days. Neuraxial analgesia (thoracic epidural) remains a gold standard for open thoracic and upper abdominal surgery, improving pain control, pulmonary function, and gut recovery. The choice depends on the surgical site, expected pain intensity, contraindications (anticoagulation, infection at the site, patient refusal, certain spinal pathology), and the availability of acute pain expertise. Regional anesthesia is not a substitute for systemic multimodal therapy — it is a powerful component of it. When a block is in place, anticipate and manage its risks: local anesthetic systemic toxicity, nerve injury, infection, hematoma, and, for neuraxial techniques, epidural hematoma and abscess, which are catastrophic and demand immediate imaging if suspected.

### Stratify and Treat PONV Proactively, Not Reactively

Postoperative nausea and vomiting is among the most distressing postoperative experiences, delays recovery and discharge, and is largely preventable when risk is assessed prospectively. Stratify every patient using validated risk factors — female sex, history of PONV or motion sickness, nonsmoking status, postoperative opioid use, and (in some scores) younger age and longer surgery — and tailor prophylaxis to risk. Low-risk patients may need no prophylaxis or a single agent; moderate-risk patients warrant one or two antiemetics from different classes; high-risk patients warrant multimodal prophylaxis (typically a 5-HT3 antagonist such as ondansetron, a corticosteroid such as dexamethasone, and a butyrophenone such as droperidol or haloperidol, with transdermal scopolamine in some pathways) plus aggressive opioid sparing. Treat established PONV with an agent from a different class than was used for prophylaxis, because repeating the same class is less effective. PONV that persists despite multimodal therapy should prompt reassessment — ileus, obstruction, medication effect, electrolyte derangement, and intracranial causes must be considered, not simply more antiemetics.

### Prevent Chronic Post-Surgical Pain Through Early Aggressive Control

Chronic post-surgical pain — pain persisting beyond three months — develops in a substantial fraction of patients after procedures such as thoracotomy, mastectomy, hernia repair, and amputation, and severe acute pain is one of its strongest predictors. The strategy to prevent it is aggressive, multimodal, early analgesia: cover the surgical insult with regional anesthesia and multimodal systemic therapy from the outset, minimize opioid-only patterns, and treat neuropathic components with agents such as gabapentinoids or ketamine where appropriate. Identify patients at elevated risk (pre-existing chronic pain, opioid tolerance, psychological distress, repeat surgery, certain procedures) and plan their analgesia deliberately rather than reactively. Uncontrolled pain on the ward is not merely uncomfortable — it is a risk factor for a chronic pain syndrome, and the acute pain service should be involved early for high-risk patients.

### Anticipate and Monitor for Opioid-Related Harm

Opioids, even when used appropriately as rescue, carry predictable harms that must be anticipated and monitored. Respiratory depression is the most feared, particularly in patients with obstructive sleep apnea, the elderly, those on chronic opioids or benzodiazepines, and those receiving neuraxial or patient-controlled analgesia — these patients require continuous pulse oximetry or capnography monitoring in many institutions. Sedation precedes respiratory depression, and a sedated opioid-treated patient must be assessed, not assumed to be resting. Nausea, ileus, urinary retention, constipation, and delirium are dose-dependent and reduce with opioid sparing. Laxatives should be prescribed with opioids by default. In high-risk patients, consider opioid-sparing regimens that minimize or avoid opioids entirely, and have naloxone available and the team trained to recognize and reverse opioid overdose. Identify the opioid-tolerant patient (chronic opioid therapy, substance use disorder) early and involve the acute pain service, because their management is complex and undertreatment is both cruel and common.

### Individualize for the Patient, Not the Procedure

Pain and nausea management must be individualized to the patient's risk factors, comorbidities, and preferences, not applied as a fixed protocol. The elderly patient is more sensitive to opioids (lower starting doses, longer intervals) and to delirium, and may need more acetaminophen and less opioid. The patient with chronic kidney disease cannot receive certain NSAIDs. The patient with obstructive sleep apnea needs opioid sparing and monitoring. The patient with chronic opioid tolerance requires a multimodal plan that often includes ketamine infusions and acute pain service involvement. The patient with a history of severe PONV needs aggressive prophylaxis regardless of the procedure. The opioid-naive patient having minor surgery may need little more than acetaminophen and a short opioid course. Match the regimen to the patient and the procedure, reassess at every encounter, and adjust based on pain scores, side effects, and function — the goal is not a numeric pain score of zero but comfort sufficient to breathe deeply, mobilize, and recover.

### Escalate to the Acute Pain Service at Defined Thresholds

The acute pain service exists for the patients whose pain or nausea cannot be managed on the standard ward pathway, and escalation should be early rather than after prolonged suffering. Escalate for pain uncontrolled by the standard multimodal regimen, opioid-tolerant or substance-use-disorder patients, patients requiring complex regional or neuraxial techniques and their management, patients with chronic pain or suspected chronic post-surgical pain risk, patients with refractory PONV, and any patient in whom pain or its treatment is causing harm (respiratory depression, uncontrolled sedation, delirium, ileus). Escalate also when pain control is blocking mobilization, feeding, or discharge — pain is a recovery problem, not just a comfort problem. Document the escalation, the plan, and the reassessment, and ensure handoffs between the surgical team and the pain service are structured and explicit.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to Opioids as the Scheduled Analgesic

Ordering opioids around the clock rather than as rescue, then managing the resulting nausea, ileus, and sedation. Counter by building a scheduled multimodal stack and reserving opioids for breakthrough pain.

### Omitting Regional Anesthesia for Block-Amenable Surgery

Proceeding with systemic analgesia alone when a regional technique would dramatically reduce opioid needs. Counter by considering regional or neuraxial anesthesia for every amenable procedure and consulting the acute pain or anesthesia team.

### Treating PONV Reactively in High-Risk Patients

Withholding prophylaxis and treating nausea only after it occurs. Counter by stratifying PONV risk and giving multimodal prophylaxis to moderate- and high-risk patients.

### Repeating the Same Antiemetic Class for Established PONV

Giving more ondansetron when prophylaxis failed. Counter by treating established PONV with an agent from a different class.

### Ignoring Opioid-Related Sedation and Respiratory Depression

Assuming a sedated patient is resting. Counter by monitoring sedation, recognizing that sedation precedes respiratory depression, and having naloxone available.

### Undertreating the Opioid-Tolerant Patient

Applying standard opioid doses to a chronic-pain or substance-use patient. Counter by identifying opioid tolerance early, using multimodal and adjuvant therapy such as ketamine, and involving the acute pain service.

### Chasing a Pain Score of Zero

Over-sedating a patient to eliminate pain entirely. Counter by targeting comfort sufficient for breathing, mobilizing, and recovering, and reassessing function alongside the pain score.

### Delaying Acute Pain Service Involvement

Persisting with an ineffective regimen for days before escalating. Counter by escalating early for uncontrolled pain, opioid tolerance, refractory PONV, or pain blocking recovery.

## Self-Check

- Have I built a scheduled multimodal analgesic stack — acetaminophen, NSAID where appropriate, regional or neuraxial block where appropriate, and adjuvants — with opioids reserved as rescue?
- Have I considered regional or neuraxial anesthesia for this procedure and consulted anesthesia or the acute pain service where indicated?
- Have I stratified PONV risk and given multimodal prophylaxis to moderate- and high-risk patients, and planned to treat established PONV with a different class?
- Have I anticipated opioid-related harms — respiratory depression, sedation, nausea, ileus, constipation, delirium — and prescribed monitoring and laxatives, with naloxone available?
- Have I identified high-risk patients for chronic post-surgical pain and planned aggressive early multimodal analgesia to prevent it?
- Have I individualized the regimen for age, renal function, sleep apnea, opioid tolerance, and patient preference rather than applying a fixed protocol?
- Have I escalated to the acute pain service for uncontrolled pain, opioid tolerance, refractory PONV, complex regional or neuraxial management, or pain blocking recovery?
- Have I documented the plan, the reassessment, and the structured handoff between the surgical team and the pain service?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as respiratory depression, uncontrolled sedation, suspected epidural hematoma or abscess, or severe uncontrolled pain appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
