# Advance Care Planning and Goals of Care

Advance care planning is not paperwork; it is the structured work of ensuring that a person receives care consistent with their values when they can no longer speak for themselves. The recurring failure is to defer these conversations until crisis — when the patient is delirious, intubated, or unconscious, and a terrified surrogate is forced to guess under pressure — and then to mistake the completion of a form for the existence of a plan. A signed directive that was never discussed with the patient or family is worth almost nothing in a crisis, because the surrogate does not understand it and the team does not trust it. The opposite failure is to conduct the conversation without honest prognostic grounding, producing plans built on unrealistic assumptions that collapse when reality arrives. This skill covers the timing and structure of advance care planning, the goals-of-care conversation, the proper use of advance directives and physician orders (POLST/MOLST), surrogate decision making, the non-negotiable role of prognostic honesty, and knowing when conflict, complexity, or ethical tension warrants palliative care or ethics consultation. The discipline is to start early, ground every plan in prognosis and values, document in the right instruments, and revisit the plan at every transition.

## Core Rules

### Initiate Advance Care Planning Early and Treat It as Iterative

Advance care planning is a process, not an event, and it works best when begun in health or stable chronic illness, well before a crisis. The conversation is far easier when the patient is well and reflective than when they are acutely ill and frightened. Use natural triggers: a new serious diagnosis, a hospitalization, a decline in function, a care setting transition, or routine visits in older or chronically ill patients. Frame it as a normal part of good care, not as a signal of imminent death, which is how patients often misread a sudden directive conversation. Return to the plan at every major transition — diagnosis, relapse, hospitalization, discharge, functional decline — because values and preferences evolve as illness progresses. A plan made once and never revisited is a snapshot that drifts out of date; treat advance care planning as a living document that must be updated as the clinical reality changes. When a patient is approaching the end of life and no planning has occurred, do not abandon the effort — crisis planning is harder but still better than no planning, and warrants prompt, structured conversation.

### Ground Every Goals-of-Care Conversation in Prognosis and Values

A goals-of-care conversation without prognosis is untethered, and one without values is generic. Open by asking what the patient understands about their illness and what they are hoping for, because the gap between medical reality and patient hopes is the central problem to navigate. Provide an honest prognostic frame — what is likely to happen, over what timeframe, with what uncertainties — using ranges and explicit uncertainty rather than false precision. Then explore values: what does living well mean to this person, what outcomes would be unacceptable to them, what tradeoffs are they willing to accept (length of life versus comfort, hospital versus home, burden versus benefit). Use a structured approach (Serious Illness Conversation Guide or equivalent) to ensure you cover understanding, prognosis, goals, fears, tradeoffs, and what they would want their surrogate to know. Translate those values into a concrete plan: what treatments align with the goals, what treatments would not, and what the priorities are as illness progresses. Document the conversation in detail, not just the resulting orders, because the reasoning is what guides future decisions.

### Use the Right Instrument for the Right Decision

Advance care planning produces two distinct kinds of documents that are often confused. An advance directive (living will, durable power of attorney for health care) is completed by the patient, names a surrogate, and expresses general wishes; it guides care when the patient loses capacity but is not itself a medical order. A physician order for life-sustaining treatment (POLST, MOLST, or equivalent depending on jurisdiction) is a medical order signed by both clinician and patient or surrogate that translates goals into specific orders — CPR versus do-not-resuscitate, level of medical intervention, artificial nutrition — and is actionable across settings. Use the right instrument: directives for appointing surrogates and expressing values, POLST/MOLST for converting those values into transportable orders for patients with serious illness facing end-of-life decisions. Ensure POLST/MOLST travels with the patient across home, ambulance, hospital, and facility; a form that lives in a chart the ambulance crew cannot see fails in the field. Review and update these instruments at every transition and when goals change, because an outdated order can produce care the patient no longer wants.

### Identify and Prepare the Surrogate Decision Maker

Most patients will lose capacity before death, so the identity and preparation of the surrogate is as important as the written plan. Ask the patient to name a surrogate explicitly, confirm legal authority under local law, and document it. Critically, prepare the surrogate: involve them in the goals-of-care conversation, ensure they understand the patient's values and wishes, and ask them what they would find hardest about deciding. Surrogates commonly suffer guilt, conflict with other family, and decision-making burden; they decide better when they have been prepared and when they understand they are to use substituted judgment (what the patient would want) rather than their own preference. When no surrogate is named and the patient lacks capacity, follow jurisdictional processes for default surrogates or guardianship, and recognize that these situations are higher-risk and warrant ethics or palliative involvement. A surrogate who has never discussed the patient's wishes is being set up to fail and to suffer; the work of preparing them is core advance care planning.

### Practice Prognostic Honesty Without Brutality

Patients and families cannot make aligned decisions without honest prognostic information, and withholding it out of a desire to preserve hope usually produces worse deaths and greater family suffering. Yet prognostication is genuinely uncertain, and overconfident numbers erode trust when they are wrong. Give honest ranges with explicit uncertainty ("I'm worried this is a life-limiting illness — likely months rather than years, though I can't be certain"), pair prognosis with what can still be done, and check understanding. Avoid the opposite failure of vagueness that leaves families unprepared ("let's hope for the best"). Recognize that some patients and cultures do not want to be told a prognosis directly; ask permission and respect their preference, while ensuring someone in the family or surrogate group has the information needed to decide. When the prognostic picture is genuinely unclear, when the family is in denial or conflict, or when disclosure is culturally fraught, palliative care consultation can help frame prognosis and guide the conversation.

### Recognize and Escalate Conflict and Ethical Tension

Goals-of-care conversations frequently surface conflict: family disagreement, surrogate decisions that seem to contradict the patient's stated values, requests for treatment that is unlikely to benefit, or refusal of clearly beneficial care. Do not avoid the conflict — name it, explore each party's understanding and concern, and realign around the patient's values rather than the loudest voice. When treatment requested is medically inappropriate or futile, you are not obligated to provide it; explain what you can and cannot offer, offer what aligns with the goals, and involve ethics consultation when the conflict persists. When a surrogate appears to be acting against the patient's known wishes, escalate to ethics, administration, or legal processes rather than acquiescing. When the patient lacks capacity, has no surrogate, and faces a major decision, ethics and palliative involvement are indicated early. These situations are high-stakes and emotionally charged; escalating to palliative care and ethics is a sign of good practice, not failure.

## Common Traps

### Deferring the Conversation Until Crisis

Waiting until the patient is unconscious or the family is in the emergency department, forcing rushed surrogate decisions. Counter by initiating advance care planning early in stable illness and treating it as iterative.

### Completing a Form Without a Conversation

Producing a directive or POLST that was never discussed, which the surrogate does not understand and the team cannot trust. Counter by conducting the goals-of-care conversation first and documenting the reasoning, not just the orders.

### Conducting the Conversation Without Honest Prognosis

Building a plan on unrealistic assumptions that collapse when the illness progresses. Counter by giving honest prognostic ranges with uncertainty, paired with what can still be done.

### Confusing Directives With Physician Orders

Treating an advance directive as a transportable medical order, or failing to complete a POLST/MOLST for a seriously ill patient. Counter by using directives for values and surrogates, and POLST/MOLST for actionable orders that travel with the patient.

### Leaving the Surrogate Unprepared

Naming a surrogate without involving them in the conversation or ensuring they understand substituted judgment. Counter by preparing the surrogate explicitly and asking what they would find hardest.

### Acquiescing to Inappropriate Requests to Avoid Conflict

Providing futile treatment or accepting surrogate decisions that contradict the patient's wishes to keep the peace. Counter by naming the conflict, offering aligned care, and escalating to ethics when it persists.

## Self-Check

- Did I initiate advance care planning early and frame it as a normal, iterative part of care rather than a death signal?
- Did I ground the goals-of-care conversation in both honest prognosis (ranges with uncertainty) and the patient's values, fears, and tradeoffs?
- Did I use the correct instrument — directive for values and surrogate, POLST/MOLST for transportable orders — and ensure it travels with the patient?
- Did I identify and explicitly prepare the surrogate, ensuring they understand substituted judgment and what the patient would want?
- Was my prognostic communication honest without being brutal, paired with what can still be done, and checked for understanding?
- Did I document the conversation and reasoning in detail, not just the resulting orders?
- When conflict, futility, surrogate disagreement, or absence of a surrogate arose, did I escalate to palliative care or ethics rather than avoiding or acquiescing?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
