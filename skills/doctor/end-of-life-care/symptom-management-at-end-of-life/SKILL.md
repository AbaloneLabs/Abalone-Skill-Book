# Symptom Management at End of Life

The dying phase produces a predictable set of symptoms — pain, dyspnea, agitation, terminal secretions, nausea — and the duty of medicine in that phase is to relieve suffering, not to prolong dying. The recurring failure is not over-treatment but a paralyzing under-treatment driven by clinicians' fear of opioids and sedatives, a confusion between symptom relief and euthanasia, and a failure to anticipate symptoms before they occur. Patients die in pain or breathlessness because the clinician hesitated to use adequate opioids; families watch a loved one agitated or rattling because no one prescribed ahead. The opposite and rarer failure is to use sedation without clear intent, blurring the line between symptom control and hastening death. This skill covers the anticipatory management of terminal symptoms, the appropriate and proportionate use of opioids and sedatives, the critical distinction between proportionate sedation for refractory symptoms and palliative sedation to unconsciousness, supporting the family through the dying process, and knowing when symptom complexity, refractory distress, or ethical tension warrants palliative care involvement. The discipline is to anticipate, prescribe ahead, titrate to comfort, and never let fear of the drug cause avoidable suffering.

## Core Rules

### Anticipate and Prescribe for Symptoms Before They Occur

The defining practice of good end-of-life care is anticipatory prescribing. Dying produces a predictable symptom cascade, and waiting until a symptom appears before ordering the medication guarantees that the patient suffers while the drug is fetched, verified, and given. For every dying patient, ensure as-needed orders are in place for the common terminal symptoms: an opioid for pain and dyspnea, an anxiolytic or sedative for agitation and anxiety, an anti-secretory agent for terminal secretions, and an antiemetic for nausea. Choose routes that work in a dying patient who cannot swallow — subcutaneous, intravenous, sublingual, or rectal — because an oral-only plan fails when swallowing is lost. Communicate the plan to the family so they understand that these medications are for comfort and are not being given to hasten death. Anticipatory prescribing is not aggressive; it is the minimum standard, and its absence is a common cause of distressing deaths and family trauma. When the symptom pattern is complex or refractory, escalate to palliative care to refine the plan rather than improvising.

### Manage Pain and Dyspnea With Adequate, Titrated Opioids

Opioids are first-line for both pain and dyspnea at end of life, and the fear of respiratory depression is the most common reason patients are under-dosed. Used at appropriate doses and titrated to effect, opioids relieve dyspnea and pain without clinically significant respiratory depression in the dying patient; the evidence is clear, and the fear is disproportionate. For opioid-naive patients, start low and titrate (for example, small doses of morphine or oxycodone at appropriate intervals, with breakthrough doses available); for opioid-tolerant patients, calculate based on existing baseline and provide adequate breakthrough doses. Reassess regularly and titrate to comfort. For dyspnea that does not respond to opioids, address reversible contributors (positioning, anxiety, secretions, effusion) and consider benzodiazepines for the anxiety component. Document the indication, the doses, the response, and the reassessment, because clear documentation of symptom-driven titration protects both the patient from under-treatment and the clinician from misunderstanding. If pain or dyspnea is severe, escalating rapidly, or poorly responsive, escalate to palliative care.

### Distinguish Proportionate Sedation From Palliative Sedation to Unconsciousness

This distinction is the ethical core of end-of-life symptom management and is routinely misunderstood. Proportionate sedation is the use of sedatives and opioids titrated to relieve symptoms, accepting that some sedation may occur as a side effect — the intent is symptom relief, and the level of consciousness is secondary. This is standard care and requires no special ethical framework beyond informed consent. Palliative sedation to unconsciousness is reserved for refractory symptoms — suffering that has not responded to all other reasonable interventions — and aims to reduce consciousness to relieve otherwise uncontrollable distress, typically in the last days of life. It is ethically distinct from euthanasia: the intent is relief of refractory suffering, not death, and appropriately used palliative sedation does not hasten death. Palliative sedation requires explicit discussion with the patient (if able) and family, clear documentation of the refractory symptom and the interventions tried, and usually palliative care or ethics involvement. Do not blur the two: escalating sedation for a controllable symptom, or labeling proportionate sedation as palliative sedation, confuses care and creates fear.

### Manage Terminal Agitation and Secretions Proactively

Agitation and terminal secretions ("death rattle") are distressing to families and often under-managed. Terminal agitation is common in the last days of life and may reflect pain, dyspnea, urinary retention, constipation, medication effects, or existential distress; address reversible contributors, then use benzodiazepines or appropriate sedatives titrated to calm. Do not assume agitation is purely psychological — untreated pain or a full bladder in a patient who cannot communicate will present as agitation, and sedating without addressing the cause compounds the suffering. Terminal secretions are the sound of pooled saliva and respiratory fluids in a patient too weak to clear them; they are usually more distressing to the family than to the patient, who is often unconscious. Manage with anti-secretory agents (such as anticholinergics), repositioning, and gentle oral care, and explain to the family that the sound, while upsetting, is generally not causing the patient distress. Position the family to be present and supported, because the appearance and sounds of dying are frightening to those who do not understand them.

### Treat Nausea and Other Symptoms Based on Cause

Nausea at end of life is not a single symptom with a single treatment; it depends on the cause — opioid-induced, bowel obstruction, raised intracranial pressure, vestibular, metabolic, or anxiety. Choose the antiemetic by mechanism: dopamine antagonists for opioid or metabolic nausea, antihistamines for vestibular, corticosteroids for raised pressure or obstruction, and consider that bowel obstruction in advanced cancer often requires a combination including antisecretory agents and may need palliative surgical or procedural input. Choose routes that work in the dying patient, and reassess. Other common symptoms — dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, fever, skin pressure — each have specific management; do not neglect them because the patient is dying. Constipation is near-universal in patients on opioids and must be anticipated with a bowel regimen; untreated constipation causes pain, agitation, and nausea. When symptoms are multiple, severe, or refractory, escalate to palliative care, which has the expertise to manage complex terminal symptom burden.

### Support the Family Through the Dying Process

The family is also your patient in the dying phase. They are frightened, exhausted, and often unprepared for what dying looks like. Explain the signs of approaching death — decreased oral intake, decreased urine output, changes in breathing pattern, mottling, cooling of extremities — so they understand these are expected, not emergencies or signs of suffering. Reassure them about medications and their purpose, and address the common fear that opioids are hastening death. Offer presence, allow them time with the patient, and respect cultural and religious practices around dying. Prepare them for what will happen at and after death so there are no surprises. Family experience of the dying and death strongly predicts their bereavement outcome; good end-of-life care is preventive bereavement care. When family distress is high, conflict is present, or the death is traumatic, involve social work, pastoral care, or palliative care to support them.

## Common Traps

### Under-treating Pain and Dyspnea Out of Fear of Opioids

Hesitating to use adequate opioid doses, leaving the patient in pain or breathless. Counter by starting at appropriate doses, titrating to comfort, and documenting symptom-driven rationale; the fear of respiratory depression is disproportionate.

### Waiting for Symptoms to Appear Before Prescribing

Having no as-needed orders in place, so the patient suffers while medication is prepared. Counter by anticipatory prescribing for pain, dyspnea, agitation, secretions, and nausea in every dying patient, in workable routes.

### Blurring Proportionate Sedation With Palliative Sedation

Escalating sedation without distinguishing symptom relief from sedation to unconsciousness, or labeling standard care as palliative sedation. Counter by reserving palliative sedation for refractory symptoms with explicit discussion and documentation.

### Sedating Agitation Without Addressing the Cause

Giving sedatives for agitation that is actually pain, urinary retention, or constipation. Counter by systematically looking for and treating reversible contributors before sedating.

### Neglecting the Family's Experience

Focusing only on the patient and leaving the family frightened and unsupported. Counter by explaining the signs of dying, reassuring about medications, and preparing them for death.

### Failing to Escalate Refractory Symptoms

Improvising with escalating doses when symptoms do not respond, rather than involving palliative care. Counter by escalating to palliative care for complex, severe, or refractory terminal symptoms.

## Self-Check

- Did I put anticipatory as-needed orders in place for pain, dyspnea, agitation, secretions, and nausea in workable routes for every dying patient?
- Did I use adequate, titrated opioids for pain and dyspnea, documenting the symptom-driven rationale rather than under-dosing from disproportionate fear?
- Did I distinguish proportionate sedation (standard care) from palliative sedation to unconsciousness (reserved for refractory symptoms), and reserve the latter with explicit discussion and documentation?
- For agitation, did I systematically seek and treat reversible causes (pain, retention, constipation, medication effects) before resorting to sedation?
- Did I choose antiemetics and other symptom treatments based on the underlying cause and reassess their effect?
- Did I explain the signs of dying, reassure the family about medications, and prepare them for death to support their bereavement?
- For complex, severe, or refractory symptoms, did I escalate to palliative care rather than improvise?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
