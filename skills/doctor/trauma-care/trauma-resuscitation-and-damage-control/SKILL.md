# Trauma Resuscitation and Damage Control

Trauma resuscitation is the deliberate, protocol-driven management of the bleeding patient, where the goal is not to restore normal physiology immediately but to stop the bleeding, restore perfusion just enough to preserve vital organ function, and correct the lethal triad of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy that kills patients who would otherwise survive their injuries. The judgment problem is that every intervention has a tradeoff: fluids dilute clotting factors, hypotension risks brain perfusion, blood products carry risk and cost, and delaying surgery for imaging risks exsanguination. The discipline of damage control resuscitation (DCR) is to make these tradeoffs deliberately and in the right order. This skill covers the principles of DCR, massive transfusion, permissive hypotension, coagulopathy prevention, and the decision between surgery, angiography, and continued resuscitation. It assumes an acute care setting and must always follow institutional massive transfusion and trauma protocols, with early escalation to trauma surgery, anesthesia, and interventional radiology.

## Core Rules

### Treat Hemorrhage Control as the First Priority Over Restoring Normal Vitals

The single most important principle in trauma resuscitation is that definitive hemorrhage control — operative, angiographic, or by direct pressure and splinting — takes precedence over normalizing vital signs. Resuscitation without hemorrhage control produces the lethal triad and death. External bleeding is controlled with direct pressure and tourniquets; pelvic bleeding with a pelvic binder; limb fractures with splinting and reduction. Internal bleeding into the chest, abdomen, or pelvis requires a decision: operating room, angiography suite, or, in the chest, a tube thoracostomy for hemothorax. Make this decision early and explicitly, and do not let fluid resuscitation substitute for source control. A patient who keeps bleeding through the resuscitation needs the bleeding stopped, not more fluid.

### Apply Damage Control Resuscitation as a Bundle, Not Item by Item

Damage control resuscitation is an integrated strategy: permissive hypotension until hemorrhage control, minimized crystalloid, balanced blood product transfusion, and prevention of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy. These components work together and are less effective in isolation. Apply them as a bundle from the moment major hemorrhage is recognized. The aim is to keep the patient alive and perfused enough to reach definitive hemorrhage control, not to chase a normal blood pressure with crystalloid that dilutes clotting factors and worsens coagulopathy. When in doubt about the depth or pace of resuscitation, consult the trauma surgeon and anesthesiologist early; DCR is a team protocol, not a solo performance.

### Use a Balanced Massive Transfusion Protocol, Approximately 1:1:1

In major hemorrhage, transfuse packed red blood cells, fresh frozen plasma, and platelets in a balanced ratio approximating whole blood — commonly cited as 1:1:1 — rather than giving crystalloid or red cells alone. The goal is to replace lost blood with components that clot. Activate the massive transfusion protocol early based on clinical assessment of hemorrhage and shock, not after labs return, because coagulopathy develops rapidly and lab values lag. Tranexamic acid is given early (within three hours of injury, ideally within the first hour) in protocols that include it. Monitor ionized calcium, because citrate in stored blood chelates calcium and hypocalcemia impairs clotting and cardiac function. Use viscoelastic testing (ROTEM or TEG), where available, to guide component therapy rather than relying on conventional coagulation tests alone. Know your institutional protocol cold, including the contents of each pack and the thaw time for plasma.

### Apply Permissive Hypotension Deliberately, With Defined Limits and Exceptions

Permissive hypotension means tolerating a lower-than-normal blood pressure (commonly a systolic around 80–90 mmHg in adults, per protocol) until hemorrhage is controlled, on the reasoning that a higher pressure dislodges clots and worsens bleeding. It is a deliberate tradeoff, not neglect. Apply it only with explicit limits: a target systolic or mean pressure, a plan for what happens if the patient deteriorates, and continuous reassessment. Critically, permissive hypotension is contraindicated or requires modification in patients who cannot tolerate low perfusion — severe traumatic brain injury, where hypotension doubles mortality and a higher blood pressure is needed to maintain cerebral perfusion — and in the elderly or those with vascular disease, where organ perfusion margins are narrow. When the indication for higher perfusion exists, raise the target and accept the bleeding tradeoff, with hemorrhage control proceeding urgently. When in doubt, consult anesthesia and trauma surgery on the target.

### Prevent and Actively Reverse the Lethal Triad

The lethal triad — hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy — is self-reinforcing and rapidly fatal. Prevent it from the moment of arrival: warm the resuscitation bay, use warmed fluids and blood, remove wet clothing, cover the patient with warm blankets, and use active warming devices. Monitor core temperature, arterial pH (or lactate and base excess as surrogates), and coagulation. Acidosis from poor perfusion is corrected by hemorrhage control and restoration of perfusion, not by bicarbonate. Coagulopathy is addressed by balanced transfusion, correction of hypothermia and acidosis, calcium replacement, and targeted component therapy guided by viscoelastic testing when available. A patient who enters the lethal triad has a high mortality; the goal is to prevent entry, and to reverse it aggressively with surgical and critical care support if it develops.

### Decide Between Operating Room, Angiography, and Continued Resuscitation Explicitly

The bleeding patient requires a clear decision about the site and method of hemorrhage control. Blunt or penetrating torso trauma with hemodynamic instability and a positive FAST or clear intraperitoneal source goes to the operating room. Pelvic fractures with ongoing hemorrhage often require angiographic embolization, frequently in coordination with pelvic binding or external fixation; the decision between OR-first and angio-first depends on the pattern, the stability, and institutional capability, and should involve trauma surgery and interventional radiology. Solid organ injuries in stable patients may be managed non-operatively with close monitoring and angiography as backup. Do not leave a bleeding patient in the resuscitation bay without a decision; if the source is unclear, escalate imaging (repeat FAST, CT only if stable) and consult early. The decision is time-sensitive: every minute of uncontrolled hemorrhage worsens outcomes.

### Use Endpoints of Resuscitation, Not the Absence of Obvious Bleeding

Resuscitation is complete when physiology is restored, not when the bleeding appears to have stopped. Use endpoints: normalization of lactate and base excess, restoration of normothermia, correction of coagulopathy, adequate urine output, and stable hemodynamics without pressor support. A patient with a normal blood pressure but persistent elevated lactate is still under-resuscitated or still bleeding. Monitor these endpoints continuously in the intensive care unit after damage control surgery, and return to the operating room for a planned second-look if physiology is not restoring. Clinical inertia — accepting a "stable" but under-resuscitated patient — causes late deaths.

## Common Traps

### Resuscitating With Crystalloid Before Controlling Hemorrhage

Large-volume crystalloid dilutes clotting factors, causes acidosis from its own acidity, and worsens coagulopathy. Counter by minimizing crystalloid, using balanced blood products, and prioritizing hemorrhage control.

### Chasing a Normal Blood Pressure in the Bleeding Patient

Pushing blood pressure to normal before hemorrhage control dislodges clots and accelerates bleeding, except in patients who need higher perfusion (severe TBI, elderly, vascular disease). Counter by applying permissive hypotension with explicit limits and exceptions, and consulting on the target when the indication is uncertain.

### Activating Massive Transfusion Too Late

Waiting for lab confirmation of coagulopathy before activating the protocol delays balanced transfusion. Counter by activating based on clinical assessment of hemorrhage and shock, and knowing your institutional triggers.

### Giving Red Cells Without Plasma and Platelets

Red cells alone restore oxygen-carrying capacity but do not clot; the patient continues to bleed. Counter by using a balanced 1:1:1 ratio from the start, per protocol.

### Forgetting Calcium and Temperature

Hypocalcemia from citrate and hypothermia from cold blood and exposure both impair clotting and cardiac function, undoing the rest of the resuscitation. Counter by monitoring ionized calcium, replacing it, and warming the patient actively throughout.

### Leaving a Bleeding Patient in the Resuscitation Bay Without a Decision

Indecision between OR, angiography, and continued resuscitation is itself a decision to delay hemorrhage control. Counter by making the site and method of hemorrhage control explicit early, with trauma surgery and interventional radiology consulted in parallel.

### Accepting a "Stable" Patient Without Checking Endpoints

A normal blood pressure with persistent elevated lactate means ongoing under-resuscitation or bleeding. Counter by using lactate, base excess, temperature, coagulation, and urine output as endpoints, and returning to surgery if physiology is not restoring.

## Self-Check

- Did I prioritize definitive hemorrhage control (operative, angiographic, or direct) over normalizing vital signs, and make the site and method of control explicit early?
- Did I apply damage control resuscitation as a bundle — permissive hypotension, minimized crystalloid, balanced transfusion, and prevention of the lethal triad?
- Did I activate the massive transfusion protocol early on clinical grounds and transfuse packed red cells, plasma, and platelets in an approximately 1:1:1 ratio?
- Did I apply permissive hypotension with explicit limits, and did I correctly identify exceptions (severe TBI, elderly, vascular disease) that require a higher perfusion target?
- Did I actively prevent and reverse hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy, including monitoring and replacing ionized calcium?
- Did I decide explicitly between operating room, angiography, and continued resuscitation, consulting trauma surgery and interventional radiology in parallel rather than waiting?
- Did I use endpoints of resuscitation (lactate, base excess, temperature, coagulation, urine output) rather than accepting apparent stability, and plan a second-look if physiology did not restore?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional massive transfusion and trauma protocols.
