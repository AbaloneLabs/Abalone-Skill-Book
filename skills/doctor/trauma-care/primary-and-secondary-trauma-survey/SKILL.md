# Primary and Secondary Trauma Survey

Trauma assessment is a time-critical, high-stakes task where the order of actions matters more than the completeness of any single action. The ATLS primary survey exists because the most common cause of preventable trauma death is treating an obvious but non-lethal injury before a less obvious but rapidly fatal one. The discipline is to follow a fixed sequence — Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure — while resuscitating simultaneously, rather than being drawn to the dramatic injury. The danger is distraction: the open femur fracture, the screaming patient, the vivid bleed all pull attention away from the tension pneumothorax, the expanding pelvic hematoma, or the compromised airway that will kill the patient first. This skill covers the structured primary and secondary survey, simultaneous resuscitation, adjuncts, team coordination, and the decision to transfer. Trauma care is a team sport with a strict hierarchy of threats; this guidance assumes an acute setting and must always defer to local trauma protocols and senior trauma or surgical consultation.

## Core Rules

### Treat the Primary Survey as a Fixed Sequence, Not a Checklist

The primary survey is a prioritized sequence in which each step addresses the most rapidly lethal problem before moving on. Airway threats kill in minutes; breathing compromise kills in minutes to tens of minutes; circulation failure kills in tens of minutes to hours; disability evolves over hours; exposure is about not missing hidden threats. Work through ABCDE in order, and address each life threat as it is found before proceeding. The classic error is to leap to a dramatic circulation problem (a bleeding limb) while an airway is lost. If a step is not cleared, do not move forward — secure the airway before assessing breathing, confirm effective breathing before assessing circulation. Reassess continuously, because a cleared airway can be lost, a stable circulation can decompensate, and the patient who was fine ten minutes ago may now be dying.

### Resuscitate Simultaneously, Not Sequentially

The primary survey is performed in parallel with resuscitation, not after it. Two large-bore intravenous lines, high-flow oxygen, cardiac monitoring, and pulse oximetry should begin as the survey starts. Blood should be drawn early for type and crossmatch, lactate, venous or arterial blood gas, hemoglobin, coagulation, and a point-of-care hemoglobin. In the deteriorating patient, activate the massive transfusion protocol and prepare for surgical or angiographic hemorrhage control while the survey continues. Waiting to finish the survey before beginning resuscitation wastes the golden hour. The team leader should assign resuscitation tasks to team members so that the assessor is not pulled away from the sequence.

### Address Each Life Threat as It Is Identified

During the primary survey, intervene immediately on the threat found. A threatened airway is secured with basic maneuvers, suction, adjuncts, or definitive airway (endotracheal intubation or surgical airway) — do not continue to B while A is lost. A tension pneumothorax is decompressed with needle thoracostomy followed by a chest tube; a massive hemothorax gets a large chest tube and volume resuscitation; an open pneumothorax is sealed with a three-sided dressing. Circulation threats are addressed by direct pressure on external bleeding, pelvic binding for an unstable pelvis, and rapid consideration of operative or angiographic control for internal hemorrhage. Disability begins with a rapid Glasgow Coma Score, pupil exam, and blood glucose; hypoglycemia is reversible and easily missed. Exposure means fully exposing the patient to find hidden injuries, then covering with warm blankets to prevent hypothermia, which worsens coagulopathy.

### Recognize the Life-Threats That Must Not Be Missed in the Primary Survey

The primary survey is designed to catch the immediately fatal conditions: airway obstruction, tension pneumothorax, massive hemothorax, flail chest with respiratory failure, cardiac tamponade, massive internal hemorrhage (chest, abdomen, pelvis, long bones), and neurogenic shock from spinal cord injury. For each, know the clinical sign and the immediate intervention. A shocky trauma patient with a distended abdomen has intra-abdominal bleeding until proven otherwise; a shocky patient with a stable pelvis and no external bleeding may still be bleeding into the chest, abdomen, or retroperitoneum. If the source of shock is not obvious, escalate imaging and surgical involvement early rather than waiting for deterioration.

### Perform the Secondary Survey Only After the Primary Survey Is Complete and the Patient Is Stable

The secondary survey is a systematic head-to-toe examination that identifies injuries not immediately life-threatening but still requiring treatment. It includes a full history (AMPLE: Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last meal, Events of injury), a complete head and face exam, inspection of the scalp, ears, eyes, nose, mouth and throat, cervical spine evaluation, chest, abdomen, pelvis, perineum and rectum, all four extremities with distal neurovascular checks, and a logroll for the entire back. Reassess vital signs throughout. The secondary survey should be deferred or interrupted if the patient deteriorates — return to the primary survey. In the unstable patient, the secondary survey may never happen before the patient goes to the operating room, and that is correct.

### Use Adjuncts Purposefully, Not Reflexively

Adjuncts support the survey but do not replace it. Focused Assessment with Sonography in Trauma (FAST) detects free fluid in the peritoneal and pericardial spaces and is most useful in the unstable blunt trauma patient to identify intra-abdominal hemorrhage needing laparotomy; it is less useful in penetrating trauma and in the stable patient where CT is preferred. Extended FAST (E-FAST) adds pneumothorax detection. Chest and pelvis x-rays are rapid and answer focused questions in the resuscitation bay. Whole-body CT is the standard for the stable polytrauma patient but is inappropriate for the unstable patient who needs immediate hemorrhage control. Choose the adjunct that answers the immediate decision: does this patient need an operation, angiography, or continued resuscitation? When in doubt, consult the trauma surgeon before sending an unstable patient to CT.

### Coordinate the Trauma Team with Clear Roles and Closed-Loop Communication

Effective trauma resuscitation depends on defined roles — team leader, airway, procedures, medications, recorder, and others depending on local protocol — and closed-loop communication in which orders are repeated back and confirmed. The team leader stands back to maintain situational awareness rather than performing procedures. If you are both leading and doing, you are doing neither well. Designate a leader explicitly, speak the plan aloud, and document timing of key events. When information is ambiguous, ask for it to be repeated. Trauma resuscitation is one of the clearest examples where communication failure directly causes preventable death; structured communication and role assignment are not optional courtesies.

### Decide Early Whether to Transfer to a Higher Level of Care

Triage to the right facility is part of the primary survey, not an afterthought. Patients meeting trauma activation criteria — physiologic (shock, depressed GCS, respiratory distress), anatomic (flail chest, two or more proximal long-bone fractures, amputation, pelvic fracture, skull fracture), or mechanism (high-speed crash, fall from height, ejection) — belong at a trauma center. If the current facility lacks the capability (no neurosurgery, no orthopedic trauma, no interventional radiology, no intensive care), initiate transfer early, in parallel with resuscitation, not after the workup is complete. Contact the accepting center, summarize the injuries and physiology, and arrange transport with appropriate escort. Delaying transfer to "finish the workup" is a common and dangerous error; the workup can continue at the receiving center. When in doubt, consult the regional trauma service.

## Common Traps

### Fixating on the Dramatic Injury

The screaming patient with a gruesome open fracture draws attention while a silent tension pneumothorax kills. Counter by following the ABCDE sequence rigidly and treating the survey as a discipline, not a suggestion.

### Completing the Survey Before Resuscitating

Running through ABCDE while the patient bleeds or hypoventilates wastes the golden hour. Counter by resuscitating in parallel, with the team leader assigning tasks so the assessor is not interrupted.

### Skipping the Secondary Survey or Performing It Superficially

Once the patient is stabilized, the temptation is to stop. Missed injuries — including spinal fractures, intra-abdominal hollow viscus injury, and occult extremity fractures — emerge later and cause harm. Counter by performing a full head-to-toe secondary survey with logroll and a complete AMPLE history, and reassess if the patient deteriorates.

### Sending an Unstable Patient to CT

CT is for the stable patient. Moving an unstable patient to an unmonitored scanner delays hemorrhage control and risks arrest in the scanner. Counter by using FAST and plain films in the resuscitation bay and escalating to the operating room or angiography for the unstable patient; consult the trauma surgeon before any scan.

### Treating FAST as Definitive

A negative FAST does not exclude intra-abdominal injury, especially in the stable patient where CT is more sensitive, nor does it exclude hollow viscus or retroperitoneal injury. Counter by interpreting FAST in context and confirming with CT or serial examination when the mechanism or physiology suggests ongoing risk.

### Failing to Reassess

A patient who was stable can deteriorate silently. Counter by continuously reassessing vital signs and the primary survey, and by treating any deterioration as a return to ABCDE.

### Delaying Transfer to Complete the Workup

The receiving trauma center can complete the workup; the referring facility cannot always provide definitive care. Counter by initiating transfer in parallel with resuscitation once a higher level of care is clearly needed, and never let "just one more scan" delay transfer of an unstable patient.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform the primary survey as a fixed ABCDE sequence, addressing each life threat before moving on, and resuscitate in parallel?
- For each life-threatening condition encountered (airway loss, tension pneumothorax, massive hemorrhage, tamponade), did I intervene immediately rather than continuing the survey?
- Did I use adjuncts (FAST, chest and pelvis x-rays, CT) purposefully to answer specific decisions, and did I avoid sending an unstable patient to CT?
- Did I perform a complete secondary survey with AMPLE history, head-to-toe exam, and logroll once the patient was stable, and return to the primary survey if the patient deteriorated?
- Did I coordinate the trauma team with explicit roles, closed-loop communication, and a team leader maintaining situational awareness?
- Did I identify early whether the patient needs transfer to a higher level of trauma care, and initiate transfer in parallel with resuscitation rather than after the workup?
- Did I reassess vital signs and the primary survey continuously rather than assuming the patient remained stable?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional trauma protocols.
