# Head, Spinal, and Polytrauma Management

Head, spinal, and polytrauma injuries concentrate the worst outcomes in trauma care because the brain and spinal cord do not regenerate, and because polytrauma forces simultaneous, competing priorities where the wrong sequence kills the patient. The central judgment problem is not the textbook management of a single injury — it is the integration of neuroprotection, hemorrhage control, and surgical prioritization under time pressure, often with incomplete information. The brain is exquisitely vulnerable to secondary injury from hypoxia, hypotension, and raised intracranial pressure; a single episode of hypotension in severe traumatic brain injury (TBI) roughly doubles mortality. The spine must be protected until cleared, but over-immobilization itself causes harm. Polytrauma demands a leader who can hold the whole patient in mind while specialists focus on their region. This skill covers neuroprotective TBI management, spinal immobilization decisions, raised intracranial pressure control, and the prioritization logic of damage control in multi-system injury. It assumes an acute care setting and must always defer to local neurotrauma, trauma surgery, and institutional mass-casualty protocols, with early specialist escalation.

## Core Rules

### Prevent Secondary Brain Injury Above Almost Everything Else

In severe TBI, the primary mechanical injury is largely irreversible; what the clinician controls is secondary injury from hypoxia, hypotension, hypercapnia, hyperthermia, and raised intracranial pressure (ICP). Treat these as emergencies in themselves. Maintain oxygenation (avoid hypoxia, target oxygen saturation at the high end of normal), avoid hypotension aggressively (a single systolic below 90 mmHg is harmful; many protocols target a mean arterial pressure adequate to maintain cerebral perfusion), and avoid both hypercapnia (which raises ICP via cerebral vasodilation) and severe hypocapnia (which causes cerebral vasoconstriction and ischemia). Normocapnia, normoxia, normotension, and normothermia are the neuroprotective baseline. Any departure from this baseline in a brain-injured patient is a threat to salvageable tissue and warrants immediate correction and, where available, neurosurgical consultation.

### Manage Raised Intracranial Pressure Systematically

Raised ICP threatens cerebral perfusion and can herniate the brainstem, which is fatal. Suspect raised ICP in any severe TBI with depressed consciousness, asymmetric or fixed pupils, posturing, or a Cushing response (hypertension with bradycardia and irregular respiration). When ICP monitoring is indicated (typically severe TBI with abnormal CT, or normal CT with high-risk features), use it to guide therapy. Target cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP = MAP minus ICP) rather than ICP alone; common adult targets sit in the 60–70 mmHg range, individualized to the patient. Treat intracranial hypertension in a stepwise fashion: head elevation to 30 degrees, adequate sedation and analgesia, normocapnia, osmolar therapy (mannitol or hypertonic saline), and in refractory cases, second-tier therapies such as decompressive craniectomy, barbiturates, or hypothermia, decided with neurocritical care and neurosurgery. Do not improvise these thresholds; use institutional protocols and escalate to neurosurgery early when ICP is uncontrolled.

### Decide Spinal Immobilization Deliberately, and Clear It Deliberately

Spinal immobilization is applied when there is a credible mechanism and any of: altered mental status, intoxication, distracting injury, neurologic deficit, or spinal pain or tenderness. Immobilization is not benign — it restricts breathing, raises ICP, causes pressure injury, and delays resuscitation — so it should be applied judiciously and removed as soon as the spine is cleared. Use validated clinical decision rules (such as the Canadian C-spine rule or NEXUS criteria for the cervical spine) to clear clinically, and image (CT) those who cannot be cleared clinically. Do not clear the spine on inadequate imaging or in a patient who cannot cooperate with a reliable exam. In the obtunded patient, clearance often requires MRI or a multidisciplinary decision; never assume the spine is safe because the patient is intubated. When in doubt, maintain precautions and consult.

### Prioritize Threats to Life Over Threats to Limb and Over Threats to Brain Recovery

In polytrauma, the sequence is: threats to life (airway, breathing, circulation), then threats to limb, then threats to brain recovery and functional outcome — but with the critical caveat that secondary brain injury is itself a threat to life and must be prevented in parallel. The damage control principle applies: stop the bleeding, control contamination from hollow viscus injury, and stabilize the patient before definitive repair. Long-bone fractures and pelvic fractures are sources of blood loss and should be stabilized early; pelvic binders, external fixation, and splinting reduce hemorrhage. Do not let a dramatic brain injury distract from an exsanguinating abdominal bleed, and do not let a bleeding limb distract from a compromised airway. The team leader must continuously re-rank the threats as physiology changes.

### Apply Damage Control Principles Across Systems

Damage control surgery and damage control resuscitation are linked concepts: control life-threatening hemorrhage and contamination with the minimum operative time, restore physiology in the intensive care unit, and return for definitive repair once the patient is warm, coagulopathy is corrected, and acidosis has resolved. In polytrauma, this often means abbreviated laparotomy, temporary abdominal closure, external fixation of fractures rather than definitive internal fixation, and deferral of non-critical repairs. The goal of the first operation is survival, not completeness. Coordinate the sequence of operations across specialties — general or trauma surgery, orthopedics, neurosurgery, vascular — so that the most lethal injury is addressed first. A patient with a severe TBI and an exsanguinating pelvic fracture needs hemorrhage control before definitive neurosurgical intervention; the brain is best protected by a perfusing, oxygenated circulation.

### Recognize and Manage Neurogenic and Spinal Shock

Spinal cord injury can produce neurogenic shock (loss of sympathetic tone below the lesion, causing hypotension with bradycardia and warm skin), which is easily confused with hemorrhagic shock but requires different management. In the polytrauma patient, always assume hemorrhage first and rule it out before attributing hypotension to neurogenic causes; treating neurogenic shock with fluids alone worsens outcomes. Neurogenic shock may require vasopressors to support mean arterial pressure and spinal cord perfusion, with targets guided by institutional neurotrauma protocols. Spinal cord perfusion pressure matters: sustained hypotension extends cord injury. Escalate to a spine or neurocritical care specialist for cord-injured patients with persistent hemodynamic instability.

### Escalate to Neurosurgery and the Trauma Service Early and Explicitly

Severe TBI, suspected raised ICP, open or depressed skull fractures, penetrating brain injury, spinal cord injury with neurologic deficit, and unstable spinal fractures require urgent specialist involvement. The error is to complete the workup before calling — the call should be made as soon as the diagnosis is suspected, in parallel with imaging and stabilization. State the injury, the physiology, the imaging available, and the specific question (does this patient need an operation, ICP monitor, or transfer?). Document the time of consultation and the plan. For polytrauma, a single trauma team leader should coordinate all specialty consultations to avoid conflicting plans.

## Common Traps

### Letting Hypoxia or Hypotension Persist in Severe TBI

A single hypotensive episode doubles mortality in severe TBI. Counter by treating oxygenation and blood pressure as neuroprotective priorities, monitoring continuously, and correcting departures immediately.

### Over-Immobilizing or Under-Clearing the Spine

Prolonged immobilization causes pressure injury, respiratory compromise, and raised ICP, while premature clearance risks catastrophic cord injury. Counter by using validated decision rules to clear clinically when possible, imaging when needed, and consulting for the obtunded patient rather than guessing.

### Attributing Shock to Neurogenic Causes Before Excluding Hemorrhage

Neurogenic shock is a diagnosis of exclusion in polytrauma; treating hypotension with fluids alone when the cause is bleeding worsens hemorrhage and coagulopathy. Counter by assuming hemorrhage first, ruling out sources with FAST, imaging, and physical exam, and using vasopressors for confirmed neurogenic shock per protocol.

### Chasing the Dramatic Brain Injury While the Patient Bleeds

A severe TBI cannot be salvaged if the patient exsanguinates. Counter by prioritizing hemorrhage control in parallel with neuroprotection, and coordinating the operative sequence so lethal bleeding is controlled first.

### Attempting Definitive Repair in the Unstable Polytrauma Patient

Definitive orthopedic or abdominal repair in a cold, acidotic, coagulopathic patient kills. Counter by applying damage control principles — abbreviated surgery, temporary closure, external fixation — and returning for definitive repair after physiology is restored.

### Delaying Neurosurgical Consultation to Complete Imaging

The neurosurgeon should be called when the diagnosis is suspected, not after every scan is done. Counter by consulting in parallel with stabilization and imaging, and stating the specific question clearly.

### Relying on Single ICP or CPP Numbers Without Trending

A single normal reading does not exclude intracranial hypertension, and aggressive treatment based on one number can cause harm. Counter by using continuous monitoring, trending values, and integrating the clinical exam and imaging with the guidance of neurocritical care.

## Self-Check

- Did I prevent secondary brain injury by maintaining normoxia, normocapnia, normotension, and normothermia, treating any departure as an emergency?
- For suspected raised ICP, did I apply systematic stepwise management (head elevation, sedation, normocapnia, osmolar therapy) and escalate to neurosurgery for refractory cases?
- Did I apply spinal immobilization judiciously, clear the spine using validated rules or imaging when appropriate, and avoid premature clearance in the obtunded patient?
- In polytrauma, did I prioritize threats to life (airway, breathing, circulation) over limb and over brain recovery, while preventing secondary brain injury in parallel?
- Did I apply damage control principles across surgical specialties, coordinating the operative sequence so the most lethal injury is addressed first?
- Did I distinguish neurogenic from hemorrhagic shock by excluding hemorrhage first, and use vasopressors appropriately for confirmed neurogenic shock?
- Did I escalate to neurosurgery and the trauma service early, in parallel with imaging, with a clear question and documented plan?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional neurotrauma and trauma protocols.
