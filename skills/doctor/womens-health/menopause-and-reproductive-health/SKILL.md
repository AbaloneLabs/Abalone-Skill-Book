# Menopause and Reproductive Health

Reproductive health across the female lifespan is a series of transitions — puberty, the reproductive years, perimenopause, and postmenopause — and at each stage the clinician balances symptom relief, prevention, fertility, and safety against a shifting hormonal and risk-profile background. The gravest errors arise from two directions: treating menopause as a disease to be medicated without weighing thrombotic, oncologic, and cardiovascular risk, and dismissing genuine symptoms (vasomotor, genitourinary, mood, sexual) as "just aging" while withholding effective, evidence-based care. Contraception and infertility decisions are similarly high-stakes: a method that is ideal for one woman may be dangerous for another, and delays in infertility evaluation squander a finite reproductive window. This skill covers menopause and perimenopause management, hormone therapy risk-benefit reasoning and contraindications, contraceptive choice by comorbidity, the initial infertility workup, and sexual health, with disciplined escalation to gynecology and reproductive endocrinology.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose Menopause Clinically and Distinguish Perimenopause from Postmenopause

Menopause is diagnosed clinically by the cessation of menstruation for twelve months in a woman of expected age (typically late forties to early fifties), not by laboratory testing, and follicle-stimulating hormone levels are generally unnecessary for diagnosis in symptomatic women of typical age. Perimenopause, the years preceding the final menstrual period, is characterized by irregular cycles, fluctuating estrogen, and the onset of vasomotor and other symptoms; it is the window during which contraception is still required and when hormone therapy decisions begin. Recognize that symptoms can begin years before the final period and persist for years after, that the genitourinary syndrome of menopause (vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, urinary symptoms) is often chronic and under-reported, and that mood and cognitive symptoms, while common, warrant assessment for independent causes before being attributed to menopause alone. Reserve laboratory testing for atypical presentations such as premature ovarian insufficiency (menopause before age 40), where two FSH values in the menopausal range a month apart confirm the diagnosis and where specialist referral is mandatory.

### Apply a Structured Risk-Benefit Assessment to Hormone Therapy

Hormone therapy (HT, also hormone replacement therapy) is the most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms and has a role in bone protection, but its use demands explicit risk-benefit reasoning rather than reflexive prescription or reflexive avoidance. For women under 60 or within ten years of menopause onset without contraindications, the benefit-harm balance generally favors systemic HT for bothersome vasomotor symptoms; the absolute risks of venous thromboembolism, stroke, and breast cancer are small in this population but real and must be discussed. Route matters: transdermal estrogen is associated with a lower venous thromboembolism risk than oral, and progestogen is required for endometrial protection in any woman with a uterus. Duration should be individualized and periodically reassessed, with the lowest effective dose for the shortest appropriate time, and discontinuation considered in light of ongoing symptoms and changing risk. For genitourinary symptoms alone, low-dose vaginal estrogen is highly effective, has minimal systemic absorption, and can be used long-term without a progestogen in most women; do not deny this treatment to symptomatic women out of misplaced caution.

### Know and Apply the Absolute Contraindications to Hormone Therapy

HT has absolute contraindications that must be actively screened for before prescription, because the harm in these settings is significant. These include current or past estrogen-dependent cancer (notably breast cancer), active or history of venous thromboembolism, known thrombophilia, active or recent arterial cardiovascular disease (stroke, myocardial infarction), active liver disease, and unexplained vaginal bleeding. Relative contraindications and cautions include migraine with aura, uncontrolled hypertension, hypertriglyceridemia, and strong family history of breast or thromboembolic disease. Screen explicitly for each before initiating systemic HT, and document the assessment. For women with contraindications to systemic estrogen, non-hormonal options for vasomotor symptoms (such as certain antidepressants, gabapentinoids, and other approved non-hormonal therapies) remain effective and should be offered rather than leaving symptoms untreated.

### Choose Contraception by Comorbidity Using the Eligibility Criteria

Contraceptive choice is not a matter of preference but of safety, and the WHO and CDC medical eligibility criteria provide a structured framework for matching method to comorbidity. Combined hormonal contraception (pill, patch, ring) is contraindicated in women with a high risk of venous thromboembolism (smokers over 35, prior VTE, thrombophilia, immobility), migraine with aura, uncontrolled hypertension, current or past cardiovascular disease, and a history of estrogen-sensitive cancer. Long-acting reversible methods (intrauterine devices, both copper and hormonal, and the progestogen implant) are highly effective, are safe in most women in whom combined methods are contraindicated, and are first-line for many women including nulliparous and adolescent patients. The progestogen-only pill, depot injection, and implant have fewer contraindications but specific considerations (bone density with long-term depot use, particularly in younger women). Match the method to the individual's medical history, preferences, and reproductive plans, and screen for contraindications explicitly rather than by assumption.

### Evaluate Infertility Systematically and Without Delay in Older Women

Infertility (failure to conceive after twelve months of regular unprotected intercourse, or six months in women over 35) warrants a structured initial workup of both partners, because delay reduces the chance of successful treatment, particularly with advancing female age. The initial assessment includes confirmation of ovulation (menstrual history, day-21 progesterone, or ovulation predictor kits), assessment of ovarian reserve where indicated (anti-Müllerian hormone, antral follicle count), evaluation of tubal patency and uterine cavity (hysterosalpingogram or equivalent), and a semen analysis of the male partner, because male factors account for a substantial proportion of infertility and are easily and inexpensively tested. Counsel explicitly that female fertility declines sharply after the mid-thirties, that advanced age is itself an indication for earlier referral, and that smoking, obesity, and extremes of weight reduce fertility in both partners. Refer to reproductive endocrinology and infertility specialists for couples with identified abnormalities, advanced female age, or failure to conceive after initial management, because assisted reproductive technology success is also age-dependent.

### Address Sexual Health Directly and Non-Judgmentally

Sexual health is a legitimate and often neglected part of reproductive care, encompassing sexual function, desire, pain, satisfaction, and safety. Take a brief sexual history that normalizes the conversation, screen for sexual concerns (low desire, arousal or orgasm difficulty, dyspareunia), and distinguish biologic causes (hormonal, vascular, neurologic, medication-related, structural) from psychological and relational causes, recognizing that they frequently coexist. Treat reversible contributors (vaginal atrophy with local estrogen, depression, medication effects, relationship distress), and refer to sexual medicine or psychosexual therapy for persistent or complex concerns. Screen for sexually transmitted infections in at-risk patients, offer cervical screening per guidelines, and address contraception, preconception care, and unintended pregnancy in an integrated way. Do not assume sexual inactivity or orientation; ask openly.

### Escalate to Gynecology and Reproductive Endocrinology by Defined Criteria

Define the thresholds for specialist referral in advance. Refer urgently to gynecology for suspected premature ovarian insufficiency (for hormone replacement and bone health), for abnormal bleeding that fails initial investigation or management, and for any adnexal or endometrial concern. Refer to reproductive endocrinology and infertility for couples meeting infertility criteria, for women with recurrent pregnancy loss, for those with known reduced ovarian reserve, and for fertility preservation discussions before gonadotoxic therapy. Refer to menopause specialists for complex perimenopausal or menopausal management, particularly when contraindications to HT coexist with severe symptoms. State the referral threshold explicitly and document the safety-netting and follow-up.

## Common Traps

### Over-Prescribing or Under-Prescribing Hormone Therapy by Default

Prescribing HT reflexively without risk-benefit reasoning, or withholding it reflexively from symptomatic women who would benefit. Counter by applying a structured, individualized risk-benefit assessment and screening for contraindications.

### Missing an Absolute Contraindication to Estrogen

Initiating combined hormonal contraception or systemic HT in a woman with prior VTE, migraine with aura, estrogen-sensitive cancer, or active cardiovascular disease. Counter by screening for each contraindication explicitly and documenting the assessment.

### Denying Vaginal Estrogen for Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause

Withholding low-dose vaginal estrogen out of misplaced concern about systemic risk, leaving women with chronic dyspareunia and urinary symptoms untreated. Counter by recognizing that local estrogen has minimal systemic absorption and is appropriate for most women.

### Choosing Contraception by Preference Rather Than Eligibility

Defaulting to combined methods without screening for comorbidities such as smoking over 35, migraine with aura, or hypertension. Counter by applying the medical eligibility criteria to match method to medical history.

### Delaying Infertility Workup, Particularly in Older Women

Reassuring couples to "keep trying" past the age-dependent threshold, squandering a finite reproductive window. Counter by initiating the structured workup at six months for women over 35 and referring promptly when abnormalities are found.

### Attributing Mood or Cognitive Symptoms Solely to Menopause

Missing depression, anxiety, thyroid disease, or other pathology by attributing symptoms to menopause without assessment. Counter by investigating independent causes before assigning symptoms to the hormonal transition.

### Neglecting Sexual Health

Failing to ask about sexual function, pain, or satisfaction, leaving treatable dysfunction unaddressed. Counter by normalizing a brief sexual history and treating or referring for identified concerns.

## Self-Check

- Have I diagnosed menopause clinically (twelve months of amenorrhea) and distinguished perimenopause from postmenopause, reserving laboratory testing for atypical presentations such as premature ovarian insufficiency?
- For hormone therapy, have I performed an explicit risk-benefit assessment, chosen the appropriate route and regimen, and documented the discussion of risks and duration?
- Have I screened for and documented every absolute contraindication to estrogen (VTE, thrombophilia, estrogen-sensitive cancer, cardiovascular disease, active liver disease, unexplained bleeding) before prescribing systemic HT or combined contraception?
- For genitourinary symptoms, have I offered low-dose vaginal estrogen rather than withholding effective treatment?
- Have I selected contraception using the medical eligibility criteria matched to the patient's comorbidities, age, and preferences, and considered long-acting reversible methods as first-line?
- For infertility, have I initiated a structured workup of both partners at the age-appropriate interval and referred promptly when abnormalities or advanced age are present?
- Have I taken a sexual history, screened for sexual dysfunction and infection risk, and treated or referred for identified concerns?
- Have I defined and documented the threshold for gynecology, reproductive endocrinology, and menopause specialist referral?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
