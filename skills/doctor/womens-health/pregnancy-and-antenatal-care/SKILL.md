# Pregnancy and Antenatal Care

Pregnancy is a physiologic state that reshapes nearly every organ system, and the clinician's central task is to distinguish normal adaptation from disease — often across a moving baseline. What is pathological in a non-pregnant adult may be expected in pregnancy (a resting heart rate of 95, a hemoglobin of 105, a systolic murmur), and what is a minor complaint in other patients (nausea, heartburn, leg swelling) may be the first sign of a disorder that threatens two lives. The gravest errors in pregnancy care come from three sources: treating normal physiologic change as disease, treating dangerous symptoms as normal, and prescribing as if the patient were not pregnant. This skill covers the recognition of must-not-miss obstetric emergencies, medication safety, the interpretation of pregnancy-altered vitals and labs, perinatal mental health, and the disciplined escalation to obstetric and specialist care.

## Core Rules

### Know the Normal Physiologic Changes and Re-Anchor Your Reference Ranges

Pregnancy alters cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, hematologic, and endocrine parameters, and failing to re-anchor reference ranges leads to both over- and under-treatment. Blood volume rises by roughly 40 to 50 percent, heart rate increases 10 to 20 beats per minute, and blood pressure falls in the first and second trimester before returning toward baseline near term; a "normal" blood pressure of 140/90 late in pregnancy is not normal. Cardiac output peaks in the second trimester, an innocent flow murmur is common, and the gravid uterus compresses the inferior vena cava in the supine position, so examine and resuscitate the symptomatic third-trimester patient in the left lateral tilt position. Respiratory changes include an increase in tidal volume and a respiratory alkalosis with compensatory renal bicarbonate loss; arterial or venous pCO2 is lower, and a pCO2 of 40 that looks "normal" may signal impending respiratory failure. Renal blood flow and glomerular filtration rate rise substantially, so serum creatinine above 0.85 to 0.9 mg/dL may already reflect renal injury. Hemoglobin falls due to hemodilution, and a mild anemia is physiologic, but a true drop requires workup. Re-anchor every vital and lab to pregnancy-specific and trimester-specific norms before deciding a value is reassuring.

### Maintain a Must-Not-Miss List and Act on It Before Certainty

Several obstetric conditions kill or maim rapidly and must be considered on any compatible presentation, not only when classic. Ectopic pregnancy presents with pain and bleeding in a reproductive-age woman with a positive or equivocal pregnancy test; any woman of childbearing potential with abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, syncope, or shoulder-tip pain has ectopic until proven otherwise, and a ruptured ectopic is a surgical emergency. Preeclampsia is new hypertension with proteinuria or end-organ dysfunction after 20 weeks; severe features (severe hypertension, thrombocytopenia, transaminitis, renal impairment, pulmonary edema, cerebral or visual symptoms) demand urgent obstetric review and often delivery, and eclampsia (seizure) and the HELLP syndrome (hemolysis, elevated liver enzymes, low platelets) are emergencies. Antepartum hemorrhage (bleeding after 20 weeks) demands assessment for placenta previa and placental abruption, with speculum and digital examination only after placental location is known, because vaginal exam against a previa can cause catastrophic hemorrhage. Sepsis in pregnancy may present with subtle signs because of the altered baseline; use pregnancy-adjusted parameters and treat suspected sepsis with the sepsis bundle, never attributing tachycardia or tachypnea to "just pregnancy." For each must-not-miss diagnosis, state explicitly what would confirm or exclude it and the threshold for escalation.

### Confirm and Date the Pregnancy Accurately, Because Everything Depends on Gestational Age

Gestational age drives the differential, the viability threshold, the interpretation of labs, and the urgency of referral. Confirm pregnancy with a quantitative beta-hCG, and date the pregnancy by the first day of the last menstrual period corrected against first-trimester ultrasound, where crown-rump length in the first trimester is the most accurate measure; dating by later ultrasound or by fundal height is progressively less reliable. A beta-hCG above the discriminatory zone (roughly 1500 to 3500 mIU/mL depending on modality) without an intrauterine pregnancy on transvaginal ultrasound raises ectopic until proven otherwise, but a low or "normal" hCG never excludes ectopic, and ectopic can coexist with an apparent intrauterine pregnancy in heterotopic pregnancy, particularly after fertility treatment. When gestational age is uncertain, image before concluding, and when dating and clinical findings disagree, trust the imaging and seek obstetric input.

### Apply Pregnancy-Safe Pharmacology and Teratogenicity Reasoning Explicitly

Prescribing in pregnancy requires explicit teratogenicity reasoning because the placenta is not a barrier and because fetal organogenesis, timing, and dose all matter. Identify and avoid known teratogens and fetotoxic agents: warfarin (embryopathy, fetal bleeding), ACE inhibitors and angiotensin receptor blockers (fetopathy, oligohydramnios), retinoids (severe malformations), tetracyclines (teeth and bone), sulfonamides near term (kernicterus), and NSAIDs after 20 weeks (oligohydramnios, fetal renal injury) and after 32 to 34 weeks (premature closure of the ductus arteriosus). Prefer agents with established safety data over newer drugs, use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time, and document the risk-benefit discussion. Do not undertreat serious disease out of fear: untreated maternal asthma, hypertension, depression, epilepsy, or infection are themselves fetotoxic. For any drug, weigh the risk of the disease untreated against the risk of the drug, and when uncertain, consult a teratology information service or the obstetric team before prescribing.

### Screen Systematically and Act on Abnormal Results, Not Just Document Them

Antenatal care is a scheduled surveillance program, and each screen exists because acting on the result changes outcome. Confirm blood group and antibody status, and give anti-D immunoglobulin to Rh-negative women at the indicated times to prevent sensitization. Screen for anemia, diabetes (gestational diabetes screening, with treatment reducing perinatal morbidity), asymptomatic bacteriuria (treatment prevents pyelonephritis and preterm birth), HIV, syphilis, hepatitis B and C, and rubella and varicella immunity. Offer aneuploidy and structural screening at the correct gestational ages, because timing windows are narrow. Identify risk factors for preterm birth, preeclampsia (consider low-dose aspirin prophylaxis in high-risk women), and gestational diabetes, and apply preventive interventions. Each abnormal result must have a documented action; a screen that is performed but not acted upon provides no protection.

### Recognize and Treat Perinatal Mental Illness as a Medical Emergency in Its Own Right

Perinatal mental illness, particularly depression, anxiety, and postpartum psychosis, is a leading cause of maternal morbidity and mortality, and it is under-recognized because symptoms overlap with normal postpartum experience. Screen with validated tools (such as the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale) in pregnancy and the postpartum period, and ask directly about intrusive thoughts of harm to self or baby. Postpartum psychosis is a psychiatric emergency: new onset of delusions, hallucinations, severe insomnia, or rapid mood change in the first two weeks postpartum demands urgent psychiatric assessment and is not a variant of the blues. When prescribing psychotropics in pregnancy or lactation, balance the teratogenic and infant-exposure risks against the substantial risks of untreated maternal mental illness, and involve perinatal psychiatry early for any new or worsening illness.

### Escalate to Obstetrics Early and Define the Threshold for Transfer

Obstetric emergencies deteriorate quickly, and the threshold to call must be deliberately low. Define in advance the presentations that mandate immediate obstetric involvement: suspected ectopic, preeclampsia with severe features, eclampsia, significant antepartum or postpartum hemorrhage, suspected abruption or previa, sepsis in pregnancy, severe headache or visual symptoms in a hypertensive pregnant patient, reduced fetal movements at a viable gestation, preterm labor, and any maternal instability. When a pregnant patient is unstable, resuscitate in parallel with obstetric notification, and prepare for transfer to a facility with obstetric and neonatal capability before deterioration, not after. State the escalation threshold explicitly in your plan and document the time of the call and the response.

## Common Traps

### Attributing Dangerous Symptoms to Normal Pregnancy

Dismissing headache as "just pregnancy," dyspnea as "the baby pushing up," or edema as normal, when these may signal preeclampsia, pulmonary edema, or venous thromboembolism. Counter by re-anchoring to red-flag symptoms and investigating rather than reassuring.

### Missing Ectopic Pregnancy in an Atypical Presentation

Assuming ectopic always presents with classic pain and bleeding, or excluding it because the hCG is low or the patient is "too well." Counter by treating any reproductive-age woman with abdominal pain, syncope, or vaginal bleeding and a positive or equivocal pregnancy test as ectopic until imaging excludes it.

### Performing Vaginal Examination Before Locating the Placenta in Antepartum Hemorrhage

Digital vaginal exam against a placenta previa can precipitate catastrophic hemorrhage. Counter by establishing placental location with ultrasound before any speculum or digital examination in antepartum bleeding.

### Prescribing Without Teratogenicity Reasoning

Defaulting to usual first-line agents (an ACE inhibitor for hypertension, an NSAID for pain, a retinoid for acne) without considering fetal risk. Counter by applying explicit pregnancy-safe pharmacology and consulting a teratologen or obstetrician when uncertain.

### Undertreating Maternal Disease Out of Fear of Fetal Harm

Withholding necessary treatment for asthma, hypertension, depression, infection, or epilepsy, exposing the fetus to the greater risk of untreated maternal disease. Counter by weighing the risk of disease against the risk of drug and documenting the reasoning.

### Failing to Screen or Failing to Act on Abnormal Screens

Performing tests but not following up abnormal results, or missing the narrow timing window for aneuploidy and structural screening. Counter by treating each screen as an action item with a documented response.

### Underestimating Perinatal Mental Illness

Conflating depression or psychosis with the "baby blues," or fearing that asking about intrusive thoughts will cause harm. Counter by screening directly and escalating postpartum psychosis immediately.

### Delaying Obstetric Escalation

Waiting for certainty or for deterioration before calling obstetrics. Counter by defining escalation thresholds in advance and calling early, accepting that over-calling is safer than delay.

## Self-Check

- Have I re-anchored vitals and labs to pregnancy-specific and trimester-specific reference ranges rather than non-pregnant norms?
- For any reproductive-age woman with abdominal pain, bleeding, or syncope, have I excluded ectopic pregnancy with appropriate imaging and beta-hCG?
- Have I maintained an explicit must-not-miss list (ectopic, preeclampsia/eclampsia/HELLP, antepartum hemorrhage, sepsis, venous thromboembolism) and stated how each is excluded?
- In antepartum hemorrhage, did I confirm placental location before any vaginal examination?
- For every prescription, have I applied explicit teratogenicity reasoning, preferred established agents, and documented the risk-benefit discussion?
- Have I performed and acted on each scheduled antenatal screen, including blood group and antibodies, diabetes, infection screening, and aneuploidy and structural screening at the correct gestational ages?
- Have I screened for perinatal mental illness with a validated tool and escalated any suspected postpartum psychosis as a psychiatric emergency?
- Have I defined the obstetric escalation threshold explicitly, called early on red-flag presentations, and documented the time and response?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
