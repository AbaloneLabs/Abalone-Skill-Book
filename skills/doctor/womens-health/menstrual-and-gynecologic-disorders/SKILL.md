# Menstrual and Gynecologic Disorders

Menstrual and gynecologic complaints are common, and their danger lies in how easily serious pathology hides behind ordinary-sounding symptoms. Heavy, irregular, or absent bleeding, chronic pelvic pain, and a pelvic mass are each the final common pathway of a wide differential that ranges from benign to lethal, and the clinician's task is to stratify by danger and by likelihood rather than to reach for a single diagnosis. Two errors dominate this domain: dismissing bleeding or pain as "just hormones" while missing pregnancy-related, structural, or malignant causes, and over-investigating benign variation without addressing the patient's actual concern. A disciplined approach uses structured frameworks to organize the differential, applies risk stratification to decide who needs urgent imaging, and never forgets that any reproductive-age woman with bleeding or pain could be pregnant. This skill covers abnormal uterine bleeding, the pelvic pain differential, ovarian mass risk stratification, endometriosis, when to image and refer, and the must-not-miss gynecologic emergencies.

## Core Rules

### Always Exclude Pregnancy First in Any Reproductive-Age Woman with Bleeding or Pain

Before attributing vaginal bleeding or pelvic pain to a menstrual or gynecologic cause, exclude pregnancy with a beta-hCG. Ectopic pregnancy is the single most dangerous must-not-miss diagnosis in this population: it can present with atypical or minimal symptoms, a low or "normal" hCG does not exclude it, and rupture causes rapid, life-threatening hemorrhage. Any woman of childbearing potential with abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, syncope, shoulder-tip pain, or hemodynamic instability has ectopic until proven otherwise, and a positive or equivocal pregnancy test with an empty uterus on transvaginal ultrasound at the discriminatory zone demands urgent gynecologic assessment. Make the pregnancy test a reflex, not an afterthought, and document it.

### Structure Abnormal Uterine Bleeding with the PALM-COEIN Framework

Abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB) is best organized by the international PALM-COEIN classification, which separates structural causes (Polyp, Adenomyosis, Leiomyoma, Malignancy and hyperplasia) from non-structural causes (Coagulopathy, Ovulatory dysfunction, Endometrial, Iatrogenic, and Not otherwise classified). This structure forces breadth and prevents premature closure on a single explanation. Quantify the bleeding objectively (menstrual loss interfering with quality of life, flooding, clots, anemia) rather than relying on the patient's word "heavy," because objective heavy menstrual bleeding with iron-deficiency anemia warrants investigation. Establish the pattern: intermenstrual bleeding, postcoital bleeding, and postmenopausal bleeding are red flags that raise concern for cervical or endometrial pathology and mandate examination and targeted investigation. Apply PALM-COEIN explicitly in your assessment so no category is overlooked.

### Investigate Based on Red Flags and Endometrial Cancer Risk, Not Routinely

The decision to investigate abnormal bleeding hinges on red flags and on endometrial cancer risk, not on blanket testing. Red flags requiring prompt evaluation include postmenopausal bleeding (consider endometrial cancer until proven otherwise), intermenstrual and postcoital bleeding (cervical pathology, including cancer), persistent heavy bleeding causing anemia, and bleeding in women with risk factors for endometrial hyperplasia and cancer (obesity, polycystic ovary syndrome, nulliparity, late menopause, unopposed estrogen, tamoxifen, Lynch syndrome). First-line investigation typically includes a pelvic examination, a full blood count and ferritin, and, where structural or endometrial pathology is suspected, transvaginal ultrasound to assess the endometrial thickness and look for polyps, fibroids, and adnexal masses. Endometrial sampling is indicated for postmenopausal bleeding, for persistent irregular bleeding in high-risk women, and when imaging suggests thickened endometrium. Avoid the trap of repeated reassurance without investigation when bleeding persists or red flags are present.

### Approach Pelvic Pain with a Broad Differential and Time-Critical Exclusions

Pelvic pain has a wide differential spanning gynecologic, gastrointestinal, urologic, and musculoskeletal causes, and the first task is to separate acute from chronic and to exclude time-critical emergencies. Acute pelvic pain must-not-miss diagnoses include ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion (a surgical emergency where the ovary is salvaged only with prompt detorsion), ruptured ovarian cyst, pelvic inflammatory disease and tubo-ovarian abscess, appendicitis, and ureteric colic. Ovarian torsion classically presents with sudden severe unilateral pain with nausea and vomiting, but the presentation is variable, and Doppler ultrasound showing absent or reduced venous flow supports the diagnosis; a normal Doppler does not fully exclude torsion, so clinical suspicion mandates urgent gynecology review. For chronic pelvic pain (lasting more than six months), consider endometriosis, adenomyosis, chronic pelvic inflammatory disease, adhesions, interstitial cystitis, irritable bowel syndrome, and pelvic floor dysfunction, and recognize that chronic pelvic pain is often multifactorial and benefits from a biopsychosocial approach rather than a single disease label.

### Risk-Stratify Ovarian Masses and Recognize the Features of Malignancy

An ovarian mass found incidentally or on investigation is not automatically benign, and the task is to risk-stratify using imaging features, menopausal status, and tumor markers rather than to assume a simple cyst. Features concerning for malignancy on ultrasound include solid components, thick septations, papillary projections, internal vascularity, and ascites; risk stratification tools (such as IOTA criteria or risk of malignancy indices) help categorize masses. CA-125 is supportive but not diagnostic: it can be elevated in benign conditions (endometriosis, fibroids, menstruation, pregnancy, pelvic infection) and can be normal in early ovarian cancer. Premenopausal women with simple cysts are usually managed conservatively with surveillance, whereas complex masses, masses in postmenopausal women, or masses with elevated markers warrant specialist gynecology or gynecologic oncology referral. Any ascites, pleural effusion, or palpable abdominal mass alongside an ovarian lesion suggests advanced disease and urgent referral.

### Diagnose and Manage Endometriosis with a Structured, Patient-Centered Approach

Endometriosis is common, frequently delayed in diagnosis, and causes pain, dyspareunia, dysmenorrhea, and infertility that profoundly affect quality of life. Suspect endometriosis in women with cyclical pain, deep dyspareunia, chronic pelvic pain, or subfertility, and recognize that imaging may be normal; transvaginal ultrasound can identify endometriomas and deep infiltrating disease but cannot exclude superficial peritoneal disease, the diagnosis of which ultimately requires laparoscopy. Empiric hormonal treatment (combined oral contraceptive or progestogen) is a reasonable first step for symptomatic suspected endometriosis without needing immediate surgery, with referral to gynecology for disease that is severe, refractory to initial management, or associated with deep infiltrating features or infertility. Address pain and quality of life in parallel with fertility goals, because the priorities sometimes conflict, and involve a multidisciplinary approach including pain specialists and psychology for refractory cases.

### Decide on Imaging, Referral, and Escalation by Danger and Likelihood

Match the investigation and referral to the danger and likelihood of the suspected diagnosis. Acute, severe pain with peritonism, hemodynamic instability, or fever mandates urgent assessment, imaging, and gynecology referral. Suspected torsion, ruptured ectopic, or septic pelvic inflammatory disease are surgical or medical emergencies requiring immediate escalation. Chronic or low-risk presentations warrant structured outpatient investigation. Always state explicitly the threshold for gynecology referral and the safety-netting advice given to the patient, including the symptoms that should prompt emergency return. When a diagnosis is uncertain but the patient is unwell, escalate to gynecology before completing the full workup.

## Common Traps

### Failing to Exclude Pregnancy

Attributing bleeding or pain to a menstrual or gynecologic cause without a beta-hCG, thereby missing ectopic pregnancy. Counter by making a pregnancy test a reflex for any reproductive-age woman with bleeding or pelvic pain.

### Dismissing Bleeding as Normal Variation

Reassuring a patient with heavy, irregular, or postcoital bleeding without investigating red flags or endometrial cancer risk. Counter by applying the PALM-COEIN framework and investigating based on red flags and risk.

### Missing Ovarian Torsion by Awaiting Classic Features or a Definitive Scan

Delaying gynecology referral because the Doppler appears normal or the pain is atypical. Counter by escalating on clinical suspicion, recognizing that torsion is a time-critical surgical emergency.

### Over-Relying on CA-125 to Rule Out Ovarian Cancer

Treating a normal CA-125 as exclusion of ovarian malignancy despite concerning imaging. Counter by integrating imaging features, menopausal status, and clinical context, and referring complex or high-risk masses.

### Labeling Chronic Pelvic Pain as Psychological Without Investigation

Dismissing chronic pelvic pain without a structured assessment for endometriosis, adenomyosis, interstitial cystitis, or bowel pathology. Counter by using a broad differential and a biopsychosocial, multidisciplinary approach.

### Delaying Investigation of Postmenopausal Bleeding

Reassuring postmenopausal bleeding as atrophy without excluding endometrial cancer. Counter by treating postmenopausal bleeding as endometrial cancer until proven otherwise with imaging and endometrial sampling.

### Inadequate Safety-Netting and Follow-Up

Failing to tell the patient which symptoms require emergency return or to arrange follow-up for persistent symptoms. Counter by documenting explicit safety-netting and a defined review point.

## Self-Check

- For every reproductive-age woman with vaginal bleeding or pelvic pain, have I excluded pregnancy with a beta-hGC and considered ectopic pregnancy?
- Have I structured abnormal uterine bleeding using the PALM-COEIN framework rather than settling on a single cause?
- Have I investigated based on red flags (postmenopausal, intermenstrual, postcoital bleeding; anemia; endometrial cancer risk factors) rather than routinely or not at all?
- For acute pelvic pain, have I excluded the time-critical emergencies (ectopic, torsion, ruptured cyst, pelvic inflammatory disease, appendicitis)?
- Have I risk-stratified any ovarian mass using imaging features, menopausal status, and tumor markers, and referred complex or high-risk masses to gynecology or gynecologic oncology?
- For suspected endometriosis, have I initiated empiric management where appropriate and referred severe, refractory, or fertility-related disease?
- Have I stated the threshold for gynecology referral, documented safety-netting advice, and arranged follow-up for persistent symptoms?
- Have I escalated immediately for hemodynamic instability, peritonism, fever, or suspected torsion rather than completing the full workup first?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
