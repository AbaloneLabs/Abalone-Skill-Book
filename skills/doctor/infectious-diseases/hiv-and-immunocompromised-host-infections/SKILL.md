# HIV and Immunocompromised Host Infections

The immunocompromised host breaks the ordinary rules of infection. Pathogens that are trivial in the immunocompetent become lethal, common infections present atypically, opportunistic organisms appear that are rarely seen in normal hosts, and the usual signs of infection — fever, pus, consolidation — are blunted or absent. The recurring failures in this population are predictable: treating a fever as routine when the host is neutropenic, attributing pulmonary disease to community pneumonia when the differential includes Pneumocystis, missing cytomegalovirus end-organ disease, initiating antiretroviral therapy at the wrong moment and triggering immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome, and forgetting prophylaxis in patients whose CD4 count or immunosuppressive regimen puts them at predictable risk. This skill covers the CD4-based and regimen-based risk stratification, opportunistic pathogen recognition, the timing of antiretroviral therapy and prophylaxis, immune reconstitution syndromes, and the escalation pathways for HIV and other immunocompromised hosts.

## Core Rules

### Stratify Risk by CD4 Count in HIV and by Immunosuppressive Regimen in Other Hosts

The single most useful organizing principle is the degree and type of immune defect. In HIV, the CD4 count defines the opportunistic infection risk: above 500 the differential resembles the immunocompetent host; from 200 to 500 bacterial pneumonia, tuberculosis, and Kaposi sarcoma become more prominent; below 200 Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia prophylaxis is indicated; below 100 toxoplasmosis and cryptococcosis emerge; below 50 cytomegalovirus (CMV) end-organ disease and disseminated Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC) become major threats. In non-HIV immunocompromised hosts, stratify by the regimen and timeframe: the neutropenic chemotherapy patient is at risk for invasive bacterial and fungal infection; the solid organ transplant recipient moves through predictable phases — first month (donor-derived and nosocomial), one to six months (opportunistic infection under peak immunosuppression), and beyond six months (community-acquired infection); the biologic recipient carries class-specific risks, such as tuberculosis and fungal infection with TNF inhibitors and hepatitis B reactivation with B-cell depleting therapy. Know the host, know the calendar, and let both shape the differential.

### Recognize the Signature Opportunistic Pathogens and Their Presentations

Each opportunistic pathogen has a signature that, once recognized, narrows the workup. Pneumocystis pneumonia presents with subacute dyspnea, dry cough, hypoxia often disproportionate to the chest examination, and a diffuse interstitial pattern with elevated lactate dehydrogenase; the absence of hypoxia argues against it. Cerebral toxoplasmosis presents with ring-enhancing brain lesions, headache, and focal deficits in a patient with a low CD4 count. Cryptococcal meningitis presents with subacute headache and fever, often without classic meningeal signs, and requires lumbar puncture with cryptococcal antigen testing and pressure measurement. CMV end-organ disease manifests as retinitis with visual change, colitis with diarrhea and bleeding, pneumonitis, or a mono-like syndrome with marrow suppression. Disseminated MAC presents with fever, weight loss, anemia, and hepatosplenomegaly. Recognizing the signature allows targeted, fast testing rather than a broad unfocused workup.

### Time Antiretroviral Therapy Initiation to the Clinical Context

The timing of antiretroviral therapy (ART) initiation is not automatic; it depends on the presenting opportunistic infection and the risk of immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS). For most patients, early ART improves outcomes, but for certain conditions — notably cryptococcal meningitis and tuberculosis with very low CD4 counts — immediate ART can provoke life-threatening IRIS, and a short deferral is recommended until the acute infection is controlled. Establish the diagnosis before starting ART when an opportunistic infection is suspected, because starting ART first can obscure the diagnosis and worsen inflammation. Coordinate with an HIV specialist on timing, and never delay ART indefinitely — the deferral is measured in days to weeks, not months — but recognize that the wrong moment can be harmful.

### Anticipate and Manage Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome

IRIS is a paradoxical worsening that occurs as the recovering immune system reacts to latent or subclinical infection or antigen. It typically presents within weeks of ART initiation, more often in patients with very low baseline CD4 counts and preexisting opportunistic infection. Manifestations include worsening of treated infection (unmasking or paradoxical IRIS), such as worsening tuberculosis, cryptococcoma, or CMV immune recovery uveitis. Distinguish IRIS from treatment failure, drug resistance, a new opportunistic infection, or drug toxicity — all of which can mimic it — by reassessing the patient and the microbiology. Mild IRIS may be managed with supportive care and continuation of ART; severe or organ-threatening IRIS may require corticosteroids and specialist input. Do not routinely stop ART for IRIS without consultation, except in specific severe syndromes.

### Provide CD4- and Regimen-Based Prophylaxis Consistently

Prophylaxis is one of the highest-yield interventions in the immunocompromised host, and its omission is a recurring, preventable failure. In HIV, start Pneumocystis prophylaxis (typically trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole) at a CD4 count below 200, and consider it below 200 based on symptoms or for specific conditions such as oropharyngeal candidiasis; add toxoplasmosis coverage when the CD4 falls below 100; consider MAC prophylaxis below 50. Discontinue prophylaxis safely once the CD4 count has recovered above thresholds on stable ART. In non-HIV hosts, provide Pneumocystis prophylaxis for the duration of significant immunosuppression (high-dose corticosteroids, transplant, certain chemotherapies, and prolonged B-cell depletion). Address vaccination status appropriate to the host — avoiding live vaccines in severely immunosuppressed patients — and screen for and manage latent tuberculosis, hepatitis B and C, and other comorbid infections before they reactivate.

### Screen for and Prevent Drug Interactions and Adherence Failures

ART and immunosuppressive regimens are unforgiving of interactions and missed doses. Many antiretrovirals — particularly non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors, ritonavir-boosted protease inhibitors, and integrase inhibitors — have clinically important interactions with tuberculosis therapy, antifungals, anticonvulsants, statins, transplant immunosuppressants, and acid suppressants. Check interactions at every regimen change. Adherence is the dominant determinant of virologic success; assess it openly, address barriers, and consider long-acting injectable therapy where adherence to daily pills is difficult. Unexplained virologic failure should prompt resistance testing and specialist review rather than an empiric dose increase.

### Escalate to HIV, Transplant, or Infectious Diseases Specialists Early

The immunocompromised host is exactly the patient in which early specialist input changes outcome. Escalate to an HIV specialist for new diagnosis, ART initiation in the setting of opportunistic infection, treatment failure, complex resistance, pregnancy, or significant comorbidity. Escalate to transplant infectious diseases for the transplant recipient with fever, opportunistic infection suspicion, donor-derived infection, or immunosuppression management questions. Escalate to infectious diseases for neutropenic fever not responding to first-line empiric therapy, suspected invasive fungal infection, or multidrug-resistant organisms. Specialist involvement should be early and parallel to stabilization, not a last resort after empiric trials have failed.

## Common Traps

### Treating Fever in the Immunocompromised Host as Routine

A low-grade fever in a neutropenic or heavily immunosuppressed patient can precede rapid deterioration. Counter by treating neutropenic fever as an emergency and lowering the threshold to act in all immunocompromised hosts.

### Missing Pneumocystis Pneumonia in Hypoxic Pneumonia with Minimal Examination Findings

Pneumocystis classically produces hypoxia disproportionate to auscultatory findings. Counter by checking oxygenation, lactate dehydrogenase, beta-D-glucan, and obtaining appropriate imaging and induced sputum or bronchoalveolar lavage when suspected.

### Starting ART at the Wrong Time and Triggering IRIS

Immediate ART in cryptococcal meningitis or very-low-CD4 tuberculosis can provoke life-threatening IRIS. Counter by establishing the diagnosis first, deferring ART for the recommended interval in high-IRIS-risk conditions, and coordinating timing with an HIV specialist.

### Forgetting Prophylaxis at the Threshold

Omission of Pneumocystis prophylaxis when the CD4 falls below 200 or during significant immunosuppression is a common preventable error. Counter by reviewing prophylaxis at every visit and documenting start and stop criteria.

### Attributing New Symptoms to Disease When They Are Drug Interaction or Adherence Failure

ART interactions with tuberculosis therapy, antifungals, and transplant drugs are common and dangerous. Counter by checking interactions at every regimen change and assessing adherence openly.

### Overlooking CMV End-Organ Disease in the Very Low CD4 Patient

CMV retinitis can cause irreversible blindness with minimal systemic symptoms. Counter by asking about visual change in patients with CD4 below 50 and arranging urgent ophthalmology evaluation when suspected.

### Discontinuing Prophylaxis Prematurely or Failing to Stop It When Safe

Stopping prophylaxis too early risks opportunistic infection; continuing it indefinitely adds cost and toxicity. Counter by using CD4-based and regimen-based stop criteria and reviewing them at each visit.

### Delaying Specialist Input Until Empiric Therapy Fails

Complex immunocompromised hosts benefit from early HIV, transplant, or infectious diseases consultation. Counter by escalating in parallel with stabilization rather than after failed empiric trials.

## Self-Check

- Have I stratified the host's risk by CD4 count in HIV and by regimen and timeframe in transplant, chemotherapy, and biologic recipients?
- Have I recognized the signature presentations of Pneumocystis, toxoplasmosis, cryptococcus, CMV, and MAC, and ordered the targeted tests they imply?
- Have I timed ART initiation to the clinical context, avoiding immediate ART in high-IRIS-risk conditions and coordinating with an HIV specialist?
- Have I anticipated IRIS, distinguished it from treatment failure or new infection, and managed it appropriately without reflexively stopping ART?
- Have I started, reviewed, and documented prophylaxis — Pneumocystis, toxoplasmosis, MAC — using CD4-based and regimen-based criteria?
- Have I screened for and managed drug interactions and adherence at every regimen change, and considered resistance testing for virologic failure?
- Have I escalated early to HIV, transplant, or infectious diseases specialists for complex, deteriorating, or resistant infections?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
