# Fever and Undifferentiated Infection Evaluation

Fever is among the most common reasons patients present for care, and it is also one of the most diagnostically seductive presentations. The temptation is to anchor on a single site — "it's a urinary infection," "it's a viral syndrome" — before the fever has been characterized, before sepsis has been excluded, and before the exposures that define must-not-miss diagnoses have been explored. The recurring failures are three: missing sepsis behind a low-grade fever, attributing a serious infection to a virus, and forgetting the exposures — travel, animal contact, immunosuppression — that turn a generic fever into a time-critical diagnosis such as malaria, endocarditis, or a neutropenic infection. This skill covers the disciplined evaluation of undifferentiated fever, the infectious-versus-non-infectious decision, the syndrome-based workup, sepsis recognition, and the special hosts whose fever demands a different threshold for action.

## Core Rules

### Recognize and Treat Sepsis Before Pursuing the Source

In any febrile patient with possible sepsis, the first priority is not the diagnostic label but the recognition and resuscitation of sepsis. Assess for the systemic inflammatory response and organ dysfunction — tachycardia, hypotension, tachypnea, altered mental status, oliguria, elevated lactate, and coagulopathy — and act on them immediately. Obtain cultures, give empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics within the first hour for suspected sepsis or septic shock, begin fluid resuscitation, and use vasopressors for refractory hypotension. The diagnostic workup for the source proceeds in parallel, but it must never delay antibiotics and resuscitation in a deteriorating patient. A normal or low temperature does not exclude sepsis, particularly in older adults, the immunosuppressed, and those on antipyretics; rely on organ dysfunction, not the thermometer.

### Separate Infectious From Non-Infectious Causes Deliberately

Not all fever is infection, and the workup differs sharply. Infectious fever is usually accompanied by objective inflammatory markers, focal symptoms, or exposure history, and tends to follow recognizable syndromes. Non-infectious fever encompasses malignancy (lymphoma, leukemia, renal cell carcinoma), autoimmune and autoinflammatory disease (adult-onset Still disease, systemic lupus, vasculitis), drug fever, thromboembolic disease, factitious fever, and central causes. The trap is pursuing an exhaustive infectious workup in a patient whose fever is from lymphoma or pulmonary embolism. Build the differential explicitly across these categories, use the pattern of fever, the presence of weight loss, night sweats, and the tempo of the illness to guide the workup, and do not let a single elevated inflammatory marker commit you to an infectious diagnosis when the picture does not fit.

### Apply a Syndrome-Based Diagnostic Approach

Rather than testing indiscriminately, frame the workup around the dominant syndrome. Respiratory-predominant fever drives chest imaging, respiratory viral testing, and consideration of pneumonia, tuberculosis, and empyema. Gastrointestinal-predominant fever drives abdominal imaging, stool studies, and consideration of biliary, hepatic, and intra-abdominal sources. Fever with rash triggers morphology-guided testing and consideration of meningococcemia, rickettsial disease, viral exanthems, and drug reactions. Fever with neurological signs mandates lumbar puncture and imaging when indicated. Fever with heart murmur or embolic phenomena raises endocarditis and drives blood cultures and echocardiography. Fever with urinary symptoms drives urinalysis and culture. A syndrome framework prevents the scattergun panel and ensures the high-yield tests are obtained first.

### Take a Structured Exposure and Host History

The exposures define the must-not-miss diagnoses, and they are routinely underexplored. Ask systematically about: travel (malaria, dengue, typhoid, viral hemorrhagic fevers, and region-specific endemic infections), animal and vector exposure (ticks, mosquitoes, livestock, rodents), sick contacts, occupational exposure (healthcare, agriculture, laboratory), sexual and bloodborne exposures, injection drug use, immunosuppression (HIV, transplant, chemotherapy, biologics, splenectomy), vaccination status, and recent procedures or hospitalization. The host determines both the differential and the threshold for action: a fever in a neutropenic chemotherapy patient is a medical emergency; a fever in an asplenic patient with a dog bite raises overwhelming post-splenectomy infection; a fever in a returning traveler from a malaria-endemic region is malaria until proven otherwise.

### Manage the Neutropenic and Immunocompromised Host at a Lower Threshold

Fever in the neutropenic host — absolute neutrophil count under 500, or under 1000 with anticipated decline — is a medical emergency regardless of how well the patient looks. Obtain cultures, begin empiric broad-spectrum antipseudomonal antibiotic coverage within an hour, assess for hemodynamic instability, and consider adding coverage for resistant organisms, indwelling catheter infection, and, when indicated, antifungal or antiviral therapy. Do not wait for focal signs, because inflammation is blunted in neutropenia and the only signal may be fever. For other immunocompromised hosts — transplant recipients, patients on biologics, those with advanced HIV — broaden the differential to opportunistic pathogens, lower the threshold to consult infectious diseases, and recognize that presentations are often atypical and infections progress rapidly.

### Approach Fever of Unknown Origin Systematically

Fever of unknown origin (FUO) is a defined entity — classically fever above a threshold for a defined duration without diagnosis despite a reasonable initial workup — and it demands a structured, staged approach rather than repeated random testing. Begin by confirming true fever, reviewing the prior workup for gaps, and re-taking a meticulous history and examination. Then pursue targeted investigations guided by syndromic clues: dedicated imaging (often CT, sometimes PET-CT for occult malignancy or large-vessel vasculitis), serologies for the relevant autoimmune and infectious causes based on exposure, echocardiography if endocarditis is plausible, and bone marrow biopsy when hematologic malignancy or disseminated infection is suspected. Avoid empiric therapeutic trials except in specific circumstances, because they obscure the diagnosis. Engage infectious diseases, rheumatology, hematology, or radiology as the differential narrows.

### Know When to Escalate to Infectious Diseases

Escalate to infectious diseases consultation when the diagnosis remains unclear after a reasonable initial workup, when the patient has complex or multidrug-resistant infection, when prolonged or unusual antimicrobial therapy is contemplated, when the host is significantly immunocompromised, when an exotic or epidemiologically important infection is suspected (travel-related, bioterrorism-related, reportable disease), or when antimicrobial stewardship concerns arise. Early consultation improves outcomes in complex infection and prevents both undertreatment and the harms of overly broad empiric therapy. Do not view referral as a last resort; for genuine diagnostic uncertainty or a deteriorating immunocompromised host, it is first-line.

## Common Traps

### Anchoring on a Viral Syndrome in a Patient with Serious Bacterial Infection

Attributing fever to a virus without excluding focal bacterial infection or sepsis is a recurring fatal error. Counter by assessing organ dysfunction, obtaining cultures, and re-evaluating the patient rather than reassuring on the basis of a nonspecific presentation.

### Missing Sepsis Because the Temperature Is Normal or Low

Older adults, the immunosuppressed, and patients on antipyretics may have sepsis without high fever. Counter by relying on organ dysfunction, lactate, and hemodynamics rather than the temperature reading.

### Forgetting Malaria in a Returning Traveler

Any fever in a traveler returning from a malaria-endemic region is malaria until proven otherwise, and delay in smear and treatment can be fatal within hours. Counter by obtaining thick and thin blood smears urgently and repeating if the first is negative but suspicion persists.

### Treating Neutropenic Fever as a Routine Infection

A well-appearing neutropenic patient can decompensate within hours. Counter by treating neutropenic fever as an emergency, giving empiric antipseudomonal coverage within the first hour, and not waiting for focal signs.

### Pursuing an Exhaustive Infectious Workup in Non-Infectious Fever

Lymphoma, autoimmune disease, drug fever, and thromboembolic disease are common non-infectious causes that are missed when the workup is restricted to infection. Counter by building the differential across infectious and non-infectious categories from the outset.

### Ordering Scattergun Panels Without a Syndromic Framework

Random testing yields incidental findings and misses the high-yield diagnosis. Counter by framing the workup around the dominant syndrome and reserving broad panels for genuinely undifferentiated cases.

### Delaying Antibiotics for Cultures in Septic Patients

Withholding antibiotics to obtain "better" cultures in a deteriorating patient worsens outcome. Counter by obtaining cultures promptly but never delaying antibiotics beyond the first hour in sepsis or septic shock.

### Underusing Infectious Diseases Consultation for Complex Cases

Complex, multidrug-resistant, immunocompromised, or epidemiologically important infections benefit from early specialist input. Counter by escalating to infectious diseases early rather than as a last resort.

## Self-Check

- Have I first assessed for and treated sepsis — cultures, empiric antibiotics within the first hour, fluids, vasopressors — before pursuing the diagnostic source?
- Have I built a differential that spans infectious and non-infectious causes rather than anchoring on infection alone?
- Have I framed the workup around the dominant syndrome and obtained the high-yield tests first?
- Have I taken a structured exposure and host history, including travel, vectors, animals, sick contacts, immunosuppression, and procedures?
- For a returning traveler from a malaria-endemic region, have I obtained urgent blood smears and treated as malaria until proven otherwise?
- For the neutropenic or immunocompromised host, have I lowered my threshold to act and begun empiric coverage within the first hour?
- Have I escalated to infectious diseases for diagnostic uncertainty, complex or resistant infection, immunocompromised hosts, or epidemiologically important disease?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
