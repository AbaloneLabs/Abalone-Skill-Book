# Zoonotic, Vector-Borne, and Travel-Related Infections

A patient's exposures — where they have been, what bit them, what animals they touched, what they ate — are not background color in the history; they are the diagnostic key for an entire family of infections that are rare in the general population but common, dangerous, and time-critical in the exposed. The recurring failures are predictable: not asking the travel and exposure questions at all, asking them too superficially to be useful, treating a returning traveler's fever as a routine virus while missing malaria, missing rickettsial disease behind a nonspecific febrile illness, and forgetting post-exposure prophylaxis for rabies, HIV, or meningococcal exposure. This skill covers the structured exposure history, the must-not-miss travel and vector-borne diagnoses, the urgency of malaria smears, post-exposure prophylaxis decisions, isolation and public health reporting, and when to escalate to infectious diseases.

## Core Rules

### Take a Structured Exposure History That Actually Changes the Differential

A vague question about travel is useless. Build the exposure history deliberately: countries and regions visited, in chronological order, with dates; rural versus urban exposure; activities (safaris, freshwater swimming, caving, agricultural work, medical procedures abroad); insect bites — mosquitoes, ticks, tsetse flies, sandflies — and whether prophylaxis was used; animal bites or scratches; consumption of untreated water, unpasteurized dairy, bushmeat, or raw seafood; sick contacts, including respiratory and bloodborne exposures; sexual exposures; occupational and laboratory exposures; vaccination and prophylaxis status; and the incubation period relative to exposures. The incubation period is decisive: a fever within a week of return points to dengue, malaria, typhoid, or arboviral disease; a fever weeks later expands the differential to tuberculosis, schistosomiasis, visceral leishmaniasis, and amebic liver abscess. An exposure history that does not change the differential has not been taken properly.

### Treat Fever in a Returning Traveler From a Malaria-Endemic Region as Malaria Until Proven Otherwise

Malaria is the single must-not-miss diagnosis in the febrile returning traveler, and falciparum malaria can kill within hours. Any fever in a traveler from a malaria-endemic region within the prior months warrants urgent blood smear (thick and thin films) and rapid diagnostic testing, regardless of whether prophylaxis was taken, because no prophylaxis is fully protective and adherence is often imperfect. A single negative smear does not exclude malaria; repeat smears every 12 to 24 hours for several samples if suspicion persists. Assess severity immediately — parasitemia level, signs of severe malaria (impaired consciousness, seizures, renal failure, acidosis, hypoglycemia, pulmonary edema, severe anemia) — because severe falciparum malaria is a medical emergency requiring parenteral artesunate, not oral therapy. Do not allow a reassuring initial appearance to delay smear and treatment.

### Recognize Dengue and Its Warning Signs

Dengue is among the most common travel-related febrile illnesses and has a phase that clinicians misjudge. The febrile phase lasts several days with headache, retro-orbital pain, myalgia, and rash; the danger arises during defervescence, when plasma leakage can produce shock. Identify warning signs — abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, clinical fluid accumulation, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, restlessness, liver enlargement, and a rising hematocrit with falling platelets — that mark the transition to severe dengue. Monitor hemodynamics, hematocrit, and platelets during the critical phase, give judicious isotonic fluids for compensated shock, and avoid both under-resuscitation and fluid overload. Aspirin and NSAIDs are contraindicated because of bleeding risk; use paracetamol for fever.

### Identify Rickettsial Disease Early and Treat Empirically

Spotted fever group and typhus group rickettsioses present with fever, headache, myalgia, and sometimes an eschar at the inoculation site and a maculopapular rash that may become petechial. The rash may be absent early, and the eschar may be overlooked in hairy or intertriginous areas. Because serology is often negative in the first week and definitive testing is retrospective, treatment must be empiric on clinical suspicion — doxycycline is first-line, including in children and pregnancy when the disease is life-threatening. Delaying doxycycline while awaiting serologic confirmation is a recurring cause of preventable death from Rocky Mountain spotted fever and scrub typhus. Treat suspected rickettsial disease empirically in any febrile patient with the right exposure and a compatible rash or eschar.

### Apply Lyme Disease Diagnosis and Treatment by Stage and Manifestation

Lyme disease presents in stages, and management differs by stage. Early localized disease is erythema migrans, the expanding erythematous rash often with central clearing; diagnosis is clinical and treatment is oral doxycycline, amoxicillin, or cefuroxime, without serologic testing, which may be negative early. Early disseminated disease includes multiple erythema migrans, neurologic involvement (facial palsy, meningitis, radiculopathy), and carditis (atrioventricular block); management shifts to appropriate oral or parenteral therapy by syndrome. Late disease includes arthritis and, less commonly, chronic neurologic manifestations. Serology supports diagnosis in later stages but must be interpreted with two-tier testing and clinical context; positive serology without symptoms does not require treatment. Recognize that post-treatment symptoms are not the same as persistent infection and do not mandate prolonged antibiotics.

### Decide on Post-Exposure Prophylaxis Promptly for Rabies, HIV, and Meningococcal Exposure

Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) decisions are time-sensitive and follow specific algorithms. For animal bites or scratches in rabies-endemic regions, assess the species, behavior, and vaccination status, and initiate wound care, rabies vaccine, and rabies immunoglobulin for high-risk exposures without delay — once rabies symptoms develop, the disease is essentially fatal. For occupational or sexual HIV exposure, begin PEP within hours, using a recommended three-drug regimen, and arrange follow-up testing and counseling; the window for effective PEP closes rapidly. For close contacts of meningococcal meningitis, provide chemoprophylaxis within the recommended timeframe to prevent secondary cases. Knowing the local protocols and acting within the time windows is essential; when exposure is uncertain, consult infectious diseases or public health rather than deferring.

### Use Isolation and Public Health Reporting Proactively

Certain travel-related and zoonotic infections require isolation to prevent transmission and reporting to public health authorities. Suspected viral hemorrhagic fevers, emerging coronaviruses, and other high-consequence pathogens require immediate isolation and notification of infection prevention and public health before diagnostic confirmation. Tuberculosis, including multidrug-resistant disease, requires airborne isolation pending rule-out. Meningococcal disease warrants droplet precautions and prophylaxis of contacts. Vector-borne diseases generally do not require isolation but may be locally transmissible if competent vectors exist. Know the locally reportable diseases and the mechanism for reporting; failure to report a reportable infection is both a clinical and a public health failure.

### Escalate to Infectious Diseases and Public Health for Unfamiliar or High-Consequence Infections

For infections outside the usual local epidemiology — imported malaria with severe features, suspected viral hemorrhagic fever, uncommon parasitic or mycotic disease, bioterrorism-related illness, or any infection requiring unfamiliar therapy — escalate to infectious diseases and, where appropriate, public health and tropical medicine specialists early. These infections are rare enough that local experience is limited, and the consequences of error are large. Specialist input should be sought in parallel with stabilization, and transfer to a higher-level center may be appropriate for severe or highly specialized disease.

## Common Traps

### Not Asking About Travel and Animal or Vector Exposure

The single most common failure is omitting the exposure history, which eliminates the diagnostic key for the entire family of infections. Counter by asking structured exposure questions for every febrile or undifferentiated presentation.

### Missing Malaria by Accepting a Single Negative Smear

One negative smear does not exclude malaria. Counter by repeating thick and thin smears every 12 to 24 hours for several samples when suspicion persists, and treat empirically for severe disease.

### Treating Dengue's Critical Phase as Recovery

Severe dengue emerges as fever falls, not rises. Counter by monitoring for warning signs and hematocrit rise during defervescence and managing plasma leakage with judicious fluids.

### Waiting for Serology Before Treating Suspected Rickettsial Disease

Rickettsial serology is often negative early, and delay for confirmation is fatal. Counter by treating empirically with doxycycline on clinical suspicion.

### Overreliance on Lyme Serology in Early Localized Disease

Early erythema migrans is a clinical diagnosis; serology may be negative and treatment should not wait. Counter by treating clinically compatible early disease without serology.

### Forgetting Time-Sensitive Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

Rabies, HIV, and meningococcal PEP are effective only within narrow windows. Counter by knowing the algorithms, initiating PEP promptly, and consulting public health or infectious diseases for uncertain exposures.

### Failing to Isolate or Report High-Consequence or Communicable Infections

Viral hemorrhagic fevers, tuberculosis, and meningococcal disease require prompt isolation and reporting. Counter by initiating isolation before confirmation and notifying infection prevention and public health immediately.

### Delaying Specialist Input for Unfamiliar Imported Infections

Imported or zoonotic infections are often outside local experience. Counter by escalating to infectious diseases, tropical medicine, or public health early and in parallel with stabilization.

## Self-Check

- Have I taken a structured exposure history — destinations, dates, activities, insect and animal exposures, food and water, sick and sexual contacts, vaccinations, and incubation period — that actually changes the differential?
- For a febrile returning traveler from a malaria-endemic region, have I obtained urgent thick and thin smears and rapid diagnostic testing, and repeated smears if the first is negative but suspicion persists?
- Have I assessed for severe malaria features and initiated parenteral artesunate for severe disease without delay?
- For suspected dengue, have I identified warning signs and monitored hemodynamics, hematocrit, and platelets during the critical defervescence phase?
- For suspected rickettsial disease with the right exposure and a compatible rash or eschar, have I started empiric doxycycline rather than waiting for serology?
- For Lyme disease, have I treated early localized erythema migrans clinically without requiring serology, and used stage-appropriate therapy for disseminated disease?
- Have I applied time-sensitive post-exposure prophylaxis for rabies, HIV, and meningococcal exposures within the recommended windows?
- Have I initiated isolation and public health reporting for high-consequence or communicable infections before diagnostic confirmation, and escalated unfamiliar imported infections to infectious diseases or tropical medicine?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
