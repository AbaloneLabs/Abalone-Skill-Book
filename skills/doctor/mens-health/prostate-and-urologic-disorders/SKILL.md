# Prostate and Urologic Disorders

Prostate and urologic complaints are among the most common reasons men seek care, and their danger lies in how readily serious conditions masquerade as benign nuisance. Lower urinary tract symptoms are shared by benign prostatic hyperplasia, prostate cancer, urinary tract infection, stones, neurogenic bladder, and urethral stricture, and a single assumed diagnosis (usually "just an enlarged prostate") is where the most dangerous errors occur. Equally, several urologic presentations are time-critical or life-altering: acute urinary retention can precipitate renal failure, testicular torsion sacrifices the testis within hours, paraphimosis can cause necrosis of the glans, and missed prostate cancer changes prognosis. A disciplined approach separates obstruction from irritation, screens for malignancy red flags, applies shared decision-making to PSA, and escalates urologic emergencies immediately. This skill covers LUTS assessment, the PSA decision, prostatitis, erectile dysfunction workup, and the must-not-miss urologic emergencies.

## Core Rules

### Separate Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms into Obstructive and Irritative and Generate a Broad Differential

Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) divide broadly into obstructive (hesitancy, weak stream, straining, incomplete emptying, terminal dribbling) and irritative (frequency, urgency, nocturia, dysuria), and the pattern points toward different causes, though they frequently overlap. Obstructive symptoms classically suggest bladder outlet obstruction from benign prostatic hyperplasia, but the same symptoms arise from urethral stricture, prostate cancer, bladder stone, neurogenic bladder, and medications. Irritative symptoms suggest infection, inflammation (interstitial cystitis, prostatitis), bladder tumor, or overactive bladder. Generate the differential deliberately rather than defaulting to BPH, because treating the wrong cause (for example, an alpha-blocker for a neurogenic bladder or a stricture) provides no benefit and may delay diagnosis. Quantify symptoms with a validated score (such as the IPSS) to establish baseline and track response, and always ask about hematuria, weight loss, bone pain, and prior urologic history as red flags.

### Screen for Malignancy Red Flags and Investigate Hematuria Promptly

Hematuria, whether visible or non-visible, is a malignancy red flag until proven otherwise and mandates investigation rather than reassurance, particularly in older men and smokers. Visible hematuria warrants urgent referral for cystoscopy and upper tract imaging, because bladder and renal cell cancers present this way and early detection changes outcome. Non-visible hematuria, identified on urinalysis in the absence of infection or other benign transient cause, also requires structured investigation after repeat confirmation. Do not attribute hematuria to BPH or a urinary tract infection without confirmation and follow-up, and never dismiss it as trivial. Combine hematuria assessment with attention to other red flags — weight loss, bone pain (suggesting metastatic prostate cancer), persistent constitutional symptoms, and a palpable bladder or abnormal prostate on examination — and refer urgently when these are present.

### Apply Shared Decision-Making to PSA Testing, Never Reflexive or Reflexive Avoidance

Prostate-specific antigen testing for prostate cancer screening is a decision that must be made with the patient, not for the patient, because the balance of benefit (reduced metastatic disease and prostate cancer mortality) against harm (overdiagnosis, biopsy complications, and treatment side effects including incontinence and erectile dysfunction) is genuinely individual. Conduct an informed shared decision-making discussion before drawing PSA, covering the potential benefits, the risks of false positives and negatives, the likelihood of finding indolent disease, and the downstream consequences of an elevated result. Do not test reflexively at a "well-man check," and do not refuse to test a fully informed man who wishes to proceed. When testing, be aware of factors that elevate PSA transiently (recent ejaculation, digital rectal exam, urinary retention, catheterization, prostatitis, bicycle riding) and avoid testing during active urinary infection or retention. Interpret PSA in context of age, prostate size, and trajectory, and use age-specific reference ranges and the rate of change (velocity) rather than a single threshold.

### Diagnose and Treat Prostatitis by Category and Do Not Overtreat Chronic Pelvic Pain

Prostatitis is not one condition but four: acute bacterial prostatitis, chronic bacterial prostatitis, chronic pelvic pain syndrome (formerly chronic non-bacterial prostatitis), and asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis. Acute bacterial prostatitis presents with fever, perineal pain, dysuria, and obstructive symptoms, may cause sepsis or acute retention, and requires prompt antibiotics (often parenteral if systemically unwell) and sometimes catheterization; a tender, boggy prostate on examination supports the diagnosis, but avoid vigorous prostate massage during acute infection. Chronic bacterial prostatitis involves recurrent urinary tract infections with the same organism and warrants prolonged, prostate-penetrating antibiotics guided by culture. Chronic pelvic pain syndrome is the most common form, is characterized by genitourinary pain without consistent infection, and is best managed with a multimodal approach (alpha-blockers, anti-inflammatories, neuromodulators, pelvic floor physical therapy, psychological support) rather than repeated courses of antibiotics in the absence of infection. Avoid the trap of prescribing antibiotics repeatedly for chronic pelvic pain when cultures are negative.

### Work Up Erectile Dysfunction as a Cardiovascular and Endocrine Sentinel, Not Just a Symptom

Erectile dysfunction is frequently an early marker of systemic vascular disease and endothelial dysfunction, often preceding coronary artery disease by several years, and should be investigated as such rather than treated symptomatically and dismissed. Assess cardiovascular risk factors (hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, smoking, obesity, family history) and consider formal cardiovascular risk stratification, because the penile arteries are smaller than the coronary arteries and reveal disease earlier. Screen for diabetes and dyslipidemia if not recently done, check morning testosterone when low libido or hypogonadism features are present, review medications (many antihypertensives, antidepressants, antiandrogens, and 5-alpha-reductase inhibitors cause ED), and assess for psychological contributors. Treat reversible causes, offer first-line phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors when not contraindicated (and note the absolute contraindication with nitrates and the caution with alpha-blockers), and refer to urology for refractory cases or when penile injection, device, or surgical options are being considered. Never prescribe a PDE-5 inhibitor to a patient on nitrates.

### Recognize and Escalate Urologic Emergencies Immediately

Several urologic presentations are time-critical and demand immediate recognition and escalation rather than outpatient workup. Acute urinary retention, with a palpable distended bladder and inability to void, requires immediate catheterization, assessment for precipitants (obstruction, infection, medication, neurologic, post-operative, clot retention), and urology involvement, with attention to post-obstructive diuresis after decompression. Testicular torsion presents with acute scrotal pain, swelling, and a high-riding or transverse-lying testis, often with nausea and an absent cremasteric reflex; it is a surgical emergency where the testis is salvaged only with detorsion within roughly six hours, and clinical suspicion mandates immediate urology referral and exploration without waiting for imaging. Paraphimosis, in which a retracted foreskin forms a constricting band behind the glans causing progressive edema and ischemia, requires urgent manual reduction and, if unsuccessful, emergency urologic intervention to prevent necrosis. Gross hematuria with clots causing retention (clot retention) requires large-bore irrigation and urology involvement. For each emergency, recognize the pattern, resuscitate or decompress in parallel, and call urology immediately.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to BPH for All Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms

Treating LUTS empirically for enlarged prostate while missing stricture, neurogenic bladder, bladder stone, or malignancy. Counter by generating a broad differential and investigating red flags.

### Dismissing Hematuria as Infection or BPH

Reassuring visible or persistent non-visible hematuria without investigation, delaying bladder or kidney cancer diagnosis. Counter by treating hematuria as malignancy until proven otherwise and referring for cystoscopy and imaging.

### Reflexive or Avoidant PSA Testing Without Shared Decision-Making

Ordering PSA routinely without counseling, or refusing to test an informed man, each denying the patient an informed choice. Counter by conducting an explicit shared decision-making discussion before testing.

### Interpreting a Single PSA in Isolation

Acting on one PSA value without accounting for recent ejaculation, infection, retention, or examination, or without considering velocity. Counter by avoiding testing during confounders and interpreting PSA with age-specific ranges and trend.

### Repeated Antibiotics for Chronic Pelvic Pain Syndrome

Prescribing repeated antibiotic courses despite negative cultures, fostering resistance and missing multimodal management. Counter by categorizing prostatitis correctly and using a multimodal approach for chronic pelvic pain.

### Treating Erectile Dysfunction Without Cardiovascular Assessment

Prescribing PDE-5 inhibitors without evaluating cardiovascular risk or checking for diabetes and dyslipidemia, missing early vascular disease. Counter by treating ED as a cardiovascular and endocrine sentinel.

### Prescribing PDE-5 Inhibitors with Nitrates

Combining these agents, which can cause profound and life-threatening hypotension. Counter by screening explicitly for nitrate use and withholding PDE-5 inhibitors.

### Delaying Urology Referral for Acute Scrotum or Retention

Waiting for imaging or certainty in suspected torsion, or delaying catheterization in retention. Counter by recognizing emergencies and escalating immediately.

## Self-Check

- Have I categorized lower urinary tract symptoms as obstructive or irritative and generated a broad differential rather than defaulting to BPH?
- For any hematuria (visible or persistent non-visible), have I arranged urgent cystoscopy and upper tract imaging rather than attributing it to infection or BPH?
- Before PSA testing, have I conducted an informed shared decision-making discussion covering benefits, harms, false results, and downstream consequences?
- Have I interpreted PSA in context of confounders, age-specific ranges, and velocity rather than as an isolated number?
- For suspected prostatitis, have I assigned the correct category and avoided repeated antibiotics for chronic pelvic pain syndrome when cultures are negative?
- For erectile dysfunction, have I assessed cardiovascular risk, screened for diabetes and dyslipidemia, reviewed medications, and checked testosterone when indicated?
- Have I confirmed the patient is not on nitrates before prescribing a phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor?
- For acute urinary retention, suspected testicular torsion, paraphimosis, or clot retention, have I escalated to urology immediately and decompressed or resuscitated in parallel?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
