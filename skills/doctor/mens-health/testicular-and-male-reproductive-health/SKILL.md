# Testicular and Male Reproductive Health

Testicular and male reproductive complaints sit at the intersection of acute surgical emergency, oncology, endocrinology, and fertility, and the cost of cognitive shortcuts here is measured in lost organs, lost fertility, and missed cancer. The defining danger is the acute scrotum: testicular torsion sacrifices the testis within hours, and the clinician who waits for classic features or a confirmatory scan will arrive too late. Beyond the emergency, a painless testicular mass is testicular cancer until proven otherwise, erectile and libido complaints may signal serious endocrine or vascular disease, and the rising use of testosterone therapy carries cardiovascular, fertility, and erythrocytotic risks that are often under-weighed. This skill covers the acute scrotum and torsion, scrotal mass evaluation, hypogonadism workup, male infertility, the risks of testosterone therapy, and disciplined escalation to urology and endocrinology.

## Core Rules

### Treat the Acute Scrotum as Torsion Until Proven Otherwise and Escalate Immediately

Any male with acute onset of testicular or scrotal pain has testicular torsion until proven otherwise, and the discipline is to act on suspicion, not on certainty. Torsion presents with sudden severe unilateral pain, swelling, a high-riding or transverse-lying testis, an absent cremasteric reflex, and often nausea and vomiting, but the presentation is variable: pain may be referred to the lower abdomen or groin, may be intermittent in intermittent torsion, and occurs at any age, including neonates and older adults. Salvage of the testis depends on the time to detorsion, with the highest salvage rates within roughly six hours of onset and falling steeply thereafter; every minute of delay for imaging or deliberation reduces viability. The correct action on clinical suspicion is immediate urology referral and surgical exploration, not waiting for ultrasound. Doppler ultrasound is useful only when torsion is genuinely unlikely and the diagnosis remains in doubt, and a normal or equivocal Doppler never overrides strong clinical suspicion, because intermittent or partial torsion can produce misleading flow. Manual detorsion may be attempted while awaiting surgery but does not replace exploration and fixation.

### Evaluate Scrotal and Testicular Masses with the Assumption of Malignancy

A painless testicular lump is testicular cancer until proven otherwise, and the workup must proceed with that assumption. Testicular cancer is the most common solid malignament in young men, typically presenting as a painless nodule or firm area within the testis, sometimes with a hydrocele, gynecomastia, or back and abdominal pain from metastatic disease. Examine both testes carefully, distinguishing intratesticular from extratesticular masses: intratesticular solid masses are malignant until proven otherwise, whereas most extratesticular masses (epididymal cysts, spermatoceles, varicoceles) are benign. Transillumination helps characterize cystic lesions but does not exclude underlying malignancy. When a suspicious intratesticular mass is found, arrange urgent scrotal ultrasound and urology referral, measure tumor markers (alpha-fetoprotein, beta-hCG, lactate dehydrogenase) as part of the staging workup, and avoid percutaneous biopsy or trans-scrotal approaches that risk tumor seeding. Do not attribute a testicular mass to trauma, infection, or hydrocele without imaging to exclude underlying tumor.

### Work Up Hypogonadism with Confirmatory Testing and a Search for Cause

Male hypogonadism presents with low libido, erectile dysfunction, fatigue, depressed mood, loss of body hair, gynecomastia, and, in younger men, infertility, but symptoms are non-specific and diagnosis requires biochemical confirmation under correct conditions. Confirm low testosterone with morning (between 7 and 11 a.m.) total testosterone on at least two separate occasions, because levels vary diurnally and transiently. When confirmed, distinguish primary (testicular) from secondary (pituitary or hypothalamic) hypogonadism with luteinizing hormone and follicle-stimulating hormone: elevated gonadotropins indicate primary testicular failure, low or inappropriately normal gonadotropins indicate secondary hypogonadism. Investigate the cause: Klinefelter syndrome (karyotype in primary hypogonadism, especially with small firm testes and gynecomastia), pituitary lesions (prolactin and pituitary imaging in secondary hypogonadism), hemochromatosis, chronic illness, and medication effects. Do not diagnose hypogonadism or prescribe testosterone on the basis of a single borderline level or symptoms alone, and never initiate testosterone before fertility assessment, because exogenous testosterone suppresses spermatogenesis and can cause infertility.

### Evaluate Male Infertility as a Couple and Start with the Semen Analysis

Male factors contribute to roughly half of infertility cases, and the initial workup is inexpensive and high-yield. Evaluate the male partner of any couple meeting infertility criteria (failure to conceive after twelve months, or six months if the female partner is over 35), beginning with at least two semen analyses separated in time, because parameters vary. Assess for contributing factors: varicocele (the most common correctable male factor), endocrine dysfunction (testosterone, FSH, LH, prolactin), genetic causes (karyotype and Y-chromosome microdeletion in severe oligo- or azoospermia), obstructive causes (including congenital absence of the vas), prior infection or trauma, and medication and recreational drug effects. Counsel explicitly that exogenous testosterone, anabolic steroids, smoking, excessive alcohol, heat exposure, and obesity impair spermatogenesis, and that stopping testosterone may restore fertility over months. Refer to urology or reproductive endocrinology for identified abnormalities, severe oligo- or azoospermia, or need for surgical sperm retrieval or assisted reproduction.

### Weigh the Risks of Testosterone Therapy and Monitor Systematically

Testosterone therapy is appropriate for men with confirmed symptomatic hypogonadism, but its use in older men with age-related decline or for "wellness" is not supported and carries real harms. Before initiating, confirm the diagnosis biochemically, exclude contraindications (prostate cancer, severe lower urinary tract symptoms, uncontrolled heart failure, recent myocardial infarction or stroke, polycythemia, untreated severe sleep apnea, active desire for fertility), and conduct a shared decision-making discussion. During therapy, monitor testosterone levels, hematocrit (for testosterone-induced erythrocytosis, which increases thrombotic risk and may require dose reduction or therapeutic phlebotomy), prostate-specific antigen and prostate exam, and cardiovascular risk, because the cardiovascular safety of testosterone remains an area of active debate and patients should be counseled accordingly. Counsel that testosterone suppresses spermatogenesis and is contraindicated in men seeking fertility. Stop or adjust therapy for adverse effects, and re-evaluate the ongoing need periodically.

### Recognize Other Time-Critical and Significant Scrotal Presentations

Beyond torsion and cancer, several scrotal presentations require prompt recognition. Epididymo-orchitis presents with gradual onset pain, swelling, fever, and dysuria, is usually infectious (often sexually transmitted in younger men, urinary pathogens in older men), and must be distinguished from torsion; when torsion cannot be excluded, image or explore. Fournier gangrene, a necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum and scrotum, is a life-threatening emergency in diabetic or immunocompromised men, presenting with severe pain out of proportion, crepitus, skin discoloration, and systemic toxicity, and requires immediate surgical debridement and broad-spectrum antibiotics. Varicocele, usually left-sided and presenting as a bag of worms on standing, is a common cause of secondary infertility and warrants urology referral if symptomatic, large, or associated with infertility or testicular atrophy. Do not dismiss chronic scrotal pain or swelling without a structured assessment.

## Common Traps

### Waiting for Classic Features or Imaging in Suspected Torsion

Delaying urology referral for a Doppler ultrasound or because the presentation is atypical, sacrificing the testis. Counter by treating the acute scrotum as torsion until proven otherwise and escalating on suspicion.

### Accepting a Normal Doppler as Exclusion of Torsion

Concluding torsion is excluded when clinical suspicion remains high, because intermittent or partial torsion can produce misleading flow. Counter by overriding equivocal imaging with clinical judgment and surgical exploration.

### Attributing a Painless Testicular Mass to Benign Causes

Labeling a firm intratesticular nodule as a cyst, hydrocele, or trauma without ultrasound, delaying cancer diagnosis. Counter by treating intratesticular solid masses as malignant until proven otherwise.

### Diagnosing Hypogonadism on Symptoms or a Single Low Level

Prescribing testosterone without confirmatory morning levels and gonadotropin measurement. Counter by confirming biochemically on two occasions and investigating the cause.

### Prescribing Testosterone Without Fertility or Contraindication Screening

Suppressing spermatogenesis in a man seeking fertility, or initiating therapy in the presence of prostate cancer or polycythemia. Counter by screening for fertility intent and contraindications before initiating.

### Failing to Investigate the Male Partner in Infertility

Focusing the workup solely on the female partner, missing easily detected male factors. Counter by beginning the infertility evaluation with a semen analysis of the male partner.

### Missing Fournier Gangrene in the Diabetic Man with Scrotal Pain

Underestimating severe perineal pain with systemic toxicity as cellulitis or epididymitis. Counter by recognizing necrotizing infection and escalating for immediate surgical debridement.

## Self-Check

- For any acute scrotal pain, have I treated torsion as the leading diagnosis and escalated to urology immediately rather than waiting for imaging?
- Have I avoided relying on a normal or equivocal Doppler to exclude torsion when clinical suspicion is high?
- For any painless intratesticular mass, have I arranged urgent scrotal ultrasound and urology referral, treating it as malignant until proven otherwise?
- Before diagnosing hypogonadism, have I confirmed low morning testosterone on two occasions and measured gonadotropins to distinguish primary from secondary causes?
- Before initiating testosterone, have I screened for contraindications (prostate cancer, uncontrolled heart failure, recent cardiovascular events, polycythemia) and assessed fertility intent?
- During testosterone therapy, am I monitoring testosterone levels, hematocrit, prostate-specific antigen, and cardiovascular risk, and counseling about fertility suppression?
- For couples with infertility, have I evaluated the male partner with semen analysis and investigated identified abnormalities?
- For severe perineal or scrotal pain with systemic toxicity in a diabetic or immunocompromised man, have I considered Fournier gangrene and escalated for emergency surgical debridement?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
