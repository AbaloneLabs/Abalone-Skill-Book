# Male Cancer Screening and Prevention

Cancer screening in men is a domain where good intentions cause harm as easily as they prevent death, because every screening test carries tradeoffs of overdiagnosis, false reassurance, downstream procedures, and psychological burden that must be weighed against mortality benefit. The clinician's task is not to maximize testing but to apply evidence-based, risk-stratified screening through genuine shared decision-making, while simultaneously addressing the modifiable risk factors that drive most male cancer burden. The two dominant errors are symmetric: ordering screening reflexively without counseling (particularly PSA), and abandoning screening entirely because the evidence is nuanced. A disciplined approach applies the right test to the right patient at the right interval, individualizes by risk, integrates prevention and lifestyle, and escalates to urology or oncology when risk warrants. This skill covers PSA shared decision-making, colorectal screening options, the evidence on testicular self-examination, risk-based screening for high-risk men, and male-specific prevention.

## Core Rules

### Apply Shared Decision-Making to Prostate Cancer Screening, Documenting the Discussion

Prostate cancer screening with PSA is the paradigm case for shared decision-making in medicine, because the mortality benefit is modest, the harms of overdiagnosis and treatment (incontinence, erectile dysfunction, bowel dysfunction) are substantial, and the right choice depends on individual values rather than a single correct answer. Before offering PSA, conduct an explicit informed discussion covering: the potential benefit (reduction in prostate cancer mortality and metastatic disease), the harms (false positives, biopsies with bleeding, infection and sepsis risk, overdiagnosis of indolent cancers, treatment side effects), the limitations of the test, and the fact that many men diagnosed would never have been harmed by their cancer. Offer screening to men at average risk generally from age 50 to 69 (with variation by guideline), earlier and more frequently for higher-risk men (African ancestry, family history of prostate cancer, known pathogenic variants such as BRCA2), and individualize the decision to stop in older or comorbid men with limited life expectancy. Document the discussion and the patient's preference, because the decision is the patient's to make, and a documented shared decision protects both patient and clinician.

### Offer Colorectal Cancer Screening with a Range of Evidence-Based Options

Colorectal cancer screening reduces both incidence and mortality, and the clinician's task is to offer screening rather than a single test, because multiple strategies are effective and adherence depends on patient preference. Options include stool-based tests (fecal immunochemical test, the high-sensitivity guaiac test, and stool DNA testing) at defined intervals, direct visualization (colonoscopy at defined intervals, flexible sigmoidoscopy, and where available CT colonography), and the choice depends on local availability, patient preference, risk, and resources. Apply risk stratification: average-risk men generally begin screening around age 45 to 50, while higher-risk men (family history of colorectal cancer or advanced polyps, personal history of inflammatory bowel disease, known hereditary syndromes such as Lynch or familial adenomatous polyposis) require earlier and more intensive screening, often with colonoscopy. Discuss the tradeoffs: colonoscopy offers the longest interval and the ability to remove polyps but carries procedural risk and requires bowel preparation, while stool tests are less invasive but more frequent and yield more false positives. Continue screening until the age and life-expectancy thresholds where the benefit of ongoing screening is unlikely, and document the stop decision.

### Apply Evidence, Not Routine Practice, to Testicular Cancer Screening

Testicular cancer screening by clinical examination or self-examination is not recommended for asymptomatic men at average risk by major guidelines, because there is no evidence that routine screening reduces mortality and because it generates false positives, anxiety, and unnecessary investigation. This is a case where the evidence-based answer is restraint, not testing. Distinguish this from the symptomatic or high-risk man: men with a history of undescended testis, testicular atrophy, personal or family history of testicular cancer, or Klinefelter syndrome are at higher risk and benefit from awareness and targeted evaluation of any testicular abnormality. For all men, the actionable practice is to teach testicular awareness — to promptly report any painless lump, swelling, or change — rather than to recommend a formal monthly self-examination ritual. When a man reports a testicular abnormality, investigate urgently as outlined in the testicular and male reproductive health skill, because the screening evidence does not apply to symptomatic evaluation.

### Risk-Stratify Screening for Higher-Risk Men and Identify Hereditary Risk

Average-risk screening guidelines fail higher-risk men, and a central task is to identify elevated risk and intensify surveillance accordingly. For prostate cancer, intensify screening discussion for men of African ancestry, those with a first-degree relative diagnosed before 60 or multiple affected relatives, and carriers of pathogenic variants such as BRCA1 and especially BRCA2, beginning earlier and considering more frequent PSA. For colorectal cancer, identify family history of early-onset disease or polyposis, personal inflammatory bowel disease, and hereditary syndromes (Lynch syndrome, familial adenomatous polyposis, MUTYH-associated polyposis), each of which mandates earlier, more frequent colonoscopy. Take a structured family history and, where the pattern suggests a hereditary syndrome, refer for genetic counseling and testing, because identification of a pathogenic variant changes surveillance for the patient and cascade testing benefits relatives. Do not apply average-risk intervals to a man whose family history or genetics place him at elevated risk.

### Address Modifiable Risk Factors as the Core of Male Cancer Prevention

Screening detects existing disease, but prevention reduces incidence, and the most impactful cancer prevention in men is modification of the factors that drive the largest burden: tobacco, alcohol, obesity, physical inactivity, diet, sun exposure, and certain infections. Tobacco remains the leading cause of preventable cancer death and cancer-specific mortality; offer evidence-based cessation support at every encounter. Alcohol is a recognized carcinogen linked to several cancers, and counseling toward reduction matters. Obesity and physical inactivity drive colorectal, kidney, esophageal, and other cancers, and structured weight management and exercise are preventive interventions, not lifestyle platitudes. Address sun protection and skin surveillance for melanoma, and offer vaccination against oncogenic viruses (hepatitis B, and HPV where indicated by age and guideline) as cancer prevention. Integrate these into the preventive visit with the same seriousness as screening tests, and document the counseling and the plan.

### Integrate Screening into a Risk-Based Preventive Visit, Not a Checklist

The mature approach to male cancer screening is not a fixed panel but a risk-based preventive visit that integrates screening, prevention, and shared decision-making according to the individual's age, life expectancy, risk factors, family history, and values. Estimate life expectancy when deciding whether to continue screening in older or comorbid men, because the benefit of screening accrues over years and men with limited life expectancy are more likely to be harmed than helped by ongoing testing. Address the whole man: cardiovascular risk (the leading cause of death), mental health (including depression and suicide, often under-detected in men), substance use, and immunization. Document the screening decisions made, the decisions declined, and the rationale, and define the next review point. When a screening result is abnormal, ensure timely follow-up and referral, because a test performed without follow-through provides no protection.

### Escalate to Urology or Oncology by Defined Criteria

Define the thresholds for specialist referral. Refer to urology for elevated or rising PSA that warrants biopsy, suspicious prostate examination, and any testicular mass or abnormality. Refer to gastroenterology or surgery for positive stool tests, suspicious colonoscopy findings, or symptoms concerning for colorectal cancer (change in bowel habit, rectal bleeding, unexplained weight loss or iron-deficiency anemia). Refer for genetic counseling when family history or personal history suggests a hereditary cancer syndrome. Refer to oncology for any confirmed malignancy. State the referral threshold and the safety-netting explicitly, and ensure that abnormal results are tracked to resolution rather than left to the patient to pursue.

## Common Traps

### Ordering PSA Without Shared Decision-Making

Drawing PSA reflexively at a check-up without counseling about harms and benefits, denying the patient an informed choice. Counter by conducting and documenting an explicit shared decision-making discussion before testing.

### Continuing Screening in Men with Limited Life Expectancy

Persisting with screening in older or comorbid men in whom the harms outweigh the benefit. Counter by estimating life expectancy and individualizing the decision to stop screening.

### Offering a Single Colorectal Screening Test Rather Than Options

Defaulting to one modality and losing adherence from patients who would prefer another. Counter by offering the range of evidence-based options matched to risk and preference.

### Recommending Routine Testicular Self-Examination Despite Evidence

Advising monthly self-exam as if it were evidence-based, generating anxiety and false positives. Counter by teaching testicular awareness and prompt reporting of abnormalities, and by targeted evaluation of high-risk men.

### Applying Average-Risk Intervals to High-Risk Men

Using standard screening schedules for men with strong family history, African ancestry, or hereditary syndromes, under-screening those who need more. Counter by risk-stratifying and intensifying surveillance accordingly.

### Treating Lifestyle Counseling as Optional

Focusing on tests while under-addressing tobacco, alcohol, obesity, and physical activity, the largest drivers of cancer burden. Counter by integrating evidence-based prevention with the same seriousness as screening.

### Failing to Follow Up Abnormal Screening Results

Performing a test but not tracking the result to resolution, negating the benefit of screening. Counter by ensuring timely referral and tracking abnormal results to closure.

## Self-Check

- Before offering PSA, have I conducted and documented a shared decision-making discussion covering benefits, harms, false results, and downstream consequences?
- Have I offered colorectal cancer screening with a range of evidence-based options, risk-stratified by age, family history, and personal risk, and individualized the decision to stop by life expectancy?
- Have I avoided recommending routine testicular self-examination for average-risk men while teaching testicular awareness and targeted evaluation for high-risk or symptomatic men?
- Have I identified higher-risk men (African ancestry, family history, hereditary syndromes) and intensified screening or referred for genetic counseling accordingly?
- Have I addressed modifiable risk factors — tobacco, alcohol, obesity, physical inactivity, sun exposure, and relevant vaccinations — as core prevention, not optional advice?
- Have I integrated screening into a risk-based preventive visit that also addresses cardiovascular risk, mental health, substance use, and immunization?
- For older or comorbid men, have I estimated life expectancy and individualized the decision to continue or stop screening?
- For any abnormal screening result, have I ensured timely follow-up and referral to urology, gastroenterology, genetics, or oncology, and tracked the result to resolution?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
