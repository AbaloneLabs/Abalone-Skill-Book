# Pathology Report Interpretation and Specimen Correlation

A pathology report is a clinical consultation, not a black-box verdict, and treating it as the final word without understanding its provenance, limitations, and uncertainty is a recurring source of harm. The judgment problem is that a histology diagnosis depends entirely on what was sampled, how it was handled, and what question was asked — and these are exactly the things clinicians skip when they scan straight to the diagnosis line. A report that reads "no malignancy" on an inadequate or crushed sample, a margin called "negative" on a specimen that was not properly oriented, or a "mild dysplasia" label that is actually a sampling artifact can each lead to false reassurance and delayed cancer diagnosis. The recurring failure modes are reading only the diagnosis, ignoring specimen adequacy and limitations, misreading diagnostic qualifiers, and failing to correlate histology against the clinical picture so that a discordant result is acted on rather than accepted. This skill covers structured report reading, adequacy and limitation assessment, diagnostic terminology, path-clinical correlation, and the disciplined response to discordance.

## Core Rules

### Read the Whole Report in Order, Starting With Specimen and Clinical Context

Begin with the clinical information provided to the pathologist, because that context shapes what is examined and what is reported. A report generated from "rule out malignancy" is interpreted differently from one generated from "benign-appearing lesion," and a missing or wrong clinical history is a leading cause of diagnostic error. Confirm the specimen identity, site, and laterality match the patient and the procedure, because wrong-patient and wrong-site errors do occur and must be caught before acting. Note the specimen type (biopsy, excision, resection), the fixative and handling, and any special requests such as frozen section or margins. Only after establishing the provenance should you read the microscopic description and the diagnosis, because the diagnosis is only as reliable as the specimen and the context that produced it.

### Assess Specimen Adequacy and Limitations Before Trusting the Diagnosis

Every report should be read against the question "was the sample adequate to answer the clinical question?" Look explicitly for statements of adequacy, and when absent, infer them from the description. A biopsy that is too small, crushed, cauterized, superficial, or poorly oriented may be non-diagnostic or may under-represent the lesion, and a negative result on an inadequate sample does not exclude disease. Recognize the common artifacts and limitations: crush artifact in lymphoid and small round cell lesions, cautery artifact at resection margins that can obscure dysplasia or carcinoma, tangential sectioning that can make an in situ process look invasive, and sampling error in a heterogeneous lesion where the biopsy may miss the highest-grade area. When the report explicitly states "limited sample," "cannot exclude," "recommend re-biopsy," or "clinical correlation advised," treat these as actionable instructions, not disclaimers to be ignored.

### Interpret Diagnostic Terminology With Precision and Understand the Spectrum

Diagnostic terms carry specific meaning and a spectrum of risk, and conflating them changes management. Distinguish reactive or regenerative atypia from dysplasia or atypia of undetermined significance, and understand that "atypia" is a hedge that demands context and often repeat sampling. Grade dysplasia where applicable (mild, moderate, severe; low-grade versus high-grade) and recognize that high-grade dysplasia and carcinoma in situ are managed as lesions with significant malignant potential, often with excision. Distinguish in situ disease (confined by the basement membrane, no metastatic potential in itself) from invasive disease (breached basement membrane, capacity for metastasis), because this single distinction drives staging, treatment, and prognosis. Understand qualifiers such as "favor," "suspicious for," "cannot exclude," and "consistent with," which convey graded certainty and which should be matched to your clinical confidence — a hedged diagnosis warrants either repeat sampling or discussion with the pathologist, not reflexive reassurance.

### Scrutinize Margin Status in the Context of How the Specimen Was Handled

Margin status determines whether a cancer resection is complete, but a margin call is only as good as the specimen orientation and the pathologist's sampling of it. Confirm that margins were inked or submitted separately and oriented, because an unoriented specimen prevents meaningful margin interpretation. Recognize that a "negative" margin reflects only what was sampled — pathologists examine representative sections, not the entire margin — so a close or positive margin is meaningful, but a negative margin does not absolutely exclude residual disease, particularly in large or irregular specimens. Understand organ-specific margin definitions: for example, what counts as an adequate margin differs by tissue (skin, breast, colorectal, pancreatic) and by guideline, and some sites accept a 1 mm clearance while others require more. When a margin is positive or close, correlate with the planned next step — further excision, adjuvant therapy, or surveillance — and confirm the decision in a multidisciplinary discussion.

### Correlate Histology With Clinical, Endoscopic, and Imaging Findings

Pathology does not exist in isolation; it must be reconciled with the clinical picture, the endoscopic or gross appearance, and the imaging. This is the triple test principle generalized: when histology, imaging, and clinical assessment agree, confidence is high; when they disagree, the disagreement must be explained before the result is acted on. A benign biopsy in a lesion that looks malignant on imaging or endoscopy, or that is growing or symptomatic, is discordant and demands re-review, repeat biopsy, or excision — not reassurance. A malignant-sounding report in a lesion that is clinically benign may reflect a labeling error, a mixed lesion, or a true unexpected finding, and must be confirmed before radical treatment. Make the correlation explicit: state the clinical impression, the imaging impression, and the histology, and explain any discrepancy in the note and the plan.

### Recognize When to Request Pathology Review, Repeat Biopsy, or Escalation

Define the triggers for going back to pathology or to the patient. Request a formal pathology review (second opinion) when the diagnosis is unexpected, the terminology is ambiguous, the grade or type would change management substantially, the case is rare or borderline, or the patient is being treated at a different center. Request repeat biopsy or excision when the sample is non-diagnostic or inadequate, when there is path-clinical discordance, when the lesion has changed since sampling, or when a high-risk lesion was undersampled. Escalate to multidisciplinary discussion when management hinges on interpretation, when there is genuine uncertainty, or when the result will trigger major surgery, chemotherapy, or radiation. Communicate directly with the pathologist — provide clinical context, ask specific questions, and request additional levels, immunostains, or molecular studies rather than acting on an incomplete picture.

## Common Traps

### Reading Only the Diagnosis Line and Ignoring the Rest

Scanning to the diagnosis and acting on it without reading the clinical context, microscopic description, adequacy, and qualifiers. Counter by reading the report in order and explicitly assessing specimen adequacy and limitations before trusting the diagnosis.

### Accepting a Negative Result on an Inadequate Sample

Treating "no malignancy identified" on a small, crushed, or poorly oriented biopsy as exclusion of cancer. Counter by assessing adequacy and treating an inadequate sample with non-diagnostic implications as an indication for repeat biopsy.

### Conflating Atypia, Dysplasia, In Situ, and Invasive Disease

Treating all of these as variants of "abnormal cells" and either over- or under-treating. Counter by interpreting each term precisely and matching the grade and in situ versus invasive distinction to the correct management.

### Trusting a Negative Margin Without Confirming Orientation and Sampling

Accepting "margins negative" as proof of complete excision regardless of how the specimen was handled. Counter by confirming orientation and inking, recognizing that margins are sampled representatively, and discussing close or positive margins in multidisciplinary review.

### Failing to Correlate Histology With the Clinical and Imaging Picture

Acting on a biopsy result in isolation, reassuring on a benign result despite a suspicious lesion, or pursuing radical treatment on a malignant result without confirming against the clinical picture. Counter by making path-clinical-imaging correlation explicit and investigating any discordance.

### Overlooking Qualifiers and Hedges as Mere Boilerplate

Dismissing "cannot exclude," "suspicious for," or "clinical correlation advised" as disclaimers rather than instructions. Counter by treating each qualifier as an actionable statement that changes the next step.

### Not Communicating With the Pathologist When Uncertain

Acting on an ambiguous or incomplete report rather than requesting clarification, additional stains, levels, or review. Counter by contacting the pathologist directly with clinical context and specific questions whenever the report is unclear or management-defining.

## Self-Check

- Have I read the full report in order, confirming specimen identity, site, laterality, and the clinical context provided to the pathologist?
- Have I explicitly assessed specimen adequacy and limitations, and treated any statement of inadequacy or "cannot exclude" as actionable?
- Have I interpreted diagnostic terminology precisely, distinguishing atypia from dysplasia and in situ from invasive disease?
- For any excision, have I confirmed margin orientation and sampling, and interpreted margin status in the context of organ-specific definitions?
- Have I explicitly correlated histology with the clinical, endoscopic, and imaging findings, and stated whether they are concordant?
- For any path-clinical discordance, have I arranged pathology review, repeat biopsy, or excision rather than reassuring on the biopsy alone?
- Have I escalated management-defining or ambiguous cases to multidisciplinary discussion and communicated directly with the pathologist where needed?
