# Immunization and Prophylaxis Decisions

Immunization is among the most effective interventions in medicine, yet it is also where clinicians cause preventable harm through two opposite errors: missing indicated vaccines in patients who need them, and giving contraindicated vaccines — particularly live vaccines — to patients in whom they are dangerous. The cognitive trap is that vaccines feel routine, so clinicians apply them from memory and habit rather than checking the specific schedule, the patient's risk profile, and the live-versus-inactivated constraint in the immunocompromised or pregnant patient. A second, distinct challenge is post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP), where decisions are time-critical, regimen-specific, and high-stakes — a missed or delayed rabies PEP can be fatal, and an incorrect HIV PEP regimen can fail or cause harm. And running through all of this is vaccine hesitancy, which cannot be overcome by reciting facts at a resistant patient but can often be moved by a respectful, presumptive, relationship-based conversation. This skill covers the disciplined practice of immunization and prophylaxis: applying schedules correctly, navigating special populations, managing time-critical PEP, counseling the hesitant, and knowing when to escalate to public health or infectious diseases. The clinician's job is to maximize protection while rigorously avoiding contraindicated interventions and to treat every exposure as a potentially fatal event until proven otherwise.

## Core Rules

### Base Decisions on the Current Authoritative Schedule, Not Memory

Immunization schedules change, and relying on memory produces both missed vaccines and incorrect timing. Use the current authoritative schedule (e.g., the relevant national immunization program or ACIP recommendations) as the source of truth for routine, catch-up, and risk-based vaccination. Confirm the patient's prior doses, allergies, and immune status before giving any vaccine, and document what was given, when, and the lot number. For catch-up scenarios, follow the minimum intervals — accelerating beyond them reduces immunogenicity, and spacing them too widely leaves the patient unprotected. Do not assume a patient is "up to date" because they believe they are; verify from records where possible, and reconstruct a catch-up plan when records are incomplete.

### Treat Live-Versus-Inactivated as a Hard Constraint in Special Populations

The single most dangerous error in immunization is giving a live vaccine (measles-mumps-rubella, varicella, live attenuated influenza, oral polio, yellow fever, BCG, oral typhoid, rotavirus) to a patient who is significantly immunocompromised or, in some cases, pregnant. Live vaccines can cause uncontrolled infection in the severely immunocompromised and can harm the fetus. Before any live vaccine, screen explicitly for: pregnancy or planned pregnancy, HIV with low CD4 count, current chemotherapy or recent cytotoxic therapy, solid organ or stem cell transplant, significant immunosuppressive biologic therapy (e.g., anti-TNF agents, rituximab), primary immunodeficiency, and recent receipt of blood products (which can interfere with live vaccines). When a live vaccine is contraindicated, use the inactivated alternative where one exists, defer until immune recovery, or seek specialist guidance. Inactivated vaccines are generally safe in immunocompromised patients but may be less immunogenic — they do not cause infection, but they may not protect fully, and revaccination after immune reconstitution is sometimes needed.

### Individualize Immunization in Pregnancy

Pregnancy is a special population with its own rules. Inactivated vaccines are generally safe in pregnancy, and some are specifically recommended: influenza (inactivated) at any stage, Tdap in every pregnancy (optimally in the third trimester to protect the infant through antibody transfer), and COVID-19 vaccination. Live vaccines are contraindicated in pregnancy and should be given postpartum; counsel the patient to avoid pregnancy for a defined period after a live vaccine. Pregnancy also changes risk-benefit for travel vaccines (e.g., yellow fever is generally avoided in pregnancy but may be required for travel to high-risk areas — seek specialist advice). Immunization in pregnancy protects two patients: the mother and, through transplacental antibody transfer, the newborn in its most vulnerable early months.

### Manage Post-Exposure Prophylaxis as Time-Critical and Regimen-Specific

PEP decisions are urgent, and the wrong delay or the wrong regimen can be fatal. Know the core scenarios and their time windows. For rabies exposure (a bite or mucous membrane exposure from a potentially rabid animal), initiate wound care immediately, give rabies immunoglobulin into and around the wound plus vaccine on the standard schedule, and consult public health or infectious diseases — rabies is nearly uniformly fatal once symptomatic, so PEP must not be delayed while awaiting confirmation of the animal's status. For HIV exposure (occupational or non-occupational, sexual or needlestick), start PEP as soon as possible, ideally within hours and generally not beyond 72 hours, using a recommended three-drug regimen, with baseline HIV testing and follow-up testing; seek specialist or PEP-line guidance for regimen selection. For meningococcal exposure, give chemoprophylaxis promptly to close contacts within the recommended window. For tetanus-prone wounds, assess immunization status and give tetanus toxoid and/or immunoglobulin per the wound's risk and the patient's vaccination history. For each scenario, do not improvise the regimen — use the current protocol and escalate to public health or infectious diseases when the exposure is complex, the source is unknown, or the patient is high-risk.

### Address Vaccine Hesitancy With a Presumptive, Relationship-Based Approach

Vaccine hesitancy is not solved by overwhelming the patient with data; it is moved by relationship, acknowledgment, and a presumptive framing. Open with a presumptive statement ("Today your child is due for their routine vaccines, and I recommend we give them") rather than a participatory one ("What did you decide about vaccines?"), which invites refusal. If the parent or patient hesitates, do not argue — acknowledge the concern with genuine empathy, explore the specific fear (safety, autism myths, ingredients, "too many too soon"), provide a focused and honest correction, and share your personal recommendation ("I gave my own children these vaccines, and I recommend them for yours"). Use a strong, personalized recommendation; it is one of the most powerful determinants of acceptance. Do not dismiss, mock, or fire the patient from your practice over hesitancy — maintain the relationship, revisit the conversation at future visits, and recognize that acceptance is often built over multiple encounters. Document the discussion and the decision.

### Recognize and Respect True Contraindications Versus False Ones

Most "contraindications" cited for deferring vaccines are not contraindications at all. Mild acute illness, antibiotic therapy, prematurity, breastfeeding, family history of allergies, and a personal or family history of seizures are not contraindications to routine vaccination. True contraindications are narrow: severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis) to a prior dose or a vaccine component, significant immunocompromise (for live vaccines), pregnancy (for live vaccines), and specific rare conditions such as Guillain-Barré within six weeks of a prior influenza or tetanus-containing vaccine (a precaution). Distinguish a severe allergy from a minor local reaction or non-anaphylactic sensitivity. When in doubt, do not simply defer indefinitely — assess properly, seek allergy or immunology input where the reaction history is severe or unclear, and use the correct alternative vaccine or a supervised administration protocol.

### Escalate to Public Health and Infectious Diseases Appropriately

Many immunization and prophylaxis decisions intersect with public health obligations and specialist expertise. Reportable diseases and exposures must be reported to public health authorities; PEP for contacts of cases of meningococcal disease, invasive Hib, or pertussus is often coordinated through public health. Refer to or consult infectious diseases for complex immunization in severe immunocompromise, stem cell transplant recipients, HIV with low CD4 counts, complex travel medicine, unclear allergy histories requiring specialist assessment, and any PEP scenario where the regimen, timing, or source is uncertain. Do not manage complex or high-stakes prophylaxis in isolation.

## Common Traps

### Giving Vaccines From Memory Without Checking the Schedule

Assuming the schedule from recall and missing a dose, giving the wrong product, or violating minimum intervals. Counter by using the current authoritative schedule for every decision and verifying prior doses.

### Giving a Live Vaccine to an Immunocompromised or Pregnant Patient

Failing to screen for immunocompromise or pregnancy before a live vaccine and causing potentially serious infection or fetal harm. Counter by explicitly screening for these conditions before every live vaccine and seeking specialist guidance when status is uncertain.

### Missing Indicated Vaccines in High-Risk Patients

Failing to give risk-based vaccines (e.g., pneumococcal, hepatitis B, meningococcal, recombinant zoster) to patients with indications such as chronic disease, immunocompromise, asplenia, or older age. Counter by reviewing risk-based indications at every visit, not just routine schedule items.

### Delaying or Improvising Post-Exposure Prophylaxis

Waiting for confirmation of the source's status before starting rabies PEP, missing the HIV PEP window, or using a non-recommended regimen. Counter by treating PEP as time-critical, using the current protocol, and escalating to public health or infectious diseases immediately.

### Treating Mild Illness as a Contraindication

Deferring vaccines for minor acute illness, antibiotic use, or other non-contraindications, leaving the patient unprotected. Counter by recognizing that most deferrals are not valid contraindications and vaccinating unless a true contraindication is present.

### Overwhelming the Hesitant Patient With Data

Responding to vaccine hesitancy with a flood of facts and statistics, which entrenches resistance rather than moving it. Counter by using a presumptive, empathetic, relationship-based approach focused on a strong personal recommendation.

### Managing Complex Prophylaxis in Isolation

Attempting complex travel, immunocompromise, or exposure-related prophylaxis without specialist or public health input. Counter by escalating to infectious diseases, travel medicine, allergy, or public health when the scenario exceeds routine practice.

## Self-Check

- Have I based the immunization decision on the current authoritative schedule and verified the patient's prior doses, allergies, and immune status rather than relying on memory?
- Before any live vaccine, have I explicitly screened for pregnancy or planned pregnancy, HIV and CD4 status, chemotherapy, transplant, immunosuppressive biologics, primary immunodeficiency, and recent blood products?
- In pregnancy, have I given the recommended inactivated vaccines (influenza, Tdap, COVID-19), avoided live vaccines, and counseled on postpartum catch-up and pregnancy avoidance after live vaccination?
- For any exposure, have I treated post-exposure prophylaxis as time-critical — initiating rabies, HIV, meningococcal, or tetanus prophylaxis promptly per the current protocol and not delaying while awaiting source confirmation?
- Have I distinguished true contraindications (anaphylaxis to a component, significant immunocompromise for live vaccines, pregnancy for live vaccines, specific precautions) from false ones (mild illness, antibiotics, prematurity, family history)?
- For vaccine hesitancy, have I used a presumptive, empathetic, relationship-based approach with a strong personal recommendation rather than overwhelming the patient with data or dismissing their concern?
- Have I identified and given risk-based vaccines (pneumococcal, hepatitis B, meningococcal, zoster) to patients with chronic disease, immunocompromise, asplenia, or age-based indications?
- Have I escalated to public health or infectious diseases for reportable exposures, complex immunocompromise, travel medicine, unclear allergy histories, and uncertain PEP scenarios?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as a high-risk exposure, possible anaphylaxis history, or significant immunocompromise appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
