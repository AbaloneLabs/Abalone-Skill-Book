# Adult Immunization

Adult immunization is consistently undervalued relative to childhood vaccination, and this is where preventable disease, preventable outbreak, and preventable death still concentrate. The recurring failure is to treat vaccination as a pediatric task: adult patients arrive undervaccinated, their status is never reconciled, boosters lapse, and opportunities at every adult encounter are missed because no one owns the question. A second failure is to apply pediatric logic to adult patients — adults are not simply older children. They carry chronic disease, pregnancy, immunocompromise, occupational exposure, travel, changing sexual risk, waning immunity, and prior incomplete or undocumented series, all of which modify what they need and when. A third failure is to defer adult vaccines on weak pretexts — a cold, antibiotics, "feeling run down" — that are not contraindications, leaving adults susceptible to influenza, pneumococcal disease, pertussis, shingles, hepatitis, and HPV-related cancer. This skill covers the disciplined practice of adult immunization: reconciling status at every encounter, applying adult-specific schedules, prioritizing the vaccines adults most often miss, addressing adult-specific risk and hesitancy, and integrating vaccination into chronic-disease and pregnancy care. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; consult current national immunization guidance (e.g., ACIP) and qualified specialists for immunocompromised, pregnant, or travel-medicine patients.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile Adult Immunization Status at Every Encounter

Adult immunization is opportunistic: the patient who comes for hypertension, a sore throat, or a preoperative assessment is also a patient whose tetanus may be a decade lapsed and whose influenza vaccine is due. Build status reconciliation into routine adult visits, chronic-disease reviews, hospital admission and discharge, and preoperative assessment. Query the immunization registry where one exists, because adults rarely recall their full history accurately. Identify gaps explicitly and close them at the same visit whenever possible — every deferred vaccine is a vaccine that may never be given. Document reconciliation and the plan for any gaps so the next encounter does not start from zero.

### Apply the Adult Schedule, Not a Generalized Pediatric Memory

Adult immunization needs are age-, risk-, and condition-specific, and they change over the lifespan. Know the current adult schedule for the relevant jurisdiction and apply it deliberately: tetanus-diphtheria-acellular pertussis boosters (with a Tdap dose in adulthood and in every pregnancy), influenza annually, COVID-19 per current guidance, pneumococcal vaccination with age- and risk-based products and timing, shingles (herpes zoster) vaccination in midlife and beyond, HPV catch-up through the recommended adult age range, hepatitis A and B where indicated by risk, and meningococcal, rabies, or other vaccines for specific risk. Do not rely on memory of the pediatric schedule, and do not assume "they got everything as a child." Re-verify at the age transitions where new vaccines become due.

### Prioritize the Vaccines Adults Most Often Miss

Certain vaccines are systematically under-delivered to adults and account for disproportionate preventable morbidity and mortality. Influenza vaccine is missed in high-risk adults every season; pneumococcal vaccine is delayed or omitted in older adults and those with chronic disease; Tdap boosters lapse for decades; shingles vaccine is underused despite strong efficacy; HPV catch-up is missed in young adults still within the eligible window; and maternal vaccination (influenza, Tdap, COVID-19, and now RSV prevention strategies) is critical to protect both the pregnant patient and the newborn through antibody transfer. Build standing orders, reminders, and clinic workflows that default to offering these vaccines rather than relying on individual clinician initiative.

### Modify the Schedule for Chronic Disease, Not Around It

Chronic disease increases both the risk and the consequence of vaccine-preventable disease, and it modifies the schedule rather than exempting the patient from it. Patients with chronic lung, heart, liver, or kidney disease; diabetes; asplenia; cochlear implants; cerebrospinal fluid leaks; and immunocompromise need additional or earlier pneumococcal vaccination, annual influenza, and often meningococcal and Haemophilus influenzae type b vaccination. Patients with HIV, transplant, malignancy, or immunosuppressive therapy need enhanced schedules and careful attention to live-vaccine contraindications. Treat chronic disease as a trigger to review and intensify the immunization plan, not as a reason to defer.

### Manage Immunization in Pregnancy Deliberately

Pregnancy is a high-stakes immunization context because vaccination protects two patients. Inactivated vaccines — influenza (at any stage, in any season), Tdap (ideally each pregnancy, in the early third trimester, to maximize antibody transfer), and COVID-19 — are recommended in pregnancy, and RSV prevention strategies now include maternal vaccination to protect the newborn. Live vaccines are contraindicated in pregnancy and are deferred to the postpartum period with counseling to avoid pregnancy for a defined interval where relevant. Update immunization status as part of routine antenatal care, and use the postpartum period to catch up any deferred vaccines. Document vaccination in both the maternal record and the infant record where relevant.

### Address Adult Vaccine Hesitancy Specifically

Adults who decline vaccination are not simply uninformed; their hesitancy often reflects perceived low risk, concern about side effects, distrust of the health system, prior adverse experience, misinformation, or a calculation that the vaccine is not worth it for them. Address hesitancy with the same discipline used in any behavior-change conversation: assess the specific concern, provide a strong, clear recommendation ("I recommend this vaccine for you, and here is why"), frame the risk of the disease in absolute terms relevant to this patient, acknowledge and contextualize the side-effect profile honestly, and offer to vaccinate at the visit or schedule a return. A strong, personal clinician recommendation is one of the most effective interventions for adult vaccine uptake. Do not dismiss hesitancy, but do not leave it unchallenged either.

### Use Travel, Occupational, and Behavioral Risk to Expand Coverage

Adult immunization extends beyond the routine schedule when risk expands. Travel medicine requires region-specific vaccines (yellow fever, typhoid, hepatitis A, Japanese encephalitis, rabies) timed before departure, with attention to live-vaccine spacing and immunocompromise. Occupational exposure (healthcare, laboratory, animal-handling, certain trades) drives hepatitis B, influenza, meningococcal, and other vaccines. Sexual and bloodborne risk drives hepatitis A and B and HPV catch-up. Ask about travel, occupation, and risk behaviors as part of the adult immunization review, and use the answers to expand coverage appropriately.

### Document, Report, and Close the Loop

As in all immunization, an adult dose given but not documented is operationally invisible. Record the vaccine, date, site, route, lot, manufacturer, and any reaction; report to the immunization registry; and provide the patient with a record. Schedule the next dose in a series explicitly and enter a reminder, because adults will not return for a second or third dose without a clear plan. For multi-dose adult series (HPV, hepatitis, shingles, pneumococcal), the completion rate is the measure of success, not the first-dose rate.

## Common Traps

### Treating Immunization as a Pediatric Task

Never reconciling adult status and missing the chronic-disease, pregnancy, and age transitions where new vaccines become due. Counter by building status reconciliation into every adult encounter and querying the registry.

### Deferring on Non-Contraindications

Withholding vaccines for mild illness, antibiotics, breastfeeding, or "feeling run down," all of which are not contraindications. Counter by screening with the current contraindication checklist and vaccinating unless a true contraindication or precaution exists.

### Applying Pediatric Logic to Adult Risk

Assuming adults need only childhood boosters, missing pneumococcal, shingles, HPV catch-up, and pregnancy-specific vaccines. Counter by applying the current adult schedule deliberately and re-verifying at age and risk transitions.

### Missing the Vaccines Adults Systematically Miss

Defaulting to clinician initiative for influenza, pneumococcal, Tdap, shingles, and maternal vaccines, leading to chronic under-delivery. Counter by using standing orders, reminders, and workflows that default to offering these vaccines.

### Leaving Hesitancy Unchallenged

Accepting adult vaccine refusal without a strong personal recommendation and an honest discussion of disease risk and side effects. Counter by making a clear recommendation, framing absolute risk, and offering to vaccinate at the visit.

### Failing to Complete Multi-Dose Adult Series

Giving the first dose of an HPV, hepatitis, or shingles series without a documented plan and reminder for completion. Counter by scheduling the next dose explicitly and entering a recall.

## Self-Check

- Did I reconcile the adult patient's immunization status at this encounter by querying the registry and prior records, rather than relying on memory or patient recall?
- Did I apply the current adult schedule — not pediatric memory — and re-verify coverage at age, risk, and condition transitions?
- Did I prioritize the systematically missed adult vaccines (influenza, pneumococcal, Tdap, shingles, HPV catch-up, maternal vaccines) and use standing orders or workflows to default to offering them?
- For patients with chronic disease, immunocompromise, or pregnancy, did I modify and intensify the schedule appropriately and screen for live-vaccine contraindications?
- Did I ask about travel, occupation, and behavioral risk and expand coverage accordingly, with attention to timing before departure?
- For vaccine hesitancy, did I make a strong personal recommendation, frame absolute disease risk, acknowledge side effects honestly, and offer vaccination at the visit?
- Did I document each dose fully, report to the registry, and schedule explicit reminders for any remaining doses in a series?
