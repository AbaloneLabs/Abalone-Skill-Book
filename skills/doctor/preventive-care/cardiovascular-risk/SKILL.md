# Cardiovascular Risk Assessment and Stratification

Cardiovascular risk assessment is the foundation on which all preventive cardiology rests, and it is where the most consequential cognitive errors occur. The recurring failure is to treat a single risk factor — an LDL number, a blood pressure reading — as if it were the patient's risk, when in reality cardiovascular events are determined by the integrated absolute risk across multiple factors over time. A patient with a strikingly high LDL but no other risk factors may have a lower event risk than a patient with modestly abnormal numbers across several factors, and treating the former aggressively while ignoring the latter is a category error. A second failure is to over-trust the risk calculator's single number, treating a population average with wide confidence intervals as if it were a precise individual prediction, and to ignore the risk enhancers and modifiers the calculator does not capture. A third failure is to assess 10-year risk in young patients, find it reassuringly low, and miss a lifetime risk that is dangerously high — the patient who has decades of accumulated exposure ahead of them. This skill covers the disciplined practice of cardiovascular risk stratification: choosing the right calculator, entering data accurately, interpreting the output with appropriate humility, incorporating risk enhancers and family history, using coronary artery calcium scoring as a tie-breaker, distinguishing short-term from lifetime risk, and stratifying risk correctly in populations the standard calculators under-serve. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; consult qualified cardiologists and lipid specialists for familial hypercholesterolemia, established disease, and complex risk.

## Core Rules

### Choose the Calculator Appropriate to the Population

Risk calculators are population-derived, and applying one to a population it was not validated for produces misleading results. ASCVD was derived from US cohorts and performs variably across subgroups; QRISK is calibrated to the UK population and incorporates additional factors; SCORE2 reflects European cohorts. Use the calculator validated for the patient's population where possible, and recognize that all of them carry wide confidence intervals at the individual level. Do not switch calculators mid-patient or mix inputs from different tools. When the patient belongs to a population the standard calculators under-serve (South Asian ancestry, chronic kidney disease, rheumatologic inflammatory disease, HIV, certain transplant populations), adjust the interpretation upward and consider population-specific or enhanced tools.

### Enter the Inputs Accurately and Verify Them

The calculator output is only as good as the inputs, and the most common source of error is wrong or stale data. Use the patient's actual age, sex (or sex assigned at birth where the calculator requires it, recognizing the limitations for transgender patients on hormone therapy), race or ethnicity as the tool defines it, treated or untreated systolic blood pressure, total and HDL cholesterol, diabetes and smoking status, and treatment status. Avoid using a single blood pressure reading; prefer averaged, validated, out-of-office readings where possible. Confirm lipid values are current and were measured in the appropriate metabolic state (fasting or non-fasting per the tool). Document the inputs and the tool version so the assessment is reproducible.

### Interpret the Output With Humility, Not False Precision

A risk calculator returns a point estimate, but the underlying confidence interval is wide, and the patient in front of you may sit well above or below the average. Treat the number as a starting point for discussion, not an oracle. Communicate the uncertainty honestly to the patient. Recognize known calibration problems: some calculators overestimate risk in certain populations (leading to overtreatment) and underestimate in others (leading to under-treatment). Where the estimate is borderline or where the decision hinges on it, do not treat the number as the final word — incorporate risk enhancers, family history, and imaging as described below.

### Incorporate Risk Enhancers the Calculator Misses

Standard calculators do not capture every factor that modifies risk, and these enhancers should move your interpretation up or down. Key enhancers include a family history of premature atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (first-degree male relative under 55, female under 65), persistently elevated primary hypercholesterolemia (LDL markedly elevated), metabolic syndrome, chronic kidney disease, chronic inflammatory autoimmune disorders (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, psoriasis), history of premature menopause, history of preeclampsia or other adverse pregnancy outcomes, South Asian and certain other high-risk ancestries, persistently elevated high-sensitivity C-reactive protein, lipoprotein(a) elevation, and ankle-brachial index abnormalities. When enhancers are present, adjust the risk estimate upward and lower the threshold for preventive therapy, even if the calculator's raw number is borderline.

### Take a Deliberate Family History

Family history of premature cardiovascular disease is one of the strongest independent risk predictors, it is frequently omitted, and it changes management. Ask specifically about first-degree relatives (parents, siblings, children) and the age at which cardiovascular events occurred, with premature disease defined as before 55 in male relatives and before 65 in female relatives. A positive family history should escalate the risk assessment and prompt consideration of genetic conditions such as familial hypercholesterolemia, which is underdiagnosed and carries a high lifetime risk that is dramatically reduced by early treatment. Do not record "family history: non-contributory" without having actually asked.

### Use Coronary Artery Calcium Scoring as a Tie-Breaker

When the risk estimate is borderline or uncertain and the decision to initiate statin therapy is genuinely in question, coronary artery calcium (CAC) scoring is a validated tool to reclassify risk. A CAC of zero is reassuring and supports deferring or de-intensifying statin therapy in selected lower-risk patients (with the important caveats of diabetes, strong family history, and smoking, where a zero score is less reassuring), while a high CAC reclassifies risk upward and supports initiating or intensifying therapy. Use CAC selectively as a decision aid, not as a routine screening test in everyone, and interpret it in the context of the full risk picture. Do not let a normal CAC override strong clinical risk (diabetes, familial hypercholesterolemia, heavy smoking).

### Distinguish 10-Year From Lifetime Risk, Especially in Young Adults

Ten-year risk calculators systematically underestimate the danger faced by young patients, because even a modest annual risk compounded over decades produces a high lifetime risk. A 35-year-old with suboptimal risk factors may have a low 10-year estimate but a high probability of a cardiovascular event over their lifetime, and the window to alter that trajectory is now, not in twenty years. In younger patients, assess and discuss lifetime risk in addition to 10-year risk, emphasize the cumulative nature of exposure, and prioritize aggressive lifestyle intervention and risk factor control early. Do not reassure a young high-risk patient solely because their 10-year number is low.

### Stratify Risk Correctly in Special Populations

Several populations carry cardiovascular risk that standard calculators systematically under-capture, and these patients require explicit, often intensified, risk stratification. South Asian populations have markedly higher event rates than calculators predict. Patients with chronic kidney disease, rheumatologic inflammatory disease, HIV on certain antiretrovirals, and solid organ or stem cell transplant recipients all carry elevated risk that warrants aggressive prevention. Women with a history of preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, or preterm delivery have a substantially elevated long-term cardiovascular risk that should be incorporated into their prevention plan. Diabetes itself is a risk equivalent in many frameworks and warrants aggressive prevention regardless of the calculated score. Identify these patients explicitly and do not let a reassuring calculator number produce under-treatment.

### Reassess Risk Periodically, Not Once

Risk is dynamic. It changes with age, with development of new risk factors (new diabetes, new hypertension), with behavioral change (smoking cessation substantially reduces risk over time), and with treatment. Reassess cardiovascular risk at appropriate intervals, update the calculator inputs with current data, and revise the prevention plan accordingly. A risk assessment performed once at age 50 and never revisited is not a risk management strategy.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Single Risk Factor as the Patient's Risk

Starting or withholding therapy based on LDL or blood pressure alone, without quantifying integrated absolute risk. Counter by using a validated calculator and deciding on the basis of total risk.

### False Precision From the Calculator

Treating the point estimate as an exact individual prediction rather than a population average with wide confidence intervals. Counter by acknowledging uncertainty, adjusting for risk enhancers, and using CAC as a tie-breaker in borderline cases.

### Ignoring Risk Enhancers and Family History

Accepting the raw calculator number without incorporating family history, metabolic syndrome, inflammatory disease, or adverse pregnancy history. Counter by explicitly assessing enhancers and adjusting the estimate and the threshold for therapy.

### Reassuring Young Patients on the Basis of Low 10-Year Risk

Missing high lifetime risk in young adults with suboptimal factors. Counter by assessing and discussing lifetime risk and intervening early to alter the long-term trajectory.

### Under-stratifying Special Populations

Applying standard calculators to South Asian, chronic kidney disease, rheumatologic, HIV, or post-preeclampsia patients without adjustment. Counter by recognizing the elevated baseline risk and intensifying prevention.

### Over-Reliance on a Normal Coronary Artery Calcium Score

Letting a zero CAC override strong clinical risk in diabetes, familial hypercholesterolemia, or heavy smoking. Counter by interpreting CAC in full context and not deferring indicated therapy solely on the basis of a normal score.

### One-Time Risk Assessment Without Reassessment

Treating a single risk calculation as permanent. Counter by reassessing at intervals and updating the plan as risk factors, age, and treatment change.

## Self-Check

- Did I choose a risk calculator validated for the patient's population, enter accurate and current inputs, and document the tool and version used?
- Did I interpret the output with appropriate humility, acknowledging the wide confidence interval and known calibration limitations, rather than treating the number as precise?
- Did I explicitly assess and incorporate risk enhancers — family history, metabolic syndrome, chronic kidney disease, inflammatory disease, adverse pregnancy outcomes, high-risk ancestry, lipoprotein(a), hs-CRP — and adjust the estimate accordingly?
- Did I take a deliberate family history of premature cardiovascular disease and consider familial hypercholesterolemia where indicated?
- Where the decision was borderline, did I consider selective use of coronary artery calcium scoring as a tie-breaker, interpreted in full clinical context?
- For younger patients, did I assess and discuss lifetime risk in addition to 10-year risk, rather than reassuring on the basis of a low short-term number?
- For special populations (South Asian, chronic kidney disease, rheumatologic, HIV, transplant, post-preeclampsia, diabetes), did I stratify risk appropriately and intensify prevention rather than relying on the standard calculator?
- Did I plan to reassess risk at appropriate intervals as age, risk factors, and treatment change?
