# Acute Coronary Syndrome and Chest Pain

Acute chest pain is one of the highest-stakes presentations in medicine, and the central judgment problem is not naming a diagnosis but calibrating risk. The danger is two-sided: missing a myocardial infarction or other lethal cause kills the patient, while over-admitting every nondescript chest pain floods the system, exposes patients to unnecessary radiation and procedures, and erodes the credibility of risk-stratification. The clinician must hold both errors in mind simultaneously and use structured tools rather than gut feeling to decide who is safe to discharge and who needs immediate reperfusion. This skill covers disciplined risk stratification, troponin interpretation, the STEMI versus NSTEMI fork, the must-not-miss differential, and the boundaries beyond which a generalist must hand off to cardiology.

## Core Rules

### Treat Chest Pain as a Risk-Calibration Problem, Not a Diagnosis Problem

The goal of the chest pain workup is not to prove the patient has ACS; it is to prove, to an acceptable margin, that they do not have a life-threatening cause. Frame every encounter around a low, moderate, and high-risk bucket using a validated score (HEART, TIMI, GRACE) rather than a subjective impression. HEART (History, ECG, Age, Risk factors, Troponin) is the most widely validated for emergency disposition and stratifies thirty-day major adverse cardiac event risk. A HEART score of 0-3 carries a roughly 1-2 percent risk and is the entry door for accelerated diagnostic protocols; 4-6 is moderate and usually requires admission or observation with serial testing; 7-10 is high and warrants aggressive evaluation and cardiology involvement. Always document the score and the resulting risk percentage, because the disposition decision rests on the number, not on a narrative.

### Obtain the ECG Early and Interpret It Actively, Not Passively

An ECG within ten minutes of arrival is a standard of care for any suspected ACS, and it must be interpreted by the clinician at the bedside, not merely filed. Look actively for STEMI criteria: ST elevation of at least 1 mm in two contiguous limb leads or at least 2 mm in men (1.5 mm in women) in two contiguous precordial leads, with reciprocal changes strengthening the call. Do not wait for the cardiologist to notice an inferior STEMI with reciprocal I and aVL depression; recognize reciprocal depression, posterior MI signs (V1-V2 tall R with ST depression), right ventricular involvement (V4R in inferior MI), and the subtlety of left main disease (diffuse ST depression with aVR elevation). Also identify STEMI equivalents: Wellens syndrome, de Winter T waves, hyperacute T waves, and new left bundle branch block in the right clinical context. A normal ECG does not exclude ACS, and serial ECGs are mandatory if pain is ongoing.

### Interpret Troponin as a Delta, Not a Single Number

High-sensitivity troponin assays have transformed ACS rule-out, but only when used with an appropriate delta threshold and validated pathway (for example, 0/1-hour or 0/2-hour algorithms). A single value is rarely diagnostic; what matters is the rise or fall over time. A delta meeting the assay-specific change criterion, in the right clinical context, indicates ongoing myocardial injury and supports an ACS diagnosis. A small elevated but flat troponin over many hours suggests chronic injury (renal failure, heart failure, sepsis, myocarditis) rather than ACS and should not reflexively trigger cath lab activation. Use the assay-specific validated algorithm, document the time of symptom onset and draw times, and never discharge a patient before the required second sample unless the pathway explicitly permits it. In renal failure, troponin still rules out ACS when high-sensitivity values are below the limit of detection with a negative delta.

### Maintain a Must-Not-Miss Differential and Explicitly Exclude Each

Chest pain has at least five lethal causes that must be addressed in every patient: ACS, pulmonary embolism, aortic dissection, tension pneumothorax, and esophageal rupture (Boerhaave), with pericarditis with tamponade and aortic stenosis sometimes added. For each, state in the assessment what rules it in or out. Tearing pain radiating to the back with a pulse or blood pressure differential demands evaluation for dissection before discharging. Sudden pleuritic pain with dyspnea, tachycardia, and risk factors demands a PE rule-out pathway. Do not anchor on a benign diagnosis such as reflux or musculoskeletal pain until the lethal causes have been actively excluded by history, exam, ECG, and targeted testing. A young patient with atypical pain still dies of dissection or PE; risk stratification tools exist precisely to discipline these low-pretest cases.

### Apply Accelerated Diagnostic Protocols Only to the Right Patients

Accelerated diagnostic protocols (for example, HEART 0-3 plus negative serial high-sensitivity troponin and no dynamic ECG changes) allow safe early discharge of truly low-risk patients. They are not a license to discharge anyone with chest pain; they apply only to patients who are pain-free, hemodynamically stable, have a low HEART score, and have completed the required objective testing. Misapplying an accelerated protocol to a moderate or high-risk patient is a direct route to a missed infarction. Document that all protocol criteria are met, give the patient clear written return precautions, and arrange follow-up within days. If any criterion is unmet, the patient is not protocol-eligible and requires standard observation and further evaluation.

### Execute the STEMI Pathway Without Delay

For STEMI, time is muscle, and the system, not the individual, determines outcome. Activate the cath lab immediately when STEMI is recognized; do not wait for troponin confirmation. First medical contact to balloon (or to thrombolytics when percutaneous intervention is unavailable within the target window) is the metric that matters. Give aspirin, a loading dose of a P2Y12 inhibitor per local protocol, anticoagulation, and consider dual antiplatelet therapy and statin. In the meantime, manage pain, oxygen only if hypoxic (not routinely), nitrates if there is ongoing ischemia or hypertension without contraindication, and beta-blockers only if the patient is hemodynamically stable and without acute heart failure. Escalate immediately to interventional cardiology; a generalist should never manage STEMI in isolation.

### Distinguish NSTEMI and Unstable Angina and Escalate Appropriately

NSTEMI (troponin-positive unstable ischemia) and unstable angina (ischemic symptoms with negative troponin) are managed with early invasive strategy for high-risk features: recurrent ischemia, hemodynamic instability, heart failure, serious arrhythmia, dynamic ECG changes, or elevated risk scores. Low-risk NSTEMI may be managed with an ischemia-guided strategy, but the default for any moderate or high-risk presentation is catheterization within the guideline-defined window. Start dual antiplatelet therapy and anticoagulation early. Identify the patient who is decompensating and move them to a higher level of care; a patient with NSTEMI who develops cardiogenic shock is a mechanical complication or ongoing ischemia until proven otherwise and needs urgent, not elective, intervention.

## Common Traps

### Anchoring on a Benign Diagnosis

Discharging a patient labeled as reflux or musculoskeletal pain without excluding the lethal causes. Counter by completing the must-not-miss list and documenting the reasoning that excludes each.

### Trusting a Single Negative Troponin

A single early troponin can be falsely negative because necrosis markers have not yet risen. Counter by using a validated serial high-sensitivity troponin algorithm with a delta criterion.

### Calling STEMI Only When the Pattern Is Textbook

Missing STEMI equivalents and subtle presentations such as posterior MI, right ventricular involvement, hyperacute T waves, and de Winter patterns. Counter by interpreting the ECG actively and obtaining right-sided and posterior leads when indicated.

### Discharging on an Accelerated Protocol Without Meeting All Criteria

Applying a fast-track rule to a moderate or high-risk patient because the score looks borderline. Counter by confirming every protocol criterion and reverting to standard observation if any is unmet.

### Ignoring Pulse and Blood Pressure Differentials

Failing to check for the dissection pattern because the ECG is normal. Counter by examining both arms and both legs, and by escalating to imaging when a differential is present.

### Over-Treating Chronic Troponin Elevations as ACS

Activating the cath lab for a flat elevated troponin in renal failure or sepsis. Counter by interpreting troponin as a delta and by considering type 2 myocardial infarction and non-ischemic injury.

### Delaying STEMI Activation for Confirmatory Tests

Waiting for troponin, echocardiogram, or specialist review before activating the cath lab. Counter by recognizing that ECG plus symptoms is sufficient to activate.

## Self-Check

- Have I assigned and documented a validated risk score (HEART, TIMI, or GRACE) and stated the resulting risk percentage before disposition?
- Did I obtain and personally interpret an ECG within ten minutes, and have I obtained serial ECGs if pain is ongoing?
- Am I interpreting troponin as a delta using an assay-specific validated algorithm, rather than a single value?
- Have I explicitly addressed each must-not-miss diagnosis (ACS, PE, dissection, pneumothorax, esophageal rupture) and documented what excludes it?
- If discharging on an accelerated diagnostic protocol, have I confirmed every criterion is met and provided written return precautions and follow-up?
- For STEMI, did I activate the cath lab immediately without waiting for confirmatory tests?
- For NSTEMI or unstable angina, have I identified high-risk features and arranged early invasive evaluation when indicated?
- Did I escalate to cardiology or intensive care for any hemodynamic instability, heart failure, serious arrhythmia, or diagnostic uncertainty?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
