# Hypertension and Vascular Disease

Blood pressure numbers alone never justify treatment intensity; the decision rests on the presence of end-organ damage, the patient's comorbidities, and the rate of change. The central judgment problem in hypertension is distinguishing a dangerous acute rise that demands intravenous therapy in a monitored bed from an asymptomatic elevation that is best lowered gradually in the outpatient setting. The clinician who treats the number aggressively in a patient without symptoms causes falls, strokes from hypoperfusion, and acute kidney injury; the clinician who treats the number casually in a patient with encephalopathy or dissection kills the patient. This skill covers the disciplined separation of emergency from urgency, comorbidity-driven drug selection, the aortic and peripheral vascular presentations that must not be missed, and the boundaries beyond which a generalist must escalate.

## Core Rules

### Separate Hypertensive Emergency from Urgency by End-Organ Damage, Not by the Number

The defining feature of hypertensive emergency is acute ongoing target-organ damage: encephalopathy, stroke (with caution about permissive hypertension in ischemic stroke), intracerebral or subarachnoid hemorrhage, acute myocardial infarction, acute heart failure or pulmonary edema, aortic dissection, acute renal injury, eclampsia or severe preeclampsia, and microangiopathic hemolytic anemia. The absolute pressure is secondary; a patient at 220/120 with no symptoms and no acute injury is urgency, while a patient at 180/110 with pulmonary edema is emergency. For emergency, admit to a monitored bed and lower pressure with titratable intravenous agents, with specific targets depending on the syndrome (for example, rapid systolic reduction to below 120 for dissection, more gradual reduction for stroke). For urgency, lower gradually with oral agents and close follow-up within days. Never give rapid-acting sublingual agents such as nifedipine or clonidine for asymptomatic elevation; the precipitous drop causes ischemic strokes and falls.

### Lower Pressure Gradually in Chronic Hypertension, with Targets Based on Comorbidity

For chronic hypertension, the goal is durable control over weeks, not hours. Initiate or intensify therapy based on validated office or out-of-office measurements, confirm with home or ambulatory monitoring when feasible, and set targets according to comorbidity. Most adults, including those with diabetes and chronic kidney disease, are targeted to below 130/80 per contemporary guidelines, but individualize for elderly, frail, or postural patients where aggressive targets provoke syncope and falls. Avoid intensifying therapy based on a single elevated reading, especially in the setting of pain, anxiety, or acute illness (reactive hypertension). Reassess after an appropriate interval, confirm adherence and white-coat effect, and adjust one change at a time so the effect of each intervention is observable. Document the target and the rationale.

### Select Antihypertensives by Comorbidity, Not by Convenience

Drug choice should be driven by the patient's comorbidities and demographics, not by habit. First-line agents for uncomplicated hypertension are thiazide-like diuretics, calcium channel blockers, and ACE inhibitors or ARBs; beta-blockers are not first-line in the absence of a cardiac indication. Match therapy to the patient: ACE inhibitor or ARB for proteinuric chronic kidney disease and diabetes with albuminuria; calcium channel blocker or thiazide for black patients in whom renin-angiotensin blockers are less effective as monotherapy; beta-blocker for coronary disease, heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, or rate-dependent atrial fibrillation; mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist for resistant hypertension or primary aldosteronism. Avoid ACE inhibitors and ARBs in pregnancy, in bilateral renal artery stenosis, and in combination with one another. Check renal function and potassium within one to two weeks of starting or titrating a renin-angiotensin blocker. Resistant hypertension, defined as blood pressure above target despite three agents including a diuretic at optimal doses, warrants evaluation for secondary causes.

### Evaluate Secondary Causes When the Picture Is Atypical

Screen for secondary hypertension when the presentation is atypical: onset before thirty or after fifty-five years, resistant hypertension, sudden worsening of long-controlled pressure, hypokalemia suggesting aldosteronism, paroxysmal episodes with headache, palpitations, and sweating suggesting pheochromocytoma, abdominal bruits or flash pulmonary edema suggesting renal artery stenosis, and a renal failure pattern. The common secondary causes are primary aldosteronism (screen with aldosterone-to-renin ratio), renal artery stenosis (especially in fibromuscular dysplasia in young women or atherosclerosis in older patients with vascular disease), obstructive sleep apnea, pheochromocytoma, Cushing syndrome, and thyroid disease. Identify and treat the cause rather than escalating antihypertensive counts. A patient on five agents with uncontrolled pressure has a secondary cause until proven otherwise.

### Recognize Aortic Disease and Treat Dissection as a Time-Critical Emergency

Aortic dissection presents with sudden, tearing chest or back pain, often with a pulse or blood pressure differential between arms, a new aortic regurgitation murmur, neurologic deficits, or syncope. It is one of the most frequently missed lethal diagnoses, in part because the ECG is often normal or nondiagnostic and the presentation overlaps with ACS. Maintain a high index of suspicion in any severe acute chest or back pain, especially in patients with hypertension, connective tissue disease (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos), bicuspid aortic valve, prior aortic surgery, or cocaine use. Obtain computed tomography angiography of the chest and abdomen emergently when dissection is suspected; do not wait for troponin or a cardiology consult to image. While awaiting imaging, control blood pressure and heart rate aggressively with intravenous beta-blockade first (to reduce the rate of pressure change), then vasodilators if needed, because uncontrolled shear propagates the dissection. Type A dissections involving the ascending aorta are surgical emergencies requiring immediate cardiothoracic surgery; type B may be managed medically with endovascular options for complicated cases. A generalist must never manage suspected dissection without immediate surgical and critical care involvement.

### Evaluate Peripheral Arterial Disease and Do Not Dismiss Claudication as Aging

Peripheral arterial disease is both a cause of disability and a marker of systemic atherosclerotic risk. Evaluate claudication with the ankle-brachial index and consider segmental pressures and imaging for atypical or severe symptoms. Critical limb ischemia, defined by rest pain, nonhealing ulcers, or gangrene, is a vascular surgery emergency; do not manage expectantly, because delay risks amputation. Treat all peripheral arterial disease patients with antiplatelet therapy, statin, smoking cessation, glycemic control, and a supervised exercise program. Distinguish arterial from venous disease, and recognize acute limb ischemia (the six Ps: pain, pallor, pulselessness, paresthesia, paralysis, poikilothermia) as a surgical emergency requiring immediate vascular consultation and anticoagulation. Also screen and counsel for the concomitant coronary and cerebrovascular risk that kills these patients.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Number Without End-Organ Assessment

Giving rapid-acting antihypertensives for an asymptomatic high reading. Counter by separating emergency from urgency through evidence of acute end-organ damage.

### Using Sublingual Nifedipine or Rapid Clonidine

Precipitating ischemic stroke, syncope, or falls with a precipitous drop. Counter by using titratable intravenous agents for emergency and oral agents for urgency.

### Intensifying Therapy for Reactive Hypertension

Increasing medications based on a single elevated reading during pain, anxiety, or acute illness. Counter by confirming sustained elevation with repeat and out-of-office measurements.

### Defaulting to the Same Drug for Every Patient

Ignoring comorbidity-driven selection that improves outcomes. Counter by matching drug class to the patient's cardiac, renal, diabetic, and demographic profile.

### Missing Secondary Hypertension in Resistant or Atypical Cases

Escalating drug counts without investigating aldosteronism, renal artery stenosis, or sleep apnea. Counter by screening for secondary causes when the presentation is atypical or resistant.

### Anchoring on ACS When the Cause Is Dissection

Treating chest pain as myocardial infarction because the ECG is abnormal while missing a pulse differential. Counter by examining both arms and considering dissection in every severe acute chest or back pain.

### Managing Critical Limb Ischemia Expectantly

Delaying vascular referral for rest pain or nonhealing ulcers. Counter by recognizing critical limb ischemia as a surgical emergency.

## Self-Check

- Have I classified this presentation as hypertensive emergency or urgency based on acute end-organ damage rather than the absolute pressure?
- For emergency, am I using titratable intravenous agents in a monitored bed with a syndrome-specific target?
- For urgency or chronic elevation, am I lowering pressure gradually with oral therapy and close follow-up, and avoiding rapid-acting sublingual agents?
- Is my drug selection driven by comorbidity (diabetes, CKD, heart failure, coronary disease, race, pregnancy) rather than habit?
- Have I screened for secondary causes when the presentation is early-onset, resistant, sudden-worsening, or accompanied by hypokalemia, paroxysms, or bruits?
- For suspected aortic dissection, did I obtain CTA emergently, control rate and pressure with beta-blockade first, and involve cardiothoracic surgery for type A?
- For peripheral arterial disease, have I assessed the ankle-brachial index, recognized critical limb ischemia as a surgical emergency, and treated systemic atherosclerotic risk?
- Have I escalated to cardiology, vascular surgery, nephrology, or endocrinology for emergencies, resistant or secondary hypertension, and acute limb or aortic events?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
