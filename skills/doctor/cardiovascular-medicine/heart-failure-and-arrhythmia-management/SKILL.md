# Heart Failure and Arrhythmia Management

Heart failure and arrhythmia management sit at the intersection of chronic optimization and acute stabilization, and the central judgment problem is deciding when to treat aggressively and when to stabilize the patient long enough to think. The danger is twofold: under-treating chronic heart failure leaves patients readmitting and dying prematurely from a disease with proven mortality-reducing therapy, while over-aggressive acute management of decompensation or arrhythmia can precipitate collapse through hypotension, over-diuresis, or proarrhythmia. A clinician must hold the chronic and acute frames simultaneously, recognize decompensation triggers, choose rate versus rhythm control deliberately, and know exactly when the situation exceeds generalist management and requires cardiology or electrophysiology. This skill covers the disciplined initiation and titration of guideline-directed therapy, decompensation workup, arrhythmia stabilization, and the escalation thresholds that protect patients.

## Core Rules

### Optimize Chronic Heart Failure with the Full Quadruple Therapy, Not Piecemeal

For heart failure with reduced ejection fraction, guideline-directed medical therapy is no longer optional, and the four pillars (beta-blocker, ACE inhibitor or ARB or angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist, and sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitor) each independently reduce mortality. The common failure is initiating one agent and never adding the others, or titrating timidly and never reaching target doses. Aim to initiate all four classes early, even in low doses, then up-titrate systematically, because the survival benefit comes from being on all classes at tolerated doses, not from maxing one before starting the next. For each agent, check the contraindications: symptomatic hypotension, acute decompensation, hyperkalemia for mineralocorticoid antagonists, and eGFR thresholds for SGLT2 inhibitors. For heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, the evidence base is weaker; focus on symptom control, diuresis, treatment of hypertension and comorbidities, and SGLT2 inhibitors where appropriate. Never declare heart failure optimized without confirming all tolerated pillars are in place.

### Identify and Reverse Decompensation Triggers Before Adjusting Diuretics

Acute decompensation is rarely random; it almost always has a trigger, and treating the edema without finding the trigger guarantees readmission. The common triggers are ischemia, arrhythmia (especially new or rapid atrial fibrillation), infection (including occult pneumonia or endocarditis), non-adherence to medications or diet, anemia, thyroid disease, renal dysfunction, and offending drugs (NSAIDs, calcium channel blockers, thiazolidinediones, some antiarrhythmics). Work up every admission with these in mind, obtain an ECG, troponin where indicated, BNP or NT-proBNP, renal panel, thyroid function, and a search for infection. Only after the trigger is identified and addressed should you focus on volume management. Patients sent home with a diuretic tweak but no trigger workup return within weeks. Document the trigger explicitly in the discharge summary.

### Manage Volume with Diuretics, Not with Vasodilators Alone

For acute decompensation with congestion, intravenous loop diuretics are the cornerstone, and the dose should be one to two and a half times the chronic oral dose given intravenously, with reassessment of urine output within two to six hours and dose escalation if the response is inadequate. Adding a thiazide (metolazone or intravenous chlorothiazide) produces sequential nephron blockade for diuretic resistance, but it carries a high risk of electrolyte disturbance and over-diuresis, so monitor potassium, magnesium, sodium, renal function, and volume status closely. Vasodilators such as nitrates help when blood pressure is adequate and there is concomitant ischemia or severe hypertension, but they are adjuncts, not primary therapy, and they are harmful in low output states. Low-dose dopamine or dobutamine is reserved for patients with hypotension or worsening renal function limiting diuresis, and it pushes the patient into a higher-acuity tier requiring monitoring. Avoid over-diuresis: a patient who becomes hypotensive, azotemic, or symptomatic from volume depletion has been pushed past their dry weight.

### Decide Rate Versus Rhythm Control Deliberately for Atrial Fibrillation

For atrial fibrillation, the choice between rate and rhythm control is a clinical decision that should be made explicitly, not by default. Rate control with a beta-blocker or non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker is appropriate for many patients, especially older patients with longstanding atrial fibrillation and minimal symptoms. Rhythm control (cardioversion, antiarrhythmics, or ablation) is preferred for younger patients, those with new-onset atrial fibrillation, those with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction where restoring sinus rhythm may improve output, and those who remain symptomatic despite adequate rate control. The rhythm-control window matters: early rhythm control within the first year appears to improve outcomes. Always assess thromboembolic risk with CHA2DS2-VASc and bleeding risk with HAS-BLED, and anticoagulate accordingly. Do not cardiovert without either adequate prior anticoagulation, confirmed absence of atrial thrombus on transesophageal echocardiogram, or acceptance of the thromboembolic risk. For new atrial fibrillation, search for reversible causes including thyroid disease, alcohol, infection, and electrolyte disturbance.

### Use a Structured Algorithm for Unstable Arrhythmias

Instability redefines the arrhythmia decision: if the patient is hypotensive, acutely altered, in heart failure, or has ischemic chest pain, the rhythm must be terminated, and synchronized cardioversion is the first-line action. For a regular narrow-complex tachycardia, try vagal maneuvers then adenosine if unstable features are not yet present; for atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response and instability, synchronized cardioversion. For stable monomorphic ventricular tachycardia, amiodarone or procainamide; for polymorphic VT or pulseless VT and ventricular fibrillation, follow the cardiac arrest algorithm with defibrillation. For bradycardia causing symptoms or hemodynamic compromise, atropine is first-line, followed by transcutaneous then transvenous pacing and an isoproterenol or dopamine infusion as bridges. Do not spend time on diagnostic elegance when the patient is unstable; restore perfusion first, then diagnose. Escalate to intensive care and electrophysiology for any sustained ventricular arrhythmia, high-degree AV block requiring pacing, or refractory arrhythmia.

### Recognize When a Device Is Indicated and Escalate to Electrophysiology

Implantable cardioverter-defibrillators reduce mortality in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction when the ejection fraction remains below guideline thresholds despite optimal medical therapy for the mandated waiting period, and cardiac resynchronization therapy helps selected patients with wide QRS and low ejection fraction. Generalists must identify candidates and refer, not manage device decisions. Refer any patient with a reduced ejection fraction persisting below the threshold despite optimized therapy, any survivor of sudden cardiac arrest not due to a reversible cause, any sustained ventricular arrhythmia, and any high-degree AV block or symptomatic bradycardia. Also recognize device malfunction: inappropriate shocks, failure to pace, and pocket infection all require urgent electrophysiology evaluation. Do not attempt to reprogram or interrogate a device based on generalist judgment alone.

## Common Traps

### Initiating One Pillar and Forgetting the Rest

Treating heart failure as monotherapy rather than quadruple therapy. Counter by tracking all four pillars at every visit and up-titrating systematically.

### Diuresing Without Searching for a Decompensation Trigger

Sending a patient home after fluid removal without identifying ischemia, arrhythmia, infection, or non-adherence. Counter by working up and documenting a trigger for every admission.

### Over-Diuresing into Hypotension and Renal Injury

Pushing diuretics past dry weight because the patient still looks edematous. Counter by tracking symptoms, orthostatic signs, renal function, and stopping when the patient reaches euvolemia.

### Defaulting to Rate Control Without Considering Rhythm

Choosing rate control reflexively in a young symptomatic patient who would benefit from rhythm control. Counter by making the rate-versus-rhythm decision explicitly and weighing early rhythm control.

### Cardioverting Without Addressing Thromboembolic Risk

Cardioverting atrial fibrillation without confirming adequate anticoagulation or excluding thrombus. Counter by applying CHA2DS2-VASc and the anticoagulation-duration rule before any cardioversion.

### Delaying Cardioversion in an Unstable Patient

Pursuing diagnostic workup or pharmacologic rate control when the patient is hypotensive or in heart failure. Counter by recognizing instability and proceeding to synchronized cardioversion.

### Managing Ventricular Arrhythmias in Isolation

Treating sustained VT with medication alone without electrophysiology and device evaluation. Counter by escalating every sustained ventricular arrhythmia to specialist care.

## Self-Check

- For HFrEF, have I confirmed that all four tolerated pillars of guideline-directed medical therapy are initiated and titrated toward target, not just one agent?
- For every decompensation, have I identified and documented a trigger (ischemia, arrhythmia, infection, non-adherence, anemia, thyroid, offending drug) rather than only adjusting diuretics?
- Is my diuretic strategy dose-appropriate (one to two and a half times the oral dose intravenously), with reassessment of output, electrolytes, renal function, and volume status?
- For atrial fibrillation, have I made an explicit rate-versus-rhythm decision and assessed CHA2DS2-VASc and HAS-BLED for anticoagulation?
- For any unstable arrhythmia, did I prioritize synchronized cardioversion or the arrest algorithm over diagnostic workup?
- Have I referred appropriate patients to electrophysiology for ICD, CRT, or ablation evaluation rather than managing device decisions alone?
- Have I escalated to cardiology or intensive care for hypotension, low-output state, sustained ventricular arrhythmia, high-degree AV block, or refractory congestion?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
