# Pulmonary Embolism and Venous Thromboembolism

Venous thromboembolism is a diagnosis where the cognitive errors are well-described and the tools to prevent them are well-validated, yet PE is still missed and over-diagnosed in equal measure. The central judgment problem is calibrating pre-test probability so that imaging and D-dimer are used appropriately, then risk-stratifying confirmed PE to match therapy intensity to severity. The danger is two-sided: missing a PE kills the patient through hemodynamic collapse or chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension, while over-investigating every dyspneic patient exposes them to contrast nephropathy, radiation, incidentaloma cascades, and false-positive imaging. The clinician must use validated pre-test probability tools, interpret D-dimer with age-adjustment and pre-test probability, choose the right imaging modality, risk-stratify confirmed PE by hemodynamic status and right ventricular strain, and know when thrombolysis and embolectomy are indicated. This skill covers disciplined VTE workup, PE risk stratification, and the escalation thresholds that protect patients.

## Core Rules

### Use a Validated Pre-Test Probability Tool Before Any Testing

The single most important step in suspected VTE is assigning a pre-test probability using a validated tool, because every subsequent decision flows from it. For PE, use the Wells score (with the simplified or two-tier version) or the revised Geneva score, and apply PERC (Pulmonary Embolism Rule-out Criteria) in low-risk patients to avoid testing altogether. PERC allows safe non-testing when the patient is low-risk, under fifty, with heart rate below 100, oxygen saturation above 94, no hemoptysis, no estrogen use, no prior DVT or PE, no surgery or trauma requiring hospitalization in the past month, and no unilateral leg swelling. If PERC is negative, no further testing is needed; if any criterion fails, proceed with Wells and D-dimer. For DVT, use the Wells DVT score. Estimate pre-test probability deliberately and document it, because the interpretation of every subsequent test depends on it. Do not order a D-dimer or imaging without first assigning a pre-test probability.

### Interpret D-Dimer with Pre-Test Probability and Age Adjustment

D-dimer is a powerful rule-out test in low and moderate pre-test probability, but it is nearly useless in high pre-test probability and in hospitalized or inflammatory states where it is frequently elevated for other reasons. In low and moderate pre-test probability patients, a negative D-dimer excludes VTE and imaging is not needed. Use the age-adjusted threshold (ten times age in micrograms per liter for patients over fifty) in older patients to improve specificity without sacrificing safety, because the standard threshold over-diagnoses and over-images the elderly. Never use a D-dimer to rule out VTE in a high pre-test probability patient; a negative D-dimer in that setting does not exclude PE, and imaging is required. Conversely, do not chase an elevated D-dimer with imaging in a low-risk patient with a clear alternative diagnosis; the false-positive rate is high. Interpret D-dimer as a probability shifter, not a binary diagnostic test.

### Choose the Imaging Modality by Pre-Test Probability, Renal Function, and Pregnancy Status

Computed tomography pulmonary angiography (CTPA) is the first-line imaging for most patients with intermediate or high pre-test probability, because it directly visualizes the clot and identifies alternative diagnoses. Check renal function before CTPA, because contrast nephropathy is a real risk in renal impairment, diabetes, and the elderly. Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scanning is preferred in patients with significant renal impairment, severe contrast allergy, or pregnancy, where the radiation dose to the fetus and maternal breast is lower than CTPA and the diagnostic yield is high when the chest X-ray is normal. In pregnancy, use a perfusion-only scan when feasible to minimize radiation. Lower-extremity compression ultrasound is the first-line test for suspected DVT and is also used in PE workup when CTPA is contraindicated, because finding a DVT in a hemodynamically stable patient confirms VTE and anticoagulation without requiring pulmonary imaging. A negative imaging study in a high pre-test probability patient does not exclude VTE; consider further testing such as serial ultrasound for DVT or alternative imaging.

### Risk-Stratify Confirmed PE by Hemodynamics and Right Ventricular Strain

Once PE is confirmed, the immediate question is severity, because it determines whether the patient needs thrombolysis, embolectomy, or anticoagulation alone. Massive (high-risk) PE is defined by hemodynamic instability: hypotension (systolic below 90 or a drop of at least 40 from baseline), shock, or cardiac arrest, and it carries high mortality and demands immediate reperfusion with systemic thrombolysis, surgical embolectomy, or catheter-directed thrombolysis. Submassive (intermediate-risk) PE is defined by preserved systemic blood pressure with evidence of right ventricular dysfunction (echocardiographic RV dilation, CT RV-to-LV ratio above 0.9, elevated troponin or BNP) and is further divided by risk into intermediate-low and intermediate-high; most are managed with anticoagulation and close monitoring, with thrombolysis reserved for those who deteriorate. Low-risk PE has no hemodynamic compromise and no RV strain and may be managed with anticoagulation, often as an outpatient. Identify the risk tier explicitly and monitor the intermediate-risk patient in a setting capable of detecting deterioration, because progression to hemodynamic collapse is rapid and lethal.

### Initiate Anticoagulation Promptly and Choose the Agent by Stability and Reversibility

Anticoagulation is the cornerstone of PE therapy and should begin as soon as VTE is confirmed or when pre-test probability is high while awaiting imaging. Direct oral anticoagulants (apixaban, rivaroxaban) are first-line for most stable patients with no renal or hepatic contraindication, because they simplify therapy and have favorable bleeding profiles. Low-molecular-weight heparin is preferred in pregnancy, in cancer patients (with extended therapy), in renal impairment where DOACs are problematic, and as a bridge for patients who may need rapid reversal or invasive procedures. Unfractionated heparin is preferred in massive PE where rapid reversal may be needed, in renal failure, and in unstable patients. Avoid DOACs in pregnancy, severe renal impairment, severe hepatic impairment, and antiphospholipid syndrome where warfarin is preferred. Duration of therapy depends on provocation (three months for provoked, extended for unprovoked or recurrent), and cancer-associated VTE requires extended therapy. Address the bleeding risk and drug interactions before prescribing.

### Reserve Thrombolysis and Embolectomy for the Right Patients

Systemic thrombolysis is indicated for massive PE with hemodynamic instability, where it reduces mortality, and should be given without delay once the diagnosis is confirmed and bleeding risk is weighed. For submassive PE, thrombolysis is selective: consider it for patients with intermediate-high risk (significant RV dysfunction and biomarker elevation) who deteriorate clinically, because the bleeding risk, including intracranial hemorrhage, is substantial and the mortality benefit in stable patients is marginal. Catheter-directed thrombolysis and surgical embolectomy are options for patients with contraindications to systemic thrombolysis, failed thrombolysis, or high bleeding risk, and they require immediate specialist involvement. Do not give thrombolysis to a stable low-risk PE; the bleeding risk outweighs the benefit. For every massive PE, escalate immediately to intensive care and involve the pulmonary, hematology, and interventional or surgical teams.

### Consider Pregnancy and Special Populations Explicitly

Pregnancy complicates VTE workup because D-dimer is frequently elevated physiologically and CTPA carries radiation concerns. Use a pre-test probability tool adapted to pregnancy (such as the YEARS algorithm or LEFt rule), use D-dimer selectively, and prefer V/Q scanning or perfusion-only scanning to minimize radiation. Treat with low-molecular-weight heparin throughout pregnancy and for at least six weeks postpartum, and avoid DOACs and warfarin in pregnancy. In cancer patients, use low-molecular-weight heparin or selected DOACs with attention to drug interactions and bleeding risk, and plan for extended therapy. In renal failure, adjust anticoagulant choice and dose, and prefer unfractionated heparin or low-molecular-weight heparin with monitoring. Recognize that these populations need explicit, documented reasoning, because the standard pathway does not apply.

## Common Traps

### Ordering D-Dimer or Imaging Without a Pre-Test Probability

Generating incidental findings and false positives without a framework. Counter by always assigning and documenting Wells, Geneva, or PERC first.

### Using D-Dimer to Rule Out in High Pre-Test Probability

Accepting a negative D-dimer in a high-risk patient and discharging. Counter by treating high pre-test probability as imaging-mandatory regardless of D-dimer.

### Ignoring the Age-Adjusted D-Dimer Threshold

Over-imaging older patients because the standard threshold is rarely normal. Counter by using the age-adjusted cutoff in patients over fifty.

### Defaulting to CTPA in Pregnancy or Renal Failure

Exposing the fetus or kidneys to unnecessary contrast. Counter by preferring V/Q or perfusion-only scanning in pregnancy and compression ultrasound plus alternative imaging in renal impairment.

### Failing to Risk-Stratify Confirmed PE

Treating every PE the same, missing the submassive patient who deteriorates. Counter by assessing hemodynamics and RV strain and assigning a risk tier explicitly.

### Giving Thrombolysis to a Stable Low-Risk PE

Exposing the patient to intracranial hemorrhage without benefit. Counter by reserving thrombolysis for massive PE and selected deteriorating submassive PE.

### Choosing the Wrong Anticoagulant for the Population

Prescribing DOACs in pregnancy or severe renal impairment, or LMWH without considering cancer. Counter by matching the agent to stability, renal function, pregnancy, cancer, and reversal needs.

## Self-Check

- Have I assigned and documented a validated pre-test probability (Wells, Geneva, or PERC) before ordering any test?
- Am I interpreting D-dimer with pre-test probability and the age-adjusted threshold, and not using it to rule out in high-risk patients?
- Have I chosen the imaging modality by pre-test probability, renal function, and pregnancy status, preferring V/Q in pregnancy and ultrasound-first for DVT?
- For confirmed PE, have I risk-stratified by hemodynamics and right ventricular strain and assigned a tier (massive, submassive, low-risk)?
- Have I initiated anticoagulation promptly and chosen the agent by stability, renal function, pregnancy, cancer, and reversal needs?
- For massive PE, did I administer thrombolysis or arrange embolectomy without delay, and for submassive PE, am I monitoring in a setting capable of detecting deterioration?
- Have I escalated to intensive care and involved pulmonary, hematology, and interventional or surgical teams for massive PE and selected submassive PE?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
