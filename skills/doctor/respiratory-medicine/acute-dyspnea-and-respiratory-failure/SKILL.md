# Acute Dyspnea and Respiratory Failure

Acute dyspnea is a symptom, not a diagnosis, and the central judgment problem is deciding how fast the patient is failing and what support will keep them alive long enough to find the cause. The danger is two-sided: delaying ventilatory support in a tiring patient leads to arrest, while intubating prematurely exposes a patient who could have been managed noninvasively to sedation, ventilator-associated pneumonia, and prolonged ICU stay. The clinician must classify the failure as hypoxemic or hypercapnic, distinguish cardiac from pulmonary etiology with point-of-care tools, titrate oxygen to a target rather than to maximal flow, and know exactly when noninvasive ventilation is indicated, when it has failed, and when intubation is unavoidable. This skill covers the disciplined workup of acute dyspnea, oxygen and ventilatory support decisions, and the escalation thresholds that protect patients.

## Core Rules

### Classify the Failure as Hypoxemic or Hypercapnic Before Choosing Therapy

Respiratory failure has two physiologic flavors, and the treatment fork depends on which is present. Type 1, hypoxemic failure, is defined by a PaO2 below 60 mmHg on room air and is caused by ventilation-perfusion mismatch or shunt (pneumonia, pulmonary edema, PE, ARDS, atelectasis). Type 2, hypercapnic failure, is defined by a PaO2 below 60 with a PaCO2 above 50 (or a rising PaCO2 with acidosis) and is caused by alveolar hypoventilation (COPD exacerbation, neuromuscular weakness, opiate overdose, obesity hypoventilation). Arterial blood gas analysis is the definitive test when pulse oximetry is unreliable, when hypercapnia is suspected, or when the patient is deteriorating. The distinction matters because the therapies diverge: hypoxemic failure responds to oxygen and positive pressure, while hypercapnic failure requires ventilatory support that increases minute ventilation, often with noninvasive ventilation as first-line. Treat the underlying cause, but choose the support modality based on the failure type.

### Distinguish Cardiac from Pulmonary Causes with Point-of-Care Tools

The single most useful early step in undifferentiated dyspnea is to separate cardiac from pulmonary etiology, because it determines therapy within minutes. Use point-of-care lung ultrasound (B-lines for pulmonary edema, A-lines with sliding lung for normal aeration, absent sliding for pneumothorax), focused cardiac ultrasound (ejection fraction, right ventricular strain, pericardial effusion), the venous exam for volume status, and BNP or NT-proBNP where available. A patient with bilateral B-lines, reduced ejection fraction, and elevated BNP has cardiogenic pulmonary edema and needs diuresis and positive pressure; a patient with A-lines, fever, and consolidation has pneumonia and needs antibiotics and oxygen. Do not wait for a formal echocardiogram or chest CT to begin syndrome-directed therapy when point-of-care findings are clear. Reassess after each intervention; if the response is inconsistent with the working diagnosis, revisit the differential (PE, tamponade, pneumothorax, sepsis).

### Titrate Oxygen to a Target Saturation, Not to Maximal Flow

Oxygen is a drug with a therapeutic window, and hyperoxia causes harm. Target saturations are 94 to 98 percent for most adults and 88 to 92 percent for patients with chronic hypercapnia such as severe COPD, in whom excessive oxygen worsens hypercapnia through the Haldane effect and ventilation-perfusion mismatch. Start with the lowest device and flow that achieves the target: nasal cannula, then simple face mask, then high-flow nasal cannula for moderate hypoxemia, then positive pressure for refractory hypoxemia or hypercapnia. Recheck the saturation and work of breathing within minutes, and adjust downward when the target is exceeded. Never leave a patient on high-flow oxygen without a documented target and a reassessment plan. Hyperoxia in stroke, MI, and COPD is associated with worse outcomes; give oxygen only when hypoxic, not reflexively to every dyspneic patient.

### Use Noninvasive Ventilation for the Right Indications and Recognize When It Fails

Noninvasive ventilation (NIV), including continuous positive airway pressure and bilevel positive airway pressure, is first-line for several specific indications: COPD exacerbation with respiratory acidosis (pH below 7.35 with PaCO2 above 45), cardiogenic pulmonary edema, and immunocompromised patients with hypoxemic respiratory failure in whom intubation carries high infection risk. It is also used as a trial in selected cases of mild ARDS and post-extubation support. Bilevel is preferred when ventilatory support is needed (hypercapnia); CPAP is preferred for pure oxygenation failure such as pulmonary edema. NIV fails when the patient cannot protect the airway, cannot clear secretions, is hemodynamically unstable, has altered mental status (with the exception of COPD with hypercapnic encephalopathy, where NIV may improve consciousness), has facial trauma or anatomy precluding a mask, or fails to improve within the first one to two hours. Recognizing NIV failure early is critical; a patient who is tiring, whose blood gas is worsening, or whose mental status is declining despite NIV must be intubated, not escalated to higher NIV settings indefinitely.

### Decide on Intubation Before the Patient Arrests

Intubation is a controlled decision, not a rescue maneuver, and the worst time to intubate is during cardiac arrest. The classic indications are failure to maintain oxygenation or ventilation despite optimal noninvasive support, inability to protect the airway, hemodynamic instability, altered mental status with loss of airway reflexes, and the anticipated clinical course (a patient who is clearly tiring or whose trajectory is worsening). Prepare thoroughly before the procedure: preoxygenate, assemble equipment and backup airway devices, ensure vascular access and monitoring, have vasopressors ready because induction commonly precipitates hypotension in the dyspneic, volume-depleted patient. Choose the induction and paralytic agents with the hemodynamic picture in mind (ketamine or etomidate for unstable patients; avoid propofol in shock). After intubation, set the ventilator to the underlying pathology: low tidal volume lung-protective ventilation for ARDS, attention to auto-PEEP in obstructive disease, and permissive hypercapnia where appropriate. Escalate to intensive care for every intubated patient and for any patient on high-flow oxygen or NIV who may deteriorate.

### Identify and Treat the Specific Cause While Supporting the Patient

Supportive therapy buys time, but definitive therapy treats the cause, and the workup must proceed in parallel with stabilization. Pursue the diagnosis with chest X-ray, blood gas, ECG, troponin and BNP where indicated, complete blood count, cultures before antibiotics in infection, D-dimer and CTPA when PE is suspected, and CT when the chest X-ray is nondiagnostic. Begin syndrome-directed therapy early: antibiotics and oxygen for pneumonia, diuretics and nitrates for pulmonary edema, bronchodilators and steroids for COPD or asthma, anticoagulation for PE, and reversal agents for opiate or benzodiazepine toxicity. Reassess the patient continuously; the patient who is not improving within the expected timeframe for the working diagnosis must be re-examined and the differential revisited. A patient labeled with pneumonia who is not improving may have PE, heart failure, empyema, or ARDS.

## Common Traps

### Giving High-Flow Oxygen to a Chronic CO2 Retainer

Worsening hypercapnia and somnolence from the Haldane effect and worsened ventilation-perfusion matching. Counter by targeting 88 to 92 percent in known or suspected chronic hypercapnia and checking a blood gas.

### Treating Dyspnea Without Classifying the Failure Type

Applying oxygen to a hypercapnic patient who needs ventilatory support. Counter by obtaining an arterial blood gas when hypercapnia is possible or the patient is deteriorating.

### Waiting for Formal Studies Before Syndrome-Directed Therapy

Delaying diuresis, antibiotics, or NIV while awaiting echocardiogram or CT. Counter by using point-of-care ultrasound and clinical assessment to begin therapy within minutes.

### Continuing NIV Past the Point of Failure

Persisting with escalating NIV settings while the patient tires and arrests. Counter by setting an explicit reassessment window and intubating when NIV is not working.

### Intubating as an Emergency Rather Than a Planned Procedure

Attempting intubation only after arrest, with no preparation or hemodynamic optimization. Counter by recognizing the trajectory and intubating in a controlled fashion with full preparation.

### Anchoring on the Initial Diagnosis When the Patient Fails to Improve

Persisting with pneumonia treatment when the cause is PE or heart failure. Counter by revisiting the differential whenever the course deviates from expectation.

### Reflexively Oxygenating Normoxic Patients

Giving supplemental oxygen to dyspneic patients with normal saturations. Counter by treating oxygen as a targeted therapy, not a comfort measure.

## Self-Check

- Have I classified the respiratory failure as hypoxemic or hypercapnic using blood gas when indicated, and chosen support accordingly?
- Have I used point-of-care lung and cardiac ultrasound plus BNP to distinguish cardiac from pulmonary causes within minutes of presentation?
- Am I titrating oxygen to a documented target saturation (94-98 percent, or 88-92 percent in chronic hypercapnia) rather than giving maximal flow?
- For COPD acidosis or cardiogenic pulmonary edema, have I initiated NIV promptly, and have I set an explicit reassessment window to detect failure?
- Have I identified the indications for intubation early, prepared thoroughly, and chosen induction agents appropriate to the hemodynamic picture?
- Am I pursuing the definitive cause in parallel with supportive therapy, and revisiting the differential when the patient does not improve as expected?
- Have I escalated to intensive care for every intubated patient and for any patient on high-flow oxygen or NIV at risk of deterioration?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
