# COPD, Asthma, and Obstructive Disease

Obstructive lung disease exacerbations are common, and the central judgment problem is matching therapy intensity to severity while recognizing the patient who is about to collapse. The danger is two-sided: under-treating a severe exacerbation allows fatigue and hypercapnia to progress to arrest, while over-treating a mild exacerbation with aggressive therapy adds harm without benefit. The clinician must distinguish COPD from asthma because the management and prognosis differ, gauge exacerbation severity objectively, escalate through bronchodilators, steroids, and noninvasive ventilation in a deliberate sequence, and recognize the life-threatening features that demand immediate intensive care. This skill covers the disciplined assessment and management of obstructive disease exacerbations, the avoidance of iatrogenic harm such as auto-PEEP, and the escalation thresholds that protect patients.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish COPD from Asthma Because the Management Forks

Although both are obstructive diseases, COPD and asthma differ in onset, reversibility, and natural history, and confusing them leads to wrong therapy. COPD is typically a disease of older patients with significant smoking history, characterized by incompletely reversible airflow obstruction and a course of progressive decline punctuated by exacerbations. Asthma is typically a disease of younger patients or those with atopy, characterized by fully reversible obstruction (often with marked variability) and a course of exacerbations interspersed with normal lung function. In an exacerbation, both receive bronchodilators and steroids, but the details differ: COPD exacerbations are driven by infection or irritants and benefit from antibiotics when sputum purulence increases, while asthma exacerbations are driven by triggers such as allergens, infection, or non-adherence with controller therapy and demand aggressive bronchodilation and early steroids. COPD patients require careful oxygen titration to avoid hypercapnia; asthma patients in status asthmaticus require attention to dynamic hyperinflation and auto-PEEP. Confirm the diagnosis with spirometry when the patient is stable, but in the acute setting, treat the exacerbation based on clinical severity.

### Gauge Exacerbation Severity Objectively, Not by Impression

Severity drives therapy intensity, so measure it rather than estimate it. For COPD, assess the respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, use of accessory muscles, ability to speak, mental status, and arterial blood gas when hypercapnia is suspected; the decompensation is significant when there is respiratory acidosis (pH below 7.35), and severe when pH falls below 7.25 or the patient is tiring. For asthma, assess the peak expiratory flow or forced expiratory volume, ability to speak in full sentences, respiratory rate, heart rate, oxygen saturation, use of accessory muscles, and mental status; a peak flow below 33 percent of predicted or best, or any of silent chest, cyanosis, exhaustion, bradycardia, or altered consciousness, indicates a life-threatening attack requiring immediate critical care. Do not be reassured by a normal or rising PaCO2 in asthma, because a rising PaCO2 in a tachypneic asthmatic signals fatigue and impending arrest. Assign the severity explicitly and document it, because the trajectory and the threshold for escalation depend on the baseline.

### Deliver Bronchodilators and Steroids in a Severity-Matched Sequence

Short-acting bronchodilators are the foundation of any obstructive exacerbation: salbutamol (albuterol) and ipratropium, delivered by nebulizer or metered-dose inhaler with spacer, repeated as needed and, for severe exacerbations, continuous. Systemic corticosteroids reduce recovery time, prevent relapse, and in COPD reduce the duration of the exacerbation; give them early, in adequate dose (prednisone equivalent of 40 to 50 mg daily for five to seven days in COPD, longer in asthma). For COPD with increased sputum purulence, add antibiotics covering typical pathogens (Haemophilus influenzae, Streptococcus pneumoniae, Moraxella catarrhalis). For severe asthma, add intravenous magnesium sulfate for patients who do not respond to initial bronchodilators and consider intravenous bronchodilators such as salbutamol. Avoid sedatives and anxiolytics, which depress respiration, and avoid high concentrations of oxygen in COPD. Match therapy to severity, reassess at defined intervals, and escalate when the response is inadequate.

### Use Noninvasive Ventigation as First-Line for COPD Acidosis

For COPD exacerbation with respiratory acidosis (pH below 7.35 with PaCO2 above 45), bilevel NIV is first-line and reduces intubation rates and mortality. Initiate it early, before the patient tires, with appropriate settings (inspiratory pressure to reduce work of breathing, expiratory pressure to overcome intrinsic PEEP and improve triggering), and titrate based on blood gas and comfort. NIV is also useful in hypercapnic patients who are not yet acidotic but are tiring, and as a bridge in selected patients who decline intubation. NIV is generally not first-line for asthma, where the problem is severe bronchoconstriction and dynamic hyperinflation rather than alveolar hypoventilation, though it may be tried in selected patients with close monitoring. Recognize NIV failure: worsening acidosis, worsening mental status, hemodynamic instability, or inability to tolerate the mask mandate intubation. Do not persist with escalating NIV indefinitely in a deteriorating patient.

### Recognize and Manage Auto-PEEP and Dynamic Hyperinflation

In severe obstructive disease, particularly asthma and COPD on mechanical ventilation, the patient cannot fully exhale before the next breath, trapping air and generating intrinsic PEEP (auto-PEEP). This causes hypotension (from reduced venous return) and barotrauma (pneumothorax), and can be fatal. Recognize auto-PEEP clinically by a rising plateau pressure, hypotension that improves when ventilation is paused, and difficulty triggering the ventilator. Manage it by reducing minute ventilation (lower tidal volume and respiratory rate), increasing expiratory time (longer expiratory time, lower inspiratory-to-expiratory ratio), ensuring adequate bronchodilation, and sometimes deep sedation and paralysis to allow patient-ventilator synchrony. In an asthmatic who arrests, disconnect the circuit briefly to allow full exhalation and reduce intrathoracic pressure. Auto-PEEP is one of the most dangerous iatrogenic complications of ventilating obstructive disease, and every clinician managing these patients must understand it.

### Apply Discharge Criteria and Action Plans to Prevent Relapse

Discharging a patient before they are stable invites relapse and readmission. For COPD, the patient should be clinically stable with bronchodilator use no more than every four to six hours, oxygenation near baseline, ability to ambulate and perform activities of daily living, and an understanding of medications and inhaler technique. For asthma, the patient should have minimal symptoms, peak flow above 70 percent of predicted or personal best, and a stable trajectory for at least twelve hours after the last bronchodilator. Before discharge, address the trigger (smoking cessation, allergen control, treatment of comorbidities), optimize controller therapy (inhaled corticosteroid and long-acting bronchodilator for asthma; dual or triple therapy for COPD), provide a written action plan with clear thresholds for escalation, ensure inhaler technique is correct, and arrange follow-up within days. Relapse after discharge is common; the action plan and follow-up are not optional.

## Common Traps

### Confusing COPD and Asthma

Applying COPD-style antibiotic and oxygen strategies to asthma, or vice versa. Counter by establishing the diagnosis from history, spirometry when stable, and the clinical picture.

### Estimating Severity by Impression

Under-treating a severe exacerbation because the patient looks better than the blood gas suggests. Counter by measuring peak flow, blood gas, and severity markers explicitly.

### Over-Oxygenating a COPD Patient

Worsening hypercapnia from excessive oxygen. Counter by targeting 88 to 92 percent saturation and checking a blood gas.

### Withholding or Under-Dosing Steroids

Giving subtherapeutic steroid doses or omitting them due to fear of side effects. Counter by giving early adequate systemic steroids for the full recommended duration.

### Persisting with NIV Past Failure

Continuing to escalate NIV in a deteriorating patient until arrest. Counter by setting an explicit reassessment window and intubating when NIV fails.

### Ignoring Auto-PEEP in the Ventilated Obstructive Patient

Failing to recognize hypotension from intrathoracic pressure and continuing high minute ventilation. Counter by monitoring plateau pressure and adjusting ventilator settings to allow full exhalation.

### Discharging Without an Action Plan or Follow-Up

Sending a patient home without addressing triggers, optimizing controller therapy, or arranging follow-up. Counter by completing discharge criteria and providing a written action plan before discharge.

## Self-Check

- Have I distinguished COPD from asthma based on history, reversibility, and spirometry when stable, and tailored therapy accordingly?
- Have I measured exacerbation severity objectively (peak flow, blood gas, vital signs, mental status) rather than by impression?
- Am I delivering bronchodilators and steroids in severity-matched doses, and adding antibiotics for COPD with purulent sputum?
- For COPD with respiratory acidosis, have I initiated bilevel NIV early and set an explicit reassessment window to detect failure?
- For the ventilated obstructive patient, am I monitoring for auto-PEEP and adjusting settings to allow full exhalation?
- Before discharge, have I confirmed clinical stability, optimized controller therapy, verified inhaler technique, provided a written action plan, and arranged follow-up?
- Have I escalated to intensive care for life-threatening features (silent chest, exhaustion, altered mental status, rising PaCO2 in asthma, severe acidosis in COPD)?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
