# Fracture and Dislocation Management

Fractures and dislocations are common, and most are managed successfully with reduction and immobilization, but a small number of errors cause permanent harm: missing compartment syndrome, missing a neurovascular injury, mishandling an open fracture, or treating a pediatric growth plate injury as a simple sprain. The recurring failure is treating the radiograph as the diagnosis rather than assessing the limb as a living structure with a vascular and neurologic supply that can be lost. This skill covers fracture description and classification, reduction and immobilization principles, open fracture emergency management, pediatric growth plate injuries, the ORIF decision, and the recognition and management of compartment syndrome and neurovascular compromise.

## Core Rules

### Describe and Classify Fractures Systematically Before Acting

A precise fracture description guides management and communication. Describe the bone and location (diaphyseal, metaphyseal, epiphyseal, intra-articular), the pattern (transverse, oblique, spiral, comminuted, segmental), displacement (translated, angulated, shortened, rotated), alignment and apposition, whether it is open or closed, and whether it is intra-articular (which demands anatomic reduction to prevent post-traumatic arthritis). State the soft tissue and neurovascular status explicitly. A spiral fracture in a non-ambulatory child raises non-accidental injury; a fracture in an older person after a low-energy fall raises osteoporosis and fragility fracture risk. The description is not bureaucratic — it determines whether reduction is needed, whether surgery is indicated, and what to watch for.

### Apply Reduction and Immobilization Principles and Reassess Neurovascular Status

Reduction restores alignment and length; immobilization maintains it while healing. Reduce displaced fractures promptly (with adequate analgesia and sedation where appropriate), using the principles of recreating the deformity, applying traction, and restoring alignment, then immobilize with a well-padded splint or cast that holds the reduction without causing pressure. After any reduction or immobilization, reassess and document neurovascular status (distal pulses, capillary refill, sensation, motor function) immediately and at intervals, because swelling under a cast can compromise circulation. Split casts in the acute setting to allow for swelling, and elevate the limb. Provide clear advice on the signs of tightness or neurovascular compromise (increasing pain, pallor, paresthesia, paralysis, poikilothermia) and the need for urgent review.

### Manage Open Fractures as Emergencies With Irrigation, Antibiotics, and Tetanus

An open fracture communicates with the outside world and carries a high risk of deep infection and osteomyelitis, which can be limb-threatening. Cover the wound with a sterile saline-soaked dressing, immobilize the fracture, do not probe or aggressively irrigate the wound in the emergency setting (definitive debridement is in the operating room), and give intravenous antibiotics immediately covering gram-positive organisms (and additional coverage for severe contamination, including gram-negatives and anaerobes). Check tetanus status and immunize as needed. Arrange urgent orthopedic referral for surgical debridement and stabilization, typically within hours. Grade the open fracture by soft tissue damage to guide management and prognosis. Do not close an open fracture wound primarily in the emergency setting; definitive closure is a surgical decision.

### Use Salter-Harris Classification for Pediatric Growth Plate Injuries

In children, the growth plate (physis) is weaker than bone and ligament, so injuries that would sprain a ligament in an adult often fracture the physis. The Salter-Harris classification (I through V) describes the pattern and predicts prognosis: type I and II generally do well, type III and IV are intra-articular and risk growth disturbance and joint incongruity and usually need anatomic reduction (often surgical), and type V is a compression injury with a high risk of growth arrest that may not be apparent initially. Suspect a physeal injury in any child with point tenderness at the growth plate after injury, even if the radiograph looks normal, because non-displaced type I and V injuries may be radiographically occult. Refer pediatric physeal injuries, especially displaced or intra-articular, to orthopedics, because growth disturbance and deformity can follow inadequate management.

### Decide When ORIF Is Indicated

Open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) is indicated when anatomic reduction cannot be achieved or maintained conservatively, when the fracture is intra-articular and joint congruity matters, when the fracture is unstable or severely comminuted, when there is neurovascular compromise requiring exploration, when conservative management would require prolonged immobilization harmful to function (such as in elderly proximal femur fractures), and in polytrauma to allow early mobilization and care. The decision balances fracture factors, patient factors (age, activity, comorbidities, bone quality), and the risks of surgery (infection, hardware failure, anesthesia). Involve orthopedics early for any fracture that may need surgery, and document the reasoning for operative versus conservative management.

### Recognize and Act on Compartment Syndrome Without Delay

Compartment syndrome is the must-not-miss emergency of fracture care: raised pressure within a fascial compartment compromises perfusion, causing irreversible muscle and nerve necrosis within hours. The cardinal early sign is pain out of proportion to the injury and pain on passive stretch of the muscles in the compartment, often with paresthesia; pulselessness is a late sign and its presence is not required to diagnose compartment syndrome. High-risk injuries include tibial shaft fractures, forearm fractures, high-energy extremity trauma, crush injuries, burns, and tight casts or dressings. Suspect it, measure compartment pressures if available, and treat with emergent fasciotomy — do not wait for the full classic picture or for pulselessness. Removing a tight cast or dressing is an immediate first step, but suspected compartment syndrome needs urgent orthopedic assessment for fasciotomy. Missing compartment syndrome causes Volkmann ischemic contracture and permanent disability.

### Document and Reassess Neurovascular Status at Every Encounter

Neurovascular compromise converts a recoverable injury into a permanent deficit. Before and after reduction, after immobilization, and at every review, document distal pulses, capillary refill, light touch sensation, and motor function of the relevant nerves (for example, radial, median, ulnar in the upper limb; peroneal, tibial in the lower limb). Any deterioration demands immediate action: loosen the cast or dressing, reassess, and escalate to orthopedics. Specific injuries carry specific risks (posterior tibial artery in ankle fractures, anterior interosseous and radial nerve in humerus fractures, sciatic in hip dislocation) — know the vascular and neurologic risk of each injury and monitor for it.

## Common Traps

### Waiting for Pulselessness to Diagnose Compartment Syndrome

Pulselessness is a late sign; by the time pulses are lost, necrosis is established. Counter by recognizing pain out of proportion and pain on passive stretch as the early signs and performing fasciotomy without delay.

### Treating a Pediatric Growth Plate Injury as a Sprain

Occult or displaced physeal injuries risk growth disturbance if missed. Counter by suspecting physeal injury with growth-plate tenderness in children and referring, especially for displaced or intra-articular Salter-Harris injuries.

### Closing an Open Fracture Wound Primarily

Primary closure traps contamination and risks deep infection. Counter by covering with sterile dressing, giving antibiotics and tetanus prophylaxis, and referring for surgical debridement and delayed closure.

### Failing to Reassess Neurovascular Status After Reduction or Immobilization

Swelling under a cast can compromise circulation after the patient leaves. Counter by documenting neurovascular status before and after reduction and at intervals, splitting casts for swelling, and giving clear advice to return if symptoms of tightness develop.

### Missing an Intra-Articular Fracture Needing Anatomic Reduction

Joint incongruity causes post-traumatic arthritis. Counter by identifying intra-articular extension on imaging and involving orthopedics for anatomic reduction, often surgical.

### Reassuring a Patient in a Cast With Increasing Pain

Increasing pain under a cast may signal compartment syndrome or pressure injury. Counter by loosening or splitting the cast and reassessing rather than prescribing more analgesia.

### Forgetting Non-Accidental Injury in Pediatric Fractures

Certain fractures (femur in a non-ambulatory infant, spiral long-bone fractures, multiple fractures of different ages) raise non-accidental injury. Counter by recognizing the patterns and following safeguarding protocols.

## Self-Check

- Did I describe the fracture systematically (bone, location, pattern, displacement, open or closed, intra-articular, soft tissue and neurovascular status) before deciding management?
- After reduction and immobilization, did I reassess and document distal pulses, capillary refill, sensation, and motor function, and split the cast for swelling?
- For an open fracture, did I cover the wound, immobilize, give intravenous antibiotics and tetanus prophylaxis, and arrange urgent orthopedic debridement without primary closure?
- For pediatric injuries, did I apply Salter-Harris classification, suspect occult physeal injury with growth-plate tenderness, and refer displaced or intra-articular injuries?
- Did I decide operative versus conservative management using fracture stability, intra-articular involvement, neurovascular compromise, and patient factors, involving orthopedics early?
- Did I recognize compartment syndrome early (pain out of proportion, pain on passive stretch) and arrange emergent fasciotomy without waiting for pulselessness?
- Did I document and reassess neurovascular status at every encounter and escalate any deterioration immediately?
- Did I consider non-accidental injury in pediatric fractures with concerning patterns?
