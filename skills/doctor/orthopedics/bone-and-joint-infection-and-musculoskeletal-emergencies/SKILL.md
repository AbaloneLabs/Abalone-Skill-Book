# Bone and Joint Infection and Musculoskeletal Emergencies

Musculoskeletal and spinal emergencies are dangerous precisely because they masquerade as common, low-acuity complaints — a swollen red leg as cellulitis, a limping child as a minor injury, back pain as a muscle strain. The recurring failure is treating the presentation as routine rather than asking whether this is septic arthritis that will destroy a joint in days, osteomyelitis that will become chronic, a necrotizing infection that will kill in hours, or a cauda equina syndrome that will cause permanent paralysis. This skill covers septic arthritis versus transient synovitis, osteomyelitis, the cellulitis-versus-DVT-versus-necrotizing infection distinction, cauda equina syndrome, and the emergency escalation thresholds.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Septic Arthritis From Transient Synovitis and Apply Kocher Criteria in Children

Septic arthritis is a joint-threatening and life-threatening infection demanding urgent diagnosis and washout, because cartilage is destroyed within hours to days. In a child with a painful joint (often hip or knee), fever, inability to bear weight, and restricted motion, septic arthritis is the leading diagnosis until proven otherwise. The Kocher criteria (fever, inability to bear weight, erythrocyte sedimentation rate above 40, white blood cell count above 12) stratify the probability of septic arthritis in a child with a hip effusion: the more criteria present, the higher the probability, and a high probability mandates joint aspiration and orthopedic involvement. Do not be reassured by a normal temperature or a settled child, because presentation can be subtle. In adults, septic arthritis presents with a hot, swollen, tender, painful joint with markedly restricted movement; aspirate the joint before antibiotics when possible, send synovial fluid for cell count, Gram stain, and culture, and involve orthopedics for washout. Crystals in the fluid do not exclude infection — gout and septic arthritis can coexist.

### Recognize Osteomyelitis Early and Image and Culture Appropriately

Osteomyelitis presents with progressive localized bone pain, swelling, warmth, and systemic features (which may be absent in chronic infection), often with a risk factor (recent bacteremia, diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, intravenous drug use, trauma or surgery, sickle cell disease). Acute hematogenous osteomyelitis is most common in children; contiguous or vascular-insufficiency osteomyelitis is common in adults, particularly diabetic foot infections. Check inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) and blood cultures, and image with MRI (the most sensitive modality, showing marrow and soft tissue changes early) rather than plain radiographs, which are often normal early and only show periosteal reaction or cortical destruction later. Obtain bone biopsy and culture where possible to guide prolonged, targeted antibiotic therapy, and involve orthopedics and infectious diseases. Do not treat suspected osteomyelitis with a short course of oral antibiotics; it requires prolonged targeted therapy and often surgical debridement.

### Differentiate Cellulitis From DVT and Necrotizing Infection

A red, swollen leg is most often cellulitis, but the differential includes deep vein thrombosis and necrotizing fasciitis, and the consequences of misclassification are severe. Cellulitis is a spreading, warm, erythematous, tender area with systemic features, usually unilateral and often with a skin portal of entry; assess for risk factors and treat with appropriate antibiotics. Deep vein thrombosis presents with unilateral swelling (often calf), pain, and warmth without the well-demarcated erythema of cellulitis; use a validated score (Wells) and D-dimer or ultrasound when indicated, because treating DVT as cellulitis risks pulmonary embolism. Necrotizing fasciitis presents with severe pain out of proportion to the visible findings, swelling beyond the erythema, skin changes progressing to bullae, ecchymosis, crepitus, and systemic toxicity with rapid deterioration; it is a surgical emergency demanding immediate debridement, not antibiotics alone. Use the LRINEC score and clinical judgment, but never let a normal-looking early presentation reassure you when pain and toxicity are disproportionate.

### Identify Cauda Equina Syndrome as a Must-Not-Miss Spinal Emergency

Cauda equina syndrome results from compression of the lumbosacral nerve roots and causes permanent paralysis, bladder and bowel dysfunction, and sexual dysfunction if not decompressed urgently. The red flags are severe lower back pain, bilateral sciatica, saddle anesthesia (perineal numbness), urinary retention or incontinence, fecal incontinence, and lower motor neuron leg weakness with absent reflexes. Any patient with back pain plus new urinary retention (assess with bladder scan), saddle anesthesia, or bilateral neurologic signs has cauda equina syndrome until proven otherwise and needs emergency MRI of the lumbosacral spine and urgent neurosurgical or orthopedic spinal assessment for decompression. Do not wait for all features to be present; the window for preserving function is narrow, and incomplete or suspected cauda equina syndrome is treated as an emergency. Document the time of symptom onset and neurologic findings, because they guide surgical urgency and prognosis.

### Apply Emergency Escalation Thresholds for Musculoskeletal and Spinal Emergencies

Define and act on the thresholds for emergency escalation: suspected septic arthritis (joint aspiration and urgent orthopedic washout), suspected osteomyelitis (imaging, cultures, orthopedics and infectious diseases), suspected necrotizing fasciitis (immediate surgical exploration and debridement, do not wait for imaging), suspected cauda equina syndrome (emergency MRI and spinal surgical assessment), compartment syndrome (emergent fasciotomy), and any musculoskeletal presentation with systemic sepsis. For each, state what would confirm or exclude the diagnosis and the action threshold. Accept that over-investigation and over-calling are safer than delay in these conditions, because the harm of missing them is irreversible.

### Take a Directed History for Infection Risk and Red Flags

The history identifies the risk that elevates a routine presentation to an emergency. Ask about diabetes, immunocompromise, intravenous drug use, recent bacteremia or infection, prosthetic joints, recent surgery or injection, trauma, sickle cell disease, and anticoagulation. For back pain, ask about red flags: trauma, age, fever or weight loss, immunosuppression, history of cancer, night pain, recent infection, and the neurologic and sphincter symptoms of cauda equina syndrome. For a red leg, ask about prior cellulitis, venous disease, portals of entry, DVT risk factors (immobility, surgery, malignancy, thrombophilia), and features suggesting necrotizing infection (rapid progression, severe pain, systemic toxicity). The history shapes the differential and the urgency.

### Choose Imaging and Investigations to Match the Suspected Diagnosis

Match investigations to the suspected diagnosis: MRI for suspected osteomyelitis and cauda equina syndrome and to define soft tissue infection; ultrasound for joint effusion and DVT; aspiration and synovial analysis for suspected septic arthritis; blood cultures and inflammatory markers for systemic infection; and plain radiographs for fracture and foreign body. Do not delay antibiotics and surgical referral for septic arthritis or necrotizing infection while waiting for confirmatory imaging, because the diagnosis is often clinical and the harm of delay is irreversible. For suspected cauda equina syndrome, MRI is the definitive imaging and should be obtained emergently.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring a Limping Child With Fever as Transient Synovitis

Septic arthritis destroys a joint rapidly; transient synovitis is a diagnosis of exclusion. Counter by applying Kocher criteria, aspirating a high-probability joint, and involving orthopedics.

### Assuming Crystals in Synovial Fluid Exclude Infection

Gout and septic arthritis can coexist; crystals do not rule out infection. Counter by sending synovial fluid for Gram stain and culture and treating for infection when the clinical picture fits.

### Treating a Red Leg as Cellulitis Without Considering DVT

DVT masquerades as cellulitis and risks pulmonary embolism. Counter by applying the Wells score and ultrasound when DVT is possible.

### Waiting for the Full Classic Picture of Necrotizing Fasciitis

Pain out of proportion with systemic toxicity is an early sign; waiting for bullae and crepitus is too late. Counter by treating clinical suspicion as a surgical emergency with immediate debridement.

### Dismissing Back Pain With Urinary Symptoms as Mechanical

Urinary retention or saddle anesthesia with back pain is cauda equina until proven otherwise. Counter by bladder scanning, emergency MRI, and urgent spinal surgical assessment.

### Treating Suspected Osteomyelitis With a Short Oral Antibiotic Course

Osteomyelitis requires prolonged targeted therapy guided by bone culture and often surgical debridement. Counter by obtaining bone biopsy and culture and involving infectious diseases.

### Delaying Surgical Referral for Imaging in Suspected Septic Arthritis or Necrotizing Infection

Imaging delay sacrifices tissue. Counter by aspirating the joint or exploring the wound promptly and treating the diagnosis as clinical.

## Self-Check

- For a child with a painful joint and fever, did I apply Kocher criteria, aspirate a high-probability joint, and involve orthopedics for suspected septic arthritis?
- For a hot swollen joint in an adult, did I aspirate synovial fluid for cell count, Gram stain, and culture before antibiotics, remembering that crystals do not exclude infection?
- For suspected osteomyelitis, did I check inflammatory markers and blood cultures, image with MRI, obtain bone biopsy and culture, and involve orthopedics and infectious diseases for prolonged targeted therapy?
- For a red swollen leg, did I differentiate cellulitis from DVT (Wells score, ultrasound) and from necrotizing fasciitis (pain out of proportion, systemic toxicity, rapid progression)?
- For suspected necrotizing infection, did I arrange immediate surgical exploration and debridement rather than waiting for confirmatory imaging?
- For back pain with urinary retention, saddle anesthesia, or bilateral neurologic signs, did I treat cauda equina syndrome as an emergency with bladder scan, MRI, and urgent spinal surgical assessment?
- Did I apply emergency escalation thresholds for septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, necrotizing infection, cauda equina, and compartment syndrome, accepting that over-calling is safer than delay?
- Did I take a directed history for infection risk and spinal red flags, and match imaging to the suspected diagnosis?
