# Joint and Soft Tissue Injury Evaluation

Sprains, strains, and tendon injuries are the most common musculoskeletal presentations, and most are minor and self-limiting, which is exactly why the serious ones are missed. The recurring failure is labeling every painful joint as a "sprain" without a structured examination that distinguishes a stable injury from an unstable one, a partial tear from a complete rupture, or a simple sprain from an occult fracture or a tendon rupture that needs surgery. This skill covers sprain and strain grading, ligament and tendon injuries, shoulder and knee focused examination, dislocation reduction, the stable-versus-unstable decision, and return-to-activity reasoning.

## Core Rules

### Grade Sprains and Strains Accurately and Match Management to Severity

Sprains (ligament) and strains (muscle-tendon unit) are graded I (mild stretch, stable, minimal functional loss), II (partial tear, some instability and functional loss), and III (complete tear, instability, significant functional loss). The grade determines management: grade I and II injuries are managed conservatively with protection, optimal loading, ice, compression, and elevation (the POLICE principle, replacing prolonged rest with early controlled loading), while grade III injuries may need immobilization and sometimes surgical repair, particularly in active patients and in locations where instability would compromise function. The error is treating all sprains identically; a grade III ACL or Achilles rupture managed as a minor sprain leaves the patient unstable and at risk of further injury. Grade the injury with stress testing and focused examination, and image when a fracture is possible (apply validated rules such as Ottawa ankle and knee rules).

### Evaluate Knee Ligament and Meniscal Injuries With Focused Examination

The painful knee after injury demands a focused examination for ligament and meniscal integrity. Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) injury classically occurs with a pivoting mechanism, an audible pop, immediate swelling (hemarthrosis), and giving way; the Lachman test is the most sensitive, with the anterior drawer and pivot shift adding confirmation. Posterior cruciate ligament injury is assessed with the posterior drawer and sag sign. Medial and lateral collateral ligaments are tested with valgus and varus stress at full and 30 degrees flexion. Meniscal injury causes joint line tenderness, effusion (often delayed), locking or catching, and positive McMurray testing. A hemarthrosis within hours of injury suggests ACL tear, fracture, or patellar dislocation and warrants imaging and often orthopedic referral. Distinguish stable from unstable knees: an unstable knee (ACL, multi-ligament, or complex injury) needs orthopedic assessment, while a stable isolated meniscal or collateral injury may be managed conservatively with rehabilitation and review.

### Recognize and Manage Tendon Ruptures, Especially Achilles Rupture

Tendon ruptures are frequently missed because the patient can still plantarflex weakly through accessory muscles. Achilles rupture classically occurs in the middle-aged "weekend warrior," often with a pop, a feeling of being kicked in the calf, and weakness; the Thompson (calf squeeze) test — squeezing the calf with the patient prone fails to produce plantarflexion in a complete rupture — is the key bedside test, with a palpable gap often present. Quadriceps and patellar tendon ruptures occur around the knee and cause inability to actively extend the knee against gravity with a palpable defect; these often need surgical repair. Suspect tendon rupture with the mechanism and the inability to perform the tendon's function against resistance, examine for a gap and the relevant functional test, image with ultrasound or MRI when uncertain, and refer to orthopedics for consideration of repair, because delayed repair is more difficult and less successful.

### Perform a Focused Shoulder Examination and Recognize Instability and Rotator Cuff Disease

Shoulder complaints divide broadly into instability (traumatic dislocation and labral injury), rotator cuff disease (tendinopathy and tears), impingement, and adhesive capsulitis. Traumatic anterior dislocation presents with pain, deformity, and a restricted, externally rotated arm; reduce using an appropriate technique with adequate analgesia, and obtain post-reduction radiographs and neurovascular assessment (axillary nerve sensation). Recurrent dislocation in a young patient after a first traumatic dislocation often needs orthopedic assessment for labral repair. Rotator cuff tears present with weakness, pain on overhead activity, night pain, and positive impingement and provocative tests (empty can, external rotation lag, drop arm); large acute tears in active patients benefit from early surgical referral, while chronic degenerative tears are often managed conservatively. Distinguish these by history and focused examination, and image (ultrasound or MRI) when a tear is suspected and management depends on it.

### Reduce Dislocations Promptly and Reassess Neurovascular Status

Joint dislocations are painful and risk neurovascular injury and cartilage damage; reduce them promptly with adequate analgesia or sedation using an appropriate technique, before swelling and muscle spasm make reduction harder. Common reductions include shoulder (anterior and posterior), patellar, finger, and temporomandibular joint. Before and after reduction, document neurovascular status (for example, axillary nerve in shoulder, digital nerves in fingers, peroneal nerve in knee). Obtain radiographs to confirm reduction and identify associated fractures (Hill-Sachs, Bankart, fracture fragments), because some fracture-dislocations need surgery. Provide appropriate immobilization, referral, and rehabilitation, and clear advice on recurrence and red flags.

### Distinguish Stable From Unstable Injuries and Define Orthopedic Referral

The central decision is whether an injury is stable (manageable with rehabilitation and review) or unstable (needing orthopedic assessment, often surgery). Unstable injuries include complete ACL or multi-ligament knee injuries, displaced or unstable ankle fractures, shoulder dislocation with bone loss or recurrent instability in young patients, complete tendon ruptures in active patients, and any injury with neurovascular compromise. Refer these to orthopedics. Stable injuries — grade I and II sprains, isolated stable meniscal tears in lower-demand patients, rotator cuff tendinopathy without acute tear — are managed with rehabilitation and structured review. Use imaging and clinical examination together, because not all unstable injuries are obvious on initial radiograph (ACL and meniscal injuries are soft tissue).

### Reason Carefully About Return to Activity

Return-to-activity decisions balance healing, restoration of strength and proprioception, sport-specific function, and risk of re-injury. Premature return risks re-injury and chronic instability; overly conservative return deconditions the patient. Use objective criteria — pain-free range of motion, restored strength approaching the uninjured side, functional and sport-specific testing — rather than time alone. For post-operative returns (ACL reconstruction, tendon repair), follow the surgical and rehabilitation protocol. Counsel the patient honestly about re-injury risk and the protective role of ongoing rehabilitation and conditioning.

## Common Traps

### Labeling Every Joint Injury a Sprain Without Grading or Examining

Calling a complete ligament or tendon rupture a sprain leads to instability and re-injury. Counter by grading the injury with stress and functional testing and applying the stable-versus-unstable decision.

### Missing an Achilles Rupture Because the Patient Can Plantarflex

Accessory muscles allow weak plantarflexion even with a complete Achilles rupture. Counter by performing the Thompson test and looking for a palpable gap.

### Missing an ACL Tear Behind a Hemarthrosis

A knee that swells within hours of injury has a hemarthrosis until proven otherwise, commonly from ACL tear, fracture, or patellar dislocation. Counter by performing Lachman testing and imaging, and referring unstable knees.

### Reducing a Dislocation Without Documenting Pre- and Post-Reduction Neurovascular Status

Neurovascular injury can occur before or during reduction. Counter by documenting status before and after reduction and obtaining post-reduction imaging.

### Treating an Acute Rotator Cuff Tear in an Active Patient Conservatively by Default

Large acute tears retract and become harder to repair. Counter by suspecting tears with weakness and positive provocative tests, imaging, and referring early for consideration of repair.

### Returning an Athlete to Sport on Time Alone

Time-based return ignores residual weakness and proprioceptive deficit and risks re-injury. Counter by using objective functional and strength criteria.

### Dismissing a Finger Dislocation as Minor

Finger dislocations can involve fractures and tendon or ligament injuries needing surgery; missed injuries cause permanent deformity. Counter by reducing, imaging, and referring complex or fracture-dislocations to hand therapy or orthopedics.

## Self-Check

- Did I grade the sprain or strain (I, II, III) with stress and functional testing, and match management to severity rather than treating all injuries identically?
- For a painful knee, did I perform Lachman, posterior drawer, collateral stress, joint line tenderness, and McMurray testing, and recognize hemarthrosis as a sign of serious injury?
- For a suspected tendon rupture (especially Achilles), did I perform the relevant functional test (Thompson for Achilles, active extension for quadriceps/patellar) and image or refer?
- For shoulder complaints, did I distinguish instability, rotator cuff disease, impingement, and adhesive capsulitis with focused examination and image when management depends on it?
- For dislocations, did I reduce promptly with adequate analgesia, document pre- and post-reduction neurovascular status, and obtain post-reduction imaging?
- Did I explicitly decide whether the injury is stable (rehabilitation and review) or unstable (orthopedic referral), and apply the Ottawa ankle and knee rules for imaging?
- For return to activity, did I use objective strength, range, proprioception, and sport-specific criteria rather than time alone?
- Did I refer complex or unstable injuries, fracture-dislocations, and acute tendon ruptures in active patients to orthopedics?
- This skill provides educational information only and does not constitute medical advice. Always consult qualified healthcare professionals for clinical decisions.
