# Acute Urologic Emergencies

Urologic emergencies are defined by the speed at which delay becomes irreversible: a torsed testicle loses viability within hours, ischemic priapism scars the erectile tissue within a day, Fournier gangrene can kill within hours, and obstructed infected kidneys cause sepsis rapidly. The recurring failure is treating these as "pain to investigate" rather than as time-critical problems where the diagnosis is made clinically and the call to urology precedes confirmation. This skill covers testicular torsion, paraphimosis, priapism, acute urinary retention, Fournier gangrene, obstructive uropathy and renal colic, and the thresholds for emergent urology involvement.

## Core Rules

### Treat Suspected Testicular Torsion as a Surgical Emergency and Do Not Delay for Imaging

Testicular torsion is the must-not-miss diagnosis in any male with acute scrotal pain, and the salvage rate falls steeply after six hours of symptoms. The classic presentation is sudden severe unilateral scrotal pain, nausea and vomiting, a high-riding or transverse ("bell clapper") testis, an absent cremasteric reflex on the affected side, and severe tenderness, often with no fever or dysuria. Do not wait for Doppler ultrasound to confirm torsion if the suspicion is high and the duration short — ultrasound is for the indeterminate case, and a normal-looking ultrasound in a high-risk patient must not delay exploration. Arrange immediate urology referral and surgical exploration; manual detorsion ("opening the book") can be attempted as a temporizing measure while preparing the operating room. Missing torsion by attributing the pain to epididymitis or appendage torsion in a young male is the cardinal error.

### Differentiate Ischemic From Non-Ischemic Priapism and Treat Ischemic Emergently

Priapism is a prolonged erection, and the subtype determines urgency. Ischemic (veno-occlusive, low-flow) priapism is a painful, rigid erection lasting more than four hours, with ischemic corpora cavernosa; it is an emergency because prolonged ischemia causes fibrosis and permanent erectile dysfunction. Causes include sickle cell disease, intracavernosal injection therapy, certain drugs (antidepressants, antipsychotics, PDE5 inhibitors in combination), malignancy, and trauma. Management involves corporal aspiration, irrigation, and intracavernosal sympathomimetics, with urology involvement, and treatment of the underlying cause (sickle cell protocol with hydration, oxygen, exchange transfusion in severe cases). Non-ischemic (arterial, high-flow) priapism, usually from perineal trauma, is painless, less rigid, and not an emergency; it is managed expectantly or with selective embolization. Distinguish the two by history (pain, rigidity) and, when needed, corporal blood gas.

### Reduce Paraphimosis Promptly to Avoid Necrosis

Paraphimosis is a retracted foreskin that cannot be reduced over the glans, forming a constricting band that causes progressive edema and ischemia of the glans. It is an emergency: delay causes necrosis. Reduce it promptly with a combination of manual compression to reduce edema, analgesia, and firm reduction of the foreskin over the glans; reduce early while edema is mild. If reduction fails, urgent urology involvement is needed for dorsal slit or circumcision. Always reposition the foreskin after catheterization or examination, because iatrogenic paraphimosis is common and preventable. Distinguish paraphimosis (emergency) from phimosis (non-retractile foreskin, not an emergency).

### Manage Acute Urinary Retention and Identify the Cause

Acute urinary retention presents with an inability to void, suprapubic pain, and a palpable distended bladder. Immediate management is urethral catheterization (suprapubic catheter if urethral catheterization fails), with monitoring for post-obstructive diuresis after decompression in high-volume retentions. Identify precipitating causes: benign prostatic hyperplasia, anticholinergic or sympathomimetic drugs, constipation, urinary tract infection, clot retention (gross hematuria), neurologic disease (spinal cord compression, multiple sclerosis), pelvic mass, and postoperative or post-anesthetic retention. A hard, irregular prostate raises cancer; a tender prostate raises prostatitis; neurologic deficits raise spinal cord pathology and demand urgent imaging. Clot retention from gross hematuria needs three-way catheter irrigation and urology input. Plan a trial without catheter and address the underlying cause.

### Recognize Fournier Gangrene and Treat as a Surgical Emergency

Fournier gangrene is a rapidly progressive necrotizing fasciitis of the perineum, genitalia, and perianal region, polymicrobial and usually in diabetic, immunocompromised, or debilitated men, but possible in any patient. It presents with severe perineal pain out of proportion to examination, swelling, erythema progressing to dusky or necrotic skin, crepitus, foul-smelling discharge, and systemic sepsis; pain may precede skin changes. Management is a surgical emergency: aggressive fluid resuscitation, broad-spectrum antibiotics covering polymicrobial organisms, and immediate surgical debridement of all necrotic tissue, often repeated, with urology or general surgery. Mortality rises with delay. Do not wait for imaging or a "confirmatory" finding — clinical suspicion mandates urgent surgical exploration.

### Manage Obstructive Uropathy and Infected Obstructed Systems as Sepsis Emergencies

A stone causing unilateral obstruction with infection (pyonephrosis) is a urologic sepsis emergency: the infected obstructed kidney cannot drain, bacteremia and septic shock develop rapidly, and antibiotics alone cannot cure an obstructed infected system. Any patient with renal colic and fever, rigors, sepsis, a solitary kidney, or signs of a complicated obstruction needs urgent decompression (ureteric stent or nephrostomy) in addition to antibiotics and resuscitation. Distinguish uncomplicated renal colic (manageable with analgesia, hydration, and expectant or surgical stone management depending on size and location) from the infected obstructed system (emergency decompression). Check renal function and arrange imaging (CT urogram) to define the obstruction.

### Define the Thresholds for Emergent Urology Involvement

Emergent urology involvement is mandatory for: suspected testicular torsion (immediate, do not delay for imaging); ischemic priapism; paraphimosis that cannot be reduced; Fournier gangrene or any necrotizing genital infection; obstructive uropathy with infection or a solitary kidney; acute retention that cannot be catheterized; gross hematuria with clot retention; and penile fracture or significant genitourinary trauma. State the call as a defined emergency with the suspected diagnosis and the time of symptom onset, because urology triages on duration (especially for torsion and priapism). Accept that over-calling is safer than delay in every time-critical urologic emergency.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Exploration to Obtain Ultrasound in Suspected Torsion

Imaging delay sacrifices salvageable testicular tissue; ultrasound is only for the indeterminate case. Counter by arranging immediate urology referral and exploration when torsion is suspected and the window is short.

### Mislabeling Torsion as Epididymitis in a Young Male

Epididymitis is more gradual, with fever, dysuria, and a tender epididymis; torsion is sudden, severe, with an absent cremasteric reflex. Counter by maintaining a high index of torsion in any young male with acute scrotal pain and exploring if in doubt.

### Treating Ischemic Priapism Expectantly

Ischemic priapism scars erectile tissue within hours; waiting is harmful. Counter by recognizing the painful rigid erection, involving urology, and performing corporal aspiration and sympathomimetics promptly while treating the cause.

### Failing to Reduce Paraphimosis Early

Delay allows edema to worsen and reduction to fail, risking glans necrosis. Counter by reducing paraphimosis promptly with compression and analgesia, and always repositioning the foreskin after procedures.

### Missing Fournier Gangrene Behind "Cellulitis" or "Abscess"

Pain out of proportion, systemic sepsis, and rapid progression signal necrotizing infection, not simple cellulitis. Counter by treating clinical suspicion as a surgical emergency with immediate debridement, not antibiotics alone.

### Treating an Infected Obstructed Kidney With Antibiotics Alone

An obstructed infected system cannot clear infection without decompression; sepsis progresses. Counter by urgent urologic decompression (stent or nephrostomy) alongside antibiotics and resuscitation.

### Discharging Renal Colic Without Addressing Stricture, Infection, or Solitary Kidney

Uncomplicated stones may pass, but fever, a solitary kidney, renal impairment, or an obstructing large stone change management. Counter by risk-stratifying and involving urology for complicated or unlikely-to-pass stones.

## Self-Check

- For any male with acute scrotal pain, did I treat testicular torsion as the leading diagnosis and arrange immediate urology referral and exploration without waiting for imaging when suspicion is high?
- For a prolonged painful erection, did I distinguish ischemic from non-ischemic priapism and treat ischemic priapism as an emergency with corporal aspiration and urology involvement?
- For paraphimosis, did I reduce it promptly with compression and analgesia, and did I reposition the foreskin after any catheterization or examination?
- For acute urinary retention, did I catheterize, monitor for post-obstructive diuresis, and identify and address the underlying cause, including neurologic deficits?
- For severe perineal pain with systemic sepsis, did I recognize Fournier gangrene as a surgical emergency and arrange immediate debridement with antibiotics and resuscitation?
- For renal colic with fever, sepsis, a solitary kidney, or renal impairment, did I arrange urgent decompression of the infected obstructed system rather than antibiotics alone?
- Did I apply the mandatory emergent urology criteria (torsion, ischemic priapism, paraphimosis, Fournier gangrene, infected obstruction, retention uncatheterizable, clot retention) and document the time of onset and the call?
- Did I accept that over-calling is safer than delay for time-critical urologic emergencies?
