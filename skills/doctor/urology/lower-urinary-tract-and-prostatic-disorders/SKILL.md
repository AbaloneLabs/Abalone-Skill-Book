# Lower Urinary Tract and Prostatic Disorders

Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS), prostatitis, recurrent urinary tract infection, stone disease, and incontinence are among the most common reasons adults seek urologic or primary care. They are rarely immediately life-threatening, which is exactly why they are mismanaged: clinicians reach for a familiar prescription (an alpha-blocker, an antibiotic, a fluid restriction) without classifying the problem precisely, and so miss a chronic prostatitis that needs a different antibiotic course, a recurrent UTI driven by a stone or obstruction, an incontinence that is surgically curable, or a BPH patient progressing to retention or renal impairment. This skill covers BPH and LUTS management, prostatitis, recurrent UTI workup, stone disease decisions, urinary incontinence classification, and the thresholds for urology referral.

## Core Rules

### Classify LUTS and Manage BPH With Attention to Complications and Progression

Lower urinary tract symptoms are divided into voiding (obstructive: hesitancy, weak stream, incomplete emptying, straining) and storage (irritative: frequency, urgency, nocturia, urge incontinence) symptoms, and BPH is a common cause but not the only one. Quantify symptoms with a validated score (such as IPSS) and assess impact on quality of life. First-line BPH management combines lifestyle measures (fluid management, timed voiding, avoiding caffeine and evening fluids) with medical therapy: an alpha-blocker for bothersome obstructive symptoms (with awareness of orthostatic hypotension and ejaculatory effects), a 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor for an enlarged prostate (slow onset, used alone or with an alpha-blocker), and consideration of anticholinergics or beta-3 agonists for prominent storage symptoms once retention is excluded. Escalate to urology for failure of medical therapy, intolerance, or complications: recurrent urinary retention, recurrent UTI, bladder stones, gross hematuria, or renal impairment from obstructive uropathy. Always check post-void residual and renal function when symptoms are severe or progressive.

### Distinguish Acute From Chronic Prostatitis and Treat With the Right Duration

Prostatitis has four categories, and conflating them causes treatment failure. Acute bacterial prostatitis presents with fever, perineal or pelvic pain, dysuria, and often urinary retention, with a tender prostate; treat with appropriate antibiotics (often intravenous initially in severe cases) and address retention (suprapubic catheter may be preferred over urethral to avoid trauma). Chronic bacterial prostatitis presents with recurrent urinary tract infections and pelvic pain, requiring a prolonged antibiotic course (weeks) tailored to cultures. Chronic pelvic pain syndrome (formerly chronic nonbacterial prostatitis) is the most common form, with pelvic or perineal pain and voiding symptoms without proven infection; management is multimodal (alpha-blockers, anti-inflammatories, neuromodulators, pelvic floor physical therapy) and antibiotics are often unhelpful. Asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis is an incidental finding. Culture the urine (and expressed prostatic secretions where appropriate) before committing to prolonged antibiotics, and avoid repeated antibiotic courses without evidence of infection.

### Work Up Recurrent UTI as Reinfection, Relapse, or Structural Cause

Recurrent urinary tract infection is not simply "another antibiotic course." Classify it as reinfection (different organism, after a clear interval, common in women) versus relapse (same organism, suggesting a persistent focus such as a stone, obstruction, or prostatic focus) versus complicated UTI (structural or functional abnormality, immunocompromise, pregnancy). Confirm each episode with culture rather than treating symptoms empirically, and review the pattern. Investigate recurrent UTI in men (uncommon without a structural cause), in pregnancy, in children, with a stone or obstruction suspected, with atypical organisms, or when relapsing: urinalysis and culture, renal function, and imaging (ultrasound or CT urogram) to exclude stones, obstruction, diverticula, and residual urine. Non-antibiotic prevention (fluids, post-coital voiding, topical estrogen in postmenopausal women, and where appropriate prophylactic strategies) is preferred over repeated courses; reserve long-term prophylaxis for clearly indicated cases to avoid resistance and side effects.

### Decide Between Conservative and Surgical Management of Stone Disease

Ureteric and renal stone management depends on stone size, location, composition, symptoms, infection, and renal function. Most small ureteric stones (under 5 to 10 mm) in an uninfected patient with controlled pain and normal renal function can be managed expectantly with hydration and an alpha-blocker (medical expulsive therapy) and close follow-up imaging to confirm passage. Indications for surgical intervention (shock wave lithotripsy, ureteroscopy with laser, percutaneous nephrolithotomy) include large stones, failure of conservative passage, persistent pain, obstruction, a solitary kidney, renal impairment, and any sign of infection (which mandates decompression, not just stone treatment). Address metabolic risk factors and prevention (fluids, dietary modification, and targeted therapy for recurrent stone formers based on stone composition and metabolic workup). Always confirm stone passage with imaging, not symptom resolution, because a silent retained stone can cause silent obstruction and renal loss.

### Classify Urinary Incontinence Before Treating

Urinary incontinence is not one condition, and the treatment differs by type. Stress incontinence (leakage with cough, sneeze, exertion) is common in women after childbirth and in men after prostate surgery; first-line management is pelvic floor exercises and weight loss, with surgical options (sling procedures) for refractory cases. Urge incontinence (leakage with urgency, part of overactive bladder) is managed with bladder training, anticholinergics or beta-3 agonists, and consideration of neuromodulation or botulinum toxin for refractory cases. Overflow incontinence (from retention and obstruction) requires addressing the obstruction or retention, not anticholinergics, which worsen it. Functional incontinence (from cognitive or mobility impairment) requires environmental and caregiver strategies. Mixing these up — giving anticholinergics to a patient with overflow, or missing a surgically curable stress incontinence — is the cardinal error. Check a post-void residual in any patient with incontinence to exclude retention before prescribing bladder relaxants.

### Define When Urology Referral Is Warranted

Refer to urology for BPH with complications or failure of medical therapy (recurrent retention, UTI, stones, renal impairment, refractory symptoms); prostatitis that is recurrent, complicated by retention or abscess, or chronic and refractory; recurrent UTI with structural cause or relapse; stones that are large, obstructing, infected, or failing conservative management; incontinence that is refractory, mixed, or potentially surgical (stress incontinence); and any suspicion of urologic malignancy (hematuria, mass, elevated PSA). State the referral pathway and timeframe, and ensure safety-net advice for retention, sepsis, and gross hematuria.

## Common Traps

### Treating All LUTS as BPH Without Quantifying or Excluding Complications

Symptom overlap with overactive bladder, prostatitis, and even bladder cancer means a generic prescription misses the diagnosis. Counter by quantifying symptoms, checking post-void residual and renal function, and screening for hematuria.

### Using the Same Antibiotic Approach for All Prostatitis

Acute, chronic bacterial, and chronic pelvic pain syndrome need different strategies. Counter by categorizing prostatitis, culturing before prolonged therapy, and avoiding repeated antibiotics without evidence of infection.

### Treating Recurrent UTI With Repeated Empiric Antibiotics Without Workup

Repeated courses without classification or investigation miss stones, obstruction, and relapse, and breed resistance. Counter by culturing each episode, classifying reinfection versus relapse, and imaging when structural cause is suspected.

### Giving Anticholinergics to a Patient With Overflow Incontinence or Retention

Bladder relaxants worsen retention and can precipitate renal impairment. Counter by checking a post-void residual before prescribing and reserving anticholinergics for confirmed overactive bladder.

### Assuming a Ureteric Stone Has Passed When Symptoms Resolve

Silent retained stones cause silent obstruction and renal loss. Counter by confirming passage with imaging, not symptom resolution.

### Missing Surgically Curable Stress Incontinence

Labeling all incontinence as overactive and treating medically misses sling-suitable stress incontinence. Counter by classifying incontinence by history and examination before treatment.

### Dismissing Nocturia as Normal Aging

Nocturia may reflect obstructive uropathy, heart failure, sleep apnea, or uncontrolled diabetes. Counter by evaluating the cause rather than reassuring.

## Self-Check

- For LUTS and BPH, did I quantify symptoms with a validated score, check post-void residual and renal function, and screen for complications (retention, UTI, stones, hematuria, renal impairment)?
- For prostatitis, did I categorize acute, chronic bacterial, or chronic pelvic pain syndrome, culture before prolonged therapy, and avoid repeated antibiotics without evidence of infection?
- For recurrent UTI, did I classify reinfection versus relapse versus complicated UTI, culture each episode, and image when a structural cause is suspected?
- For stone disease, did I decide between conservative and surgical management using size, location, infection, obstruction, and renal function, and confirm passage with imaging?
- For incontinence, did I classify stress, urge, overflow, or functional type and check a post-void residual before prescribing bladder relaxants?
- Did I address prevention (fluids, lifestyle, metabolic workup for stones, non-antibiotic UTI prevention) rather than relying on prescriptions alone?
- Did I define and document the urology referral pathway and timeframe for complications, refractory symptoms, and suspected malignancy?
- Did I provide safety-net advice for retention, sepsis, and gross hematuria requiring emergency care?
- This skill provides educational information only and does not constitute medical advice. Always consult qualified healthcare professionals for clinical decisions.
