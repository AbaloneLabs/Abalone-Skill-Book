# Hematuria and Urologic Malignancy Evaluation

Hematuria and an elevated PSA are two of the most common triggers for urologic investigation, and both have failure modes at opposite extremes. With hematuria, the danger is reassuring a patient with visible blood that "it's just an infection" when the cause is bladder or upper tract cancer, where early diagnosis determines survival. With PSA and prostate cancer screening, the danger is the opposite: cascading every mildly elevated PSA into biopsy and treatment of an indolent cancer that would never have harmed the patient, causing incontinence, impotence, and anxiety. The recurring failure is treating both as a number to act on reflexively rather than applying risk-stratified, shared reasoning. This skill covers the visible and microscopic hematuria workup, the risk-stratified approach to urologic malignancy, and PSA shared decision-making.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Visible From Microscopic Hematuria and Risk-Stratify Before Investigating

Hematuria is categorized as visible (gross) or microscopic, and the risk of malignancy differs substantially. Any adult with visible hematuria (single episode, painless or painful) requires complete urologic evaluation — cystoscopy and upper tract imaging (CT urogram) — because visible hematuria has a high yield for bladder and upper tract urothelial cancer; do not attribute visible hematuria to infection, menstruation, or benign prostatic hyperplasia without excluding malignancy. Microscopic hematuria is risk-stratified by age, sex, smoking history, occupational exposures, and other risk factors: low-risk patients may be observed with repeat testing, while higher-risk patients require the same full evaluation. Confirm persistent microscopic hematuria (not a single transient finding from exercise or menstruation) before committing to full workup. Always exclude a urinary tract infection as the cause before labeling it hematuria requiring malignancy workup.

### Apply a Risk-Stratified Approach to Bladder, Renal, and Upper Tract Malignancy

Urologic malignancies present along a spectrum and require targeted evaluation. Bladder cancer (urothelial) classically presents with painless visible hematuria; smoking and occupational exposures (dyes, rubber, chemicals) are major risk factors, and diagnosis is by cystoscopy with biopsy. Upper tract urothelial carcinoma (renal pelvis, ureter) also presents with hematuria and is evaluated with CT urogram and ureteroscopy. Renal cell carcinoma presents with hematuria, flank pain, and a mass, but is increasingly found incidentally on imaging; CT characterizes the renal mass. Prostate cancer is discussed separately but may cause hematuria when advanced. The principle is to match the evaluation to the risk profile: a 60-year-old smoker with visible hematuria gets full evaluation; a 25-year-old woman with hematuria during a confirmed UTI does not need urology referral unless hematuria persists after treatment.

### Conduct PSA Screening as Shared Decision-Making, Not Reflexive Testing

Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) screening for prostate cancer reduces mortality modestly but causes substantial harm through overdiagnosis and overtreatment of indolent cancers, with biopsy and treatment complications (infection, bleeding, incontinence, erectile dysfunction). The correct approach is shared decision-making: inform the patient about the uncertain benefit and the real harms, consider age, life expectancy, family history, and race (higher risk in men of African descent), and test only if the patient wants to act on the result. Do not order PSA without this conversation. When PSA is elevated, avoid reflexive biopsy: consider free-to-total PSA, PSA velocity, multiparametric MRI, and risk calculators to refine the probability of clinically significant cancer, and refer to urology for structured assessment. The goal is to find and treat clinically significant cancer while avoiding detection and treatment of indolent disease that would never harm the patient.

### Avoid Both Over-Investigation and Under-Investigation

Both extremes harm patients. Over-investigation — full urologic workup for every transient microscopic hematuria in a young low-risk patient, or reflexive biopsy for every mildly elevated PSA — causes anxiety, complications, and detection of indolent disease. Under-investigation — attributing visible hematuria to infection or BPH without evaluation, or dismissing a persistent PSA rise — delays cancer diagnosis. The balance comes from disciplined risk stratification: use age, smoking, exposures, family history, persistence of findings, and validated risk tools to decide who needs full evaluation and who can be observed. Document the reasoning and the follow-up plan so that low-risk observation does not drift into neglect.

### Take a Directed History Including Risk Factors and Medications

The history shapes the differential and the workup. Ask about smoking (the dominant bladder cancer risk), occupational and chemical exposures, pelvic radiation, cyclophosphamide chemotherapy, analgesic abuse (renal cell and urothelial), schistosomiasis exposure (in endemic regions, a cause of squamous bladder cancer), family history of urologic cancer, and recent vigorous exercise or trauma (benign causes of transient hematuria). Review medications for anticoagulants (which do not cause hematuria but may unmask an underlying lesion — never attribute hematuria to anticoagulants without full evaluation), and renal colic symptoms. For PSA, ask about family history, race, recent ejaculation or instrumentation (which can transiently raise PSA), and symptoms of benign prostatic hyperplasia.

### Define When to Refer to Urology and the Timeframe

Refer to urology for any visible hematuria (urgent referral, because bladder cancer is time-sensitive), microscopic hematuria in higher-risk patients (smokers, older age, persistent), any renal mass found on imaging, and elevated or rising PSA after shared decision-making. The timeframe matters: visible hematuria and a suspected renal mass warrant prompt referral (within weeks), while low-risk microscopic hematuria may be observed with repeat testing. State the referral pathway and timeline, and ensure the patient understands the plan and the safety-net symptoms (clot retention, new pain) that require emergency care.

### Recognize Hematuria Emergencies

Gross hematuria with clot retention is an emergency requiring three-way catheter irrigation and urology input to prevent bladder tamponade. Hematuria with sepsis, severe anemia, or hemodynamic instability requires admission and resuscitation. Hematuria in the anticoagulated patient is not explained by the anticoagulant — it still requires malignancy workup once stabilized. Recognize and escalate these while arranging the structured outpatient evaluation for stable cases.

## Common Traps

### Attributing Visible Hematuria to Infection or BPH Without Evaluation

Visible hematuria has a high yield for malignancy and demands cystoscopy and upper tract imaging. Counter by evaluating all visible hematuria and not attributing it to infection, menstruation, or BPH without excluding cancer.

### Reflexively Ordering PSA Without Shared Decision-Making

PSA testing without a discussion of benefits and harms leads to overdiagnosis and overtreatment of indolent cancer. Counter by conducting shared decision-making before testing and documenting the conversation.

### Investigating a Single Transient Microscopic Hematuria in a Low-Risk Patient

Over-investigation of clearly benign transient hematuria causes anxiety and complications. Counter by confirming persistence and risk-stratifying before full urologic workup.

### Attributing Hematuria to Anticoagulants

Anticoagulants do not cause hematuria; they unmask underlying lesions. Counter by performing full evaluation regardless of anticoagulant use once the patient is stable.

### Missing Clot Retention as an Emergency

Gross hematuria with clot retention can cause bladder tamponade. Counter by inserting a three-way catheter with irrigation and involving urology urgently.

### Reflexively Biopsying Every Elevated PSA

Biopsy without refining the probability of clinically significant cancer detects indolent disease. Counter by using free-to-total PSA, PSA velocity, MRI, and risk calculators before biopsy.

### Dismissing a Renal Mass as a Benign Cyst Without Characterization

A complex or solid renal mass needs CT characterization and urology referral. Counter by fully characterizing incidental renal masses and not assuming benignity without imaging criteria.

## Self-Check

- For visible hematuria, did I arrange full urologic evaluation (cystoscopy and CT urogram) rather than attributing it to infection, BPH, or anticoagulants?
- For microscopic hematuria, did I confirm persistence, exclude infection, and risk-stratify by age, smoking, and exposures before deciding on observation versus full workup?
- For PSA, did I conduct shared decision-making before testing, and document the discussion of benefits and harms?
- For an elevated PSA, did I avoid reflexive biopsy and use free-to-total PSA, PSA velocity, MRI, and risk calculators before urology referral?
- Did I take a directed history including smoking, occupational and chemical exposures, radiation, chemotherapy, schistosomiasis, family history, and medications?
- Did I recognize and escalate hematuria emergencies (clot retention, sepsis, hemodynamic instability, severe anemia) while arranging structured outpatient evaluation for stable cases?
- Did I document the urology referral pathway and timeline, and provide safety-net advice for clot retention and new pain?
- Did I balance over- and under-investigation using disciplined risk stratification rather than reflexive action?
