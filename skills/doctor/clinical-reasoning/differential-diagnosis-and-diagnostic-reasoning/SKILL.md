# Differential Diagnosis and Diagnostic Reasoning

Diagnostic reasoning is the core cognitive task of clinical medicine, and it is where some of the most consequential errors occur. A differential diagnosis is not a list to be memorized — it is a living, evolving hypothesis set that must be generated, prioritized, tested, revised, and sometimes abandoned as new data arrives. The danger is not ignorance alone; it is the illusion of certainty. Clinicians most often miss diagnoses not because they do not know the disease, but because they stop searching too early, overweight the first plausible explanation, or fail to revise when the picture changes. This skill covers the disciplined process of building and testing a differential, recognizing cognitive pitfalls, and knowing when to slow down.

## Core Rules

### Generate a Broad Differential Before Narrowing

Before committing to a leading diagnosis, deliberately generate a wide set of possibilities. The goal is to avoid premature closure — the tendency to accept the first reasonable explanation without considering alternatives. Organize the differential by category: the most likely diagnosis given the presentation, the most dangerous diagnoses that cannot be missed (the "must-not-miss" list), and the less common but plausible alternatives. Use frameworks appropriate to the presentation — anatomical (for pain), pathological (infectious, inflammatory, neoplastic, vascular, toxic), or syndrome-based. The act of writing the differential down forces breadth and creates a check against anchoring. If you find yourself with a single diagnosis and no alternatives, you have not finished the differential.

### Prioritize by Likelihood and by Danger

Not every diagnosis on the differential carries the same weight. Prioritize using two axes simultaneously: how likely is this diagnosis given the evidence, and how dangerous is it if missed or delayed. A moderately likely but highly dangerous condition (aortic dissection in chest pain, pulmonary embolism in dyspnea, sepsis in fever) demands explicit ruling-out even if a more benign diagnosis seems more probable. This dual-axis prioritization prevents the common error of chasing the most likely diagnosis while ignoring the lethal one. For each high-danger diagnosis, state explicitly what would rule it in or out, and what threshold of concern requires imaging, labs, or specialist consultation.

### Use Bayesian Reasoning for Test Interpretation

Diagnostic tests do not give binary answers; they shift probability. Interpret every test result using pre-test probability, test characteristics (sensitivity, specificity, likelihood ratios), and the resulting post-test probability. A positive troponin in a low-pre-test-probability patient may be a false positive; a negative D-dimer in a high-pre-test-probability patient may not exclude the diagnosis. Estimate pre-test probability using validated tools (Wells score, CURB-65, HEART score, etc.) rather than gut feeling. Understand that the same test result means different things in different populations — the positive predictive value of any test rises with disease prevalence. When a test result conflicts strongly with clinical judgment, revisit the pre-test probability, the test's limitations, and the possibility of a different diagnosis rather than blindly accepting or rejecting the result.

### Actively Seek Disconfirming Evidence

Human reasoning naturally seeks confirmation — we notice data that supports our hypothesis and discount data that contradicts it (confirmation bias). Counter this by deliberately seeking disconfirming evidence. Ask: what would make this diagnosis wrong? What finding, if present, would force me to reconsider? Pursue those findings specifically. If a symptom or result does not fit the leading diagnosis, do not explain it away; give it weight. The single inconsistent data point is often the clue that the diagnosis is wrong. A disciplined clinician treats contradictory evidence as signal, not noise, and revises the differential rather than rationalizing the anomaly.

### Revisit the Differential When the Course Deviates

A differential is not set at the initial encounter — it must be revisited whenever the clinical course deviates from expectation. If the patient is not improving as predicted for the working diagnosis, if new symptoms appear, or if treatment response is atypical, return to the differential. Ask: am I treating the right disease, is there a complication, is there an additional diagnosis, or is the original diagnosis wrong? The most dangerous phase of diagnostic reasoning is the comfortable middle, when a diagnosis has been assigned and the clinician stops questioning it. Build in forced re-evaluation at defined intervals for undifferentiated or non-resolving presentations.

### Recognize and Counter Cognitive Biases Explicitly

Name the biases that threaten the current diagnostic reasoning and counter them deliberately. Anchoring — fixating on the initial impression despite new data. Premature closure — accepting a diagnosis before it is fully verified. Availability bias — overestimating diagnoses that come easily to mind (recent cases, memorable cases). Framing bias — accepting a referral diagnosis or the patient's own framing without independent assessment. Diagnostic momentum — accepting prior clinicians' diagnoses without re-evaluating. The simple act of naming the bias reduces its power. When the diagnosis feels "too easy" or you notice you have stopped considering alternatives, pause and name what bias might be operating.

### Know When to Slow Down: Type 1 vs Type 2 Thinking

Clinical reasoning operates in two modes: Type 1 (fast, intuitive, pattern recognition) and Type 2 (slow, analytical, systematic). Type 1 is efficient and often correct for common, classic presentations, but it is where bias lives. Type 2 is effortful but catches what Type 1 misses. Recognize when to switch from Type 1 to Type 2: when the presentation is atypical, when the patient is not improving, when the stakes are high, when you feel uncertainty or discomfort, or when multiple diagnoses are competing. Atypical presentations, high-acuity situations, and patients with complex comorbidities demand deliberate Type 2 reasoning even if a pattern seems recognizable. Do not rely on intuition alone when the consequences of error are severe.

### Document the Diagnostic Reasoning, Not Just the Conclusion

The medical record should capture not only the final diagnosis but the reasoning that produced it — the differential considered, the diagnoses ruled out and how, the remaining uncertainty, and the plan if the working diagnosis proves wrong. This documentation serves multiple purposes: it forces the clinician to articulate and examine their reasoning, it enables colleagues to evaluate and continue the reasoning, it creates a baseline for revisiting if the course deviates, and it provides medicolegal documentation of a thoughtful process. A note that states only a diagnosis without the differential and reasoning is both clinically and legally weaker than one that shows the thinking.

## Common Traps

### Premature Closure

Accepting the first plausible diagnosis without generating or considering alternatives. Counter by deliberately generating a broad differential before narrowing.

### Anchoring on the Initial Impression

Fixating on the first diagnosis even as contradictory data accumulates. Counter by actively seeking disconfirming evidence and revisiting the differential when the course deviates.

### Ignoring the Must-Not-Miss Diagnoses

Focusing on the most likely diagnosis and failing to explicitly rule out dangerous conditions. Counter by maintaining a must-not-miss list and stating how each is excluded.

### Treating Test Results as Binary

Interpreting tests as simply positive or negative without considering pre-test probability and test characteristics. Counter by using Bayesian reasoning and validated pre-test probability tools.

### Confirmation Bias

Noticing supportive data and discounting contradictory data. Counter by deliberately pursuing disconfirming findings.

### Accepting the Referral Diagnosis Without Independent Assessment

Framing and diagnostic momentum — accepting prior clinicians' diagnoses without re-evaluating from scratch. Counter by independently forming your own differential before accepting or rejecting the referral impression.

### Failing to Slow Down for Atypical Presentations

Relying on intuitive Type 1 reasoning when the presentation is atypical or high-stakes. Counter by recognizing triggers for Type 2 analytical reasoning.

## Self-Check

- Have I generated a broad differential organized by likelihood and danger, including must-not-miss diagnoses, before committing to a leading diagnosis?
- For each high-danger diagnosis on the differential, have I stated explicitly what rules it in or out and what threshold requires action?
- Am I interpreting test results using pre-test probability and test characteristics, not treating them as binary?
- Have I deliberately sought disconfirming evidence rather than only confirmation?
- Have I planned to revisit the differential if the clinical course deviates from expectation?
- Have I named the cognitive biases that threaten this reasoning and countered them?
- Have I used Type 2 analytical reasoning rather than Type 1 intuition for atypical, complex, or high-stakes presentations?
- Does my documentation capture the differential, the reasoning, and the plan if the working diagnosis is wrong, not just the conclusion?
