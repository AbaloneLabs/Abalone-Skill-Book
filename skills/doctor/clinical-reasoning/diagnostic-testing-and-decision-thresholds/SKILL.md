# Diagnostic Testing and Decision Thresholds

Forming a differential is only half of clinical reasoning. The other half is deciding what to do with it: which tests to order, when to treat without further testing, and when to stop investigating and observe. These decisions are where clinicians cause some of their most preventable harm — not only by missing diagnoses, but by overtesting. Every test carries direct harms (radiation, contrast, bleeding, false positives), indirect harms (cascade workup, incidentaloma chasing, anxiety, cost, delay), and opportunity costs. A clinician who orders "everything" is not being thorough; they are externalizing the uncertainty onto the patient's body and the laboratory. This skill covers the disciplined use of diagnostic thresholds, the avoidance of overtesting, and the explicit management of residual uncertainty.

## Core Rules

### Decide Whether to Test Before Choosing Which Test

Before ordering any test, answer a prior question: will the result change what I do? A test is justified only if its result could plausibly move the patient across a treatment threshold — that is, if a positive result would make you treat and a negative result would make you withhold treatment. If you would treat regardless of the result (the pre-test probability is already high enough), the test is unnecessary. If you would not treat regardless of the result (the pre-test probability is too low, or the condition is not actionable), the test is also unnecessary. State the decision the test is meant to inform before ordering it. Tests ordered "just to be complete" or "to cover myself" generate results without decisions, and those results are the ones most likely to lead downstream harm.

### Use the Test-Treatment Threshold Framework

Diagnostic decisions can be structured around two thresholds derived from the harms and benefits of treatment. The testing threshold is the probability of disease below which you neither test nor treat, because the risk of the workup and of treating a false positive exceeds the benefit of catching the disease. The treatment threshold is the probability above which you treat without further testing, because the probability is high enough that confirming would add risk without changing the plan. Between the two thresholds lies the zone where testing is genuinely useful, because results could move the patient across the treatment boundary. You do not need to compute these thresholds numerically for every case, but you should be able to say, for each differential, whether the patient is clearly below testing threshold, clearly above treatment threshold, or in the indeterminate zone where testing earns its place.

### Recognize the Cascade and Resist It

A cascade begins when an unexpected or incidental finding triggers further testing that would not otherwise have occurred, and that testing produces further findings requiring still more workup. The incidental pulmonary nodule, the borderline abnormal lab, the unexpected imaging finding — each can launch a chain of increasingly invasive investigation, much of it on tissue that was never the problem. Cascades cause real harm: biopsies with complications, radiation exposure, anxiety, labeling, and cost. Resist the cascade by asking, before chasing an incidental finding, whether it changes the management of the original problem, whether it represents a condition worth detecting in this patient, and what the downstream plan would be if the next test is also ambiguous. Not every abnormality needs an explanation; some need only documentation and surveillance.

### Account for the Harms of Testing, Not Just Its Accuracy

Tests are evaluated on sensitivity and specificity, but the decision to test must also weigh the harms the test itself inflicts. CT scans deliver radiation and find incidentalomas; angiography carries vascular and contrast risk; biopsies bleed and can seed; broad panels return statistically abnormal values in healthy people. Weigh these harms against the value of the information. A more sensitive test is not always a better test if its false positives lead to harmful downstream procedures. When two strategies yield similar diagnostic accuracy, choose the one with fewer harms, less invasiveness, and lower cost. Make the harm calculus explicit for high-stakes or invasive tests: state what the test's complications are and why the information is worth that risk in this patient.

### Treat Empirically When the Probability Justifies It

When pre-test probability exceeds the treatment threshold, begin treatment without delaying for confirmatory testing, especially when the treatment is low-harm and the disease is time-sensitive or dangerous. Empirical antibiotics in suspected sepsis, anticoagulation in suspected pulmonary embolism while awaiting imaging, and steroids in suspected adrenal crisis are examples where waiting for confirmation costs more than treating. Conversely, when the probability is below the testing threshold and the patient is stable, observe rather than investigate or treat. The discipline is in resisting the reflex to order a test simply because uncertainty exists; some uncertainty is best managed with time and reassessment rather than intervention.

### Communicate Residual Uncertainty Honestly

Patients and colleagues deserve an honest account of what is and is not known. Avoid false certainty ("the tests rule it out") and false mystery ("we just can't tell"). Frame residual uncertainty in terms of what has been ruled out, what remains most likely, what would prompt return or escalation, and the planned reassessment. A patient who understands that a serious condition is unlikely but not impossible, and who knows the specific warning signs that should bring them back, is safer than one given either false reassurance or unexplained anxiety. Document the uncertainty and the safety-net plan so that the next clinician can continue the reasoning rather than restart it.

### Reassess Rather Than Accumulate Tests

When the initial workup is unrevealing but the presentation persists, the answer is rarely "more of the same tests." Reassess the differential, reconsider whether the question being asked is the right one, and look for what the pattern of negative results implies. A string of negative tests is itself information: it shifts probability away from the conditions tested and toward those not yet considered. Repeatedly reordering the same panel in hopes of a different result reflects diagnostic stuckness, not thoroughness. Build in a defined reassessment point and, if the diagnosis remains elusive, consider whether specialist input, a different framing of the problem, or time-limited observation is more informative than another round of testing.

## Common Traps

### Ordering Tests That Cannot Change the Plan

Ordering a test whose result will not alter management, either because you would treat regardless or would not treat regardless. Counter by stating the decision the test is meant to inform before ordering it.

### Chasing the Incidentaloma

Pursuing every incidental or borderline abnormal finding, launching cascades of progressively more invasive workup on findings unrelated to the presenting problem. Counter by asking whether the finding changes management and what the downstream plan would be before chasing it.

### Treating Greater Test Sensitivity as Always Better

Preferring the most sensitive test without weighing the false positives, incidental findings, and downstream harm it generates. Counter by weighing test harms alongside accuracy and choosing the least harmful adequate strategy.

### Failing to Treat Empirically When Probability Is High

Delaying low-harm, time-sensitive treatment while awaiting confirmatory testing that will not change the plan. Counter by recognizing when pre-test probability exceeds the treatment threshold.

### Repeating the Same Tests Instead of Reassessing

Reordering an unrevealing panel hoping for a different result rather than revisiting the differential. Counter by treating a string of negatives as information that shifts the reasoning.

### Conveying False Certainty or False Mystery

Telling patients a condition is definitively excluded when probability is merely reduced, or leaving them with unexplained uncertainty and no safety net. Counter by communicating what is ruled out, what remains likely, and the specific signs that warrant return.

## Self-Check

- For each test I plan to order, have I stated the decision its result is meant to inform, and confirmed the result could move the patient across a treatment threshold?
- Is the patient's pre-test probability in the zone where testing is useful, rather than clearly below testing threshold or clearly above treatment threshold?
- For any incidental or borderline finding, have I asked whether chasing it changes management and what the cascade would look like before pursuing it?
- Have I weighed the direct and indirect harms of each test, not just its accuracy, and chosen the least harmful adequate strategy?
- Where pre-test probability is high and treatment is low-harm and time-sensitive, am I treating empirically rather than delaying for confirmation?
- Have I communicated residual uncertainty honestly — what is ruled out, what remains likely, and the warning signs that should prompt return?
- When the workup is unrevealing, am I reassessing the differential rather than repeating the same tests?
