# Prognostication and Illness Severity Assessment

Prognostication is the act of predicting the future course of an illness, and it is one of the clinician's most difficult and most consequential judgments. Severity scores and prognostic models promise objectivity, but they are statistical summaries of populations, not verdicts on individuals. The danger is twofold: false certainty that a number tells you what will happen to this patient, and false mystery that nothing can be known. Both harm patients — the first by driving premature withdrawal of care or false reassurance, the second by denying patients and families the information they need to make decisions aligned with their values. Prognostication also carries an emotional weight: a prediction can become self-fulfilling when it changes the intensity of care delivered. This skill covers the disciplined use of severity scoring, the communication of prognostic uncertainty, and the linkage of prognosis to goals of care. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when prognostic uncertainty is high or goals-of-care decisions are at stake, seek specialist and palliative-care consultation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Severity Scores as Population Estimates, Not Individual Predictions

Scores such as APACHE II/IV, SOFA, NEWS2, qSOFA, CURB-65, and the Glasgow Coma Scale summarize average outcomes in the populations used to derive them. They quantify illness severity in a comparable way and support research, audit, and triage communication — but they do not determine what will happen to the patient in front of you. A SOFA score does not dictate mortality; it estimates risk in a cohort. Apply scores to inform the conversation and the plan, never to replace clinical judgment. Understand each score's derivation population and its limitations: APACHE was built on ICU admissions and behaves poorly on general wards; qSOFA was retired as a sepsis screening tool because it misses early sepsis; GCS is reproducible within a rater but unstable across raters and in intubated or sedated patients. State explicitly, when using a score, what it does and does not tell you about this individual.

### Anchor Prognosis in Trajectory and Response, Not a Single Snapshot

A single time-point estimate is fragile. Trajectory — the direction and rate of change over hours and days — is far more informative than any cross-sectional score. A patient whose organ support requirements are falling is on a different arc than one whose are rising, even at identical SOFA scores today. Layer in response to treatment: improvement after appropriate therapy predicts a different outcome than failure to respond. Build prognostication around repeated measurements and the slope of change, and revise the estimate as the trajectory reveals itself. The patients whose prognosis is most often misjudged are those judged once and never re-estimated; force a re-prognostication at defined intervals for any serious illness.

### Adjust for Baseline, Frailty, and Comorbidity

Severity scores assume a representative baseline and routinely mislead at the extremes of age, frailty, disability, and comorbidity. An eighty-five-year-old with significant frailty does not have the same reserve or expected outcome as the score predicts for an average adult; conversely, a young athlete may score low on severity while deteriorating. Incorporate validated frailty measures (e.g., Clinical Frailty Scale), baseline functional status, and advanced comorbidity indices into the prognostic synthesis. Do not apply a score derived on younger, fitter populations to an older frail patient without explicit caveat. The same physiological derangement means different things in different bodies.

### Communicate Prognosis as Probabilities and Ranges, Not Verdicts

Patients and families deserve honest prognostic information, but honesty does not require false precision. Communicate prognosis as a range of likely outcomes with explicit uncertainty: "most people in this situation improve, but some do not, and I cannot yet tell which group you will be in." Avoid single-number predictions presented as certainties ("you have three months"), which are frequently wrong and damage trust when contradicted by the course. Offer best-case, worst-case, and most-likely scenarios, and connect each to what would be done in response. Tailor the framing to what the patient wants to know — some want numbers, some want direction, some want to defer — and check understanding rather than assuming the message landed. When the prognosis is genuinely uncertain, say so plainly; "I don't know yet, and here is what would change my estimate" is more honest and more useful than a confident guess.

### Link Prognosis to Goals of Care, Not to a Default Path

Prognosis is clinically meaningful chiefly because it informs what care aligns with the patient's goals. A poor prognosis does not by itself dictate withdrawal; it triggers a goals-of-care conversation. Conversely, a favorable prognosis does not mandate maximal intervention if the patient's values point elsewhere. Separate the prognostic estimate (what is likely to happen) from the care decision (what to do about it), and ensure the latter is driven by the patient's expressed values, advance directives, and surrogate input. When prognosis is uncertain, favor time-limited trials of intensive therapy with predefined reassessment rather than irreversible commitments in either direction. This is educational not medical advice; for complex goals-of-care decisions, involve palliative care, ethics, and the patient's established clinicians.

### Beware the Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

A prognostic estimate can shape the outcome it claims only to predict. When clinicians believe a patient will die, they may reduce monitoring, defer escalation, or shift to comfort care earlier — and the patient dies, confirming the prediction. This is not always wrong; aligning care with prognosis is appropriate. But the clinician must distinguish "this patient is dying despite optimal care" from "this patient is dying because we stopped trying." Examine whether the prognostic estimate is based on disease trajectory or on resource and morale factors, and be explicit about which. Re-evaluate the basis for the estimate before any major shift away from active treatment.

### Recognize When to Seek Specialist and Palliative Input

Prognostication in serious illness is a specialized skill. Seek palliative care consultation for complex symptom burden, difficult goals-of-care conversations, prognostic uncertainty in advanced disease, and any case where the team and family are misaligned. Seek disease-specialist input for condition-specific prognostication (oncology, advanced heart failure, neurodegeneration) where course patterns are well characterized. Escalate prognostic uncertainty rather than carrying it alone when the stakes are high.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Score as a Verdict

Reading a severity score as a deterministic prediction of death or survival for the individual. Counter by treating scores as population estimates and layering clinical judgment, trajectory, and baseline.

### Single-Point Prognostication

Estimating prognosis once and never revising it as the course unfolds. Counter by building in forced re-prognostication at intervals and weighting trajectory over snapshot.

### Ignoring Frailty and Baseline

Applying scores derived on fit populations to frail, elderly, or highly comorbid patients without adjustment. Counter by incorporating frailty measures and baseline function into the synthesis.

### False Precision in Communication

Offering single-number survival estimates as certainties, which erode trust when contradicted. Counter by communicating ranges, best/worst/most-likely scenarios, and explicit uncertainty.

### Self-Fulfilling Pessimism

Reducing the intensity of care based on a pessimistic estimate, then citing the resulting death as confirmation. Counter by separating disease trajectory from care-team morale and re-examining the basis before withdrawing.

### Prognosis Without Goals Linkage

Treating a poor prognosis as an automatic trigger to withdraw, or a good one as an automatic mandate to intensify. Counter by separating the estimate from the decision and driving care from the patient's values.

### Overconfident Disease-Specific Prediction

Applying population survival statistics to an individual without considering their unique modifiers. Counter by seeking specialist input for condition-specific prognostication in serious illness.

## Self-Check

- Did I treat the severity score as a population estimate, stating its derivation population and limitations, rather than as a verdict on this individual?
- Did I anchor prognosis in trajectory and response to treatment, with repeated measurements, rather than a single snapshot?
- Did I adjust the prognostic synthesis for age, frailty (e.g., Clinical Frailty Scale), comorbidity, and baseline function?
- Did I communicate prognosis as ranges and scenarios with explicit uncertainty, checking understanding, rather than as single-number certainties?
- Did I separate the prognostic estimate from the goals-of-care decision, and drive the latter from the patient's values and directives?
- Did I examine whether my estimate reflects disease trajectory or care-team morale, guarding against a self-fulfilling prophecy?
- Did I seek specialist and palliative-care input for complex, uncertain, or high-stakes prognostication and goals-of-care decisions?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
