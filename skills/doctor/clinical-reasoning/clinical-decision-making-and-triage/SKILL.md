# Clinical Decision-Making and Triage

Acute clinical decision-making is where speed, uncertainty, and stakes collide. In time-pressured situations the clinician must decide who is sick now, who can wait, who needs the next bed, and who can be sent home safely — often with incomplete information and under cognitive load. The danger is not slowness alone; it is confident action on the wrong priority. A stable-looking patient who is quietly deteriorating, a reassuring vital sign that masks compensation, a discharge decision made to free a bed — each can cause preventable harm. Triage and disposition are not administrative tasks; they are clinical judgments with life-or-death consequences. This skill covers the disciplined process of acuity assessment, structured prioritization, disposition decisions, and the cognitive management of high-pressure environments. This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; follow institutional protocols and escalate when red flags appear.

## Core Rules

### Assess Acuity Before Diagnosis

In acute care, the first question is not "what is the diagnosis" but "how sick is this patient, and how fast." Acuity assessment must precede diagnostic workup because a well-oxygenated, perfused, alert patient can tolerate investigation, while a patient with airway compromise, respiratory failure, or shock cannot. Use a structured primary survey (ABCDE — Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure) to identify immediate threats to life in order, and treat them in order as found. Do not skip to history-taking while the airway is unprotected or perfusion is failing. Validated triage systems (e.g., ESI, ATS, MTS) and early-warning scores (MEWS, NEWS2, PEWS for children) provide a common language for acuity and reduce reliance on gut feeling, but they are decision support, not decision makers. A normal early-warning score does not exclude a dangerous condition; it only means the patient is not yet scoring. When the score and your impression disagree, trust the clinical picture and reassess frequently.

### Use Structured Tools to Reduce Cognitive Load Under Pressure

Time pressure degrades reasoning. Structured tools exist to externalize thinking so it does not collapse under stress. Use SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) for handovers and escalation calls so that critical information is transmitted completely and in the right order. Use ABCDE for resuscitation so that life threats are addressed in priority sequence rather than by what catches the eye. Use a structured disposition framework (e.g., the "sick/not sick" call followed by admit/observe/discharge criteria) so that the decision is explicit rather than drifted into. These tools are not bureaucratic overhead; they are cognitive prosthetics for moments when human memory and sequencing fail. When you feel rushed or overwhelmed, that is precisely the moment to reach for the structure rather than abandon it.

### Make Disposition Decisions Explicit and Threshold-Based

Admission, observation, and discharge are not default settings; each is a decision with explicit criteria and risks. State the disposition decision and the reasoning behind it. Admission is warranted when the patient requires monitoring, treatment, or expertise unavailable as an outpatient, when the trajectory is uncertain or potentially deteriorating, or when safe follow-up cannot be assured. Observation is appropriate for patients who may stabilize within a short window and whose risk is low enough to permit reassessment rather than full admission, but it requires a defined reassessment time and clear failure criteria. Discharge is safe only when the diagnosis or risk profile is compatible with outpatient management, the patient can access follow-up, and a credible safety-net plan exists — specific return precautions, who to contact, and what symptoms should bring them back. Never discharge to free capacity; discharge because the patient is safe to go. When capacity is strained, escalate the resource decision to a senior clinician rather than absorbing it silently into a clinical disposition.

### Allocate Limited Resources Transparently and Ethically

When resources (beds, staff, equipment, time) are limited, allocation becomes a clinical and ethical act. Triage under scarcity should follow explicit, defensible principles: maximize benefit, treat people equally, prioritize the worst-off where rescue is possible, and avoid allocating based on subjective social worth. Use objective severity and likelihood-of-benefit criteria rather than intuition or who is most vocal. Document the triage rationale so it can be reviewed. Recognize that allocation decisions under scarcity are a known driver of moral injury; do not make them in isolation — involve a second clinician or an ethics resource when the call is close or the stakes are high. This is educational not medical advice; follow your institution's mass-casualty or surge protocol and escalate to incident command when activated.

### Know When to Consult and Escalate Early

A common and dangerous error is delaying consultation to "handle it myself." Define, in advance, the conditions and thresholds that require specialist input: the must-not-miss diagnoses, the procedures outside your scope, the unstable patient who may need intensive care, and any case where your confidence has dropped below a safe margin. Escalation is not failure; it is appropriate use of expertise. When escalating, use a structured handover (SBAR), state the specific question, and convey urgency explicitly rather than hoping the listener infers it. A vague "can you take a look" understates a deteriorating patient; a clear "I have a hypotensive patient with suspected sepsis who is not responding to fluids, I need ICU assessment now" gets the right response. Seek immediate specialist consultation whenever a patient meets red-flag criteria or fails to respond as expected to initial management.

### Reassess Continuously and Build in Forced Re-evaluation

Triage and disposition are not one-time decisions; they are hypotheses that must be retested. A patient who was stable may deteriorate; a patient assigned low acuity may reveal dangerous features on fuller assessment. Build in forced reassessment at defined intervals for any patient with potential to worsen, and explicitly hand over the reassessment responsibility at shift change. Use trending of vital signs and early-warning scores rather than single values — the direction of change is often more informative than the absolute number. The most dangerous assumption in acute care is that a patient who looked well will continue to look well. Plan for the possibility of deterioration: who will reassess, when, and what will trigger escalation.

### Manage Your Own Cognitive State

Fatigue, hunger, interruption, and emotional charge all degrade clinical judgment. Recognize the factors degrading your reasoning in real time and counter them: take a structured pause before high-stakes decisions, use checklists when distracted, and ask a colleague to verify critical calls when you are impaired. Interruption during prescribing and procedure setup is a documented cause of error; create a protected zone for these tasks. The clinician who acknowledges "I am too tired to make this call safely" and escalates is making a better decision than one who pushes through silently.

## Common Traps

### Treating Normal Vitals as Reassurance

Accepting normal vital signs as proof of stability when the patient is compensating. Tachycardia, narrowed pulse pressure, and tachypnea often precede hypotension; their absence early does not guarantee safety. Counter by reassessing trends and trusting clinical concern over a single normal reading; escalate when the patient "looks sick" regardless of numbers.

### Discharging to Free Capacity

Lowering the admission threshold to what the system can absorb rather than what the patient needs. Counter by separating the clinical disposition decision from the resource decision, and escalating the resource conflict to a senior clinician.

### Drifting Into Disposition Without Criteria

Allowing the decision to admit, observe, or discharge to emerge by default rather than by explicit criteria. Counter by stating the disposition and the reasoning, and by requiring a credible safety-net plan before any discharge.

### Delaying Consultation to Save Face

Avoiding escalation because it feels like failure or because the specialist is difficult to reach. Counter by predefining the conditions that require consultation and treating early escalation as appropriate, not weak. Seek immediate specialist consultation when red flags appear.

### Abandoning Structure Under Time Pressure

Dropping ABCDE, SBAR, and checklists exactly when they are most needed because the situation feels too urgent. Counter by recognizing that structure is a cognitive prosthetic for high-pressure moments, not a luxury for calm ones.

### Single-Point Triage Without Reassessment

Assigning an acuity level once and never revisiting it. Counter by building forced reassessment intervals and handing over the reassessment responsibility explicitly.

### Subjective Resource Allocation

Allocating scarce beds or attention based on who is loudest or most sympathetic rather than objective severity and benefit. Counter by using explicit triage criteria, documenting the rationale, and involving a second clinician for close calls.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess acuity (ABCDE, early-warning score) before pursuing diagnosis, and did I treat life threats in priority order?
- For each disposition decision (admit, observe, discharge), did I state explicit criteria and reasoning rather than drifting into a default?
- Before any discharge, did I establish a credible safety-net plan — specific return precautions, follow-up, and warning symptoms?
- Did I use structured tools (ABCDE, SBAR, checklists) under pressure rather than abandoning them when rushed?
- Did I define and act on the thresholds that require specialist consultation or escalation, and did I escalate early rather than late?
- Did I reassess the patient at defined intervals and hand over reassessment responsibility, rather than treating the initial triage as final?
- Did I separate clinical disposition from resource allocation, and escalate resource conflicts rather than absorbing them into clinical decisions?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
