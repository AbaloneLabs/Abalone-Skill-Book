# Thrombosis and Anticoagulation Management

Anticoagulation is a high-risk therapy where benefit and harm are tightly balanced and where small errors cause catastrophic bleeding or fatal thrombosis. The judgment problem is not recalling dose tables — it is selecting the right agent for the right patient given renal and hepatic function, age, weight, and comorbidity; recognizing and reversing life-threatening bleeding; detecting heparin-induced thrombocytopenia before it causes limb- or life-threatening thrombosis; and knowing when bridging, thrombophilia testing, or hematology escalation is warranted. Agents tend to err by applying a single preferred agent to all patients, by missing the falling platelet count that signals heparin-induced thrombocytopenia, by overusing bridging, and by delaying reversal in major bleeding. This skill covers the disciplined selection and monitoring of anticoagulants, bleeding and thrombosis-on-anticoagulation management, thrombophilia reasoning, heparin-induced thrombocytopenia recognition, and the escalation points that must not be delayed.

## Core Rules

### Match the Anticoagulant to the Patient, Not to Habit

Direct oral anticoagulants (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) are first-line for most patients with venous thromboembolism and non-valvular atrial fibrillation because they have predictable pharmacokinetics, fewer food and drug interactions, and lower intracranial bleeding risk than warfarin, and require no routine monitoring. But DOAC selection requires renal and hepatic adjustment: dabigatran is predominantly renally cleared and accumulates in significant renal impairment, apixaban and rivaroxaban need dose reduction in specific combinations of renal function, age, weight, and indication, and DOACs are inappropriate or unvalidated in mechanical heart valves, severe renal failure, antiphospholipid syndrome with high-risk profile, and pregnancy. Warfarin remains the agent for mechanical valves, severe renal impairment where DOACs are unsuitable, and selected antiphospholipid syndrome, and requires INR monitoring with attention to diet, medications, and intercurrent illness. State explicitly why the chosen agent fits this patient's renal, hepatic, weight, age, and indication profile; never default to a single agent without that reasoning.

### Adjust for Renal, Hepatic, Age, and Weight Factors

Renal function is the single most important modifier of anticoagulant dosing and bleeding risk, and it changes over time — recheck creatinine clearance at defined intervals, especially in older adults and the chronically ill. Low body weight increases accumulation and bleeding risk for many agents; extremes of weight (over 120 kilograms or under 50) may alter DOAC pharmacokinetics and warrant specialist input. Advanced age, frailty, anemia, and concurrent antiplatelet or non-steroidal anti-inflammatory therapy multiply bleeding risk and may shift the choice or dose. Liver disease affects both warfarin metabolism and vitamin K-dependent factor synthesis, complicating management. Document the calculated creatinine clearance and the dose adjustment rationale at initiation and at intervals, and reconsider the agent when renal function crosses thresholds or the patient's clinical status changes significantly.

### Recognize and Manage Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia Promptly

Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) is a prothrombotic, antibody-mediated adverse effect that causes platelet fall and paradoxical thrombosis — both venous and arterial — and it is missed because the platelet count may not fall dramatically and thrombosis can be delayed. Apply the 4Ts score (platelet fall timing and magnitude, timing relative to heparin exposure, thrombosis or skin necrosis, and alternative causes) to estimate pre-test probability; an intermediate or high score demands stopping all heparin (including flushes and heparin-coated lines) and starting a non-heparin parenteral anticoagulant such as argatroban, bivalirudin, or fondaparinux while sending confirmatory antibody testing. Do not wait for the antibody result to act in a high-probability case, because thrombosis can occur while you wait. Platelet transfusion is generally avoided in HIT unless life-threatening bleeding. Warfarin must not be started until the platelet count recovers, as premature warfarin can cause skin necrosis and venous limb gangrene. Escalate to hematology for confirmation and management of complex cases.

### Manage Major Bleeding with Immediate Reversal and Resuscitation

Life-threatening or critical-organ bleeding on anticoagulation requires simultaneous resuscitation and reversal. Stop the anticoagulant, support the airway and circulation, control the bleeding source with the relevant specialty (endoscopy, interventional radiology, surgery), and administer the specific reversal agent: idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa or four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate for factor Xa inhibitors (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban), prothrombin complex concentrate plus vitamin K for warfarin, and protamine for heparin. For warfarin with major bleeding, give vitamin K intravenously alongside prothrombin complex concentrate for sustained reversal. Know the time since last dose, because reversal efficacy falls as the drug clears. For non-major bleeding, consider holding doses, local measures, and timing rather than full reversal. Document the reversal agent, dose, and rationale, and plan anticoagulation resumption once bleeding is controlled — most patients need to restart within days, balancing re-thrombosis against re-bleeding.

### Handle Thrombosis Despite Anticoagulation as a Red Flag

New or extending thrombosis despite therapeutic anticoagulation demands reassessment, not simply dose escalation. Confirm adherence and therapeutic levels (anti-Xa or INR as appropriate), check for malabsorption or interacting medications, evaluate for heparin-induced thrombocytopenia if on heparin, consider antiphospholipid syndrome or malignancy as drivers, and assess whether the agent is appropriate for the patient (renal function, weight). Switching agent class, intensifying to therapeutic low-molecular-weight heparin, or investigating for an underlying prothrombotic state may be indicated, in consultation with hematology. Cancer-associated thrombosis has specific management — low-molecular-weight heparin or selected DOACs depending on the cancer, interactions, and bleeding risk, with attention to thrombocytopenia and drug interactions. Escalate recurrent or extensive thrombosis to hematology for evaluation of refractory disease and underlying causes.

### Use Thrombophilia Testing Selectively, Not Reflexively

Thrombophilia testing is overused and frequently misinterpreted. It rarely changes management in unprovoked venous thromboembolism (decisions on duration are based on bleeding risk, recurrence risk, and patient preference), and results are confounded by acute thrombosis, anticoagulation, and inflammation — protein C, protein S, and antithrombin levels are unreliable during the acute event and on warfarin. Reserve testing for young patients with unprovoked thrombosis, recurrent thrombosis, thrombosis at unusual sites, or a strong family history, and time it appropriately (often weeks after the event and off confounding anticoagulants). Antiphospholipid syndrome testing (lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin, beta-2 glycoprotein I) is the exception that does change management, as high-risk triple-positive patients are often managed with warfarin. Do not let thrombophilia testing delay anticoagulation, and interpret results with hematology input.

### Decide on Bridging and Periprocedural Management Deliberately

Bridging with heparin around warfarin interruption is overused and increases bleeding; reserve it for patients at high thrombotic risk (mechanical mitral valve, recent venous thromboembolism, high-risk antiphospholipid syndrome) and avoid it for low-risk indications such as atrial fibrillation without other risk factors. For DOACs, the predictable half-life usually allows simple cessation and resumption around procedures without bridging, timing based on renal function and bleeding risk of the procedure. Coordinate timing with the proceduralist, document the plan, and have a clear resumption strategy. For emergent surgery on anticoagulated patients, coordinate reversal with anesthesia and surgery, weighing bleeding against thrombotic risk. When complexity exceeds routine pathways, escalate to hematology or the periprocedural clinic.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting to One DOAC for All Patients

Applying a preferred agent without renal, hepatic, age, and weight adjustment causes accumulation, underdosing, or inappropriate use in contraindicated settings such as mechanical valves. Counter by documenting the patient-specific rationale for agent and dose selection at initiation and at intervals.

### Missing the Falling Platelet Count of HIT

HIT is missed because the platelet fall may be moderate and thrombosis delayed; the diagnosis must be considered in any patient on heparin whose platelets drop. Counter by checking platelet counts regularly in heparin-exposed patients and applying the 4Ts score to a falling count.

### Delaying Reversal in Major Bleeding

Waiting for levels or over-relying on nonspecific measures while a patient bleeds on anticoagulation costs lives. Counter by administering the specific reversal agent immediately in major or critical-organ bleeding while resuscitating and locating the source.

### Overusing Bridging Anticoagulation

Routine bridging for atrial fibrillation or low-risk venous thromboembolism increases bleeding without reducing thrombosis. Counter by reserving bridging for high-thrombotic-risk patients and using simple cessation-resumption for most DOAC interruptions.

### Misinterpreting Thrombophilia Panels

Testing in the acute setting or on warfarin yields unreliable results, and positive results rarely change duration decisions. Counter by reserving testing for selected patients, timing it appropriately, and interpreting with hematology input.

### Simply Escalating Dose for Thrombosis on Anticoagulation

New thrombosis despite therapy demands reassessment of adherence, levels, HIT, and underlying drivers — not blind dose increase. Counter by systematically evaluating causes and escalating to hematology for refractory disease.

### Forgetting Cancer-Associated Thrombosis Specifics

Cancer patients have different recurrence risk, thrombocytopenia, and drug interactions that change agent choice and duration. Counter by following cancer-associated thrombosis pathways and coordinating with oncology and hematology.

## Self-Check

- Have I selected the anticoagulant based on this patient's renal, hepatic, age, weight, and indication profile, with the rationale documented, rather than defaulting to habit?
- Have I calculated and documented creatinine clearance and dose adjustments, and planned rechecks at intervals?
- For any patient on heparin with a falling platelet count, have I applied the 4Ts score, considered HIT, stopped all heparin, and started a non-heparin anticoagulant while awaiting confirmatory testing?
- For major or critical-organ bleeding, have I stopped the anticoagulant, resuscitated, and administered the specific reversal agent (idarucizumab, andexanet or prothrombin complex concentrate, vitamin K, protamine) without delay?
- For thrombosis despite anticoagulation, have I reassessed adherence, levels, HIT, and underlying drivers rather than simply escalating dose, and escalated to hematology for refractory disease?
- Have I reserved bridging for high-thrombotic-risk patients and used simple cessation-resumption for most DOAC periprocedural management?
- Have I used thrombophilia testing selectively and appropriately timed, recognizing that antiphospholipid syndrome is the result that most changes management?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
