# Oncologic Emergencies and Cancer Care Coordination

Cancer care is a domain where time-sensitive emergencies sit alongside long, complex multidisciplinary pathways, and where the agent's role spans the entire trajectory from acute presentation through treatment toxicity to survivorship. The judgment problem is not recalling the list of named emergencies — it is recognizing that a febrile neutropenic patient is a same-hour emergency, that back pain in a cancer patient is malignant spinal cord compression until proven otherwise, that immunotherapy toxicity mimics common disease and can be fatal if missed, and that cancer care coordination is itself a safety-critical task where dropped handoffs cause real harm. Agents tend to err by applying routine management to immunocompromised patients, by attributing neurologic symptoms to degenerative disease in patients with cancer, and by treating the acute episode in isolation from the multidisciplinary plan. This skill covers the disciplined recognition and management of oncologic emergencies, treatment toxicity including immunotherapy adverse effects, and the coordination and survivorship decisions that define good cancer care.

## Core Rules

### Treat Febrile Neutropenia as a Same-Hour Emergency

Fever in a patient with neutrophils below 0.5 times ten to the ninth per liter (or expected to fall to that level after recent chemotherapy) is a medical emergency in which delay in antibiotics measurably increases mortality. Take cultures (blood, urine, relevant site) but do not wait for results or imaging to give empiric broad-spectrum antipseudomonal intravenous antibiotics within the first hour; use a validated risk-stratification tool where appropriate to distinguish low-risk patients suitable for oral or outpatient management from high-risk patients requiring admission and intravenous therapy. Assess for hemodynamic instability, source of infection, and signs of sepsis, and escalate to critical care for organ dysfunction. Document the time from presentation to antibiotics — this is a audited quality metric for good reason. Neutropenic sepsis does not present with the usual signs of infection because the neutrophil response is absent; a low threshold for evaluation and treatment is essential, and escalation to oncology or the acute oncology service must be immediate.

### Recognize Malignant Spinal Cord Compression Before Neurologic Loss

Back pain in a patient with known cancer — especially breast, prostate, lung, myeloma, or any cancer prone to bone metastasis — is malignant spinal cord compression until proven otherwise when accompanied by radicular pain, nocturnal pain, or any neurologic symptom. The window for salvage is narrow: once motor deficit, sensory level, or sphincter dysfunction develops, the likelihood of recovering ambulation falls sharply. Urgent whole-spine MRI is the diagnostic standard for suspected cord compression; do not rely on plain films or delayed imaging. Give high-dose dexamethasone immediately on suspicion while arranging imaging, and involve clinical oncology and neurosurgery urgently for consideration of radiotherapy or surgical decompression. A patient who can still walk at diagnosis is far more likely to remain ambulatory, which is why early recognition before neurologic loss is the single most important determinant of outcome. Treat any new or progressive back pain in a cancer patient as a red flag requiring escalation.

### Manage Tumor Lysis Syndrome Prophylactically in High-Risk Patients

Tumor lysis syndrome is a metabolic emergency (hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, hyperuricemia, and acute kidney injury) caused by rapid tumor cell breakdown, most often after initiating cytotoxic therapy for high-tumor-burden, high-cell-turnover malignancies such as acute leukemias and high-grade lymphomas, but also in rapidly responding solid tumors. The key principle is prevention: risk-stratify before treatment, provide aggressive hydration, start urate-lowering therapy (allopurinol or rasburicase depending on risk level), and monitor electrolytes, renal function, and uric acid frequently during the at-risk window. Recognize established tumor lysis as an emergency requiring renal and hematology input, aggressive fluid and electrolyte management (especially hyperkalemia and hypocalcemia with cardiac monitoring), and renal replacement therapy in severe cases. Do not assume tumor lysis only occurs with hematologic malignancy — high-burden, treatment-sensitive tumors of any type warrant prophylactic reasoning.

### Address Hypercalcemia of Malignancy and Superior Vena Cava Syndrome Promptly

Hypercalcemia of malignancy, whether from paraneoplastic parathyroid hormone-related peptide secretion or extensive bone metastasis, presents with confusion, polyuria, dehydration, and renal impairment and can be fatal. Treat with aggressive isotonic saline rehydration followed by bisphosphonate or denosumab, with attention to the underlying malignancy. Avoid routine furosemide in the under-resuscitated patient. Superior vena cava syndrome, from mediastinal tumor or thrombosis compressing the SVC, presents with facial and upper-limb swelling, distended neck and chest-wall veins, dyspnea, and, in severe cases, airway compromise or cerebral venous congestion. It is an emergency when airway or cerebral flow is threatened; involve oncology, interventional radiology, and respiratory teams urgently for stenting, thrombolysis or anticoagulation if catheter-related, and definitive oncologic treatment. Recognize that these syndromes often coexist with other oncologic emergencies and require coordinated multidisciplinary escalation.

### Recognize and Manage Immunotherapy Immune-Related Adverse Events

Immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy has transformed oncology, but it causes immune-related adverse events that can affect any organ and mimic common disease: colitis and diarrhea, pneumonitis, hepatitis, endocrinopathy (thyroiditis, hypophysitis, type 1 diabetes, adrenal insufficiency), nephritis, dermatitis, myocarditis, and neurologic toxicity. The critical judgment is to attribute new symptoms to immunotherapy rather than infection or routine side effects, because the treatment is immune suppression (corticosteroids, and for severe cases other immunosuppressants) plus holding the agent — the opposite of the usual approach to infection. Suspect immune-related adverse events in any patient on checkpoint inhibitors presenting with new organ-specific symptoms, grade severity, investigate appropriately, and escalate to oncology early because severe events (pneumonitis, myocarditis, severe colitis) can be fatal. Have a low threshold to check cortisol and thyroid function in unwell immunotherapy patients, because adrenal crisis and severe hypothyroidism are easily missed and rapidly treatable. Counsel patients to report symptoms early and carry an alert card identifying their immunotherapy.

### Coordinate Multidisciplinary Cancer Care as a Safety-Critical Task

Cancer care involves surgeons, medical and radiation oncologists, radiologists, pathologists, specialist nurses, palliative care, primary care, and allied health, and the patient moves between them along time-pressured pathways. Coordination failures — delayed referral, lost results, unclear ownership of a decision, treatment started without staging — cause real harm. Use multidisciplinary team meetings for treatment decisions, ensure staging is complete before definitive treatment where possible, communicate the plan and the responsible clinician clearly to the patient and to primary care, and document who owns each next step and by when. Track time-critical windows (urgent referral targets, treatment initiation intervals) explicitly. For patients on systemic therapy, ensure toxicity monitoring, dose adjustments, and supportive care are owned by a named team. Survivorship requires a defined transition: a care plan, surveillance schedule, management of long-term effects (cardiotoxicity, neuropathy, osteoporosis, secondary malignancy risk, psychological sequelae), and clear re-entry pathways if symptoms recur.

### Escalate Early and Coordinate Across Specialties

Oncologic emergencies rarely respect single-organ boundaries, and escalation must be rapid and multidisciplinary. Suspected neutropenic sepsis, malignant spinal cord compression, severe immunotherapy toxicity, tumor lysis with metabolic derangement, and airway-threatening superior vena cava syndrome require same-hour involvement of oncology, the relevant organ specialty, and often critical care. Communicate the cancer type, recent treatments (especially timing of chemotherapy and immunotherapy), performance status, and goals of care, because these reshape management decisions. For patients with advanced disease, escalate in parallel with palliative care involvement so that symptom control and goals-of-care conversations are not delayed until after acute stabilization. When red flags appear, escalate immediately and follow institutional oncologic emergency pathways.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Antibiotics for Workup in Febrile Neutropenia

Waiting for cultures or imaging before antibiotics in a neutropenic febrile patient increases mortality. Counter by giving empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics within the first hour after cultures, before further workup.

### Attributing Back Pain in Cancer to Degenerative Disease

Routine back pain management in a cancer patient misses malignant spinal cord compression until neurologic loss occurs. Counter by treating new or progressive back pain in a cancer patient as a red flag and arranging urgent whole-spine MRI with early dexamethasone.

### Failing to Prevent Tumor Lysis in High-Risk Patients

Treating high-burden, treatment-sensitive tumors without prophylactic hydration and urate-lowering therapy leads to preventable metabolic emergency and renal failure. Counter by risk-stratifying before treatment and providing prophylaxis with close monitoring.

### Misattributing Immunotherapy Toxicity to Infection

Diarrhea, dyspnea, or endocrine symptoms on checkpoint inhibitors are often treated as infection while immune-related adverse events progress. Counter by suspecting immune-related adverse events in any unwell immunotherapy patient, checking endocrine panels, and escalating to oncology for steroid-based management.

### Under-Resuscitating Hypercalcemia Before Bisphosphonate

Giving bisphosphonate or furosemide before adequate saline rehydration worsens renal injury. Counter by rehydrating aggressively with isotonic saline first, then adding bisphosphonate or denosumab.

### Losing Ownership of the Care Plan Across Specialties

Ambiguity about which team owns the next step leads to delayed referral, lost results, and missed surveillance. Counter by documenting the responsible clinician and timeline for each step and communicating the plan to the patient and primary care.

### Delaying Palliative Care Until After Acute Stabilization

Waiting to involve palliative care until the acute episode resolves forfeits symptom control and goals-of-care conversations. Counter by escalating palliative care in parallel with acute management in advanced disease.

## Self-Check

- For any febrile patient with neutropenia (or recent chemotherapy), have I given empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics within the first hour after cultures and escalated to oncology or the acute oncology service?
- For any cancer patient with new or progressive back pain or neurologic symptoms, have I treated malignant spinal cord compression as a red flag, given dexamethasone, and arranged urgent whole-spine MRI with oncology and neurosurgery involvement?
- For high-risk tumors, have I risk-stratified for tumor lysis and provided prophylactic hydration and urate-lowering therapy with close electrolyte and renal monitoring?
- For hypercalcemia of malignancy, have I rehydrated aggressively with isotonic saline before bisphosphonate or denosumab, and for superior vena cava syndrome with airway or cerebral compromise, have I escalated urgently to oncology and interventional specialties?
- For any unwell patient on checkpoint inhibitors, have I considered immune-related adverse events, checked endocrine panels, and escalated to oncology for steroid-based management rather than assuming infection?
- Have I coordinated the multidisciplinary plan with clear ownership, timelines, and communication to the patient and primary care, and tracked time-critical referral and treatment windows?
- Have I involved palliative care in parallel with acute management for advanced disease rather than waiting until after stabilization?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
