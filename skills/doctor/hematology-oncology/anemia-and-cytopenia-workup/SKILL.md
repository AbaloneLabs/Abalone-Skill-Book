# Anemia and Cytopenia Workup

Anemia and cytopenia are among the most common abnormal laboratory findings, and they are where both over-investigation and dangerous under-recognition occur. The judgment problem is not memorizing a morphology table — it is using the reticulocyte count to separate underproduction from destruction or loss, interpreting iron studies without being misled by inflammation, recognizing that pancytopenia and severe isolated cytopenias carry a different urgency than mild chronic anemia, and knowing when a patient needs transfusion, bone marrow biopsy, or urgent hematology referral. Agents tend to err by treating every low hemoglobin as iron deficiency without confirming the cause, by missing hemolysis behind a "low hemoglobin" label, and by under-recognizing the sepsis and bleeding risk that accompany severe cytopenias. This skill covers the disciplined morphologic and kinetic approach to anemia and cytopenia, the interpretation of iron, hemolytic, and nutritional workups, transfusion reasoning, and the escalation thresholds that must not be delayed.

## Core Rules

### Anchor the Anemia Workup on Mean Cell Volume and Reticulocyte Count

Classify anemia by cell size first (microcytic, normocytic, macrocytic) and then by the reticulocyte response, which distinguishes underproduction from destruction or loss. A low reticulocyte count in an anemic patient indicates underproduction — bone marrow failure, iron or nutrient deficiency, chronic inflammation, or marrow infiltration — whereas a high reticulocyte count indicates the marrow is responding appropriately to hemolysis or acute blood loss. This single binary (inappropriately low versus appropriately high reticulocyte response) reshapes the entire investigation pathway and prevents the common error of reflexively giving iron to every anemic patient. Always interpret the reticulocyte count in absolute terms corrected for the degree of anemia, not just the percentage, because the percentage rises artifactually as hematocrit falls. State the kinetic category explicitly before ordering the next tier of tests.

### Interpret Iron Studies with Inflammation in Mind

Iron studies are frequently misread because ferritin is an acute-phase reactant. A normal or high ferritin does not exclude iron deficiency in the setting of inflammation, infection, liver disease, or malignancy; a low ferritin is specific for iron deficiency, but a ferritin in the normal range in a chronically ill patient can still mask true deficiency. Use the transferrin saturation alongside ferritin — a low transferrin saturation with normal or high ferritin suggests functional iron deficiency or anemia of chronic inflammation, while low ferritin with high transferrin binding capacity indicates classic iron deficiency. When the picture is mixed, soluble transferrin receptor or a therapeutic iron trial may clarify. Always look for and document the source of iron loss (gastrointestinal, gynecologic, other) rather than simply replacing iron, because unexplained iron deficiency warrants endoscopic or other investigation to exclude malignancy and treatable lesions.

### Distinguish Nutritional Macrocytosis from Other Causes

Macrocytosis is not automatically B12 or folate deficiency. Megaloblastic macrocytosis from B12 or folate deficiency typically shows oval macrocytes, hypersegmented neutrophils, and may accompany pancytopenia and neurologic features; check B12 and folate, and when B12 is borderline, confirm with methylmalonic acid and homocysteine. Non-megaloblastic macrocytosis includes alcohol-related and liver disease (round macrocytes with target cells), reticulocytosis from hemolysis or bleeding (large polychromatic cells), hypothyroidism, myelodysplasia, and medications such as hydroxyurea or chemotherapy. Critically, folate replacement alone in a combined deficiency can precipitate subacute combined degeneration of the cord from unmasked B12 deficiency — never give folate without ensuring B12 is adequate. Check both, and replace B12 first or together when there is any doubt.

### Work Up Hemolysis Systematically When Reticulocytes Are High

A high reticulocyte count with falling hemoglobin, jaundice, dark urine, or splenomegaly demands a hemolysis workup. Divide into intravascular (low haptoglobin, elevated lactate dehydrogenase, hemoglobinuria, hemoglobinemia) and extravascular (splenic sequestration) mechanisms, and into immune (positive direct antiglobulin test, spherocytes) versus non-immune causes. The direct antiglobulin test separates immune hemolytic anemia (autoimmune, drug-induced, transfusion-related) from non-immune causes (hereditary spherocytosis, G6PD deficiency, sickle cell, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia with schistocytes from thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura, hemolytic uremic syndrome, or mechanical valves). Schistocytes on the blood film are a red flag for microangiopathic hemolytic anemia and demand urgent escalation — thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura is a same-day emergency requiring urgent plasma exchange. Do not attribute hemolysis to a benign cause until the life-threatening mimics are excluded.

### Stratify Cytopenias by Severity and Risk

Isolated mild cytopenias are common and often benign, but severe or multi-lineage cytopenias carry real risk and demand urgent evaluation. Neutropenia below 0.5 times ten to the ninth per liter (severe) — especially with fever — is a medical emergency requiring immediate empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics after cultures, because the neutropenic septic patient can deteriorate within hours. Thrombocytopenia below 20 to 30 times ten to the ninth per liter raises bleeding risk and may require platelet transfusion, with lower thresholds for active bleeding, fever, or planned procedures. Pancytopenia indicates marrow failure or infiltration (aplastic anemia, leukemia, myelodysplasia, megaloblastic anemia, marrow infiltration by malignancy or infection such as visceral leishmaniasis or tuberculosis) and warrants urgent hematology referral and usually bone marrow biopsy. Always assess for sepsis risk, bleeding risk, and the pace of fall — a rapid drop is more dangerous than a chronic stable low count.

### Use Transfusion Thresholds Deliberately, Not Reflexively

Transfusion is a procedure with real risk (reaction, alloimmunization, infection, circulatory overload, transfusion-related acute lung injury) and should be used when benefit outweighs risk, not to chase a number. In stable, non-bleeding adults, transfuse to maintain hemoglobin around 70 to 80 grams per liter; use a higher threshold (around 80 to 100 grams per liter) in acute coronary syndrome, severe heart failure, or active bleeding, and in symptomatic patients. For platelets, prophylactic transfusion is generally considered below 10 times ten to the ninth per liter (lower thresholds in chronic stable thrombocytopenia, higher in fever, bleeding, or before procedures). Always transfuse the unit being given, recheck counts after transfusion, and investigate the cause rather than relying on chronic transfusion. Massive or ongoing transfusion requires protocol-driven major hemorrhage management with blood bank coordination.

### Escalate to Hematology at Defined Thresholds

Escalate urgently for suspected acute leukemia (blasts on film, pancytopenia with constitutional symptoms), severe or rapidly progressive cytopenias, suspected thrombotic microangiopathy, symptomatic severe hemolysis, aplastic anemia, or any cytopenia with diagnostic uncertainty and red flags (constitutional symptoms, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy). Bone marrow biopsy is the definitive test for marrow failure, infiltration, and many hematologic malignancies and should be arranged by or in consultation with hematology. For chronic stable cytopenias with a clear benign explanation and no red flags, routine hematology input is appropriate. When red flags appear — fever with severe neutropenia, bleeding with severe thrombocytopenia, schistocytes with neurologic or renal features — escalate immediately and follow institutional protocols.

## Common Traps

### Treating Every Anemia as Iron Deficiency

Giving iron empirically without confirming the cause misses hemolysis, marrow disease, and anemia of chronic inflammation, and masks the source of occult bleeding including malignancy. Counter by using the reticulocyte count and iron studies to classify before treating, and investigating unexplained iron deficiency.

### Misreading Ferritin in Inflammation

A normal ferritin in an inflamed patient does not exclude iron deficiency, and a high ferritin may reflect inflammation rather than overload. Counter by interpreting ferritin with transferrin saturation and the clinical context, and considering functional iron deficiency.

### Giving Folate Without Ensuring B12 Adequacy

Folate alone in combined deficiency can precipitate subacute combined degeneration of the cord from unmasked B12 deficiency. Counter by checking both and replacing B12 first or together whenever there is any doubt.

### Missing Microangiopathic Hemolytic Anemia

Schistocytes with thrombocytopenia and neurologic or renal features suggest thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura or hemolytic uremic syndrome, which are same-day emergencies. Counter by reviewing the blood film and escalating immediately for plasma exchange consideration.

### Under-Treating Febrile Severe Neutropenia

Fever with neutrophils below 0.5 times ten to the ninth per liter is a medical emergency; delay in antibiotics increases mortality. Counter by taking cultures and giving empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics immediately, before definitive workup.

### Transfusing to a Number Rather Than Clinical Need

Reflexive transfusion to normalize counts exposes patients to avoidable risk without benefit. Counter by using restrictive thresholds in stable patients and reserving higher thresholds for symptomatic, bleeding, or cardiac patients.

### Missing Marrow Failure or Malignancy Behind Cytopenias

Pancytopenia or persistent unexplained cytopenias with constitutional symptoms may indicate leukemia, myelodysplasia, or aplastic anemia. Counter by reviewing the blood film for blasts and dysplasia and arranging urgent hematology referral and marrow biopsy when red flags appear.

## Self-Check

- Have I classified the anemia by mean cell volume and reticulocyte response (underproduction versus destruction or loss) before ordering further tests?
- Have I interpreted iron studies with inflammation in mind, using transferrin saturation alongside ferritin, and investigated the source of any iron loss?
- For macrocytosis, have I checked both B12 and folate, and ensured B12 is adequate before or alongside folate replacement?
- For high-reticulocyte anemia, have I worked up hemolysis systematically and reviewed the blood film for schistocytes to exclude microangiopathic causes?
- For cytopenias, have I stratified by severity and pace, assessed sepsis and bleeding risk, and escalated febrile severe neutropenia as an emergency with immediate empiric antibiotics?
- Have I used restrictive transfusion thresholds appropriate to the patient's symptoms and comorbidity rather than chasing a number?
- Have I escalated urgently to hematology for suspected acute leukemia, severe or rapid cytopenias, thrombotic microangiopathy, or marrow failure, and arranged bone marrow biopsy when indicated?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
