# Drug Interaction Screening and Prevention

Drug interactions are among the most common preventable causes of adverse events, yet they are routinely missed because they are invisible at the point of prescribing and because interaction-checking software generates so much noise that real alerts are dismissed. The judgment problem is not detecting that an interaction exists — databases do that — but deciding which interactions matter clinically, which require a change in therapy, and which can be managed with monitoring. The most dangerous interactions involve narrow therapeutic index drugs, drugs that inhibit or induce metabolism, and pharmacodynamic combinations that amplify toxicity (serotonin syndrome, QT prolongation, bleeding, sedation, hypotension). A clinician who treats every flagged interaction as equivalent will both miss the lethal ones and paralyze care with unnecessary changes. This skill covers disciplined interaction screening, severity interpretation, management of the highest-risk classes, and when to escalate to pharmacy.

## Core Rules

### Build a Complete Medication List Before Screening

Interaction screening is only as good as the medication list. Before assessing interactions, assemble the full list including prescription drugs, over-the-counter agents, herbal and complementary products, recreational substances, and as-needed medications. Omitted agents are the most common reason a lethal interaction is missed — St. John's wort inducing warfarin failure, grapefruit juice raising statin levels, or illicit serotonergic drugs precipitating serotonin syndrome. Reconcile the list at every transition of care (admission, transfer, discharge) because new drugs are added and old ones dropped at each handoff. A medication list that has not been reconciled in the last 24 to 48 hours in a hospitalized patient is unreliable for interaction analysis. When the list is uncertain, verify with the patient, family, pharmacy, and prior records before relying on it.

### Classify Interactions as Pharmacokinetic or Pharmacodynamic

Understanding mechanism determines management. Pharmacokinetic interactions alter absorption, distribution, metabolism, or excretion of a drug — most clinically important are metabolic interactions via CYP450 enzyme induction or inhibition, and altered renal excretion. Pharmacodynamic interactions occur when two drugs have additive or opposing effects at the same receptor or physiologic pathway — additive sedation, additive QT prolongation, additive bleeding risk, opposing antihypertensive effects. Pharmacokinetic interactions are often manageable by dose adjustment and monitoring of drug levels or surrogate markers; pharmacodynamic interactions often require choosing one agent or monitoring the shared effect closely. Name the mechanism explicitly so the management follows logically rather than applying a generic "avoid" reflexively.

### Map CYP450 Inducers, Inhibitors, and Substrates

The CYP450 system drives most serious metabolic interactions. Know the high-yield enzymes and their key modulators. Strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (clarithromycin, ketoconazole/itraconazole, ritonavir, grapefruit) dramatically raise levels of substrates such as statins (especially simvastatin and atorvastatin), calcium channel blockers, benzodiazepines, and many chemotherapeutics, causing rhabdomyolysis, hypotension, or respiratory depression. Strong inducers (rifampin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, St. John's wort) lower substrate levels and cause therapeutic failure — most dangerously warfarin, oral contraceptives, and antiretrovirals. CYP2C9 inhibition (amiodarone, sulfonamides) raises warfarin effect; CYP2D6 inhibition (fluoxetine, paroxetine, bupropion) affects tamoxifen, codeine, and tricyclics. The time course differs: inhibitors act within days, while inducers require one to two weeks to reach full effect and persist for weeks after withdrawal. When adding or stopping a strong modulator, anticipate the lag and adjust monitoring accordingly.

### Treat Narrow Therapeutic Index Drugs as the Highest Risk

Narrow therapeutic index drugs — warfarin, digoxin, lithium, phenytoin, theophylline, tacrolimus and cyclosporine, aminoglycosides, and insulin — have a small margin between ineffective and toxic, so even modest interaction-induced changes in level or effect produce harm. These drugs require proactive interaction screening at every change, intensified monitoring of levels or surrogate markers, and dose adjustment when modulators are added or removed. Warfarin interactions are the classic example: amiodarone, fluconazole, metronidazole, and sulfamethoxazole sharply raise INR and bleeding risk; rifampin, barbiturates, and carbamazepine lower it and risk thrombosis. For any new drug in a patient on warfarin, plan the INR monitoring schedule and dose adjustment before the first dose. Never add or remove a drug interacting with a narrow therapeutic index agent without a documented monitoring plan.

### Recognize and Prevent Serotonin Syndrome

Serotonin syndrome is a potentially fatal pharmacodynamic interaction caused by excess central serotonergic activity, most often from combining two serotonergic agents. Recognize the triad of mental status change (agitation, confusion), autonomic instability (hyperthermia, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis), and neuromuscular excitation (clonus — especially inducible or spontaneous, hyperreflexia, tremor, rigidity). Onset is typically within hours of a dose change or addition. Common precipitants include combining SSRIs or SNRIs with MAOIs (absolutely contraindicated), tramadol, linezolid (which has MAOI activity), triptans, fentanyl, dextromethorphan, and illicit serotonergic drugs. Severe cases progress to hyperthermia, seizures, rhabdomyolysis, and death. Management is discontinuation of all serotonergic agents, benzodiazepines for agitation and rigidity, aggressive cooling, and supportive care — cyproheptadine is an adjunct in severe cases. MAOIs require a washout of at least 14 days before starting another serotonergic agent. Any patient on multiple serotonergic drugs who develops agitation and clonus should be assumed to have serotonin syndrome until proven otherwise; escalate immediately.

### Screen for and Manage QT Prolongation Risk

QT prolongation and torsades de pointes are caused by additive pharmacodynamic effects of multiple QT-prolonging drugs plus predisposing factors (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, bradycardia, structural heart disease, hepatic impairment). High-risk combinations include certain antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, sotalol, quinidine, procainamide), antipsychotics (haloperidol, especially IV, ziprasidone), macrolides (especially azithromycin and clarithromycin), fluoroquinolones, methadone, and ondansetron at high doses. Risk rises steeply when two or more QT-prolonging drugs are combined or when electrolytes are deranged. Before combining such drugs, correct potassium and magnesium, obtain a baseline ECG, and plan serial ECGs — a QTc above 500 ms or a rise above 60 ms from baseline warrants reassessment and often discontinuation. Patients with known long QT syndrome, recent syncope, or a family history of sudden death require cardiology input before any QT-prolonging drug.

### Interpret Severity and Decide Whether the Interaction Changes Therapy

Not every flagged interaction requires a change. Use a severity framework: contraindicated combinations (e.g., MAOI with SSRI, pimozide with macrolides) must be avoided; major interactions require a change in drug, dose, or intensive monitoring; moderate interactions often need monitoring or dose adjustment; minor interactions rarely need action. The decision to change therapy depends on the clinical consequence (is it reversible nuisance or life-threatening?), the availability of alternatives, the monitoring feasibility, and the patient's specific risk factors. Document the rationale when you choose to continue a flagged combination with monitoring rather than switching — this protects the patient and the record. When the consequence is severe and the management uncertain, consult pharmacy before proceeding.

## Common Traps

### Alert Fatigue and Dismissing Real Interactions

Interaction checkers flag so many minor interactions that clinicians habitually override all alerts, including the lethal ones. Counter by reading each major or contraindicated alert individually and documenting a specific rationale for override.

### Screening With an Incomplete Medication List

Herbals, over-the-counter drugs, and recreational substances are omitted and cause the most surprising interactions. Counter by explicitly asking about all agents and reconciling at every transition before screening.

### Ignoring the Time Lag of Enzyme Inducers

Inducers take one to two weeks to reach full effect and persist for weeks after withdrawal, so a newly stopped inducer continues to lower substrate levels and a newly started one takes time to act. Counter by planning monitoring over the full induction and de-induction window.

### Adding a Warfarin-Interacting Drug Without an INR Plan

Warfarin interactions can cause bleeding or thrombosis within days. Counter by defining the INR monitoring schedule and anticipated dose adjustment before the first dose of any interacting drug.

### Attributing Serotonin Syndrome Features to Infection or Withdrawal

Agitation, hyperthermia, and rigidity in a patient on serotonergic drugs are often misattributed to sepsis, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, or withdrawal. Counter by checking the medication list for serotonergic combinations and examining specifically for clonus and hyperreflexia.

### Combining QT-Prolonging Drugs Without Checking Electrolytes and ECG

Additive QT risk is amplified by hypokalemia and bradycardia. Counter by correcting electrolytes, obtaining a baseline ECG, and planning serial ECGs whenever combining QT-prolonging agents.

### Treating All Flagged Interactions as Equivalent

Applying "avoid" to every interaction paralyzes care and causes unnecessary switches. Counter by classifying by severity and consequence, and consulting pharmacy when the decision is genuinely uncertain.

## Self-Check

- Have I assembled and reconciled the complete medication list, including over-the-counter, herbal, and recreational substances, before screening?
- For each significant interaction, have I classified it as pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic and named the specific mechanism?
- For any narrow therapeutic index drug (warfarin, digoxin, lithium, phenytoin, theophylline, calcineurin inhibitors, aminoglycosides), have I planned level or surrogate monitoring and dose adjustment for every interacting change?
- Have I checked for serotonergic combinations and recognized the triad of mental status change, autonomic instability, and neuromuscular excitation as serotonin syndrome until excluded?
- Before combining QT-prolonging drugs, have I corrected potassium and magnesium, obtained a baseline ECG, and planned serial ECGs?
- Have I interpreted each interaction by severity and consequence, and documented the rationale when continuing a flagged combination with monitoring?
- For any contraindicated or major interaction with uncertain management, have I escalated to pharmacy or the relevant specialist before proceeding?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
