# Adverse Drug Reaction Recognition and Management

Adverse drug reactions (ADRs) are a leading cause of morbidity, prolonged hospitalization, and preventable death, and they are among the easiest clinical events to misattribute. The central judgment problem is not memorizing a list of side effects — it is deciding, in a patient on multiple medications with evolving symptoms, whether a new problem is caused by a drug, by the underlying disease, by a new independent illness, or by an interaction. The danger lies in two opposite errors: dismissing a serious reaction as disease progression and continuing the offending drug, or labeling any symptom as a drug reaction and stopping essential therapy without evidence. Severe cutaneous, hepatic, and hematologic reactions can progress in hours, and delayed recognition is often fatal. This skill covers disciplined ADR recognition, causality assessment, management of severe reactions, dechallenge/rechallenge reasoning, cross-reactivity, and reporting — and when to escalate immediately.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish ADR From Disease Progression Before Acting

When a new symptom arises in a medicated patient, do not default to "drug reaction" or "disease progression" without reasoning explicitly. Establish the temporal relationship: when did the drug start relative to symptom onset? Most reactions are temporally plausible (days to weeks for immune-mediated, hours for dose-dependent or infusion reactions). Check whether the symptom is a known pharmacologic effect of the suspected drug, and whether the underlying disease itself commonly causes the symptom. Explicitly weigh three competing explanations — drug-induced, disease progression, or a new independent process — and document which is most supported. Stopping a drug reflexively can harm the patient if the true cause is untreated disease; continuing a drug through a true reaction can be lethal. When the picture is ambiguous, seek pharmacist or specialist input before irreversible changes occur.

### Apply a Structured Causality Assessment

Use a reproducible causality framework rather than impression. The Naranjo algorithm scores probability across ten dimensions: previous conclusive reports, temporal relationship, dechallenge, rechallenge, alternative causes, placebo response, drug levels, reaction severity, and objective confirmation. A Naranjo score of greater than 8 is definite, 5 to 8 probable, 1 to 4 possible, and 0 doubtful. Causality is strengthened by a clear temporal link, improvement on dechallenge, recurrence on rechallenge (where ethically permissible), and exclusion of alternatives. It is weakened by competing diagnoses, atypical timing, or absence of the expected effect on dechallenge. Record the score and reasoning in the chart so colleagues can audit and continue the assessment. Do not claim "definite" causality without objective evidence and documented dechallenge.

### Recognize Severe Cutaneous Adverse Reactions as Emergencies

Drug reactions with skin and mucosal involvement can be immediately life-threatening. Recognize the severe cutaneous adverse reaction (SCAR) syndromes by their signatures: Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis present with flu-like prodrome, painful skin, atypical target lesions, mucosal involvement (oral, conjunctival, urogenital), and sheet-like epidermal detachment — SJS involves under 10 percent body surface area, SJS/TEN overlap 10 to 30 percent, and TEN over 30 percent. Drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms (DRESS) presents two to eight weeks after exposure with rash, fever, lymphadenopathy, eosinophilia, and visceral involvement (hepatitis, interstitial nephritis, pneumonitis). Acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis presents with hundreds of non-follicular pustules on erythema. Any suspected SCAR mandates immediate drug cessation, specialist dermatology and burn-unit or ICU consultation, and supportive care — these are not outpatient problems. Delay for biopsy should not delay stopping the suspected drug.

### Identify and Treat Anaphylaxis Without Delay

Anaphylaxis is under-recognized because it is defined by rapid multi-system involvement, not by a single sign. The diagnostic criteria are any one of: rapid onset skin/mucosal change plus respiratory compromise or hypotension; or two or more of skin/mucosal, respiratory, cardiovascular, or persistent gastrointestinal symptoms after likely allergen exposure; or hypotension after known allergen. The first-line treatment is intramuscular epinephrine at the anterolateral thigh, repeated every 5 to 15 minutes as needed — not antihistamines, not corticosteroids, not oxygen alone. Antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts and must never delay epinephrine. Position the patient supine with legs elevated (unless respiratory distress demands otherwise), establish airway and IV access, and give fluids for shock. Observe for biphasic reaction (typically 4 to 10 hours, occasionally longer). Failure to give epinephrine early is the most common preventable cause of fatal anaphylaxis — escalate to the resuscitation team immediately.

### Reason Carefully About Dechallenge and Rechallenge

Dechallenge — stopping the suspected drug and observing for improvement — is both therapeutic and diagnostic, and it is central to causality. For most reactions, improvement after withdrawal supports drug causality; failure to improve weakens it, though some reactions (autoimmune, late fibrotic) persist after withdrawal. Rechallenge — re-exposing the patient to confirm causality — is ethically constrained: it is absolutely contraindicated for immune-mediated severe reactions (SJS/TEN, DRESS, anaphylaxis, drug-induced immune cytopenias) where re-exposure can be fatal. Accidental rechallenge (e.g., the patient takes a forgotten dose, or a new prescription is dispensed under a different name) is a sentinel event that strongly supports causality and must be documented and reported. Never recommend intentional rechallenge for a serious immune-mediated reaction; the diagnostic value never outweighs the risk.

### Manage Cross-Reactivity and Structurally Related Drug Classes

When a patient reports a reaction to a drug class, decide which related drugs are safe and which are contraindicated based on mechanism and structure. Beta-lactam cross-reactivity is far lower than historically taught: true IgE-mediated penicillin allergy carries roughly 1 to 2 percent cross-reactivity with modern cephalosporins and 1 percent with carbapenems, but side-chain similarity (e.g., aminopenicillin and aminocephalosporins sharing the R1 side chain) raises risk and requires graded challenge or avoidance. Non-IgE reactions (SJS/TEN) to any beta-lactam contraindicate the entire class. Sulfa cross-reactivity is class-specific: antibiotic sulfonamides, non-antibiotic sulfonamides (furosemide, thiazides, celecoxib), and sulfites are chemically distinct, and cross-reactivity is largely a myth except for true immune-mediated sulfa reactions. Do not deny patients diuretics or COX-2 inhibitors on the basis of a vague "sulfa allergy" without assessing the original reaction type. When uncertain, consult pharmacy or allergy-immunology before substitution.

### Report Serious and Unexpected Reactions

ADR reporting is a professional and often legal obligation, not optional documentation. Report serious reactions (death, life-threatening events, hospitalization, disability, congenital anomaly, or required intervention) and any unexpected reaction (not in the labeling) to the national pharmacovigilance system — for example, the FDA MedWatch program in the United States or the Yellow Card scheme in the United Kingdom. Reporting is especially important for newly marketed drugs, biologics, and pediatric or geriatric reactions where data are sparse. A single credible report can trigger labeling changes or withdrawal. Document the reaction, causality assessment, dechallenge outcome, and reporter identity. Do not under-report because causality is uncertain — the system is designed to detect signals from aggregated uncertain cases.

## Common Traps

### Attributing Symptoms to Disease Progression and Continuing the Offending Drug

The most dangerous trap in chronic disease management is labeling new symptoms (fatigue, rash, transaminitis, cytopenia) as disease progression and escalating immunosuppression or adding more drugs rather than stopping the culprit. Counter by explicitly considering drug causality for every new symptom in a medicated patient and applying a causality tool.

### Stopping Essential Therapy on Weak Evidence

The mirror error is reflexively stopping a necessary drug (antibiotic, antiepileptic, anticoagulant) at the first nonspecific complaint without assessing causality or alternatives. Counter by weighing the risk of stopping against the strength of drug causality, and seek specialist input before withdrawing essential therapy.

### Treating Anaphylaxis With Antihistamines Instead of Epinephrine

Antihistamines relieve itch and urticaria but do not reverse airway obstruction or shock; they create false reassurance while the patient deteriorates. Counter by giving intramuscular epinephrine first for any anaphylaxis meeting criteria, and escalating to the resuscitation team immediately.

### Missing SJS/TEN Because the Mucosal Component Is Mild

Early SJS/TEN can present with minimal skin findings and prominent mucosal involvement mistaken for viral stomatitis or conjunctivitis. Counter by examining all mucosal sites in any drug-exposed patient with fever and rash, and by treating suspected SCAR as an emergency pending dermatology review.

### Assuming All Beta-Lactam or Sulfa Cross-Reactivity Is Absolute

Over-broad avoidance denies patients first-line antibiotics and diuretics. Counter by assessing the original reaction type (IgE versus non-IgE, severe versus mild) and consulting allergy or pharmacy for side-chain-specific decisions.

### Intentional Rechallenge of a Severe Immune-Mediated Reaction

Re-exposing a patient with prior SJS/TEN, DRESS, or anaphylaxis to confirm causality is contraindicated and can be fatal. Counter by treating a documented severe immune reaction as absolute and lifelong, and flagging the allergy prominently in the record.

### Under-Reporting Because Causality Is Uncertain

Causality is rarely certain in a single case, and pharmacovigilance depends on aggregated reports. Counter by reporting all serious and unexpected reactions even when causality is only possible, and escalating ambiguous cases to pharmacovigilance or pharmacy.

## Self-Check

- For every new symptom in a medicated patient, have I explicitly weighed drug-induced, disease progression, and independent causes rather than defaulting to one?
- Have I applied a structured causality tool (such as Naranjo) and recorded the score and reasoning, rather than asserting causality by impression?
- For any suspected severe cutaneous adverse reaction, have I stopped the drug immediately, examined all mucosal sites, and escalated to dermatology, burn unit, or ICU without delay?
- For any presentation meeting anaphylaxis criteria, have I given intramuscular epinephrine first and escalated to the resuscitation team rather than relying on antihistamines or steroids?
- Have I avoided intentional rechallenge for any serious immune-mediated reaction and treated documented severe reactions as lifelong contraindications?
- Have I assessed cross-reactivity based on mechanism and side-chain similarity rather than blanket class avoidance, and consulted pharmacy or allergy when uncertain?
- Have I reported all serious and unexpected reactions to the national pharmacovigilance system regardless of causality certainty?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
