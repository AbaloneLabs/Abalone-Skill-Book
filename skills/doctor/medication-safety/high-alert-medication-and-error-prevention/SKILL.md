# High-Alert Medication and Error Prevention

High-alert medications are drugs that bear a heightened risk of causing significant patient harm when used in error — not because errors are more frequent with these drugs, but because the consequences of an error are severe. Insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, chemotherapy, and concentrated electrolytes together account for a disproportionate share of preventable deaths and permanent harm. The judgment problem is that these drugs are used constantly, often under time pressure, by multiple clinicians across transitions of care, so the systems protecting patients must be applied consistently even when the task feels routine. The danger is that familiarity breeds shortcuts: an insulin dose written from memory, a heparin infusion started without a double-check, a chemotherapy agent verified by a single signature. This skill covers the high-alert drug classes, the error-prevention systems that must be applied to them, look-alike/sound-alike risks, medication reconciliation, and when to stop and escalate to pharmacy.

## Core Rules

### Know the High-Alert List and Treat Each Class According to Its Failure Mode

The Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) maintains the canonical high-alert medication list, and every prescriber should know its core members: insulin (especially subcutaneous concentrated forms and IV infusions), anticoagulants (heparin, low-molecular-weight heparins, warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants), opioids (especially IV and long-acting forms), chemotherapy (oral and parenteral), concentrated electrolytes (potassium chloride, hypertonic saline, magnesium sulfate, calcium salts), sedatives, neuromuscular blocking agents, and parenteral nutrition. Each class fails in characteristic ways: insulin by dosing-unit confusion and sliding-scale drift; anticoagulants by weight-based calculation errors and failure to reverse; opioids by cumulative dose and respiratory depression; chemotherapy by wrong drug, wrong route (intrathecal vincristine catastrophe), or wrong patient; concentrated electrolytes by look-alike vials and rapid infusion. Know the dominant failure mode for each class you prescribe and apply the specific safeguard that addresses it.

### Apply Independent Double-Checks for the Highest-Risk Steps

An independent double-check is a second qualified clinician separately verifying the drug, dose, route, rate, patient identity, and calculation — without being prompted by the first clinician's answer — before administration. Independent double-checks are most effective for high-alert infusions (heparin, insulin, vasoactive drugs, chemotherapy, pediatric weight-based doses), where calculation and pump-programming errors are common and lethal. The check must be independent: the second clinician performs the calculation from scratch rather than confirming the first's result, because dependent checks merely propagate errors. Document the double-check in the record. Do not let time pressure erode this step — the few seconds saved are trivial against the harm prevented. When a second qualified clinician is unavailable, escalate rather than proceed without verification.

### Perform Medication Reconciliation at Every Transition of Care

Medication errors cluster at transitions — admission, transfer between units, and discharge — because the medication list changes and information is lost. Formal reconciliation means comparing the patient's pre-admission medication list against the current orders, resolving every discrepancy intentionally, and communicating the final list to the next setting. The most dangerous omissions are continuation failures (a chronic anticoagulant, antiepileptic, immunosuppressant, or antiretroviral stopped inadvertently on admission) and unintended duplications (two anticoagulants or two opioids continued). At discharge, reconcile the hospital regimen with the outpatient list, explicitly state which medications are continued, stopped, or changed, and communicate this to the patient and the next provider. A discharge summary that does not reconcile medications is a leading cause of readmission and harm.

### Eliminate Look-Alike/Sound-Alike and Error-Prone Prescribing

Look-alike/sound-alike (LASA) drug pairs — hydralazine/hydroxyzine, epinephrine/ephedrine, vincristine/vinblastine, potassium chloride/potassium phosphate, cyclosporine/cyclophosphamide — cause selection and dispensing errors that kill patients. Mitigate LASA risk by using tall-man lettering where established (ePIPHRINE, DOPamine, hydrALAZINE), by including the indication on the order, by storing look-alike products separately, and by reading labels rather than recognizing packaging. Eliminate error-prone abbreviations and conventions: never use "U" for units (misread as zero causing tenfold insulin overdose), never use trailing zeros (write 5, not 5.0), always use leading zeros for fractions (write 0.5, not .5), avoid ambiguous abbreviations such as "QD," "QOD," "IU," and "μg" (use "mcg"). Spell out "units," "daily," and "every other day." These rules exist because specific patients have died from each abbreviation; treat them as non-negotiable.

### Manage Insulin Safely Across the Care Continuum

Insulin is the high-alert drug most commonly implicated in serious harm, and its errors stem from dosing-unit confusion, sliding-scale drift, and failure to match dose to intake. Never write "U" or "units" ambiguously — spell it out. Specify the concentration (U-100 versus U-500) explicitly, because U-500 insulin errors are frequently tenfold and fatal. Avoid sliding-scale insulin as the sole regimen in hospitalized patients, because it treats hyperglycemia reactively and causes wide glycemic fluctuation; use scheduled basal-bolus or correction regimens instead. When converting between IV insulin infusion and subcutaneous insulin, overlap the doses to avoid rebound hyperglycemia. Verify pump settings and concentrations independently. For any insulin dose that seems unusual, stop and recalculate rather than administering on assumption.

### Manage Anticoagulants With Weight, Renal Function, and Reversal in Mind

Anticoagulant errors cause both catastrophic bleeding and thrombotic events from underdosing. Dose heparin and low-molecular-weight heparins by actual body weight with renal-function adjustment; do not use rounded or default weights. Check renal function before starting direct oral anticoagulants and adjust dose for creatinine clearance, because accumulation causes bleeding. For warfarin, plan INR monitoring and know the reversal strategy (vitamin K timing and dosing, four-factor prothrombin complex concentrate for major bleeding). Hold anticoagulants appropriately around procedures with explicit stop and restart times documented. When bleeding occurs, know which agent is responsible, when it was last given, and the specific reversal — these drugs are not interchangeable, and escalation to hematology or pharmacy is warranted for complex reversal.

### Verify Chemotherapy With Multiple, Independent Controls

Chemotherapy errors are often devastating because the agents are toxic by design and some routes are lethal if wrong (intrathecal vincristine is uniformly fatal). Chemotherapy orders require protocol-driven verification: confirm the patient, the protocol, the cycle, the dose calculation against body surface area or weight, the cumulative anthracycline dose, and the route. Intrathecal medications must be prepared and labeled separately and delivered separately from IV medications, with a final verification at the bedside. Oral chemotherapy is often overlooked but carries the same toxicity and requires the same prescription controls as parenteral. Never prescribe or verify chemotherapy alone; pharmacy verification and, in many institutions, a second prescriber are mandatory. Any uncertainty in a chemotherapy order mandates stopping and escalating to oncology pharmacy and the treating oncologist.

## Common Traps

### Skipping the Independent Double-Check Under Time Pressure

The most common reason a high-alert infusion error reaches the patient is that the second check was omitted or became dependent. Counter by performing the check independently even when busy, and escalating when a second qualified clinician is unavailable.

### Continuing a Chronic Medication That Was Stopped on Admission

Omission of anticoagulants, antiepileptics, immunosuppressants, antiretrovirals, and Parkinson's medications at admission causes thrombosis, seizures, rejection, viral resistance, and aspiration. Counter by formal reconciliation against the pre-admission list within 24 hours of admission.

### Using Error-Prone Abbreviations Out of Habit

"U," trailing zeros, and ambiguous dose shorthand have killed patients and are still used from familiarity. Counter by spelling out units, using leading zeros, and never using trailing zeros regardless of custom.

### Confusing Insulin Concentrations or Sliding-Scale Drift

U-500 insulin and sliding-scale-only regimens cause tenfold errors and wide glycemic swings. Counter by specifying concentration, using scheduled basal-bolus regimens, and recalculating any unusual dose.

### Dosing Anticoagulants Without Renal Function or Weight

Default or rounded weights and failure to adjust for renal function cause both bleeding and thrombosis. Counter by using actual body weight, checking creatinine clearance, and knowing the reversal strategy for each agent.

### Verifying Chemotherapy Without Protocol-Level Controls

Single-prescriber verification and route confusion (intrathecal vincristine) are catastrophic. Counter by requiring pharmacy and second-prescriber verification, separating intrathecal preparation, and stopping for any uncertainty.

### Relying on Packaging Recognition Rather Than Reading Labels

LASA errors occur because clinicians recognize packaging rather than reading the label. Counter by reading the drug name and concentration every time, using tall-man lettering, and including the indication on orders.

## Self-Check

- For every high-alert medication I prescribe or verify (insulin, anticoagulants, opioids, chemotherapy, concentrated electrolytes), have I applied the class-specific safeguard that addresses its dominant failure mode?
- For high-risk infusions and weight-based pediatric or chemotherapy doses, have I performed and documented an independent double-check by a second qualified clinician?
- At admission, transfer, and discharge, have I reconciled the medication list and resolved every discrepancy, especially continuation of chronic anticoagulants, antiepileptics, immunosuppressants, and antiretrovirals?
- Have I avoided all error-prone abbreviations — spelling out "units," using leading zeros, never using trailing zeros, and avoiding ambiguous shorthand?
- For insulin, have I specified the concentration, avoided sliding-scale-only regimens, and recalculated any unusual dose rather than administering on assumption?
- For anticoagulants, have I dosed by actual body weight with renal adjustment, planned monitoring, and identified the reversal strategy?
- For chemotherapy, have I required protocol-level pharmacy and second-prescriber verification, separated intrathecal preparation, and stopped for any uncertainty?
- Have I read labels rather than recognized packaging, and used tall-man lettering and indication to mitigate look-alike/sound-alike risk?
- For any high-alert prescribing or verification uncertainty, have I escalated to pharmacy or the relevant specialist before proceeding?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
