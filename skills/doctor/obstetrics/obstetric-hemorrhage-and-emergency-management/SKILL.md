# Obstetric Hemorrhage and Emergency Management

Obstetric hemorrhage is the leading direct cause of maternal death worldwide, and the difference between a controlled resuscitation and a catastrophe is measured in minutes. The judgment problem is that young pregnant and postpartum women tolerate blood loss extraordinarily well until they do not, so the classic signs of shock appear late, after a third or more of blood volume is lost, and by then coagulopathy, acidosis, and hypothermia are compounding. The recurring failure is sequential rather than simultaneous management — waiting to see if the next uterotonic works before calling for blood, calling the anesthetist after the patient is unstable, deferring the senior obstetrician until the bedside measures have failed. This skill covers the cause-based framework for hemorrhage, the massive transfusion protocol, uterine atony management, abnormal placentation and accreta spectrum, uterine rupture, amniotic fluid embolism, and the escalation and team-response logic that determines outcome.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Every Hemorrhage to the Four Ts and Treat Causes in Parallel

The four Ts — tone, trauma, tissue, thrombin — provide the diagnostic scaffold for every obstetric hemorrhage, and they must be addressed in parallel, not sequentially. Tone (uterine atony) accounts for the majority of cases: palpate the fundus, and if it is soft and boggy, begin bimanual uterine massage immediately and give a sequence of uterotonic agents — oxytocin first, then ergometrine (avoid in hypertension), then carboprost (avoid in asthma), then misoprostol. Trauma (cervical, vaginal, or perineal lacerations) requires a systematic inspection and repair under adequate visualization and anesthesia; do not assume the bleeding is atony just because the uterus is soft. Tissue (retained placenta or clots) demands exploration and evacuation. Thrombin (coagulopathy) may be primary, as in placental abruption, severe preeclampsia, or amniotic fluid embolism, or acquired from massive transfusion; send clotting studies early and treat with blood products guided by the massive transfusion protocol. While you address the cause, resuscitate simultaneously — the team does not wait.

### Run the Massive Hemorrhage Protocol Deliberately and Early

The defining error in fatal obstetric hemorrhage is late activation of the massive transfusion protocol. Set a low threshold to activate: estimated loss over 1500 mL, ongoing bleeding, hemodynamic instability, or any clinical concern. Activation mobilizes blood bank, senior obstetric and anesthetic staff, theater, and portering simultaneously. Use a balanced transfusion strategy — red cells, fresh frozen plasma, and platelets in an approximate ratio, with cryoprecipitate for low fibrinogen, which is critical in obstetrics because fibrinogen falls early and profoundly in obstetric hemorrhage and a normal fibrinogen in late pregnancy is higher than in non-pregnant adults, so a "normal" value may already be abnormal. Use viscoelastic testing (thromboelastography or rotational thromboelastometry) where available to guide product administration in real time. Maintain normothermia, calcium (citrate in transfused blood chelates calcium), and acid-base balance, because the lethal triad of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy drives the spiral toward irreversible hemorrhage.

### Identify Placenta Accreta Spectrum and Abnormal Placentation Before Delivery

Placenta accreta spectrum — accreta, increta, percreta — is increasingly common with rising cesarean rates and is a leading cause of peripartum hysterectomy and massive hemorrhage. Identify risk antenatally: previous cesarean combined with placenta previa carries a high and escalating risk that rises with the number of prior cesareans. Image with ultrasound in the second and third trimester looking for loss of the normal retroplacental clear space, placental lacunae, and abnormal uterine-bladder interface, and use magnetic resonance imaging where ultrasound is inconclusive. Plan delivery in a tertiary center with a multidisciplinary team — obstetrician, anesthetist, interventional radiologist, urologist, neonatologist, and blood bank — and schedule an elective cesarean at 34 to 37 weeks. Decide the surgical strategy in advance: planned cesarean hysterectomy with the placenta in situ for known severe accreta, versus attempted placental removal with conservative management in selected cases. Unanticipated accreta at delivery is a catastrophe; anticipated accreta is a planned operation.

### Recognize Uterine Rupture Rapidly in the Laboring or Recently Delivered Woman

Uterine rupture is a life-threatening emergency for both mother and fetus and presents variably, which is the trap. Classic features — sudden severe abdominal pain, cessation of contractions, loss of fetal station, vaginal bleeding, maternal hemodynamic instability, and fetal bradycardia — are not always all present, and rupture may manifest only as a pathological CTG with subtle maternal signs. The strongest risk factor is a previous cesarean, particularly with attempted vaginal birth after cesarean, augmented labor, or a single-layer closure. Maintain a high index of suspicion in any woman with a scarred uterus who develops abdominal pain, a concerning tracing, or instability in labor or postpartum. When rupture is suspected, resuscitate, crossmatch, and proceed immediately to laparotomy — there is no role for conservative management. Time from rupture to delivery determines fetal outcome.

### Suspect Amniotic Fluid Embolism on a Compatible Presentation and Resuscitate

Amniotic fluid embolism is rare, unpredictable, and frequently fatal, presenting around delivery or in the immediate postpartum period with sudden cardiovascular collapse, hypoxia, coagulopathy, and often seizures, sometimes preceded by premonitory symptoms of agitation, breathlessness, or a sense of doom. It is a diagnosis of exclusion based on a compatible clinical picture in the absence of another explanation. The immediate management is high-quality cardiopulmonary resuscitation, oxygenation and ventilation, correction of coagulopathy with blood products, and vasopressor and inotropic support, often requiring critical care and possibly extracorporeal support. Do not delay resuscitation while seeking confirmation; activate the massive hemorrhage protocol early because coagulopathy is typically severe and rapid. Simultaneously assess for and exclude the mimics — pulmonary embolism, anesthetic complications, eclampsia, hemorrhage, and sepsis.

### Coordinate the Team Response and Escalate Before the Bedside Measures Fail

Outcome in obstetric emergency depends on team response as much as on individual clinical decisions. Declare the emergency explicitly, use a structured communication style, assign roles — team leader, airway, access, uterotonics, blood, scribe — and call for senior help early, accepting that over-calling is far safer than delay. Run a brief and debrief for any emergency. Maintain situational awareness of resources: blood availability, theater and intensive care capacity, and the need for transfer to a higher-level facility. Document timings — onset of hemorrhage, activation of the protocol, arrival of senior staff, decision to operate, delivery — because timing drives both clinical decisions and later review. After any major hemorrhage or emergency, conduct a structured debrief to identify system and clinical learning.

## Common Traps

### Managing Causes Sequentially Instead of in Parallel

Waiting to see whether the next uterotonic works before calling for blood, examining for trauma, or treating coagulopathy. Counter by assigning the four Ts to parallel streams of work and activating the full team response at the outset.

### Activating the Massive Transfusion Protocol Too Late

Delaying because blood loss "does not look that bad" or because the patient is compensating. Counter by setting a low activation threshold based on estimated loss, ongoing bleeding, instability, or concern, and erring toward over-activation.

### Underestimating the Significance of Fibrinogen in Obstetric Hemorrhage

Treating obstetric coagulopathy with the same thresholds as trauma, when fibrinogen falls early and profoundly in obstetric bleeding. Counter by checking fibrinogen early, using viscoelastic testing, and giving cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate for low values.

### Missing Placenta Accreta on Antenatal Imaging in a Woman With Prior Cesarean and Previa

Treating placenta previa after cesarean as routine when the risk of accreta is high. Counter by targeted ultrasound in all women with previa and prior cesarean, MRI where uncertain, and planned multidisciplinary delivery.

### Attributing a Pathological CTG or Pain in a Scarred Uterus to Anything Other Than Rupture

Reassuring that fetal heart rate abnormalities are due to oxytocin or position while rupture progresses. Counter by maintaining a high index of suspicion for rupture in any scarred uterus with concerning features and proceeding to laparotomy when suspected.

### Failing to Declare the Emergency and Assign Roles

Allowing an unstructured response where no one is in charge and tasks are duplicated or omitted. Counter by explicitly declaring the emergency, naming a team leader, assigning roles, and calling senior help early.

### Not Debriefing After a Major Hemorrhage or Emergency

Moving on without reviewing what happened and why, missing opportunities to improve the system. Counter by conducting a structured debrief after every major event and feeding learning into protocols and training.

## Self-Check

- For any obstetric hemorrhage, have I applied the four Ts framework and treated causes in parallel with resuscitation rather than sequentially?
- Have I activated the massive transfusion protocol early based on a low threshold, and used a balanced transfusion strategy guided by fibrinogen and viscoelastic testing?
- For women with prior cesarean and placenta previa, have I screened for accreta antenatally and planned multidisciplinary delivery at a tertiary center?
- In any scarred uterus with a concerning tracing, abdominal pain, or instability, have I considered uterine rupture and proceeded to laparotomy when suspected?
- For sudden peripartum collapse with hypoxia and coagulopathy, have I suspected amniotic fluid embolism, resuscitated aggressively, activated the hemorrhage protocol, and excluded mimics?
- Have I declared the emergency, named a team leader, assigned roles, called senior help early, and documented key timings?
- Have I conducted a structured debrief after the event and identified clinical and system learning?
