# Labor and Delivery Management

Intrapartum care is a high-acuity, time-pressured discipline where two patients are monitored simultaneously and where most harm comes not from ignorance of the stages of labor but from failing to recognize slow deterioration — a fetal heart rate tracing that drifts, a labor curve that stalls, a maternal reserve that erodes silently before decompensation. The recurring failure modes are treating the partogram as a passive record rather than a decision tool, under-reading cardiotocography by focusing on the current minute instead of the trend, tolerating failure to progress because "she is still contracting," and delaying the decision for operative delivery until the situation has become an emergency rather than a plan. This skill covers the structured assessment of labor progress, disciplined fetal monitoring interpretation, recognition and management of prolonged and obstructed labor, the indications and limits of operative vaginal delivery and cesarean, and the escalation logic that protects both mother and baby.

## Core Rules

### Use the Partogram as a Decision Tool, Not Just a Record

The partogram exists to make deviation from normal progress visible early enough to act. Plot cervical dilation against time, and recognize that active labor normally progresses at least 1 cm per hour (commonly faster in multiparous women), with descent of the presenting part tracking dilation in the active phase. Alert and action lines are not decorative — crossing the alert line by two to four hours should prompt active review of the powers (uterine contractions), the passenger (fetal size, position, attitude), and the passages (pelvic adequacy). Dilation that plateaus for two hours in the active phase, or a second stage that exceeds recommended limits (roughly one hour without and two hours with epidural in nulliparous women, shorter in multiparous women), is a clinical signal demanding a structured reassessment, not passive waiting. Document the partogram in real time, because retrospective reconstruction hides the moment progress was lost.

### Interpret CTG in Context and Act on Category, Not on a Single Variable

Cardiotocography reduces perinatal death from intrapartum hypoxia but has poor specificity and drives over-intervention when read badly. Classify every tracing by category: baseline rate (normal 110 to 160), baseline variability (normal 5 to 25 bpm), presence and type of decelerations, and accelerations. A normal tracing (category I) reliably excludes current hypoxia, but a suspicious or pathological tracing (category II or III) demands immediate structured interpretation — is the baseline rising, is variability reducing, are there late or prolonged decelerations, a sinusoidal pattern, or a bradycardia? Always correlate the tracing with the clinical context: maternal fever, oxytocin, epidural hypotension, recent vaginal examination, meconium, and prematurity all change the meaning and the threshold to act. Apply conservative measures first for a concerning tracing — maternal repositioning, stop oxytocin, treat hypotension, correct pyrexia, consider a fluid bolus and paracetamol — but set an explicit time limit, typically 15 to 30 minutes, after which if the tracing has not improved you escalate to expedited delivery.

### Diagnose Prolonged and Obstructed Labor Through Structured Assessment, Not Impression

Failure to progress is a symptom with a differential, and treating every stall with oxytocin without diagnosis causes harm. Perform a structured review: assess the powers (frequency, duration, and strength of contractions, and whether they are synchronous), the passenger (estimated fetal weight, lie, presentation, position, attitude, caput and moulding), and the passages (clinical pelvic assessment, any cephalopelvic disproportion). Deep transverse arrest, occiput posterior, brow, or face presentation may explain a stalled labor and change the route of delivery. Marked caput succedaneum and moulding (overlapping skull bones not reducing) are signs of obstruction, not progress. Augment with oxytocin only when the uterus is genuinely underactive and obstruction has been excluded, and titrate to adequate contraction frequency while continuously monitoring the fetal heart rate, because hyperstimulation (more than five contractions in ten minutes) causes fetal hypoxia.

### Apply Clear Indications for Operative Vaginal Delivery and Know Its Limits

Operative vaginal delivery (ventouse or forceps) is appropriate when there is a clear indication — fetal compromise, prolonged second stage, or maternal exhaustion or medical condition precluding pushing — and when the prerequisites are met: fully dilated cervix, ruptured membranes, engaged head with at least one-fifth palpable abdominally, known position, adequate pelvis, empty bladder, adequate analgesia, and consent. Choose the instrument deliberately: ventouse is associated with less maternal trauma but more fetal scalp and intracranial risk, and is contraindicated below 34 weeks and in face or breech presentation; forceps may be preferred for occiput posterior or when maternal pushing is contraindicated. Define a clear stopping point — no more than three contractions or pulls, or 15 to 20 minutes, and abandon to cesarean if there is no descent — because failed operative delivery with prolonged traction causes fetal harm. Never attempt a high-cavity or mid-cavity delivery when the head is not engaged or position unknown; that is a cesarean.

### Reserve Cesarean for Clear Indications and Recognize the Urgency Categories

Cesarean delivery is indicated for failure to progress where operative delivery is unsafe, malpresentation, fetal compromise not responsive to conservative measures, antepartum hemorrhage with maternal or fetal compromise, cord prolapse, failed instrumental delivery, and selected maternal or fetal conditions. Classify urgency explicitly: category 1 (immediate threat to life of woman or fetus, target decision-to-delivery within 30 minutes), category 2 (maternal or fetal compromise that is not immediately life-threatening), category 3 (needs early delivery but no maternal or fetal compromise), and category 4 (elective, scheduled). The category determines team mobilization, anesthesia choice, and consent pace. Communicate the category, the indication, and the time of decision verbally to the team. Remember that category 1 cesarean carries higher maternal morbidity and anesthetic risk, particularly in a patient who has eaten recently or is hemodynamically unstable, so prepare for difficult airway and aspiration risk.

### Maintain Continuous Situational Awareness and Escalate Before the Crisis

Intrapartum deterioration is often gradual until it is sudden. Maintain explicit situational awareness: who is the most senior obstetrician present, where is the anesthetist, where is the neonatal team, is theater available, is blood available, what is the backup plan? For any abnormal tracing, abnormal progress, or maternal instability, call for senior review early and state the concern and the question — not just "can you review," but "category III tracing with reduced variability for 20 minutes despite repositioning and stopping oxytocin, I am preparing for category 1 cesarean." Run regular structured team communication, especially in prolonged or complex labor. Define and rehearse the escalation thresholds in advance so that the decision to act is not negotiated under pressure.

## Common Traps

### Tolerating Failure to Progress Because Contractions Continue

Persistent contractions can mask a complete lack of progress, and clinicians reassure themselves that "she is still working." Counter by plotting the partogram and treating a plateau in the active phase as a structured problem requiring reassessment of powers, passenger, and passages — not continued expectant management.

### Over-Reading or Under-Reading a Single CTG Segment

Focusing on the last five minutes, or conversely ignoring a slowly worsening tracing because "it was normal an hour ago," both cause error. Counter by reading the full trend, classifying by category, identifying the dominant abnormal feature, and setting an explicit time-bound reassessment or action.

### Augmenting With Oxytocin Without Excluding Obstruction

Reaching for oxytocin for a stalled labor without ruling out cephalopelvic disproportion, malposition, or obstruction risks hyperstimulation, fetal hypoxia, and uterine rupture. Counter by completing a structured passenger-and-passage assessment before augmentation and never titrating oxytocin without continuous fetal monitoring.

### Persisting Too Long With Operative Vaginal Delivery

Continued traction when the head is not descending risks fetal intracranial injury, scalp trauma, and maternal trauma. Counter by setting and respecting a clear stopping rule — limited pulls and limited time — and converting to cesarean when progress is inadequate.

### Attempting Instrumental Delivery Without Meeting Prerequisites

Applying ventouse or forceps when the cervix is not fully dilated, the head is high, or the position is unknown risks failed delivery, fetal injury, and maternal trauma. Counter by verifying every prerequisite before applying the instrument, and choosing cesarean when prerequisites are unmet.

### Misclassifying Cesarean Urgency and Under-Mobilizing the Team

Calling a category 2 as a category 3, or failing to communicate the category, delays anesthesia, neonatal, and theater response. Counter by stating the urgency category, indication, and decision time explicitly to the whole team at the moment of decision.

### Ignoring Maternal Status While Focusing on the Fetus

Concentrating on the tracing while the mother develops hypotension, fever, exhaustion, or hemorrhage. Counter by monitoring maternal observations and condition in parallel and treating maternal compromise as an independent reason to change the plan.

## Self-Check

- Have I plotted and actively interpreted the partogram, and have I identified any crossing of the alert or action line with a documented plan?
- For any concerning CTG, have I classified it by category, identified the dominant abnormal feature, applied conservative measures, and set an explicit time limit before escalation?
- For failure to progress, have I completed a structured assessment of powers, passenger, and passages before deciding on augmentation versus delivery?
- Before any operative vaginal delivery, have I verified every prerequisite and set a clear stopping rule?
- Have I stated the cesarean urgency category, indication, and decision time to the team, and mobilized anesthesia, neonatal, and theater appropriately?
- Have I monitored maternal observations and condition in parallel with fetal monitoring throughout labor?
- Have I escalated to senior obstetric input early and communicated the specific concern and question rather than a vague request to review?
