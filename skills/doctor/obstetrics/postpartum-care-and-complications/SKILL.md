# Postpartum Care and Complications

The puerperium is a recovery period that is also a window of concentrated risk: most maternal deaths occur in the days after delivery, and the causes — hemorrhage, sepsis, venous thromboembolism, hypertensive complications, and perinatal mental illness — are eminently treatable if recognized early. The central judgment problem is that postpartum symptoms overlap heavily with normal recovery. Fatigue, mild fever in the first 24 hours, lochia, perineal discomfort, breast engorgement, and emotional lability are all expected, and this overlap breeds complacency. The recurring failure is reassuring a deteriorating woman that her symptoms are "normal postpartum" while she is developing hemorrhage, sepsis, a deep vein thrombosis, or puerperal psychosis. This skill covers immediate postpartum recovery, hemorrhage and sepsis recognition, wound care, thromboembolism risk, lactation problems, and the structured follow-up and contraception planning that close the obstetric episode safely.

## Core Rules

### Monitor the Immediate Postpartum Period Actively, Not Passively

The first hours after delivery are the highest-risk window for postpartum hemorrhage and hemodynamic instability. Monitor blood pressure, pulse, respiratory rate, temperature, oxygen saturation, uterine tone, lochia, and urine output at defined intervals, and recognize that tachycardia and tachypnea often precede hypotension in a young, fit woman who compensates until late. A pulse above 100, a systolic below 90, or a falling urine output demands immediate review for hemorrhage or other compromise. Palpate the fundus and assess tone every 15 minutes in the first hour: a boggy, poorly contracted uterus is the most common cause of early hemorrhage and is corrected by uterine massage and uterotonic agents. Quantify blood loss deliberately rather than estimating, because visual estimation undercounts loss, especially at cesarean and after large-volume deliveries. Do not attribute persistent tachycardia, hypotension, or oliguria to "normal postpartum" without excluding hemorrhage, sepsis, and thromboembolism.

### Prevent, Recognize, and Treat Postpartum Hemorrhage Systematically

Postpartum hemorrhage is commonly defined as blood loss of 500 mL after vaginal delivery or 1000 mL after cesarean, or any loss causing hemodynamic instability, and it demands a structured cause-based approach using the four Ts: tone (uterine atony, the leading cause), trauma (cervical, vaginal, or perineal lacerations), tissue (retained placenta or clots), and thrombin (coagulopathy). Active management of the third stage — prophylactic uterotonic, controlled cord traction, uterine massage — reduces hemorrhage incidence and should be standard unless contraindicated. When hemorrhage occurs, resuscitate in parallel with diagnosis: large-bore access, fluids, crossmatch and activate the massive transfusion protocol, and simultaneously address the cause with uterotonics (oxytocin, then ergometrine, then carboprost, then misoprostol), bimanual compression, examination for and repair of trauma, evacuation of retained tissue, and correction of coagulopathy. Escalate to senior obstetric and anesthetic input early; hemorrhage that has not responded to first-line measures within minutes is a surgical and interventional radiology problem.

### Treat Suspected Puerperal Sepsis as an Emergency With a Bundle

Puerperal sepsis remains a leading, preventable cause of maternal death and presents subtly because tachycardia, mild fever, and leukocytosis overlap with normal postpartum physiology. Suspect sepsis in any postpartum woman with fever, tachycardia, tachypnea, uterine tenderness, foul lochia, wound erythema or discharge, breast erythema and pain, dysuria, or simply feeling unwell. Apply the sepsis six within an hour: give oxygen, take blood cultures and other cultures, give intravenous antibiotics, give intravenous fluids, measure lactate and urine output, and treat the source. Choose broad-spectrum antibiotics covering gram-positive, gram-negative, and anaerobic organisms including group A streptococcus, which can cause rapidly fatal necrotizing infection. Do not delay antibiotics for investigation; source control — wound debridement, evacuation of retained products, drainage of abscess — follows resuscitation. Reassess the response to resuscitation within an hour and escalate to critical care for persistent hypotension, rising lactate, or organ dysfunction.

### Assess and Manage Perineal and Cesarean Wounds Proactively

Wound complications — breakdown, infection, hematoma, and dehiscence — are common and under-recognized because patients attribute pain to normal healing. Examine any postpartum woman with increasing pain, fever, wound discharge, or separation of the wound edges. For perineal tears, assess for hematoma, infection, breakdown, and, with third- or fourth-degree tears, anal sphincter dysfunction (incontinence, flatus, fecal urgency) that may need surgical review. For cesarean wounds, distinguish superficial infection (managed with antibiotics and local care) from deep dehiscence and fascial breakdown requiring surgical exploration. Provide clear patient advice on warning signs at discharge, because most complications present after the woman has gone home. Women who have had operative delivery, obesity, diabetes, or prolonged rupture of membranes are at higher risk and warrant a lower threshold for review.

### Apply Structured Venous Thromboembolism Risk Assessment and Prophylaxis

Pregnancy and the puerperium are prothrombotic states, and venous thromboembolism is a leading cause of maternal death. Assess every woman's risk factors at admission and postpartum using a validated tool: age over 35, obesity, cesarean delivery, immobilization, preeclampsia, prolonged labor, previous venous thromboembolism, thrombophilia, smoking, and medical comorbidity. Prescribe pharmacological thromboprophylaxis (low-molecular-weight heparin) for women at elevated risk for the recommended duration — typically at least ten days postpartum, and longer (six weeks) for the highest-risk groups — unless contraindicated. Combine pharmacological prophylaxis with early mobilization, hydration, and mechanical prophylaxis. Reassess risk if the clinical course changes, for example with readmission, infection, or reoperation.

### Manage Lactation Problems Promptly to Prevent Progression

Breastfeeding problems cause distress, readmission, and cessation of breastfeeding, and most are treatable if recognized early. Distinguish engorgement (bilateral, diffuse, often in the first days, managed with frequent feeding, warm compresses before and cold after, and support) from mastitis (unilateral, localized erythema, pain, fever, systemic illness, managed with continued feeding or expressing, antibiotics covering staphylococcus, and review within 24 hours) from breast abscess (a fluctuant collection requiring ultrasound-guided drainage in addition to antibiotics). Persistent mastitis or inadequate response to antibiotics within 48 hours raises abscess or inflammatory cancer and demands imaging. Address infant latch, feeding frequency, and maternal technique, and involve a lactation specialist early. Recognize that severe postpartum pain, difficulty feeding, and fatigue compound mental health risk and must be addressed together.

### Plan Structured Postpartum Follow-up and Contraception

Postpartum care does not end at discharge. Provide a structured follow-up that addresses physical recovery (involution, lochia, wound healing, continence, contraception), mental health (screening for depression with a validated tool, and direct questioning about thoughts of self-harm or harm to the infant), infant feeding, perineal or pelvic floor rehabilitation, and management of complications of pregnancy such as gestational diabetes and hypertension, which flag long-term cardiometabolic risk. Discuss contraception before discharge and provide an effective method, recognizing that ovulation can resume within weeks, particularly in non-lactating women, and that interpregnancy spacing under 12 to 18 months increases adverse outcomes. Document the follow-up plan, the warning signs that should prompt re-presentation, and the contraception choice.

## Common Traps

### Reassuring That Symptoms Are "Normal Postpartum"

Attributing tachycardia, fever, breathlessness, or heavy bleeding to normal recovery while hemorrhage, sepsis, thromboembolism, or cardiomyopathy develop. Counter by re-anchoring to red-flag thresholds and investigating any persistent abnormal vital sign or systemic symptom.

### Underestimating Blood Loss by Visual Estimation

Eyeballing blood loss undercounts it by a third or more, delaying recognition of hemorrhage. Counter by quantifying loss deliberately, weighing swabs, using calibrated drapes, and treating hemodynamic instability as hemorrhage until excluded.

### Delaying Antibiotics in Suspected Puerperal Sepsis

Waiting for cultures or a rising white count before treating a clinically septic postpartum woman allows progression to septic shock and group A streptococcal necrotizing infection. Counter by giving broad-spectrum antibiotics within the hour as part of a sepsis bundle.

### Omitting Thromboprophylaxis in Higher-Risk Women

Forgetting postpartum venous thromboembolism risk assessment, especially after cesarean or in obese or immobilized women. Counter by making risk assessment and prescription a mandatory discharge checklist item.

### Treating Mastitis With Antibiotics Alone Without Continued Milk Removal

Stopping or reducing feeding during mastitis worsens stasis and promotes abscess. Counter by combining antibiotics with continued feeding or expressing and reviewing at 24 to 48 hours for response.

### Missing Postpartum Mental Illness by Conflating It With the Blues

Dismissing low mood, anxiety, insomnia, or intrusive thoughts as the blues, delaying diagnosis of depression or postpartum psychosis. Counter by screening with a validated tool, asking directly about harm, and escalating postpartum psychosis immediately.

### Discharging Without a Follow-up or Contraception Plan

Closing the obstetric episode without structured follow-up, mental health screening, contraception, and cardiometabolic risk counseling. Counter by documenting a postpartum plan covering recovery, mental health, contraception, and warning signs at discharge.

## Self-Check

- Have I monitored postpartum observations actively and excluded hemorrhage, sepsis, and thromboembolism before attributing abnormal vitals to normal recovery?
- For any postpartum hemorrhage, have I applied the four Ts framework, resuscitated in parallel with treating the cause, and escalated early to senior and anesthetic input?
- For suspected puerperal sepsis, have I delivered the sepsis bundle within an hour including broad-spectrum antibiotics and source control?
- Have I assessed venous thromboembolism risk and prescribed prophylaxis for the appropriate duration in higher-risk women?
- Have I examined any painful or draining wound, distinguished superficial from deep infection, and arranged surgical review where indicated?
- For mastitis or breast problems, have I combined antibiotics with continued milk removal and set a review for response within 24 to 48 hours?
- Have I screened for postpartum mental illness, asked directly about thoughts of harm, and escalated suspected postpartum psychosis as an emergency?
- Have I documented a structured postpartum follow-up plan including contraception, warning signs, and management of pregnancy complications?
