# Acute Psychiatric Emergencies and Compulsory Care

Acute behavioral emergencies — severe agitation, psychosis, threatened or actual violence, acute self-harm — are situations where clinicians simultaneously face immediate physical danger, legal constraints on liberty, and the temptation to resolve distress with a syringe. The central discipline is to treat these as medical emergencies first: an agitated patient may be hypoxic, hypoglycemic, septic, in withdrawal, or suffering an intracranial catastrophe, and sedating them without diagnosis can kill. The safest sequence is always to secure the environment, rule out reversible medical causes, de-escalate verbally, and use restraint or rapid tranquillization only when less restrictive measures have failed or are unsafe to attempt. Restraint and involuntary measures carry real morbidity and mortality and must meet legal thresholds, be time-limited, and be documented with specific facts. This skill covers de-escalation as the first intervention, the safe use of rapid tranquillization with monitoring, restraint risks, capacity assessment, involuntary admission criteria, and the medical causes of agitation that must not be missed. When acuity exceeds your team's capability, escalate to psychiatry, security, and where needed critical care, early.

## Core Rules

### Secure Safety First and Never Manage Acute Agitation Alone

Before any assessment, ensure the safety of the patient, staff, and other patients: adequate staffing, removal of weapons and ligature risks, open exit routes, and access to a team trained in restraint if needed. Never interview an acutely agitated or potentially violent patient alone or in a confined space without a clear escape path. Position yourself near the door, remove personal items that could be used as weapons, and have security or a code team immediately available. Safety is not a preamble to care — it is the first clinical act. If the situation cannot be made safe with available staff, summon help before engaging; a clinician injured in an uncontrolled encounter cannot care for anyone.

### Rule Out Medical Causes of Agitation Before Attributing It to Psychiatric Illness

New, acute, or atypical agitation in any patient — especially the elderly, those with no psychiatric history, or those with medical comorbidity — must be treated as possibly delirium or an organic emergency until proven otherwise. Check glucose immediately, and assess for hypoxia, sepsis, CNS infection or hemorrhage, hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia, uremia or hepatic encephalopathy, thyroid storm, drug intoxication or withdrawal, head trauma, and post-ictal states. Obtain vital signs, glucose, and where indicated bloods, toxicology, and imaging. Sedating a patient with undiagnosed hypoxia, hypoglycemia, sepsis, or intracranial hemorrhage can precipitate respiratory arrest or death. The default assumption that "agitation equals psychiatric" is one of the most dangerous errors in emergency care; when the cause is uncertain or the picture is atypical, escalate for medical workup and treat it as delirium until excluded.

### Use Verbal De-Escalation as the First and Preferred Intervention

De-escalation is a skill, not a delay tactic, and it succeeds in the majority of cases when applied competently. Approach calmly, respect personal space, avoid provocative posture or language, use a quiet and non-confrontational tone, listen actively, set clear and reasonable limits, offer choices where possible, and identify and address the patient's stated needs (pain, thirst, fear, withdrawal). Avoid power struggles, avoid forcing compliance, and give the patient time to respond. Verbal de-escalation preserves dignity, reduces trauma, and avoids the morbidity of restraint. Only when de-escalation fails, is refused, or is unsafe because of imminent danger to self or others should physical or chemical restraint be considered. Document which de-escalation techniques were tried and why they were insufficient.

### Apply Restraint and Rapid Tranquillization Only With Justification, Monitoring, and a Plan

When restraint is necessary, use the least restrictive measure for the shortest possible time, with adequate trained personnel, and document the specific behaviors that justified it. Physical restraint carries risks of positional asphyxia, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac arrest, and psychological trauma; never restrain a patient prone or leave them unmonitored. Rapid tranquillization, when required, should follow a clear protocol: typically an oral offer first (e.g., lorazepam with or without an antipsychotic), moving to intramuscular only if oral is refused or unsafe. A common IM combination is an antipsychotic (e.g., haloperidol or olanzapine) with lorazepam, but know your institution's protocol and contraindications — avoid olanzapine IM with lorazepam in the elderly, QT-prolonging combinations in cardiac disease, and excessive dosing in substance-intoxicated patients. After any IM medication, monitor vital signs, level of consciousness, airway, and ECG continuously, because respiratory depression, hypotension, QT prolongation, and oversedation can occur. Have reversal agents and resuscitation equipment available. Reassess the patient at defined intervals and release from restraint as soon as safely possible.

### Assess Capacity as Decision-Specific and Time-Specific

Capacity is not global and not permanent; it must be assessed for the specific decision at hand, at the time the decision is needed. A patient may lack capacity to refuse admission during acute psychosis yet regain it after treatment. Apply the functional test: does the patient understand the information relevant to the decision, retain it, weigh it as part of a reasoning process, and communicate the choice? Assume capacity unless demonstrated otherwise, and do not equate a diagnosis (psychosis, intoxication) with incapacity. When capacity is in doubt, assess carefully, involve a second senior clinician, and document the specific findings — not a conclusion. Where a patient lacks capacity, act in their best interests using the least restrictive option and follow the relevant mental-capacity legislation and any advance directives or surrogate decision-makers.

### Know and Apply Involuntary Admission Criteria Correctly

Involuntary admission or treatment is justified only when legal criteria are met — typically a mental disorder of a nature or degree warranting hospital treatment, a serious risk to the health or safety of the patient or others, and the necessity of treatment that cannot be provided less restrictively. Know your jurisdiction's exact statutes, the roles of the clinicians who must sign, the durations, and the patient's rights to review and advocacy. Involuntary measures are grave restrictions of liberty and must be justified by specific facts and behaviors, not by inconvenience or disagreement. When criteria are met, act without dangerous delay; when they are not, do not coerce. Involve psychiatry, administration, and legal/advocacy services early, and document meticulously.

### Escalate Early When Acuity Exceeds Capability

Recognize the limits of your setting and team. Call psychiatry for diagnostic and disposition decisions in acute psychiatric presentations; call security or a code team for imminent violence; call critical care or anesthesia for airway compromise, severe medical instability, or complications of intoxication or restraint. Do not attempt to manage a violent or medically complex psychiatric emergency short-staffed or in an unmonitored area. Early escalation prevents harm to patients and staff and is a sign of competence, not failure.

## Common Traps

### Sedating Agitation Before Excluding a Medical Cause

Treating agitation as psychiatric when it is delirium, hypoxia, hypoglycemia, sepsis, or intracranial disaster can be fatal. Counter by checking glucose and vitals immediately and pursuing medical causes, especially in the elderly, the medically ill, and those without psychiatric history.

### Skipping or Rushing De-Escalation

Jumping to restraint when verbal de-escalation has not been genuinely attempted increases harm and trauma. Counter by applying structured de-escalation first and documenting the attempt.

### Physical Restraint Without Monitoring or With Unsafe Positioning

Prone restraint, inadequate staffing, or leaving a restrained patient unmonitored causes positional asphyxia and death. Counter by using trained teams, supine or seated positioning, and continuous monitoring until release.

### Giving Rapid Tranquillization Without Post-Dose Monitoring

Oversedation, respiratory depression, hypotension, and QT prolongation occur after IM medication. Counter by continuous monitoring of airway, vitals, and level of consciousness, with resuscitation equipment available.

### Treating Capacity as Global or Permanent

A diagnosis does not equal incapacity, and capacity is decision- and time-specific. Counter by assessing the specific decision with a functional test, involving a second clinician when in doubt, and documenting findings.

### Applying Involuntary Measures Without Meeting Legal Criteria

Coercing admission or treatment when statutory thresholds are not met violates rights and invites legal harm. Counter by knowing and applying your jurisdiction's exact criteria, involving the required clinicians, and documenting specific facts.

## Self-Check

- Have I secured the safety of the patient, staff, and others before engagement, and avoided managing acute agitation alone or in an unsafe space?
- Have I ruled out medical causes of agitation (glucose, vitals, and where indicated bloods, toxicology, imaging) before attributing it to psychiatric illness, especially in atypical or elderly presentations?
- Did I attempt structured verbal de-escalation first and document why restraint or medication became necessary?
- If I used restraint or rapid tranquillization, was it the least restrictive option with adequate trained staff, correct positioning, and continuous post-dose monitoring of airway, vitals, and consciousness?
- Did I assess capacity for the specific decision at the specific time using a functional test, and document findings rather than conclusions?
- If I considered involuntary admission, did I apply the correct jurisdictional criteria, involve the required clinicians, and document the specific facts that justify the restriction of liberty?
- Have I escalated to psychiatry, security, or critical care early when acuity exceeded my team's capability?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
