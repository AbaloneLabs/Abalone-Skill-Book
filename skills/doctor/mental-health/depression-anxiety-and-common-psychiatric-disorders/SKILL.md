# Depression, Anxiety, and Common Psychiatric Disorders

Mood, anxiety, and psychotic disorders make up a large share of general medical presentations, and they are where clinicians most often commit two opposite errors: treating a symptom checklist as sufficient for diagnosis, or dismissing genuine psychiatric illness as a "situational" problem needing only reassurance. Both errors harm patients. A high PHQ-9 score is not a diagnosis of major depressive disorder until organic causes, bipolar disorder, substance use, and psychotic features have been considered; a "mild" presentation can still carry serious suicide risk. The disciplined approach uses validated scales to quantify severity and track response, but anchors every decision in a syndromal diagnosis, a search for organic and mimicking causes, explicit attention to bipolar versus unipolar distinction, and a clear threshold for specialist escalation. This skill covers first-line pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy choices, the monitoring that antipsychotics and mood stabilizers demand, the suicide-risk overlay that applies to every mood-disorder patient, and the judgment of when a presentation exceeds primary-care scope and requires psychiatry.

## Core Rules

### Quantify With Validated Scales, but Diagnose Syndromally

Use PHQ-9 for depression, GAD-7 for anxiety, and equivalent tools to establish baseline severity and to monitor response over time; serial scores are far more informative than impression alone. But never let a score substitute for a diagnosis. A high PHQ-9 can reflect bereavement, bipolar depression, substance-induced mood change, hypothyroidism, anemia, sleep apnea, or an underlying medical illness. Make a syndromal diagnosis using DSM/ICD criteria, then use the scale to track it. Always review the suicide item (PHQ-9 item 9) explicitly and act on it regardless of the total score — a low total with an affirmative item 9 is a high-risk patient. Re-administer the scale at each follow-up to confirm response and detect deterioration; lack of response is a signal to revisit the diagnosis, not merely to push the dose.

### Rule Out Organic Causes and Mimics Before Committing to a Primary Psychiatric Diagnosis

Before labeling a presentation as primary depression or anxiety, screen for and address organic contributors: thyroid disease, anemia, vitamin B12 deficiency, sleep disorders, chronic infection or malignancy, medication side effects (corticosteroids, beta-blockers, hormonal agents), and substance use or withdrawal. New psychiatric symptoms in older patients, atypical presentations, cognitive change, or neurological signs demand investigation for delirium, dementia, space-occupying lesion, or metabolic disturbance. A psychiatric diagnosis made without excluding reversible organic causes is a common source of harm and missed serious disease; when the presentation is atypical or first-episode late in life, escalate for further workup rather than defaulting to an SSRI.

### Always Distinguish Bipolar From Unipolar Depression Before Prescribing an Antidepressant

Prescribing an antidepressant to a patient with underlying bipolar disorder can precipitate mania, mixed states, rapid cycling, and increased suicide risk. Screen every depressed patient for prior manic or hypomanic episodes using a structured tool (MDQ) and direct questioning about periods of elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, racing thoughts, impulsive behavior, and grandiosity; collateral is essential because patients often do not recognize past hypomania as pathological. A history of antidepressant-induced activation, early-onset or postpartum depression, psychotic features, or a strong family history of bipolar disorder should raise suspicion. When bipolar depression is possible, do not start an antidepressant alone; involve psychiatry and consider a mood stabilizer or atypical antipsychotic with bipolar indication. Document the screening explicitly — missing bipolar disorder is one of the most consequential and most avoidable errors in primary psychiatric care.

### Choose First-Line Therapy by Diagnosis and Patient Factors, and Set Expectations

For mild to moderate unipolar depression and most anxiety disorders, first-line treatment is psychotherapy (cognitive behavioral therapy has the strongest evidence), an SSRI, or both; combined treatment outperforms either alone for moderate-to-severe presentations. Choose a specific SSRI based on side-effect profile, interactions, prior response, and patient factors (e.g., avoid paroxetine in pregnancy and in the elderly due to anticholinergic load). Start low, titrate slowly, and counsel that full benefit takes four to six weeks — premature discontinuation from perceived non-response is a major cause of treatment failure. For schizophrenia and other psychoses, an antipsychotic is first-line, but choice, dosing, and monitoring belong with a prescriber experienced in these agents. Set explicit expectations about onset, duration of trial (treat to remission, then continue at least six to twelve months for a first episode; longer for recurrent), and the monitoring plan.

### Monitor Pharmacotherapy Actively, Especially Metabolic and Extrapyramidal Effects

Antipsychotics require baseline and serial monitoring: weight, waist circumference, fasting glucose or HbA1c, lipids, and blood pressure for metabolic syndrome; prolactin where symptomatic; ECG for QT-prolonging agents and in cardiac patients; and active surveillance for extrapyramidal symptoms, akathisia, tardive dyskinesia, and neuroleptic malignant syndrome. SSRIs and SNRIs require counseling on activation, gastrointestinal effects, sexual dysfunction, hyponatremia (especially in the elderly), bleeding risk, and serotonin syndrome when combined with other serotonergic agents. Lithium demands baseline and serial renal function, thyroid function, and levels, with attention to toxicity signs and drug interactions (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, diuretics). Lamotrigine requires slow titration to avoid Stevens-Johnson syndrome. Document the monitoring plan at initiation and act on abnormal results rather than filing them away.

### Layer Suicide Risk Assessment Onto Every Mood-Disorder Patient

Depression and bipolar disorder carry substantial suicide risk, and risk fluctuates during treatment — including during early recovery when energy may return before mood. Assess suicide risk explicitly at every encounter using a structured approach (ideation, intent, plan, means, protective factors), and escalate disposition when imminent risk is present. Do not assume that a patient started on an antidepressant is now safe; the early treatment period and any dose change are windows of heightened risk. When risk is present, follow the dedicated suicide-risk workflow before any pharmacotherapy decision.

### Define Clear Thresholds for Psychiatric Referral

Refer to psychiatry, without delay, for: suspected or confirmed bipolar disorder, psychosis or schizophrenia, treatment-resistant depression (failure of two adequate trials), severe or rapidly worsening symptoms, significant suicide or self-harm risk, complex comorbidity (substance use, personality disorder, medical complexity), diagnostic uncertainty, and pregnancy or significant medical illness complicating pharmacotherapy. Refer urgently for first-episode psychosis, manic or mixed states, catatonia, and any presentation with command hallucinations or imminent suicide risk. A delayed referral in these situations worsens outcomes; do not manage these in isolation, and escalate to crisis or emergency services when acuity is high.

## Common Traps

### Diagnosing From a Score Alone

A high PHQ-9 or GAD-7 does not establish major depressive or generalized anxiety disorder. Counter by making a syndromal diagnosis and excluding organic causes and bipolar disorder before committing to treatment.

### Missing Bipolar Disorder and Triggering Mania With an Antidepressant

Failure to screen for past hypomania or mania leads to antidepressant-induced destabilization. Counter by screening every depressed patient with a structured tool and collateral, and involving psychiatry when bipolar is possible.

### Dismissing the Suicide Item Because the Total Score Is Low

A low total PHQ-9 with an affirmative item 9 is a high-risk patient. Counter by reviewing item 9 explicitly and acting on it regardless of the total.

### Assuming Non-Response Means the Dose Is Too Low

Non-response at an adequate dose and duration should prompt diagnostic reconsideration (bipolar? organic cause? substance use? comorbid anxiety?) before escalation. Counter by revisiting the diagnosis after an inadequate trial.

### Under-Monitoring Antipsychotics and Mood Stabilizers

Skipping baseline and serial metabolic, renal, thyroid, or ECG monitoring allows preventable harm. Counter by documenting and scheduling the monitoring at initiation and acting on abnormal results.

### Treating "Mild" Symptoms as Not Requiring Follow-Up

Even mild depression or anxiety can worsen, and the early treatment period is a window of suicide risk. Counter by scheduling structured follow-up with serial scales and explicit suicide reassessment.

## Self-Check

- Have I used a validated scale to quantify severity and track response, while making a syndromal diagnosis rather than treating the score as the diagnosis?
- Have I screened for and addressed organic causes and mimics before committing to a primary psychiatric diagnosis, especially in atypical or late-onset presentations?
- Have I explicitly screened for bipolar disorder (with collateral) before prescribing an antidepressant, and involved psychiatry when bipolar is possible?
- Is my first-line therapy choice appropriate to the diagnosis and patient factors, with clear counseling on onset, duration, and the plan to treat to remission?
- Have I documented baseline and serial monitoring for the specific agent chosen (metabolic for antipsychotics, renal/thyroid/levels for lithium, and so on)?
- Have I assessed suicide risk at this encounter and escalated disposition when risk is present?
- Have I defined and acted on a clear threshold for psychiatric referral for bipolar, psychotic, treatment-resistant, severe, or high-risk presentations?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
