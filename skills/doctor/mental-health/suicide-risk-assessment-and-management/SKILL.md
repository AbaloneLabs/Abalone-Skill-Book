# Suicide Risk Assessment and Management

Suicide risk assessment is one of the highest-stakes encounters in clinical medicine, and it is the encounter where clinician discomfort most often produces dangerous shortcuts. The central error is treating risk assessment as a box-checking exercise that yields a tidy score, rather than as a structured clinical judgment built from ideation, intent, plan, means access, risk factors, protective factors, and the dynamic interaction between them. No tool replaces a direct, unhurried conversation. Patients at genuine imminent risk rarely present with a single dramatic clue; more often the danger is assembled from details a rushed clinician never asks about. This skill covers how to gather the information that actually predicts near-term risk, how to translate that into a defensible disposition, and the non-negotiable safety behaviors (never leaving a high-risk patient alone, means restriction, escalation) that prevent death. Underestimate risk and a patient dies; overestimate and you harm trust and autonomy. The discipline is to be thorough, direct, and humble, and to escalate whenever uncertainty exceeds your competence.

## Core Rules

### Ask Directly and Specifically About Ideation, Intent, Plan, and Means

Asking about suicide does not plant the idea; this is a discredited myth that still quietly shapes avoidance. Ask in plain, direct language: "Are you thinking about killing yourself?" Then escalate specificity in sequence. Ideation — passive ("sometimes I wish I wouldn't wake up") versus active, frequency, duration, controllability. Intent — do they intend to act, and how soon. Plan — specificity of method, time, location, preparatory acts (giving away possessions, writing a note, rehearsal, acquiring means). Means access — do they have firearms, medications, ligatures, or other lethal means available right now. Lethality of plan and access to means are the strongest predictors of near-term death; a detailed plan with available firearms is a far graver signal than vague passive ideation. Document each element in the patient's own words where possible. When in doubt about intent, ask the follow-up question rather than assuming.

### Build a Dynamic Risk Formulation, Not a Single Number

Structured tools (Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale, SAD PERSONS) are aids to structure thinking, not decision rules. Use them to ensure you have covered the domains, then synthesize a formulation that weighs modifiable versus fixed factors and acute versus chronic risk. Risk factors include prior attempts (the single strongest predictor), psychiatric illness (especially depression, bipolar, schizophrenia, substance use), recent loss or crisis, hopelessness, agitation, insomnia, chronic pain, and access to lethal means. Protective factors include responsibility to children or dependents, religious or moral objection, social support, future orientation, and engagement in care. Crucially, risk is dynamic — reassess after any change: a new crisis, intoxication, discharge from a higher level of care, or a failed attempt. A static one-time stratification into "low/moderate/high" that is never revisited is a common cause of adverse events. When the formulation is uncertain or the acuity exceeds your training, escalate to psychiatry or the crisis team before disposition.

### Make Disposition Match the Imminent Risk, and Document the Reasoning

Disposition follows from the formulation, not from bed availability or patient preference alone. Imminent risk — active intent, plan, means, or a recent attempt — generally requires constant observation, psychiatric evaluation, and admission or involuntary hold; the patient must not be left alone at any point, including during waits and transfers. High but not imminent risk usually warrants crisis evaluation, urgent psychiatric follow-up within 24-72 hours, and consideration of partial hospitalization. Lower risk may be manageable in the community with a safety plan and close follow-up, but only if protective factors are genuine and the patient can articulate what they will do if ideation intensifies. State explicitly in the note why the chosen disposition is safe enough and what would change it. Never discharge a patient solely because they "deny" ideation when collateral history, behavior, or means access contradict the denial; behavior and history outweigh verbal reassurance, and when they conflict, escalate.

### Construct a Collaborative, Concrete Safety Plan and Restrict Means

A safety plan is not a no-suicide contract — those have no demonstrated protective value and create false reassurance. A real safety plan (e.g., Stanley-Brown) is a written, collaborative document covering: personal warning signs, internal coping strategies, people and social settings that distract, people to ask for help, professionals and crisis lines (including the local crisis number) to contact, and steps to make the environment safe — most importantly restricting access to lethal means. Means restriction is one of the few interventions with strong evidence for reducing death: firearm storage off-site or locked, medication quantity limits, removal of ligatures. Counsel family or household members explicitly and document. A safety plan without means restriction in a patient with firearm access is dangerously incomplete. Confirm the patient understands and agrees, and arrange the follow-up that makes the plan real.

### Obtain Collateral and Treat the Assessment as Continuous

Self-report is necessary but insufficient. Obtain collateral from family, partners, prior treaters, and records whenever possible — patients routinely minimize or conceal. Check prescription drug monitoring programs, recent discharges, and prior psychiatric contacts. Reassess at every encounter and at transitions of care, because risk rises sharply around discharge from inpatient or emergency settings. Communicate risk and the plan across the team and to the receiving clinician; a risk assessment that lives only in one clinician's head is a failure of handover. When you cannot obtain collateral and risk is uncertain, lean toward a safer disposition and escalate.

### Know the Threshold and Process for Involuntary Intervention

Involuntary hold or commitment is justified when a patient poses an imminent danger to self (or others) or is gravely disabled and lacks capacity to accept needed care, and when less restrictive alternatives cannot ensure safety. Know your jurisdiction's exact criteria, duration, and documentation requirements. Involuntary measures are serious restrictions of liberty and must meet the legal threshold — but when that threshold is met, hesitation kills. Do not let procedural uncertainty delay safety; involve psychiatry, security, and administration early, and document the specific facts (not conclusions) that justify the hold.

## Common Traps

### Treating Denial as Reassurance When Behavior Contradicts It

A patient who denies ideation but has stockpiled pills, written a note, or made preparatory acts is high-risk regardless of the denial. Counter by weighting observed behavior, history, and means access above verbal denial, and escalate when they conflict.

### Relying on a No-Suicide Contract for Safety

Contracts have no protective efficacy and can substitute for real intervention. Counter by building a concrete written safety plan with means restriction and confirmed follow-up instead.

### Leaving a High-Risk Patient Unattended

A patient identified as at risk must not be left alone to use the bathroom, wait for transport, or sit in a hallway. Counter by instituting constant one-to-one observation until handover to a responsible clinician, and physically removing means.

### Forgetting Means Restriction, Especially Firearms

A safety plan without removing access to the chosen lethal means does not address the actual risk. Counter by explicitly counseling and arranging firearm and medication restriction with household members and documenting it.

### Using a Risk Score as a Discharge Reflex

A "low" score can lull a clinician into discharge when dynamic factors (acute crisis, intoxication, recent loss) are present. Counter by synthesizing a formulation that weighs acute, modifiable factors and reassesses at every change.

### Discharging Without Concrete Follow-Up

"Told to follow up" is not follow-up. Counter by arranging a specific appointment, confirming contact information, providing crisis numbers, and, for higher-risk patients, making a welfare-check or assertive outreach plan.

## Self-Check

- Have I asked directly and specifically about ideation, intent, plan, preparatory acts, and access to lethal means, and documented each in the patient's words?
- Have I built a dynamic formulation weighing acute and chronic risk and protective factors, rather than relying on a single score, and planned to reassess at changes?
- Does the disposition match the imminent risk, with explicit reasoning in the note for why it is safe enough and what would change it?
- If the patient is at risk, is constant observation in place and are they never left alone during waits, transfers, or procedures?
- Is there a concrete, collaborative safety plan with means restriction (especially firearms and medications) and confirmed follow-up, not a no-suicide contract?
- Have I obtained collateral and treated the assessment as continuous across encounters and handovers?
- If I considered involuntary intervention, did I apply the correct jurisdictional criteria, involve the right services early, and document the specific facts?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
