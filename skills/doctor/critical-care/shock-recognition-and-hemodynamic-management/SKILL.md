# Shock Recognition and Hemodynamic Management

Shock is a life-threatening state of inadequate cellular oxygen delivery relative to demand, and the window for correcting it before irreversible organ injury is narrow. The danger in shock management is not only the failure to recognize it early — it is the failure to recognize it at all. Compensated shock can hide behind a "normal" blood pressure and a calm-looking patient while perfusion is silently collapsing, and by the time hypotension is obvious, reserves are nearly exhausted. A second danger is treating the number (the pressure) rather than the problem (the perfusion): pushing fluids or titrating vasopressors to an arbitrary target without asking whether the heart can handle the load, whether the patient is fluid-responsive, or whether the underlying cause is being fixed. This skill covers the disciplined recognition of shock, the classification of its physiology, and the escalation of hemodynamic support — always with the understanding that shock is a time-critical, high-mortality condition requiring early intensivist involvement.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Shock Before Hypotension Is Present

Do not wait for the blood pressure to drop before diagnosing shock. Compensated shock produces tachycardia, tachypnea, cool or mottled extremities, delayed capillary refill, narrowed pulse pressure, altered mental status, oliguria, and elevated lactate — often while mean arterial pressure (MAP) is still technically normal. Hypotension is a late, decompensated sign and signals failure of compensatory mechanisms. Treat the constellation of perfusion signs, the rising lactate, and the clinical picture, not the single systolic number. A patient with a normal blood pressure but cold, clammy skin, confusion, and a lactate of 4 is in shock. When in doubt, escalate to a higher level of care immediately — under-recognition of compensated shock is a recurring cause of preventable death.

### Classify the Physiology to Guide Therapy, but Expect Overlap

Classify shock by hemodynamic profile to frame therapy, while remembering that mixed and evolving physiology is the rule, not the exception. Hypovolemic and distributive (septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic) shock are preload-dependent and fluid-responsive early on; cardiogenic and obstructive shock are not — fluids can worsen them. Use the bedside picture: warm shock (warm extremities, wide pulse pressure, low diastolic) suggests distributive; cold shock (cool extremities, narrow pulse pressure) suggests low-output states. Bedside ultrasound (cardiac, IVC, lung, focused assessment) rapidly distinguishes a failing, underfilled, or obstructed heart from vasodilatory states and should be performed early. When the picture is mixed or unclear, do not commit to a single pathway — reassess continuously and involve critical care.

### Assess Fluid Responsiveness Before Every Fluid Bolus

Fluid helps only if it increases stroke volume, and roughly half of critically ill patients are not fluid-responsive at any given moment. Give fluids as a deliberate test, not an automatic reflex. Use dynamic measures — passive leg raise, fluid challenge with reassessment of stroke volume variation, pulse pressure variation in appropriately sedated ventilated patients, or point-of-care cardiac output — rather than static measures like central venous pressure, which poorly predict responsiveness. Reassess after every bolus: did perfusion improve, did lactate fall, did the patient develop pulmonary edema? If the patient is no longer fluid-responsive or is showing signs of volume overload (rising oxygen requirement, crackles, B-lines), stop fluids and move to vasopressors or inotropes. Blind fluid stacking causes harm, including pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome, and worsening of right heart strain.

### Choose Vasopressors and Inotropes by the Underlying Defect

Select vasoactive agents to correct the specific physiologic defect, not by habit. Norepinephrine is first-line for septic and most distributive shock because it provides both alpha and modest beta activity. Vasopressin is added as a catecholamine-sparing agent in septic shock with rising norepinephrine requirements. Epinephrine is useful for anaphylaxis and as an inotrope in selected low-output states but increases lactate and myocardial oxygen demand. Phenylephrine is pure alpha and useful when tachyarrhythmia limits other agents, but it reduces stroke volume. Dobutamine or milrinone are added for cardiogenic shock with low cardiac output and adequate or high filling pressures, but they can worsen hypotension and should generally follow, not precede, blood pressure restoration. In refractory cardiogenic shock, early mechanical circulatory support and urgent cardiology or intensivist consultation are indicated — do not delay escalation while escalating drug doses indefinitely.

### Target Perfusion Endpoints, Not Just Blood Pressure

A MAP of 65 mmHg is a reasonable floor, not the goal. The goal is restored cellular perfusion. Track multiple endpoints simultaneously: mental status, urine output, skin perfusion, lactate clearance (aim for a fall of at least 10% per hour in septic shock), central venous oxygen saturation where available, and resolution of base deficit. A patient can have a "good" blood pressure and still be poorly perfused; conversely, a chronically hypertensive patient may need a higher MAP to perfuse adequately. Reassess endpoints frequently and adjust therapy to the trend, not a single value. Persistent lactate elevation or rising lactate despite adequate pressure is an ominous sign demanding re-evaluation for ongoing ischemia, inadequate source control, or mitochondrial dysfunction — and warrants escalation.

### Identify and Treat the Underlying Cause Concurrently

Hemodynamic support buys time; it does not cure shock. Pursue the cause in parallel with resuscitation: cultures and broad-spectrum antibiotics for sepsis, hemorrhage control and transfusion for hemorrhagic shock, reperfusion for cardiogenic shock from infarction, decompression for tension pneumothorax or tamponade, reversal agents and supportive care for toxidromes. Source control — drainage, debridement, device removal — is as important as fluids and pressors in septic shock and should be achieved as soon as the patient can tolerate it. Resuscitation without cause-directed therapy is a bridge to nowhere.

### Escalate Early and Communicate Clearly

Shock is a time-critical, high-mortality condition. Escalate to ICU or a higher level of care at the first sign of instability rather than after multiple failed interventions. Communicate with a structured format (SBAR or equivalent): the working diagnosis, what has been done, the current hemodynamic status, the trends, and what you need. Reassess and re-communicate frequently. Document the reasoning behind each intervention and the response. When the patient is not improving as expected, explicitly reconsider the diagnosis (is this really septic shock, or is there occult cardiogenic, obstructive, or hemorrhagic shock?) and seek specialist consultation without delay.

## Common Traps

### Equating Normal Blood Pressure with Adequate Perfusion

Relying on blood pressure alone misses compensated shock. Counter by assessing perfusion signs, lactate, mental status, urine output, and skin perfusion in every acutely ill patient, and by treating the patient, not the number.

### Reflexive Fluid Stacking

Giving repeated fluid boluses without assessing responsiveness or watching for overload. Counter by testing fluid responsiveness, reassessing after every bolus, and moving to vasopressors or inotropes when the patient is no longer responsive or shows signs of overload.

### Treating the Pressure Instead of the Defect

Titrating vasopressors to a number without diagnosing whether the problem is low preload, pump failure, obstruction, or vasodilation. Counter by classifying the physiology with bedside ultrasound and clinical exam and matching therapy to the defect.

### Missing Obstructive and Mixed Shock

Attributing hypotension to sepsis or hypovolemia when tension pneumothorax, tamponade, or massive pulmonary embolism is the cause, or failing to recognize that cardiogenic shock can coexist with sepsis. Counter by performing early bedside ultrasound and reconsidering the diagnosis whenever the response to therapy is poor.

### Delaying Vasopressors While Chasing Fluids

Prolonged fluid loading before starting vasopressors in septic shock can cause harm and does not reliably improve outcomes. Counter by starting vasopressors when fluids are not restoring perfusion, and by using peripheral vasopressors early when central access is not yet available, per institutional protocols.

### Overlooking Source Control

Focusing on resuscitation while neglecting drainage, debridement, or removal of an infected source. Counter by identifying and addressing the source as soon as feasible, in parallel with hemodynamic support.

### Failing to Escalate

Continuing to manage deteriorating shock in a lower-acuity setting because "the patient is holding." Counter by escalating to ICU or requesting intensivist consultation at the first sign of instability or non-response.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed for compensated shock using perfusion signs, lactate, mental status, urine output, and skin perfusion, rather than relying on blood pressure alone?
- Have I classified the shock physiology (hypovolemic, distributive, cardiogenic, obstructive) and considered mixed or evolving causes, using bedside ultrasound where available?
- Before each fluid bolus, have I assessed fluid responsiveness, and have I reassessed after each bolus for improvement or overload?
- Have I chosen vasopressors and inotropes to match the specific physiologic defect rather than by habit?
- Am I tracking perfusion endpoints (lactate clearance, urine output, mental status, skin perfusion) as the goal, with a MAP target appropriate to the patient?
- Have I identified and begun treating the underlying cause concurrently with resuscitation, including source control for sepsis?
- Have I escalated to ICU or sought intensivist consultation at the first sign of instability or non-response, and communicated clearly with a structured format?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
