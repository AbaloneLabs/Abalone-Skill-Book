# Sepsis and Organ Dysfunction Management

Sepsis is a dysregulated host response to infection that produces life-threatening organ dysfunction, and septic shock is its most lethal tier, with mortality that remains high despite decades of protocolized care. The danger in sepsis management is not ignorance of the bundle — it is the failure to act on it with discipline and speed, and the failure to recognize that sepsis is not a single event but a cascading, multi-organ process that evolves hour by hour. A patient who looks "stable" can be in the latent phase of profound collapse; a patient who "responded" to the first liter of fluid can deteriorate as a second or third organ fails. The second danger is treating sepsis as an antibiotics-and-fluids problem while neglecting source control, perfusion endpoints, and the organ failures that actually kill the patient. This skill covers the disciplined application of the sepsis bundle, the support of failing organs, and the coordination of multi-organ care — always with the understanding that sepsis is time-critical and demands early escalation when response is inadequate.

## Core Rules

### Act on the Hour-1 Bundle as a Bundle, Not a Menu

The Surviving Sepsis Hour-1 bundle is a set of actions to be done together and rapidly, not a checklist to complete at leisure. Measure lactate, obtain blood cultures before antibiotics if it does not delay them, administer broad-spectrum antibiotics within one hour, begin balanced crystalloid resuscitation (typically 30 mL/kg for septic shock or sepsis with elevated lactate), and start vasopressors if hypotension persists during or after fluids to maintain a mean arterial pressure of 65 mmHg or higher. The bundle's power comes from simultaneous execution — antibiotics without hemodynamic support will not save a patient in shock, and fluids without antibiotics will not control the source. Designate a team leader, assign tasks explicitly, and run the resuscitation as a coordinated event. When recognition is delayed or the bundle is incomplete, mortality rises; treat every minute of delay as a measurable harm.

### Choose Antibiotics for Coverage, Source, and Resistance Risk

Empiric antibiotic choice must cover the likely source and the likely organisms, accounting for local resistance patterns, recent antibiotic exposure, healthcare exposure, immunocompromise, and allergy history. Broad coverage initially (for example, an extended-spectrum beta-lactam plus a second agent with gram-negative and anaerobic or resistant-organism coverage depending on source and risk) is appropriate, with rapid de-escalation once cultures and sensitivities return. Do not narrow empirically before source and organism are identified. Source matters: biliary and abdominal sources need anaerobic coverage; line and skin sources need gram-positive coverage including MRSA; neutropenic fever needs antipseudomonal coverage. Reassess antibiotic choice daily, stop redundant agents, and limit duration to what is necessary — but never under-treat suspected septic shock for fear of overuse. When the source or organism is unclear and the patient is deteriorating, expand coverage and seek infectious disease consultation.

### Pursue Source Control as an Equal Priority to Antibiotics

Antibiotics alone cannot sterilize an abscess, an infected device, necrotic tissue, or an obstructed infected biliary or urinary tree. Source control — drainage, debridement, device removal, or relief of obstruction — is a core component of sepsis management and should be achieved as soon as the patient is stable enough, ideally within 6-12 hours. Identify the source with imaging early (bedside ultrasound, CT) and involve surgery, interventional radiology, or the relevant specialist promptly. A patient with persistent septic shock and an undrained source will not improve no matter how many pressors are titrated. Document the source control plan and timing explicitly, and treat delays in source control as a critical failure requiring escalation.

### Resuscitate to Perfusion, Then Reassess Relentlessly

Fluid resuscitation in sepsis must be dynamic, not fixed. Give fluids as a test of responsiveness, reassess after each bolus, and stop when the patient is no longer fluid-responsive or shows signs of overload. Use dynamic measures (passive leg raise, stroke volume variation, point-of-care cardiac output) where possible, and track lactate clearance (target a fall of at least 10% per hour), capillary refill, mental status, and urine output. Balanced crystalloids are preferred over normal saline to avoid hyperchloremic acidosis and worsening renal function. Persistent or rising lactate despite adequate resuscitation is an ominous sign demanding re-evaluation for ongoing ischemia, inadequate source control, or mitochondrial dysfunction — not simply more fluids. Escalate to vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line) when fluids are insufficient, and consider inotropes if cardiac dysfunction contributes to hypoperfusion.

### Support Each Failing Organ Explicitly and Concurrently

Sepsis causes multi-organ dysfunction, and each failing organ needs explicit, concurrent support. Acute kidney injury: monitor urine output and creatinine, adjust nephrotoxic and renally cleared medications, correct electrolytes, and initiate renal replacement therapy for refractory hyperkalemia, acidosis, volume overload, or uremia — do not wait for "hard" indications while the patient deteriorates. Respiratory failure (ARDS): apply lung-protective ventilation, titrate PEEP, and consider prone positioning for moderate-severe ARDS. Hepatic dysfunction: monitor coagulation and glucose, correct hypoglycemia, and watch for encephalopathy. Coagulation (DIC): distinguish septic coagulopathy from bleeding; transfuse for active bleeding or before procedures, avoid prophylactic factor correction of asymptomatic abnormalities. Cardiovascular: treat shock as its own entity. Coordinate these supports so that treating one organ does not harm another — for example, fluids for shock can worsen ARDS and AKI, and PEEP for ARDS can reduce venous return and worsen shock.

### Monitor Response with Multiple Endpoints and Reconsider the Diagnosis

Track perfusion endpoints (lactate, capillary refill, mental status, urine output, MAP), organ-specific trends (creatinine, bilirubin, platelets, PaO2/FiO2), and the overall trajectory. If the patient is not improving as expected, explicitly reconsider: is the source identified and controlled, is antibiotic coverage adequate, is there a complication (line infection, Clostridioides difficile, embolic event), is there a non-infectious mimic (pancreatitis, adrenal crisis, toxicity, cardiogenic shock), or is the diagnosis simply wrong? The most dangerous phase of sepsis care is the comfortable middle, when the bundle has been given and the team stops questioning. Build in forced re-evaluation at defined intervals, and escalate when the trajectory is poor.

### Escalate and Coordinate Care Across the System

Sepsis with multi-organ dysfunction requires ICU-level care and coordinated input from multiple specialties. Escalate to ICU at the first sign of shock, rising lactate, respiratory failure, or multi-organ involvement. Communicate with a structured format across teams (medicine, surgery, nephrology, infectious disease, pharmacy) and designate a single attending responsible for the overall plan to avoid fragmented care. When the patient exceeds local capability (refractory shock, need for advanced respiratory support, RRT unavailable), arrange transfer to a higher level of care early — waiting until the patient is unsalvageable is a recurring failure. Document goals of care discussions and ensure the plan reflects the patient's values and prognosis.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Antibiotics or Bundle Elements

Waiting for cultures, imaging, or ICU transfer before giving antibiotics and fluids. Counter by initiating the Hour-1 bundle immediately on recognition, and drawing cultures only if it does not delay antibiotics.

### Fixed Fluid Resuscitation Without Reassessment

Giving 30 mL/kg as a mandatory bolus and then more without assessing responsiveness. Counter by treating fluids as a dynamic test, reassessing after each bolus, and stopping when no longer responsive or when overload appears.

### Neglecting Source Control

Focusing on antibiotics and pressors while leaving an abscess, infected line, or obstruction in place. Counter by identifying the source early and pursuing drainage, debridement, or device removal as soon as feasible.

### Under-Treating for Fear of Resistance

Choosing narrow empiric coverage or delaying broad coverage in septic shock to avoid overuse. Counter by giving broad empiric coverage first, then de-escalating based on data.

### Treating Sepsis as Single-Organ Disease

Addressing infection and blood pressure while ignoring concurrent AKI, ARDS, DIC, or hepatic failure. Counter by explicitly assessing and supporting each organ system.

### Failing to Reconsider the Diagnosis When Response Is Poor

Persisting with the sepsis plan when the patient is not improving, without questioning the source, coverage, complications, or alternative diagnoses. Counter by building in forced re-evaluation and seeking specialist input when the trajectory is poor.

### Delaying ICU Transfer or RRT

Waiting for "hard" indications before escalating to ICU or initiating renal replacement therapy. Counter by escalating early based on trajectory and multi-organ involvement.

## Self-Check

- Have I initiated the Surviving Sepsis Hour-1 bundle as a coordinated set (lactate, cultures, antibiotics, fluids, vasopressors) within the first hour of recognition?
- Is my empiric antibiotic coverage appropriate for the suspected source, local resistance patterns, and patient risk factors, with a plan to de-escalate?
- Have I identified the source and pursued source control (drainage, debridement, device removal) with the relevant specialist as an equal priority to antibiotics?
- Am I resuscitating dynamically, reassessing fluid responsiveness and perfusion endpoints after each intervention, and escalating to vasopressors when fluids are insufficient?
- Have I explicitly assessed and supported each failing organ (kidney, lung, liver, coagulation, cardiovascular) while coordinating care so one support does not harm another?
- Am I tracking multiple endpoints and reconsidering the diagnosis, source, coverage, and complications when the patient is not improving as expected?
- Have I escalated to ICU and coordinated multi-specialty care, and arranged transfer to a higher level when local capability is exceeded?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
