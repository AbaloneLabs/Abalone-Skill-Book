# Mechanical Ventilation and Airway Management

Mechanical ventilation is simultaneously one of the most powerful and most injurious interventions in medicine. A ventilator can save a life by oxygenating and ventilating a failing patient, and it can injure that same patient through volutrauma, barotrauma, atelectrauma, and oxygen toxicity if set carelessly. The danger lies in two opposite failures: treating the ventilator as a black box that "breathes for the patient," and micromanaging numbers without understanding the underlying pathophysiology. A ventilator is an extension of lung mechanics — it reflects what the lungs and airways are doing, and every alarm is a question the patient's physiology is asking. This skill covers the disciplined setup, monitoring, and troubleshooting of mechanical ventilation and the airway, with the constant recognition that airway loss and ventilator mismanagement are immediately life-threatening and demand early intensivist, anesthesia, or ENT involvement when difficulty arises.

## Core Rules

### Apply Lung-Protective Ventilation as the Default, Not the Exception

For nearly every intubated patient, and unconditionally for ARDS, use lung-protective ventilation: tidal volume of 4-6 mL/kg predicted body weight (never actual body weight), and plateau pressure (Pplat) below 30 cm H2O. Predicted body weight is calculated from sex and height, not from measured weight — using actual weight in an obese patient produces dangerously large tidal volumes. Monitor plateau pressure with an inspiratory hold maneuver; if Pplat exceeds 30 cm H2O, reduce tidal volume further. The goal is to minimize cyclic overstretch of recruitable alveoli and to prevent inflammatory injury. Permissive hypercapnia is acceptable and often necessary to achieve these targets; do not normalize PaCO2 at the cost of injurious tidal volumes. This is one of the few interventions in critical care with robust mortality benefit, and deviating from it must be a deliberate, documented decision, not an oversight.

### Match Ventilator Strategy to the Disease: Restrictive vs Obstructive

Different lung pathologies demand fundamentally different strategies. In restrictive disease (ARDS, pulmonary edema, pneumonia, fibrosis), the problem is low compliance and small functional lung size — use low tidal volume, higher PEEP to keep alveoli open, and accept permissive hypercapnia. In obstructive disease (COPD, asthma), the problem is high resistance and air trapping — the danger is not overdistension of healthy alveoli but dynamic hyperinflation and auto-PEEP. For obstructive disease, use a lower respiratory rate, shorter inspiratory time, longer expiratory time, and lower tidal volume to allow full exhalation. Monitor auto-PEEP by checking expiratory flow waveform (does flow return to zero before the next breath?) or by an expiratory hold. Severe auto-PEEP can cause hypotension and even cardiac arrest through reduced venous return — the response is to disconnect the circuit, allow exhalation, then reduce rate or adjust settings, not to give more fluids or vasopressors first.

### Set and Adjust PEEP Deliberately

PEEP is not a setting to leave at a default. In ARDS, PEEP recruits collapsed alveoli and improves oxygenation, but excessive PEEP overdistends healthy regions, worsens right heart strain, and reduces venous return. Titrate PEEP using an FiO2/PEEP ladder or, in severe cases, esophageal pressure or recruitment maneuvers, and reassess oxygenation, hemodynamics, and driving pressure (Pplat minus PEEP). In obstructive disease, set extrinsic PEEP to roughly 80% of measured auto-PEEP to reduce the work of triggering without worsening hyperinflation. A sudden change in oxygenation or hemodynamics after a PEEP change should prompt immediate reassessment, not further blind adjustment.

### Treat Every Alarm as a Physiologic Question

Alarms are not nuisance noise to be silenced; they are the ventilator telling you that something has changed. Approach alarms systematically. A high-pressure alarm with low returned tidal volume suggests a circuit or airway obstruction (kinked tube, mucous plug, biting, endobronchial intubation) or increased resistance (bronchospasm) or decreased compliance (worsening edema, pneumothorax, mainstem intubation). A low-pressure or apnea alarm suggests a leak or disconnect, cuff leak, or dislodged tube. The first response to a high-pressure alarm is to assess the patient and the circuit: listen to breath sounds, check the tube and circuit, suction if needed, and consider an inspiratory hold to measure plateau pressure (high plateau = compliance problem; high peak with normal plateau = resistance problem). Do not simply silence the alarm and walk away — a missed tension pneumothorax or tube migration presents as a high-pressure alarm.

### Manage the Difficult Airway with a Plan, Not Improvisation

Airway management fails when there is no plan for failure. Before any intubation, assess for predicted difficulty (limited mouth opening, short thyromental distance, restricted neck extension, obesity, beard, history of difficult airway, hypoxemia, hemodynamic instability) and assemble a plan A, plan B, and plan C. Have rescue devices (supraglottic airway, video laryngoscope, bougie) immediately available and know how to use them. For rapid sequence intubation, have medications drawn up, suction running, and a second provider ready for cricoid pressure and external laryngeal manipulation. Maintain oxygenation throughout with preoxygenation and apneic oxygenation. If the first attempt fails, do not make repeated blind attempts — this causes trauma, bleeding, and lost time. Move to the next planned approach and call for experienced help (anesthesia, ENT, or a difficult airway team) early. A surgical airway (cricothyrotomy) is the final rescue for the "can't intubate, can't oxygenate" situation and must be performed without delay when indicated — waiting too long is the fatal error.

### Recognize and Correct Ventilator Dyssynchrony

Patient-ventilator dyssynchrony causes discomfort, increased work of breathing, hypoventilation, and delayed liberation. Watch for it actively: double triggering (breath stacking), ineffective efforts (the patient tries to trigger but the ventilator does not deliver), auto-triggering, and flow asynchrony. Dyssynchrony is often misread as "the patient is fighting the vent" and treated with sedation or paralysis, when the real fix is adjusting trigger sensitivity, flow, inspiratory time, or mode. Assess the patient and the flow and pressure waveforms before adding paralytics. Persistent dyssynchrony in a patient who should be weaning should prompt evaluation for underlying causes (pain, anxiety, metabolic acidosis driving tachypnea, inadequate support) rather than deeper sedation.

### Escalate Early for Deterioration and Difficulty

Ventilator and airway deterioration can be rapid and fatal. Escalate to intensivist, anesthesia, or ENT at the first sign of refractory hypoxemia, rising plateau pressures, hemodynamic instability on the ventilator, difficult ventilation, or a difficult airway. Do not wait until the situation is unsalvageable. Communicate with a structured format: the patient, the ventilator settings and trend, the specific problem, and what you need. Document the reasoning for each setting change and the response. When a patient is failing conventional ventilation in ARDS, consider lung rescue therapies (prone positioning, neuromuscular blockade, inhaled pulmonary vasodilators, ECMO referral) — these require specialist involvement and should not be delayed.

## Common Traps

### Using Actual Body Weight for Tidal Volume

Calculating tidal volume from actual weight in obese or edematous patients produces injurious tidal volumes. Counter by always using predicted body weight from sex and height.

### Silencing Alarms Without Assessment

Treating alarms as nuisance and silencing them without evaluating the patient or circuit. Counter by treating every alarm as a physiologic question and assessing the patient first.

### Failing to Recognize Auto-PEEP in Obstructive Disease

Attributing hypotension on the ventilator to hypovolemia when the cause is dynamic hyperinflation from air trapping. Counter by checking expiratory flow and expiratory hold, and by disconnecting the circuit to allow exhalation before giving fluids or pressors.

### Repeated Laryngoscopy After Failed Intubation

Making multiple blind or traumatic attempts that cause bleeding and edema and make subsequent attempts harder. Counter by having a difficult airway plan with alternatives and by calling for experienced help early.

### Normalizing PaCO2 at the Cost of Lung Injury

Increasing tidal volume or rate to achieve a normal CO2 in a patient who needs permissive hypercapnia. Counter by accepting respiratory acidosis (within tolerance) to maintain lung-protective settings.

### Treating Dyssynchrony Only with Sedation or Paralysis

Misreading ventilator dyssynchrony as agitation and deepening sedation rather than adjusting the ventilator. Counter by reviewing trigger, flow, and timing settings and waveform patterns first.

### Delaying Escalation for Refractory Respiratory Failure

Continuing to escalate FiO2 and PEEP without involving specialists when conventional ventilation is failing. Counter by escalating to intensivist and considering rescue therapies and ECMO referral early.

## Self-Check

- Am I using lung-protective ventilation (4-6 mL/kg predicted body weight, Pplat below 30 cm H2O) as the default, with permissive hypercapnia accepted?
- Have I matched the ventilator strategy to the disease (restrictive vs obstructive), and for obstructive disease, am I monitoring for and managing auto-PEEP?
- Have I set and titrated PEEP deliberately, and do I reassess oxygenation and hemodynamics after each change?
- When an alarm sounds, do I assess the patient and circuit systematically and distinguish resistance from compliance problems, rather than silencing the alarm?
- Before intubation, do I have a plan A, B, and C for a difficult airway, with rescue devices and experienced help available?
- Am I actively evaluating for ventilator dyssynchrony and adjusting the ventilator rather than defaulting to deeper sedation?
- Have I escalated to intensivist, anesthesia, or ENT at the first sign of refractory hypoxemia, rising pressures, hemodynamic instability, or airway difficulty, and considered lung rescue therapies?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
