# Functional Assessment and Assistive Technology

Functional assessment and assistive-technology prescription sit at the intersection of medicine, engineering, environment, and the patient's life goals, and the central judgment problem is that a device or modification succeeds or fails not on its technical specification but on its fit to the person, the environment, and the activities that matter to them. A wheelchair that does not fit causes pressure injury and postural deformity; a prosthesis that is abandoned wastes resources and signals a mismatched goal; an orthotic that the patient will not wear provides no benefit; a home modification that ignores the caregiver fails the family. The recurring failure modes are prescribing equipment from a catalog rather than from an assessment, optimizing one impairment while ignoring comorbidity and cognition, under-documenting disability for certification, overriding patient autonomy in the name of safety without negotiation, and failing to involve specialist rehabilitation input for complex needs. This skill covers comprehensive functional assessment, assistive-technology prescription, environmental modification, disability documentation, the autonomy-safety balance, and escalation to specialist care.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Every Prescription to a Comprehensive Functional Assessment

Equipment and modifications must flow from a structured assessment, not from a single impairment or a generic request. Assess the whole person: the medical conditions and their trajectory (stable, improving, progressive), the impairments (motor, sensory, cognitive, perceptual, communicative, continence), the functional abilities (mobility, transfers, self-care, domestic tasks, community participation), the cognition and learning capacity that determine whether a device can be used safely, the prognosis, and the patient's own goals and priorities. Assess the environment in which the equipment will be used: the home (stairs, doorways, bathroom, surfaces, thresholds), the workplace or school, the transport situation, and the outdoor environment. Assess the support available — family, caregivers, formal services — because a device that requires assistance cannot succeed without that assistance. Document the assessment in enough detail that the prescription and its rationale are transparent and reviewable, because complex prescriptions are often challenged or need revision.

### Prescribe Wheelchairs, Seating, and Postural Support From Measurement and Need

Wheelchair prescription is a clinical intervention with measurable consequences for skin, posture, function, and independence, and a poorly matched chair causes harm. Determine whether a wheelchair is needed at all, whether it is for indoor, outdoor, or both use, whether it will be self-propelled or attendant-propelled, and whether powered mobility is required (for patients who cannot self-propel or who fatigue rapidly). Match the chair to the patient's dimensions (seat width, depth, height, back height) measured on the patient, not estimated, and select the seating system to manage posture and pressure — a patient with impaired sensation or sitting balance needs pressure-relieving cushioning and postural support, and a patient with progressive deformity needs modular seating. Assess for and prevent the complications of seating: pressure injury (assess risk with a validated tool and prescribe pressure relief accordingly), postural collapse, contracture, and aspiration in dependent postures. Arrange a trial where possible and a review after issue, because prescriptions often need adjustment, and involve a specialist seating service for complex needs.

### Match Prosthetics and Orthotics to Functional Potential and Adherence

Prosthetic and orthotic prescription must integrate the biomechanics of the device with the patient's functional potential, their goals, and the likelihood of adherence. For prosthetics, the level of amputation, the condition of the residual limb, the patient's balance, strength, cardiovascular fitness, cognition, and motivation determine whether a prosthesis is appropriate and which type — and not every patient is a candidate, because a poorly selected prosthesis causes skin breakdown, falls, and abandonment. Set realistic goals: a cosmetic prosthesis, a body-powered or myoelectric upper limb prosthesis, or a functional or mobility lower limb prosthesis each serve different aims and demand different capacities. For orthotics, define the specific purpose — control of a drop foot, stabilization of an unstable ankle, offloading of a diabetic ulcer, correction or support of a spinal deformity — and select the device, material, and fit to that purpose, recognizing that comfort, cosmesis, and ease of use drive adherence. Arrange training with the device, because a prosthesis or orthotic is useless without structured rehabilitation to teach its use, and review adherence and skin integrity at follow-up.

### Reason About Home and Workplace Modification Against Real Tasks and Environment

Environmental modification extends function beyond the person, and it must be reasoned against the specific tasks and the specific environment, not applied generically. Perform a home assessment, ideally a visit, to identify the barriers (steps, stairs, narrow doors, inaccessible bathroom, poor lighting, floor surfaces) and the enablers, and match modifications to the patient's current and projected function. Prioritize the high-impact changes: access (ramps, rails, thresholds), bathroom safety (grab rails, shower seating, raised toilet), and transfers and mobility (furniture height, clear pathways, lifts where indicated), because these prevent falls and enable basic function. For workplace and school modification, assess the demands of the role and the environment, apply the principles of reasonable accommodation, and involve occupational health and the employer or school. Recognize that modification is iterative: needs change as function changes, and a modification made for a stable disability may need revision as a disease progresses. Document the rationale for modifications, particularly when funding is sought, because approvals depend on a clear link between the modification and the functional need.

### Document Disability With Precision for Certification and Funding

Disability certification and documentation carry legal, financial, and access consequences, and imprecise or incomplete documentation fails the patient and exposes the clinician. Describe the impairment in medical terms and then translate it into functional limitations: what the patient can and cannot do, in what setting, with what assistance or equipment, and for what duration. Use standardized frameworks where applicable (for example, the WHO International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health) and validated tools where available, because structured documentation is more defensible and more useful to decision-makers. Distinguish impairment (the medical abnormality), activity limitation (what the person cannot do), and participation restriction (the social and occupational consequence), because certification schemes address these at different levels. State the expected duration (temporary, fluctuating, permanent) and the trajectory, and review and update the documentation as the condition changes. Avoid both over-statement, which undermines credibility and may expose the patient to risk, and under-statement, which denies needed support.

### Balance Patient Autonomy With Safety Through Negotiation, Not Override

A central ethical task is balancing the patient's right to make decisions about their function and risk against the clinician's duty to protect from harm. Competent adults are entitled to make decisions that the clinician considers unwise, including declining equipment or taking risks, and the clinician's role is to inform, to negotiate, and to document, not to override. Assess decision-making capacity specifically for the decision at hand, recognizing that capacity is decision-specific and may fluctuate, and involve advocates, proxies, and legal frameworks where capacity is impaired. Present the risks and benefits of options honestly, explore the patient's reasons and values, offer alternatives that reduce risk while preserving autonomy (for example, a supervised risk, a less restrictive device, a staged plan), and document the discussion and the decision. Recognize that risk is not eliminated by equipment alone: a patient may fall with a walker, refuse a wheelchair, or use a device unsafely, and the response is ongoing review and negotiation, not coercion. Escalate to specialist, ethics, or legal input when the autonomy-safety conflict cannot be resolved in the clinical encounter.

### Recognize When Specialist Rehabilitation Input Is Required

Generalist assessment is appropriate for straightforward needs, but complex situations demand specialist rehabilitation medicine and allied health input, and the failure is not recognizing the threshold. Refer for specialist input when the disability is complex or progressive (spinal cord injury, major amputation, progressive neuromuscular disease, severe acquired brain injury), when the assistive-technology need is complex (custom seating, powered mobility, myoelectric or specialized prosthetics, complex orthotics), when the environmental modification is major or contested, when capacity or autonomy-safety conflicts are unresolved, or when the patient is not achieving expected functional outcomes. Refer early rather than late, because delay compounds deconditioning, contracture, and lost potential, and because specialist services have waiting lists. Coordinate care across the team — rehabilitation medicine physician, occupational therapist, physiotherapist, orthotist or prosthetist, speech and language therapist, social worker, and the primary clinician — and designate a coordinator to prevent fragmented care.

## Common Traps

### Prescribing From a Catalog Instead of From an Assessment

Ordering a standard wheelchair, walker, or device based on a single impairment without measuring the patient or assessing the environment. Counter by anchoring every prescription to a comprehensive functional and environmental assessment.

### Ignoring Cognition and Comorbidity in Device Selection

Prescribing a technically appropriate device that the patient cannot use because of cognitive, perceptual, or physical comorbidity. Counter by integrating cognition, learning capacity, and comorbidity into the prescription decision.

### Under-documenting Disability for Certification

Writing vague impairment statements that fail to convey functional limitation and deny the patient support. Counter by documenting impairment, activity limitation, participation restriction, duration, and trajectory with precision.

### Overriding Patient Autonomy in the Name of Safety

Imposing equipment or restrictions against a competent patient's wishes without negotiation. Counter by assessing capacity, informing, negotiating alternatives, and documenting the decision.

### Abandoning the Device at Issue

Failing to review fit, adherence, and skin integrity after prescribing, leading to harm and abandonment. Counter by arranging trials, training, and structured follow-up with revision as needed.

### Failing to Modify the Environment to Match Function

Focusing on the person while ignoring the home or workplace barriers that defeat the equipment. Counter by performing environmental assessment and prioritizing high-impact modifications.

### Missing the Threshold for Specialist Referral

Managing complex disability or assistive-technology needs in a generalist setting without specialist input. Counter by referring early for complex, progressive, or non-achieving cases and coordinating care across the team.

## Self-Check

- Have I based every equipment or modification prescription on a comprehensive assessment of the person, the function, the cognition, the environment, and the goals?
- For wheelchair and seating prescription, have I measured the patient, addressed pressure and posture, and arranged a trial and follow-up?
- For prosthetic or orthotic prescription, have I matched the device to functional potential, adherence, and goals, and arranged training?
- Have I assessed the home and workplace against real tasks and prioritized high-impact modifications with documented rationale?
- For disability certification, have I documented impairment, activity limitation, participation restriction, duration, and trajectory with precision?
- Where autonomy and safety conflict, have I assessed capacity, informed and negotiated alternatives, and documented the decision rather than overriding?
- Have I recognized and referred complex, progressive, or non-achieving cases to specialist rehabilitation early and coordinated care across the team?
