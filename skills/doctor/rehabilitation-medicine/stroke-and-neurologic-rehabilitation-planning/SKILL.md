# Stroke and Neurologic Rehabilitation Planning

Stroke rehabilitation is a race against a time-limited window of neuroplasticity, and the central judgment problem is matching the intensity, timing, and content of rehabilitation to each patient's recoverable potential while honestly confronting the limits of that potential. The recurring failure modes are under-dosing therapy in the early window when the brain is most plastic, treating impairment rather than function, setting vague goals that cannot be measured or revised, allowing complications (spasticity, contracture, depression, shoulder pain, malnutrition) to accumulate unchecked, and discharging patients to environments or levels of support that cannot sustain their gains. Rehabilitation is not a passive convalescence; it is an active, structured, goal-directed process that requires disciplined assessment, deliberate dosing, active complication management, and honest prognosis. This skill covers post-stroke rehabilitation assessment and goal-setting, neuroplasticity and timing, the management of common neurologic impairments, functional independence reasoning, escalation of intensity, and discharge and community planning.

## Core Rules

### Assess Comprehensively and Set SMART Goals Before Prescribing Therapy

Rehabilitation begins with a structured multidisciplinary assessment that defines the baseline and the targets, not with a generic "physiotherapy." Assess motor function (strength, tone, coordination, balance), sensation, vision and neglect, language and cognition, swallowing and nutrition, continence, skin, mood, pre-morbid function, social context, and the patient's own goals. Translate this assessment into specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound (SMART) goals — for example, "independently transfer from bed to wheelchair within two weeks" rather than "improve mobility" — because measurable goals drive therapy dosing, allow progress to be tracked, and make discharge planning concrete. Reassess goals at defined intervals and revise them based on observed recovery, because a goal set at week one may be obsolete by week three. Anchor goals to activities and participation (what the patient can do in life), not only to impairments (what is wrong with the limb), because functional goals predict real-world outcome better than impairment scores.

### Exploit the Time-Limited Window of Neuroplasticity With Adequate Dose

The greatest gains after stroke occur in the first weeks to few months, driven by spontaneous recovery and experience-dependent neuroplasticity, and the dose and timing of therapy in this window matter. High-intensity, task-specific, repetitive practice — far more than passive movement or low-repetition exercise — drives cortical reorganization and functional gain, and the therapy should target the patient's goals (gait, reach and grasp, speech, swallowing) with enough repetition and challenge to induce adaptation. Recognize that under-dosing is the default failure: patients in inpatient rehab commonly receive less active practice than the evidence supports, diluted by rest, transport, and low-challenge exercises. Balance intensity against fatigue, medical stability, and the risk of overexertion in the acute phase, but do not use "rest" as a substitute for structured therapy. Early mobilization is beneficial, but very intense mobilization within the first 24 hours can be harmful in some patients; calibrate to the clinical state and the guideline.

### Manage Spasticity, Hemiparesis, and Motor Impairment Actively and in Parallel

Motor impairment after stroke is not a single problem; it is a combination of weakness, loss of dexterity, altered tone, and learned non-use, and each component needs active management. Prevent the complications that freeze recovery: range-of-motion and positioning to prevent contracture, shoulder support and careful handling to prevent subluxation and hemiplegic shoulder pain, splinting where indicated, and early, repeated task practice to counter learned non-use. Manage spasticity in a stepwise fashion — remove triggers (pain, infection, constipation, pressure), use stretching and positioning, add oral agents where focal spasticity is disabling, use focal chemodenervation (botulinum toxin) for focal problematic spasticity, and reserve intrathecal baclofen or orthopedic procedures for severe generalized spasticity. Integrate tone management with functional therapy, because reducing tone without strengthening and task practice does not improve function. Recognize that motor recovery often plateaus earlier than patients and families expect, and that adaptation and compensation become legitimate goals once recovery potential is largely realized.

### Address Aphasia, Neglect, Cognition, and Mood as Functional Determinants

Non-motor impairments are often the dominant limit on independence and on quality of life, and they are under-treated. Aphasia requires early and ongoing speech and language therapy, with communication strategies (augmentative communication, partner training) matched to the deficit type and severity; do not assume a mute patient cannot understand, and assess comprehension directly. Unilateral neglect, common after right hemisphere stroke, impairs safety, function, and rehabilitation participation, and requires specific intervention (visual scanning training, cueing, prism adaptation in selected cases) and environmental adaptation. Screen cognition formally, because executive dysfunction limits the patient's ability to plan, transfer learning, and live safely alone. Screen mood early and repeatedly with a validated tool, because post-stroke depression is common, impairs rehabilitation, and is treatable; treat depression actively because doing so improves participation and outcome. Treat these domains in parallel with motor recovery, not sequentially, because they interact and compound.

### Reason Explicitly About ADLs and Functional Independence

Functional independence is the practical endpoint of rehabilitation and the basis for discharge planning, and it must be assessed explicitly with standardized tools (such as the Barthel Index, Functional Independence Measure) and observed performance, not inferred from limb strength. Break function into its components — transfers, mobility, dressing, feeding, toileting, bathing, stairs, and cognition-dependent tasks such as medication management and finance — and assess each, because a patient may be independent in transfers but unsafe in the kitchen. Identify the gap between current function and the demands of the discharge environment, and direct therapy at closing that gap or at modifying the environment and the support. Recognize that independence is relative: a patient may be safe with supervision, with a device, or with a caregiver, and the level of support must be specified precisely in the discharge plan. Avoid the trap of declaring a patient "independent" based on inpatient performance that does not transfer to the home, where surfaces, stairs, lighting, and distance differ.

### Escalate or De-escalate Rehab Intensity Based on Measured Progress

Rehabilitation intensity is not a fixed prescription; it should be titrated to the patient's trajectory. Patients who are making steady functional gains and who can tolerate intensive therapy should continue in or be escalated to a higher-intensity setting (inpatient or specialized rehab) where the dose supports further recovery. Patients who have plateaued, who cannot participate because of medical instability or severe cognitive impairment, or whose goals are better met at home should be de-escalated to a less intensive setting with community services. Use objective measures of progress — change in functional scores, achievement of goals, walking speed and distance — to justify the intensity decision, and document the rationale. Recognize that escalation is sometimes needed for a specific complication (a contracture, a worsening spasticity, a new depression) even when overall recovery has plateaued. Avoid both premature de-escalation that forfeits recoverable function and prolonged intensive rehab that has ceased to yield measurable gain.

### Plan Discharge and Community Support to Sustain Gains

Discharge is not the end of rehabilitation; it is a transition that must be planned to sustain and build on gains. Assess the home environment (stairs, access, bathroom layout, obstacles), the available informal support, and the need for formal services (home therapy, personal care, meals, transport). Prescribe and arrange equipment (mobility aids, seating, rails, adaptive devices) before discharge, and arrange a home visit or assessment where the environment is complex. Provide structured secondary prevention — antithrombotic, antihypertensive, lipid, and diabetes management, smoking cessation, and lifestyle — because recurrent stroke is the major threat to sustained function. Educate the patient and caregiver on warning signs, medication, and what to do in a setback, and arrange clear follow-up with primary care and rehabilitation. Recognize that caregiver capacity and burnout are determinants of community outcome, and support the caregiver as part of the plan.

## Common Traps

### Setting Vague Goals That Cannot Be Measured or Revised

Writing "improve mobility" or "increase strength" as goals, which cannot be tracked or used to dose therapy. Counter by setting SMART functional goals and reassessing them at defined intervals.

### Under-dosing Therapy in the Early Plastic Window

Providing low-repetition, low-challenge therapy during the weeks of greatest recovery potential. Counter by prescribing high-intensity, task-specific, repetitive practice matched to the patient's goals and tolerance.

### Treating Impairment Without Addressing Function

Focusing on limb strength and tone while neglecting the activities that determine independence. Counter by anchoring goals to activities and participation and measuring functional outcomes.

### Allowing Complications to Accumulate Unchecked

Letting contracture, shoulder pain, spasticity, depression, or malnutrition develop while therapy proceeds. Counter by preventing and actively managing complications in parallel with rehabilitation.

### Neglecting Aphasia, Neglect, Cognition, and Mood

Treating stroke rehabilitation as primarily motor and under-treating the non-motor impairments that dominate function and quality of life. Counter by assessing and treating language, neglect, cognition, and mood in parallel.

### Declaring Independence Based on Inpatient Performance

Assuming function observed in a structured ward transfers to the home environment. Counter by assessing function against the specific demands of the discharge environment and specifying the level of support.

### Discharging Without a Sustainability Plan

Sending the patient home without environmental modification, equipment, caregiver support, and structured secondary prevention. Counter by planning discharge comprehensively, including home assessment, equipment, services, and follow-up.

## Self-Check

- Have I completed a structured multidisciplinary assessment and translated it into SMART functional goals with defined reassessment intervals?
- Have I prescribed therapy of adequate intensity, repetition, and task-specificity to exploit the early window of neuroplasticity?
- Am I managing spasticity, contracture, shoulder pain, and learned non-use actively and in parallel with functional therapy?
- Have I assessed and am I treating aphasia, neglect, cognition, and mood, recognizing these as major determinants of function?
- Have I measured functional independence with standardized tools and observed performance across the relevant ADLs?
- Have I titrated rehab intensity to measured progress, escalating those still gaining and de-escalating those who have plateaued, with documented rationale?
- Have I planned discharge to sustain gains, including home assessment, equipment, formal and informal support, and structured secondary prevention?
- This skill provides educational information only and does not constitute medical advice. Always consult qualified healthcare professionals for clinical decisions.
