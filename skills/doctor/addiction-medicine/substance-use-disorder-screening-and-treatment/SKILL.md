# Substance Use Disorder Screening and Treatment

Substance use disorder (SUD) is a chronic, relapsing medical illness, and it remains one of the areas where stigma most distorts clinical decision-making. Two opposite errors recur: dismissing SUD as a moral failing or a lifestyle choice not warranting medical treatment, and over-medicalizing in a way that strips patients of agency. The disciplined clinician treats SUD with the same evidence-based seriousness as diabetes or heart failure — screening universally, diagnosing by severity criteria, offering medication-assisted treatment (MAT) as first-line for opioid use disorder, combining pharmacotherapy with psychosocial care, and embedding harm reduction throughout. Punitive or coercive approaches worsen outcomes and drive patients away from care. This skill covers screening tools, MAT initiation and induction, harm reduction, the management of comorbidity, and the thresholds for specialist addiction referral. Throughout, recognize that SUD rarely travels alone — co-occurring psychiatric illness, chronic pain, infectious disease, and social instability are the rule, and effective care coordinates across all of them. When complexity or risk exceeds your scope, escalate to an addiction specialist rather than abandoning the patient.

## Core Rules

### Screen Universally and Diagnose by Severity, Not by Stereotype

SUD is under-diagnosed because clinicians screen selectively, relying on stereotypes of who "looks like" an addict. Screen all adults (and adolescents where appropriate) with validated tools: AUDIT or AUDIT-C for alcohol, DAST for drugs, and a single-item brief screen for unhealthy drug use. A positive screen prompts a structured assessment using DSM/ICD severity criteria (mild, moderate, severe), because severity drives treatment intensity. Take a non-judgmental, trauma-informed history that covers substances used, quantities, frequency, route, consequences, prior treatment, and readiness to change. Use the language of medicine ("person with substance use disorder," "positive urine test") rather than stigmatizing labels ("addict," "dirty"), because language measurably affects engagement and outcomes. Document severity and the specific substances involved, as this determines the pharmacotherapy and psychosocial plan.

### Offer Medication-Assisted Treatment as First-Line for Opioid Use Disorder

Opioid use disorder has a strong pharmacotherapy evidence base, and MAT (buprenorphine, methadone, or naltrexone) markedly reduces mortality, illicit opioid use, infectious risk, and criminal justice involvement compared to abstinence-only approaches. Withholding MAT is not a neutral act — untreated OUD carries high overdose death risk. Buprenorphine is the most accessible option in many settings; initiate only after the patient is in mild-to-moderate withdrawal (use a validated scale such as COWS) to avoid precipitated withdrawal, and confirm that dosing follows current guidance. Methadone requires a licensed opioid treatment program in most jurisdictions but is appropriate for many patients, including those with severe OUD or pregnancy. Extended-release naltrexone is an option for patients who have fully detoxified. Match the agent to the patient's goals, comorbidity, access, and prior response, and arrange the follow-up and psychosocial supports that make retention in care likely. When OUD is severe, treatment-resistant, or complicated by polysubstance use or pregnancy, escalate to an addiction specialist.

### Combine Pharmacotherapy With Psychosocial Care and Harm Reduction

Medication addresses the neurobiology; psychosocial interventions address the context. Offer evidence-based psychosocial care — motivational interviewing, cognitive behavioral therapy, contingency management (especially for stimulant use disorder), peer support, and structured residential or intensive outpatient programs — matched to patient preference and severity. Equally important is harm reduction, which keeps patients alive and engaged even when abstinence is not the immediate goal: naloxone provision and overdose education, syringe service referral, fentanyl and xylazine awareness, safer-use counseling, testing for HIV and hepatitis C with linkage to treatment, vaccination (hepatitis A and B), and contraception and prenatal care where relevant. Harm reduction is not enabling — it is evidence-based care that reduces death and disease. Integrate it into every SUD encounter regardless of the patient's stated goal.

### Assess and Manage Comorbidity as the Rule, Not the Exception

Most patients with SUD have co-occurring psychiatric disorders, chronic pain, and/or medical complications, and treating SUD in isolation usually fails. Screen for depression, anxiety, PTSD, bipolar disorder, and ADHD, and treat them concurrently rather than sequentially; untreated psychiatric illness drives relapse. Assess for chronic pain and develop a multimodal, non-opioid-centered plan (see the dedicated pain-in-addiction skill). Evaluate for infectious complications (endocarditis, abscess, hepatitis, HIV), liver disease, and the medical consequences of specific substances, and arrange the relevant workup and treatment. Recognize that social determinants — housing, food, employment, intimate partner violence, legal status — powerfully shape outcomes, and connect patients to social work and community resources. A treatment plan that addresses only the substance, without the comorbidity and context, is incomplete.

### Avoid Punitive Approaches and Build a Therapeutic Alliance

Coercion, confrontation, and contingent dismissal for relapse worsen outcomes and reflect stigma rather than evidence. Use motivational interviewing to elicit and strengthen intrinsic motivation, meet patients where they are, set collaborative goals, and treat relapse as an expected part of a chronic illness course — an occasion to adjust treatment, not to punish or discharge. Positive urine drug tests are clinical information, not evidence of moral failure; respond with curiosity about triggers and treatment gaps, not with sanctions. When a patient misses appointments, reach out assertively rather than dropping them from care. The therapeutic alliance is itself one of the strongest predictors of outcome; protect it.

### Define and Act on Clear Thresholds for Specialist Referral

Refer to an addiction medicine specialist or program for: severe or treatment-resistant SUD, complex polysubstance use, OUD requiring methadone or specialist induction, alcohol use disorder with prior complicated withdrawal or significant comorbidity, stimulant use disorder needing structured programming, co-occurring severe mental illness, pregnancy with SUD, adolescents, and patients with recurrent overdoses or unstable social circumstances. Refer urgently for overdose, infectious complications, withdrawal emergencies, and any presentation with suicidal ideation or acute psychiatric crisis. A delayed or absent referral in these situations risks death; escalate to emergency and addiction services rather than managing alone.

## Common Traps

### Selective Screening Based on Stereotype

Screening only patients who "look like" they use substances misses the majority of cases. Counter by screening all adults with validated tools and diagnosing by severity criteria.

### Withholding MAT for Opioid Use Disorder

Treating OUD with abstinence-only approaches when MAT is indicated markedly increases overdose death. Counter by offering buprenorphine, methadone, or naltrexone as first-line and arranging appropriate induction.

### Initiating Buprenorphine Too Early and Precipitating Withdrawal

Starting buprenorphine before the patient is in adequate withdrawal causes precipitated withdrawal and erodes trust. Counter by waiting for objective withdrawal signs on a validated scale before the first dose.

### Treating Relapse as a Reason to Discharge

Discharging patients for relapse reflects stigma and worsens outcomes. Counter by treating relapse as clinical information and adjusting the treatment plan.

### Neglecting Harm Reduction

Failing to provide naloxone, syringe service referral, and infectious screening misses preventable deaths and disease. Counter by embedding harm reduction in every SUD encounter.

### Ignoring Comorbidity and Social Context

Treating only the substance without the psychiatric, pain, infectious, and social dimensions leads to relapse. Counter by assessing and coordinating care across all comorbidities and connecting to social resources.

## Self-Check

- Have I screened universally with validated tools (AUDIT/AUDIT-C, DAST) and diagnosed SUD by severity criteria, using non-stigmatizing language?
- For opioid use disorder, have I offered MAT as first-line and arranged appropriate induction (waiting for objective withdrawal before buprenorphine, or referral for methadone)?
- Have I combined pharmacotherapy with evidence-based psychosocial care and embedded harm reduction (naloxone, syringe services, infectious screening and vaccination)?
- Have I assessed and arranged treatment for co-occurring psychiatric illness, chronic pain, and medical and infectious complications?
- Have I avoided punitive approaches, used motivational interviewing, and treated relapse as clinical information rather than a reason to discharge?
- Have I defined and acted on clear thresholds for specialist addiction referral for severe, complex, treatment-resistant, or high-risk presentations?
- Have I coordinated care across the medical, psychiatric, and social dimensions rather than treating SUD in isolation?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
