# Pain and Co-Occurring Disorder Management in Addiction

Pain in a patient with substance use disorder is one of the most judgment-intensive problems in medicine, and it is where two failure modes dominate: withholding adequate analgesia out of fear of "feeding the addiction," and prescribing opioids carelessly in ways that precipitate relapse or overdose. Both are rooted in stigma and both harm patients. The disciplined approach treats pain as real until proven otherwise, uses multimodal non-opioid strategies as the foundation, continues opioid use disorder pharmacotherapy throughout acute pain episodes, treats co-occurring psychiatric illness concurrently, and coordinates across pain, addiction, and psychiatric services rather than fragmenting care. Patients with SUD are entitled to the same standard of pain care as any other patient; undertreatment is neither safe nor ethical, and it independently drives relapse. This skill covers multimodal analgesia, the perioperative and acute-pain management of patients on MAT, the integration of co-occurring mental health treatment, the avoidance of stigma, and the thresholds for specialist escalation. When the complexity exceeds your scope, escalate to pain medicine, addiction, and psychiatry in a coordinated way.

## Core Rules

### Believe and Treat the Pain First, Without Letting Stigma Drive Undertreatment

Patients with substance use disorder are systematically undertreated for pain, and undertreatment itself worsens outcomes by driving uncontrolled suffering, destabilizing recovery, and pushing patients toward illicit use. Treat reports of pain as real and assess them with the same rigor as for any patient, while recognizing that tolerance may alter dose requirements. Do not withhold opioids when they are genuinely indicated (e.g., acute surgical pain, sickle crisis, trauma) solely because of a history of SUD; the ethical and clinical standard is adequate analgesia. At the same time, do not default to opioids when non-opioid options would suffice. The goal is neither opioid avoidance at all costs nor reflexive prescribing, but the right analgesia through a reasoned, multimodal plan. Examine your own assumptions explicitly — if you notice reluctance to treat a patient's pain because of their history, name it and correct it.

### Build a Multimodal, Non-Opioid-Centered Analgesic Plan as the Foundation

For acute and chronic pain in patients with SUD, construct a multimodal plan in which non-opioid strategies do the heavy lifting: regional anesthesia and nerve blocks where feasible, scheduled acetaminophen and NSAIDs (where not contraindicated), ketamine and lidocaine infusions for severe or neuropathic pain, gabapentinoids and other neuropathic agents, topical agents, heat, ice, positioning, and physical therapy. For chronic pain, prioritize interdisciplinary rehabilitation, cognitive behavioral therapy for pain, functional goal-setting, and movement-based therapies over long-term opioid escalation. When opioids are required, use them at the lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary duration, with a clear exit plan, and integrate them into — not as a replacement for — the multimodal foundation. Document the rationale, the plan, and the exit strategy.

### Continue Opioid Use Disorder Pharmacotherapy Through Acute Pain and Surgery

A common and dangerous error is stopping buprenorphine or methadone perioperatively or during acute illness in the mistaken belief that it improves opioid analgesia or prevents relapse. Discontinuing MAT increases relapse and overdose risk and does not reliably improve pain outcomes. Continue MAT through acute pain episodes and the perioperative period; for buprenorphine, common strategies include continuing the maintenance dose and adding full-agonist opioids for acute pain, or splitting the buprenorphine dose to allow analgesic effect, depending on the situation and local guidance. Plan perioperative pain in advance with the patient, the surgeon, anesthesia, and the addiction prescriber. For unexpected acute pain, continue MAT and layer additional multimodal and full-agonist analgesia as needed, anticipating that higher full-agonist doses may be required due to opioid tolerance and partial blockade. Re-engage the addiction prescriber early and arrange continuity of MAT throughout.

### Treat Co-Occurring Psychiatric Disorders Concurrently, Not Sequentially

Pain, SUD, and psychiatric illness reinforce one another in a vicious cycle, and treating them in isolation fails. Screen for and treat depression, anxiety, PTSD, and sleep disorders concurrently with the pain and addiction plan, because untreated psychiatric illness amplifies pain and drives relapse. Coordinate prescribing to avoid dangerous interactions — for example, sedating combinations of opioids, benzodiazepines, and gabapentinoids increase overdose risk and should be avoided or tightly managed. Use non-pharmacologic psychiatric treatments (psychotherapy, trauma-focused care) that also serve pain and recovery goals. Share the care plan across the addiction, pain, and mental health teams so that no single prescriber is acting in isolation, and so that the patient receives a coherent message rather than contradictory instructions.

### Avoid Stigma and Build a Transparent, Collaborative Care Agreement

Stigma is not only an ethical failure; it is a clinical one that worsens pain control, engagement, and outcomes. Use non-stigmatizing language, treat the patient as a partner, set collaborative and realistic functional goals, and be transparent about the reasoning behind prescribing decisions. For chronic pain requiring ongoing management, a clear care agreement — documenting the treatment plan, the roles of each clinician, the monitoring (including prescription drug monitoring program checks and urine drug testing used as a clinical tool, not a surveillance weapon), and the contingencies for aberrant use — reduces ambiguity and conflict. Apply monitoring consistently and without singling out patients by history; frame urine drug testing and PDMP checks as standard safety practice for everyone on controlled substances, not as evidence of distrust.

### Define and Act on Clear Thresholds for Specialist Coordination

Coordinated escalation is the norm for these complex patients. Involve pain medicine for pain that is severe, refractory, or requiring interventional approaches; addiction medicine for MAT management, relapse risk, and complex SUD; psychiatry for severe or treatment-resistant co-occurring illness, suicidality, or diagnostic complexity; and anesthesia and acute pain services for perioperative planning in patients on MAT. Do not allow these services to operate in silos — the value is in the coordination, and a fragmented plan with contradictory advice harms the patient. Escalate urgently for overdose, withdrawal emergencies, acute psychiatric crisis, or uncontrolled severe pain, and ensure a single accountable clinician is coordinating the overall plan.

## Common Traps

### Withholding Adequate Analgesia Because of SUD History

Stigma-driven undertreatment causes suffering and drives relapse and is neither ethical nor safe. Counter by treating pain as real, assessing it rigorously, and providing adequate analgesia through a multimodal plan.

### Stopping MAT Perioperatively or During Acute Illness

Discontinuing buprenorphine or methadone increases relapse and overdose risk without reliably improving analgesia. Counter by continuing MAT and layering additional multimodal and full-agonist analgesia, planning perioperative care in advance.

### Defaulting to Opioids Without a Multimodal Foundation

Reflexive opioid prescribing neglects the non-opioid strategies that should carry most of the analgesic load. Counter by building regional anesthesia, scheduled non-opioids, ketamine, neuropathic agents, and rehabilitation into the foundation.

### Combining Sedating Agents Without Regard to Overdose Risk

Opioids plus benzodiazepines plus gabapentinoids multiply overdose risk. Counter by avoiding or tightly managing sedating combinations and coordinating prescribing across teams.

### Treating Pain, Addiction, and Psychiatry in Isolation

Fragmented care with contradictory instructions worsens outcomes. Counter by coordinating across pain, addiction, and mental health services with a single accountable clinician.

### Using Urine Drug Testing and PDMP Checks Punitively

Surveillance framed as punishment erodes trust and engagement. Counter by applying monitoring consistently to all patients on controlled substances as standard safety practice, communicated transparently.

## Self-Check

- Have I treated the patient's pain as real and provided adequate analgesia without letting stigma drive undertreatment, while avoiding reflexive opioid prescribing?
- Have I built a multimodal, non-opioid-centered analgesic plan (regional anesthesia, scheduled non-opioids, ketamine, neuropathic agents, rehabilitation) as the foundation?
- Have I continued buprenorphine or methadone through acute pain or the perioperative period and planned full-agonist analgesia with the addiction prescriber?
- Have I screened for and treated co-occurring psychiatric disorders concurrently, while avoiding dangerous sedating drug combinations?
- Have I used non-stigmatizing language, set collaborative functional goals, and framed monitoring (PDMP, urine testing) as standard safety practice rather than surveillance?
- Have I coordinated care across pain medicine, addiction medicine, and psychiatry with a single accountable clinician and a clear care agreement?
- Have I escalated urgently for overdose, withdrawal emergencies, acute psychiatric crisis, or uncontrolled severe pain, and ensured continuity of MAT throughout?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
