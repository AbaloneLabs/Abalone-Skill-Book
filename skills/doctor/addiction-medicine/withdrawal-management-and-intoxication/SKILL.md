# Withdrawal Management and Intoxication

Withdrawal states are where addiction medicine becomes acute medicine, and they are where preventable deaths still occur. The recurring failures are not ignorance of syndromes but failure to anticipate severity, under-dosing benzodiazepines in alcohol withdrawal out of fear, missing Wernicke encephalopathy because the triad was expected to be complete, and treating withdrawal as a discrete episode rather than a gateway to ongoing care. Alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal can kill through seizure, delirium tremens, and cardiovascular collapse; opioid withdrawal is rarely lethal but drives relapse and overdose; stimulant intoxication causes hyperthermia, cardiac, and psychiatric emergencies. The disciplined approach assesses severity with validated scales, treats proactively rather than reactively, gives thiamine before glucose to prevent Wernicke, and escalates to higher acuity the moment severity outstrips the setting. This skill covers alcohol withdrawal severity and treatment, the must-not-miss Wernicke, benzodiazepine tapering, opioid and stimulant presentations, and the thresholds for ICU and specialist escalation. Every withdrawal encounter is also an opportunity to initiate long-term treatment; do not discharge a detoxified patient with no plan, because the post-withdrawal period carries the highest overdose-death risk.

## Core Rules

### Assess Alcohol Withdrawal Severity With a Validated Scale and Treat Proactively

Use a symptom-triggered scale (CIWA-Ar) to assess alcohol withdrawal severity and guide benzodiazepine dosing, reassessing at defined frequent intervals. Identify patients at risk of severe withdrawal or delirium tremens using history (prior DT or withdrawal seizures, heavy chronic use, prior complicated withdrawal, age, medical comorbidity) and consider a fixed-schedule or front-loaded benzodiazepine regimen rather than purely symptom-triggered dosing for high-risk patients. The common and dangerous error is under-treatment — withholding benzodiazepines out of fear of oversedation while the patient progresses toward seizure and DT. Treat proactively in high-risk patients, dose to symptoms, and do not let the scale override clinical judgment when signs of severe withdrawal (autonomic instability, tremor, confusion) are present. Severe withdrawal, DT, or withdrawal seizure warrants a higher level of care; escalate to a monitored or ICU setting early rather than waiting for deterioration.

### Prevent Wernicke Encephalopathy: Give Thiamine Before Glucose

Wernicke encephalopathy is a must-not-miss diagnosis, and the classic triad (ataxia, confusion, ophthalmoplegia) is absent in most cases — waiting for the full triad means missing it. Give parenteral thiamine to every patient at risk (heavy alcohol use, malnutrition, prolonged vomiting, liver disease, any suspected Wernicke), and give it before or alongside glucose, because glucose administration in a thiamine-deficient patient can precipitate or worsen Wernicke. Use adequate parenteral doses per local protocol, continue for several days, and maintain oral thiamine and multivitamin supplementation thereafter. Suspect Wernicke in any patient with alcohol use disorder and acute confusion, ataxia, nystagmus, or hypothermia and hypotension, and treat empirically — do not wait for confirmation. Untreated Wernicke progresses to irreversible Korsakoff syndrome, a devastating and preventable outcome.

### Manage Benzodiazepine Withdrawal With a Structured, Gradual Taper

Benzodiazepine withdrawal can be severe, with seizure risk, and must not be stopped abruptly after long-term use. Convert to a long-acting agent (e.g., diazepam) where appropriate and taper gradually, typically around 10% every one to two weeks with slower decrements at the end, individualized to the patient's tolerance and the original dose and duration. Use a validated withdrawal scale to monitor symptoms. Address the underlying anxiety or insomnia with non-benzodiazepine strategies, because simply tapering without treating the driving condition leads to relapse. In patients taking high doses or with polysubstance use, comorbidity, or a history of seizures, taper in a supervised setting and escalate to addiction or psychiatric input. Beware of co-ingestion with opioids or alcohol, which raises overdose risk, and provide naloxone and overdose education where relevant.

### Treat Opioid Withdrawal to Prevent Relapse and Overdose, Not Just to Relieve Discomfort

Opioid withdrawal is intensely distressing and is a major driver of premature discharge and relapse; the period immediately after detox carries the highest overdose-death risk because tolerance is lost. Manage withdrawal actively with buprenorphine (once the patient is in adequate withdrawal to avoid precipitated withdrawal), symptomatic agents (clonidine or lofexidine for autonomic symptoms, antiemetics, antidiarrheals, simple analgesia), and supportive care, and — critically — initiate or continue opioid use disorder pharmacotherapy as part of the same encounter. Detox without a plan for ongoing MAT is dangerous, not merely incomplete. Assess for and treat comorbidities, provide naloxone and overdose education emphasizing reduced tolerance, and arrange warm handover to ongoing care.

### Recognize and Manage Stimulant Intoxication as a Medical and Psychiatric Emergency

Stimulant (cocaine, amphetamine, methamphetamine, cathinones) intoxication can cause hyperthermia, hypertensive emergency, arrhythmia, myocardial infarction, stroke, seizure, severe agitation, and psychosis. Management is supportive and symptom-directed: aggressive cooling for hyperthermia, benzodiazepines as the cornerstone for agitation, sympathetic overdrive, and seizure (benzodiazepines first, not antipsychotics), blood pressure control, and cardiac monitoring. Avoid beta-blockers as monotherapy in cocaine intoxication due to unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction. Treat stimulant-induced psychosis with antipsychotics once medically stable, and assess suicide risk, which is elevated during intoxication and the subsequent crash. Severe toxicity, hyperthermia, cardiac instability, or status epilepticus warrants ICU escalation.

### Escalate to Higher Acuity When Severity Exceeds the Setting

Know the thresholds: alcohol withdrawal with seizure, DT, severe autonomic instability, or need for high benzodiazepine doses warrants monitored or ICU care; benzodiazepine withdrawal with seizure or high-dose polysubstance use warrants supervised admission; stimulant intoxication with hyperthermia, cardiac instability, or status epilepticus warrants ICU; any withdrawal with altered mental status demands investigation for Wernicke, infection, intracranial event, or metabolic derangement and escalation. Do not manage severe withdrawal in an unmonitored outpatient or general ward setting out of optimism. Involve addiction medicine for ongoing care planning and critical care or internal medicine for medical instability, and escalate early.

## Common Traps

### Under-Dosing Benzodiazepines in Alcohol Withdrawal

Fear of oversedation leads to inadequate treatment and progression to seizure or DT. Counter by treating proactively in high-risk patients, dosing to symptoms, and escalating when severity increases.

### Missing Wernicke Because the Full Triad Is Absent

Waiting for ataxia, confusion, and ophthalmoplegia together misses most cases. Counter by giving parenteral thiamine empirically to all at-risk patients, before glucose, and treating suspected Wernicke without waiting for confirmation.

### Giving Glucose Before Thiamine

Administering glucose to a thiamine-deficient patient precipitates or worsens Wernicke. Counter by giving thiamine before or alongside any glucose-containing fluid.

### Abrupt Cessation of Long-Term Benzodiazepines

Stopping abruptly after long-term use risks seizure and severe withdrawal. Counter by converting to a long-acting agent and tapering gradually under monitoring.

### Detoxifying Opioid Withdrawal Without Ongoing MAT

Detox alone leaves the patient at high overdose-death risk due to lost tolerance. Counter by initiating or continuing opioid use disorder pharmacotherapy and arranging warm handover.

### Using Beta-Blockers Alone for Cocaine Intoxication

Unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction can worsen coronary vasospasm and blood pressure. Counter by using benzodiazepines first for sympathetic overdrive and avoiding beta-blocker monotherapy.

### Managing Severe Withdrawal in an Unmonitored Setting

Optimism about a general ward or outpatient course in high-risk withdrawal leads to adverse events. Counter by escalating to monitored or ICU care when severity thresholds are met.

## Self-Check

- Have I assessed alcohol withdrawal severity with CIWA-Ar and used a proactive, adequately dosed benzodiazepine regimen in high-risk patients rather than under-treating?
- Have I given parenteral thiamine before or alongside glucose to every at-risk patient, and treated suspected Wernicke empirically without waiting for the full triad?
- For benzodiazepine withdrawal, have I converted to a long-acting agent and planned a gradual, monitored taper with treatment of the underlying condition?
- For opioid withdrawal, have I managed symptoms actively and initiated or continued MAT with naloxone provision and a warm handover, rather than detox alone?
- For stimulant intoxication, have I used benzodiazepines first for agitation, sympathetic overdrive, and seizure, cooled hyperthermia aggressively, monitored cardiac status, and avoided beta-blocker monotherapy?
- Have I escalated to monitored or ICU care when withdrawal severity, altered mental status, or medical instability exceeded my setting's capability?
- Have I planned ongoing addiction care and overdose-risk counseling for the post-withdrawal period, when overdose death risk is highest?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
