# Neurological Examination: Rapid and Emergency Assessment

The full, localization-focused neurological examination is a powerful diagnostic tool in the stable outpatient or ward patient, but a large fraction of neurologically relevant encounters demand something different: a rapid, focused, decision-driving assessment of a patient who is acutely altered, deteriorating, or potentially in a time-critical emergency. The patient with sudden weakness, the unresponsive patient brought to the emergency department, the post-ictal patient who is not waking, the trauma patient with a head injury, and the ward patient who has become confused and drowsy all require a neuro exam whose purpose is not exhaustive localization but rapid triage — is there a life-threatening intracranial process, is this a stroke eligible for time-critical treatment, is the airway and the brainstem at risk, and what must happen in the next few minutes? The recurring failure in these settings is to either skip the neuro exam entirely (because the patient is uncooperative or the situation feels too urgent) or to attempt a full textbook exam that consumes precious time without answering the decisive questions. Both errors harm patients: skipping the exam misses the focal sign that identifies a stroke or a herniation syndrome, while an unfocused exhaustive exam delays the imaging and treatment that determine outcome in conditions where minutes matter. This skill covers the rapid neurological assessment designed for acutely ill, altered, or emergency presentations: the structured coma exam, the rapid stroke screen, the assessment of raised intracranial pressure and herniation, the airway-and-brainstem priorities, and the decision thresholds for urgent neuroimaging and escalation. The discipline is to know exactly what to examine in the first few minutes, to compare sides relentlessly because asymmetry is the key sign, and to escalate on the basis of the exam without waiting for certainty.

## Core Rules

### Define the Purpose: Triage and Time-Critical Decisions, Not Exhaustive Localization

In the acutely altered or emergency patient, the neuro exam serves a specific purpose that differs from the outpatient localization exam. Its goals are, in order: to assess and protect the airway and identify brainstem compromise; to detect the focal or lateralizing signs that indicate a structural intracranial lesion (stroke, hemorrhage, mass, herniation); to identify the patient who may be eligible for time-critical stroke treatment; to recognize the signs of raised intracranial pressure and impending herniation; and to determine the urgency and type of neuroimaging. Keep these goals explicit, because they dictate a focused exam rather than an exhaustive one. A full cranial nerve, motor, sensory, reflex, coordination, and gait assessment is appropriate for the stable patient with a subacute presentation, but in the acutely altered patient it delays the decisions that determine outcome. Decide within the first minute whether this is a "rapid triage" situation (altered, deteriorating, time-critical) or a "full localization" situation (stable, subacute), and tailor the exam accordingly.

### Assess Airway, Breathing, and Brainstem Function First in the Altered Patient

In any patient with depressed consciousness, the first neurological priority is the brainstem, because brainstem compromise threatens the airway and breathing and signals impending herniation. Assess the respiratory pattern: Cheyne-Stokes respiration suggests bilateral cerebral or diencephalic dysfunction; central neurogenic hyperventilation suggests midbrain or pontine lesions; ataxic or irregular breathing suggests medullary dysfunction and imminent respiratory failure — a deteriorating respiratory pattern is a herniation sign and demands immediate escalation. Assess pupil size and reactivity, because the pupils are the most reliable bedside sign of brainstem function: symmetric reactive pupils indicate intact brainstem pathways; a unilateral dilated unreactive pupil (with a down-and-out eye) indicates third nerve compression from uncal herniation and is a neurosurgical emergency; bilateral fixed dilated pupils indicate severe brainstem damage or brain death (after excluding drug effect, particularly atropine and sympathomimetics). Assess eye position and movement: a conjugate gaze deviation suggests a frontal (looking toward the lesion) or pontine (looking away) pathology; dysconjugate gaze suggests a cranial nerve or brainstem lesion. Test the corneal reflex and gag reflex in the unresponsive patient to assess brainstem integrity. Test the oculocephalic (doll's eye) reflex only if the cervical spine is cleared, because in the unexamined trauma patient the spine may be injured. A patient with deteriorating brainstem signs is herniating and needs immediate neurosurgical and critical care escalation.

### Use a Validated Coma Scale Serially and Act on Deterioration

Level of consciousness is the single most important neurological observation, and in the altered or unresponsive patient it must be measured with a validated scale (such as the Glasgow Coma Scale) and recorded serially so that trends are visible. The Glasgow Coma Scale assesses eye opening, verbal response, and motor response, and its components matter individually as much as the total: a patient whose motor response drops from "obeying commands" to "withdrawing to pain" or who develops an abnormal (extensor or flexor) response to pain is deteriorating and may be herniating. Always test for the best motor response and compare sides: ask the patient to squeeze your hand and lift the arms, looking for asymmetry, because a subtle hemiparesis in an altered patient is a stroke or mass until proven otherwise. Apply a painful stimulus (supraorbital pressure, trapezius squeeze, or nail bed pressure) to elicit a response in the patient who does not obey commands, and grade the response (localizes, withdraws, abnormal flexion, extension, none). Record the scale at defined intervals — every 15 minutes in an acutely deteriorating patient — and treat any drop as a trigger for reassessment, imaging, and escalation. Do not rely on a single value; the trend is the signal.

### Perform a Rapid Stroke Screen in Any Sudden Focal Deficit

In any patient with sudden onset neurological symptoms, perform a rapid, validated stroke screen (such as the NIH Stroke Scale or a focused FAST assessment) immediately, because the eligibility for time-critical thrombolytic or endovascular treatment depends on accurate, rapid identification of the deficit and the time of onset. The rapid stroke screen assesses level of consciousness, gaze, visual fields, facial palsy, limb strength, limb ataxia, sensation, language, articulation, and extinction or neglect — and it must be done within minutes, not after a full workup. Establish the time of symptom onset precisely, or the time the patient was last seen well, because the treatment window is measured from that moment. Recognize the stroke mimics (hypoglycemia, post-ictal state, complicated migraine, electrolyte disturbance, sepsis with delirium) and check a bedside glucose immediately, because hypoglycemia is a rapid, reversible mimic that must not be missed. Do not let the possibility of a mimic delay stroke imaging in a patient with a sudden focal deficit — image first and exclude stroke, then consider mimics. Compare sides at every step, because subtle asymmetry (a mild facial droop, a pronator drift, a slightly weaker grip) may be the only sign of an evolving stroke.

### Recognize the Signs of Raised Intracranial Pressure and Herniation

Raised intracranial pressure and herniation syndromes are life-threatening, and their signs must be actively sought in any patient with altered consciousness, a structural intracranial lesion, or head trauma. The signs include a decreasing level of consciousness, a falling Glasgow Coma Scale (especially the motor component), abnormal pupil reactivity (unilateral dilated pupil in uncal herniation), abnormal posturing (decorticate flexion or decerebrate extension), a Cushing response (hypertension with bradycardia and irregular breathing — a late sign), a deteriorating respiratory pattern, and the development of new focal deficits. A patient with any of these signs, or with a known or suspected intracranial mass, hemorrhage, or infection, needs urgent neuroimaging (typically non-contrast computed tomography as the fastest available modality) and immediate neurosurgical and critical care involvement. Recognize that herniation can progress over minutes, and that waiting for a fuller picture is not an option — escalate on the basis of the exam and the trend. In the trauma patient, maintain cervical spine protection until the spine is cleared, because an unrecognized spinal injury can cause catastrophic deterioration during manipulation.

### Examine for Meningeal Signs and Sources in Suspected CNS Infection

In any patient with fever, headache, altered consciousness, or photophobia, examine for the meningeal signs (neck stiffness, Kernig sign, Brudzinski sign) and recognize that their absence does not exclude meningitis or subarachnoid hemorrhage, particularly at the extremes of age and in the immunocompromised. A patient with a thunderclap headache, neck stiffness, a decreasing conscious level, or a new seizure needs urgent imaging and, where indicated, lumbar puncture and empiric antimicrobial therapy — do not delay antibiotics for the lumbar puncture if bacterial meningitis is suspected. Look for the source and the systemic signs: a petechial or purpuric rash suggests meningococcal disease and demands immediate treatment; immunosuppression, recent neurosurgery, or a CSF shunt changes the likely organisms and the empiric regimen. In the acutely altered febrile patient, treat the possibility of central nervous system infection as a time-critical emergency and investigate and treat in parallel, not sequentially.

## Common Traps

### Skipping the Neuro Exam Because the Patient Is Uncooperative or the Situation Feels Urgent

Declaring the neuro exam "unassessable" in the altered or agitated patient and missing the focal sign. Counter by performing a focused, rapid exam — pupils, GCS with side comparison, and a stroke screen — that is feasible even in the uncooperative patient.

### Attempting a Full Textbook Exam in a Time-Critical Emergency

Consuming minutes on an exhaustive cranial nerve and sensory exam while the stroke treatment window closes. Counter by defining the purpose as triage and performing only the focused exam that drives the immediate decision.

### Recording a Single GCS Without Serial Reassessment

Accepting one normal value and missing the deterioration that occurs over the next hour. Counter by recording the GCS (especially the motor component) at defined intervals and escalating on any drop.

### Missing the Asymmetry in an Altered Patient

Failing to compare sides in the patient who cannot give a clear history, missing a subtle hemiparesis. Counter by always testing grip, arm lift, and pronator drift bilaterally and recording asymmetry.

### Treating a Pupil Change as a Drug Effect Without Excluding Herniation

Attributing a dilated pupil to atropine or eye drops while uncal herniation progresses. Counter by interpreting pupil changes in context and escalating to imaging if herniation is possible.

### Delaying Imaging or Treatment While Considering Mimics

Postponing stroke imaging or antibiotics while debating hypoglycemia, post-ictal state, or sepsis. Counter by checking bedside glucose, then imaging and treating in parallel rather than sequentially.

## Self-Check

- Did I define the purpose of the exam as rapid triage and time-critical decision-making, and perform a focused rather than exhaustive exam in the acutely altered or emergency patient?
- Did I assess airway, breathing, respiratory pattern, pupils, eye movements, and brainstem reflexes first in the patient with depressed consciousness?
- Did I measure the Glasgow Coma Scale (especially the motor component) and record it serially, escalating on any deterioration?
- Did I perform a rapid validated stroke screen in any sudden focal deficit, establish the time of onset or last-seen-well, and check bedside glucose to exclude hypoglycemia?
- Did I actively seek the signs of raised intracranial pressure and herniation (decreasing GCS, pupil change, abnormal posturing, Cushing response, respiratory deterioration) and escalate to urgent imaging and neurosurgery?
- Did I examine for meningeal signs in suspected CNS infection or subarachnoid hemorrhage, recognize that their absence does not exclude the diagnosis, and treat empirically without delay?
- Did I compare sides at every step, record asymmetry, and maintain cervical spine protection in the trauma patient?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
