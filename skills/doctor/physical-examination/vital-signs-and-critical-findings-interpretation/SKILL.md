# Vital Signs and Critical Findings Interpretation

Vital signs are the earliest and most objective signal of physiological deterioration, yet they are routinely misread in two opposite ways. They are dismissed as "baseline abnormal" in chronically ill patients who are in fact acutely decompensating, and they are read as isolated numbers rather than as a trend that tells the real story. A single blood pressure means little; the trajectory of blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, and consciousness over hours is what reveals impending collapse. The high-stakes skill is not memorizing thresholds but recognizing the patient who looks stable and is not, distinguishing compensated from decompensated physiology, identifying examination findings that demand immediate action, and knowing exactly when to escalate. This skill covers trend-based vital sign interpretation, early warning scores, shock recognition, the must-act critical findings, and the escalation thresholds that should override any temptation to "watch and wait."

## Core Rules

### Interpret Vitals as Trends, Not Single Values

A blood pressure of 100/60 in a hypertensive elderly patient may represent a dangerous fall even though the number sits within a population normal range. The most dangerous error in vital sign interpretation is treating each value in isolation. Always place the current reading against the patient's own baseline and against the preceding readings. Quantify the change and the rate of change: a drop of 30 mmHg systolic over two hours in a patient whose baseline is 160 is a red flag even though the absolute value of 130 looks reassuring. Heart rate rising while blood pressure holds, respiratory rate creeping upward, or a gradual fall in oxygen saturation are all early warnings that precede the dramatic collapse. When you receive a single set of vitals, your first question should be what the previous sets showed, and if there are no previous sets, obtain them before concluding the patient is stable.

### Apply an Early Warning Score, but Trust Clinical Concern More

Validated track-and-trigger systems such as NEWS2 and MEWS aggregate vital signs into a composite score that predicts deterioration and prompts escalation. Use them consistently: they force attention to respiratory rate (the single most under-recorded and most predictive vital sign) and they reduce the variability of human judgment. But a normal aggregate score does not override clinical concern. The score is a trigger, not a verdict. A patient who "looks unwell" — the gut feeling that experienced clinicians learn to respect — has a higher mortality risk than the score alone suggests, and the absence of a high score must never be used as a reason to defer escalation when the clinician is worried. Document the score, act on its thresholds, and explicitly note when clinical concern exceeds what the score reflects.

### Recognize Compensated Shock Before It Decompensates

Shock is not defined by hypotension; hypotension is a late sign indicating failure of compensation. Compensated shock is the dangerous window in which perfusion is failing but blood pressure is maintained by tachycardia, vasoconstriction, and increased contractility. The signs of compensation — tachycardia, cool peripheries with prolonged capillary refill, narrowed pulse pressure, tachypnea, anxiety or mild confusion, reduced urine output — are the real early markers of shock. Waiting for hypotension to confirm shock is waiting too long. Identify the compensated patient early, seek the cause (hypovolemic, distributive, cardiogenic, obstructive), and escalate to resuscitation before blood pressure collapses. A patient with normal pressure but cool clammy skin, tachycardia, and altered mentation is in shock until proven otherwise.

### Identify the Must-Act Critical Findings Immediately

Certain examination findings carry such high specificity for life-threatening pathology that their detection mandates immediate action rather than further observation. Recognize them as triggers, not as items to document and revisit later. These include signs of an acute abdomen (rigidity, guarding, rebound tenderness) suggesting perforation or peritonitis; meningismus (neck stiffness, photophobia, altered consciousness with or without rash) suggesting meningitis or subarachnoid hemorrhage; signs of airway compromise (stridor, drooling, tripod posture); signs of tension physiology (tracheal deviation, unilateral absent breath sounds, hypotension); and the classic presentations of massive hemorrhage, anaphylaxis, or stroke within the thrombolysis window. When any of these is present, the priority shifts from diagnosis to resuscitation and escalation; investigation follows stabilization, not the reverse.

### Use Explicit Escalation Thresholds and Never Substitute Watching for Acting

Define the thresholds at which you will escalate, in advance, and act on them without negotiation. Systolic blood pressure below 90, heart rate above 130 or below 40, respiratory rate above 30 or below 8, oxygen saturation below 90 percent on supplemental oxygen, a Glasgow Coma Scale drop of two points, or a lactate above 4 are not findings to trend passively — they are triggers for senior review, critical care involvement, or resuscitation. The common and dangerous failure is the soft escalation: "will review in an hour," "continue observations," "recheck blood pressure." In a deteriorating patient, an hour is a long time. When a threshold is crossed, escalate now, communicate the concern in a structured way (situation, background, assessment, recommendation), and state explicitly what you need from the person you are calling.

### Reassess Frequently in Unstable or Evolving Situations

A single assessment of an unstable patient is a snapshot that quickly becomes stale. In any patient with abnormal or worsening vitals, set an explicit reassessment interval and shorten it as the trajectory worsens. Serial examination is what catches the evolving abdomen that was soft and is now rigid, the perfusion that was adequate and is now failing, the consciousness that was intact and is now clouding. Build reassessment into the plan as a concrete action with a named time and a named responsible clinician, not as a vague intention.

## Common Traps

### Dismissing Abnormal Vitals as "Baseline"

Accepting an abnormal reading because the patient is chronically ill, when it actually represents acute deterioration from their true baseline. Counter by comparing against the patient's own prior readings, not population normals.

### Treating Vitals as Isolated Numbers

Reading each value independently and missing the trend that reveals impending collapse. Counter by always reviewing the preceding readings and the rate of change.

### Waiting for Hypotension to Diagnose Shock

Defining shock by blood pressure and missing the compensated phase where tachycardia, cool peripheries, narrowed pulse pressure, and altered mentation already indicate failing perfusion. Counter by recognizing the signs of compensation as the real early markers.

### Letting a Normal Early Warning Score Prevent Escalation

Using a normal aggregate score to justify inaction when clinical concern is high. Counter by treating the score as a trigger and explicitly escalating when the patient looks unwell regardless of the score.

### Soft Escalation Instead of Definitive Action

Responding to crossed thresholds with passive observation ("will recheck in an hour") rather than immediate senior or critical care involvement. Counter by using predefined escalation thresholds and structured communication at the moment they are crossed.

### Investigating Before Stabilizing

Pursuing diagnostic workup in a patient with a must-act critical finding while perfusion or airway is compromised. Counter by shifting to resuscitation and escalation first, with investigation following stabilization.

## Self-Check

- Did I interpret the vital signs as a trend against the patient's own baseline and prior readings, rather than as isolated numbers?
- Did I apply an early warning score consistently, and did I escalate when clinical concern exceeded the score rather than hiding behind a normal aggregate?
- Did I recognize the signs of compensated shock (tachycardia, cool peripheries, narrowed pulse pressure, altered mentation) before waiting for hypotension?
- Did I identify any must-act critical findings (acute abdomen, meningismus, airway compromise, tension physiology) and shift immediately to resuscitation and escalation?
- Did I use explicit escalation thresholds and act on them at the moment they were crossed, with structured communication, rather than deferring to passive observation?
- Did I set a concrete reassessment interval for an unstable or evolving patient, with a named time and responsible clinician?
- Did I prioritize stabilization over investigation when a critical finding was present?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
