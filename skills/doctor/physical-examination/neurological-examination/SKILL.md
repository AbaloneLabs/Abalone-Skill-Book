# Neurological Examination

A neurological examination is the most localization-driven examination in medicine: its entire purpose is not merely to detect abnormality but to determine where in the nervous system the lesion lies, because the location dictates the differential, the imaging, and the urgency. The recurring failure is to perform a fragmentary exam — testing power in one limb, checking the pupils, and moving on — and then to be unable to localize the deficit, leading to the wrong imaging and dangerous delay. Equally common is the failure to compare sides: subtle asymmetry is the heart of the neuro exam, and a power grade of "5/5" recorded without comparing left to right misses the mild hemiparesis of an evolving stroke. The high-stakes misses are the evolving focal deficit that is examined once and not reassessed serially, the meningeal signs that are never tested in the patient with headache and fever, and the confusion between upper and lower motor neuron signs that misdirects the workup. A normal single exam does not exclude serious disease, because neurological deficits evolve over time, and a patient whose exam was normal an hour ago may have a developing hemiparesis now. This skill covers the structured assessment of mental status, cranial nerves, motor and sensory function, reflexes, coordination, and gait, the localization of lesions, and the judgment of when findings demand urgent neuroimaging and escalation. The discipline is to compare sides at every step, to localize before imaging, and to reassess serially in any evolving or fluctuating deficit.

## Core Rules

### Tailor the Exam to the Clinical Question and Localize the Lesion

Neurological examination is not a fixed ritual performed identically on every patient; it is directed by the presenting problem. A patient with sudden focal weakness needs a focused, rapid exam to confirm a stroke syndrome and establish eligibility for time-critical treatment; a patient with progressive limb weakness needs a detailed motor and reflex exam to distinguish upper from lower motor neuron disease; a patient with headache and fever needs meningeal signs and a conscious-level assessment. Before imaging, ask "where is the lesion?" — central (brain, spinal cord) versus peripheral (nerve root, plexus, peripheral nerve, neuromuscular junction, muscle) — because the location determines whether to image the brain, the spine, or neither. A lesion above the foramen magnum causes cranial nerve signs and crossed deficits; a cord lesion causes a sensory level and bilateral limb signs below the lesion; a root lesion follows a dermatomal and myotomal pattern; a peripheral nerve lesion follows the territory of that nerve. Localization is the central skill, and a fragmentary exam that does not allow localization is a failed exam.

### Assess Mental Status and Conscious Level With a Validated Tool

Level of consciousness is the most important neurological observation, because a deteriorating conscious level signals a life-threatening rise in intracranial pressure or a metabolic encephalopathy. Use a validated tool — the Glasgow Coma Scale or, in stroke, the four features of the NIH stroke scale level of consciousness — and record it serially so that trends are visible. Assess orientation in time, place, and person, and test attention with a task such as serial sevens or spelling backward, because inattention is an early and easily missed sign of delirium and of neglect syndromes. Assess language — fluency, comprehension, repetition, and naming — to distinguish aphasia (a focal cortical sign, often stroke) from confusion (a non-focal encephalopathy), because a patient with aphasia is commonly mislabeled as confused and the stroke is missed. Test for neglect (extinction on double simultaneous stimulation, ignoring one side), which localizes to the non-dominant parietal lobe and is a stroke sign. Assess mood and thought content when relevant, because depression and psychosis have neurological presentations.

### Examine the Cranial Nerves Systematically and Compare Sides

Examine all twelve cranial nerves, comparing sides, because cranial nerve deficits localize the lesion to the brainstem or peripheral course of the nerve. Test visual acuity, fields (confrontation, comparing quadrants), and fundoscopy, because a visual field defect localizes to the optic pathway and papilledema indicates raised intracranial pressure. Examine the pupils for size, shape, and reactivity to light and accommodation, comparing sides: a unilateral dilated unreactive pupil with a down-and-out eye is a third nerve palsy (aneurysm until proven otherwise); a small reactive pupil with ptosis is Horner syndrome (apical tumor, carotid dissection, brainstem stroke); an Argyll Robertson pupil accommodates but does not react. Test eye movements, asking about diplopia and looking for nystagmus, because a gaze palsy, internuclear ophthalmoplegia, or fatigable ptosis localizes to the brainstem, the pons, or the neuromuscular junction (myasthenia). Test the corneal reflex, facial sensation, and facial movement (asking the patient to raise the eyebrows, close the eyes against resistance, and show the teeth), distinguishing an upper motor neuron facial weakness (forehead spared, due to bilateral cortical innervation) from a lower motor neuron weakness (whole side of face affected, Bell palsy or stroke). Test hearing with a whisper and, if asymmetric, perform Rinne and Weber tuning fork tests to distinguish conductive from sensorineural loss. Examine the palate, gag reflex, tongue (wasting and fasciculations suggest lower motor neuron), and sternocleidomastoid and trapezius strength.

### Distinguish Upper From Lower Motor Neuron Signs in the Motor Exam

Examine tone, power, and reflexes systematically in the upper and lower limbs, comparing left with right and proximal with distal. Assess tone at the wrist and elbow, and at the knee and ankle, with the patient relaxed, looking for spasticity (upper motor neuron, velocity-dependent "clasp-knife" increase), rigidity (extrapyramidal, constant "lead-pipe" or "cogwheel" resistance), and hypotonia (lower motor neuron or cerebellar). Test power using the Medical Research Council scale (0 to 5), but always compare sides, because a 5/5 that is clearly weaker than the other side is abnormal. Distinguish the patterns: upper motor neuron lesions produce increased tone, increased reflexes, clonus, weakness predominantly in the extensors of the upper limb and flexors of the lower limb (pyramidal distribution), and an extensor plantar (Babinski) response; lower motor neuron lesions produce decreased tone, decreased or absent reflexes, fasciculations, wasting, and a flexor plantar. A mixed picture suggests motor neuron disease or a combined central and peripheral process. Test for pronator drift, a sensitive sign of subtle upper motor neuron weakness: the patient holds the arms outstretched, palms up, eyes closed, and the affected side pronates and drifts down. Grade and record reflexes symmetrically (0 to 4+), and test for the plantar response, because an extensor plantar is a reliable upper motor neuron sign.

### Examine Sensation Comparatively and Map the Level or Pattern

Test light touch, pinprick, and vibration (with a 128 Hz tuning fork at the distal interphalangeal joint and the metatarsal) and joint position sense, comparing left with right and distal with proximal. Look for the patterns that localize: a sensory level on the trunk localizes to the spinal cord at that level and is a cord compression emergency; a dermatomal pattern localizes to a nerve root; a stocking-glove distal sensory loss suggests a peripheral polyneuropathy; a hemisensory loss suggests a cortical or subcortical lesion; a dissociated sensory loss (pain and temperature lost but touch preserved) suggests a central cord lesion such as syringomyelia. Do not accept "sensation intact" without comparing sides and mapping any deficit, because the pattern is the diagnosis. Recognize that sensory examination is subjective and affected by attention and cooperation, so interpret it alongside the motor and reflex findings rather than in isolation.

### Test Coordination, Gait, and the Cerebellar and Meningeal Signs

Coordination and gait testing localizes to the cerebellum, the proprioceptive pathways, and the motor system, and it is often omitted. Test finger-to-nose and heel-to-shin, looking for intention tremor and dysmetria (cerebellar), and rapid alternating movements, looking for dysdiadochokinesia. Test gait: normal, heel-to-toe (tandem, for cerebellar), on the heels (for foot drop and distal weakness), and on the toes (for calf weakness), because gait abnormality reveals subtle weakness, spasticity, ataxia, and parkinsonism that the bedside motor exam may miss. Test for Romberg sign (stance steady eyes open, unsteady eyes closed), which distinguishes sensory ataxia (positive Romberg, proprioceptive loss) from cerebellar ataxia (negative Romberg, unsteady eyes open and closed). Test for the meningeal signs in any patient with headache, fever, photophobia, or altered consciousness: neck stiffness, Kernig sign (resistance to knee extension with the hip flexed), and Brudzinski sign (hip flexion on neck flexion). A patient with a thunderclap headache, neck stiffness, or a rapidly decreasing conscious level may have subarachnoid hemorrhage or meningitis and needs urgent imaging, lumbar puncture, and antibiotics.

### Reassess Serially in Evolving or Fluctuating Deficits

A single normal neurological exam does not exclude serious disease, because deficits evolve. In any patient with an acute, fluctuating, or progressive presentation — suspected stroke, transient ischemic attack, subdural hematoma, encephalopathy, cord compression, or rising intracranial pressure — reassess the neurological exam serially and record the trend, because deterioration between exams is the trigger for escalation. Use a validated stroke scale in suspected cerebrovascular events to track change. Recognize the red flags that demand urgent neuroimaging and specialist referral: sudden onset focal deficit, thunderclap headache, decreasing conscious level, new seizures, a sensory level on the trunk suggesting cord compression, and a pattern of rapidly progressive weakness suggesting Guillain-Barre syndrome (which can ascend to respiratory failure). Cord compression is a time-critical emergency in which the neurological exam defines the window for intervention — a complete cord lesion has a poor prognosis, so any patient with back pain and limb signs needs urgent imaging.

## Common Traps

### Performing a Fragmentary Exam That Cannot Localize

Testing power in one limb and the pupils, then being unable to say where the lesion is. Counter by performing a directed but complete exam whose purpose is localization, central versus peripheral, before imaging.

### Not Comparing Sides at Every Step

Recording power, reflexes, and sensation without comparing left with right, missing subtle asymmetry that defines a hemiparesis or focal deficit. Counter by comparing sides explicitly at each step and recording asymmetry.

### Confusing Upper and Lower Motor Neuron Signs

Misreading increased tone and reflexes as lower motor neuron, or missing the extensor plantar, and ordering the wrong workup. Counter by systematically assessing tone, reflexes, pattern of weakness, wasting, fasciculations, and the plantar response to classify the lesion.

### Labeling Aphasia as Confusion

Mistaking a fluent or non-fluent aphasia for delirium and missing a stroke. Counter by formally testing fluency, comprehension, repetition, and naming in any patient with abnormal or sparse speech.

### Examining Once in an Evolving Deficit

Accepting a single normal exam in a patient with a fluctuating or progressive presentation. Counter by reassessing serially with a validated scale and escalating on deterioration.

### Forgetting Meningeal Signs and Cord Levels

Not testing neck stiffness in headache with fever, or not mapping a sensory level on the trunk in limb weakness. Counter by routinely testing meningeal signs in suspected meningitis or subarachnoid hemorrhage, and mapping a sensory level in any bilateral or trunk-associated limb deficit.

## Self-Check

- Did I tailor the exam to the clinical question and aim to localize the lesion as central versus peripheral before ordering imaging?
- Did I assess conscious level with a validated tool (Glasgow Coma Scale or NIH stroke scale), orientation, attention, language, and neglect, recording the level serially?
- Did I examine all cranial nerves comparing sides, including pupils, eye movements, facial movement distinguishing upper from lower motor neuron, and hearing?
- Did I assess tone, power with side-to-side comparison, and reflexes, and distinguish upper from lower motor neuron signs including the plantar response?
- Did I test sensation comparatively, mapping any deficit to a level, dermatome, nerve, or stocking-glove pattern rather than recording "sensation intact"?
- Did I test coordination, gait (including tandem, heel, and toe walking), Romberg sign, and the meningeal signs where indicated?
- Did I reassess serially in any evolving or fluctuating deficit, and recognize red flags (sudden focal deficit, thunderclap headache, decreasing conscious level, sensory level, ascending weakness) requiring urgent imaging and referral?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
