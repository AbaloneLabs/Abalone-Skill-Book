# Respiratory Examination

A respiratory examination is a search for evidence of respiratory failure and of the focal pathology causing it, and it is the bedside skill most often performed superficially. The recurring failure is to auscultate the chest of a fully clothed patient, hear "air entry bilaterally," and declare the exam normal — while the patient is sitting forward using accessory muscles, breathing at 30 times a minute, and unable to speak in full sentences. The most important signs of respiratory distress are visual, not auscultatory, and they are missed because the examiner goes straight to the stethoscope. Equally dangerous is the failure to assess oxygenation: a patient can be hypoxemic with clear lungs (pulmonary embolism, early ARDS, shunt), so a "normal-sounding chest" does not exclude life-threatening disease. The silent chest — the absence of breath sounds indicating minimal air movement in severe asthma or a large pneumothorax — is a pre-arrest sign that is missed when the examiner assumes quiet lungs are normal. This skill covers the structured inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation of the respiratory system, the recognition of respiratory distress and the patterns of consolidation, effusion, collapse, and pneumothorax, and the judgment of when bedside findings demand urgent escalation. The discipline is to look first and longest, to correlate what you hear with what you see, and to never trust auscultation alone to declare the chest normal.

## Core Rules

### Assess Work of Breathing and Respiratory Distress Before Touching the Patient

The most life-saving respiratory observations are made at the end of the bed before auscultation. Assess the rate, depth, and pattern of breathing, and recognize the patterns of failure: rapid shallow breathing suggests restrictive disease, pneumonia, or pulmonary embolism; deep sighing breathing (Kussmaul) suggests metabolic acidosis; slow irregular breathing suggests respiratory depression or rising intracranial pressure. Look for the signs of increased work of breathing — use of accessory muscles (sternocleidomastoid, scalenes), intercostal recession, subcostal recession, tracheal tug, nasal flaring, and the inability to speak in full sentences, which correlates with severity more reliably than any single measurement. Look for the posture: a patient sitting forward resting on their hands (tripoding) is in severe respiratory distress. Assess the pattern of breathing over a full minute while the patient is unaware, because conscious rate counting changes the rate. Cyanosis — central (tongue and mucous membranes, indicating hypoxemia) versus peripheral (fingers, indicating poor perfusion) — is a late and unreliable sign; its absence does not exclude hypoxia, and pulse oximetry must be measured. A patient who looks unwell, is using accessory muscles, and cannot complete sentences needs urgent escalation regardless of what the stethoscope shows.

### Inspect the Hands, Face, and Neck for Respiratory and Systemic Clues

Examine the hands for clubbing (which, with a respiratory history, suggests lung cancer, pulmonary fibrosis, bronchiectasis, or chronic suppurative lung disease, and must be distinguished from the non-specific peripheral cyanosis), peripheral cyanosis, and a fine tremor from beta-agonist use. Assess the face for the plethoric, cyanotic, and puffy appearance of superior vena cava obstruction, the Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) of an apical lung tumor, and conjunctival pallor of anemia that worsens breathlessness. Examine the neck for the raised jugular venous pressure of cor pulmonale or tension pneumothorax, and for cervical lymphadenopathy, which with a lung mass suggests metastatic disease and changes the diagnostic pathway. Check the position of the trachea in the suprasternal notch: deviation away from a side suggests a large pleural effusion or tension pneumothorax; deviation toward a side suggests collapse or fibrosis. Measure chest expansion by placing the hands firmly around the lower chest and observing thumb divergence on deep inspiration; reduced unilateral expansion indicates pathology on that side (effusion, consolidation, collapse, pneumothorax), and reduced bilateral expansion suggests restrictive disease or neuromuscular weakness.

### Percuss Systematically and Compare Side to Side

Percussion is performed symmetrically, side to side, at identical levels, because respiratory signs are comparative — a finding is meaningful in relation to the opposite side. Percuss the supraclavicular fossae (for apical disease, including old tuberculosis and apical tumors), then the anterior and lateral chest, then the axillae, then the posterior chest, always comparing left with right at each level. Interpret the note: resonant is normal; dull suggests consolidation, collapse, or a small effusion; stony dull suggests a pleural effusion; hyper-resonant suggests a pneumothorax or hyperinflation in COPD. A hyper-resonant hemithorax with reduced breath sounds and a deviated trachea is a tension pneumothorax — a clinical diagnosis requiring immediate decompression, not a chest X-ray first. Tactile vocal fremitus or vocal resonance (say "99," feel or listen) increases over consolidation (transmits sound) and decreases over effusion, pneumothorax, or collapse; this confirms the percussion findings and localizes the pathology.

### Auscultate Comparatively and Characterize Breath Sounds and Added Sounds

Listen with the diaphragm, asking the patient to breathe deeply through an open mouth, comparing identical points side to side at each level, covering the same areas as percussion. Assess the intensity of breath sounds: reduced globally suggests hyperinflation, obesity, or poor effort; reduced unilaterally suggests effusion, pneumothorax, collapse, or pleural disease on that side; absent breath sounds suggest a large effusion, pneumothorax, or complete collapse. Characterize the breath sounds: vesicular is normal, with the inspiratory phase longer than expiratory; bronchial breathing (harsh, with equal or longer expiration and a gap between inspiration and expiration) over the lung periphery indicates consolidation, collapse, or a cavity — and is the auscultatory hallmark of pneumonia. Identify added sounds and their significance: fine inspiratory crackles (Velcro-like, late, non-clearing) suggest pulmonary fibrosis or pulmonary edema; coarse inspiratory crackles that may clear with coughing suggest bronchiectasis or chronic secretions; wheeze indicates airway narrowing (asthma, COPD) and is typically expiratory, but remember that wheeze is a sign of airflow, not a measure of severity — a silent chest in asthma means airflow has nearly stopped and is a pre-arrest sign. A pleural rub (grating, creaking, worse on inspiration) suggests pleuritis or pulmonary infarction. Whispering pectoriloquy (clearly heard whispered words over consolidation) confirms dense lung.

### Recognize the Patterns of Common Pathology and Their Red Flags

Synthesize inspection, expansion, percussion, and auscultation into a pattern. Consolidation: reduced expansion, dull percussion, bronchial breathing, coarse crackles, increased vocal resonance, with a productive cough and fever. Pleural effusion: reduced expansion, stony dull percussion, reduced or absent breath sounds, reduced vocal resonance, with a stony dull level. Pneumothorax: reduced expansion, hyper-resonant percussion, reduced or absent breath sounds, reduced vocal resonance; if the trachea is deviated away, the patient is hypotensive and distressed, it is a tension pneumothorax needing immediate needle decompression. Collapse: reduced expansion, dull or resonant percussion depending on cause, reduced breath sounds, with tracheal deviation toward the side. Recognize the red flags that override a reassuring exam: a patient with pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, tachycardia, and risk factors for venous thromboembolism may have a pulmonary embolism with a normal chest exam — the diagnosis rests on pretest probability and imaging, not auscultation. A patient with sudden dyspnea and clear lungs may have pulmonary embolism, metabolic acidosis, or early pulmonary edema; check oxygenation, work of breathing, and the whole clinical picture.

### Correlate With Oxygenation and Never Trust Auscultation Alone

A normal-sounding chest does not mean normal gas exchange. Hypoxemia can occur with clear breath sounds in pulmonary embolism, early ARDS, shunt, pulmonary edema before crackles develop, and carbon monoxide poisoning. Measure oxygen saturation on room air when possible, and recognize that a patient already on oxygen whose saturation is normal may still be in respiratory failure if the oxygen is removed. Interpret the saturation against the work of breathing: a patient maintaining a normal saturation only by breathing at 35 times a minute with accessory muscle use is fatiguing and will fail. In COPD patients, recognize that oxygen targets differ and that excessive oxygen can worsen hypercapnia, but never withhold oxygen from a genuinely hypoxic patient. When the clinical picture suggests serious disease but the chest exam is unremarkable, escalate to chest imaging, arterial blood gases, and senior review rather than reassuring on the basis of auscultation.

## Common Traps

### Going Straight to the Stethoscope and Missing Distress

Auscultating first and declaring the chest normal while the patient is in obvious respiratory distress. Counter by observing work of breathing, rate, posture, and speech first, and escalating on distress regardless of auscultation.

### Auscultating Through Clothing With Inadequate Exposure

Listening through a gown or shirt that muffles breath sounds and hides the chest wall signs of recession and accessory muscle use. Counter by exposing the chest fully and examining anterior, lateral, and posterior chest.

### Assuming Quiet Lungs Are Normal

Interpreting reduced or absent breath sounds as a normal or unremarkable finding, missing the silent chest of severe asthma or the large pneumothorax. Counter by treating reduced air entry as abnormal until explained, and the silent chest as a pre-arrest sign.

### Failing to Compare Side to Side

Percussing and auscultating without comparing identical points on each side, missing the asymmetry that defines consolidation, effusion, and pneumothorax. Counter by examining symmetrically, side to side, at every level.

### Trusting a Normal Chest Exam to Exclude Serious Disease

Reassuring a dyspneic patient because the chest sounds clear, missing pulmonary embolism, metabolic acidosis, or early pulmonary edema. Counter by measuring oxygenation, assessing work of breathing, and escalating to imaging and blood gases when the picture does not fit the auscultation.

### Missing the Tension Pneumothorax at the Bedside

Waiting for a chest X-ray in a hypotensive, distressed patient with a hyper-resonant silent hemithorax and deviated trachea. Counter by recognizing tension pneumothorax as a clinical diagnosis requiring immediate needle decompression.

## Self-Check

- Did I assess work of breathing — rate, depth, pattern, accessory muscle use, posture, and ability to speak in full sentences — before auscultation, and escalate on distress regardless of lung sounds?
- Did I inspect the hands, face, and neck for clubbing, cyanosis, lymphadenopathy, Horner syndrome, tracheal position, and chest expansion, comparing sides?
- Did I percuss symmetrically side to side at identical levels, and interpret resonance, dullness, stony dullness, and hyper-resonance to localize consolidation, effusion, collapse, and pneumothorax?
- Did I auscultate comparatively with the mouth open, characterizing breath sound intensity and quality (vesicular versus bronchial), and identifying and interpreting crackles, wheeze, and pleural rubs?
- Did I synthesize the findings into a recognized pattern (consolidation, effusion, pneumothorax, collapse) and recognize tension pneumothorax as a clinical emergency needing immediate decompression?
- Did I measure oxygenation, interpret it against the work of breathing, and recognize that clear lungs do not exclude hypoxemia or serious disease such as pulmonary embolism?
- Did I escalate to imaging, blood gases, and senior review when the clinical picture suggests serious disease despite an unremarkable chest exam?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
