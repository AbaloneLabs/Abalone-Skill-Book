# Abdominal Examination

An abdominal examination is a structured search for peritonitis, obstruction, organomegaly, masses, and vascular disease, and its most important rule — auscultate before palpation — is the one most often broken, because it feels counterintuitive. The recurring failure is to palpate first, alter the bowel sounds by pressing on a tender abdomen, and then report a bowel sound pattern that is no longer trustworthy. Equally common is the failure to prepare the patient: cold hands, a supine flat patient, and a rushed deep palpation that elicits guarding interpreted as true peritonitis when it was simply discomfort. The high-stakes misses are the peritoneal signs — guarding, rigidity, and rebound tenderness — that indicate a surgical abdomen and are dismissed as "tender," and the special signs of appendicitis (Rovsing, psoas, obturator) that are never elicited. Organomegaly is under-detected because the examiner starts too low and does not climb up to the costal margin, and masses are missed because deep palpation is never performed gently enough for the patient to tolerate. This skill covers the disciplined sequence of inspection, auscultation, percussion, and palpation of the abdomen, the recognition of peritoneal and surgical signs, and the judgment of when abdominal findings demand urgent surgical referral. The discipline is to auscultate first, palpate last and gently, and to interpret tenderness in the context of the whole picture rather than a single finding.

## Core Rules

### Prepare the Patient and Position Correctly Before Touching

The quality of the abdominal exam depends entirely on preparation. Warm your hands, because cold hands cause reflex guarding that mimics peritonitis. Position the patient flat, supine, with the arms at the sides and a single pillow under the head, and — critically — flex the hips and knees slightly to relax the abdominal wall muscles; a fully extended patient has a tense abdominal wall that makes palpation unreliable and painful. Expose the abdomen from the xiphisternum to the pubic symphysis while preserving dignity with draping. Ensure the patient is comfortable and has emptied their bladder if possible, because a full bladder masquerades as a suprapubic mass. Explain what you are going to do and that you will start gently and stop if it hurts, because a relaxed and informed patient lets you examine more deeply and accurately. Watch the patient's face throughout palpation, because the facial expression reveals true tenderness and voluntary guarding before the verbal report does.

### Inspect From the End of the Bed Before Touching

Stand back and inspect for the findings that change the differential before you touch the patient. Look for distension, its distribution, and its character: generalized distension suggests obstruction, ascites, or ileus; localized distension suggests a mass or organomegaly. Inspect the skin for scars (which reveal prior surgery and change the differential), striae, visible veins (caput medusae of portal hypertension), and the direction of venous flow. Look for visible peristalsis (suggesting obstruction in a thin patient), visible pulsation of an abdominal aortic aneurysm in the epigastrium, and visible masses or hernias (incisional, umbilical, inguinal). Inspect the hernial orifices, because an incarcerated hernia is a common cause of obstruction and is missed when not examined. Recognize the patient lying rigidly still and motionless because movement worsens peritoneal pain — this is peritonitis until proven otherwise, in contrast to the patient with colic who writhes around trying to find a comfortable position.

### Auscultate Before Percussion and Palpation

Auscultation comes before palpation because pressing on the abdomen alters bowel sounds and renders them uninterpretable. Listen with the diaphragm in the right iliac fossa and other quadrants for at least 30 seconds before concluding. Interpret bowel sounds: normal are intermittent gurgles; hyperactive, tinkling, or rushing sounds suggest early intestinal obstruction; absent or profoundly reduced bowel sounds suggest paralytic ileus or peritonitis. Listen for bruits over the aorta and renal arteries, which suggest aortic aneurysm or renal artery stenosis. Listen for succussion splash (the sound of fluid splashing when the patient is shaken), which suggests gastric outlet obstruction. Recognize that bowel sounds are a poor discriminator in isolation — absent bowel sounds do not confirm peritonitis, and present bowel sounds do not exclude it — but auscultating first preserves the information and is the correct sequence.

### Percuss to Detect Organomegaly, Ascites, and Tympany

Percuss all four quadrants to map the abdomen. Tympany suggests gas-filled bowel or obstruction; dullness suggests a solid organ, mass, or fluid. Percuss for the liver span in the mid-clavicular line, starting in the chest where the note is resonant and moving down to where it becomes dull, then percussing up from the abdomen; the liver edge is where dullness appears. A liver span greater than the normal range suggests hepatomegaly; a shrunken hard liver suggests cirrhosis. Percuss for splenomegaly starting in the right lower quadrant and moving toward the left upper quadrant, because an enlarged spleen displaces the tympanitic colonic gas with dullness (Traube space and Castell sign are useful screening adjuncts). Percuss for shifting dullness and test for a fluid thrill to detect ascites: shifting dullness — moving the boundary of dullness when the patient rolls onto their side — is the more reliable bedside sign of significant ascites. A distended tympanitic abdomen with hyperactive bowel sounds suggests obstruction; a distended dull abdomen with shifting dullness suggests ascites.

### Palpate Gently First, Then Deeply, and Elicit the Peritoneal Signs

Palpate all nine regions lightly first, using the flat of the hand, mapping tenderness and superficial masses, and watching the patient's face. Then palpate deeply, one region at a time, saving the region the patient identified as most painful for last so that early discomfort does not prevent the rest of the exam. Distinguish voluntary guarding (the patient tenses the wall consciously due to tenderness, which relaxes with distraction and deeper breathing) from involuntary guarding or rigidity (the abdominal wall is reflexively and persistently tense due to peritoneal inflammation, which does not relax) — rigidity is a sign of peritonitis and a surgical abdomen. Test for rebound tenderness, but recognize that it is painful and uncomfortable for the patient; many clinicians prefer percussion tenderness (tapping the abdomen to jar the peritoneum) as a gentler equivalent. Elicit the special signs of peritoneal inflammation in suspected appendicitis: Rovsing sign (palpation of the left iliac fossa producing pain in the right iliac fossa), psoas sign (pain on resisted hip flexion or extension, suggesting a retrocaecal or psoas abscess), and obturator sign (pain on internal rotation of the flexed hip, suggesting a pelvic appendix). Palpate for organomegaly on deep inspiration, starting low in the right iliac fossa and climbing toward the costal margin with each breath so that an enlarged liver or spleen is met by the examining hand; never start at the costal margin, because you will miss an enlarged organ that extends below your starting point. Examine for a pulsatile, expansile epigastric mass suggesting an abdominal aortic aneurysm, especially in an older man with risk factors.

### Examine the Hernial Orifices, Genitalia, Rectum, and Urinalysis

The abdominal exam is incomplete without examining the hernial orifices (inguinal and femoral) for incarcerated or strangulated hernia, which is a common and easily missed cause of obstruction and acute pain. Examine the external genitalia when relevant, because testicular torsion can present as abdominal pain and is missed when not considered. Perform a rectal examination when indicated, assessing for masses, tenderness, the prostate, and the character and color of the stool (melena suggests upper gastrointestinal bleeding, fresh blood suggests lower bleeding). Check a urinalysis and pregnancy test, because a urinary tract infection, renal colic, or an ectopic pregnancy can present as abdominal pain. Check the costovertebral angle for tenderness (Murphy punch), because renal and retroperitoneal pathology can present as abdominal pain and is missed when only the anterior abdomen is examined. The abdomen is not an isolated organ system — the cause of the pain may be urological, gynecological, vascular, or retroperitoneal.

### Correlate Findings With the History and Recognize the Surgical Abdomen

Interpret the abdominal exam against the pain history, because the character and evolution of pain often matter more than the single exam finding. Recognize the surgical abdomen — peritonitis (rigidity, guarding, rebound, and a motionless patient), obstruction (distension, hyperactive then absent bowel sounds, colicky pain, and vomiting), ischemia (pain out of proportion to the exam), and perforation (sudden severe pain, rigid abdomen, and free air) — and escalate urgently to surgical review and imaging. Recognize that early peritonitis and early obstruction can have a soft abdomen and unremarkable exam, so a worsening or atypical picture demands serial reassessment, not a single reassuring exam. A patient whose abdominal pain is severe and constant, who looks unwell, or who has peritoneal signs needs urgent investigation and surgical consultation regardless of a single set of vitals or one exam.

## Common Traps

### Palpating Before Auscultating

Pressing on the abdomen first and then listening to bowel sounds altered by the palpation. Counter by auscultating before percussion and palpation, every time, to preserve the bowel sound information.

### Cold Hands and a Flat, Tense Patient

Examining with cold hands and the patient fully extended, producing reflex guarding misread as peritonitis. Counter by warming the hands, flexing the hips and knees, and reassuring the patient before palpating.

### Missing Peritoneal Signs by Not Eliciting Them

Reporting "tender abdomen" without distinguishing voluntary from involuntary guarding, or never testing for Rovsing, psoas, and obturator signs in suspected appendicitis. Counter by deliberately eliciting rigidity, rebound or percussion tenderness, and the special signs in every suspected acute abdomen.

### Starting Palpation for Organomegaly Too High

Beginning liver and spleen palpation at the costal margin and missing an enlarged organ that hangs below. Counter by starting low in the right iliac fossa and climbing up with each inspiration.

### Forgetting the Hernial Orifices, Rectum, and Urinalysis

Declaring the abdominal exam complete without examining hernial orifices, performing a rectal exam when indicated, or checking urinalysis and pregnancy. Counter by completing the full assessment, because hernias, urological, and gynecological causes are commonly missed.

### Reassuring on a Single Soft Exam in a Worsening Picture

Discharging a patient with severe constant pain on the basis of one unremarkable exam. Counter by recognizing that early peritonitis and obstruction can be soft, and reassessing serially in any evolving or atypical picture.

## Self-Check

- Did I warm my hands, position the patient supine with the hips and knees flexed, expose the abdomen fully, and explain the exam before starting?
- Did I inspect from the end of the bed for distension, scars, visible veins, peristalsis, pulsation, hernias, and whether the patient lies still (peritonism) or writhes (colic)?
- Did I auscultate before percussion and palpation, listening for bowel sounds, bruits, and succussion splash?
- Did I percuss all quadrants and assess liver span, splenomegaly, and shifting dullness or fluid thrill for ascites?
- Did I palpate lightly then deeply, watching the face, distinguishing voluntary from involuntary guarding, and eliciting rebound or percussion tenderness and the Rovsing, psoas, and obturator signs?
- Did I palpate for organomegaly starting low and climbing up on inspiration, and check for a pulsatile expansile epigastric mass suggesting an aneurysm?
- Did I complete the exam with hernial orifices, rectal examination when indicated, urinalysis, pregnancy test when relevant, and costovertebral angle tenderness?
- Did I recognize the surgical abdomen and escalate to urgent surgical review and imaging, and plan serial reassessment for an evolving picture?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
