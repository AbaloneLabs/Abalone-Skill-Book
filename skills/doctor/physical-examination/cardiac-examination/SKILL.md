# Cardiac Examination

A cardiac examination is not a ritual of listening to the chest and moving on. It is a structured search for evidence of valvular disease, heart failure, pericardial disease, and hemodynamic compromise, and it only works when it is performed in the correct sequence, with the patient in the correct position, and with the findings interpreted against the clinical context. The recurring failure is to reduce the exam to auscultation alone, to auscultate through clothing in a half-sitting patient, and then to attach a murmur grade to a sound that was never properly characterized. Murmurs are misgraded, radiations are never sought, the jugular venous pressure is skipped because it is hard, and the peripheral signs of heart failure are forgotten — so a patient with new pulmonary edema gets a confident "normal exam" that missed the elevated JVP and the displaced apex. Worse, a critical finding like a pericardial rub, a silent diastolic murmur of severe aortic regurgitation, or an irregularly irregular pulse of new atrial fibrillation is overlooked because the examiner did not position, did not palpate, and did not listen long enough. This skill covers the disciplined sequence of inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation of the cardiovascular system, the recognition of critical findings, and the judgment of when a bedside finding changes management or demands urgent escalation. The discipline is to treat the cardiac exam as a hypothesis-generating procedure whose every component matters, and to never let auscultation substitute for the whole.

## Core Rules

### Perform the Examination in the Correct Sequence and Position

The cardiovascular examination follows a fixed order — general inspection, vital signs and peripheral pulses, jugular venous pressure, precordial inspection, palpation, percussion, then auscultation — because each step informs the next and auscultation performed first skips the context that makes it interpretable. Position the patient correctly: examine the jugular venous pressure with the patient at 30 to 45 degrees, head turned slightly away, with tangential light across the sternocleidomastoid; examine the praecordium and apex at 45 degrees, but reposition the patient into the left lateral position to bring the apex closer to the chest wall when listening for the mitral murmurs and the mid-diastolic rumble of mitral stenosis. Exposure must be adequate — auscultating through a gown or shirt muffles high-pitched sounds and is a leading cause of missed murmurs. Ensure a quiet environment, because ambient noise obscures low-pitched sounds like the diastolic rumble and the third heart sound. Do not begin listening until inspection, palpation, and the JVP are done, because the apex character and venous pressure determine what you are listening for.

### Characterize Pulses and the Jugular Venous Pressure Deliberately

The pulse is not just a rate. Assess rate, rhythm, character, and volume, comparing radial and femoral pulses simultaneously to screen for coarctation (radio-femoral delay). Recognize the abnormal pulse characters that point to specific pathology: a slow-rising small-volume pulse (pulsus parvus et tardus) in severe aortic stenosis, a collapsing/bounding pulse (water-hammer) in aortic regurgitation, an alternating strong and weak pulse (pulsus alternans) in severe left ventricular failure, and a paradoxical fall in systolic pressure on inspiration (pulsus paradoxus) in cardiac tamponade or severe asthma. Check for an irregularly irregular rhythm indicating atrial fibrillation, and confirm at the apex, because a radial pulse undercounts the ventricular rate in fast atrial fibrillation with a weak pulse. The jugular venous pressure is the most underused and most informative bedside hemodynamic measure: an elevated JVP is the most specific sign of decompensated heart failure, far more so than basal crackles or peripheral edema. Distinguish the venous waveform (biphasic, non-palpable, occlusible with light pressure, varies with respiration and position) from the carotid arterial pulse (monophasic, palpable, not occlusible). Measure the vertical height above the sternal angle; more than 3 to 4 cm is elevated. A dominant venous a wave suggests pulmonary hypertension or tricuspid stenosis; cannon a waves indicate complete heart block; large v waves indicate tricuspid regurgitation.

### Locate and Characterize the Apex Beat and Palpate for Thrills

Palpate the apex beat (point of maximal impulse) in the fifth intercostal space, mid-clavicular line, when the patient is in the left lateral position. A displaced apex (lateral to the mid-clavicular line or into the sixth intercostal space) indicates cardiomegaly or volume overload and is a reliable sign of left ventricular dilation. A sustained or pressure-loaded apex suggests left ventricular hypertrophy (aortic stenosis, hypertension); a tapping, localized apex suggests mitral stenosis; a hyperdynamic, displaced apex suggests volume overload (aortic or mitral regurgitation, high-output states). Palpate for heaves (a sustained parasternal impulse suggesting right ventricular hypertrophy or volume overload) and thrills (a palpable murmur indicating at least a grade 4/6 intensity and significant turbulence). A thrill over the aortic area suggests significant aortic stenosis; a thrill at the apex suggests significant mitral regurgitation. If you can feel it, the finding is important — do not dismiss a thrill as normal.

### Auscultate Systematically With the Right Stethoscope and Technique

Use the diaphragm for high-pitched sounds (S1, S2, aortic and mitral regurgitation, aortic stenosis, pericardial rub) pressed firmly against the skin, and the bell for low-pitched sounds (S3, S4, mitral stenosis rumble, tricuspid murmurs) applied lightly. Listen at the four primary areas in sequence — aortic (right second intercostal space), pulmonary (left second intercostal space), tricuspid (left lower sternal border), and mitral (apex) — but recognize these are screening zones, not the only places pathology is heard; listen wherever the murmur is loudest. Time every sound to systole or diastole using the carotid pulse or S2 as a reference, because timing is the single most important step in murmur identification. Identify S1 and S2, then listen for added sounds (S3 in heart failure, S4 in hypertrophy or ischemia, an ejection click in aortic stenosis, an opening snap in mitral stenosis, a pericardial rub in pericarditis). Have the patient lean forward in full expiration to listen for the early diastolic murmur of aortic regurgitation at the left sternal border, and lie in the left lateral position in held expiration to listen for the mitral stenosis rumble at the apex. These dynamic maneuvers are not optional — they are how subtle murmurs are found.

### Characterize Murmurs Fully and Always Seek Radiation

A murmur is not fully described until you have noted its timing (systolic, diastolic, or continuous), location of maximal intensity, radiation, grade (1 to 6, with a thrill defining 4 and above), pitch, and quality. Radiation is diagnostic: an ejection systolic murmur radiating to the carotids suggests aortic stenosis; a pansystolic murmur radiating to the axilla suggests mitral regurgitation; an early diastolic murmur radiating down the left sternal border when leaning forward suggests aortic regurgitation. Always listen at the carotids and the axilla, not just the praecordium. Use dynamic maneuvers to differentiate: handgrip increases left-sided murmurs (mitral regurgitation, aortic regurgitation) and decreases hypertrophic cardiomyopathy; the Valsalva maneuver and standing decrease aortic stenosis and increase hypertrophic cardiomyopathy; squatting does the reverse. A new murmur in a febrile or septic patient raises infective endocarditis until proven otherwise and demands blood cultures and echocardiography, not reassurance. A pericardial rub — a coarse, superficial, three-component leather-grating sound best heard with the patient leaning forward in expiration — diagnoses pericarditis; its absence does not exclude it, but its presence is pathognomonic.

### Correlate Every Finding With the History and Recognize Heart Failure Signs

The cardiac exam is meaningless in isolation. Interpret findings against the history: a systolic murmur in a patient with exertional chest pain, syncope, and heart failure symptoms is critical; the same murmur as an incidental finding in an asymptomatic patient may be benign flow murmur. Actively look for the signs of left and right heart failure: elevated JVP, basal inspiratory crackles (though crackles are an insensitive sign of pulmonary edema), a third heart sound (the gallop of heart failure), peripheral edema, hepatomegaly with hepatojugular reflux, and ascites. Recognize that peripheral edema has many non-cardiac causes and that crackles clear with coughing in chronic lung disease — do not over-diagnose heart failure on a single sign. Conversely, do not under-diagnose it: an elevated JVP with an S3 and exertional dyspnea is heart failure even if the chest is clear. When findings suggest acute decompensation — new S3, elevated JVP, hypotension, cool peripheries — escalate urgently, because bedside assessment can identify cardiogenic shock before the echocardiogram is done.

## Common Traps

### Auscultating Through Clothing in a Poorly Positioned Patient

Listening through a gown or with the patient bolt upright muffles murmurs and is the leading cause of the "missed murmur." Counter by exposing the chest fully, positioning at 45 degrees and in the left lateral position as needed, and listening in a quiet room with both diaphragm and bell.

### Reducing the Exam to Auscultation Alone

Skipping inspection, the JVP, the apex, and the pulses and going straight to the stethoscope, then calling the exam normal. Counter by performing the full sequence every time, because the JVP and apex often reveal heart failure and cardiomegaly that auscultation misses.

### Skipping the Jugular Venous Pressure Because It Is Hard

Omitting the JVP because it takes time and skill, and thereby missing the most specific sign of decompensated heart failure. Counter by practicing and measuring it at every cardiac exam, distinguishing venous from arterial pulsation, and reporting the vertical height.

### Mischaracterizing or Not Timing a Murmur

Describing a murmur as "systolic" without timing it, or assigning a grade without noting location, radiation, and quality. Counter by timing every murmur against the carotid pulse, characterizing all six features, and always listening for radiation to the carotids and axilla.

### Ignoring the New Murmur in a Febrile Patient

Dismissing a new murmur in a septic or febrile patient as benign flow. Counter by treating a new regurgitant murmur with fever as infective endocarditis until proven otherwise, drawing blood cultures, and ordering echocardiography urgently.

### Forgetting Dynamic Maneuvers and Special Positions

Listening only supine and missing the aortic regurgitation murmur that appears leaning forward in expiration, or the mitral stenosis rumble in the left lateral position. Counter by using position and respiratory maneuvers routinely for suspected diastolic murmurs.

## Self-Check

- Did I perform the full sequence — general inspection, pulses and pulse character, JVP, praecordial inspection, palpation of the apex and thrills, percussion, and auscultation — rather than jumping to the stethoscope?
- Did I position the patient correctly: 45 degrees for the JVP and praecordium, left lateral for the apex and mitral murmurs, leaning forward in expiration for aortic regurgitation, with the chest fully exposed in a quiet room?
- Did I measure and characterize the JVP and distinguish it from the carotid pulse, recognizing it as the most specific sign of decompensated heart failure?
- Did I locate and characterize the apex beat, noting displacement, a sustained or hyperdynamic character, heaves, and thrills?
- Did I auscultate with both diaphragm and bell, time every murmur to systole or diastole, and characterize each murmur by timing, location, radiation, grade, pitch, and quality?
- Did I actively seek the signs of heart failure (elevated JVP, S3 gallop, crackles, edema, hepatomegaly) and correlate them with the history rather than diagnosing on a single sign?
- Did I recognize critical findings — a new murmur in a febrile patient, a pericardial rub, pulsus paradoxus, a collapsing pulse, an irregularly irregular rhythm — and escalate appropriately?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
