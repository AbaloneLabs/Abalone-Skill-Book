# Systematic Examination Technique

Physical examination is a structured act of evidence gathering, not a ritual performed to complete a checklist. The way an examination is sequenced, the order of inspection-palpation-percussion-auscultation, the decision to examine a system fully versus focus on a complaint, and the honesty with which findings are recorded all change what the clinician concludes and what the next patient receives. The recurring failure is not clumsiness with technique; it is examining to confirm an existing suspicion rather than to discover the truth. A confirmation-biased exam finds what the examiner expects and misses what does not fit. This skill covers disciplined examination sequencing, completeness versus focus, adaptation to body habitus and immobility, accurate documentation, and the cognitive discipline needed to keep the examination honest. It applies to bedside, ward, and clinic encounters, and to any context where an agent is describing or guiding a physical examination.

## Core Rules

### Follow the Canonical Sequence, Inverting Only When the System Demands

Most systems follow inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation in that order. The cardinal exception is the abdomen, where auscultation precedes palpation and percussion so that manual manipulation does not alter bowel sounds. The respiratory and cardiac examinations are built around a reproducible regional sequence (e.g., compare left with right across lung zones; palpate the precordium in the standard valve areas). Following the canonical order is not pedantry: percussion before auscultation of the chest, or palpation before auscultation of the abdomen, can create or erase the very signs you are trying to detect. Know the exception for each system and apply it deliberately. If you cannot recall the correct order for a system, that is a signal to slow down rather than improvise.

### Decide Completeness Versus Focus Explicitly

A full head-to-toe examination is appropriate for the undifferentiated patient, the first encounter, the perioperative assessment, and any situation where the diagnosis is genuinely unknown. A focused examination is appropriate when a working diagnosis is well established and the question is narrow (e.g., confirming resolution of consolidation). The error is not choosing one over the other; it is failing to make the choice explicit. State what you are doing and why: "focused cardiac exam to assess for new murmur" versus "full examination because the presentation is undifferentiated." A focused exam that was never acknowledged as focused silently becomes an incomplete exam, and the missing regions are never examined by anyone. When in doubt about whether a finding is isolated, widen the examination rather than narrowing it.

### Examine for the Diagnosis, Not for the Suspicion

Examine to discover findings, not to confirm a prior hypothesis. This is the central cognitive discipline of physical examination. If you have decided the patient has pneumonia, you will hear crackles; if you have decided it is heart failure, you will hear them as fine and late-inspiratory. Counter confirmation bias by deliberately asking what finding would refute the leading diagnosis, and by examining comparison zones and asymptomatic regions. Always compare left with right, and symptomatic with asymptomatic. Examine the unaffected side first so the patient's baseline and your reference point are established before the abnormal side is assessed. Record negative findings that matter (e.g., "no rebound tenderness," "no meningeal signs," "no peripheral edema") because their absence is as diagnostically important as a positive sign.

### Adapt Technique to Body Habus, Mobility, and Patient Factors

The textbook examination assumes a cooperative, mobile, thin patient. Real patients are often obese, immobilized, intubated, agitated, or in pain. Adapt deliberately rather than abandoning the exam. In obesity, reposition for cardiac auscultation (roll the patient left lateral decubitus to bring the apex closer to the chest wall, use the stethoscope bell for low-pitched sounds), exert deeper palpation to reach abdominal structures, and recognize that normal percussion and auscultation may be technically limited. In the immobilized or critically ill patient, log-roll for posterior chest and back examination, use the lateral approach, and accept that some maneuvers are impossible rather than recording a false normal. In agitated or cognitively impaired patients, shorten the sequence, prioritize the highest-yield maneuvers, and return when cooperation improves. Document the limitation honestly: "abdomen poorly palpable due to body habitus; no rebound elicited but exam limited" is far more useful than "abdomen soft, non-tender," which implies a completeness that was not achieved.

### Pursue and Correlate Findings Across Systems

An isolated finding is weaker than a corroborated pattern. When you detect an abnormality, actively look for its systemic correlates before concluding. A new murmur should prompt pulse character, blood pressure, JVP, and signs of heart failure. Hepatomegaly should prompt stigmata of chronic liver disease and lymphadenopathy. A focal neurologic deficit should prompt a structured cranial nerve, motor, sensory, and coordination assessment to localize the lesion. This cross-system correlation is what transforms examination from a list of signs into a coherent clinical picture, and it is what distinguishes a thoughtful examiner from one who simply reports findings in isolation.

### Document Findings Objectively and Specifically

Record what you found, not what you concluded, and separate observation from interpretation. "Systolic murmur, grade 3/6, heard loudest at the right second intercostal space, radiating to the carotids" is a finding; "aortic stenosis" is a conclusion that belongs in the assessment. Describe findings with the specificity that lets a colleague reproduce or challenge them: location, intensity, timing, radiation, and what exacerbates or relieves them. Note the conditions of the examination (patient position, cooperation, pain, body habitus) because they determine how much weight the findings carry. Distinguish "not examined" from "normal": writing "normal" for a region you did not assess is a falsification that propagates through the record and can mask serious disease. If an exam was limited, say so and explain why.

### Recognize When Examination Alone Is Insufficient and Escalate

Physical examination has real sensitivity limits. A normal abdomen does not exclude perforation or ischemia; absent crackles do not exclude early pulmonary edema; a non-tender abdomen does not exclude retroperitoneal pathology. When the clinical suspicion is high but the examination is unrevealing, do not let a "normal exam" provide false reassurance — escalate to imaging, serial examination, or specialist input. In high-risk presentations (possible aortic dissection, mesenteric ischemia, cauda equina syndrome, meningitis), a single examination is rarely sufficient; plan explicit reassessment at defined intervals and lower the threshold for definitive investigation.

## Common Traps

### Confirmation-Biased Examination

Examining to confirm a prior diagnosis and finding only supportive signs while discounting contradictory ones. Counter by deliberately searching for the finding that would refute the leading diagnosis and by examining comparison regions.

### The Silent Focused Exam That Becomes Incomplete

Perform a focused exam without acknowledging it as focused, so the unexamined systems are never assessed by anyone. Counter by stating explicitly whether the exam is full or focused and why, and widen the scope when uncertainty is high.

### Recording "Normal" for an Unexamined Region

Writing "abdomen soft, non-tender" when the abdomen was not actually palpated, or "neurologically intact" when no formal neuro exam was done. Counter by documenting "not examined" or "exam limited" with the reason, never a fabricated normal.

### Failing to Adapt for Obesity or Immobility

Applying textbook technique unchanged to an obese or immobilized patient and either missing findings or recording a false normal. Counter by deliberately modifying positioning, pressure, and sequence, and by documenting the limitation.

### Abandoning the Canonical Sequence

Improvising the inspection-palpation-percussion-auscultation order and altering the very signs being sought (e.g., palpating the abdomen before auscultating bowel sounds). Counter by following the canonical order and applying the known exceptions deliberately.

### Treating a Single Isolated Finding as Diagnostic

Building a diagnosis on one sign without seeking its systemic correlates. Counter by actively pursuing corroborating findings across systems before concluding.

### Letting a "Normal" Exam Provide False Reassurance

Concluding that a serious diagnosis is excluded because one examination was normal, ignoring the limited sensitivity of physical exam for high-risk conditions. Counter by escalating to imaging, serial exams, or specialist consultation when suspicion remains high despite a normal exam.

## Self-Check

- Did I follow the canonical inspection-palpation-percussion-auscultation sequence, applying the abdominal auscultation-first exception correctly?
- Did I explicitly decide and document whether the exam was full or focused, and is that choice justified by the clinical question?
- Did I examine to discover findings rather than to confirm a prior suspicion, deliberately seeking disconfirming evidence?
- Did I adapt technique for body habitus, mobility, pain, and cooperation, and did I document any resulting limitation honestly?
- Did I correlate an abnormal finding across systems to build a coherent picture rather than reporting signs in isolation?
- Did I record objective, specific findings with location, intensity, timing, and radiation, clearly separating observation from interpretation?
- Did I distinguish "not examined" and "exam limited" from "normal," and avoid fabricating a normal for regions I did not assess?
- Did I recognize the sensitivity limits of examination and escalate to imaging, serial assessment, or specialist consultation when suspicion remained high despite a normal exam?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
