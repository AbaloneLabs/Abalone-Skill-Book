# Musculoskeletal Examination

The musculoskeletal examination is one of the most frequently performed and most frequently inadequate assessments in medicine, and the inadequacy matters because the difference between a benign soft-tissue complaint and a limb- or life-threatening condition often rests entirely on physical findings. The recurring failure is the generic "looks swollen and tender" exam that does not distinguish the inflamed from the infected joint, does not test stability and range of motion systematically, does not compare with the contralateral side, and does not elicit the specific signs that separate a septic arthritis (a destructive emergency) from an effusion, a crystal arthropathy, or a flare of inflammatory arthritis. Equally common is the failure to recognize the musculoskeletal red flags: the hot, swollen, exquisitely tender joint that may be septic; the back pain with neurological deficit that may be cauda equina compression; the injured joint with instability that needs surgical assessment; and the limb with pain, pallor, pulselessness, and paralysis that is acute limb ischemia. The musculoskeletal exam is also where the comparison with the other side is most powerful and most often omitted — asymmetry is the key sign in both acute injury and chronic arthritis, and a joint described as "normal" without comparison to its partner is a joint incompletely examined. This skill covers the structured regional musculoskeletal examination, the distinction of inflammatory, mechanical, and septic presentations, the look-feel-move-special tests framework, the recognition of musculoskeletal red flags, and the decision thresholds for imaging, aspiration, and urgent referral. The discipline is to examine each joint region systematically, to compare with the contralateral side at every step, and to never let a swollen joint be dismissed as "just arthritis" until sepsis has been excluded.

## Core Rules

### Use the Look, Feel, Move, Special Tests Framework for Every Joint

A structured approach prevents the omission of key findings and is applicable to every joint region. Look from the end of the bed for alignment, deformity, swelling, muscle wasting, scars, and skin changes (erythema suggesting inflammation or infection, psoriasis suggesting psoriatic arthritis, nodules suggesting rheumatoid or gouty tophi). Look at the joint in comparison with the contralateral side, because subtle swelling and deformity are visible only against the normal partner. Feel for skin temperature (warmth suggesting inflammation or infection; compare with the other side), swelling (effusion, synovial thickening, or a boggy joint), tenderness (localize it precisely to the joint line, the overlying soft tissues, the tendon, or the bursa, because the site of tenderness distinguishes arthritis from periarticular disease), and crepitus on movement. Move the joint actively first (the patient moves it, testing range of motion and revealing pain and willingness), then passively (the examiner moves it, testing the full range and comparing active with passive — a reduced passive range suggests a mechanical block, effusion, or contracture, while a reduced active range with a full passive range suggests pain, weakness, or tendon rupture). Then perform the special tests specific to the region — stability tests, impingement tests, meniscal tests, ligament tests — that identify the specific structure at fault. Following this sequence for every joint ensures that no component is skipped and that the findings are comparable across examinations and examiners.

### Compare With the Contralateral Side at Every Step

The single most powerful habit in musculoskeletal examination is comparison with the unaffected side, because normal ranges of motion and normal findings vary widely between individuals, and asymmetry is the most reliable sign of abnormality. A joint that has 90 degrees of flexion may be normal for one patient and a severe restriction for another whose contralateral joint flexes to 130 degrees. Always examine the asymptomatic side first to establish the patient's baseline, then compare the symptomatic side against it, for range of motion, swelling, warmth, tenderness, stability, and strength. This is especially important in patients with pre-existing disease (osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, hypermobility) whose "normal" is already abnormal, and in bilateral conditions where comparison with the patient's own baseline requires asking about their pre-morbid function. Record the comparison explicitly: "left knee flexes to 110 degrees, right to 130 degrees; left is the symptomatic side" is far more informative than "left knee flexes to 110 degrees."

### Distinguish Inflammatory, Mechanical, and Septic Presentations

The character of the joint findings points toward the underlying process and determines urgency. Mechanical or degenerative disease (osteoarthritis, internal derangement) typically produces pain that worsens with use, improves with rest, short-lived morning stiffness (under 30 minutes), crepitus, and a restricted range of motion with a hard, capsular end-feel. Inflammatory arthritis (rheumatoid, psoriatic, reactive, ankylosing spondylitis) produces warmth, swelling with a boggy synovial feel, prolonged morning stiffness (over an hour) that improves with activity, and often symmetrical small-joint involvement. Crystal arthropathies (gout, pseudogout) produce an acutely hot, red, exquisitely tender joint, classically the first metatarsophalangeal joint in gout but potentially any joint. The presentation that must never be missed is septic arthritis: a single hot, swollen, exquisitely tender joint with severe pain on any movement, often with systemic features of fever and malaise, which destroys cartilage within hours and demands urgent aspiration and treatment. Septic arthritis is a clinical diagnosis of exclusion in the acutely hot joint — aspirate and analyze the synovial fluid (cell count, Gram stain, culture, crystals) before concluding the cause is crystal or inflammatory, because infection and crystal arthropathy coexist and mimic each other. The patient who is immunocompromised, at the extremes of age, or unable to mount a fever may have septic arthritis without the classic systemic features, so maintain a low threshold for aspiration.

### Perform the Regional Examination With the Region-Specific Special Tests

Each joint region has high-yield special tests that identify the specific structure at fault, and omitting them leaves the examination incomplete. The shoulder: test the rotator cuff (resisted movements for tendinopathy or tears, the painful arc, drop-arm test for a cuff tear), impingement tests, and stability (apprehension and relocation tests). The knee: test the collateral ligaments (varus and valgus stress), the cruciate ligaments (anterior and posterior drawer, Lachman test for the anterior cruciate ligament), the menisci (McMurray test, joint line tenderness), and assess for an effusion (patellar tap, bulge sign). The hip: assess range of motion (especially internal rotation, lost early in arthritis), gait, and leg length, and distinguish hip from knee and back pain by examining all three. The hand and wrist: inspect for deformities (swan neck, boutonniere, ulnar deviation of rheumatoid arthritis, Heberden and Bouchard nodes of osteoarthritis), assess the grip and pinch, test the median nerve (carpal tunnel) and the peripheral nerves, and squeeze the metacarpophalangeal joints for the tenderness of inflammatory arthritis. The spine: assess posture, range of motion, and perform a neurological exam (motor, sensory, reflexes) to detect nerve root or cord compression; examine for the red flags of cauda equina and cord compression. The foot and ankle: inspect the arch and alignment, test range of motion, and examine for the signs of inflammatory arthritis and gout at the first metatarsophalangeal joint. Knowing and performing the region-specific tests is what transforms a generic exam into a diagnostic one.

### Screen for the Musculoskeletal Red Flags That Demand Urgent Action

Certain musculoskeletal findings are red flags for limb- or life-threatening conditions and must be actively sought and acted upon. The acutely hot, swollen, single joint demands exclusion of septic arthritis by aspiration. Back pain with any of: new urinary or fecal incontinence or retention, saddle anesthesia, bilateral neurological deficit, or progressive lower limb weakness, suggests cauda equina syndrome and demands urgent imaging and surgical referral. Back pain with systemic features (fever, weight loss), a history of cancer, immunosuppression, or intravenous drug use, or nocturnal pain unrelieved by rest, suggests infection or malignancy and demands imaging. A painful joint with overlying cellulitis or a break in the skin raises the possibility of septic arthritis or osteomyelitis. Acute limb pain with pallor, pulselessness, pain, paresthesia, paralysis, and perishing cold (the six Ps) is acute limb ischemia and is a surgical emergency. A swollen limb, especially after immobilization or surgery, with calf tenderness and swelling, suggests deep vein thrombosis. A child with a limp, fever, and a painful joint or limb may have septic arthritis or osteomyelitis and needs urgent assessment. Recognize these red flags, do not attribute them to a benign cause without investigation, and escalate to imaging, aspiration, or surgical referral as the situation demands.

### Assess Function and Impact, Not Just the Joint

The musculoskeletal examination is incomplete without an assessment of the patient's function, because function is the outcome that matters most to the patient and is often more affected than the joint examination alone would suggest. Assess gait (an immensely informative observation that reveals pain, weakness, instability, and neurological deficit), the ability to perform activities of daily living (walking, stairs, dressing, grooming), grip strength, and the impact on work and independence. Use validated functional and disease-specific assessment tools where appropriate. Recognize that musculoskeletal disease frequently coexists with frailty, obesity, cardiovascular disease, and depression, and that the functional impact is determined by the whole patient, not the joint alone. Integrate the functional assessment into the plan: the goal of treatment is often pain relief and preserved function rather than anatomical correction, and the plan must address the patient's goals and their whole-person context.

## Common Traps

### The Generic "Swollen and Tender" Exam Without Structure or Comparison

Recording a joint as "swollen and tender" without following the look-feel-move-special tests sequence or comparing with the contralateral side. Counter by performing the structured exam and comparing sides at every step, recording asymmetry explicitly.

### Missing Septic Arthritis Behind a Diagnosis of Gout or Flare

Concluding that an acutely hot joint is a crystal arthropathy or an inflammatory flare without aspirating to exclude infection. Counter by aspirating and analyzing the synovial fluid of any acutely hot, single, swollen joint before concluding the cause.

### Omitting the Region-Specific Special Tests

Examining range of motion without testing the ligaments, menisci, or impingement signs specific to the region. Counter by learning and performing the high-yield special tests for each joint region.

### Attributing Back Pain to a Benign Cause Without Excluding Red Flags

Dismissing back pain as mechanical without screening for cauda equina, infection, malignancy, or cord compression. Counter by screening for the red flags (neurological deficit, sphincter disturbance, systemic features, cancer history) and escalating when present.

### Treating the Joint in Isolation From Function and the Whole Patient

Focusing on the joint without assessing gait, activities of daily living, and the patient's goals. Counter by integrating a functional assessment and treating the patient's function and context, not just the joint.

### Failing to Recognize Acute Limb Ischemia and Deep Vein Thrombosis

Attributing a painful swollen limb to a sprain or cellulitis while missing the six Ps of ischemia or the signs of thrombosis. Counter by assessing pulses, perfusion, and swelling, and escalating when ischemia or thrombosis is possible.

## Self-Check

- Did I follow the look, feel, move, special tests framework for the joint, and did I examine the contralateral side first to establish the baseline?
- Did I compare the symptomatic with the asymptomatic side at every step for range of motion, swelling, warmth, tenderness, stability, and strength, recording asymmetry explicitly?
- Did I distinguish the inflammatory, mechanical, crystal, and septic presentations, and did I aspirate the acutely hot single joint to exclude septic arthritis?
- Did I perform the region-specific special tests (rotator cuff and impingement for the shoulder; ligament and meniscal tests for the knee; nerve compression tests for the hand; neurological screen for the spine)?
- Did I actively screen for the musculoskeletal red flags — septic arthritis, cauda equina, cord compression, infection or malignancy, acute limb ischemia, and deep vein thrombosis — and escalate when present?
- Did I assess gait, activities of daily living, grip strength, and the functional and whole-person impact of the musculoskeletal problem?
- Did I integrate the findings into a plan that addresses the patient's pain, function, and goals, and did I arrange imaging, aspiration, or referral where the red flags or the diagnostic uncertainty demanded it?
- This guidance is educational and informational only and is not medical advice; a qualified healthcare professional must be consulted for actual clinical decisions; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
