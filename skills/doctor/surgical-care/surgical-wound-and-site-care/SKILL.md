# Surgical Wound and Site Care

Surgical wound care looks deceptively simple — clean it, dress it, watch it heal — and that appearance is precisely where the danger lies. Most wound decisions are low-stakes, but a small minority are limb- or life-threatening, and the clinician's job is to never miss that minority while not over-treating the routine majority. The two catastrophic failures are, first, treating a necrotizing soft tissue infection or fascial dehiscence as a minor wound issue and watching it progress to sepsis or evisceration, and second, occluding an infected or ischemic wound under an occlusive dressing where it cannot be seen. Between these extremes sits the daily judgment of distinguishing normal healing from infection, choosing the right dressing for the right wound phase, deciding when to debride versus when to wait, and knowing when a wound has crossed the line that requires surgical or specialist involvement. This skill covers the structured assessment of surgical wounds, recognition of infection and dehiscence, dressing and debridement decisions, and the escalation thresholds that protect patients from catastrophic misses.

## Core Rules

### Assess the Wound Systematically at Every Encounter

A wound assessment is not a glance at the dressing — it is a structured examination. Inspect the wound only after clean technique and document: location and size (length, width, depth), wound bed appearance (granulation, slough, eschar, necrosis, exposed tendon or bone), exudate (serous, sanguineous, serosanguineous, purulent; volume and odor), periwound skin (erythema, induration, warmth, maceration, ecchymosis), and the integrity of closure (intact sutures or staples, dehiscence, evisceration). Assess the patient systemically: fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis, and pain out of proportion are systemic signs that change a local wound into a surgical emergency. A wound that is not measured and described at each encounter cannot be tracked, and a wound that is not tracked cannot be recognized as failing. Make the assessment reproducible so that the next clinician can compare.

### Classify the Wound by Healing Intention and Phase

Wound management depends on whether the wound is healing by primary intention (closed surgically, expected to heal without opening), secondary intention (left open to granulate), or tertiary intention (delayed primary closure after a period of open management). Each requires different care. Then classify the phase of healing: hemostasis and inflammation (days 0-4), proliferation (granulation, angiogenesis, epithelialization, days 4-21), and remodeling (weeks to months). Match the intervention to the phase. A clean, granulating wound needs a moist environment and protection; a wound with necrotic eschar needs debridement; a wound with heavy exudate needs absorption; a wound that is infected needs source control and antibiotics, not just a dressing change. Applying the wrong intervention for the phase — for example, occluding a necrotic infected wound — causes harm.

### Recognize Surgical Site Infection and Grade Its Severity

Surgical site infection is the most common postoperative complication and exists on a spectrum that determines management. Superficial incisional SSI involves skin and subcutaneous tissue within 30 days, presenting with erythema, warmth, pain, and purulent drainage; management is usually opening the wound, local care, and antibiotics when systemic signs are present. Deep incisional SSI involves fascial or muscle layers within 30 days (or up to one year with an implant), presenting with deeper induration, severe pain, and systemic signs; it often requires imaging, drainage, and antibiotics. Organ-space SSI involves any part of the anatomy opened or manipulated, presenting with abscess, peritonitis, or sepsis, and almost always requires drainage or reoperation. Grade the infection explicitly, because the grade changes management — a superficial SSI managed like a deep one leads to overtreatment and delay of recovery, while a deep or organ-space SSI managed like a superficial one leads to sepsis.

### Treat Dehiscence and Evisceration as Emergencies

Wound dehiscence — separation of the fascial layers — and evisceration — protrusion of abdominal contents through the wound — are surgical emergencies. Evisceration is immediately life-threatening: cover the exposed viscera with sterile saline-soaked gauze, keep the patient nil by mouth, do not attempt to push contents back, place the patient supine with knees flexed, and call the surgical team immediately for return to the operating room. Dehiscence without evisceration is still an emergency: it indicates fascial failure, often from infection, ischemia, malnutrition, or increased intra-abdominal pressure, and requires urgent surgical assessment. A wound that suddenly drains copious serosanguineous fluid ("pink discharge") may be the first sign of dehiscence — do not dismiss it as expected drainage. Any sudden separation of a closed wound, particularly with a popping sensation reported by the patient, mandates immediate surgical evaluation.

### Never Miss Necrotizing Soft Tissue Infection

Necrotizing fasciitis and related necrotizing soft tissue infections are the single most dangerous wound-related diagnosis, and they are missed because early findings overlap with cellulitis. Suspect necrotizing infection when a wound or adjacent skin shows pain out of proportion to visible findings, rapidly spreading erythema, edema beyond the redness, grayish or dusky discoloration, bullae, crepitus or gas on imaging, skin anesthesia (due to cutaneous nerve necrosis), or systemic toxicity out of proportion to local findings. The LRINEC score helps but has limited sensitivity — a low score does not exclude necrotizing infection. When suspicion is high, do not wait for laboratory or imaging confirmation: obtain surgical consultation immediately, start broad-spectrum antibiotics, resuscitate, and prepare for emergent surgical exploration and debridement. Mortality rises by the hour with delayed debridement. Erring on the side of surgical assessment is always correct; a negative exploration is acceptable, a missed necrotizing infection is not.

### Match the Dressing to the Wound Phase and Exudate

Dressing choice is a clinical decision driven by wound characteristics, not habit. The core principle is moist wound healing: a granulating wound heals faster in a moist, protected environment, but an infected or heavily exuding wound must not be occluded. For a clean, dry, closed wound, a simple semi-occlusive dressing for 24-48 hours is usually sufficient. For a granulating open wound with minimal exudate, use a hydrogel or non-adherent dressing to maintain moisture. For moderate to heavy exudate, use alginates, foams, or hydrofibers that absorb and wick. For a wound with slough or necrosis, prioritize debridement rather than covering it. For a clean, large, complex wound, negative-pressure wound therapy (wound VAC) can accelerate granulation and manage exudate but is contraindicated in untreated infection, malignancy in the wound, untreated osteomyelitis, non-enteric unexplored fistulas, and necrotic tissue with eschar. Match the dressing to the wound, reassess at each change, and change the plan when the wound phase changes.

### Decide on Debridement Based on Tissue Viability and Infection

Debridement removes necrotic, infected, or non-viable tissue to convert a chronic, contaminated wound into a clean, healing one. Indications include necrotic eschar, slough that impedes granulation, frank infection with necrotic tissue, and wounds failing to progress. Methods include surgical (sharp) debridement for extensive or deep necrosis, enzymatic debridement for patients who cannot tolerate sharp debridement, autolytic debridement with moisture-retentive dressings for stable patients with modest necrosis, and mechanical debridement for selective cases. Sharp debridement of ischemic tissue in a poorly perfused limb can worsen the wound and must be preceded by vascular assessment — debriding a dry, stable eschar on an ischemic heel, for example, can convert a stable wound into a non-healing one. Before debridement, assess vascular supply, coagulation status, and the depth of necrosis; when in doubt, involve surgery or a wound-care specialist.

### Optimize Systemic Factors That Block Healing

Local wound care fails when systemic factors oppose healing. Assess and address malnutrition (serum albumin and prealbumin, weight loss, and protein intake), uncontrolled diabetes (hyperglycemia impairs leukocyte function and collagen synthesis), anemia and hypoxia (tissue oxygen delivery is essential), smoking (vasoconstriction and impaired healing), edema and venous or lymphatic insufficiency, immunosuppression, and ongoing infection elsewhere. A wound that is not healing despite correct local care is almost always telling you that a systemic factor is unaddressed. Investigate rather than escalating local therapy indefinitely, and involve dietetics, endocrinology, vascular surgery, or other specialists as the picture demands.

### Know When to Involve Surgery, Wound Care, and Specialists

Escalation thresholds for wounds are explicit. Involve the operating surgeon immediately for suspected dehiscence or evisceration, for any wound with signs of necrotizing infection, for deep or organ-space SSI, for wounds with exposed vital structures (vessel, nerve, tendon, bone, joint, viscera), and for wounds failing to progress despite correct care. Involve a wound-care specialist or service for complex chronic wounds, large wounds requiring advanced dressings or negative-pressure therapy, wounds needing ongoing debridement, and pressure injuries requiring staging and prevention protocols. Involve vascular surgery for ischemic wounds or wounds with absent pulses, infectious disease for severe or atypical infection, and plastic surgery for wounds likely to need flap or graft reconstruction. Do not manage a deteriorating wound in isolation — escalation is the rule, not the exception, when the wound is not progressing or when red flags appear.

## Common Traps

### Occluding an Infected or Ischemic Wound

Covering a wound with an occlusive dressing that hides deterioration. Counter by inspecting the wound directly at each encounter and avoiding occlusion of infected or necrotic wounds until source control is achieved.

### Dismissing Pain Out of Proportion as Normal Healing

Attributing severe wound pain to expected postoperative recovery when it is the first sign of necrotizing infection. Counter by treating pain out of proportion, with systemic toxicity or rapid spread, as a surgical emergency.

### Treating All SSI the Same

Managing a deep or organ-space infection with local wound care alone. Counter by grading the SSI explicitly (superficial, deep, organ-space) and escalating imaging, drainage, and surgery for deeper infections.

### Relying on the LRINEC Score to Exclude Necrotizing Infection

Being reassured by a low LRINEC score in a clinically concerning wound. Counter by recognizing the score's limited sensitivity and seeking surgical assessment when clinical suspicion is high regardless of the score.

### Debriding Ischemic Eschar Without Vascular Assessment

Sharp-debriding a dry, stable eschar on a poorly perfused limb and creating a non-healing wound. Counter by assessing vascular supply first and involving vascular surgery when perfusion is inadequate.

### Choosing Dressings by Habit Rather Than Wound Phase

Applying the same dressing regardless of exudate, necrosis, or infection. Counter by matching the dressing to the wound phase and exudate and reassessing at each change.

### Forgetting Systemic Factors in a Non-Healing Wound

Escalating local therapy indefinitely without addressing diabetes, malnutrition, anemia, smoking, or edema. Counter by investigating and optimizing systemic factors when a wound fails to progress despite correct local care.

### Watching a Dehiscing Wound Instead of Escalating

Observing a wound that has separated or drained copious serosanguineous fluid. Counter by treating dehiscence and evisceration as surgical emergencies and escalating immediately.

## Self-Check

- Have I performed and documented a structured wound assessment — size, bed, exudate, periwound skin, closure integrity, and systemic signs — at this encounter?
- Have I classified the wound by healing intention and phase and matched my intervention to that phase?
- Have I graded any surgical site infection as superficial, deep, or organ-space, and escalated deeper infections for imaging, drainage, or surgery?
- Have I recognized and escalated immediately for dehiscence, evisceration, or any wound with pain out of proportion, rapid spread, crepitus, dusky discoloration, or systemic toxicity suggesting necrotizing infection?
- Have I matched the dressing to the wound phase and exudate, and avoided occluding infected or necrotic wounds?
- Have I assessed vascular supply before debridement and avoided sharp debridement of stable ischemic eschar?
- Have I investigated and addressed systemic factors — diabetes, malnutrition, anemia, smoking, edema, immunosuppression — when a wound is not progressing?
- Have I escalated to surgery, wound care, vascular surgery, infectious disease, or plastic surgery at the appropriate thresholds rather than managing a complex or deteriorating wound in isolation?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as evisceration, necrotizing infection, deep or organ-space SSI, or exposed vital structures appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
