# Postoperative Complication Recognition and Management

The postoperative period is a narrow window in which the line between expected recovery and life-threatening deterioration is thin, and the most dangerous error is not missing a rare complication — it is normalizing abnormal vital signs because "the patient just had surgery." Postoperative patients are expected to have pain, ileus, mild fever in the first 48 hours, and transient fluid shifts. But that same expectation creates a cognitive trap: tachycardia is attributed to pain, hypotension to anesthesia, fever to atelectasis, and oliguria to dehydration, when in fact each may be the first sign of hemorrhage, sepsis, leak, or pulmonary embolism. The disciplined postoperative clinician treats every deviation from the expected trajectory as signal until proven otherwise, knows the time windows in which specific complications emerge, and has a low threshold to return to the operating room or escalate to the surgical team when the picture does not resolve. This skill covers the structured recognition of early and late complications, the differentiation of expected from deteriorating postoperative courses, and the decision points for reoperation and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Know the Time Windows of Postoperative Complications

Postoperative complications cluster in predictable time windows, and knowing these windows sharpens suspicion. In the first 24 hours, the dominant threats are hemorrhage (surgical bleeding), airway compromise, residual anesthesia effects, and early fluid or electrolyte derangement. From 24 to 72 hours, the concerns shift to fever from atelectasis versus early infection, acute kidney injury, urinary retention, and early signs of leak or ischemia. From days 3 to 7, surgical site infection, anastomotic leak, pneumonia, line infection, and DVT or PE emerge. Beyond the first week, wound dehiscence, late leak, abscess, ileus progressing to obstruction, and persistent fever dominate. Anchor your differential to the postoperative day, but never exclude a complication solely because it is "too early" or "too late" — atypical timing is itself a red flag that demands escalation.

### Define and Track the Expected Trajectory Explicitly

Before you can recognize deterioration, you must define the expected trajectory for this patient and this operation. A healthy patient after an uncomplicated laparoscopic cholecystectomy should be ambulating, tolerating oral intake, and producing urine by the evening of surgery. A patient after an open bowel resection has a different expected trajectory. Write down the expected milestones: vital sign trends, pain control, intake and output, ambulation, flatus, and diet advancement. Then track the patient against that trajectory at every assessment. The single most useful question is not "is the patient sick?" but "is the patient improving as expected?" A patient who is not improving, or who is plateauing at a worse-than-expected baseline, is deteriorating until proven otherwise.

### Interpret Vital Sign Deviations as Signal, Not Noise

Postoperative vital sign changes are often dismissed as expected, yet they are the earliest markers of deterioration. Unexplained tachycardia is hemorrhage, sepsis, pain, or pulmonary embolism until proven otherwise — never assume pain alone without reassessing after analgesia. Hypotension that does not respond to fluid resuscitation suggests ongoing bleeding, sepsis, or cardiogenic causes. Tachypnea and hypoxia point to atelectasis initially but must raise PE, pneumonia, fluid overload, or respiratory depression from opioids. Fever in the first 48 hours is commonly atelectasis, but fever beyond 72 hours, or high fever with rigors, demands investigation for infection. Oliguria reflects hypovolemia, bleeding, acute kidney injury, or obstruction. Use an early warning score (MEWS, NEWS) systematically, and treat any trigger as a prompt to reassess the whole patient, not to silence an alarm.

### Distinguish Bleeding From Expected Postoperative Drainage

Postoperative bleeding is a leading cause of return to the operating room and is missed most often when drainage is attributed to expected ooze. Assess the volume, rate, and character of drain output and wound drainage: bright red blood that is increasing, large-volume output, or output that does not diminish over hours suggests active hemorrhage. Combine drain assessment with hemodynamics (tachycardia, hypotension), falling hemoglobin or hematocrit, and abdominal or wound distension. Occult bleeding may present as unexplained tachycardia and hypotension with a normal initial hemoglobin that then drops sharply — hemoglobin lags behind acute hemorrhage. Establish a trend with serial hemoglobin checks rather than relying on a single value. When bleeding is suspected, establish large-bore access, check coagulation and platelets, type and crossmatch, reverse any anticoagulation, and escalate to the surgeon immediately — the threshold to return to the operating room for hemorrhage is low.

### Recognize Anastomotic Leak and Postoperative Ischemia Early

Anastomotic leak and bowel ischemia are among the most lethal postoperative complications because they present subtly and progress to sepsis and multi-organ failure. After gastrointestinal surgery, any patient with new or worsening abdominal pain, fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis (or leukopenia), oliguria, or hemodynamic instability within the first two weeks must be assumed to have a leak or ischemia until proven otherwise. A patient who was improving and then deteriorates is a leak until ruled out. Do not attribute postoperative tachycardia and fever to "normal postoperative course" in a patient with a fresh anastomosis. Obtain CT with intravenous and often water-soluble oral contrast, obtain cultures if septic, start broad-spectrum antibiotics, resuscitate, and escalate to the surgical team urgently — many leaks require reoperation or drainage, and delay is fatal.

### Differentiate Ileus From Mechanical Obstruction

Postoperative ileus is expected and usually resolves within 3 to 5 days, but persistent or worsening abdominal distension, nausea, vomiting, and failure to pass flatus beyond that window, or a patient who initially passed flatus and then stops, raises the question of mechanical obstruction. Distinguishing the two requires clinical judgment supported by imaging: ileus typically produces diffuse, mild distension with minimal pain and absent or hypoactive bowel sounds, while obstruction produces colicky pain, distension, and hyperactive or high-pitched tinkling sounds early, then silence. Obtain an upright abdominal radiograph or CT with oral contrast. A nasogastric tube decompresses and may be diagnostic and therapeutic. Escalate to surgery for complete obstruction, signs of strangulation (fever, severe pain, leukocytosis, lactic acidosis), or failure to resolve with conservative management — strangulation is a surgical emergency.

### Apply Active VTE Prophylaxis and Recognize DVT and PE

Every postoperative patient must be assessed for VTE risk and prescribed appropriate prophylaxis unless contraindicated — mechanical (sequential compression devices) and pharmacologic (low-molecular-weight heparin or unfractionated heparin) prophylaxis reduce DVT and PE, which remain leading causes of postoperative death. Hold pharmacologic prophylaxis only for active bleeding or very high bleeding risk, and document the reason. Recognize DVT through unilateral leg swelling, calf tenderness, and unexplained fever or tachycardia; confirm with compression ultrasound. Suspect PE in any postoperative patient with new dyspnea, tachycardia, hypoxia, pleuritic chest pain, hemoptysis, or syncope — and especially in any deterioration without another explanation. Wells score and D-dimer have limited utility postoperatively; CT pulmonary angiography is usually required. A postoperative patient who suddenly deteriorates is PE until proven otherwise, and escalation to the surgical and medical team must be immediate.

### Assess the Wound at Every Encounter

The surgical wound is a window into the patient's recovery and must be inspected, not assumed. Erythema, increasing pain, purulent drainage, dehiscence, crepitus, and systemic signs of infection indicate surgical site infection or, in the worst case, necrotizing infection. Distinguish superficial SSI (manageable with opening the wound and antibiotics) from deep or organ-space infection (requiring imaging, drainage, and often reoperation). Any wound with crepitus, rapidly spreading erythema, severe pain out of proportion, blistering, or systemic toxicity is a surgical emergency suggesting necrotizing fasciitis — escalate immediately, do not wait for laboratory confirmation. See the wound-care skill for detailed wound assessment, but never defer wound inspection because the dressing "looks fine."

### Define Explicit Escalation and Reoperation Thresholds

For every postoperative patient, define in advance the findings that mandate escalation to the surgical team or return to the operating room: hemodynamic instability despite resuscitation, suspected or confirmed hemorrhage, suspected leak or ischemia, signs of strangulation or peritonitis, sepsis without a source, respiratory failure, or any deterioration without explanation. Communicate escalation with a structured handoff (SBAR), document it, and do not accept reassurance without a concrete plan and a timeframe for reassessment. The threshold to return to the operating room for hemorrhage, leak, or ischemia is deliberately low; a negative exploration is far preferable to a delayed one.

## Common Traps

### Normalizing Tachycardia and Hypotension as Expected

Attributing tachycardia to pain and hypotension to anesthesia without reassessing after intervention. Counter by treating unexplained tachycardia and hypotension as hemorrhage or sepsis until proven otherwise and reassessing after analgesia and fluid.

### Dismissing Fever as Atelectasis Beyond 72 Hours

Excusing persistent or high fever with "the patient is postoperative." Counter by investigating any fever beyond 72 hours, fever with rigors, or fever with hypotension for infection, leak, or PE.

### Relying on a Single Hemoglobin to Exclude Bleeding

Accepting a normal initial hemoglobin in a bleeding patient. Counter by tracking serial hemoglobin and hematocrit trends and recognizing that hemoglobin lags behind acute hemorrhage.

### Attributing Abdominal Deterioration to Ileus in a Patient With a Fresh Anastomosis

Calling worsening pain and tachycardia "ileus" after bowel surgery. Counter by assuming leak or ischemia in any GI-surgery patient who deteriorates and obtaining CT and surgical review urgently.

### Forgetting VTE Prophylaxis or Withholding It Without Cause

Omitting pharmacologic prophylaxis or holding it indefinitely without active bleeding. Counter by assessing VTE risk, prescribing prophylaxis unless contraindicated, and documenting any hold.

### Deferring Wound Inspection

Assuming the wound is fine because the dressing is intact. Counter by inspecting the wound at every encounter and escalating crepitus, spreading erythema, or pain out of proportion as necrotizing infection.

### Delaying Reoperation for Hemorrhage or Leak

Waiting for definitive imaging or laboratory confirmation before escalating. Counter by setting low thresholds for surgical review and return to the operating room when hemodynamics, abdomen, or trajectory are concerning.

### Accepting Reassurance Without a Concrete Plan

Accepting "the patient is fine" from a verbal handoff without a plan and timeframe. Counter by demanding a structured handoff, a documented plan, and a defined reassessment interval.

## Self-Check

- Have I anchored my differential to the postoperative day while still considering atypical timing?
- Have I defined and documented the expected trajectory for this patient and operation, and tracked the patient against it?
- Have I treated unexplained tachycardia, hypotension, fever, oliguria, or hypoxia as signal rather than expected postoperative noise?
- For suspected bleeding, have I tracked serial hemoglobin trends, assessed drains and wound, and escalated to surgery with a low threshold for reoperation?
- For any GI-surgery patient who deteriorates, have I assumed leak or ischemia and obtained CT, cultures, antibiotics, and urgent surgical review?
- Have I differentiated ileus from mechanical obstruction and escalated for signs of strangulation or failure to resolve?
- Have I prescribed VTE prophylaxis unless contraindicated, and considered PE in any unexplained deterioration?
- Have I inspected the wound and escalated crepitus, spreading erythema, or pain out of proportion as a surgical emergency?
- Have I defined explicit escalation and reoperation thresholds and communicated them with a structured, documented handoff?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as hemodynamic instability, suspected hemorrhage, anastomotic leak, necrotizing infection, or suspected PE appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
