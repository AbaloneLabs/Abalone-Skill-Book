# Preoperative Assessment and Risk Stratification

Preoperative assessment is not a checkbox exercise to "clear" a patient for surgery — it is a structured estimation of how likely a patient is to suffer a serious perioperative event and what can be done to lower that probability before the knife touches skin. The most common failure is not missing a rare disease; it is treating low-risk patients as if they were high-risk (chasing unnecessary cardiac testing that delays surgery) while simultaneously underestimating the truly fragile patient who needs optimization. A second, equally dangerous failure is proceeding with elective surgery on a patient whose comorbidities are poorly controlled because "the surgeon wants to operate." Risk stratification exists to make the tradeoff between surgical benefit and perioperative harm explicit, shared, and documented. This skill covers the disciplined process of assessing cardiac, pulmonary, bleeding, and thrombotic risk, optimizing modifiable factors, deciding when additional workup changes management, and knowing when to escalate to anesthesiology, cardiology, or the operating surgeon.

## Core Rules

### Start With Surgical Risk, Not Patient Risk

Before assessing the patient, classify the surgery itself. Cardiac perioperative risk is driven first by the procedure: low-risk surgery (cataract, endoscopy, superficial procedures, breast surgery) carries less than 1% cardiac event risk and rarely requires cardiac workup regardless of patient risk factors. Intermediate-risk surgery (intraperitoneal, intrathoracic, orthopedic, head and neck, prostate) carries roughly 1-5% risk. High-risk surgery (aortic and major vascular, prolonged thoracic, anticipated large fluid shifts) carries greater than 5% risk and demands the most rigorous cardiac evaluation. A common error is applying high-risk workup pathways to patients having low-risk surgery — this wastes time, generates false positives, and delays care without changing outcomes. Always pair patient risk with surgical risk; the combination, not either alone, determines the workup.

### Apply the Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI) Explicitly

For patients undergoing non-cardiac surgery who require cardiac risk stratification, apply the Revised Cardiac Risk Index (Lee index) deliberately rather than estimating by gestalt. The RCRI assigns one point each for high-risk surgery, history of ischemic heart disease, history of congestive heart failure, history of cerebrovascular disease, insulin-dependent diabetes, and renal insufficiency (creatinine greater than 2 mg/dL). An RCRI of 0 or 1 generally indicates low risk and supports proceeding without further noninvasive testing in most cases; an RCRI of 2 or higher, particularly combined with poor functional capacity, warrants consideration of stress testing — but only if the result would change management. Document the score explicitly; "low cardiac risk per RCRI 1" is far stronger than "patient appears fit for surgery."

### Assess Functional Capacity Before Ordering Stress Tests

Functional capacity, measured in metabolic equivalents (METs), is one of the most powerful and underused risk modifiers. The ability to perform activities requiring 4 METs or greater (climbing a flight of stairs, walking up a hill, brisk walking, heavy housework) without symptoms correlates strongly with low perioperative risk and often obviates the need for further cardiac testing regardless of clinical risk factors. When a patient reports good, asymptomatic functional capacity, additional noninvasive cardiac testing rarely changes management. Conversely, poor or indeterminate functional capacity in a patient with elevated clinical risk is a trigger for further evaluation. Do not order a stress test reflexively; first ask whether the patient can exercise, and whether the result will alter the perioperative plan.

### Identify and Optimize Modifiable Risk Factors Before Elective Surgery

Preoperative assessment is only valuable if it leads to optimization. The modifiable factors with the strongest evidence are smoking cessation (ideally four or more weeks preoperatively to reduce pulmonary and wound complications), glycemic control in diabetes (target hemoglobin A1c generally below 8% for elective surgery, recognizing that extremes of control carry risk), hypertension control (avoid profound hypotension but treat uncontrolled severe hypertension), anemia evaluation and correction when feasible, and alcohol use disorder treatment to prevent withdrawal. Malnutrition and obesity each independently increase complication risk and may warrant preoperative nutrition support or weight optimization for elective procedures. For each modifiable factor, weigh the delay required for optimization against the urgency of surgery — in emergency surgery, optimization happens intraoperatively and postoperatively, not before.

### Apply Pulmonary Risk Stratification Separately and Deliberately

Pulmonary complications (atelectasis, pneumonia, respiratory failure, prolonged ventilation) are as common and as morbid as cardiac complications after non-cardiac surgery, yet they are routinely under-assessed. Risk factors include upper abdominal or thoracic incision, prolonged surgery (greater than 3 hours), general anesthesia, age over 60, COPD, current smoking, heart failure, functional dependence, and poor nutritional status. Optimize pulmonary risk with smoking cessation, treat obstructive lung disease aggressively with bronchodilators and inhaled steroids, use incentive spirometry and chest physiotherapy postoperatively, consider regional anesthesia where feasible, and minimize nasogastric tube use. A patient with poorly controlled COPD or active wheeze is not ready for elective surgery; escalate to pulmonology or anesthesia rather than proceeding.

### Balance Bleeding and Thrombosis Risk Explicitly

Every preoperative patient sits on a spectrum between bleeding and thrombosis, and the assessment must make this balance explicit rather than implicit. For patients on anticoagulants or antiplatelets, determine the indication (mechanical valve, atrial fibrillation, recent stent, venous thromboembolism), the thrombotic risk if held, and the bleeding risk of the procedure. For patients not on anticoagulation, assess baseline VTE risk (Caprini or similar) to plan prophylaxis. Do not default to "stop everything a week before" — a patient with a mechanical mitral valve or recent coronary stent who has anticoagulation held inappropriately may suffer a catastrophic thrombotic event. Conversely, continuing anticoagulation for a high-bleeding-risk procedure can cause fatal hemorrhage. This balance is a high-stakes decision that often requires cardiology, hematology, or the anesthesiologist's input.

### Decide on Further Workup by Asking Whether It Will Change Management

Preoperative testing should be guided by history and physical examination, not by reflex panels. Routine ECG is reasonable for patients with cardiac risk factors or undergoing intermediate-to-high-risk surgery, but is not required for healthy asymptomatic patients having low-risk surgery. Chest radiography and laboratory panels should be ordered only when results will change management. Stress testing, echocardiography, and pulmonary function tests are indicated by specific risk factors and poor functional capacity, not by age alone or by protocol. Before ordering any test, answer the question: if this test is abnormal, what will I do differently? If the answer is "nothing" or "the surgery is urgent regardless," the test does not add value and may add harm through false positives, delays, and downstream invasive procedures.

### Know When to Escalate to Anesthesiology, Cardiology, and the Surgeon

Preoperative assessment is a team activity, not a solo clearance. Escalate to anesthesiology early for patients with difficult airway, severe cardiopulmonary disease, complex medication regimens, or anticipated complex anesthesia. Escalate to cardiology for recent stent placement, unstable cardiac symptoms, severe valvular disease (particularly severe aortic stenosis), significant arrhythmia, or when stress testing is being considered in a high-risk patient. Escalate to the operating surgeon when optimization will delay surgery, when risk is high enough that the risk-benefit balance is questionable, or when the patient may benefit from a less invasive alternative. Document the consultation and its conclusions; verbal handoffs are insufficient. When in doubt, a preoperative anesthesia consultation is almost always appropriate — it is the anesthesiologist, not the assessor, who will manage the patient through the perioperative period.

### Document the Risk Estimate, the Optimization, and the Shared Decision

The preoperative note should capture the surgical risk class, the cardiac risk score (RCRI), functional capacity, pulmonary risk factors, medication plan, the workup performed and why, the residual risk, and the shared decision to proceed. A note that states only "cleared for surgery" is clinically and medicolegally weak. State the estimated risk, the optimization performed, the patient's informed understanding of that risk, and the contingency plans. This documentation protects the patient, the team, and the assessor.

## Common Traps

### Treating Low-Risk Surgery Like High-Risk Surgery

Applying aggressive cardiac workup pathways to patients having cataract, endoscopy, or superficial surgery. Counter by classifying surgical risk first and recognizing that low-risk surgery rarely justifies cardiac testing regardless of patient factors.

### Ordering Stress Testing Reflexively

Ordering a stress test for every elderly or hypertensive patient without asking whether the result will change management. Counter by first assessing functional capacity and clinical risk, and ordering noninvasive testing only when it will alter the perioperative plan.

### Ignoring Functional Capacity

Estimating risk from comorbidity lists alone without asking whether the patient can climb a flight of stairs. Counter by explicitly assessing METs, because good functional capacity often overrides clinical risk factors.

### Underestimating Pulmonary Risk

Focusing exclusively on the heart and forgetting that pulmonary complications are equally common and morbid. Counter by applying pulmonary risk stratification separately and optimizing obstructive lung disease before elective surgery.

### Holding Anticoagulation Without Considering Thrombotic Risk

Defaulting to stopping anticoagulants or antiplatelets without weighing the indication. Counter by identifying the thrombotic indication (mechanical valve, recent stent, recent VTE) and bridging when thrombotic risk is high.

### Proceeding With Elective Surgery on Poorly Controlled Comorbidity

Operating on a patient with uncontrolled diabetes, active wheeze, or recent cardiac symptoms because the surgeon is ready. Counter by treating optimization as a precondition for elective surgery and escalating when the team pressures to proceed prematurely.

### Relying on Verbal Handoffs Instead of Documentation

Clearing a patient verbally without documenting the risk estimate, optimization, and shared decision. Counter by writing the risk class, RCRI, functional capacity, medication plan, and contingency in the record.

### Over-Testing Healthy Patients

Ordering routine labs, ECG, and chest radiography on every patient regardless of need. Counter by ordering tests only when history, examination, or the planned surgery indicates that results will change management.

## Self-Check

- Have I classified the surgical risk (low, intermediate, high) before assessing patient-specific risk?
- Have I calculated and documented an explicit cardiac risk score (RCRI) rather than estimating by gestalt?
- Have I assessed and documented functional capacity in METs before deciding whether stress testing is needed?
- Have I stratified pulmonary risk separately and optimized obstructive lung disease, smoking, and nutrition where feasible?
- Have I made the bleeding-versus-thrombosis balance explicit for any patient on anticoagulants or antiplatelets, and sought specialist input for high-risk indications such as mechanical valves or recent stents?
- For every test I ordered, can I state what I will do differently if it is abnormal?
- Have I escalated to anesthesiology, cardiology, or the surgeon for patients with difficult airway, severe cardiopulmonary disease, complex medications, or questionable risk-benefit balance?
- Have I documented the risk estimate, the optimization performed, and the shared decision to proceed, rather than writing only "cleared for surgery"?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags such as unstable cardiac symptoms, severe valvular disease, or poor functional capacity in a high-risk patient appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
