# Complex and Frequent Attender Management

A small fraction of patients account for a disproportionate share of primary care encounters, emergency attendances, and admissions. These complex and frequent attenders are rarely complex because of a single disease; they are complex because multimorbidity, polypharmacy, fragmented care across specialists, unmet mental health needs, social adversity, and health behaviors interact and amplify one another. The frequent attender is often signaling something the system has not yet heard — uncontrolled pain, untreated anxiety or depression, social isolation, a caregiving collapse, an unspoken fear, or a serious diagnosis that has not yet declared itself. The judgment problem is not "how do I manage this visit?" but "what is the underlying pattern driving these visits, and what coordinated response would actually change the trajectory?" Clinicians get into trouble when they treat each frequent attendance as an isolated complaint, when frustration or compassion fatigue erodes diagnostic vigilance, when polypharmacy accumulates without deprescribing, or when unmet psychosocial needs are medicalized into unnecessary investigations and referrals. This skill covers complex care management, recognizing frequent-attender patterns, surfacing unmet biopsychosocial needs, coordinated care planning, avoiding fragmentation and burnout, and when to escalate to care management or social work.

## Core Rules

### Recognize the Frequent Attender as a Pattern, Not a Series of Visits

Frequent attendance is itself a clinical sign. Track attendance patterns across primary care, urgent care, and the emergency department, and treat a rising or sustained pattern as a signal that demands a coordinated response rather than episodic management. Ask what is driving the pattern: is it uncontrolled physical symptoms, unmet mental health need, social crisis, a serious undiagnosed condition, health anxiety, fragmented care with no integrator, or a breakdown in access to the right service? The same patient returning repeatedly is telling you that the current plan is not working. Respond by convening a review of the whole patient rather than continuing to triage individual complaints. Document the pattern recognition so the team shares the picture.

### Search for the Unmet Biopsychosocial Need Beneath the Presenting Complaint

In complex and frequent attenders, the presenting complaint is frequently not the real problem. A patient returning with recurrent chest pain, abdominal pain, or nonspecific symptoms may have untreated anxiety or depression, domestic violence, bereavement, addiction, social isolation, financial crisis, or a serious underlying disease that has been repeatedly reassured away. Use validated screening tools for depression, anxiety, substance use, and social determinants, and create space for the patient to voice what they have not yet said. The single most useful question is often "what is worrying you the most about this?" Pursue the unmet need directly, and resist the pull to medicalize distress with investigations that will not answer it. At the same time, never let "this is probably anxiety" become a reason to miss evolving physical disease — both can be true.

### Build One Coordinated Care Plan With a Named Integrator

Complex patients accumulate specialists, medications, and parallel plans until no one holds the whole picture. The intervention that most reliably improves outcomes for high-need patients is a single coordinated care plan with a named integrator — usually the primary care clinician, often supported by a care manager. Convene (literally or virtually) the patient, their specialists, their caregivers, and the care team to agree on goals, active problems, medication decisions, and the boundaries of each clinician's role. Document the plan in a shared record, give the patient a copy, and review it at defined intervals. The plan should answer: what are we trying to achieve, who is responsible for what, what would prompt escalation, and what is the patient's own priority? A complex patient without a coordinated plan will re-present until they have one.

### Deprescribe Actively in the Setting of Polypharmacy

Multimorbidity inevitably produces polypharmacy, and polypharmacy produces adverse drug events, drug-disease interactions, falls, cognitive impairment, and hospitalization — much of it iatrogenic and preventable. Treat deprescribing as a standing duty, not a last resort. Review the medication list at every encounter with the explicit question "what can be stopped, reduced, or simplified?" Prioritize drugs with the highest burden-to-benefit ratio in this individual patient, considering life expectancy and goals of care, and taper rather than abruptly stopping where withdrawal is a risk. Reconcile medications after every transition. Recognize that every new prescription in a complex patient is a future adverse event waiting to happen, and weigh additions accordingly.

### Address Fragmentation and Closed Loops Explicitly

Complex patients fall through gaps: referrals that never close, tests that are ordered but never followed up, discharge summaries that arrive late or not at all, and recommendations that conflict. Build closed-loop processes — track referrals to attendance, track tests to result and to patient communication, and chase missing information rather than assuming no news is good news. When a complex patient attends urgently, pull the recent records from every setting before deciding, because the missing piece is often the decisive one. Fragmentation is a system problem, but the harm lands on the patient; the primary care team is best placed to close the loops.

### Maintain Diagnostic Vigilance Despite the Pattern

The most dangerous trap in frequent-attender management is diagnostic complacency — assuming the latest presentation is "the same as always" and missing a new, serious problem. Each presentation deserves at least a brief independent assessment for red flags and change from baseline, even in a patient you know well. The fact that a patient has attended ten times this year does not mean the eleventh attendance is benign; it may be the one that matters. Use the longitudinal record to detect deviation from the patient's own pattern, and lower your threshold to investigate a genuinely new feature even amid an otherwise familiar picture.

### Protect Against Burnout and Compassion Fatigue in the Team

Caring for complex, high-need patients is demanding, and frequent attenders can generate frustration, negative labeling, and compassion fatigue that degrade care for everyone. Recognize this as a system issue: distribute complex patients across the team rather than concentrating them, use multidisciplinary case conferences to share the cognitive load, debrief difficult cases, and avoid stigmatizing language in the record that can bias future care. Labeling a patient as a "frequent flyer" can become a self-fulfilling barrier to good care. Escalate to supervision, ethics, or a care management team when a patient's needs exceed what the current arrangement can meet.

### Escalate to Care Management and Social Work Deliberately

Complex care often exceeds what visit-based primary care can deliver. Escalate to a care manager when a patient needs intensive coordination, frequent contact, or transition support; to social work when unmet social needs (housing, food, benefits, safety) are driving health crises; to integrated behavioral health when mental health needs are central; and to palliative care when goals should shift toward quality of life. Escalation is not abandonment — the primary care clinician remains the integrator — but recognizing the limits of solo management is part of competent care.

## Common Traps

### Managing Each Attendance as an Isolated Complaint

Triaging the symptom of the day without recognizing the pattern driving the attendances. Counter by tracking attendance patterns and convening a whole-patient review.

### Medicalizing Distress and Missing the Unmet Need

Investigating and reassuring repeatedly while the real driver — mental health, social crisis, or undisclosed fear — goes unaddressed. Counter by screening for biopsychosocial needs and asking what worries the patient most.

### Missing New Disease Behind a Familiar Pattern

Assuming the latest presentation is "the same as always" and failing to assess for red flags or change from baseline. Counter by independently screening each presentation for new features.

### Polypharmacy Without Deprescribing

Adding medications indefinitely without reviewing for burden-to-benefit or stopping what is no longer helpful. Counter by treating deprescribing as a standing duty at every encounter.

### Open Loops and Fragmentation

Referrals, tests, and discharges that never close, leaving the patient to fall through gaps. Counter by building closed-loop tracking and chasing missing information.

### Negative Labeling and Compassion Fatigue

Stigmatizing language and team frustration that bias future care against the patient. Counter by distributing complex patients, using case conferences, and avoiding labels in the record.

### Holding Solo When Multidisciplinary Care Is Needed

Continuing visit-based management alone when care management, social work, behavioral health, or palliative care should be involved. Counter by escalating to the right team while remaining the integrator.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize this patient's attendance as a pattern and convene a whole-patient review rather than managing the complaint of the day?
- Did I actively search for the unmet biopsychosocial need driving the attendances, using validated screening and direct questioning?
- Is there a single coordinated care plan with a named integrator, documented goals, and defined roles for each clinician?
- Did I review the medication list with the explicit intent to deprescribe, weighing burden against benefit for this individual?
- Did I close the loops on referrals, tests, and discharges rather than assuming missing information meant no problem?
- Did I assess this presentation independently for red flags and change from baseline, despite the familiar pattern?
- Did I protect the team from burnout and avoid stigmatizing language that would bias future care?
- Did I escalate to care management, social work, behavioral health, or palliative care when needs exceeded visit-based primary care?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
