# Diabetes Mellitus Management and Complications

Diabetes management is a long game of individualized risk reduction, and it is easy to get wrong in both directions — overtreatment that drives hypoglycemia and undertreatment that allows silent end-organ damage. The recurring error is applying a uniform protocol to a heterogeneous disease: targets, agents, and monitoring must be matched to the person's life expectancy, comorbidities, hypoglycemia risk, capacity for self-management, and resources. This skill covers the judgment calls an agent must make when setting glycemic targets, choosing and titrating therapy, recognizing and treating acute hyperglycemic and hypoglycemic emergencies, and structuring complication surveillance — and, critically, knowing when a presentation has crossed the line from outpatient adjustment to a problem requiring endocrine or emergency involvement.

## Core Rules

### Individualize the Glycemic Target Before Choosing Therapy

Never apply a single HbA1c target to every patient. Decide the target first, then select agents that can reach it safely. For most non-pregnant adults, an HbA1c around 7% is reasonable, but tighten toward 6.5% in younger, healthier patients with short diabetes duration and no hypoglycemia, and loosen to 7.5–8% or higher in those with limited life expectancy, advanced CKD, established cardiovascular disease, frequent hypoglycemia, frailty, or cognitive impairment. Hypoglycemia is the single most dangerous consequence of tight control in vulnerable patients, and a severe event in an elderly patient can trigger falls, arrhythmia, and loss of independence. Always ask: what is the goal of treatment for this specific person — microvascular prevention, macrovascular protection, symptom relief, or avoiding hypoglycemia? Document the reasoning behind the chosen target so future clinicians understand the tradeoffs.

### Match Drug Choice to the Patient, Not to a Default Algorithm

Non-insulin agents should be selected by comorbidity and risk profile, not by habit. SGLT2 inhibitors and GLP-1 receptor agonists have proven cardiovascular and renal benefits independent of glycemic effect and are strongly preferred in patients with established atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease; prioritize these before simply intensifying sulfonylureas or pushing insulin. Metformin remains a sensible first-line for many with type 2 diabetes but dose-adjust for renal function and watch for GI intolerance and B12 deficiency with long-term use. Sulfonylureas carry real hypoglycemia risk, especially in older adults and renal impairment — use cautiously or avoid. Pioglitazone risks fluid retention, heart failure exacerbation, and fractures. Before adding any agent, reconcile it with renal and hepatic function, current medications, affordability, and the patient's ability to adhere; a perfectly chosen drug the patient cannot take or afford is the wrong choice.

### Initiate and Titrate Insulin Conservatively and Defensively

Insulin is the most error-prone therapy in diabetes. When starting basal insulin in type 2 diabetes, begin low (commonly 0.1–0.2 units/kg/day, or 10 units) and titrate gradually based on fasting glucose every few days, with explicit patient instructions on adjustment rules and hypoglycemia response. For type 1 diabetes, basal-bolus regimens or pumps are required; never manage type 1 with sliding scale insulin alone. Prandial insulin should be matched to carbohydrate intake and activity. Sliding-scale insulin as the sole strategy in inpatients is widely recognized as inferior — pair scheduled basal therapy with correctional doses instead. Always assess hypoglycemia risk before intensifying: prior severe hypoglycemia, autonomic failure, erratic meals, renal impairment, and alcohol use all raise risk. When in doubt about a complex insulin regimen, pump management, or brittle diabetes, escalate to an endocrinologist or diabetes team rather than improvising.

### Recognize and Treat Hyperglycemic Emergencies Without Delay

Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) and hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS) are time-critical. Suspect DKA in any diabetic with vomiting, abdominal pain, tachypnea, altered mental status, or unexplained hyperglycemia — and confirm with ketones (beta-hydroxybutyrate preferred), venous pH/bicarbonate, and anion gap. HHS presents with marked hyperglycemia, severe dehydration, and altered mental status, often in older type 2 patients, and may have minimal ketosis. The pillars of management are aggressive isotonic fluid resuscitation first, then intravenous insulin, and vigilant potassium replacement — insulin drives potassium intracellularly and hypokalemia is a leading cause of death during treatment. Do not give insulin before addressing volume and potassium. Use a validated protocol and monitor glucose, electrolytes, anion gap, and mental status frequently. Switch to subcutaneous insulin only after the anion gap has closed and the patient is eating. Cerebral edema is a feared complication, especially in children — avoid overly rapid osmolar correction. These patients need an inpatient or intensive care setting with endocrine input; do not attempt outpatient management.

### Treat Hypoglycemia Proactively and Prevent Recurrence

Hypoglycemia is an emergency and a warning sign, not a minor side effect. Severe hypoglycemia (requiring third-party assistance) demands immediate treatment — oral fast-acting carbohydrate if conscious, or IV dextrose or IM/SC glucagon if not — followed by rechecking glucose in 15 minutes and re-treating as needed, then a longer-acting carbohydrate to sustain recovery. Every hypoglycemic event should trigger a root-cause review: was the dose too high, was a meal missed, did activity increase, is renal function declining, did a new interaction occur? Recurrent hypoglycemia causes hypoglycemia unawareness, which dramatically raises the risk of fatal events. When a patient has had a severe event, relax the glycemic target, reduce offending agents, and arrange structured education and possibly continuous glucose monitoring. Repeated unexplained hypoglycemia warrants reassessment for adrenal insufficiency, hypothyroidism, gastroparesis, occult infection, malignancy, or renal deterioration.

### Build a Structured Complication Surveillance Program

Diabetes care is incomplete without systematic surveillance. Confirm annual screening for retinopathy via retinal photography or ophthalmology, nephropathy via urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio and eGFR, and neuropathy via foot exam including monofilament testing. Optimize blood pressure (commonly to <130/80, individualized) and lipids — statin therapy is indicated for most adults with diabetes over 40 or with additional risk factors. Reinforce foot care education and provide urgent pathways for any foot ulcer or infection, which can deteriorate to amputation within days. Ensure vaccination coverage. Address smoking cessation aggressively. Coordinate care so that results are not merely collected but acted upon — a documented abnormal albuminuria that never triggers an ACE inhibitor or ARB is a system failure.

## Common Traps

### Treating the HbA1c Number Instead of the Patient

Chasing a numeric target in a frail, elderly, or hypoglycemia-prone patient causes harm. Always weigh the burden of treatment and the risk of hypoglycemia against the diminishing benefit of tight control when life expectancy is limited or comorbidity is advanced.

### Defaulting to Sliding-Scale Insulin

Using reactive correction doses without scheduled basal therapy leads to erratic glucose and recurrent hyperglycemia. Pair scheduled insulin with correctional dosing and escalate to a structured regimen when control is poor.

### Ignoring Cardiovascular and Renal Indications for SGLT2 and GLP-1 Agents

Selecting agents solely by glucose-lowering potency misses the mortality and renal-protection benefit these classes offer in patients with heart failure, atherosclerotic disease, or CKD. Prioritize organ-protection evidence, not just HbA1c effect.

### Underestimating Hypoglycemia in Renal Impairment and the Elderly

Insulin and sulfonylureas accumulate in CKD, and older patients have blunted counterregulatory responses. A "mild" intensification can precipitate a severe, even fatal, event. Reduce doses and favor agents with low hypoglycemia risk in these groups.

### Giving Insulin Before Fluids and Potassium in DKA or HHS

Premature insulin in a volume-depleted, hypokalemic patient precipitates cardiovascular collapse. Resuscitate with fluids and correct potassium first, per protocol, in a monitored setting.

### Dismissing Recurrent Hypoglycemia as Non-Adherence

Assuming the patient "just took too much" without investigating gastroparesis, declining renal function, new interactions, or adrenal insufficiency misses reversible causes and guarantees recurrence.

### Collecting Complication Screening Results Without Acting

Ordering annual labs and eye exams that no one reviews or acts upon is surveillance theater. Build closed-loop follow-up so abnormal albuminuria, retinopathy, or foot changes trigger timely intervention.

## Self-Check

- Have I set an individualized HbA1c target that accounts for life expectancy, comorbidities, hypoglycemia risk, and patient capacity, and documented the reasoning?
- Have I selected agents based on cardiovascular, renal, and hypoglycemia profile rather than default habit, and reconciled each with renal and hepatic function?
- If insulin is involved, have I started conservatively, paired basal with correctional dosing, and given the patient clear titration and hypoglycemia instructions?
- For any suspected DKA or HHS, have I prioritized fluids first, ensured potassium is addressed before insulin, and arranged monitored inpatient or ICU care with endocrine input?
- Have I treated every hypoglycemic event as a signal, performed a root-cause review, and adjusted therapy to prevent recurrence?
- Have I organized and acted upon retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy, blood pressure, lipid, foot, and vaccination surveillance rather than merely ordering tests?
- Have I escalated to an endocrinologist or diabetes team for brittle diabetes, pump management, recurrent emergencies, pregnancy, or atypical presentations?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
