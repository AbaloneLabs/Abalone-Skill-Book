# Thyroid and Adrenal Disorders

Thyroid and adrenal disease are deceptive because the laboratory numbers often look straightforward while the clinical stakes do not. A mildly abnormal TSH in an asymptomatic patient is rarely an emergency, yet a "normal-looking" cortisol in septic shock can mask life-threatening adrenal failure, and a tachycardic, febrile hyperthyroid patient can be hours from cardiovascular collapse. The recurring failure is treating the lab value in isolation — chasing a number without interpreting it against the clinical picture, the assay's limitations, and the medications that distort it. This skill covers the disciplined interpretation of thyroid and adrenal tests, the outpatient management of common disorders, the recognition and empiric treatment of the endocrine emergencies (thyroid storm, myxedema coma, adrenal crisis), the workup of Cushing syndrome, and the critical rule of never withdrawing chronic steroids abruptly. It is written for the agent deciding when to treat, when to observe, and when to escalate to endocrinology or critical care.

## Core Rules

### Interpret Thyroid Function Tests in Clinical Context, Not in Isolation

A TSH and free T4 must be read against the patient's symptoms, age, pregnancy status, cardiac disease, and medications. A normal TSH with a slightly low free T4 is usually non-thyroidal illness, not central hypothyroidism, in a sick inpatient; do not start levothyroxine on the basis of a single abnormal value in acute illness without confirming a pattern or checking TSH repeatedly. A mildly elevated TSH with a normal free T4 is subclinical hypothyroidism — treat only when TSH is persistently above roughly 10 mIU/L, or earlier if symptomatic, pregnant, or with dyslipidemia; otherwise monitor. Amiodarone, lithium, contrast agents, biotin supplements, dopamine, and steroids all distort thyroid tests — always reconcile the medication list before acting on an unexpected result. When central hypothyroidism is possible (pituitary disease, prior radiation, severe head injury), TSH is unreliable and you must interpret free T4 directly.

### Manage Hyperthyroidism Deliberately and Watch for Storm

Overt hyperthyroidism demands a structured approach: confirm with suppressed TSH and elevated free T4 (and T3 if T3 toxicosis is suspected), identify the cause (Graves, toxic nodule, thyroiditis, exogenous hormone), and choose between antithyroid drugs, radioactive iodine, or surgery based on cause, gland size, pregnancy, eye disease, and patient preference. Start beta-blockade for symptom control (propranolol also impairs peripheral T4-to-T3 conversion), and counsel on the rare but serious risk of agranulocytosis with antithyroid drugs — any fever or sore throat on these medications is a medical emergency requiring an urgent CBC. Critically, learn the features of thyroid storm: hyperthermia, marked tachycardia or atrial fibrillation, heart failure, altered mental status, and a precipitating trigger such as infection, surgery, iodine load, or non-adherence. Storm carries high mortality and treatment must not wait for lab confirmation — give beta-blocker, antithyroid drug, then iodine an hour later, plus glucocorticoid, all while treating the precipitant in a monitored setting. Escalate to endocrinology and critical care immediately.

### Treat Hypothyroidism Conservatively and Adjust by TSH

Levothyroxine remains first-line for primary hypothyroidism. In young, otherwise healthy patients a full replacement dose can be started, but in older patients or those with coronary disease, start low (commonly 25 mcg daily) and titrate slowly to avoid precipitating angina, arrhythmia, or myocardial infarction. Recheck TSH roughly six weeks after any dose change, as equilibration takes time. Counsel patients to take levothyroxine consistently — ideally fasting, separated from calcium, iron, proton pump inhibitors, and coffee, which impair absorption. Recognize myxedema coma as the extreme presentation: hypothermia, bradycardia, hypoventilation, hyponatremia, and altered mental status, often in an elderly hypothyroid patient with a precipitant such as infection or sedatives. Treat empirically with intravenous levothyroxine (and sometimes liothyronine) plus hydrocortisone before labs return, because coexisting adrenal insufficiency can be fatal if steroids are withheld; admit to a critical care environment and warm the patient slowly.

### Evaluate Adrenal Insufficiency Before Assuming, but Treat Crisis Empirically

Adrenal insufficiency is easily missed because fatigue, hypotension, and GI symptoms are nonspecific. Suspect it in patients on chronic glucocorticoids (especially withdrawn or under stress), those with autoimmune disease, metastatic cancer, anticoagulation-related hemorrhage, tuberculosis, or recent pituitary events. A morning cortisol can screen, but interpretation depends on context — a low-to-normal cortisol in severe illness may be inappropriately low. For definitive diagnosis, an ACTH stimulation test is usually required, ideally arranged with endocrinology rather than interpreted casually. The cardinal emergency is adrenal crisis: hypotension refractory to fluids, abdominal pain, nausea, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia (in primary), and altered mental status. Do not delay treatment for confirmatory tests — draw a random cortisol and ACTH if possible, then give intravenous hydrocortisone immediately along with aggressive fluid resuscitation, and identify and treat the precipitant. Every patient with known adrenal insufficiency needs a stress-dose plan and an emergency intramuscular hydrocortisone kit for illness or surgery, plus a medical-alert identifier.

### Never Withdraw Chronic Glucocorticoids Abruptly

Prolonged glucocorticoid therapy suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, and abrupt cessation can precipitate adrenal crisis weeks to months later. Taper slowly, guided by the original dose, duration, and the underlying disease being treated, and during intercurrent illness or surgery these patients require supplemental "stress-dose" steroids because their axis cannot respond. When a patient on chronic steroids presents with hypotension, vomiting, or collapse, assume adrenal crisis and treat empirically — the cost of a dose of hydrocortisone is trivial; the cost of missing crisis is death. This is one of the clearest cases in medicine where empiric escalation is mandatory.

### Work Up Cushing Syndrome Systematically and Refer Early

Cushing syndrome is overdiagnosed from a single random cortisol and underdiagnosed when subtle. Begin with reliable first-line screening — late-night salivary cortisol, 24-hour urine free cortisol, or a low-dose dexamethasone suppression test — and use at least two abnormal results on different days before pursuing further testing, while excluding exogenous steroid use (the most common cause). Confirm the source with ACTH level, then imaging or further dynamic testing, ideally under endocrine guidance because the biochemical workup is intricate and false positives abound. Refer to endocrinology early; the differentiation of Cushing disease, adrenal causes, and ectopic ACTH requires specialized testing and often repeated evaluation.

## Common Traps

### Acting on a Single Abnormal Thyroid Test in Acute Illness

Non-thyroidal illness and many drugs distort thyroid labs. Confirm a persistent pattern before diagnosing thyroid disease or starting therapy in sick inpatients.

### Diagnosing Central Hypothyroidism Using TSH Alone

In pituitary disease, TSH can be low, normal, or even mildly elevated and misleading. Interpret free T4 directly and image the pituitary when central disease is suspected.

### Waiting for Lab Confirmation in Suspected Thyroid Storm or Adrenal Crisis

Both carry high mortality and treatment is empiric. Start beta-blockade, antithyroid drug, and steroid for storm, and hydrocortisone plus fluids for crisis, while awaiting results.

### Withholding Steroids in a Patient Who Might Have Adrenal Crisis

The risk of a single empiric dose of hydrocortisone is minimal; the risk of missing crisis is fatal. Treat first, confirm later, especially in chronic steroid users presenting with hypotension.

### Stopping Chronic Glucocorticoids Suddenly

Abrupt withdrawal precipitates adrenal crisis. Taper gradually and provide stress-dose coverage for illness and surgery.

### Overdiagnosing Cushing Syndrome From a Random Cortisol

Random cortisol is unhelpful for screening. Use late-night salivary cortisol, 24-hour urine free cortisol, or dexamethasone suppression, and exclude exogenous steroids first.

### Missing Antithyroid-Drug Agranulocytosis

Fever or sore throat in a patient on methimazole or propylthiouracil is a medical emergency — check the CBC immediately and stop the drug.

## Self-Check

- Have I interpreted thyroid tests against the clinical context, medications, and pregnancy status rather than the number alone?
- For hyperthyroidism, have I started symptom control and counselled on agranulocytosis, and recognized the features of thyroid storm that demand empiric treatment and escalation?
- For hypothyroidism, have I started levothyroxine at an age- and cardiac-appropriate dose and planned a TSH recheck in roughly six weeks?
- For suspected adrenal insufficiency, have I arranged appropriate testing but treated crisis empirically with hydrocortisone and fluids without waiting for results?
- Have I confirmed that any patient on chronic steroids has a taper plan and stress-dose coverage for illness and surgery?
- For possible Cushing syndrome, have I used validated first-line screening, excluded exogenous steroids, and referred to endocrinology for confirmation of the source?
- Have I escalated to endocrinology or critical care for thyroid storm, myxedema coma, adrenal crisis, complex thyroid disease in pregnancy, or an unclear biochemical picture?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
