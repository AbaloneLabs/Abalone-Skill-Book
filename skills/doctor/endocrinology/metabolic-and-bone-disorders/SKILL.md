# Metabolic and Bone Disorders

Metabolic bone disease and calcium disorders are fields where overtreatment and undertreatment both cause real harm, and where the easy reflex — a bisphosphonate for any low DEXA, calcium for any borderline value — produces as many problems as it solves. Osteoporosis therapy must be matched to true fracture risk, not to a T-score alone; calcium abnormalities must be interpreted against albumin, parathyroid hormone, and the clinical setting; and the rare but serious complications of bone-active drugs (osteonecrosis of the jaw, atypical femur fractures, hypocalcemia after denosumab) must be weighed before prescribing. The recurring error is treating a number rather than a person. This skill covers fracture risk assessment and therapy selection, the safe use and "drug holidays" of antiresorptive agents, the differential and management of hyper- and hypocalcemia, vitamin D repletion, and the judgment of when to involve endocrinology, rheumatology, nephrology, or oncology rather than managing alone.

## Core Rules

### Base Osteoporosis Treatment on Fracture Risk, Not the T-Score Alone

A DEXA T-score is a snapshot of bone mineral density, not a complete picture of fracture risk. Always integrate it with a validated fracture risk tool such as FRAX, which incorporates age, sex, weight, prior fracture, parental hip fracture, smoking, alcohol, glucocorticoid use, rheumatoid arthritis, secondary osteoporosis, and femoral neck density. Treat patients whose absolute fracture risk is high — typically those with a prior fragility fracture (a clinical fracture of the hip or spine, or a wrist fracture from standing height in older age), a T-score in the osteoporotic range, or a FRAX 10-year hip fracture probability above the treatment threshold. Do not treat isolated osteopenia in a young, low-risk patient solely on the number, because the absolute benefit is small and the harms of long-term therapy accumulate. Reassess risk periodically, because age and new fractures change the calculus.

### Choose the Agent by Fracture Site, Severity, and Patient Factors

Bisphosphonates are appropriate first-line for most patients with osteoporosis, but they are not interchangeable with all alternatives. Anabolic agents (teriparatide, romosozumab) are preferred for very high-risk patients — those with multiple or recent vertebral fractures, very low T-scores, or fractures while on oral therapy — because they build bone rather than merely slowing loss, and are typically followed by an antiresorptive to preserve gains. Denosumab is potent but must never be stopped without a plan, because discontinuation causes rapid bone loss and rebound vertebral fractures; transition to a bisphosphonate after stopping. For glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis, treat early and prefer agents with proven efficacy in this setting. Always address secondary causes — hyperthyroidism, hyperparathyroidism, hypogonadism, premature menopause, malabsorption, chronic liver or kidney disease, and chronic PPI or anticonvulsant use — before assuming primary osteoporosis.

### Use Drug Holidays Deliberately, Not as Routine Default

Long-term bisphosphonate use carries small but real risks of osteonecrosis of the jaw (chiefly with high-dose IV forms in oncology) and atypical subtrochanteric femur fractures. After roughly three to five years of oral therapy (or three years of IV zoledronate), reassess fracture risk: patients at lower risk can enter a "drug holiday" with monitoring, while those at high risk (recent fracture, very low T-score, ongoing glucocorticoids, or high FRAX) should continue. A drug holiday is a monitored pause, not a permanent stop — re-evaluate DEXA and bone turnover markers and resume therapy if bone density declines or fracture risk rises. Never apply a drug holiday to denosumab, where stopping without a transition causes rapid bone loss.

### Correct Calcium and Vitamin D Status Before and During Therapy

Antiresorptive and anabolic therapy assumes adequate calcium and vitamin D; treating osteoporosis without ensuring this baseline is ineffective and, with potent agents like denosumab or zoledronate in deficient patients, can precipitate symptomatic hypocalcemia. Assess 25-hydroxyvitamin D and renal function, replete vitamin D deficiency, and ensure adequate dietary calcium with supplementation only as needed. Avoid indiscriminate high-dose calcium supplementation in patients at risk of nephrolithiasis or vascular calcification; aim for total intake near recommended levels rather than supraphysiologic doses.

### Interpret Hypercalcemia by Severity, PTH, and Clinical Setting

Hypercalcemia is not a single disease. First, correct total calcium for albumin (or use ionized calcium), then categorize by parathyroid hormone: PTH-dependent causes include primary hyperparathyroidism (often mild, chronic, in postmenopausal women) and familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (a benign inherited trait that must not be operated on), while PTH-independent causes include malignancy (PTHrP-mediated, lytic bone metastases, myeloma), granulomatous disease, vitamin D toxicity, thyrotoxicosis, and immobilization. Symptomatic or severe hypercalcemia is a medical emergency requiring aggressive isotonic saline, followed by bisphosphonate or denosumab for refractory cases, and treatment of the underlying cause. Mild primary hyperparathyroidism can often be monitored, but parathyroidectomy is curative and preferred in younger patients, those with renal stones, reduced bone density, or renal function decline — refer to endocrinology and endocrine surgery rather than defaulting to observation.

### Treat Hypocalcemia Urgently When Symptomatic and Identify the Cause

Symptomatic hypocalcemia — tetany, carpopedal spasm, seizures, prolonged QT, or laryngospasm — is an emergency requiring intravenous calcium, ideally with cardiac monitoring. Chronic asymptomatic hypocalcemia is treated with oral calcium and active vitamin D (calcitriol), especially after thyroid or parathyroid surgery, in hypoparathyroidism, in severe vitamin D deficiency, or with malabsorption. Always measure magnesium, because hypomagnesemia both causes hypocalcemia and renders it refractory until corrected. Post-thyroidectomy hypocalcemia is common in the early days after surgery and warrants close monitoring; persistent hypoparathyroidism needs structured endocrine management. When evaluating Paget disease, confirm an elevated alkaline phosphatase with characteristic imaging, treat symptomatic disease or active sites with a bisphosphonate, and watch for rare complications such as high-output cardiac failure, hearing loss, or malignant transformation signaled by new pain.

## Common Traps

### Treating Osteopenia With Bisphosphonates in Low-Risk Younger Patients

Osteopenia alone, especially in a younger patient with low FRAX, has a small absolute benefit and real long-term harms. Treat by fracture risk, not by the T-score number.

### Stopping Denosumab Without a Transition Plan

Discontinuing denosumab causes rapid bone loss and a cluster of rebound vertebral fractures. Always plan a transition to a bisphosphonate before stopping.

### Giving Potent Antiresorptive or Anabolic Therapy Without Correcting Vitamin D

Treating osteoporosis in a vitamin-D-deficient patient risks failure and, with potent agents, dangerous hypocalcemia. Replete first.

### Treating All Hypercalcemia the Same Way

Mild primary hyperparathyroidism and familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia are managed very differently from malignancy-associated hypercalcemia. Interpret PTH, and never operate on familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia.

### Forgetting Magnesium in Refractory Hypocalcemia

Hypomagnesemia causes hypocalcemia and makes it unresponsive to calcium or vitamin D until corrected. Always check magnesium.

### Applying Drug Holidays Indiscriminately

A bisphosphonate holiday is appropriate only for lower-risk patients after several years of therapy. High-risk patients — recent fracture, very low T-score, ongoing steroids — should continue. Never "holiday" denosumab.

### Missing Secondary Causes of Osteoporosis

Prescribing a bisphosphonate without evaluating for hyperparathyroidism, thyrotoxicosis, hypogonadism, malabsorption, or myeloma treats the bone loss but not its driver. Screen for secondary causes.

## Self-Check

- Have I based the decision to treat osteoporosis on fracture risk (FRAX, prior fragility fracture, T-score) rather than the T-score alone?
- Have I chosen the agent by fracture site, severity, and patient factors, and preferred anabolic therapy for very high-risk patients?
- Have I planned a monitored bisphosphonate drug holiday only for lower-risk patients after several years, and never for denosumab without a transition?
- Have I corrected calcium for albumin, interpreted PTH, and categorized hypercalcemia before choosing management, with emergency saline and bisphosphonate for severe or symptomatic cases?
- For hypocalcemia, have I treated symptomatic cases with IV calcium under monitoring, checked magnesium, and identified the underlying cause?
- Have I repleted vitamin D and ensured calcium intake before and during bone therapy, and screened for secondary causes of osteoporosis?
- Have I escalated to endocrinology, rheumatology, nephrology, or endocrine surgery for complex hyperparathyroidism, hypoparathyroidism, Paget complications, refractory disease, or uncertainty about agent selection?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
