# Anesthesia Emergencies and Malignant Hyperthermia

Anesthesia emergencies are defined by speed: they develop over minutes, they demand immediate pattern recognition, and they kill if the response is delayed or disorganized. Malignant hyperthermia, anaphylaxis, bronchospasm, laryngospasm, and high spinal do not allow time for leisurely differential reasoning — the clinician must recognize the syndrome from a few cardinal signs, commit to the treatment, and mobilize the team simultaneously. The recurring failure modes are delayed recognition (attributing rising end-tidal CO2 to the machine, hypotension to depth of anesthesia, or desaturation to a kinked circuit while a lethal process unfolds), failure to call for help early enough, and breakdown of crisis resource management under stress — unclear roles, no leader, critical tasks undone. This skill covers the disciplined recognition and management of anesthesia emergencies, with the constant recognition that the first action in any unexplained perioperative deterioration is to call for help, that dantrolene must be given early in suspected MH, and that structured crisis teamwork is itself a treatment.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Malignant Hyperthermia Early and Treat Aggressively

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) is a hypermetabolic crisis triggered by volatile anesthetics or succinylcholine in susceptible individuals, and its mortality rises steeply with delayed dantrolene. The earliest and most specific sign is an unexplained, rapidly rising end-tidal CO2 despite increased ventilation; other signs include tachycardia, masseter or generalized muscle rigidity, rising temperature (a late sign, and its absence does not exclude MH), arrhythmia, hyperkalemia, and acidosis. The diagnosis is clinical and made intraoperatively — do not wait for confirmation. The moment MH is suspected, act in parallel: call for help and announce "malignant hyperthermia," stop all triggering agents and disconnect the vaporizer, switch to a non-triggering anesthetic (total intravenous anesthesia with propofol), hyperventilate with 100% oxygen, and administer dantrolene immediately at the recommended initial dose, repeating until signs subside. Begin active cooling (ice, cooled IV fluids, surface cooling, gastric and bladder lavage), treat hyperkalemia and arrhythmias, monitor for and manage rhabdomyolysis (forced diuresis, alkalinization) and disseminated intravascular coagulation, and arrange intensive care admission. Contact the MH consultation hotline for guidance. After survival, refer the patient and family for confirmed diagnosis and counseling, because MH is inherited and future anesthetics must be trigger-free. The fatal error is delaying dantrolene while considering alternatives.

### Treat Intraoperative Anaphylaxis as a Cardiovascular and Airway Emergency

Anaphylaxis under anesthesia presents differently from awake anaphylaxis: the patient cannot report symptoms, and the signs are cardiovascular (sudden hypotension, tachycardia, cardiovascular collapse) and respiratory (bronchospasm, high airway pressures, desaturation), often with cutaneous signs (flushing, urticaria) that may be hidden under drapes. The most common triggers are neuromuscular blocking agents, antibiotics (especially beta-lactams), latex, and chlorhexidine. Treat immediately and aggressively: call for help, stop the suspected trigger, give 100% oxygen, elevate the legs and give IV fluid boluses, and administer intramuscular or IV epinephrine titrated to effect — epinephrine is the definitive treatment and under-dosing or delaying it is the common error. Treat bronchospasm with bronchodilators and deepened anesthesia. If the airway is compromised by edema, maintain the existing airway and have a difficult-airway plan, because intubation can become impossible as edema progresses. After stabilization, send tryptase levels (peaks within an hour or two) and refer the patient for allergy evaluation to identify the trigger and prevent recurrence. Document the event and the suspected agent, and flag the allergy prominently in the record.

### Manage Laryngospasm and Bronchospasm Before They Cause Harm

Laryngospasm — reflex closure of the vocal cords — is most common in children and in light planes of anesthesia, and it can progress rapidly to hypoxia, negative-pressure pulmonary edema, bradycardia, and cardiac arrest. Recognize it by stridor, failed ventilation, high pressures, and a "tight" airway with paradoxical chest movement. The immediate response is the Larson maneuver (firm pressure at the laryngospasm notch, jaw thrust, 100% oxygen with continuous positive airway pressure), deepening anesthesia with propofol or an intravenous agent, and, if these fail, a small dose of succinylcholine to break the spasm (with airway rescue ready). Avoid repeated airway trauma that worsens spasm. Bronchospasm under anesthesia presents as wheeze, high peak pressures, prolonged expiration, and desaturation, and is common in asthmatic patients, smokers, and those with airway irritation. Treat by deepening anesthesia (which relieves reflex bronchoconstriction), giving inhaled or intravenous bronchodilators, ensuring the tube is not mainstem or irritating the carina, and excluding mechanical causes (kinked circuit, blocked tube, anaphylaxis). If bronchospasm is severe and refractory, escalate to anesthesia and consider IV steroids, magnesium, and, in extremis, epinephrine. Both conditions deteriorate fast — intervene early and call for help.

### Recognize and Treat the High or Total Spinal Immediately

A high or total spinal occurs when neuraxial local anesthetic spreads excessively, blocking the sympathetic chain and the thoracic and cervical nerves. It presents as sudden, profound hypotension and bradycardia (from blocked cardiac accelerator fibers), progressing to respiratory difficulty (intercostal and, with higher spread, phrenic paralysis), upper-limb weakness, and, in the total spinal, loss of consciousness and apnea. The response is immediate and simultaneous: call for help, support the airway and ventilate (the apneic patient needs intubation), give IV fluids and vasopressors (epinephrine or phenylephrine, often in escalating doses) to restore blood pressure, and treat bradycardia with atropine or epinephrine. The cardiovascular collapse of a high spinal can be abrupt and severe — do not wait for full progression before treating. Distinguish a high spinal from local anesthetic systemic toxicity (which causes CNS excitation and arrhythmia rather than a dense sympathetic block) and from vagal reactions. After stabilization, monitor in a high-dependency setting until the block recedes. Prevention rests on correct dosing, aspiration and test doses for epidural catheters, and avoiding injection during high venous pressure.

### Respond to Awareness Under Anesthesia With Validation and Follow-Up

Awareness under anesthesia — explicit recall of intraoperative events — is rare but devastating, and it demands a serious, validating response. Suspect it when a patient reports intraoperative recall, sounds, pain, or paralysis, particularly after a technique at risk (rapid sequence intubation with light anesthesia, high-dose opioid or neuromuscular blockade without amnesia, equipment failure reducing anesthetic delivery). When a patient reports awareness, believe them, listen without minimizing, explain what likely happened and why, apologize for the experience, document the report and the discussion in detail, and arrange prompt follow-up with anesthesia and with mental health support, because awareness can cause post-traumatic stress. Investigate the event: review the anesthetic record for periods of light anesthesia, equipment function, and dosing, and consider whether depth-of-anesthesia monitoring (processed EEG) was or should have been used. Awareness prevention rests on ensuring adequate anesthetic depth (especially during high-risk techniques), monitoring end-tidal anesthetic concentrations, avoiding unnecessary neuromuscular blockade, and considering depth monitoring for high-risk cases. Never dismiss a report of awareness — the patient's account is the primary evidence.

### Apply Crisis Resource Management to Every Perioperative Emergency

In an emergency, the team is as important as the drugs. Apply crisis resource management principles: declare the emergency and assign a leader whose sole job is to direct, not to perform tasks; assign specific roles (airway, compressions, drugs, recorder, runner); call for help early and explicitly (anesthesia, surgery, intensive care, the code team) rather than waiting; use closed-loop communication (state the instruction, confirm receipt, confirm completion); and call out critical information aloud (the rhythm, the blood pressure, the time since onset). Use cognitive aids and emergency checklists — for MH, anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest, and difficult airway — and read them aloud during the event; checklists reduce omission errors under stress. Designate someone to note timings and interventions. After the event, hold a structured debrief to review what happened, what went well, what to improve, and to support the team, because perioperative emergencies are psychologically taxing. The fatal error is not lack of knowledge but failure of organization: no clear leader, critical tasks undone, help called too late.

### Know When and Whom to Call, and Have the Hotlines Available

Anesthesia emergencies require rapid escalation to the right resources. Know your institutional pathways: the anesthesia consultant on call, the difficult airway team, ENT for surgical airway, the intensivist for post-event care, and the rapid response or code team. For malignant hyperthermia, know the MH consultation hotline number and the location and dose of dantrolene, and have an MH kit and checklist immediately available wherever triggering agents are used. For anaphylaxis, have epinephrine, fluids, and airway equipment immediately available. Communicate with a structured format (the patient, the procedure, the problem, what has been done, what is needed) and document the timeline and interventions. The instinct to manage alone must be overridden — in anesthesia emergencies, calling for help early is the single highest-yield action.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Dantrolene While Considering Alternative Diagnoses

Attributing rising end-tidal CO2 to the machine or hypercarbia to shallow breathing while MH progresses. Counter by treating suspected MH immediately with dantrolene — the risk of giving it unnecessarily is far lower than the risk of delaying it.

### Under-Dosing or Delaying Epinephrine in Anaphylaxis

Giving fluids and antihistamines while withholding epinephrine. Counter by administering epinephrine early and titrating to the cardiovascular and respiratory response.

### Treating Laryngospasm With Repeated Airway Manipulation

Worsening spasm with repeated instrumentation. Counter by using the Larson maneuver, 100% oxygen with CPAP, deepening anesthesia, and a small dose of succinylcholine if needed.

### Misattributing High-Spinal Hypotension to Other Causes

Treating hypotension as hypovolemia or anesthetic depth while the block ascends. Counter by recognizing the high spinal pattern (hypotension with bradycardia after neuraxial block) and treating immediately with vasopressors, fluids, and airway support.

### Dismissing a Patient's Report of Awareness

Minimizing or explaining away intraoperative recall. Counter by believing the report, documenting it, apologizing, investigating, and arranging follow-up and psychological support.

### Failing to Call for Help Early

Managing alone until the situation deteriorates. Counter by declaring the emergency, assigning a leader and roles, and calling anesthesia, surgery, and intensive care help immediately.

### Not Using Cognitive Aids and Checklists

Relying on memory during a high-stress crisis. Counter by using and reading aloud the MH, anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest, and difficult airway checklists.

### Missing Post-Event Follow-Up and Debrief

Failing to arrange intensive care, MH referral and family counseling, allergy evaluation, or a team debrief after an emergency. Counter by planning follow-up for the patient and the team and documenting the event and learning.

## Self-Check

- For suspected malignant hyperthermia, have I stopped triggering agents, switched to non-triggering anesthesia, hyperventilated with oxygen, and given dantrolene immediately while calling for help and the MH hotline?
- For intraoperative anaphylaxis, have I stopped the trigger, given 100% oxygen, fluids, and early epinephrine titrated to effect, and sent tryptase for later allergy evaluation?
- For laryngospasm, am I using the Larson maneuver, CPAP with oxygen, and anesthesia deepening, with succinylcholine and airway rescue ready?
- For high or total spinal, have I recognized the pattern (sudden hypotension with bradycardia after neuraxial block) and treated immediately with airway support, fluids, vasopressors, and atropine?
- When a patient reports awareness under anesthesia, have I believed and validated the report, documented it, apologized, investigated, and arranged follow-up and psychological support?
- Am I applying crisis resource management — declaring the emergency, assigning a leader and roles, using closed-loop communication, and reading checklists aloud?
- Have I called for help early to the right resources (anesthesia, difficult airway, ENT, intensivist, code team) and communicated with a structured format?
- Have I arranged post-event intensive care, MH referral and family counseling, allergy evaluation, and a team debrief, and documented the event?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
