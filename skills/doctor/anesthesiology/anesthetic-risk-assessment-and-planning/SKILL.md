# Anesthetic Risk Assessment and Planning

Anesthesia is the controlled, reversible suppression of physiology, and the pre-anesthetic assessment is where the clinician decides whether the patient can tolerate that suppression and how to deliver it with the least harm. The danger is not usually the anesthetic technique itself but the failure to anticipate how the patient's comorbidities, airway, physiology, and medications will interact with sedation, paralysis, positive pressure ventilation, and hemodynamic fluctuation. A patient who is "cleared for surgery" is not necessarily optimized for anesthesia — clearance is a cardiopulmonary opinion, not an anesthetic plan. The recurring failure mode is proceeding with a generic anesthetic plan on a patient who needed specific optimization, a different technique, or a higher level of anesthetic expertise, and then discovering the mismatch intraoperatively when reserves are gone. This skill covers the disciplined pre-anesthetic assessment and plan, with the constant recognition that high-risk patients demand early anesthesiology involvement and that identifying risk before induction is far safer than managing crisis after it.

## Core Rules

### Assign ASA Physical Status Honestly and Use It to Drive Planning

The ASA physical status classification (ASA I through VI, plus E for emergency) is a quick, reproducible summary of baseline physiologic reserve, and it correlates strongly with perioperative risk. Assign it deliberately, not reflexively. A patient with well-controlled hypertension is ASA II; a patient with severe systemic disease with functional limitation (significant heart failure, COPD on oxygen, end-stage renal disease) is ASA III or IV. Be honest about severity — under-classifying a sick patient conceals risk from the team and from the plan. Use the ASA class to drive the level of anesthetic expertise and monitoring required: ASA III and IV patients, and essentially all emergencies, warrant anesthesiology-led care and enhanced monitoring, and many warrant optimization or a higher-acuity venue. Document the ASA class and the reasoning, and ensure it is communicated to the anesthetic team. ASA classification is not a substitute for a detailed assessment, but it is a forcing function that prevents treating a fragile patient as routine.

### Assess the Airway Systematically and Flag Difficulty Early

Airway difficulty is the most feared anesthetic risk, and it must be assessed before induction, not discovered during it. Use a structured assessment: Mallampati (oropharyngeal view), mouth opening (less than three finger-breadths is concerning), thyromental distance (less than three finger-breadths suggests an anterior larynx), neck mobility and extension, beard or obesity that complicate mask ventilation, and any history of difficult intubation or difficult mask ventilation. Use the LEMON framework (Look, Evaluate, Mallampati, Obstruction, Neck mobility) as a structured screen. Combine anatomic difficulty with physiologic difficulty: the patient who is hypoxemic, acidotic, hypotensive, or in shock has little reserve for apnea and is at risk of cardiovascular collapse on induction. Document the airway assessment and explicitly flag predicted difficulty. For a predicted difficult airway, plan awake intubation, video laryngoscopy, or a supraglottic-airway-first strategy, and ensure the difficult-airway cart and an experienced second operator are available before induction. Never paralyze a difficult airway without a rescue plan.

### Match the Anesthetic Technique to the Patient and the Procedure

Anesthetic technique — general anesthesia, neuraxial (spinal or epidural), regional (peripheral nerve block), monitored anesthesia care with sedation, or a combination — should be chosen deliberately, weighing the procedure's requirements against the patient's physiology. General anesthesia with positive pressure ventilation may be poorly tolerated by a patient with severe pulmonary hypertension, severe aortic stenosis, or severe heart failure, where preserving spontaneous circulation and avoiding vasodilation matters. Neuraxial anesthesia is excellent for many lower-body and abdominal procedures but is contraindicated or risky in coagulopathy, severe aortic stenosis, or when hemodynamic fluctuation cannot be tolerated. Regional anesthesia can spare the airway and reduce opioid use but has its own risks (local anesthetic systemic toxicity, nerve injury, failed block). For high-risk cardiac and pulmonary patients, consider combined techniques, goal-directed hemodynamic management, and enhanced monitoring (arterial line, central venous pressure, cardiac output). Do not default to general endotracheal anesthesia out of habit; choose the technique that best preserves the patient's reserves, and discuss the choice with the anesthesiologist and surgeon.

### Apply and Communicate Fasting and Medication Guidance Correctly

NPO status and medication management are pre-anesthetic essentials that are often mishandled. Apply standard fasting guidance: clear fluids up to two hours, a light meal up to six hours, and fatty food or a heavy meal up to eight hours before anesthesia, adjusted for gastrointestinal reflux, gastroparesis, pregnancy, and opioid use which all increase aspiration risk. Do not cancel cases for modest NPO violations without weighing the urgency; for emergencies, treat the patient as full stomach and use rapid sequence induction. Manage medications explicitly: continue most cardiac and pulmonary medications (beta-blockers, most antihypertensives, inhalers), hold diuretics and oral hypoglycemics, manage insulin carefully, and follow specific guidance for anticoagulants around neuraxial procedures (the ASRA or local guidelines on holding and resuming). Document the plan and communicate it to the patient and the team. A patient who arrives having taken or held the wrong medications is at avoidable risk.

### Optimize High-Risk Patients and Escalate Early to Anesthesiology

High-risk patients — those with significant cardiac, pulmonary, renal, hepatic, or neurologic disease; the frail elderly; the severely obese; the septic or hemodynamically unstable; and those undergoing high-risk surgery — benefit from preoperative optimization and early anesthesiology involvement, ideally in a pre-admission or perioperative medicine clinic. Assess functional capacity (metabolic equivalents) and use risk tools (revised cardiac risk index, metabolic equivalents, frailty scores) to stratify. Optimize what is optimizable: heart failure, hypertension, asthma or COPD, diabetes (glycemic control), anemia, electrolyte disturbances, and substance withdrawal risk. For the highest-risk patients, consider cardiopulmonary exercise testing, echocardiography, or specialist consultation, and plan for enhanced postoperative monitoring (high-dependency or intensive care). Do not treat "the patient is scheduled" as "the patient is ready"; escalate to anesthesiology early for any patient with concerning physiology or airway, and document the consultation and the shared plan. For emergencies where optimization is impossible, plan for a higher level of intraoperative and postoperative monitoring and communicate the elevated risk.

### Screen for and Plan Around Malignant Hyperthermia and Anesthesia-Specific Risks

Certain anesthesia-specific risks must be actively screened and planned for, because they are life-threatening if missed. Take a personal and family history of malignant hyperthermia (MH), anesthesia-related death, or unexplained reactions, and treat any such history as real until proven otherwise — plan a trigger-free anesthetic (no volatile agents or succinylcholine), use a clean MH-protocol machine or total intravenous anesthesia, and have dantrolene available. Screen for pseudocholinesterase deficiency (history of prolonged paralysis after succinylcholine), significant anesthesia-related allergies (especially to neuromuscular blockers, antibiotics, latex), and a history of postoperative nausea and vomiting or awareness under anesthesia. Screen for chronic opioid use and tolerance, which alters dosing. Document these risks and build the plan around them; a missed MH history can be fatal, and an unaddressed severe allergy can cause intraoperative anaphylaxis.

### Coordinate the Handoff and the Shared Plan

Anesthesia care does not happen in isolation — it depends on coordination with surgery, the ward or ICU, and the post-anesthesia care unit. Communicate the anesthetic plan, the patient's specific risks, the monitoring plan, and the anticipated postoperative needs (oxygen, analgesia, anticoagulation resumption, level of care) explicitly and in writing. For high-risk patients, agree on thresholds for escalation and on who will manage specific eventualities. A structured preoperative handoff prevents the situation where the team discovers a critical issue (a difficult airway, an MH history, an anticoagulation hold) only after induction. Document the assessment, the plan, and the discussions, and ensure the patient understands the anesthetic approach and its risks through informed consent.

## Common Traps

### Treating "Cleared for Surgery" as Optimized for Anesthesia

Accepting a cardiology or medicine clearance note as sufficient without assessing airway, physiology, and anesthetic-specific risks. Counter by conducting an independent anesthetic assessment and escalating to anesthesiology for high-risk patients.

### Under-Classifying ASA Status

Labeling a fragile patient ASA II and concealing the elevated risk from the plan. Counter by assigning ASA honestly based on systemic disease severity and functional impact, and using it to drive the level of care.

### Defaulting to General Endotracheal Anesthesia

Choosing general anesthesia out of habit for a patient whose physiology would be better served by regional or neuraxial technique. Counter by matching technique to the patient's cardiac, pulmonary, and hemodynamic reserves.

### Discovering Airway Difficulty at Induction

Failing to assess and document airway difficulty preoperatively. Counter by using a structured airway assessment (Mallampati, LEMON) and flagging predicted difficulty with a rescue plan before induction.

### Proceeding with Neuraxial Anesthesia Despite Coagulopathy or Severe Aortic Stenosis

Placing a spinal or epidural without checking coagulation or assessing for severe aortic stenosis. Counter by applying neuraxial anticoagulation guidelines and screening for severe valvular disease before neuraxial blockade.

### Missing a Malignant Hyperthermia or Severe Allergy History

Failing to elicit or act on a personal or family history of MH or anesthesia allergy. Counter by actively screening for these and planning a trigger-free or allergen-free anesthetic with the relevant rescue agents available.

### Cancelling or Proceeding Without Addressing Medications

Either cancelling for trivial NPO violations or proceeding despite unmanaged anticoagulants, insulin, or cardiac medications. Counter by applying evidence-based fasting and medication guidance and documenting the plan.

### Late or Absent Anesthesiology Escalation

Involving anesthesiology only when the patient is already in trouble. Counter by escalating early for high-risk physiology, difficult airway, or complex comorbidity, and documenting the shared plan.

## Self-Check

- Have I assigned an honest ASA physical status class and used it to drive the level of anesthetic expertise and monitoring?
- Have I assessed the airway systematically (Mallampati, mouth opening, thyromental distance, neck mobility, prior history) and flagged predicted difficulty with a rescue plan before induction?
- Have I matched the anesthetic technique (general, neuraxial, regional, sedation) to the procedure and the patient's cardiac, pulmonary, and hemodynamic reserves?
- Have I applied correct fasting and medication guidance, with specific plans for anticoagulants, insulin, and cardiac and pulmonary medications?
- For high-risk patients, have I optimized what is optimizable, stratified risk, planned enhanced monitoring, and escalated early to anesthesiology or perioperative medicine?
- Have I screened for and planned around anesthesia-specific risks — malignant hyperthermia, pseudocholinesterase deficiency, severe allergies, and chronic opioid tolerance?
- Have I coordinated the anesthetic plan, specific risks, and postoperative needs with surgery, the ward or ICU, and the PACU through a structured, documented handoff?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
