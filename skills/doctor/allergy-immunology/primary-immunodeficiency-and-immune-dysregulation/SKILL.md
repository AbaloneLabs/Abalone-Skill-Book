# Primary Immunodeficiency and Immune Dysregulation

Primary immunodeficiency is routinely diagnosed years too late, and the delay is costly. These conditions are rare individually but collectively common enough that every clinician will encounter them, and the typical patient has suffered years of recurrent infections, multiple antibiotic courses, organ damage, and repeated specialist contacts before the underlying immune defect is recognized. The failure is rarely a lack of sophisticated testing; it is a failure to suspect immunodeficiency in the first place, because recurrent infections are so often attributed to daycare exposure, normal childhood illness, or bad luck. Beyond infection, many primary immune errors present not as susceptibility to bugs but as immune dysregulation: autoimmunity, lymphoproliferation, granulomatous inflammation, or recurrent fever syndromes. The clinician's discipline is to recognize the warning patterns, to order a rational first-line immunologic workup and interpret it correctly, to distinguish primary from secondary causes, to recognize the autoinflammatory and lymphoproliferative syndromes that masquerade as infection or malignancy, and to refer early to immunology before irreversible organ damage accrues. This skill covers warning signs, laboratory interpretation, primary versus secondary causes, immune dysregulation syndromes, immunoglobulin replacement considerations, and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Recognize the Warning Patterns Using the SPUR Framework

The most important step is to suspect immunodeficiency at all. Use a memorable framework such as SPUR — Severe, Persistent, Unusual, or Recurrent infections — to decide when to investigate beyond a single episode. Severe means infections requiring intravenous antibiotics, hospitalization, or intensive care, or infections with unusually aggressive organisms. Persistent means infections that do not respond to standard therapy or recur immediately after completing it. Unusual means infections caused by opportunistic or atypical organisms, infections in unexpected sites, or complications such as abscess, osteomyelitis, or empyema. Recurrent means multiple distinct episodes, particularly at the same site or with the same organism, beyond what is plausible for age and exposure. The number of infections matters less than their pattern: ten typical viral colds in a school-aged child may be normal, but two episodes of invasive pneumococcal disease, a persistent oral candidiasis beyond infancy, or a Pseudomonas soft-tissue infection in an otherwise well adult demand investigation. Document the pattern explicitly, because the trajectory over time is more informative than any single episode.

### Order a Rational First-Line Workup and Interpret It in Context

When warning signs are present, order a structured first-line immunologic evaluation rather than a scattersheet of tests. The core screen includes quantitative immunoglobulins, full blood count with differential and lymphocyte count, and, where indicated, vaccine response testing, complement levels, and lymphocyte subset enumeration. Interpret each result in clinical context, because reference ranges are wide and age-dependent, and a value within the quoted range can still be pathologic. Low immunoglobulin G with recurrent sinopulmonary infection suggests a humoral defect; isolated low immunoglobulin A is common and usually clinically silent but flags risk of a broader defect; high immunoglobulins can reflect chronic infection, autoimmunity, or immune dysregulation rather than protection. Lymphocyte subsets must be interpreted against age-specific norms, particularly in infants, where what looks like lymphopenia in an adult may be normal and vice versa. A normal total lymphocyte count does not exclude a specific cellular defect, and vaccine antibody responses, particularly to pneumococcal polysaccharide, are often the most revealing functional test of humoral competence. Do not over-interpret a single borderline value; repeat abnormal results and correlate with the clinical pattern before labeling a patient immunodeficient.

### Distinguish Primary From Secondary Causes Before Committing to a Diagnosis

Secondary immunodeficiency is far more common than primary immunodeficiency, and treating the wrong cause wastes time and exposes the patient to inappropriate therapy. Consider and exclude secondary causes systematically: protein loss through nephrotic syndrome, protein-losing enteropathy, or severe burns; medication effects including immunosuppressants, biologics, antiepileptics, and prolonged steroids; infections including untreated advanced human immunodeficiency virus, Epstein-Barr virus, cytomegalovirus, and other chronic viral infections; metabolic and nutritional causes including severe diabetes, renal failure, and micronutrient deficiency; and malignancy involving the hematopoietic system. A low immunoglobulin level in a patient on an immunosuppressive biologic, or with heavy proteinuria, is not a primary defect and should not trigger an immunoglobulin replacement pathway. Conversely, do not assume a secondary cause in a patient with a genuinely suggestive primary pattern; investigate both in parallel when the picture is ambiguous, and refer to immunology for definitive classification.

### Recognize Immune Dysregulation Syndromes That Masquerade as Infection or Malignancy

A growing fraction of primary immune errors present not as infection susceptibility but as dysregulation, and missing these causes real harm. Autoinflammatory syndromes, including familial Mediterranean fever and other periodic fever syndromes, present with recurrent fevers, serositis, rash, and elevated inflammatory markers between attacks, and are often misdiagnosed as recurrent infection or psychogenic illness; untreated, they cause amyloidosis and renal failure. Autoimmune lymphoproliferative syndrome presents with chronic non-malignant lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, and autoimmune cytopenias, and is often mistaken for lymphoma; the key clue is the combination of chronic lymphoproliferation and autoimmune cytopenia with a double-negative T-cell population on flow cytometry. Immune dysregulation polyendocrinopathy enteropathy X-linked and similar conditions present with severe early-onset autoimmunity, particularly enteropathy and endocrinopathy, and require urgent specialist input. Common variable immunodeficiency frequently presents with autoimmunity, granulomatous disease, or lymphoproliferation alongside infection susceptibility. When a patient has autoimmunity plus lymphoproliferation, or recurrent fever plus serositis, or enteropathy plus endocrinopathy in a child, suspect immune dysregulation and escalate rather than treating each manifestation in isolation.

### Consider Immunoglobulin Replacement as a Specialist-Initiated, Targeted Therapy

Immunoglobulin replacement is effective for confirmed significant antibody deficiency with documented functional impairment, but it is not appropriate for every low immunoglobulin level, every recurrent infection, or every nonspecific immune complaint. Before initiation, document the defect rigorously: quantitative immunoglobulins, vaccine response testing demonstrating failure of specific antibody generation, and the clinical phenotype of recurrent or severe infection. Replacement is a long-term, resource-intensive therapy with real risks, including infusion reactions, thromboembolic events, headache and aseptic meningitis, and, with subcutaneous formulations, local reactions; it is not a low-stakes intervention. Decisions to start, continue, or stop replacement should be made by immunology specialists, with periodic reassessment of ongoing indication, because some secondary and transient defects recover and do not require lifelong therapy. Do not initiate replacement empirically for nonspecific symptoms or isolated borderline laboratory values.

### Refer Early to Immunology Before Irreversible Damage Accrues

The goal of primary and secondary care recognition is timely specialist referral, not definitive diagnosis or treatment. Refer when the warning pattern is present even if initial labs are equivocal, when immunoglobulin or subset results are significantly abnormal, when immune dysregulation is suspected, or when a patient has recurrent infection plus autoimmunity, lymphoproliferation, or granulomatous disease. Early referral enables definitive phenotyping including advanced flow cytometry, genetic testing, family screening, and the initiation of targeted therapies such as replacement, biologics, or, for severe combined immunodeficiency and related conditions, urgent hematopoietic stem cell transplantation. Severe combined immunodeficiency in infancy is a pediatric emergency where delay measured in weeks affects survival; any infant with persistent lymphopenia, failure to thrive, opportunistic infection, or vaccine-strain infection requires immediate specialist escalation, not outpatient follow-up.

## Common Traps

### Normalizing Recurrent or Severe Infections as Bad Luck or Exposure

Attributing invasive, persistent, or unusual infections to daycare, occupation, or chance, and never investigating the underlying pattern. Counter by applying the SPUR framework and investigating when the pattern exceeds what exposure alone explains.

### Interpreting Immunologic Tests Without Age-Specific and Functional Context

Reading a lymphocyte count or immunoglobulin level against adult norms in an infant, or treating a single borderline value as diagnostic. Counter by using age-specific reference ranges, repeating abnormal results, and adding functional vaccine response testing.

### Assuming Secondary Cause Without Investigating Primary, or Vice Versa

Stopping at a medication or protein-losing state as the explanation while missing a primary defect, or labeling a secondary defect as primary and committing the patient to lifelong replacement. Counter by investigating both in parallel and referring for definitive classification when the picture is ambiguous.

### Missing Autoinflammatory and Lymphoproliferative Syndromes

Treating recurrent fever and serositis as infection, or chronic lymphadenopathy and autoimmune cytopenia as lymphoma, without considering immune dysregulation. Counter by recognizing the syndromic combinations and escalating to immunology for flow cytometry and genetic evaluation.

### Initiating Immunoglobulin Replacement Empirically

Starting replacement for nonspecific symptoms or isolated low immunoglobulin without documented functional antibody deficiency. Counter by requiring rigorous phenotyping including vaccine response testing and reserving initiation for specialist-confirmed significant antibody deficiency.

### Delaying Referral in Infantile Severe Immunodeficiency

Treating suspected severe combined immunodeficiency as an outpatient problem, losing critical weeks. Counter by treating persistent infantile lymphopenia, failure to thrive, opportunistic infection, or vaccine-strain infection as an emergency requiring immediate specialist escalation.

## Self-Check

- Did I apply a Severe, Persistent, Unusual, or Recurrent framework to decide whether the infection pattern warrants investigation?
- Did I order a rational first-line immunologic workup, including quantitative immunoglobulins, full blood count, and vaccine response testing where indicated?
- Did I interpret immunoglobulin and lymphocyte subset results against age-specific reference ranges and functional context, rather than treating single values as diagnostic?
- Did I systematically consider and exclude secondary causes of immunodeficiency before attributing the pattern to a primary defect?
- Did I recognize autoinflammatory, autoimmune lymphoproliferative, and early-onset autoimmune enteropathy syndromes as immune dysregulation rather than isolated infection or malignancy?
- Did I avoid empirical immunoglobulin replacement, reserving it for specialist-confirmed significant functional antibody deficiency?
- Did I refer early to immunology, treating suspected severe combined immunodeficiency in infancy as an emergency?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
