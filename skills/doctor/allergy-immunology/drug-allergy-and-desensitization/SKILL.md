# Drug Allergy and Desensitization

Drug allergy labels are among the most consequential annotations in a medical record, and the majority of them are wrong. Most patients carrying a penicillin allergy label are not, in fact, allergic: many were labeled after a non-immunologic side effect, a viral exanthem misattributed to an antibiotic, or a childhood reaction long since resolved. The cost of an inaccurate label is not trivial — it drives use of broader-spectrum antibiotics, longer admissions, higher rates of resistant organisms and Clostridioides difficile, and avoidance of first-line chemotherapy, anticoagulants, and anesthetics. The clinician's task is to take a structured allergy history that separates true immunologic reactions from intolerances and side effects, to stratify severity so that management matches risk, to know when an oral challenge or formal desensitization is indicated and when it is dangerous, to apply cross-reactivity principles rationally rather than excluding entire drug classes reflexively, and to delabel and document accurately so the record reflects reality. This skill covers allergy versus intolerance, severity stratification, beta-lactam delabeling, challenge versus desensitization, cross-reactivity, documentation, and escalation to allergy and infectious diseases.

## Core Rules

### Take a Structured Allergy History That Separates Allergy From Intolerance

The single most valuable step is a detailed history of the reported reaction, because most labels dissolve on interrogation. Establish the drug and formulation, the year and the patient's age, the timing of onset relative to dosing, the specific symptoms, and the course after the drug was stopped. True immunologic reactions typically manifest as urticaria, angioedema, anaphylaxis, bronchospasm, serum sickness, or severe cutaneous adverse reactions such as Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms, and acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis. Nausea, headache, diarrhea, fatigue, mild self-limited rash without urticarial features, and isolated gastrointestinal upset are almost always intolerances or expected side effects, not allergies, and should not be recorded as such. A viral exanthem that coincided with an antibiotic course, extremely common in children, is not a drug allergy. When the history is vague or remote, the label is often removable through risk-stratified challenge rather than lifelong avoidance.

### Stratify Severity Because It Determines Everything Downstream

Severity, not the mere presence of a label, drives management. Low-risk reactions include distant childhood rashes without detail, non-urticarial rashes, gastrointestinal symptoms, and family history alone. Moderate-risk reactions include urticaria without anaphylaxis within the past several years, and reactions where the history is unclear but an immunologic mechanism is plausible. High-risk reactions include any anaphylaxis, severe cutaneous adverse reactions, organ involvement, recurrent reactions, and reactions to agents where re-exposure could be catastrophic. Never challenge a patient with a history of a severe cutaneous adverse reaction to the same or cross-reactive drug; these are absolute contraindications to challenge and usually to desensitization. Match the management to the tier: low-risk patients can often be delabeled by history or direct challenge, moderate-risk patients may need graded challenge or specialist input, and high-risk patients require allergy referral and a formal desensitization pathway only when the drug is essential.

### Delabel Penicillin Allergy Rationally Because the Stewardship Benefit Is Large

Approximately nine in ten patients with a penicillin allergy label can tolerate penicillins, and delabeling is one of the highest-yield antimicrobial stewardship interventions available. A patient with a low-risk history, particularly a distant or non-specific childhood reaction, can often be delabeled directly or with a single-dose oral amoxicillin challenge under observation. A patient with a moderate-risk history generally warrants allergy specialist evaluation with skin testing followed by challenge when negative. High-risk histories, including any severe cutaneous adverse reaction or recent anaphylaxis, should not be challenged outside a specialist setting. When you delabel, update the record, the pharmacy profile, and the patient's own understanding, because a label that persists in one system continues to drive avoidance. Document the method of delabeling, the agent challenged, the dose, and the observation outcome so that future clinicians can trust the updated record.

### Choose Between Challenge and Desensitization Based on Mechanism and Need

Graded challenge and desensitization are different procedures for different situations. A graded challenge, or test dosing, is used when the probability of true allergy is low to moderate and the goal is to confirm tolerance; it gives incrementally increasing doses with observation and is appropriate for patients whose label is questionable or whose prior reaction was remote and mild. Desensitization is a longer, more intensive procedure that temporarily induces tolerance in a patient who is, or is presumed to be, allergic, by administering progressively increasing doses over hours to days until the full therapeutic dose is reached; it is reserved for when the drug is essential and no alternative exists, such as beta-lactams in pregnancy, sulfonamides in pneumocystis pneumonia, platinum chemotherapy, or aspirin in cardiovascular disease. Desensitization must be performed in a monitored setting with immediate access to anaphylaxis treatment, because reactions occur, and the tolerant state is lost if dosing is interrupted. Never confuse the two: challenging a truly allergic patient without a desensitization protocol risks anaphylaxis, and desensitizing a patient who was never allergic wastes resources and exposes them to risk.

### Apply Cross-Reactivity Principles Rather Than Excluding Entire Classes Reflexively

Reflexive avoidance of an entire drug class because of one allergy is a common and harmful error. For beta-lactams, the cross-reactivity between penicillins and cephalosporins is far lower than historically taught, particularly with modern cephalosporins, and depends on side-chain similarity rather than the beta-lactam ring itself; cefalexin shares a side chain with aminopenicillins and warrants caution, whereas many third-generation cephalosporins have minimal cross-reactivity in patients with non-severe penicillin allergy. For sulfonamides, cross-reactivity between antibiotic and non-antibiotic sulfonamides is not clinically meaningful. For non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, cross-reactivity is mechanism-based and applies across the class for patients with cyclooxygenase-1 inhibition reactions, but selective agents may be tolerated after challenge. Always consider side-chain cross-reactivity, the severity of the index reaction, and the necessity of the target drug, and seek allergy input when the reaction was severe and the target drug shares structural features.

### Document Accurately and Persistently to Prevent Relabeling

A delabeling effort is wasted if the record is not updated everywhere the label lives. Record the specific drug, the reaction described and its severity category, the assessment, the action taken, and the explicit clearance of any agents challenged. Use the institution's allergy reconciliation process to remove the flag from the electronic record and pharmacy profile, and give the patient written documentation of the outcome so they can correct the record with other providers. Conversely, when a true allergy is confirmed, document the drug, the reaction, the severity, the cross-reactive agents to avoid, and the safe alternatives, and communicate the plan to the patient and all treating teams. Inaccurate or ambiguous documentation is itself a patient safety hazard, because it propagates avoidance or inappropriate re-exposure across every future encounter.

### Escalate to Allergy and Infectious Diseases for High-Risk or Essential-Drug Decisions

Involve allergy specialists when the reaction was severe cutaneous adverse disease or anaphylaxis, when skin testing and supervised challenge are required, when desensitization is being considered, or when the label is blocking first-line therapy for a serious infection, malignancy, or procedural need. Involve infectious diseases when the allergy label is forcing suboptimal antibiotic choices in complex infection, because combined allergy and stewardship pathways produce better outcomes than either alone. Do not attempt outpatient delabeling, challenge, or desensitization in patients with a history of severe reactions, significant comorbidity, beta-blocker use, or limited access to emergency care; these patients require a monitored specialist environment.

## Common Traps

### Recording Intolerances and Side Effects as Allergies

Labeling nausea, diarrhea, headache, or a non-specific rash as drug allergy, which then drives lifelong avoidance and broader-spectrum alternatives. Counter by recording these as intolerances or adverse effects, not allergies, and by taking a structured history before labeling.

### Misattributing a Viral Exanthem to an Antibiotic

Labeling a childhood antibiotic allergy when the rash was almost certainly viral. Counter by recognizing the temporal and clinical pattern and by offering risk-stratified delabeling rather than permanent avoidance.

### Excluding an Entire Drug Class Reflexively

Avoiding all cephalosporins, all sulfonamides, or all non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs because of a single reaction, ignoring side-chain and mechanism-based cross-reactivity. Counter by applying cross-reactivity principles and seeking allergy input for severe index reactions.

### Challenging a Patient With a Severe Cutaneous Adverse Reaction History

Performing a graded challenge in a patient with prior Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, or DRESS to the same or related drug, which is contraindicated. Counter by treating these histories as absolute contraindications to re-exposure and referring to allergy for any essential-drug decision.

### Confusing Challenge With Desensitization

Using a short graded challenge where a full desensitization protocol is required for a truly allergic patient who needs the drug, risking anaphylaxis. Counter by matching the procedure to the probability of allergy and the necessity of the drug, and reserving desensitization for essential agents in confirmed allergy.

### Failing to Update the Record After Delabeling

Removing the label verbally or in one note while it persists in the pharmacy profile and patient record, perpetuating avoidance. Counter by updating every relevant system, providing written documentation to the patient, and confirming the change at subsequent encounters.

### Delabeling High-Risk Patients in an Unmonitored Setting

Attempting challenge or desensitization in patients with recent anaphylaxis, severe cutaneous adverse reactions, beta-blocker use, or poor access to emergency care without specialist oversight. Counter by referring these patients to allergy for monitored evaluation.

## Self-Check

- Did I take a structured history that separated true immunologic allergy from intolerance, side effect, and viral exanthem?
- Did I stratify the reaction by severity, and did I treat severe cutaneous adverse reactions and anaphylaxis as absolute contraindications to routine challenge?
- Did I consider risk-stratified delabeling for low-risk penicillin allergy labels, using direct or single-dose challenge where appropriate?
- Did I distinguish graded challenge from desensitization, and reserve desensitization for essential drugs in confirmed or presumed allergy performed in a monitored setting?
- Did I apply side-chain and mechanism-based cross-reactivity principles rather than excluding entire drug classes reflexively?
- Did I document the reaction, the assessment, the action, and the clearance or restriction clearly, and update every system where the label persists?
- Did I escalate to allergy and infectious diseases for high-risk reactions, essential-drug decisions, and patients unsuitable for outpatient delabeling?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
