# Anaphylaxis and Acute Allergic Reactions

Anaphylaxis is a rapidly progressive, potentially fatal systemic hypersensitivity reaction, and the central failure in its care is not pharmacological complexity but hesitation. The single most dangerous and most common error is delayed epinephrine, driven by uncertainty about diagnostic criteria, fear of the injection, or confusion with mimics such as vasovagal syncope or an isolated asthma flare. Anaphylaxis does not require a known trigger, does not require shock, and does not always produce urticaria or stridor; it can present as isolated collapse after an insect sting, as progressive dyspnea after a meal, or as gut symptoms alone after an antibiotic dose. The clinician's discipline is to recognize the syndrome from its pattern and trajectory, give intramuscular epinephrine early as first-line therapy, position the patient correctly, arrange an observation window that accounts for biphasic recurrence, identify the trigger through history, and equip the patient for safe discharge with an adrenaline autoinjector and an allergy action plan. This skill covers diagnostic criteria, the primacy of epinephrine, recognition of mimics, observation and biphasic risk, trigger identification, autoinjector provision, and escalation to allergy and critical care.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Anaphylaxis by Criteria, Not by Certainty

Anaphylaxis is highly likely when any one of three criteria is met: acute onset of skin or mucosal involvement plus respiratory compromise or hypotension or end-organ dysfunction; two or more of skin or mucosal involvement, respiratory compromise, reduced blood pressure, or persistent gastrointestinal symptoms after likely exposure to an allergen; or reduced blood pressure after exposure to a known allergen for that patient. Crucially, skin features are absent in a meaningful minority of true anaphylaxis, so their absence must never be used to exclude the diagnosis. Treat acute onset of hypotension, bronchospasm, laryngeal involvement, or collapse after a plausible trigger as anaphylaxis until proven otherwise, and escalate to senior or critical care input early when the presentation is ambiguous or severe.

### Give Intramuscular Epinephrine First and Without Delay

Intramuscular epinephrine is first-line, definitive therapy and should be given as soon as anaphylaxis is recognized, not after adjuncts have failed. The intramuscular route into the anterolateral thigh produces the most reliable absorption, including in shock, and should not be delayed for intravenous access. Repeat every five to fifteen minutes if there is no improvement, and use multiple doses without hesitation in refractory cases. The fear of epinephrine, particularly in older patients or those with cardiovascular disease, is a leading cause of fatal under-treatment; the risks of untreated anaphylaxis far exceed the risks of correctly dosed intramuscular epinephrine. Adjuncts such as antihistamines, corticosteroids, and bronchodilators treat symptoms and may reduce recurrence, but none substitutes for epinephrine and none should delay it. In refractory anaphylaxis despite multiple epinephrine doses, escalate immediately for intravenous epinephrine infusion, fluid resuscitation, and critical care admission.

### Position the Patient Correctly and Avoid Sudden Changes

Position is an underappreciated life-saving intervention. Supine positioning with elevated legs maximizes venous return in a patient whose vascular compartment is rapidly emptying; in pregnancy, use the left lateral position. Patients with primarily respiratory distress may prefer sitting up, but sudden postural change, particularly standing up or moving to a chair or toilet, can precipitate instantaneous empty-vena-cava cardiac arrest and must be prevented. Keep the patient flat and monitored until the reaction has clearly resolved, and treat any urge to sit, stand, or walk as a warning sign rather than a comfort request.

### Distinguish Anaphylaxis From Its Mimics Deliberately

The common mimics are vasovagal syncope, acute asthma, anxiety or panic with globus sensation, carcinoid syndrome, scombroid poisoning, hereditary angioedema, and vocal cord dysfunction. Vasovagal reactions produce pallor, bradycardia, and rapid return to baseline on lying flat, without the urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, or progressive hypotension of anaphylaxis. Isolated asthma without skin or mucosal features can be anaphylaxis if a trigger is present, so treat asthma after an allergen exposure as possible anaphylaxis and give epinephrine if there is hypotension, angioedema, or gut involvement. Hereditary angioedema, carcinoid, and scombroid each have distinct patterns and treatments, and misclassifying them as anaphylaxis can delay the specific therapy they require. When the diagnosis is genuinely unclear, treat empirically as anaphylaxis and investigate afterward rather than withholding epinephrine.

### Plan Observation Around Biphasic Risk

A proportion of anaphylactic reactions recur hours after the initial event resolves, without re-exposure, and these biphasic reactions can be severe or fatal. Observation duration should be individualized to risk rather than applied uniformly. Lower-risk patients with prompt single-dose epinephrine response and no features of severity may be observed for a shorter period, while higher-risk patients, including those requiring multiple epinephrine doses, presenting with hypotension or airway involvement, having a known severe trigger such as peanut or insect sting, taking beta-blockers, or having limited access to emergency care, warrant extended observation or admission. Do not discharge any patient who has not returned to baseline hemodynamics and airway status, and arrange follow-up before discharge.

### Identify the Trigger Through Structured History

After stabilization, identify the trigger by correlating exposure with onset, typically minutes to two hours for food, drug, and insect triggers. Take a detailed history of all ingestants, medications, stings or bites, physical triggers such as exercise or cold, and any latex exposure in the preceding hours, and document the temporal relationship precisely. Because cofactors such as exercise, alcohol, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, infection, and menstruation can amplify a reaction that would otherwise be mild, their presence changes both the assessment and the advice. When the trigger is unclear, do not guess; arrange formal allergy evaluation with skin testing and specific immunoglobulin testing timed correctly after the event, and counsel the patient to avoid only the suspected agents pending definitive workup.

### Equip the Patient for Safe Discharge Before They Leave

Every patient treated for anaphylaxis should leave with three things: an adrenaline autoinjector or a prescription with demonstrated technique, a written personalized anaphylaxis action plan describing when and how to use it, and a referral to an allergy specialist. Teach autoinjector technique with a trainer device and have the patient or caregiver demonstrate it back, because technique decay is rapid and incorrect use in a real event is common. Counsel on trigger avoidance specific to the identified agent, including label reading for food allergy, cross-reactivity, and high-risk settings such as restaurants and dental procedures. Confirm the patient understands the observation window for biphasic recurrence and knows to call emergency services and use the autoinjector at the first sign of recurrence. Arrange primary care and specialist follow-up before discharge and document the reaction in the patient's record as a flag for future care.

## Common Traps

### Delaying Epinephrine for Adjuncts or Certainty

Giving antihistamines, corticosteroids, or bronchodilators first, or waiting for definitive confirmation, while the reaction progresses. Counter by treating intramuscular epinephrine as the immediate first action whenever criteria are met.

### Withholding Epinephrine Due to Fear of Adverse Effects

Avoiding epinephrine in older, pregnant, or cardiac patients because of perceived cardiovascular risk. Counter by recognizing that untreated anaphylaxis is the greater danger and that correctly dosed intramuscular epinephrine has a wide safety margin; escalate for senior input in genuinely complex cases rather than withholding therapy.

### Misclassifying Anaphylaxis as Vasovagal or Asthma

Attributing collapse to a faint or dyspnea to isolated asthma when skin, mucosal, gut, or hypotensive features indicate a systemic reaction. Counter by applying the diagnostic criteria and treating empirically when the picture is mixed.

### Allowing the Patient to Sit or Stand

Permitting postural change to a chair or toilet during the reaction, which can precipitate sudden cardiovascular collapse. Counter by keeping the patient supine with legs elevated, left lateral in pregnancy, and monitored until full resolution.

### Discharging Too Early and Missing Biphasic Recurrence

Sending the patient home after initial resolution without accounting for biphasic risk factors. Counter by individualizing observation to severity, epinephrine requirements, trigger type, beta-blocker use, and access to care.

### Discharging Without an Autoinjector, Plan, or Referral

Releasing the patient without the tools and follow-up needed to survive a recurrence. Counter by ensuring every patient leaves with an adrenaline autoinjector, a written action plan, demonstrated technique, trigger avoidance advice, and an allergy referral.

### Anchoring on the Wrong Trigger

Assuming the trigger is the most obvious ingestant without a structured temporal history, leading to incorrect lifelong avoidance. Counter by mapping all exposures against onset time and deferring definitive attribution to formal allergy workup.

## Self-Check

- Did I recognize anaphylaxis using the diagnostic criteria rather than waiting for certainty, and did I treat skin-feature-negative presentations as possible anaphylaxis?
- Did I give intramuscular epinephrine into the anterolateral thigh immediately as first-line therapy, repeating as needed and escalating to infusion for refractory cases?
- Did I position the patient correctly and prevent any sudden postural change during the reaction?
- Did I deliberately distinguish anaphylaxis from vasovagal syncope, asthma, hereditary angioedema, carcinoid, and scombroid, treating empirically when the picture was mixed?
- Did I individualize the observation period to biphasic risk factors rather than applying a uniform duration?
- Did I take a structured trigger history mapping exposures to onset and identifying cofactors, deferring definitive attribution to specialist testing?
- Did I discharge the patient with an adrenaline autoinjector, demonstrated technique, a written action plan, trigger avoidance advice, and an allergy referral?
- Did I escalate to critical care for refractory, recurrent, or severe reactions, and document the reaction as a permanent flag in the record?
- This guidance is educational and does not substitute for professional clinical judgment; when in doubt or when red flags appear, seek immediate specialist consultation and follow institutional protocols.
