# Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Collection Audit

A diversity, equity, and inclusion audit is a specialized collection assessment that examines who is represented in the collection, how they are represented, who is doing the representing, and whether the collection reflects the full community the library serves. Unlike general assessment, which asks how much the collection is used, an inclusion audit asks whether the collection is just: whether it includes the voices of marginalized communities, whether those voices are authentic or stereotyped, and whether gaps and biases have accumulated through years of default selection. The work is politically sensitive, methodologically demanding, and easy to perform superficially, because the comforting answer, that the collection is more diverse than it is, is always available unless the audit is rigorous. Done well, an inclusion audit transforms selection and weeding for years; done as a gesture, it provides cover without change.

## Core Rules

### Define the audit's scope and categories before coding

An inclusion audit needs a clear scope and a defined set of categories before any title is examined, because ad hoc coding produces inconsistent, indefensible results. Decide which dimensions to audit, such as race and ethnicity, Indigenous identity, LGBTQ+ identity, disability, religion, immigration status, language, and socioeconomic background, and define each category precisely so coders apply it consistently. Decide whether to audit creator identity, character or subject representation, or both, recognizing that these answer different questions. Define the audience scope, such as children's picture books, adult fiction, or the whole collection, because mixing audiences muddies the findings. Document the methodology in writing so the audit is repeatable and the results can be compared over time.

### Sample rigorously rather than auditing everything

Auditing every title in a collection is rarely feasible, so sampling is necessary, and the quality of the sample determines the validity of the findings. Use systematic or random sampling within defined sections, ensure the sample is large enough to support meaningful conclusions, and document the sampling method so it can be replicated. Avoid convenience samples, such as auditing only the most accessible shelves, which bias the results. For targeted questions, such as representation in a specific section, a census of that section may be appropriate. Be transparent about the sample size and limits when reporting, because overclaiming from a small sample undermines credibility and invites dismissal of legitimate findings.

### Code for creator identity, not just content presence

A common audit failure is counting only whether diverse characters or subjects appear, which overstates inclusion because it ignores who created the work. Code separately for creator identity, asking whether the author or illustrator shares the identity being depicted, often discussed as own-voices, because authentic representation carries different weight than outsider representation. Surface diversity without creator diversity can reproduce the very exclusion it appears to address, by centering outsider narratives about marginalized communities. Recording both dimensions allows the audit to distinguish a collection with genuine inclusion from one with token presence filtered through dominant voices, and it points selection toward creators from the communities being represented.

### Evaluate the quality, not just the quantity, of representation

Counting titles is the easy part; evaluating how groups are portrayed is harder and more important. A collection can show numerical presence while relying on stereotypes, single stories, trauma-only narratives, or portrayals that reduce a community to its oppression. Develop coding criteria for quality, such as whether characters have agency and interiority, whether multiple varied portrayals exist, whether joy and everyday life appear alongside struggle, and whether outdated or harmful portrayals are present. Include a check for material that may warrant deselection or replacement because its portrayal has aged poorly. Quality coding is more subjective and requires coder training and periodic calibration, but without it the audit measures presence while missing harm.

### Compare findings against community and benchmarks

Audit findings gain meaning only in comparison. Compare the representation in the collection against the demographics of the community the library serves, to identify which groups are underrepresented relative to the population. Compare against the broader publishing landscape, using industry statistics on diverse publishing, to distinguish gaps the library can fix through better selection from gaps driven by publishing itself. Compare across sections and over time, to see where the collection is improving and where it is stagnant. Without comparison, an audit produces numbers with no context, and the library cannot tell whether 15 percent representation is strong, weak, or typical.

### Report honestly, including uncomfortable findings

The value of an inclusion audit depends on honest reporting, including findings that are uncomfortable or that contradict the library's self-image. Report both strengths and gaps, acknowledge methodological limits, and avoid spinning the numbers to look better than they are, because the patrons the audit is meant to serve are best positioned to detect dishonesty and will distrust the library if the report feels like public relations. Present findings in plain language with concrete examples, and share them with the communities affected, inviting their response. Honest reporting builds the credibility needed to act on the findings, while defensive reporting squanders the opportunity.

### Turn findings into selection, weeding, and policy change

An audit that ends with a report has not finished its work. Translate findings into action: adjust selection priorities and budgets to close identified gaps, with measurable targets and timelines; seek out specialized sources and own-voices creators to fill them; apply the quality findings to weeding decisions, replacing outdated or harmful portrayals; and revise the collection-development policy to embed inclusion as a standing obligation. Set a date to re-audit and measure whether the changes worked, because a single audit is a snapshot, and only repeated measurement shows whether the collection is becoming more inclusive over time. The audit is a tool for change, not an end in itself.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is performing an inclusion audit as a gesture, to demonstrate commitment without intending to act on the findings, which provides cover without change and erodes trust when communities notice. Another is defining categories loosely or coding ad hoc, which produces inconsistent results that cannot be defended or compared over time. Counting only content presence while ignoring creator identity overstates inclusion and misses the gap between surface diversity and authentic representation. Relying on convenience samples, such as the most accessible shelves, biases the findings and undermines validity. Stopping at quantity and skipping quality evaluation measures presence while missing harmful stereotypes, single stories, and outdated portrayals that may warrant deselection. Reporting numbers without comparison, against community demographics or publishing benchmarks, leaves the library unable to interpret whether its representation is strong, weak, or typical. Spinning findings to look better than they are, or omitting uncomfortable gaps, destroys the credibility the audit needs to drive change. Finally, ending the audit with a report rather than translating findings into selection, weeding, budget, and policy changes ensures the exercise produces no lasting improvement.

## Self-Check

- Did you define the audit's scope, categories, and audience before coding, and document the methodology so it is repeatable?
- Did you use systematic or random sampling of sufficient size, and avoid convenience samples that bias the findings?
- Did you code for creator identity in addition to content presence, to distinguish authentic representation from surface diversity?
- Did you evaluate the quality of representation, including agency, multiple portrayals, joy alongside struggle, and outdated or harmful material?
- Did you compare findings against community demographics and publishing benchmarks, rather than reporting numbers in isolation?
- Did you report honestly, including uncomfortable gaps and methodological limits, without spinning the results to look better than they are?
- Did you share findings with the affected communities and invite their response?
- Did you translate findings into selection priorities, budget changes, weeding of harmful material, policy revision, and a plan to re-audit over time?
