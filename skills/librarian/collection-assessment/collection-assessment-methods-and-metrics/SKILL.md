# Collection Assessment Methods and Metrics

Collection assessment is the systematic study of a collection to understand what it holds, how it is used, how well it meets community needs, and where it falls short. Without assessment, collection decisions rest on impression and anecdote, and the library cannot tell whether its spending is producing value, whether gaps are closing or widening, or whether weeding is improving the collection. The challenge is that assessment methods vary widely in cost, rigor, and what they can reveal, and no single method answers every question. Done well, assessment turns the collection from a static accumulation into a managed asset; done poorly, it produces impressive-looking reports that no one acts on, because they answered the wrong question or measured what was easy rather than what mattered.

## Core Rules

### Start from a clear question, not a method

The most common assessment failure is choosing a method first and then looking for something to measure, which produces data without insight. Begin instead with a concrete question: Is the medical collection current enough to support patient questions? Are Spanish-language holdings meeting community demand? Is the children's picture-book collection balanced across diverse authors? Each question points to different methods, timeframes, and metrics. State the question, define what evidence would answer it, and only then select the method. A focused question also defines the audience for the findings and the decisions they should inform, which keeps the assessment from becoming an exercise in description.

### Match the method to the question and resources

Different assessment methods reveal different things, and each carries costs in staff time, expertise, and tools. Circulation and use analysis shows what patrons actually borrow or access, but not why, and it undervalues in-house and reference use. Collection mapping and shelf scanning reveals the shape, age, and balance of the collection but not use. Inventory identifies what is missing, lost, or misshelved but is labor-intensive. Patron surveys and focus groups capture needs, satisfaction, and unmet demand but suffer from response bias. Checklist or standard-list comparison measures the collection against an external benchmark but assumes the benchmark fits the community. Combine methods where possible, because the strongest assessments triangulate quantitative and qualitative evidence, and choose methods the library can actually sustain rather than an ambitious design that collapses halfway.

### Use circulation and use data with explicit caveats

Circulation data is the most accessible and most misused assessment input. It reveals what is being borrowed, but it does not capture in-house use, reference consultation, browsing, or the use of digital surrogates, so a low-circulation title is not necessarily a low-value title. Analyze patterns rather than individual titles: turnover rate, the ratio of circulations to holdings, shows how actively a section is used relative to its size; percentage of the collection that has not circulated in a defined period identifies dead stock; and comparisons across sections reveal where use concentrates. Always pair circulation analysis with shelf examination and qualitative input, because data alone cannot distinguish a worn classic awaiting rediscovery from genuinely obsolete material.

### Map the collection to see shape, age, and balance

Collection mapping uses classification, subject, or format data to visualize the distribution of holdings, age, and use across the collection. Mapping reveals patterns invisible in title-by-title review: subject areas that are over- or under-represented relative to community need; sections dominated by older material that may warrant weeding or updating; format imbalances, such as over-investment in print where patrons want digital; and gaps in diverse, language, or specialized coverage. Age analysis by section is especially powerful, because the average copyright date of a section is a quick indicator of currency and a flag for where outdated material may be misleading patrons. Mapping turns the collection from a list of titles into a visible landscape with clear strengths and gaps.

### Triangulate with patron input and community data

The collection exists to serve a community, so assessment that looks only inward at holdings and circulation misses whether needs are actually being met. Combine internal analysis with patron input through surveys, focus groups, suggestion forms, and observation, and with community data such as demographics, language profiles, school and program enrollment, and local economic trends. Patron input reveals unmet demand, frustrations, and aspirations that usage data cannot, and community data reveals who the library is and is not currently reaching. Be alert to response bias, because patrons who respond to surveys are not representative of the whole community, and deliberately seek input from underrepresented groups rather than relying on whoever speaks up.

### Compare against external benchmarks critically

Standard lists, peer comparisons, and accreditation standards offer external reference points that reveal how the collection compares to expectations or to similar libraries. Use them critically, because no standard list fits every community, and peer comparisons can obscure differences in mission, demographics, and budget. Checklist methods work well for targeted questions, such as whether the collection includes core works in a field or award titles, but they measure presence, not quality or use. Treat external benchmarks as one input among several, and always ask whether the benchmark reflects this community's actual needs rather than an abstract ideal.

### Report findings to drive decisions, not to describe

An assessment that produces a report and then sits on a shelf has failed, regardless of its rigor. Design the assessment from the outset to inform specific decisions: selection priorities, weeding targets, budget reallocation, grant applications, or policy revision. Report findings in plain language tied to those decisions, with clear recommendations and the evidence behind them, and present to the audiences who can act, including selectors, leadership, and governance. Build in follow-up so that the assessment's recommendations are tracked and the next assessment cycle measures whether changes worked. Assessment is valuable only when it changes what the library does.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is choosing a method before defining a question, which produces data that does not answer anything the library needs to know. Another is relying on circulation data alone, which undervalues in-house and reference use and mislabels dormant classics as dead stock. Running an ambitious, multi-method assessment that the library cannot sustain, so it collapses halfway and produces incomplete, unusable results, wastes the effort invested. Treating collection mapping as description rather than diagnosis, producing attractive charts with no recommendations, leaves findings unused. Relying on patron surveys without accounting for response bias over-represents the loudest patrons and misses the needs of those who do not respond. Using standard lists or peer benchmarks uncritically, as if they defined what this community needs, leads to collecting for an abstract ideal rather than real patrons. Reporting findings as description rather than as the basis for specific decisions ensures the assessment changes nothing. Finally, treating assessment as a one-time project rather than a recurring cycle means the library cannot tell whether its interventions worked or whether gaps are closing over time.

## Self-Check

- Did you start from a clear, concrete question and select the method to answer it, rather than choosing a method and looking for something to measure?
- Did you match the method to the question and to the staff time, expertise, and tools the library can realistically sustain?
- Did you use circulation and use data with explicit caveats, analyzing patterns and turnover rather than judging individual titles on circulation alone?
- Did you map the collection to reveal shape, age, balance, and gaps across subjects, formats, and diversity, rather than reviewing title by title?
- Did you triangulate internal data with patron input and community demographics, and deliberately seek input from underrepresented groups to counter response bias?
- Did you use external benchmarks and standard lists critically, checking whether they reflect this community's actual needs rather than an abstract ideal?
- Did you report findings in plain language tied to specific decisions, with recommendations and evidence, to the audiences who can act?
- Did you build in follow-up and a recurring cycle so the assessment drives change and measures whether interventions worked?
