# Usage Data and Patron Feedback Analysis

Libraries generate large volumes of data, from circulation and holds to database sessions, gate counts, program attendance, and patron surveys, and that data is the primary evidence base for decisions about collections, services, staffing, and space. Yet library data is easy to misread, because every metric reflects choices about what is measured, every dataset carries biases, and every comparison hides assumptions. A collection decision based on misread data can waste budget, cut valued services, or reinforce existing inequities. The work is not just number-crunching; it is interpreting evidence under uncertainty, with honesty about what the data can and cannot say, and with constant attention to the patrons the data fails to capture at all.

## Core Rules

### Understand what each metric actually measures

Before interpreting any number, establish precisely what it counts and what it excludes. A circulation statistic counts checkouts, not reads, and misses in-house use entirely. A database session count records logins, not meaningful research, and a patron who logs in and finds nothing still counts as a session. Gate counts capture entries, not unique visitors, so one person returning five times looks like five patrons. Holds measure demand that exceeds supply, not total interest. Program attendance counts those who came, not those who wanted to but could not. State the definition of each metric explicitly, and never compare metrics across systems that define them differently, because vendor-defined numbers in particular are optimized to look favorable.

### Recognize and adjust for bias in every dataset

No library dataset is neutral. Circulation data over-represents patrons who already use the library and under-represents those who cannot get there, cannot afford fines or transport, or feel unwelcome. Survey responses skew toward patrons with time, language, literacy, and comfort enough to respond, and away from the most marginalized. Digital usage data over-represents patrons with devices, connectivity, and digital skills. Holds data reflects only titles the library chose to buy in limited quantities. Name the bias in each dataset before drawing conclusions, and where possible, adjust by seeking complementary data from the underrepresented groups. A conclusion drawn from biased data, presented as if neutral, will compound inequity rather than reduce it.

### Combine quantitative and qualitative evidence

Quantitative data shows what is happening; qualitative data explains why. A circulation drop in a section could mean declining interest, but it could also mean the shelves are disorganized, the signage is poor, or the material is outdated. Pair the numbers with shelf observation, patron comments, staff input, and targeted conversations to interpret the pattern. Surveys should include open-ended questions alongside scales, and the open responses often reveal more than the ratings. The strongest analysis triangulates: it looks for convergence across different data types, and it investigates divergence, where the numbers and the stories disagree, because that is usually where the real insight lies.

### Interpret trends over time, not single snapshots

A single data point proves almost nothing, because a month's circulation can be distorted by weather, a holiday, a school assignment, a system outage, or a one-time event. Analyze trends across multiple months or years, seasonally adjusted where relevant, to distinguish signal from noise. Compare year-over-year, look for sustained shifts rather than one-month spikes, and correlate changes with known events, such as a new program, a budget cut, or a pandemic. Be cautious with small numbers, where a handful of events can swing a percentage dramatically. A trend grounded in multiple periods supports a decision; a single snapshot invites overreaction.

### Use comparison thoughtfully and define the comparison group

Comparisons give data meaning, but only when the comparison group is appropriate. Comparing a section's circulation to the whole collection shows its relative weight; comparing to peer libraries shows its standing against similar institutions; comparing branches reveals equity across service points; comparing before and after an intervention shows whether it worked. Each comparison answers a different question and carries different caveats. Avoid comparing metrics across institutions without confirming they are defined the same way, and avoid comparing sections of vastly different size without normalizing, such as by turnover rate rather than raw circulation. State the comparison group explicitly so the reader understands what the number is being measured against.

### Design patron surveys to minimize bias

Surveys are powerful but riddled with traps that distort results. Write neutral questions, because leading or loaded wording produces the answer the survey writer expected rather than the patron's real view. Offer balanced response options, including a don't-know or not-applicable option, to avoid forcing false opinions. Keep surveys short to protect response rate and quality. Translate and offer the survey in the community's languages, and offer non-digital modes for patrons without internet access, to reduce response bias. Pre-test questions with a small group to catch confusion. Report the response rate and sample characteristics alongside results, because a survey of engaged patrons does not represent the whole community, and overclaiming from it misleads decisions.

### Present findings with honesty about uncertainty

Library data is imperfect, and honest reporting names the limits rather than hiding them. Report sample sizes, response rates, and known biases. Distinguish strong findings from tentative ones, and avoid presenting correlation as causation, because two trends moving together do not prove one caused the other. Use clear visualizations that show the real distribution rather than exaggerating differences, and label axes and units so readers can interpret honestly. When the data is ambiguous, say so, and recommend gathering more evidence rather than forcing a conclusion. Honest reporting builds the trust that makes data-driven decisions credible, while overclaiming destroys credibility the first time a decision goes wrong.

### Connect findings to decisions and follow up

Data analysis earns its cost only when it changes what the library does. Tie each finding to a specific decision, whether reallocating budget, adjusting hours, shifting staffing, revising a program, or changing the collection, and present it to the people who can act. Build in follow-up measurement, so the library can tell whether the decision improved the outcome it was meant to improve. Without this loop, analysis becomes reporting for its own sake, and the library accumulates dashboards and studies that no one uses. The test of good data work is not the elegance of the analysis but whether it led to better service for patrons.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is treating library data as neutral and complete, when every metric reflects choices about what is counted and every dataset over-represents some patrons while invisibly excluding others. Another is comparing metrics across systems or vendors that define them differently, producing meaningless or misleading comparisons. Interpreting a single month or year without trend analysis leads to overreaction to noise, while ignoring small-number effects lets a handful of events swing a percentage into a false narrative. Relying on quantitative data alone, without qualitative input to explain the why behind the what, produces confident conclusions that miss the real cause. Designing surveys with leading questions, unbalanced options, or no language and non-digital access produces results that confirm the survey writer's assumptions rather than capturing patron views. Comparing sections or institutions without normalizing for size, such as comparing raw circulation rather than turnover, makes large collections look successful and small ones look failing regardless of merit. Presenting correlation as causation, or overclaiming from biased or small samples, destroys credibility when decisions based on the analysis go wrong. Finally, producing reports and dashboards that no one acts on, because they were not tied to decisions and follow-up, wastes the entire analytical effort.

## Self-Check

- Did you state precisely what each metric counts and excludes, and confirm definitions match before comparing across systems or vendors?
- Did you name the bias in each dataset, and seek complementary data from patrons the main dataset under-represents?
- Did you combine quantitative trends with qualitative evidence, such as observation, comments, and open-ended responses, to explain the why behind the numbers?
- Did you analyze trends across multiple periods, seasonally adjusted where relevant, rather than reacting to a single snapshot?
- Did you define the comparison group explicitly and normalize for size where needed, such as using turnover rather than raw circulation?
- Did you design surveys with neutral questions, balanced options, translation, and non-digital modes, and report response rate and sample characteristics?
- Did you present findings with honesty about uncertainty, distinguishing strong from tentative conclusions and avoiding correlation-as-causation?
- Did you tie findings to specific decisions and build in follow-up measurement to test whether the decisions improved outcomes?
