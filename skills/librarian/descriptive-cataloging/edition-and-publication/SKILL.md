# Edition And Publication

Edition and publication data look like simple transcription, but they are where catalog records most often diverge from the item and from each other. The edition statement distinguishes one intellectual iteration of a work from another, and the publication statement anchors the item in time, place, and producer. Users rely on both to identify the exact manifestation they need, to confirm a citation, or to distinguish a reprint from the original. Confusing an edition with a printing, mishandling a multi-place or multi-publisher publication, or failing to capture a serial's complex publication history produces records that mislead. Good edition and publication description requires understanding what each statement means, transcribing faithfully, and recording the relationships between manifestations.

Use this skill when transcribing edition, publication, distribution, or manufacture statements, handling reprints and impressions, describing simultaneous or shared publications, or cataloging serials and integrating resources. The goal is to prevent the agent from conflating editions with printings, inventing or regularizing publication data, or producing records that cannot distinguish the specific manifestation.

## Core Rules

### Understand What An Edition Statement Represents

An edition statement identifies a manifestation that differs intellectually from other manifestations of the same work. It is not the same as a printing or impression, which is a production batch of the same edition.

Key distinctions:

- edition, a manifestation with changes in content, such as a revised text or new introduction;
- impression or printing, a production run of the same edition, with no intellectual change;
- issue, a variant of the same impression, often a different cover or price;
- state, a minor variant within an issue.

Transcribe the edition statement as found. If the item shows only printing or impression data and no edition statement, record the printing data in a note, not as the edition statement. Conflating the two misrepresents the item's intellectual identity.

### Transcribe The Edition Statement Faithfully

The edition statement is transcribed from the chief source, typically the title page, colophon, or verso. Faithful transcription supports identification.

Rules:

- transcribe the edition statement in the wording and language found;
- record numeric editions as found, such as "2nd edition" or "Second edition";
- record revised, enlarged, or corrected editions with their specific terms;
- use abbreviations prescribed by the standard where appropriate;
- distinguish edition statements in different languages on multilingual sources.

Do not invent an edition statement if none appears. An item with no edition statement is presumed to be the first edition, and the field is left blank rather than guessed.

### Handle Publication, Distribution, And Manufacture Distinctly

Modern standards separate the functions of publication, distribution, and manufacture, because they are often performed by different entities in different places. Conflating them loses information.

Elements:

- place of publication, distribution, or manufacture;
- name of publisher, distributor, or manufacturer;
- date of publication, distribution, or manufacture.

When all three functions are performed by the same entity in the same place, transcription is straightforward. When they differ, record each in its prescribed position or field. A book printed in China for a publisher in New York has distinct manufacture and publication data that users and supply-chain researchers need.

### Transcribe Places Of Publication Accurately

Place of publication is transcribed from the source, but judgment is needed for ambiguous or multi-place cases.

Considerations:

- transcribe the place as found, in the language of the source;
- if multiple places are listed, transcribe the first and others as needed per the standard;
- add the country or larger jurisdiction in brackets if the place is obscure;
- distinguish place of publication from place of manufacture when they differ;
- handle places in non-Latin scripts with romanization as prescribed.

A user searching for a London edition should find it under London, not under an inferred or regularized place.

### Record Publisher Names Faithfully But Accessibly

Publisher names are transcribed as found, but they also feed access points and supply identification.

Practice:

- transcribe the publisher name as it appears on the source;
- preserve the form, including abbreviations the publisher uses;
- do not expand or regularize in the transcription field;
- use authorized access points for publisher as a corporate body when warranted;
- record variant publisher names through authority control.

A publisher that has changed names over time should be collocated through authority control, not by silently regularizing the transcription.

### Determine The Correct Date

The date is among the most important and most problematic elements. Items may carry multiple dates, conflicting dates, or no date.

Approaches:

- transcribe the date of publication as found on the source;
- if no publication date appears, use the date of manufacture, distribution, or copyright as prescribed;
- record copyright dates with the copyright symbol or letter designation;
- supply an approximate date in brackets when no date is found, using conventions like [approximately 1990] or [between 1970 and 1980];
- note the basis for a supplied date.

A supplied or estimated date must be clearly marked as such. Silent invention of a date destroys the record's reliability.

### Handle Reprints And Facsimiles Explicitly

Reprints and facsimiles are distinct manifestations and must be described as such, with the relationship to the original recorded.

For a reprint:

- describe the reprint as the manifestation in hand, with its own publication data;
- record the original edition in a note or relationship field;
- do not copy the original's publication data into the reprint's record.

For a facsimile:

- describe the facsimile as the manifestation in hand;
- record a note describing the original being reproduced;
- use a relationship designator linking the facsimile to the original.

Users need to know whether they hold the 1850 original or a 1995 facsimile. Conflating them defeats identification.

### Describe Simultaneous And Shared Publications Carefully

Some works are published simultaneously in multiple places, by multiple publishers, or as co-publications. These cases need careful handling.

Cases:

- simultaneous publication in multiple countries, transcribe places and publishers per the standard;
- co-edition with shared publishers, record all named publishers;
- distributed by a different entity than the publisher, record distribution separately;
- print-on-demand or self-published works, record the producer and date as found.

Do not arbitrarily choose one place or publisher when multiple are equally prominent. Follow the standard's rule for order and completeness.

### Capture Serial And Integrating Resource Publication Patterns

Serials and integrating resources have complex, ongoing publication patterns that differ from monographs.

For serials:

- record the first issue's title and publication data as the basis of description, or the latest issue per the standard in use;
- note numbering, frequency, and publication dates;
- record title changes as new records or linking entries per the standard;
- capture publisher and frequency changes over time.

For integrating resources:

- describe the current iteration;
- update the record when the title, publisher, or other core data changes;
- note the date of the latest update viewed.

Serials and integrating resources are living records. Static description fails them.

### Record Dates And Editions In Access Points And Notes

Beyond transcription, edition and date data appear in authorized access points and notes that support discovery.

Use:

- the date in the authorized access point for the work to distinguish editions;
- notes for reprint, translation, and revision relationships;
- linking fields for related manifestations;
- uniform titles with date qualifiers when needed to disambiguate.

The transcription field describes the item; access points and notes connect it to the broader bibliographic universe.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Edition With Printing

A printing is not an edition. Recording printing data as the edition statement misrepresents the intellectual identity of the item.

### Inventing An Edition Or Date

If no edition or date appears, do not guess. Use prescribed conventions for supplied data and note the basis.

### Conflating Publication And Manufacture

A book printed in one country for a publisher in another has distinct data. Record each function in its prescribed position.

### Choosing One Place Or Publisher Arbitrarily

When multiple places or publishers are equally prominent, follow the standard rather than personal preference.

### Copying Original Data Into A Reprint Record

A reprint is a distinct manifestation. Describe the reprint in hand and record the original in a note or relationship field.

### Ignoring Copyright Dates As Evidence

When no publication date appears, the copyright date may be the best evidence. Record it with proper designation rather than leaving the date blank.

### Static Serial Records

Serials change over time. A record never updated for title, publisher, or frequency changes becomes inaccurate.

### Silent Date Estimation

An estimated date must be bracketed and explained. Presenting a guess as fact undermines the record.

## Self-Check

- Is the edition statement transcribed faithfully, and is it distinguished from printing or impression data?
- Are publication, distribution, and manufacture functions recorded distinctly when performed by different entities?
- Are places of publication transcribed as found, with obscure places qualified in brackets?
- Are publisher names transcribed faithfully and collocated through authority control?
- Is the date determined correctly, with supplied or estimated dates clearly marked and explained?
- Are reprints and facsimiles described as distinct manifestations, with originals recorded in notes or relationships?
- Are simultaneous and shared publications handled per the standard, without arbitrary choice of place or publisher?
- For serials and integrating resources, are publication patterns, changes, and updates captured over time?
- Are edition and date data reflected in authorized access points and relationship notes?
- Could a user distinguish this specific manifestation from related editions, reprints, and translations using this record?
