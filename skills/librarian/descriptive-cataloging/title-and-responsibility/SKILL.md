# Title And Responsibility

The title and the statement of responsibility are the most searched and most scrutinized elements of a catalog record. Users search by title far more than by any other element, and they expect to find a work under the name of the person or body responsible for it. Yet titles are often ambiguous, multipart, or presented in multiple forms, and responsibility is frequently shared, layered, or obscured. A performer, a translator, an editor, a corporate body, a pseudonymous author, or a film's director may all have claims to access points. Getting title and responsibility right is not transcription clerical work; it is the core of whether users can find and identify the work.

Use this skill when transcribing titles, constructing statements of responsibility, choosing which creators receive access points, assigning role designators, or handling complex or shared responsibility. The goal is to prevent the agent from transcribing titles loosely, missing variant titles, under- or over-assigning creator access points, or misidentifying who is responsible for the intellectual content.

## Core Rules

### Transcribe The Title From The Chief Source Faithfully

The title proper is transcribed from the chief source of information, exactly as it appears. Faithful transcription preserves identity and supports matching across catalogs.

Rules:

- transcribe the title in the order, wording, and spelling found on the source;
- preserve capitalization and punctuation as found, within reason;
- record errors as found, with bracketed corrections where needed;
- if the title appears in multiple languages on the source, transcribe the title in the language of the main content;
- distinguish title proper from parallel titles and other title information.

Do not paraphrase, abbreviate, or silently regularize the title. The catalog record's title field reflects the item; the access points reflect controlled forms.

### Record All Title Variants As Added Entries

Users search using whatever title they encountered, which may be a spine title, a cover title, a caption title, a title from a different edition, or a translated title. Recording variants as added entries multiplies findability.

Variants to capture:

- cover title, spine title, caption title if different from title proper;
- parallel titles in other languages on the source;
- variant titles found in other reference sources;
- titles from earlier or later editions;
- distinctive titles within a larger work;
- popular or commonly known titles that differ from the formal title;
- titles in romanized and original scripts for non-Latin scripts.

Each variant is a potential search path. Omitting variants closes discovery routes that users actually use.

### Distinguish Title Proper From Other Title Information

The title page often contains the title proper plus subtitles, statements of responsibility, and other title information. Each has a defined place in the record.

Separate:

- title proper, the main title;
- other title information, subtitles and explanatory phrases;
- parallel titles, titles in other languages presented equally;
- statements of responsibility, names of persons or bodies responsible.

Conflating these into one string destroys structure and searchability. Use the prescribed field structure to keep them distinct.

### Identify The True Statement Of Responsibility

The statement of responsibility names the persons or bodies responsible for the content. Identifying it correctly determines who gets credit and access.

Consider:

- authors, composers, artists, directors, and primary creators;
- editors, compilers, translators, and adaptors;
- performers, conductors, and production bodies for audiovisual works;
- corporate bodies when an organization is the author;
- mixed responsibility when multiple creators have different roles;
- shared responsibility when multiple creators have the same role.

Transcribe the statement as found, then construct authorized access points for the creators who warrant them.

### Apply Access Points Based On Intellectual Contribution

Not every named person gets an access point. Access points are assigned based on the significance of the contribution to the intellectual or artistic content.

Guidelines:

- the principal creator always gets an access point;
- secondary creators, translators, editors, illustrators, get access points when their contribution is significant;
- performers and production personnel get access points when their role is central to the work type, for example a film director or a recording's conductor;
- minor contributors, such as a cover designer or a proofreader, generally do not;
- corporate bodies get access points when they have principal or shared responsibility.

The standard provides role-based criteria. Apply them consistently rather than by personal judgment of importance.

### Use Correct Relationship Designators

Modern standards use relationship designators to specify the nature of the creator's relationship to the work, such as author, editor, translator, composer, director, performer. These designators carry meaning for users and for data exchange.

Practice:

- assign the most specific accurate designator;
- use controlled vocabulary for designators;
- avoid vague terms like contributor when a specific role applies;
- record multiple designators when a person has multiple roles;
- keep designators consistent with the standard in use.

Accurate designators let users filter and understand relationships, not just see names.

### Handle Corporate And Shared Authorship Carefully

Many works have corporate authorship or shared responsibility, and these cases are common sources of cataloging error.

Cases:

- corporate body as author, for reports, standards, organizational publications;
- shared personal authorship, for collaborative works;
- mixed responsibility, for works with distinct roles like author and illustrator;
- works with no clear author, described by title with no main entry personal name;
- collected works with an editor but no single author.

For corporate bodies, determine whether the body is the author or merely the publisher. For shared works, decide on main entry and added entries per the standard's rules. Document the reasoning for ambiguous cases.

### Resolve Pseudonyms And Name Variants Through Authority Control

Creators write under pseudonyms, change names, or appear in variant forms across languages and scripts. Authority control resolves these into consistent access points.

Practice:

- create or use an authority record for the creator;
- choose the authorized form per the standard's rules;
- record pseudonyms and variant names as see references;
- link variant forms so a search under any finds all works;
- handle real-name-revealed pseudonyms per the standard's preference rules.

Without authority resolution, a pseudonymous author's works scatter and users cannot find them under the name they know.

### Record Performers And Production Roles For Audiovisual Works

For sound recordings, films, and other audiovisual works, responsibility is layered and the question of who gets access points is more complex than for books.

Consider:

- composers for musical works;
- performers, conductors, and ensembles for recordings;
- directors, screenwriters, and producers for films;
- production companies as corporate bodies;
- cast members when their performance is central.

Apply the standard's specific rules for each format. The director of a film and the conductor of a recording typically warrant access points; a minor cast member may not.

### Document Complex Responsibility Cases

When responsibility is ambiguous or contested, document the decision in the record and in local practice notes. This preserves consistency and aids future catalogers.

Document:

- the basis for choosing main entry;
- why secondary creators were or were not given access points;
- the resolution of pseudonym or variant name issues;
- any deviation from default rules with rationale.

Complex cases recur. Documentation turns a one-off judgment into reusable practice.

## Common Traps

### Paraphrasing Or Abbreviating The Title

Users search the exact title they saw. Paraphrasing breaks that search. Transcribe faithfully.

### Missing Variant Titles

Omitting cover, spine, caption, parallel, or translated titles closes search paths users actually take. Record all variants as added entries.

### Conflating Title And Subtitle

Merging title proper and other title information into one field destroys structure. Keep them in prescribed fields.

### Over-Assigning Access Points

Giving every named person an access point clutters the record and distorts collocation. Assign based on significant intellectual contribution.

### Under-Assigning Access Points

Omitting significant translators, editors, or performers hides the work from users searching by those names. Apply the standard's criteria consistently.

### Vague Role Designators

Using generic terms like contributor instead of translator or editor loses information. Use the most specific accurate designator.

### Confusing Publisher With Corporate Author

A publishing body is not always the author. Determine whether the corporate body has principal responsibility before assigning it as an author access point.

### Ignoring Pseudonym Authority

Failing to resolve pseudonyms scatters an author's works. Use authority control to link variant names.

## Self-Check

- Is the title proper transcribed faithfully from the chief source, with errors preserved and bracketed corrections?
- Are all title variants, cover, spine, caption, parallel, translated, recorded as added entries?
- Are title proper, other title information, parallel titles, and statements of responsibility kept in distinct prescribed fields?
- Is the true statement of responsibility identified and transcribed accurately?
- Are access points assigned based on significant intellectual or artistic contribution, per the standard's criteria?
- Are relationship designators specific, accurate, and consistently applied?
- Are corporate authorship and shared responsibility handled per the standard, with main entry and added entries correct?
- Are pseudonyms and name variants resolved through authority control?
- For audiovisual works, are composers, performers, directors, and production roles assigned access points appropriately?
- Are complex or ambiguous responsibility cases documented for consistency?
