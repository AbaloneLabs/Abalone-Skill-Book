# Resource Description

Resource description is the foundation of cataloging. Every choice about what to describe, how fully, and in what terms determines whether a user can find, identify, select, and obtain the item. Description looks mechanical, transcribe what is on the item, but it is full of judgment. The title page may be ambiguous, the responsibility may be shared across many creators, the edition may be unclear, and the item in hand may differ from what other catalogs record. Good description follows standards consistently, records what is needed for the four user tasks of find, identify, select, and obtain, and resists both over-description that clutters records and under-description that hides items.

Use this skill when creating or editing a descriptive catalog record, deciding description depth, handling ambiguous or complex items, or applying RDA or another descriptive standard. The goal is to prevent the agent from transcribing blindly, inventing data, ignoring the user tasks, or producing inconsistent records across a collection.

## Core Rules

### Anchor Description To The User Tasks

Descriptive cataloging exists to serve the four user tasks defined in cataloging theory: find, identify, select, and obtain. Every element recorded should serve at least one.

- Find: enable a user to locate a known item or search by attributes.
- Identify: distinguish the item from similar items and confirm it is the right one.
- Select: judge whether the item suits the need, format, language, level.
- Obtain: acquire or access the item, location, availability, link.

Before recording an element, ask which task it serves. If none, reconsider whether it belongs. This prevents both clutter and gaps.

### Transcribe What Is On The Source, Then Regularize

The first principle of description is to transcribe the information as it appears on the chief source of information, typically the title page for a book. Accuracy of transcription preserves the item's identity and supports collocation.

Rules:

- transcribe title, statement of responsibility, edition, publication, and series as found;
- preserve the language and spelling of the source;
- record errors as found but correct with bracketed interpolation where needed for clarity;
- use prescribed abbreviations and capitalization in the regularized access points;
- distinguish transcription from controlled access points.

Do not silently correct the title page. Do not invent information not present without clear bracketed indication.

### Identify The Chief Source Correctly

Different resource types have different chief sources, and choosing the wrong one produces inconsistent description.

Chief sources by type:

- printed book, the title page or title page substitute;
- serial, the title page, analytic, or first issue;
- integrating resource, the latest issue or home page;
- sound recording, the label or container;
- video, the title frame or container;
- electronic resource, the title screen or home page;
- map, the title on the map itself.

When the chief source is missing or ambiguous, use a prescribed substitute and note the basis of description. Document the choice so the record is reproducible.

### Determine Description Depth Deliberately

Not every item warrants full-level cataloging. Description depth should match the item's value, use, and institutional context.

Levels to consider:

- full-level, all applicable elements, for research or high-value items;
- core-level, essential elements for reliable discovery;
- minimal-level, basic identification for quick processing;
- collection-level, for archival or aggregated materials;
- batch or vendor-supplied, with review for local needs.

Match depth to purpose. Over-cataloging low-use items wastes effort; under-cataloging high-use items hides them. Have a policy that assigns depth by collection or format.

### Handle Ambiguity With Documented Judgment

Items frequently present ambiguity: a title that runs across pages, a creator role that is unclear, an edition statement that conflicts with other sources. Resolve ambiguity through documented judgment, not guesswork.

Approaches:

- prefer the chief source for transcription;
- consult prescribed reference sources for authorized access points;
- use bracketed interpolation to supply missing or corrected data;
- add a note explaining the basis for a judgment;
- record variant titles found elsewhere as added entries;
- when in genuine doubt, describe what is present and note the uncertainty.

Ambiguity is normal in cataloging. The record should make the judgment visible to future catalogers and users.

### Describe The Item In Hand, Not An Idealized Version

Catalog the specific manifestation you hold, including its peculiarities. A reprint, a variant printing, a paperback edition, or a local binding is a different manifestation from the hardcover first edition.

Capture:

- the specific edition or impression;
- the specific format and extent;
- local peculiarities such as inscriptions, inserted plates, or binding;
- accompanying materials and their relationship;
- reproduction details for facsimiles or reprints.

Linking to related manifestations is valuable, but the record describes the item in hand. Do not copy a record for a different edition without adjusting the description.

### Record Relationships Explicitly

Modern descriptive standards emphasize relationships between works, expressions, manifestations, and items, the WEMI model. Recording relationships helps users navigate related materials.

Relationships to capture:

- work to expression, translations, revisions;
- manifestation to manifestation, reprints, simultaneous editions, reproductions;
- item to manifestation, specific copy information;
- whole to part, chapters, volumes, contained works;
- related works, adaptations, sequels, source works.

Use relationship designators or linking fields as the standard provides. Relationships are not optional decoration; they are how users move through the catalog.

### Apply The Current Standard Consistently

Whether using RDA, AACR2, Dublin Core, or another standard, apply it consistently across the collection. Mixed practice confuses users and breaks collocation.

Consistency requires:

- training and documentation of local practice;
- decision logs for recurring ambiguities;
- regular review of records for standard adherence;
- awareness of standard revisions and their local adoption timeline;
- clear handling of legacy records under older standards.

Inconsistency is often invisible to the cataloger but obvious to the user searching the catalog.

### Maintain Authority Control

Descriptive elements feed access points, and access points need authority control to collocate works by the same creator or on the same subject.

Practice:

- use authorized access points from authority files;
- create local authority records when no shared authority exists;
- record variants as see and see-also references;
- keep personal name authority consistent across name changes and pseudonyms;
- review and update authority records as standards evolve.

Without authority control, the same creator's works scatter under variant names, defeating the find task.

### Document Local Practice And Exceptions

Local cataloging practice includes decisions about depth, local subjects, classification additions, call number schemes, and exceptions to the standard. Document these so records are consistent across staff and time.

Maintain:

- a local cataloging manual;
- decision logs for recurring cases;
- examples of correctly handled complex items;
- a process for revising practice as standards change.

Undocumented local practice dies with the cataloger who held it.

## Common Traps

### Silent Correction Of The Title Page

Correcting errors on the source without bracketing changes the item's identity. Transcribe faithfully and interpolate corrections in brackets.

### Copy Cataloging Without Adjusting To The Item

Borrowing a record for a different edition or binding produces an inaccurate record. Verify every element against the item in hand.

### Over-Description Of Low-Use Items

Full-level cataloging on everything wastes scarce cataloger time. Match depth to value and use.

### Under-Description Of High-Use Items

Minimal records on heavily used materials hide them from precise searching. Invest description where it pays.

### Ignoring The Four User Tasks

Recording elements that serve no task clutters records; omitting elements that serve obtain or select frustrates users. Test each element against the tasks.

### Inconsistent Standard Application

Mixing RDA and AACR2, or applying rules selectively, breaks collocation. Adopt a standard and apply it consistently.

### Neglecting Authority Control

Without authority records, variant names scatter a creator's works. Maintain authorities alongside descriptions.

### Failing To Record Relationships

Treating each item as isolated hides related editions, translations, and source works. Use linking fields and relationship designators.

## Self-Check

- Does each recorded element serve at least one of the find, identify, select, obtain tasks?
- Is information transcribed from the correct chief source, faithfully and with bracketed corrections?
- Is the description depth matched to the item's value, use, and institutional policy?
- Are ambiguities resolved through documented judgment visible in notes?
- Does the record describe the specific manifestation in hand, including local peculiarities?
- Are relationships to related works, expressions, and manifestations recorded?
- Is the current descriptive standard applied consistently across the collection?
- Are access points supported by authority control with variants recorded?
- Is local practice documented for consistency across staff and time?
- Would another cataloger reproduce this record from the documentation and the item?
