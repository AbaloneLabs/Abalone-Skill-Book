# Nonbook And Audiovisual Description

Nonbook materials, video, audio, still and moving images, three-dimensional objects, kits, games, and mixed media, are not text in another shape. They have their own sources of information, their own descriptive elements, and their own discovery needs. A common error is to treat a film or a recording as if it were a book with a different physical description, applying the title page logic of monographs to resources that have no title page. Nonbook items often carry their identifying information in credits, containers, labels, menus, or accompanying documentation, and the choice of source shapes the entire record. They also require format-specific elements: duration, sound and color characteristics, file types, system requirements, and the relationship between the intellectual content and its many possible carriers. Describing nonbook materials well means understanding each format's conventions, choosing the correct source of information, and recording the elements that make these resources discoverable and usable, rather than forcing them into a monograph mold.

Use this skill when cataloging video, audio, images, objects, kits, games, and mixed media, when choosing sources of information for nonbook materials, and when describing format-specific characteristics. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying monograph logic to nonbook formats, from choosing the wrong source of information, from omitting format-specific elements, or from neglecting the relationship between content and carrier.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Correct Source Of Information

Nonbook materials do not have a single equivalent of a title page. The source of information varies by format, and choosing correctly is foundational.

Sources vary by format:

- **Video and film:** title frames or credits, or the container if no title frame exists.
- **Sound recordings:** the disc or tape label, or the container.
- **Images and graphics:** the image itself, accompanying text, or the mat or frame.
- **Three-dimensional objects:** the object itself, a base or label, or accompanying documentation.
- **Kits and games:** the container, a title piece, or accompanying documentation.
- **Online media:** the resource itself (title screen, embedded metadata), or the source file.

Follow the prescribed order of preference for each format. The source chosen determines the title proper and other transcribed elements.

### Transcribe The Title Proper From The Chosen Source

Once the source is chosen, transcribe the title proper exactly as it appears, applying the same transcription discipline as for monographs.

Transcribe:

- the title proper exactly, preserving wording, order, and spelling;
- parallel titles and other title information where present;
- statements of responsibility from the chosen source;
- variant titles from other sources in notes.

The title proper from the correct source is the anchor of the record.

### Identify And Record Statements Of Responsibility

Nonbook materials often have complex or distributed responsibility: directors, performers, producers, composers, animators, and studios. Identify and record these appropriately.

Record:

- primary creators and performers as stated in the source;
- the form of statement (e.g., "directed by," "performed by");
- corporate bodies where they have primary responsibility;
- the relationship of contributors to the work.

Use authorized access points for significant creators, and consider notes for extensive credits that cannot all be access points.

### Describe Format-Specific Physical Characteristics

Nonbook materials require format-specific physical and technical details that have no monograph equivalent.

Record for relevant formats:

- **Video:** duration, sound (sound/silent), color (color/b&w), aspect ratio, video format.
- **Audio:** duration, type of sound (mono, stereo, surround), recording format.
- **Images:** color, process, dimensions, mounting.
- **Objects:** material, technique, dimensions, markings.
- **Digital files:** file type, encoding, resolution, file size, system requirements.

Format-specific details are essential for use; a patron must know whether a video has sound, color, and the right region or format.

### Handle Duration And Extent Carefully

Duration and extent for nonbook materials are critical for access and use. Record them accurately.

Record:

- the duration of moving image and sound recordings;
- the extent of image sets, kits, or object collections;
- the number and type of units (discs, reels, pieces);
- any variations or segments within the resource.

Imprecise duration or extent makes the resource harder to use and to manage.

### Describe Accompanying Material

Nonbook items frequently come with accompanying material: booklets, inserts, manuals, maps, or digital supplements. Describe these appropriately.

Options:

- describe accompanying material in a note;
- describe it as part of the extent where significant;
- create a separate analytical record for substantial accompanying material;
- note the relationship clearly so patrons understand what is included.

Accompanying material is often important for use and should not be silently ignored.

### Address Multiple Carriers And Versions

A single intellectual work may appear on multiple carriers (e.g., a film on DVD, streaming, and Blu-ray) or in multiple versions. Decide how to handle this.

Address:

- whether to describe one iteration or multiple iterations;
- the relationship between the intellectual content and its carriers;
- version differences (director's cut, remastered, edited);
- format-specific details for each carrier described.

The relationship between content and carrier is central to nonbook description.

### Use Format-Appropriate Subject And Genre Access

Nonbook materials benefit from format-specific subject and genre terms that text resources may not.

Use:

- genre and form terms appropriate to the medium (e.g., documentary films, audiobooks, board games);
- subject analysis that reflects the content as expressed in the medium;
- standardized thesauri for medium-specific terms;
- access points that reflect how patrons search for the format.

Format-appropriate subject and genre access improves discovery for nonbook collections.

### Note System Requirements And Access Conditions

For digital and playable media, system requirements and access conditions are essential.

Note:

- hardware and software requirements for digital files and games;
- region codes, format compatibility, and DRM for media;
- licensing and access restrictions for streaming or licensed content;
- any special equipment needed for use in the library.

Without these notes, patrons may borrow or access material they cannot use.

## Common Traps

### Applying Monograph Logic To Nonbook Materials

Nonbook formats have different sources, elements, and access needs. Do not force them into a monograph mold.

### Choosing The Wrong Source Of Information

The source of information varies by format. Follow the prescribed order for each format.

### Omitting Format-Specific Physical Characteristics

Duration, sound, color, file type, and system requirements are essential for use. Record them.

### Ignoring Accompanying Material

Booklets, inserts, and supplements are often important. Describe them appropriately.

### Confusing Content And Carrier

A work may appear on multiple carriers. Decide how to describe the relationship clearly.

### Overloading Or Under-Representing Responsibility

Complex credits need judgment. Record primary creators as access points and note extensive credits.

### Using Text-Centric Subject And Genre Terms

Nonbook materials benefit from medium-specific genre and form terms. Use appropriate thesauri.

### Forgetting System Requirements And Access Notes

Digital and playable media need hardware, software, and access notes for usability.

## Self-Check

- Did you choose the correct prescribed source of information for the format?
- Did you transcribe the title proper and statements of responsibility accurately from that source?
- Did you identify and record primary creators and significant contributors appropriately?
- Did you describe format-specific physical and technical characteristics (duration, sound, color, file type)?
- Did you record duration and extent accurately, including segments and variations?
- Did you handle accompanying material appropriately, in notes, extent, or analytical records?
- Did you address multiple carriers and versions and the content-carrier relationship?
- Did you use format-appropriate subject and genre terms from relevant thesauri?
- Did you note system requirements, region codes, DRM, and access conditions for digital and playable media?
- Could a patron determine from the record whether they can access and use this resource?
