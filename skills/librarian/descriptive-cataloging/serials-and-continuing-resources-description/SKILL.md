# Serials And Continuing Resources Description

Serials and continuing resources break the assumptions of monograph cataloging. A monograph is a stable, finite item: one title, one publication, one description. A serial is an ongoing, evolving publication that may change its title, publisher, numbering, frequency, or scope over a run that spans decades. Describing serials requires a different mental model and a different set of rules. The cataloger must decide when a change is significant enough to require a new record (successive entry) versus when it can be noted on an existing record (latest entry or integrated entry), must capture the complex history of a title through linking relationships, and must record numbering, frequency, and publication patterns that have no analogue in monograph work. Treating serials like monographs produces records that misrepresent the publication's history, break the links between related records, and confuse patrons trying to find a specific issue or title. Serials description is a discipline of representing change, stability, and relationship over time.

Use this skill when cataloging serials, journals, integrating resources, and other continuing resources, when handling title changes and successive records, and when managing numbering, frequency, and linking relationships. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying monograph logic to serials, from mishandling title changes, from omitting linking relationships, or from failing to capture the distinctive elements that make serial records useful.

## Core Rules

### Adopt The Serials Mindset: Describe Change Over Time

The fundamental shift in serials cataloging is to describe an ongoing entity that changes, not a single stable publication.

This means:

- the record represents the publication over a span of time, not one issue;
- changes in title, numbering, publisher, or scope are expected and must be represented;
- the history and relationships between records matter as much as the current state;
- earlier and later titles must be linked so patrons can navigate the run;
- the record must support finding any issue across the publication's history.

Approaching a serial as if it were a monograph produces incomplete and misleading records.

### Apply The Correct Mode: Successive Versus Latest Versus Integrated Entry

Different resource types require different approaches to change. Apply the correct mode.

Modes include:

- **Successive entry:** a major change (e.g., a major title change) creates a new record, with links to earlier and later titles. Used for serials under standards like RDA and ISSN rules.
- **Latest entry:** the current record is updated to reflect the latest title, with notes for earlier titles. (Largely historical now.)
- **Integrated entry:** for integrating resources (e.g., loose-leafs, updating websites, databases), the record is updated in place to reflect the current state, with change notes.

Using the wrong mode misrepresents the resource and breaks discovery.

### Identify Major Versus Minor Changes For Successive Entry

Under successive entry, the cataloger must judge whether a change is major (new record) or minor (note on existing record). This judgment drives the structure of the record set.

Major changes (typically new record):

- a major change in the title proper;
- a change in the corporate body responsible, when that body is the title proper;
- a fundamental change in scope or content.

Minor changes (note on existing record):

- minor wording changes, additions, or reordering in the title;
- changes in numbering or frequency;
- changes in publisher or place that do not affect the title proper.

Standards provide detailed guidance; apply it consistently.

### Capture Numbering And Chronology Accurately

Serials are identified and accessed by their numbering and chronology. Record these precisely.

Record:

- the first and latest issue examined, with numbering and dates;
- numbering patterns (volume, issue, part);
- chronology (year, month, season);
- alternative or parallel numbering;
- any gaps, restarts, or anomalies in numbering.

Accurate numbering and chronology lets patrons and staff locate specific issues and understand the run.

### Record Frequency And Publication Patterns

Frequency and publication patterns help patrons and staff manage expectations and check holdings.

Record:

- the current frequency (monthly, quarterly, irregular);
- changes in frequency over the run;
- publication patterns where relevant;
- dates of publication versus dates of coverage.

Frequency notes support claiming, holdings maintenance, and patron understanding.

### Establish Linking Relationships Between Records

Serials records are connected. Linking relationships let patrons navigate a title's full history.

Establish links for:

- successive titles (earlier and later);
- mergers and splits;
- supplements and parent publications;
- language editions and translations;
- reproductions and originals.

Without links, related records are isolated and patrons cannot trace a publication across its changes.

### Describe The Physical Or Digital Format Appropriately

Serials appear in print, digital, and microform, often simultaneously. Describe the format of the iteration being cataloged.

Address:

- the specific iteration being described (print, online, microform);
- the source of title proper for that iteration;
- format-specific details (e.g., file characteristics for online, dimensions for microform);
- whether multiple iterations are described on one record or separate records are used;
- access and licensing notes for digital serials.

Format-appropriate description prevents confusion across iterations.

### Use Authorized Access Points And Series Consistently

Serials often have corporate authors, uniform titles, and series relationships that require authority control.

Establish:

- the correct authorized access point for the serial (title proper, corporate body, uniform title);
- series authority records where the serial is part of a series;
- consistent forms for bodies that change name over the run;
- cross-references for variant titles.

Consistent authority work makes serials findable across variant titles and name changes.

### Maintain Holdings And Check-In Information

Serials description supports ongoing holdings management. Coordinate description with holdings.

Coordinate:

- holdings statements that reflect the actual run held;
- check-in and receipt patterns consistent with the description;
- gaps and missing issues noted in holdings;
- electronic holdings and access links kept current.

Description and holdings must agree, or patrons are misled about availability.

### Follow Current Standards And Keep Records Updated

Serials cataloging standards evolve, and serials themselves change over time. Keep records current.

Follow:

- current descriptive standards (e.g., RDA, ISSN rules) and local practice;
- updates when major changes occur in the serial;
- periodic review of serial records for accuracy;
- cooperative cataloging and CONSER or equivalent program standards where applicable.

Stale serial records accumulate errors that mislead patrons and staff.

## Common Traps

### Applying Monograph Logic To Serials

Serials are ongoing and changing. Describe the entity over time, not as a single stable item.

### Mishandling Title Changes

A major title change requires a new record under successive entry. Misjudging major versus minor changes breaks the record set.

### Omitting Linking Relationships

Without links to earlier, later, and related titles, patrons cannot navigate the run.

### Inaccurate Numbering And Chronology

Imprecise numbering makes specific issues impossible to locate and verify.

### Ignoring Frequency And Pattern Changes

Frequency changes affect claiming and holdings. Record them accurately.

### Confusing Iterations And Formats

Print, online, and microform iterations may need separate records or careful notes. Describe the correct one.

### Inconsistent Authority Work

Variant titles and changing corporate names require authority control and cross-references.

### Letting Records Go Stale

Serials change and standards evolve. Update records when changes occur and review periodically.

## Self-Check

- Did you adopt the serials mindset, describing an ongoing changing entity rather than a single item?
- Did you apply the correct mode (successive, latest, integrated entry) for the resource type?
- Did you correctly judge major versus minor changes for successive entry decisions?
- Did you capture numbering and chronology accurately, including anomalies?
- Did you record frequency and publication patterns and their changes?
- Did you establish linking relationships to earlier, later, and related titles?
- Did you describe the correct physical or digital iteration with format-appropriate details?
- Did you use authorized access points and series authority records consistently?
- Did you coordinate the description with accurate holdings and check-in information?
- Did you follow current standards and keep the record updated as the serial changes?
