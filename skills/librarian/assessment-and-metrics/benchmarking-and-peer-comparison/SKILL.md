# Benchmarking And Peer Comparison

Benchmarking is the practice of understanding a library by comparing it to others. Done well, it provides perspective that internal data alone cannot: it reveals whether a number is high or low relative to context, surfaces practices worth adopting, and gives funders and governance a basis for judgment. Done poorly, it produces false confidence, destructive imitation, or defensive excuses. The judgment problem is that comparison only means something if the peers are genuinely comparable, the metrics are defined the same way, and the differences are interpreted rather than merely reported. A library that benchmarks headcount against a peer with twice the service hours draws the wrong conclusion. A library that copies a peer's service model without understanding the context imports a solution to a problem it does not have. Good benchmarking is humble about comparability, curious about the reasons behind differences, and disciplined about using comparison to learn rather than to justify.

Agents helping with benchmarking tend to err by selecting non-comparable peers, by treating metric definitions as equivalent when they are not, by reading gaps as failures without understanding causes, or by using benchmarking to validate rather than to investigate. This skill covers the judgment needed to benchmark in a way that generates genuine insight rather than false reassurance.

## Core Rules

### Benchmark To Learn, Not To Validate

The purpose of benchmarking is to understand and improve, not to confirm what you already believe. Benchmarking that only seeks favorable comparisons is advocacy, not analysis.

Adopt a learning stance by:

- seeking peers both stronger and weaker on metrics of interest;
- examining practices behind the numbers, not just the numbers;
- being open to finding that the library is behind where it assumed strength;
- using gaps as questions to investigate, not verdicts to defend;
- sharing findings honestly, including uncomfortable ones.

Validation-seeking benchmarking finds what it looks for and misses what matters. Learning-seeking benchmarking surfaces real opportunities.

### Select Genuinely Comparable Peers

The single most important decision in benchmarking is peer selection. Non-comparable peers make every comparison meaningless.

Select peers by considering:

- mission and type, research university, liberal arts college, public library system size;
- scale, enrollment, service population, budget, FTE;
- scope, branches, disciplines served, special collections;
- governance and funding model;
- geography and cost of living where relevant;
- maturity of comparable services.

A peer that looks similar on one dimension may differ critically on another. Document why each peer was selected and what makes it comparable. Aspirational peers can be included but must be labeled as such, not treated as direct comparators.

### Verify That Metrics Are Defined The Same Way

Identically named metrics are often defined differently across institutions. Comparing them without checking produces false gaps and false comfort.

Verify definitions for:

- what is counted, total circulations versus unique items, gate counts versus occupied seats;
- the time period and population denominator;
- inclusions and exclusions, what staff categories count as FTE;
- data collection methods and systems;
- how consortial or shared resources are treated;
- whether the metric is audited or self-reported.

Where definitions differ, either adjust the data to a common basis or flag the comparison as approximate. Honest approximation beats precise-looking false comparison.

### Interpret Gaps As Questions, Not Verdicts

A gap between the library and peers is the beginning of investigation, not the end. Gaps have many causes, only some of which indicate a problem.

Investigate causes by asking:

- is the gap real or a definitional artifact;
- does it reflect different priorities, which may be legitimate;
- does it reflect different contexts, scale, funding, demographics;
- does it reflect different practices worth examining or adopting;
- does it reflect an actual deficiency requiring action;
- what is the trend, is the gap widening, narrowing, stable.

A library may legitimately spend less on a collection area because a consortium covers it, or more on access services because of longer hours. Understand before judging.

### Use Multiple Metrics And Avoid Single-Number Thinking

A single benchmark metric is easily misleading. Context comes from patterns across multiple measures.

Use complementary metrics such as:

- inputs, budget, staff, space, alongside outputs and outcomes;
- ratios, spending per student, items per capita, alongside totals;
- efficiency and quality measures together;
- trend data over time alongside point-in-time comparison;
- both quantitative metrics and qualitative practice comparison.

A library low on one metric may be strong on a related one. Single-number benchmarking invites wrong conclusions; multi-metric benchmarking reveals the real picture.

### Combine Quantitative Benchmarking With Practice Visits

Numbers show where a gap exists; practice inquiry shows why and what to do about it. The strongest benchmarking combines both.

Combine by:

- using data to identify gaps worth investigating;
- conducting site visits, calls, or document exchange with peer libraries;
- asking how peers achieve their results, processes, staffing, technology;
- assessing transferability to the local context;
- piloting adopted practices before full implementation.

Practices that work in one context may fail in another. Understanding the conditions behind success is essential before transferring it.

### Set Targets In Context, Not Against Arbitrary Peers

Benchmarking can inform target-setting, but targets pulled directly from peer medians or top quartiles without context become arbitrary.

Set targets by:

- understanding where the library stands and why;
- identifying peers whose practices and results are worth emulating;
- considering what is achievable given local constraints and priorities;
- aligning targets with strategic priorities, not just peer averages;
- building in timeframes and intermediate milestones;
- revisiting targets as context and data evolve.

A target to reach peer median on a metric may be too modest or too ambitious depending on starting point and constraints. Context, not comparison alone, should set targets.

### Be Transparent About Sources, Definitions, And Limits

Benchmarking loses credibility when its basis is opaque. Transparency allows scrutiny and builds trust.

Document and share:

- the data source and year for each metric;
- the exact definition used by each institution;
- any adjustments made for comparability;
- response rates or data quality caveats;
- which peers were included and why;
- what the comparison cannot tell you.

Transparency protects against both internal overclaiming and external challenge. A benchmark that can be inspected is a benchmark that can be trusted.

### Use Benchmarking To Support, Not Replace, Local Judgment

Peer comparison is one input among many. It should inform local decisions, not dictate them.

Balance benchmarking with:

- the library's own mission, priorities, and community needs;
- local constraints, budget, governance, staffing, space;
- qualitative understanding of the community;
- outcome and satisfaction data from local users;
- strategic direction that may legitimately diverge from peers.

A library that does everything exactly like its peers has no distinctive strategy. Benchmarking should sharpen local judgment, not substitute for it.

### Refresh Peers And Metrics Periodically

Contexts change. A peer set chosen a decade ago may no longer be comparable, and metrics that mattered then may not matter now.

Refresh by:

- reviewing peer composition every few years against current comparability;
- retiring metrics that no longer reflect strategic priorities;
- adding metrics for emerging service areas;
- tracking whether the field's benchmarking standards have evolved;
- re-examining definitions as systems and practices change.

Stale benchmarking produces stale insight. Periodic refresh keeps comparison relevant.

## Common Traps

### Benchmarking To Validate

Seeking only favorable comparisons misses real opportunities. The trap is confirmation. Benchmark to learn.

### Non-Comparable Peers

Comparing to institutions that differ in scale, mission, or scope produces meaningless gaps. The trap is false legitimacy. Select and document comparable peers.

### Assuming Metric Equivalence

Identically named metrics defined differently yield false comparison. The trap is precise-looking error. Verify definitions.

### Reading Gaps As Failures

A gap may reflect legitimate priority differences or context, not deficiency. The trap is defensive or self-flagellating reaction. Investigate causes.

### Single-Metric Thinking

One benchmark number is easily misleading. The trap is false clarity. Use multiple complementary metrics.

### Copying Practices Without Context

Adopting a peer's practice without understanding its conditions often fails locally. The trap is imitation. Investigate transferability.

### Arbitrary Targets

Targets set at peer median or top quartile without context are ungrounded. The trap is benchmark-driven rather than strategy-driven goals. Set targets in context.

### Opaque Method

Benchmarking without documented sources and definitions cannot withstand scrutiny. The trap is unchallengeable-looking but fragile numbers. Be transparent.

### Letting Peers Dictate Strategy

Doing everything like peers erases distinctive direction. The trap is conformity. Use benchmarking to inform local judgment.

### Stale Peer Sets And Metrics

Outdated peers and metrics produce outdated insight. The trap is inertia. Refresh periodically.

## Self-Check

- Is the benchmarking driven by a learning question rather than a desire to validate existing beliefs?
- Are peers genuinely comparable on mission, scale, scope, governance, and context, with selection documented?
- Have metric definitions been verified as equivalent, with adjustments or caveats where they differ?
- Are gaps interpreted as questions to investigate rather than verdicts to defend or accept?
- Are multiple complementary metrics used rather than relying on a single number?
- Is quantitative benchmarking combined with practice inquiry to understand the why behind the numbers?
- Are targets set in local context rather than copied from peer averages or quartiles?
- Are sources, definitions, adjustments, and limitations documented transparently?
- Does benchmarking inform rather than replace local judgment and strategic direction?
- Are peer sets and metrics refreshed periodically to remain relevant?
- Could a skeptical reviewer inspect the comparison and find it methodologically sound?
