# Borrowing And Lending

Interlibrary loan is how libraries share resources so that no single institution must own everything its users need. The operation looks transactional, submit a request, receive a copy or a loan, but it involves judgment about routing, cost, speed, lending policy, and relationship management. Borrowing decisions affect how fast patrons get materials and at what cost. Lending decisions affect staff workload, the institution's standing in the resource-sharing community, and the security of its collections. Good ILL management balances patron needs against cost and turnaround, maintains fair and reciprocal lending relationships, follows copyright and license constraints, and treats each transaction as part of a cooperative system rather than an isolated favor.

Use this skill when managing ILL borrowing or lending, choosing request routes and suppliers, setting lending policies, or handling partner relationships. The goal is to prevent the agent from routing requests without cost or speed analysis, declining lending reflexively, ignoring copyright and license limits, or treating ILL as isolated transactions rather than cooperative resource sharing.

## Core Rules

### Treat ILL As Cooperative Resource Sharing

ILL is not a vendor service; it is a reciprocal cooperative system. Libraries borrow and lend, and the health of the system depends on participation from all.

Cooperative principles:

- lend as generously as you expect to borrow;
- honor turnaround commitments to lending partners;
- communicate clearly about problems and cancellations;
- follow shared protocols and standards;
- contribute to and use shared infrastructure and union catalogs;
- treat lending partners with the professionalism you expect as a borrower.

Libraries that borrow freely but lend reluctantly erode the system and eventually find partners reluctant to serve them.

### Route Borrowing Requests By Cost And Speed

Borrowing requests can often be filled through multiple routes, each with different cost and speed. Routing decisions should be deliberate, not habitual.

Routing considerations:

- local ownership or licensed electronic access first, avoiding ILL entirely;
- consortial or regional partners, often faster and free or low-cost;
- national systems and networks for broader reach;
- direct requests to likely holding libraries;
- commercial document delivery for speed when cost is justified;
- international lending for materials not available domestically.

Build routing logic that prioritizes free or low-cost sources, then escalates to paid sources based on urgency and budget. Track fill rates and turnaround by route to refine the logic.

### Manage Borrowing Costs And Subsidies

ILL borrowing has costs, sometimes borne by the library and sometimes by the patron. Cost policy affects both access equity and budget.

Cost management:

- decide whether the library subsidizes all ILL or passes costs to patrons;
- set a per-request cost ceiling above which patron approval is needed;
- use free or consortial sources first to minimize cost;
- track average cost per request and total borrowing expenditure;
- consider fee caps and deposit accounts with high-volume suppliers.

Cost should not block access for patrons who cannot pay, but uncontrolled costs strain budgets. Balance subsidy with sustainability.

### Set Lending Policies Clearly

Lending policies determine what the institution will and will not lend, to whom, and under what conditions. Clear policies make lending consistent and defensible.

Lending policy elements:

- what materials are lendable versus restricted, reference, special collections, reserves;
- loan periods and renewal policies for lent materials;
- fees, if any, for lending;
- formats lent, physical loans versus copies, and electronic delivery;
- restrictions on who may borrow the borrowed material, in-library use only;
- insurance and tracking requirements for high-value loans;
- geographic scope, regional, national, international.

Publish lending policies so borrowing partners know what to expect, reducing unnecessary requests and cancellations.

### Balance Lending Generosity With Collection Security

Lending puts materials in transit and out of direct control. Balance the commitment to resource sharing with protection of the collection.

Security measures:

- restrict lending of rare, fragile, high-value, or in-demand materials;
- require insurance or tracking for valuable loans;
- use secure packaging and trackable shipping;
- set reasonable loan periods that allow return without excessive risk;
- charge replacement fees for lost or damaged lent materials;
- monitor lending patterns to detect excessive loss with specific partners.

Generosity and security are not opposed; both are served by clear policies and careful handling.

### Track And Manage Turnaround

Turnaround, the time from request to fulfillment, is the key service metric for borrowing. Patrons and faculty judge ILL by speed.

Manage turnaround:

- process incoming requests promptly, daily or more often;
- choose faster routes for urgent requests;
- track average turnaround and set service targets;
- communicate realistic expectations to patrons;
- identify and address bottlenecks in the workflow.

Slow turnaround often stems from processing delays rather than lending partner speed. Audit the internal workflow.

### Handle Problematic Partners Professionally

Some borrowing or lending partners are chronically slow, lose materials, or violate policies. Manage these relationships professionally but firmly.

Approaches:

- track performance by partner, turnaround, loss rate, communication;
- communicate problems directly and document them;
- escalate persistent issues through the network or consortium;
- suspend lending to partners with excessive loss or non-payment, with notice;
- maintain professional courtesy even when enforcing consequences.

The cooperative system depends on accountability. Tolerating abuse from partners harms all participants.

### Follow Copyright And License Constraints

ILL is governed by copyright law and by the licenses of electronic resources. Violations create legal risk and can trigger license audits.

Constraints:

- follow CONTU guidelines or national equivalents for article copy limits;
- respect license terms that may prohibit ILL for specific e-resources;
- include copyright notices on supplied copies;
- track copy requests to stay within guideline limits;
- refuse requests that would violate license or law.

Copyright and license compliance protects the institution and the cooperative system. Document the basis for supplied copies.

### Use Technology And Standards Effectively

ILL efficiency depends on standards and technology that automate request routing, tracking, and communication.

Use:

- standardized protocols like ISO 18626 or the relevant national ILL standard;
- shared request management systems that integrate with catalogs and union lists;
- automated routing and status updates;
- electronic delivery for copies where permitted;
- integration with circulation for lending check-in and tracking.

Manual, email-based ILL does not scale and invites errors. Invest in standards-based systems.

### Evaluate And Report ILL Performance

ILL performance data supports staffing decisions, budget requests, and service improvement.

Track and report:

- borrowing fill rate and turnaround;
- lending volume and turnaround;
- cost per request and total expenditure;
- top requested subjects and sources;
- unfulfilled requests and reasons;
- partner performance metrics.

Data demonstrates ILL's value to administrators and identifies improvement opportunities.

## Common Traps

### Borrowing Freely But Lending Reluctantly

Free-riding erodes the cooperative system. Lend as you expect to borrow.

### Habitual Routing Without Analysis

Defaulting to one supplier regardless of cost or speed wastes budget and slows service. Build deliberate routing logic.

### Cost Blocking Access For Vulnerable Patrons

Passing all costs to patrons blocks those who cannot pay. Subsidize strategically.

### Vague Lending Policies

Unclear policies cause unnecessary requests and cancellations. Publish clear lending terms.

### Lending Restricted Or Fragile Materials

Lending what should not travel risks loss and damage. Restrict lendable materials clearly.

### Slow Internal Processing

Turnaround often suffers from internal delays, not partner speed. Audit and streamline workflows.

### Tolerating Abusive Partners

Chronic loss or non-payment harms the system. Track and address partner problems.

### Ignoring Copyright And License Limits

Violations create legal and contractual risk. Follow guidelines and license terms.

## Self-Check

- Is ILL treated as reciprocal cooperative resource sharing, with lending matching borrowing expectations?
- Are borrowing requests routed deliberately by cost and speed, with free and consortial sources prioritized?
- Are borrowing costs managed with subsidies that do not block access for vulnerable patrons?
- Are lending policies clear, published, and consistent, covering materials, periods, fees, and formats?
- Is collection security balanced with lending generosity through restrictions, insurance, and tracking?
- Is turnaround tracked and managed, with internal bottlenecks identified and addressed?
- Are problematic partners managed professionally but firmly, with performance tracked and consequences enforced?
- Are copyright and license constraints followed, with copy limits tracked and notices included?
- Are standards-based ILL technologies used to automate routing, tracking, and delivery?
- Is ILL performance evaluated and reported to demonstrate value and guide improvement?
