# Copyright And Licensing ILL

Interlibrary loan operates within a web of copyright law and licensing terms that govern what may be copied, lent, and delivered. These constraints are not bureaucratic obstacles; they are the legal and contractual basis on which resource sharing depends. Violating them exposes the institution to liability and can trigger license audits or termination of access to expensive resources. At the same time, over-compliance, refusing permissible requests out of excessive caution, denies users access to materials they have a right to. Good copyright and licensing practice for ILL understands the relevant guidelines, applies them consistently, checks license terms before acting, documents the basis for decisions, and knows when to seek permission or escalate.

Use this skill when handling copyright or license questions for ILL, applying copy guidelines, checking license permissions, managing document delivery, or deciding when permission is required. The goal is to prevent the agent from supplying copies beyond guideline limits, ignoring license restrictions, refusing permissible requests out of fear, or failing to document the compliance basis.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Copyright Framework For ILL

ILL copying is governed by copyright law, specifically the provisions that allow libraries to reproduce works under certain conditions, supplemented by guidelines that interpret fair use for the ILL context.

Key elements:

- library copying exceptions in copyright law that permit certain reproductions;
- fair use, which is flexible but uncertain and fact-specific;
- CONTU guidelines in the US, or national equivalents, which provide a safe harbor for article copying;
- the distinction between the law, which sets outer limits, and guidelines, which offer practical rules.

Understand which framework applies in your jurisdiction. Guidelines are not law, but following them provides protection and consistency.

### Apply CONTU Or National Copy Guidelines Consistently

CONTU guidelines, and their equivalents elsewhere, set practical limits on how many copies of articles from a given journal a library may request through ILL within a period, commonly the rule of five within a calendar year.

Apply consistently:

- track copy requests by journal title and year;
- count toward the guideline limit only those requests that fall within its scope;
- when the limit is reached, seek permission or pay royalties for further copies;
- reset counts at the guideline interval, typically annually;
- document the counting and the basis for each request.

Inconsistent or untracked counting leads to inadvertent violations. Use the ILL system's tracking features and audit them.

### Check License Terms Before Supplying Or Requesting

Licensed electronic resources carry terms that may permit, restrict, or prohibit ILL. These terms are contractually binding and override general copyright permissions.

Check:

- the license for each e-resource before using it for ILL;
- whether the license permits ILL lending or document supply;
- any conditions, such as persistent linking only, no downloading, or attribution;
- geographic or user restrictions in the license;
- whether the license prohibits printing or electronic delivery.

Maintain a database or list of license terms for ILL-relevant resources. Supplying from a licensed resource in violation of its terms is a breach of contract, not just a copyright issue.

### Distinguish Permitted From Prohibited Uses

Not all ILL requests are permissible. Distinguishing permitted from prohibited uses requires applying the guidelines and license terms to the specific request.

Permitted typically includes:

- one copy of an article or chapter for a user's private study or research;
- requests within CONTU or guideline limits;
- lending of physical materials the library owns;
- supply from resources whose licenses permit ILL.

Prohibited or requiring permission typically includes:

- copies beyond guideline limits without permission or payment;
- supply from resources whose licenses prohibit ILL;
- copies for systematic distribution, such as repeated course use without clearance;
- copies of entire works or substantial portions beyond fair use.

Apply these distinctions consistently and document the reasoning for borderline cases.

### Document The Compliance Basis For Each Decision

Every ILL copy or supply should have a documented basis, whether it is guideline compliance, license permission, fair use, or paid permission. Documentation protects the institution and supports audit.

Document:

- the copyright basis for supplied copies;
- the license basis for supply from electronic resources;
- permission sought and granted, with dates and fees;
- royalty payments through collective rights organizations;
- requests declined and the reason.

Documentation is the evidence of compliance. Without it, the institution cannot demonstrate good faith if challenged.

### Handle Course Reserve And Document Delivery Carefully

ILL-adjacent services, course reserves and document delivery, carry their own copyright considerations that intersect with ILL practice.

For reserves:

- multiple copies or repeated use across terms may require permission;
- electronic reserves must comply with fair use and license terms;
- persistent linking to licensed content is often the cleanest approach.

For document delivery:

- supplying copies to non-affiliates or for commercial use may exceed library exceptions;
- fee-based document delivery has additional copyright considerations;
- track and limit delivery to stay within guidelines.

These services are not exempt from copyright because they serve education. Apply the same rigor as ILL.

### Know When To Seek Permission Or Pay Royalties

When a request exceeds guideline limits or license permissions, the path forward is to seek permission or pay royalties, not to decline reflexively or to proceed anyway.

Options:

- seek permission directly from the rights holder;
- use a collective rights organization or copyright clearance center;
- pay the royalty and pass the cost per policy;
- decline if permission is denied or cost is prohibitive, and inform the user;
- consider alternative sources or formats.

Seeking permission is a normal part of ILL, not a failure. Build the workflow and budget for it.

### Communicate Constraints To Users Clearly

Users often do not understand why a request is declined, delayed, or costs a fee. Clear communication manages expectations and builds trust.

Communicate:

- why a request may take longer due to permission seeking;
- why a fee may apply for royalty payments;
- why certain materials cannot be supplied electronically;
- what alternatives exist;
- the legal basis, explained accessibly.

Users who understand the constraints are more patient and less likely to push for noncompliant shortcuts.

### Train Staff On Copyright And Licensing

Copyright and licensing compliance depends on staff understanding. Untrained staff make errors that create risk.

Train staff on:

- the copyright framework and guidelines applicable to ILL;
- how to check and interpret license terms;
- how to count and track copy requests;
- when to seek permission or escalate;
- how to document decisions;
- how to explain constraints to users.

Provide reference materials and a clear escalation path for uncertain cases. Copyright questions should not be guessed at.

### Review And Update Practice As Law And Licenses Change

Copyright law, guidelines, and license terms change over time. ILL practice must keep up.

Review periodically:

- changes in copyright law and library exceptions;
- updates to guidelines and safe harbors;
- new and renegotiated licenses and their ILL terms;
- collective rights organization procedures and fees;
- emerging formats and their copyright implications.

Assign responsibility for monitoring changes and updating practice. Stale compliance practice becomes noncompliance.

## Common Traps

### Supplying Beyond Guideline Limits Without Permission

Exceeding CONTU or national limits without permission violates the safe harbor. Track and seek permission when limits are reached.

### Ignoring License Terms

Licenses override general copyright permissions. Supplying from a licensed resource in violation of its terms breaches the contract.

### Refusing Permissible Requests Out Of Fear

Over-compliance denies access users have a right to. Apply guidelines and fair use confidently where they apply.

### No Documentation Of Compliance Basis

Without documentation, the institution cannot demonstrate good faith. Record the basis for each decision.

### Treating Reserves And Document Delivery As Exempt

These services carry their own copyright considerations. Apply the same rigor as ILL.

### Declining Instead Of Seeking Permission

Permission seeking is a normal ILL path. Build the workflow rather than reflexively declining.

### Unclear Communication To Users

Users frustrated by unexplained constraints push for shortcuts. Explain the legal basis accessibly.

### Untrained Staff Guessing At Copyright

Guesses create risk. Train staff and provide escalation paths for uncertain cases.

## Self-Check

- Is the applicable copyright framework, law, exceptions, and guidelines, understood and applied?
- Are CONTU or national copy guidelines applied consistently with tracked counting?
- Are license terms checked before supplying from or requesting electronic resources?
- Are permitted and prohibited uses distinguished and documented for each request?
- Is the compliance basis documented for every copy or supply decision?
- Are course reserves and document delivery handled with the same copyright rigor as ILL?
- Is permission sought or royalties paid when limits are exceeded, rather than declining or proceeding?
- Are copyright and licensing constraints communicated clearly and accessibly to users?
- Are ILL staff trained on copyright, licensing, counting, documentation, and escalation?
- Is practice reviewed and updated as law, guidelines, and licenses change?
