# Evaluating And Maintaining Reference Collections

A reference collection is not a static honor guard of important-looking books. It is a working set of sources that must answer the community's current questions accurately. Reference sources age, go digital, get superseded, and drift out of alignment with what patrons actually ask. A reference collection that is never evaluated becomes a graveyard of outdated authority: statutes from repealed eras, medical guides predating current guidelines, encyclopedias whose articles have been corrected by later scholarship, and print sets whose online versions the library also licenses. Maintaining a reference collection is continuous judgment about what to add, what to keep, what to move, and what to retire, balanced against budget, space, and the community's real questions. Neglect turns a reference collection from an asset into a source of wrong answers.

Use this skill when selecting new reference sources, when weeding or auditing the existing reference collection, when deciding between print and digital, and when allocating the reference budget. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating reference as a set-and-forget collection, from keeping superseded editions out of inertia, and from buying breadth that the community never uses while missing depth it needs.

## Core Rules

### Align The Reference Collection With Community Need

The reference collection exists to answer the community's questions, not to mirror a generic ideal of what a library should own. A public library, a school library, an academic library, a law library, and a medical library need radically different reference collections, and even libraries of the same type differ by community.

Assess need by:

- analyzing the questions actually asked at the reference desk and through digital channels;
- identifying the domains, languages, and levels your community uses;
- consulting subject faculty, teachers, community organizations, and patron surveys;
- tracking which reference sources are used and which sit untouched;
- recognizing underserved communities whose needs are not yet reflected in the collection.

A reference collection perfectly curated for the wrong community is a failure of selection.

### Apply Clear Selection Criteria To Reference Sources

Reference sources cost more and age faster than circulating materials, so selection criteria must be deliberate. Evaluate each candidate against the same standards you would apply to any authoritative source, plus reference-specific considerations.

Selection criteria:

- authority: publisher, editorial board, author credentials, and disciplinary reputation;
- accuracy: checked against known facts and compared to established sources;
- scope: breadth, depth, time period, geography, and audience level;
- currency: revision cycle and date of the latest edition;
- arrangement and access: indexing, cross-references, and findability;
- format: print, online, or licensed, and the trade-offs of each;
- cost relative to expected use and to alternative sources;
- overlap with sources already held or licensed;
- reviews in professional reference literature.

A source that is authoritative but redundant with one already held may not be worth the cost; a source that fills a real gap may be worth a premium.

### Decide Between Print And Digital Deliberately

The print-versus-digital decision for reference is not ideological; it is practical, and it differs by source and by community. Some sources are better in print, some better online, and many are best held in both for different uses.

Consider:

- currency needs: fast-changing domains favor online; stable reference works may suit print;
- access: can the community reach the digital source, and is it licensed for them?;
- browsing and serendipity: some print sources reward browsing that search does not;
- simultaneous users: licensed digital often allows more concurrent use than a single print copy;
- space and preservation: print consumes space but does not disappear when a license ends;
- cost models: one-time print purchase versus ongoing subscription or license.

Avoid defaulting to either format. Choose per source based on how it will actually be used.

### Weed Superseded And Outdated Authority Relentlessly

The most important maintenance task in a reference collection is removing sources whose authority has expired. An outdated reference source is worse than a missing one, because it delivers wrong answers with the library's implicit endorsement.

Weed when:

- a newer edition supersedes the current one, unless the older edition has distinct research value;
- the content has been corrected or overturned by later scholarship;
- statutes, regulations, guidelines, or statistics have changed;
- the source's facts, names, borders, or values are no longer current;
- a licensed digital version fully replaces the print and is reliably accessible;
- the source is no longer used and holds no archival value.

For legal, medical, financial, scientific, and statistical reference, currency is a safety issue. Weed aggressively and document the reasoning.

### Balance Breadth, Depth, And Budget

No reference budget covers everything. Allocate deliberately between breadth (covering many domains at a basic level) and depth (covering fewer domains richly), guided by community need.

Balance by:

- funding core ready-reference across all domains the community uses;
- investing depth where the community's questions concentrate;
- using licensed packages and consortial deals to stretch breadth;
- reserving funds for high-value specialized sources that fill real gaps;
- avoiding prestige purchases that look impressive but go unused;
- reviewing cost-per-use for expensive subscriptions regularly.

A reference budget spent on impressive but unused sets is budget not spent on the sources patrons actually need.

### Track Use And Evaluate Continuously

Reference collections decay in usefulness silently. Use data and evaluation must be continuous, not occasional.

Track:

- use of print reference (shelf counts, consultation logs where feasible);
- use of licensed digital reference (sessions, searches, full-text downloads);
- questions the collection could not answer, signaling gaps;
- questions answered by sources not in the collection, signaling selection needs;
- cost-per-use for expensive sources;
- feedback from reference staff and patrons.

Use the data to reallocate. A source with strong and growing use may deserve deeper investment; one with declining use may be a weeding candidate.

### Preserve Distinctive And Archival Reference Value

Not every older reference source should be weeded. Some retain value as historical or archival reference: older editions that document a field at a moment, statistical compendia whose data is not available elsewhere, or local and regional reference works. Distinguish current authority (weed when superseded) from archival value (retain, possibly relocate to special collections).

### Coordinate With Interlibrary Loan And Consortial Access

No reference collection is self-sufficient. Coordinate with interlibrary loan and consortial agreements to extend access to specialized sources the library cannot own. Knowing what can be borrowed or accessed collaboratively lets you focus local collection budget on what must be on-site or immediately available.

### Document Selection And Weeding Decisions

Reference collection decisions should be documented against a written collection development policy. Documentation makes the collection defensible, supports continuity when staff change, and provides evidence during budget reviews or challenges. Record what was added, what was weeded, and why.

## Common Traps

### Treating Reference As Set-And-Forget

Without continuous evaluation, the collection drifts into outdated authority and irrelevance.

### Keeping Superseded Editions Out Of Inertia

An outdated reference source delivers wrong answers with the library's endorsement. Weed it.

### Defaulting To One Format

Print and digital each suit different sources and uses. Choose deliberately.

### Buying Prestige Over Need

Impressive reference sets that go unused waste budget that real community needs require.

### Ignoring Use Data

Without tracking use, the collection cannot be reallocated toward what the community actually needs.

### Over-Weeding Archival Value

Some older reference works retain historical value. Distinguish current authority from archival reference.

### Redundant Purchases

A source that overlaps one already held may not be worth the cost, even if authoritative.

### Misalignment With Community

A collection curated for a generic ideal rather than the actual community fails its purpose.

## Self-Check

- Is the reference collection aligned with the actual questions, domains, languages, and levels your community uses?
- Are selection criteria (authority, accuracy, scope, currency, access, format, cost, overlap, reviews) applied to every addition?
- Are print-versus-digital decisions made deliberately per source rather than by default?
- Are superseded and outdated sources weeded relentlessly, especially in legal, medical, financial, scientific, and statistical reference?
- Is the budget balanced between breadth and depth based on community need and cost-per-use?
- Is use tracked continuously and used to reallocate toward gaps and away from unused sources?
- Are distinctive and archival reference works preserved or relocated rather than weeded?
- Is the collection coordinated with interlibrary loan and consortial access to extend reach?
- Are selection and weeding decisions documented against a written collection development policy?
