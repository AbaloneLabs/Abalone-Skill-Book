# Ready Reference And Fact Checking

Ready reference is the everyday work of answering quick, factual questions: a date, a definition, a statistic, a conversion, a biographical fact, a quotation, a chemical property, an official name. It looks simple, and that appearance of simplicity is the danger. A quick fact delivered from the first search result, a convenient but unreliable source, or a memory is often wrong, and a wrong fact stated confidently by a library propagates. Ready reference demands the same discipline as any reference work: choosing the right source type, verifying across authoritative sources, checking dates and editions, and being honest when a fact cannot be confirmed. The difference is that the discipline must be applied in seconds, not hours, which makes the habits matter even more.

Use this skill whenever you are answering a factual question, looking up a statistic or date, verifying a claim, or building a ready-reference collection. The goal is to prevent the agent from trusting the first hit, from using outdated editions, from confusing a popular source with an authoritative one, and from presenting an unverified fact as certain.

## Core Rules

### Match The Source Type To The Fact Type

Different facts live in different sources. A definition lives in a dictionary; a measurement standard lives in a technical handbook; an official statistic lives in a government or organizational source; a biographical fact lives in a biographical reference or authoritative profile. Choosing the wrong source type produces plausible but wrong answers.

Match by fact type:

- definitions and word usage: dictionaries and historical dictionaries for etymology;
- quick facts and overview: encyclopedias, with subject-specific encyclopedias for depth;
- statistics: official statistical agencies, international organizations, vetted data portals;
- dates, events, and biographical facts: encyclopedias, official biographies, institutional records;
- scientific constants and properties: technical handbooks and standards bodies;
- quotations: verified quotation dictionaries and the original source, not quote aggregators;
- geographic and demographic facts: gazetteers, atlases, official census and mapping sources;
- current names, offices, and officials: official organizational sites, checked for recency.

### Verify Important Facts Across More Than One Source

A single source can be wrong, mistyped, or outdated. For any fact that matters, confirm it in at least two independent authoritative sources. Independence matters: two sites copying the same error do not count as verification.

Verification strategy:

- find a second authoritative source that did not derive from the first;
- check the original or primary source where feasible;
- for statistics, trace to the issuing agency's own publication;
- for quotations, locate the original speech or text;
- for dates and biographical facts, cross-check a biographical reference against an institutional source.

For trivial facts (a common spelling, a routine conversion), one authoritative source may suffice. For consequential facts (a statistic cited in a decision, a date cited in a legal context), verify rigorously.

### Check Currency, Edition, And Revision Date

Facts change. Borders shift, officials leave office, statistics are revised, scientific values are updated, and organizations rename themselves. A reference source that was authoritative five years ago may now be wrong. Always check the date of the source and, where relevant, the edition.

Currency checks:

- when was the source last updated or revised?;
- for statistics, what period does the figure cover, and has a newer release superseded it?;
- for scientific and technical values, is the edition current?;
- for biographical and organizational facts, has the situation changed since publication?;
- for online sources, is there a "last updated" or publication date, and is it credible?

Prefer the most recent authoritative edition. When using an older source for a historical fact, confirm it has not been corrected by later scholarship.

### Prefer Authoritative Over Convenient

The first search result is usually the most popular, not the most authoritative. Popularity and accuracy diverge sharply for statistics, quotations, biographical facts, and any topic that attracts commercial or ideological content. Train the reflex to look past the top of the results.

Authoritative source preferences:

- official agency and institutional sources over aggregators;
- subject-specific reference works over general ones for depth;
- peer-reviewed or editorially controlled sources over user-contributed ones;
- primary sources over secondary summaries where feasible;
- the original publication over a republished or scraped copy.

Convenience is a real constraint under time pressure, but it never overrides authority for a consequential fact.

### Handle Statistics With Special Care

Statistics are the most error-prone ready-reference category. They are frequently miscited, misdated, compared across incompatible definitions, or drawn from preliminary releases later revised. A number without its source, year, definition, and geography is not a usable fact.

For any statistic, capture:

- the exact figure and unit;
- the source organization and specific publication;
- the year or period the figure covers;
- the geographic scope and population definition;
- any methodology notes that affect comparability;
- whether a newer or revised figure exists.

Refuse to deliver a bare number. A statistic stripped of its context misleads even when the digits are right.

### Verify Quotations Against The Original

Quotations are notoriously misattributed and paraphrased over time. Quote aggregators and web pages propagate invented or altered attributions. For any quotation that matters, locate the original source.

Quotation verification:

- find the original speech, book, letter, or interview;
- confirm the exact wording, since paraphrases often circulate as direct quotes;
- confirm the attribution, since famous quotes are often reassigned to more famous people;
- note the date and context, since meaning shifts with circumstance.

If the original cannot be located, say so rather than passing along an unverified quote.

### Recognize And Report Unverifiable Facts

Some facts cannot be confirmed. The source may be lost, contradictory, behind a paywall, or simply not authoritative. Honest ready reference reports uncertainty rather than inventing confidence.

When a fact is unverifiable:

- state what you searched and where;
- explain why confidence is low (conflicting sources, no primary source, outdated material);
- offer the best-available source with its caveat;
- suggest where the patron might find a more authoritative answer.

"I could not confirm this" is a better reference answer than a confident guess.

### Build And Maintain A Ready-Reference Collection

Speed in ready reference comes from knowing your sources, not from improvising each time. Maintain a curated set of go-to ready-reference sources by category, in print and online, and keep it current. Review it periodically for dead links, superseded editions, and better sources that have emerged.

## Common Traps

### Trusting The First Search Result

Popularity is not authority. Look past the top hit for consequential facts.

### Using An Outdated Edition

Facts change. Check the revision date and prefer the current authoritative edition.

### Delivering A Statistic Without Its Source, Year, Or Definition

A bare number misleads. Provide the full context.

### Accepting A Quotation From An Aggregator

Quotations are frequently misattributed or altered. Verify against the original.

### Confusing A Popular Source With An Authoritative One

Wikipedia and similar sources are useful starting points but should be confirmed against authoritative sources for facts that matter.

### Single-Sourcing A Consequential Fact

One source can be wrong. Verify important facts independently.

### Presenting An Unverified Fact As Certain

Honest uncertainty is better than false confidence. Report what could and could not be confirmed.

### Ignoring Geographic And Definitional Scope

A statistic for one country, year, or definition is not transferable to another. Capture the scope.

## Self-Check

- Did you match the source type to the fact type rather than grabbing the first available source?
- Did you verify consequential facts across at least two independent authoritative sources?
- Did you check the currency, edition, and revision date of every source used?
- Did you prefer authoritative over convenient sources, especially for statistics and quotations?
- For any statistic, did you capture figure, source, year, geography, definition, and methodology?
- For any quotation, did you verify wording and attribution against the original?
- Did you recognize and report unverifiable facts honestly rather than fabricating confidence?
- Is your ready-reference collection curated, current, and organized by fact type?
