# Specialized And Subject Reference Sources

General reference sources, encyclopedias, dictionaries, almanacs, answer broad questions at a surface level. Specialized and subject reference sources answer questions that demand depth, authority, discipline-specific vocabulary, or domain expertise: a medical condition's standard treatment, a legal term's jurisdiction-specific meaning, a statistical series' methodology, a historical event's primary documentation, a scientific constant's precise value. These sources are where reference work becomes genuinely expert. They are also where mistakes are most consequential, because patrons act on medical, legal, financial, and technical information. Selecting, navigating, and knowing the limits of specialized reference sources is a distinct competence, built on understanding each domain's source hierarchy, vocabulary, and scope.

Use this skill whenever a question requires depth beyond a general source, when you are building or weeding a subject reference collection, and when a patron's question touches a specialized domain. The goal is to prevent the agent from answering a specialized question with a general source, from mishandling sensitive-domain references, and from missing the authoritative source a discipline actually relies on.

## Core Rules

### Know Each Domain's Source Hierarchy

Every discipline has a hierarchy of authoritative sources, and the hierarchy differs by field. A medical question's authoritative sources are not the same as a legal question's, which are not the same as a historian's. Knowing the hierarchy for each domain you serve is the foundation of specialized reference.

Typical hierarchies:

- medicine: clinical guidelines from professional bodies, systematic reviews, peer-reviewed clinical literature, then patient-level authoritative sources;
- law: official statutes and case law, official regulatory texts, then secondary legal encyclopedias and recognized self-help publishers, always jurisdiction-specific;
- science and technology: standards bodies, peer-reviewed literature, technical handbooks, then subject encyclopedias;
- history: peer-reviewed scholarship, then specialized encyclopedias and dictionaries, then primary-source editions and archival finding aids;
- business and finance: official regulatory filings, recognized financial data providers, then trade and industry sources;
- statistics: official statistical agencies and international organizations, with methodology documentation.

General encyclopedias sit below specialized sources in every hierarchy. Use them for orientation, not for answers that matter.

### Match Source Depth To The Question's Depth

Not every specialized question needs the deepest source. A layperson asking about a medical condition needs an authoritative patient-level source; a clinician needs the clinical literature. Matching depth prevents both under-answering and overwhelming.

Calibrate depth by asking:

- who is the patron (layperson, student, professional, specialist)?;
- what will they do with the answer (general understanding, a decision, professional work)?;
- what source type does their task require?;
- is a patient-level or practitioner-level source appropriate?;

Offer the right level and, where useful, show the patron how to access deeper sources if they want them.

### Master Discipline-Specific Vocabulary And Indexing

Specialized sources use controlled vocabulary and indexing that differ from general tools. Medical sources use MeSH; education sources use ERIC descriptors; legal sources use jurisdiction-specific terminology and citators. Effective use of specialized reference requires learning each domain's vocabulary.

For each domain you serve:

- learn the main controlled vocabulary or thesaurus;
- understand how the source indexes and retrieves;
- recognize discipline-specific abbreviations and citation formats;
- know the standard reference works by name and reputation.

Without this, even the best source returns poor results because the search uses the wrong terms.

### Verify Currency, Edition, And Authority Rigorously In Specialized Domains

Specialized domains change fast, and outdated authority is dangerous. Medical guidance is revised; statutes are amended; scientific values are updated; statistical methodologies change. Currency and authority checks that are good practice in general reference become mandatory in specialized reference.

Check:

- the edition and revision date against the domain's update cycle;
- whether a newer guideline, statute, or value has superseded the source;
- the authority and credentials of the source's publisher or editorial board;
- whether the source is recognized as authoritative within the discipline;
- jurisdiction and scope for legal, regulatory, and statistical sources.

A reputable-looking but superseded source is a common and serious failure mode.

### Handle Sensitive Specialized Domains Within Role Boundaries

Medical, legal, financial, and tax reference sources support information needs, not professional advice. The line is the same as in any high-stakes reference work: provide authoritative sources and referrals; do not interpret, diagnose, advise, or decide.

In specialized reference:

- provide the authoritative source and explain what it contains;
- decline to apply it to the patron's specific situation;
- refer to qualified professionals and accredited services;
- be explicit that the source is not a substitute for professional advice;
- favor patient-level and lay-accessible authoritative sources for non-specialist patrons.

### Use Primary Sources And Finding Aids For Historical And Archival Questions

Historical and archival questions often require primary sources, accessed through finding aids, calendars, and published documentary editions. Specialized reference in these domains means knowing how to navigate archival description, not just secondary scholarship.

For historical and archival questions:

- identify the relevant repositories and their finding aids;
- distinguish published primary-source editions from original manuscripts;
- understand provenance, access restrictions, and reproduction rights;
- connect patrons to archival staff for in-depth work;
- use specialized bibliographies and union catalogs to locate scattered materials.

### Evaluate Digital Versus Print For Each Specialized Source

Specialized sources exist in print, online, and licensed database forms, each with trade-offs. Print offers stability and sometimes richer indexing; online offers currency and searchability; licensed databases offer depth but access limits. Choose per source and per patron.

Consider:

- how current the source must be (favor online for fast-changing domains);
- whether the patron can access a licensed database;
- whether the print edition's indexing or browsing is superior for the question;
- preservation and backup if the online source disappears;
- accessibility needs across formats.

### Build A Balanced Subject Reference Collection

A strong subject reference collection spans the domains your community needs, at appropriate depth, in current editions, across formats. Build it deliberately, review it for currency, and weed superseded editions. Track which domains your community actually uses and adjust depth accordingly. ## Common Traps

### Answering A Specialized Question With A General Source

General encyclopedias lack the depth and authority specialized questions require. Use them for orientation only.

### Mishandling Sensitive-Domain Sources

Providing a medical or legal source is information; interpreting it for the patron is advice. Hold the line.

### Using An Outdated Authority

Specialized domains change. A superseded guideline or statute is a serious error.

### Ignoring Discipline-Specific Vocabulary

Searching a specialized source with general terms misses the controlled vocabulary that makes it work.

### Over-Depth Or Under-Depth

Matching the source's level to the patron prevents both overwhelming and under-answering.

### Forgetting Primary Sources For Historical Work

Secondary scholarship is not the same as archival evidence. Know the finding aids.

### Assuming One Format Suffices

Print, online, and licensed sources have different strengths. Choose per source and patron.

### Collecting Without Currency Review

A subject reference collection with superseded editions misleads. Review and weed regularly.

## Self-Check

- Did you identify and use the source hierarchy authoritative for the specific domain?
- Did you match the source depth and level to the patron's identity and task?
- Did you use the domain's controlled vocabulary and indexing rather than general terms?
- Did you verify currency, edition, authority, jurisdiction, and scope rigorously for the specialized source?
- Did you handle sensitive domains (medical, legal, financial) as information with referrals, not advice?
- For historical and archival questions, did you use primary sources, finding aids, and repository expertise?
- Did you choose the appropriate format (print, online, licensed) for the source and patron's access?
- Is the subject reference collection balanced, current, and reviewed for the domains your community needs?
