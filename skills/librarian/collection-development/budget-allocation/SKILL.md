# Budget Allocation

A library budget is a statement of values expressed in numbers. How money is divided among subjects, formats, programs, and audiences determines what the collection becomes far more than any policy document. Budget allocation looks like arithmetic but is actually a series of judgments about priority, risk, obligation, and equity. Get it wrong and the collection drifts toward whoever had funding last year, toward formats that quietly consume more each year, or toward subject areas with the loudest advocates. Good allocation is transparent, data-informed, revisable, and honest about the tradeoffs every dollar represents.

Use this skill when planning a materials budget, allocating funds across subjects or formats, preparing a budget request or justification, or managing the tension between ongoing commitments and one-time purchases. The goal is to prevent the agent from rolling forward last year's numbers, underestimating recurring costs, starving emerging needs, or treating budgeting as pure accounting rather than a collection-shaping decision.

## Core Rules

### Treat Allocation As A Collection Decision

Budget allocation is not separate from collection development. The allocation is the collection development decision, expressed in dollars. A policy that promises depth in a subject while allocating it no money is just words.

Recognize that:

- the allocation determines what actually gets acquired;
- ongoing commitments, especially subscriptions, limit future flexibility;
- format shifts, print to digital, change cost structures permanently;
- underfunded areas do not stay static, they age and lose relevance;
- overfunded areas crowd out emerging needs.

Frame every allocation discussion as a question about what the collection should become.

### Base Allocations On Multiple Data Sources

No single metric is sufficient. Use a combination to avoid distortion by any one measure.

Useful data:

- circulation and use by subject and format;
- interlibrary loan borrowing requests by subject, indicating unmet need;
- enrollment, course offerings, or community demographics by interest;
- cost trends by format and subject;
- existing collection strength and age by subject;
- publication output and price by discipline;
- consortial or shared collection agreements that reduce local need;
- user requests and faculty or community input.

Triangulate. Circulation alone favors popular materials; publication output alone favors expensive research disciplines. Combine demand, need, cost, and existing strength.

### Separate Ongoing From One-Time Funds

This distinction is foundational and often mishandled. Ongoing commitments, chiefly subscriptions and standing orders, consume budget year after year and usually grow. One-time funds buy monographs and other non-recurring items.

Manage them differently:

- track the percentage of budget locked into ongoing commitments;
- cap ongoing commitments to preserve flexibility, often around seventy percent as a guideline;
- use one-time funds for new directions, gaps, and backfiles;
- avoid funding ongoing commitments with one-time money that will disappear;
- review subscription cost-per-use annually and cancel low-value titles.

A budget that is ninety percent ongoing has almost no room to respond to change. Protect one-time capacity deliberately.

### Account For Price Inflation And Exchange Rates

Library materials costs do not stay flat. Journal subscriptions, foreign-language materials, e-book packages, and database licenses typically inflate faster than general budgets.

Build in:

- an inflation assumption based on real vendor data, not optimism;
- exchange rate sensitivity for foreign materials;
- expected annual increases in big deals and aggregator packages;
- a contingency for unexpected price spikes or currency shifts.

Without an inflation allowance, the real buying power of a flat budget shrinks every year, and the collection erodes silently.

### Justify Allocations With A Clear Methodology

Opaque allocations invite conflict and drift. A documented methodology makes allocations defensible and revisable.

A methodology should specify:

- the formula or criteria used to divide funds;
- the data inputs and their sources;
- the weights assigned to each input;
- how adjustments are made for special cases;
- who has authority to reallocate;
- the review cycle.

Even a simple methodology, such as proportional to a weighted combination of use and enrollment, beats rolling forward last year's numbers with no reasoning.

### Protect Emerging And Underfunded Areas

Routine allocation tends to entrench existing distributions. New programs, growing fields, and underserved communities need explicit protection or they never get funded.

Mechanisms:

- set aside a percentage for new initiatives each year;
- review allocations against curriculum or community change;
- give growth areas a floor that cannot be cut;
- sunset declining areas gradually rather than abruptly;
- consult with liaisons or community representatives about emerging needs.

Without protection, the allocation calcifies around historical priorities.

### Manage The Big Deal And Aggregator Risk

Large journal packages and e-book aggregators offer volume discounts but create concentrated risk. They consume large budget shares, are hard to exit, and their annual increases can crowd out everything else.

Assess:

- total spend and percentage of budget in big deals;
- cost per use across the package;
- title-level use to identify low-value content;
- exit costs and alternatives, including interlibrary loan and pay-per-view;
- renegotiation leverage and timing;
- consortial options for shared purchasing.

Treat big deals as strategic decisions reviewed regularly, not as fixed costs.

### Build In Transparency And Accountability

Budget decisions affect the whole institution and should be visible enough to be trusted and questioned.

Practice:

- publish the allocation methodology and current allocations;
- explain major changes and their rationale;
- invite input from liaisons, faculty, and community representatives;
- report actual spending against allocation at year end;
- document cancellations and their reasons;
- separate collection budget from operations budget clearly.

Transparency reduces suspicion and improves decisions through wider input.

### Plan For Cuts As Well As Growth

Budgets go down as well as up. A plan only for growth fails when cuts come. Build a prioritized cut list in advance.

A cut plan considers:

- which subscriptions have lowest cost-per-use;
- which formats have alternatives;
- which subjects are covered consortially;
- which standing orders are least essential;
- what protects the core mission under reduced funds.

Having a reasoned cut list prevents panicked, arbitrary cuts under pressure.

### Align With Consortial And Shared Collections

No library collects alone anymore. Budget decisions should account for what partners provide.

Consider:

- shared print repositories that reduce local retention burden;
- consortial ebook and database deals that lower per-member cost;
- reciprocal borrowing that reduces need for local ownership;
- collaborative collection development agreements by subject;
- document delivery as an alternative to ownership.

Aligning local allocation with shared infrastructure stretches every dollar.

## Common Traps

### Rolling Forward Last Year's Numbers

Defaulting to prior year allocations entrenches the past and ignores change. Re-examine allocations against current data regularly.

### Underestimating Recurring Cost Growth

Subscriptions and licenses inflate. A flat budget quietly buys less each year. Model real inflation.

### Funding Ongoing Commitments With One-Time Money

Using temporary funds for a recurring subscription creates a cliff when the money ends. Match commitment duration to fund duration.

### Letting The Big Deal Crowd Out Monographs

A growing journal package can silently consume the monograph budget. Track the ratio and defend monograph capacity.

### Allocating By Squeaky Wheel

The loudest department or patron should not set the allocation. Use methodology and data, with input channels for all.

### Ignoring Cost-Per-Use

A subscription that costs ten thousand dollars and is used twice is a candidate for cancellation regardless of prestige. Use use data alongside prestige.

### Forgetting Foreign And Niche Materials

Exchange rates and niche publisher prices can spike. Small budgets for foreign or specialized materials need monitoring.

### No Plan For Cuts

When cuts come, institutions without a prioritized plan make damaging, arbitrary choices. Prepare the cut list before it is needed.

## Self-Check

- Is budget allocation treated as a collection development decision, not just accounting?
- Are allocations based on multiple data sources, including use, need, cost, and existing strength?
- Are ongoing and one-time funds separated, with ongoing commitments capped to preserve flexibility?
- Is price inflation and exchange rate risk built into the budget model?
- Is there a documented, transparent allocation methodology that could be defended and revised?
- Are emerging and underfunded areas explicitly protected from historical entrenchment?
- Are big deals and aggregator packages reviewed regularly for cost-per-use and exit options?
- Is the budget process transparent to stakeholders with input and reporting?
- Is there a prioritized cut plan ready for revenue reductions?
- Are consortial and shared collection options integrated into local allocation decisions?
