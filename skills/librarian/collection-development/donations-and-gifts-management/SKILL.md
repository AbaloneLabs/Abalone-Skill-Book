# Donations And Gifts Management

Gifts seem like free additions to a collection, but they are among the most operationally and ethically complex transactions a library undertakes. A donation carries hidden costs: staff time to evaluate, the space to house or store it, the labor to process and catalog, the disposal burden for unwanted material, and the ongoing obligation to steward the items accepted. Donors often attach expectations, sometimes formal restrictions, about how materials will be used, stored, displayed, or kept together, and these expectations can conflict with the library's mission, policies, and need to manage the collection over time. Accepting a gift can also create legal and tax obligations, reputational exposure, and ethical dilemmas around provenance, restriction, and deaccessioning. Treating donations as simple windfalls leads to collections burdened with unwanted material, donors aggrieved by broken expectations, and libraries constrained by restrictions they did not fully understand when accepting the gift. Managing donations well means applying the same rigorous selection criteria used for purchases, documenting transfers clearly, and declining gracefully but firmly when a gift does not serve the collection.

Use this skill when evaluating donated materials, drafting or applying gift policies, managing donor relations, processing deeds of gift, and deciding whether to accept or decline donations. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting gifts that do not meet selection criteria, from accepting restrictions that compromise the collection, from mishandling donor expectations, or from neglecting the legal and tax dimensions of gifts.

## Core Rules

### Apply The Same Selection Criteria As For Purchases

A gift is not exempt from selection discipline. The first question is whether the material meets the collection development policy.

Evaluate gifts against:

- relevance to the collection's scope, mission, and community needs;
- quality, condition, and physical appropriateness;
- redundancy with existing holdings;
- the cost of processing, cataloging, and housing;
- long-term maintenance and preservation burden;
- format viability and obsolescence risk.

If a gift would not be purchased, it usually should not be accepted as a gift. Free is not the same as worth the cost of keeping.

### Have A Written Gift Policy

Gift management requires a clear, written policy that sets expectations before donor conversations begin.

A gift policy should specify:

- the types of materials accepted and not accepted;
- the criteria for evaluation;
- the library's right to dispose of unwanted or duplicate material;
- that the library does not appraise gifts for tax purposes;
- restrictions the library will and will not accept;
- the process for deeds of gift and transfer of ownership;
- how accepted materials are acknowledged.

A written policy protects the library and gives staff a consistent basis for decisions.

### Use A Deed Of Gift To Transfer Ownership

A deed of gift is the formal instrument that transfers legal ownership and documents the terms. Do not accept significant gifts without one.

A deed of gift should address:

- clear transfer of legal ownership to the library;
- a description of the materials;
- any agreed restrictions and their duration;
- the library's disposition rights for unwanted or duplicate items;
- acknowledgment that the library does not provide appraisals;
- signatures and dates.

Verbal understandings are insufficient and lead to later disputes. Document the transfer.

### Clarify And Limit Restrictions

Donors often propose restrictions: that material be kept together, never weeded, displayed prominently, or restricted to certain uses. Evaluate restrictions critically.

Evaluate restrictions by asking:

- Does the restriction align with the library's mission and policies?
- Does it constrain future collection management unacceptably?
- Is it time-limited or perpetual?
- Can the library realistically honor it indefinitely?
- Does it create access, storage, or cataloging burdens?

Decline restrictions that compromise the library's ability to manage the collection. Prefer time-limited or flexible terms.

### Do Not Provide Appraisals For Tax Purposes

Libraries and librarians generally should not appraise donated materials for tax purposes, due to conflict of interest and IRS rules.

Follow the standard that:

- the library does not value gifts for the donor's tax deduction;
- the donor obtains an independent qualified appraisal if needed;
- the library provides an acknowledgment of receipt describing the items but not their value;
- staff understand the boundary between acknowledging a gift and valuing it.

Crossing this line creates legal and ethical exposure.

### Manage Donor Relations Thoughtfully

Donors often have emotional and intellectual investment in their gifts. Relationships matter, even when declining.

Manage relations by:

- acknowledging the generosity and intent behind the gift;
- explaining the policy and criteria clearly and respectfully;
- offering alternatives when declining (other institutions, different formats);
- being transparent about disposition of unwanted material;
- providing appropriate acknowledgment for accepted gifts;
- maintaining records of significant donations and donor relationships.

Thoughtful relations preserve goodwill and the library's reputation even when a gift is declined.

### Plan For Disposition Of Unwanted Material

Many gifts include material the library cannot use. Plan for disposition before accepting.

Disposition options include:

- offering duplicates or unwanted items to other libraries;
- sale through friends-of-the-library book sales or dealers;
- recycling or disposal of material with no value;
- returning material to the donor if agreed in advance.

The deed of gift should make clear that the library may dispose of unwanted material, so disposition is not a later controversy.

### Evaluate Provenance And Ethical Concerns

Some donations raise provenance and ethical issues, especially for special collections, rare materials, archives, and cultural heritage items.

Investigate:

- the legal ownership and clear title of the materials;
- the history of acquisition and any questionable provenance;
- cultural sensitivity, especially for materials from Indigenous or marginalized communities;
- the presence of confidential, personal, or restricted information;
- any conditions that would embarrass or compromise the institution.

Ethical and legal provenance review is essential for significant or sensitive gifts.

### Track The Full Cost Of Gifts

Gifts are not free. Track the real costs to make informed decisions.

Costs include:

- staff time for evaluation, negotiation, and processing;
- cataloging and physical processing;
- housing, shelving, and storage;
- preservation and conservation;
- ongoing maintenance and space.

For large gifts, a cost assessment before acceptance prevents later regret.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Gifts That Do Not Meet Selection Criteria

Free is not worth the cost of keeping. Apply the same criteria as for purchases.

### Accepting Verbal Donations Without A Deed Of Gift

Verbal understandings lead to disputes. Document ownership transfer formally.

### Accepting Restrictive Conditions That Compromise The Collection

Restrictions on weeding, display, or use can burden the collection indefinitely. Decline or limit them.

### Appraising Gifts For Tax Purposes

Libraries should not value gifts for donors. Acknowledge receipt, do not appraise.

### Failing To Set Disposition Expectations Upfront

If the library may dispose of unwanted material, say so in the deed of gift before accepting.

### Ignoring Provenance And Ethical Issues

Significant or sensitive gifts require provenance and ethical review, not just content evaluation.

### Underestimating Processing And Storage Costs

Gifts carry real costs in staff time, space, and maintenance. Assess these before accepting.

### Damaging Donor Relations Through Poor Communication

Even declined gifts can preserve goodwill with respectful, transparent communication.

## Self-Check

- Did you evaluate the gift against the same selection criteria used for purchases?
- Is there a written gift policy that sets expectations before donor conversations?
- Does a deed of gift document the transfer of ownership and any terms?
- Did you critically evaluate and limit any restrictions attached to the gift?
- Did you avoid providing appraisals for tax purposes and instead acknowledge receipt only?
- Did you manage donor relations respectfully, including when declining?
- Is there a clear plan and documented right to dispose of unwanted or duplicate material?
- Did you investigate provenance, legal title, and ethical concerns for significant or sensitive gifts?
- Did you assess the full cost (staff time, processing, storage, maintenance) before accepting?
- Could you defend the decision to accept or decline this gift based on policy and criteria?
