# Selection Criteria And Policies

Selection is the act of deciding what a library will acquire, retain, license, or accept as a gift. It looks deceptively simple, pick good materials, but every selection encodes a theory of the community, a stance on quality, a budget commitment, and a future obligation to maintain or weed the item. A library without a written, reasoned selection policy selects by habit, by vendor default, or by the loudest requestor. Policies exist not to slow selection down but to make it consistent, defensible, and aligned to the community the library actually serves. Good selection balances demand, quality, budget, space, diversity of viewpoint, and the long tail of needs that no single request will surface.

Use this skill when drafting or revising a collection development policy, setting selection criteria, evaluating a prospective purchase, or defending a selection decision. The goal is to prevent the agent from selecting by personal taste, over-relying on bestseller lists, ignoring underserved populations, or writing policies so vague they cannot guide real decisions.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Community, Not The Catalog

Selection serves a specific population. A public library, an academic library, a school library, a law library, and a hospital library serve different communities with different needs, and the same title may be essential in one and irrelevant in another.

Define the community before defining criteria:

- who is served, age range, reading level, languages, professions;
- what the community uses the library for;
- what gaps exist in current coverage;
- what underserved or underrepresented groups need representation;
- what local history, industry, or culture deserves depth;
- what adjacent institutions, schools, archives, or consortia already provide.

Selection criteria that do not begin from community analysis produce collections that mirror the selector's preferences rather than the community's needs.

### Write A Policy That Can Actually Guide Decisions

A collection development policy is not decorative. It should help a selector choose between two books and explain why one was chosen. Vague policies, "select materials of lasting value," fail this test.

A useful policy specifies:

- the community and mission the collection serves;
- scope and depth targets by subject, using a framework like conspectus if appropriate;
- selection criteria, with priorities ranked;
- formats included and excluded, and why;
- treatment of controversial materials;
- gifts and donations policy;
- weeding and deselection criteria;
- reconsideration process for challenges;
- responsibility and authority for selection decisions;
- review cycle for the policy itself.

Write the policy so a new staff member could select consistently with it. If it cannot guide a real decision, it is too abstract.

### Use Explicit, Ranked Selection Criteria

Selection criteria should be named and prioritized, because criteria often conflict. Demand may pull toward popular fiction while quality pulls toward literary merit; budget may pull toward one format while access pulls toward another.

Common criteria, to be ranked per collection:

- relevance to community need and mission;
- accuracy, authority, and currency of content;
- quality of writing, production, or scholarship;
- demand or anticipated use;
- cost relative to value and expected use;
- durability and format suitability;
- balance of viewpoints and diversity of voices;
- representation of underserved or marginalized groups;
- relationship to existing collection, filling gaps or avoiding duplication;
- availability through other means, consortial borrowing or open access;
- lasting value versus ephemeral relevance;
- accessibility, including formats for users with disabilities.

Rank these for each collection. A popular materials library ranks demand high; a research library ranks lasting value and depth high. Neither ranking is universal.

### Balance Demand And Quality Deliberately

The demand-versus-quality tension is permanent and must be managed, not resolved. Pretending it does not exist produces collections that serve neither side well.

Approaches:

- allocate a percentage of the budget to high-demand popular materials and a percentage to quality depth;
- use multiple copies for demand titles and single copies for depth titles;
- lease or borrow heavily demanded ephemeral titles rather than owning;
- treat bestsellers as short-term demand and classics as long-term depth;
- review the balance annually against circulation and community data.

Avoid the two extremes: a collection of only what circulates today, which abandons depth and breadth, or a collection of only what experts value, which ignores what the community actually wants.

### Plan For Diversity Of Viewpoint

A core library value is representing multiple perspectives, including those the selector or community majority may disagree with. Selection should not curate toward a single ideology.

Apply this by:

- seeking materials from diverse authors, publishers, and viewpoints;
- ensuring controversial topics have more than one perspective represented;
- including materials that some patrons may find offensive, when they meet criteria;
- not self-censoring to avoid anticipated complaints;
- documenting the rationale when a viewpoint-sensitive title is selected or declined;
- connecting diversity of collection to the intellectual freedom policy.

Diversity is not tokenism. It is systematic attention to whose voices are present and absent.

### Distinguish Selection From Censorship

Selectors make choices constantly, and every choice not to buy is not censorship. The distinction matters and should be explicit in policy.

Selection is choosing among materials based on criteria applied consistently. Censorship is removing or excluding access to materials based on disapproval of content, viewpoint, author identity, or pressure.

Selectors should:

- apply criteria consistently, not by personal agreement with content;
- document when a borderline decision is made;
- resist pressure to remove access based on viewpoint;
- use the reconsideration process for challenges rather than ad hoc removal;
- distinguish between "not selected for this collection" and "suppressed."

### Treat Formats Strategically

Format decisions, print, e-book, audiobook, streaming, journal subscription, are selection decisions with long consequences. Each format carries access, cost, licensing, preservation, and accessibility implications.

Consider:

- licensing terms for digital formats, especially DRM and simultaneous use limits;
- cost per use across formats;
- accessibility for users with print or other disabilities;
- preservation and permanence, owned versus licensed;
- user preference and device access;
- vendor lock-in and platform stability;
- whether a format serves the whole community or only those with devices.

Do not default to one format. Match format to use case and budget.

### Build Selection Workflows That Surface Gaps

Routine selection tends to reinforce what already circulates. Without deliberate gap-finding, collections drift toward the majority's preferences.

Gap-finding practices:

- review circulation and hold data for underrepresented subjects;
- consult community organizations and underserved groups;
- use diversity audit tools and inclusive publisher lists;
- track requests that cannot be filled;
- review awards and review sources beyond mainstream outlets;
- audit representation by author identity, language, and topic.

Make gap-finding a scheduled activity, not an afterthought.

### Document And Defend Decisions

Selection decisions may be questioned by patrons, administrators, or challengers. Documentation makes decisions defensible and consistent.

For significant or borderline decisions, record:

- the criteria applied;
- the sources consulted, reviews, awards, community input;
- the alternatives considered;
- the rationale for the final choice;
- any conditions, such as location or format.

Documentation protects both the decision and the selector, and it creates an institutional memory that survives staff turnover.

## Common Traps

### Selecting By Personal Taste

The selector's preferences are not the community's needs. Apply criteria, not preference, and audit for bias toward familiar genres or authors.

### Defaulting To Vendor Approval Plans

Approval plans and standing orders are efficient but can quietly shape the collection toward vendor defaults. Review and adjust profiles regularly.

### Ignoring Underrepresented Voices

Mainstream review sources and bestseller lists overrepresent dominant voices. Actively seek diverse sources or the collection narrows over time.

### Self-Censoring To Avoid Complaints

Anticipating challenges and pre-emptively declining titles silently shrinks the collection. Apply criteria and let the reconsideration process handle disputes.

### Confusing Circulation With Value

High circulation justifies popular materials but does not invalidate low-circulation depth materials. Both serve the mission.

### Policies That Cannot Decide Anything

Vague policies full of aspirational language guide no one. Test the policy against real decisions before adopting it.

### Treating Gifts As Free

Donations carry processing, cataloging, shelving, and weeding costs, and may include unwanted or duplicated materials. Apply selection criteria to gifts too.

### Forgetting Formats Have Consequences

A shift to e-books can lock the library into licensing terms that reduce access. Evaluate format decisions as strategic, not just convenient.

## Self-Check

- Is the community analyzed and described before selection criteria are set?
- Does the policy specify ranked criteria that could guide a real purchase decision?
- Is the demand-versus-quality balance explicitly managed, with budget allocations?
- Does the collection systematically represent diverse viewpoints and underserved voices?
- Is the distinction between selection and censorship clear in policy and practice?
- Are format decisions evaluated for licensing, accessibility, cost per use, and preservation?
- Is there a scheduled process for finding collection gaps beyond routine circulation?
- Are significant or borderline selection decisions documented with rationale?
- Is there a reconsideration process that does not collapse into ad hoc removal?
- Is the policy itself scheduled for regular review against community change?
