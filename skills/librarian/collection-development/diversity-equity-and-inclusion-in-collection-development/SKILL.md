# Diversity Equity And Inclusion In Collection Development

A library collection is a statement about whose knowledge, stories, and voices the institution considers worth preserving and providing. When a collection is built from mainstream publishers, review sources, and canonical lists without conscious attention to who is represented and who is absent, it tends to over-represent dominant groups and under-represent or misrepresent everyone else. The result is a collection that claims to serve a whole community while its shelves tell a narrower story, and patrons from underrepresented groups find few mirrors of their own experience and limited windows into others. The judgment problem is building a collection that genuinely reflects the diversity of the community and the broader world, doing so through deliberate selection rather than tokenism, and defending inclusive collections against the challenges that diverse materials disproportionately attract. The failure modes are real: performative diversity that adds a few titles and declares the work done, tokenism that reduces marginalized experience to a single representative voice, and capitulation to challenges that remove the very materials that make the collection inclusive.

Use this skill when auditing a collection for representation, when selecting to address gaps, when balancing canonical and diverse materials, when defending challenged materials, or when diagnosing why a collection does not reflect its community. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating diversity as a checkbox, from tokenizing marginalized voices, and from allowing collection development to reproduce the exclusions of the mainstream publishing and review ecosystem.

## Core Rules

### Audit The Collection Against The Community, Not Against Comfort

You cannot improve what you have not measured. A collection diversity audit compares what is on the shelves against the diversity of the community and the broader world, identifying whose voices are present, whose are absent, and how marginalized groups are represented when they appear. The audit is the foundation of intentional inclusive collection development.

Audit by:

- surveying the collection by creator identity, subject, and perspective;
- comparing representation to community demographics and to the diversity of the field;
- identifying gaps, absences, and stereotyped or single-story representations;
- using established audit tools and diversity statistics where available;
- repeating the audit periodically to track whether the collection is becoming more representative.

An audit that is never conducted leaves the library asserting inclusivity it has not verified. An audit that is conducted and ignored is worse, because it documents the gap without acting.

### Select Beyond Mainstream Publishers And Review Sources

The mainstream publishing and review ecosystem systematically privileges certain voices and overlooks others. If selection relies only on major publishers, mainstream review journals, and bestseller lists, the collection will reproduce that bias. Inclusive collection development requires deliberately seeking out small, independent, university, and international presses, and creators from underrepresented groups.

Select beyond the mainstream by:

- using diverse publisher lists, awards, and curated bibliographies as selection sources;
- seeking review sources that center marginalized voices;
- attending to self-published and community-produced materials where mainstream gatekeeping has excluded voices;
- building relationships with vendors and distributors who specialize in diverse materials.

The collection can only be as diverse as the selection sources that feed it. Relying on the same sources produces the same gaps.

### Distinguish Representation From Tokenism

Adding a single title by or about a marginalized group and treating the category as covered is tokenism, not inclusion. Tokenism reduces a diverse group to a single representative voice, implies that one title can stand for a whole experience, and often selects the most palatable rather than the most authentic voice. Genuine inclusion builds depth, so that marginalized experiences are represented by multiple voices, perspectives, and genres.

Avoid tokenism by:

- building depth within categories, not just presence;
- selecting multiple creators from each underrepresented group;
- choosing authentic voices, ideally own-voices creators, over palatable approximations;
- representing the full range of experience within a group, not only struggle or trauma narratives;
- treating inclusion as ongoing, not as a one-time achievement.

A collection with one LGBTQ+ novel, one book by a Black author, and one disability memoir has checked boxes, not built inclusion.

### Balance Canonical And Marginalized Voices Deliberately

Collections serve multiple purposes, including preserving cultural heritage and reflecting the current community. Both canonical works and diverse contemporary voices belong, and the balance should be deliberate rather than accidental. Neither excluding the canon to center marginalized voices nor excluding marginalized voices to preserve the canon serves the community.

Balance by:

- retaining canonical works while ensuring they do not crowd out diverse voices;
- contextualizing older canonical works that reflect the prejudices of their time;
- actively acquiring diverse works of enduring quality alongside contemporary relevance;
- representing diverse creators within the canon itself, which has always been more diverse than narrow canons acknowledge.

The goal is a collection where both heritage and the full community are present, with neither sacrificed to the other.

### Prioritize Authentic Voices And Accurate Representation

Representation can harm when it is inaccurate, stereotyped, or created by outsiders who flatten or misrepresent an experience. A collection that includes harmful or stereotyped depictions under the banner of diversity can reinforce the very biases it claims to counter. Authenticity and accuracy matter as much as presence.

Prioritize authenticity by:

- favoring own-voices creators, those writing from within the experience;
- evaluating outsider-created materials for accuracy, complexity, and respect;
- avoiding materials that rely on stereotypes, single stories, or deficit framing;
- seeking reviews and recommendations from members of the represented community;
- being willing to weed outdated or harmful representations even if they are diverse.

A stereotyped diverse title can do more damage than an absence, because it teaches a distorted picture under the appearance of inclusion.

### Defend Diverse Materials Against Disproportionate Challenges

Diverse materials are challenged disproportionately. Books by and about LGBTQ+ people, people of color, religious minorities, and marginalized groups dominate annual challenge lists. A collection development policy that builds an inclusive collection but cannot defend it will lose its diverse materials to the first sustained challenge. Defense must be built into the policy and the practice.

Defend by:

- having a clear, public materials reconsideration policy and process;
- training staff to handle challenges professionally and consistently;
- documenting the selection rationale for diverse materials when they are acquired;
- relying on professional organizations and intellectual freedom principles;
- not preemptively self-censoring diverse materials to avoid anticipated challenges.

Preemptive self-censorship, removing or not acquiring diverse materials to avoid conflict, is censorship by the library against its own mission. The defense must be as intentional as the selection.

### Connect Collection Diversity To Community Engagement

A collection that reflects the community is most valuable when the community knows it and participates in shaping it. Engagement ensures the collection addresses real needs rather than librarian assumptions about what diverse communities want, and it builds the relationships that sustain inclusive collection development through challenges.

Engage by:

- seeking input from community members and groups about their needs and interests;
- involving community members in selection advisory or co-curation where appropriate;
- promoting diverse collections actively so the community knows they exist;
- being responsive to community feedback without ceding selection decisions to pressure.

Engagement distinguishes a collection built with the community from one built for them based on assumptions.

### Treat Inclusion As Ongoing, Not Achieved

A collection is never permanently inclusive. The community changes, new voices emerge, understanding of representation evolves, and materials age out or are challenged. Inclusive collection development is a continuous practice, not a milestone to be reached and declared complete.

Sustain inclusion by:

- repeating diversity audits on a regular cycle;
- continuously seeking new and emerging voices;
- revisiting selection sources as the publishing landscape changes;
- training new staff in inclusive collection practices;
- resisting the complacency of declaring the collection diverse based on past effort.

## Common Traps

### Performative Diversity

Adding a few titles and declaring the collection inclusive without auditing depth or authenticity. Inclusion is ongoing, not a checkbox.

### Tokenism

Representing each marginalized group with a single title or voice, often the most palatable one. Build depth and authenticity.

### Relying Only On Mainstream Sources

Selecting from major publishers and mainstream reviews reproduces their exclusions. Seek diverse sources deliberately.

### Authenticity Neglected In Pursuit Of Presence

Including stereotyped or inaccurate diverse materials harms more than it helps. Prioritize own-voices and accurate representation.

### Preemptive Self-Censorship

Not acquiring or quietly removing diverse materials to avoid anticipated challenges. This is censorship against the library's own mission.

### Canon Versus Diversity As A Zero-Sum

Treating inclusion as requiring the exclusion of canonical works, or canon as requiring the exclusion of diverse voices. Balance both deliberately.

### Declaring Inclusion Achieved

A collection is never permanently inclusive. The community changes and the work continues.

### Building For Rather Than With The Community

Assuming what diverse communities want without engagement produces a collection that misses real needs. Engage and co-shape.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The collection has been audited for representation against the community and the broader world, with gaps identified.
- [ ] Selection sources extend beyond mainstream publishers and reviews to include diverse presses and curated bibliographies.
- [ ] Representation includes depth, multiple voices, and full range of experience, not single-token titles.
- [ ] Authentic voices and accurate representation are prioritized over mere presence.
- [ ] Canonical and diverse materials are balanced deliberately, with neither sacrificed to the other.
- [ ] A clear reconsideration policy and staff training exist to defend diverse materials against disproportionate challenges.
- [ ] No preemptive self-censorship has removed or prevented acquisition of diverse materials to avoid conflict.
- [ ] Community members are engaged in shaping and knowing about the diverse collection.
- [ ] Inclusion is treated as ongoing, with periodic re-audits and continuous seeking of new voices.
- [ ] The collection can demonstrate, with evidence, that it reflects the community it claims to serve.
