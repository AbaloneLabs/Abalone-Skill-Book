# Collection Development For Specialized Formats

Most collection development guidance assumes the monograph: a discrete, stable, owned item with predictable selection and processing. But libraries increasingly develop collections in formats that break those assumptions. Serials change titles, merge, and cease. Audiovisual and media materials depend on equipment, licensing, and format viability that can render them unusable. Maps and geospatial data require specialized access tools and metadata. Music scores, games, technology lending, realia, and zines each carry their own selection, access, and preservation challenges. Applying monograph-centric thinking to these formats produces predictable failures: serials whose runs are incomplete and confusing, media that cannot be played, datasets whose access depends on tools the library cannot sustain, and realia that consumes space without clear use. Specialized format collection development requires understanding the distinctive lifecycle, access model, and operational burden of each format, and selecting deliberately for what the library can actually support.

Use this skill when selecting or managing non-standard formats, designing format-specific collection policies, deciding between ownership and access models for media, or planning the operational support a format requires. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying monograph assumptions to specialized formats, from selecting formats the library cannot sustainably support, from ignoring format viability and obsolescence, or from neglecting the access, equipment, and metadata needs of non-standard materials.

## Core Rules

### Understand Each Format's Distinctive Lifecycle

Specialized formats do not behave like monographs. Understand the lifecycle of each format before selecting.

Consider for each format:

- **Serials and continuing resources:** ongoing, title-changing, with cumulative runs and subscription dependencies.
- **Audiovisual and media:** equipment-dependent, format-fragile, often licensed rather than owned.
- **Maps and geospatial data:** scale- and projection-sensitive, requiring specialized access and discovery tools.
- **Music scores:** editions and arrangements matter, with performance and study use cases.
- **Games and interactive media:** platform-dependent, with hardware and licensing complexity.
- **Technology and equipment lending:** high-wear, short-lifecycle, with maintenance and replacement burdens.
- **Realia and objects:** space-intensive, with display, security, and handling needs.
- **Zines and ephemera:** fragile, often uncataloged or minimally processed, with community and cultural value.

Selecting without understanding the lifecycle leads to unsustainable collections.

### Match Selection Criteria To Format

General selection criteria apply, but each format needs format-specific criteria. Adapt the policy accordingly.

Add format-specific criteria:

- **Serials:** indexing coverage, full-text access, title stability, archival value of the run.
- **Media:** equipment availability, format viability, licensing model, streaming versus physical.
- **Maps and data:** currency, scale, projection compatibility, software and tool support.
- **Scores:** edition quality, performance versus study suitability, instrumentation.
- **Games:** platform support, age ratings, multiplayer and online dependencies.
- **Equipment:** expected lifespan, maintenance, replacement cost, demand patterns.
- **Realia:** storage, security, display, and handling requirements.
- **Zines:** community representation, collecting scope, processing approach.

A single generic policy underserves every specialized format.

### Decide Between Ownership And Access Models

Many specialized formats are available through both ownership and access models. Choose deliberately.

Consider:

- **Own or license:** physical media owned outright versus streaming or subscription access.
- **Stable or ephemeral:** whether the format's content is stable or changes over time.
- **Cost patterns:** one-time purchase versus ongoing subscription, and total cost of ownership.
- **Access infrastructure:** what tools, equipment, or platforms are needed for each model.
- **Archival need:** whether perpetual access or preservation matters for the content.

Streaming media may offer access but no archival control; physical ownership may offer stability but require equipment.

### Assess Format Viability And Obsolescence

Formats become obsolete. Selecting a format the library cannot sustain is a predictable failure.

Assess:

- the current viability of the format and its equipment;
- the trajectory of the format (emerging, stable, declining, obsolete);
- the availability of playback or access equipment;
- migration and reformatting options as formats age;
- the cost and feasibility of preservation.

Selecting declining formats without a migration plan burdens the collection with unusable material.

### Plan The Access Infrastructure

A specialized format is only useful if patrons can access it. Plan the infrastructure before selecting.

Plan for:

- playback, viewing, or reading equipment for media;
- specialized software or platforms for data, maps, and games;
- physical housing, shelving, and display for scores, maps, realia, and zines;
- course reserve, circulation, and in-library use models;
- assistive technology and accessibility for all formats.

Acquiring material without access infrastructure strands the collection.

### Address Metadata And Discovery Needs

Specialized formats require specialized metadata for discovery. Plan the metadata approach.

Address:

- format-specific metadata standards (e.g., for cartographic, music, serials, data);
- encoding formats, equipment requirements, and access notes in the record;
- discovery layer configuration to surface format-specific fields;
- the level of processing appropriate to the format and its use;
- integration with finding aids or specialized interfaces where relevant.

Generic monograph metadata leaves specialized formats undiscoverable.

### Consider Preservation And Conservation

Specialized formats often have acute preservation needs. Factor preservation into selection.

Consider:

- the inherent fragility of the format (e.g., magnetic media, acidic zines, fold maps);
- environmental and housing requirements;
- conservation treatment costs and expertise availability;
- reformatting and digitization for preservation access;
- the preservation burden relative to the format's value to the collection.

Selecting without preservation planning can commit the library to unsustainable care.

### Evaluate Demand And Use Patterns

Specialized formats serve specific use patterns. Understand demand before investing.

Evaluate:

- curricular, research, and community demand for the format;
- circulation and in-library use patterns;
- course and program alignment;
- the cost per use relative to other ways of meeting the need;
- whether demand is sustained or trend-driven.

High-cost, low-use formats may be better met through interlibrary loan or access models.

### Integrate Specialized Formats Into Policy

Specialized formats should not be ad hoc. Integrate them into the collection development policy.

Integrate by:

- stating the scope and level for each format the library collects;
- documenting selection criteria, access models, and limitations;
- defining the operational support required and who provides it;
- setting review cycles for format viability and continued collecting.

Policy integration prevents drift and ensures sustained, deliberate collecting.

## Common Traps

### Applying Monograph Assumptions To Specialized Formats

Specialized formats have different lifecycles, access, and metadata needs. Adapt criteria accordingly.

### Selecting Formats The Library Cannot Sustain

Equipment, software, and preservation needs must be supportable. Selecting unviable formats strands material.

### Ignoring Format Obsolescence

Declining formats become unusable. Assess viability and plan migration before selecting.

### Acquiring Without Access Infrastructure

Material without playback equipment or access tools is inaccessible. Plan infrastructure first.

### Using Generic Metadata For Specialized Formats

Format-specific discovery requires format-specific metadata. Use appropriate standards.

### Neglecting Preservation Needs

Specialized formats are often fragile. Factor preservation into selection and ongoing care.

### Choosing Access Models Without Archival Consideration

Streaming and subscription access may lack perpetual or archival rights. Weigh ownership versus access deliberately.

### Collecting Ad Hoc Without Policy Integration

Ad hoc collecting drifts. Integrate specialized formats into the collection development policy.

## Self-Check

- Did you understand the distinctive lifecycle of the format before selecting?
- Did you adapt selection criteria to the format rather than applying monograph assumptions?
- Did you choose deliberately between ownership and access models for the format?
- Did you assess format viability and plan for obsolescence and migration?
- Did you plan the access infrastructure (equipment, software, housing) before acquiring?
- Did you address format-specific metadata and discovery needs?
- Did you consider preservation and conservation requirements and costs?
- Did you evaluate demand, use patterns, and cost per use for the format?
- Is the format integrated into the collection development policy with scope and review cycles?
- Could you defend collecting this format as sustainable and aligned with community need?
