# Approval Plans And Vendor Relations

A library collection is supplied largely through ongoing relationships with vendors, publishers, and aggregators, not only through individual title selection. Approval plans, blanket orders, standing orders, and subscription packages shape what flows into the collection routinely, and the terms of those relationships determine cost, coverage, and flexibility for years. Managing these relationships is not a clerical task. It is a strategic activity that determines whether the collection actually reflects the library's priorities, whether the budget is spent efficiently, and whether the library retains the ability to adapt as needs and markets change. The trap is to treat vendors as neutral suppliers and approval plans as automatic, when in fact each plan encodes assumptions about what is worth acquiring, each contract allocates risk and cost, and each vendor relationship needs active management, evaluation, and periodic renegotiation. Strong vendor management treats these relationships as instruments of collection policy, not as background infrastructure.

Use this skill when setting up or revising approval plans, negotiating with vendors and publishers, evaluating vendor performance, managing firm and standing orders, and aligning supply workflows with collection policy. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting default vendor terms, from letting approval plans drift from policy, from neglecting performance evaluation, or from treating vendor relationships as static rather than actively managed.

## Core Rules

### Align Approval Plans With Collection Policy

An approval plan is an automated selection mechanism. Its profile must reflect the collection development policy, or it will deliver material that does not serve the collection.

Align the plan by specifying:

- subject coverage matched to collection priorities;
- levels (research, instructional, popular) appropriate to the community;
- non-English language coverage and limits;
- format preferences and exclusions (print, ebook, with or without DRM);
- publication type and publisher filters;
- price ceilings and budget pacing;
- duplication rules against existing holdings.

Review the plan profile regularly, as both the collection and the publishing market shift over time.

### Choose The Right Acquisition Model For Each Need

Different acquisition models serve different purposes. Match the model to the need.

Common models:

- **Approval plans:** automated delivery of newly published titles matching a profile.
- **Firm orders:** specific title-by-title selection for known needs.
- **Standing orders:** automatic delivery of ongoing series or serials.
- **Patron-driven acquisition (PDA/DDA):** purchase triggered by use (see the related skill).
- **Subscriptions and packages:** bundled access to journals or ebooks.

Each model has cost, coverage, and flexibility tradeoffs. Combine them deliberately.

### Negotiate Terms Actively

Vendor terms are negotiable. Default contracts favor the vendor; active negotiation favors the library.

Negotiate:

- pricing, discounts, and tier benefits;
- the approval plan return rate and return privileges;
- invoice processing, shipping, and processing fees;
- ebook licensing terms, including DRM, concurrent users, and interlibrary loan rights;
- subscription price caps and renewal increase limits;
- data delivery, MARC records, and discovery integration;
- cancellation or exit terms.

Document negotiated terms in writing. Verbal assurances do not protect the library.

### Scrutinize Ebook And Digital Licensing Terms

Digital acquisitions are licensed, not owned, and the license terms determine real value. Scrutinize them carefully.

Examine:

- whether access is owned, leased, or subscription-based;
- digital rights management (DRM) restrictions on printing, downloading, and copying;
- concurrent user limits and access models;
- interlibrary loan and course reserve rights;
- archival and perpetual access rights if the license ends;
- privacy and data collection on user behavior.

Favorable pricing on restrictive licenses is often a poor long-term value.

### Manage The Approval Slip And Notification Workflow

Approval plans generate slips or notifications for titles that match the profile. Active management keeps the plan useful.

Manage by:

- reviewing slips regularly rather than letting them accumulate;
- approving, deferring, or rejecting based on current needs and budget;
- tuning the profile based on what is being delivered versus rejected;
- tracking the ratio of approvals to returns;
- flagging recurring mismatches for profile adjustment.

A neglected approval plan drifts from policy and wastes budget.

### Evaluate Vendor Performance Periodically

Vendor relationships need evaluation, not just renewal. Set criteria and review performance.

Evaluate:

- fill rate and speed of delivery for orders;
- accuracy of approval profiling and low return rates;
- invoice accuracy and responsiveness to discrepancies;
- quality and timeliness of MARC records and processing;
- customer service and problem resolution;
- pricing competitiveness over time;
- transparency of renewals and pricing changes.

Use evaluation results to renegotiate, adjust, or change vendors.

### Maintain Independence And Avoid Lock-In

Vendor consolidation and bundled packages can create dependency that limits future flexibility. Protect independence.

Protect against lock-in by:

- avoiding over-concentration of spending with one vendor;
- preserving the ability to shift titles or packages;
- negotiating exit and perpetual access terms;
- maintaining title-by-title selection capacity alongside packages;
- watching for bundling that obscures the value of individual components.

Independence preserves the library's ability to adapt as markets and needs change.

### Document And Renew Deliberately

Renewals are decision points, not automatic continuations. Approach them deliberately.

At renewal:

- review usage, cost, and value of subscriptions and packages;
- compare against alternatives and market pricing;
- renegotiate terms rather than accepting default increases;
- cancel or restructure what no longer serves the collection;
- document decisions and rationale.

Routine renewal without review locks in inefficiency and drift.

### Build Collaborative Vendor Relationships

Vendors are partners as well as suppliers. Strong relationships improve service and outcomes.

Build relationships by:

- communicating collection priorities and changes clearly;
- providing constructive feedback on performance;
- collaborating on profile tuning and problem-solving;
- recognizing good service;
- maintaining professional, good-faith negotiation.

Collaboration and firm negotiation are compatible and mutually reinforcing.

## Common Traps

### Letting Approval Plans Drift From Policy

Unreviewed profiles deliver material that no longer serves the collection. Review and tune regularly.

### Accepting Default Vendor Terms

Default contracts favor the vendor. Negotiate pricing, licensing, and exit terms actively.

### Neglecting Ebook And Digital License Terms

Restrictive DRM and unclear perpetual access undermine digital acquisitions. Scrutinize licenses.

### Renewing Without Review

Automatic renewal locks in inefficiency. Treat renewals as decision points.

### Over-Concentrating Spending With One Vendor

Dependency limits flexibility. Maintain independence and the ability to shift.

### Ignoring Performance Evaluation

Without evaluation, poor service and pricing go unaddressed. Review vendors periodically.

### Treating Approval Slips As Automatic

Slips need active review and tuning. Neglect wastes budget and drifts from policy.

### Forgetting Return Rates And Disposition

Approval plans depend on the ability to return unsuitable material. Track and use return privileges.

## Self-Check

- Is the approval plan profile aligned with the current collection development policy?
- Did you choose acquisition models (approval, firm, standing, PDA, subscription) deliberately for each need?
- Did you negotiate pricing, licensing, processing, and exit terms rather than accepting defaults?
- Did you scrutinize ebook and digital license terms (DRM, concurrent users, ILL, perpetual access)?
- Are approval slips and notifications actively reviewed and the profile tuned?
- Do you evaluate vendor performance periodically against clear criteria?
- Have you protected against vendor lock-in and over-concentration of spending?
- Are renewals treated as decision points with usage, cost, and alternatives reviewed?
- Are vendor relationships collaborative yet firmly negotiated?
- Could you defend the current vendor arrangements as serving collection policy and budget efficiency?
