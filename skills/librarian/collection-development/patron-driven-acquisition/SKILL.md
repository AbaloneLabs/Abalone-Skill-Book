# Patron-Driven Acquisition

Patron-driven acquisition, also called demand-driven acquisition, turns some selection decisions over to patrons by triggering a purchase when a title is used or requested. It promises a collection shaped by actual demand rather than selector prediction, and it often produces high cost-per-use efficiency. But PDA is not a way to avoid selection judgment. It embeds selection decisions in the configuration, the profiles, the loan thresholds, the budget caps, and the review rules. A poorly configured PDA program can blow a budget on ephemeral use, acquire titles that do not fit the collection, or create unpredictable spending that destabilizes planning. Good PDA is deliberate configuration, active monitoring, and honest evaluation against the same standards as any other acquisition method.

Use this skill when designing, configuring, monitoring, or evaluating a patron-driven or demand-driven acquisition program. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating PDA as set-and-forget, ignoring the selection judgments hidden in configuration, or assuming usage always equals collection value.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Configuration Is Selection

PDA does not remove selection. It relocates it. Every parameter in the profile, the subject scope, the publisher inclusion list, the format limits, the price caps, the loan-to-purchase trigger, is a selection decision. A patron only triggers purchases from the pool you configured.

Make the embedded selection explicit:

- document the profile and its rationale;
- state which subjects, formats, and publishers are included or excluded;
- explain the trigger rules and their cost implications;
- review the profile as a collection decision, not a technical setting.

If the configuration is not examined, the collection is being shaped invisibly.

### Choose The Right Model For The Goal

Several PDA models exist, and they serve different goals. Choosing the wrong model produces mismatched results.

Common models:

- demand-driven acquisition for e-books, where a short-term loan triggers a purchase after a threshold;
- print PDA, where a patron request triggers purchase and shipping;
- evidence-based acquisition, where a period of use determines which titles to purchase from a pool;
- patron-select programs, where patrons suggest titles the library then evaluates;
- approval-plan-with-PDA hybrids.

Match the model to the collection goal. E-book DDA suits demand-driven breadth; evidence-based suits informed selection backed by use data; print PDA suits responsive public library collection building.

### Set Triggers And Thresholds Deliberately

The trigger rule determines when a use becomes a purchase. It is the single most consequential configuration and the most common source of budget surprises.

Consider:

- short-term loan cost versus purchase price ratio;
- number of short-term loans before auto-purchase, often two or three;
- the cost cap above which titles are excluded from auto-purchase;
- whether deep, table-of-content, or chapter-level use counts as a trigger;
- mediation rules for high-cost titles;
- time windows for trigger counting.

A trigger set too low causes budget to convert to purchases on casual browsing. A trigger set too high means demand goes unmet. Calibrate using real use data and revise.

### Budget For Unpredictability

PDA spending is inherently less predictable than firm-order spending. Patron demand, not a selection calendar, drives the spend rate.

Manage the risk:

- set a firm annual budget cap with monthly monitoring;
- use a deposit account or prepayment to prevent overcommit;
- build in auto-pause when spending approaches the cap;
- track spend rate monthly and adjust profile mid-cycle if needed;
- reserve contingency for end-of-year spikes.

Unmonitored PDA can exhaust a year's budget in months. Monitoring is not optional.

### Balance PDA With Curated Selection

PDA captures demand, but demand is not the only collection value. Depth, breadth, diverse voices, and materials without immediate demand but lasting value still need curated selection.

Combine:

- PDA for responsive, demand-driven acquisition;
- firm orders for gaps, diversity, and depth that demand will not surface;
- standing orders for essential series and reference;
- evidence-based rounds to let use inform final selection.

A collection built only on PDA reflects only current demand and drifts toward the majority's interests.

### Evaluate Cost-Per-Use Honestly

PDA's appeal is high cost-per-use efficiency, but the metric must be computed correctly and compared fairly.

Compute:

- total program cost including short-term loan fees and purchases;
- uses per title and per dollar;
- comparison to firm-ordered titles on the same metric;
- hidden costs, staff time, platform fees, profile management;
- the value of titles purchased but little used afterward.

A title triggered by three short-term loans and then never used again may be efficient in the moment but a weak long-term acquisition. Look at post-purchase use too.

### Watch For Drift And Gaming

PDA profiles drift over time as vendor catalogs change, publishers merge, and subject scopes shift. Patrons or faculty may also learn the triggers and game them.

Monitor:

- unexpected spikes in triggers by subject or publisher;
- titles purchased that fall outside intended scope;
- repeated triggers from the same users or courses;
- changes in vendor pool composition;
- format or price distribution of purchases.

Adjust the profile when drift appears. A profile set once and never revisited silently changes the collection.

### Protect Diversity And Underserved Needs

PDA reflects demand, and demand often reflects majority preferences and already-privileged users. Underserved communities, minority-language speakers, and niche subjects may never generate enough demand to trigger purchase.

Counter this by:

- running diversity audits on PDA purchases;
- supplementing PDA with curated diverse selection;
- configuring profiles to include diverse publishers and voices;
- consulting underserved communities about needs PDA will not surface;
- setting aside budget for representation that demand alone will not fund.

Demand-driven does not automatically mean equitable.

### Document And Report PDA As A Program

PDA is a program with configuration, budget, outcomes, and risks. Treat it with the same documentation as any collection program.

Report:

- annual spend and title counts;
- cost-per-use and comparison to other methods;
- subject and format distribution of purchases;
- profile changes and their rationale;
- titles or subjects that did not perform;
- recommendations for the next cycle.

Documentation makes PDA accountable and improvable rather than a black box.

### Plan For Exit And Transition

Vendors change terms, platforms merge, and budgets shift. A PDA program should have an exit and transition plan.

Plan for:

- how to retain access to purchased titles if the vendor relationship ends;
- portability of purchased content and metadata;
- alternative suppliers for the same demand;
- wind-down procedures that protect already-spent investment;
- renegotiation leverage from documented use data.

Lock-in is a real risk. Build portability into contracts from the start.

## Common Traps

### Treating PDA As Set-And-Forget

The profile and triggers silently shape the collection. Without periodic review, the program drifts from its intent.

### Triggers Set Too Low

Casual browsing converts to purchases and exhausts budget on low-value titles. Calibrate triggers to real demand patterns.

### Assuming Usage Equals Lasting Value

A title triggered by momentary demand may never be used again. Track post-purchase use, not just trigger events.

### Letting Budget Be Driven By Patron Demand Alone

Unmonitored PDA can overspend or underspend unpredictably. Caps and monthly monitoring are essential.

### Ignoring Diversity In A Demand-Driven Model

Demand reflects existing privilege and majority interests. Supplement PDA with curated diverse selection.

### Confusing Short-Term Loan Cost With Value

Short-term loan fees can accumulate to near purchase price without ownership. Review the loan-to-purchase economics.

### Profile Drift Unnoticed

Vendor catalog changes and publisher mergers shift what patrons can trigger. Audit the pool periodically.

### No Exit Plan

Vendor dependence without portability creates lock-in. Negotiate retention rights up front.

## Self-Check

- Is the PDA configuration recognized and documented as a set of selection decisions?
- Is the model, DDA, evidence-based, print PDA, matched to the collection goal?
- Are triggers and thresholds calibrated to real use data and reviewed periodically?
- Is there a firm budget cap with monthly monitoring and auto-pause?
- Is PDA balanced with curated selection for depth, breadth, and diversity?
- Is cost-per-use computed honestly, including hidden costs and post-purchase use?
- Is the profile audited for drift in subject, publisher, format, and price?
- Are diversity and underserved needs protected beyond what demand alone surfaces?
- Is the program documented and reported with spend, use, and recommendations?
- Is there an exit and transition plan protecting purchased content and access?
