# Records Appraisal And Disposition

Records appraisal is the judgment of which records warrant permanent retention and which may be destroyed, and disposition is the action that follows that judgment. Together they are the point at which records management either preserves institutional memory or loses it irreversibly. The work is harder than it looks because the value of records is rarely obvious at the moment of appraisal: routine administrative records can become vital historical evidence decades later, while records that seem important can turn out to be duplicative or low-value. Agents tend to appraise from the record's apparent importance rather than from a structured analysis of its evidential, informational, and historical value, and they tend to treat disposition as a cleanup task rather than a documented, reviewable decision. Destruction is irreversible, and a disposition decision made casually cannot be corrected. Good appraisal is conservative in the sense that it errs toward retention when value is uncertain, but it is also disciplined, because retaining everything is not appraisal but avoidance.

Use this skill when appraising records, making disposition decisions, or documenting disposition. The goal is to prevent the agent from destroying records of lasting value, applying appraisal criteria inconsistently, treating disposition as cleanup rather than decision, or failing to document decisions so they can be reviewed.

## Core Rules

### Appraise Against Explicit Criteria, Not Impression

Records appraisal must be conducted against explicit, documented criteria, not against a general impression of importance. Impression-based appraisal is inconsistent across appraisers and over time and produces decisions that cannot be defended. The standard appraisal criteria, drawn from archival theory, provide a structure that makes appraisal deliberate and reviewable.

Apply structured appraisal criteria:

- evidential value: the record's ability to document the origin, structure, functions, and activities of the creating body, which is highest for records of core functions and decision-making;
- informational value: the record's value for research about the people, places, events, and phenomena it documents, independent of the creating body;
- primary value: the record's ongoing value to the creating body for its operations, legal, or fiscal purposes;
- secondary value: the record's value to other users, including historians, genealogists, and the public, after primary value ends;
- uniqueness: whether the information exists elsewhere, because duplicates and copies have lower value than unique records;
- context and completeness: whether the record is part of a coherent series, because isolated records often lose meaning.

Document which criteria drove each appraisal decision. Decisions without stated criteria are assertions.

### Use Macro-Appraisal To Understand Functions Before Records

Effective appraisal often begins not with individual records but with an understanding of the functions and activities that generated them. Macro-appraisal or functional analysis maps what an organization does, which functions are core to its mission and identity, and which records best document those functions. This top-down view prevents the common error of appraising records in isolation and missing that the valuable records are those documenting the organization's most significant functions.

Conduct functional appraisal by:

- mapping the organization's functions and identifying which are mission-critical;
- identifying the records that document the origin, mandate, policies, and major decisions of those functions;
- prioritizing records of highest-level decision-making over routine transactional records;
- recognizing that some administrative records (financial, personnel, legal) document functions whose value is primarily legal or fiscal and is captured in retention scheduling, not archival appraisal;
- using the functional map to guide which series to appraise in depth.

Functional context turns appraisal from a series of isolated judgments into a coherent selection strategy.

### Distinguish Archival From Operational Disposition

Disposition serves two different purposes that must not be confused. Operational disposition, governed by the retention schedule, destroys records that have reached the end of their retention period and have no archival value. Archival disposition transfers records of permanent value to the archives for long-term preservation. Confusing the two, either destroying archival records as routine disposition or transferring routine records to the archives, causes harm in both directions.

Keep the two dispositions distinct:

- operational disposition applies the retention schedule to records of finite value, with documented destruction;
- archival disposition applies appraisal to records of potential permanent value, with transfer to archival custody;
- the retention schedule should flag series with archival potential so they reach appraisal rather than automatic destruction;
- archival transfer is a separate process with its own accessioning, description, and preservation steps;
- records awaiting appraisal should not be destroyed, because appraisal may extend their value.

A series that might have archival value should be appraised, not destroyed by default.

### Document Every Disposition Decision

Because destruction is irreversible, every disposition decision must be documented well enough to be reviewed and defended. Documentation serves audit, legal, and historical purposes: it demonstrates that destruction was authorized and justified, and it records what was destroyed so future researchers know what is missing. Undocumented destruction is indistinguishable from loss or negligence.

Document disposition with:

- the series or records disposed of, with identifiers and date range;
- the appraisal or retention basis for the decision;
- the disposition action, destruction or transfer, and the date;
- the authorizing official and the appraiser;
- for destruction, a certificate or record of destruction, especially for sensitive categories;
- for transfer, the accession record and receiving archive;
- any exceptions or special considerations.

Disposition documentation is the institutional record of what was kept and what was lost, and it should be preserved permanently.

### Treat Destruction As Authorized And Verifiable

Destruction is not something that happens to records; it is something done to them under authority. Unauthorized or uncontrolled destruction, even of low-value records, creates the appearance of spoliation or negligence. Destruction must be authorized, controlled, and verifiable, especially for records containing personal, confidential, or sensitive information.

Control destruction by:

- requiring authorization from the responsible official before any destruction;
- ensuring destruction is complete and secure, especially for confidential records, using shredding or secure electronic wiping;
- verifying destruction, because partial or insecure destruction defeats the purpose;
- recording the destruction with sufficient detail to demonstrate it occurred and was authorized;
- suspending destruction immediately upon any litigation hold or legal preservation notice.

Destruction that is not authorized, secure, and documented is a liability, not records management.

### Be Conservative When Value Is Uncertain

Appraisal often involves uncertainty: the future research value of records is hard to predict, and the cost of destruction is irreversible while the cost of retention is merely storage. When the archival value of a series is genuinely uncertain, the disciplined choice is to retain and reappraise later rather than to destroy. This is not hoarding; it is acknowledging the asymmetry of the decision.

Apply conservative appraisal by:

- retaining series of uncertain value for reappraisal rather than destroying them;
- using sampling to reduce volume while preserving representative evidence, when full retention is not feasible;
- documenting the uncertainty and the plan for reappraisal;
- avoiding destruction of records documenting marginalized communities or underrepresented perspectives, whose future value is often highest and hardest to predict;
- recognizing that the absence of current demand is not evidence of absence of future value.

Conservative appraisal accepts some over-retention as the cost of avoiding irreversible loss.

### Coordinate Appraisal With Stakeholders

Appraisal decisions affect multiple stakeholders: the creating office, which may need the records; the archives, which will preserve transferred records; legal counsel, which may have hold or compliance concerns; and researchers and communities, whose future use is at stake. Appraisal conducted in isolation misses these perspectives and produces decisions that are overturned or regretted. Coordinate appraisal with the relevant stakeholders.

Coordinate by:

- consulting the creating office about ongoing operational need and context;
- involving the archives in appraisal of series with potential permanent value;
- checking with legal counsel for any hold or preservation obligations;
- considering researcher and community perspectives, especially for records documenting underrepresented groups;
- documenting stakeholder input in the appraisal record.

## Common Traps

### Impression-Based Appraisal

Appraising by gut feel rather than documented criteria produces inconsistent and indefensible decisions. This is a trap because the decisions cannot be reviewed and vary by appraiser.

### Destroying Archival Records As Routine Disposition

Treating records of permanent value as routine operational records subject to scheduled destruction causes irreversible loss. This is a trap because the loss is discovered too late to correct.

### Appraising Records In Isolation

Judging individual records without functional context misses which records document core functions. This is a trap because isolated appraisal preserves the wrong records and loses the valuable ones.

### Undocumented Destruction

Destroying records without recording the decision and authorization looks like negligence or spoliation. This is a trap because the institution cannot demonstrate that destruction was proper.

### Retaining Everything To Avoid Decisions

Defaulting to permanent retention to avoid appraisal judgment is not appraisal but avoidance. This is a trap because it transfers the decision and its cost to the future without resolving it.

### Destroying Records Of Marginalized Communities

Applying routine disposition to records documenting underrepresented groups erases evidence of high future value. This is a trap because the harm is to communities whose history is already under-preserved.

### Confusing Duplicates With Records

Destroying records as duplicates without verifying they are truly duplicative can lose variant copies with distinct value. This is a trap because apparent duplicates often differ in annotation, provenance, or completeness.

### Ignoring Litigation Holds During Disposition

Proceeding with destruction when a hold applies is spoliation. This is a trap because the legal consequence is severe and the action cannot be undone.

## Self-Check

- Is each appraisal decision made against explicit criteria (evidential, informational, primary, secondary, uniqueness, context) and documented?
- Did you conduct functional or macro-appraisal to identify which records document the organization's core functions before appraising individual series?
- Are operational disposition and archival transfer kept distinct, with series of potential archival value routed to appraisal rather than automatic destruction?
- Is every disposition decision documented with series, basis, action, date, authorizer, and, for destruction, a certificate?
- Is destruction authorized, secure, verified, and suspended upon any litigation hold?
- For series of uncertain value, did you retain and plan reappraisal rather than destroy?
- Did you coordinate appraisal with the creating office, archives, legal counsel, and, where relevant, researchers and communities?
- Did you avoid destroying records documenting marginalized or underrepresented communities without careful appraisal of their future value?
- Did you verify that records slated for destruction as duplicates are genuinely duplicative, including annotations and provenance?
