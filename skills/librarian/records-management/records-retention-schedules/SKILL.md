# Records Retention Schedules

A records retention schedule is the authoritative instrument that determines how long each category of record is kept and what happens at the end of that period. It looks like a table of periods, but it is actually a risk allocation document: every retention period balances the cost and risk of keeping records too long against the cost and risk of destroying them too soon. Agents tend to treat retention scheduling as a clerical exercise in looking up legal minimums, when it is actually a judgment-intensive process of reconciling legal requirements, operational needs, research and historical value, privacy obligations, and the real cost of storage and discovery. A schedule that keeps everything forever creates legal and storage liability; one that destroys aggressively creates gaps that surface during litigation, audit, or historical research. The schedule is also only as good as its operational enforcement, and a schedule that exists on paper but is not applied to actual records provides false assurance.

Use this skill when drafting or revising retention schedules, assigning retention periods, or mapping legal retention requirements. The goal is to prevent the agent from assigning retention periods from generic templates without legal validation, ignoring operational and historical value, creating schedules that cannot be enforced, or destroying records that hold legal or archival value.

## Core Rules

### Build The Schedule From A Records Inventory, Not From A Template

A retention schedule that is not grounded in an inventory of the organization's actual records is a fiction. The inventory identifies what records exist, where they live, who owns them, what systems hold them, and how they are used, and this reality must drive the schedule. Starting from a generic template produces periods that do not match the organization's records and that staff cannot apply.

A records inventory should capture:

- the record series or category, with a clear definition of what it contains;
- the creating office or system of record and the business process it supports;
- the format and location, paper, shared drives, email, databases, cloud systems;
- the volume and growth rate;
- the legal and regulatory requirements that apply;
- the operational need for retention, how long the office uses the records;
- the potential archival or historical value.

The inventory is the foundation; the schedule is the decision applied to it.

### Determine Retention From Multiple, Sometimes Conflicting, Drivers

Retention periods are driven by several factors that can conflict, and the schedule must reconcile them deliberately rather than picking one. The drivers include legal and regulatory minimums, operational need, audit and litigation hold requirements, financial and tax obligations, and archival appraisal. The correct retention period is usually the longest of the applicable requirements, but the reasoning must be explicit.

Reconcile retention drivers by:

- identifying all legal and regulatory requirements, from statutes, regulations, and grant terms, that mandate minimum retention;
- determining operational retention, how long the office actually needs the records to function;
- checking for audit or grant requirements that extend retention beyond the operational need;
- assessing archival value, whether the records warrant permanent preservation, which extends retention indefinitely;
- resolving conflicts by taking the longest applicable period and documenting why;
- recognizing that "permanent" is a real commitment with real cost, not a default for uncertain cases.

Document the basis for each retention period so the schedule is defensible and reviewable.

### Map Legal Requirements Rigorously And Validate Them

Legal and regulatory retention requirements are the floor of any schedule, and getting them wrong creates real liability. Requirements vary by jurisdiction, industry, and record type, and they change over time. A schedule built on unverified assumptions about legal minimums is a liability. Legal requirements must be researched, cited, and validated, typically with counsel, and revisited as law changes.

Map legal requirements by:

- identifying the jurisdiction and regulatory regime applicable to each record type;
- citing the specific statute, regulation, or grant term that sets the requirement, not a general assertion;
- recognizing where no explicit requirement exists and the period must be set by operational or risk judgment;
- validating requirements with legal counsel, especially for high-risk categories like personnel, student, health, and financial records;
- tracking the source and date of each legal citation so the schedule can be reviewed when law changes;
- distinguishing minimum retention from recommended retention, because the law sets a floor, not a ceiling.

A retention period without a traceable legal basis is an assertion that will not survive a challenge.

### Design Disposition As An Action, Not A Date

The retention period is only half the schedule; the other half is what happens at the end of the period, the disposition. Disposition is an action, destruction, transfer to archives, or continued retention, and it must be designed to actually occur. A schedule that lists disposition but has no operational mechanism for carrying it out produces records that are retained long past their period, recreating the liability the schedule was meant to control.

Design disposition operationally:

- specify the disposition action for each series: destroy, transfer to archives, or review for archival selection;
- define the trigger for disposition, a fixed period, an event such as separation or project close, or a combination;
- establish the mechanism, who performs the disposition, how it is authorized, and how it is recorded;
- require documentation of destruction, including certificates of destruction for sensitive records;
- build disposition into the systems that hold the records, such as automated deletion in email or document systems, where feasible;
- handle transfers to archives as a distinct process with appraisal, not as automatic disposition.

Disposition that is not operationalized is a schedule that exists but does not work.

### Handle Special Categories With Heightened Care

Certain record categories carry heightened risk or value and require more careful scheduling than routine administrative records. These include personnel and HR records, student records under FERPA or equivalent, health records, financial and tax records, legal and litigation files, and research records. Generic treatment of these categories creates compliance failures or privacy breaches.

For special categories:

- personnel records: distinguish types (payroll, performance, medical) with different retention and access rules;
- student records: apply FERPA or equivalent, distinguishing directory from education records and handling requests;
- health records: apply HIPAA or equivalent health privacy law, with long retention and strict access control;
- financial and tax records: apply tax authority retention, often 3-7 years, with audit extensions;
- legal and litigation files: apply litigation hold procedures that suspend destruction, and retain per legal counsel guidance;
- research records: apply funder and research integrity retention, often long, with data sharing considerations.

Each special category deserves its own analysis, not a generic period.

### Account For Litigation Holds That Override The Schedule

A retention schedule is suspended for any records subject to a litigation hold or legal preservation obligation. Destroying records under a hold is spoliation, with serious legal consequences. The schedule and the records management program must include a hold process that reliably identifies affected records and suspends their disposition until the hold is released.

Build hold management by:

- establishing a process for issuing holds in coordination with legal counsel;
- identifying and flagging affected records so they are not destroyed under the schedule;
- communicating holds to the offices and systems that hold the records;
- tracking holds and releasing them formally when the obligation ends;
- documenting that disposition resumed only after hold release.

A schedule without a working hold process is a spoliation risk waiting to materialize.

### Review And Maintain The Schedule As A Living Document

A retention schedule is not a one-time deliverable; it is a living document that must be reviewed as laws change, as the organization's operations and systems change, and as records themselves evolve. A schedule that is not reviewed decays silently, retaining periods that no longer match legal requirements or business reality. Build a review cycle into the schedule's governance.

Maintain the schedule by:

- scheduling regular review, typically every one to three years, with legal validation of changed requirements;
- updating the schedule when systems change, such as migration to a new email or document platform;
- reviewing when business processes change, such that new record series appear or old ones disappear;
- versioning the schedule so it is clear which period applied when;
- training staff on schedule changes so the operational practice follows the document.

A schedule that is not maintained becomes a liability because it provides assurance that no longer holds.

## Common Traps

### Scheduling From A Generic Template

Applying a boilerplate schedule without an inventory produces periods that do not match the organization's records. This is a trap because the schedule looks complete but cannot be applied to real records.

### Unverified Legal Citations

Asserting retention periods without tracing them to specific law creates a false floor. This is a trap because the schedule will not withstand legal or audit challenge.

### Destroying Records With Archival Value

Setting retention to destroy records that have permanent historical or research value causes irreversible loss. This is a trap because the loss is discovered only when the records are needed for institutional history or research.

### Retaining Everything Forever To Be Safe

Defaulting to permanent retention to avoid destruction decisions creates storage, discovery, and privacy liability. This is a trap because the perceived safety inverts into increased legal and cost exposure.

### Disposition That Never Happens

Listing disposition actions without operational mechanisms leaves records retained past their period. This is a trap because the schedule's benefit is unrealized and the liability persists.

### Ignoring Litigation Holds

Destroying records under the schedule when a hold applies is spoliation. This is a trap because the legal consequence can be severe and the action is irreversible.

### Static Schedules That Decay

Failing to review the schedule as law and operations change makes it progressively inaccurate. This is a trap because the assurance it provides becomes false over time.

### Conflating Minimum And Recommended Retention

Treating legal minimum retention as the recommended period destroys records still needed operationally. This is a trap because the law sets a floor that operational need often exceeds.

## Self-Check

- Is the schedule built from an inventory of the organization's actual records, with series, owners, formats, and volumes?
- For each series, are legal, operational, audit, and archival drivers identified and reconciled, with the longest applicable period chosen and documented?
- Are legal requirements cited to specific statutes or regulations, validated with counsel, and dated for review?
- Is disposition designed as an operational action with triggers, mechanisms, authorization, and documentation, including destruction certificates?
- Are special categories (personnel, student, health, financial, legal, research) scheduled with category-specific analysis rather than generic periods?
- Is there a working litigation hold process that suspends disposition for affected records and documents release?
- Is the schedule versioned, scheduled for regular review, and updated when law, systems, or processes change?
- Is staff trained on the schedule so operational practice follows the document, and are exceptions documented?
