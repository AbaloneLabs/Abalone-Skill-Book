# Performance Management And Staff Development

Performance management is often reduced to the annual evaluation form, but its real substance is the ongoing work of setting clear expectations, giving honest feedback, developing people's capabilities, and addressing performance problems before they harden. In libraries, where much work is knowledge work performed by dedicated professionals across specialized domains, this is especially delicate. A cataloger, a subject librarian, an archivist, and a systems developer each do work their supervisor may not fully understand, and generic evaluation rubrics often fail to capture what matters. The result is a common failure pattern: expectations never made explicit, feedback withheld until it curdles into criticism, development treated as a luxury rather than a core management responsibility, and underperformance avoided rather than addressed. Strong performance management treats staff development as inseparable from organizational effectiveness, because the library's capacity is literally the aggregate capability and well-being of its people. Neglecting it weakens services, drives away good staff, and leaves performance problems to fester.

Use this skill when managing staff performance, setting goals and expectations, conducting evaluations, supporting professional development, addressing underperformance, and building team capability. The goal is to prevent the agent from reducing performance management to annual forms, from withholding feedback, from neglecting development, from avoiding difficult conversations, or from applying generic rubrics that miss what matters in specialized library work.

## Core Rules

### Treat Performance Management As Ongoing, Not Annual

The annual evaluation is a summary, not the substance. Performance management happens continuously.

Practice ongoing management by:

- setting expectations and goals collaboratively and revisiting them;
- giving timely feedback close to the behavior or outcome;
- having regular one-on-one conversations about work and development;
- addressing problems as they arise, not storing them for review season;
- documenting significant conversations and decisions over time.

Annual-only feedback is too late, too abstract, and too often surprising to be useful.

### Set Clear, Mutally Understood Expectations

People cannot meet expectations that were never made clear. Set them explicitly.

Set expectations by:

- defining the responsibilities and standards for each role;
- setting goals that are specific, meaningful, and achievable;
- clarifying how performance will be evaluated;
- aligning individual goals with team and organizational goals;
- confirming understanding and revisiting as roles evolve.

Unclear expectations set both the staff member and the manager up for frustration.

### Give Honest, Specific, Timely Feedback

Feedback is the core tool of development. Give it honestly and well.

Give feedback that:

- is specific, citing concrete behaviors and outcomes, not vague impressions;
- balances recognition of strengths with areas for growth;
- is timely, close to the behavior it addresses;
- is actionable, pointing toward what to continue, start, or change;
- separates the person from the performance.

Vague, delayed, or purely critical feedback does not develop people; it demoralizes them.

### Understand The Work Before Evaluating It

Library work is specialized. Managers must understand the work to evaluate it fairly.

Understand the work by:

- learning the standards, methods, and challenges of each domain;
- consulting with peers or subject experts when evaluating specialized work;
- focusing evaluation on outcomes and quality rather than prescriptive methods;
- recognizing professional judgment in areas outside the manager's expertise;
- asking the staff member to explain their work and reasoning.

Evaluating work you do not understand produces generic, often unfair judgments.

### Invest In Professional Development

Development is a core management responsibility, not a perk. Invest in it.

Invest by:

- discussing development goals and career aspirations regularly;
- providing learning opportunities, training, and conference attendance;
- supporting stretch assignments and new responsibilities;
- mentoring and connecting staff to mentors and networks;
- allocating time and budget for growth, not just output.

Development builds the library's capacity and retains engaged, growing staff.

### Address Underperformance Directly And Fairly

Avoiding underperformance problems harms the team and the individual. Address them.

Address underperformance by:

- naming the specific gap between expectations and performance;
- exploring the causes (skills, resources, clarity, personal circumstances);
- agreeing on a clear improvement plan with support and timeline;
- documenting expectations, support, and progress;
- following through on consequences if improvement does not occur.

Avoidance is not kindness; it lets problems damage the whole team and denies the individual a chance to improve.

### Recognize And Reward Good Performance

Recognition reinforces good work and signals what the organization values. Provide it.

Recognize by:

- acknowledging specific contributions and their impact;
- providing both informal and formal recognition;
- advocating for advancement, compensation, and opportunities for strong performers;
- distributing recognition equitably across visible and less visible work;
- connecting recognition to what the organization genuinely values.

Unrecognized good work leads to disengagement and attrition of the people you most want to keep.

### Build Psychological Safety And Well-Being

Performance requires an environment where people can do their best work. Build safety and well-being.

Build by:

- making it safe to raise problems, ask questions, and admit mistakes;
- addressing conflict and harmful behavior promptly;
- respecting work-life balance and sustainable workloads;
- supporting staff through personal and organizational challenges;
- modeling the honesty and care you expect.

Fear and burnout destroy performance no matter how clear the expectations.

### Evaluate Fairly And Document Thoroughly

Evaluations must be fair, consistent, and well-documented. Approach them rigorously.

Evaluate fairly by:

- applying consistent standards across staff in similar roles;
- basing evaluations on evidence of performance over time, not recent impressions;
- avoiding bias related to identity, tenure, visibility, or personality;
- documenting significant performance events and conversations;
- following institutional processes and requirements.

Fair, documented evaluation protects both staff and the institution.

### Connect Individual Growth To Organizational Capacity

Individual development is how the organization builds capacity. Connect the two.

Connect by:

- aligning development with the library's strategic needs and future roles;
- building cross-training and redundancy in critical skills;
- succession planning for key positions;
- treating staff capability as a strategic asset to be grown;
- recognizing that the library's capacity is the sum of its people's growth.

Performance management is not separate from strategy; it is how strategy becomes capacity.

## Common Traps

### Reducing Performance Management To Annual Forms

Annual evaluation is a summary. The substance is ongoing expectations, feedback, and development.

### Withholding Feedback Until It Curdles

Delayed feedback is surprising and demoralizing. Give it timely and specifically.

### Unclear Or Unspoken Expectations

People cannot meet expectations that were never made clear. Set them explicitly.

### Evaluating Work You Do Not Understand

Specialized library work requires understanding or consultation. Avoid generic judgments.

### Treating Development As A Luxury

Development builds organizational capacity. Invest in it as a core responsibility.

### Avoiding Underperformance Conversations

Avoidance harms the team and the individual. Address problems directly and fairly.

### Failing To Recognize Good Work

Unrecognized performance leads to disengagement and attrition of top staff.

### Ignoring Psychological Safety And Burnout

Fear and burnout destroy performance regardless of expectations and feedback.

## Self-Check

- Did you treat performance management as ongoing, with regular feedback and conversations?
- Did you set clear, mutually understood expectations and goals?
- Did you give honest, specific, timely, and actionable feedback?
- Did you understand the specialized work before evaluating it, consulting experts where needed?
- Did you invest in professional development aligned with growth and organizational needs?
- Did you address underperformance directly, fairly, and with documented support?
- Did you recognize and reward good performance equitably, including less visible work?
- Did you build psychological safety, address conflict, and respect well-being?
- Did you evaluate fairly, consistently, and with thorough documentation?
- Did you connect individual development to the library's strategic capacity and succession?
