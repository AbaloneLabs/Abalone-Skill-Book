# Project And Portfolio Management In Libraries

Libraries run on projects as much as on ongoing services. A new discovery platform, a collections move, a digitization grant, a strategic initiative, a building renovation, a process redesign, each is a project: a bounded effort with a beginning, an end, a scope, and a set of deliverables that must be delivered alongside the services that never stop. When projects are managed ad hoc, they miss deadlines, exceed budgets, exhaust staff, and produce deliverables that do not meet the need. When multiple projects run simultaneously without portfolio-level coordination, they compete for the same staff and resources, create invisible dependencies, and collectively overload the organization even when each individual project looks reasonable. Project and portfolio management in libraries is the discipline of delivering bounded work successfully while protecting ongoing services and staff capacity. It requires methods suited to library environments, which are often collaborative, cross-functional, and staffed by people whose primary jobs are not project management.

Use this skill when planning or overseeing library projects and portfolios, scoping and scheduling work, managing cross-functional teams and stakeholders, and selecting project management methods. The goal is to prevent the agent from running projects ad hoc, from neglecting portfolio-level capacity and dependencies, from under-scoping or over-committing, or from applying methods that do not fit the library context.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Projects From Ongoing Operations

Projects and operations require different management. Distinguish them clearly.

Distinguish:

- **Projects:** bounded efforts with defined scope, schedule, and deliverables (a migration, a move, a grant).
- **Operations:** ongoing services that must continue regardless of projects (reference, circulation, cataloging).

Conflating the two leads to projects treated as open-ended and operations disrupted by project demands. Manage each appropriately.

### Define Clear Scope, Deliverables, And Success Criteria

A project without a defined scope will expand and drift. Define scope explicitly.

Define:

- the specific deliverables the project will produce;
- what is in scope and, equally important, what is out of scope;
- the success criteria and how they will be measured;
- the boundaries that prevent scope creep;
- the assumptions and constraints the project operates under.

A clear scope is the foundation of realistic planning and of saying no to expansion.

### Plan Schedule, Resources, And Dependencies

A project plan connects scope to time, people, and dependencies. Build a realistic plan.

Plan:

- the sequence of tasks and milestones;
- the people and skills required, and their availability;
- dependencies between tasks and on external factors;
- the budget and material resources needed;
- buffers for uncertainty and for the reality that staff have other duties.

Plans that ignore dependencies and capacity are optimistic fictions that collapse on contact with reality.

### Manage Stakeholders And Communication

Projects affect and are affected by many stakeholders. Manage them deliberately.

Manage stakeholders by:

- identifying who is affected, who has influence, and who must be informed;
- establishing communication channels and cadence;
- setting expectations about scope, timeline, and tradeoffs;
- involving stakeholders in decisions that affect them;
- surfacing concerns and resistance early rather than at launch.

Poor stakeholder management is a leading cause of project failure despite good technical execution.

### Build And Support Cross-Functional Teams

Library projects usually span departments and job types. Build and support effective teams.

Build and support:

- teams with the skills and authority needed to deliver;
- clear roles, responsibilities, and decision rights;
- shared understanding of goals and approach;
- time and space for collaboration amid other duties;
- psychological safety for raising problems and dissent.

A team assembled in name only, without time or authority, cannot deliver a complex project.

### Select Methods Suited To The Work

Different projects suit different methods. Choose deliberately rather than by default.

Consider:

- **Waterfall methods:** for projects with clear, stable requirements and sequential phases (building moves, migrations with fixed scope).
- **Iterative and agile methods:** for projects with evolving requirements and benefit from feedback (software, service redesign).
- **Hybrid approaches:** combining phases with iterative components.
- **Lightweight methods:** for small projects that do not warrant heavy process.

Match the method to the project's nature, size, and uncertainty.

### Manage The Portfolio, Not Just Individual Projects

Projects do not run in isolation. Manage the portfolio of initiatives collectively.

Manage the portfolio by:

- tracking all active and planned projects and their resource demands;
- identifying conflicts and dependencies across projects;
- balancing the portfolio against available staff capacity;
- prioritizing projects against strategic goals when capacity is constrained;
- saying no or deferring projects that overload the organization.

Portfolio management prevents the collective overload that dooms individually reasonable projects.

### Protect Ongoing Services And Staff Capacity

Projects must not consume the capacity needed for operations. Protect both.

Protect by:

- accounting for project work against staff's operational responsibilities;
- avoiding assigning the same people to too many concurrent projects;
- providing backfill or workload adjustment for heavy project commitments;
- recognizing the limits of volunteer or "extra" effort;
- monitoring for burnout and overload signals.

Projects that rely on unsustainable effort produce exhausted staff and degraded services.

### Track Progress, Risks, And Issues

Active projects need tracking to stay on course. Monitor progress, risks, and issues.

Track:

- progress against milestones and deliverables;
- budget and resource consumption;
- risks and mitigation actions;
- issues requiring decisions or escalation;
- changes to scope, schedule, or approach.

Tracking turns a plan into a managed effort that can adapt to reality.

### Close Projects Deliberately And Capture Learning

Projects need deliberate closure, not just drift into operations. Close them properly.

Close by:

- confirming deliverables meet the success criteria;
- transitioning the work to ongoing operations with clear ownership;
- documenting decisions, configurations, and lessons learned;
- releasing project resources back to the portfolio;
- celebrating completion and acknowledging contributors.

Deliberate closure captures learning and prevents orphaned deliverables.

## Common Traps

### Running Projects Ad Hoc

Without defined scope, plan, and tracking, projects miss deadlines and exhaust staff.

### Conflating Projects With Operations

Projects are bounded; operations are ongoing. Manage each appropriately.

### Under-Scoping Or Ignoring Dependencies

Optimistic plans collapse on contact with dependencies and capacity limits.

### Over-Committing The Portfolio

Individually reasonable projects collectively overload the organization. Manage the portfolio.

### Neglecting Stakeholder Management

Good technical execution fails without stakeholder communication and buy-in.

### Assembling Teams In Name Only

Teams need time, authority, and psychological safety, not just a roster.

### Applying One Method To All Projects

Match the method to the project's nature, size, and uncertainty.

### Relying On Unsustainable Effort

Projects built on volunteer or overload effort degrade services and burn out staff.

### Drifting Into Operations Without Closure

Close projects deliberately, transition ownership, and capture lessons learned.

## Self-Check

- Did you distinguish the project from ongoing operations and manage each appropriately?
- Did you define clear scope, deliverables, success criteria, and boundaries?
- Did you build a realistic plan accounting for schedule, resources, and dependencies?
- Did you identify and manage stakeholders with clear communication and expectations?
- Did you build and support a cross-functional team with real time and authority?
- Did you select a project method suited to the work's nature and uncertainty?
- Did you manage the portfolio, balancing projects against capacity and dependencies?
- Did you protect ongoing services and staff capacity from project overload?
- Did you track progress, risks, issues, and changes actively?
- Did you close the project deliberately, transition ownership, and capture learning?
