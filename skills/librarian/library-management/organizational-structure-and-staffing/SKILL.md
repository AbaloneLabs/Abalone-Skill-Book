# Organizational Structure And Staffing

Organizational structure is the architecture that determines whether a library's strategy can actually be executed. It looks like boxes and lines on a chart, but it is really a set of decisions about power, accountability, communication, and capacity. Every structure enables some behaviors and suppresses others. A structure built for print-era technical services will quietly resist digital initiatives. A structure that concentrates all decisions in one director will bottleneck growth and disempower front-line staff. The judgment problem is that there is no neutral org chart: any arrangement creates tradeoffs between specialization and flexibility, hierarchy and agility, stability and innovation. Restructuring is also high-risk because it disrupts the lives and morale of real people, so it must be done with evidence, transparency, and follow-through rather than as a reflex to dissatisfaction.

Agents helping with structure and staffing tend to err by redrawing charts without diagnosing the real problem, by copying peer structures that fit a different context, or by treating staffing as headcount arithmetic rather than a question of skills, roles, and workflow. This skill covers the judgment needed to align structure with strategy, right-size staffing to actual workload, and manage the human consequences of change.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose Before Redrawing

The most common failure is solving the wrong problem with a reorganization. Before touching the chart, diagnose what is actually broken.

Diagnose by examining:

- where decisions stall or pile up, the real bottlenecks;
- where work falls between departments or is duplicated;
- whether the problem is structure, process, skills, or culture;
- what the strategy now requires that the current structure cannot deliver;
- staff perceptions gathered through structured input, not anecdotes;
- span-of-control and workload data for managers and individual contributors.

If the diagnosis points to process or skills, restructuring will not fix it and may make things worse. Restructure only when structure is genuinely the constraint.

### Align Structure To Strategy, Not To History

Structures tend to inherit the past. Departments formed around legacy functions persist long after their rationale fades, while emerging needs go unstaffed or are bolted onto existing units.

Align by asking:

- what capabilities the strategy requires that do not yet have a home;
- which legacy units have shrinking rationale and could be merged or sunset;
- where cross-functional work is needed and how structure enables or blocks it;
- whether current reporting lines route decisions to the right authority;
- how new functions, digital scholarship, data services, makerspaces, are integrated rather than orphaned.

Structure should follow strategy. When strategy shifts, structure must be willing to follow, even when it is uncomfortable.

### Balance Specialization Against Integration

Deep specialization produces expertise but creates silos. Total integration produces flexibility but dilutes expertise. Every library must find its balance.

Consider:

- functional teams build depth, for example cataloging, preservation, instruction;
- cross-functional teams or service teams build integration, for example a team spanning liaison, instruction, and collection work for a discipline;
- matrix structures can combine both but require clear authority to avoid confusion;
- communities of practice can preserve expertise across team boundaries without formal reporting lines.

There is no universally correct balance. Match the structure to the work: stable, repeatable work favors functional teams; complex, evolving work favors integrated teams.

### Right-Size Staffing To Real Workload

Staffing decisions need evidence about actual workload, not impressions or headcount comparisons alone.

Gather:

- service point coverage requirements by hour and location;
- transaction volume and complexity across desks and channels;
- project and program workload beyond steady-state services;
- collection and technical services throughput against acquisitions and processing load;
- administrative and governance load on managers;
- realistic non-productive time, training, meetings, breaks, leave coverage.

Use this to model required capacity and identify gaps or surpluses. Beware of benchmarking headcount alone without accounting for service hours, scope, and automation.

### Design Positions Around Skills And Growth

Job design is a staffing decision with long consequences. Poorly designed positions either underuse talent or set people up to fail.

Design positions by:

- defining the core purpose and outcomes, not just tasks;
- identifying required competencies and their level;
- building in growth and development, not dead-end roles;
- distinguishing professional from paraprofessional scope honestly;
- aligning compensation with responsibility and market;
- writing descriptions flexible enough to evolve.

Rigid task-based descriptions calcify. Outcome-based descriptions let roles grow as needs change.

### Manage Span Of Control Deliberately

Too many direct reports overwhelm managers; too few create redundant layers. Span of control should be a deliberate choice.

Guidance:

- first-line supervisors of complex services often manage five to seven direct reports;
- managers of routine operations can manage more;
- spans shrink when work is complex, ambiguous, or crisis-prone;
- every management layer adds communication overhead and distance from the front line;
- flat structures improve agility but can overload managers and limit advancement paths.

Choose span to balance managerial capacity, employee support, and organizational agility.

### Build Transparent Reporting And Accountability

Ambiguous authority produces conflict and inaction. Clear reporting lines and accountability reduce friction.

Establish:

- one clear primary supervisor per position;
- defined decision rights for each role and level;
- mechanisms for cross-unit collaboration without confusing authority;
- performance expectations tied to outcomes;
- regular review of whether the structure is working.

Matrix and team structures can work but only with explicitly negotiated authority. Ambiguity is not flexibility; it is a source of stress.

### Plan For Workforce Transitions

Staffing is dynamic. People retire, skills become obsolete, and new capabilities are needed.

Plan transitions by:

- mapping aging skills and pending retirements;
- identifying reskilling paths for current staff;
- designing recruitment pipelines for scarce skills;
- using temporary, grant-funded, or partnership roles for experimental functions;
- building cross-training so critical knowledge is not held by one person.

Workforce planning prevents the crisis of sudden, unrecoverable knowledge loss.

### Manage The Human Consequence Of Change

Restructuring affects careers, identities, and trust. Treating it as a technical exercise destroys morale.

Manage change by:

- communicating the rationale honestly and early;
- involving affected staff in redesign where possible;
- providing transition support, retraining, and clear timelines;
- protecting people, not just positions, where feasible;
- being transparent about what is decided versus open for input;
- following through on commitments made during the process.

A restructure that loses trust costs more than it saves.

## Common Traps

### Restructuring As A Reflex

Redrawing the chart feels like action but often masks process, skills, or culture problems. The trap is mistaking motion for progress. Diagnose first.

### Copying A Peer Structure

What works for a peer with different scale, funding, and culture will not transplant cleanly. The trap is benchmarking without context. Adapt principles, not blueprints.

### Preserving Legacy Departments

Units persist because they have advocates and history, not because they serve current strategy. The trap is inertia. Review legacy structures against strategy regularly.

### Headcount Benchmarking Without Context

Comparing total staff to a peer ignores service hours, scope, automation, and mission. The trap is false precision. Model real workload instead.

### Task-Based Job Descriptions

Descriptions that enumerate tasks become obsolete and constrain staff. The trap is over-specification. Define outcomes and competencies.

### Overloaded Managers

Adding management duties without reducing other work, or setting spans too wide, burns out supervisors. The trap is treating management as a side task. Size spans and load realistically.

### Ambiguous Authority In Matrix Structures

Matrix structures without clear decision rights produce conflict and paralysis. The trap is assuming collaboration replaces authority. Negotiate decision rights explicitly.

### Ignoring Workforce Transitions

Waiting for retirements or skill gaps to become crises forces reactive, damaging choices. The trap is short-term thinking. Plan transitions proactively.

### Change Without Trust

Restructuring imposed opaquely damages morale for years. The trap is treating people as components. Lead change with honesty and support.

## Self-Check

- Was the real problem diagnosed, and is structure genuinely the constraint rather than process, skills, or culture?
- Does the proposed structure align with current strategy rather than inherited history?
- Is the balance of specialization and integration matched to the actual nature of the work?
- Are staffing levels based on real workload evidence, including service hours, transaction complexity, and non-productive time?
- Are positions designed around outcomes, competencies, and growth rather than rigid task lists?
- Are spans of control and management layers chosen deliberately, not accidentally?
- Are reporting lines and decision rights clear enough to prevent conflict and inaction?
- Is there a workforce transition plan addressing retirements, obsolete skills, and new capabilities?
- Was the human impact of any change managed with honesty, involvement, and support?
- Could a new staff member understand who decides what and who they go to for each issue from the structure alone?
