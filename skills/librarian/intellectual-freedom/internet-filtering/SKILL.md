# Internet Filtering

Internet filtering on library computers sits at the intersection of intellectual freedom, legal compliance, community expectations, and practical technology management. The decision to filter, and how to configure filters, determines what patrons can access, what the library is liable for, and how it upholds its mission to provide unbiased access to information. Filtering is never a clean technical solution. Filters over-block legitimate content, especially on health, LGBTQ, and minority subjects, and under-block content they are supposed to catch. They are opaque, vendor-controlled, and often inflexible. A librarian approaching filtering must understand the legal requirements that may compel it, the intellectual freedom principles that argue against it, the practical harms of over-blocking, and the policies and procedures that mitigate those harms when filtering is required.

Use this skill when deciding on or configuring internet filtering, handling over-blocking complaints, responding to patron concerns about online content, or developing internet use policy. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating filtering as a simple on-off decision, ignoring over-blocking harms, accepting vendor categories uncritically, or failing to provide unblocking mechanisms for adults.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Legal Context That May Compel Filtering

In some jurisdictions, libraries face legal requirements related to internet filtering, often tied to funding or child protection laws. Understand what applies.

Legal considerations:

- funding conditions, such as US E-rate requirements, that mandate filtering for compliance;
- the distinction between filtering for minors and for adults;
- legal requirements to disable filters for adults on request;
- local laws and governance directives;
- the limits of what filtering laws actually require.

Do not assume filtering is universally required or universally prohibited. Know the specific legal obligations and their boundaries.

### Apply Intellectual Freedom Principles

Professional intellectual freedom principles hold that libraries should provide access to information without censorship. Filtering, by design, restricts access based on content categories determined by vendors.

Principles:

- adults have the right to access lawful information without restriction;
- filtering imposes a third party's, the vendor's, judgment on what is appropriate;
- filters are opaque and not accountable to the community;
- the library's mission is to provide access, not to substitute its judgment for the user's;
- where filtering is not legally required, it should generally be avoided.

When filtering is required by law or policy, mitigate its effects rather than embracing it as a value.

### Recognize That Filters Over-Block And Under-Block

Filters are imperfect. They block content they should not and fail to block content they should.

Over-blocking:

- blocks legitimate health, medical, and LGBTQ content;
- blocks educational and research materials;
- disproportionately affects minority and marginalized subjects;
- blocks based on keyword matching without context.

Under-blocking:

- fails to catch content in blocked categories;
- cannot keep up with the volume and change of online content;
- gives a false sense of protection.

Treat filters as imperfect tools, not reliable guardians. Their failures cut both ways and must be managed.

### Scrutinize And Customize Vendor Categories

Vendor filters come with predefined categories, often opaque, that determine what is blocked. Accepting them uncritically cedes control to the vendor.

Scrutinize:

- request and review the category definitions from vendors;
- identify categories that over-block legitimate content;
- customize or disable problematic categories where the system allows;
- avoid categories based on viewpoint or political content;
- document the configuration and its rationale.

Vendor categories reflect vendor judgments, not library values. Customize them to align with intellectual freedom principles where possible.

### Provide A Clear, Accessible Unblocking Mechanism For Adults

Where filtering is required, adults must have a way to access lawful content that the filter blocks. This is both a legal requirement in many jurisdictions and an intellectual freedom imperative.

Unblocking:

- provide a simple, prompt process for adults to request unblocking;
- train staff to handle requests without judgment or interrogation;
- do not require the user to justify the request;
- unblock at the session or site level, not by content review;
- publicize the availability of unblocking.

A filter without an unblocking mechanism is censorship. A cumbersome or judgmental unblocking process effectively is too.

### Handle Patron Complaints About Content With Policy

Patrons may complain about content others are accessing or about content being blocked. Handle both through clear policy.

For complaints about accessed content:

- explain the library's internet use policy and intellectual freedom stance;
- clarify that the library does not monitor or police lawful use;
- offer privacy screens or designated areas as practical measures;
- do not remove access based on individual objection.

For complaints about blocked content:

- explain the filtering requirement and the unblocking mechanism;
- offer to unblock for adult users promptly;
- document over-blocking patterns to inform configuration.

Policy-based responses prevent ad hoc reactions that undermine principles or service.

### Differentiate Filtering For Minors And Adults

Legal and policy requirements often differ for minors and adults. Understand and apply the distinction.

Considerations:

- some laws require filtering for minors but allow adult unblocking;
- policies may set different filtering levels by age group;
- minor filtering, if required, should still minimize over-blocking of educational content;
- respect parental guidance while not imposing one family's standards on all.

Do not apply minor-level filtering to adults, and do not apply adult-level access to minors where law or policy requires otherwise. Configure age-appropriate access thoughtfully.

### Maintain Privacy In Internet Access

Filtering and monitoring can compromise patron privacy. Maintain privacy as a core value.

Privacy practices:

- do not monitor or log individual browsing beyond what is technically necessary;
- do not retain browsing histories longer than needed;
- do not require identification to use filtered or unfiltered access beyond standard login;
- protect any access data from misuse;
- align filtering and monitoring with the library's privacy policy.

Filtering infrastructure can become surveillance infrastructure. Limit its data collection and retention.

### Educate Staff And Patrons On Policy And Rights

Staff and patrons should understand the filtering policy, its rationale, and users' rights.

Education:

- train staff on the policy, unblocking procedures, and how to handle complaints;
- inform patrons of filtering, unblocking rights, and privacy practices;
- provide clear signage and accessible policy documents;
- explain the legal context where relevant.

Informed staff handle situations consistently; informed patrons know and can exercise their rights.

### Review Filtering Policy And Configuration Periodically

Filtering technology, legal requirements, and community needs change. Review the policy and configuration regularly.

Review:

- changes in law or funding requirements;
- vendor category updates and their effects;
- over-blocking and under-blocking patterns from logs and complaints;
- community feedback and usage data;
- alignment with current intellectual freedom interpretations.

Static filtering policy becomes misaligned over time. Schedule periodic review as part of technology and policy stewardship.

## Common Traps

### Treating Filtering As A Simple On-Off Decision

Filtering involves categories, configurations, and mitigations. Engage with the details.

### Ignoring Over-Blocking Harms

Over-blocking disproportionately harms marginalized subjects and legitimate research. Recognize and mitigate it.

### Accepting Vendor Categories Uncritically

Vendor categories reflect vendor judgment. Scrutinize and customize them.

### No Or Judgmental Unblocking For Adults

Without accessible, non-judgmental unblocking, filtering is censorship. Provide and publicize the mechanism.

### Removing Access Based On Individual Complaint

Ad hoc removals bypass policy and undermine principles. Respond through policy.

### Applying Minor Filtering To Adults

Age-appropriate access matters. Do not over-restrict adults or under-protect minors where required.

### Filtering Infrastructure Becoming Surveillance

Limit data collection and retention to protect privacy.

### Static Policy And Configuration

Review filtering periodically as law, technology, and needs change.

## Self-Check

- Is the legal context for filtering understood, including funding conditions and age distinctions?
- Are intellectual freedom principles applied, treating filtering as a constraint to mitigate rather than a value?
- Is the reality of over-blocking and under-blocking recognized and managed?
- Are vendor categories scrutinized and customized to align with library values where possible?
- Is there a clear, accessible, non-judgmental unblocking mechanism for adults, publicized and staffed?
- Are patron complaints about content handled through policy, not ad hoc reactions?
- Is filtering differentiated appropriately for minors and adults per law and policy?
- Is patron privacy maintained, with minimal logging and data retention?
- Are staff and patrons educated on filtering policy, rights, and procedures?
- Is filtering policy and configuration reviewed periodically as law, technology, and needs change?
