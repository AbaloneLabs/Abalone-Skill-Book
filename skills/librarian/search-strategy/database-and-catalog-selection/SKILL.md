# Database And Catalog Selection

The single most consequential decision in a search is which tool to search. A catalog answers known-item and holdings questions but not article questions. A subject database answers scholarly questions but not current community-service questions. A government site answers official-statistic and benefits questions but not literary-criticism questions. The open web answers some current and popular questions but is unreliable for scholarly, historical, licensed, or sensitive topics. Searchers who default to one tool regardless of need produce poor results: too many irrelevant hits, missed authoritative sources, and conclusions that "nothing exists" when the right tool was never tried. Selecting the source type deliberately, before choosing search terms, is what separates a competent search from keyword guessing.

Use this skill before building search terms, before running a search, and whenever a search is producing poor results. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to a familiar tool, from confusing full-text convenience with source quality, and from concluding nothing exists when the right source was never consulted.

## Core Rules

### Match The Source Type To The Information Need

Different questions live in different tools. Identify the kind of answer required, then choose the tool that holds that kind of answer.

Common source types and what they answer:

- catalog: does the library own this item, and where is it? Holdings, known items, browse by call number;
- discovery layer: a unified search across catalog and many databases, good for breadth but noisy;
- subject database: scholarly articles in a discipline (e.g., PubMed for medicine, ERIC for education, Historical Abstracts for history);
- general academic database: multidisciplinary articles, good starting breadth;
- index or bibliography: citations and abstracts, often deeper coverage than full-text databases;
- reference work: encyclopedias, dictionaries, handbooks for overview, definitions, and background;
- news database: current and historical news, with stable archives unlike the open web;
- statistical source: government agencies, international organizations, vetted data portals;
- government source: laws, regulations, statistics, forms, benefits, official records;
- archive or special collections finding aid: primary sources, manuscripts, organizational records;
- community and directory source: local services, organizations, contact information;
- open web: current events, official sites, some gray literature, but uneven quality.

Choose based on what the patron needs, not on what opens fastest.

### Decide Between Known-Item And Topic Search

A known-item search (a specific title, author, or citation) is a retrieval problem best solved by the catalog or a citation linker. A topic search is an exploration problem best solved by subject databases, reference works, and indexes. Mixing these up wastes effort: searching a topic in the catalog finds whole books but misses articles, while searching a known title in a subject database often finds nothing.

Confirm which kind of search you are running:

- known item: use catalog, citation linker, or Google Scholar to locate full text;
- topic exploration: use reference works for background, then subject databases for depth;
- both: start with the catalog to see what the library owns, then move to databases.

### Check Coverage, Scope, And Date Range Before Trusting Results

A database's results are bounded by what it covers. Two databases with the same interface can cover different years, disciplines, languages, regions, and publication types. A search that returns nothing may mean the topic is absent from that database, not from the literature.

Before trusting results, check:

- date coverage: does the database include the years you need?;
- disciplinary scope: does it cover the relevant field, or only adjacent ones?;
- publication types: articles, books, dissertations, reports, news?;
- language and geographic coverage: English-only databases miss large literatures;
- full-text versus indexing: an index may cite more than it delivers;
- update frequency: how current is the most recent content?.

Read the database's scope note or "about" page. Coverage gaps are the most common cause of false "nothing exists" conclusions.

### Use Subject Databases For Depth, Discovery For Breadth

Discovery layers are convenient because they search many sources at once, but their breadth produces noise and they often rank familiar full-text items over more relevant indexed ones. For serious research, go to the specialized subject database.

Rule of thumb:

- discovery layer: good for a first look, for undergraduates starting out, and for cross-disciplinary topics;
- subject database: good for depth, for precise vocabulary, and for discipline-specific features like thesauri;
- both: use discovery to identify which subject database is relevant, then search that database directly.

### Layer Sources From Background To Depth

Strong searches move through source types in a sequence that builds understanding.

A typical research sequence:

- reference work or encyclopedia for background, key terms, and landmark sources;
- catalog for books that synthesize the topic;
- subject databases for articles with current research;
- indexes and bibliographies for comprehensive coverage;
- primary sources or archives for original evidence;
- citation tracing from key sources to find related work.

Jumping straight to articles without background produces shallow searches that miss foundational work.

### Consider Access, Licensing, And Format Constraints

The best source is useless if the patron cannot access it. Consider:

- is the database licensed for this patron's affiliation?;
- is full text available, or only indexing?;
- is the item available in print, microform, or off-site storage?;
- can it be requested through interlibrary loan?;
- does the patron have the technology or connectivity for streaming or large downloads?;
- are there accessibility needs (screen reader, captioning, alternative format)?.

Offer paths around access barriers rather than abandoning the source. Interlibrary loan, document delivery, and accessibility services exist for this reason.

### Evaluate The Open Web Critically Before Using It

The open web is a legitimate source for current events, official agency sites, some gray literature, and primary materials that institutions have posted. It is unreliable for scholarly depth, historical accuracy, licensed content, and sensitive topics where commercial or scam content dominates.

When using the web:

- prefer official domains (.gov, established institutions);
- verify authorship and authority;
- check the date;
- compare against a vetted source;
- never present a random web page as equivalent to a peer-reviewed source or official record.

### Record Which Sources Were Searched

For research that matters, record which databases and catalogs were searched, with what terms and dates. This prevents repeated dead ends, supports an honest "here is what I tried" explanation, and lets a colleague continue the work. It also protects against the false conclusion that nothing exists.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To The Open Web

Convenient and fast, but it misses licensed databases, scholarly depth, and authoritative records, and it surfaces commercial and inaccurate content for sensitive topics.

### Treating The Discovery Layer As Sufficient

Discovery is broad and noisy. Serious depth requires the specialized subject database.

### Confusing Full-Text Availability With Source Quality

The item that opens instantly is not necessarily the best source. An indexed-only citation may be the most relevant item, worth requesting.

### Ignoring Coverage And Scope Notes

A "no results" message often reflects the database's coverage, not the literature. Check what the database actually includes.

### Searching A Topic In The Catalog And Concluding Nothing Exists

The catalog holds whole items, not articles. Topic searches need databases and indexes.

### Forgetting Print, Microform, And Off-Site Holdings

Digital convenience can hide rich print and archival collections. Check all formats.

### Overlooking Government And Community Sources

For statistics, laws, benefits, and local services, official and community sources often beat commercial databases.

### Not Considering Access Barriers

Recommending a source the patron cannot open helps no one. Plan around licensing, technology, and accessibility.

## Self-Check

- Did you identify the information need type and choose the source type that holds that kind of answer?
- Did you distinguish known-item retrieval from topic exploration and choose the tool accordingly?
- Did you check each database's coverage, scope, date range, publication types, and language before trusting results?
- Did you use discovery for breadth and a subject database for depth rather than relying on one?
- Did you layer sources from background to depth rather than jumping to articles?
- Did you account for access, licensing, format, technology, and accessibility constraints?
- Did you evaluate open-web sources critically, preferring official and authoritative domains?
- Did you record which sources and terms were searched so the work is reproducible and honest?
- Did you avoid concluding "nothing exists" when the right source was never tried?
