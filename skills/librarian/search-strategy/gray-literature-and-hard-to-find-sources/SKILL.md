# Gray Literature And Hard-To-Find Sources

Not all evidence lives in indexed databases. Much of the most useful material for a serious question, theses, government and intergovernmental reports, technical standards, white papers, clinical trial protocols, statistical releases, institutional repositories, working papers, and preprints, is published outside the commercial channels that mainstream databases index. This is gray literature, and finding it requires a different posture from ordinary catalog or database searching. Gray literature is valuable precisely because it is less filtered: it may contain negative results, local data, earlier-stage findings, or perspectives excluded from mainstream journals. But the same qualities make it hard to find, hard to evaluate, and easy to miss. An agent who searches only the major databases can return a confident answer that is silently incomplete, missing the very sources that would change the conclusion. Searching gray literature is a discipline of knowing where to look, who to ask, and how to recognize and verify material that lacks the familiar signals of peer-reviewed publication.

Use this skill when databases return too little relevant material, when the question concerns local, institutional, government, or unpublished evidence, when performing a systematic review that requires gray literature, when standards, patents, or theses are needed, and when teaching researchers to look beyond indexed journals. The goal is to prevent the agent from over-relying on mainstream databases, from treating absence of evidence as evidence of absence, and from presenting unverified gray sources as authoritative.

## Core Rules

### Recognize When Gray Literature Is Necessary

Gray literature is not optional for many questions. Recognize the signals that indexed databases will be insufficient.

Gray literature is necessary when:

- the question concerns policy, government action, statistics, or regulation;
- systematic reviews and guidelines require comprehensive evidence including unpublished studies;
- the topic is emerging, interdisciplinary, or under-represented in mainstream journals;
- local, regional, or institutional evidence is needed;
- negative or null results, which are under-published in journals, may matter;
- technical standards, specifications, or patents are relevant.

If the question fits any of these, plan to search beyond mainstream databases from the start.

### Map The Landscape Of Gray Literature Sources

Gray literature is distributed across many source types. Know the landscape so you search the right places.

Major categories include:

- **Government and intergovernmental:** agency reports, statistical releases, legislative documents, white papers, treaties.
- **Institutional repositories:** theses and dissertations, faculty working papers, university research outputs.
- **Clinical trial registries:** trial protocols, results postings, and ongoing studies.
- **Standards bodies and professional organizations:** technical standards, guidelines, conference proceedings.
- **Think tanks and research institutes:** policy briefs, working papers, reports.
- **Preprint servers:** early-stage research before peer review.
- **Patent databases:** technical and prior-art information.
- **Conferences and professional bodies:** proceedings, presentations, posters.

Each category has its own search tools and access patterns. Map the relevant categories before searching.

### Use Specialized Search Tools For Each Source Type

Gray literature is rarely found through a single search box. Use the tool matched to the source type.

Matched tools include:

- **Theses and dissertations:** dissertation databases, national thesis repositories, institutional repositories, catalog advanced searches limited to thesis format.
- **Government documents:** government catalog services, agency websites, legal and legislative databases, depository libraries.
- **Clinical trials:** trial registries, searched by condition, intervention, and sponsor.
- **Standards:** standards body databases and library subscriptions to standards.
- **Preprints:** preprint aggregators and discipline-specific servers.
- **Patents:** patent office databases and commercial patent search platforms.
- **Reports and white papers:** think-tank sites, repository aggregators, and advanced web search limited to file types and domains.

Using the wrong tool is a common reason gray literature searches fail.

### Use Advanced Web Search Strategically

Much gray literature lives on the open web, but finding it requires more than a keyword search. Use advanced operators deliberately.

Useful techniques:

- limit to file types likely to contain reports (PDF, DOCX);
- limit to authoritative domains (government, intergovernmental, academic, institutional);
- search site-specific when an organization is known;
- use quotation marks for exact phrases and titles;
- check multiple search engines, since coverage differs;
- use archive and cache tools when pages have moved or disappeared.

Web search is a supplement to specialized tools, not a replacement for them.

### Search Trial Registries And Ongoing Work

For health, social, and policy questions, ongoing and unpublished studies are evidence. Trial registries capture studies that may never be journal-published.

Search:

- trial registries by condition, intervention, and sponsor;
- protocol registrations for ongoing systematic reviews;
- funder databases for funded but not yet published work;
- conference proceedings for recently presented findings.

Ongoing and unpublished work is essential for completeness in systematic reviews and for assessing publication bias.

### Trace Citations And Contact Authors

When formal searches stall, citation tracing and direct contact often find gray literature that no database indexes.

Trace by:

- backward citation tracing from key known sources to their references;
- forward citation tracing to find later work citing a key source;
- contacting authors of relevant work for unpublished data or in-progress studies;
- contacting experts, organizations, and relevant mailing lists or communities.

Citation tracing and author contact are especially powerful for emerging topics and for finding unpublished negative results.

### Document Gray Literature Searches As Rigorously As Mainstream Ones

Gray literature searches are easy to under-document because they are less standardized. For systematic work, document them fully.

Record:

- each source searched (website, registry, repository, database);
- the date searched;
- the search terms or approach used;
- any limits applied (date, file type, domain);
- the number of results and how they were screened;
- hand-searching of specific journals or sites;
- contacts with authors or experts and their responses.

Under-documented gray literature searching weakens the credibility of the whole evidence base.

### Evaluate Gray Sources Carefully

Gray literature lacks the familiar signal of peer review. Evaluate each source against clear criteria before relying on it.

Evaluate:

- **Authority:** Who produced it? Is the institution credible and competent in the area?
- **Purpose and audience:** Is it advocacy, marketing, or objective analysis?
- **Methodology:** Is the method transparent and appropriate, or are claims unsupported?
- **Currency:** Is it recent enough for the question?
- **Accuracy and transparency:** Are sources cited, data available, and limitations acknowledged?
- **Bias:** Does the producer have a stake in the findings?

(See the related skill on evaluating sources for general evaluation principles.)

### Acknowledge Coverage Limits And Access Barriers

Gray literature searching has structural limits. Be honest about them.

Limits include:

- inconsistent indexing across repositories and databases;
- language and geographic bias, especially against Global South sources;
- access barriers (paywalls, registration, embargoes, withdrawn pages);
- link rot and unstable URLs;
- variable quality and the absence of peer review.

Acknowledging these limits in reporting is part of an honest gray literature search.

## Common Traps

### Treating Absence In Mainstream Databases As Evidence Of Absence

If the major databases return little, that signals the need for gray literature, not that little exists.

### Using A Single Search Tool For All Gray Literature

Gray literature is distributed. Use the tool matched to each source type.

### Over-Relying On Generic Web Search

Web search alone misses much gray literature and returns noisy results. Combine it with specialized tools.

### Skipping Trial Registries And Ongoing Work

Unpublished and ongoing studies are evidence. Omitting them biases systematic reviews.

### Under-Documenting Gray Literature Searches

Because these searches are less standardized, they are easy to under-report. Document source, date, terms, and results.

### Presenting Gray Sources As Authoritative Without Evaluation

Gray literature lacks peer review. Evaluate authority, methodology, bias, and currency before relying on it.

### Ignoring Language And Geographic Bias

Gray literature searching inherits coverage biases. Acknowledge and mitigate them where possible.

### Assuming URLs Are Stable

Gray literature links rot. Capture identifiers, access dates, and archived copies where possible.

## Self-Check

- Did you recognize when the question requires gray literature rather than mainstream databases alone?
- Did you map the relevant source categories (government, theses, registries, standards, preprints, patents, reports) before searching?
- Did you use the specialized tool matched to each source type rather than a single search box?
- Did you use advanced web search operators (file type, domain, exact phrase) strategically?
- Did you search trial registries and ongoing work for unpublished and in-progress studies?
- Did you trace citations and contact authors or experts to find unindexed material?
- For systematic work, did you document each gray source, date, terms, limits, and results?
- Did you evaluate each gray source for authority, methodology, bias, and currency before relying on it?
- Did you acknowledge coverage limits, language and geographic bias, and access barriers in reporting?
- Are identifiers and access dates captured so unstable sources can be relocated or verified?
