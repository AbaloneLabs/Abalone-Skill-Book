# Digital Resource Deselection

Digital resource deselection, the review and cancellation of databases, e-journals, e-book packages, and other electronic subscriptions, is where the financial and political pressures of collection management concentrate most intensely. Unlike print weeding, where the main risk is public perception, digital deselection usually involves large recurring costs, binding contracts, vendor relationships, and patrons, often faculty or entire departments, who depend on specific titles and will resist their loss. A library that never cancels underused digital resources will see its budget consumed by escalating subscriptions while new needs go unfunded. The work demands rigorous usage analysis, clear criteria, careful stakeholder communication, and the discipline to follow through even under pressure, because every dollar retained in an underused resource is a dollar unavailable for higher-value needs.

## Core Rules

### Establish a regular review cycle, not crisis cancellations

The worst time to evaluate digital resources is during a budget crisis, when decisions must be made fast, under pressure, and without sufficient analysis. Establish a standing annual or biennial review cycle in which every digital subscription is evaluated against usage, cost, and alignment with current needs, regardless of whether the budget is tight. A regular cycle normalizes deselection as responsible stewardship, builds the data and relationships needed for informed decisions, and ensures that when a crisis does require cuts, the library has already identified the weakest resources. Treat digital deselection as ongoing management, not an emergency response.

### Analyze usage with the right metrics and caveats

Usage data is the primary input for digital deselection, but it must be interpreted carefully. Examine cost-per-use as a core metric, comparing the subscription cost against sessions, searches, downloads, or full-text accesses, but recognize that raw usage metrics have limits. Some resources are used intensively by a small, vital audience; others show low downloads but high value for a specific program; some usage is driven by a single course or assignment that could shift. Look at trends over multiple years rather than a single year, distinguish between sustained and declining use, and segment usage by department, discipline, or patron type where possible. Beware of vendor-provided metrics that may be defined favorably, and prefer standardized reporting such as COUNTER reports where available. Use data to identify candidates, then apply judgment.

### Apply criteria beyond raw usage

Cost-per-use alone is not enough, because some essential resources have modest usage but irreplaceable value, and some heavily used resources are redundant with cheaper alternatives. Layer additional criteria: uniqueness, whether the content is available elsewhere through open access, consortial agreements, or interlibrary loan; curricular and research alignment, whether the resource supports current and planned programs; archival or preservation value, whether the library retains perpetual post-cancellation rights; and the cost trajectory, whether the price is escalating unsustainably. A resource that is expensive, low-use, non-unique, and poorly aligned is a clear cancellation candidate; a resource that is expensive but unique and essential may need to be retained while alternatives are sought. Weight criteria explicitly rather than letting any single number decide.

### Manage journal packages and big deals strategically

Journal packages and database bundles, often called big deals, concentrate large portions of the budget and are the most consequential deselection decisions a library can make. Analyze the actual usage of titles within the package against the cost, and compare against title-by-title subscription costs for the most-used journals. Many libraries find that a small fraction of titles drives most usage, making the full package poor value, and that moving to selective subscriptions plus interlibrary loan or open access delivers equivalent access at lower cost. Negotiate with vendors for better terms or smaller packages before cancelling outright, use consortial leverage, and model the transition costs carefully, including interlibrary-loan volume and staff time. These decisions reshape access for years, so model them thoroughly and phase changes when possible.

### Communicate with affected stakeholders before deciding

Digital deselection almost always affects specific patrons who use the resource, and surprising them with a cancellation generates resistance that can reverse the decision. Identify the departments, programs, or patron groups most affected by each candidate resource, and consult them before the decision is finalized. Share the usage data and criteria, ask whether there are uses the data does not capture, and listen for legitimate cases where low apparent use masks essential value. Consultation does not mean veto power; the library must still decide based on the whole picture. But early consultation builds understanding, surfaces hidden value, and converts potential opponents into informed participants in the decision.

### Plan the transition and preserve access where possible

Cancellation does not have to mean total loss of access. Before cancelling, confirm what perpetual or post-cancellation rights the library retains, whether through archived content, LOCKSS or PORTICO participation, or negotiated terms. Ensure continued access to essential titles through interlibrary loan, document delivery, open-access versions, or alternative subscriptions, and communicate these pathways to affected patrons. Update discovery systems, research guides, and course materials to remove dead links and point to alternatives. A well-managed transition preserves access to what matters most and reduces the practical harm of cancellation.

### Document decisions and build the renewal record

Every digital deselection decision should be documented with the criteria, the usage data, the stakeholder consultation, and the transition plan. This record serves multiple purposes: it justifies the decision to leadership and affected patrons, it provides the evidence base if the decision is questioned, and it builds institutional memory so the analysis is not repeated from scratch each cycle. Maintain a running record of subscriptions, costs, usage, and decisions over time, which becomes invaluable for future negotiations and for demonstrating responsible stewardship to funders and governance.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is deferring digital deselection until a budget crisis forces fast, under-analyzed cancellations that surprise stakeholders and damage trust. Another is relying on a single year of usage data or a single metric like total downloads, which obscures trends, hides the value of intensively-used niche resources, and misleads about sustainability. Focusing only on cost-per-use without considering uniqueness, curricular alignment, and post-cancellation rights leads to cancelling irreplaceable resources while retaining redundant ones. Letting big deals and journal packages continue unexamined because they are complex and politically difficult allows large portions of the budget to underperform for years. Surprising affected departments with cancellations after the decision, rather than consulting before, generates resistance that can reverse sound decisions and poison future relationships. Cancelling without planning for continued access through interlibrary loan, open access, or perpetual rights causes real harm to patrons and undermines the library's credibility. Failing to update discovery systems and research guides after cancellation leaves dead links and frustrated users. Finally, treating each cancellation as a one-off rather than part of a documented, recurring review cycle forces the library to rebuild the analysis every time and prevents strategic budget management.

## Self-Check

- Did you evaluate digital resources on a regular review cycle rather than only during budget crises?
- Did you analyze usage with the right metrics, including cost-per-use and multi-year trends, and prefer standardized reporting over vendor-defined numbers?
- Did you layer criteria beyond raw usage, including uniqueness, curricular alignment, post-cancellation rights, and cost trajectory?
- For journal packages and big deals, did you analyze title-level usage, compare against selective subscriptions, and negotiate or use consortial leverage before outright cancellation?
- Did you consult affected departments and patron groups before finalizing cancellations, sharing data and listening for uses the metrics miss?
- Did you confirm perpetual and post-cancellation access rights, and plan continued access through interlibrary loan, open access, or alternatives?
- Did you update discovery systems, research guides, and course materials to remove dead links and point to alternatives?
- Did you document each decision with criteria, data, consultation, and transition plan, and maintain a running record for future cycles?
