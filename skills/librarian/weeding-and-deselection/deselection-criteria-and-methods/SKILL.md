# Deselection Criteria and Methods

Weeding, or deselection, is the disciplined removal of materials from a library collection, and it is one of the most professionally necessary yet publicly sensitive tasks in collection management. A collection that is never weeded becomes crowded, outdated, and unreliable, burying current and useful material under obsolete and inaccurate titles, and signaling to patrons that the library does not care about quality. Yet weeding is also the collection task most likely to generate public controversy, media scrutiny, and accusations of censorship or disposal of public property. The work therefore demands both rigorous professional criteria and careful communication, because a defensible weeding decision executed without transparency can still become a crisis. Done well, weeding revitalizes a collection; done badly or secretly, it undermines trust that takes years to rebuild.

## Core Rules

### Ground every removal in a written deselection policy

Weeding must be governed by a written policy that is consistent with the collection-development policy and approved through the library's governance. The policy should state the criteria, the methods, the schedule, and the disposition of withdrawn material, and it should be publicly available before weeding begins. Removals made without a policy look arbitrary and become indefensible under challenge, while removals traceable to clear criteria can be explained and defended. Treat the policy as the primary shield against accusations of censorship or whim, and ensure staff apply it consistently rather than each selector weeding by personal preference.

### Apply CREI or comparable criteria systematically

The standard deselection framework, often remembered as CREI, evaluates each title against currency, reliability, and use, plus the physical and informational condition of the item. Currency asks whether the information is still accurate and relevant, which is especially critical in fast-changing fields like medicine, law, technology, and science, where outdated material can be actively harmful. Reliability asks whether the content remains trustworthy and whether later editions have superseded it. Use asks whether anyone is actually borrowing or consulting the title, typically measured by circulation and in-house use data. A title that is outdated, superseded, and unused across multiple years is a strong candidate for removal regardless of condition. Apply the criteria section by section, because appropriate thresholds differ by subject and format.

### Use MUSTIE to catch additional removal candidates

Beyond CREI, the MUSTIE framework identifies titles that should be removed for reasons beyond simple currency and use: misleading or inaccurate content that could harm a reader; ugly or worn condition beyond reasonable repair; superseded by a newer edition or better source; trivial, meaning of no lasting value; irrelevant to the current collection and community; and available elsewhere, through consortial access, digital resources, or another branch. MUSTIE is especially useful for catching material that circulates rarely for good reason but that selectors hesitate to remove on circulation alone. Combining CREI and MUSTIE gives a fuller picture than either framework alone.

### Distinguish section-by-section thresholds

Weeding criteria are not uniform across the collection, because subjects age at different rates and communities differ. Medical, legal, scientific, and technological material must be weeded aggressively, often within a few years, because outdated information can mislead or harm. Fiction, humanities, and history age more slowly and may retain value for decades, though use still matters. Reference, local-history, and special collections may warrant minimal weeding or different criteria entirely. Children's collections need attention to condition, currency, and the constant turnover of school curriculum needs. Set and document section-specific thresholds rather than applying a single rule, and revisit thresholds as community needs and publishing patterns shift.

### Combine data with shelf examination

Circulation and use data are essential inputs, but they cannot be the only basis for weeding, because a title with no recent circulations might be a dormant classic awaiting rediscovery, a research title consulted in-house, or a gap-filler that should be replaced rather than removed. Combine quantitative data with physical shelf examination, checking condition, edition, accuracy, and fit with the rest of the section. Shelf examination also reveals patterns data cannot, such as a run of outdated series, duplicated copies of former bestsellers, or sections so crowded that patrons cannot browse. The strongest weeding decisions combine both: data to identify candidates, and shelf judgment to confirm or override.

### Plan disposition responsibly and transparently

What happens to withdrawn material matters as much as the withdrawal decision, because public sensitivity often centers on disposal rather than the removal itself. Establish clear disposition procedures: offer material to other branches, the consortium, or sister libraries first; then consider book-sale donations, community partners, schools, or recycling; and only as a last resort, discard. Be transparent that some withdrawn material, especially damaged or obsolete material, cannot be donated or sold and will be recycled, and explain why keeping it is not in the public interest. Document the disposition path so accusations of trashing books can be answered factually. Avoid mass disposals that generate media images of dumpsters full of books, which damage trust regardless of the underlying merit.

### Communicate weeding proactively before controversy

The most common reason weeding becomes a public crisis is that it was done without communication, so patrons and stakeholders discover it after the fact and assume the worst. Communicate the deselection policy and schedule in advance, explain the professional rationale in plain language, invite questions, and make the criteria public. Train front-line staff to explain weeding confidently when patrons ask why a favorite title disappeared. Frame weeding as responsible stewardship that keeps the collection current and usable, not as discarding books. Proactive communication turns weeding from a liability into a demonstration of professional care.

### Handle contested withdrawals through reconsideration

Even with strong criteria, some withdrawals will be contested by patrons who valued a specific title or who suspect bias. Provide a reconsideration process for withdrawals parallel to the one for challenges to retained material, and apply it consistently. Reconsider a contested withdrawal against the stated criteria, the title's actual use and currency, and the availability of the content through other means, and be willing to reinstate or replace if the original decision was wrong. Document the reconsideration outcome. A fair process protects both the integrity of the collection and the patron's right to be heard.

## Common Traps

The most damaging trap is weeding without a written, public policy, which makes every removal look arbitrary and leaves the library unable to defend itself when controversy erupts. Another is applying uniform criteria across the whole collection rather than section-specific thresholds, leading to over-weeding of slow-aging humanities material or dangerous under-weeding of fast-changing medical, legal, and technological content. Relying on circulation data alone, without shelf examination, removes dormant classics and research material that deserve retention while missing worn, duplicated, or superseded titles that data does not flag. Weeding secretly or in large sudden batches, without proactive communication, virtually guarantees a public crisis complete with images of discarded books, regardless of how sound the decisions were. Failing to plan disposition responsibly, so that usable material is trashed rather than offered to partners or recycled, wastes resources and hands critics a visible grievance. Aggressive weeding of diverse or controversial material, even under neutral criteria, can look like censorship and must be handled with particular transparency. Skipping the reconsideration process for contested withdrawals signals that the library does not take patron concerns seriously. Finally, treating weeding as a one-time project rather than an ongoing cycle allows the collection to decay between bursts, undoing the benefits and forcing crisis-scale removals that are harder to defend.

## Self-Check

- Is every removal traceable to a written, publicly available deselection policy consistent with the collection-development policy?
- Did you apply CREI or comparable criteria, checking currency, reliability, use, and condition, with thresholds adjusted for each section's aging pattern?
- Did you use MUSTIE or equivalent checks to catch misleading, superseded, trivial, irrelevant, or elsewhere-available material that circulation alone would miss?
- Did you combine circulation and use data with physical shelf examination, rather than relying on data alone?
- Did you set section-specific thresholds, weeding fast-changing subjects like medicine, law, and technology more aggressively than slow-aging humanities?
- Did you plan and document disposition, offering material to branches and partners before recycling, and avoid visible mass disposal?
- Did you communicate the weeding plan and rationale proactively, and train staff to explain withdrawals confidently?
- Did you provide a fair reconsideration process for contested withdrawals and document the outcome?
