# Lateral Reading And SIFT

For years, source evaluation was taught as a checklist: look at the URL, the design, the "about" page, the date, and decide. That approach, rooted in the early web, fails against the modern information environment. Professional-looking sites spread misinformation; slick design signals funding, not accuracy; "about" pages are self-serving; and a source's own claims about itself are the least reliable evidence about itself. The methods professional fact-checkers actually use are different: they leave the source and investigate it from the outside, reading laterally across other sources to learn who is behind it, what others say about it, and what the broader coverage looks like. The SIFT method (Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims) captures this practice in teachable steps. Teaching lateral reading and SIFT equips learners to navigate the modern web; teaching the old checklist leaves them vulnerable to exactly the sources that look most credible.

Use this skill whenever you are teaching web source evaluation, when helping a learner assess an unfamiliar online source, and when evaluating sources yourself. The goal is to prevent the agent from teaching or applying outdated checklist evaluation, from trusting a source's self-description, and from judging credibility by surface design.

## Core Rules

### Replace Checklist Evaluation With Lateral Reading

Checklist evaluation asks learners to judge a source by features visible on the source itself. This fails because the most misleading sources invest heavily in looking credible. Lateral reading instead asks learners to leave the source and investigate it from outside, the way fact-checkers work.

The contrast:

- checklist (vertical reading): stay on the site, check URL, design, about page, date, ads;
- lateral reading: open new tabs, search for the source and author elsewhere, read what trusted third parties say, find the broader coverage.

Teach learners to resist the urge to judge a source by its own page. The source's own claims about its authority are the least useful evidence.

### Teach SIFT As A memorable Sequence

SIFT distills lateral reading into four moves that learners can remember and apply under time pressure. Teach each move and when to use it.

SIFT:

- Stop: before engaging deeply or sharing, pause. Ask whether you know the source or the claim, and whether you have the emotional reaction that misinformation exploits. Stopping interrupts automatic sharing.
- Investigate the source: before reading further, learn who is behind it. Use a web search, Wikipedia, or a trusted reference to see what reputable sources say about the source and author.
- Find better coverage: for a specific claim, look for other coverage. The original source may be unreliable, but strong reporting or research elsewhere can confirm, refute, or contextualize the claim.
- Trace claims: when a source cites a study or original, trace to the original to verify the claim represents it accurately. Quotes and findings are routinely distorted in retelling.

Not every source requires all four moves. Stop is always first; the depth of the rest scales with stakes.

### Investigate The Source From The Outside

The second SIFT move is the heart of lateral reading. Before trusting a source, learn who is behind it from sources other than itself.

Investigate by:

- searching the name of the publication, organization, or author along with terms like "Wikipedia," "bias," "fact check," or "credibility";
- checking established fact-checking and source-evaluation services;
- looking at the source's funding, ownership, and mission as reported by others;
- verifying author credentials through independent sources, not the source's own bio;
- noting whether reputable outlets cite or avoid the source.

A source that looks professional but has no reputable outside footprint is a red flag. A source with a known reputation, positive or negative, is easier to place.

### Find Better Coverage Rather Than Judging Only The Source

A common evaluation error is fixating on whether one source is trustworthy. Often the better move is to set the question of that source aside and find stronger coverage of the same claim. If multiple reputable sources report the same finding, the original source's credibility matters less. If no reputable source covers the claim, that itself is information.

Find better coverage by:

- searching the claim, not just the source;
- looking for the original study, press release, or primary document;
- comparing how reputable outlets frame the same claim;
- checking whether the claim appears only in ideological or low-credibility outlets.

### Trace Claims To The Original

Sources routinely distort studies, quotations, and data in retelling. The fourth SIFT move traces a claim back to its origin to verify it has been represented accurately.

Trace by:

- following the source's own citation or link, if any;
- locating the original study, speech, dataset, or press release;
- checking whether the original supports the claim, contradicts it, or is more nuanced;
- noting who funded or conducted the original and any limitations they state.

A claim that "a study says" often does not survive contact with the study. Tracing is how learners catch the gap.

### Teach That Emotion Exploits Evaluation

Misinformation is engineered to trigger strong emotion: outrage, fear, validation, schadenfreude. Strong emotion suppresses the reflective evaluation that catches bad sources. The Stop move exists partly to interrupt this. Teach learners to treat their own emotional reaction as a signal to slow down, not as a reason to share.

### Match Evaluation Depth To Stakes

Not every source deserves a full SIFT. A casual curiosity can be satisfied with a quick investigate-the-source check; a source feeding a consequential decision, a health choice, a vote, a citation in a paper, warrants the full sequence. Teach learners to calibrate effort to stakes.

### Practice With Real, Current Examples

Lateral reading and SIFT are skills, not concepts. They are learned by doing with real sources. Use current, contested, or misleading examples in instruction rather than sanitized textbook cases. Let learners encounter the discomfort of a source that looks credible but fails outside investigation.

### Acknowledge The Limits Of Any Method

No method makes evaluation infallible. Reputable sources make errors; consensus can be wrong; fact-checking services have their own perspectives. Teach SIFT and lateral reading as strong defaults that reduce, not eliminate, error. Maintain humility about uncertainty.

## Common Traps

### Teaching Or Applying The Old Checklist

URL, design, and about-page checks fail against sources engineered to look credible. Move to lateral reading.

### Trusting A Source's Self-Description

A source's claims about its own authority are the least reliable evidence. Investigate from outside.

### Fixating On One Source Instead Of Finding Coverage

Often the better move is to find stronger coverage of the claim elsewhere, not to adjudicate one source.

### Skipping The Trace Step

Claims about "what a study says" often distort the study. Trace to the original.

### Ignoring Emotional Triggers

Outrage and fear suppress evaluation. Treat strong emotion as a signal to slow down.

### Applying Full SIFT To Every Source

Calibrate effort to stakes. Trivial lookups do not need the full sequence.

### Using Only Sanitized Examples

Real, current, contested sources teach the skill; textbook cases do not transfer.

### Treating Any Method As Infallible

SIFT reduces error; it does not eliminate it. Keep humility about uncertainty.

## Self-Check

- Did you replace checklist evaluation with lateral reading that investigates the source from outside?
- Did you teach or apply SIFT (Stop, Investigate, Find better coverage, Trace) as a memorable sequence?
- Did you investigate the source using independent third parties rather than its own claims?
- Did you find better coverage of the claim rather than fixating on judging one source?
- Did you trace claims to the original study, speech, or dataset to verify accurate representation?
- Did you teach that strong emotion is a signal to slow down, exploited by misinformation?
- Did you calibrate evaluation depth to the stakes of the decision?
- Did you practice with real, current examples rather than sanitized cases?
- Did you acknowledge the limits of the method and maintain humility about uncertainty?
