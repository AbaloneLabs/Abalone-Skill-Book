# Evaluating Authority, Bias, And Purpose

Quick fact-checking methods like SIFT handle the question "is this specific claim accurate?" But many evaluation tasks demand deeper judgment: Is this author an authority on this topic? Does a conflict of interest distort this source? What is the source trying to accomplish, and for whom? What kind of evidence supports it? These questions resist checklist answers because authority is contextual, bias is universal, and purpose can be legitimate or manipulative. Teaching source evaluation well means helping learners reason about authority, bias, and purpose as nuanced judgments, not as binary labels. Done badly, evaluation instruction produces two opposite failures: learners who dismiss everything as "biased" and learn nothing, or learners who treat "bias" as a slur to apply only to opponents. Done well, it produces readers who can place a source accurately in the landscape of knowledge and interests.

Use this skill when teaching deeper source evaluation, when helping a learner assess an author or publication's authority and motives, and when evaluating sources for a research project. The goal is to prevent the agent from teaching evaluation as a binary credibility label, from treating bias as automatic disqualification, and from confusing authority with agreement.

## Core Rules

### Treat Authority As Contextual, Not Absolute

Authority is not a property a person has; it is a relationship between a person and a question. A Nobel laureate in physics is an authority on quantum mechanics and not on public health policy. Authority earned in one domain does not transfer to another, however famous the person is.

Teach learners to assess authority by asking:

- what are this author's credentials and experience relevant to this specific question?;
- is the author's expertise recognized within the relevant field?;
- does the author have a track record of reliable work on this topic?;
- is the author writing within or outside their domain of expertise?;
- what institution or organization stands behind the work, and what is its reputation in this area?;

Celebrity, fame, and confidence are not authority. A media celebrity opining outside their field carries no more weight than any other layperson.

### Recognize That Bias Is Universal, Not Disqualifying

Every source has perspectives, assumptions, and interests. "Bias" in the sense of having a viewpoint is universal and not by itself a reason to reject a source. The question is whether the bias distorts the evidence and reasoning to the point of unreliability.

Distinguish:

- perspective: every author has a standpoint shaped by discipline, culture, experience, and interests;
- conflict of interest: financial, political, or personal stakes that incentivize a particular conclusion;
- distortion: when perspective or interest causes a source to misrepresent, omit, or cherry-pick evidence.

A source with a clear perspective can still be reliable if it is transparent and its evidence holds. A source with an undisclosed conflict of interest is suspect regardless of its conclusion. Teach learners to look for transparency about perspective and conflict, not to hunt for any sign of a viewpoint.

### Identify Purpose And Intended Audience

Why a source exists shapes how it should be read. A source written to inform differs from one written to persuade, sell, entertain, or advocate. Misreading purpose leads to misusing the source.

Identify purpose by asking:

- is the primary purpose to inform, persuade, sell, entertain, advocate, or provoke?;
- who is the intended audience, and how does that shape depth, tone, and framing?;
- does the source disclose its purpose, or disguise advocacy as information?;
- what does the source want the reader to do, believe, or feel?;

An advocacy piece can contain accurate information, but reading it as neutral reporting misleads. A sales-oriented "informational" site exists to convert readers to customers. Purpose and audience set the lens.

### Distinguish Source Types And Their Evidence Standards

Different source types carry different evidence standards and serve different purposes. A news report, a peer-reviewed study, a systematic review, a government report, an advocacy brief, a blog post, and a press release are not interchangeable.

Help learners recognize:

- primary research: original study or data, with methods to scrutinize;
- synthesis and review: integrates many sources, good for the state of a field;
- news reporting: current events, varying in depth and verification;
- opinion and advocacy: argues a position, useful for understanding views not as evidence;
- gray literature: reports, white papers, preprints, variable quality;
- reference and tertiary: encyclopedic overviews, good for orientation not as primary evidence.

Match the source type to the question. A blog post is not evidence against a peer-reviewed study; a single study is not conclusive against a systematic review.

### Evaluate Evidence Quality, Not Just Conclusion

A common evaluation error is judging a source by whether you agree with its conclusion. The better question is whether its evidence and reasoning support the conclusion, regardless of whether you like it.

Evaluate evidence by asking:

- what evidence supports the claims, and is it primary or secondhand?;
- are methods appropriate and described, for research sources?;
- are sources cited and traceable?;
- does the source acknowledge limitations and counter-evidence?;
- is the reasoning logical, or does it rely on fallacy, cherry-picking, or emotive language?;

A source you disagree with may be well-evidenced; a source you agree with may be weak. Evaluate the support, not the conclusion.

### Avoid False Balance And Both-Sides Framing

In an effort to be fair, evaluation can slip into false balance: treating unequal claims as equally credible because they come from opposing sides. Not all positions have equal evidential support, and presenting them as equivalent misleads.

Avoid false balance by:

- weighting claims by their evidence, not by the number of sides presented;
- noting when a position is fringe or unsupported even if prominently voiced;
- distinguishing genuine expert disagreement from manufactured controversy;
- recognizing that some topics have strong consensus with legitimate minority views, and others have no consensus.

Fairness is accuracy about the weight of evidence, not equal time for any claim.

### Avoid Ad Hominem Dismissal

The opposite error from false balance is dismissing a source because of who wrote it rather than what it says. An author's conflicts and perspective matter, but a claim still stands or falls on its evidence. Teach learners to use authority and bias to set prior expectations and to look harder, not to reject claims without examining them.

### Teach That Evaluation Serves A Decision

Evaluation is not an end in itself; it serves a decision: whether to use, cite, trust, or share a source for a particular purpose. Anchor evaluation to the use. A source fine for background may be unacceptable as evidence; a source unsuitable for a scholarly paper may be perfect for understanding a viewpoint. The standard of evaluation scales with the stakes of the decision.

## Common Traps

### Treating Authority As Absolute Or Transferable

Authority is contextual to the question. Fame and confidence are not expertise.

### Treating Bias As Automatic Disqualification

Every source has perspective. The issue is distortion and undisclosed conflict, not the existence of a viewpoint.

### Judging Sources By Agreement With Your Views

Evaluate the evidence and reasoning, not whether you like the conclusion.

### False Balance

Unequal claims are not made equal by presenting opposing sides. Weight by evidence.

### Ad Hominem Dismissal

Who wrote it matters, but a claim still stands or falls on its evidence.

### Confusing Source Types

A blog post is not peer-reviewed research; a single study is not a systematic review. Match type to question.

### Ignoring Purpose And Audience

A source's purpose and audience shape its framing. Misreading purpose misuses the source.

### Evaluation Detached From Decision

The standard of evaluation should scale with the stakes of how the source will be used.

## Self-Check

- Did you treat authority as contextual to the specific question rather than absolute or transferable?
- Did you distinguish perspective, conflict of interest, and distortion, treating bias as universal but not automatically disqualifying?
- Did you identify the source's purpose, intended audience, and what it wants the reader to do or believe?
- Did you distinguish source types and their differing evidence standards?
- Did you evaluate the quality of evidence and reasoning rather than agreement with the conclusion?
- Did you avoid false balance by weighting claims by evidence rather than by the number of sides?
- Did you avoid ad hominem dismissal, using authority and bias to look harder rather than reject without examination?
- Did you anchor the depth of evaluation to the decision the source will serve?
