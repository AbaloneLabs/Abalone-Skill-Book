# Collection Development And Weeding

Collection development is not buying what the loudest requester wants, and weeding is not removing what one person dislikes. A library collection should reflect the mission, community needs, intellectual freedom, diversity of viewpoints, budget, accessibility, format needs, preservation obligations, and documented selection policy. Decisions should be explainable, equitable, and consistent.

Use this skill before recommending materials, building a collection plan, reviewing gaps, deselecting materials, responding to a challenge, evaluating digital licenses, or deciding whether to retain, replace, relocate, preserve, or remove resources. The goal is to make the agent treat collection decisions as policy-guided stewardship rather than preference.

## Core Rules

### Start With Mission, Community, And Policy

Collection decisions should be anchored in the library's mission and written policy.

Check:

- library type;
- service population;
- curriculum or community needs;
- collection development policy;
- selection criteria;
- reconsideration policy;
- weeding criteria;
- gift policy;
- accessibility policy;
- budget;
- format priorities;
- preservation responsibilities.

Do not make decisions solely from personal taste, popularity, controversy, or one patron request.

### Use Multiple Selection Criteria

No single criterion determines value. A resource may be selected for quality, demand, representation, local relevance, curriculum support, historical significance, or viewpoint diversity.

Consider:

- relevance to users;
- accuracy;
- author or creator credibility;
- reviews;
- demand;
- format;
- language;
- reading level;
- accessibility;
- price;
- durability;
- licensing terms;
- diversity of perspective;
- local interest;
- curricular fit;
- gap filling;
- anticipated use.

Document the reason when a decision is sensitive, expensive, unusual, or likely to be questioned.

### Protect Intellectual Freedom

Libraries serve users with different views, backgrounds, ages, and needs. Collections should not be shaped only by pressure to avoid controversy.

Apply:

- viewpoint diversity;
- resistance to censorship;
- access for all users under policy;
- distinction between selection and endorsement;
- fair reconsideration process;
- no removal without review;
- respect for unpopular or contested materials;
- professional separation of personal beliefs and collection duties.

ALA documents emphasize intellectual freedom and privacy as core professional commitments. Local law and institutional policy still matter.

### Build Inclusive And Representative Collections

Representation should be active, not accidental. Collections should include many communities, languages, perspectives, identities, formats, and reading levels.

Review:

- underrepresented authors;
- local communities;
- disability access;
- multilingual needs;
- age and reading level;
- cultural relevance;
- Indigenous and community knowledge;
- historically marginalized voices;
- accessible formats;
- patron-request patterns;
- gaps in subject coverage.

Avoid tokenism. One book does not represent a community or viewpoint.

### Evaluate Digital Resources And Licenses

Digital access is not only about content. Licensing terms can restrict use, privacy, preservation, accessibility, interlibrary loan, course use, and long-term access.

Check:

- authentication method;
- user privacy;
- data collection by vendor;
- accessibility conformance;
- simultaneous users;
- download or printing limits;
- interlibrary loan rights;
- text and data mining rights;
- archival rights;
- perpetual access;
- price escalation;
- cancellation terms;
- platform stability.

Do not assume a digital subscription is equivalent to ownership.

### Weed With Evidence And Care

Weeding improves collections when done systematically. It should not be a hidden censorship tool.

Consider:

- physical condition;
- outdated information;
- circulation or use;
- duplicate copies;
- newer edition;
- accuracy;
- local or historical value;
- replacement availability;
- curriculum relevance;
- diversity impact;
- preservation need;
- space constraints;
- digital availability.

Sensitive subjects such as health, law, technology, test preparation, and travel may require currency review. Historical materials may remain valuable even if outdated.

### Respond To Challenges Through Policy

Challenges to materials should follow a written reconsideration process.

Use:

- respectful listening;
- explanation of policy;
- formal reconsideration form where applicable;
- review committee or designated process;
- retention of material during review unless policy says otherwise;
- documentation;
- written decision;
- appeal path;
- consistency.

Do not remove or hide materials informally to avoid conflict. Do not treat a complaint as proof that a material violates policy.

### Track Collection Data Without Letting Metrics Rule

Use data to understand the collection, but do not let circulation alone decide value.

Review:

- circulation;
- in-house use;
- holds;
- interlibrary loan requests;
- database usage;
- course use;
- age of collection;
- cost per use;
- patron requests;
- demographic and language needs;
- collection diversity audits;
- community feedback.

Some important materials are low-use but high-value. Balance data with mission and professional judgment.

### Plan Preservation And Access

Some materials have long-term value beyond immediate demand.

Consider:

- local history;
- archives;
- rare materials;
- institutional records;
- fragile formats;
- digitization;
- metadata;
- rights;
- storage environment;
- disaster planning;
- access restrictions;
- community ownership concerns.

Do not digitize or discard materials without rights, privacy, or cultural sensitivity review.

## Common Traps

### Equating Selection With Endorsement

Including a resource does not mean the library endorses every idea in it. Libraries provide access to ideas.

### Letting The Loudest Voice Define The Collection

Requests and complaints matter, but policy and community-wide needs should guide decisions.

### Weeding Only By Circulation

Low circulation may still be acceptable for reference, local history, minority language, classic, or specialized materials.

### Ignoring License Restrictions

Digital resources can limit use in ways print does not. Review terms.

### Removing Controversial Materials Quietly

Informal removal undermines trust and policy. Use the reconsideration process.

### Buying Diversity Without Accessibility

Representation also requires formats, languages, discoverability, and physical or digital access.

### Forgetting Replacement Cost

Weeding a worn item may create a gap if replacement is unavailable or expensive.

## Self-Check

- Is the decision grounded in mission, community needs, and written policy?
- Are selection criteria broader than personal preference, demand, or controversy?
- Are intellectual freedom and viewpoint diversity protected?
- Are inclusive representation, language, accessibility, and local relevance considered?
- Are digital license terms, privacy, accessibility, access limits, and preservation rights reviewed?
- Is weeding based on documented criteria rather than hidden censorship?
- Are challenges handled through a formal reconsideration process?
- Are metrics used as evidence without replacing professional judgment?
- Are preservation, local history, rights, and cultural sensitivity considered?
- Is the decision documented enough to explain later?
