# Audience Targeting

Library programs and services serve communities, but communities are not monolithic. Different groups have different needs, schedules, languages, access barriers, and relationships with the library. A program developed for "the community" often serves only the already-engaged, the people who already use the library and feel comfortable there. Audience targeting is the deliberate work of identifying who a program is for, understanding that audience's characteristics and barriers, and designing the program to actually reach and serve them. Without targeting, libraries reproduce inequity, serving the privileged while the underserved remain unreached. Good audience targeting is grounded in data and community relationships, honest about who is and is not being reached, and willing to design specifically for groups that generic programming misses.

Use this skill when identifying target audiences, analyzing community segments, assessing underserved populations, tailoring programs, or reviewing who library services actually reach. The goal is to prevent the agent from designing for a generic audience, ignoring underserved groups, assuming attendance equals success, or failing to understand the audience before designing for them.

## Core Rules

### Define The Target Audience Specifically

"The community" is not an audience. Effective programs target specific groups whose needs and characteristics are understood.

Define audiences by combinations of:

- age and life stage, children, teens, adults, seniors;
- language and cultural background;
- socioeconomic status and access barriers;
- educational background and literacy;
- interests and information needs;
- geographic location relative to the library;
- digital access and comfort;
- relationship to the library, current user, lapsed, never-used.

A program for "new parents seeking early literacy support in the neighborhood" is targetable; a program for "the community" is not.

### Base Targeting On Data And Relationships

Audience understanding should come from evidence, not assumption. Combine data with direct community relationship.

Sources of understanding:

- census and demographic data for the service area;
- library use data by age, language, and location;
- community needs assessments and surveys;
- input from community organizations serving the target group;
- conversations with current and potential users;
- observation of who is and is not in the library;
- feedback from failed or under-attended programs.

Assumptions about what an audience wants or needs are often wrong. Verify through evidence and relationship.

### Identify And Prioritize Underserved Groups

Most libraries have groups they serve well and groups they underserve. Effective targeting identifies the underserved and prioritizes reaching them.

Underserved groups may include:

- non-dominant language speakers;
- low-income and transportation-limited residents;
- people with disabilities;
- seniors and homebound individuals;
- immigrants and refugees;
- rural or remote residents;
- people experiencing homelessness;
- incarcerated or justice-involved populations;
- teens and young adults, often underserved between children's and adult services.

Prioritizing underserved groups is an equity choice. It may mean lower attendance numbers but higher impact for those reached.

### Understand Barriers To Participation

Target audiences often face barriers that prevent participation. Understanding and addressing barriers is central to reaching underserved groups.

Common barriers:

- time and scheduling, work, childcare, school;
- transportation and location;
- language and cultural relevance;
- cost, even free programs have hidden costs;
- technology and digital access;
- trust and prior negative experiences with institutions;
- accessibility for disabilities;
- awareness, the audience does not know the program exists.

Design programs to reduce barriers rather than blaming audiences for not attending. If the audience is not coming, the program design is usually the problem.

### Tailor Content And Format To The Audience

Content and format should fit the target audience's needs, preferences, and context. Generic content repackaged for different audiences rarely resonates.

Tailor:

- language and reading level;
- cultural references and examples;
- timing to fit the audience's schedule;
- location, in the library, in the community, online;
- format, formal, informal, hands-on, social;
- duration and depth;
- food, childcare, or other supports that enable participation.

Tailoring requires knowing the audience. Invest in that understanding before designing.

### Build Relationships, Not Just Events

Reaching underserved audiences often requires relationship-building before programming. Trust cannot be shortcut through a single event.

Relationship practices:

- partner with community organizations already trusted by the audience;
- attend community events and spaces before inviting people to the library;
- listen to what the audience wants rather than assuming;
- hire or recruit staff who reflect and speak the language of the audience;
- sustain engagement over time, not just for one program;
- show up consistently and follow through on commitments.

Programs that emerge from relationships succeed where standalone events fail. Budget time for relationship-building.

### Avoid Serving Only The Already-Engaged

A common failure is programming that reaches only frequent library users. This feels successful, attendance is good, but it does not extend the library's reach.

Check:

- who actually attends, demographics and library-use history;
- whether attendees are new to the library or familiar faces;
- whether underserved groups are represented;
- what would reach beyond the usual audience.

Serving the already-engaged is valuable but is not the same as community engagement. Be honest about who is being reached.

### Segment Within Apparent Audiences

Even a defined audience contains segments with different needs. "Teens" includes middle schoolers, high schoolers, and out-of-school youth with very different needs.

Segment further:

- within age groups, by interest, need, or circumstance;
- within language groups, by country of origin, generation, or literacy;
- within geographic areas, by neighborhood characteristics;
- within interest groups, by depth of engagement sought.

Segmentation enables more precise and effective programming. Over-broad targeting blunts impact.

### Evaluate Whether The Target Audience Was Reached

Evaluation should measure not just attendance but whether the intended audience actually participated.

Measure:

- attendance against the target audience definition;
- demographics through voluntary, privacy-respecting methods;
- new versus returning participants;
- participant feedback on relevance and barriers;
- reach into underserved groups specifically;
- outcomes for the target audience.

Attendance by the wrong audience is not success. Evaluate against the targeting goal.

### Adjust Based On Who Is And Is Not Reached

Targeting is iterative. If the intended audience is not reached, adjust rather than declare failure or blame the audience.

Adjust:

- promotion channels and messaging;
- timing, location, and format;
- partnerships and community connections;
- barriers that were not addressed;
- the program concept itself if it does not fit.

Honest adjustment, informed by evaluation and community input, improves reach over time.

## Common Traps

### Designing For A Generic Community

"The community" is not an audience. Define and understand specific target groups.

### Assuming Rather Than Investigating

Assumptions about audience needs are often wrong. Base targeting on data and relationships.

### Ignoring Underserved Groups

Generic programming serves the already-engaged and reproduces inequity. Identify and prioritize underserved audiences.

### Blaming Audiences For Non-Attendance

If the audience is not coming, barriers and design are usually the cause. Address barriers rather than assigning blame.

### Untailored Content For Different Audiences

Repackaged generic content rarely resonates. Tailor language, format, timing, and location.

### One-Off Events Without Relationships

Trust cannot be built through single events. Invest in sustained relationships.

### Equating Attendance With Success

Good attendance by the wrong audience is not success. Evaluate against the targeting goal.

### No Adjustment When Targeting Fails

When the intended audience is not reached, adjust the design rather than abandoning the goal.

## Self-Check

- Is the target audience defined specifically rather than as a generic community?
- Is audience understanding based on data and community relationships, not assumptions?
- Are underserved groups identified and prioritized for reaching?
- Are barriers to participation understood and addressed in program design?
- Are content, format, timing, and location tailored to the target audience?
- Are relationships built with the audience and trusted community partners?
- Does evaluation measure whether the intended audience was actually reached?
- Is the program honest about whether it serves only the already-engaged?
- Are audiences segmented further where warranted for more precise targeting?
- Does the program adjust based on who is and is not reached?
