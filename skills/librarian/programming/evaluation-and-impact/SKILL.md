# Evaluation And Impact

Program evaluation is where libraries discover whether their work matters. The trap is confusing activity with impact: counting attendance, sessions delivered, and materials distributed measures what the library did, not what changed for participants. Stakeholders increasingly demand evidence of outcomes and impact, and libraries that cannot provide it are vulnerable to budget cuts and irrelevance. Good evaluation is planned when the program is designed, measures outcomes tied to goals, uses methods appropriate to the question, and reports honestly including disappointments. Evaluation is not about proving the library is wonderful; it is about learning what works, for whom, and why, so that scarce resources serve the community better.

Use this skill when designing program evaluation, measuring outcomes and impact, analyzing results, or reporting program value. The goal is to prevent the agent from relying on activity counts, using satisfaction as a proxy for impact, overclaiming from weak evidence, or skipping evaluation entirely.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Outputs, Outcomes, And Impact

These three levels of measurement are often conflated, and confusing them produces misleading claims. Understand and separate them.

- Outputs are what the library did: programs offered, attendance, materials distributed.
- Outcomes are what changed for participants: knowledge gained, skills built, attitudes shifted, behaviors changed.
- Impact is the longer-term, broader effect on the community or condition the program addresses.

Outputs are easy to count; outcomes require design to measure; impact is hardest and often takes years. Report each at its proper level and do not claim impact from output data.

### Plan Evaluation When Designing The Program

Evaluation designed after the program has ended can only measure what was convenient to capture, often just attendance. Effective evaluation is built in from the start.

Build in at design:

- the outcomes the program intends, stated observably;
- the indicators that would show those outcomes;
- the methods to capture the indicators;
- the baseline, what participants knew or did before;
- the timing of measurement, immediately and delayed;
- the resources needed for evaluation.

Evaluation is part of the program, not a separate task added at the end.

### Measure Outcomes Tied To Goals

Every program has goals, and evaluation should measure whether those goals were achieved. Disconnected evaluation produces data that cannot answer the key question.

For each goal:

- what would a participant who achieved the goal now know, feel, or do;
- what indicator captures that change;
- what method, survey, observation, artifact, interview, captures the indicator;
- what is the threshold for success.

If a goal is "participants gain confidence using technology," the evaluation must measure confidence, not just attendance.

### Use Mixed Methods Appropriate To The Question

Different evaluation questions need different methods. Relying on one method, usually a satisfaction survey, limits what can be learned.

Methods and their strengths:

- attendance and output tracking, for reach and activity;
- pre and post surveys, for knowledge or attitude change;
- observation, for engagement and behavior;
- participant artifacts, for skills demonstrated;
- interviews and focus groups, for depth and nuance;
- delayed follow-up, for retention and longer-term effect;
- comparison groups, where feasible, for attribution.

Mix methods to triangulate. Quantitative data shows scope; qualitative data shows meaning.

### Capture Authentic Participant Voice

Participants' own words and experiences reveal impact that metrics miss. Build in ways to hear them.

Approaches:

- open-ended survey questions;
- interviews or focus groups with willing participants;
- comment cards and stories;
- observation of participant engagement;
- follow-up contact for longer-term stories, with consent.

Anonymize and aggregate to protect privacy. Authentic stories, properly consented, are powerful evidence and powerful advocacy.

### Establish A Baseline Where Possible

Without knowing the starting point, you cannot measure change. Baselines make outcome claims credible.

Baseline options:

- pre-program surveys or assessments;
- participant self-report of prior knowledge or behavior;
- comparison to similar non-participants where ethical and feasible;
- historical data on the condition the program addresses.

Post-only data shows status, not change. Acknowledge the limitation when no baseline exists.

### Address Attribution Honestly

A common overclaim is attributing all participant change to the program. Many factors influence outcomes, and honest evaluation accounts for them.

Consider:

- what else might have caused the observed change;
- whether a comparison group is feasible;
- participant self-report of what made the difference;
- the contribution of partners and other influences;
- the limits of claiming causation from correlation.

Report contributions rather than claiming sole causation where the evidence does not support it. "Participants who attended showed gains, which may reflect the program's contribution alongside other factors" is honest and credible.

### Analyze For Equity And Distribution

Aggregate outcomes can hide that the program served some groups well and others poorly. Analyze by segment where data allows.

Analyze:

- outcomes by age, language, or demographic segment;
- whether underserved groups achieved outcomes at similar rates;
- whether some groups were reached but not served;
- barriers that affected outcomes for specific groups.

Equity analysis reveals whether the program reduced or reproduced disparities. This is essential for mission-driven libraries.

### Close The Loop By Using Findings

Evaluation data that is collected and not used wastes participant time and staff effort. Build a process to act on findings.

Close the loop:

- review findings promptly after the program;
- identify specific changes for the next iteration;
- share findings with staff, partners, and where appropriate participants;
- track whether changes improved outcomes over iterations;
- report findings to administrators and funders.

The purpose of evaluation is improvement and accountability, in that order. Improvement requires acting on what is learned.

### Report Honestly With Context

Stakeholders want strong results. Resist the pressure to overclaim. Honest reporting builds credibility and supports real improvement.

Report:

- what was measured and how;
- the sample and response rate;
- results including disappointments and surprises;
- limitations, no control group, self-report, small sample;
- what the results suggest, not prove;
- planned changes and next steps.

A modest honest report builds more trust than an inflated one, and it positions the library as a learning organization.

### Connect Evaluation To Advocacy And Funding

Evaluation evidence supports the library's case to funders and stakeholders. Connect findings to the value proposition.

Use evaluation for advocacy:

- tie outcomes to community priorities and funder interests;
- use authentic participant stories alongside data;
- show return on investment where calculable;
- demonstrate accountability and continuous improvement;
- make the case for sustained or increased investment.

Advocacy grounded in honest evaluation is far more persuasive than assertion.

## Common Traps

### Counting Outputs As Impact

Attendance and sessions measure activity, not change. Report outputs as outputs and measure outcomes separately.

### Satisfaction As Proxy For Impact

Happy participants may have learned nothing. Measure outcomes, not just reactions.

### Evaluation Added At The End

Post-hoc evaluation can only capture what was convenient. Build evaluation in at design.

### No Baseline

Without a starting point, change cannot be measured. Establish baselines where possible.

### Overclaiming Attribution

Many factors cause change. Credit partners and acknowledge limits rather than claiming sole causation.

### Ignoring Equity In Distribution

Aggregate outcomes can hide disparities. Analyze by segment to ensure equitable service.

### Collecting Data And Not Using It

Unused evaluation wastes time and erodes trust. Review findings and act on them.

### Inflated Reporting

Overclaiming damages credibility under scrutiny. Report honestly with context and limitations.

## Self-Check

- Are outputs, outcomes, and impact distinguished and reported at their proper levels?
- Is evaluation planned when the program is designed, not added at the end?
- Does each evaluation measure tie to a specific program goal and outcome?
- Are mixed methods used to triangulate quantitative scope and qualitative meaning?
- Is authentic participant voice captured through open-ended methods and stories?
- Is a baseline established where possible to measure actual change?
- Is attribution addressed honestly, crediting partners and acknowledging limits?
- Are outcomes analyzed by segment to check for equitable distribution?
- Is there a process to use findings for improvement and to close the loop?
- Are reports honest about limitations, disappointments, and what results suggest versus prove?
- Is evaluation evidence connected to advocacy and funding cases grounded in honesty?
