# Program Development

A library program is a planned event or series designed to inform, entertain, connect, or educate a community. Programs look like simple events, but developing one well requires aligning purpose, audience, format, resources, partners, and evaluation into a coherent whole. Programs developed casually, copying last year's event or chasing a trend, often miss their audience, drain staff time, or fizzle after one iteration. Good program development starts from community need and library mission, chooses format to fit purpose, budgets honestly, builds in sustainability, and treats each program as a hypothesis to be tested and improved. The goal is meaningful engagement, not activity for its own sake.

Use this skill when developing a new program, planning program structure and logistics, allocating resources, coordinating partners, or deciding whether to continue or end a program. The goal is to prevent the agent from copying programs without purpose, underestimating resource needs, running one-off events with no follow-through, or ignoring whether the program actually achieved anything.

## Core Rules

### Start From Community Need And Library Mission

A program should serve a real community need and connect to the library's mission. Without that anchor, programs drift toward whatever is trendy or convenient.

Ground the program:

- identify a community need through data, observation, or input;
- connect the need to a library mission goal;
- define what the program will contribute to addressing the need;
- check that the library is the right host, or whether a partner is better positioned;
- ensure the program does not duplicate what others already offer.

Programs without a needs basis consume resources without producing value. The need, not the format, should come first.

### Define Clear Goals And Outcomes

Before designing the program, define what success looks like. Goals and outcomes guide every design decision and make evaluation possible.

Define:

- what participants should know, feel, or be able to do;
- what community or relationship outcomes are intended;
- attendance or reach targets where relevant;
- how outcomes will be measured;
- how the program connects to broader library goals.

Vague goals like "engage the community" cannot be evaluated. Specify outcomes in observable terms, even for cultural and social programs.

### Match Format To Purpose And Audience

The format, lecture, workshop, book club, performance, drop-in, series, should fit the purpose and the audience. Mismatched format undermines even good content.

Consider:

- does the format achieve the intended outcomes;
- is the format accessible and welcoming to the target audience;
- does the format fit the available space, time, and budget;
- does the format suit the content and presenter;
- is a single event sufficient, or does the topic need a series.

A lecture format for a hands-on skill, or a drop-in for deep discussion, are mismatches. Choose format deliberately to serve purpose.

### Budget Resources Honestly

Programs consume staff time, materials, space, marketing, and often money for presenters or refreshments. Underestimating these leads to burnout and shortfall.

Budget:

- staff time for planning, promotion, delivery, and cleanup;
- presenter or performer fees;
- materials and supplies;
- refreshments if appropriate;
- marketing and promotion costs;
- space and equipment needs;
- contingency for the unexpected.

Track actual costs against the budget. Underbudgeted programs quietly absorb staff time that should be visible to administrators.

### Build Sustainability From The Start

One-off events have their place, but programs that can repeat or scale create more value over time. Design for sustainability from the beginning.

Sustainability considerations:

- can the program be repeated with reasonable effort;
- can it be adapted to different topics or audiences;
- what documentation or templates would ease repetition;
- is there a pipeline of presenters or partners;
- does the program build on or feed into other library services;
- what would make this a series rather than a single event.

Sustainable programs compound value. Design the first iteration with repetition in mind, even if it remains a one-off.

### Coordinate Partners And Presenters Effectively

Many programs involve external partners or presenters. Managing these relationships well determines program quality.

Coordinate:

- clear agreements on roles, expectations, and compensation;
- alignment on audience, content, and format;
- logistics, space, equipment, timing, setup;
- promotion roles and responsibilities;
- contingency for cancellations or changes;
- acknowledgment and relationship maintenance.

Partners and presenters are extensions of the library. Clear communication and fair treatment produce better programs and ongoing relationships.

### Plan Promotion To Reach The Target Audience

A program that the target audience never hears about fails. Promotion must be planned and targeted.

Promotion planning:

- identify where the target audience gets information;
- use multiple channels, in-library, web, social, community partners, press;
- time promotion appropriately before the event;
- craft messages that speak to the audience's interests and needs;
- remove barriers to participation, registration, cost, transportation, language;
- leverage partners' networks.

Promotion is not an afterthought. Budget time and channels for it as part of the program.

### Design For Accessibility And Inclusion

Programs should be accessible and welcoming to the whole community, including people with disabilities and underserved groups.

Design for:

- physical access to the space;
- sensory access, captioning, interpretation, clear sound;
- linguistic access, multilingual promotion or materials where needed;
- cultural inclusion and relevance;
- affordability, free or low-cost;
- timing that fits the audience's schedules;
- childcare, transportation, or other barriers where relevant.

Inclusion is a design principle, not an accommodation added late. Plan it from the start.

### Build In Evaluation From The Beginning

Evaluation should be planned when the program is designed, not tacked on at the end. It measures whether outcomes were achieved and informs improvement.

Build in:

- participant feedback through surveys or conversations;
- attendance and demographic data where appropriate;
- observation of engagement and outcomes;
- staff and partner reflections;
- comparison to goals and targets;
- a process to use findings for the next iteration.

Evaluation closes the loop and turns programs from activities into learning opportunities.

### Document For Repetition And Handoff

Program documentation enables repetition, handoff to other staff, and institutional memory. Undocumented programs die when the originator leaves.

Document:

- the program concept, goals, and target audience;
- the format, timeline, and logistics;
- partners, presenters, and agreements;
- budget and actual costs;
- promotion plan and materials;
- evaluation methods and results;
- lessons learned and recommendations.

Documentation turns individual effort into institutional capacity.

## Common Traps

### Copying Programs Without Purpose

Replicating another library's program without a local needs basis wastes resources. Start from community need.

### Vague Goals

Goals like "engage the community" cannot be evaluated. Define observable outcomes.

### Format Mismatch

A format that does not fit the purpose or audience undermines the program. Match format to goals.

### Underbudgeting Staff Time

Planning, promotion, and cleanup consume hours that are often invisible. Track and budget honestly.

### One-Offs With No Follow-Through

Single events with no repetition or connection have limited impact. Design for sustainability.

### Weak Or Untargeted Promotion

A program the audience never hears about fails. Plan targeted promotion.

### Accessibility As Afterthought

Retrofitting accessibility is costly and incomplete. Design inclusion from the start.

### No Evaluation

Without evaluation, lessons are lost and programs cannot improve. Build evaluation in from the beginning.

## Self-Check

- Is the program grounded in a community need and connected to library mission?
- Are goals and outcomes defined in observable, evaluable terms?
- Does the format match the purpose, audience, content, and available resources?
- Are all resources, staff time, materials, promotion, budgeted honestly?
- Is the program designed for sustainability, repetition, or scaling?
- Are partners and presenters coordinated with clear agreements and fair treatment?
- Is promotion planned and targeted to reach the intended audience?
- Is accessibility and inclusion designed in from the start?
- Is evaluation built in from the beginning to measure outcomes and inform improvement?
- Is the program documented for repetition, handoff, and institutional memory?
