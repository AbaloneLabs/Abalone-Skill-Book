# Subject Metadata

Subject metadata is what lets users find materials about a topic rather than just by title or author. It is also where cataloging most often goes wrong, because determining what a work is about, its aboutness, is a judgment, and assigning consistent subject terms across thousands of items is hard. Free keywords fragment, inconsistent headings scatter related works, over-specific subjects bury items, and biased vocabularies misrepresent people and topics. Good subject assignment determines aboutness from the work itself, uses controlled vocabularies consistently, applies the right level of specificity, and recognizes the cultural and political dimensions of the words chosen.

Use this skill when assigning subject headings or keywords, choosing vocabularies, determining a work's subject, handling complex or fictional works, or auditing subject consistency. The goal is to prevent the agent from guessing subjects from titles, mixing controlled and free text, over- or under-specific subjects, or ignoring how vocabulary choices affect representation.

## Core Rules

### Determine Aboutness From The Work, Not The Title

The title rarely tells the whole subject. A work's true aboutness emerges from its content, scope, emphasis, and audience. Assigning subjects from the title alone produces shallow and often wrong analysis.

Determine aboutness by examining:

- the table of contents and chapter titles;
- the preface, introduction, and abstract;
- the scope and depth of treatment of each topic;
- the intended audience and purpose;
- illustrations, examples, and case studies;
- the conclusion and index terms.

A work titled "Water" might be about water chemistry, water rights, water pollution, or water in ritual. The content, not the title word, decides the subject.

### Use Controlled Vocabularies Consistently

Controlled vocabularies collocate works on the same subject under one heading, which free keywords cannot do. Consistency in vocabulary choice is what makes subject searching work.

Choose and apply:

- Library of Congress Subject Headings for broad interoperability;
- MeSH for medical and health;
- ERIC descriptors for education;
- Getty AAT for art and architecture;
- FAST as a simplified postcoordinate alternative;
- local thesauri for specialized collections, with documented crosswalks.

Record which vocabulary each term comes from. Mixing vocabularies without indication breaks precision and confuses users.

### Assign The Most Specific Appropriate Heading

Subject headings should be as specific as the work's treatment. A work solely about sea turtles should get the heading for sea turtles, not the broader reptiles or animals. Over-broad headings bury specific works; over-specific headings fragment related works.

Specificity rules:

- use the most specific heading that accurately represents the work's treatment;
- do not also assign all broader headings, the hierarchy is implicit in the authority structure;
- avoid assigning a heading narrower than the work's actual scope;
- when no specific heading exists, use the closest broader heading and consider a local term.

Specificity is about matching the heading to the work's depth, not about listing every possible related topic.

### Follow The Vocabulary's Structure And Rules

Each controlled vocabulary has its own structure, subdivision rules, and conventions. Applying one vocabulary's rules to another produces invalid headings.

For LCSH specifically:

- follow the prescribed order of topical, geographic, chronological, and form subdivisions;
- use free-floating subdivisions only where permitted;
- apply pattern headings consistently for recurring topics;
- check the authority record for each heading before use;
- do not invent subdivisions not in the authority file.

For other vocabularies, follow their respective application guidelines. A heading valid in one system may be invalid in another.

### Assign Multiple Subjects For Complex Works

Many works treat multiple subjects, and each significant subject warrants its own heading. Assigning only the primary subject hides the work from users searching the secondary topics.

Guidelines:

- assign a heading for each subject receiving significant treatment;
- do not assign headings for merely mentioned or tangential topics;
- generally three to six headings cover most works, more only when justified;
- order headings with the primary subject first;
- ensure each heading is specific and accurate on its own.

The goal is findability without clutter. Each heading should represent a real search path a user would take.

### Handle Fiction And Imaginative Works Appropriately

Fiction, poetry, drama, and other imaginative works need subject treatment that reflects their nature. They are about their form, genre, and themes, not usually about real-world topics in the same way as nonfiction.

For imaginative works:

- assign form and genre headings, such as fiction, poetry, mystery fiction;
- assign topical headings only for significant themes, such as war, family, immigration;
- assign geographic and chronological headings for setting when significant;
- use the vocabulary's fiction and literary guidelines;
- consider reader advisory access points like mood, appeal, or audience.

Do not treat a novel set in Paris as a travel guide to Paris. Match the headings to how readers actually search for fiction.

### Recognize And Address Vocabulary Bias

Controlled vocabularies carry the biases of their origins and revisions. Some headings use outdated, offensive, or inadequate terms for people, cultures, and topics. Ignoring this perpetuates harm and misrepresents subjects.

Address bias by:

- following current revisions that replace offensive terms;
- using local alternatives where the standard term is harmful, with documentation;
- consulting communities affected by the terminology;
- recording variant and earlier terms to support legacy searching;
- advocating for vocabulary change through proper channels.

Bias awareness does not mean abandoning controlled vocabularies, but it means using them critically and documenting local decisions.

### Build And Maintain Local Authority Records

When a subject or name has no standard authority heading, local authority records keep usage consistent. Without them, local terms fragment.

Maintain local authorities for:

- local people, places, organizations, and events;
- specialized topics not in standard vocabularies;
- preferred local terms that differ from the standard;
- variant terms that should lead to the authorized form.

Document local authority practice so it survives staff turnover and stays consistent.

### Audit Subject Consistency Across The Collection

Subject consistency degrades over time as different catalogers make different choices. Periodic audits catch drift and improve findability.

Audit by:

- sampling records by subject area and checking heading consistency;
- reviewing use of subdivisions and pattern headings;
- checking for mixed vocabularies within a collection;
- identifying frequently mis-assigned topics;
- planning cleanup projects for high-impact inconsistencies.

Consistency is maintained, not achieved once.

### Balance Precision With Discovery

Over-precise subject assignment can fragment discovery; under-precise assignment buries works. Balance matters.

Consider:

- whether a heading is specific enough to be useful but broad enough to collocate;
- whether adding a form or genre heading improves discovery;
- whether local thesaurus terms complement standard headings;
- how faceted discovery systems use the subject data.

The subject metadata should serve both precise catalog search and faceted discovery interfaces.

## Common Traps

### Assigning Subjects From The Title

The title rarely captures the full subject. Examine the content to determine true aboutness.

### Mixing Controlled And Free Text

Free keywords fragment discovery. Use controlled vocabularies and record their source.

### Over-Broad Or Over-Specific Headings

Match the heading specificity to the work's actual treatment. Both extremes harm findability.

### Ignoring Subdivision Rules

Each vocabulary has subdivision conventions. Applying them incorrectly builds invalid headings.

### Single Subject For Complex Works

Works with multiple significant subjects need multiple headings to be findable on each.

### Treating Fiction Like Nonfiction

Imaginative works need form, genre, and theme headings, not real-world topical treatment alone.

### Accepting Biased Vocabulary Uncritically

Some headings are outdated or harmful. Use current revisions and document local alternatives.

### No Consistency Maintenance

Subject practice drifts without audits. Schedule periodic reviews and cleanup.

## Self-Check

- Is aboutness determined from the work's content, not just the title?
- Is a controlled vocabulary used consistently, with the source recorded for each term?
- Are headings as specific as the work's treatment, neither over-broad nor over-narrow?
- Are the vocabulary's subdivision rules and structure followed for each heading?
- Are multiple headings assigned for works with several significant subjects?
- Are fiction and imaginative works given appropriate form, genre, and theme headings?
- Is vocabulary bias recognized and addressed through current terms and documented local alternatives?
- Are local authority records maintained for subjects and names not in standard vocabularies?
- Is subject consistency audited periodically across the collection?
- Do the assigned subjects serve both precise catalog search and faceted discovery?
