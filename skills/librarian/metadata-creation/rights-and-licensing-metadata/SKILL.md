# Rights And Licensing Metadata

Rights metadata is the part of a record that tells users what they may legally do with a resource: view it, download it, reuse it, adapt it, redistribute it, or use it commercially. It is also the part most often treated as an afterthought. A digital collection can be beautifully described and superbly discoverable, yet useless in practice if users cannot determine whether they may include an image in a publication, post it in a course, or adapt it for a project. Rights metadata is not a single field to be filled with a default. It is a layered judgment that distinguishes copyright status from licensing terms, that requires evidence about provenance and permissions, and that carries real legal and institutional risk when wrong. Over-claiming rights (asserting restrictions the institution does not hold) suppresses lawful use and betrays the public domain; under-claiming rights (inviting reuse the institution cannot authorize) exposes the institution and its users to infringement. Recording rights well means being precise about what is known, what is unknown, and on what evidence each statement rests.

Use this skill when creating or managing rights and licensing metadata, choosing rights statements or licenses, documenting permission provenance, and enabling clear discovery of permitted uses. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating rights as a default field, from conflating copyright status with license terms, from over- or under-claiming rights, or from failing to document the evidence behind each rights statement.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Copyright Status From Licensing Terms

Rights are layered. Copyright status and licensing terms are different things and must be recorded separately.

Distinguish:

- **Copyright status:** the legal status under copyright law (in copyright, public domain, orphan work, unknown).
- **License or permission terms:** the terms under which the holding institution permits use of its copy or digital surrogate (e.g., a Creative Commons license, a custom rights statement, a contact-for-permission notice).

A public domain work can still carry access or use terms set by the institution that holds the physical item or created the digital surrogate. Conflating the two confuses users and misrepresents the law.

### Use Standardized Rights Statements And Licenses

Custom rights language is hard for users to interpret and hard to aggregate. Use standardized statements.

Use standardized vocabularies:

- **RightsStatements.org:** standardized statements for cultural heritage, covering in-copyright, public domain, and unknown-status scenarios.
- **Creative Commons licenses:** standardized licenses for clear reuse permissions, when the institution holds the rights to license.
- **Public domain marks:** for works known to be in the public domain.

Standardized statements are machine-readable, interoperable, and understandable by users across platforms.

### Base Rights Statements On Evidence

A rights statement is a claim that should rest on evidence. Record the basis for each statement.

Base statements on:

- the date and jurisdiction of creation to assess copyright term;
- the authorship and publication history;
- the terms of any transfer, bequest, or license under which the institution holds the item;
- donor or seller agreements and restrictions;
- any prior rights determinations.

Record the evidence in administrative or provenance notes so the statement can be reviewed and updated.

### Record The Source And Provenance Of Permissions

When rights depend on permission from a third party, document the source and scope of that permission.

Document:

- who granted the permission (author, donor, publisher, rights holder);
- the date and form of the grant (written agreement, deed of gift, license);
- the scope of uses permitted;
- any conditions, limitations, or expiration;
- contact information for further permissions.

Undocumented permissions are unverifiable and create risk if challenged.

### Handle Unknown And Orphan Rights Honestly

Many cultural heritage materials have unclear or unlocatable rights holders. Represent this honestly rather than guessing.

Handle by:

- using standardized "unknown" or "orphan work" statements;
- documenting the due diligence performed to locate the rights holder;
- noting the risk of use under the unknown status;
- providing a takedown process for rights holders who come forward;
- avoiding blanket assertions of public domain or permission without evidence.

Honest representation of uncertainty is safer and more ethical than false certainty.

### Make Rights Discoverable And Actionable

Rights metadata is useless if users cannot find or understand it. Make it discoverable and actionable.

Make rights usable by:

- exposing rights statements in the discovery layer and record display;
- linking to the full text of standardized statements and licenses;
- clearly distinguishing what users may do (view, download, reuse, adapt);
- providing contact information for permissions beyond the standard terms;
- using machine-readable rights where possible to support aggregation and reuse.

Actionable rights metadata enables lawful use and reduces permission inquiries.

### Address Rights For Different Layers Of A Resource

A single digital resource may have multiple layers with different rights. Address each layer.

Layers include:

- the underlying work (its copyright status);
- the physical carrier or original held by the institution;
- the digital surrogate created by the institution;
- metadata about the resource (often under its own license);
- any third-party content embedded in the resource.

Each layer may carry different rights, and the metadata should clarify which applies to what.

### Coordinate Rights With Access And Takedown Policy

Rights metadata should be coordinated with the institution's access and takedown policies.

Coordinate:

- access restrictions consistent with the rights status;
- a clear takedown process for rights disputes;
- documentation of any access limitations beyond rights (donor restrictions, cultural sensitivity);
- review processes for contested or sensitive materials.

Rights metadata does not exist in isolation; it reflects and supports broader access policy.

### Plan For Rights Review And Updates

Rights status changes over time: works enter the public domain, permissions expire, and new evidence emerges. Plan for review.

Plan by:

- recording review dates for uncertain or time-bound rights;
- updating statements when works enter the public domain;
- revisiting orphan work determinations periodically;
- tracking changes in standardized vocabularies and licenses.

A one-time rights statement decays; planned review keeps rights metadata accurate.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Copyright Status With License Terms

These are different layers. Record copyright status and institutional license terms separately.

### Using Custom Or Vague Rights Language

Custom language is hard to interpret and aggregate. Use standardized statements and licenses.

### Asserting Rights Without Evidence

A rights statement is a claim. Base it on documented evidence about provenance and status.

### Over-Claiming Rights

Asserting restrictions the institution does not hold suppresses lawful use and the public domain.

### Under-Claiming Rights

Inviting reuse the institution cannot authorize exposes it and its users to infringement.

### Ignoring Unknown And Orphan Status

Many materials have unclear rights. Represent uncertainty honestly with due diligence documented.

### Hiding Rights In Inaccessible Fields

Rights must be discoverable and actionable in the interface users actually see.

### Neglecting Different Layers Of A Resource

Underlying work, physical carrier, digital surrogate, and metadata may each carry different rights.

## Self-Check

- Did you distinguish copyright status from institutional license or permission terms?
- Did you use standardized rights statements (RightsStatements.org, Creative Commons, public domain marks)?
- Is each rights statement based on documented evidence about creation, jurisdiction, and provenance?
- Did you record the source, date, scope, and conditions of any third-party permissions?
- Did you represent unknown or orphan rights honestly with documented due diligence?
- Are rights statements discoverable, linked, and actionable in the user-facing interface?
- Did you address rights for each layer (underlying work, carrier, surrogate, metadata, embedded content)?
- Is rights metadata coordinated with access restrictions and a takedown process?
- Did you plan for periodic review and updates as rights status changes?
- Could a user determine from the record what they may legally do with this resource?
