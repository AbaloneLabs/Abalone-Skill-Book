# Crowdsourced And Community Metadata

Crowdsourced and community metadata invites the public to contribute descriptions, transcriptions, translations, tags, identifications, and contextual knowledge to library and archival materials. Done well, it can accomplish work that institutions could never complete alone: transcribing millions of manuscript pages, identifying people and places in photographs, translating texts, and surfacing community knowledge that no cataloger would have known. Done poorly, it produces noisy, inconsistent, or misleading metadata, alienates contributors, and creates integration problems that outweigh the contributions. The central challenge is that crowdsourcing is not free labor that can simply be harvested. It is a relationship with volunteers and communities that requires careful project design, clear tasks, quality control, contributor support, and a plan for integrating contributed knowledge with authoritative metadata. Treating crowdsourcing as a shortcut to cheap metadata leads to inconsistent results and burned-out communities; treating it as a structured engagement program produces high-quality contributions and lasting relationships.

Use this skill when designing or managing crowdsourced metadata and transcription projects, establishing quality control for public contributions, motivating contributors, and integrating community knowledge into metadata workflows. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating crowdsourcing as free labor without structure, from neglecting quality control, from demotivating contributors, or from failing to integrate contributions into authoritative systems.

## Core Rules

### Design Clear, Bounded Tasks

Contributors do best with clear, bounded tasks. Design the work to be approachable and verifiable.

Design tasks that:

- are small enough to complete in a short session;
- have clear instructions and examples;
- are well-scoped (transcribe one page, identify one person, tag one image);
- provide context about the larger project and purpose;
- offer multiple difficulty levels for different contributors.

Vague or unbounded tasks overwhelm contributors and produce inconsistent results.

### Provide Excellent Instructions And Examples

Quality depends on contributors understanding what is expected. Invest in instructions and examples.

Provide:

- concise, plain-language instructions for each task type;
- worked examples showing good and bad contributions;
- guidance on conventions (transcription standards, tagging norms, formatting);
- FAQs and help for common challenges;
- a way to ask questions and get help.

Good instructions are the single highest-leverage quality intervention.

### Establish Quality Control And Review

Crowdsourced contributions vary in quality. Build quality control into the workflow.

Build in:

- review by moderators or staff for contributions before publication;
- consensus or multiple-contributor models for critical tasks;
- automated checks for format and completeness;
- versioning so contributions can be corrected without loss;
- clear processes for disputing or revising contributions.

Quality control protects the integrity of the metadata while respecting contributor effort.

### Motivate And Recognize Contributors

Contributors are volunteers. Motivation and recognition sustain participation.

Motivate by:

- explaining the impact and purpose of the work;
- providing feedback and acknowledgment of contributions;
- offering progression, badges, or leaderboards where appropriate;
- recognizing top contributors and community members;
- creating a sense of community among contributors.

Unrecognized, unacknowledged labor leads to contributor burnout and attrition.

### Support And Engage The Community

Crowdsourcing is a community activity. Support and engage contributors as a community.

Support by:

- providing responsive help and moderation;
- fostering discussion and collaboration among contributors;
- welcoming new contributors and onboarding them;
- addressing conflict and inappropriate behavior promptly;
- listening to contributor feedback and improving the project.

A supported community produces sustained, high-quality contributions.

### Capture And Preserve Contributor Attribution

Contributors deserve attribution for their work. Capture and preserve it.

Capture:

- contributor identifiers or names (with privacy options);
- the specific contributions made by each contributor;
- the date and nature of contributions;
- attribution displayed alongside the contributed metadata where appropriate.

Attribution respects contributors and supports transparency about the metadata's origins.

### Integrate Contributions With Authoritative Metadata

Crowdsourced contributions must integrate with authoritative metadata systems. Plan the integration.

Integrate by:

- distinguishing contributor-added metadata from staff-created metadata;
- mapping contributions to the appropriate metadata fields and standards;
- routing contributions through review before integration;
- maintaining provenance so the source of each element is clear;
- handling conflicts between contributor and authoritative metadata thoughtfully.

Integration turns contributions into usable metadata rather than orphaned text.

### Respect Community And Cultural Knowledge

Community contributions often carry cultural, local, or specialized knowledge. Respect and value it.

Respect by:

- recognizing community knowledge as legitimate expertise;
- avoiding frameworks that dismiss non-cataloger contributions as inferior;
- handling culturally sensitive materials and knowledge appropriately;
- consulting with source communities on materials about them;
- providing mechanisms for communities to correct misrepresentations.

Community metadata can surface knowledge that authoritative metadata systematically misses.

### Plan For Sustainability And Lifecycle

Crowdsourcing projects have lifecycles. Plan for sustainability from the start.

Plan for:

- the eventual completion or transition of the project;
- maintenance of contributed metadata after the active project ends;
- migration of contributions if platforms change;
- ongoing moderation and community support needs;
- measuring and reporting on project impact.

Unsustained projects leave orphaned contributions and disappointed communities.

### Evaluate Quality And Impact

Measure the quality and impact of crowdsourced contributions to improve the project.

Evaluate:

- the accuracy of contributions against verified samples;
- the coverage and completeness achieved;
- contributor retention and satisfaction;
- the value added relative to staff effort;
- downstream uses of the contributed metadata.

Evaluation informs improvement and demonstrates the project's worth.

## Common Traps

### Treating Crowdsourcing As Free Labor Without Structure

Crowdsourcing requires task design, instructions, quality control, and community support.

### Vague Or Unbounded Tasks

Contributors need clear, scoped tasks. Vague tasks produce inconsistent results.

### Neglecting Quality Control

Without review and checks, contributions vary in quality and may mislead users.

### Demotivating Or Ignoring Contributors

Unrecognized volunteers burn out. Provide feedback, recognition, and community.

### Failing To Integrate Contributions

Contributions that do not integrate with authoritative metadata become orphaned and unusable.

### Disrespecting Community Knowledge

Community and cultural knowledge is legitimate expertise. Do not dismiss non-cataloger contributions.

### Losing Contributor Attribution

Contributors deserve attribution. Capture and preserve the source of contributions.

### No Sustainability Plan

Projects end or platforms change. Plan for maintenance and migration of contributions.

## Self-Check

- Did you design clear, bounded tasks that contributors can complete with confidence?
- Did you provide excellent instructions, examples, and help for each task type?
- Did you build quality control (review, consensus, automated checks) into the workflow?
- Did you motivate and recognize contributors through feedback, attribution, and community?
- Did you support and engage the contributor community responsively?
- Did you capture and preserve contributor attribution and provenance?
- Did you integrate contributions with authoritative metadata while distinguishing their source?
- Did you respect community and cultural knowledge and handle sensitive materials appropriately?
- Did you plan for sustainability, maintenance, and migration of contributed metadata?
- Did you evaluate quality, coverage, contributor satisfaction, and downstream impact?
