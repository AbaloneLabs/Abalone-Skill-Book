# Access Policies And Restrictions

Archival access policy is where the archive's public mission meets its legal, ethical, and donor obligations, and it is one of the most judgment-intensive areas of archival work. The tension is structural: archives exist to provide access, yet they hold materials restricted by donor agreement, law, privacy, confidentiality, and security. Agents tend to resolve this tension in one of two failing ways, either by defaulting to openness and exposing the institution to privacy breaches and legal liability, or by defaulting to restriction and denying access that the mission requires and the law permits. Both are failures of judgment. Good access policy is neither open nor closed by default but calibrated: it distinguishes what must be restricted, what may be restricted, and what must be open, and it provides clear, timely processes for restriction review and access decisions. The policy must also be operational, because a restriction that no one enforces or an access process that takes months both undermine the archive's credibility. This is a high-risk domain where privacy errors can harm living individuals, and escalation guidance is essential for uncertain cases.

Use this skill when writing access policies, setting or reviewing restrictions, handling access requests, or managing privacy in archival materials. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to open or closed without analysis, applying restrictions inconsistently, ignoring privacy of living individuals, or creating access processes that are neither timely nor defensible.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate Access By Distinguishing Must-Restrict, May-Restrict, And Must-Open

Access decisions should begin by classifying the material into three categories, because each has a different decision logic. Conflating them produces either over-restriction or under-protection. The classification must be explicit for each collection or access request.

Classify access status:

- must-restrict: material restricted by law, such as personally identifiable information under data protection law, student records under FERPA, health records under HIPAA, sealed records, or national security classification, where access is legally prohibited except under defined conditions;
- may-restrict: material where restriction is permitted but not required, such as donor-imposed embargoes, fragile condition, or sensitive content about living individuals, where the institution exercises judgment;
- must-open: material with no legal or donor restriction that is open for research, where access denial would be unjustified.

For may-restrict material, document the basis and duration of the restriction and the conditions for access. For must-restrict material, document the legal basis and the permitted access conditions. Defaulting everything to one category avoids the judgment the policy requires.

### Identify And Honor The Sources Of Restriction

Restrictions come from distinct sources, each with different authority and duration, and access policy must identify and honor each. Mixing them up leads to restrictions that expire without notice or persist beyond their basis. The four main sources are legal, donor, institutional, and condition-based.

Distinguish restriction sources:

- legal restrictions: imposed by statute or regulation, with defined scope and conditions, non-negotiable without legal process;
- donor restrictions: imposed by the donor in the deed of gift, honored as a condition of acquisition, with defined duration or conditions for review;
- institutional restrictions: imposed by the archive for privacy, security, or ethical reasons, with the institution setting the terms;
- condition-based restrictions: imposed because the material is too fragile to handle, lifted when treatment or surrogates enable access.

Each source should be recorded with its basis, duration, and review conditions. A restriction whose source is unknown cannot be reviewed or reliably enforced.

### Protect Privacy Of Living Individuals As A Default Obligation

Archival materials frequently contain personal information about living individuals, and protecting that privacy is both a legal and ethical obligation, not an optional courtesy. Materials containing medical, financial, legal, or sensitive personal information about living people may require restriction or redaction before access. The default for such material is protection, with access provided through redacted copies, mediated review, or time-based restrictions that expire when the privacy concern ends.

Protect privacy by:

- identifying materials containing personal information about living individuals during processing;
- applying restrictions or redactions to protect privacy, with defined review dates tied to the privacy concern;
- using time-based restrictions, such as a set number of years, for sensitive personal materials;
- providing mediated or partial access where full restriction is unnecessary;
- documenting the privacy basis and review date for each restriction.

Privacy protection is especially critical because harm to living individuals is immediate and real. When in doubt about whether material requires privacy protection, escalate rather than expose.

### Write Access Policy That Is Clear, Timely, And Defensible

An access policy should tell both staff and researchers what is accessible, under what conditions, and how to request access, and it should produce decisions in a reasonable timeframe. Vague policies, "access at the discretion of the archivist," produce inconsistent decisions and researcher frustration. The policy should specify the categories of restriction, the process for requesting access, the timeframe for response, and the appeal or review mechanism.

A useful access policy specifies:

- the categories of material and their default access status;
- the process for requesting access to restricted material, including any application or justification required;
- the timeframe for response, because indefinite delays are effectively denial;
- the conditions for mediated, partial, or redacted access;
- the process for reviewing and lifting restrictions, especially time-based ones;
- the appeal mechanism for denied access requests;
- the documentation required for access decisions.

Test the policy by walking through real access requests to confirm it produces timely, defensible decisions.

### Review And Lift Restrictions On Schedule

Restrictions are not permanent unless their basis is permanent, and many restrictions have defined durations or review dates that are routinely ignored. A time-based donor restriction that has expired but remains enforced denies access the donor permitted; a privacy restriction that outlives its concern over-protects unnecessarily. Restriction review must be operational, with scheduled reviews and a mechanism to lift expired restrictions.

Operationalize restriction review:

- record restriction end dates or review dates as machine-actionable data;
- schedule regular reviews of restrictions approaching or past their review date;
- define the process for lifting restrictions, including any required approvals;
- document restriction changes so the access history is auditable;
- handle donor restrictions that require donor or executor contact for review.

Restrictions that are never reviewed accumulate and silently narrow access, undermining the archive's mission.

### Handle Donor Restrictions Through The Deed Of Gift

Donor restrictions are governed by the deed of gift, the legal agreement under which the materials were acquired, and honoring them is both a legal and ethical obligation. The deed specifies what restrictions the donor imposed, their duration, and any conditions for access or review. Access policy must reference and honor the deed, and staff must be able to locate the relevant deed terms for any donor-restricted collection.

Manage donor restrictions by:

- retaining and indexing deeds of gift so restriction terms are discoverable;
- recording donor restrictions with their duration and access conditions in the access management system;
- negotiating reasonable restriction terms at acquisition, avoiding perpetual or vague restrictions;
- contacting donors or their executors when restrictions require review or modification;
- documenting any agreed changes to donor restrictions.

A donor restriction whose terms are lost or ignored cannot be honored or reviewed, creating both access and legal risk.

### Provide Equitable Access Processes

Access processes should be equitable, not favoring certain researchers or institutions. Practices that give preferential access to affiliated scholars, or that create barriers for community members, distance learners, or researchers without institutional backing, undermine the archive's public mission. Access policy should ensure that processes are transparent and do not systematically disadvantage any researcher group.

Promote equitable access by:

- publishing access policies and procedures so all researchers face the same information;
- providing remote access options, including digitization and reprographic services, for researchers who cannot visit;
- avoiding preferential treatment that cannot be justified by policy;
- recognizing and removing barriers faced by underrepresented researcher communities;
- ensuring access decisions are based on policy, not on the researcher's identity or affiliation.

### Escalate Uncertain Or High-Risk Access Decisions

Some access decisions involve genuine uncertainty: ambiguous restriction terms, materials with unclear privacy implications, legally sensitive content, or requests that may set precedent. These should not be resolved by a single staff member's judgment but escalated to a defined authority, often the head of the repository, legal counsel, or an access review committee. Escalation is not avoidance; it is the recognition that some decisions carry risk beyond an individual's authority.

Escalate when:

- restriction terms are ambiguous or conflicting;
- materials may contain legally protected information with unclear access conditions;
- a request may set precedent for similar materials;
- privacy implications are uncertain, especially for living individuals;
- donor restrictions require interpretation or donor contact;
- the material is culturally sensitive and may require community consultation.

Define the escalation path in policy so staff know when and to whom to escalate, and document the escalated decision.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Open Without Privacy Analysis

Opening materials containing personal information about living individuals without review causes privacy harm. This is a trap because the harm is immediate, real, and potentially irreversible.

### Defaulting To Restrict Without Basis

Restricting materials without a legal, donor, or ethical basis denies access the mission requires. This is a trap because over-restriction is often invisible and erodes the archive's public value.

### Restrictions That Never Get Reviewed

Failing to review time-based or conditional restrictions lets them persist beyond their basis. This is a trap because expired restrictions silently narrow access.

### Lost Or Ignored Deed Terms

Losing track of donor restriction terms means they cannot be honored or reviewed. This is a trap because the institution may breach the acquisition agreement.

### Vague Discretionary Policies

Policies granting unfettered discretion produce inconsistent and indefensible decisions. This is a trap because similar requests receive different outcomes, undermining credibility.

### Access Delays That Function As Denial

Slow or unresponsive access processes effectively deny access while appearing to permit it. This is a trap because the denial is hidden behind a process that never concludes.

### Inequitable Access Practices

Preferential access for some researchers or barriers for others undermines the public mission. This is a trap because the inequity is often unintentional but systematic.

### Single-Person High-Risk Decisions

Resolving legally or ethically uncertain access decisions without escalation concentrates risk inappropriately. This is a trap because the decision exceeds the individual's authority and may harm the institution.

## Self-Check

- Does the policy classify materials into must-restrict, may-restrict, and must-open, with explicit decision logic for each?
- Are restriction sources (legal, donor, institutional, condition-based) identified, with basis, duration, and review conditions recorded?
- Is privacy of living individuals protected as a default, with redaction, mediation, or time-based restrictions and documented review dates?
- Does the access policy specify categories, request process, response timeframe, mediated access options, review process, and appeal mechanism?
- Are restrictions reviewed and lifted on schedule, with machine-actionable review dates and documented changes?
- Are donor restrictions governed by indexed deeds of gift, with terms honored and review processes defined?
- Are access processes equitable, transparent, and free of unjustified preferential treatment?
- Is there a defined escalation path for uncertain, legally sensitive, or precedent-setting access decisions, and are escalated decisions documented?
- Have you walked through real access requests to confirm the policy produces timely, defensible decisions?
