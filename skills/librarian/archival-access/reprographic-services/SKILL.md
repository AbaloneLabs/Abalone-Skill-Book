# Reprographic Services

Reprographic services, photocopying, photography, scanning, and digitization, are where researcher demand for copies meets the archive's obligations to protect collections, respect rights, and recover costs. The work looks transactional, but every reproduction decision involves preservation risk (handling and exposure during copying), rights risk (copyright and donor restrictions on reproduction), and service risk (whether the process meets researcher needs). Agents tend to treat reprographics as a simple copy service and to under-attend to the preservation damage that copying can cause, the copyright and permission obligations that reproduction triggers, and the policy consistency that prevents disputes. A reprographics program that copies anything on request damages collections and exposes the institution to rights liability; one that refuses most requests fails researchers and drives them to risky alternatives. Good reprographic service is a calibrated program that provides needed reproductions through low-risk methods, under clear rights and use terms, with consistent policy.

Use this skill when designing reprographic services, setting reproduction policies and fees, handling reproduction requests, or managing reproduction risk. The goal is to prevent the agent from enabling damaging copying, ignoring copyright and use restrictions, setting fees without cost basis, or creating inconsistent reproduction decisions.

## Core Rules

### Assess Preservation Risk Before Reproduction

Reproduction is a handling event, and some reproduction methods, especially photocopying and self-serve scanning, impose physical stress, light exposure, and handling risk that can damage fragile materials. Before approving any reproduction, assess whether the method is safe for the specific item, and refuse or substitute methods that risk damage. The preservation obligation takes precedence over the reproduction request.

Assess reproduction risk:

- evaluate the item's condition and fragility, including bindings, paper stability, and emulsions;
- assess the stress of the proposed method: photocopying presses materials flat, photography does not;
- consider light exposure for light-sensitive materials during scanning or photography;
- determine whether staff-mediated reproduction is required rather than researcher self-service;
- substitute surrogates, existing digital copies or microfilm, where they exist to avoid handling originals;
- refuse reproduction of items too fragile to copy, offering alternatives such as transcription or later digitization.

Preservation-based refusal is a professional decision, not poor service; communicate it with alternatives.

### Resolve Copyright And Reproduction Rights Before Providing Copies

Reproduction triggers copyright considerations distinct from access: providing a copy of a copyrighted work to a researcher is a reproduction that may require permission, and the institution may be liable for facilitating infringement. Reprographics policy must address copyright, including what the institution will copy, what disclaimers it provides, and what permissions it requires. Donor and use restrictions may also limit reproduction.

Address rights before reproduction:

- determine the copyright status of materials requested for reproduction, recognizing that unpublished, published, and orphan works differ;
- apply institutional policy on copying copyrighted materials, often limiting copies to portions and requiring researcher assumption of responsibility;
- honor donor restrictions on reproduction, which may prohibit or condition copying;
- distinguish providing a copy for personal research from granting permission to publish, which requires separate rights clearance;
- provide clear copyright and use statements with reproductions so researchers understand their obligations;
- decline reproduction that would violate copyright or donor restrictions, documenting the basis.

Copyright in archives is complex and often uncertain; when status is unclear, conservative practice and clear disclaimers protect both the researcher and the institution.

### Distinguish Reproduction From Publication Permission

A frequent confusion is between providing a reproduction copy and granting permission to publish or publicly display the material. These are separate transactions with separate rights implications. Providing a copy for research does not grant publication rights, and publication may require copyright permission, donor permission, and credit and fee obligations. Conflating the two misleads researchers and creates rights risk.

Keep the two distinct:

- reproduction: providing a copy for the researcher's use, subject to copyright and condition limits;
- publication permission: authorizing public use, which may require copyright clearance, donor approval, and use agreements;
- make clear in policy and in every reproduction that a copy is not publication permission;
- require a separate publication permission process, including use agreements and reproduction fees where applicable;
- document publication permissions granted, with conditions and credit requirements.

Researchers often assume a copy includes the right to publish; policy and communication must correct this assumption.

### Set Fees On A Transparent Cost And Value Basis

Reproduction fees should be set transparently, reflecting the cost of service and, for commercial or publication use, the value of the reproduction. Fees that are opaque or arbitrary generate disputes; fees that are too low do not recover costs; fees that are too high deter research and access. The fee structure should distinguish personal research from commercial and publication use.

Set transparent fees:

- base personal research fees on cost recovery, including staff time, materials, and equipment;
- set higher fees for commercial or publication use, reflecting the value of the reproduction;
- publish the fee schedule so researchers know costs before requesting;
- distinguish self-service from staff-mediated reproduction in pricing;
- consider fee waivers or reductions for categories of research that serve the public mission;
- document the cost basis for fees so they are defensible.

Opaque or inconsistent fees erode trust; transparent, justified fees support both cost recovery and service.

### Prefer Staff-Mediated Reproduction For Vulnerable Materials

Researcher self-service copying, whether photocopying or personal photography, is convenient but imposes uncontrolled handling risk on fragile materials. For vulnerable collections, staff-mediated reproduction, where trained staff perform the copying under controlled conditions, is safer and more consistent. The choice between self-service and mediated should be based on material condition and value, not on staff convenience.

Decide mediation by material:

- allow self-service only for robust materials in good condition;
- require staff-mediated reproduction for fragile, rare, or high-value materials;
- prohibit photocopying of bound volumes, fragile paper, and light-sensitive materials, offering photography instead;
- standardize staff-mediated methods and equipment for consistent quality and safety;
- train staff in safe handling during reproduction.

Self-service convenience never outweighs preservation risk for vulnerable materials.

### Manage Image Quality And Metadata For Reproductions

Reproductions should meet defined quality standards appropriate to their purpose, and they should carry the metadata needed for citation and rights attribution. Low-quality reproductions fail researcher needs, while reproductions without metadata become untraceable and encourage misuse. Quality and metadata standards should be part of the reprographics workflow.

Manage quality and metadata:

- define resolution, format, and color standards for reproductions based on intended use;
- capture technical metadata, including capture device and settings, for reproductions that may be archived;
- include descriptive and rights metadata with every reproduction, citing the source collection and any use conditions;
- apply consistent file naming and identification so reproductions are traceable;
- provide citation guidance so researchers credit the institution correctly.

A reproduction without metadata is an orphan that invites misuse and fails to serve citation needs.

### Provide Digital Delivery And Access Securely

Digital reproduction increasingly replaces physical copies, and the delivery of digital files must be secure, reliable, and rights-respecting. Unsecured delivery, such as uncontrolled email of large or sensitive files, creates access and rights risk. Digital delivery should use appropriate platforms and access controls.

Manage digital delivery:

- use secure delivery methods, such as a reading room system or controlled download, rather than ad hoc email;
- apply access controls consistent with the material's restriction status;
- size files appropriately for delivery while preserving quality;
- provide reproductions with embedded or accompanying rights and use statements;
- retain records of what was provided to whom, supporting accountability.

### Build Consistency Through Policy And Documentation

Reprographics decisions recur constantly, and inconsistency, different answers to similar requests, generates disputes and researcher frustration. Consistency depends on written policy, documented decisions, and trained staff who apply policy uniformly. The policy should be specific enough to guide real decisions.

Build consistency:

- write a reprographics policy covering methods, rights, fees, mediation, and refusal criteria;
- document reproduction decisions, especially refusals and exceptions, so they are reviewable;
- train staff on the policy and on consistent application;
- review the policy periodically against actual practice and researcher feedback;
- communicate the policy clearly to researchers before they request reproductions.

## Common Traps

### Copying Without Preservation Assessment

Approving reproduction requests without assessing item condition causes damage during copying. This is a trap because the damage is often invisible until cumulative and irreversible.

### Ignoring Copyright In Reproduction

Providing copies of copyrighted materials without addressing reproduction rights exposes the institution to liability. This is a trap because the copy feels like a service while creating rights risk.

### Conflating Copy With Publication Permission

Allowing researchers to assume a reproduction grants publication rights misleads them and creates downstream rights problems. This is a trap because the misunderstanding surfaces only at publication.

### Arbitrary Or Opaque Fees

Setting fees without a transparent cost or value basis generates disputes and distrust. This is a trap because researchers cannot assess whether fees are fair.

### Self-Service For Vulnerable Materials

Allowing researcher self-service copying of fragile materials imposes uncontrolled handling risk. This is a trap because the convenience is purchased with collection damage.

### Reproductions Without Metadata

Delivering reproductions without source citation and rights metadata creates orphan files that invite misuse. This is a trap because the reproductions cannot be properly cited or attributed.

### Inconsistent Decisions

Giving different answers to similar reproduction requests undermines credibility and fairness. This is a trap because inconsistency is visible to researchers and erodes trust.

### Unsecured Digital Delivery

Emailing large or sensitive files without access controls creates security and rights exposure. This is a trap because the delivery method itself introduces risk.

## Self-Check

- Is the preservation risk of each reproduction assessed before approval, with refusal or substitution for fragile materials and use of existing surrogates?
- Are copyright status and donor restrictions resolved before reproduction, with clear disclaimers and documented basis?
- Is reproduction clearly distinguished from publication permission, with a separate permission process for public use?
- Are fees set transparently on a cost and value basis, published, and differentiated for personal versus commercial use?
- Is staff-mediated reproduction required for vulnerable materials, with self-service limited to robust items?
- Do reproductions meet defined quality standards and carry descriptive, technical, and rights metadata?
- Is digital delivery secure, access-controlled, and rights-respecting, with records of what was provided?
- Is there a written reprographics policy, documented decisions, trained staff, and periodic review for consistency?
- Are researchers informed of policies, fees, and rights obligations before they request reproductions?
