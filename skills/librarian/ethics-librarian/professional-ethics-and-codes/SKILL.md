# Professional Ethics And Codes

Professional ethics are the stated principles that bind library workers to a shared standard of conduct, and in libraries those principles are distinctive. Unlike many professions, librarianship explicitly commits to intellectual freedom, equitable access, confidentiality of patron use, resistance to censorship, and service that does not depend on a patron's beliefs, origin, or status. These commitments are captured in codes such as the American Library Association Code of Ethics and Library Bill of Rights, the IFLA Statement on Libraries and Intellectual Freedom, and equivalent national and institutional statements. These codes are not decorative; they are operational. They shape how collections are built, how reference questions are answered, how privacy is protected, and how challenges and pressures are resisted.

The judgment problem is that ethical codes are easy to cite and hard to apply. They state high-level principles that collide with real situations: a demand to remove a book, a request for patron records, a collection decision with budget limits, a reference question on a charged topic, a conflict between institutional policy and professional duty. In those moments, staff may default to personal opinion, institutional convenience, or fear of controversy, rather than reasoning from the professional framework. Ethics codes also sometimes conflict with law, with employer policy, or with each other, and resolving those conflicts requires judgment, not rote citation. The agent's job is to ground decisions in the professional ethical framework, reason through conflicts deliberately, document the basis, and escalate when the stakes are high or the principles are in tension.

Use this skill when interpreting or applying professional library ethics, working with ethics codes, resolving conflicts between ethics and policy or law, advising on neutrality and intellectual freedom, handling conflicts of interest, or training staff. The goal is to prevent the agent from substituting personal preference for professional principle, treating ethics codes as slogans, ignoring conflicts between ethics and policy, or making high-stakes ethical decisions without escalation.

## Core Rules

### Know The Core Professional Ethical Commitments

Ethical reasoning starts with knowing the framework. The core commitments recur across library ethics codes worldwide.

Core commitments:

- intellectual freedom: the right to access information and ideas without restriction;
- equitable access: service to all members of the community without discrimination;
- confidentiality and privacy: protection of what patrons read, seek, and borrow;
- resistance to censorship: defending collections and access against removal demands;
- objectivity and neutrality in selection and service: not letting personal viewpoint determine access;
- accuracy and competence: providing correct information and acknowledging limits;
- respect for intellectual property and proper attribution;
- separation of personal convictions from professional duty.

These commitments are the default reference points. When a situation is unclear, return to them.

### Apply The Code As An Operational Framework, Not A Slogan

Ethics codes must drive decisions, not decorate them. Apply the principles to the specific situation.

Operational application:

- identify which principles are engaged by the situation;
- reason about how each principle bears on the decision;
- weigh competing principles when they conflict;
- document the reasoning and the basis for the decision;
- avoid citing a principle to justify a predetermined outcome.

An ethics code that is cited but not applied is a slogan. The work is translating principle into specific action.

### Resolve Conflicts Between Ethics, Policy, And Law Deliberately

Ethical principles sometimes conflict with institutional policy, with law, or with each other. These conflicts require deliberate reasoning, not default.

Conflict resolution:

- identify the specific conflict, such as a policy that limits access versus an equity principle;
- determine whether the conflict is real or apparent, since principles and policy often can be reconciled;
- seek the path that best honors the core commitments;
- consult colleagues, ethics committees, and counsel where appropriate;
- document the conflict and the reasoning;
- escalate when the conflict is high-stakes or unresolved.

Do not assume policy or law always overrides ethics. Sometimes the ethical duty is to advocate for change, to resist, or to find a better path.

### Uphold Intellectual Freedom As A Default

Intellectual freedom is a defining commitment of the profession. Treat it as the default, requiring strong justification to override.

Intellectual freedom in practice:

- select materials to represent diverse viewpoints, not to endorse them all;
- resist removal demands based on content, author, or viewpoint;
- provide access to controversial, minority, or unpopular ideas;
- do not label materials as warnings of ideological content;
- support the right to access information on any topic without surveillance;
- distinguish selection criteria, such as relevance and quality, from censorship.

Overriding intellectual freedom requires a compelling, principled reason, not convenience or fear of complaint.

### Maintain Neutrality Without Abandoning Values

Professional neutrality means serving all patrons and representing diverse viewpoints without imposing personal beliefs. It does not mean abandoning professional values.

Neutrality distinction:

- neutrality in service: serve every patron regardless of their beliefs or yours;
- neutrality in selection: build collections representing many perspectives, not a sanctioned one;
- neutrality is not valuelessness: the profession still commits to access, privacy, accuracy, and intellectual freedom;
- neutrality does not require giving equal weight to misinformation and verified fact in reference;
- neutrality does not mean silence when core values like access or privacy are threatened.

Neutrality is a discipline of fair service, not an excuse to avoid defending professional principles.

### Recognize And Manage Conflicts Of Interest

Conflicts of interest, personal, financial, or ideological, can compromise professional judgment. Identify and manage them.

Conflict of interest management:

- disclose any personal or financial interest that could influence a decision;
- recuse from selection, procurement, or service decisions where a conflict exists;
- be aware of ideological conflicts, such as pressure to favor or exclude materials based on personal belief;
- do not accept gifts or benefits that could influence collection or service decisions;
- document disclosures and recusals.

Undisclosed conflicts erode trust and skew decisions. Manage them transparently.

### Protect Confidentiality As An Ethical, Not Just Legal, Duty

Confidentiality of patron use is an ethical commitment that may exceed legal requirements. Treat it as a professional obligation.

Confidentiality as ethics:

- do not disclose what patrons read, borrow, or ask, beyond legal compulsion;
- protect confidentiality even when law does not require it;
- resist voluntary disclosure as an ethical matter, not only a legal one;
- train staff that confidentiality is a condition of professional service;
- recognize that ethics may demand more protection than the law.

Where law permits disclosure, ethics may still forbid it. Apply the higher standard.

### Acknowledge Limits And Refer When Appropriate

Professional ethics include competence and honesty about limits. Do not provide service beyond competence.

Competence and referral:

- provide accurate information and correct errors promptly;
- acknowledge when a question exceeds your expertise;
- refer patrons to appropriate resources, experts, or professionals;
- do not provide legal, medical, or financial advice beyond pointing to resources;
- maintain and improve professional competence.

Overreaching competence harms patrons. Honest referral is an ethical act.

### Balance Individual Service With Community Good

Library decisions often balance an individual's need against the community's. Reason about both.

Balancing:

- consider the impact of decisions on the whole community, not only the immediate patron;
- apply policies consistently while allowing legitimate exceptions;
- avoid decisions that advantage one patron at the cost of others' access;
- weigh privacy of the individual against safety of the community carefully and rarely override privacy.

Balancing is judgment work. Make it deliberate and document the reasoning.

### Document Ethical Decisions And Rationale

Ethical decisions should be traceable. Documentation supports consistency and accountability.

Documentation:

- record the situation, the principles engaged, and the decision;
- note conflicts and how they were resolved;
- retain documentation of challenges, complaints, and disclosures;
- use documentation to inform future decisions and training.

Undocumented decisions are hard to defend and hard to learn from. Write them down.

## Escalation Guidance

Ethical decisions can be high-stakes and contested. Escalate in these situations:

- Any challenge to materials, programs, or displays based on content or viewpoint: escalate to leadership and follow the formal reconsideration process; do not remove materials ad hoc.
- Any conflict between institutional policy or pressure and professional ethical duties, such as a demand to restrict access or disclose records: escalate to leadership, ethics bodies, and counsel to reconcile.
- Any legal demand that conflicts with ethical duties: escalate to counsel and leadership; document the conflict and the response.
- Any conflict of interest that cannot be resolved by recusal: escalate to leadership or ethics committee.
- Any situation where personal safety, legal risk, or significant controversy is involved: escalate rather than deciding alone.
- Any pattern of ethical concerns or staff conduct issues: escalate for training, policy review, or corrective action.

When the stakes are high or principles conflict, escalate, document, and seek counsel from colleagues and ethics bodies.

## Common Traps

### Citing Ethics As Slogans Without Application

Codes that are quoted but not applied to decisions provide no real guidance. Translate principle into action.

### Personal Preference Over Professional Principle

Substituting personal belief for professional duty undermines the framework. Reason from the code, not from opinion.

### Assuming Policy Or Law Always Overrides Ethics

Sometimes ethics demands more than policy or law. Identify conflicts and reason deliberately.

### Misunderstanding Neutrality As Valuelessness

Neutrality is fair service, not abandonment of professional values. Defend access, privacy, and accuracy.

### Undisclosed Conflicts Of Interest

Unmanaged personal, financial, or ideological conflicts skew decisions. Disclose and recuse.

### Treating Confidentiality As Only Legal

Confidentiality is an ethical duty that may exceed law. Apply the higher standard.

### Overreaching Competence

Providing service beyond expertise harms patrons. Acknowledge limits and refer.

### Undocumented Ethical Decisions

Decisions without documented reasoning are hard to defend and learn from. Record the situation, principles, and decision.

## Self-Check

- Are decisions grounded in the core professional commitments of intellectual freedom, equitable access, confidentiality, and resistance to censorship?
- Is the ethics code applied operationally to specific situations, with principles identified, weighed, and documented, not merely cited?
- Are conflicts between ethics, policy, and law identified and reasoned through deliberately, with escalation when high-stakes?
- Is intellectual freedom treated as the default, requiring compelling justification to override?
- Is professional neutrality distinguished from valuelessness, maintaining fair service while defending core values?
- Are conflicts of interest disclosed and managed through recusal where they exist?
- Is patron confidentiality treated as an ethical duty that may exceed legal requirements?
- Are competence limits acknowledged, with honest referral when a question exceeds expertise?
- Are individual and community interests balanced deliberately and documented?
- Are ethical decisions, challenges, conflicts, and disclosures documented for accountability and learning, with escalation for high-stakes or contested situations?
