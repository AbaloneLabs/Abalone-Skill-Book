# Digitization Quality Control And Workflow

Quality control in digitization is what separates a usable digital collection from a pile of image files. Every stage of digitization, selection, preparation, capture, post-processing, metadata, ingest, introduces the possibility of defects: poor focus, color casts, cropped content, mislabeled files, missing pages, corrupted derivatives, and metadata mismatched to objects. Without systematic QC, these defects accumulate and reach users, undermining trust in the collection and, for preservation masters, compromising the archival record. QC in digitization is not a single inspection at the end; it is a set of checkpoints woven through the workflow, each with defined acceptance criteria, each catching the defects most likely to arise at that stage. Designing and sustaining QC is a workflow management task: it requires defining what quality means at each stage, who checks, what happens to defects, how throughput and quality are balanced, and how QC findings feed back to prevent recurrence. The failure mode is treating QC as optional or as a final spot-check, which lets defects through that are expensive to find and fix after publication.

Use this skill when establishing digitization quality control, defining QC checkpoints and criteria, managing digitization throughput and technicians, handling rework, or sustaining capture quality at scale. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating QC as a final spot-check, omitting acceptance criteria, letting defects reach users, or balancing throughput against quality without safeguards.

## Core Rules

### Define Quality Criteria For Each Workflow Stage

Quality is not one thing; it differs by stage. Capture quality means focus, color, and completeness; metadata quality means accuracy and consistency; ingest quality means file integrity and correct linking. Define criteria for each stage.

Stage-specific criteria:

- capture, focus, color accuracy, tonal range, completeness, skew, artifacts;
- post-processing, cropping, rotation, derivative generation, no degradation of masters;
- naming and organization, files named and organized per project convention;
- metadata, accuracy, completeness, consistency, correct object linkage;
- ingest, file integrity, fixity, correct system entry, access derivatives served.

Define criteria concretely enough that a reviewer can apply them consistently. Vague criteria produce inconsistent QC.

### Establish Checkpoints At Each Stage

QC should happen where defects arise, not only at the end. Build checkpoints into the workflow.

Checkpoints:

- after capture, before post-processing;
- after post-processing, before metadata;
- after metadata, before ingest;
- after ingest, before publication;
- periodic audit of already-published objects.

Each checkpoint catches the defects most likely at that stage. End-only QC lets defects propagate through later stages, making root causes harder to trace and rework more expensive.

### Define Acceptance And Rejection Criteria

Reviewers need to know what passes, what fails, and what is acceptable with documentation. Define acceptance criteria explicitly.

Criteria definition:

- what constitutes a pass, no defects or only documented acceptable ones;
- what constitutes a fail requiring rework;
- what defects are acceptable with a note, such as a defect present in the original;
- who has authority to accept borderline cases;
- how accepted defects are documented for users and future maintainers.

Without defined criteria, QC is inconsistent across reviewers and shifts under throughput pressure. Define and document the standard.

### Use Sampling Strategically For Scale

Full QC on every object is often infeasible at scale. Use sampling where appropriate, with awareness of its limits.

Sampling practice:

- 100 percent QC for masters and high-value or complex materials;
- sampling for routine, homogeneous batches;
- set sample rates that balance confidence against effort;
- escalate to 100 percent if a sample reveals systematic problems;
- rotate samples across batches and operators to catch drift.

Sampling is a tool, not a shortcut. Document the sample rate and rationale, and never sample masters where fidelity is critical.

### Manage Rework Efficiently

Defects require rework, and rework is expensive. Manage it to minimize cost and recurrence.

Rework management:

- route failed items back to the responsible stage quickly;
- provide specific feedback on the defect and the fix;
- track rework rates by stage, operator, and material type;
- identify and address root causes of recurring defects;
- do not let rework queues accumulate unmanaged.

Rework is a signal, not just a cost. High rework rates point to training, equipment, or specification problems that need root-cause fixes.

### Balance Throughput Against Quality Explicitly

Digitization has production pressure, and quality can erode under it. Balance the two explicitly rather than letting quality slip silently.

Balance safeguards:

- define minimum quality standards that cannot be bypassed for speed;
- track quality metrics alongside throughput metrics;
- resist pressure to skip QC checkpoints under deadline;
- use equipment and workflow improvements to raise throughput without cutting quality;
- escalate conflicts between throughput and quality to project leadership.

Throughput and quality are not inherent opposites, but protecting quality under pressure requires explicit standards and management support.

### Train Technicians On Quality Standards

QC effectiveness depends on technicians understanding what quality means and how to achieve it. Training is foundational.

Training:

- train technicians on the quality criteria for each stage they perform;
- provide examples of defects and correct captures;
- explain the rationale so technicians can handle edge cases;
- calibrate reviewers and technicians on borderline cases;
- offer refresher training as standards or equipment change.

Technicians who understand quality produce fewer defects and catch more themselves before QC.

### Feed QC Findings Back Into The Workflow

QC findings are data about workflow health. Use them to prevent recurrence, not just to catch individual defects.

Feedback use:

- track defect types, frequencies, and sources over time;
- identify systematic problems and address root causes;
- share findings with technicians and adjust training;
- revise specifications or equipment where defects indicate gaps;
- report quality trends to project leadership.

QC without feedback is a filter that catches defects endlessly. QC with feedback improves the workflow so fewer defects arise.

## Common Traps

### End-Only QC

Final spot-checks let defects propagate through stages. Build checkpoints at each stage.

### Vague Quality Criteria

Undefined criteria produce inconsistent review. Define acceptance and rejection concretely.

### Sampling Masters For Fidelity

Sampling misses critical master defects. Use 100 percent QC for masters and high-value items.

### Unmanaged Rework Queues

Accumulated rework hides problems and delays outputs. Track and address rework promptly.

### Quality Eroding Under Throughput Pressure

Silent quality loss under deadlines undermines the collection. Define and protect minimum standards.

### Untrained Technicians

Technicians without quality understanding produce more defects. Train on criteria, examples, and rationale.

### QC As A Filter, Not A Feedback System

Catch-only QC recurs endlessly. Feed findings back to fix root causes.

### No Quality Metrics Tracking

Unmeasured quality drifts. Track defect rates, rework, and trends over time.

## Self-Check

- Are quality criteria defined concretely for each workflow stage, capture, post-processing, metadata, ingest?
- Are QC checkpoints established at each stage where defects arise, not only at the end?
- Are acceptance, rejection, and acceptable-with-documentation criteria explicitly defined?
- Is sampling used strategically, with 100 percent QC for masters and escalation when samples reveal problems?
- Is rework managed promptly with specific feedback and root-cause tracking?
- Are throughput and quality balanced through explicit minimum standards and protected under pressure?
- Are technicians trained on quality criteria, examples, rationale, and calibration?
- Are QC findings fed back into the workflow to address root causes and prevent recurrence?
