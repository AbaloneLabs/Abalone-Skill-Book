# Digitization Project Planning

Digitization projects fail more often from poor planning than from poor technology. A project that selects the wrong materials, underestimates the labor, skips metadata planning, ignores rights, or has no clear audience produces digital objects that serve no one and that drain resources from higher-value work. Planning a digitization project is a sequence of judgment calls: what to digitize and why, who the users are, what level of capture and description is justified, what rights and access constraints apply, what the project will cost in staff time and equipment, and how the outputs will be sustained after the project ends. Each decision constrains the others, and the central planning failure is treating any one of them in isolation, selecting materials without considering description effort, or planning capture without considering long-term storage. Good project planning integrates selection, audience, capture, metadata, rights, workflow, budget, and sustainability into a coherent plan that can be executed, measured, and sustained.

Use this skill when planning a digitization project, selecting materials, estimating resources, defining goals and audiences, or coordinating project workflows and stakeholders. The goal is to prevent the agent from digitizing without clear purpose, underestimating metadata and sustainability costs, ignoring rights and access constraints, or planning capture in isolation from description and preservation.

## Core Rules

### Start With Purpose And Audience

Digitization without a clear purpose produces outputs no one uses. Define why the project exists and who it serves before selecting materials or technology.

Purpose and audience definition:

- the scholarly, educational, community, or access need the project addresses;
- the primary and secondary user audiences;
- how users will discover and use the digital objects;
- what outcomes define success, usage, research impact, access preserved;
- how the project fits the institution's mission and collection strengths.

A project justified by "we should digitize this" rather than a defined need will struggle to justify its cost and to measure its success.

### Select Materials Strategically

Not everything should be digitized. Selection should prioritize materials that serve the project's purpose and that are feasible to digitize well.

Selection criteria:

- relevance to the project's purpose and audience;
- research, cultural, or access value;
- physical condition and digitization feasibility;
- rights status and any restrictions;
- existing metadata and description effort required;
- relationship to already-digitized materials, avoiding duplication;
- risk of loss or degradation if not digitized.

Balance value against cost and feasibility. A high-value but fragile item may warrant conservation before digitization; a low-value item may not warrant digitization at all. See selection-for-digitization guidance for deeper treatment.

### Estimate The Full Cost, Not Just Capture

Digitization cost is dominated by labor beyond capture: metadata creation, quality control, rights review, and project management. Underestimating these dooms projects to incomplete outputs.

Cost components:

- selection and preparation, including conservation if needed;
- capture, equipment, operator time, and QC;
- metadata creation, often the largest labor component;
- rights review and clearance;
- system setup, ingest, and access platform configuration;
- project management and coordination;
- long-term storage and preservation, ongoing.

Build estimates from realistic per-item times, not optimistic assumptions. A project budgeted for capture alone will run out of resources before metadata and access are complete.

### Plan Metadata Before Capture

Metadata is not a post-capture task. Planning it before capture ensures objects are described consistently and that capture and description align.

Metadata planning:

- choose the schema and application profile before digitization begins;
- define the metadata elements, obligations, and vocabularies;
- plan who creates metadata and when, at capture, after, or both;
- integrate object identifiers between capture and metadata systems;
- account for metadata effort in the project timeline and budget.

Projects that digitize first and plan metadata later produce inconsistent description and backlogs. Plan metadata as part of project design.

### Resolve Rights And Access Constraints Early

Rights and access constraints determine what can be digitized, published, and reused. Discovering them late halts projects or suppresses outputs.

Rights and access planning:

- assess the copyright and rights status of candidate materials before commitment;
- identify materials with restrictions, donor, privacy, cultural sensitivity;
- determine what can be published openly versus with access controls;
- plan rights metadata, standardized statements, from the start;
- consult rights expertise for complex or ambiguous cases.

A project that digitizes restricted materials without resolving rights wastes effort and creates legal or ethical risk. Resolve rights during selection.

### Design The Workflow End To End

A digitization workflow runs from selection through capture, metadata, QC, ingest, publication, and preservation. Designing it end to end prevents bottlenecks and gaps.

Workflow design:

- map each stage and its inputs and outputs;
- define handoffs between selection, capture, metadata, and ingest;
- identify bottleneck stages and plan capacity for them;
- build in quality control at each stage, not just at the end;
- track each object through the workflow with identifiers and status.

A workflow designed stage by stage in isolation produces piles of unprocessed material at each handoff. Design the whole flow.

### Coordinate Stakeholders And Departments

Digitization projects cross departments: collection curators, digitization technicians, metadata creators, rights staff, IT, and preservation. Coordination is a planning responsibility.

Coordination:

- identify all stakeholders and their roles at project start;
- establish communication channels and decision-making authority;
- align schedules and dependencies across departments;
- resolve conflicts, curatorial priorities versus technical capacity, explicitly;
- document roles and responsibilities.

Projects that leave coordination to chance stall at handoffs and produce conflict under pressure.

### Plan For Sustainability From The Start

A digitization project that ends at publication has created an ongoing preservation obligation. Sustainability must be planned from the start, not after.

Sustainability planning:

- budget long-term storage and preservation as ongoing costs;
- plan format monitoring and migration for the digital outputs;
- assign responsibility for the collection after the project ends;
- ensure metadata and documentation survive staff turnover;
- integrate outputs into institutional preservation systems.

Digital objects without a sustainability plan degrade or become inaccessible. Plan for their life after the project.

## Common Traps

### Digitizing Without A Clear Purpose

Purposeless projects produce unused outputs. Define audience and need first.

### Selecting Materials Without Feasibility Or Rights Checks

Infeasible or restricted materials stall projects. Select strategically.

### Budgeting Capture Only

Metadata, QC, and management dominate cost. Estimate the full lifecycle.

### Planning Metadata After Capture

Late metadata planning produces inconsistency and backlogs. Plan it before digitization.

### Discovering Rights Issues Late

Late rights discovery halts projects or suppresses outputs. Resolve rights during selection.

### Stage-By-Stage Workflow Design

Isolated stages create handoff bottlenecks. Design the workflow end to end.

### Uncoordinated Stakeholders

Uncoordinated departments stall and conflict. Establish roles and communication at start.

### No Sustainability Plan

Projects ending at publication create unmanaged preservation obligations. Plan sustainability from the start.

## Self-Check

- Is the project's purpose and audience defined before materials or technology are selected?
- Are materials selected strategically for value, feasibility, rights, and relationship to existing digitized content?
- Does the budget estimate the full lifecycle cost, including metadata, QC, rights, management, and sustainability?
- Is the metadata schema, profile, and creation plan defined before capture begins?
- Are rights and access constraints assessed and resolved during selection, not discovered late?
- Is the workflow designed end to end with defined stages, handoffs, QC, and object tracking?
- Are all stakeholders and departments identified, with roles, communication, and decision authority established?
- Is sustainability, storage, preservation, responsibility, and documentation, planned from the start?
