# Minimal Processing And Access Prioritization

Archival repositories almost always have more material than they can process to traditional full-level standards. Backlogs grow, and unprocessed collections sit inaccessible for years or decades, violating the archival obligation to make records available. Minimal processing, often associated with the "More Product, Less Process" (MPLP) approach, responds to this reality by reducing the level of intervention to the minimum needed to provide safe, useful access, so that more collections become available sooner. The challenge is that minimal processing is not simply doing less work and calling it done. It is a deliberate set of choices about what level of arrangement, description, preservation, and screening is necessary for each collection to be accessible and usable. Applied carelessly, minimal processing releases collections with hidden restrictions, lost context, or unsafe materials. Applied thoughtfully, it dramatically expands access while preserving the integrity of the records. The discipline lies in matching the processing level to the collection's needs, the access priorities, and the available resources.

Use this skill when deciding processing levels for archival collections, applying minimal or MPLP-style processing, prioritizing collections for access, and managing backlogs under resource constraints. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying a single processing level to all collections, from neglecting restriction and preservation review in minimal processing, from over-processing low-value collections, or from letting backlogs grow while awaiting ideal resources.

## Core Rules

### Match Processing Level To Collection Needs

Not every collection warrants the same level of processing. Match the level to the collection's needs.

Match by considering:

- the research value and demand for the collection;
- the complexity and size of the materials;
- the presence of restrictions requiring screening;
- the physical condition and preservation needs;
- the available resources and competing priorities.

A high-value, complex collection may warrant full processing; a low-demand, homogeneous collection may need only minimal processing for access.

### Apply The Principle Of Minimal Necessary Intervention

Process to the level needed for access, not to an ideal maximum. Apply minimal necessary intervention.

Apply by:

- arranging only to the level needed for usable access;
- describing at the collection and series level rather than item level where possible;
- preserving original order rather than re-sorting where it is usable;
- performing preservation actions only where needed for safe handling;
- screening for restrictions sufficient to protect users and subjects.

Minimal intervention is deliberate, not negligent; it is the least work that achieves genuine access.

### Prioritize Access Over Perfect Description

The goal is access. Prioritize getting collections usable over achieving perfect description.

Prioritize access by:

- releasing collections with collection and series level description sooner;
- accepting description that is sufficient rather than exhaustive;
- avoiding item-level description unless research value demands it;
- using efficient description tools and templates;
- treating description as iterative, refinable as use warrants.

A collection with good-enough description that is available serves researchers better than perfect description that never appears.

### Screen For Restrictions Before Access

Minimal processing does not mean skipping restriction review. Screen before access.

Screen for:

- personal and confidential information requiring restriction;
- donor and legal restrictions on access or use;
- third-party copyrighted content;
- materials that could harm subjects if released;
- the level of screening sufficient given the collection's risk.

Restriction screening at the level appropriate to the collection protects users, subjects, and the institution.

### Preserve Original Order Where Usable

Re-sorting is expensive and can destroy evidence. Preserve original order where it is usable.

Preserve original order by:

- maintaining the creator's arrangement where it provides usable access;
- documenting any reorganization imposed during processing;
- recognizing when original order is absent or unusable and arranging as needed;
- avoiding the reflex to impose a "better" order on usable arrangements.

Original order is evidence; preserving it serves both access and authenticity.

### Assess Preservation Needs Pragmatically

Full conservation is not always necessary. Assess preservation needs pragmatically.

Assess and act on:

- immediate threats (mold, pests, water damage, extreme fragility);
- housing needs sufficient for safe handling and storage;
- the need for reformatting or stabilization before access;
- the cost-benefit of conservation versus access priorities;
- flagging items for later conservation rather than blocking access to the whole collection.

Pragmatic preservation prevents harm without stalling access.

### Manage Backlogs Strategically

Backlogs are a fact of archival life. Manage them strategically rather than letting them grow unmanaged.

Manage by:

- surveying and inventorying unprocessed collections to inform priorities;
- prioritizing high-value and high-demand collections for access;
- applying minimal processing to release lower-priority collections;
- tracking backlog size, processing throughput, and access outcomes;
- communicating transparently about what is available and what is not.

Strategic backlog management converts an undifferentiated mass into prioritized, accessible collections.

### Document Processing Decisions And Levels

Processing level should be documented so users understand what was done. Record decisions.

Document:

- the level of processing applied (minimal, partial, full);
- what arrangement, description, and screening were performed;
- what was deferred or left for future processing;
- the date and rationale for the processing approach;
- any access restrictions and their basis.

Documentation lets researchers judge the reliability and completeness of the description.

### Plan For Iterative Enhancement

Minimal processing need not be final. Plan for iterative enhancement as resources and demand allow.

Plan for:

- refining description as the collection is used;
- performing additional arrangement or item-level work where warranted;
- revisiting restrictions as time passes and sensitivities diminish;
- incorporating user feedback into description improvements;
- scheduling reviews of minimally processed collections.

Iterative enhancement treats minimal processing as a starting point, not a dead end.

### Balance Speed, Quality, And Risk

Minimal processing balances speed of access against quality and risk. Make the balance deliberate.

Balance:

- speed (faster access to more collections) against;
- quality (depth of description and arrangement) against;
- risk (of releasing restricted, harmful, or poorly described material).

There is no universal correct balance; each collection requires a deliberate judgment.

## Common Traps

### Applying A Single Processing Level To All Collections

Different collections need different levels. Match processing to need, not to a default.

### Confusing Minimal Processing With Negligent Processing

Minimal processing is deliberate, not skipping restriction, preservation, or context work.

### Over-Processing Low-Demand Collections

Spending full effort on low-value collections starves high-value ones of access.

### Skipping Restriction Screening In Minimal Processing

Restrictions must be screened before access, even in minimal processing.

### Re-Sorting Usable Original Order

Imposing a "better" order destroys evidence and wastes effort. Preserve usable order.

### Letting Backlogs Grow Unmanaged

Unmanaged backlogs block access indefinitely. Survey, prioritize, and process strategically.

### Undocumented Processing Levels

Users need to know what level of processing was applied. Document decisions and rationale.

### Treating Minimal Processing As Final

Minimal processing can be enhanced over time. Plan for iterative refinement.

## Self-Check

- Did you match the processing level to the collection's value, complexity, and restrictions?
- Did you apply minimal necessary intervention rather than defaulting to full or to neglect?
- Did you prioritize access with sufficient (not exhaustive) description?
- Did you screen for restrictions appropriate to the collection's risk before access?
- Did you preserve usable original order rather than re-sorting unnecessarily?
- Did you assess preservation needs pragmatically, addressing threats without stalling access?
- Did you manage the backlog strategically through survey, prioritization, and minimal processing?
- Did you document the processing level, decisions, restrictions, and rationale?
- Did you plan for iterative enhancement as resources and demand allow?
- Did you balance speed, quality, and risk deliberately for this collection?
