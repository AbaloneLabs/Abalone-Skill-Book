# Arrangement

Arrangement is the intellectual and physical organization of an archival collection. It is not filing; it is the act of determining the structure that makes a collection understandable and usable. Archival arrangement operates on principles quite different from library cataloging, because archives are unique accumulations, not published items, and their meaning depends heavily on the context of their creation and accumulation. The core principle of respect for original order, that the creator's own organization should be preserved when it exists, exists in tension with the reality that many collections arrive disordered, mixed, or damaged. Good arrangement balances principle with pragmatism, organizes at the series level rather than item level, preserves evidence of provenance and order, and documents the decisions made so researchers and future archivists can trust and navigate the collection.

Use this skill when arranging a new accession, deciding series structure, handling disordered materials, or revising an existing arrangement. The goal is to prevent the agent from imposing arbitrary order, destroying original order through over-processing, arranging at item level when series level suffices, or failing to document the arrangement decisions.

## Core Rules

### Respect Original Order When It Exists

The principle of respect for original order holds that the arrangement imposed by the creator carries evidentiary value and should be preserved. The creator's filing system reveals how they worked, what they considered related, and how they understood their own records.

Apply the principle:

- identify whether a discernible original order exists;
- preserve that order unless it is clearly accidental or harmful;
- do not reorganize to impose a "better" system on top of a meaningful one;
- document the original order in the finding aid even if some reorganization was necessary;
- recognize that original order may be by function, chronology, subject, or correspondence partner.

Original order is evidence. Destroying it to impose a more convenient arrangement destroys information about the creator.

### Respect Provenance As The Organizing Principle

Archives are organized by provenance, the origin and chain of custody, not by subject. Materials from different creators are not intermingled, even if they cover the same topic.

Apply provenance:

- keep records from different creators or sources separate;
- document the provenance and chain of custody for each accession;
- do not merge collections from different creators into one arrangement;
- distinguish the creator's records from later additions or related but separate accessions;
- note when materials from multiple provenances are physically stored together for efficiency while remaining intellectually separate.

Subject-based arrangement, natural in libraries, violates archival principle and destroys provenance evidence.

### Arrange At The Series Level, Not Item Level

Archival collections are typically large, and item-level arrangement is rarely feasible or useful. The series, a group of records with a common function, format, or provenance-based relationship, is the primary unit of arrangement.

Series-level practice:

- identify natural groupings based on the creator's functions or the records' form;
- arrange series in a logical order, often hierarchical with subseries;
- arrange within series, often chronologically or alphabetically, as appropriate;
- reserve item-level arrangement for small, high-value, or heavily requested collections;
- let the finding aid describe series and their arrangement, not list every item.

Over-processing at item level consumes scarce time and can impose artificial structure. Series-level arrangement serves most research needs.

### Identify Series By Function, Form, Or Provenance

Series emerge from how the creator generated and used records, or from the form the records take. Identifying series requires understanding the collection's content and context.

Common series patterns:

- by function, correspondence, financial records, subject files, publications;
- by form, photographs, maps, audiovisual materials, digital files;
- by provenance, when an accession combines materials from distinct activities;
- by chronology, for records naturally arranged by date;
- by correspondent or name, for letter collections.

Examine the collection to find its natural series rather than imposing a generic template. The creator's own divisions, where they exist, are the strongest guide.

### Handle Disordered Collections With Documented Judgment

Many collections arrive with no discernible original order, or with order disrupted by prior handling. In these cases, arrangement requires judgment and must be documented.

Approaches to disordered collections:

- survey the collection to identify any surviving order or natural groupings;
- impose a logical arrangement based on function, form, or chronology;
- preserve any groupings that may reflect original order even if partial;
- note in the finding aid that the arrangement was imposed and explain the basis;
- avoid extensive reorganization that destroys whatever context remains.

Imposed arrangement is sometimes necessary, but it should be minimal, logical, and transparent.

### Preserve Evidence Of The Collection's History

Collections accumulate evidence beyond their content: labels, folder titles, annotations, additions, and reorganizations all tell the collection's story. Arrangement should preserve this evidence.

Preserve:

- the creator's original folder titles and labels;
- annotations and markings that reveal use or relationships;
- evidence of prior arrangements or reorganizations;
- additions and accretions as distinct groups;
- notes about weeding, conservation, or other processing actions.

This evidence supports researchers' understanding of provenance and authenticity. Discarding it during processing is a loss.

### Balance Processing Depth With Available Resources

More processing yields better access but costs more time. Archivists face a permanent tension between thorough processing and the backlog of unprocessed collections.

Approaches:

- apply MPLP, More Product Less Process, principles for rapid baseline access;
- process to series level for most collections;
- reserve full item-level processing for high-value or high-demand materials;
- document the level of processing so researchers know what to expect;
- revisit minimal processing when resources allow.

A minimally processed collection that is accessible serves researchers better than a fully processed collection that sits in the backlog for years.

### Document Arrangement Decisions In The Finding Aid

The finding aid must explain the arrangement so researchers and future archivists understand the collection's structure and the decisions behind it.

Document:

- the principles applied, original order preserved or arrangement imposed;
- the series structure and the rationale for each series;
- any reorganization, weeding, or consolidation performed;
- the relationship of additions to the original accession;
- the processing level and date.

Documentation turns arrangement from invisible labor into trustworthy, reproducible practice.

### Coordinate Physical And Intellectual Arrangement

Physical storage and intellectual arrangement can differ. Materials may be stored by format for preservation while intellectually arranged by series.

Coordinate:

- maintain clear links between intellectual series and physical locations;
- use box and folder lists that map intellectual order to physical storage;
- note when oversized, fragile, or restricted items are stored separately;
- keep digital and physical components of a collection intellectually linked;
- ensure the finding aid reflects both intellectual and physical reality.

Researchers navigate intellectually but retrieve physically. The finding aid must bridge both.

### Handle Mixed Media And Born-Digital Components

Modern collections mix paper, photographs, audiovisual media, and born-digital files. Arrangement must integrate these components.

Approaches:

- arrange born-digital and audiovisual materials within the same series structure;
- preserve the relationships between digital files and their paper counterparts;
- note format-specific storage, preservation, and access issues;
- describe digital components with sufficient technical and administrative metadata;
- ensure researchers can discover all components of a collection.

Treating digital files as a separate, undescribed appendage fragments the collection and hides content.

## Common Traps

### Imposing Arbitrary Order On Meaningful Records

Reorganizing to suit the archivist's preference destroys original order evidence. Preserve creator order where it exists.

### Arranging By Subject Instead Of Provenance

Subject arrangement violates archival principle and scrambles provenance. Organize by creator and function.

### Item-Level Processing Of Large Collections

Item-level arrangement consumes time and imposes artificial structure. Arrange at series level for most collections.

### Destroying Processing Evidence

Discarding labels, annotations, and prior arrangement evidence during processing loses context. Preserve it.

### Undocumented Imposed Arrangement

When arrangement is imposed, the basis must be recorded. Undocumented arrangement confuses future users.

### Over-Processing While Backlog Grows

Full processing of every collection leaves most inaccessible. Balance depth against backlog with minimal processing where appropriate.

### Disconnecting Physical And Intellectual Order

When storage differs from arrangement, the finding aid must bridge them. Unclear mapping frustrates retrieval.

### Treating Digital As An Appendage

Born-digital files hidden in a separate undescribed component fragment the collection. Integrate them into the series structure.

## Self-Check

- Is original order preserved where it exists, and is its preservation documented?
- Are materials organized by provenance, keeping different creators' records separate?
- Is arrangement primarily at the series level, with item level reserved for justified cases?
- Are series identified by function, form, or provenance rather than imposed generically?
- For disordered collections, is imposed arrangement minimal, logical, and documented?
- Is evidence of the collection's history, labels, annotations, additions, preserved?
- Is processing depth balanced against backlog, with minimal processing used where appropriate?
- Does the finding aid document the arrangement principles, series structure, and processing decisions?
- Are physical storage and intellectual arrangement coordinated and clearly mapped?
- Are mixed media and born-digital components integrated into the series structure?
