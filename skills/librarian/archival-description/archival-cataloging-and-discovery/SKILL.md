# Archival Cataloging And Discovery

Archival materials live in two worlds. They are described in finding aids that capture their multi-level, hierarchical nature, and they must also be represented in catalogs and discovery systems that integrate them with the rest of the library's holdings and with regional, national, and aggregator portals. The bridge between these worlds is archival cataloging: the creation of collection-level and component-level records in standardized formats (MARC, EAD) according to archival descriptive standards (DACS), with authority control that connects archival materials to the people, organizations, subjects, and places they document. A common failure is to treat the finding aid as the only description that matters, leaving archival materials invisible in the catalog and in aggregators where most researchers begin. Another failure is to create catalog records so thin that researchers cannot judge the collection's relevance from the catalog alone. Archival cataloging and discovery is the discipline of representing complex, hierarchical materials in the interoperable systems where researchers actually search, without losing the richness that makes archival description valuable.

Use this skill when creating archival catalog records, integrating finding aids with catalogs and discovery systems, applying MARC, EAD, and DACS, managing archival authority control, and ensuring discovery across platforms. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the finding aid as sufficient on its own, from creating thin catalog records, from neglecting authority control, or from failing to expose archival materials in catalogs and aggregators.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Two-Tier Descriptive World

Archival materials are described at multiple levels and in multiple systems. Understand the relationship.

Understand:

- the finding aid provides detailed multi-level description within the archival repository;
- the catalog record provides collection-level discovery within the library catalog;
- discovery systems and aggregators may draw from both;
- the two must be consistent and mutually reinforcing, not divergent.

Treating one as sufficient without the other leaves archival materials under-discoverable.

### Create Strong Collection-Level Catalog Records

The catalog record is often a researcher's first encounter with a collection. Make it strong.

Include in the catalog record:

- an accurate, informative title and creator statement;
- a substantive scope and content summary that conveys what the collection is about;
- clear dates, extent, and physical description;
- access and use restrictions and conditions;
- the collection's provenance and acquisition source;
- a link to the full finding aid.

A thin catalog record (title, dates, extent only) fails to help researchers judge relevance.

### Apply Archival Descriptive Standards Consistently

Use the appropriate standards for archival description and cataloging.

Apply:

- archival descriptive standards (e.g., DACS) for content rules;
- MARC format for collection-level catalog records, with archival-specific fields;
- EAD for encoded finding aids that preserve the multi-level hierarchy;
- standardized content and carrier type terms;
- consistent application across collections and over time.

Inconsistent standards undermine interoperability and discovery.

### Maintain Multi-Level Hierarchy In Description

Archival description is multi-level: collection, series, file, item. Preserve this hierarchy.

Maintain hierarchy by:

- describing at each level appropriate to the materials and research value;
- ensuring each level's description nests within the level above;
- carrying context from higher levels to lower levels;
- avoiding flat, item-level description that loses the archival structure;
- reflecting the hierarchy in both finding aid and encoded formats.

Flattening the hierarchy destroys the contextual relationships that make archives useful.

### Establish Name, Subject, And Place Authority Control

Authority control connects archival materials to the entities they document. Establish it.

Establish:

- authorized access points for creators, subjects, places, and genres;
- cross-references for variant names and forms;
- consistent forms across collections and catalog records;
- archival authority records that capture biographical and administrative context;
- links to union vocabularies and authority files where available.

Without authority control, related materials are scattered under variant names and forms.

### Capture Biographical And Historical Context

Archival materials require context about the creators and the records' history. Capture it.

Capture:

- biographical or historical notes for the creators (persons, families, organizations);
- the history and functions that generated the records;
- the acquisition and transfer history of the collection;
- processing history and any major interventions;
- contextual information that aids interpretation.

Context notes are what allow researchers to understand and trust the records.

### Expose Archival Materials In Discovery And Aggregators

Researchers search in catalogs, discovery layers, and aggregators, not only in the finding aid. Expose materials there.

Expose by:

- contributing catalog records to local, regional, and national union catalogs;
- contributing finding aids to archival aggregators and portals;
- ensuring records carry the identifiers and links needed for aggregation;
- mapping to aggregator schemas and requirements;
- providing collection-level and component-level records as appropriate.

Materials not exposed in aggregators are invisible to researchers who start there.

### Provide Clear Access And Use Information

Access and use conditions must be visible in the catalog and discovery system, not only in the finding aid.

Provide:

- access restrictions and their basis (donor, legal, preservation);
- use and reproduction conditions and rights statements;
- physical access requirements and location;
- the process for requesting materials;
- links to more detailed access information.

Researchers who learn of restrictions only after traveling to the repository are poorly served.

### Link Finding Aids, Catalog Records, And Digital Objects

The finding aid, catalog record, and any digital objects must be linked. Create a coherent network.

Link:

- the catalog record to the full finding aid;
- the finding aid to digital objects and their metadata;
- digital objects back to their finding aid context;
- related collections through shared creators, subjects, or provenance;
- authority records to the materials they describe.

A coherent link network lets researchers move fluidly across systems and levels.

### Keep Description Synchronized Across Systems

Description exists in multiple systems that can drift apart. Keep them synchronized.

Synchronize by:

- updating catalog records when finding aids are revised;
- keeping extent, dates, and access conditions consistent across systems;
- using shared identifiers to detect and resolve discrepancies;
- establishing workflows that update all affected systems;
- auditing periodically for drift.

Divergent descriptions across systems confuse researchers and erode trust.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Finding Aid As Sufficient On Its Own

The catalog record and aggregator exposure are essential for discovery. Do not omit them.

### Creating Thin Collection-Level Catalog Records

A title, dates, and extent do not let researchers judge relevance. Provide substantive scope and content.

### Flattening Or Ignoring The Multi-Level Hierarchy

Archival description is hierarchical. Preserve the nesting of collection, series, file, item.

### Neglecting Authority Control

Without authority work, related materials scatter under variant names and forms.

### Omitting Biographical And Historical Context

Context notes are essential for interpreting archival records. Do not skip them.

### Failing To Expose Materials In Aggregators

Researchers start in catalogs and aggregators. Contribute records and finding aids there.

### Hiding Access And Use Conditions

Restrictions must be visible in the catalog and discovery system, not only in the finding aid.

### Letting Systems Drift Apart

Synchronize description across catalog, finding aid, and digital object systems.

## Self-Check

- Did you create both a detailed finding aid and a strong collection-level catalog record?
- Does the catalog record include a substantive scope and content summary, not just title and extent?
- Did you apply archival descriptive standards (DACS, MARC, EAD) consistently?
- Did you preserve the multi-level hierarchy in description and encoding?
- Did you establish name, subject, place, and genre authority control with cross-references?
- Did you capture biographical, historical, and acquisition context for the collection?
- Did you expose materials in catalogs, discovery layers, and archival aggregators?
- Are access and use conditions visible in the catalog and discovery system?
- Did you link finding aids, catalog records, and digital objects into a coherent network?
- Did you keep description synchronized across systems to prevent drift?
