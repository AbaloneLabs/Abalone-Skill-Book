# Born-Digital Archival Description

Archival collections increasingly contain born-digital components: email archives, office documents, drafts, presentations, websites, datasets, and digital photographs that are part of a creator's records. Describing these materials within archival finding aids is not the same as describing paper series. Born-digital materials have extents measured in files and gigabytes rather than linear feet, carry access conditions shaped by format viability and privacy review rather than physical fragility, and require description that connects the digital components to the intellectual and provenance structure of the collection. A common failure is to treat born-digital materials as an afterthought: a vague series note mentioning "digital files" with no meaningful extent, format, or access information, leaving researchers unable to determine what exists or how to use it. Another failure is to describe digital materials so heavily at the item level that the archival principles of provenance and original order are lost in a flood of file-level records. Born-digital archival description must integrate digital components into the collection's intellectual structure while capturing the technical, access, and preservation realities that make those components usable.

Use this skill when describing born-digital components within archival collections, integrating digital materials into finding aids, recording digital extent and access conditions, and handling hybrid and fully digital collections. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating born-digital materials as an afterthought, from over- or under-describing at the wrong level, from omitting access-critical technical and rights information, or from severing digital components from the collection's provenance structure.

## Core Rules

### Integrate Digital Materials Into The Collection's Intellectual Structure

Born-digital materials are part of the archival collection, not a separate annex. Integrate them into the finding aid's intellectual structure.

Integrate by:

- placing digital series and components within the provenance-based hierarchy;
- relating digital materials to their paper counterparts where they form a unified record;
- maintaining original order in digital folder and file structures where it has meaning;
- describing digital components at the same levels (collection, series, file) appropriate to the materials;
- avoiding the creation of a disconnected "digital" series that severs context.

Digital materials described outside the collection's structure lose their archival meaning.

### Describe Extent In Meaningful Digital Terms

Linear feet do not capture born-digital extent. Describe extent in terms meaningful to digital materials.

Record:

- the number of files and their total size;
- the formats present and their distribution;
- the equivalent physical extent of any original media retained;
- the relationship between digital extent and the series' overall scope;
- growth over time for accruing digital records.

Meaningful extent tells researchers the scale and nature of the digital materials.

### Document Formats, Software, And Environment

Born-digital materials depend on their creation and access environment. Document it.

Document:

- the file formats present and their viability;
- the software and operating systems used to create the materials;
- any specialized software required for access or interpretation;
- the original hardware or media if relevant to context;
- migrations, normalizations, or emulations applied during processing.

Format and environment documentation lets researchers judge what they can access and how.

### Record Access Conditions And Restrictions Clearly

Born-digital access is shaped by format, privacy, and legal considerations. Record access conditions clearly.

Record:

- the access status (open, mediated, restricted, embargoed);
- the reasons for restrictions (privacy, confidentiality, donor terms, legal);
- the access method (direct delivery, derived copies, emulation, reading-room only);
- any screening or redaction applied;
- the process for requesting access to restricted materials.

Vague access notes leave researchers unable to determine whether and how they can use the materials.

### Choose The Appropriate Level Of Description

Born-digital materials can be over- or under-described. Choose the level deliberately.

Choose by:

- describing at the collection and series level for large, homogeneous digital accumulations;
- providing file or item level description only where warranted by research value or distinctiveness;
- using directory listings or inventories where they convey structure efficiently;
- balancing description depth against processing resources and access benefit;
- following archival principles of aggregation rather than item-level reflex.

Item-level description of every digital file is neither feasible nor archival; under-description hides valuable materials.

### Connect Digital Components To Provenance And Context

Born-digital materials carry the same provenance obligations as paper. Connect them to context.

Connect by:

- documenting the creator, transfer history, and acquisition of digital materials;
- preserving and describing original digital order where meaningful;
- noting relationships between digital files and related paper records;
- capturing contextual metadata from the source systems;
- explaining any reorganization applied during processing.

Provenance context is what makes digital files interpretable as archival records rather than loose files.

### Address Privacy And Confidentiality

Born-digital archival materials frequently contain personal and confidential information. Address privacy in description.

Address by:

- identifying sensitive personal, confidential, or restricted content;
- applying access restrictions consistent with the sensitivity;
- noting screening, redaction, or withdrawal of restricted materials;
- describing restrictions at the appropriate level (series, file);
- documenting review processes and their limitations.

Privacy obligations are especially acute in email, personnel, and case-file materials.

### Coordinate Description With Digital Preservation Systems

Born-digital description should coordinate with the digital preservation system. Avoid duplication and drift.

Coordinate by:

- linking finding aid components to preservation system objects and identifiers;
- ensuring extent, format, and fixity information is consistent across systems;
- using shared identifiers to connect description, preservation, and access;
- updating description when preservation actions change the materials;
- avoiding parallel, divergent descriptive records.

Coordination prevents the description and the preservation system from disagreeing.

### Make Digital Materials Discoverable

Born-digital materials must be discoverable, not just preserved. Ensure they surface in discovery.

Make discoverable by:

- including digital series and components in the finding aid with clear extent and access notes;
- exposing digital components in discovery platforms and aggregators;
- providing direct links to accessible digital content where rights allow;
- using subject and name access points that cover digital as well as paper content;
- avoiding burying digital materials in notes that discovery tools cannot parse.

Undiscoverable digital materials are effectively inaccessible regardless of preservation.

### Plan For Accruals And Evolving Formats

Born-digital collections accrue and their formats evolve. Plan for ongoing description.

Plan for:

- describing accruals as they arrive, with updated extent and dates;
- handling format evolution and migration over time;
- maintaining consistency across accruals and processing batches;
- reviewing and updating description as access conditions change;
- documenting the history of the collection's digital components.

Static description decays as born-digital collections grow and formats shift.

## Common Traps

### Treating Born-Digital Materials As An Afterthought

Vague "digital files" notes hide materials researchers need. Describe them meaningfully.

### Severing Digital Materials From Provenance Structure

Digital components belong in the collection's intellectual hierarchy, not a disconnected series.

### Using Physical Extent For Digital Materials

Linear feet do not capture digital extent. Use file counts, sizes, and formats.

### Over-Describing At Item Level

Item-level description of every file is infeasible and unarchival. Describe at appropriate levels.

### Omitting Access Conditions And Methods

Researchers need to know the access status, method, and restrictions. Record them clearly.

### Neglecting Privacy And Confidentiality

Born-digital materials often contain sensitive information. Address privacy in description.

### Description And Preservation Systems Drifting Apart

Coordinate description with the preservation system to avoid inconsistency.

### Burying Digital Materials In Undiscoverable Notes

Digital components must surface in discovery, not just exist in prose notes.

## Self-Check

- Did you integrate digital materials into the collection's provenance-based hierarchy?
- Did you describe extent in meaningful digital terms (files, size, formats)?
- Did you document formats, software, environment, and any migrations or emulations?
- Did you record access status, method, restrictions, and the request process clearly?
- Did you choose the appropriate level of description rather than defaulting to item level?
- Did you connect digital components to provenance, transfer history, and related paper records?
- Did you address privacy and confidentiality with appropriate restrictions and screening notes?
- Did you coordinate description with the digital preservation system using shared identifiers?
- Did you make digital materials discoverable in the finding aid and discovery platforms?
- Did you plan for accruals, format evolution, and ongoing description updates?
