# Finding Aids

A finding aid is the primary access tool for an archival collection. Unlike a catalog record, which describes an item, a finding aid describes a complex, multi-part collection at multiple levels of detail, from the collection overview down to the contents of individual boxes. A weak finding aid hides a collection that may be the only source on its subject; a strong one opens the collection to research. Writing a finding aid is not transcription; it is intellectual labor that requires understanding the collection, its creator, its context, and the needs of researchers. Good finding aids follow descriptive standards, provide honest and specific scope and content notes, structure the description in a navigable hierarchy, and balance completeness with the reality of processing resources.

Use this skill when writing or revising a finding aid, structuring multi-level description, drafting biographical or scope notes, or deciding description depth. The goal is to prevent the agent from writing vague notes, skipping context, producing unusable container lists, ignoring descriptive standards, or describing at item level when series level serves researchers better.

## Core Rules

### Follow A Descriptive Standard Consistently

Finding aids should follow a recognized standard, such as DACS, Describing Archives, A Content Standard, and be encoded in a standard format like EAD for interoperability. Consistency enables aggregation and researcher familiarity.

Apply standards:

- use DACS or the relevant national standard for content rules;
- encode in EAD or an equivalent structured format;
- include the required elements, identity statement, context, content, access, materials;
- follow the standard's rules for names, dates, and extent;
- ensure records harvest into consortial and national aggregators.

Ad hoc finding aids that follow no standard cannot be aggregated and vary unpredictably in quality.

### Structure Description In A Hierarchy

Archival description is multi-level. A finding aid moves from the general to the specific, describing the collection, then series, then subseries, then containers. This hierarchy reflects the arrangement and supports navigation.

Typical hierarchy:

- collection-level overview, the summary that orients the researcher;
- biographical or historical note on the creator;
- scope and content note for the collection;
- series-level descriptions with their own scope and arrangement notes;
- subseries where the collection warrants;
- container or folder list mapping intellectual arrangement to physical storage.

Each level should be coherent on its own but connect clearly to the levels above and below.

### Write A Substantive Biographical Or Historical Note

The biographical or historical note contextualizes the collection by explaining who created it and under what circumstances. Researchers need this to understand and use the records.

A strong note includes:

- who the creator was, person, family, or organization;
- significant dates, events, and roles;
- the creator's relationship to the collection's subject;
- the context in which the records were created;
- enough detail to orient researchers without becoming a full biography.

Vague notes like "a collection of papers" fail this task. Specific, sourced context enables researchers to assess the collection's relevance and reliability.

### Write An Honest And Specific Scope And Content Note

The scope and content note tells researchers what is actually in the collection. It is the most consulted part of the finding aid and must be accurate and specific.

Cover:

- the date range and bulk dates;
- the types of materials, correspondence, photographs, reports, digital files;
- the main subjects, functions, or activities documented;
- significant gaps or absences;
- languages represented;
- any particularly significant or unusual components.

Avoid generic phrases like "papers relating to various topics." Name the topics. A researcher should be able to judge relevance from the scope note alone.

### Document Provenance And Acquisition

The finding aid must record where the collection came from and how the institution acquired it, because provenance is foundational to archival authenticity and access.

Record:

- the source of the collection, donor, transfer, or purchase;
- the date and method of acquisition;
- the accession number and any prior custodial history;
- restrictions resulting from the deed of gift;
- any separated materials or weeding performed.

Transparent provenance supports trust and helps researchers trace a collection's history.

### Provide Clear Access And Use Information

Researchers need to know whether and how they can access and use the collection. This information must be prominent and accurate.

Include:

- access restrictions and their basis, donor, privacy, classification;
- use and reproduction rights, copyright status where known;
- citation format preferred by the repository;
- physical access notes, fragile materials, offsite storage;
- digital access, what is available online and how;
- contact and request procedures.

Buried or missing access information wastes researchers' time and generates reference inquiries.

### Create A Usable Container List

The container list maps the intellectual arrangement to physical storage and is what researchers use to request materials. It must be accurate and navigable.

A usable list:

- reflects the series and subseries structure;
- provides box and folder numbers that match physical reality;
- includes folder titles that are specific enough to guide requests;
- preserves original folder titles where meaningful;
- notes restricted or separated materials in place;
- is organized for browsing, often with consistent formatting.

A container list that does not match physical storage causes retrieval failures and frustrates researchers and staff.

### Match Description Depth To Research Need And Resources

Not every collection warrants item-level description, and not every repository can afford it. Choose description depth deliberately.

Depth options:

- collection and series level, sufficient for most research;
- folder level, for collections where folder titles guide requests;
- item level, for small, high-value, or heavily requested collections;
- minimal processing with box-level listing for rapid access.

Document the level so researchers know what to expect. Over-describing wastes resources; under-describing hides content.

### Preserve And Explain Original Order And Arrangement

The finding aid must explain the collection's arrangement so researchers understand its structure and the archivist's decisions.

Explain:

- whether original order was preserved or arrangement imposed;
- the series structure and rationale;
- the arrangement within series, chronological, alphabetical, by function;
- any reorganization, consolidation, or weeding;
- the relationship of additions to the original accession.

Researchers rely on this to navigate and interpret the collection. Unexplained arrangement is a barrier.

### Keep The Finding Aid Current

Collections change: additions arrive, restrictions expire, materials are digitized, errors are found. A finding aid that is never updated becomes inaccurate.

Maintain:

- record additions and update extent and scope notes;
- update access restrictions as they expire or change;
- note digitization and online availability;
- correct errors found through use;
- record the date of the last revision.

A finding aid is a living document. Stale finding aids mislead researchers and staff.

### Make Finding Aids Discoverable

A finding aid that researchers cannot find serves no one. Discovery must be planned, not assumed.

Discovery practices:

- publish finding aids online in a searchable interface;
- contribute to regional and national aggregators;
- provide a collection-level catalog record linking to the finding aid;
- ensure finding aids are indexed by search engines;
- use persistent identifiers for stable linking.

Discovery multiplies the finding aid's value. A finding aid available only in the reading room reaches a fraction of its potential users.

## Common Traps

### Vague Scope And Content Notes

"This collection contains various materials" tells researchers nothing. Be specific about dates, types, and subjects.

### Missing Or Weak Biographical Context

Without creator context, researchers cannot assess relevance or reliability. Write substantive biographical or historical notes.

### Container Lists That Do Not Match Storage

Inaccurate box and folder numbers cause retrieval failures. Keep the list aligned with physical reality.

### Ignoring Descriptive Standards

Ad hoc finding aids cannot aggregate and vary in quality. Follow DACS and encode in EAD.

### Item-Level Description Of Large Collections

Item-level description consumes resources and often serves no research need. Describe at series or folder level for most collections.

### Buried Access Information

Hidden restrictions and rights waste researcher time. Make access and use information prominent.

### Unexplained Arrangement

Researchers cannot navigate what they do not understand. Explain the arrangement and any imposed decisions.

### Stale Finding Aids

Collections change, and finding aids that are never updated become inaccurate. Maintain them as living documents.

## Self-Check

- Does the finding aid follow a recognized descriptive standard and structured encoding?
- Is the description organized in a clear hierarchy from collection to series to containers?
- Does the biographical or historical note provide specific, sourced context about the creator?
- Is the scope and content note honest and specific about dates, materials, subjects, and gaps?
- Are provenance, acquisition method, and any restrictions documented transparently?
- Is access and use information, restrictions, rights, citation, prominent and accurate?
- Does the container list accurately reflect physical storage and the series structure?
- Is the description depth matched to research need and available resources?
- Is the arrangement, original or imposed, explained so researchers can navigate?
- Is the finding aid kept current with additions, restriction changes, digitization, and corrections?
- Is the finding aid discoverable through online publication, aggregation, and catalog linking?
