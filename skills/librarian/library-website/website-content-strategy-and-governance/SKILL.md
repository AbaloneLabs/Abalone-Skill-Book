# Website Content Strategy And Governance

A library website is a living publication, not a one-time build. Content, the words, images, documents, links, and structured data across the site, is what patrons actually consume, and it decays. Hours change, policies shift, staff leave, databases migrate, forms break, and links rot. Without governance, a website that launched clean becomes a sprawling, contradictory, outdated mess within a couple of years, and the cost of the next redesign balloons because no one knows what is accurate, who owns what, or whether anyone still uses a given page. Content strategy and governance are the disciplines that prevent this decay. They define what content exists, why, who owns it, when it is reviewed, and how it stays consistent and accurate over time.

The judgment problem is that libraries rarely treat web content as a managed asset. Content is created ad hoc by whoever has permission, with no shared standards, no ownership records, and no review schedule. The result is duplication, contradiction, and drift: three pages with different hours, a policy page last updated five years ago, a PDF that contradicts the HTML version, dead links to discontinued services. Governance is often perceived as bureaucracy, but its absence is far more expensive: confused patrons, support load, legal and accessibility risk, and repeated costly redesigns. The agent's job is to establish lightweight, sustainable governance that keeps content accurate without paralyzing the people who maintain it.

Use this skill when planning a library website's content, defining voice and style, setting up workflows and ownership, scheduling reviews, migrating content during a redesign, auditing existing content, or building a governance framework. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating content as a launch deliverable, allowing uncontrolled growth, skipping ownership and review cycles, migrating everything blindly, or building governance so heavy that staff stop maintaining the site.

## Core Rules

### Treat Content As A Lifecycle Asset, Not A Launch Deliverable

Content has a lifecycle: plan, create, publish, maintain, review, revise or retire. Designing for the whole lifecycle is what keeps a site healthy.

Lifecycle practices:

- define a purpose and audience for every piece of content before creating it;
- establish a review date or review frequency at creation time;
- plan for retirement, not just addition; every page should have a path to update or removal;
- track content in a central inventory or content audit spreadsheet;
- treat the launch as the start of maintenance, not the end of the project.

Content without a lifecycle plan accumulates indefinitely. A page that no one owns and no one reviews becomes a liability.

### Assign Clear Ownership For Every Section

Governance fails without ownership. Every piece of content needs a named owner accountable for accuracy.

Ownership model:

- assign a primary owner for each major section or content type;
- define what ownership means: keeping content accurate, reviewed on schedule, and compliant with standards;
- maintain a current roster of owners, updated when staff change roles;
- distinguish content owners, who are responsible for substance, from web maintainers, who handle technical publishing;
- have a fallback owner or team for orphaned content when an owner leaves.

"Everyone's responsibility" means no one's responsibility. Named ownership is the foundation of governance.

### Define A Sustainable Review Cycle

Content must be reviewed on a schedule. Different content types need different frequencies.

Review frequency guidance:

- hours, contact info, and current events: review monthly or on change;
- policies and service pages: review at least annually;
- research guides: review annually or per academic cycle;
- evergreen background content: review every one to two years;
- event and news content: archive or remove promptly after the event;
- legal, accessibility, and privacy notices: review on legal change and at least annually.

Build the review cycle into workflows with reminders and a content calendar. Without scheduled review, content drifts toward inaccuracy.

### Establish A Content Audit Baseline

Before governing content, you must know what exists. A content audit is the baseline.

Audit captures for each page:

- URL, title, and owner;
- last reviewed and last updated dates;
- content type and purpose;
- accuracy status and issues found;
- usage data, such as pageviews and entry or exit metrics;
- accessibility status;
- action: keep, revise, merge, archive, or delete.

A thorough audit often reveals that a large fraction of pages are outdated, duplicated, or unused. This is the evidence base for rationalizing the site.

### Define Editorial Voice And Style Standards

Consistency builds trust and reduces cognitive load. A shared voice and style make the site feel like one library, not a patchwork.

Style standards to define:

- voice and tone, for example helpful, plain, and respectful;
- plain language rules and reading-level targets for public pages;
- terminology and preferred phrasing, including how to handle library jargon;
- formatting conventions for headings, lists, dates, times, and names;
- image and media standards including alt text requirements;
- link standards, including descriptive link text and external link policies;
- accessibility requirements baked into the style guide.

Document standards in a living style guide that all contributors follow. A style guide that exists only in one person's head produces inconsistency.

### Control Content Growth And Eliminate Duplication

Uncontrolled growth is the primary cause of content rot. Governance must constrain addition.

Growth controls:

- require a purpose and owner before publishing new content;
- check for existing content before creating new, and merge or link instead of duplicating;
- prefer updating an existing page over creating a new one;
- archive or delete content that is no longer needed rather than leaving it live;
- consolidate fragmented pages about the same service into one authoritative page;
- use analytics to identify low- or zero-traffic pages and evaluate them for removal.

Every duplicate page is a future contradiction. Every orphan page is a future inaccuracy. Rationalize aggressively.

### Plan Content Migration Carefully During Redesigns

Redesign migrations are where content debt comes due. Migrating everything blindly carries the rot into the new site.

Migration practices:

- audit and evaluate all existing content before migrating;
- decide for each page: migrate as-is, revise then migrate, merge, or do not migrate;
- use migration as an opportunity to reduce, not preserve, content volume;
- map old URLs to new ones and set up redirects to preserve search and links;
- preserve or update metadata, including review dates and owners;
- test migrated content for accuracy, formatting, and accessibility before launch;
- avoid the "migrate now, fix later" trap, which rarely results in fixing.

A migration that carries forward outdated and duplicated content wastes the redesign. Clean first, then migrate.

### Bake Accessibility Into Content Standards

Accessibility is a content concern, not just a design concern. Content authors create or break accessibility daily.

Content accessibility rules:

- use proper heading structure and semantic HTML;
- write meaningful alt text for informative images;
- use descriptive link text, not "click here";
- provide transcripts and captions for media;
- avoid conveying meaning through color alone;
- structure tables and lists semantically;
- write plainly to support users with cognitive and language needs.

Train all content contributors on these rules and verify compliance in review. Accessibility cannot be retrofitted at the end.

### Use Analytics To Guide Content Decisions

Content decisions should be evidence-based. Analytics reveal what patrons actually use.

Analytics uses:

- identify high-traffic pages and ensure they are accurate and well-maintained;
- find low- or zero-traffic pages and evaluate for removal;
- identify common search terms and exit pages to find content gaps;
- track task completion, such as form submissions and account logins;
- compare performance before and after content changes;
- avoid vanity metrics; focus on task success and user outcomes.

Analytics turn content strategy from opinion into evidence. Review them regularly and act on findings.

### Keep Governance Lightweight And Sustainable

Governance that is too heavy is ignored. The goal is the lightest structure that keeps content accurate.

Sustainable governance:

- define the minimum viable governance: ownership, review dates, and basic standards;
- automate reminders and reporting where possible;
- integrate content tasks into existing workflows rather than adding separate burden;
- provide templates and checklists to make compliance easy;
- train contributors once and provide quick-reference guides;
- review the governance process itself periodically and simplify what is not working.

The best governance is the one staff actually follow. Design for adoption, not theoretical completeness.

## Common Traps

### Treating Content As A Launch Deliverable

Content created for launch without a maintenance plan decays. Plan the full lifecycle from the start.

### No Named Ownership

"Everyone owns it" means no one does. Assign a named owner to every section and content type.

### Uncontrolled Content Growth

Allowing anyone to add pages without purpose or review creates duplication and rot. Control addition and rationalize regularly.

### Migrating Everything Blindly During Redesign

Migrating all content carries rot into the new site. Audit, evaluate, and clean before migrating.

### No Review Cycle

Content without scheduled review drifts toward inaccuracy. Build review dates into workflows with reminders.

### Inconsistent Voice And Style

Without shared standards, the site feels like a patchwork and erodes trust. Maintain a living style guide.

### Accessibility Treated As Design Only

Content authors create accessibility barriers daily with poor headings, link text, and alt text. Train contributors and verify in review.

### Governance So Heavy It Is Ignored

Over-engineered governance produces non-compliance. Keep governance lightweight, automated, and integrated into real workflows.

## Self-Check

- Does every piece of content have a defined purpose, audience, named owner, and review date?
- Is there a documented review cycle with frequencies appropriate to each content type, and are reviews actually happening?
- Has a content audit been completed, capturing URL, owner, last reviewed date, accuracy, usage, and action for each page?
- Is there a living style guide defining voice, plain language, terminology, formatting, and accessibility standards?
- Is content growth controlled, with checks for duplication and a process to archive or delete unneeded pages?
- For any redesign migration, has each page been evaluated for migrate, revise, merge, or drop, with redirects set for changed URLs?
- Are accessibility rules baked into content standards and verified during review?
- Are analytics used to identify high-value pages to maintain and low-value pages to remove?
- Is governance lightweight enough that staff actually follow it, with automation and templates reducing burden?
- Is there a current, maintained roster of content owners with fallbacks for orphaned content when staff leave?
