# Responding To Information Requests

Requests for library patron information are the moments when privacy commitments are tested for real. A patron's reading history, borrowing records, computer use, or reference interactions can be sought by law enforcement, government agencies, courts, parents, spouses, schools, or third parties, and each request arrives with its own claimed authority, urgency, and pressure. How the library responds determines whether the professional duty to protect reader privacy holds or collapses. The default professional stance is clear: library records are confidential, disclosed only through proper legal process, and even then only to the extent compelled. The difficulty is that requests rarely arrive in clean, well-documented form. They come with intimidation, with urgency, with partial authority, or with appeals to cooperation, and a poorly handled response can waive rights, over-disclose, or betray the trust patrons place in the library.

The judgment problem is that staff are not lawyers, and the pressure to comply, especially from law enforcement, is intense. A verbal request, a phone call, a letter on official letterhead, or an agent in the building can feel like authority even when it carries no legal force. Staff may disclose voluntarily to be helpful, to avoid trouble, or because they do not know the policy. Even with good intent, voluntary disclosure violates the professional duty and cannot be undone. The agent's job is to ensure every request is met with a disciplined process: route it to the right people, require proper legal process, consult counsel, minimize disclosure, notify the patron where permitted, and document everything. This is a high-stakes domain where mistakes harm real patrons and require clear escalation at every step.

Use this skill when responding to, evaluating, or preparing for any request for patron information or records. The goal is to prevent the agent from disclosing voluntarily, assessing legal validity without counsel, over-disclosing beyond what is compelled, failing to notify patrons, or handling requests without documentation and escalation.

## Core Rules

### Require Proper Legal Process Before Disclosure

Library records are confidential. Disclosure requires proper legal process, not mere request or assertion of authority.

Legal process requirements:

- a subpoena compels some records but has limits and may be challenged;
- a court order or warrant carries greater authority but still must be reviewed;
- different request types, criminal, civil, administrative, intelligence, carry different weight and obligations;
- informal requests, verbal asks, letters, or phone calls, carry no legal force on their own;
- voluntary disclosure without compulsion violates the professional duty.

Treat any request lacking proper legal process as a request that need not, and should not, be honored without escalation and counsel.

### Route Every Request Through A Defined Process

No staff member should evaluate or respond to a request alone. A defined process prevents ad hoc, pressured decisions.

Response process:

- designate who receives and handles requests, typically the director or privacy officer;
- train all staff to recognize a request and immediately route it without responding;
- provide staff with a standard response, such as "I am not authorized to release records; let me connect you with the appropriate office";
- never let front-line staff disclose or deny on the spot;
- have the designated officer consult counsel before any response.

A clear process removes the burden of judgment from the moment of pressure and ensures consistent, defensible handling.

### Consult Legal Counsel Before Complying

Legal process must be interpreted by counsel, not by library staff. Never assess validity or scope alone.

Counsel involvement:

- have counsel review the request for legal sufficiency and scope;
- determine whether the request can be challenged or narrowed;
- identify exactly what records are compelled and what is beyond scope;
- clarify obligations regarding notification, gag orders, and timing;
- document counsel's guidance and the basis for the response.

Even a valid-looking warrant may be overbroad or challengeable. Counsel determines what the library must actually do.

### Disclose Only What Is Compelled, Nothing More

When disclosure is legally required, disclose only the specific records compelled, not the whole database or related data.

Minimization of disclosure:

- produce only the records described in the legal process;
- avoid volunteering additional information;
- redact or withhold records not covered by the order;
- do not provide broader access than compelled, such as live system access;
- confirm scope with counsel before production.

Over-disclosure, even under a valid order, is a privacy failure. Match production precisely to what is compelled.

### Notify The Patron Where Legally Permitted

Patrons have a right to know their records were sought, unless a gag order prohibits notification.

Notification practices:

- determine whether notification is permitted or prohibited by the request;
- notify the patron where permitted, so they can seek their own counsel;
- respect gag orders and nondisclosure requirements where they apply;
- document the notification decision and its legal basis;
- challenge gag orders through counsel where appropriate.

Notification preserves the patron's ability to protect their own interests. Do not skip it where it is allowed.

### Document The Request And Response Thoroughly

Every request and response must be documented for accountability and legal protection.

Documentation:

- record who made the request, when, and the claimed authority;
- retain a copy of the legal process;
- note counsel review and guidance;
- document what was disclosed, to whom, and when;
- record notification decisions and any gag order;
- store documentation securely and retain per policy.

Thorough documentation protects the library, demonstrates compliance, and creates an accountability record.

### Recognize And Resist Pressure Tactics

Requests often come with pressure: urgency, implied threats, appeals to patriotism or cooperation, or presence in the building.

Pressure awareness:

- urgency does not override the requirement for legal process;
- a law enforcement presence does not itself compel disclosure;
- appeals to cooperation do not replace legal compulsion;
- staff are not obligated to answer questions or consent on the spot;
- politeness and firmness can coexist: cooperate with process, not with pressure.

Train staff to remain calm, route the request, and not act under pressure. The process exists precisely for these moments.

### Prepare In Advance With Policy And Training

The worst time to decide how to handle a request is when one arrives. Prepare beforehand.

Preparation:

- maintain a written policy on responding to information requests;
- designate the responsible officer and backup;
- train all staff, including student and part-time staff, on the policy;
- rehearse scenarios, including in-person and urgent requests;
- maintain counsel contact information current and accessible;
- review and update the policy and training regularly.

Preparation turns a high-pressure moment into a procedural one. Practice before the real request.

### Handle Special Request Types Correctly

Different request types have distinct handling.

Special types:

- National Security Letters and FISA requests carry gag provisions; route to counsel immediately and follow challenge procedures where appropriate;
- civil subpoenas in litigation may be challengeable or narrowable;
- freedom of information or public records requests generally do not reach confidential library records protected by statute;
- requests from parents, spouses, or schools generally have no authority over adult patron records;
- third-party data demands from vendors or companies require contractual and legal review.

Understand the categories and route each appropriately through counsel.

### Balance Cooperation With The Professional Duty

Libraries are not adversarial to law enforcement, but they are not extensions of it either. The duty is to protect privacy within the law.

Balanced stance:

- cooperate fully with proper legal process;
- do not obstruct legitimate investigations;
- but do not volunteer, over-disclose, or waive confidentiality;
- protect the patron's rights as the law allows;
- maintain the professional boundary between library service and law enforcement function.

The professional duty and legal cooperation are not in conflict when the process is followed correctly.

## Escalation Guidance

This entire domain is an escalation domain. Specific escalation triggers:

- Any request for patron records, regardless of form or claimed authority: escalate immediately to the designated privacy officer or director. Do not respond at the front line.
- Any request without proper legal process: escalate; do not comply voluntarily under any pressure.
- Any National Security Letter, FISA request, or request with a gag order: escalate immediately to counsel; follow challenge procedures where appropriate.
- Any in-person law enforcement presence seeking records: escalate to leadership and counsel; do not disclose on the spot.
- Any request whose scope or legality is unclear: escalate to counsel before any production.
- Any internal or external pressure to disclose without process: escalate to leadership and document.
- Any suspected that a request was handled improperly or disclosure occurred: escalate immediately to leadership and counsel for remediation.

When in any doubt, escalate, document, and wait for counsel. Disclosure errors cannot be undone.

## Common Traps

### Voluntary Disclosure To Be Helpful

Disclosing without legal compulsion, even helpfully, violates the professional duty and cannot be undone. Require proper process.

### Front-Line Staff Handling Requests Alone

Pressure at the moment of contact leads to bad decisions. Route every request through a defined process and trained officer.

### Assessing Legal Validity Without Counsel

Staff are not lawyers. Have counsel review every request for sufficiency and scope before responding.

### Over-Disclosing Beyond What Is Compelled

Producing more than the order requires is a privacy failure. Disclose only the specific records compelled.

### Failing To Notify The Patron

Where notification is permitted, skipping it denies the patron their rights. Notify unless a gag order applies.

### Succumbing To Pressure And Urgency

Urgency and presence do not override process. Train staff to route and wait, not act under pressure.

### No Preparation Or Training

Deciding how to respond during a request is too late. Prepare policy, designate officers, and train staff in advance.

### Incomplete Documentation

Failing to document the request and response removes accountability and legal protection. Document thoroughly and securely.

## Self-Check

- Is every request for patron records met with a requirement for proper legal process before any disclosure?
- Is there a defined process routing all requests to a designated officer, with front-line staff trained not to respond on the spot?
- Is legal counsel consulted to review legal sufficiency and scope before any compliance?
- When disclosure is compelled, is production limited precisely to the records described, with no voluntary over-disclosure?
- Is the patron notified where legally permitted, with gag orders respected and challenged through counsel where appropriate?
- Is every request, the legal process, counsel guidance, disclosure, and notification thoroughly documented and securely retained?
- Are staff trained to recognize and resist pressure tactics, urgency, and informal authority?
- Is there a written, rehearsed policy with a designated officer, backup, current counsel contact, and regular training?
- Are special request types, including National Security Letters, civil subpoenas, public records requests, and family requests, handled according to their distinct requirements?
- Are escalation paths clear for any request, any lack of process, any gag order, any in-person presence, and any uncertainty, with the default to escalate and wait for counsel?
