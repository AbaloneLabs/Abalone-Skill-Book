# Patron Privacy Policies

A patron privacy policy is the written commitment that defines how the library protects the confidentiality of what people read, seek, and borrow. It is not a legal disclaimer or a compliance artifact; it is the institutional expression of the professional principle that the right to read includes the right to read without surveillance. Privacy is the condition that makes intellectual freedom possible. People will not explore sensitive health, legal, immigration, political, or personal topics if they believe their reading is monitored, recorded, or could be disclosed. A strong privacy policy makes that protection concrete: what is collected, why, how long it is kept, who can see it, and under what conditions it may be released.

The judgment problem is that library systems collect patron data by default, and a policy that is not actively enforced is just words. Many policies are written once, filed away, and contradicted daily by systems that retain borrowing history indefinitely, cameras that record more than needed, analytics that track reading, and staff who casually discuss patrons. Privacy policies also sit in tension with security, convenience, and external demands from law enforcement or vendors. The agent's job is to draft policies that are specific and enforceable, grounded in professional ethics and law, and matched by actual technical and operational practice, and to recognize that privacy decisions in a library carry real consequences for vulnerable patrons and require escalation when legal or political pressure is involved.

Use this skill when drafting, revising, interpreting, or applying a patron privacy policy, setting confidentiality rules, evaluating surveillance technology, balancing security with privacy, or training staff. The goal is to prevent the agent from writing policies that are unenforceable, treating privacy as a checkbox, adopting surveillance without weighing chilling effects, or handling legal demands without proper escalation.

## Core Rules

### Ground The Policy In The Right To Read Privately

Privacy is not primarily a data-security or compliance matter in a library; it is a condition of intellectual freedom. State this explicitly in the policy.

Foundational framing:

- the right to read includes the right to read without surveillance;
- people explore sensitive topics only when they trust their privacy is protected;
- confidentiality of library use is a professional ethical duty, reflected in codes such as the ALA Library Bill of Rights and Code of Ethics;
- privacy enables inquiry, dissent, and personal autonomy;
- the policy exists to protect patrons, not to protect the institution from liability.

When the policy is grounded in principle, downstream decisions about data and surveillance follow from values rather than convenience.

### Be Specific About What Is Collected And Why

A privacy policy must clearly state what patron data is collected and the purpose for each category. Vague language hides over-collection.

Collection specificity:

- enumerate data types: registration information, circulation records, computer and session logs, reference interactions, facility access, and analytics;
- state the legitimate purpose for each type;
- avoid collecting data for unspecified future uses;
- distinguish data needed to operate the service from data collected for analytics or convenience;
- commit to collecting the minimum necessary.

If a data type has no clear operational purpose, the policy should commit to not collecting it. Purposeless collection is a privacy failure.

### Define Confidentiality Of Library Use

The policy must explicitly protect the confidentiality of what patrons read, search, and borrow.

Confidentiality commitments:

- circulation records are confidential and not retained beyond the transaction;
- reference interactions are confidential;
- computer use and browsing are not linked to identity longer than necessary;
- staff do not disclose what a patron read, borrowed, or asked to anyone without proper authorization;
- confidentiality applies to all staff and is a condition of employment.

Confidentiality of library use is the core professional obligation. Make it explicit and train staff on it.

### Set Clear Retention Limits For Each Data Type

Retained data is data that can be breached, subpoenaed, or misused. The policy must set short, enforced retention limits.

Retention commitments:

- define retention periods for each data type based on operational need;
- purge circulation and borrowing records once transactions are complete and fines resolved;
- set short retention for computer use, search, and session logs;
- automate deletion where possible;
- document retention and audit compliance;
- avoid indefinite retention, which is the default of many systems.

Indefinite retention is a privacy failure. Short, enforced limits are active protection.

### Address Surveillance Technologies Explicitly

Cameras, RFID, computer monitoring, and analytics are surveillance technologies. The policy must address them directly.

Surveillance policy:

- state whether and where security cameras are used, and limit them to genuine security needs;
- prohibit using cameras or monitoring to track what patrons read or view;
- limit camera retention to the minimum needed for security;
- address RFID and tracking technologies and their data implications;
- constrain analytics so they do not track individual reading;
- be transparent with patrons about what is monitored and why.

Surveillance technologies should serve narrow, justified purposes. When in doubt, favor privacy over additional monitoring.

### Align Policy With Applicable Law And Professional Ethics

The policy must reflect both legal obligations and professional ethical standards, which sometimes exceed legal minimums.

Legal and ethical alignment:

- identify applicable law, including state library confidentiality statutes and data protection law such as GDPR where relevant;
- recognize that professional ethics may require more protection than law mandates;
- do not use legal minimums as an excuse for weaker practice;
- consult legal counsel for interpretation, but retain professional ethical judgment;
- note where law and ethics align and where ethics goes further.

A policy that only meets legal minimums under-protects patrons. Professional ethics often demand more.

### Make The Policy Enforceable And Match Practice

A policy that is not enforced, or that contradicts daily practice, is worse than no policy because it creates false assurance.

Enforceability:

- ensure technical systems are configured to match policy, such as retention settings;
- train staff on policy and confidentiality obligations;
- build compliance checks into workflows;
- assign responsibility for policy maintenance and enforcement;
- audit practice against policy periodically;
- revise policy when practice or technology changes.

A gap between policy and practice is a liability. Close it from both directions: adjust practice to policy, and revise policy to be realistic.

### Be Transparent With Patrons

Patrons should understand what data is collected and how it is protected. Transparency builds trust.

Transparency:

- publish the privacy policy in clear, accessible language;
- explain what is and is not collected and retained;
- inform patrons of surveillance technologies and their purposes;
- describe patron rights regarding their data;
- provide ways for patrons to manage their privacy;
- avoid opaque practices that breed suspicion.

Honest communication about privacy, including its limits, strengthens the patron relationship.

### Plan For Disclosure Demands In Advance

The policy should anticipate requests for patron information and define the response process. See the responding-to-information-requests skill for depth, but the policy must establish the baseline.

Disclosure policy baseline:

- patron records are released only through proper legal process;
- staff do not voluntarily disclose records without authorization;
- the process requires legal counsel review;
- patrons are notified where legally permitted;
- all requests and responses are documented.

Establishing this in policy prevents ad hoc, panic-driven responses when a demand arrives.

## Escalation Guidance

Privacy decisions can carry legal, political, and safety consequences. Escalate in these situations:

- Any law enforcement or government request for patron records, regardless of apparent authority: escalate immediately to library leadership and legal counsel. Do not respond or disclose on your own.
- Any request that arrives without proper legal process, such as a verbal or informal ask: escalate; do not comply voluntarily.
- Any conflict between a surveillance or security demand and patron privacy: escalate to leadership to weigh chilling effects before adopting the technology.
- Any suspected breach of patron data: escalate immediately to leadership, security, and legal counsel per the incident response plan.
- Any external pressure, political or public, to weaken privacy protections: escalate to leadership and, where appropriate, professional organizations for support.
- Any uncertainty about legal obligations: escalate to legal counsel before acting. Do not guess.
- Any staff conduct that violates confidentiality: escalate to supervisors for corrective action and retraining.

When in doubt, escalate and document. Privacy errors are hard to undo.

## Common Traps

### Privacy As Compliance Checkbox

Treating privacy as a legal formality rather than a condition of intellectual freedom leads to weak, unenforced policy. Ground it in principle.

### Vague Collection And Retention Language

Policies that do not specify what is collected and how long it is kept hide over-collection and indefinite retention. Be specific.

### Policy That Contradicts Practice

A policy unenforced or contradicted by system settings creates false assurance. Match technical configuration and staff behavior to policy.

### Adopting Surveillance Without Weighing Chilling Effects

Cameras and monitoring have real costs to the right to read. Evaluate chilling effects before adoption.

### Using Legal Minimums As The Ceiling

Professional ethics often demand more than law. Do not let legal minimums justify weak practice.

### Voluntary Disclosure Under Pressure

Handing over records without proper legal process betrays the professional duty. Require legal process and counsel review.

### Opaque Practices

Hidden surveillance and data practices breed suspicion and erode trust. Be transparent with patrons.

### Static Policy

Law, technology, and threats change. A policy that is not reviewed and updated becomes stale and ineffective.

## Self-Check

- Is the policy grounded in the right to read privately as a condition of intellectual freedom, not merely compliance?
- Does it specifically enumerate what data is collected and the legitimate purpose for each type, committing to minimum necessary collection?
- Does it explicitly protect the confidentiality of circulation, reference, and computer use, and bind all staff?
- Are retention limits defined, short, enforced, and automated for each data type, with no indefinite retention?
- Are surveillance technologies, including cameras and RFID, addressed with narrow purposes and transparency to patrons?
- Is the policy aligned with applicable law and professional ethics, recognizing where ethics exceeds legal minimums?
- Is the policy matched by actual system configuration, staff training, and audited practice, not just words on paper?
- Is the policy published in clear, accessible language so patrons understand what is collected and protected?
- Does the policy establish a baseline for disclosure demands requiring legal process and counsel review?
- Are escalation paths defined for law enforcement requests, breaches, surveillance demands, and legal uncertainty, with documentation of actions taken?
