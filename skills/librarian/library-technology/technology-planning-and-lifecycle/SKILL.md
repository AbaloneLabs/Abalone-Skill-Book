# Technology Planning And Lifecycle

Library technology planning is the discipline of deciding what technology the library will invest in, when, and for how long, and it is where strategic intent meets fiscal reality. The danger is that technology planning becomes either reactive, buying what breaks or what a vendor promotes, or aspirational, writing plans that list desired technology without budgets, timelines, or lifecycle commitments. Both fail. Reactive planning produces a fragmented technology portfolio with unpredictable costs and frequent failures; aspirational planning produces documents that guide no decisions. Good technology planning is lifecycle-oriented: it treats every system and device as having a finite life with acquisition, operation, and replacement costs, and it plans investment against service priorities and a realistic budget horizon. Agents tend to focus on acquisition and to under-plan operation and replacement, ignoring that the total cost and risk of technology is dominated by the years after purchase. A technology plan that does not account for end-of-life is a plan for future crises.

Use this skill when developing technology plans, managing system and hardware lifecycles, planning technology budgets, or conducting technology assessments. The goal is to prevent the agent from planning acquisition without lifecycle, ignoring total cost of ownership, buying on vendor promotion rather than need, or writing plans that cannot guide real investment decisions.

## Core Rules

### Plan Technology As Lifecycle, Not As Acquisition

Every piece of library technology, from servers and staff computers to the ILS and discovery layer, has a lifecycle: acquisition, deployment, operation, maintenance, and eventual replacement. Planning only the acquisition ignores the majority of the cost and all of the risk. Lifecycle planning treats technology as a continuing obligation with predictable stages and costs, enabling rational budgeting and avoiding the crisis of unexpected end-of-life.

Plan the full lifecycle:

- define the expected useful life of each technology class, which varies: staff computers 4-6 years, servers 5-7 years, major systems 7-10 years;
- budget for operation and maintenance across the life, not just purchase;
- plan replacement before end-of-life, so replacement is scheduled, not emergency;
- account for the costs of deployment, training, data migration, and decommissioning, not just the sticker price;
- recognize that extending life beyond useful limits increases failure risk and support cost.

Lifecycle planning converts technology from a series of surprises into a predictable investment pattern.

### Base The Plan On Service Priorities, Not On Technology Trends

Technology investment should serve the library's service priorities, not the pursuit of new technology for its own sake. A technology plan that starts from what is new or what vendors promote produces investment disconnected from what patrons and staff need. The plan should begin with the services the library provides and the technology required to deliver them, then evaluate technology options against those requirements.

Ground planning in service:

- identify the services the library prioritizes and the technology each requires;
- assess where current technology enables or impedes those services;
- evaluate new technology against specific service needs, not general capability;
- resist vendor-driven investment that does not map to a service priority;
- involve service staff in technology assessment, because they know what helps and hinders service;
- be willing to conclude that no new technology is needed when current technology serves priorities.

Technology that does not advance a service priority is a cost without a benefit, however impressive its features.

### Budget Total Cost Of Ownership Across The Lifecycle

The purchase price of technology is a minority of its total cost; operation, maintenance, support, training, integration, and eventual replacement dominate. Technology budgets that consider only acquisition under-fund the reality of running the technology and create pressure to extend life unsustainably or to cut support. Total cost of ownership (TCO) must be the budgeting frame.

Budget TCO:

- include staffing for administration, support, and maintenance, often the largest cost;
- include infrastructure costs: hosting, storage, network, and power;
- include integration and customization costs, which recur with each system;
- include training for staff and ongoing professional development;
- include replacement cost, accrued across the useful life so funds exist at end-of-life;
- include the cost of decommissioning and data migration at replacement.

A technology budget that covers only acquisition will be exceeded every year by the costs it ignored.

### Schedule Replacement Before End-Of-Life

End-of-life, when a vendor stops supporting a system or hardware reaches unreliable age, is a deadline, and missing it creates risk: unsupported software with security vulnerabilities, failing hardware with service disruption, and emergency replacement under pressure. Replacement should be scheduled before end-of-life, with analysis, procurement, and migration completed in advance. A replacement schedule, maintained as part of the technology plan, prevents end-of-life from becoming a crisis.

Schedule replacement:

- track the support status and useful life of each system and hardware class;
- begin replacement planning 12-24 months before end-of-life, depending on complexity;
- sequence replacements to avoid simultaneous major migrations that strain capacity;
- budget replacement as a scheduled event, not a surprise;
- have contingency for early end-of-life, when vendors discontinue support unexpectedly.

Systems run to failure because no one planned their replacement; a replacement schedule is the antidote.

### Conduct Needs Analysis Before Procurement

Procurement should follow needs analysis, not precede it. Buying technology and then figuring out how to use it, or selecting based on vendor demos rather than documented requirements, produces mismatched investments. Needs analysis establishes what the library requires, in functional and technical terms, and procurement evaluates options against those requirements.

Conduct needs analysis:

- define the functional requirements based on service priorities and staff and patron needs;
- define technical requirements: integration, authentication, accessibility, data standards, performance;
- identify constraints: budget, staffing, timeline, existing infrastructure;
- use the requirements to evaluate options, including the option of improving current technology;
- involve stakeholders who will use or support the technology in defining requirements.

Needs analysis prevents the common failure of buying capable technology that does not fit the library's actual needs.

### Manage Risk And Continuity In Technology Planning

Library technology supports critical services, and its failure disrupts access, circulation, and research. Technology planning must account for continuity risk: what happens when a system fails, a vendor goes out of business, or data is lost. Planning that ignores continuity treats availability as assumed rather than designed.

Plan for continuity:

- identify critical systems and their acceptable downtime;
- ensure backups and recovery for data, with tested restore procedures;
- evaluate vendor viability and have contingency for vendor failure or acquisition;
- avoid single points of failure in infrastructure where feasible;
- plan for security, including patching, access control, and incident response;
- document recovery procedures so they survive staff turnover.

Technology that supports critical services without continuity planning is a service risk masquerading as a service enabler.

### Align The Plan With Governance And Budget Cycles

A technology plan that is not aligned with the institution's governance and budget cycles cannot be funded or approved. The plan must fit the institution's planning rhythm: capital and operating budget cycles, governance approval processes, and strategic planning horizons. A plan written in isolation from these cycles is aspirational; one written to fit them is actionable.

Align with governance and budget:

- time the plan to inform budget development, not to follow it;
- structure investments to fit capital versus operating budget distinctions;
- present major investments with the governance approval they require;
- connect the plan to the strategic plan so technology investment is seen as strategic;
- revisit the plan on the institution's planning cycle, because technology and priorities change.

### Build Staff Capacity For Planned Technology

Technology requires staff to implement, operate, and support it, and planning that ignores staff capacity produces technology the library cannot sustain. Each significant technology investment should be assessed for the staffing it requires, and capacity should be built or procured as part of the plan. The most common technology failure is not the technology but the absence of someone to run it.

Plan staff capacity:

- assess the staffing required to operate and support each technology, honestly;
- include training and professional development in the plan and budget;
- recognize where current staff lack capacity and plan to build or obtain it;
- avoid technology that requires capacity the library cannot sustain;
- plan for the institutional knowledge that staff hold, documenting to survive turnover.

## Common Traps

### Acquisition-Centric Planning

Planning purchases without operation, maintenance, and replacement produces unsustainable technology. This is a trap because the costs ignored dominate the lifecycle.

### Trend-Driven Investment

Buying new technology because it is promoted rather than because it serves a service priority. This is a trap because the investment lacks a service justification.

### Ignoring Total Cost Of Ownership

Budgeting only purchase price under-funds the reality of running technology. This is a trap because the budget is exceeded every year by ignored costs.

### Running Systems To Failure

Extending technology past useful life without replacement planning creates crises. This is a trap because end-of-life arrives as an emergency.

### Procurement Before Needs Analysis

Buying based on demos rather than documented requirements produces mismatched technology. This is a trap because the technology does not fit actual needs.

### Continuity Assumed Rather Than Designed

Treating availability as given rather than planning for failure and recovery. This is a trap because the assumption breaks under real failure.

### Plans Disconnected From Budget Cycles

Writing plans that do not fit governance and budget rhythms cannot be funded. This is a trap because the plan is aspirational, not actionable.

### Technology Without Staff Capacity

Investing in technology the library cannot operate or support. This is a trap because the technology fails for lack of people, not capability.

## Self-Check

- Does the plan address the full lifecycle (acquisition, operation, maintenance, replacement) of each technology class?
- Is technology investment grounded in service priorities and stakeholder needs rather than trends or vendor promotion?
- Does the budget reflect total cost of ownership, including staffing, infrastructure, integration, training, and replacement accrual?
- Is replacement scheduled before end-of-life, with a maintained replacement schedule and contingency for early end-of-life?
- Does procurement follow documented needs analysis with functional and technical requirements?
- Are critical systems assessed for continuity risk, with backups, tested recovery, vendor viability contingency, and security planning?
- Is the plan aligned with governance approval and budget cycles so it can be funded and implemented?
- Is staff capacity for operation and support assessed, with training and documentation planned?
- Is the plan revisited on the institution's planning cycle to reflect changing technology and priorities?
