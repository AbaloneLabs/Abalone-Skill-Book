# Vendor Management And Licensing

Library vendor management and licensing is where the library's service ambitions meet the commercial terms under which content and systems are provided, and it is one of the highest-stakes and least visible areas of library operations. The danger is that licensing and vendor management are treated as administrative procurement rather than strategic work: licenses are signed with terms the library does not fully understand, vendors are managed reactively only when something breaks, and the cumulative effect is a portfolio of resources and systems whose terms, costs, and dependencies the library cannot control. Agents tend to focus on the acquisition and price and to under-attend to the license terms that govern use, access, and rights; to the total cost and dependency that accumulate across renewals; and to the vendor performance and exit considerations that determine whether the relationship serves the library. A library that does not manage its vendors and licenses strategically ends up over-paying, under-served, and locked into terms that constrain service and access. This is also a domain where privacy and accessibility obligations intersect with vendor terms, requiring careful attention.

Use this skill when managing vendor relationships, negotiating or evaluating licenses, conducting procurement, or handling renewals. The goal is to prevent the agent from signing licenses without understanding terms, managing vendors reactively, ignoring total cost and lock-in, or treating licensing as procurement rather than strategic stewardship of access and rights.

## Core Rules

### Read And Understand License Terms Before Signing

The license governs what the library and its users may do with a resource, and its terms have consequences far beyond price. Licenses define authorized users, permitted uses, interlibrary loan rights, course reserves rights, text and data mining rights, archival and perpetual access rights, and the library's liability. Signing a license without understanding these terms cedes control over how the resource can be used and can expose the library to breach. License review is a core professional responsibility, not a formality.

Review license terms for:

- authorized users and sites: who may access the resource and from where;
- permitted uses: printing, downloading, course reserves, interlibrary loan, and any conditions or prohibitions;
- text and data mining rights: whether mining is permitted and under what conditions, increasingly important;
- perpetual and archival access: what the library retains if it cancels, and the mechanism for post-cancellation access;
- warranty and indemnification: who bears liability for content errors or infringement claims;
- privacy and data: what user data the vendor collects and how it is used, a growing concern;
- termination and exit: how the license ends and what happens to access and data.

Maintain a license file with the signed terms for each resource, because the terms govern operations and must be discoverable when questions arise.

### Negotiate Strategically, Not Just On Price

License negotiation is an opportunity to shape terms to serve the library and its users, and focusing only on price leaves value on the table. Vendors expect negotiation on terms such as authorized users, interlibrary loan, perpetual access, and data privacy, and libraries that negotiate achieve better terms than those that accept the vendor's standard license. Negotiation is most effective when the library knows its alternatives, including consortial options and the resource's importance to the vendor's revenue.

Negotiate strategically:

- identify the terms that matter most to the library and its users, beyond price;
- use license review tools and consortial expertise to identify non-standard or unfavorable terms;
- leverage the library's position, including consortial membership and the resource's importance;
- seek favorable terms on perpetual access, ILL, TDM, and privacy, which protect long-term value;
- be willing to walk away or substitute when terms are unacceptable, which requires knowing alternatives;
- coordinate with consortial partners to amplify negotiating power.

A license accepted as-is often contains terms that constrain service; negotiation is where those constraints are shaped.

### Manage Total Cost And Price Increases Across Renewals

The listed price of a resource is only part of its cost, and unchecked annual increases compound significantly over time. A resource affordable at acquisition can become unaffordable after years of increases, especially when increases exceed the library's budget growth. Total cost management requires tracking price history, projecting future cost, and making cancellation or renegotiation decisions before cost overwhelms value.

Manage total cost:

- track price history for each resource, including percentage increases year over year;
- project future cost based on increase patterns, to anticipate unsustainable trajectories;
- evaluate cost per use, not just absolute cost, because an expensive resource with high use may be good value;
- benchmark prices against consortial and peer deals to identify overpricing;
- trigger cancellation or renegotiation reviews when cost exceeds value thresholds;
- coordinate cancellations with faculty and stakeholders to manage the service impact.

Cost that is not tracked and managed grows until it forces crisis cancellations, which are more disruptive than planned ones.

### Assess And Manage Vendor Lock-In Risk

Many library resources and systems create lock-in: the cost and disruption of switching to an alternative is so high that the library is effectively captive, which weakens its negotiating position and its control. Lock-in is not always avoidable, but it must be assessed and managed, because a locked-in library cannot negotiate effectively, respond to poor vendor performance, or adapt to changing needs. Lock-in assessment should inform both selection and ongoing management.

Assess and manage lock-in:

- evaluate lock-in at selection: how hard would it be to switch, and does the vendor support export and migration;
- prefer vendors that support standards-based export and interoperability, which reduce lock-in;
- negotiate exit and data portability terms in contracts, including data return formats;
- avoid building critical workflows on proprietary formats or APIs without an exit path;
- monitor lock-in over the relationship, because it deepens as the library invests;
- maintain awareness of alternatives so that switching remains a credible option.

Lock-in that is not assessed becomes leverage the vendor holds over the library; managing it preserves the library's agency.

### Monitor Vendor Performance And Compliance

Vendor management does not end at signing; it requires ongoing monitoring of performance and compliance with license terms. Vendors may deliver less than promised: uptime below guarantees, content gaps, slow support, or terms that drift from the agreed license. Without monitoring, these issues accumulate and the library pays for service it does not fully receive. Performance monitoring also provides evidence for negotiation and renewal decisions.

Monitor performance and compliance:

- track uptime and availability against service level guarantees, and pursue remedies for shortfalls;
- monitor content completeness against the licensed scope, because coverage gaps are common;
- track support responsiveness and issue resolution;
- verify compliance with license terms, including any usage restrictions and privacy commitments;
- gather feedback from users and staff on resource quality and vendor support;
- use performance evidence in renewal and renegotiation.

Vendors manage themselves when they know the library is watching; unmonitored vendors drift toward their own convenience.

### Protect User Privacy In Vendor Relationships

Library eresources and systems often collect data about user behavior, and vendors may use that data in ways that conflict with the library's privacy obligations to its users. License terms and vendor practices around data collection, retention, and use are a privacy concern that the library must address, not defer to the vendor. Privacy is a high-risk area where inattention harms users, and escalation is appropriate for uncertain terms.

Protect privacy:

- review license and vendor terms for what user data is collected, retained, and shared;
- negotiate to minimize data collection and to prohibit sale or secondary use of user data;
- prefer vendors with transparent privacy practices and compliance with relevant privacy law;
- inform users when their data may be collected by third parties, respecting transparency;
- escalate privacy terms that are unclear or unfavorable to appropriate governance, because the risk is to users;
- revisit privacy terms at renewal, because vendor practices change.

Privacy obligations run from the library to its users and cannot be delegated to vendors whose interests differ; the library remains accountable.

### Ensure Accessibility In Vendor Selection And Contracts

Library eresources and systems must be accessible to users with disabilities, and accessibility is both an ethical and often a legal obligation. Vendor products vary widely in accessibility, and relying on vendor assertions without verification exposes the library to providing inaccessible service. Accessibility must be evaluated in selection and addressed in contracts.

Ensure accessibility:

- evaluate vendor products against accessibility standards such as WCAG during selection;
- request and verify Voluntary Product Accessibility Templates (VPATs) rather than accepting assertions;
- include accessibility requirements and remedies in contracts, including the right to require remediation;
- test products with real users and assistive technologies where feasible;
- prefer vendors with demonstrated accessibility commitment and ongoing remediation;
- escalate accessibility failures, because providing inaccessible service may breach obligations.

Accessibility is a requirement, not a feature; vendor products that fail it should not be selected without a remediation path.

### Coordinate With Consortia And Peer Institutions

Few libraries negotiate vendor relationships in isolation, and consortial and peer coordination amplifies the library's position. Consortia negotiate better terms, share intelligence on vendor practices, and provide alternatives that reduce lock-in. A library that does not engage its consortium leaves value and protection on the table.

Coordinate with consortia:

- participate in consortial licensing to achieve better terms and prices;
- share vendor performance and pricing intelligence with peers;
- use consortial deals as leverage and as alternatives in negotiation;
- contribute to consortial strategy so collective interests are represented;
- consider consortial alternatives when individual deals are unfavorable.

## Common Traps

### Signing Licenses Without Term Review

Accepting vendor licenses without understanding use, access, and rights terms cedes control. This is a trap because the terms govern operations and constrain service invisibly.

### Price-Only Negotiation

Focusing negotiation on price while accepting unfavorable use, access, and privacy terms. This is a trap because the non-price terms often carry greater long-term cost.

### Unmanaged Price Increases

Letting annual increases compound without tracking or review until cost forces crisis cancellation. This is a trap because the crisis is more disruptive than managed planning.

### Ignoring Lock-In Until Trapped

Selecting systems and resources without assessing exit difficulty, then discovering switching is prohibitive. This is a trap because the library loses negotiating agency.

### Reactive Vendor Management

Engaging vendors only when something breaks, allowing performance and compliance to drift. This is a trap because the library pays for service it does not receive.

### Deferring Privacy To Vendors

Allowing vendor data practices to override the library's privacy obligations to users. This is a trap because the harm falls on users and the library remains accountable.

### Accepting Accessibility Assertions

Selecting vendor products on accessibility claims without verification or contractual remedies. This is a trap because inaccessible service may breach obligations and excludes users.

### Going It Alone On Negotiation

Negotiating individually when consortial options offer better terms and protection. This is a trap because the library forgoes leverage it could have had.

## Self-Check

- Are license terms reviewed for authorized users, permitted uses, ILL, reserves, TDM, perpetual access, privacy, and exit before signing?
- Is negotiation strategic beyond price, leveraging the library's position and consortial power for favorable terms?
- Is total cost tracked across renewals, with price history, projections, cost per use, and cancellation triggers?
- Is vendor lock-in assessed at selection and managed through standards, export, and exit terms?
- Is vendor performance monitored for uptime, content completeness, support, and license compliance, with evidence used at renewal?
- Are user privacy terms reviewed, negotiated, and escalated where unclear or unfavorable?
- Is accessibility evaluated against WCAG, verified via VPATs, and addressed contractually with remediation rights?
- Does the library coordinate with consortia and peers for better terms, intelligence, and alternatives?
- Is a license file maintained so signed terms are discoverable for operational and compliance questions?
