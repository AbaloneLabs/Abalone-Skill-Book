# Designing Research Skills Sessions

Research-skills instruction fails most often at the design stage, not the delivery stage. A session that tries to cover everything, the catalog, three databases, evaluation, citation, and ethics, in fifty minutes teaches nothing that sticks. A session disconnected from the students' actual assignment teaches skills they will never apply. A session built around the librarian demonstrating while students watch teaches watching, not researching. Good instruction design is ruthless prioritization: a few outcomes students will actually achieve, anchored to a real task, with enough active practice that the skills transfer. The design decisions made before the session matter more than any charisma during it.

Use this skill before teaching any research-skills session, whether a one-shot, a workshop, a credit course, or an embedded module. The goal is to prevent the agent from overloading the session, from teaching abstract skills detached from a real assignment, and from defaulting to demonstration when active learning is what builds skill.

## Core Rules

### Start From Learning Outcomes, Not A Topic List

The first design decision is what students will be able to do by the end. Outcomes drive everything else: activities, examples, assessment, and what to cut. Without outcomes, a session becomes a tour of features the librarian finds interesting.

Write outcomes that are:

- specific and observable (students will formulate a research question; students will combine concepts with Boolean operators);
- achievable in the time available;
- aligned to the assignment or real task students face;
- focused on transferable skills, not tool-specific button locations that will change;
- limited in number, usually two to four for a single session.

If you cannot state what students will do differently after the session, you are not ready to design it.

### Anchor The Session To A Real Assignment Or Task

Research skills taught in the abstract do not transfer. Students learn when the skills serve a task they actually face: their current assignment, a real research question, a genuine information need. Build the session around that task.

Anchoring means:

- coordinating with the course instructor on the assignment in advance;
- using the assignment's topic, source requirements, and deadlines as the context;
- designing activities around students' actual research questions where possible;
- avoiding generic placeholder topics that bear no relation to students' work.

When no assignment exists, create a realistic simulated task rather than teaching skills in a vacuum.

### Ruthlessly Prioritize For The Time Available

Time is the binding constraint, especially in one-shot sessions. Trying to cover the entire research process guarantees shallow coverage of everything. Choose the few outcomes that matter most and teach them deeply.

Prioritization principles:

- prefer fewer outcomes taught well to many outcomes taught superficially;
- front-load the highest-value skill (often question definition or source evaluation);
- cut anything that does not directly serve an outcome;
- defer advanced topics to follow-up sessions, guides, or consultations;
- accept that one excellent session beats three rushed ones.

A session that teaches students to define a searchable question and evaluate one source deeply is more valuable than one that flashes through ten tools.

### Balance Demonstration With Active Practice

Watching a search is not the same as doing one. Skills build through practice with feedback. Design sessions so students spend substantial time doing, not watching.

Aim for active learning:

- demonstrate briefly, then have students try immediately;
- use a real or realistic search problem;
- circulate to coach and correct as students work;
- have students compare and discuss their results;
- build in a reflection step on what worked and what did not.

Demonstration has a place, for showing non-obvious features or modeling expert thinking, but it should be short and immediately followed by practice.

### Sequence Activities To Build Toward The Outcome

Sequence matters. Skills that depend on earlier skills must come after them. A logical build keeps students oriented and prevents the confusion of jumping between unrelated steps.

A typical sequence:

- connect to students' prior knowledge or current struggle;
- introduce the outcome and why it matters;
- demonstrate or model the skill with thinking aloud;
- guided practice with coaching;
- independent or small-group practice;
- debrief and reflection;
- bridge to how students will apply it in their assignment.

Each step should visibly serve the outcome. Cut steps that do not.

### Plan For Different Skill Levels In The Room

Students arrive with widely varying prior experience. A session pitched at the middle loses both the novices and the advanced students. Plan differentiation.

Differentiation strategies:

- diagnostic questions or a quick pre-task to gauge the room;
- layered activities with basic and stretch versions;
- peer support pairing more and less experienced students;
- advanced challenges or resources for fast finishers;
- follow-up options (consultations, guides) for those who need more.

### Design Assessment And Feedback Into The Session

Instruction without assessment is unverified. Build in ways to see whether students achieved the outcomes, both for their benefit and to improve future sessions.

Assessment options:

- a short applied task at the end (formulate a search; evaluate a source);
- a ticket-out-the-door reflection or question;
- observation and coaching during practice;
- a quick poll or quiz;
- follow-up data on assignment quality or later consultations.

Use the assessment to adjust in the moment where possible, and to revise the session design afterward.

### Prepare For The Things That Will Go Wrong

Sessions rarely go as planned. Technology fails, the room is wrong, the instructor is absent, students arrive unprepared, or the time is cut. Build resilience.

Resilience planning:

- have a low-tech backup for any technology-dependent activity;
- know which activities are essential and which can be cut if time shrinks;
- prepare a condensed version for shortened sessions;
- have extension activities if time expands or students finish early;
- confirm room setup, software access, and materials in advance.

### Bridge To Independent Application And Follow-Up

A single session cannot build durable skill alone. Design the session to connect to what comes after: a research guide, a consultation service, a follow-up session, or an embedded presence in the course.

Build bridges by:

- pointing to a tailored research guide for the assignment;
- showing how to get help (chat, consultation, desk);
- offering to return for a follow-up at a later research stage;
- coordinating with the instructor on reinforcement.

## Common Traps

### Covering Everything Superficially

Breadth without depth teaches nothing that sticks. Prioritize ruthlessly.

### Teaching Abstract Skills Without An Assignment

Skills taught in a vacuum do not transfer. Anchor to a real task.

### Demonstration-Heavy Sessions

Watching is not doing. Build in active practice with feedback.

### Too Many Outcomes

Two to four outcomes are realistic for one session. More dilutes learning.

### Ignoring Mixed Skill Levels

A session pitched at the middle loses both ends. Differentiate.

### No Assessment Or Feedback Loop

Without checking learning, you cannot improve the session or confirm outcomes.

### No Backup Plan

Technology and timing fail. Build resilience into the design.

### Treating The Session As Standalone

One session cannot build durable skill alone. Bridge to follow-up support.

## Self-Check

- Did you write specific, observable, achievable learning outcomes aligned to a real assignment or task?
- Is the session anchored to students' actual work rather than abstract skills?
- Did you ruthlessly prioritize two to four outcomes for the time available?
- Is the session balanced toward active practice with coaching rather than demonstration?
- Is the activity sequence logical, each step serving the outcome?
- Did you plan for different skill levels with layered or differentiated activities?
- Is assessment and feedback built in to verify learning and improve the session?
- Did you prepare backups for technology, timing, and room failures?
- Did you bridge the session to independent application, guides, consultations, and follow-up?
