# Assessing Research Skills Learning

Teaching without assessment is unverified instruction. The librarian delivers a session, students seem engaged, and everyone leaves uncertain whether any skill was actually acquired. Assessment closes that loop: it reveals what students learned, exposes where instruction failed, and provides evidence to improve future sessions and to demonstrate value to stakeholders. Yet assessment is often skipped in library instruction because sessions are short, because grades are not always the librarian's to assign, and because designing good assessment is hard. The result is instruction that cannot improve because no one knows whether it worked. Assessment is not an add-on; it is the feedback mechanism that makes instruction a craft rather than a performance.

Use this skill whenever you teach research or information-literacy skills and need to know whether learning happened, when you design assessment for a session or course, and when you use results to improve instruction. The goal is to prevent the agent from teaching without any measure of learning, from relying on satisfaction surveys as evidence of skill, and from collecting assessment data that never feeds back into design.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Formative From Summative Assessment

The two assessment types serve different purposes, and conflating them produces poor design.

- formative assessment happens during learning, is low-stakes, and aims to adjust instruction in the moment or improve the next session;
- summative assessment happens after learning, measures achievement against outcomes, and supports grading or reporting.

Library instruction leans heavily on formative assessment because sessions are short and grades often belong to the course instructor. Use summative assessment where you control a grade or need to report outcomes, and formative everywhere else. Both should map directly to the stated learning outcomes.

### Align Assessment To Learning Outcomes

Assessment measures whether outcomes were achieved. If the outcome is "students will formulate a searchable research question," the assessment must require students to formulate one, not answer a multiple-choice question about what a research question is. Misalignment produces assessment that measures the wrong thing.

For each outcome, ask:

- what would a student do to demonstrate this outcome?;
- what task makes that demonstration visible?;
- what criteria distinguish strong from weak performance?;
- can this be assessed in the time and format available?

If you cannot design an assessment that maps to the outcome, the outcome may need revision.

### Prefer Authentic, Performance-Based Assessment

Research skills are performed, not recalled. The strongest assessment asks students to actually do the skill: run a search, evaluate a source, build a citation, synthesize a small set of sources. Performance-based assessment captures capability that recall-based tests miss.

Authentic assessment tasks:

- have students run a real search and report the strategy and results;
- have students evaluate a provided source against criteria and justify the judgment;
- have students produce a correct citation from a real source;
- have students map a topic into concepts and synonyms;
- have students locate and assess a source for their own assignment.

Performance tasks take more time to design and score, but they measure what matters.

### Use Rubrics To Make Judgment Consistent And Transparent

Evaluating a search, a source evaluation, or a citation involves judgment. Rubrics make that judgment consistent across students and scorers, and they communicate expectations to students in advance.

A good rubric:

- states the criteria derived from the outcome;
- describes performance levels for each criterion;
- is shared with students before they attempt the task;
- is brief enough to use at scale;
- distinguishes genuine skill from surface compliance.

Avoid rubrics so detailed they become checklists that reward formatting over thinking.

### Build In Quick Formative Checks During The Session

Short formative checks during a session reveal in real time whether students are with you, so you can adjust rather than discover the gap at the end.

Quick formative tools:

- a poll or show of hands on a diagnostic question;
- a one-minute paper on the muddiest point;
- observation and coaching during practice;
- a quick applied task (build one search term set) reviewed on the spot;
- a ticket-out-the-door (one thing learned, one question remaining).

Use the results immediately: re-explain what was muddy, extend what landed, adjust the next activity.

### Avoid Confusing Satisfaction With Learning

End-of-session satisfaction surveys, "was this session useful?", measure how students felt, not what they learned. Students rate entertaining sessions highly even when they learned little, and rate challenging sessions poorly even when they learned a lot. Satisfaction data has a place, but it is not evidence of skill acquisition.

Pair any satisfaction measure with a performance measure. "Students reported the session was useful, and 80 percent correctly evaluated a provided source" is meaningful; "students reported the session was useful" alone is not.

### Assess Transfer, Not Just In-Session Performance

The goal of research-skills instruction is transfer: students using the skills later, in their own work, without the librarian present. In-session assessment is a proxy; true assessment of transfer happens later, in the assignment.

Ways to assess transfer:

- coordinate with the course instructor to examine assignment quality on the targeted skills;
- offer and track follow-up consultations that show students applying the skills;
- use a delayed post-assessment weeks after the session;
- review later work for evidence of the taught skills.

Transfer data is harder to collect but far more meaningful than immediate post-session measures.

### Use Results To Improve Instruction

Assessment data that is not used to improve instruction is wasted effort. Build a cycle: assess, analyze what the data reveals about the instruction's strengths and gaps, revise the session design, and reassess.

Improvement uses:

- common errors revealed by assessment, signaling what to teach differently;
- outcomes consistently unmet, signaling redesign needs;
- differences across sections or instructors, signaling what works;
- trends over time, signaling drift or improvement.

Treat assessment as formative for the instructor and the program, not only for the students.

### Report Outcomes To Stakeholders Responsibly

Assessment evidence supports reporting to stakeholders: department chairs, deans, accreditation bodies, funders. Report honestly, including what did not work and what is being improved. Cherry-picked success metrics erode credibility when the underlying instruction is weak; honest reporting of gaps and improvements builds it.

## Common Traps

### Teaching Without Any Assessment

Without measuring learning, instruction cannot improve and its value is unverified.

### Satisfaction Surveys As Proof Of Learning

Feeling useful is not the same as acquiring skill. Pair satisfaction with performance.

### Assessment Misaligned To Outcomes

Testing recall when the outcome is performance measures the wrong thing.

### Recall-Based Tests For Performance Skills

Research skills are performed. Use authentic performance tasks.

### Rubrics So Detailed They Become Checklists

Over-specified rubrics reward formatting over thinking. Keep them focused on criteria.

### Collecting Data That Never Feeds Back

Assessment unused for improvement is wasted. Close the loop into design.

### Assessing Only In-Session Performance

In-session success is a proxy. Seek evidence of transfer to later work.

### Cherry-Picked Reporting

Honest reporting of gaps and improvements builds more credibility than curated successes.

## Self-Check

- Did you distinguish formative and summative assessment and use each for its purpose?
- Is every assessment aligned to a specific learning outcome, measuring performance not just recall?
- Are assessment tasks authentic, asking students to perform the actual skill?
- Are rubrics clear, criterion-based, shared in advance, and focused on thinking over formatting?
- Are quick formative checks built into the session to adjust in real time?
- Is satisfaction data paired with performance data rather than used as proof of learning?
- Is there a plan to assess transfer to later work, not just in-session performance?
- Are assessment results analyzed and used to revise session and program design?
- Is outcome reporting to stakeholders honest about gaps and improvements?
