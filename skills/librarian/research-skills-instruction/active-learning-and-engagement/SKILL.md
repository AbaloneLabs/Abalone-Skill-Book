# Active Learning And Engagement

Lecturing about research skills is the easiest teaching mode and one of the least effective. Students listen, nod, take notes, and leave unable to perform the skill. Active learning, where students do the work in structured ways with feedback, produces dramatically better retention and transfer, especially for skills like searching, evaluating, and synthesizing that cannot be learned by hearing about them. The barrier to active learning is not ignorance of its value; it is the design effort, the classroom-management challenge, and the risk of student resistance. Librarians who master active learning turn one-shot sessions from forgettable feature tours into experiences that change how students research. Those who do not default back to demonstration and wonder why nothing sticks.

Use this skill whenever you are teaching research or information-literacy skills and want students to actually acquire them. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to lecture and demonstration, from designing activities that are busywork rather than learning, and from mishandling the participation and resistance that active learning surfaces.

## Core Rules

### Make Students Do The Cognitive Work

The core principle of active learning is that the learner, not the instructor, performs the cognitive work of the skill. Searching, evaluating, synthesizing, and citing are learned by doing them, with feedback, not by watching someone else do them.

Design so that students:

- formulate their own questions and searches;
- evaluate their own sources against criteria;
- make decisions and explain their reasoning;
- encounter and recover from mistakes;
- reflect on what worked and what did not.

If the instructor is doing all the thinking, the students are being entertained, not educated.

### Choose Activity Types That Match The Outcome

Different outcomes call for different activity types. Matching the activity to the skill prevents the mismatch of, say, lecturing about evaluation when students need to evaluate.

Common active-learning activity types:

- think-pair-share: students think individually, discuss with a partner, then share, good for generating questions and criteria;
- guided search: students run a search on a real topic while the librarian coaches;
- source evaluation stations: small groups evaluate different sources against criteria and report;
- problem-based inquiry: students work a realistic research problem start to finish;
- concept mapping: students map a topic into concepts and synonyms;
- compare-and-contrast: students compare search results, source types, or evaluations;
- jigsaw: groups master one source type or database and teach peers.

Choose the activity that makes students perform the target skill, not an adjacent one.

### Use Authentic Tasks And Real Stakes

Active learning lands hardest when the task is authentic, tied to students' real work, with real consequences. Generic placeholder topics ("research paper on a social issue") produce generic engagement. The students' own assignment, a live community question, or a realistic professional scenario produce investment.

Make tasks authentic by:

- using students' actual research questions where possible;
- tying the activity to the assignment's real source requirements and deadline;
- using real sources and real databases, not simulated ones;
- creating stakes, such as producing something they will use or present.

### Build In Feedback, Not Just Activity

Activity without feedback can entrench mistakes. The value of active learning is the loop: try, get feedback, adjust, try again. Design feedback into every activity.

Feedback sources:

- instructor coaching during practice (circulate, observe, correct);
- peer feedback through structured comparison and discussion;
- whole-class debriefs that surface common errors and insights;
- self-assessment against criteria or a model;
- immediate results (a search that returns better sources is its own feedback).

Aim for feedback that is specific, timely, and actionable, not just praise or generic correction.

### Manage Time And Pacing Deliberately

Active learning takes more time than lecture, which collides with the short sessions librarians often have. Plan pacing so activities fit and do not get cut.

Pacing tactics:

- time-box each activity and announce the time;
- keep demonstrations short to preserve practice time;
- have a cut plan if an activity runs long;
- use small-group work, which scales better than full-class discussion;
- resist the urge to fill every minute with talk; silence during work is productive.

### Handle Participation And Resistance

Active learning surfaces participation dynamics that lecture hides: the dominant student, the silent group, the resistant learner who would rather be told. Plan for these rather than being surprised.

Participation strategies:

- use structured protocols (think-pair-share, numbered roles) so everyone participates;
- call on volunteers broadly and avoid relying on the same few voices;
- normalize struggle and mistakes as part of learning;
- give think time before expecting responses;
- use small groups so quieter students contribute in lower-stakes settings.

For resistance, students who see library sessions as pointless or who feel put on the spot:

- connect the skill visibly to their assignment and grade;
- start low-stakes and build;
- acknowledge the time pressure students feel;
- avoid cold-calling in ways that embarrass.

### Scaffold Complex Skills

Skills like evaluating sources or building a search have multiple components. Teaching them all at once overwhelms. Scaffold by breaking the skill into steps and supporting each.

Scaffolding example for source evaluation:

- first, students identify the source type and author;
- then, they locate the evidence and check the date;
- then, they compare to another source;
- then, they make and justify an overall judgment;
- finally, the scaffold is removed and they evaluate independently.

### Use Technology To Enable, Not Replace, Activity

Technology can enable active learning (polling, collaborative documents, shared pads) or undermine it (endless demonstration, passive slides). Use technology that makes students do the work.

Useful technologies:

- live polling for diagnostics and opinions;
- shared documents for collaborative search or evaluation;
- screen sharing for students to demonstrate their process;
- annotation tools for marking up sources together.

Avoid technology that turns the session back into a lecture with fancier slides.

### Reflect And Debrief To Consolidate Learning

The debrief is where active learning consolidates. Students make their implicit learning explicit, see patterns across the class, and connect the activity to future application. Never skip the debrief to save time.

Debrief by:

- asking what worked, what surprised them, what was hard;
- surfacing common mistakes and correcting them;
- connecting the activity to their assignment;
- having students state how they will apply the skill.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Lecture And Demonstration

Easiest to prepare, least effective for skill-building. Make students do the work.

### Activity Without Feedback

Unreviewed practice can entrench errors. Build the feedback loop.

### Generic Placeholder Tasks

Abstract topics produce low engagement. Use authentic, real-stakes tasks.

### Mismatched Activity And Outcome

Choose the activity that makes students perform the target skill, not an adjacent one.

### Letting Dominant Students Carry The Room

Structured protocols ensure broad participation and surface quieter voices.

### Skipping The Debrief To Save Time

The debrief consolidates learning. Cutting it sacrifices retention.

### Cold-Calling That Embarrasses

Resistance grows when students feel exposed. Start low-stakes and build.

### Technology That Re-Creates Lecture

Fancy slides and long demos are still passive. Use tech that enables student work.

## Self-Check

- Does the design make students perform the cognitive work of the target skill?
- Is the activity type matched to the outcome (searching, evaluating, synthesizing)?
- Are tasks authentic, tied to students' real assignment or stakes?
- Is feedback built into every activity, specific, timely, and actionable?
- Is time and pacing planned so activities fit and demonstrations stay short?
- Are participation and resistance managed through structured, low-stakes protocols?
- Are complex skills scaffolded into supported steps before independent practice?
- Does technology enable student work rather than recreate lecture?
- Is there a debrief that consolidates learning and connects to future application?
