# Personal And Corporate Name Authorities

Authority control for names is what allows a catalog to gather everything by one creator under a single heading. Without it, the same author appears as "Tolstoy," "Tolstoi," "Tolstoj," and "Tolstaya," and their works scatter across the catalog. Establishing a name authority is not typing a name correctly; it is a research and judgment task that determines the authorized form, records the variants, distinguishes entities that share a name, and documents the evidence so the decision is defensible and reproducible. Names are unstable. People change their names, use pseudonyms, hold titles, and share identical names with unrelated people. Corporate bodies merge, split, rename, and nest inside one another. Good authority work resolves all of this into a stable authorized access point backed by documented evidence, so that collocation holds across the collection and across cooperative authority files.

Use this skill when establishing or revising a personal or corporate name authority, choosing the authorized form of name, handling variants and pseudonyms, disambiguating entities, or contributing to cooperative authority files. The goal is to prevent the agent from inventing authorized forms without evidence, conflating distinct entities, ignoring variant names that users search, or creating authority records that cannot be maintained or shared.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Authorized Form By The Prescribed Rules And Sources

The authorized form of a name is not the cataloger's preference. It is determined by the cataloging standard, RDA or AACR2, and the prescribed reference sources for each element.

For personal names, determine:

- the preferred name, the name by which the person is most commonly known;
- the form found in the person's own works, their native language, and reference sources;
- additions needed for identification, fullness of dates, title, or distinguishing terms;
- the prescribed order and capitalization for the entry element.

For corporate bodies, determine:

- the preferred name in the body's own language and form;
- the jurisdiction or higher body when needed for context;
- additions for conferences, dates, and subordinate bodies;
- changes of name over time as separate authorities.

Consult the prescribed reference sources, the LC Name Authority File, VIAF, national libraries, and the body's own publications. Do not guess the authorized form from the item in hand alone.

### Record All Variant Names As References

Users and other catalogs will use many forms of a name. The authority record must capture these variants so searches connect to the authorized form.

Variants to record:

- alternative spellings and romanizations;
- full forms versus common forms;
- pseudonyms and pen names;
- name changes due to marriage, gender transition, or choice;
- names in other languages and scripts;
- former and later names for corporate bodies;
- abbreviations and inverted forms.

Each variant becomes a see reference leading to the authorized heading. Missing variants means users who search that form find nothing.

### Distinguish Entities With The Same Name

Two different people or organizations can share an identical name. The authority record must distinguish them so their works do not collocate incorrectly.

Disambiguation tools:

- life or activity dates for persons;
- fuller form of name;
- titles of nobility, honor, or office;
- profession, occupation, or field of activity;
- affiliation or jurisdiction for corporate bodies;
- a distinguishing biographical or corporate note.

"John Smith, 1950-" and "John Smith, 1972-" are different people. Without dates or other additions, their works merge into one fictional entity, corrupting the catalog.

### Document The Basis For The Authorized Form

An authority decision must be defensible. Future catalogers, cooperative partners, and users need to know why this form was chosen.

Document:

- the reference sources consulted;
- the evidence for the preferred name and dates;
- the source of variant names;
- the reasoning for additions or distinguishing terms;
- any conflicting evidence and how it was resolved.

Cite sources in the authority record. An authorized form without a cited basis is an assertion that cannot be verified or trusted.

### Handle Name Changes As Separate But Linked Authorities

When a person or corporate body changes its name, the change may warrant a new authority rather than an edit to the old one.

Personal name changes:

- a person known under more than one name may need one authority with variants, or separate authorities linked, depending on the standard;
- pseudonyms used for distinct bodies of work may warrant separate authorities;
- name changes due to gender transition should be handled respectfully, using the person's current name with appropriate references.

Corporate name changes:

- a rename is generally a new corporate body for cataloging purposes, with a linking reference from the old name;
- mergers, splits, and absorptions each create relationship links between authorities;
- document the history in notes so the relationships are clear.

Do not overwrite an old authority when the entity has genuinely become a different one. Link them instead.

### Maintain Corporate Body Hierarchy And Jurisdiction

Corporate bodies often nest within larger bodies, and their authorized form may require the parent body or jurisdiction for context and uniqueness.

Structure:

- enter subordinate bodies under the higher body when prescribed;
- add the jurisdiction, country, state, for government bodies when needed;
- record direct order versus hierarchical order per the standard;
- handle conferences with their numbers, dates, and places;
- link related bodies through see-also references.

A "Department of Education" without its jurisdiction is ambiguous. The hierarchy and jurisdiction make the heading precise and unique.

### Contribute To And Use Cooperative Authority Files

Authority work is most valuable when shared. Cooperative files like the LC Name Authority File and VIAF collocate names across institutions.

Cooperative practice:

- search the cooperative file before creating a new authority;
- use existing authorities rather than creating local duplicates;
- propose new or revised authorities through the contribution process, SACO for LC;
- report errors in existing authorities rather than silently overriding locally;
- keep local authority records compatible with cooperative conventions.

Creating a local authority when a cooperative one exists fragments the bibliographic universe. Search first, contribute second, create locally only when necessary.

### Review And Update Authorities Over Time

Names and standards change. An authority file that is never reviewed decays.

Maintenance:

- apply standard revisions, such as RDA updates, to affected authorities;
- update authorities when new information resolves an old ambiguity;
- merge duplicate authorities created in error;
- revise offensive or outdated name forms as standards evolve;
- schedule periodic review of high-use or problematic authorities.

Authority control is ongoing, not a one-time task at cataloging.

## Common Traps

### Inventing The Authorized Form Without Evidence

Choosing a name form by preference rather than by reference sources produces an unverifiable authority. Cite sources for every decision.

### Conflating Entities That Share A Name

Without dates or distinguishing additions, distinct people or bodies merge into one. Always check for disambiguation needs.

### Missing Variant Names Users Would Search

A variant not recorded is a dead end for users who search it. Capture spellings, pseudonyms, and other-language forms.

### Overwriting A Name Change Instead Of Linking

A corporate rename or a distinct pseudonym body of work may need a separate linked authority. Do not collapse distinct entities.

### Creating Local Authorities That Duplicate Cooperative Files

Failing to search the cooperative file first creates duplicates that fragment collocation. Search before creating.

### Undocumented Authority Decisions

An authorized form without cited sources cannot be verified or maintained. Document the basis for every authority.

### Ignoring Corporate Hierarchy And Jurisdiction

A subordinate body without its parent or jurisdiction is ambiguous. Build the heading with the required context.

### Treating Authority Work As A One-Time Task

Standards and information change. Authorities need periodic review and update.

## Self-Check

- Is the authorized form of name chosen according to the prescribed standard and reference sources, not personal preference?
- Are all relevant variant names, spellings, pseudonyms, and other-language forms recorded as references?
- Are entities sharing a name distinguished by dates, fuller form, profession, or other identifying additions?
- Is the basis for the authorized form documented with cited reference sources and reasoning?
- Are name changes handled as separate linked authorities where appropriate, rather than overwritten?
- Do corporate body authorities include the required hierarchy, jurisdiction, and relationship links?
- Has the cooperative authority file been searched before creating a new authority, and are contributions made through the proper process?
- Are authorities reviewed and updated as standards evolve and new information becomes available?
