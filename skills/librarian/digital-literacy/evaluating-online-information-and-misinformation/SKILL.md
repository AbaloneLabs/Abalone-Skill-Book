# Evaluating Online Information and Misinformation

Misinformation is not a new problem, but the speed, volume, and persuasiveness of false and misleading content online have made information evaluation a core public-library competency. Patrons arrive believing a viral claim, a manipulated image, a conspiracy, or an AI-generated video, and they may act on it, share it, or lose trust in institutions because of it. The librarian's role is not to win arguments or to become the arbiter of truth, but to equip patrons with verification habits they can apply themselves, and to model a calm, curious, evidence-based stance that lowers the temperature. This work is delicate because it touches identity, politics, and emotion; done poorly, it feels like condescension or censorship and hardens the patron's false belief.

## Core Rules

### Adopt a stance of curiosity, not correction

When a patron brings a questionable claim, resist the urge to immediately declare it false, which triggers defensiveness and turns the exchange into a credibility contest. Instead, adopt genuine curiosity: ask where they saw it, what made it seem convincing, and what they hope to learn. Invite them to investigate alongside you rather than positioning yourself as the authority who will settle it. This collaborative stance keeps the patron engaged, models the verification process, and often leads the patron to recognize problems themselves, which is far more persuasive than being told. Curiosity also protects you when the claim turns out to be more nuanced than it first appeared.

### Teach lateral reading as the core verification habit

The single most powerful habit for evaluating online information is lateral reading: leaving the source itself and opening new tabs to investigate what other, trusted sources say about it. Professional fact-checkers do this instinctively; most patrons instead stay on the page and look for internal clues, which sophisticated manipulators know how to fake. Teach patrons to ask, before trusting a site, what the broader web says about its reputation, who is behind it, and whether credible outlets have covered the same claim. Lateral reading defeats most manipulation because false sources rarely survive cross-checking. Pair it with the SIFT method: stop, investigate the source, find better coverage, and trace claims to original context.

### Prioritize the most harmful categories of misinformation

Not all false content is equally worth addressing. Focus energy on categories that cause real-world harm: medical and health misinformation that drives dangerous decisions, election and civic misinformation that undermines democratic participation, financial scams, manipulated evidence used to accuse or defame, and content that targets vulnerable groups. A silly celebrity rumor is low priority; a false cancer treatment claim is urgent. When designing programming, frame the work around harm reduction rather than abstract truth, because patrons engage more readily when the stakes are concrete and shared.

### Address emotional and identity-driven belief carefully

The hardest cases are not factual errors but claims tied to a patron's identity, community, or fear. A patron may cling to a false belief because it confirms their worldview, expresses their grievance, or offers belonging, and no amount of evidence will move them in one conversation. In these cases, prioritize the relationship over winning: listen, acknowledge the underlying concern, offer to look something up together, and plant seeds rather than demanding capitulation. Avoid public confrontation, which forces the patron to double down to save face. Recognize your own limits; you cannot deconvert a patron from a deeply held conspiracy in a reference interview, and trying to will damage trust.

### Teach verification of images, video, and AI-generated content

Visual misinformation is increasingly powerful and increasingly hard to detect, especially with AI-generated images, voice clones, and deepfake video. Teach patrons practical reverse-image search to find the original context of a photo, and to watch for telltale signs of manipulation, such as inconsistent lighting, extra fingers, mismatched audio, or footage presented as recent that is actually from an older event. For AI content, emphasize that the absence of obvious flaws no longer proves authenticity, so the reliable approach is lateral: check whether reputable sources have confirmed the image or video, and trace it to its origin. Encourage healthy skepticism toward sensational visuals, especially those that provoke outrage.

### Respond to patrons spreading misinformation to others

When a patron is actively sharing false content in the library, on library social media, or in a program, the situation shifts from individual help to community protection. Address it privately and calmly, focus on the specific content rather than the person, and offer to verify it together. If the content targets or harasses others, apply library behavior and harassment policies. On library-operated social media, apply posted comment policies consistently. Avoid getting drawn into public arguments, which amplify the misinformation. Document repeated patterns and consult leadership on persistent cases.

### Build durable media-literacy programming

For classes and ongoing programming, design around transferable skills rather than a list of current examples, which date quickly. Core skills include lateral reading and SIFT, reverse-image search, recognizing emotional manipulation, understanding algorithms and engagement, distinguishing news from opinion and from sponsored content, and recognizing the role of bots and coordinated campaigns. Use interactive exercises with real examples rather than lectures, and create a judgement-free space where patrons can practice being wrong. Partner with local journalists, educators, or fact-checking organizations when possible, and keep materials updated as the landscape shifts.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is immediately declaring a patron's claim false, which triggers defensiveness, frames the exchange as a credibility contest, and often hardens the false belief. A related trap is treating misinformation work as winning debates rather than building verification habits, which satisfies the librarian's ego but does not help the patron. Staying on the suspicious page to evaluate it, rather than leaving to investigate the source laterally, mirrors the very mistake patrons make and misses manipulation that internal cues cannot reveal. Focusing on trivial false content, like celebrity rumors, while ignoring high-harm categories such as medical or election misinformation, wastes effort. Pushing evidence at a patron whose belief is tied to identity or community, and expecting capitulation, leads to frustration and damaged trust. Assuming an image or video is authentic because it has no obvious flaws is increasingly dangerous as AI generation improves. Getting drawn into public arguments with patrons spreading misinformation amplifies the false content and puts the library in a partisan posture. Using only current examples in programming guarantees the materials feel dated within months, and lecturing rather than letting patrons practice verification ensures the skills do not stick.

## Self-Check

- When a patron brought a questionable claim, did you respond with curiosity and an invitation to investigate together rather than immediate correction?
- Did you teach or model lateral reading, leaving the source to check what reputable outside sources say, rather than evaluating the page on its own terms?
- Did you prioritize high-harm categories, such as medical, election, financial, or targeted-harassment misinformation, over trivial false content?
- When belief was tied to identity or emotion, did you prioritize the relationship and plant seeds rather than demanding the patron concede on the spot?
- For visual or AI-generated content, did you recommend reverse-image search and tracing to origin, and avoid assuming authenticity from the absence of obvious flaws?
- Did you keep your own stance calm and non-partisan, avoiding public arguments that would amplify the misinformation?
- In programming, did you build around transferable skills and interactive practice rather than a static catalog of current examples?
- Did you protect the patron's dignity throughout, so they are willing to come back with the next question?
