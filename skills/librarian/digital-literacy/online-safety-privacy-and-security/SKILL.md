# Online Safety, Privacy, and Security

Online-safety and privacy help sits at an awkward intersection: the stakes are high, the threats change constantly, and the patrons most at risk are often the least confident with technology. A patron who clicks a phishing link, reuses one password everywhere, or overshared on social media can lose money, identity, reputation, or access to essential accounts, and the library may be the only place they can get trustworthy, non-commercial guidance. The work is not about making patrons paranoid; it is about giving them a small set of durable habits that reduce risk without paralyzing them. Done well, it builds practical resilience; done badly, it either overwhelms with fear or hands out rigid rules that patrons abandon the next day.

## Core Rules

### Treat safety help as teaching durable habits, not a one-time fix

The goal is not to solve a single incident but to leave the patron with habits they can apply to the next threat, because the specific scam will change but the underlying patterns rarely do. Teach a few transferable principles, such as slowing down before clicking, verifying identity through a known channel, and never giving credentials to someone who contacted them. Prioritize the highest-impact habits over exhaustive coverage, because a patron who internalizes three practices will be safer than one who memorized twenty rules and uses none. Reinforce that feeling pressured or urgent is itself a warning sign, since urgency is the core tool of almost every scam.

### Lead with the highest-impact protections

Not all safety measures are equal. The measures that prevent the most harm are: unique, strong passwords managed by a password manager; multi-factor authentication on email, banking, and benefits accounts; regular software updates; and the habit of verifying unexpected requests through a separate known channel. Focus energy here rather than on lower-value practices like changing passwords constantly or hiding behind obscure settings. If a patron will adopt only one thing, make it a password manager and multi-factor authentication on their email account, because email is the recovery key to everything else.

### Respond to a suspected scam or compromise calmly and in order

When a patron reports they may have been scammed, clicked a link, or lost access to an account, stay calm and reassure them they are not stupid or alone. Then act in priority order: secure the most valuable accounts first, starting with email and banking; change passwords for affected and reused accounts from a known-safe device; enable multi-factor authentication; contact banks or card providers to flag or freeze compromised accounts; report the scam to the appropriate authority; and monitor for follow-up attempts, since victims are often targeted again. Do not blame the patron, and do not minimize the loss. If the harm exceeds what the library can address, refer to identity-theft resources, consumer-protection agencies, or legal aid.

### Teach scam and phishing recognition through patterns

Patrons cannot memorize every scam, so teach them the recurring patterns rather than a catalog. Common red flags include unsolicited contact, urgency or threats, requests for payment in gift cards or cryptocurrency, requests for passwords or one-time codes, mismatches between display name and actual address, generic greetings, poor spelling or awkward phrasing, and offers that seem too good to be true. Emphasize the single most powerful habit: if contacted unexpectedly, do not use the link, number, or address the message provides; instead look up the official contact information independently and verify. This one practice defeats most phishing, regardless of how convincing the message looks.

### Address privacy settings without inducing paralysis

Privacy settings on social media, apps, and devices are sprawling and change frequently, so avoid trying to walk a patron through every toggle. Instead, identify the two or three highest-value settings for their situation, such as making social profiles private, limiting who can see contact information, reviewing app permissions, and understanding what location data is being collected. Frame privacy as a series of small ongoing choices rather than a one-time lockdown, and help the patron form the habit of pausing before signing up for a new service to consider what they are trading. Acknowledge that total privacy is impossible online and that the realistic goal is informed, intentional sharing.

### Respect patron autonomy and avoid surveillance instincts

Privacy guidance can shade into telling patrons how they should live online, which crosses a line. Offer information and recommendations, but let the patron decide what level of risk and convenience they accept. Do not judge patrons who choose broad sharing, use simple passwords despite your advice, or decline multi-factor authentication; document their choice and leave the door open. Equally important, protect the patron's privacy during the help itself: do not look at their passwords, do not read their messages, and do not store their credentials. The library's own handling of patron data should model the practices you teach.

### Keep current without chasing every new threat

The threat landscape shifts constantly, so build a small set of trusted sources, such as government cybersecurity agencies, consumer-protection organizations, and reputable security news outlets, and check them periodically rather than reacting to every headline. Avoid passing on unverified warnings, which themselves can be misinformation. When designing classes or handouts, focus on durable principles and include a note about where to verify current specifics, rather than embedding details that will be outdated in months.

## Common Traps

A common trap is overwhelming patrons with every possible threat and setting, which induces anxiety and leads them to do nothing because the task feels impossible. Another is handing out rigid, memorable rules, such as never click a link, that do not survive contact with real life and which patrons abandon once broken. Focusing on low-value measures, like frequent password changes or hiding behind obscure privacy toggles, while neglecting the high-impact basics of password managers and multi-factor authentication, gives a false sense of security. Telling a patron they were scammed because they were careless, or showing visible judgment, shames them into silence and ensures they will not report the next incident. Trying to walk a patron through every privacy setting on every app burns the session and produces no lasting habit. A serious trap is storing, viewing, or typing a patron's password during help, which both violates their privacy and creates liability for the library. Reacting to every new scam headline and passing along unverified warnings turns the library into an amplifier of misinformation. Finally, designing safety programming purely around fear, rather than around a few durable habits, leaves patrons paralyzed rather than resilient.

## Self-Check

- Did you focus on a few high-impact, durable habits rather than an exhaustive catalog of threats and settings?
- Did you prioritize password managers and multi-factor authentication on email, banking, and benefits accounts as the highest-value protections?
- When responding to a suspected scam or compromise, did you secure the most valuable accounts first, in priority order, without blaming the patron?
- Did you teach the pattern of verifying unexpected requests through a separate known channel rather than the contact information the message provided?
- Did you avoid looking at, typing, or storing the patron's passwords or reading their private messages during the session?
- Did you respect the patron's autonomy to accept a different level of risk and convenience than you would choose, without judgment?
- Did you ground any specific threat information in trusted, current sources rather than passing along unverified warnings?
- Did you frame privacy as ongoing informed choices rather than a one-time lockdown that will feel impossible to maintain?
