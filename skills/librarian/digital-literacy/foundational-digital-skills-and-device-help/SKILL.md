# Foundational Digital Skills and Device Help

Many patrons arrive at the library with a brand-new device, a forgotten password, a form they must fill out online, or a phone they cannot connect to Wi-Fi, and they have nowhere else to turn. Foundational digital-skills help is one of the most common and most consequential services a public library offers, because the people who need it are often the same people excluded from jobs, benefits, healthcare, banking, and family connection without it. The work is easy to underestimate: it looks like simple troubleshooting, but it is actually patient teaching under pressure, often with a patron who is embarrassed, frustrated, or afraid of breaking something. Done well, it builds confidence and independence; done poorly, it reinforces the belief that technology is something the patron can never master.

## Core Rules

### Frame the goal as independence, not a quick fix

The temptation in tech help is to grab the device and do the task for the patron, because it is faster and the patron often asks you to. Resist this. If you complete the task on their behalf, they leave dependent on you and must return for the same problem next time. Whenever possible, narrate each step, point to the screen, and have the patron perform the action themselves, even if it takes three times longer. The goal is a patron who can repeat the task alone, not a patron whose problem is solved for today only. When time or complexity forces you to intervene directly, name what you did and write it down so they have a reference.

### Diagnose before touching the device

Before making changes, establish what the patron actually wants to accomplish, what device and operating system they are using, and what they have already tried. Ask what changed recently, whether the device updated, whether a password was reset, and whether the problem is consistent or intermittent. Confirm whether the issue is hardware, connectivity, account, or application, because patrons often misidentify the layer. Write down error messages verbatim rather than paraphrasing. A minute of diagnosis prevents hours of fixing the wrong thing and protects you from "fixing" a setting the patron needed left alone.

### Protect accounts and data before changing anything

Foundational help frequently involves email, banking, benefits portals, or photos, which means the device holds irreplaceable or sensitive material. Before resetting, updating, signing out, or clearing data, ask whether anything important is backed up and whether the patron knows their passwords and can get back in after a change. Never sign a patron out of an account, factory-reset a device, or clear stored data without explicit confirmation that they can recover access. If a reset would lock them out, stop and help them recover credentials first. Treat a stranger's phone as holding the only copy of something precious, because it often does.

### Match the explanation to the patron's level

Assess proficiency quickly by asking what they already do comfortably, and adjust vocabulary accordingly. Avoid jargon, explain necessary terms once, and check understanding by asking the patron to show you rather than telling you they understand. Watch for the nod that masks confusion, common among patrons who do not want to feel slow. Break tasks into small steps, complete one step fully before introducing the next, and pause to let them practice. Recognize that literacy, language, vision, motor, memory, and anxiety all shape what a patron can absorb, and adapt pace and method rather than repeating the same explanation louder.

### Use plain-language handouts and visual aids

Memory fails the moment the patron leaves the desk, so provide written or printed steps for anything they will need to repeat. Write handouts in plain language with numbered steps, screenshots or simple diagrams, the exact names of buttons, and a note about where to get more help. Avoid assuming the patron can find "the gear icon" without showing it. Offer large-print versions and keep handouts organized by topic so staff can grab the right one fast. A good handout multiplies a single ten-minute session into lasting capability.

### Design the service for realistic demand

One-on-one tech help is labor-intensive and demand usually exceeds supply, so design the service deliberately rather than offering it ad hoc at the desk. Decide whether you offer drop-in hours, scheduled appointments, or both; set a time limit per session so one patron does not consume the whole shift; decide whether staff, volunteers, or both provide help; and define the boundaries of what you will and will not do. Publish the service clearly so patrons know how to access it and what to expect. Track common questions to identify topics that warrant a class, a handout, or a website guide.

### Set and communicate service boundaries

Foundational help has limits that protect both patron and institution. Decide in advance what staff will not do: typically entering or storing passwords, accessing banking or benefits accounts on the patron's behalf, performing paid transactions, giving financial or legal advice, or repairing hardware. Explain these limits kindly and offer alternatives, such as having the patron type their own password while you look away. Boundaries prevent staff from taking on liability and prevent patrons from expecting services the library cannot safely provide. Document the policy so staff apply it consistently.

## Common Traps

A frequent trap is taking the device and doing the work for the patron because it is faster or because they ask you to; this solves the immediate problem but teaches nothing and guarantees repeat visits. Another is fixing the wrong layer, such as reinstalling an app when the real issue is a forgotten password or expired payment method, because the patron described a symptom rather than the cause. Staff often assume a patron understands because they nod and say yes, when in fact they are masking confusion or embarrassment. Resetting a device, signing out of accounts, or clearing data without confirming the patron can get back in can lock them out of email, banking, or benefits, creating a crisis far worse than the original problem. Using unexplained jargon, such as "the cloud," "browser," or "cache," makes patrons feel inadequate and shuts down learning. Another trap is offering tech help informally at the desk with no time limit, so one complex case consumes a staff member's entire shift and leaves other patrons waiting. Volunteers may give inconsistent or outdated advice, or may overstep into accounts, if not trained and supervised. Finally, assuming all patrons have the same starting point, such as already knowing how to use a mouse or what an app icon looks like, alienates the very beginners the service is meant to reach.

## Self-Check

- Did you confirm what the patron actually wants to accomplish before changing anything on the device?
- Did the patron perform the key steps themselves, with you narrating, rather than you doing it for them?
- Before any reset, sign-out, update, or data clearing, did you confirm the patron can recover access to their accounts and that important data is backed up?
- Did you avoid jargon or explain each necessary term, and did you check understanding by having the patron demonstrate rather than just say yes?
- Did you provide written or printed steps the patron can follow after leaving, in plain language with the exact button names?
- Did you stay within service boundaries, declining to enter or store passwords, access financial accounts, or perform transactions on the patron's behalf?
- Did you set or respect a time limit so the session did not consume the entire shift or leave other patrons unattended?
- Did you record the common question so it can inform a future class, handout, or guide?
