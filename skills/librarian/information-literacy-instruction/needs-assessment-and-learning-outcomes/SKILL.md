# Needs Assessment And Learning Outcomes

Instruction planning fails most often at the very first step: the librarian assumes what learners need instead of finding out. A needs assessment is not a formality. It is the process that determines whether the session will be useful, redundant, or actively harmful to learning. Without it, learning outcomes become generic ("students will understand research"), activities drift toward what the librarian finds comfortable, and assessment measures the wrong thing. Good library instruction starts with a clear picture of who the learners are, what they already can do, what the assignment demands, and what gap the session is meant to close.

Use this skill before designing any instruction session, workshop, or tutorial. The goal is to prevent the agent from writing generic outcomes, ignoring the course context, overloading a short session, or designing instruction that does not connect to the actual assignment.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Learners Before The Content

Learner characteristics change everything about instruction design. The same topic taught to first-year students, graduate researchers, faculty, or community members needs different framing, vocabulary, pace, and examples.

Gather context about:

- academic level and discipline;
- prior exposure to library research;
- comfort with technology and databases;
- language background and reading level;
- accessibility needs;
- motivation level, whether voluntary or required attendance;
- class size and setting, in person, online, or hybrid;
- cultural and generational context.

Do not assume a session is for "students" in the abstract. A nursing cohort writing evidence-based care plans needs very different instruction than a first-year composition class writing argument essays.

### Anchor Outcomes To The Assignment

Instruction that is not connected to a real assignment tends to feel abstract and is quickly forgotten. The most effective one-shot sessions teach skills learners need within days, not months.

Clarify with the instructor:

- the actual assignment description and requirements;
- the due date relative to the session;
- source type and number requirements;
- citation style expected;
- whether topics are pre-assigned or student-chosen;
- what the instructor has already covered;
- where students typically struggle;
- whether the instructor will attend and participate.

If the instructor cannot share the assignment, push gently but firmly. Instruction without assignment context usually misses.

### Write Measurable Learning Outcomes

Learning outcomes describe what learners will be able to do by the end, stated in observable terms. Vague outcomes cannot be assessed and lead to unfocused sessions.

Weak outcomes:

- "Students will understand research."
- "Students will learn about the library."
- "Students will appreciate scholarly sources."

Strong outcomes use observable verbs:

- "Learners will identify three peer-reviewed articles relevant to their topic."
- "Learners will narrow a broad topic using subject headings."
- "Learners will distinguish between a popular and a scholarly source using given criteria."
- "Learners will construct a Boolean search combining two concepts."

Avoid verbs that describe internal states that cannot be observed, such as understand, know, appreciate, or feel. Use the framework of Bloom's taxonomy action verbs, but keep outcomes realistic for the time available.

### Limit The Number Of Outcomes

A common failure is trying to teach too much. A fifty-minute one-shot cannot produce mastery of database searching, evaluation, citation, and topic development. Choose one to three outcomes maximum and design everything around them.

Prioritize by asking:

- Which outcome will most reduce assignment struggle?
- Which outcome transfers to other courses?
- Which outcome can actually be practiced in the time available?
- Which outcome can be assessed within the session?

Cut outcomes that are nice-to-have. Depth on one transferable skill beats shallow coverage of five.

### Distinguish Between Skills, Concepts, And Dispositions

Instruction can target different dimensions of learning, and confusing them muddies design.

- Skills are doable and observable, such as constructing a search or requesting an interlibrary loan.
- Concepts are understandings, such as how peer review works or why citation matters.
- Dispositions are habits of mind, such as persistence in searching or skepticism toward unverified claims.

Name which dimension each outcome targets. A session full of concepts with no practiced skill will not stick. A session full of mechanical skills with no concept will not transfer.

### Assess Prior Knowledge

Do not assume a baseline. What seems basic may be unfamiliar, and what seems advanced may already be known.

Methods include:

- a short pre-session survey;
- a question to the instructor about prior coverage;
- a quick poll or brainstorm at session start;
- reviewing past student work if available;
- asking the instructor where students get stuck.

Prior knowledge assessment prevents two failures: teaching content learners already know, which bores them, and skipping foundations learners lack, which loses them.

### Align Outcomes With Institutional Frameworks

Many libraries and institutions adopt frameworks such as the ACRL Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education, school-level standards, or workplace competency models. Outcomes should connect to these without becoming buried in jargon.

Use frameworks to:

- justify instruction to faculty and administrators;
- ensure outcomes build across a curriculum;
- connect one-shot sessions to broader program goals;
- provide shared vocabulary with teaching partners.

Do not let framework language replace clear, learner-facing outcomes. A frame like "Research as Inquiry" is a guide for the instructor, not a learning outcome for students.

### Consider Sequencing And Curriculum Integration

A single session is more effective when it sits inside a sequence. Think about what comes before and after.

Ask:

- Is this the first library session these learners will have?
- Will there be a follow-up session?
- Can the librarian embed in the course over time?
- Are there online modules that can precede or extend the session?
- How does this session connect to a broader information literacy program?

Even when only one session is possible, design it with awareness of where it fits in the learner's larger experience.

## Common Traps

### Designing For An Imaginary Generic Student

Generic outcomes produce generic sessions. Real learners have a discipline, a level, an assignment, and constraints. Design for the actual cohort.

### Accepting "Cover Everything" Requests

Instructors sometimes ask the librarian to teach "all of research" in one session. This is not possible and produces shallow, forgettable instruction. Negotiate down to the highest-value outcomes.

### Skipping The Instructor Conversation

Designing from a syllabus alone misses the lived reality of the course. The instructor knows where students actually struggle, which is rarely visible in the syllabus.

### Writing Outcomes That Cannot Be Assessed

If you cannot tell whether a learner achieved the outcome by the end of the session, the outcome is not measurable. Rewrite it as an observable behavior.

### Treating Prior Knowledge As Uniform

A class is not homogeneous. Some students arrive over-prepared and others under-prepared. Plan for differentiation or at least for a baseline that does not lose the least-prepared learner.

### Confusing Coverage With Learning

Covering a topic is not the same as teaching it. A slide deck that lists ten databases has covered content but may have taught nothing. Outcomes should describe what learners do, not what the librarian says.

### Ignoring Motivation

Learners who see no relevance will not engage. Connect outcomes to the assignment, to their lives, or to a goal they already hold. Required attendance without relevance produces compliance, not learning.

## Self-Check

- Are the actual learners identified by level, discipline, prior exposure, and accessibility needs, rather than treated as generic?
- Is the real assignment known, including requirements, due date, and source expectations?
- Are learning outcomes written as observable behaviors rather than internal states?
- Are there no more than three outcomes, each justified as the highest-value choice?
- Is it clear whether each outcome targets a skill, a concept, or a disposition?
- Has prior knowledge been assessed through a survey, instructor input, or session-start activity?
- Do outcomes connect to an institutional framework without being buried in its jargon?
- Is the session placed in the context of what comes before and after in the curriculum?
- Could each outcome actually be practiced and assessed within the time available?
- Has the instructor conversation happened, and did it reveal where students genuinely struggle?
