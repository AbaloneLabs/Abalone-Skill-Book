# Assessment Of Learning

Assessment is the part of instruction most often skipped, faked, or reduced to a satisfaction survey. Without genuine assessment, the librarian cannot know whether a session taught anything, cannot improve future sessions, and cannot demonstrate value to faculty, administrators, or funders. The trap is confusing satisfaction, attendance, or activity completion with learning. Real assessment asks whether learners can now do what the outcomes promised, and it gathers evidence specific enough to guide improvement. Good assessment is planned before the session, aligned to outcomes, feasible within the one-shot format, and honest about what it can and cannot show.

Use this skill when planning how to measure instruction effectiveness, designing assessment instruments, interpreting results, or reporting instructional impact. The goal is to prevent the agent from relying on feel-good metrics, overclaiming from weak evidence, or skipping assessment entirely.

## Core Rules

### Align Assessment To Outcomes First

Every assessment must map to a specific learning outcome. If an outcome says learners will construct a Boolean search, the assessment must require them to construct one. If an outcome says learners will distinguish scholarly from popular sources, the assessment must present sources to distinguish.

Before designing any instrument, list each outcome and ask:

- what would a learner do if they achieved this outcome;
- what would a learner do if they did not;
- what artifact or response captures that difference;
- can this be gathered within the session constraints.

Assessment that does not map to outcomes produces data that cannot answer whether learning happened.

### Distinguish Assessment Types By Purpose

Different assessment purposes require different methods. Confusing them produces misleading conclusions.

- Diagnostic or pre-assessment measures what learners know before instruction, to calibrate the session.
- Formative assessment happens during instruction, to adjust in the moment.
- Summative assessment measures achievement after instruction, to judge learning.
- Programmatic assessment aggregates across sessions, to evaluate and improve the instruction program.

Name the purpose before choosing the method. A satisfaction survey is neither formative nor summative for learning; it measures reaction, not achievement.

### Measure Learning, Not Just Satisfaction

Satisfaction surveys, the most common library instruction assessment, tell you whether learners liked the session. They do not tell you whether learners learned. Both matter, but they are different questions.

Satisfaction questions:

- Was the session useful?
- Was the pace appropriate?
- Was the librarian clear?

Learning questions:

- Which of these is a peer-reviewed source?
- Write a search combining these two concepts.
- Identify the strongest source for this assignment and explain why.

Report satisfaction as satisfaction and learning as learning. Do not claim learning gains from satisfaction data.

### Use Pre And Post When Feasible

The strongest one-shot evidence compares what learners could do before and after. A short pre-assessment and an equivalent post-assessment show change attributable, at least partially, to the session.

Design considerations:

- keep pre and post short, three to five items;
- use the same or equivalent items;
- administer pre at session start and post at session end;
- consider a delayed post one to two weeks later for retention;
- account for the Hawthorne effect and novelty.

If pre-assessment is not feasible, a well-designed post-assessment alone still beats satisfaction-only, but interpret it cautiously since you lack the baseline.

### Gather Authentic Artifacts

The most convincing evidence of learning is real student work produced during or after the session. Artifacts show what learners can actually do, not what they believe they can do.

Valuable artifacts:

- search strategies written on worksheets;
- a citation for a source the learner found;
- a completed evaluation checklist on a real source;
- a topic narrowed from broad to focused;
- a reflection on what changed in their approach.

Collect and analyze these with learner consent and attention to privacy. Aggregate rather than identify individuals in reporting.

### Build Assessment Into The Session

Assessment that happens after the session, by email or separate survey, suffers from low response rates. Embed assessment in the session itself so capture is near-complete.

Embedded methods:

- collect worksheets at the end;
- have groups post to a shared document;
- use a closing poll or quiz;
- ask for a one-minute paper;
- observe and circulate during practice.

Embedded assessment doubles as a learning activity when designed well. Learners consolidate by articulating what they learned.

### Analyze For Patterns, Not Just Averages

A mean score hides the distribution that matters most. Ten learners who improved and ten who did not average out and look fine, but half the class failed to learn.

Look at:

- how many learners met the outcome threshold;
- which items had the most failures;
- whether certain subgroups struggled differently;
- common misconceptions in open responses;
- patterns across sections and instructors.

Qualitative analysis of worksheets and reflections often reveals more than numeric scores. Categorize responses to find recurring gaps.

### Close The Loop By Using Results

Assessment data that is collected and not used is wasted effort and mildly unethical, since it consumed learner time. Build a process to review results and change practice.

Close the loop by:

- reviewing results within a week of the session;
- identifying one or two changes for next time;
- sharing aggregate results with the instructor;
- tracking changes and their effects over iterations;
- reporting program-level findings to administrators.

Improvement is the primary purpose of assessment in instruction. Accountability reporting is secondary.

### Report Honestly And With Appropriate Caution

Stakeholders want strong claims. Resist them when the evidence is weak. Overclaiming damages credibility when scrutinized.

Report:

- what was measured and how;
- the sample and response rate;
- the actual results, including disappointments;
- limitations, such as no control group or self-report bias;
- what the results suggest rather than prove;
- planned changes.

A modest, honest report builds more trust than an inflated one.

### Consider Ethical And Privacy Dimensions

Assessment data about learners is sensitive. Names, topics, struggles, and search histories can reveal beliefs, health, identity, or academic difficulty.

Protect learners by:

- collecting anonymously or with consent;
- separating identity from artifacts where possible;
- not quoting identifiable student work without permission;
- storing data securely and retaining only as long as needed;
- following institutional and legal privacy requirements.

Assessment that violates privacy to prove value undermines the library's ethical standing.

## Common Traps

### Equating Satisfaction With Learning

Happy learners may have learned nothing, and frustrated learners may have learned a great deal. Do not report satisfaction scores as evidence of learning outcomes.

### Using Attendance As Impact

Counting sessions delivered and students reached measures activity, not effect. Activity counts are useful for workload reporting but say nothing about learning.

### Assessing Only What Is Easy To Measure

Multiple-choice recall is easy to grade but may miss the actual skill. Choose assessment for alignment to outcomes, not for convenience.

### Skipping The Baseline

Without a pre-assessment or equivalent, you cannot attribute change to the session. Be honest that post-only data shows status, not growth.

### Overclaiming From Small Samples

A single section of twenty students does not prove a method works generally. Report scope honestly and avoid generalizing beyond the data.

### Collecting Data And Not Using It

Asking learners to complete assessments and then ignoring the results wastes their time and erodes trust. Review and act on findings.

### Ignoring The Instructor's Contribution

Learning gains often reflect the instructor's assignment design and reinforcement as much as the librarian's session. Credit collaborators and avoid implying the library alone caused the gain.

### Treating One Bad Result As Failure

A session that produces weak results is data, not disaster. The point is to learn and improve. Defensive reporting hides the signal.

## Self-Check

- Does every assessment item map to a specific learning outcome?
- Is the assessment purpose, diagnostic, formative, summative, or programmatic, explicitly named?
- Are learning measures distinct from satisfaction measures, and reported separately?
- Is there a pre-assessment or equivalent baseline, or is the limitation of post-only acknowledged?
- Are authentic artifacts, such as worksheets and searches, collected and analyzed?
- Is assessment embedded in the session to maximize capture?
- Are results analyzed for distribution and patterns, not just averages?
- Is there a process to use results to change practice and track improvement?
- Are claims reported with appropriate caution about limitations and scope?
- Is learner privacy protected in collection, storage, and reporting?
- Are collaborators, especially the course instructor, credited for their contribution to any gains?
