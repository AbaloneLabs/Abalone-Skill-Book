# Session Design For One-Shot

The one-shot session is the most common and most constrained teaching format in libraries. Typically forty-five to seventy-five minutes, often mandatory for students, and usually the only direct contact the librarian has with that class. The danger is treating the one-shot as a compressed lecture, racing through content, and mistaking coverage for learning. Good one-shot design is ruthless about prioritization, built around active practice, sequenced so learners do something with each concept immediately, and flexible enough to adapt when technology fails or time runs short.

Use this skill when designing the structure, activities, and materials for a single instruction session. The goal is to prevent the agent from over-packing the session, lecturing instead of teaching, demonstrating without practice, or building rigid plans that collapse on contact with reality.

## Core Rules

### Design Backward From The Outcomes

Start from the one to three outcomes identified in needs assessment and build the session backward. For each outcome, decide what learners must do to achieve it, then what activity lets them practice it, then what introduction sets up the activity.

The backward chain is:

- outcome, what learners will do;
- assessment, how you will know they did it;
- activity, the practice that produces the evidence;
- introduction, the minimum framing needed before practice;
- materials, the handout, slide, or worksheet that supports the activity.

Do not start by listing content to cover. Content-first design produces overstuffed sessions.

### Make Active Learning The Default

Learners retain little from watching a database demonstration. They retain more from searching themselves with support. Build the session so learners are doing, not watching, for the majority of the time.

Active learning structures include:

- think-pair-share on a source evaluation;
- guided search on the learner's own topic;
- worksheet that structures a search strategy;
- source comparison in small groups;
- concept mapping of a research question;
- a scavenger hunt through the catalog or database;
- a problem scenario that requires a decision.

Reserve demonstration for moments when learners cannot proceed without seeing a step. Keep demonstrations short, ideally under three minutes, and immediately follow with hands-on practice.

### Sequence For Immediate Application

Introduce a concept or skill, then have learners use it within minutes. The gap between explanation and practice should be small. Long lectures followed by a single activity at the end lose learners.

A workable sequence for a fifty-minute session:

- hook or relevance prompt, three to five minutes;
- outcome framing, what we will do and why, two minutes;
- first concept and immediate activity, twelve minutes;
- second concept and immediate activity, twelve minutes;
- synthesis or application to the real assignment, twelve minutes;
- reflection or assessment, five minutes;
- buffer for questions and transitions, remaining time.

This is a template, not a script. Adapt to the outcomes and the cohort.

### Connect Every Activity To The Real Assignment

Generic exercises feel like busywork. When learners practice on their actual research topic for their actual assignment, engagement and transfer both rise.

Design activities so that:

- learners search their own topic, not a sample topic;
- the source requirements match the assignment;
- the worksheet produces something they can use in the paper;
- the citation practice uses a source they actually found;
- the evaluation uses a source relevant to their field.

If the assignment is unknown or too varied, build in a choice step where learners define their own working topic early in the session.

### Build In Choice And Autonomy

Adult learners and motivated students respond to control over their own learning. Build choice into activities where possible.

Options for choice:

- which database to search first;
- which topic angle to pursue;
- which source to evaluate;
- whether to work alone or with a partner;
- which citation tool to try.

Choice increases engagement and reduces the feeling of being marched through steps. Balance choice with structure so less confident learners are not paralyzed.

### Plan For Technology Failure

One-shot sessions frequently encounter login problems, database outages, slow networks, missing plugins, or classroom equipment failures. A plan that depends entirely on live technology will eventually fail in front of students.

Build resilience:

- have a low-tech or paper version of the core activity;
- know the key searches well enough to narrate without the screen;
- bring a printed worksheet that works without the database;
- have a backup topic or cached results to discuss;
- confirm classroom access and logins before the session.

Treat technology as an enhancement, not the foundation. The learning should survive a dead projector.

### Manage Time With Explicit Buffers

Time in a one-shot is scarce and disappears faster than planned. Build the session with one or two activities marked as optional or compressible, so you can expand or contract based on actual pace.

Mark each segment as:

- essential, must happen for outcomes;
- important, should happen if time allows;
- optional, cut without loss if running long.

Never cut the practice to preserve the lecture. If time is short, cut content, not activity.

### Design Materials That Survive Without The Librarian

Worksheets, guides, and slides often outlive the session. Students return to them later, or the instructor shares them with future cohorts. Design materials that are usable without your narration.

Good materials:

- state the purpose and steps clearly;
- include examples, not just instructions;
- link to stable, current resources;
- use screenshots with annotations;
- avoid jargon or define it inline;
- work on mobile as well as desktop;
- have a clear title and date.

A worksheet that only makes sense with you explaining it fails the transfer test.

### Plan For The Instructor's Role

The course instructor's presence and participation dramatically affect session quality. Clarify expectations before the session.

Negotiate:

- will the instructor attend and circulate during activities;
- will the instructor connect the session to the assignment verbally;
- will the instructor reinforce skills after the session;
- will the instructor share student work or topics in advance.

An engaged instructor multiplies the session's effect. An absent or disengaged instructor signals to students that the session does not matter.

### Embed Assessment Within Activities

Assessment in a one-shot is usually informal and embedded, not a separate test. Build moments where learner understanding becomes visible.

Embedded assessment options:

- collecting worksheets or search strategies;
- a shared document where groups post a found source;
- a poll or quiz at the end;
- a one-minute paper on what was learned and what remains confusing;
- observation of learner searches with targeted coaching.

Use what you learn to adjust in the moment and to improve the session next time.

## Common Traps

### The Database Tour

Walking students through every feature of a database teaches them to watch, not to search. Demonstrate only what they need for the immediate task, then have them search.

### Overpacking The Session

Trying to cover catalog, databases, evaluation, citation, and interlibrary loan in fifty minutes guarantees shallow treatment of all. Choose the highest-value outcomes and go deep.

### Using Sample Topics That Do Not Match The Class

A generic sample topic disconnects the session from the assignment. Let learners work on their own topics whenever possible.

### Reading From Slides

Slides full of text that the librarian reads aloud is not teaching. Use slides for visuals, prompts, and structure, not as a script.

### Ignoring The Room

A rigid plan that does not respond to visible confusion, disengagement, or questions will fail. Watch the learners and adjust pace and depth in real time.

### Assuming Technology Will Work

Live demos die often. Have a fallback and practice the session without relying on a perfect connection.

### Treating The One-Shot As Standalone

A one-shot with no follow-up rarely produces lasting change. Build in a bridge, a guide, an assignment connection, or a follow-up offer so learning continues.

### Skipping Reflection

Learners who do not reflect on what they learned and what they still need rarely consolidate the skill. A short closing reflection improves retention.

## Self-Check

- Is the session designed backward from outcomes, with activity and assessment decided before introduction and materials?
- Is active learning the default, with demonstration reserved for moments learners cannot proceed without it?
- Does each concept get immediate practice within minutes of introduction?
- Do activities connect to the learners' real assignment and their own topics?
- Is there meaningful choice built into at least one activity?
- Is there a low-tech fallback if the database, network, or projector fails?
- Is each segment marked essential, important, or optional, with buffers for time?
- Do the materials, worksheet, guide, and slides work without the librarian present?
- Is the course instructor's role clarified and ideally active during the session?
- Is assessment embedded so learner understanding becomes visible during the session?
- Is there a closing reflection or bridge to continued learning?
