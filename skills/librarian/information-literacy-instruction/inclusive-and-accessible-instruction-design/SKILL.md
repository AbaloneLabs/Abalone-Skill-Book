# Inclusive And Accessible Instruction Design

Library instruction reaches learners with widely varying backgrounds, abilities, languages, prior knowledge, and relationships to academic institutions. A session designed for an assumed default learner, neurotypical, native English speaker, digitally fluent, familiar with academic norms, confidently reaches some learners and quietly fails others. First-generation students may not share the assumed familiarity with academic research conventions. Multilingual learners may be fully competent researchers in another language but struggle with English-dominant databases. Students with disabilities may be excluded by materials that assume sight, hearing, or standard motor interaction. Learners from marginalized backgrounds may not see themselves reflected in examples, or may encounter materials that reproduce stereotypes. Inclusive and accessible instruction design is the practice of anticipating this variation and designing teaching that reaches the full range of learners rather than an assumed norm, so that information literacy instruction does not become another site where already-advantaged learners succeed and others are left to adapt or fail.

Use this skill when designing library instruction for diverse audiences, when making sessions accessible to learners with disabilities, when addressing equity and inclusion in teaching, when working with first-generation or multilingual learners, or when auditing instruction for bias and exclusion. The goal is to prevent the agent from designing for an assumed default learner, from treating accessibility as an accommodation added after the fact, and from allowing instruction to reproduce the inequities it should counteract.

## Core Rules

### Design For The Margins, Not The Default

Inclusive design starts from the recognition that there is no default learner. Designing for an assumed average and then accommodating exceptions produces instruction that works for some and requires others to request special treatment. Designing for the margins, the learners with the most barriers, tends to produce instruction that works better for everyone. A session designed for a multilingual learner is clearer for all learners; a session designed for a screen-reader user is more navigable for everyone.

Design for the margins by:

- identifying who is most likely to be excluded by default design and designing from their needs;
- treating accessibility and inclusion as design requirements, not accommodations;
- testing instruction against the needs of learners with the most barriers;
- recognizing that what helps the margin often improves the experience for all.

Designing for the default and accommodating the rest is integration through exception. Designing for the margin is integration by default.

### Make Accessibility A Design Principle, Not An Accommodation

Accessibility is often treated as something added when a learner requests it, an accommodation layered onto already-designed instruction. This approach is reactive, slow, and stigmatizing, requiring learners to disclose disability and request adjustments. Building accessibility into the design from the start, through universal design for learning principles, makes instruction accessible without requiring disclosure or special requests.

Build accessibility in by:

- providing captions and transcripts for all video and audio by default, not on request;
- ensuring all digital materials are screen-reader compatible and keyboard navigable;
- using sufficient color contrast, readable fonts, and scalable text;
- providing materials in multiple formats so learners can access through their preferred mode;
- describing images and visual content rather than relying on sight alone.

Accessibility added after the fact is always partial and always late. Accessibility built in is seamless and dignified.

### Address Varied Prior Knowledge And Academic Familiarity

Learners arrive with very different levels of familiarity with academic research conventions, databases, and information structures. First-generation students, students from under-resourced schools, and students returning to study after years away may lack the assumed baseline that instruction takes for granted. Failing to address this variation leaves capable learners confused by conventions that were never explained.

Address prior knowledge by:

- not assuming familiarity with academic databases, citation styles, or research terminology;
- scaffolding from foundational concepts before advancing to specialized skills;
- providing pre-session materials for learners who want to build baseline familiarity;
- creating low-stakes ways for learners to surface confusion without embarrassment;
- recognizing that confusion often reflects unexplained conventions, not lack of ability.

A learner who seems to struggle may be fully capable but missing context that was never provided.

### Support Multilingual And Non-Native English Learners

Library databases, catalogs, and research terminology are heavily English-dominant and jargon-laden. Multilingual learners, who may be highly competent researchers in another language, face additional barriers from terminology, syntax, and culturally specific research conventions. Instruction that ignores this treats language difficulty as a deficit rather than a barrier the instruction can reduce.

Support multilingual learners by:

- defining terminology and jargon rather than using it assuming shared understanding;
- speaking clearly and at measured pace, avoiding unnecessary idioms;
- providing written materials learners can review at their own pace;
- acknowledging that research competence in another language transfers, even if English fluency is developing;
- offering examples and databases in other languages where the collection supports them.

A multilingual learner struggling with English terminology is not struggling with research concepts.

### Choose Inclusive Examples And Materials

The examples, scenarios, and sample searches used in instruction signal who belongs and what counts as legitimate inquiry. Examples drawn only from dominant cultures, or that reproduce stereotypes, tell learners from other backgrounds that their experiences and questions are not the expected ones. Inclusive examples broaden whose interests and knowledge the instruction recognizes.

Choose inclusively by:

- using examples and sample topics that reflect diverse cultures, identities, and interests;
- avoiding stereotypes in scenarios, such as assuming disciplinary interests by gender or background;
- ensuring visuals and case studies represent a range of people and contexts;
- being willing to surface and discuss bias in examples rather than pretending neutrality.

Learners notice whether the instruction assumes them as its audience. Inclusive examples widen who feels addressed.

### Vary Teaching Methods To Reach Different Learners

A single teaching mode, such as lecture plus demonstration, reaches learners who learn well from that mode and loses others. Varying methods, combining explanation, demonstration, hands-on practice, discussion, and visual, auditory, and kinesthetic engagement, reaches a wider range of learners and reduces the chance that any learner is left behind by modality mismatch.

Vary methods by:

- combining telling, showing, and doing within a session rather than relying on one mode;
- offering individual, pair, and group activities so introverted and extroverted learners both engage;
- using visual, verbal, and written reinforcement of key concepts;
- allowing multiple ways for learners to demonstrate understanding, not only verbal response.

Modality variety is not entertainment; it is reach. Different learners engage through different modes.

### Create Psychological Safety For Participation

Learners participate when they feel safe asking questions, admitting confusion, and taking intellectual risks. Many learners, especially those from groups historically excluded from academia, may fear that asking basic questions will confirm stereotypes about their competence. Instruction that does not actively create safety silences the learners who most need to ask.

Create safety by:

- explicitly inviting questions and normalizing confusion as part of learning;
- responding to all questions with respect, regardless of how basic they seem;
- avoiding singling out learners based on assumed identity or background;
- providing anonymous ways to ask questions, such as digital submission;
- addressing disrespectful dynamics between learners immediately.

A learner who fears their question confirms a stereotype will not ask it, and will not learn.

### Audit Instruction For Bias And Exclusion

Inclusive design requires ongoing auditing. Instruction accumulates assumptions, outdated examples, and unintended biases over time. Regular auditing, examining examples, language, materials, and structure for who is included and who is excluded, keeps instruction honest and improving.

Audit by:

- reviewing examples and materials for representation and stereotype;
- examining language for assumptions about background, ability, or identity;
- soliciting feedback from learners about what felt excluding or alienating;
- revising based on what the audit reveals rather than defending the existing design.

Instruction that is never audited drifts toward the biases of its original context.

## Common Traps

### Designing For An Assumed Default Learner

There is no default learner. Design for the margins, which tends to improve instruction for everyone.

### Treating Accessibility As Reactive Accommodation

Adding accessibility on request is late and stigmatizing. Build accessibility into design from the start.

### Assuming Shared Prior Knowledge And Academic Familiarity

Many capable learners lack assumed baseline conventions. Scaffold and explain rather than assuming familiarity.

### Ignoring Language Barriers For Multilingual Learners

English-dominant terminology and jargon exclude competent multilingual researchers. Define terms and provide reviewable materials.

### Using Exclusive Or Stereotyped Examples

Examples signal who belongs. Use diverse, non-stereotyping examples that address a range of learners.

### Relying On A Single Teaching Modality

One mode reaches some learners and loses others. Vary methods to reach different learning preferences.

### Failing To Create Psychological Safety

Learners who fear confirming stereotypes will not participate. Actively create safety for questions and risk-taking.

### Never Auditing Instruction For Bias

Instruction accumulates bias over time. Audit regularly and revise based on findings.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Instruction is designed for learners at the margins, not an assumed default, with accessibility and inclusion built in from the start.
- [ ] All materials are accessible by default, including captions, transcripts, screen-reader compatibility, and multiple formats.
- [ ] Prior knowledge and academic familiarity are scaffolded rather than assumed, with foundations explained before advanced concepts.
- [ ] Multilingual and non-native English learners are supported through defined terminology, clear speech, and reviewable materials.
- [ ] Examples, scenarios, and visuals are inclusive, representing diverse identities and avoiding stereotypes.
- [ ] Teaching methods vary across modalities to reach learners with different preferences and abilities.
- [ ] Psychological safety is actively created so learners can ask questions and admit confusion without fear.
- [ ] Instruction has been audited for bias and exclusion, and revised based on what the audit revealed.
- [ ] Accessibility is treated as a design principle, not an accommodation requested after the fact.
- [ ] Feedback from diverse learners has been sought and incorporated into ongoing design.
