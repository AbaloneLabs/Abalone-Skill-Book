# Subject Vocabulary Evaluation And Bias

Subject vocabularies are not neutral instruments. They were built over decades, often centuries, by specific institutions reflecting specific cultural assumptions, and they carry those assumptions into every catalog record. Headings that were once standard now read as offensive, exclusionary, or absurdly centered on a dominant perspective. A vocabulary may have rich coverage of Western political history and thin or pathologizing coverage of non-Western cultures, LGBTQ+ lives, disability, or indigenous peoples. Evaluating and, where possible, correcting these biases is not political activism imposed on cataloging; it is core cataloging work, because biased vocabulary directly degrades the find and select tasks for real users. Ignoring the problem produces a catalog that tells some users their topics do not exist or are defined in terms they would reject. Good practice acknowledges the limits of any vocabulary, uses the most respectful current terminology available, documents local revisions, and contributes improvements back to the vocabulary maintainers.

Use this skill when reviewing subject headings for bias, responding to concerns about offensive terms, choosing between vocabularies for underrepresented topics, updating legacy headings, or deciding whether to adopt local or alternative terms. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the vocabulary as untouchable, assigning offensive terms without thought, overcorrecting in ways that break collocation, or making silent local changes that confuse other institutions.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Vocabularies Carry Historical Bias

Every major subject vocabulary reflects the priorities and blind spots of its origin. Acknowledging this is the first step to working with them responsibly.

Common forms of bias:

- dominant-group-as-default, where the unmarked heading assumes a Western, white, male, heterosexual, or able-bodied norm;
- pathologizing or deficit framing of marginalized groups;
- anachronistic terminology retained for collocation that now reads as offensive;
- uneven granularity, deep coverage of some cultures and shallow or absent coverage of others;
- geopolitical framing that centers one nation's perspective on world events.

Do not assume a heading is neutral because it is established. Evaluate it against current usage and the impact on the users it describes.

### Use The Most Respectful Current Terminology Available

Within the constraints of a controlled vocabulary, choose terminology that respects the people and topics described. Vocabularies do change, and catalogers should use the current authorized term even when legacy records use older ones.

Practice:

- track vocabulary revisions that update offensive or outdated terms;
- apply current headings to new cataloging even if legacy records retain older ones;
- when a respectful heading exists alongside an offensive one, use the respectful one and record a cross-reference;
- follow the vocabulary maintainer's guidance on preferred terms for specific communities;
- when the community itself prefers a term the vocabulary lacks, document the local preference.

Respect is not a one-time decision; it requires following ongoing revisions in the field.

### Balance Respectful Terminology With Collocation

Changing terminology can break collocation if done carelessly. A wholesale local rewrite of headings may isolate a collection from the broader bibliographic universe.

Balance the two:

- prefer official vocabulary revisions when they exist, because they preserve collocation across institutions;
- when local change is necessary, record see references from the old term so existing searches still work;
- avoid creating parallel local vocabularies that do not map to any standard;
- document the mapping between local and standard terms for future migration;
- participate in the vocabulary's change process to push respectful terms upstream.

The goal is respectful access without orphaning the collection from cooperative cataloging.

### Evaluate Granularity And Coverage Gaps

Bias also appears as absence. A vocabulary may have no heading, or only a pejorative heading, for topics important to a community.

Check for gaps:

- are there headings for the cultural, religious, or social practices of the communities in your collection;
- are marginalized groups represented with the same granularity as dominant groups;
- do headings for non-Western topics use the group's own framing or only an external framing;
- are emerging fields, identities, or movements represented or absent;
- does the geographic subdivision structure work for the regions in your collection.

Where gaps exist, use the closest valid heading, document the gap, and consider a local heading or a proposal to the vocabulary maintainer.

### Handle Offensive Legacy Headings Deliberately

Legacy catalogs contain headings that are now offensive. Ignoring them perpetuates harm; deleting them silently breaks access and collocation. A deliberate approach is required.

Options:

- replace with the current respectful heading where one exists, with a reference from the old term;
- retain the old heading with a clear note explaining it is legacy and no longer used for new cataloging;
- add a local see reference from the respectful term users are likely to search;
- flag for batch cleanup as part of a vocabulary revision project;
- never assign an offensive heading to new cataloging when a respectful alternative exists.

The choice depends on the institution's capacity, the cooperative environment, and the impact on users. The wrong choice is to do nothing.

### Document Local Vocabulary Decisions

Local deviations from a standard vocabulary must be documented so they are consistent, defensible, and reversible.

Document:

- the local term and the standard term it replaces or supplements;
- the rationale, including community preference or harm reduction;
- the cross-references that preserve discoverability;
- the date of adoption and any review schedule;
- the staff responsible for the decision.

Undocumented local terms become unexplainable artifacts that the next cataloger cannot interpret or maintain.

### Engage With Vocabulary Maintenance Processes

Subject vocabularies are maintained by institutions that accept proposals and corrections. Engaging with these processes is how bias gets corrected at scale.

Engagement:

- learn the proposal process for the vocabularies you use, LC SACO, MeSH, Getty;
- submit proposals for missing headings, especially for underrepresented topics;
- report offensive or outdated terms with evidence of community usage;
- track the status of proposals and apply revisions when adopted;
- coordinate with cooperative cataloging partners to build consensus.

Individual local fixes help one collection; upstream changes help every institution using the vocabulary.

### Consider Alternative And Community-Sourced Vocabularies

For some topics, community-sourced or specialized vocabularies offer better representation than the major general vocabularies.

Examples:

- Homosaurus for LGBTQ+ topics;
- AAT for art and material culture;
- local indigenous knowledge vocabularies developed with the communities;
- discipline-specific vocabularies for fields with their own terminology.

Use these alongside or instead of general vocabularies where they serve users better, recording the source so terms are interpretable. Do not adopt a vocabulary uncritically; evaluate its maintenance, governance, and community endorsement.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Vocabulary As Neutral And Untouchable

Established headings carry historical assumptions. Evaluate them rather than assigning them uncritically.

### Assigning Offensive Terms To New Cataloging

When a respectful alternative exists, using the offensive legacy term to match old records perpetuates harm. Use current terminology for new work.

### Silent Local Rewrites That Break Collocation

Changing headings locally without references or documentation isolates the collection and confuses cooperative partners. Document and cross-reference.

### Overcorrecting Into Unmaintainable Local Vocabularies

Creating bespoke local terms for everything produces a vocabulary no one else can use. Prefer upstream revisions and document necessary local deviations.

### Ignoring Coverage Gaps As Neutral Absence

Absence of a heading is itself a form of bias. Identify gaps and propose or supplement rather than accepting silence.

### Assuming One Vocabulary Fits All Communities

General vocabularies underrepresent many groups. Evaluate specialized and community-sourced vocabularies for the topics they cover better.

### Failing To Track And Apply Vocabulary Revisions

Vocabularies change. Not tracking revisions leaves new cataloging stuck on outdated terms and legacy cleanup perpetually deferred.

### Making Changes Without Documentation

Undocumented local terms and rewrites become unexplainable. Record rationale, mappings, and review schedules.

## Self-Check

- Has the assigned heading been evaluated for historical bias, default-perspective framing, or offensive terminology?
- Is the most respectful current terminology available in the vocabulary being used for new cataloging?
- Do local deviations preserve collocation through cross-references and documented mappings to standard terms?
- Has the vocabulary been checked for coverage gaps affecting the communities and topics in the collection?
- Are offensive legacy headings handled with a deliberate, documented strategy rather than ignored or silently deleted?
- Are local vocabulary decisions documented with rationale, mappings, dates, and review schedules?
- Is the institution engaging with vocabulary maintenance processes to push respectful terms and gap-filling upstream?
- Where general vocabularies underrepresent a community, has a specialized or community-sourced vocabulary been considered and its source recorded?
