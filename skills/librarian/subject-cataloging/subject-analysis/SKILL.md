# Subject Analysis

Subject analysis is the intellectual work of deciding what a resource is about before any heading is assigned. It looks like reading and summarizing, but it is a series of judgment calls that determine whether users can ever find the work by topic. A work is rarely about one thing. It has a primary topic, secondary topics, a form, a time period, a place, and often an audience or purpose, and the cataloger must decide which of these deserve access points and which are merely incidental. Subject analysis done well yields headings that collocate like works and lead users from their own vocabulary to the controlled vocabulary. Done poorly, it produces headings that are too broad, too narrow, biased, or simply wrong, and the work becomes invisible to the users who need it.

Use this skill when analyzing a resource for subject content, deciding which topics warrant headings, determining depth of subject analysis, or resolving what a complex or ambiguous work is really about. The goal is to prevent the agent from assigning headings mechanically from the title, over-assigning to inflate discoverability, importing bias into subject access, or missing the topics that the work's actual audience would search for.

## Core Rules

### Analyze The Work, Not Just The Title

The title is a starting point, not the analysis. Titles are often figurative, partial, or marketing-oriented, and relying on them produces shallow or misleading subject access.

Sources for analysis:

- table of contents and chapter headings;
- preface, introduction, or abstract;
- summaries on covers, jackets, or publisher descriptions;
- index entries and key terms;
- illustrations, captions, and figures;
- reviews and reference sources for ambiguous works;
- the actual content when time and item value permit.

Read enough of the work to understand its real intellectual content. A title like "The Long Shadow" tells the cataloger nothing; the content reveals it is about the legacy of slavery, economic inequality, or a military campaign.

### Identify The Of-About Distinction

Many works are about a topic in service of a deeper purpose. A book about chess may be about chess, or it may use chess to teach strategic thinking, or it may be a memoir about a chess player's life. The cataloger must identify what the work is fundamentally about.

Test the distinction:

- of: the subject the work explains, depicts, or studies;
- about: the deeper argument, theme, or purpose;
- form: whether it is fiction, history, criticism, instruction, or reference.

A biography is about a person. A novel set during a war is of that war but about its characters and themes unless the work is studied as war literature. Record the of, and consider the about when it is the work's primary intellectual contribution.

### Determine Primary Versus Secondary Topics

Most works have one or two primary topics and several secondary ones. Assigning every topic equally dilutes collocation and confuses ranking in search.

Heuristics for primary topics:

- the topic that dominates the work's structure and extent;
- the topic a user would name when asking for this work;
- the topic that, if removed, would change the work's identity;
- the topic emphasized in the summary, contents, and index.

Assign full subject headings to primary topics. Consider secondary topics for additional headings only when they are substantial and searchable, not when they are passing references. A single chapter on a subtopic rarely warrants its own heading unless the work is a collection.

### Decide How Many Subjects To Assign

Too few subjects hide the work; too many scatter it and dilute precision. The right number depends on the work's complexity and the collection's depth.

Guidance:

- most works warrant two to four subject headings;
- complex or multidisciplinary works may warrant more;
- a single heading is acceptable for narrowly focused works;
- avoid assigning headings for every chapter or section;
- match the count to the cataloging level and item value.

The goal is not exhaustive coverage but reliable discovery. A heading that serves no realistic search should be reconsidered.

### Consider The Form And Genre Separately

What a work is about is distinct from what it is. A history of architecture is about architecture and is a history. Form and genre may be captured in separate access points or facets.

Separate the analysis:

- topical content, what the work discusses;
- form or genre, the type of work, biography, fiction, poetry, atlas;
- audience, juvenile, scholarly, popular;
- treatment, handbooks, catalogs, exhibition guides.

Conflating form with topic produces headings like "Architecture" for a building catalog that is really a pictorial work. Keep the dimensions distinct so users can filter by what the work is as well as what it is about.

### Analyze For The Intended Audience's Vocabulary

Subject access serves users, not the cataloger. The headings must connect to the terms users actually search, even when those differ from the work's specialized vocabulary.

Consider:

- would a general user find this work under the assigned heading;
- does the heading bridge from common terms to the controlled vocabulary;
- are there see references from natural-language terms to the authorized heading;
- is the heading too technical for the work's likely audience.

A medical textbook for practitioners may use precise MeSH terms. A consumer health book needs headings that connect lay terms like "stomach ache" to the authorized vocabulary.

### Recognize And Counter Bias In Subject Analysis

Subject analysis inherits bias from the cataloger, the source vocabulary, and the work itself. Unexamined, this embeds bias into discovery.

Bias surfaces as:

- assuming a dominant perspective as default, "women astronauts" versus "astronauts";
- using outdated or offensive terminology still present in legacy vocabularies;
- assigning identity-based headings only to works about marginalized groups, treating dominant groups as unmarked;
- centering one culture's framing of an event or concept;
- under-analyzing works by or about underrepresented groups.

Counter bias by analyzing consistently across all works, using current and respectful terminology where the vocabulary permits, documenting local practice for sensitive terms, and reviewing legacy headings for revision.

### Document The Basis For Difficult Assignments

When a work's subject is genuinely ambiguous or contested, document the reasoning so the assignment is defensible and revisable.

Record:

- the sources consulted for the analysis;
- why a primary topic was chosen over alternatives;
- any local interpretation of a vocabulary rule;
- unresolved uncertainty for future review.

Documentation turns a private judgment into an institutional decision that survives scrutiny and staff turnover.

## Common Traps

### Assigning Subjects From The Title Alone

Titles are unreliable evidence. A poetic or catchy title yields meaningless headings. Analyze the content, not the cover.

### Over-Assigning To Maximize Discoverability

Assigning a heading for every minor topic dilutes collocation and lowers precision. Users get noise, not better access.

### Missing The Work's Primary Intellectual Contribution

A work that uses a topic as a vehicle for a deeper argument needs a heading for the argument, not just the vehicle.

### Confusing Form With Topic

A pictorial work, a biography, or a novel is a form. Recording only the topical content hides the form from users who filter by type.

### Importing Cataloger Bias As Neutral Analysis

Treating one perspective as default or assigning identity headings only to marginalized works embeds bias into the catalog. Analyze consistently and use respectful current terms.

### Using Vocabulary The Audience Would Not Search

A heading too technical or too narrow for the work's users defeats discovery. Bridge from common terms through references.

### Ignoring Secondary Topics That Users Would Search

The reverse of over-assignment: omitting a substantial secondary topic that a real user would search hides the work from a legitimate need.

### Treating Subject Analysis As Mechanical

Subject analysis is intellectual work requiring judgment. Applying headings by rote from a list or a vendor record produces shallow access.

## Self-Check

- Is the analysis based on the work's content, not just the title or publisher description?
- Is the primary topic distinguished from secondary topics and from the work's deeper purpose?
- Is the number of headings appropriate to the work's complexity, neither exhaustive nor sparse?
- Are form, genre, audience, and treatment analyzed separately from topical content?
- Do the headings connect to the vocabulary the work's intended audience would actually search?
- Has bias been considered, including default perspectives, outdated terms, and inconsistent assignment to marginalized groups?
- Are ambiguous or contested assignments documented with the basis for the decision?
- Would a user asking for this work by topic find it through the assigned headings?
