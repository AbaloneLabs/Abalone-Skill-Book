# Research Data Management Support

Research data is the underlying evidence on which scholarship is built, and funders, journals, and institutions increasingly require that data be managed, documented, and shared, which has moved data management from an optional good practice to a required competency that researchers must demonstrate and that libraries increasingly support. The defining risk in data management support is treating it as a filing task, when in fact research data management is a discipline that spans the entire research lifecycle, from planning (data management plans for grants), through collection and organization (file formats, naming, documentation), to preservation and sharing (repositories, metadata, licensing), with each stage carrying decisions that determine whether the data remains usable, verifiable, and shareable. A researcher who produces data without a management plan discovers, at publication or audit, that the data is undocumented, in proprietary formats, or impossible to share, which can block publication, violate funder requirements, or render the research unverifiable. The discipline is to support researchers in planning data management before collection, in organizing and documenting data during research, and in preserving and sharing data responsibly, while recognizing that the librarian supports data stewardship rather than doing the researcher's data analysis.

Use this skill when helping researchers or students with research data management, data management plans, data organization and sharing, or data stewardship. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating data management as a filing task, and from neglecting the planning, documentation, format, and sharing decisions that determine whether data remains usable and compliant.

## Core Rules

### Support Data Management Planning Before Collection

Data management is most effective when planned before data collection, and the data management plan (DMP) is the primary tool:

- Funder requirements: funders (NSF, NIH, national and foundation funders) require DMPs with specific elements; know the funder's requirements and help the researcher address each.
- DMP elements: a complete DMP covers what data will be produced, how it will be documented, what formats will be used, how it will be stored and backed up, how it will be shared, and how it will be preserved.
- Realistic planning: the DMP must reflect the researcher's actual practice; a plan the researcher cannot execute is non-compliant in substance even if it passes review.
- DMP tools: use DMP tools and templates that align with funder requirements and guide the researcher through the elements.

Help researchers plan data management at the proposal stage, because retrofitting management onto already-collected data is far harder and often incomplete.

### Guide Data Organization, Documentation, And Metadata

Data must be organized and documented to remain usable by the researcher, collaborators, and future users:

- File organization: consistent file naming, folder structures, and version control prevent confusion and loss; establish conventions early.
- Documentation and readme files: document what the data is, how it was collected, what each variable means, and any processing; a readme file accompanying the data is essential.
- Metadata standards: where discipline-specific metadata standards exist, use them; standardized metadata makes data discoverable and interoperable in repositories.
- Codebooks and data dictionaries: for quantitative data, codebooks and data dictionaries define variables, codes, and units; for qualitative data, documentation explains coding and context.

Undocumented data becomes unusable, often within months, even to the researcher who produced it. Support documentation as an integral part of research, not an afterthought.

### Advise On Data Formats And Preservation

Data formats determine long-term usability, and format choices should favor preservation:

- Open vs. proprietary formats: prefer open, non-proprietary formats (CSV, TXT, TIFF, open geospatial formats) for preservation and sharing; proprietary formats may become unreadable as software changes.
- Preservation formats: for long-term preservation, choose stable, widely-supported formats; migrate data from at-risk formats before they become unreadable.
- Raw vs. processed data: preserve raw data alongside processed versions, with documentation of processing steps, so the research is verifiable and reanalyzable.
- Versioning: maintain clear versioning of datasets, so that the version underlying a publication is identifiable and retrievable.

Format choices made during research determine whether data survives. Guide researchers toward preservation-friendly formats and away from at-risk ones.

### Support Data Sharing And Repository Deposit

Funders and journals increasingly require data sharing, and repository deposit is the primary mechanism:

- Repository selection: help researchers choose an appropriate repository, considering discipline, data type, funder requirements, and the repository's certification (CoreTrustSeal) and preservation commitments.
- General vs. domain repositories: domain repositories offer discipline-specific metadata and discovery; general repositories (Zenodo, Dryad, institutional repositories) accept any data type; match the repository to the data and community.
- Licensing: apply an appropriate data license (often CC0 or CC-BY) so that others know how they may reuse the data; unlicensed data is legally ambiguous.
- Persistent identifiers: ensure the deposited data receives a persistent identifier (DOI) so it can be cited and located reliably.

Data sharing is increasingly mandatory. Support repository selection, licensing, and citation so that sharing is compliant and the data is discoverable and reusable.

### Respect Privacy, Consent, And Ethical Restrictions

Not all data can be shared freely, and ethical and legal restrictions must be respected:

- Human subjects data: data involving human subjects may be restricted by consent, IRB approval, and privacy; sensitive data may require de-identification, restricted-access repositories, or may not be shareable at all.
- Privacy and de-identification: ensure that ostensibly anonymized data cannot be re-identified, especially with the combination of multiple datasets; de-identification is harder than it appears.
- Legal restrictions: some data is restricted by law (health data under HIPAA, data with contractual restrictions); respect these limits in any sharing plan.
- Controlled access: where sensitive data has research value, controlled-access repositories allow vetted researchers to use it under conditions, balancing access and protection.

Data sharing requirements do not override privacy, consent, and legal restrictions. Help researchers navigate the tension, choosing appropriate sharing mechanisms for sensitive data.

### Distinguish Data Management Support From Data Analysis

The librarian supports data stewardship, not the researcher's analysis:

- Stewardship support: help with planning, organization, documentation, formats, sharing, and preservation.
- Analysis boundary: do not perform the researcher's statistical analysis, interpret their results, or choose their methods; that is the researcher's work, possibly with a data scientist or methodologist.
- Referral: where the researcher needs analysis support, refer to statistical consulting, data science services, or disciplinary methods experts.

Support the data's management and sharing without taking over the research analysis. The line is between enabling responsible stewardship and substituting for the researcher's intellectual work.

## Common Traps

### Treating Data Management As Filing

Organizing files without planning, documentation, or format choices. The trap is that filing feels sufficient, but undocumented or poorly formatted data becomes unusable.

### DMPs That Do Not Reflect Practice

Plans written to satisfy funders but not executed. The trap is that the plan passes review, but non-execution is substantive non-compliance.

### Proprietary And At-Risk Formats

Data in formats that become unreadable. The trap is that proprietary formats feel normal, but they threaten long-term usability.

### Sharing Without Privacy Review

Depositing sensitive data without de-identification or access controls. The trap is that sharing feels compliant, but privacy violations create legal and ethical harm.

### Unlicensed Or Poorly Documented Deposits

Data in repositories without licenses or metadata. The trap is that the deposit feels complete, but unlicensed data is legally ambiguous and undocumented data is unusable.

### Doing The Researcher's Analysis

Performing statistical analysis or interpreting results. The trap is that analysis feels supportive, but it substitutes the librarian's judgment for the researcher's work.

## Self-Check

- Is data management planning supported before collection, with DMPs aligned to funder requirements and reflecting realistic practice?
- Is data organization, documentation (readme files, codebooks), and metadata guided so the data remains usable to the researcher and others?
- Are data formats chosen for preservation (open, stable) with raw data preserved alongside processed versions?
- Is data sharing supported through appropriate repository selection, licensing, persistent identifiers, and citation?
- Are privacy, consent, IRB, and legal restrictions respected, with sensitive data handled through de-identification or controlled access?
- Is the boundary between data stewardship and data analysis maintained, with referral for analysis support?
- Has the researcher left with a data management approach that is compliant, documented, preservation-friendly, and shareable where appropriate?
- Has the researcher been enabled to steward their own data, rather than made dependent on the librarian for ongoing management?
