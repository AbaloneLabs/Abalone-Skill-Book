# GIS And Geospatial Reference

Geospatial data, maps that carry data, and the GIS tools that analyze them have become standard research infrastructure across disciplines from public health to history to urban planning, and many libraries now offer geospatial services that go beyond traditional map reference into data discovery, software support, and spatial analysis guidance. The defining risk in geospatial reference is treating it like ordinary data reference, when in fact geospatial data carries a spatial dimension, coordinate systems, projections, scale, resolution, and attribute accuracy, that determines whether the data can be correctly used, combined, or interpreted. A patron who overlays two datasets without understanding that they use different coordinate systems, or who analyzes data at a scale and resolution that does not support their conclusion, produces maps that look convincing but are spatially wrong. A second risk is overstepping from geospatial support into performing the patron's analysis: a librarian can help a patron find data, use the software, and understand spatial concepts, but the patron's research question and its interpretation are the patron's own. The discipline is to provide geospatial data with the spatial context that makes it usable, to support software and concept literacy, and to help patrons avoid the spatial errors that invalidate their work, without taking over their analysis.

Use this skill when helping patrons find, use, or analyze geospatial data, maps, or GIS tools in a library context. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating geospatial data like ordinary data, and from neglecting the coordinate systems, projections, scale, and accuracy that determine whether spatial analysis is valid.

## Core Rules

### Clarify The Patron's Spatial Question And Purpose

Geospatial questions vary widely, and the first task is to clarify what the patron is trying to do:

- The research question: what spatial phenomenon is the patron investigating, and what does the map or analysis need to show?
- The geography: what area (a neighborhood, a city, a country, the world) and what geographic level (points, lines, polygons, raster) does the analysis require?
- The purpose: coursework, research, grant proposal, community advocacy, or operational decision; the purpose shapes the appropriate data, tools, and rigor.
- Software and skill level: what GIS software (ArcGIS, QGIS, web tools) does the patron use, and what is their skill level, so support matches their capability?

Do not begin data discovery on a vague spatial question. Geospatial data and tools are complex, and an unscoped search returns data that does not fit the geography, scale, or purpose.

### Help Patrons Find The Right Geospatial Data

Geospatial data discovery requires knowledge of specialized portals and sources:

- Government geospatial portals: national mapping agencies, census geography, and open data portals are primary sources for administrative boundaries, transportation, hydrography, and demographic data.
- Domain-specific repositories: discipline-specific geospatial data (health, environment, historical) lives in specialized repositories; know the major ones for the disciplines the library serves.
- Metadata evaluation: geospatial data must be evaluated through its metadata for source, date, accuracy, scale, and lineage; teach patrons to read metadata, not just download files.
- Licensing and attribution: geospatial data carries usage terms; ensure patrons understand and can comply with attribution and reuse restrictions.

Match the data to the patron's geography, topic, and scale. A dataset covering the wrong geography or at the wrong scale is useless regardless of its topical relevance.

### Convey Coordinate Systems, Projections, And Scale

Every geospatial dataset has a spatial reference, and the patron must understand it to use the data correctly:

- Coordinate systems and datums: data is referenced to a coordinate system and datum; datasets in different systems must be transformed to align, or overlays and analyses will be wrong.
- Projections: projecting the earth onto a flat surface distorts area, shape, distance, or direction; choose a projection appropriate to the analysis (equal-area for density, equidistant for distance).
- Scale and resolution: data captured at a certain scale and resolution supports analysis only to that level; analyzing small-scale data at fine resolution produces false precision.
- Attribute accuracy: the non-spatial data attached to geospatial features has its own accuracy and currency; evaluate it as rigorously as the spatial component.

A map that looks right but uses mismatched coordinate systems or inappropriate projections is spatially invalid. Teach patrons these concepts so their analyses are defensible.

### Support GIS Software Use And Literacy

GIS software is complex, and patrons need support to use it effectively:

- Software availability: know what GIS software the library or institution provides (ArcGIS, QGIS, web GIS), including licensing and access.
- Core skills: help patrons with core tasks (data import, projection management, attribute table use, symbology, layout) that underlie most analyses.
- Tutorials and training: point patrons to tutorials, workshops, and documentation for deeper learning, rather than performing every task for them.
- Troubleshooting: help patrons diagnose common errors (coordinate system mismatches, attribute joins, topology problems) that derail analyses.

Support the patron's growing competence with the software. The goal is to enable patrons to do their own work, not to make them dependent on the librarian for every step.

### Distinguish Geospatial Support From Performing The Analysis

The librarian's role is to support geospatial work, not to do the patron's research:

- Data and tool support: help find data, use software, understand concepts, and troubleshoot.
- Methodological boundary: do not design the patron's analysis, choose their methods, or interpret their results; that is the patron's research, possibly with a methodologist or advisor.
- Referral: where the patron needs methodological guidance beyond geospatial tools, refer to disciplinary experts, GIS specialists, or research methods support.

Support the patron's analysis without substituting for it. The line is between enabling geospatial competence and taking over the patron's intellectual work.

### Handle Historical Maps And Geospatial Special Collections

Many libraries hold historical map collections with distinct reference needs:

- Historical map access: historical maps may be in special collections with access and handling requirements; know the collection and its access procedures.
- Georeferencing: historical maps can be georeferenced (aligned to real-world coordinates) for overlay with modern data; understand the process and its limitations.
- Historical geospatial data: historical boundaries, place names, and features differ from modern ones; use historical sources for historical analysis, not modern data projected backward.
- Preservation and reproduction: historical maps may have reproduction and use restrictions; understand and communicate them.

Historical geospatial reference is a distinct service. Know the collections, the georeferencing possibilities, and the historical data sources, and do not impose modern geography on historical questions.

## Common Traps

### Searching On A Vague Spatial Question

Providing data without clarifying geography, scale, and purpose. The trap is that geospatial data feels findable, but the wrong geography or scale makes it useless.

### Ignoring Coordinate Systems And Projections

Overlapping datasets in different systems without transformation. The trap is that the map looks fine, but the spatial analysis is invalid.

### Analyzing Beyond The Data's Scale And Resolution

Fine-grained analysis on coarse data. The trap is that the tools allow it, but the data does not support the precision claimed.

### Performing The Patron's Analysis

Designing methods or interpreting results for the patron. The trap is that analysis feels supportive, but it substitutes the librarian's judgment for the patron's research.

### Software Dependence

Doing every task for the patron rather than building their competence. The trap is that help feels generous, but it creates dependence rather than capability.

### Imposing Modern Geography On Historical Questions

Using modern boundaries and place names for historical analysis. The trap is that modern data feels authoritative, but historical questions need historical sources.

## Self-Check

- Is the patron's spatial question clarified for geography, level, purpose, and software skill before data discovery?
- Is the right geospatial data found through specialized portals, with metadata evaluated for source, date, accuracy, scale, and lineage?
- Are coordinate systems, datums, projections, scale, and resolution conveyed so the patron's analysis is spatially valid?
- Is GIS software use supported through core skills, tutorials, and troubleshooting, building the patron's competence?
- Is the boundary between geospatial support and performing the analysis maintained, with referral for methodological guidance?
- For historical map and geospatial collections, are access procedures, georeferencing, historical data sources, and reproduction rules understood?
- Has the patron left with data and understanding that support a defensible spatial analysis, including its spatial reference and limitations?
- Has the patron been enabled to do their own work, rather than made dependent on the librarian for analysis?
