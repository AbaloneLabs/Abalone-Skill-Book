# Appeal Factors And Read-Alikes

Reader advisory is not the same skill as reference work. A reference transaction ends when the patron has the right information. A reader-advisory transaction succeeds when the patron leaves with a book they will actually enjoy, and ideally comes back for more. The mistake most people make, including many librarians, is recommending books the way they recommend to themselves: by genre, by author, or by personal taste. That works occasionally and fails constantly, because two readers who love the same book often love it for opposite reasons. The professional foundation of reader advisory is appeal factors, the experiential qualities that make a book satisfying to a particular reader, and the practice of matching those factors rather than matching titles or genres. Readers' advisory built on appeal factors serves far more readers far more accurately than recommendations built on personal enthusiasm.

Use this skill whenever a patron is looking for something to read for pleasure, when you are building read-alike lists or displays, and when a reader is stuck after finishing a book they loved. The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending based on its own taste, from over-relying on genre labels, and from handing a reader a book that shares a category with a beloved title but none of its appeal.

## Core Rules

### Ask What The Reader Liked And Why

The starting point of good reader advisory is learning what the reader has enjoyed and what specifically they enjoyed about it. The "why" matters more than the "what," because the why reveals the appeal factors.

Ask about:

- a recent book they loved, and what they loved about it;
- a book they disliked, and what turned them off (dislikes are as diagnostic as likes);
- the mood they are seeking right now (escapist, challenging, comforting, suspenseful);
- format preferences (print, audio, e-book) and time available;
- content limits (violence, sex, language) they want to avoid;
- whether they want to stay in a familiar zone or try something new.

Listen for the experiential language: "I couldn't put it down," "I loved the world," "the characters felt real," "it made me think." These phrases point to the appeal factors that drive the match.

### Identify Appeal Factors Rather Than Genre

Appeal factors describe how a book feels to read. They are more predictive of satisfaction than genre, because the same genre contains books of wildly different appeal. Genre says what shelf a book sits on; appeal says whether a given reader will enjoy reading it.

Core appeal factors:

- pacing: fast and plot-driven versus slow and reflective;
- characterization: richly developed, interior characters versus broad casts or archetypal figures; character-driven versus plot-driven;
- story line: linear and eventful versus layered, ambiguous, or episodic; emphasis on plot, on relationships, or on ideas;
- setting: vivid and immersive world versus incidental backdrop; real place, historical period, or invented world;
- language: lyrical, literary, or experimental versus plain and transparent;
- tone: dark, hopeful, funny, eerie, comforting, suspenseful, melancholic;
- point of view and voice: intimate first person, sweeping omniscient, multiple perspectives.

A reader who loved a thriller for its breakneck pace may also love a fast-paced science fiction or historical novel; a reader who loved a literary novel for its language may love a memoir or narrative nonfiction with the same prose quality. Appeal crosses genre boundaries.

### Build Read-Alikes On Shared Appeal, Not Shared Genre

A read-alike is a book that shares the appeal factors a reader valued, not necessarily the genre. The strongest read-alikes often come from outside the original book's genre, because they recapture the experience without repeating the plot.

To build read-alikes:

- name the appeal factors the reader valued in the source book;
- find books that share those factors, in any genre or format;
- prepare a small set of options spanning a range (a close match, a stretch, and something different but appealing);
- be ready to explain why each option fits, in terms of appeal.

Professional tools, NoveList, genre guides, and curated lists support this, but the reasoning is appeal-based, not category-based.

### Use A Mix Of Tools And Your Own Reading

Reader advisory draws on professional tools, community knowledge, and the librarian's own reading. Relying on only one source narrows the recommendations.

Use:

- readers' advisory databases and read-alike tools;
- award and best-of lists;
- reviews in professional and trade sources;
- staff picks and patron reviews;
- your own reading and that of colleagues;
- the reader's own suggestions to extend.

Be honest about what you have and have not read. Recommending a book you have not read is fine if you are drawing on reliable appeal descriptions, but do not fabricate personal enthusiasm.

### Offer Choice, Not A Single Prescription

Readers differ in how much guidance they want. Some want one strong recommendation; most benefit from a small, curated set of three to five options with a sentence on each. Offering choice respects the reader's agency and hedges against a mismatch.

Frame options by appeal:

- "If you want more of that breakneck pace, try X.";
- "If you loved the setting, Y goes deeper into a similar world.";
- "If you want something that feels different but has that same emotional pull, try Z.".

Avoid dumping a long undifferentiated list. A pile of titles is not reader advisory; it is a search result.

### Include Fiction And Narrative Nonfiction

Many readers who think they want fiction are delighted by narrative nonfiction with strong appeal, and vice versa. A history written with novelistic pacing, a memoir with literary language, or a science book with a vivid setting can satisfy a fiction reader. Cross the fiction-nonfiction boundary when appeal supports it.

### Respect The Reader's Taste Without Judgment

Readers read what they read for their own reasons. Genre fiction, series, comfort re-reads, young adult books read by adults, and light reading are all legitimate. Reader advisory is not an occasion to upgrade the reader's taste or steer them toward what the librarian considers worthwhile. Recommend within the reader's expressed interests, and offer stretch options only when welcomed.

### Handle "I Don't Know What I Want" With Open Exploration

Some readers cannot name a book they liked or want something completely different. Meet them with open, low-pressure questions: what was the last thing you enjoyed, on screen or in print? What mood are you after? Offer a few widely appealing options across tones and let the reader react. Their reactions ("not that," "maybe something like that") are diagnostic.

### Follow Up And Build A Reading Relationship

Reader advisory works best as a relationship over time. Invite the reader to come back and report what worked. What they liked and disliked refines future recommendations and builds loyalty. Keep light notes only where policy and privacy allow, and never record more than needed.

## Common Traps

### Recommending From Personal Taste

What you love is not what every reader loves. Match appeal factors, not your favorites.

### Matching Genre Instead Of Appeal

Two books in the same genre can have opposite appeal. Genre labels are a starting point, not a match.

### Overloading The Reader With Titles

A long list overwhelms. Curate a few options with appeal-based explanations.

### Judging Or Upgrading Taste

Steering a reader toward "better" books betrays the service. Respect the reader's interests.

### Forgetting Narrative Nonfiction

Strong appeal often crosses the fiction-nonfiction line. Offer it.

### Ignoring Dislikes

A book the reader hated is as informative as one they loved. Ask why it failed.

### Fabricating Personal Reading

Honesty about what you have read builds trust. Use appeal descriptions for unread titles rather than false enthusiasm.

### Recommending Without Considering Format And Limits

A great match in the wrong format, or with content the reader wants to avoid, still fails. Ask about format and content limits.

## Self-Check

- Did you ask what the reader liked and specifically why, including dislikes and current mood?
- Did you identify appeal factors (pace, characterization, story, setting, language, tone, voice) rather than relying on genre?
- Did you build read-alikes on shared appeal, including options outside the source genre?
- Did you use a mix of professional tools, lists, reviews, and honest personal reading knowledge?
- Did you offer a small curated set with appeal-based explanations rather than a single title or a long list?
- Did you include narrative nonfiction where appeal supports it?
- Did you respect the reader's taste without judgment or attempts to upgrade it?
- Did you handle "I don't know what I want" with open, diagnostic questions?
- Did you invite follow-up to refine future recommendations, respecting privacy in any notes?
