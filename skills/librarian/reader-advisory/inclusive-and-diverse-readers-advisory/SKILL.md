# Inclusive And Diverse Readers' Advisory

Readers' advisory that serves only the mainstream fails a large share of the community and reinforces whose stories count. Inclusive readers' advisory actively seeks out and recommends books by and about people of color, LGBTQ+ readers, disabled readers, religious and ethnic minorities, immigrants, and other underrepresented communities, both to reflect those readers back to themselves and to expand every reader's world. Done well, it is one of the most powerful equity tools a library has. Done carelessly, it reduces marginalized authors to tokens, leans on stereotypes, or treats a whole identity as a single story. The skill is to recommend diversely with intention, accuracy, and respect, recognizing that representation is a craft as much as a value.

Use this skill whenever you are building displays, lists, or recommendations; whenever you serve a reader from an underrepresented community; and whenever you audit your readers' advisory practice for whose voices appear and whose do not. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to mainstream and white-authored lists, from recommending diverse books uncritically, and from treating marginalized authors as spokespeople rather than artists.

## Core Rules

### Treat Inclusion As Intentional Practice, Not An Accident

Diverse books do not appear in recommendations by default. Mainstream publishing, review attention, and bestseller lists skew toward established, majority voices. Inclusive readers' advisory requires deliberate effort to seek out, read about, and recommend books by and about underrepresented communities.

Build inclusive practice by:

- actively following diverse publishers, imprints, and award lists (e.g., awards centered on specific communities);
- reading reviews and blogs by reviewers from the communities represented;
- tracking own-voices and #OwnVoices-adjacent recommendations while understanding the term's limits;
- building standing lists of diverse titles across genres and formats;
- auditing your displays and recommendation sets for representation before they go public.

Inclusion that is not intentional reverts to the mainstream under time pressure.

### Distinguish Authentic Representation From Stereotype And Tokenism

Not every book that includes a marginalized character represents that community well. Some rely on stereotypes, single stories, or outsider framing. Inclusive readers' advisory requires evaluating representation, not just counting identities.

Evaluate representation by asking:

- who wrote the book, and from what relationship to the experience (own-voices is a strong signal but not the only one)?;
- are the marginalized characters fully human, with interiority, agency, and variety, or reduced to a trait or a lesson?;
- does the book rely on a single story that flattens a diverse community into one experience?;
- does it reproduce harmful tropes (the magical marginalized mentor, the tragedy-only narrative, the assimilation-as-success arc)?;
- is the book endorsed or criticized by readers from the community it represents?

A diverse list built on stereotyped titles does more harm than a homogenous one, because it teaches distorted images under the banner of inclusion.

### Avoid Treating Marginalized Authors As Spokespeople

A harmful inversion of inclusive advisory is treating every book by a marginalized author as a representative statement about their identity. Marginalized authors write across genres, tones, and subjects, including books that have nothing to do with their marginalization. Recommending a Black author only for issue books, or a queer author only for coming-out stories, flattens their work and burdens them with a representative role majority authors never carry.

Recommend marginalized authors:

- across the full range of their work, including joy, genre fiction, and everyday stories;
- for appeal factors the same way you would any author;
- without assuming the reader wants an identity-themed book;
- while still making own-voices identity-specific titles available to readers who seek them.

Balance matters: do not erase identity-specific work, but do not reduce the author to it.

### Serve Diverse Readers By Reflecting Them And Expanding Them

Readers from underrepresented communities deserve to see themselves in the collection and to read beyond themselves. Both matter. Reflective reading affirms identity and belonging; expansive reading builds empathy and broadens horizons. Inclusive advisory serves both goals.

For readers seeking mirror books:

- offer authentic own-voices and community-endorsed titles;
- avoid pushing "classics" about them written by outsiders as defaults;
- respect that some readers want joy and everyday stories, not only trauma narratives.

For all readers:

- regularly include diverse titles in general recommendations, not only in dedicated "diversity" lists;
- normalize diversity in displays so it is not segregated into a single month or shelf.

### Audit Displays, Lists, And Recommendations For Representation

Inclusion must be visible in the outputs of readers' advisory, not just the intention. Audit before publishing.

Audit for:

- author demographics across the set;
- character and subject representation;
- genre and format diversity within diverse titles (not only issue books);
- balance of joy, everyday life, and struggle in the stories represented;
- avoidance of a single story for any community;
- whether diverse titles appear in general displays or only in segregated ones.

A quick audit catches defaults: if every author on a general fiction list is white and straight, the list is not general, it is mainstream, and it needs revision.

### Respect That Identity Is Not Monolithic

No community is a single bloc. A "diverse" book about one experience within a community may not represent others in that community, and readers within a community have their own tastes that have nothing to do with identity. Do not assume a reader wants a book about their identity, and do not assume one title speaks for a whole group.

Ask the reader what they want rather than assigning identity-based recommendations. Offer identity-specific options as one strand among many.

### Handle Sensitive And Challenged Material Thoughtfully

Diverse books are disproportionately challenged and censored. Inclusive advisory includes defending access to them, knowing your library's reconsideration policy, and supporting readers who seek them, including minors whose families may differ from the library's values. Follow intellectual freedom principles and library policy, and be prepared to explain why diverse materials belong in the collection.

### Keep Learning And Accept Correction

Representation standards evolve, and communities disagree internally about what counts as authentic. Stay humble, keep reading from diverse sources, and accept correction from community members gracefully. Inclusive advisory is a practice of ongoing learning, not a fixed expertise.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Mainstream Lists Under Time Pressure

Without intentional effort, recommendations revert to majority voices. Build standing diverse lists to draw on.

### Counting Identities Without Evaluating Representation

A stereotyped diverse title can harm more than help. Evaluate authenticity, not just presence.

### Reducing Marginalized Authors To Identity Issue Books

Marginalized authors write across genres and subjects. Recommend their full range.

### Segregating Diverse Books Into A Single Shelf Or Month

Diversity belongs in general displays and lists, not only in dedicated observances.

### Assuming A Reader Wants Books About Their Identity

Readers have tastes beyond identity. Ask rather than assign.

### Treating One Book As Representative Of A Whole Community

Communities are not monolithic. Avoid the single story.

### Pushing Only Trauma Narratives

Communities deserve joy, everyday life, and genre fiction too, not only struggle stories.

### Presenting As An Expert On Communities You Are Not Part Of

Stay humble, read from community sources, and accept correction.

## Self-Check

- Did you treat inclusion as intentional practice, seeking out and tracking diverse titles across genres?
- Did you evaluate representation for authenticity, full humanity, and freedom from stereotypes, not just count identities?
- Did you avoid treating marginalized authors as spokespeople, recommending their full range of work?
- Did you serve diverse readers with both mirror books and expansive reading, respecting their tastes?
- Did you audit displays, lists, and recommendations for representation before publishing?
- Did you respect that identity is not monolithic and avoid assuming what a reader wants?
- Did you handle sensitive or challenged material in line with intellectual freedom and library policy?
- Did you stay open to learning and correction from community members?
- Did you normalize diversity in general displays rather than segregating it?
