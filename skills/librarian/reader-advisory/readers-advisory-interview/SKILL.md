# Readers' Advisory Interview

The readers' advisory interview is a distinct skill from the reference interview. A reference interview clarifies an information need that can be verified; a readers' advisory interview explores a subjective taste that cannot. There is no "correct" book for a leisure reader, only a book that fits this reader at this moment. The interview's job is to surface enough about the reader's preferences, mood, and reading history to make a good match, without turning a pleasurable browse into an interrogation. Readers who feel questioned, judged, or steered will shut down; readers who feel heard and offered appealing choices will return. The interview is where reader advisory succeeds or fails, before any title is named.

Use this skill at the start of any readers' advisory interaction, when a patron asks for "something good to read," when working with reluctant or returning readers, and when a recommendation has fallen flat. The goal is to prevent the agent from jumping to titles before understanding the reader, from interrogating rather than conversing, and from letting personal taste or assumptions about "good" books drive the recommendation.

## Core Rules

### Open The Conversation Warmly And Without Pressure

A reader asking for help is often unsure how to describe what they want. The opening sets the tone. Make it easy to talk, low-stakes, and welcoming.

Open with:

- an open, friendly prompt: "What have you enjoyed reading lately?";
- an invitation to think broadly, including screen as well as print: "Books, or are you open to audio or shows too?";
- reassurance that not knowing is fine: "It's totally okay if you can't name a title; we can figure it out together.".

Avoid opening with a closed question ("Do you like mysteries?") that funnels the reader prematurely. Let them describe before you categorize.

### Learn Taste Through Likes, Dislikes, And Mood

Taste is revealed by what a reader loved, what they abandoned, and what they are in the mood for now. All three are diagnostic.

Explore:

- recent books or series they loved, and what specifically worked;
- books they started and quit, and what lost them (dislikes are gold);
- the mood they are seeking: escapist, gripping, comforting, thought-provoking, funny;
- how much time or attention they have (a quick vacation read versus a dense immersive one);
- format and access (print, e-book, audio; available now versus willing to wait);
- content boundaries (violence, explicit content, language) they want avoided.

Listen for experiential language. "I couldn't put it down" signals pace and tension; "I felt like I was there" signals setting; "the characters stayed with me" signals characterization. These phrases map to appeal factors more reliably than genre names.

### Ask Questions That Invite Story, Not Yes-No Answers

The best readers' advisory questions get the reader talking about their reading experience rather than answering a checklist. Open questions surface richer information than closed ones.

Prefer:

- "Tell me about a book you loved.";
- "What's the last thing you stayed up too late reading?";
- "What makes you put a book down?";
- "Are you in the mood for something familiar or something new?".

Use closed questions to confirm specifics once you have a direction ("Do you prefer first-person or multiple viewpoints?"), not to start the conversation.

### Handle Vague Requests Without Forcing Specificity

Many readers arrive with only "something good" or "a quick read." Do not force them to specify before you help. Work with the vagueness by offering a small spread of options across appeals and moods, then read their reactions.

Strategy for the vague request:

- acknowledge it warmly: "Let me pull a few different things and you can tell me what grabs you.";
- offer three to five options spanning tones (a fast thriller, a warm character story, an immersive historical, a funny memoir);
- watch which way the reader leans and refine from there;
- treat their reactions ("not that," "ooh maybe") as the real interview.

The reader's response to options is often more honest than their answer to a direct question about taste.

### Work With Reluctant And Returning Readers Patiently

Not every reader is confident. Reluctant readers, lapsed readers, and those who feel "behind" or ashamed of their level need extra care. Pushing literary quality or length at them backfires.

For reluctant readers:

- prioritize engagement over prestige: a page-turner they finish beats a classic they abandon;
- consider shorter formats, high-interest nonfiction, graphic novels, or audio;
- remove shame about level or pace;
- celebrate what they do read;
- offer series entry points that reward commitment.

For returning adult readers, avoid assuming a school-era reading identity. Their tastes may have changed; let them discover fresh.

### Respect Taste Without Judgment Or Upgrading

Readers' advisory is service to the reader's actual interests, not a campaign to improve them. Genre fiction, romance, westerns, series, young adult read by adults, and light reading are all valid. Do not steer a reader toward what you consider worthwhile unless they ask to stretch. Judgment, even subtle, ends the relationship.

This includes avoiding:

- surprise or condescension about what they enjoy;
- unsolicited "if you liked that, you'll love this literary classic" upgrades;
- assumptions about what a reader of their age, gender, or background "should" like.

### Recommend Across Genres And Formats When Appeal Supports It

A reader who loved one book may love its appeal in another genre or format. Offer cross-genre and cross-format options when the appeal matches: a fiction lover may adore a narrative nonfiction with the same pace; a print reader may become an audio convert with the right narrator. Do not assume format or genre loyalty unless the reader states it.

### Close With Options And An Invitation To Return

End the interaction with a curated set, an explanation of why each fits, and a clear invitation to come back. A good close builds the reading relationship and makes the next visit easier.

Close with:

- a small set of three to five options with one-sentence appeal explanations;
- a mix that includes a safe match and a stretch;
- an invitation: "Tell me what worked and what didn't, and we'll refine it next time.";
- a note on how to reach you or the service again.

### Protect Privacy In Readers' Advisory Notes

Reading taste can be sensitive: it may reveal mental health, sexuality, faith, politics, or trauma. Any notes, holds, or reading history you keep must follow the library's privacy policy. Record only what serves the reader and retain it only as long as policy allows. Do not discuss a reader's tastes where others can hear.

## Common Traps

### Jumping To Titles Before Understanding The Reader

Naming books too early funnels the reader toward your assumptions. Explore taste first.

### Interrogating Instead Of Conversing

A checklist of closed questions feels like an exam. Ask open questions that invite story.

### Forcing Specificity On A Vague Request

"I don't know what I want" is workable. Offer a spread and read the reactions.

### Upgrading The Reader's Taste

Steering toward "better" books betrays service. Respect what they actually enjoy.

### Assuming Genre Or Format Loyalty

Appeal crosses boundaries. Offer cross-genre and cross-format options when they fit.

### Overlooking Dislikes And Abandoned Books

What a reader quit is as diagnostic as what they finished. Ask why.

### Pushing Length Or Prestige On Reluctant Readers

Engagement beats quality for readers building a habit. Meet them where they are.

### Exposing Reading Taste Publicly

Taste can be sensitive. Keep notes and conversations private per policy.

## Self-Check

- Did you open warmly and without pressure, inviting the reader to describe rather than categorize?
- Did you learn taste through likes, dislikes, mood, format, and content boundaries?
- Did you ask open questions that invite story rather than a yes-no checklist?
- Did you handle vague requests by offering a spread of options and reading reactions?
- Did you work patiently with reluctant or returning readers, prioritizing engagement over prestige?
- Did you respect taste without judgment or attempts to upgrade it?
- Did you recommend across genres and formats where appeal supported it?
- Did you close with a curated set, appeal explanations, and an invitation to return?
- Did you protect the privacy of reading taste in notes, holds, and conversation?
