# Accessible Content Procurement

Most of the digital content and platforms a library offers come from vendors: e-books, databases, streaming media, discovery layers, repository software, learning materials, and embedded tools. If those products are inaccessible, the library has purchased exclusion. A blind student cannot read the e-book, a deaf patron cannot use the video, a keyboard user cannot navigate the database, and the library, not the vendor, is accountable to the patron and often to the law. Procurement is therefore the most powerful leverage point for accessibility in a library, because the moment to require accessibility is before the contract is signed, not after patrons are turned away. Once a product is purchased and integrated, fixing it is slow, expensive, and often unsuccessful.

The judgment problem is that procurement is driven by content, price, and urgency, with accessibility treated as a late question or a nice-to-have. Vendors provide VPATs, Voluntary Product Accessibility Templates, or Accessibility Conformance Reports that are often marketing documents overstating conformance. Libraries accept them at face value because evaluating them is hard and deadlines are tight. Contracts get signed without accessibility clauses, and when barriers emerge, the library has no leverage to demand remediation. The agent's job is to make accessibility a non-negotiable procurement requirement, to verify vendor claims through independent testing, to embed accessibility obligations and remedies in contracts, and to escalate when inaccessible products are being considered for core or required services.

Use this skill when acquiring, licensing, evaluating, or negotiating for any library content or platform, writing accessibility into RFPs and contracts, reviewing VPATs, testing vendor products, or deciding on procurement of content with accessibility gaps. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting vendor claims without verification, signing contracts without accessibility clauses, prioritizing price or content over access, or procuring inaccessible content for required services without alternatives.

## Core Rules

### Make Accessibility A Core Procurement Requirement

Accessibility must be a primary criterion in every digital procurement, evaluated alongside content, functionality, and price.

Requirement as core:

- include accessibility in the initial requirements, not as a final checklist;
- weight accessibility in evaluation scoring;
- treat severe inaccessibility as a disqualifier for required or core services;
- communicate to vendors that accessibility is a condition of doing business;
- educate procurement and selection committees on why this matters.

Accessibility added late is always weakened. Establish it as foundational from the first requirement.

### Write Accessibility Into RFPs And Contracts

Requirements that are not in the contract are unenforceable. Embed accessibility obligations formally.

Contract and RFP clauses:

- specify the required conformance level, commonly WCAG 2.1 or 2.2 AA;
- require a current VPAT or Accessibility Conformance Report as part of the proposal;
- require the vendor to remediate identified barriers within a defined timeline;
- require notification of known accessibility issues and updates;
- reserve the right to independent testing;
- include remedies for non-conformance, up to and including termination;
- require continued conformance through updates and new versions.

A contract without these clauses leaves the library no leverage when barriers appear. Legal review of accessibility language is worthwhile.

### Evaluate VPATs Critically, Not Acceptingly

VPATs are vendor self-reports and frequently overstate conformance. Read them critically.

Critical VPAT evaluation:

- check which WCAG version and level is claimed;
- look for "supports with exceptions" or "partially supports," which indicate real gaps;
- examine the details of exceptions, not just the summary;
- look for vague language that obscures actual barriers;
- compare claims against independent testing;
- request clarification from the vendor on any ambiguity;
- weight recent, detailed reports over old or generic ones.

A VPAT that claims full support but lists exceptions is not full support. Interpret claims against the details.

### Test Vendor Products Independently

Do not rely on vendor self-reporting. Test products with both automated and assistive technology.

Independent testing:

- request a trial or demo account for testing;
- run automated accessibility scans;
- test with screen readers such as NVDA, JAWS, or VoiceOver;
- test keyboard-only navigation;
- test on mobile and with zoom and reflow;
- test the highest-stakes workflows patrons will actually perform;
- document specific barriers with evidence.

Independent testing reveals what VPATs hide. Use the findings to negotiate remediation or reject the product.

### Prioritize Accessibility For Required And Core Content

Not all content carries the same stakes. Required and core services demand the highest accessibility scrutiny.

Stakes-based prioritization:

- course reserves and required readings must be accessible; students have no alternative;
- core databases and discovery layers used by everyone demand strong conformance;
- streaming media used in instruction must be captioned;
- institutional platforms such as the repository must be accessible;
- supplementary or niche content may be acceptable with known gaps if alternatives exist.

When content is required, inaccessibility is denial of education. Apply the strictest standard.

### Require Remediation Timelines For Known Gaps

Few products are perfect. When gaps exist, require a credible remediation plan before procuring.

Remediation requirements:

- obtain a specific timeline for fixing identified barriers;
- secure commitments in writing as part of the contract;
- tie payment or renewal to remediation progress;
- request regular accessibility updates from the vendor;
- track promised fixes and verify them;
- avoid open-ended "working on it" without dates.

A remediation promise without a timeline is not a commitment. Make it concrete and contractual.

### Provide Alternatives When Procuring Imperfect Content

Sometimes an imperfect product must be acquired, for example a unique database with no accessible equivalent. Plan for the gap.

Alternative planning:

- document the known accessibility gaps clearly;
- provide an alternative means of access, such as staff-mediated retrieval or an accessible format service;
- inform patrons of the limitations and the alternatives;
- continue pressing the vendor for remediation;
- reassess when accessible alternatives become available;
- never accept inaccessible required content without an alternative path.

Procuring imperfect content is sometimes necessary, but denying service is not. Always provide an alternative.

### Build Accessibility Into Renewal And Lifecycle Decisions

Accessibility should be reviewed at renewal, not just at initial purchase. Products and standards change.

Renewal review:

- re-test accessibility at renewal, especially after upgrades;
- check whether the vendor has met prior remediation commitments;
- reassess against current WCAG versions;
- consider replacing products that remain inaccessible;
- use renewal as leverage for remediation.

A product that was acceptable at purchase may have regressed, or standards may have advanced. Review at renewal.

### Collaborate Across The Institution

Procurement accessibility is stronger when coordinated across the institution.

Collaboration:

- work with procurement, IT, disability services, and legal;
- share accessibility findings across departments to avoid duplicate testing;
- align library requirements with institutional accessibility policy;
- leverage institutional purchasing power for stronger vendor terms;
- contribute to shared vendor accessibility evaluations.

Institutional coordination amplifies leverage and reduces redundant effort.

### Document Decisions And Maintain Records

Keep records of accessibility evaluations, vendor commitments, and decisions for accountability.

Documentation:

- retain VPATs, test results, and vendor correspondence;
- document known gaps and alternative access plans;
- record remediation commitments and their status;
- maintain an accessibility status overview of licensed products;
- use records to inform renewal and future procurement.

Documentation creates an evidence trail and supports consistent decisions over time.

## Escalation Guidance

Procurement of inaccessible content can create legal risk and deny service. Escalate in these situations:

- Any procurement of required or core content that fails accessibility testing without a credible remediation plan: escalate to leadership and procurement before signing.
- Any vendor VPAT that significantly misrepresents the product's accessibility: escalate to procurement and legal for contract or accountability action.
- Any pressure to procure inaccessible content for required services due to deadline or faculty demand: escalate to leadership and disability services to find an accessible alternative or accommodation.
- Any renewal of a product that remains inaccessible despite commitments: escalate to consider replacement or stronger leverage.
- Any situation where students or patrons are denied required content due to inaccessibility: escalate immediately to provide alternative access and address the barrier.

When required or core access is at stake, escalate before procurement is finalized.

## Common Traps

### Accessibility Added Late In Procurement

Treating accessibility as a final checkbox weakens it. Make it a core requirement from the start.

### Accepting VPATs At Face Value

VPATs are self-reports that often overstate conformance. Evaluate critically and test independently.

### Contracts Without Accessibility Clauses

Requirements not in the contract are unenforceable. Embed conformance, remediation, and remedies in writing.

### Prioritizing Content Or Price Over Access

Unique content or low price does not justify procuring exclusion, especially for required services.

### No Remediation Timeline

Promises to fix without dates are not commitments. Require specific timelines and verify progress.

### Procuring Inaccessible Required Content Without Alternatives

Acquiring imperfect content is sometimes necessary, but denying service is not. Always provide an alternative access path.

### No Renewal Review

Products regress and standards advance. Re-test accessibility at renewal and use it as leverage.

### Siloed Procurement Decisions

Acting alone weakens leverage. Coordinate with procurement, IT, disability services, and legal.

## Self-Check

- Is accessibility a core, weighted requirement in every digital procurement, evaluated from the start?
- Do RFPs and contracts specify WCAG conformance, require a current VPAT, mandate remediation timelines, reserve independent testing rights, and include remedies for non-conformance?
- Are VPATs evaluated critically, with attention to exceptions and vague language rather than summary claims?
- Is each vendor product tested independently with automated tools and assistive technology before procurement?
- Is the strictest accessibility standard applied to required and core content, where inaccessibility denies education or service?
- Are credible, contractual remediation timelines secured for known gaps, with progress tracked and verified?
- When imperfect content is procured, are known gaps documented and alternative access paths provided?
- Is accessibility reviewed at renewal, with regressions caught and leverage applied?
- Is procurement coordinated across procurement, IT, disability services, and legal for stronger leverage?
- Are evaluation records, vendor commitments, known gaps, and decisions documented for accountability and future use?
