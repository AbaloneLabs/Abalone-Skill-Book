# Assistive Technology Support

Assistive technology is what makes library computers, collections, and spaces genuinely usable for patrons with disabilities. A screen reader turns a visual interface into spoken navigation; a magnifier makes text legible to someone with low vision; captioning makes video accessible to deaf and hard-of-hearing patrons; alternative input lets someone navigate without a mouse. Providing this technology is not an optional enhancement; it is how the library fulfills its obligation to serve the whole community. But providing it well requires more than installing software on one computer. It requires the right tools, configured correctly, maintained reliably, located accessibly, and supported by staff who know how to help without making the patron feel like a problem.

The judgment problem is that assistive technology is often treated as a set-and-forget installation: one adapted workstation in a corner, software installed once and never updated, staff untrained, and no clear process when something breaks. Patrons who depend on these tools then arrive, find them broken, outdated, or unfamiliar, and leave without service, often without complaining because the burden of advocacy is exhausting. Worse, staff may be well-meaning but untrained, hovering uncomfortably or unable to troubleshoot, which humiliates the patron. The agent's job is to treat assistive technology as a core, maintained service, to train staff in respectful and competent support, and to recognize that when assistive technology fails, a patron has been denied service and the situation may require escalation.

Use this skill when planning, providing, configuring, supporting, or troubleshooting assistive technology in a library, training staff, coordinating with disability services, or developing accessible technology services. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating assistive technology as an afterthought, letting it fall into disrepair, under-training staff, intruding on patron independence, or failing to provide pathways when technology fails.

## Core Rules

### Provide A Core Suite Of Assistive Technology

Determine what the community needs and provide a baseline suite of tools across appropriate workstations.

Core suite to consider:

- screen readers such as JAWS, NVDA, or VoiceOver for blind and low-vision patrons;
- screen magnification such as ZoomText or built-in magnifiers;
- voice control and dictation for patrons who cannot use a keyboard or mouse;
- alternative input devices, including adaptive keyboards, switches, and trackballs;
- captioning and transcription tools for video and audio;
- assistive listening systems for events and meetings;
- text-to-speech and reading support tools for patrons with cognitive or learning disabilities;
- adjustable furniture, ergonomic seating, and height-adjustable tables.

Match the suite to community needs through consultation with disability services and patron feedback. Do not assume; ask and verify.

### Configure And Maintain The Technology Reliably

Installed but broken technology is worse than none, because it promises service and then denies it. Maintenance is mandatory.

Configuration and maintenance:

- configure profiles so settings persist and are easy to reset;
- keep software updated and licensed;
- test the technology regularly, not just at installation;
- have a rapid repair process when something breaks;
- ensure workstations are in accessible locations, not isolated corners;
- provide clear signage and instructions for locating and using the tools;
- include assistive technology in routine IT maintenance schedules.

Reliability is the core service metric. A patron's visit should never be wasted by broken tools.

### Locate Technology Accessibly And Prominently

Assistive technology hidden in a back room or unlabeled is effectively unavailable. Location and discoverability matter.

Location practices:

- place adapted workstations in mainstream areas, not segregated spaces, unless privacy is needed;
- ensure paths to the workstations are accessible;
- provide clear signage and wayfinding;
- list available technology on the website and at service points;
- ensure the technology is available during all open hours;
- avoid requiring patrons to request access through burdensome processes.

If patrons cannot find or reach the technology, or must ask permission in a stigmatizing way, it is not truly accessible.

### Train Staff To Support Competently And Respectfully

Staff competence and demeanor determine whether the service works in practice. Train all service-point staff, not just specialists.

Staff training should cover:

- what assistive technology is available and where;
- basic startup, shutdown, and reset procedures;
- how to offer help without intruding on independence;
- respectful communication, including not grabbing controls or speaking to companions instead of the patron;
- when and how to escalate to specialized support;
- awareness that patrons are experts on their own needs and tools;
- avoiding assumptions about what a patron can or cannot do.

Patrons should never have to educate staff on basic technology or endure condescension. Training prevents both.

### Respect Patron Independence And Expertise

Patrons who use assistive technology are generally experts on their own tools and needs. Support should enable, not take over.

Respect principles:

- ask before helping, and accept a no;
- let the patron direct the interaction;
- do not grab controls, keyboards, or devices;
- speak directly to the patron, not to a companion or interpreter;
- do not make assumptions about ability based on appearance;
- recognize that the patron knows their technology better than staff do;
- provide help that is requested, not help that is assumed.

Over-helping is as much a barrier as no help. The goal is to remove obstacles, not to manage the patron.

### Provide Support For Personal Devices Too

Many patrons bring their own assistive technology, phones, tablets, laptops, and personal screen readers. The library must support BYOD access.

Personal device support:

- ensure Wi-Fi and network access works with personal devices;
- ensure library websites and resources work with common personal assistive technology;
- provide accessible power outlets and charging;
- support connection to library resources, printing, and scanning;
- do not require patrons to use library computers to access services.

The library's digital resources must work with the tools patrons already use, not just the tools the library provides.

### Coordinate With Disability Services And Experts

Library staff cannot be experts in everything. Coordinate with institutional and community partners.

Coordination:

- work with the disability or accessibility services office on needs and referrals;
- consult assistive technology specialists for configuration and complex needs;
- connect patrons to broader community resources when appropriate;
- share feedback across units to improve service;
- participate in accessibility committees and networks.

Coordination extends the library's capacity and connects patrons to fuller support.

### Provide Accommodation Pathways When Technology Fails

Technology will fail. There must be a clear path to alternative service when it does.

Accommodation pathways:

- have a process for immediate alternative access, such as staff assistance or a different workstation;
- provide alternative formats for materials when digital access fails;
- document and track failures to drive fixes;
- communicate known issues and workarounds;
- treat a technology failure as a service failure requiring prompt response.

A patron turned away by broken technology has been denied service. Have a recovery path ready.

### Develop Loan And Specialized Programs Where Needed

Some patrons benefit from borrowing assistive technology or using specialized equipment.

Loan and specialized programs:

- consider loan programs for devices, headphones, or specialized tools;
- provide accessible study rooms for patrons who need quiet or privacy;
- offer captioning and transcription services for events;
- ensure makerspaces, media labs, and other specialized services are accessible;
- evaluate community needs before investing in specialized equipment.

Loan programs extend access to patrons who cannot afford their own technology.

### Evaluate And Improve The Service Continuously

Assistive technology services must evolve with technology and community needs.

Continuous improvement:

- gather feedback from patrons who use the services;
- review usage data and failure reports;
- stay current with assistive technology developments;
- reassess the suite regularly;
- benchmark against peer institutions and standards;
- advocate for adequate funding as a core service, not an extra.

A service installed once and never reviewed becomes obsolete. Maintain it as a living service.

## Escalation Guidance

Assistive technology failures can amount to denial of service and carry legal and equity consequences. Escalate in these situations:

- Any report that assistive technology is broken, unavailable, or insufficient for a patron's need: escalate for immediate accommodation and prompt repair.
- Any situation where a patron is denied service due to technology failure or staff inability to help: escalate to provide alternative access and address the root cause.
- Any pattern of recurring failures or staff knowledge gaps: escalate to leadership for training, maintenance process improvement, or funding.
- Any request for accommodation that exceeds current capabilities: escalate to disability services and leadership to find a solution.
- Any formal complaint about inaccessible technology or staff conduct: escalate to leadership and the appropriate office, and document the response.

When a patron cannot access service, escalate immediately to restore access, not just to log the problem.

## Common Traps

### Set-And-Forget Installation

Installing technology once and never maintaining it leads to broken, outdated tools. Maintain and test regularly.

### Hidden Or Stigmatizing Location

Technology in a back corner or requiring special request discourages use. Locate accessibly and promote prominently.

### Untrained Staff

Staff who cannot start the technology or who behave disrespectfully drive patrons away. Train all service-point staff.

### Over-Helping And Intrusion

Grabbing controls or speaking to companions denies patron autonomy. Ask first and let the patron direct.

### Ignoring Personal Devices

Patrons bring their own technology. Library resources must work with personal assistive technology, not just library-provided tools.

### No Failure Recovery Path

When technology breaks, patrons need an immediate alternative. Have accommodation pathways ready.

### Treating It As Optional Or Extra

Assistive technology is core service, not an enhancement. Fund and staff it accordingly.

### No Feedback Or Improvement Loop

Services installed without review become obsolete. Gather feedback and reassess regularly.

## Self-Check

- Is a core suite of assistive technology provided, matched to verified community needs through consultation and feedback?
- Is the technology configured, updated, licensed, and tested regularly, with a rapid repair process when it breaks?
- Is the technology located in accessible, mainstream areas with clear signage and availability during all hours?
- Are all service-point staff trained to start, support, and respectfully assist patrons without intruding on independence?
- Does staff behavior respect patron expertise and autonomy, asking before helping and speaking directly to the patron?
- Do library digital resources work with patrons' personal assistive technology, not just library-provided tools?
- Is there coordination with disability services and assistive technology specialists for needs and referrals?
- Is there a clear accommodation pathway when technology fails, providing immediate alternative access?
- Are loan programs and specialized services evaluated against community needs and provided where justified?
- Is the service continuously improved through feedback, usage review, and adequate funding, with escalation for failures and service denial?
