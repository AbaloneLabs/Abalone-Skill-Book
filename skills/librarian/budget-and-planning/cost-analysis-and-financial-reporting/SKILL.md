# Cost Analysis And Financial Reporting

Libraries spend substantial sums on materials, systems, staff, and facilities, yet the true cost and value of services is often invisible. Cost analysis is the work of making financial reality legible so that decisions about renewals, cancellations, staffing, and service models rest on evidence rather than habit or advocacy. Financial reporting is the companion work of communicating that reality to leaders, funders, and stakeholders in a form they can act on. The judgment problem is that costs are easy to mismeasure: allocating overhead badly distorts comparisons, ignoring staff time hides the real cost of "free" services, and reporting numbers without context invites misinterpretation. A service that looks expensive on a spreadsheet may be central to mission; a cheap subscription may be a waste. Good cost analysis separates signal from noise, and good reporting turns accurate numbers into trustworthy decisions.

Agents helping with library financial analysis tend to err by producing numbers without decision context, by using inconsistent allocation methods that make comparisons meaningless, by confusing cost with value, or by reporting in a way that obscures rather than clarifies. This skill covers the judgment needed to analyze costs rigorously and report them usefully.

## Core Rules

### Connect Analysis To Decisions

Cost analysis that does not inform a decision is overhead. Before computing anything, identify the decision the analysis must support.

Common decisions include:

- renew or cancel a subscription or license;
- continue, reduce, or expand a service;
- justify a budget request or defend against a cut;
- compare in-house versus outsourced provision;
- allocate shared costs across departments;
- evaluate the efficiency of a program.

Frame the analysis around the decision. A cost-per-use figure means nothing until it is compared to a threshold, an alternative, or a benchmark that triggers action.

### Capture Full Costs, Including Hidden Ones

Undercounting cost produces dangerously optimistic decisions. The most commonly hidden cost is staff time.

Capture:

- direct costs, license fees, materials, equipment;
- staff time, including management, support, and overhead, not just direct service staff;
- infrastructure costs, servers, space, utilities, shared systems;
- training and ongoing maintenance;
- transition costs for migration or implementation;
- opportunity cost of capacity consumed.

A service labeled "free" because it has no direct budget line still consumes staff time and infrastructure. Make those costs visible or decisions will be distorted.

### Use Consistent Allocation Methods

Shared costs must be allocated to be useful, but inconsistent allocation makes comparisons meaningless.

Establish methods for:

- allocating overhead, using a defensible basis such as FTE, square footage, or usage;
- allocating staff time across activities, through time studies or estimates;
- handling shared resources used by multiple services;
- treating one-time versus recurring costs consistently;
- documenting assumptions so figures can be reproduced.

Document the allocation methodology and apply it consistently. Changing methods mid-analysis invalidates trend comparisons.

### Compute Cost-Per-Use Honestly

Cost-per-use is a powerful metric but only when computed and interpreted correctly.

Compute honestly by:

- using comparable use definitions across resources, sessions, downloads, circulations;
- including full cost, not just the license fee;
- accounting for bundled content where per-title use is inferred;
- flagging low-use periods or startup phases that distort averages;
- comparing to alternatives, interlibrary loan, pay-per-view, open access.

A cost-per-use of fifty dollars means little alone. Compared to an interlibrary loan cost of fifteen dollars or a pay-per-view of thirty dollars, it becomes actionable.

### Separate Cost From Value

Low cost is not the same as high value, and high cost is not the same of low value. Conflating them produces bad decisions.

Distinguish:

- cost, what the library spends;
- use, how much the resource is consumed;
- value, the contribution to mission, outcomes, or strategic priorities;
- ROI, return relative to investment, where returns can be financial or mission-based.

A low-use archival resource may have high strategic value for a special collections mission. A high-use database may be replaceable. Analyze cost and value together, never cost alone.

### Track Variance And Investigate Causes

Budget versus actual reporting is most useful when variances are explained, not just reported.

For each significant variance:

- identify whether it is timing, price, volume, or scope;
- determine if it is one-time or recurring;
- assess whether it signals a problem or an opportunity;
- decide on a response, reallocate, adjust, investigate further.

Variance without explanation is noise. Variance with cause becomes management information.

### Report In A Form Decision-Makers Can Use

Financial reports fail when they bury the actionable insight under detail or present numbers without context.

Design reports to:

- lead with the decision or question, not the data;
- use consistent formats and periods for comparability;
- include trend and comparison context, not just current figures;
- highlight exceptions and their causes;
- use visuals for patterns and tables for precision;
- separate summary for leadership from detail for analysts.

A report that requires the reader to do the analysis has not finished its job.

### Benchmark Against Meaningful Comparators

Cost figures gain meaning through comparison, but only against appropriate comparators.

Use comparators such as:

- peer libraries of similar size and mission;
- prior periods of the same library;
- alternative delivery models;
- sector benchmarks from consortia or associations;
- internal service-to-service comparisons.

Ensure comparators are truly comparable in scope, scale, and method. Benchmarking against institutions with different missions produces false conclusions.

### Maintain Data Integrity And Auditability

Financial analysis depends on trustworthy data. Errors and opacity destroy credibility.

Maintain integrity by:

- reconciling figures to authoritative financial systems;
- documenting sources, methods, and assumptions;
- versioning analyses so changes are traceable;
- having a second person review significant figures;
- retaining documentation for audit and future comparison.

A challenged figure that cannot be reproduced loses the argument regardless of its accuracy.

### Time Reporting To Decision Cycles

Reports are useful only when they arrive in time to act. Annual reports that land after budgets are set are historical, not actionable.

Align reporting to:

- budget development cycles, with data ready before requests are built;
- renewal and cancellation cycles, with cost-per-use ready before decisions;
- governance review cycles, with reports ready before meetings;
- management review cadence, monthly or quarterly for operational decisions.

Timing determines whether analysis informs decisions or merely documents them.

## Common Traps

### Analysis Without A Decision

Computing costs without a decision in mind produces reports no one uses. The trap is activity mistaken for productivity. Start from the decision.

### Ignoring Staff Time

Counting only direct costs makes "free" or cheap services look efficient when they consume large staff capacity. The trap is partial accounting. Capture full cost.

### Inconsistent Allocation

Changing allocation methods between analyses makes comparisons invalid. The trap is false precision. Document and apply methods consistently.

### Cost-Per-Use Without Comparators

A cost-per-use figure alone triggers no action. The trap is metric without threshold. Compare to alternatives and benchmarks.

### Conflating Cost With Value

Cancelling based on cost alone destroys strategically valuable resources. The trap is efficiency without mission. Analyze value alongside cost.

### Variance Without Explanation

Reporting budget-versus-actual without causes produces noise. The trap is data without insight. Investigate and explain variances.

### Reports That Bury The Insight

Detailed reports that hide the actionable conclusion go unread. The trap is comprehensiveness over clarity. Lead with the decision.

### Incomparable Benchmarks

Comparing to institutions with different scope or methods yields false conclusions. The trap is false legitimacy. Verify comparability.

### Unreproducible Figures

Analyses that cannot be reconstructed lose credibility when challenged. The trap is informal process. Document sources and methods.

### Reporting Too Late

Reports that arrive after decisions are made are useless. The trap is misaligned timing. Sync reporting to decision cycles.

## Self-Check

- Is each cost analysis tied to a specific decision rather than produced as a standing report?
- Are full costs captured, including staff time, overhead, and infrastructure, not just direct costs?
- Is the cost allocation methodology documented and applied consistently across analyses?
- Are cost-per-use figures computed with comparable use definitions and compared to alternatives?
- Is value, contribution to mission and outcomes, analyzed alongside cost?
- Are budget variances explained by cause, not just reported as numbers?
- Do financial reports lead with the decision or question and use appropriate formats for the audience?
- Are benchmarks drawn from genuinely comparable peers, periods, or alternatives?
- Can significant figures be reproduced and audited from documented sources and methods?
- Does reporting arrive in time to inform the relevant budget, renewal, or governance decisions?
- Would a non-financial leader understand the key conclusion and what to do about it?
