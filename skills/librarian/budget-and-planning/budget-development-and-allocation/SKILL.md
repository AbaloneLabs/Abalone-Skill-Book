# Budget Development And Allocation

A library budget is the most honest expression of its actual priorities. Whatever the strategic plan says, the budget reveals what the library truly intends to do. Budget development is therefore not an accounting exercise but an act of leadership: it converts mission and strategy into the resources that make them real, and it forces hard choices that vision statements can evade. The judgment problem is that budgets are built under uncertainty, contested by competing legitimate needs, constrained by parent institutions and funding cycles, and scrutinized by stakeholders who read them as political documents. A budget built by rolling forward last year's numbers, by satisfying the loudest voices, or by optimizing for ease of defense will quietly starve emerging needs and entrench the past. Good budget development is transparent, scenario-aware, aligned to strategy, defensible to funders, and honest about tradeoffs.

Agents helping with library budgets tend to err by treating budgeting as arithmetic rather than strategy, by underestimating recurring cost growth, by failing to model downside scenarios, or by presenting budgets without the narrative funders need to approve them. This skill covers the judgment needed to build a budget that is accurate, strategic, and persuasive.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Budget As A Strategic Document

The budget should be readable as a translation of strategy into resources. A reader should be able to infer the library's priorities from how money is allocated.

Make strategy visible by:

- mapping each strategic priority to its budget line or program;
- showing where new investments are being made and where reductions are taken;
- explaining how allocations differ from prior year and why;
- ensuring the budget can fund the plan, not just describe it;
- flagging where strategy outstrips resources, forcing honest prioritization.

A budget that funds everything the same as last year funds the past, not the future.

### Build From A Real Base, Not Last Year's Numbers

Accurate budgeting starts with understanding what is actually being spent, not what was budgeted.

Establish the base by:

- analyzing actual expenditures versus budget for prior years;
- identifying one-time costs that should not recur;
- identifying recurring costs that were underfunded and are creating deficits;
- separating committed costs, salaries, subscriptions, licenses, from discretionary;
- documenting every significant line and its driver.

The gap between budget and actual is information. A department that consistently overspends or underspends is signaling something the next budget must address.

### Separate Ongoing From One-Time Funds

This distinction governs long-term flexibility and is frequently mishandled.

Manage the distinction by:

- tracking the percentage of budget locked into ongoing commitments;
- capping recurring commitments, often around seventy percent, to preserve flexibility;
- using one-time funds for pilots, equipment, backfiles, and new directions;
- never funding an ongoing commitment with money that will disappear;
- reviewing each recurring commitment annually for continued value.

A budget that is mostly ongoing has almost no capacity to respond to change or opportunity.

### Model Inflation And Recurring Cost Growth

Library costs do not stay flat. Subscriptions, licenses, and materials typically inflate faster than general budgets.

Build in:

- realistic inflation assumptions based on vendor data, not optimism;
- expected increases for big deals and aggregator packages;
- exchange rate sensitivity for foreign materials;
- salary step and benefit cost growth;
- a contingency for unexpected spikes.

Without inflation modeling, a flat budget quietly buys less every year, and the erosion is invisible until a crisis appears.

### Develop Multiple Scenarios

Budgets are built under uncertainty. A single point estimate hides risk.

Develop scenarios for:

- the requested or target budget;
- a flat or status quo budget;
- a reduction scenario, often five to ten percent;
- a growth scenario tied to new revenue or initiatives;
- contingency triggers that activate if revenue falls short.

Scenario planning prevents panicked, arbitrary decisions when actual revenue differs from projection. It also demonstrates fiscal responsibility to funders.

### Use A Transparent Allocation Methodology

Opaque allocations invite conflict and drift. A documented methodology makes allocations defensible and revisable.

A methodology should specify:

- the criteria or formula used to divide funds;
- the data inputs and their sources;
- the weights assigned to each input;
- how adjustments are made for special cases;
- who has authority to reallocate mid-year;
- the review cycle.

Even a simple methodology beats rolling forward numbers with no reasoning. Transparency builds trust and improves decisions through wider input.

### Protect Strategic And Emerging Priorities

Routine allocation entrenches existing distributions. New programs and underserved communities need explicit protection.

Mechanisms:

- set aside a percentage for new initiatives each year;
- give growth areas a floor that cannot be cut;
- review allocations against curriculum, enrollment, or community change;
- sunset declining areas gradually rather than abruptly;
- consult liaisons or community representatives about emerging needs.

Without protection, the budget calcifies around historical priorities and cannot fund the future.

### Prepare A Compelling Budget Narrative

Funders approve budgets based on story as much as numbers. A budget presented as spreadsheets alone will be cut.

Build a narrative that includes:

- the connection to institutional mission and priorities;
- the outcomes the budget will produce, not just what it buys;
- the cost of not funding the request;
- efficiency measures already taken;
- benchmarking against peers where favorable;
- clear asks and the impact of each.

The narrative turns a request into a case. Funders fund cases, not line items.

### Plan For Cuts As Well As Growth

Budgets go down as well as up. A plan only for growth fails when cuts arrive.

Prepare a prioritized cut list in advance that considers:

- which subscriptions have lowest cost-per-use;
- which formats have alternatives;
- which services are covered consortially;
- what protects the core mission under reduced funds;
- what reductions minimize harm to users.

A reasoned cut list prevents arbitrary, damaging choices under pressure.

### Align With Consortial And Shared Resources

No library operates alone. Budget decisions should account for what partners provide.

Consider:

- shared print repositories reducing local retention burden;
- consortial deals lowering per-member cost;
- reciprocal borrowing reducing need for local ownership;
- document delivery as an alternative to ownership.

Aligning local budget with shared infrastructure stretches every dollar and should be visible in the narrative.

## Common Traps

### Rolling Forward Last Year's Numbers

Defaulting to prior year allocations entrenches the past and ignores change. The trap is convenience. Re-examine allocations against current data.

### Underestimating Recurring Cost Growth

Subscriptions and salaries inflate. A flat budget quietly buys less each year. The trap is optimism. Model real inflation.

### Funding Ongoing Commitments With One-Time Money

Using temporary funds for recurring costs creates a cliff when the money ends. The trap is short-term relief. Match commitment duration to fund duration.

### Single-Scenario Budgeting

A single point estimate hides risk and forces panic when reality differs. The trap is false precision. Model scenarios.

### Opaque Allocation

Allocations without methodology invite conflict and drift. The trap is avoiding conflict by avoiding transparency. Document the method.

### Squeaky-Wheel Allocation

The loudest department should not set the budget. The trap is conflict avoidance. Use data and methodology with input channels for all.

### No Cut Plan

When cuts come, institutions without a prioritized plan make damaging arbitrary choices. The trap is hoping cuts will not come. Prepare the list before it is needed.

### Numbers Without Narrative

A budget presented as spreadsheets alone gets cut. The trap is assuming the numbers speak for themselves. Build the case.

### Ignoring Shared Resources

Budgeting as if the library operates alone wastes money on duplicative ownership. The trap is insularity. Integrate consortial options.

## Self-Check

- Is the budget readable as a translation of strategy into resources, with priorities visible in allocations?
- Is the base built from actual expenditures and committed costs, not just last year's numbers?
- Are ongoing and one-time funds separated, with ongoing commitments capped to preserve flexibility?
- Are inflation, salary growth, and recurring cost increases modeled realistically?
- Are multiple scenarios developed, including reduction and contingency cases?
- Is there a transparent, documented allocation methodology that could be defended and revised?
- Are strategic and emerging priorities explicitly protected from historical entrenchment?
- Is there a compelling budget narrative connecting the request to mission and outcomes?
- Is a prioritized cut plan ready for revenue reductions?
- Are consortial and shared resource options integrated into local budget decisions?
- Could a funder explain why this budget matters after reading the narrative?
