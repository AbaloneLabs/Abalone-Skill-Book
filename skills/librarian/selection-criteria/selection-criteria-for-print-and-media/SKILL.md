# Selection Criteria for Print and Media

Selecting materials for a library collection is a series of judgment calls that look deceptively simple from the outside. Each title is a small commitment of budget, shelf space, and staff time, and each carries the collection's promise to serve a whole community, not just the loudest or most visible part of it. The work is not personal shopping for titles the selector happens to love; it is disciplined application of published criteria, awareness of what the collection already holds, and constant attention to whose needs are being met and whose are being overlooked. Done well, selection builds a collection that is balanced, relevant, and defensible; done casually, it produces gaps, duplicates, bias, and challenges that the library cannot answer for.

## Core Rules

### Select against a written policy and criteria, not personal taste

Every selection decision should be traceable to a written collection-development policy with stated criteria. Standard criteria include relevance to community needs and the library's mission; accuracy and authority of the content; literary, artistic, or informational merit; currency and timeliness; reputation of the author, publisher, or producer; physical quality and durability; price relative to value; and relationship to existing holdings. Apply these consistently rather than reaching for titles you personally enjoy, because personal taste produces a collection that reflects one person rather than a community. When a decision is challenged later, the ability to point to the criterion that justified the choice is what protects the library.

### Know the collection you are selecting into

A title's value depends on what the collection already holds. Before adding, consider whether the collection already has sufficient coverage of the topic, whether this title duplicates existing works, whether it fills a gap, and whether it updates outdated material. A brilliant book on a subject already overrepresented may be a poor choice, while a modest book on an underrepresented subject may be essential. Use circulation statistics, shelf scans, and collection maps to understand strengths and gaps, and select to build a coherent whole rather than a series of unrelated individual picks. Selection without collection awareness produces redundancy and holes.

### Use professional reviews as the primary evidence base

Professional reviews are the backbone of defensible selection because they offer independent assessment of quality, accuracy, and audience, and they create a documentary record of the basis for purchase. Prioritize established review sources appropriate to the audience and format, such as library review journals, subject-specialist outlets, and recognized award lists. Use starred reviews, editor's-choice lists, and best-of compilations to identify high-demand and high-quality titles. Reviews are not infallible, and absence of review is not automatic disqualification, especially for self-published, local, or specialized material, but when you select outside the review base, document the alternative justification so the decision remains defensible.

### Anticipate and balance demand

Selection must balance what patrons already ask for against what the collection ought to contain. Track holds, bestseller lists, local interest, and media tie-ins to anticipate high-demand titles, and purchase in sufficient quantity to meet reasonable demand without overcommitting budget to a single phenomenon. At the same time, resist the gravitational pull of bestsellers alone, which would produce a collection of only the already-popular and starve midlist, literary, diverse, and specialized works that serve smaller audiences. A healthy collection carries both the high-circulation titles that justify the budget and the lower-circulation titles that fulfill the library's deeper mission.

### Balance the collection across audiences, viewpoints, and formats

A central selection obligation is balance, which is both an intellectual-freedom principle and a practical service standard. Actively seek titles representing diverse authors, subjects, and viewpoints, including those that may be controversial or that some community members would prefer excluded. Ensure the collection serves the full age range, reading levels, languages, and accessibility needs of the community. Balance formats thoughtfully, recognizing that different patrons rely on audiobooks, large print, media, or digital access, and that over-investing in one format excludes others. Balance is not a one-time achievement but an ongoing posture that requires deliberate attention, because the default pull of publishing and demand will reproduce existing biases unless countered.

### Evaluate local, self-published, and donated material with care

Local authors, self-published works, and donations often fall outside the standard review base and require a different evaluation path. Apply the same core criteria of accuracy, quality, relevance, and condition, but add scrutiny for self-published material, which lacks the editorial gatekeeping of traditional publishing. Consider local relevance, the author's connection to the community, and whether the work fills a genuine gap, while resisting pressure to add material simply because the author is local or the donation is free. A clear donation and self-published-review policy prevents both arbitrary acceptance and arbitrary rejection, and protects staff from pressure.

### Document decisions and maintain the selection record

Keep records of what was selected, from what source, on what criterion, and at what cost, both for accountability and for future collection assessment. Track vendor performance, budget allocation by category and format, and the fate of selections through circulation. This record transforms selection from a series of private judgments into a managed program, and it provides the evidence base for budget requests, policy reviews, and responses to challenges. Selection that is not documented cannot be defended, improved, or audited.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is selecting titles based on personal taste or familiarity rather than published criteria, which produces a collection that reflects the selector rather than the community and is indefensible when challenged. Another is selecting each title in isolation without awareness of what the collection already holds, leading to duplication of overrepresented subjects and persistent gaps in underrepresented ones. Over-relying on bestseller lists and holds data, while ignoring midlist, literary, diverse, and specialized works, produces a popular but shallow collection that fails the library's deeper mission. Ignoring professional reviews, or selecting from publisher blurbs and marketing alone, removes the independent evidence base that makes decisions defensible. Failing to balance across audiences, viewpoints, languages, and formats lets the default pull of publishing reproduce existing biases and excludes patrons who rely on less-dominant formats. Accepting local or self-published material out of obligation or community pressure, without applying the same quality criteria, clutters the collection with low-quality work. Avoiding controversial or potentially challenged material, in a mistaken effort to prevent trouble, is itself a form of censorship that abandons the patrons who need those titles most. Finally, treating selection as private judgment rather than a documented program leaves the library unable to defend decisions, justify budgets, or improve over time.

## Self-Check

- Was each selection decision traceable to a written collection-development policy and stated criteria rather than personal preference?
- Did you consider what the collection already holds, so the title fills a gap or updates outdated material rather than duplicating existing coverage?
- Did you rely primarily on professional reviews or recognized award lists, and document alternative justification when selecting outside the review base?
- Did you balance high-demand titles against midlist, literary, diverse, and specialized works, rather than letting bestsellers alone shape the collection?
- Did you actively seek titles representing diverse authors, subjects, viewpoints, languages, and reading levels to counter default biases?
- Did you evaluate local, self-published, and donated material against the same core criteria rather than accepting on obligation or convenience?
- Did you avoid self-censorship by including controversial or potentially challenged material that serves community needs?
- Did you record what was selected, the source, the criterion, and the cost, so the decision can be defended, audited, and used for future assessment?
