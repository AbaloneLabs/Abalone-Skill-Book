# Selection for Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion

A library collection that claims to serve a whole community must actually contain the voices, stories, and information that reflect that whole community, including members who are underrepresented, marginalized, or historically excluded from mainstream publishing. Diversity in collection development is not a decorative add-on or a marketing angle; it is a core obligation tied to the library's mission, its intellectual-freedom commitments, and its duty to serve every patron. The work is easy to perform superficially, by adding a few token titles and declaring the job done, and it is easy to abandon under pressure when diverse materials are challenged. Doing it well requires sustained, critical attention to who is represented, how they are represented, who is doing the representing, and whether the collection is actually reaching the patrons it claims to serve.

## Core Rules

### Treat inclusion as a standing obligation, not a project

The most common failure of diverse collection development is treating it as a one-time initiative that can be completed and checked off. Inclusion is a standing posture that must be applied to every selection cycle, every budget, and every collection assessment, because publishing output, community demographics, and language needs shift constantly. Build diversity goals into the written collection-development policy, set measurable targets, and review progress regularly. Without embedded structure, inclusion drifts back toward the default of mainstream, already-visible publishing, which is exactly the bias the work is meant to counter.

### Prioritize own-voices and authentic representation

Representation is not just about who appears on the page or in the content; it is about who created it. Prioritize works by creators who share the identity or lived experience being depicted, often discussed as own-voices, because authentic representation carries a different weight than representation filtered through outsiders. Outsider representation can still be valuable, especially when researched and respectful, but it carries higher risk of stereotype, inaccuracy, and harm, and should be evaluated critically. When assessing a collection, look not only at the presence of diverse characters or subjects but at the proportion of works by creators from those communities, because surface diversity without creator diversity can reproduce the very exclusion it appears to address.

### Evaluate representation for stereotypes and single stories

Presence alone is not enough; the quality of representation matters. A collection full of diverse characters who exist only as sidekicks, villains, trauma victims, or cultural lessons can do active harm by reinforcing narrow narratives. Apply critical evaluation to how groups are depicted: Are they fully human, with agency and interiority? Does the collection offer multiple, varied portrayals, or does it collapse a whole community into a single story? Seek works that present joy, complexity, everyday life, and counter-narratives alongside, not instead of, stories of struggle. A useful test is whether a patron from the depicted group would feel seen and respected, or reduced and surveilled, by the collection's portrayal.

### Audit the existing collection before adding

Sustainable inclusion requires knowing what the collection already holds and what it lacks. Conduct regular diversity audits by sampling the collection, coding for creator identity, subject, and audience, and comparing against community demographics and against the broader publishing landscape. Use the audit to identify gaps, overrepresented single stories, and outdated or stereotypical material that may warrant deselection or replacement. Document the methodology so the audit is repeatable and the results are comparable over time. An audit transforms inclusion from impression into evidence and prevents the comforting but false belief that the collection is more diverse than it is.

### Use specialized sources to find diverse material

Mainstream review sources and vendor approval plans under-serve diverse publishing, so selectors must deliberately seek out specialized resources. Use dedicated awards, such as those recognizing Black, Indigenous, Latino, Asian, LGBTQ+, and disability literature; follow #WeNeedDiverseBooks and related creator and publisher networks; consult blogs, podcasts, and review outlets run by members of the communities being represented; and build relationships with independent and small publishers that center marginalized voices. Relying only on mainstream bestseller lists will systematically exclude the very material inclusion work requires, so diversifying the selection sources is as important as diversifying the titles.

### Connect selection to the patrons it is meant to serve

Diverse materials serve no one if they sit unread, so connect selection to outreach, programming, displays, and readers'-advisory work that puts the material into patrons' hands. Build relationships with community organizations, listen to what communities actually want rather than assuming, and avoid the trap of selecting only trauma narratives that may not reflect how a community wishes to be seen. Ensure that staff are trained to recommend diverse materials without making patrons feel singled out or tokenized, and that displays and booklists are integrated rather than segregated into a diversity section that signals othering. Inclusion that stops at acquisition is incomplete.

### Defend diverse materials under challenge

Diverse materials are disproportionately targeted for challenge, and the strength of the collection's inclusion depends on the library's willingness to defend it. Maintain a clear reconsideration policy, train staff in intellectual-freedom principles, document the selection criteria that justified each title, and prepare challenge responses in advance. Do not quietly remove or relocate challenged material to avoid conflict, which abandons the patrons the material serves and normalizes censorship. Stand publicly for the principle that the library reflects the whole community, including those whose lives or viewpoints some would silence.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is treating diversity as a one-time project or token gesture, adding a few titles and declaring the collection inclusive, rather than embedding inclusion as a standing obligation across every selection cycle. Another is counting surface representation, such as diverse characters present, while ignoring whether the creators share the depicted identities, which can reproduce exclusion behind a veneer of inclusion. Filling a collection with single-story portrayals, such as only trauma narratives or only historical struggle for a given group, reduces whole communities to one dimension and can alienate the very patrons the material is meant to serve. Relying only on mainstream review sources and bestseller lists systematically excludes diverse publishing, because those channels under-represent marginalized creators. Skipping the audit and assuming the collection is more diverse than it is prevents honest progress and lets gaps persist. Selecting diverse material but leaving it to sit unread, without displays, programming, or readers'-advisory connections, wastes the investment and fails the patrons it was meant to reach. Segregating diverse material into a special section, rather than integrating it, signals othering and implies these titles are not for everyone. The most serious trap is quietly removing or relocating challenged diverse materials to avoid conflict, which abandons marginalized patrons and normalizes censorship under pressure.

## Self-Check

- Did you treat inclusion as a standing obligation embedded in policy and every selection cycle, rather than a one-time project?
- Did you prioritize own-voices and authentic representation, evaluating not only who appears in the content but who created it?
- Did you evaluate the quality of representation, seeking multiple, varied portrayals with agency and joy rather than single-story or stereotypical depictions?
- Did you conduct or consult a diversity audit of the existing collection to identify gaps, single stories, and outdated material?
- Did you use specialized awards, networks, and community-run review sources rather than relying only on mainstream channels?
- Did you connect selection to programming, displays, and readers'-advisory work so diverse materials actually reach patrons, without tokenizing or segregating them?
- Did you avoid selecting only trauma narratives, and seek works that reflect the full humanity of the communities depicted?
- If material was challenged, did you defend it under the reconsideration policy rather than quietly removing or relocating it to avoid conflict?
