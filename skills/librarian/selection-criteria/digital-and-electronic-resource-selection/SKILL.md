# Digital and Electronic Resource Selection

Selecting digital resources is fundamentally different from selecting physical materials, because the library rarely buys a digital title outright; instead it licenses access under terms that can change, expire, or be revoked. An e-book can be removed mid-use, a database can jump in price, a platform can impose new digital-rights-management restrictions, and a streaming film can disappear when the licensor withdraws it. The selector who treats digital selection like print selection, evaluating only content quality and audience fit, will build a collection that is fragile, expensive, and less usable than it appears. The work demands the same content judgment as print selection plus close attention to licensing, access models, platform behavior, total cost of ownership, and the long-term sustainability of the collection being built.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate licensing and access models, not just content

The single most important difference in digital selection is that the license terms determine what the library and its patrons can actually do with the resource. Examine whether access is purchased outright, leased, or subscription; whether there are limits on simultaneous users, checkouts, or annual uses; whether the library owns a perpetual copy or only temporary access; what happens to access if the subscription lapses; and what digital-rights-management restrictions apply to printing, downloading, copying, and accessibility features. A brilliant title with a restrictive one-user-at-a-time license and high per-use cost may serve the community far worse than a slightly older title with broad access. Read the license, not just the description.

### Calculate total cost of ownership over time

Digital resources carry costs that extend far beyond the listed price. Factor in annual platform fees, subscription increases, per-use or per-title charges, interlibrary-loan restrictions that raise fulfillment costs, staff time for setup and management, cataloging and discovery integration, and the cost of patron support when platforms are confusing. Compare the lifetime cost of a subscription against one-time purchases where possible, and model realistic price escalation, because database vendors routinely raise subscription prices substantially at renewal. A resource that looks affordable in year one can become unaffordable in year three, and dropping it then loses everything the library paid for, since subscription access typically does not accumulate.

### Assess platform usability and accessibility

Patrons abandon resources they cannot use, so platform quality is a selection criterion as important as content. Evaluate the user experience across devices, the quality of search and discovery, the clarity of the interface, download and offline options, and the authentication method for remote access. Critically, evaluate accessibility: whether the platform supports screen readers, offers adjustable text and captions, provides alternative formats, and complies with accessibility standards, because inaccessible platforms exclude patrons with disabilities and may create legal exposure. Test the platform yourself with patron scenarios before committing, and gather feedback during trials from a range of users, not just the selector.

### Compare vendors and avoid over-concentration

Digital resource markets are concentrated, which gives vendors leverage and limits leverage for libraries. When selecting, compare multiple vendors and platforms for the same or similar content, and resist building the entire digital collection on a single vendor, which creates dependence and weakens negotiating position. Where consortial or state-level agreements exist, use them to gain better terms than an individual library could secure. Track which vendors have raised prices, withdrawn content, or imposed restrictive terms, and let that history inform future decisions. Diversifying vendors and participating in consortia are the main tools libraries have to counter market concentration.

### Use trials and patron feedback before committing

Never commit to a major digital subscription based on vendor demos and marketing alone. Run a structured trial of sufficient length, promote it to the relevant patron segments, collect both usage data and qualitative feedback, and compare results against the resources already held. Define in advance what success looks like, such as a target number of sessions, positive feedback from a specific department, or displacement of a more expensive resource. Trials that are not promoted or measured produce no useful evidence and lead to decisions driven by inertia or sales pressure. Document trial outcomes so they inform not only the immediate decision but future evaluations.

### Plan for discovery, integration, and support

A digital resource only delivers value if patrons can find it and use it, which depends on integration into the library's discovery systems, accurate cataloging, clear linking from relevant pages, and staff who can support it. Before selecting, assess the staff time required for setup, ongoing management, catalog updates, and patron troubleshooting, and confirm the library can sustain that effort. Plan how the resource will be discovered, through the catalog, research guides, instruction sessions, or marketing, and build that plan into the selection decision. A resource no one finds or supports is a wasted subscription regardless of its content quality.

### Build a sustainable digital collection strategy

Individual digital selections accumulate into a collection strategy whether intended or not, so make the strategy explicit. Decide the balance between one-time purchases, subscriptions, and demand-driven models; set targets for perpetual-ownership content versus leased access; plan for regular review and deselection of underused subscriptions; and align digital spending with the overall collection-development policy. Revisit the strategy annually against usage, cost, and community need, and be willing to cut underperforming resources even when vendors push renewal. Sustainability is the difference between a digital collection that grows in value and one that drains the budget while delivering less each year.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is treating digital selection like print selection, evaluating only content and audience while ignoring licensing terms, access models, and digital-rights-management restrictions that determine actual usability. Another is focusing on the year-one subscription price without modeling annual increases and total cost of ownership, leading to resources that become unaffordable and must be cut, losing all the value paid in. Choosing platforms without testing usability and accessibility excludes patrons with disabilities and drives away users who find the interface confusing. Building the digital collection on a single vendor creates dependence and weakens the library's negotiating position when prices rise or terms tighten. Committing to major subscriptions based on vendor demos and marketing, without structured trials and patron feedback, leads to purchases driven by sales pressure rather than evidence. Selecting resources without planning for discovery, catalog integration, and staff support results in valuable content that patrons never find or cannot use. Assuming subscription access accumulates like ownership is a costly error, because dropping a subscription typically loses all paid access. Finally, letting digital selections accumulate without an explicit strategy and annual review allows underperforming resources to drain the budget while the collection delivers less value each year.

## Self-Check

- Did you read and evaluate the license terms, access model, and digital-rights-management restrictions, not just the content description?
- Did you calculate total cost of ownership over time, including annual fees, realistic price escalation, and staff management costs, rather than only the year-one price?
- Did you test platform usability and accessibility, including screen-reader support, captions, and adjustable text, before committing?
- Did you compare multiple vendors and platforms, and use consortial agreements where available, rather than over-concentrating on a single vendor?
- Did you run a structured, promoted trial with defined success criteria and gather both usage data and patron feedback before subscribing?
- Did you plan for discovery, catalog integration, linking, and ongoing staff support, and confirm the library can sustain the required effort?
- Did you distinguish between perpetual-ownership content and leased or subscription access, and set targets for each within a broader digital strategy?
- Did you build in an annual review against usage and cost, with willingness to cut underperforming resources even under vendor renewal pressure?
