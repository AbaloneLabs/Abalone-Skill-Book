# Government Information Reference

Government information is one of the most valuable and most underused collections in a library, but it is also one of the hardest to navigate because it is produced by thousands of agencies across multiple levels of government, organized under specialized systems (SuDoc, government-specific classification), published in formats ranging from print monographs to sprawling statistical databases, and frequently relocated or withdrawn as agencies reorganize their websites. The defining risk in government information reference is treating it like ordinary reference work, when in fact the patron's need often carries legal, medical, financial, or regulatory weight that requires careful scope-setting and referral rather than direct advice. A patron asking for "the law on X" or "the regulation that applies to my business" needs accurate retrieval of the authoritative text, but the librarian must not interpret what the law means for the patron's situation; the line between finding the information and practicing law or giving professional advice is one that government information reference constantly tests. A second risk is over-reliance on the open web: agency websites reorganize, documents are removed, and links rot, so a librarian who relies on a quick search rather than depository collections, authoritative indexes, and permanent archives can give a patron a source that vanishes the next day.

Use this skill when helping patrons find government information, statistics, legislation, regulations, or agency publications, or navigating depository collections. The goal is to prevent the agent from overstepping into legal, medical, or professional advice, and from relying on unstable web sources where authoritative depository or archived sources exist.

## Core Rules

### Clarify The Patron's Actual Need And Its Jurisdictional Scope

Government information requests often arrive vague, and the first task is to clarify what the patron actually needs:

- Level of government: federal, state/provincial, local/municipal, or international; each level publishes through different channels and systems.
- Type of information: legislation, regulation, statistics, agency reports, forms, court decisions, or historical documents; each has distinct finding tools.
- Jurisdictional specificity: a "law on eviction" means very different things depending on the state, county, or city; pin down the jurisdiction before searching.
- Purpose and deadline: is this for a school assignment, a business decision, a legal matter, or personal need? The purpose shapes the appropriate source and depth.

Do not begin searching on a vague request. Government information is vast and jurisdictionally fragmented, and a search without clarified scope wastes effort and risks returning the wrong jurisdiction's law or data.

### Know The Finding Tools Specific To Government Information

Government information requires specialized finding tools beyond the general catalog:

- Legislation and regulation: legislative portals (Congress.gov, state legislature sites), the Federal Register and Code of Federal Regulations, and state equivalents; understand the publication lifecycle (bill, slip law, code) and how to trace it.
- Statistics: agency statistical agencies (Census Bureau, Bureau of Labor Statistics, statistical offices), with awareness of their methodologies, geographies, and update frequencies.
- Agency publications: agency websites, the Government Publishing Office (GPO) catalog, and depository collections for print and historical materials.
- Court decisions: court portals and legal databases, with clear referral to legal resources rather than interpretation.

Build familiarity with these tools before the patron arrives. Government information reference is not improvisable; it requires knowledge of the specialized finding systems.

### Navigate Depository Collections And Their Organization

Many libraries are depository libraries holding government documents in specialized arrangements:

- Classification systems: federal documents use the Superintendent of Documents (SuDoc) classification, which organizes by issuing agency rather than subject; learn to navigate it, because it differs from LC or Dewey.
- Print vs. digital: older materials may exist only in print depository collections; newer materials are increasingly digital-only, creating a format gap for mid-period research.
- Depository obligations: depository libraries have specific obligations to provide access and retain materials; understand the collection's scope and retention policies.
- Catalog integration: many depository items are not fully cataloged in the main catalog; use GPO and specialized indexes in addition to the local catalog.

Do not assume the main catalog reveals all government holdings. Depository collections have their own organization and finding tools that must be used.

### Provide Authoritative Text Without Interpreting It

The central discipline in government information reference, especially for legal and regulatory requests, is providing the authoritative text without telling the patron what it means for them:

- Find the authoritative source: the official published statute, regulation, or decision, not a secondary summary; point to the authoritative text.
- Do not interpret: explain what the document says at a factual level if asked, but do not advise the patron on how the law applies to their situation; that is the practice of law.
- Refer appropriately: for legal questions, refer to legal aid, bar association referral services, or law libraries; for medical, financial, or tax questions, refer to qualified professionals.
- State the boundary clearly: patrons often want the librarian to tell them what to do; explain that the librarian can help find information but cannot advise on its application.

The line between information and advice is the defining ethical boundary of government information reference. Honor it consistently, because crossing it, even helpfully, can harm the patron and exceed the librarian's role.

### Prefer Stable And Archived Sources Over Fragile Web Links

Government websites are unstable: agencies reorganize, administrations change content, and documents are removed. Prefer stable sources:

- Official publications: the published statute, regulation, or report in its official form (slip law, Federal Register, official code) is more stable than an agency webpage summarizing it.
- Permanent archives: use archival repositories and the depository collection for materials that may be withdrawn from the web.
- Citation over link: where possible, give the patron a citation (title, section, document number) that can be re-found, not just a URL that may rot.
- Verify currency: confirm the version found is current, because regulations and statutes are amended; check the effective date and any amendments.

A patron who leaves with a URL that dies the next week has been poorly served. Prefer sources that endure and citations that can be re-found.

### Handle Sensitive Government Information Requests With Care

Some government information requests are sensitive and require care:

- Personal information: requests for information about specific individuals (vital records, court records, property records) may be restricted or raise privacy concerns; follow access rules.
- Legal and regulatory matters involving the patron: a patron researching their own legal situation may reveal sensitive context; maintain privacy and refer to legal services.
- Politically sensitive or contested information: provide authoritative sources neutrally, without editorializing on the agency or the policy.

Government information reference is not exempt from the library's privacy and neutrality obligations. Handle sensitive requests with the same discretion and non-judgmental service as any other.

## Common Traps

### Searching On A Vague Request

Beginning a search without clarifying jurisdiction, level of government, and type of information. The trap is that government information is vast, and unscoped searches return the wrong jurisdiction or type.

### Relying On The General Catalog Alone

Missing depository materials organized under SuDoc or not fully cataloged. The trap is that the catalog feels comprehensive, but depository collections have separate finding tools.

### Interpreting Law Or Regulation For The Patron

Crossing from finding the text to advising on its application. The trap is that interpretation feels helpful, but it is the practice of law and can harm the patron.

### Relying On Fragile Web Links

Giving the patron a URL to an agency page that may be reorganized or removed. The trap is that the web feels authoritative, but government web content is unstable.

### Providing Secondary Summaries As Authoritative

Pointing to a summary or news article instead of the official statute or regulation. The trap is that summaries are easier to find, but the authoritative text is what the patron needs.

### Overlooking Referral For Sensitive Matters

Trying to answer a legal, medical, or financial question directly. The trap is that the information seems findable, but the application requires a qualified professional.

## Self-Check

- Is the patron's need clarified for level of government, jurisdiction, type of information, and purpose before searching?
- Are the specialized finding tools (legislative portals, Federal Register, statistical agencies, GPO catalog, depository indexes) used rather than relying on the general catalog?
- Is the depository collection navigated using SuDoc and specialized indexes, with awareness of print-digital format gaps?
- Is the authoritative text provided without interpretation, with clear referral to legal, medical, or financial professionals for application?
- Are stable, archived sources and citations preferred over fragile web links, with currency verified?
- Are sensitive requests handled with privacy, neutrality, and appropriate referral?
- Has the patron left with a source that can be re-found, not just a URL that may rot?
- Has the boundary between information and advice been maintained consistently, with referral offered where the patron needs professional guidance?
