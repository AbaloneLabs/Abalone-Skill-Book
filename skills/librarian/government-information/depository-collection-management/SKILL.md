# Depository Collection Management

A depository library holds government publications on behalf of the public under a formal relationship with the issuing government, and that relationship carries obligations that ordinary collection development does not: depository libraries must provide public access, retain materials according to rules, report on the collection, and undergo inspection, and failure to meet these obligations can result in loss of depository status, which for many libraries means loss of a irreplaceable collection of public information. The defining risk in depository management is treating the collection like any other library collection, weeding by space pressure or usage, when in fact retention is governed by depository rules that may prohibit discarding items except through specified channels. A second risk is failing to manage the transition from tangible (print, microform, CD-ROM) to digital depository items, which has left many depositories with a hybrid collection whose digital portion is accessed through portals the library does not fully control and whose tangible portion requires specialized storage and preservation. The discipline is to manage the depository as a governed collection, to apply retention and weeding rules correctly, and to maintain access across the format transition so that the public's right to government information is preserved.

Use this skill when managing a government documents depository collection, applying depository obligations, or handling the tangible-to-digital transition. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the depository as an ordinary collection, and from neglecting the access, retention, and reporting obligations that define depository status.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Depository Relationship And Its Obligations

Depository status is a formal relationship with specific obligations:

- The depository program: whether the Federal Depository Library Program (FDLP) or a state equivalent, the program defines what the library receives, what it must retain, and how it must provide access.
- Public access obligation: depository materials must be accessible to the public, often without restrictions that ordinary library collections may impose; free, unmediated access is typically required.
- Selection profile: many depositories select items by category or agency; manage the selection profile to match community needs and collection scope.
- Reporting and inspection: depositories must report on the collection and are subject to inspection; maintain records and compliance.

Do not assume the depository operates under ordinary collection rules. The depository relationship imposes access, retention, and reporting obligations that must be understood and met.

### Apply Retention And Weeding Rules Correctly

Weeding a depository collection is governed by rules that ordinary weeding is not:

- Retention requirements: certain items must be retained permanently or for specified periods; identify the retention category before considering any discard.
- Superseded and ephemeral items: some items may be discarded after supersession or after a retention period, but only through approved weeding processes.
- Weeding channels: depository items generally cannot be discarded freely; they may need to be offered to other depositories, returned to the issuing program, or disposed through specified means.
- Documentation: document all weeding decisions and disposals to maintain compliance and survive inspection.

Weeding a depository collection by space pressure or usage, without applying the retention rules, is a violation of the depository relationship. Apply the rules before any discard.

### Manage The Tangible-To-Digital Format Transition

Depository collections have shifted dramatically from tangible to digital, creating management challenges:

- Tangible collections: print, microform, and CD-ROM materials require specialized storage, environmental control, and equipment (microform readers) for access.
- Digital depository items: an increasing share of government publications is digital-only, accessed through portals (govinfo, agency sites) rather than received as tangible items.
- The hybrid gap: mid-period collections may have materials in formats (CD-ROM, legacy digital) that require emulation or specialized access; do not assume these are accessible without planning.
- Preservation of tangible assets: historically significant tangible materials may warrant preservation treatment, especially where no digital surrogate exists.

Manage the format transition deliberately. A depository that neglects its tangible collection loses irreplaceable information, and one that neglects its digital access loses current information.

### Provide And Maintain Public Access

The core obligation of a depository is public access to government information:

- Unrestricted access: depository materials must be accessible to the public, often including users who are not affiliated with the library's primary constituency.
- Finding and discovery: ensure depository materials are discoverable, through cataloging, specialized indexes, and clear signage, so that patrons can actually find them.
- Equipment and format access: provide the equipment (microform readers, CD-ROM drives, large-format readers) needed to access tangible formats.
- Digital access support: help patrons navigate digital government portals, which can be complex and poorly organized.

Access is not just holding the materials; it is making them findable and usable. A depository whose materials are stored but inaccessible does not meet its obligation.

### Coordinate With The Depository Network

Depository libraries operate within a network, not in isolation:

- Regional and selective depositories: the network includes regional depositories (comprehensive retention) and selective depositories (item-by-item selection); understand the library's role and the roles of partners.
- Inter-depository transfer: items weeded by a selective depository may be offered to a regional or to other selectives, supporting the network's collective retention.
- Referral and shared access: where the library lacks an item, refer to or borrow from network partners rather than leaving the patron unmet.
- Program coordination: stay current with program communications, updates, and changes that affect obligations and access.

The depository network exists so that no single library must hold everything. Use the network for referrals, transfers, and shared access.

### Preserve Historically And Locally Significant Materials

Within the depository, certain materials warrant special preservation attention:

- Local and regional significance: materials documenting the local area, congressional district, or state may have significance beyond their depository retention category.
- Unique or rare items: older tangible materials, especially those not digitized, may be rare and warrant preservation treatment.
- Condition assessment: assess the condition of significant tangible materials and prioritize preservation for those at risk.
- Digital preservation: where the library creates or hosts digital surrogates, apply digital preservation practices to ensure they endure.

Not every depository item warrants preservation investment, but those of local, regional, or unique significance do. Identify and prioritize them within the collection.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Depository As An Ordinary Collection

Weeding by space or usage without applying retention rules. The trap is that ordinary collection management feels applicable, but depository retention is governed by specific rules.

### Violating Retention Obligations

Discarding items that must be retained or through unapproved channels. The trap is that space pressure feels urgent, but improper discard violates the depository relationship.

### Neglecting The Tangible Collection

Letting print, microform, and legacy formats deteriorate without access equipment. The trap is that digital feels current, but tangible materials hold information not available digitally.

### Assuming Digital Access Is Permanent

Treating govinfo and agency portals as stable without local preservation. The trap is that portals feel authoritative, but government digital content can be reorganized or removed.

### Inaccessible Materials

Holding depository items that patrons cannot find or use. The trap is that holding feels like access, but undiscoverable or equipment-dependent materials are functionally inaccessible.

### Operating In Isolation From The Network

Failing to use inter-depository transfer and referral. The trap is that self-sufficiency feels responsible, but the network exists for shared retention and access.

## Self-Check

- Is the depository relationship and its obligations (access, retention, selection, reporting, inspection) understood and met?
- Are retention and weeding rules applied before any discard, with disposals through approved channels and documented?
- Is the tangible-to-digital transition managed, with tangible storage, equipment access, and digital portal access both maintained?
- Is public access unrestricted, with materials discoverable through cataloging, indexes, and signage, and supported by necessary equipment?
- Is the depository network used for inter-depository transfer, referral, and shared access rather than operating in isolation?
- Are historically, locally, and uniquely significant materials identified and prioritized for preservation, including digital surrogates?
- Are legacy formats (microform, CD-ROM) provided with working access equipment?
- Has the depository's compliance with program obligations been reviewed, given that status depends on meeting access, retention, and reporting requirements?
