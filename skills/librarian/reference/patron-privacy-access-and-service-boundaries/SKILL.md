# Patron Privacy Access And Service Boundaries

Library service depends on trust. Patrons should be able to seek information without unnecessary surveillance, judgment, disclosure, or discrimination. At the same time, library staff need clear boundaries for safety, legal requests, professional advice, accessibility, minors' access, meeting rooms, and behavior policies. Good service protects both access and confidentiality while following local law and institutional policy.

Use this skill before designing patron service policy, responding to a sensitive question, handling patron records, deciding what information to share, reviewing meeting room access, helping minors, responding to law enforcement, or setting service boundaries. The goal is to prevent helpfulness from becoming privacy exposure, censorship, unequal access, or unauthorized advice.

## Core Rules

### Treat Library Use As Private

Library records and interactions can reveal beliefs, health concerns, legal problems, finances, identity, family issues, immigration status, job search, political views, or personal curiosity.

Protect:

- reference questions;
- circulation records;
- search history;
- database use;
- computer sessions;
- program attendance;
- room reservations;
- interlibrary loan requests;
- holds;
- fines or account status;
- chat or email transcripts.

Follow library policy and law. Collect less data when possible, retain it only as long as needed, and restrict access.

### Minimize Personally Identifiable Information

Do not collect or display patron information unless necessary for service.

Check:

- why the data is needed;
- who can access it;
- how long it is retained;
- whether it can be anonymized;
- whether the patron understands the process;
- whether a vendor receives it;
- whether staff need it to answer the question.

If a statistic can be collected without identity, prefer that. Avoid writing patron names next to sensitive topics unless required.

### Handle Sensitive Questions Discreetly

Patrons may ask about domestic violence, housing, health, law, benefits, identity, sexuality, job loss, criminal records, or immigration. Provide privacy-aware service.

Use:

- lower voice;
- private service area if available;
- neutral language;
- source options;
- referral to qualified help;
- printed or digital privacy options;
- reminder about public computer privacy when appropriate.

Do not make the patron explain more than needed. Do not offer professional advice outside library role.

### Distinguish Information From Advice

Librarians can help locate and understand information sources, but should not act as lawyers, doctors, tax preparers, financial advisers, therapists, or social workers unless specifically qualified and operating under that role.

For high-stakes areas:

- provide authoritative sources;
- explain source type;
- show how to navigate;
- identify official forms;
- suggest referral services;
- avoid interpreting eligibility, diagnosis, legal rights, or best action.

Say, "This source explains the process," not "you should file this claim."

### Apply Access Policies Equitably

Library service should not depend on a patron's origin, age, background, views, disability, housing status, immigration status, language, income, or identity except where law or policy requires specific treatment.

Review:

- borrowing eligibility;
- computer access;
- room use;
- program access;
- conduct policy;
- ID requirements;
- fine and fee impact;
- accessibility;
- language access;
- youth services;
- outreach.

Policies should be clear, public, and consistently applied. Exceptions should be documented and justified.

### Protect Minors' Access And Privacy Within Policy

Minors have information needs and privacy interests. Local law, school policy, parental rights, and library policy vary, but do not assume minors have no privacy.

Check:

- library card policy;
- parental access rules;
- school library rules;
- circulation confidentiality;
- internet filtering policy;
- age-based program rules;
- safety obligations;
- mandatory reporting duties.

Avoid unnecessary restriction based only on age. When law requires disclosure, consult policy and supervisor.

### Respond To Law Enforcement And Legal Requests Through Procedure

Requests for patron records or surveillance access should not be handled casually.

If law enforcement asks for records:

- follow library policy;
- contact supervisor or director;
- verify identity;
- request written legal process;
- do not disclose beyond required scope;
- preserve records only if legally required;
- document the request;
- consult legal counsel where policy requires.

Do not volunteer patron information. Staff should know who is authorized to respond.

### Maintain Accessible And Safe Service

Access includes disability access, language access, physical space, digital usability, and a reasonably safe environment.

Check:

- accessible entrances and service points;
- assistive technology;
- screen reader compatibility;
- captioned programs where needed;
- accessible signage;
- accessible websites and catalogs;
- interpreter or translation process;
- service animal policy;
- emergency procedures;
- behavior policy.

Safety rules should be content-neutral and behavior-based where possible. Do not use behavior policies to exclude people based on appearance, housing status, disability, or viewpoint.

### Keep Meeting Rooms And Programs Fair

If the library provides meeting rooms, exhibits, or program spaces, policies should be equitable and viewpoint-neutral within the library's adopted framework.

Define:

- eligibility;
- reservation process;
- fees;
- time limits;
- capacity;
- behavior rules;
- insurance or permit needs;
- publicity rules;
- cancellation conditions;
- appeal process.

Do not deny access because staff dislike a viewpoint if policy permits similar groups. Distinguish library-sponsored programs from public room use.

## Common Traps

### Discussing Patron Questions In Public

Even casual repetition can expose sensitive information. Keep reference interactions discreet.

### Retaining Data Because It Might Be Useful

Unneeded data creates risk. Data minimization protects patrons and the library.

### Treating Vendor Systems As Automatically Safe

Databases, e-book platforms, learning tools, and event systems may collect user data. Review contracts and privacy practices where possible.

### Over-Helping Into Advice

Helping a patron fill out a form, choose an option, or interpret a right can cross professional boundaries. Know the line.

### Assuming Parents Can Always See Minor Records

Rules vary. Follow library and legal policy rather than assumptions.

### Handling Police Requests Personally

Front-line staff should not decide legal sufficiency. Use the designated process.

### Applying Behavior Rules Unevenly

Unequal enforcement can become discrimination. Focus on conduct, documentation, and policy.

## Self-Check

- Are reference, circulation, computer, program, and room-use records treated as confidential?
- Is personally identifiable information minimized and retained only as needed?
- Are sensitive questions handled discreetly and respectfully?
- Is the service limited to information support rather than professional advice where appropriate?
- Are access policies equitable, public, and consistently applied?
- Are minors' access and privacy handled according to law and policy rather than assumption?
- Are law enforcement and legal requests routed through authorized procedure?
- Are disability, language, digital, and physical access needs considered?
- Are meeting room and program policies viewpoint-neutral and clearly distinguished from library endorsement?
- Are safety and behavior rules applied based on conduct, not identity, appearance, housing status, or viewpoint?
