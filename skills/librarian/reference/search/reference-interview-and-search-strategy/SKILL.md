# Reference Interview And Search Strategy

Reference work is not answering the first question literally. Patrons often ask with incomplete vocabulary, unclear scope, hidden constraints, time pressure, privacy concerns, or a task they do not yet understand. A librarian should help clarify the need, identify the right source type, build a search strategy, explain limitations, and support the patron's own decision-making. Good reference service is accurate, unbiased, courteous, and organized around the patron's goal.

Use this skill before answering a research question, planning a catalog or database search, helping a patron narrow a topic, recommending source types, or deciding when a request needs referral. The goal is to prevent the agent from dumping search results, making unsupported claims, or answering a sensitive question beyond the librarian's role.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Patron's Actual Task

The stated question may not be the real information need. Ask what the patron is trying to do and how the information will be used.

Clarify:

- assignment, project, decision, curiosity, or urgent need;
- audience;
- deadline;
- required source type;
- reading level;
- preferred format;
- language;
- accessibility needs;
- geographic or historical scope;
- prior searches attempted;
- constraints such as paywalls, travel, or technology access.

Do not assume the patron wants the deepest academic source. Some need a quick fact, a government form, a children's book, a scholarly article, a legal information referral, or a local service directory.

### Distinguish Question Types

Different questions need different service patterns.

Identify whether the request is:

- known item search;
- quick factual lookup;
- topic exploration;
- scholarly research;
- consumer health information;
- legal information;
- government or benefits information;
- genealogy or local history;
- reader advisory;
- technology help;
- archival or special collections request;
- referral to another institution.

Sensitive domains such as legal, medical, tax, immigration, or financial questions should be handled as information support, not professional advice. Provide authoritative sources and referral paths.

### Conduct A Reference Interview Without Interrogating

Clarification should feel respectful and efficient. Ask enough to help, not enough to intrude.

Use:

- open question first;
- restatement of need;
- targeted follow-up;
- confirmation of scope;
- privacy-aware wording;
- neutral tone;
- check for urgency;
- offer of options.

Example: "Are you looking for general background, current statistics, or peer-reviewed research?" is better than forcing the patron to know the database name.

### Choose Source Types Before Search Terms

Search tools differ. A catalog, discovery layer, subject database, government site, archive finding aid, encyclopedia, news database, community directory, and web search answer different needs.

Select based on:

- source type required;
- authority needed;
- recency;
- subject depth;
- local versus global coverage;
- full-text access;
- language;
- audience level;
- licensing restrictions;
- preservation or archival need.

Do not search only the open web when a specialized source is needed. Do not search only subscription databases when a current government or community source is more appropriate.

### Build Search Strategy Deliberately

A search strategy should combine concepts, synonyms, controlled vocabulary, filters, and iteration.

Plan:

- main concepts;
- synonyms;
- broader terms;
- narrower terms;
- related terms;
- subject headings;
- names and variant spellings;
- date range;
- place names;
- format;
- language;
- Boolean operators;
- phrase searching;
- truncation or wildcards if supported;
- exclusion terms.

Record effective searches when the task matters. This helps explain what was tried and prevents repeated dead ends.

### Evaluate Results As Evidence Of Search Quality

Search results teach the librarian about the question. Too many, too few, or irrelevant results usually mean the strategy needs adjustment.

Review:

- relevance of top results;
- missing expected authors or sources;
- repeated terms in good results;
- subject headings;
- citation trails;
- database coverage;
- date gaps;
- language gaps;
- geographic bias;
- paywall or access limits.

If nothing appears, do not immediately conclude nothing exists. Check vocabulary, source coverage, spelling, transliteration, historical terminology, and alternate formats.

### Teach The Process When Useful

Reference work often includes instruction. Show enough of the process that the patron can continue independently.

Teach:

- how to refine terms;
- how to use subject headings;
- how to read database records;
- how to request materials;
- how to evaluate source type;
- how to save citations;
- how to use interlibrary loan;
- how to ask follow-up questions.

Do not make the patron dependent on the librarian for every step when instruction is possible.

### Explain Limits And Uncertainty

A librarian should be clear about what was searched and what remains unknown.

State:

- sources searched;
- search terms used;
- date or coverage limitations;
- access limitations;
- whether sources are authoritative or preliminary;
- whether the answer is incomplete;
- referral options;
- next search steps.

This is especially important for high-stakes questions. A source list is not advice.

### Protect Patron Privacy During Reference Work

Reference questions can reveal beliefs, health issues, legal trouble, finances, immigration status, identity, safety concerns, or personal interests.

Minimize:

- unnecessary personal details;
- public repetition of the question;
- saved chat transcripts;
- email trails;
- shared screens with private records;
- visible search history;
- identifying notes.

Follow library policy for retention and confidentiality. Ask only what is needed.

## Common Traps

### Answering The First Question Too Literally

The patron may ask "Do you have books on taxes?" but need local tax forms, a small business deduction guide, or referral to a free tax help program. Clarify before searching.

### Overwhelming The Patron With Results

Twenty links are not reference help. Prioritize a few relevant sources and explain why they fit.

### Confusing Source Access With Source Quality

Full text availability does not mean the source is best. An unavailable source may be worth requesting.

### Giving Professional Advice

Do not interpret law, diagnose health problems, choose investments, prepare taxes, or decide benefits eligibility. Provide information sources and referral.

### Ignoring Database Coverage

A database may not cover the right years, disciplines, languages, regions, or publication types. Check scope notes.

### Treating No Results As Proof Of Absence

No result may reflect bad terms, poor indexing, paywalls, archival gaps, or wrong source choice.

### Saving More Patron Data Than Needed

Helpful notes can become privacy risk. Keep only what policy and service require.

## Self-Check

- Is the patron's real task, deadline, source requirement, level, language, and access need understood?
- Is the request type identified before choosing tools?
- Did the reference interview clarify without intruding?
- Are source types chosen based on the information need rather than habit?
- Are search concepts, synonyms, controlled vocabulary, filters, and alternate terms planned?
- Are results used to refine the search rather than accepted blindly?
- Is the patron taught the search process where useful?
- Are source coverage, access limits, uncertainty, and next steps explained?
- Are sensitive questions handled as information support rather than professional advice?
- Is patron privacy protected during questioning, searching, note-taking, and follow-up?
