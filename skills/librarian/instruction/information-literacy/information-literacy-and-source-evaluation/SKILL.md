# Information Literacy And Source Evaluation

Information literacy is not a checklist of "good" and "bad" sources. It is the ability to ask better questions, understand how information is created, evaluate authority in context, recognize that information has value, search strategically, and participate in knowledge conversations responsibly. A librarian should help users reason about sources rather than simply hand them a ranking.

Use this skill before teaching information literacy, designing a research workshop, explaining source evaluation, helping a student choose sources, addressing misinformation, or building a source-evaluation guide. The goal is to prevent the agent from using shallow rules like "peer reviewed equals true" or "website equals unreliable."

## Core Rules

### Start With The Information Task

Source quality depends on purpose. A source useful for background may be weak for evidence. A primary source may be essential for history but not sufficient for current medical guidance.

Clarify:

- assignment or decision;
- required source types;
- audience;
- claim being supported;
- discipline;
- date sensitivity;
- depth needed;
- whether the user needs background, evidence, data, interpretation, or examples.

Do not evaluate a source in the abstract. Evaluate whether it fits the task.

### Treat Authority As Contextual

Authority is not a fixed label. It depends on expertise, evidence, community recognition, lived experience, institutional role, method, and relevance to the question.

Ask:

- Who created the source?
- What expertise or experience do they bring?
- What evidence do they provide?
- Who reviewed or published it?
- What community recognizes it?
- What perspective is missing?
- Is the authority relevant to this question?
- Are there conflicts of interest?

A government statistic, scholarly article, local community testimony, trade report, and archival document can each be authoritative for different purposes.

### Explain Information Creation As A Process

Information format reflects creation process, speed, review, purpose, and audience.

Compare:

- scholarly article;
- preprint;
- book;
- news article;
- opinion piece;
- government report;
- dataset;
- blog post;
- encyclopedia entry;
- social media post;
- podcast;
- archival record;
- AI-generated summary.

Do not teach format stereotypes. Teach what creation process makes a source strong or limited.

### Connect Claims To Evidence

Users should learn to inspect the relationship between claim and support.

Check:

- main claim;
- evidence cited;
- method;
- sample or source base;
- data availability;
- whether evidence directly supports claim;
- whether limitations are stated;
- whether alternative explanations are considered;
- whether the claim overreaches.

Strong source evaluation asks, "How do they know?" and "Does that evidence justify this claim?"

### Teach Lateral Reading And Verification

Users should not rely only on what a source says about itself. Verification often requires leaving the source.

Practice:

- search the author or organization;
- check funding or ownership;
- compare coverage across sources;
- trace citations;
- check fact-checking or correction history;
- search for critiques;
- verify images or quotes when relevant;
- inspect original data or report.

Lateral reading is especially useful for web sources, advocacy materials, viral claims, and unfamiliar organizations.

### Address Bias Without Simplistic Neutrality

All sources have perspective. Bias is not only political opinion; it can come from method, funding, ideology, audience, selection, language, platform incentives, and omission.

Ask:

- What is the source trying to do?
- Who benefits?
- What is included or excluded?
- What language frames the issue?
- What assumptions are visible?
- What data could not be captured?
- What voices are missing?

Do not teach that "neutral" always means better. Some questions require positional, community, or primary perspectives. The key is fit and transparency.

### Teach Searching As Iteration

Searching is strategic exploration, not typing the perfect phrase once.

Show:

- concept mapping;
- synonyms;
- broader and narrower terms;
- subject headings;
- database filters;
- citation chaining;
- using one good source to find more;
- adjusting after irrelevant results;
- recording useful terms.

Normalize confusion early in research. The first search often teaches vocabulary.

### Respect Information Value And Attribution

Information has labor, ownership, access, and ethical dimensions.

Teach:

- citation purpose;
- plagiarism avoidance;
- intellectual property basics;
- open access versus paywalled access;
- data citation;
- image and media reuse;
- fair use or local policy where relevant;
- Indigenous, community, or culturally sensitive knowledge concerns;
- AI-generated content disclosure where required.

Citation is not only formatting. It acknowledges sources, enables verification, and enters the conversation responsibly.

### Make Instruction Actionable

Information literacy instruction should produce usable student behavior, not abstract slogans.

Design:

- practice with actual sources;
- comparison tasks;
- source annotation;
- claim-evidence mapping;
- search reflection;
- citation tracing;
- small-group discussion;
- exit ticket;
- transfer task.

If instruction happens in a short one-shot session, focus on the highest-value decisions students will immediately make.

## Common Traps

### Teaching Checklist Thinking

Checklists can help beginners but can become mechanical. Users need judgment, context, and evidence.

### Treating Peer Review As A Truth Stamp

Peer review improves screening but does not eliminate error, bias, retraction, or weak methods.

### Dismissing All Web Sources

Government data, organizational reports, archives, technical documentation, and local sources may live on the web and be highly relevant.

### Ignoring Algorithms

Search engines and platforms rank by systems that are not the same as scholarly value or truth. Teach users to search beyond the first result.

### Teaching Citation As Punishment

Citation should be framed as participation, credit, and verification, not only avoiding penalties.

### Avoiding Controversial Sources

Controversial topics can be taught through source comparison, evidence, and respectful inquiry. Avoiding them leaves users less prepared.

### Overloading Beginners

Information literacy develops over time. Teach the next useful move, not every concept at once.

## Self-Check

- Is source evaluation tied to the user's actual information task?
- Is authority treated as contextual rather than absolute?
- Is information format connected to creation process, review, speed, and purpose?
- Are claims traced to evidence and methods?
- Are users taught to verify sources through lateral reading?
- Are bias, perspective, omission, funding, and audience addressed without pretending all sources must be neutral?
- Is searching taught as iterative exploration?
- Are citation, attribution, reuse, access, and information value explained?
- Are users practicing with real sources rather than only hearing principles?
- Is the instruction scoped to what learners can apply immediately?
