# Scholarly Communication

Scholarly communication is the system by which research is created, evaluated, published, disseminated, and preserved. It is in a period of deep transformation, shaped by open access mandates, rising subscription and article processing charges, author rights concerns, data sharing requirements, and the emergence of new publishing models. Researchers must navigate this landscape at every career stage, and they frequently make decisions, where to publish, what rights to retain, whether to pay processing charges, how to share data, how to spot predatory publishers, that have lasting consequences for their reach, their rights, and their reputations. Librarians advising on scholarly communication must understand the publishing ecosystem, the economics and politics that shape it, the rights at stake in author agreements, and the emerging requirements around open access and data. The work is not about giving a single correct answer; it is about equipping researchers to make informed decisions aligned with their goals, their funders' requirements, and the values of open and equitable scholarship.

Use this skill when advising on scholarly publishing, open access, author rights, research data management, copyright in publishing agreements, or scholarly communication policy. The goal is to prevent the agent from reducing complex publishing decisions to slogans, ignoring author rights in agreements, advising on open access without understanding the economics, neglecting data sharing requirements, or failing to help researchers spot predatory or exploitative publishers.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Scholarly Publishing Ecosystem

Advising researchers requires understanding how scholarly publishing works, its economics, its actors, and its pressures.

The ecosystem:

- commercial publishers, society publishers, university presses, and open access publishers with different models and motives;
- subscription, article processing charge, and emerging transformative or diamond models;
- the role of peer review, editing, and infrastructure in publishing costs;
- the consolidation and profit margins that shape the system;
- the role of funders, institutions, and mandates in driving change.

Researchers' decisions are shaped by this ecosystem. Understand it to advise on where and how to publish strategically.

### Help Researchers Choose Publishing Venues Strategically

Where to publish is a high-stakes decision affecting reach, credibility, and career. Guide researchers through the considerations.

Venue considerations:

- the venue's reputation, peer review rigor, and editorial standards;
- audience and discoverability for the research field;
- open access options and their costs;
- alignment with funder and institutional mandates;
- speed of review and publication;
- licensing terms and how they affect reuse and sharing.

There is no single best venue. Help researchers weigh these factors against their goals and constraints.

### Protect Author Rights In Publishing Agreements

Publishing agreements transfer rights from authors to publishers, often broadly. Researchers frequently sign them without understanding the consequences.

Rights guidance:

- read the agreement before signing, not after;
- understand what rights are transferred and what are retained;
- consider retaining rights to use the work in teaching, future research, and institutional repositories;
- negotiate where possible, many agreements allow modification;
- use author addenda to retain specific rights;
- understand preprint, postprint, and version-of-record distinctions for self-archiving.

Authors who sign away all rights lose the ability to use their own work. Empower them to retain the rights they need.

### Navigate Open Access Models And Mandates

Open access, making research freely available, is driven by funder mandates, institutional policies, and disciplinary norms. The models and their economics vary.

Open access guidance:

- understand gold, green, hybrid, and diamond open access models;
- know funder mandates and their compliance requirements;
- understand article processing charges and whether the institution has agreements to cover them;
- use institutional repositories for green open access where gold is not feasible;
- be aware of embargo periods and how they interact with mandates;
- consider transformative agreements that shift subscription spending toward open access.

Open access is not a single choice but a landscape of options. Help researchers find the compliant, affordable path for their work.

### Manage Research Data For Sharing And Preservation

Funders increasingly require research data to be managed and shared. Librarians advise on data management throughout the research lifecycle.

Data management:

- help researchers write data management plans for grant proposals;
- advise on data organization, documentation, and metadata;
- identify appropriate repositories for data sharing;
- address privacy, confidentiality, and consent issues in data;
- consider licensing and reuse terms for shared data;
- plan for long-term preservation of valuable datasets.

Data is a research output with its own rights and responsibilities. Treat data management as integral to scholarly communication, not peripheral.

### Help Researchers Spot Predatory And Exploitative Publishers

Predatory publishers exploit the open access author-pays model by publishing with little or no peer review for profit. Researchers need help identifying and avoiding them.

Spotting predatory publishers:

- check for transparent peer review and editorial processes;
- verify listing in reputable indexes and directories;
- watch for deceptive journal names mimicking established ones;
- be wary of unsolicited recruitment emails with rapid acceptance promises;
- check the publisher's transparency about fees and processes;
- use established checklists and community warnings.

Predatory publishing harms researchers' reputations and pollutes the scholarly record. Equip researchers to evaluate venues critically.

### Address Copyright And Licensing In Scholarly Outputs

Scholarly outputs, articles, monographs, data, and educational materials, involve copyright and licensing decisions that affect reuse and impact.

Copyright guidance:

- understand copyright ownership, typically the author unless transferred;
- choose appropriate licenses, such as Creative Commons, for open outputs;
- understand how licensing affects reuse, adaptation, and attribution;
- address third-party copyright in figures, data, and quotations;
- consider licensing implications for educational and commercial reuse.

Licensing choices shape how work can be used and built upon. Guide researchers to licenses that match their dissemination goals.

### Support Open Educational Resources And Alternative Outputs

Scholarly communication extends beyond formal publication to educational materials, preprints, and other outputs. Support these alternative forms.

Alternative outputs:

- open educational resources reduce student costs and allow adaptation;
- preprints accelerate dissemination and establish priority;
- open monographs and scholarly editions expand access to long-form work;
- digital humanities projects and datasets are scholarly outputs in their own right;
- altmetrics can capture impact beyond citation counts.

Recognize and support the full range of scholarly output, not just journal articles. Researchers' contributions take many forms.

### Engage With Scholarly Communication Policy And Advocacy

Scholarly communication is shaped by policy at institutional, funder, and national levels. Librarians engage with and influence this policy.

Policy engagement:

- develop and promote institutional open access policies;
- participate in funder mandate consultations;
- advocate for equitable publishing models that do not exclude under-resourced authors;
- educate the community on the economics and politics of publishing;
- collaborate with consortia on transformative agreements.

Policy shapes the system researchers work within. Engagement is part of advancing equitable, open scholarship.

### Keep Current With A Rapidly Evolving Landscape

Scholarly communication changes constantly, new mandates, models, technologies, and actors. Staying current is essential to useful advice.

Stay current:

- follow funder policy developments and mandate changes;
- track emerging publishing models and transformative agreements;
- monitor predatory publishing trends and warnings;
- learn about new technologies affecting dissemination and preservation;
- engage with the scholarly communication librarian community.

Static knowledge becomes outdated quickly. Commit to ongoing learning as part of the role.

## Common Traps

### Reducing Complex Decisions To Slogans

Publish or go open access is not guidance. Help researchers weigh factors strategically.

### Ignoring Author Rights In Agreements

Signing away all rights limits future use. Read, understand, and negotiate agreements.

### Advising On Open Access Without Understanding Economics

APCs, embargoes, and mandates interact in complex ways. Understand the models to advise well.

### Neglecting Data Sharing Requirements

Data management is integral to modern research. Advise on plans, repositories, and privacy.

### Failing To Spot Predatory Publishers

Predatory publishing harms reputations and the record. Equip researchers to evaluate venues.

### Default Licensing Without Considering Reuse

Licensing shapes how work can be used. Guide researchers to licenses matching their goals.

### Recognizing Only Journal Articles As Outputs

Scholarly output takes many forms. Support OER, preprints, monographs, and projects.

### Static Knowledge In A Changing Landscape

Scholarly communication evolves constantly. Commit to ongoing learning.

## Self-Check

- Is the scholarly publishing ecosystem understood, including actors, economics, and pressures?
- Are researchers guided to choose publishing venues strategically, weighing reputation, access, cost, and mandates?
- Are author rights protected through reading, understanding, and negotiating publishing agreements?
- Are open access models and funder mandates navigated to find compliant, affordable paths?
- Is research data managed and shared with attention to plans, repositories, privacy, and licensing?
- Can predatory and exploitative publishers be spotted and avoided through critical evaluation?
- Are copyright and licensing decisions in scholarly outputs aligned with dissemination and reuse goals?
- Are open educational resources and alternative scholarly outputs supported alongside formal publication?
- Is the library engaged with scholarly communication policy and advocacy at relevant levels?
- Is knowledge kept current with the rapidly evolving scholarly communication landscape?
