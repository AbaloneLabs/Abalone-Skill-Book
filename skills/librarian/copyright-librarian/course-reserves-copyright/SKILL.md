# Course Reserves Copyright

Course reserves, whether physical or electronic, are where copyright law meets teaching practice most directly. Faculty want their students to have easy access to assigned readings; copyright law and license terms constrain what can be copied, posted, and distributed. The reserves operation is therefore a site of ongoing judgment about fair use, licensing, permissions, and policy. Getting it wrong creates institutional copyright risk; getting it overly right, in the sense of applying rules so strictly that legitimate educational uses are denied, undermines teaching and pushes faculty toward less compliant workarounds. Good reserves copyright practice understands the legal framework, applies fair use thoughtfully to reserve readings, respects license terms that may override copyright permissions, manages the special problem of repeated use across terms, and maintains clear, enforceable policies that faculty can follow without constant librarian intervention.

Use this skill when managing reserves copyright, deciding what may be placed on reserve, handling repeated or multi-term use, developing reserves policy, or advising faculty on reserves compliance. The goal is to prevent the agent from posting materials without fair use or license analysis, treating all repeated use as prohibited, ignoring license terms that govern electronic resources, or creating policies so strict they undermine teaching.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Legal Framework For Reserves

Course reserves operate within copyright law, fair use, and the terms of licenses for electronic resources. Each layer must be respected.

The framework:

- copyright law governs reproduction and distribution of protected works;
- fair use permits certain educational uses without permission;
- the classroom and reserve exceptions in copyright law provide additional limited permissions;
- licenses for electronic resources are contracts that may override copyright permissions;
- permissions and royalties are the path when fair use and exceptions do not apply.

No single layer is sufficient alone. Reserves compliance requires applying all applicable layers to each item.

### Apply Fair Use Thoughtfully To Reserve Readings

Fair use can support placing readings on reserve, but it is not unlimited. Apply the four-factor analysis to reserve uses.

Fair use for reserves:

- the purpose is educational, favoring fair use;
- limited access to enrolled students favors fair use;
- smaller portions relative to the whole favor fair use;
- minimal market effect when students would not otherwise purchase favor fair use;
- entire books or large portions of creative works are weaker cases.

Fair use supports reserve use of portions for enrolled classes. It does not automatically support copying entire works or broad distribution.

### Respect License Terms For Electronic Resources

Licensed e-resources, e-books, journals, and databases, carry terms that govern whether and how they may be used on reserve. Licenses are contracts and override general copyright permissions.

License considerations:

- check whether the license permits e-reserve or course pack use;
- some licenses permit persistent linking but not downloading or posting;
- some prohibit ILL or reserves entirely;
- geographic or user restrictions may apply;
- maintain a database of license terms for reserve-relevant resources.

Posting licensed content in violation of its terms is a breach of contract. Prefer persistent linking where licenses require it.

### Manage Repeated And Multi-Term Use

A key reserves copyright issue is repeated use of the same materials across multiple terms. Fair use is strongest for spontaneous, one-time use and weaker for repeated, systematic use.

Managing repeated use:

- first-term use of a portion may rely on fair use;
- repeated use across terms generally requires permission or royalties;
- track materials used repeatedly to identify permission needs;
- seek permission or pay royalties for repeated use;
- consider adopting a course pack or licensed reader model for stable repeated readings.

Treating all repeated use as prohibited is overly strict; treating all repeated use as fair use is risky. Apply the distinction and seek permission when needed.

### Distinguish Print And Electronic Reserves

Print and electronic reserves have different practical and legal considerations. Apply appropriate analysis to each.

Print reserves:

- physical copies at a service point with limited, enrolled-student access;
- fair use generally supports placing owned copies or portions on print reserve;
- the library's own copy or a legally obtained copy is the typical basis.

Electronic reserves:

- digital postings accessible to enrolled students;
- fair use analysis applies, with attention to access controls and distribution;
- license terms may permit or prohibit e-reserve use;
- persistent linking to licensed content is often the cleanest approach.

Do not assume print and e-reserves are legally identical. Apply the right analysis to each format.

### Use Persistent Linking Where Possible

For licensed electronic resources, persistent linking, directing students to the resource through the library's subscription, is often the cleanest reserves approach.

Linking advantages:

- avoids reproduction and distribution copyright issues;
- complies with licenses that permit linking but not posting;
- ensures students access the authoritative, current version;
- provides usage data that supports collection decisions;
- works for both e-reserves and learning management systems.

Prefer linking to licensed content over downloading and posting PDFs. Configure link resolution so off-campus students can access seamlessly.

### Develop Clear, Enforceable Reserves Policy

A written reserves copyright policy guides faculty, protects the institution, and ensures consistent practice. Develop and maintain one.

Policy elements:

- what may be placed on reserve under fair use, license, or permission;
- portion limits and access controls;
- repeated use rules and permission procedures;
- faculty responsibilities for providing legally obtained copies;
- the library's role in seeking permission and paying royalties;
- procedures for materials that cannot be placed on reserve.

A clear policy reduces ad hoc decisions and faculty confusion. Communicate it accessibly and apply it consistently.

### Seek Permission And Pay Royalties When Required

When fair use or license does not cover a reserve use, seek permission or pay royalties. This is a normal part of reserves operations, not a failure.

Permission processes:

- identify the rights holder, often through a collective rights organization;
- request permission with the specific use, term, and enrollment;
- pay the royalty fee, potentially through a course pack or reserves budget;
- document the permission and its terms;
- decline or find alternatives if permission is denied or cost is prohibitive.

Build the workflow and budget for permissions. Do not avoid seeking permission by overreaching on fair use or by declining legitimate requests.

### Integrate Reserves With Course Packs And Licensed Readers

For courses with extensive or repeated readings, course packs or licensed digital readers may be more appropriate than ad hoc reserves.

Integration considerations:

- course packs with obtained permissions provide legal clarity;
- licensed reader platforms streamline permission and royalty management;
- open educational resources reduce copyright burden for stable readings;
- consider cost to students when choosing models.

Reserves are one tool. For complex or repeated reading needs, recommend models that handle rights more systematically.

### Educate Faculty On Reserves Copyright

Faculty often do not understand reserves copyright and may submit materials that cannot be used as requested. Education reduces problems.

Education:

- explain the policy and the legal basis accessibly;
- provide guidance on what can be placed on reserve and how;
- offer alternatives like linking and OER;
- communicate timelines for permission seeking;
- respond to questions with practical, non-judgmental guidance.

Faculty who understand the framework submit compliant requests and value the library's expertise. Treat education as ongoing, not one-time.

## Common Traps

### Posting Materials Without Fair Use Or License Analysis

Every reserve item needs a legal basis. Analyze before posting.

### Ignoring License Terms

Licenses override copyright permissions. Check terms for e-resources.

### Treating All Repeated Use As Prohibited

First-term fair use differs from repeated use. Apply the distinction and seek permission when needed.

### Assuming Print And E-Reserves Are Identical

Formats have different considerations. Apply the right analysis.

### Downloading And Posting Instead Of Linking

Persistent linking is often cleaner for licensed content. Prefer it where licenses require.

### Vague Or Unenforced Policy

Unclear policies cause confusion and risk. Develop, communicate, and apply policy consistently.

### Avoiding Permission-Seeking

Permission is a normal reserves path. Build the workflow and budget for it.

### Faculty Left To Guess At Compliance

Uninformed faculty submit problematic requests. Educate proactively and accessibly.

## Self-Check

- Is the legal framework for reserves, copyright, fair use, exceptions, and licenses, understood and applied to each item?
- Is fair use applied thoughtfully to reserve readings with attention to portion, access, and market effect?
- Are license terms for electronic resources checked and respected, with persistent linking preferred where required?
- Is repeated and multi-term use managed with permission sought when fair use no longer applies?
- Are print and electronic reserves distinguished with format-appropriate analysis?
- Is persistent linking to licensed content preferred over downloading and posting where licenses permit?
- Is there a clear, enforceable reserves copyright policy covering fair use, licenses, permissions, and faculty responsibilities?
- Are permission and royalty workflows built and budgeted for uses beyond fair use and license?
- Are course packs and licensed readers considered for extensive or repeated reading needs?
- Is faculty education on reserves copyright ongoing, accessible, and practical?
