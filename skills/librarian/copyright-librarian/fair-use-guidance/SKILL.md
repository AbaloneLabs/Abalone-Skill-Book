# Fair Use Guidance

Fair use is the flexible doctrine that permits use of copyrighted material without permission under certain conditions. It is the legal foundation for much of what happens in education, research, criticism, and libraries, from quoting sources in scholarship to placing readings on reserve to digitizing materials for access. But fair use is not a clear rule; it is a four-factor balancing test applied to specific facts, and its outcomes are uncertain by design. Providing fair use guidance is therefore a judgment problem. The librarian must help users understand the factors, apply them honestly to the actual use, resist both excessive caution that denies legitimate uses and reckless overreach that creates risk, and know when fair use is strong enough to proceed and when permission or alternatives are needed. Good fair use guidance is principled, fact-specific, documented, and humble about the doctrine's inherent uncertainty.

Use this skill when applying fair use, analyzing the four factors, advising faculty and researchers, or deciding between fair use and permission-seeking. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating fair use as a rigid checklist, applying factors mechanically, refusing legitimate uses out of fear, or greenlighting uses without genuine factor analysis.

## Core Rules

### Understand Fair Use As A Flexible, Fact-Specific Doctrine

Fair use is not a set of bright-line rules. It is a balancing test applied to the specific circumstances of each use. Guidance must reflect this.

The nature of fair use:

- it is determined case by case, not by formula;
- the four factors are weighed together, not scored numerically;
- outcomes are uncertain and depend on the totality of circumstances;
- courts look at whether the use transforms the original and its market effect most heavily;
- guidance should reason through the factors, not assert a conclusion.

Treating fair use as a checklist with fixed thresholds misrepresents the doctrine. Guide users through reasoning, not rote application.

### Analyze All Four Factors In Context

The four statutory factors must each be considered, but their weight varies by case. Analyze each in the context of the specific use.

The four factors:

1. the purpose and character of the use, including whether it is commercial or educational, and whether it is transformative;
2. the nature of the copyrighted work, whether factual or creative, published or unpublished;
3. the amount and substantiality of the portion used relative to the whole;
4. the effect of the use on the potential market for or value of the work.

No single factor controls. Analyze how they interact for the specific use and explain the reasoning.

### Give Transformative Use Heavy Weight

Modern fair use jurisprudence gives substantial weight to whether a use is transformative, meaning it adds new meaning, message, or purpose rather than merely repackaging the original.

Transformative use:

- using a work for criticism, commentary, or scholarship is often transformative;
- using a work in a new context or for a different audience can be transformative;
- mere copying without added purpose is less likely to be transformative;
- transformation is about purpose and character, not just format change.

When a use is strongly transformative, fair use is more likely to apply even if other factors lean against it. Assess transformation honestly, not as a magic word.

### Assess Market Effect Honestly

The fourth factor, effect on the market, asks whether the use, if widespread, would harm the market for the original or a derivative. Courts weigh this heavily.

Market effect analysis:

- would the use substitute for purchasing or licensing the original?
- does it harm a market the rights holder reasonably exploits?
- educational or research uses with limited distribution have less market effect;
- uses that compete directly with the original's market weigh against fair use;
- consider both current and potential future markets.

Be honest about market effect. A use that clearly substitutes for a purchase or license is weaker, even in an educational context.

### Distinguish Strong From Weak Fair Use Cases

Not all fair use claims are equal. Some are strong and safe to proceed; others are weak and require permission or alternatives.

Strong fair use typically involves:

- transformative purposes like criticism, commentary, teaching, scholarship;
- small portions relative to the whole;
- factual or published works;
- limited distribution with no market harm.

Weak fair use typically involves:

- non-transformative, consumptive uses like copying entire works for convenience;
- creative or unpublished works;
- large or central portions;
- broad distribution that substitutes for the market.

Guide users to recognize where their case falls and act accordingly. Strong cases can proceed with documentation; weak cases need permission or alternatives.

### Resist Both Excessive Caution And Reckless Overreach

Two failure modes undermine fair use guidance. Excessive caution denies legitimate uses; reckless overreach creates risk.

Excessive caution:

- refusing uses that fair use clearly permits;
- demanding permission for de minimis or clearly transformative uses;
- treating any risk as unacceptable;
- eroding fair use through institutional timidity.

Reckless overreach:

- asserting fair use for clearly consumptive or market-substituting uses;
- ignoring factors that weigh against fair use;
- treating fair use as a license to copy freely.

Good guidance occupies the principled middle, applying the factors honestly and proceeding where fair use is genuinely strong.

### Document The Fair Use Analysis

When relying on fair use, document the analysis. Documentation demonstrates good faith and supports the institution if questioned.

Document:

- the specific material and use;
- the analysis of each factor;
- the reasoning for the fair use conclusion;
- any alternatives considered;
- the date and who made the determination.

Undocumented fair use decisions are hard to defend. Documentation is the evidence of reasoned, good-faith application.

### Know When To Seek Permission Or Use Alternatives

When fair use is weak, the path is to seek permission, use a licensed alternative, or find a different approach. Guide users to these options.

Alternatives:

- seek permission from the rights holder;
- use materials licensed by the library, open access, or public domain;
- link to legally available online sources rather than copying;
- use a smaller portion to strengthen fair use;
- find a comparable work with clearer rights.

Fair use is not the only tool. Knowing when it does not apply and what to do instead is part of competent guidance.

### Apply Codes Of Best Practices As Context

Various disciplines and library communities have developed codes of best practices in fair use that articulate how fair use applies in their contexts.

Using codes:

- academic and research library codes address common library uses;
- discipline-specific codes address teaching, scholarship, and creative practice;
- codes reflect community consensus and strengthen good-faith application;
- codes are persuasive but not legally binding.

Reference relevant codes to ground guidance in community practice. They help users see fair use as a living, applied doctrine, not an abstract test.

### Provide Guidance With Appropriate Humility

Fair use is inherently uncertain. Guidance should be confident where the doctrine is strong and honest about uncertainty where it is not.

Humility in guidance:

- state conclusions as well-reasoned judgments, not guarantees;
- acknowledge where factors are mixed or close;
- explain that fair use is a defense, not a permission;
- recommend consultation for high-stakes or novel uses;
- avoid absolutist claims in either direction.

Users need practical guidance to act, but overpromising certainty misrepresents the doctrine. Balance practicality with honesty about limits.

## Common Traps

### Treating Fair Use As A Rigid Checklist

Fixed thresholds misrepresent the flexible doctrine. Reason through the factors in context.

### Ignoring Transformative Use

Transformation is heavily weighted in modern jurisprudence. Assess it honestly.

### Dishonest Market Effect Analysis

A use that substitutes for the market is weak regardless of context. Assess market effect candidly.

### Refusing Legitimate Uses Out Of Fear

Excessive caution erodes fair use. Proceed where the doctrine is genuinely strong.

### Greenlighting Without Factor Analysis

Fair use is not a license to copy. Analyze the factors for each use.

### Undocumented Fair Use Decisions

Without documentation, good-faith application is hard to demonstrate. Record the analysis.

### Fair Use As The Default Without Considering Alternatives

When fair use is weak, seek permission or alternatives. Fair use is not the only option.

### Overpromising Certainty

Fair use is uncertain. Guide with confidence where strong and humility where not.

## Self-Check

- Is fair use understood as a flexible, fact-specific doctrine requiring reasoning, not rote application?
- Are all four factors analyzed in the context of the specific use, with their interaction explained?
- Is transformative use assessed honestly and given appropriate weight?
- Is market effect analyzed candidly, including substitution and potential markets?
- Are strong and weak fair use cases distinguished, with action matched to the strength of the case?
- Is guidance free of both excessive caution and reckless overreach?
- Is the fair use analysis documented with the material, use, factor reasoning, and conclusion?
- Are permission, licensed alternatives, and other options considered when fair use is weak?
- Are relevant codes of best practices referenced to ground guidance in community consensus?
- Is guidance provided with appropriate confidence where strong and humility about uncertainty where not?
