# Reserves And Course Materials

Course reserves are how libraries support teaching by making required materials available to students. The work looks operational, set readings on a shelf or in a system, but it sits at the intersection of pedagogy, copyright, licensing, and equity. Reserves that are poorly managed, with unclear copyright compliance, broken e-reserve links, or materials that do not match the syllabus, undermine teaching and create legal risk. Reserves that are well managed reduce student cost, ensure equitable access to required readings, and strengthen the library's partnership with faculty. Good reserves service coordinates closely with instructors, respects copyright and license terms, chooses appropriate formats, and removes barriers to student access.

Use this skill when managing course reserves, selecting print or electronic formats, handling copyright for reserve readings, coordinating with faculty, or designing reserves workflows. The goal is to prevent the agent from placing materials on reserve without copyright review, using e-reserves beyond license terms, failing to coordinate with the syllabus, or creating access barriers for students.

## Core Rules

### Coordinate With Faculty And The Syllabus

Reserves exist to support specific courses, and their value depends on alignment with what instructors actually assign. Close coordination with faculty is essential.

Coordinate:

- request reading lists and syllabi well before the term;
- clarify citations, editions, and specific chapters or page ranges;
- confirm whether readings are required or optional;
- identify materials the library already owns or can license;
- flag gaps and offer alternatives;
- establish a turnaround time and communication channel for late additions.

Reserves that do not match the syllabus frustrate students and faculty. Treat faculty coordination as the foundation of the service.

### Choose Print Or Electronic Format Deliberately

Format choice for reserves depends on the material, the course, copyright, licensing, and student access patterns. Defaulting to one format underserves some needs.

Format considerations:

- print reserves suit physical books and materials not licensed electronically;
- electronic reserves suit articles, chapters, and materials available digitally;
- electronic reserves enable simultaneous access by the whole class;
- print reserves limit access to one borrower at a time, requiring short loan periods;
- licensing may permit or restrict electronic use of specific titles;
- student device access and preferences affect format effectiveness.

Match format to the material and the access need. Some materials belong on both print and electronic reserves.

### Navigate Copyright For Reserve Readings

Copyright is the central legal issue for reserves, especially electronic reserves. Compliance requires understanding what can be placed on reserve and under what conditions.

Copyright considerations:

- generally, one copy of a book chapter or a journal article may be placed on reserve;
- repeated use of the same readings across terms may require permission or licensing;
- fair use applies but has limits, especially for substantial portions or repeated use;
- license terms for electronic resources may permit or restrict reserve use;
- course packs and anthologies require explicit permission beyond fair use;
- library-owned ebooks may have reserve-compatible or incompatible licenses.

Document the copyright basis for each reserve item. When in doubt, seek permission or use licensed alternatives. Consult institutional copyright policy and counsel.

### Respect License Terms For Electronic Resources

Licensed electronic resources, ebooks, databases, and journals come with terms that govern reserve use. Ignoring these terms violates contracts and risks access loss.

Respect:

- read the license for each resource before placing it on e-reserve;
- distinguish concurrent user limits, course use permissions, and DRM restrictions;
- some licenses permit persistent links to articles, which is often the cleanest e-reserve method;
- some licenses prohibit downloading and reposting chapters;
- track which resources have reserve-friendly licenses.

Persistent linking to licensed content, where permitted, often satisfies reserve needs without copyright exposure. Build this into workflows.

### Design For Equitable Student Access

Reserves exist to ensure all students can access required materials, often as a cost-saving measure. Design the service to maximize equitable access.

Equity practices:

- prioritize required over optional materials for limited reserve space;
- use electronic reserves to enable simultaneous whole-class access;
- keep print reserve loan periods short enough to serve the whole class;
- ensure e-reserve platforms are accessible to students with disabilities;
- provide materials in multiple formats where possible;
- remove cost barriers by using library-owned or licensed content.

Reserves that favor students who can buy the book fail the equity mission. Design for the student who relies on the library.

### Manage Turnaround And Deadlines

Faculty often submit reserve requests close to the start of term, creating spikes. Managing turnaround while setting expectations is operationally critical.

Manage turnaround:

- communicate deadlines to faculty well in advance;
- process requests in priority order, required readings first;
- have workflows for copyright clearance, acquisition, and scanning;
- track and report on outstanding requests;
- build in capacity for mid-term additions and changes.

Late or incomplete reserves undermine teaching. Set realistic expectations and meet them.

### Handle Acquisition And Licensing Gaps

Reading lists often include materials the library does not own or license. Reserves staff must close these gaps.

Gap handling:

- purchase or license needed materials, considering reserve-friendly terms;
- request items through interlibrary loan for short-term reserve, respecting ILL copyright and license limits;
- work with faculty to identify alternatives when materials cannot be acquired;
- track recurring gaps to inform collection development;
- build a reserve-friendly acquisition workflow.

Gaps are common and expected. Have a process to address them rather than placing incomplete lists on reserve.

### Ensure Accessibility Of Electronic Reserves

Electronic reserves must be accessible to students with disabilities. Inaccessible e-reserves exclude students and may violate legal obligations.

Accessibility practices:

- use accessible PDFs with text rather than image-only scans;
- provide captions or transcripts for audiovisual reserve materials;
- ensure the e-reserve platform conforms to accessibility standards;
- offer alternative formats on request;
- test with screen readers and other assistive technology.

Accessibility is a legal and ethical requirement. Build it into scanning and posting workflows, not as an afterthought.

### Maintain Accurate And Current Lists

Reserve lists go stale as courses change. Maintain accuracy to avoid confusing students and wasting limited copies.

Maintain:

- remove reserves for courses no longer using them;
- update readings when faculty revise syllabi;
- verify links and availability before each term;
- archive or weed outdated reserve materials;
- communicate with faculty about needed updates.

Stale reserves signal neglect. Schedule cleanup each term.

### Document And Report Reserves Activity

Reserves activity data supports copyright compliance, staffing decisions, and collection development.

Document and report:

- items placed on reserve and their copyright basis;
- usage data, how often items are borrowed or accessed;
- turnaround times and outstanding requests;
- copyright clearance actions and costs;
- gaps and acquisitions triggered by reserves;
- compliance with license terms.

Documentation protects the institution and informs service improvement.

## Common Traps

### Reserves Without Copyright Review

Placing readings on reserve without assessing copyright creates legal risk. Review each item's copyright basis.

### Ignoring License Terms

Licensed resources have terms governing reserve use. Violating terms risks access loss and contract breach.

### Mismatch With The Syllabus

Reserves that do not match what is assigned frustrate students and faculty. Coordinate closely.

### Defaulting To One Format

Print or electronic alone underserves some materials and courses. Match format to need.

### Inaccessible E-Reserves

Image-only scans and uncaptioned media exclude students with disabilities. Build accessibility in.

### Late Or Incomplete Processing

Reserves not ready when the course needs them undermine teaching. Manage turnaround and deadlines.

### Unresolved Acquisition Gaps

Reading lists with materials not owned or licensed leave students without access. Close gaps proactively.

### Stale Lists

Outdated reserves confuse students and waste copies. Maintain and clean up each term.

## Self-Check

- Are reserve lists coordinated with faculty and aligned to the actual syllabus?
- Are print and electronic formats chosen deliberately based on material, access need, and licensing?
- Is copyright assessed and documented for each reserve item?
- Are license terms for electronic resources respected, with persistent linking used where permitted?
- Is the service designed for equitable access by all students, including those who rely on the library?
- Are turnaround times managed with clear deadlines and priority processing?
- Are acquisition and licensing gaps closed through purchase, ILL, or faculty consultation?
- Are electronic reserves accessible to students with disabilities?
- Are reserve lists maintained and updated each term to remove stale materials?
- Is reserves activity documented for copyright compliance, staffing, and collection development?
