# Circulation Policy

Circulation policy is the rulebook of who can borrow what, for how long, and under what conditions. It looks administrative, but every rule encodes a value judgment about access, equity, security, and the library's relationship with its community. Punitive fine structures create barriers for low-income users. Overly generous loan periods starve high-demand collections. Inconsistent policies across user types generate resentment. Good circulation policy balances broad access with material security, treats fines and fees as a policy lever rather than a revenue stream, and is reviewed against its actual effects on different user groups. Circulation is often the public's most frequent library interaction, and its policies shape how the community experiences the institution.

Use this skill when setting or revising circulation policies, loan periods, fine structures, holds and recalls, or reviewing policy effects on users. The goal is to prevent the agent from copying policies without analysis, using fines punitively, creating inequitable access across user types, or ignoring how policies affect vulnerable users.

## Core Rules

### Ground Policy In Mission And Community

Circulation policy should express the library's mission and fit its community. A research library, a public library, a school library, and an academic library have different goals and need different circulation rules.

Ground policy by clarifying:

- the library's primary purpose, access, research, education, preservation;
- the community's characteristics, income, age, language, mobility;
- the balance between maximum access and material security;
- the role of circulation in equity and inclusion;
- how policy affects the most vulnerable users.

Policy that is not grounded in mission drifts toward convention or toward whatever is administratively convenient.

### Set Loan Periods By Material Type And Demand

Loan periods should match how materials are used. A blanket loan period for everything underserves both high-demand and low-demand materials.

Set loan periods considering:

- demand, shorter for high-demand, longer for research and depth;
- format, different for bestsellers, reference, equipment, course reserves;
- user type, faculty, students, community, with justified differences;
- renewability and renewal limits;
- seasonal or academic calendar considerations.

Periodic review of circulation data reveals whether loan periods fit actual use. Materials with chronic holds need shorter periods; materials rarely borrowed may warrant longer.

### Treat Fines And Fees As Policy Levers, Not Revenue

Fines are a policy tool with significant equity implications. Treating them as a revenue stream corrupts the policy and harms vulnerable users.

Considerations:

- fines disproportionately block low-income users from library access;
- the revenue from fines is often small relative to the harm and administrative cost;
- many libraries have eliminated overdue fines while maintaining material return through other means;
- replacement fees for genuinely lost materials remain legitimate;
- blocks and collections actions should be designed to recover materials, not punish.

Evaluate the fine structure against its effects on different user groups. If fines are blocking the users the library most needs to serve, the policy is failing its mission.

### Design Holds And Recalls For Fairness

Holds and recalls manage competition for popular or shared materials. Their design affects fairness and user experience.

Design considerations:

- hold queues that are transparent and fair;
- recall policies that balance the current borrower's need with others' access;
- pickup location and notification options that reduce barriers;
- time limits on holds to keep materials moving;
- special recall rules for course reserves and faculty needs.

Opaque or unfair hold systems generate frustration. Design them for transparency and equitable access.

### Differentiate By User Type Justifiably

Libraries often serve multiple user types with different borrowing privileges. Differences should be justified by purpose, not by status alone.

Justified differences:

- shorter loan periods for high-demand community use in public libraries;
- longer loan periods for faculty researchers in academic libraries;
- restricted borrowing for high-value or specialized materials;
- different limits for minors, with appropriate privacy protections;
- community borrower privileges distinct from primary user privileges.

Document the rationale for each difference. Unjustified privilege distinctions erode trust and equity.

### Protect Privacy In Circulation Records

Circulation records reveal what people read, watch, and borrow. Protecting this privacy is a core library value and often a legal requirement.

Privacy practices:

- retain borrowing records only as long as needed for the transaction;
- purge records after return, not retaining history of what was borrowed;
- protect records from law enforcement requests without proper legal process;
- limit staff access to borrowing records to legitimate service needs;
- do not use borrowing data for surveillance or marketing.

Privacy is a policy issue, not just a technical one. Circulation policy should explicitly address record retention and confidentiality.

### Build In Accommodation And Flexibility

Rigid policies harm users with circumstances the policy did not anticipate. Build in accommodation for legitimate needs.

Accommodations:

- extended loan periods for users with disabilities or illness;
- alternative pickup or delivery for homebound or remote users;
- fine forgiveness or payment plans for users in hardship;
- proxy borrowing for caregivers or assistants;
- appeal processes for unusual circumstances.

Accommodation does not undermine policy; it recognizes that one-size-fits-all rules fail real people. Make the accommodation process clear and non-stigmatizing.

### Communicate Policy Clearly And Accessibly

Users cannot comply with policies they do not understand. Communication should be clear, accessible, and prominent.

Communicate:

- loan periods, limits, and renewal rules at checkout and online;
- fine and fee structures upfront, not as surprises;
- hold and recall procedures;
- privacy protections and record practices;
- accommodation and appeal processes;
- in the languages and formats the community uses.

Clear communication reduces confusion, disputes, and the staff time spent explaining rules.

### Monitor Policy Effects And Revise

Circulation policy is not permanent. Monitor its effects and revise based on evidence.

Monitor:

- circulation rates by material type and user group;
- overdue and loss rates;
- fine accumulation and its distribution across user groups;
- hold queue lengths and wait times;
- user complaints and accommodation requests;
- effects of policy changes over time.

Data reveals whether the policy achieves its goals and whether it creates unintended harms. Schedule periodic policy review.

### Align With Consortial And Interlibrary Practices

Many libraries participate in consortial borrowing or interlibrary loan. Circulation policy should align with these partnerships.

Align:

- reciprocal borrowing privileges with partner institutions;
- consistent loan periods and fine structures where feasible;
- clear responsibility for lost or damaged partner materials;
- tracking and reporting for shared collections.

Misaligned policies create friction in resource sharing. Coordinate with partners on shared rules.

## Common Traps

### Copying Policies Without Analysis

Adopting another library's policies without fitting them to local mission and community produces mismatched rules. Analyze before adopting.

### Fines As Revenue Or Punishment

Punitive fines block vulnerable users and often cost more to administer than they collect. Treat fines as a policy lever with equity effects.

### Blanket Loan Periods

One loan period for everything underserves both high-demand and low-demand materials. Differentiate by type and demand.

### Unjustified User Type Differences

Privilege distinctions without rationale erode equity. Document the purpose of each difference.

### Retaining Borrowing History

Keeping records of what users borrowed violates privacy. Purge records after return.

### No Accommodation Process

Rigid policies without accommodation harm users with legitimate special circumstances. Build in flexibility.

### Unclear Communication

Users cannot follow policies they do not understand. Communicate clearly and accessibly.

### Never Reviewing Policy Effects

Policies that are never monitored and revised accumulate unintended harms. Schedule review.

## Self-Check

- Is circulation policy grounded in the library's mission and community characteristics?
- Are loan periods differentiated by material type and demand, informed by circulation data?
- Are fines and fees evaluated as policy levers with equity effects, not as revenue?
- Are holds and recalls designed for transparency and fairness?
- Are user type differences justified by purpose and documented?
- Are circulation records protected and purged to preserve user privacy?
- Is there a clear, non-stigmatizing process for accommodation and appeals?
- Are policies communicated clearly, accessibly, and in community languages?
- Are policy effects monitored and revised based on evidence?
- Are policies aligned with consortial and interlibrary lending practices?
