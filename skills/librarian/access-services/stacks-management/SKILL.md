# Stacks Management

The stacks are the library's largest and most physical interface. How materials are shelved, spaced, signed, and maintained determines whether the catalog's promise of availability becomes reality. Stacks management looks like shelving books, but it encompasses collection layout, space allocation, inventory accuracy, shelfreading, shifting, signage, and the physical browsing experience. Neglected stacks produce lost items, misshelved materials, browseable disorder, and a collection that exists on paper but not in practice. Good stacks management keeps the physical collection accurate, accessible, and welcoming, balances the competing demands of growth and space, and treats the stacks as a discovery interface that deserves the same care as the online catalog.

Use this skill when managing stacks organization, planning shifts, conducting inventory, designing signage and layout, or addressing browseability and accessibility. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating shelving as an afterthought, letting inventory accuracy degrade, shifting without planning, or ignoring the browsing experience and accessibility of the physical space.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Stacks As A Discovery Interface

The stacks are how users browse and discover by proximity, an access mode the catalog cannot replicate. Manage them with the care an interface deserves.

Implications:

- shelving order must match the catalog's classification exactly;
- materials must be where the catalog says they are;
- the physical arrangement must support browsing, not just storage;
- signage and layout must guide users intuitively;
- the space must be welcoming and accessible.

A user who finds the shelf in disorder loses trust in both the stacks and the catalog. Stacks quality is a service quality issue.

### Maintain Inventory Accuracy

Inventory accuracy is the correspondence between what the catalog says is on the shelf and what is actually there. It degrades continuously through misshelving, loss, theft, and incomplete processing.

Maintain accuracy through:

- regular inventory cycles, scanning or checking the shelf against the catalog;
- shelfreading, checking call number order, on a scheduled basis;
- prompt reshelving of returned materials;
- tracking and searching for missing items;
- weeding and withdrawal processed promptly and accurately;
- investigating discrepancies rather than ignoring them.

Set an inventory cycle realistic for the collection size. Even partial annual inventory beats none. Track missing-item rates as a stacks health metric.

### Plan Shifts Deliberately

Shifting, moving sections of the collection to redistribute space, is labor-intensive and disruptive. Unplanned shifts create chaos and damage.

Plan shifts:

- calculate space needs based on growth projections, not just current fill;
- leave growth room, commonly ten to twenty percent, in each section;
- sequence the shift to minimize disruption and double handling;
- communicate with users about access during the shift;
- staff adequately and train shift teams;
- protect materials during moving;
- verify order after the shift.

Reactive, crisis-driven shifts, when shelves are overflowing, are more damaging and costly than planned shifts done with growth room.

### Allocate Space By Growth And Use

Space allocation should reflect where the collection is growing and how it is used, not just historical layout.

Allocate considering:

- growth rates by classification area;
- usage patterns, high-use materials near access points;
- format-specific needs, oversized, media, periodicals;
- special or restricted collections with separate storage;
- future collection development plans;
- consortial or shared collection commitments.

Reallocate space periodically as collections grow and change. Static allocation leads to overcrowding in growth areas and wasted space in stagnant ones.

### Design Effective Signage And Wayfinding

Users navigate the stacks primarily through signage. Poor signage makes a browsable collection unnavigable.

Effective signage:

- clear call number ranges on each stack end;
- directional signs to major sections and services;
- consistent terminology with the catalog;
- readable type size and contrast;
- multilingual signage where the community needs it;
- accessible placement and height.

Wayfinding is part of access. Users who cannot find the section cannot use the collection, regardless of catalog accuracy.

### Ensure Physical Accessibility

The stacks must be accessible to all users, including those with mobility, visual, and other disabilities.

Accessibility provisions:

- aisles wide enough for wheelchairs and mobility devices;
- reachable shelf heights, with staff assistance for high or low shelves;
- accessible paths throughout the stacks;
- adequate lighting for visual access;
- braille or tactile signage where appropriate;
- seating and rest areas in larger stacks.

Accessibility is a legal and ethical obligation. Physical barriers exclude users from the collection.

### Manage Shelving And Reshelving Workflows

Efficient and accurate shelving workflows keep materials moving from return to shelf without backlog or error.

Manage workflows:

- sort returned materials by call number before shelving;
- shelve promptly to avoid backlogs and "in transit" status;
- train shelvers in classification order and local practices;
- quality-check shelving through shelfreading;
- track shelving backlog and turnaround;
- handle special cases, holds, repairs, withdrawals separately.

Backlogs and misshelving degrade availability. Monitor shelving metrics and address bottlenecks.

### Conduct Regular Shelfreading

Shelfreading, checking that materials are in correct call number order, catches misshelving that inventory scans may miss. It is essential for browseability.

Shelfreading practice:

- schedule regular reading of the entire collection on a cycle;
- read for correct order, condition, and obvious problems;
- correct misshelved items immediately;
- flag damaged or missing items;
- track sections read and issues found.

Shelfreading is unglamorous but irreplaceable. A section that is never read accumulates disorder that frustrates browsing.

### Handle Weeding And Withdrawal In The Stacks

Weeding, removing materials from the collection, has a stacks dimension. Withdrawn materials must be removed promptly and accurately.

Stacks aspects of weeding:

- remove withdrawn materials from the shelf promptly;
- update the catalog to reflect withdrawals;
- realign and fill gaps left by withdrawals;
- use the freed space for growth or improved browsability;
- handle withdrawn materials per disposition policy.

Weeding that leaves ghost records or gap-filled shelves undermines both the catalog and the stacks.

### Monitor Stacks Health And Environment

The stacks environment affects preservation. Monitor conditions that damage materials.

Monitor:

- temperature and humidity within preservation ranges;
- light levels, especially UV, that fade and damage materials;
- pest activity and signs of infestation;
- mold and water damage;
- cleanliness and dust;
- structural issues with shelving.

Environmental problems in the stacks cause slow, cumulative damage. Regular monitoring catches issues before they become disasters.

## Common Traps

### Shelving As Afterthought

Treating shelving as low-skill labor produces misshelving and disorder. Train and value shelvers.

### Inventory Accuracy Left To Degrade

Without regular inventory and shelfreading, the catalog and stacks diverge. Schedule and perform both.

### Reactive Crisis Shifting

Waiting until shelves overflow forces damaging emergency shifts. Plan shifts with growth room.

### Poor Signage And Wayfinding

Users cannot navigate unclear stacks. Invest in effective, accessible signage.

### Inaccessible Stacks

Narrow aisles and unreachable shelves exclude users. Ensure physical accessibility.

### Shelving Backlogs

Returned materials not reshelved promptly show as unavailable. Monitor and clear backlogs.

### Ghost Records After Weeding

Withdrawn materials left on shelves or catalog records left after removal create confusion. Keep catalog and stacks in sync.

### Unmonitored Environment

Temperature, humidity, pests, and light cause slow damage. Monitor stacks conditions.

## Self-Check

- Are the stacks managed as a discovery interface, with order matching the catalog exactly?
- Is inventory accuracy maintained through regular inventory cycles and shelfreading?
- Are shifts planned with growth room, sequencing, communication, and verification?
- Is space allocated by growth rates and usage, with periodic reallocation?
- Is signage clear, consistent, accessible, and multilingual where needed?
- Are the stacks physically accessible to users with mobility, visual, and other disabilities?
- Are shelving and reshelving workflows efficient, trained, and monitored for backlog?
- Is shelfreading conducted on a regular cycle to catch misshelving?
- Are weeding and withdrawals reflected promptly in both stacks and catalog?
- Are stacks environmental conditions, temperature, humidity, light, pests, monitored?
