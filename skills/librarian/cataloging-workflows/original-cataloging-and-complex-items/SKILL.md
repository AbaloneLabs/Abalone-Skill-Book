# Original Cataloging And Complex Items

Original cataloging is what catalogers do when no usable record exists. It is the most demanding cataloging work because every decision must be made from the item and the standards, with no template to follow. Complex items compound the difficulty: multipart works with inconsistent title pages, items with shared or unclear responsibility, rare materials with idiosyncratic features, integrating resources that change over time, and resources that no rule addresses directly. In these cases the cataloger cannot look up the right answer; they must construct a defensible record from principles, document the reasoning, and often establish new authorities. The risk is not just error but paralysis, either freezing on ambiguous items or forcing them into ill-fitting records. Good original cataloging of complex items combines deep standards knowledge with documented judgment, so the record is accurate, reproducible, and useful even when the situation is unprecedented.

Use this skill when doing original cataloging, handling complex or multipart items, cataloging rare or unique materials, or resolving situations where no rule or record directly applies. The goal is to prevent the agent from guessing without documentation, forcing complex items into simple records, skipping authority work, or producing records that another cataloger could not reproduce or trust.

## Core Rules

### Work From The Standard's Principles, Not Just Its Rules

When no specific rule covers the item, the cataloger must reason from the standard's underlying principles. Knowing the rules is not enough; understanding why they exist lets you extend them to new situations.

Principles to apply:

- the four user tasks, find, identify, select, obtain, as the test for every element;
- transcription from the chief source as the basis of description;
- collocation through consistent access points and authority control;
- description of the specific manifestation in hand;
- recording relationships to related works and expressions.

When a situation is unprecedented, ask what choice best serves the user tasks and the principles, document the reasoning, and apply it consistently. A principled, documented decision is defensible even when no rule names the case.

### Identify The Chief Source And Basis Of Description Deliberately

Complex items often lack a clear title page. The cataloger must choose the chief source and basis of description deliberately and document the choice.

Decisions for complex items:

- when no title page exists, use the prescribed substitute and note it;
- when sources conflict, choose the one that best represents the work and document why;
- for multipart items, decide whether to describe the whole or analyze parts;
- for integrating resources, describe the current iteration and note the integrating nature;
- for collections, decide between collection-level and item-level description.

The basis of description determines what is transcribed. A different basis produces a different record. Document it so the choice is visible and reproducible.

### Resolve Shared And Unclear Responsibility

Complex items often have many creators, unclear roles, or responsibility distributed across editors, compilers, translators, and corporate bodies. Resolving this determines the access points.

Resolution:

- identify the primary creator or creators by their contribution and the work's nature;
- apply the standard's rules for shared responsibility versus mixed responsibility;
- use relationship designators to specify roles, author, editor, compiler, translator;
- add access points for substantial secondary contributors;
- handle corporate body authorship per the rules for that body type;
- record variant forms of responsibility found on the source.

A work's responsibility is not always obvious from the title page. Analyze the actual contributions and document ambiguous cases.

### Handle Multipart And Serial Items Coherently

Multipart monographs, serials, and integrating resources each have specific description challenges that simple monograph cataloging does not address.

Multipart monographs:

- describe the whole, with analysis of significant parts if warranted;
- record the extent and the specific volumes held;
- handle varying titles across volumes with notes and added entries;
- link to related multipart items or preceding and succeeding titles.

Serials:

- use the latest title as the basis, with notes for changes;
- record numbering, dates, and publication frequency;
- link preceding and succeeding entries for title changes;
- handle the serial's unique access point and uniform title.

Integrating resources:

- describe the current iteration;
- note the resource type and update mechanism;
- record the date of the iteration consulted.

Do not force these into a simple monograph record. Use the appropriate description pattern and link related items.

### Catalog Rare And Unique Materials With Full Documentation

Rare and unique materials require fuller description and more documentation than routine items, because each copy may differ and provenance matters.

Rare materials practice:

- describe copy-specific features, bindings, inscriptions, provenance, markings;
- record distinguishing physical features, paper, type, illustrations;
- note the provenance and acquisition source where relevant;
- establish or use specialized rare-book authorities and vocabularies;
- link to ESTC, ISTC, or relevant bibliographies;
- provide fuller access points for discovery by researchers.

Rare materials cataloging is often the only record of a unique item. Document thoroughly so the record serves researchers who cannot examine the physical item.

### Establish New Authorities As Needed

Original cataloging frequently requires establishing new name, work, or subject authorities. Skipping this breaks collocation.

Authority establishment:

- search cooperative files thoroughly before creating a new authority;
- establish the authorized form per the standard and reference sources;
- record variants, dates, and distinguishing information;
- document the basis and cite sources;
- contribute new authorities to cooperative programs when appropriate;
- link the bibliographic record to the authority.

An original record without authority support scatters the work from related materials. Build the authorities alongside the description.

### Document Unprecedented Decisions For Reproducibility

When the cataloger makes a judgment call that no rule directly covers, the reasoning must be documented so the record is reproducible and defensible.

Document:

- the situation and why no rule directly applies;
- the principle or analogous rule used as the basis;
- the choice made and the alternatives considered;
- the sources consulted;
- the date and cataloger, for future reference and revision.

A documented unprecedented decision becomes a precedent that informs future cases. An undocumented one is an inscrutable artifact.

### Seek Review For Complex Or High-Value Items

Complex and high-value original cataloging benefits from review by experienced colleagues. Review catches errors and builds shared practice.

Review practice:

- route complex items to senior or specialist catalogers;
- use peer review for rare, archival, or high-research-value materials;
- discuss unprecedented cases in cataloging meetings;
- maintain examples of well-handled complex items as references.

Review is not a sign of inexperience; it is a quality safeguard for the most consequential records.

## Common Traps

### Guessing Without Documenting The Reasoning

An undocumented judgment call cannot be reproduced or trusted. Document the basis for every non-routine decision.

### Forcing Complex Items Into Simple Records

A multipart, serial, or integrating resource forced into a monograph record loses essential information. Use the appropriate pattern.

### Skipping Authority Work On Original Records

Without established authorities, the work scatters from related materials. Build authorities alongside the description.

### Under-Describing Rare Or Unique Materials

Routine-level description loses copy-specific and provenance information researchers need. Describe rare materials fully.

### Choosing The Chief Source Without Documentation

A different basis of description yields a different record. Document the choice of chief source.

### Ignoring Relationship Links Between Parts And Related Works

Treating each item as isolated hides connections users need. Record relationships explicitly.

### Treating Unprecedented Decisions As Private Judgment

Undocumented decisions do not inform future work. Document them as precedents.

### Skipping Review On High-Value Original Cataloging

Errors in high-value records persist and mislead. Use peer review for complex items.

## Self-Check

- When no rule directly applies, is the decision reasoned from the standard's principles and documented?
- Is the chief source and basis of description chosen deliberately and noted in the record?
- Is shared or unclear responsibility resolved with relationship designators and documented judgment?
- Are multipart, serial, or integrating resources described with the appropriate pattern and linked to related items?
- Are rare and unique materials described with copy-specific features, provenance, and fuller access points?
- Are new name, work, or subject authorities established, documented, and linked to the bibliographic record?
- Are unprecedented decisions documented with situation, principle, choice, sources, and date for reproducibility?
- Are complex or high-value original records reviewed by experienced colleagues before finalizing?
