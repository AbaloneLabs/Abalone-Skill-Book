# Copy Cataloging And Record Reuse

Most cataloging today is copy cataloging: taking an existing record from a shared utility or vendor and adapting it to the local item. Done well, this is efficient and produces consistent records. Done poorly, it propagates errors across institutions, describes the wrong edition, overrides good data with local quirks, or strips useful information because the cataloger did not understand it. Copy cataloging is not a lower-skill task than original cataloging; it is a different skill. The cataloger must judge what to trust, what to verify, what to revise, and what to leave alone, and they must do it faster than original cataloging allows. The central risk is treating a borrowed record as authoritative for the item in hand when it describes a different manifestation entirely. A copy-cataloged record is only correct when it has been verified against the actual item and adjusted to match it.

Use this skill when copy cataloging, adapting vendor or utility records, deciding what to accept or revise in a borrowed record, or managing copy-cataloging quality. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting records uncritically, describing the wrong edition or item, destroying useful data through ignorant edits, or letting copy-cataloging quality drift over time.

## Core Rules

### Verify The Record Matches The Item In Hand

The single most important step in copy cataloging is confirming the borrowed record describes the same manifestation as the item in hand. Editions, reprints, bindings, publishers, and years differ in ways that look trivial but make the record wrong for the item.

Verify:

- edition and impression statements;
- publication date, publisher, and place;
- physical description, extent, format, and accompanying material;
- ISBN or other standard number;
- series statement;
- any local peculiarities of the specific copy.

A record for the hardcover first edition does not serve a paperback reprint. If the manifestation differs, find or derive the correct record rather than forcing a mismatch.

### Trust But Verify Each Element Category

Different elements of a borrowed record warrant different levels of trust. A blanket policy of accept-all or revise-all is inefficient and error-prone.

Element trust levels:

- transcription elements, title, statement of responsibility, edition, publication, transcribe from the item, do not trust the borrowed record blindly;
- access points, subject headings, name authorities, generally reliable from major utilities, but verify for local accuracy and completeness;
- classification, often needs local adjustment for the collection's scheme and cutters;
- fixed-length data and coding, verify and correct, as errors here break sorting and limiting;
- notes, evaluate for relevance and accuracy to the local item.

Calibrate effort. Spend verification time on the elements most likely to differ or to cause discovery failures.

### Adapt Classification And Call Numbers To Local Practice

Call numbers from a shared utility rarely match local shelf arrangement exactly. Local adaptation is a routine and necessary part of copy cataloging.

Local adjustments:

- apply the local classification scheme, LC, Dewey, NLM, as appropriate;
- adjust cutters to fit the local shelf and to avoid collisions;
- add local location, collection, and copy-specific information;
- handle local subject or genre additions for the collection's needs;
- verify the call number against the local catalog for conflicts.

Do not accept a utility call number that does not fit the local collection. A misfit call number misshelves the item for its entire life.

### Preserve Useful Data, Do Not Strip It

Copy catalogers sometimes delete elements they do not understand or that their local system does not display. This destroys valuable data for other institutions and for future system migrations.

Preserve:

- access points and subject headings, even if locally non-displaying;
- relationship links and contents notes;
- fixed-field codes that support machine processing;
- vernacular or alternative script data;
- series authority data and tracing.

If an element is not locally used, suppress its display rather than deleting it. Deleting data that other systems or future migrations might need is a one-way loss.

### Add Local Data Without Corrupting The Shared Record

Local additions, location, collection, local subjects, local notes, belong in locally controlled fields. They must not overwrite the shared bibliographic data that other institutions depend on.

Separation:

- put local holdings, location, and copy data in holdings or item records, not the bibliographic record;
- use locally-defined fields for local subjects or notes;
- do not change the encoding level or cataloging level to imply more or less than was done;
- when contributing corrections back to the utility, follow the contribution process rather than silently editing.

Corrupting a shared record with local data affects every institution that later reuses it.

### Match The Record To The Cataloging Level

Not every item warrants the same depth. Copy cataloging should respect the intended level and not over- or under-invest.

Level matching:

- high-use or research items may warrant enhancement beyond the borrowed record;
- routine items may be accepted at the borrowed level with verification;
- low-use items may be accepted at minimal level to maintain throughput;
- batch or vendor records may be accepted with sampling rather than item-by-item review.

Have a policy that assigns level by collection, format, or use, so effort is spent where it pays.

### Manage Copy Cataloging Quality Over Time

Copy cataloging at scale accumulates quality issues. A workflow that never reviews copy-cataloged records drifts toward inconsistency.

Quality management:

- sample-review copy-cataloged records on a schedule;
- track recurring errors from specific vendors or utilities;
- feed corrections back to the utility when errors are in shared data;
- train catalogers on common copy-cataloging pitfalls;
- maintain a local guide for what to accept, revise, and add.

Copy cataloging quality is a collective property maintained by review, not by individual good intentions.

### Document Local Copy-Cataloging Practice

The decisions about what to trust, revise, and add must be documented so they are consistent across staff and time.

Document:

- which elements are verified, accepted, or revised;
- local classification and call number conventions;
- which fields are preserved versus locally suppressed;
- the cataloging level policy by collection or format;
- the process for contributing corrections to the utility.

Undocumented copy-cataloging practice produces inconsistency that grows with every new cataloger.

## Common Traps

### Accepting A Record For The Wrong Edition

A borrowed record for a different manifestation is wrong for the item. Verify edition, date, publisher, and physical description every time.

### Trusting All Elements Equally

Transcription elements need item verification; access points are more trustworthy. Calibrate effort by element type.

### Accepting Utility Call Numbers Without Local Adjustment

A call number that does not fit the local shelf misshelves the item. Adapt classification and cutters to local practice.

### Stripping Data The Local System Does Not Display

Deleting non-displaying elements destroys data for other systems and future migrations. Suppress display, do not delete.

### Overwriting Shared Data With Local Additions

Local data in shared fields corrupts the record for every reuser. Keep local data in local fields.

### Over-Cataloging Routine Items

Spending full-level effort on low-use items wastes scarce time. Match depth to value.

### No Quality Review Of Copy-Cataloged Records

Without sampling and feedback, copy-cataloging quality drifts. Review on a schedule.

### Undocumented Local Practice

Inconsistent decisions across catalogers grow over time. Document what to accept, revise, and add.

## Self-Check

- Has the borrowed record been verified to describe the same manifestation as the item in hand?
- Is each element category trusted at the appropriate level, with transcription verified against the item?
- Has the call number been adapted to local classification and shelf practice?
- Is useful data preserved rather than stripped because the local system does not display it?
- Are local additions kept in local fields, separate from shared bibliographic data?
- Is the cataloging depth matched to the item's value and use per a documented policy?
- Is copy-cataloging quality managed through scheduled sampling, error tracking, and feedback?
- Is local copy-cataloging practice documented for consistency across staff and time?
