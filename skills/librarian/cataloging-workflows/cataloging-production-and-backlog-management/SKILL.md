# Cataloging Production And Backlog Management

Cataloging is a production environment as much as a craft. Materials arrive continuously, each waits for a record, and the cataloger's time is the scarce resource that determines how fast items reach the shelf or the discovery system. Managing this production well is a distinct skill from doing the cataloging itself. It requires deciding what gets cataloged first, what depth each item receives, what to outsource or batch-process, how to handle the backlog that always threatens to grow, and how to maintain quality when the queue never empties. The central failure mode is not individual poor cataloging but a system that misallocates effort: over-investing in low-use items while high-use materials sit in the backlog, or letting quality slip under throughput pressure until records stop serving users. Good production management matches cataloging effort to value, keeps high-priority materials moving, controls the backlog deliberately, and protects quality without pretending the queue can be ignored.

Use this skill when managing cataloging priorities and throughput, allocating effort across collections, handling backlogs, deciding outsourcing and batch strategies, or balancing quality with production pressure. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating all items as equal, letting the backlog grow unmanaged, sacrificing quality silently under pressure, or outsourcing without oversight.

## Core Rules

### Prioritize By Demand, Value, And Urgency

Not all materials warrant the same speed or depth. A production system must prioritize so the most needed items move first.

Prioritization factors:

- patron requests and holds, the clearest demand signal;
- course reserves and faculty needs;
- high-use collections and new acquisitions in demand;
- special collections, archival, or high-value items needing expert handling;
- format-specific urgency, such as time-sensitive electronic resources;
- deadlines tied to grants, exhibits, or acquisitions.

Establish a priority order and apply it consistently. A first-in-first-out queue serves the oldest item, not the most needed one.

### Match Cataloging Depth To Value And Use

Cataloging depth is a production lever. Full-level cataloging on every item is impossible at scale; minimal-level on high-use items hides them.

Depth allocation:

- full or core level for research, special, or high-use materials;
- core or minimal level for routine general collections;
- minimal or batch level for low-use or backlog-clearance items;
- collection-level for archival aggregations;
- vendor-supplied with review for batch acquisitions.

Have a documented policy that assigns depth by collection, format, or use. The policy makes allocation consistent and defensible under pressure.

### Manage The Backlog Deliberately, Not As A Failure

A backlog is not necessarily a sign of failure, but an unmanaged backlog that grows indefinitely is. Treat the backlog as a managed inventory.

Backlog management:

- measure the backlog by count, age, and collection;
- set a target size or age and track against it;
- allocate dedicated time to backlog reduction, separate from new acquisitions;
- use batch or minimal-level cataloging for backlog clearance where appropriate;
- identify and address root causes, staffing, workflow, or intake surges;
- decide explicitly whether to catalog, batch-process, or weed backlog items.

A backlog that is measured and targeted shrinks. One that is ignored grows until it becomes a crisis.

### Decide Outsourcing And Batch Processing Strategically

Outsourcing and batch processing expand throughput but shift quality control to a different point in the workflow. Use them strategically.

Outsourcing decisions:

- outsource routine, low-complexity cataloging to free expert time for complex items;
- use vendor authority control and processing for batch loads;
- define quality expectations and acceptance criteria in contracts;
- sample-review vendor output rather than accepting blindly;
- retain in-house the work requiring local judgment, special collections, complex items.

Outsourcing is a tool, not a solution. Without quality oversight it shifts problems rather than solving them.

### Protect Quality Under Throughput Pressure

Production pressure is real and constant. The risk is that quality erodes silently as catalogers rush, and the erosion shows up later as broken discovery.

Quality safeguards:

- define minimum quality standards that cannot be bypassed for speed;
- use templates and macros to speed routine work without losing accuracy;
- build validation into the workflow so common errors are caught automatically;
- sample-review records under heavy throughput;
- resist pressure to drop verification steps that prevent discovery failures;
- track quality metrics alongside throughput metrics.

Throughput and quality are not opposites; a well-designed workflow achieves both. But quality must be defined and protected, not assumed.

### Allocate Expertise To The Right Work

Catalogers have different specializations. A production system should route work to match expertise rather than treating all catalogers as interchangeable.

Routing:

- complex, rare, or original cataloging to senior or specialist catalogers;
- routine copy cataloging to paraprofessional or junior staff;
- language or subject specialists to relevant collections;
- authority work to trained authority staff;
- batch and vendor processing to technical staff with oversight.

Misrouting wastes expertise on routine work and overloads generalists with complex items they cannot handle well.

### Track And Report Production Metrics

Production management requires data. Without metrics, priorities and backlog are guesses.

Track:

- items cataloged by staff, collection, and level;
- backlog size and age over time;
- turnaround time from acquisition to availability;
- outsourcing volume and quality;
- error rates from sampling and review.

Report metrics regularly to inform staffing, priorities, and process improvements. Production that is not measured cannot be managed.

### Document The Production System

The priorities, depth policy, backlog strategy, outsourcing arrangements, and routing rules must be documented so the system survives staff turnover.

Document:

- the priority order and depth policy by collection;
- the backlog targets and reduction strategy;
- outsourcing contracts, quality criteria, and review process;
- routing rules by work type and expertise;
- the metrics tracked and their reporting cadence.

A production system that lives only in the manager's head collapses when they leave. Document it as institutional capability.

## Common Traps

### First-In-First-Out Without Prioritization

Serving the oldest item ignores demand. Prioritize by need, value, and urgency.

### Full-Level Cataloging On Everything

Equal depth on all items wastes scarce time. Match depth to value and use.

### Ignoring The Backlog Until It Becomes A Crisis

An unmeasured backlog grows indefinitely. Measure, target, and reduce it deliberately.

### Outsourcing Without Quality Oversight

Vendor cataloging without sampling shifts problems rather than solving them. Define and review quality.

### Letting Quality Erode Under Pressure

Rushed cataloging produces discovery failures that surface later. Define and protect minimum quality standards.

### Treating All Catalogers As Interchangeable

Misrouting wastes expertise and overloads generalists. Route work to match specialization.

### No Production Metrics

Without data, priorities and backlog are guesses. Track and report throughput, backlog, and quality.

### Undocumented Production System

A system that lives in one person's head dies with them. Document priorities, policies, and processes.

## Self-Check

- Is cataloging prioritized by demand, value, and urgency rather than by arrival order?
- Is cataloging depth matched to item value and use through a documented policy?
- Is the backlog measured by count and age, with targets and dedicated reduction effort?
- Are outsourcing and batch processing used strategically with defined quality criteria and sample review?
- Are minimum quality standards defined and protected under throughput pressure?
- Is work routed to match cataloger expertise rather than treating staff as interchangeable?
- Are production metrics, throughput, backlog, turnaround, quality, tracked and reported regularly?
- Is the entire production system, priorities, policies, outsourcing, routing, documented for institutional continuity?
