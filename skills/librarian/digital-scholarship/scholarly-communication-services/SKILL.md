# Scholarly Communication Services

Scholarly communication services sit at the intersection of publishing, copyright, open access policy, research impact, and the economics of scholarship, and they are among the most politically and financially charged services a library offers. The danger is that these services become either purely transactional, processing article processing charge (APC) payments and compliance checks, or purely ideological, advocating open access without engaging the real constraints researchers face. Both fail researchers. Transactional services miss the chance to shape researcher behavior toward more open and rights-retentive practice; ideological services alienate researchers whose promotion, funding, and field norms push them toward prestigious subscription venues. Good scholarly communication support integrates practical compliance help, rights education, publishing infrastructure, and honest advising about the tradeoffs researchers navigate in a system that is mid-transition between subscription and open models. Agents tend to underestimate how much of this work is advising under uncertainty, where the correct answer depends on the researcher's field, career stage, funder, and goals.

Use this skill when designing scholarly communication services, advising on open access and compliance, supporting publishing or journal hosting, or managing open access funds and agreements. The goal is to prevent the agent from giving generic open access advice that ignores field and career realities, mishandling copyright and author rights, or building services that process transactions without shaping practice.

## Core Rules

### Advise On Open Access Within The Researcher's Real Constraints

Open access is not a single decision; it is a landscape of routes, each with tradeoffs that depend on the researcher's field, funder, career stage, and target venue. Generic advice to "publish open access" fails because it ignores that APCs may be unaffordable, that green open access embargoes may violate funder mandates, that some fields lack robust open access venues, and that promotion committees may not yet value open venues. Effective advising situates open access within the researcher's actual situation.

Tailor advising by considering:

- the funder's open access policy and its route preferences, read vs green, and any embargo limits;
- the researcher's field norms, because humanities, STEM, and clinical fields have very different open access maturity;
- the career implications, especially for early-career researchers facing promotion committees with traditional expectations;
- the available routes: gold (open at the publisher, often via APC), green (deposit in a repository, possibly embargoed), and diamond (open with no fees, often community-supported);
- the affordability of APCs and the availability of institutional or transformative agreement coverage;
- the author's rights and whether they can deposit the accepted manuscript in the repository.

The advisor's job is to map the realistic options and their consequences, not to prescribe a single path.

### Make Author Rights And Copyright Education Practical

Researchers routinely sign copyright transfer agreements without understanding what they give up: the right to deposit in a repository, the right to use their own work in teaching, the right to share with collaborators, and the right to comply with funder mandates. Scholarly communication services must make rights practical and actionable, not abstract. This means helping researchers retain rights where possible and understand the consequences of transfer where not.

Practical rights education includes:

- explaining the difference between assigning copyright and granting a license, and the implications of each;
- promoting author addenda or rights retention strategies that preserve deposit and reuse rights;
- using tools like Sherpa Romeo to show what a publisher's policy permits for repository deposit and which version;
- clarifying funder rights retention policies, such as those requiring a CC-BY license on the accepted manuscript;
- helping researchers understand preprint rights and whether their target venue accepts prior preprint posting;
- documenting the rights retained or transferred for each publication so compliance is verifiable.

Rights work is most effective when it happens before submission, not after the agreement is signed.

### Manage Open Access Funds And Agreements Transparently

Open access funds and transformative agreements are powerful levers, but they create administrative complexity and equity concerns that must be managed transparently. An open access fund that runs out mid-year, or that benefits only researchers in APC-funded fields, creates resentment. Transformative agreements that bundle reading and publishing must be communicated clearly so researchers know what they cover.

Manage funds and agreements by:

- publishing clear eligibility criteria for open access fund support, including eligible article types, venues, and routes;
- tracking fund usage against budget and communicating availability proactively;
- recognizing equity issues: APC-based funding advantages researchers in well-funded fields and disadvantages humanities and early-career scholars;
- maintaining an accurate list of transformative agreements and what each covers, including eligible journals and the deposit requirements;
- coordinating with the office of sponsored programs or research administration to align with funder compliance;
- reporting on fund and agreement outcomes to demonstrate value and inform renewal decisions.

Transparency about what is and is not covered prevents researcher frustration and builds trust in the service.

### Support Compliance As A Service, Not A Burden

Funder open access policies, from the US NIH Public Access Policy to the European Plan S and various institutional mandates, create compliance obligations that researchers experience as bureaucratic burden. The library can turn this burden into a service by proactively identifying compliance requirements, tracking deposits, and providing verification. Compliance support is often the most appreciated and most impactful scholarly communication service because it solves a real researcher problem.

Build compliance support by:

- maintaining current knowledge of major funder policies and their specific deposit, embargo, and license requirements;
- integrating compliance checks into the publication workflow, ideally before publication rather than after;
- using identifier and metadata infrastructure, ORCID, Crossref, and the repository, to automate compliance tracking where possible;
- providing researchers with compliance verification they can use for reporting;
- flagging non-compliant publications early and offering remediation paths;
- coordinating with research administration to present a unified compliance picture.

Compliance done well reduces researcher burden and increases repository deposit simultaneously.

### Build Publishing Infrastructure With Sustainability In Mind

Many libraries host or support publishing infrastructure: journal hosting platforms, institutional publishing services, or diamond open access venues. These are valuable but carry sustainability obligations that must be planned from the start. A journal the library hosts becomes the library's ongoing responsibility, and a publishing service that cannot sustain its platform or editorial support becomes a liability.

Approach publishing infrastructure with:

- a clear governance and sustainability model, identifying who owns the platform, who maintains it, and how it is funded;
- realistic assessment of the library's capacity to provide hosting, support, and potentially editorial services;
- selection of sustainable platforms, such as Open Journal Systems, Janeway, or PubPub, with active communities;
- a plan for the journals or publications if library support changes, including migration paths;
- recognition that diamond open access venues depend on sustained institutional or community support, not market revenue;
- integration with preservation and indexing services so hosted publications are preserved and discoverable.

Hosting a journal is a long-term commitment; treat the sustainability question as a precondition.

### Address Research Impact And Metrics Critically

Scholarly communication services often include research impact support, helping researchers demonstrate and understand the reach of their work. This must be done critically, because impact metrics, citation counts, h-index, journal impact factor, and altmetrics, are frequently misused and can incentivize harmful behavior. The library should help researchers use metrics responsibly and understand their limitations.

Approach metrics critically by:

- explaining what each metric does and does not measure, and the biases they carry;
- promoting responsible metrics principles, such as the Leiden Manifesto or DORA, that caution against misuse;
- offering a range of indicators appropriate to the research type, not a single ranking;
- supporting narrative impact descriptions alongside quantitative metrics;
- being transparent about the limitations of proprietary metrics and the value of open infrastructure;
- resisting pressure to use journal-based metrics as proxies for individual researcher quality.

Metrics support is most valuable when it educates researchers and administrators about responsible use, not when it optimizes for a single number.

### Connect Open Scholarship Beyond Articles

Open access to articles is one part of open scholarship, which also includes open data, open methods, open educational resources, preprints, and open peer review. Scholarly communication services should connect these, because a researcher's openness in one dimension often enables openness in others, and because funders increasingly expect openness across outputs. Treating open access as only about articles narrows the service.

Connect the dimensions of open scholarship by:

- linking open access advising with research data management and open data deposit;
- supporting preprint posting and explaining its relationship to formal publication;
- promoting open educational resources as part of the open scholarship ecosystem;
- explaining open peer review and other evolving review models where relevant;
- presenting open scholarship as an integrated set of practices, not isolated services.

## Common Traps

### Generic Open Access Prescriptions

Advising all researchers to publish gold open access ignores field norms, APC affordability, and career realities. This is a trap because it alienates researchers for whom the advice is impractical or harmful.

### Rights Education That Is Too Abstract

Explaining copyright in the abstract without actionable tools leaves researchers signing away rights they needed. This is a trap because the knowledge does not change behavior at the moment of decision.

### Equity-Blind Fund Administration

Open access funds that serve only APC-funded fields disadvantage humanities and underfunded researchers. This is a trap because the fund reinforces existing inequities while appearing to promote openness.

### Compliance As Gatekeeping

Presenting compliance as a hurdle rather than a service burdens researchers and reduces deposit. This is a trap because it positions the library as an obstacle rather than a partner.

### Unsustainable Publishing Commitments

Hosting journals or platforms without a sustainability plan creates future liabilities. This is a trap because the commitment outlives the enthusiasm and the funding that initiated it.

### Metrics Optimization Over Metrics Literacy

Helping researchers game metrics rather than understand them misuses the indicators. This is a trap because it entrenches the harmful use of metrics the library should be challenging.

### Treating Open Access As Only Articles

Focusing services only on article open access misses data, methods, and educational resources. This is a trap because it narrows the open scholarship the library supports.

### Ignoring Transformative Agreement Complexity

Assuming researchers understand what transformative agreements cover leads to missed publishing opportunities and confusion. This is a trap because the benefit is real but unrealized without clear communication.

## Self-Check

- Does open access advising account for the researcher's funder, field, career stage, and the realistic routes available to them?
- Is author rights education practical and actionable, with tools like Sherpa Romeo, addenda, and rights retention, delivered before submission?
- Are open access fund eligibility and transformative agreement coverage published clearly and tracked against budget with equity awareness?
- Is compliance support proactive, integrated into the publication workflow, and providing researchers with usable verification?
- For any publishing infrastructure, is there a governance, funding, and migration sustainability plan before commitment?
- Is research impact support grounded in responsible metrics principles, educating rather than optimizing?
- Does the service connect open access with open data, preprints, OER, and broader open scholarship?
- Are funder policy requirements maintained current and translated into actionable deposit, embargo, and license steps?
- Is there a mechanism to learn which transformative agreements apply and to communicate them to affected researchers?
