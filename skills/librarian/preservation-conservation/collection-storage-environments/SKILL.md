# Collection Storage Environments

The storage environment, temperature, relative humidity, light, air quality, is the single largest determinant of how fast collections deteriorate, and it is the area where libraries and archives most often operate on outdated assumptions. The legacy model prescribed tight, cold, dry conditions for everything, which preserved materials but at enormous energy cost and with risks of mechanical system failure. The contemporary model, grounded in decades of preservation science, recognizes that different materials have different needs, that stability often matters more than absolute setpoints, and that sustainable, lower-energy environments can provide excellent preservation when designed around material requirements and risk. Agents tend to apply one-size-fits-all targets, to over-prioritize absolute setpoints over stability, or to treat sustainability and preservation as opposed when they are often aligned. A storage environment designed without understanding material needs, building capabilities, and energy reality either over-spends on unnecessary control or under-protects vulnerable collections.

Use this skill when designing or assessing storage environments, setting environmental targets, planning HVAC, or balancing sustainability with preservation. The goal is to prevent the agent from applying generic tight specs that waste energy, ignoring material-specific needs, mistaking stability for a fixed setpoint, or designing environments the building and budget cannot sustain.

## Core Rules

### Set Environmental Targets By Material Type, Not Universally

Different materials deteriorate by different mechanisms and at different rates, and a single environmental target cannot serve them all. Paper, photographs, film, magnetic media, textiles, and bound volumes have distinct optimal ranges, and forcing a mixed collection into one environment either over-controls stable materials at high cost or under-protects vulnerable ones. The first step in storage environment design is to know what is being stored and what each material requires.

Differentiate by material:

- paper and bound volumes: moderate temperature and relative humidity, with stability, tolerate a wider range than photographic materials;
- photographs and film: colder and drier conditions, with strict limits for color and acetate materials prone to acetate decay;
- magnetic media and optical discs: moderate, dry conditions, with concern for binder degradation and layer separation;
- textiles and organic materials: stable moderate conditions, sensitive to humidity fluctuations that cause dimensional change;
- electronic and digital storage media: moderate conditions, but the greater risk is format obsolescence, addressed by migration not environment.

Where a mixed collection must share a space, set the environment for the most vulnerable material or segregate the most vulnerable into dedicated spaces. Universal specs waste energy on materials that do not need them.

### Prioritize Stability Over Absolute Setpoint

Preservation science has established that for many materials, stability within a range matters more than holding a single precise setpoint. Short-term fluctuations and seasonal drift within acceptable ranges cause less damage than the legacy assumption suggested, while the energy and equipment cost of holding a tight setpoint is substantial. This insight enables sustainable environments that relax absolute targets while maintaining stability.

Apply stability-first thinking:

- define a target range and an acceptable tolerance, rather than a single setpoint;
- allow managed seasonal drift within the range, which reduces energy cost and mechanical stress;
- minimize rapid short-term fluctuations, which cause more physical stress than gradual change;
- recognize that the rate of change often matters as much as the magnitude;
- use the concept of "proofed fluctuation," the largest historical swing, to understand the risk of relaxing control.

Stability-first design often allows warmer, more energy-efficient environments that still preserve collections well, but it must be grounded in the specific materials' tolerances.

### Design For The Building's Real Capabilities

A storage environment is only as good as the building and mechanical systems that create it, and designs that ignore building reality fail. A historic building with single-pane windows and an aging HVAC system cannot maintain tight cold-dry conditions regardless of the specification, and attempting to do so produces chronic failure, condensation, and energy waste. The environment must be designed around what the building can actually achieve and sustain.

Design for building reality:

- assess the building envelope's insulation, vapor barrier, and leakage, which set the achievable environment;
- evaluate the HVAC system's capacity, reliability, and redundancy, because a system that fails regularly is worse than a modest stable one;
- consider zoning, because different spaces have different loads and capabilities;
- recognize that improving the envelope, sealing and insulating, often yields more stable environments than upgrading HVAC;
- plan for system failure, because mechanical systems fail and the environment must survive temporary outages without catastrophic change.

An environment designed for a building that cannot sustain it provides false assurance and chronic risk.

### Balance Preservation, Energy, And Sustainability Deliberately

Storage environments are among the largest energy consumers in cultural institutions, and the tension between preservation and sustainability is real but not simple. The contemporary approach is not to sacrifice preservation for energy or vice versa, but to find environments that preserve well at lower energy cost, often through material-specific targets, stability ranges, and envelope improvement. This balance must be made deliberately and documented.

Balance preservation and sustainability by:

- using material-specific and stability-based targets to avoid over-controlling stable materials;
- evaluating energy use and carbon impact of proposed environments, not just preservation outcomes;
- considering seasonal shutdowns, setbacks, and free cooling where the climate allows;
- investing in envelope improvement, which reduces the load on mechanical systems permanently;
- using energy monitoring to verify that the environment is achieved efficiently;
- documenting the preservation-energy tradeoffs so decisions are explicit and reviewable.

Sustainable preservation is achievable, but only when the tradeoffs are understood and designed, not assumed.

### Manage Seasonal Drift And Setbacks With Risk Awareness

Allowing the environment to drift seasonally within an acceptable range is a key sustainability strategy, but it must be managed with awareness of the risks. Unmanaged drift that exceeds material tolerances causes damage, while well-managed drift within tolerances preserves well at lower cost. The distinction is in the design and monitoring of the drift.

Manage drift safely:

- define the acceptable seasonal range for each material type, based on preservation science;
- ensure drift is gradual, not abrupt, because rapid change stresses materials;
- monitor that drift stays within range and investigate excursions;
- avoid drift for the most vulnerable materials, segregating them into stable dedicated spaces if needed;
- recognize that some materials, like film and photographs, have narrow tolerances that preclude drift.

Drift is a tool, not a default; it must be designed for the materials actually present.

### Control Light And Air Quality As Part Of The Environment

Temperature and humidity dominate storage environment discussions, but light and air quality are equally part of the environment and often easier to control. Light causes cumulative irreversible damage to organic materials, and pollutants, both outdoor and off-gassed from storage materials, accelerate deterioration. These factors must be part of the environment design, not afterthoughts.

Address light and air quality:

- eliminate light in storage spaces where possible, or limit to safe levels with UV filtering for working light;
- recognize that light damage is cumulative, so even low levels over long exposure harm sensitive materials;
- control particulate and gaseous pollutants through filtration and intake management;
- avoid storage materials that off-gas harmful compounds, such as acidic boxes or certain woods and adhesives;
- monitor air quality in urban or industrial environments where pollutant loads are high.

A storage space with excellent temperature and humidity but uncontrolled light or pollutants is still damaging its collections.

### Verify The Environment Matches The Design

A designed environment is only valuable if the actual environment matches it, and verification is essential. Specifications on paper do not preserve collections; the actual conditions in the stacks do. Verification through monitoring confirms that the design is achieved and maintained, and it catches the gaps between specification and reality.

Verify by:

- monitoring at the collection location, not just at the HVAC sensor, because stack conditions differ from mechanical room readings;
- checking microclimates, because shelves, cases, and enclosures create local conditions that differ from the room;
- verifying after any system change, renovation, or collection move;
- comparing actual performance to design targets and investigating discrepancies;
- maintaining monitoring over time, because performance drifts as systems age.

## Common Traps

### Universal Tight Specs For All Materials

Applying cold-dry tight specifications to all materials wastes energy on stable materials and may even harm some. This is a trap because the over-control feels rigorous while serving materials that do not need it.

### Prioritizing Setpoint Over Stability

Chasing a precise setpoint while allowing fluctuations causes more stress than a stable range. This is a trap because the effort produces less preservation than a stability-focused approach at lower cost.

### Designing Beyond Building Capability

Specifying environments the building envelope and HVAC cannot sustain produces chronic failure and condensation. This is a trap because the specification is unachievable and the attempt creates new risks.

### Treating Sustainability As Opposed To Preservation

Assuming energy efficiency requires sacrificing preservation prevents adoption of aligned solutions. This is a trap because the two are often achievable together through material-specific and stability-based design.

### Unmanaged Seasonal Drift

Allowing drift without defining tolerances or monitoring causes damage to vulnerable materials. This is a trap because the drift exceeds what materials can tolerate.

### Ignoring Light And Air Quality

Focusing only on temperature and humidity while neglecting light and pollutants leaves active damage pathways uncontrolled. This is a trap because these agents cause cumulative, irreversible harm.

### Trusting Specifications Over Verified Conditions

Assuming the designed environment exists because it is specified, without monitoring at the collection, hides gaps. This is a trap because the actual conditions, not the specifications, determine preservation.

### One Environment For A Mixed Collection

Forcing materials with very different needs into one space under-controls the vulnerable and over-controls the stable. This is a trap because neither group is well served.

## Self-Check

- Are environmental targets set by material type, with vulnerable materials segregated or prioritized, rather than universally?
- Does the design prioritize stability within an acceptable range over a single tight setpoint, using preservation science on tolerances?
- Is the environment designed for the building envelope and HVAC system's actual capabilities, with envelope improvement considered?
- Are preservation, energy, and sustainability tradeoffs made deliberately and documented, using material-specific targets to reduce over-control?
- Is seasonal drift managed within defined material tolerances, gradual, monitored, and avoided for the most vulnerable materials?
- Are light and air quality controlled as part of the environment, with UV filtering, pollutant filtration, and safe storage materials?
- Is the actual environment verified at the collection location, including microclimates, and re-verified after system or collection changes?
- Are monitoring data reviewed and acted upon, with excursions investigated and corrected?
