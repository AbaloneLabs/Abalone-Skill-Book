# Preventive Conservation

Preventive conservation is the set of measures that slow deterioration by controlling the agents of damage before they affect individual items, and it is almost always more cost-effective and less risky than treating items after damage occurs. The danger is that preventive conservation is invisible when it works: nothing dramatic happens when temperature is stable, pests are excluded, and handling is careful, so it is easy to under-invest in until a disaster, infestation, or systematic damage reveals the gap. Agents tend to focus on the dramatic, treatment of damaged items, and to under-attend to the unglamorous, monitoring, maintenance, and protocol enforcement that actually prevents damage. Preventive conservation is also a system: each measure interacts with others, and a weakness in one layer, a broken door seal, an uncalibrated datalogger, a lapsed monitoring routine, can undermine the whole program. A preventive conservation program that is a set of disconnected activities rather than an integrated risk-based system provides uneven protection.

Use this skill when planning or assessing preventive conservation, designing handling and housing protocols, controlling exhibition and loan risk, or building disaster preparedness. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating preventive measures as optional, relying on unverified monitoring, ignoring the interactions between measures, or responding to incidents without addressing root causes.

## Core Rules

### Build The Program Around The Agents Of Deterioration

Preventive conservation is most coherent when organized around the ten agents of deterioration: physical forces, thieves and vandals, dissociation, fire, water, pests, pollutants, light and ultraviolet, incorrect temperature, and incorrect relative humidity. Each agent has distinct mechanisms, warning signs, and controls, and a complete program addresses all ten rather than focusing on the few that are most visible. Mapping the program to the agents ensures no major risk is overlooked.

For each agent, define:

- the sources and pathways by which it threatens the collections;
- the materials and collection areas most vulnerable;
- the preventive controls in place and their effectiveness;
- the monitoring that detects incipient damage;
- the response when a threat materializes.

A program organized around the agents is auditable: you can see which risks are controlled and which are not.

### Prioritize By Risk Assessment, Not By Habit

Preventive resources are finite, and they should be allocated by risk, not by tradition or convenience. A risk assessment identifies which agents threaten which collections most severely, considering both the likelihood and the magnitude of potential loss. Without risk assessment, institutions over-invest in familiar measures, often environmental control, while under-investing in less visible but higher-likelihood risks such as water leaks or dissociation. Risk assessment converts preventive conservation from a checklist into a prioritized strategy.

Conduct risk assessment by:

- mapping the collections by material type, value, and vulnerability;
- assessing each agent's likelihood and potential impact for each collection area;
- identifying the highest-priority risks, those with high likelihood and high impact;
- allocating preventive investment to reduce the highest risks first;
- revisiting the assessment as collections, buildings, and climate change.

Risk-based prioritization is especially important for institutions with limited budgets, because it prevents resources being consumed by low-impact measures.

### Monitor Environments With Verified, Actionable Data

Environmental monitoring is the foundation of preventive conservation for temperature, relative humidity, light, and pollutants, but monitoring only helps if the data is accurate, reviewed, and acted upon. A common failure is deploying dataloggers that drift, are never calibrated, or whose data is never analyzed, producing a false record of stable conditions. Monitoring must be a loop: collect, verify, review, act.

Make monitoring operational:

- use calibrated dataloggers placed to represent the collection environment, not the staff environment;
- verify data quality by checking for drift, gaps, and implausible readings;
- review data on a regular schedule, not just when a problem is suspected;
- define the parameters that matter for each material type, because photographs, paper, and film have different needs;
- act on excursions, investigating causes and correcting them, and recording the response;
- recognize that short spikes and long-term drift have different effects and require different responses.

Data that is collected but not verified or acted upon is worse than no data, because it suggests control that does not exist.

### Design Handling And Housing As The First Line Of Defense

Most collection damage occurs during handling and use, and careful handling and housing protocols prevent more damage than any environmental control. Housing, enclosures, supports, and handling procedures are the most cost-effective preventive measures available, and they apply to every item. Agents often under-invest here because housing is less technically glamorous than environmental control.

Build handling and housing protocols:

- provide appropriate housings for vulnerable materials: phase boxes, folders, sleeves, and supports matched to material type;
- establish handling procedures and train all staff and readers, because most damage comes from improper handling;
- use supports and weights during reading room use to protect fragile bindings and documents;
- control photocopying and reproduction, which are high-risk handling events;
- sequence workflows to minimize handling, because each touch is a risk;
- prioritize housing for the most vulnerable and most used materials.

Housing and handling are where preventive conservation meets daily operations, and they are where small investments yield large returns.

### Control Exhibition And Loan Risk Explicitly

Exhibition and loan expose collections to elevated risk: increased light, changed environment, handling, transit, and display methods that may stress materials. These risks are accepted because access is part of the mission, but they must be controlled explicitly, not assumed. Each exhibition and loan should be assessed for the risks it introduces and the measures that mitigate them.

Control exhibition and loan risk by:

- setting light levels and exposure limits by material type, with cumulative light budgets for light-sensitive materials;
- limiting exhibition duration for vulnerable materials and providing rest periods;
- assessing loan requests for the condition of the item, the borrower's environment and security, and the transit risk;
- using appropriate display supports and cases that protect without stressing the item;
- documenting condition before and after exhibition or loan to detect damage;
- refusing or conditioning loans that exceed acceptable risk.

Exhibition and loan are access activities with preservation costs; the costs must be weighed and bounded.

### Build Disaster Preparedness As Prevention, Not Just Response

Disaster preparedness is often framed as response, but its greatest value is preventive: the measures that reduce the likelihood and severity of disasters. A preparedness program that focuses only on response, what to do after the flood, misses the opportunity to prevent the flood or reduce its impact. Effective preparedness integrates prevention, response, and recovery.

Build comprehensive preparedness:

- conduct a vulnerability assessment for fire, water, and other disasters specific to the building and location;
- install and maintain detection and suppression, smoke detectors, sprinklers, water alarms;
- locate collections away from water sources and pipes where feasible, and protect vulnerable areas;
- maintain an updated disaster plan with contacts, priorities, and procedures;
- train staff and conduct drills, because an untested plan fails under stress;
- stage supplies and establish relationships with recovery vendors before disaster strikes;
- prioritize collections for salvage so response focuses on what matters most.

Prevention reduces the disasters that occur; preparedness reduces the damage of those that do.

### Manage Pests Through Integrated Pest Management

Pest infestation can devastate collections, and reactive pesticide use can damage materials and expose people. Integrated Pest Management (IPM) is the preventive approach: exclude, monitor, identify, and respond with the least invasive effective method. IPM treats pests as a symptom of conditions that allow them, not just as a target to eliminate.

Implement IPM:

- exclude pests through building sealing, screening, and intake quarantine of incoming materials;
- monitor with traps inspected on a regular schedule, identifying catches to species;
- maintain environments, especially temperature and humidity, that discourage pests;
- restrict food and plant materials in collection areas;
- respond to infestations by identifying the species, locating the source, and using targeted methods such as freezing or modified atmosphere, avoiding broad pesticide application;
- document monitoring and response so trends are visible.

IPM is preventive because it addresses conditions; reactive spraying treats the symptom while the conditions persist.

### Treat Preventive Conservation As A System With Maintenance

Preventive measures decay if not maintained: door seals fail, filters clog, dataloggers drift, staff forget protocols, and disaster plans go stale. A preventive conservation program is a system that requires ongoing maintenance and reinvestment, not a set of one-time installations. The program must include scheduled maintenance, calibration, retraining, and plan review.

Maintain the system by:

- scheduling calibration and replacement of monitoring equipment;
- maintaining building envelopes, HVAC, and detection systems on preventive maintenance schedules;
- refreshing staff training as staff turn over;
- reviewing and updating the disaster plan and risk assessment regularly;
- auditing the program against the agents of deterioration periodically.

A preventive program that is not maintained provides declining protection and, worse, false assurance that protection exists.

## Common Traps

### Reacting To Damage Instead Of Preventing It

Investing in treatment after damage while under-funding prevention is more expensive and riskier. This is a trap because prevention is almost always cheaper and less risky than intervention.

### Monitoring That Is Never Verified Or Acted Upon

Deploying dataloggers whose data is never checked or used produces false records of control. This is a trap because the institution believes conditions are stable when they may not be.

### Ignoring Handling And Housing

Focusing on environmental control while neglecting the handling that causes most damage misses the highest-impact prevention. This is a trap because the most frequent damage source goes unaddressed.

### Exhibition Without Risk Assessment

Displaying vulnerable materials without light, duration, and support limits causes cumulative damage. This is a trap because the damage is slow, cumulative, and often invisible until advanced.

### Disaster Plans That Are Never Tested

Writing a plan without drills or updates means it fails when needed. This is a trap because the plan's existence suggests readiness that is not real.

### Pesticide-First Pest Response

Reaching for broad pesticide application instead of IPM damages materials and exposes people. This is a trap because it treats symptoms while conditions persist.

### Treating Measures As Independent

Assuming each preventive measure works in isolation ignores that weaknesses in one layer undermine others. This is a trap because a program with a single weak layer provides less protection than it appears.

### Letting The Program Decay

Failing to maintain equipment, retrain staff, and update plans allows protection to decline silently. This is a trap because the decline is invisible until an incident reveals the gap.

## Self-Check

- Is the preventive conservation program organized around all ten agents of deterioration, with controls and monitoring for each?
- Was resource allocation driven by a risk assessment of likelihood and impact, not by habit?
- Is environmental monitoring calibrated, verified, regularly reviewed, and acted upon, with parameters matched to material types?
- Are handling and housing protocols established, with appropriate enclosures and trained staff and readers?
- For exhibitions and loans, are light levels, durations, supports, and condition documentation in place, with risk-based refusal where needed?
- Does disaster preparedness integrate prevention, response, and recovery, with a tested and updated plan?
- Is pest management integrated (IPM), with exclusion, monitoring, and least-invasive response, documented over time?
- Is the program maintained through scheduled calibration, preventive maintenance, retraining, and plan review?
- Has the program been audited against the agents of deterioration to identify gaps?
