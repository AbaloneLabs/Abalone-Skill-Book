# Mission, Vision, And Values Articulation

Mission, vision, and values statements are the foundational documents of a library's identity. They look like simple sentences, but they carry enormous weight: they define why the library exists, what it intends to become, and what principles guide its choices. When done well, these statements align effort, clarify tradeoffs, and give staff and stakeholders a shared reference point. When done poorly, they become generic decoration that no one consults and that guides no decision. The judgment problem is that the work is deceptively hard. It is easy to produce statements that sound good and mean little, that flatter the library rather than direct it, or that list values everyone already assumes. The deeper failure is treating these statements as branding exercises rather than commitments. A values statement that lists "equity" but does not change how the library allocates resources is not a value; it is an aspiration at best and hypocrisy at worst. Good articulation produces statements that are specific enough to guide choices, honest enough to be credible, and operational enough that they actually shape behavior.

Agents helping with mission, vision, and values tend to err by producing generic language, by confusing the three constructs, by writing statements that flatter rather than direct, or by treating values as decoration disconnected from operations. This skill covers the judgment needed to articulate statements that are clear, distinctive, decision-useful, and genuinely operational.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Mission, Vision, And Values Precisely

These three are constantly conflated, and the conflation weakens all of them.

- Mission states why the library exists, whom it serves, and what it does; it is relatively stable and present-tense.
- Vision states what the library intends to become within a horizon; it is aspirational and future-tense.
- Values state the principles that guide how the library operates and decides; they are normative and testable against behavior.

A mission that reads like a vision offers no grounding. A vision that reads like a mission offers no direction. Values that read like mission goals offer no guidance. Keep each distinct in function and tense.

### Make Mission Specific Enough To Bound The Library

A mission so broad that any institution could claim it guides nothing. "We serve our community" describes every library and therefore distinguishes none.

Make mission specific by:

- naming the primary communities served;
- stating the core functions, access, learning, preservation, creation;
- conveying what makes this library distinctive;
- bounding what the library does and, by implication, does not;
- keeping it concise enough to remember and repeat.

A specific mission lets the library decline work outside its purpose. A generic mission cannot.

### Make Vision Aspirational Yet Credible

Vision must stretch the organization without breaking credibility. A vision perceived as fantasy demoralizes; a vision indistinguishable from the status quo directs nothing.

Balance aspiration and credibility by:

- grounding ambition in evidence about community and context;
- setting a horizon clear enough to be time-bound, often three to seven years;
- making the future state concrete enough to imagine;
- ensuring the vision implies real change, not just continuation;
- connecting to a realistic path, even if not fully specified.

The test: does the vision excite stakeholders and also seem achievable with serious effort? Both must hold.

### Make Values Operational, Not Decorative

Values are the most abused of the three. Lists of admirable words, excellence, integrity, diversity, that change no behavior are decoration. Real values guide decisions and can be violated.

Make values operational by:

- defining each value concretely, what it requires and forbids;
- giving examples of decisions the value would shape;
- connecting values to policies, allocation, and evaluation;
- being willing to name tensions between values and how to navigate them;
- including values that might actually be costly to uphold.

A value that never constrains a choice is not a value. If "intellectual freedom" never leads the library to defend a controversial item, it is decoration.

### Ensure Distinctiveness And Honesty

Generic statements signal that the library has not done the hard work of self-examination. Distinctive, honest statements signal genuine engagement.

Pursue distinctiveness by:

- avoiding cliché phrases common to all libraries;
- reflecting the specific community, history, and strengths;
- acknowledging real tensions and tradeoffs honestly;
- resisting the temptation to claim virtues not evidenced in practice;
- testing statements against what an outside observer would recognize as true.

Honesty builds credibility. A mission claiming universal excellence that the community does not experience breeds cynicism.

### Align With The Parent Institution

Most libraries exist within a larger organization whose mission and priorities constrain and enable them. Statements that ignore or contradict the parent create friction.

Align by:

- mapping the library mission explicitly to the parent's mission and priorities;
- using language consistent with the parent where appropriate;
- demonstrating how the library advances the parent's goals;
- differentiating the library's specific role within the larger enterprise;
- checking for contradictions that create political vulnerability.

Alignment is not subservience. The strongest library statements advance the parent's purpose in ways the parent values.

### Develop Statements Through Genuine Engagement

Statements imposed or drafted in isolation lack ownership. Engagement builds understanding and commitment.

Engage by:

- involving staff at multiple levels in discussion and drafting;
- seeking input from stakeholders, users, governance, partners;
- testing drafts with diverse audiences for clarity and resonance;
- iterating based on feedback rather than pronouncing once;
- documenting how input shaped the final statements.

Engagement takes longer but produces statements people understand and use. Imposition produces statements people ignore.

### Test Statements For Decision-Usefulness

The real test of these statements is whether they guide decisions. A statement that cannot resolve or inform a tradeoff is not doing its job.

Test by:

- posing real decisions and asking what the statement implies;
- checking whether the statement would help choose between competing options;
- identifying whether the statement could justify saying no to a tempting opportunity;
- verifying the statement distinguishes this library from peers;
- confirming staff could explain and apply the statement without prompting.

If a statement changes no decision, revise it until it does or acknowledge it is aspirational only.

### Connect Statements To Operations And Accountability

Statements become real when they shape operations. Without connection, they remain documents.

Connect by:

- linking strategic priorities and goals to mission and vision;
- tying budget allocation to values and priorities;
- incorporating values into hiring, evaluation, and recognition;
- using statements in orientation, planning, and communication;
- reviewing periodically whether behavior matches stated values.

The gap between stated values and actual behavior is where credibility lives or dies. Close the gap deliberately.

### Review And Revise Periodically But Not Constantly

Statements need stability to serve as reference points, but contexts change. Balance continuity with renewal.

Manage revision by:

- reviewing mission rarely, only when fundamental purpose shifts;
- reviewing vision at each strategic planning cycle;
- reviewing values when behavior consistently diverges or context changes;
- involving stakeholders in any significant revision;
- preserving continuity in language where it still holds.

Constant revision signals instability and undermines the statements' authority. Stagnation signals irrelevance. Find the rhythm.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Mission, Vision, And Values

Blurring the three weakens all of them. The trap is imprecision. Keep each distinct in function and tense.

### Generic, Any-Library Language

Broad statements guide nothing and distinguish no one. The trap is safety over specificity. Reflect the real, distinctive library.

### Aspiration Without Credibility

A vision perceived as fantasy demoralizes. The trap is overreaching to sound bold. Ground ambition in evidence and a path.

### Values As Decoration

Admirable words that change no behavior are hollow. The trap is virtue signaling. Make values operational and costly.

### Flattery Over Honesty

Claiming virtues not evidenced breeds cynicism. The trap is self-congratulation. Be honest about tensions and gaps.

### Ignoring Parent Alignment

Statements contradicting the parent create political risk. The trap is insularity. Map alignment explicitly.

### Top-Down Drafting

Imposed statements lack ownership and use. The trap is speed over engagement. Develop through genuine input.

### Statements That Guide No Decision

A statement that cannot inform a tradeoff is decoration. The trap is eloquence without function. Test for decision-usefulness.

### Disconnection From Operations

Statements not linked to budget, evaluation, and policy remain documents. The trap is symbolism. Connect to accountability.

### Revision Whiplash Or Stagnation

Constant revision undermines authority; never revising signals irrelevance. The trap is poor rhythm. Review on appropriate cycles.

## Self-Check

- Are mission, vision, and values clearly distinguished in function and tense?
- Is the mission specific enough to bound what the library does and does not do?
- Is the vision aspirational enough to excite yet credible enough to believe?
- Are values defined operationally, with examples of decisions they shape and costs they impose?
- Do the statements reflect what is distinctive and honest about this library, not generic any-library language?
- Are statements explicitly aligned with the parent institution's mission and priorities?
- Were statements developed through genuine engagement with staff and stakeholders?
- Do the statements pass a decision-usefulness test, able to inform real tradeoffs and justify saying no?
- Are statements connected to operations through budget, evaluation, hiring, and planning?
- Is there an appropriate review rhythm, stable enough to anchor, current enough to remain relevant?
- Could a new staff member read these statements and understand what the library stands for and how it decides?
