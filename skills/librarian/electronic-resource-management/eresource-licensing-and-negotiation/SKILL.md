# E-Resource Licensing And Negotiation

Electronic resources are licensed, not owned, and the license, not the purchase, determines what the library and its users may actually do with the content for the duration of the subscription and after. This is the fundamental difference between print and electronic collections, and it is the source of most of the value and most of the risk in e-resource management. The defining risk in e-resource licensing is treating the license as a formality, signing the publisher's standard terms without negotiation, when in fact the standard terms routinely restrict interlibrary loan, course reserves, text and data mining, walk-in user access, and perpetual access in ways that gut the library's ability to serve its mission. A library that accepts restrictive license terms discovers, only when a faculty member needs to mine a corpus or a distance student needs access, that the license forbids it, and the remedy, renegotiation, is far harder after signature than before. A second risk is neglecting perpetual access, the right to retain content after cancellation: a library that cancels a subscription without perpetual access rights loses years of paid-for content entirely. The discipline is to read every license as a contract that constrains the mission, to negotiate the terms that matter before signing, and to secure perpetual access so that the library's investment endures beyond the subscription.

Use this skill when licensing, negotiating, or evaluating electronic resource licenses, or managing the legal and access terms of e-resources. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating licenses as forms, and from neglecting the usage rights, perpetual access, and user protections that determine the e-resource's real value to the library.

## Core Rules

### Read The License As A Contract That Constrains The Mission

An e-resource license is a contract, and its terms determine what users may do:

- Authorized users and sites: who may access the resource (students, faculty, staff, walk-in users, distance learners, alumni) and from where; restrictive definitions exclude legitimate users.
- Permitted uses: the license defines what users may do with the content (download, print, email, use in course reserves, use in coursepacks, interlibrary loan, text and data mining); standard licenses often restrict these.
- Prohibited uses: systematic downloading, redistribution, commercial use, and removal of DRM are typically prohibited, but the line between permitted and systematic use is often unclear.
- Indemnification and liability: the license allocates risk for misuse; understand the library's and institution's exposure.

Read the license to identify where it constrains the library's mission before signing, because renegotiation after signature is far harder.

### Negotiate The Usage Rights That Matter Before Signing

Standard publisher licenses are starting points for negotiation, not take-it-or-leave-it terms:

- Interlibrary loan: negotiate the right to supply articles via ILL, including by secure electronic transmission; some licenses prohibit electronic ILL, forcing print, which is impractical.
- Course reserves and electronic reserves: negotiate the right to use licensed content in course reserves and learning management systems, with clear scope.
- Text and data mining: negotiate explicit TDM rights for researchers, as standard licenses often prohibit or leave TDM ambiguous.
- Walk-in users: ensure walk-in user access is permitted, as many licenses restrict access to authenticated authorized users only.
- Multi-campus and consortium: ensure the license covers all the institution's campuses and, where relevant, consortium sharing.

Negotiate the terms that affect actual use before signing. Publishers expect negotiation on these points, and the library's leverage is greatest before the contract is executed.

### Secure Perpetual Access Rights

Perpetual access, the right to retain content after cancellation, is critical to protecting the library's investment:

- Post-cancellation access: negotiate the right to retain access to content subscribed to during the license term, even after cancellation; without it, cancellation means total loss.
- Access method: clarify how post-cancellation access will be provided (publisher's archive, Portico, LOCKSS, the library's local archive) and at what cost.
- Backfile and archive purchases: where the library purchases a backfile or archive, confirm it is owned or perpetually accessible, not merely leased.
- Trigger events: some perpetual access rights activate only on specific events (publisher cessation, platform withdrawal); understand the conditions.

A library that cancels without perpetual access loses years of content it paid for. Secure perpetual access in every license that covers journal or serial content.

### Evaluate Pricing Models And Total Cost

E-resource pricing is complex and often opaque, and the total cost must be understood:

- Subscription vs. one-time: subscriptions are ongoing costs with annual increases; one-time purchases (backfiles, evidence-based acquisitions) have different budget profiles.
- Bundles and Big Deals: large journal bundles offer breadth but at high cost and with limited flexibility; evaluate whether the bundle's titles justify the cost vs. title-by-title selection.
- Usage-based metrics: cost-per-use analysis helps evaluate value, but low use in essential titles (for research depth) may still justify retention.
- Hidden costs: above-list-price fees, platform fees, access management system costs, and staff time must be included in the total cost.

Do not evaluate an e-resource on the subscription price alone. Analyze the total cost, the usage value, and the flexibility tradeoffs, especially for large bundles.

### Manage License Compliance And Authorized Use

Once licensed, the library must manage compliance with the license terms:

- Access management: configure authentication (IP, proxy, SSO, OpenAthens, Shibboleth) to restrict access to authorized users while enabling legitimate use.
- User education: educate users on permitted and prohibited uses, especially systematic downloading, which can trigger license violations and suspension.
- Monitoring and enforcement: monitor for excessive downloading and respond to vendor complaints; unaddressed violations can suspend access for all users.
- Term tracking: track license terms (permitted uses, ILL rights, TDM rights) so that staff and users know what is allowed.

A license that is not managed for compliance can be violated by well-meaning users, triggering suspension. Configure access, educate users, and track the terms.

### Handle License Disputes And Violations

When violations or disputes arise, handle them through the license framework:

- Alleged violations: respond to vendor allegations promptly, investigate, and address the underlying conduct; document the response.
- Cure periods: most licenses provide cure periods for violations; use them to remedy the issue before escalation.
- Suspension and reinstatement: understand the vendor's suspension rights and the process for reinstatement.
- Escalation: where a vendor's terms or enforcement are unreasonable, escalate through consortia, legal counsel, and professional networks.

License disputes are contractual matters. Handle them through the license's dispute and cure provisions, with documentation and, where needed, counsel.

## Common Traps

### Signing The Standard License Without Negotiation

Accepting restrictive terms on ILL, reserves, TDM, and walk-in users. The trap is that the standard license feels take-it-or-leave-it, but these terms are negotiable.

### Neglecting Perpetual Access

Canceling without post-cancellation access rights. The trap is that the subscription feels like ownership, but without perpetual access, cancellation means total loss.

### Evaluating On Subscription Price Alone

Ignoring total cost, bundle inflexibility, and hidden fees. The trap is that the list price feels comparable, but total cost and flexibility differ sharply.

### Violating License Through User Conduct

Excessive downloading or systematic use triggering suspension. The trap is that users act freely, but violations suspend access for everyone.

### Ambiguous TDM And ILL Terms

Leaving text and data mining and electronic ILL ambiguous. The trap is that ambiguity feels safe, but it blocks researchers when the need arises.

### Losing Track Of License Terms

Staff and users not knowing what the license permits. The trap is that the license exists, but if its terms are not tracked, compliance is accidental.

## Self-Check

- Is the license read as a contract constraining the mission, with authorized users, permitted and prohibited uses, and liability understood before signing?
- Are the usage rights that matter (ILL, course reserves, TDM, walk-in users, multi-campus) negotiated before signing rather than accepted as standard?
- Is perpetual access secured for journal and serial content, with the access method and trigger events clarified?
- Is the total cost evaluated, including subscription vs. one-time, bundle flexibility, usage value, and hidden costs?
- Is license compliance managed through access configuration, user education, monitoring, and term tracking?
- Are violations and disputes handled through the license's cure and dispute provisions, with documentation and escalation where needed?
- Are staff and users aware of what each major license permits, so compliance is intentional rather than accidental?
- Has the license been reviewed (with counsel for significant contracts) for terms that constrain the library's mission, before execution?
