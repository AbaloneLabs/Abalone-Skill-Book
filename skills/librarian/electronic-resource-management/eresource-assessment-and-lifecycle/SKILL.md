# E-Resource Assessment And Lifecycle

Electronic resources represent a large and growing share of library budgets, and unlike print, they are rented annually under licenses that can be renewed, restructured, or cancelled, which makes ongoing assessment not optional but essential to responsible stewardship of the collection budget. The defining risk in e-resource management is treating acquisition as the endpoint, when in fact the subscription model means every resource must justify its renewal annually against alternatives, and a resource that was valuable at acquisition can become poor value as usage shifts, prices escalate, or better alternatives emerge. A library that does not systematically assess its e-resources accumulates a portfolio of inherited commitments, some essential, some marginal, some redundant, each consuming budget that could fund higher-value resources. A second risk is assessment by usage alone: cost-per-use is a powerful metric, but low usage in a resource essential for specific research depth, or in a resource that serves a small but critical community, may still justify retention, while high usage in a redundant resource may not. The discipline is to manage the e-resource lifecycle actively, to assess with multiple metrics aligned to the library's mission, and to make renewal and cancellation decisions on evidence rather than inertia.

Use this skill when evaluating electronic resource collections, conducting usage and cost analysis, or managing the e-resource lifecycle from trial through cancellation. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating e-resources as permanent acquisitions, and from relying on single-metric assessment that misses essential-but-low-use resources or retains redundant ones.

## Core Rules

### Manage The E-Resource Lifecycle Actively

Each e-resource moves through a lifecycle, and each stage requires distinct management:

- Trial: run trials with clear evaluation criteria, gather user feedback, and assess whether the resource meets a demonstrated need before acquisition.
- Acquisition: acquire based on the trial evidence, the collection development policy, and budget availability, with license terms negotiated to protect usage rights.
- Renewal: review each renewal against usage, cost, mission alignment, and alternatives; do not auto-renew without assessment.
- Cancellation: cancel resources that no longer justify their cost, with attention to perpetual access, user communication, and transition to alternatives.

Treat the subscription model as requiring active lifecycle management. A resource that is never assessed is a resource that is never reconsidered, and inertia is not a collection development policy.

### Use Multiple Metrics Aligned To Mission

No single metric captures an e-resource's value; use a portfolio of metrics aligned to the library's mission:

- Usage statistics: COUNTER reports provide standardized usage data (downloads, searches, sessions); analyze trends, not just totals.
- Cost-per-use: total cost divided by usage gives a comparable efficiency metric, but it must be interpreted alongside mission alignment.
- Mission and uniqueness: a resource essential for specific research depth or a small critical community may justify retention despite low use; a redundant resource may not justify retention despite high use.
- User feedback: faculty, student, and researcher input on the resource's importance provides qualitative evidence usage cannot.

Assess with metrics that reflect what the library is trying to achieve. Cost-per-use alone would cancel essential specialized resources; mission alignment prevents that error.

### Analyze Usage Data Critically

Usage statistics are powerful but require critical interpretation:

- COUNTER standards: use COUNTER-compliant reports for comparability across resources; understand which report (journal, database, book) fits the resource type.
- Trends over snapshots: analyze multi-year trends, not single-year snapshots; a declining trend may signal a resource losing relevance.
- Title-level analysis: for big deals and bundles, analyze title-level usage to identify low-use titles and potential for title-by-title reallocation.
- Anomalies and context: spikes or drops may reflect assignments, research projects, or platform changes rather than lasting shifts; investigate anomalies.

Usage data tells a story only when read critically. Look at trends, title-level detail, and context, not just headline totals.

### Evaluate Bundles And Big Deals For Value And Flexibility

Large journal bundles (Big Deals) are major commitments that require particular scrutiny:

- Cost vs. title-by-title: compare the bundle's cost and title breadth against title-by-title subscription to the most-used titles; the bundle premium must be justified by the breadth.
- Usage distribution: bundle usage is typically concentrated in a subset of titles; analyze whether the low-use titles justify their bundled cost.
- Flexibility and exit: assess the bundle's flexibility (can titles be added or dropped?) and the exit terms (cancellation caps, perpetual access) before renewal.
- Alternatives: document delivery, ILL, and open access may substitute for low-use bundle titles, freeing budget for higher-value commitments.

Big Deals are often the largest single e-resource commitments. Scrutinize them for value, flexibility, and exit options, because their size makes inertia costly.

### Apply Evidence-Based And Demand-Driven Models Thoughtfully

EBA and DDA models shift acquisition from pre-selection to usage-triggered purchase, and they require specific management:

- Evidence-based acquisition (EBA): users access a pool of titles, and the library uses the evidence to purchase the most-used titles at the end of the period; manage the pool, the budget, and the selection criteria.
- Demand-driven acquisition (DDA): a usage trigger (view, download) automatically purchases a title; manage the triggers, the budget caps, and the short-term loan costs that precede purchase.
- Pool curation: the available pool determines what users can discover and trigger; curate the pool to match the collection scope, or DDA becomes a random accumulation.
- Budget control: both models can exceed budget if not monitored; set caps and review regularly.

EBA and DDA align spending with use, but they are not self-managing. Curate the pool, control the budget, and apply selection judgment to the evidence.

### Communicate Renewal And Cancellation Decisions

Renewal and cancellation decisions affect users, and communication matters:

- Stakeholder notification: notify affected departments and faculty of significant cancellations, with rationale and alternatives.
- Transition support: where a resource is cancelled, support the transition to alternatives, including ILL, document delivery, and open access.
- Transparent criteria: explain the assessment criteria so that stakeholders understand decisions as evidence-based rather than arbitrary.
- Feedback incorporation: incorporate stakeholder input into the assessment, especially for specialized resources where faculty know the field.

Cancellations done without communication or transition support damage trust and can reverse sound decisions under pressure. Communicate, support transitions, and ground decisions in transparent criteria.

## Common Traps

### Auto-Renal Without Assessment

Treating subscriptions as permanent. The trap is that renewal feels routine, but unaudited renewals accumulate marginal resources.

### Single-Metric Assessment

Cancelling based on cost-per-use alone. The trap is that efficiency feels objective, but it cancels essential specialized resources.

### Snapshot Usage Interpretation

Judging on one year's data. The trap is that a snapshot feels current, but trends and context determine the real picture.

### Inherited Big Deals

Renewing large bundles without scrutiny. The trap is that the bundle feels comprehensive, but low-use titles and inflexibility waste budget.

### Unmanaged EBA/DDA

Letting demand-driven models accumulate without curation or budget control. The trap is that use-alignment feels efficient, but uncured pools accumulate randomly.

### Cancellation Without Communication

Cutting resources without notice or transition. The trap is that the decision feels final, but unsupported cancellations damage trust and get reversed.

## Self-Check

- Is each e-resource managed through its lifecycle (trial, acquisition, renewal, cancellation) with distinct management at each stage?
- Are multiple metrics (COUNTER usage, cost-per-use, mission alignment, uniqueness, user feedback) used rather than a single measure?
- Is usage data analyzed critically for trends, title-level distribution, and context, rather than headline totals?
- Are bundles and Big Deals scrutinized for value, flexibility, and exit terms, given their budget dominance?
- Are EBA and DDA models managed with pool curation, budget caps, and selection judgment applied to the evidence?
- Are renewal and cancellation decisions communicated to stakeholders with rationale, alternatives, and transition support?
- Are assessment criteria transparent and evidence-based, so decisions are understood rather than contested?
- Has the e-resource portfolio been reviewed holistically for balance, redundancy, and mission alignment, not just resource by resource?
