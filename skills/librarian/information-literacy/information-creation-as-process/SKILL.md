# Information Creation As Process

Information does not arrive fully formed. It is created through processes that take time, pass through stages, and appear in different formats, each with characteristic strengths and limitations. A tweet, a news report, a preprint, a peer-reviewed article, a monograph, and a systematic review may all address the same event or question, but each reflects a different stage and process of creation, and each is appropriate for different uses. Treating sources as interchangeable, or judging them by a single standard of "good" versus "bad," misses the point. The skilled information user understands the information life cycle, recognizes how the process of creation shapes what a source can and cannot offer, and matches the format and stage of a source to the specific need. Teaching this well prevents learners from dismissing early-stage sources as worthless or treating late-stage sources as automatically authoritative, and helps them use each source for what it is genuinely good for.

Use this skill when teaching about source formats and the information life cycle, when helping learners choose formats appropriate to their need, when evaluating how creation process affects a source's value, and when designing instruction on the nature of information. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating formats as interchangeable, from applying a single ranking of formats, from ignoring how timeliness and process shape value, or from failing to teach the strategic matching of format to purpose.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Information Life Cycle

Information about an event or question evolves through recognizable stages, each with characteristic formats and qualities.

Recognize the life cycle:

- **Immediate (minutes to hours):** social media posts, alerts, breaking news; fast but unverified and incomplete.
- **Early (days to weeks):** news analysis, blog posts, press releases; more context but still developing.
- **Intermediate (months):** magazine features, preliminary reports, some preprints; more reflection and emerging analysis.
- **Scholarly (months to years):** peer-reviewed articles, conference papers; vetted but slower and narrower in scope.
- **Synthetic (years):** monographs, systematic reviews, textbooks; comprehensive and contextual but least current.

Understanding the life cycle helps match a source's stage to the need.

### Match Format To Purpose

Different information needs call for different formats. The right choice depends on what the user needs the source to do.

Match by asking:

- Is currency or comprehensiveness more important?
- Does the need require peer vetting or immediate reporting?
- Is depth (monograph, review) or breadth (news, web) more useful?
- Does the task require primary, secondary, or tertiary sources?
- Are data, analysis, synthesis, or commentary needed?

A current-events question needs early-stage sources; a synthesis needs later-stage sources. Matching format to purpose is the core skill.

### Recognize How Process Shapes Value

The process by which a source is created determines its strengths and limitations. Evaluate sources through this lens.

Consider how process affects:

- **Verification:** peer review and editorial processes add vetting but take time.
- **Currency:** immediate formats are current but unverified; scholarly formats are verified but dated.
- **Depth and scope:** monographs and reviews offer depth; articles and news offer focus.
- **Perspective:** primary sources offer direct evidence; secondary sources offer interpretation.
- **Audience:** popular sources are accessible but simplified; scholarly sources are rigorous but dense.

No process is universally superior; each is fit for different purposes.

### Evaluate Sources By Their Process, Not Only Their Content

A source's creation process is itself evidence about its reliability and appropriate use.

Evaluate:

- Was it peer-reviewed, editorially vetted, self-published, or unfiltered?
- Is it a primary report, a synthesis, or commentary on other work?
- What review and fact-checking processes, if any, were applied?
- Does the format's typical timeline suit the question's need for currency or verification?
- Are the limitations of this format acknowledged and accounted for?

Process evaluation prevents both over-trusting and under-valuing sources.

### Use Early-Stage Sources Appropriately

Early-stage sources (social media, preprints, news) are not inferior; they serve different purposes. Teach their proper use.

Early-stage sources are valuable for:

- capturing immediate reactions and breaking developments;
- surfacing emerging research before formal publication;
- providing primary accounts of events;
- identifying topics that later sources will address.

But they require:

- awareness that they are unverified or preliminary;
- corroboration against later or independent sources;
- caution in citation for claims requiring vetted evidence.

Teach learners to use early sources for what they offer, not to dismiss or over-rely on them.

### Use Late-Stage Sources Appropriately

Late-stage sources (peer-reviewed articles, monographs, reviews) are not automatically authoritative. They too have limitations.

Late-stage sources are valuable for:

- vetted, methodologically scrutinized findings;
- comprehensive synthesis and contextualization;
- established consensus and foundational knowledge.

But they have limitations:

- lag, sometimes years, behind current developments;
- narrow disciplinary scope;
- potential for publication bias and paradigm entrenchment;
- revision as later evidence emerges.

Teach learners to weigh late-stage sources critically, not deferentially.

### Teach The Strategic Use Of Multiple Formats

Strong information use combines formats strategically, drawing on each for its strengths.

Teach learners to:

- use early sources for currency and primary accounts;
- use scholarly sources for vetted findings and method;
- use synthetic sources for context and overview;
- triangulate across formats to compensate for individual limitations;
- cite each format for what it is genuinely good for.

Strategic combination produces stronger, more balanced work than reliance on a single format.

### Acknowledge Bias Across The Life Cycle

Each stage and format carries characteristic biases. Naming them improves evaluation.

Acknowledge:

- immediate sources may be sensational, unverified, or rumor-prone;
- news analysis may reflect editorial and ownership bias;
- preprints bypass peer review and may not survive scrutiny;
- scholarly publishing carries publication bias, paywalls, and paradigm bias;
- synthetic sources may lag and entrench outdated views.

Bias awareness is part of format literacy, not a reason to discard any format.

## Common Traps

### Treating Formats As Interchangeable

Formats are not equivalent. Each reflects a different process and serves different purposes.

### Applying A Single Ranking Of Formats

No format is universally best. Match format to purpose rather than ranking abstractly.

### Dismissing Early-Stage Sources As Worthless

Social media, preprints, and news have real value for currency and primary accounts when used critically.

### Treating Peer-Reviewed Sources As Automatically Authoritative

Peer review adds vetting but does not guarantee correctness, currency, or freedom from bias.

### Ignoring The Information Life Cycle

The same event yields very different sources over time. Match the stage to the need.

### Using A Source For A Purpose It Cannot Serve

Do not cite a tweet for vetted evidence or an outdated monograph for current data.

### Forgetting Bias Within Each Format

Every format carries characteristic biases. Name and compensate for them.

### Teaching Formats As A Fixed Hierarchy

A hierarchy of formats misleads learners. Teach strategic matching of format to purpose instead.

## Self-Check

- Did you explain the information life cycle and how formats evolve over time?
- Did you help learners match format to purpose rather than treat formats as interchangeable?
- Did you show how the creation process shapes a source's strengths and limitations?
- Did you evaluate sources by their process (peer review, editorial vetting, primary vs. secondary), not only content?
- Did you teach appropriate use of early-stage sources (currency, primary accounts) with their caveats?
- Did you teach critical, not deferential, use of late-stage sources?
- Did you model strategic combination of multiple formats to compensate for individual limitations?
- Did you acknowledge the characteristic biases of each format and stage?
- Did you avoid presenting formats as a fixed hierarchy?
- Could a learner justify why a chosen format is appropriate for their specific need?
