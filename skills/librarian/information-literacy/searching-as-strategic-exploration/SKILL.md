# Searching As Strategic Exploration

Searching for information is rarely a single lookup. It is an iterative, strategic exploration in which each result reshapes the question, the terms, and the sources. A novice types a phrase into one search box, glances at the first page, and either declares success or gives up. A skilled searcher moves differently: they map the information landscape, choose sources deliberately, refine terms based on what they find, follow citations and subject headings, and recognize that the first search is a probe, not an answer. Treating search as lookup produces shallow, biased results; treating it as strategic exploration produces deeper, more complete, and more defensible findings. The challenge for instruction and for practice is that strategic exploration cannot be reduced to a fixed sequence, because the right next move depends on what the last search revealed. The skill is in learning to read results as feedback and to adjust the strategy in response.

Use this skill when teaching learners to search strategically, when designing search instruction, when modeling iterative search refinement, and when applying strategic exploration in reference work. The goal is to prevent the agent from teaching search as a single query, from encouraging premature stopping, from ignoring the feedback loop between results and strategy, or from presenting searching as mechanical rather than strategic.

## Core Rules

### Frame Searching As Iterative, Not Linear

The foundational shift is to treat searching as a cycle of probe, learn, and refine, not a one-shot query.

Teach and model that:

- the first search is a probe to learn the landscape, not a final answer;
- results reveal which terms, sources, and frameworks are productive;
- each iteration refines the question, the terms, or the sources;
- stopping at the first acceptable result sacrifices depth and completeness;
- strategic searchers expect to revise their approach several times.

Framing search as iterative counteracts the lookup reflex.

### Map The Information Landscape Before Searching

Strategic search begins with understanding where relevant information is likely to live, not with typing keywords.

Map:

- which disciplines and communities address this question;
- which source types are relevant (scholarly, professional, governmental, community);
- which databases, catalogs, and platforms cover those sources;
- which controlled vocabularies and terminology the field uses;
- where gray literature and non-indexed material might be found.

Landscape mapping prevents over-reliance on a single source and surfaces gaps before searching.

### Match Sources To The Question

Different questions live in different places. Strategic searchers choose sources to match the question.

Match by asking:

- Is this a scholarly, professional, policy, or community question?
- Which databases index the relevant disciplines?
- Are books, journals, reports, data, or primary sources most relevant?
- Does the question need specialized tools (standards, patents, legal, statistical)?
- Would gray literature or local sources add essential material?

(See related skills on database and catalog selection and on gray literature.)

### Use Results As Feedback To Refine

The core strategic move is to read results as feedback and adjust. Each result set tells you how to improve the search.

Read results for:

- better terminology and subject headings used in relevant records;
- authors and institutions that recur, indicating key voices;
- broader, narrower, or related terms to add or substitute;
- source types and journals that appear frequently;
- gaps: what is missing that should be there?

Refining based on results is what separates strategic from mechanical searching.

### Expand And Narrow Deliberately

Strategic search alternates between broadening and narrowing. Each move serves a purpose.

Use expansion to:

- increase recall when too few relevant results appear;
- add synonyms, broader terms, and related concepts;
- search additional databases and source types;
- remove overly restrictive limits.

Use narrowing to:

- increase precision when results are flooded with irrelevant material;
- apply subject headings, date limits, or methodology filters;
- target specific subtopics or populations.

Deliberate expansion and narrowing prevents both flooding and starvation of results.

### Follow Lateral Pathways

Strategic search is not only keyword-based. Lateral pathways often find the best material.

Use lateral moves:

- follow subject headings and controlled vocabulary from a relevant record;
- trace citations forward and backward from key works;
- use author searches to find more by key voices;
- browse serial titles and shelves (physical or virtual) near relevant works;
- check the references and methods of strong sources.

Lateral pathways exploit the structure of the literature, not just keyword matching.

### Track And Document The Strategy

Strategic exploration can become chaotic without tracking. Document the strategy so it is reproducible and refinable.

Track:

- which sources were searched and when;
- the queries and terms used, including refinements;
- useful subject headings and key authors discovered;
- sources flagged for follow-up;
- dead ends and approaches to avoid repeating.

Documentation turns exploration into a reproducible, defensible process.

### Recognize When To Stop

Strategic exploration must converge. Knowing when to stop is part of the strategy.

Stop signals:

- saturation: new searches return mostly already-found material;
- the question is answered at the depth the context requires;
- the key voices and sources are represented;
- additional searches show diminishing returns.

Balance thoroughness against the purpose; not every search warrants exhaustive exploration.

### Teach Strategy, Not Just Mechanics

Instruction should build transferable strategic reasoning, not memorization of database features.

Teach learners to:

- plan before searching;
- read results as feedback;
- refine deliberately;
- use lateral pathways;
- document and reflect on their strategy.

Transferable strategy outlasts any specific database interface.

## Common Traps

### Teaching Search As A Single Query

A one-shot query produces shallow results. Frame searching as iterative exploration.

### Stopping At The First Acceptable Result

Premature stopping sacrifices depth, completeness, and the discovery of key sources.

### Ignoring The Feedback Loop

Results are feedback. Failing to refine terms, sources, and strategy based on results is mechanical searching.

### Over-Relying On One Source Or Database

No single source covers a question fully. Map the landscape and match sources to the question.

### Skipping Landscape Mapping

Searching without understanding where information lives leads to gaps and weak results.

### Expanding Or Narrowing Without Purpose

Random adjustments waste effort. Expand or narrow deliberately to serve recall or precision.

### Forgetting Lateral Pathways

Keyword search alone misses material found through subject headings, citations, and author searches.

### Not Documenting The Strategy

Undocumented exploration cannot be reproduced, refined, or defended. Track sources, terms, and refinements.

## Self-Check

- Did you frame searching as iterative exploration rather than a single lookup?
- Did you map the information landscape (disciplines, source types, databases, terminology) before searching?
- Did you match sources to the question rather than defaulting to one database?
- Did you read results as feedback and refine terms, subject headings, and sources accordingly?
- Did you expand and narrow deliberately to balance recall and precision?
- Did you use lateral pathways (subject headings, citations, authors, browsing)?
- Did you track and document the strategy so it is reproducible?
- Did you recognize and act on stop signals (saturation, answered question, diminishing returns)?
- Did you teach or model transferable strategy, not just database mechanics?
- Could a learner reproduce and justify their search strategy from your guidance?
