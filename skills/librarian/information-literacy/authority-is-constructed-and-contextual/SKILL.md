# Authority Is Constructed And Contextual

Authority is not a fixed property of a source or an author. It is a judgment that communities construct, and it shifts with context. A peer-reviewed neuroscientist is authoritative about brain function but not about local oral history. A community elder may be the most authoritative voice on traditional knowledge that no journal has ever indexed. A practicing engineer may be the best source on current industry practice that lags in the published literature. Treating authority as a simple badge, the presence of a PhD, a university affiliation, or a peer-reviewed imprint, leads to two opposite errors: over-trusting sources that carry the badges of authority but are wrong or irrelevant, and dismissing sources that lack those badges but are in fact the most credible voice for the question. Teaching and applying authority well means recognizing that different questions admit different kinds of authority, that authority is granted by communities and can be contested, and that the learner's job is to match the type of authority to the question rather than apply a single universal ranking.

Use this skill when teaching source evaluation, when helping learners judge credibility, when designing assignments that require authority analysis, and when applying authority judgment in reference and instruction. The goal is to prevent the agent from reducing authority to credentials, from treating peer review as the only valid marker, from ignoring community and experiential authority, or from failing to surface how privilege and bias shape whose authority gets recognized.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Types Of Authority

Authority comes in more than one form, and matching the type to the question is the core skill.

Recognize at least three types:

- **Subject or disciplinary authority:** expertise in a field, often evidenced by training, publication, and peer recognition.
- **Professional or experiential authority:** authority from sustained practice, such as a clinician, engineer, journalist, or practitioner with deep field experience.
- **Lived and community authority:** authority from direct experience, cultural position, or community recognition, including traditional knowledge and local expertise.

A strong evaluation names which type of authority a question needs, rather than assuming one type fits all.

### Match Authority To The Question And Context

Different questions require different authorities. The first step is to ask what kind of authority this question demands.

Ask:

- Does this question need disciplinary expertise, professional practice, or lived experience?
- Whose perspective is necessary to answer this fully, and whose might be missing?
- Is the context academic, professional, cultural, or personal, and how does that shift authority?
- Would a credentialed expert, a practitioner, or a community member be most credible here?

A question about clinical efficacy, local history, and traditional ecological knowledge each calls for a different authoritative voice.

### Treat Authority As Granted By Communities

Authority is not intrinsic; it is recognized and granted by communities of practice, discourse communities, and cultural groups. Recognizing this prevents over-reliance on external badges.

Consider:

- Which community recognizes this source as authoritative, and is that the relevant community?
- Is the authority contested within or across communities?
- Does the source hold authority in one context but not another?
- How was the authority established: through peer recognition, community standing, or institutional position?

A source authoritative in one community may be marginal or contested in another.

### Recognize How Privilege And Bias Shape Authority

Who gets recognized as authoritative is shaped by structural privilege. Naming this is part of rigorous authority evaluation.

Acknowledge:

- systemic bias in publication, citation, and credentialing that privileges some voices;
- the historical exclusion of marginalized, Global South, and non-English voices from "authoritative" channels;
- that community and experiential authority is often undervalued relative to credentialed authority;
- that dominant frameworks may frame marginalized knowledge as less rigorous.

Rigorous evaluation compensates for these biases rather than reproducing them.

### Use Credentials As Evidence, Not Proof

Credentials and markers of peer review are evidence of authority, not proof of it. Use them critically.

Use credentials to:

- establish baseline competence and relevant training;
- signal peer recognition within a field;
- indicate editorial and review processes;

But do not treat credentials as:

- proof that the source is correct;
- the only valid form of authority;
- a substitute for evaluating the actual argument and evidence.

Even authoritative sources make errors, overstate, or speak outside their expertise.

### Evaluate The Argument And Evidence, Not Only The Author

Authority is a starting point for credibility, not a substitute for engaging the content. Evaluate the substance.

Evaluate:

- the quality of the evidence and its sourcing;
- the transparency of the method or reasoning;
- whether claims are proportionate to the evidence;
- whether the source acknowledges limitations and counter-evidence;
- corroboration across independent authoritative sources.

A credible author making an unsupported claim is not automatically credible on that point.

### Surface Contested And Emerging Authority

Authority is dynamic. In emerging fields, during crises, or on contested questions, authority may be uncertain or shifting.

Surface:

- where expert consensus exists and where it is contested;
- emerging voices and preprints that may become authoritative;
- instances where yesterday's authoritative view is being revised;
- the difference between settled consensus and active debate.

Teaching learners to recognize the state of consensus is as important as evaluating individual sources.

### Teach Authority As A Practice, Not A Checklist

Authority evaluation is a judgment practice, not a fixed rubric. Teach it as transferable reasoning.

Teach learners to:

- ask what kind of authority the question needs;
- investigate who is recognized as authoritative and by whom;
- weigh credentials, community recognition, and evidence together;
- reflect on their own biases about whose authority they trust;
- revise their judgment as context and evidence change.

A checklist approach produces shallow evaluation; a practice approach builds durable skill.

## Common Traps

### Reducing Authority To Credentials

A PhD or peer-reviewed imprint is evidence, not proof. Evaluate the argument and the type of authority the question needs.

### Treating Peer Review As The Only Valid Marker

Peer review matters but is not the only form of authority. Professional, experiential, and community authority are valid for many questions.

### Ignoring Community And Experiential Authority

Lived experience and community recognition are authoritative for many questions, especially cultural and local ones. Do not dismiss them.

### Applying One Universal Ranking Of Authority

Authority is contextual. A ranking valid in one discipline or community may not apply in another.

### Failing To Surface How Bias Shapes Recognition

Systemic privilege shapes who is recognized as authoritative. Reproducing it silently weakens evaluation.

### Trusting An Authoritative Source Outside Its Expertise

Authority in one area does not transfer. Evaluate whether the source is authoritative for this specific question.

### Treating Authority As Fixed

Authority is constructed and can be contested or revised. Surface emerging and contested authority where relevant.

### Presenting Authority Evaluation As A Checklist

A fixed rubric produces shallow evaluation. Teach and apply it as contextual judgment.

## Self-Check

- Did you identify which type of authority the question requires (subject, professional, lived/community)?
- Did you match the authority to the question and context rather than applying a universal ranking?
- Did you investigate which community recognizes the source as authoritative and whether that is the relevant community?
- Did you acknowledge how privilege and bias shape whose authority gets recognized?
- Did you use credentials as evidence, not proof, and evaluate the actual argument and evidence?
- Did you evaluate the substance (evidence, method, limitations, corroboration), not only the author?
- Did you surface where authority is contested or emerging, rather than treating it as fixed?
- Did you avoid reducing evaluation to a checklist and instead reason about context?
- Did you compensate for structural bias rather than reproduce it?
- Could a learner explain why this source is authoritative for this question in this context?
