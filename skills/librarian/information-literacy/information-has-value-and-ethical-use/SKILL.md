# Information Has Value And Ethical Use

Information is not free in every sense that matters. It is created by labor, owned or licensed under terms, embedded in power relationships, and capable of causing harm when misused. The ethical use of information goes well beyond "don't plagiarize." It includes respecting copyright and licensing, attributing ideas to their originators, protecting the privacy of people described in data, honoring indigenous and community data sovereignty, recognizing the economic barriers that make information inaccessible, and understanding that information itself has social and political value. Librarians who teach only the mechanics of citation miss the larger ethical framework that makes citation, licensing, and responsible use meaningful. Patrons who learn only to avoid plagiarism learn to game a rule rather than to act with integrity.

Use this skill whenever you are teaching citation, copyright, research ethics, data handling, or open access, and whenever a patron's project raises questions about whose information is being used and on what terms. The goal is to prevent the agent from reducing ethical use to a plagiarism warning, from giving careless copyright guidance, and from ignoring the power and privacy dimensions of information.

## Core Rules

### Treat Information As Created Labor With Value

Information does not appear from nowhere. Research, writing, data collection, journalism, and creative work all represent someone's labor. Acknowledging that labor through attribution is an ethical act, not just a formatting requirement. When patrons understand that citation honors the work behind a source, they cite more reliably than when they cite only to avoid punishment.

Teach that ethical use recognizes:

- the author's intellectual labor and originality;
- the publisher's and platform's role in disseminating it;
- the funders and institutions that supported it;
- the communities or subjects who contributed data or stories.

### Distinguish Plagiarism, Copyright Infringement, And Poor Attribution

These three are related but distinct, and patrons conflate them.

- plagiarism: presenting someone else's work, words, or ideas as your own, an academic integrity issue;
- copyright infringement: using a work in ways the rights holder has not authorized, a legal issue;
- poor attribution: citing incorrectly or incompletely, a quality issue that may or may not rise to plagiarism.

A patron can plagiarize without infringing copyright (using an unattributed idea from a public-domain source) and can infringe copyright without plagiarizing (copying text with attribution but without permission). Clarify which problem is in play before advising.

### Give Copyright Guidance Conservatively

Copyright is complex and jurisdiction-dependent. Librarians can explain general principles and educational exceptions but should not give legal advice or definitive rulings on fair use for a specific case.

Cover responsibly:

- the difference between ideas and their expression;
- the basics of fair use or fair dealing and its four-factor analysis;
- educational exceptions, classroom and course-reserve limits;
- the role of licensing, which often overrides default copyright;
- Creative Commons and open licenses as pre-authorized permissions;
- public domain and how works enter it;
- the limits of your role: provide information, refer complex questions to specialists or counsel.

Encourage patrons to lean on licensed library resources, open-access sources, and their own original work to reduce risk.

### Teach Attribution As A Practice, Not A Format

Citation styles (APA, MLA, Chicago) are formatting conventions. The underlying practice is attribution: making clear which ideas, words, data, and media came from where, so readers can verify, follow, and credit. Teach the practice first and the style second.

A strong attribution practice:

- marks direct quotation, paraphrase, and summary distinctly;
- cites the origin of data, images, and media, not just text;
- distinguishes the patron's analysis from the source's claims;
- provides enough information for a reader to find the source;
- follows the required style consistently.

Patrons who understand the practice adapt to any style; those who memorize one style's rules struggle when the style changes.

### Respect Privacy And Data Ethics

Information about people has ethical weight beyond copyright. Research data, interviews, photographs, and datasets can expose individuals or communities to harm. Ethical use includes protecting the people behind the information.

Address:

- de-identifying data before sharing or publishing;
- informed consent for interviews and human-subjects research;
- institutional review board requirements where applicable;
- avoiding the extraction of community data without benefit to the community;
- respecting the privacy of patrons and subjects described in archives and records.

### Honor Indigenous And Community Data Sovereignty

Indigenous and local communities have distinct rights over knowledge, stories, images, and data originating from them. Western copyright and citation norms often fail to protect indigenous knowledge, which may be sacred, collectively held, or restricted. Ethical use requires recognizing these rights even when the law does not.

Consider:

- indigenous data sovereignty principles such as CARE (Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics);
- community protocols for using traditional knowledge;
- whether a source was shared with permission for the use intended;
- attribution that names communities and knowledge holders appropriately;
- restrictions on sacred or sensitive materials.

### Recognize The Economic Value And Access Barriers Of Information

Information has economic value, and access to it is unequal. Paywalls, licensing, and geography determine who can read what. This shapes whose knowledge is cited and whose is invisible. Teaching ethical use includes teaching why open access, library licensing, and equitable access matter.

Discuss:

- why paywalled sources dominate citations and what that excludes;
- how open access shifts who can participate in scholarship;
- the role of interlibrary loan in reducing access barriers;
- the value of the library's licensed resources as an equity tool;
- the ethics of using pirate or shadow libraries, recognizing both the access argument and the legal risk.

### Model Ethical Use In Your Own Work

Librarians teach by example. Cite your sources in guides and handouts, respect licensing in course materials, attribute images and media, and handle patron data carefully. Inconsistent modeling undermines explicit teaching.

## Common Traps

### Reducing Ethics To "Don't Plagiarize"

Plagiarism avoidance is the floor, not the ceiling. Ethical use includes copyright, privacy, attribution quality, and power.

### Conflating Plagiarism And Copyright

They are different problems with different consequences. Clarify which is in play.

### Giving Definitive Legal Advice On Fair Use

Fair use is fact-specific and jurisdiction-dependent. Provide frameworks and refer complex cases.

### Teaching Citation Style Before Attribution Practice

Memorized styles break when they change. Teach the practice of attribution first.

### Ignoring The People Behind The Data

Research data, interviews, and archives involve human subjects whose privacy and consent matter.

### Treating Indigenous Knowledge As Freely Available

Western copyright does not protect much indigenous knowledge. Ethical use requires going beyond the law.

### Assuming Equal Access To Information

Paywalls and licensing exclude voices. Acknowledge the access dimension of information's value.

### Inconsistent Modeling

Teaching ethics while mishandling attribution, licensing, or patron data in your own work undercuts the lesson.

## Self-Check

- Did you frame ethical use as recognizing labor, value, and power, not just avoiding plagiarism?
- Did you distinguish plagiarism, copyright infringement, and poor attribution before advising?
- Did you give copyright guidance conservatively, explaining principles and referring specific legal questions?
- Did you teach attribution as a practice (quotation, paraphrase, source type, verifiability) before style mechanics?
- Did you address privacy, consent, and de-identification for data involving people?
- Did you recognize indigenous and community data sovereignty beyond what copyright requires?
- Did you discuss the economic value of information and the access barriers that shape whose knowledge is cited?
- Did you model ethical use in your own guides, handouts, and handling of patron data?
