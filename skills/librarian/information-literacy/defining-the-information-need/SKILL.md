# Defining The Information Need

Research fails most often at the very beginning, not at the search. A student who has not defined a question cannot recognize a relevant source. A topic that is too broad drowns in results; one that is too narrow returns nothing. An assignment misread as a general-interest browse produces the wrong source type entirely. The first and most consequential information-literacy skill is defining the information need: turning a vague interest or an assignment prompt into a clear, scoped, searchable question with a known audience, depth, and source requirement. Librarians who skip this step teach searching techniques to patrons who do not yet know what they are searching for.

Use this skill before any search instruction, before helping a patron pick a database, and whenever a research project is stalling. The goal is to prevent the agent from jumping to search mechanics before the need is defined, and from letting patrons start searching a topic that cannot succeed as scoped.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Task, Not The Topic

Information needs exist to serve a task: an assignment, a decision, a project, a curiosity, a professional question. The task determines what kind of information, how much, at what depth, and in what form. Two patrons with the same topic can have completely different needs.

Establish the task first:

- what will the patron do with the information (write a paper, make a decision, give a presentation, satisfy curiosity)?;
- what is the assignment or real-world requirement, if any?;
- what length, format, and source-type constraints apply?;
- who is the audience, and what do they expect?;
- what is the deadline, and how much time is available for searching?.

A ten-page argumentative essay, a five-minute presentation, and a personal health decision all need different information even on the same topic.

### Turn A Topic Into A Question

A topic is a noun phrase ("climate change and agriculture"). A research question is an interrogative that the research will answer ("How are smallholder farmers in sub-Saharan Africa adapting staple crop choices to shifting rainy seasons?"). The question makes relevance decidable: a source either helps answer it or it does not.

Help the patron convert topic to question by:

- identifying the key concepts and variables;
- adding specificity: population, place, time, aspect, comparison;
- phrasing as a question that has an answer;
- checking that the question is neither obvious nor unanswerable.

A good research question is specific enough to be searchable and broad enough to have a literature behind it.

### Diagnose And Fix Scope Problems

The most common scoping failure is a topic that is too broad. The second most common is one that is too narrow. Both produce frustrating searches, but for opposite reasons.

Too broad signals:

- overwhelming result counts;
- results covering the topic at a surface level only;
- inability to identify a focused argument or contribution;
- a question that could fill a book rather than a paper.

Too narrow signals:

- almost no results;
- results that address only one tiny facet;
- a question so specific that little has been written on it;
- a population, place, or time so constrained that the literature is thin.

Broaden by removing a constraint (drop the specific place or population; widen the time range; move to the parent concept). Narrow by adding a constraint (add a population, place, time, aspect, or comparison). Iterate until the result set is workable.

### Identify The Type And Level Of Information Needed

Different tasks need different source types and depths. Defining the need includes specifying what kind of information will satisfy it.

Consider:

- overview or background (encyclopedia, handbook) versus current research (articles) versus primary evidence (data, archives);
- scholarly peer-reviewed sources versus trade or popular sources;
- quantitative data versus qualitative analysis;
- historical versus current;
- discipline-specific depth (a public health question needs different sources than a literary one);
- reading level and language.

A mismatch between need and source type wastes the whole search. A fifth-grader's report does not need peer-reviewed clinical trials; a graduate lit review cannot rest on popular articles.

### Surface And Test Assumptions

Patrons arrive with assumptions that shape, and sometimes distort, their need. They may assume a topic has been studied when it has not, or that it has not when it has. They may assume only certain sources count. They may assume a controversial topic has a settled answer.

Test assumptions by:

- doing a quick exploratory search to see what the literature looks like;
- checking whether the assumed source type exists for the topic;
- asking what the patron expects to find, and whether finding the opposite would change the approach;
- flagging when a question assumes a fact that may not hold.

### Recognize When The Need Is Misaligned With The Task

Sometimes the stated need does not actually serve the task. A student may want to research a favorite topic that does not fit the assignment. A decision-maker may ask for data that will not actually decide the question. Gently surface the mismatch and realign the need to the task.

### Make The Need Searchable And Measurable

A well-defined need can be expressed as a search: the concepts, the source types, the scope, and a criterion for "done." Help the patron state, in advance, what a successful result would look like. "I will have what I need when I find three peer-reviewed sources from the last ten years addressing X in population Y" is a need that can be searched and that tells the patron when to stop.

### Teach Need-Definition As A Repeatable Step

Need-definition is not a one-time intervention; it recurs as the research develops. The initial question often shifts once the patron sees what the literature contains. Teach patrons to revisit and revise the need as they learn, rather than locking in a question at the start and forcing the research to fit it.

## Common Traps

### Jumping To Search Before Defining The Need

Teaching database mechanics to a patron with an undefined question produces searches that cannot succeed. Define first.

### Leaving The Topic As A Noun Phrase

"Gun control" is not a research question. Without conversion to a specific question, relevance is undecidable.

### Accepting A Topic That Is Too Broad

"Social media and mental health" drowns in results. Add population, platform, outcome, and time constraints.

### Over-Narrowing Until Nothing Exists

A question so specific that no literature addresses it is as useless as one too broad to manage. Widen strategically.

### Mismatching Source Type To Task

Sending a school-age patron to peer-reviewed clinical literature, or a graduate student to popular magazines, fails the task.

### Locking The Question Too Early

Research is iterative. The first question often changes once the literature is visible. Build in revision.

### Ignoring The Assignment Or Real-World Constraint

A beautiful research question that violates the assignment or the decision timeline is not a success.

### Assuming The Literature Matches The Patron's Expectations

Quick exploratory searches reveal whether the assumed body of work exists. Do not let patrons search for weeks on a false assumption.

## Self-Check

- Did you start from the task, audience, format, depth, and deadline rather than from the topic alone?
- Did you help convert the topic into a specific, answerable research question?
- Did you diagnose whether the scope is too broad or too narrow and adjust constraints accordingly?
- Did you identify the source type, level, currency, and discipline appropriate to the need?
- Did you surface and test assumptions about what the literature contains?
- Did you realign the need when it did not actually serve the task?
- Did you express the need as a searchable, measurable goal with a stopping criterion?
- Did you teach need-definition as a recurring step rather than a one-time event?
