# Acquisition And Appraisal

Special collections acquisition is irreversible in a way that ordinary collection development is not. Once an institution accepts a collection, it takes on permanent obligations of storage, processing, description, preservation, and access that can span generations. Accepting the wrong materials consumes scarce resources, crowds storage, and creates backlogs that may never clear. Declining the right materials loses unique evidence. Appraisal, the judgment of whether and what to acquire, is therefore the most consequential special collections decision. Good appraisal evaluates research value, uniqueness, condition, and fit against the institution's mission and capacity, negotiates clear terms through deeds of gift, and is willing to decline or redirect materials that do not belong.

Use this skill when appraising a potential acquisition, evaluating monetary or research value, negotiating a deed of gift, or deciding whether to accept or decline materials. The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting everything offered, ignoring processing and preservation costs, accepting materials without clear title or terms, or acquiring outside the collecting scope.

## Core Rules

### Appraise Against Mission And Collecting Scope First

The first appraisal question is whether the materials fit the institution's mission and collecting scope. Materials outside scope, however valuable, become a burden and a distraction.

Assess fit:

- does the collection align with the institution's documented collecting areas;
- does it strengthen existing holdings or open an unjustified new area;
- does it serve the institution's primary user communities;
- would it be better placed at another institution with stronger related holdings;
- does accepting it commit the institution to ongoing collecting in the area.

Scope exists to focus resources. Accepting out-of-scope materials, however attractive, dilutes the collection and burdens the institution. Redirect to a better home when appropriate.

### Evaluate Research And Cultural Value, Not Just Monetary Worth

Special collections exist to support research and cultural memory, so appraisal must weigh intellectual and cultural value heavily, often above market price.

Value dimensions:

- uniqueness, is this the only or best copy or collection on the subject;
- research demand, do scholars and communities need these materials;
- evidentiary strength, do the materials document significant people, events, or movements;
- representational value, do they fill gaps or represent underrepresented voices;
- contextual richness, do associated materials, correspondence, drafts add depth;
- provenance and association value, the history of ownership and notable connections.

Monetary appraisal is a separate function, often requiring a qualified appraiser, and should not drive the acquisition decision for mission-focused institutions.

### Assess Condition And Preservation Burden Realistically

The cost of accepting a collection includes its preservation and processing burden, which can be substantial. Condition assessment must be part of appraisal.

Assess:

- physical condition, mold, brittleness, damage, pest infestation;
- format mix, including audiovisual and born-digital with high preservation cost;
- storage requirements, oversized, specialized environments, security;
- processing requirements, extent of arrangement and description needed;
- conservation needs and their cost;
- hidden costs, legal review, restriction management, rights clearance.

A collection that is free to acquire but costs a fortune to house and process is not free. Factor the full lifecycle cost into the decision.

### Secure Clear Title Through A Proper Deed Of Gift

Accepting materials without clear title creates legal and ethical problems that can persist for decades. A deed of gift or equivalent transfer instrument is essential.

The deed should specify:

- the donor's authority to transfer and warranty of title;
- what is being transferred, with an inventory or description;
- any restrictions on access, use, or reproduction, and their duration;
- disposition of unwanted materials, return, discard, or transfer;
- copyright status and any transfer of rights;
- future additions or related materials expectations;
- signatures and dates.

Verbal promises or informal transfers are not sufficient. Engage legal counsel for significant or complex gifts. Unclear title blocks future disposition and publication.

### Negotiate Restrictions Before Acceptance

Donors often propose restrictions. Negotiating them before acceptance is far easier than managing them afterward, and excessive restrictions can justify declining the gift.

Negotiate:

- the specific basis and necessity of each restriction;
- a defined duration rather than perpetual closure;
- redaction or partial access as alternatives to full restriction;
- separation of restricted from open materials to maximize access;
- the process for researchers to request restricted access;
- the institution's right to reconsider restrictions over time.

Perpetual, vague, or sweeping restrictions may make a gift more burden than benefit. Be willing to decline or to require modification.

### Consider Processing Capacity And Backlog

Every accepted collection enters a processing queue. Appraisal must consider whether the institution can process the materials within a reasonable time.

Consider:

- current backlog and processing rate;
- staff capacity and expertise for the collection's formats and languages;
- estimated processing time at the appropriate level;
- whether grant or project funding can support processing;
- the cost of leaving materials inaccessible in the backlog.

Accepting collections that will sit unprocessed for decades serves neither the materials nor researchers. Honest capacity assessment prevents overcommitment.

### Evaluate Born-Digital And Audiovisual Components

Modern collections frequently include born-digital files, audiovisual media, or both, and these carry the highest preservation cost and obsolescence risk. Appraisal must account for them specifically.

Assess:

- the volume and formats of digital and audiovisual materials;
- the institution's capacity to ingest, preserve, and provide access;
- the cost of specialized equipment, software, and storage;
- the risk of format obsolescence and the migration burden;
- privacy and security concerns in digital content;
- whether digital materials can be separated or require integrated processing.

A collection with significant born-digital content may be beyond an institution's capacity. Decline or partner rather than accept materials that cannot be preserved.

### Document The Appraisal Decision

Whether accepting or declining, document the appraisal so the decision is defensible and informative for future acquisitions.

Document:

- the materials appraised and their extent;
- the appraisal criteria applied and findings;
- the decision and its rationale;
- any conditions, restrictions, or negotiations;
- the deed of gift or decline letter;
- the relationship to collecting scope and existing holdings.

Documentation supports institutional memory, audit, and the consistency of future appraisal.

### Be Willing To Decline And Redirect

Declining materials is a legitimate and necessary appraisal outcome. Institutions that accept everything become warehouses of unprocessed, inaccessible materials.

Decline or redirect when:

- materials are outside collecting scope;
- the preservation or processing burden exceeds capacity;
- restrictions are unacceptable or perpetual;
- title is unclear and cannot be resolved;
- another institution is a clearly better home;
- the materials duplicate existing holdings without added value.

A respectful decline, with a referral to a better-fit institution, serves the materials and the donor better than reluctant acceptance.

### Plan For Future Additions And Relationships

Many donors expect to make future additions or maintain a relationship with the institution. Appraisal should consider the long-term relationship, not just the immediate gift.

Clarify:

- expectations for future additions and their terms;
- the donor's ongoing role, if any, in access decisions;
- the institution's commitment to processing and access timelines;
- recognition and acknowledgment expectations;
- the process for revisiting restrictions or terms over time.

A gift is often the start of a relationship. Set expectations clearly to avoid future conflict.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Everything Offered

Guilt or enthusiasm leads to accepting out-of-scope or burdensome materials. Appraise against mission and capacity, and decline when warranted.

### Ignoring Processing And Preservation Cost

A free collection can be expensive to house and process. Factor the full lifecycle cost into appraisal.

### Accepting Without Clear Title

Verbal or informal transfers create persistent legal problems. Secure a proper deed of gift.

### Accepting Unreasonable Restrictions

Perpetual or sweeping restrictions can make a gift more burden than benefit. Negotiate terms before acceptance.

### Underestimating Born-Digital Burden

Digital and audiovisual materials carry high preservation cost and obsolescence risk. Assess capacity before accepting.

### No Documentation Of The Decision

Undocumented appraisal cannot be defended or learned from. Record criteria, findings, and rationale.

### Never Declining

Institutions that cannot decline become warehouses. Decline and redirect when materials do not fit.

### Overlooking Better Homes Elsewhere

Materials may serve research better at an institution with stronger related holdings. Refer when appropriate.

## Self-Check

- Does the collection fit the institution's mission and documented collecting scope?
- Is research and cultural value weighed alongside, and often above, monetary worth?
- Is the preservation and processing burden assessed realistically, including hidden costs?
- Is clear title secured through a proper deed of gift with specified terms?
- Are restrictions negotiated before acceptance, with defined durations and alternatives?
- Is processing capacity and backlog considered in the decision to accept?
- Are born-digital and audiovisual components assessed for preservation feasibility and cost?
- Is the appraisal decision documented with criteria, findings, and rationale?
- Is the institution willing to decline or redirect materials that do not fit?
- Are expectations for future additions and the donor relationship clarified?
