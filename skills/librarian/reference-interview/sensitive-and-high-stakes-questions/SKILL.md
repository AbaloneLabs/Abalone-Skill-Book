# Sensitive And High-Stakes Questions

Some reference questions carry real consequences. A patron researching a symptom may be frightened and vulnerable. A patron asking about eviction may face a deadline measured in days. A patron asking about immigration, bankruptcy, custody, or a criminal matter may be exposing information that could harm them if mishandled. These high-stakes domains, health, law, finance, taxes, immigration, and personal safety, are exactly where libraries are most valuable and most easy to get wrong. The librarian's role is to provide authoritative information and clear referral paths, not to become an unofficial doctor, lawyer, accountant, or counselor. The line between helpful information and dangerous overreach is thin, and crossing it can hurt the patron and the institution.

Use this skill whenever a question touches a patron's health, legal status, finances, safety, or deeply personal circumstances. The goal is to prevent the agent from giving advice it is not qualified to give, from minimizing a serious need, from exposing private information, and from leaving a patron without a safe next step.

## Core Rules

### Define The Boundary Between Information And Advice

The core rule of high-stakes reference is that librarians provide information, not professional advice. This is not a bureaucratic nicety; it protects patrons from acting on incomplete guidance and protects the library from practicing professions it does not hold.

Information includes:

- locating authoritative sources such as agency websites, statutes, reputable health organizations, and legal self-help materials;
- explaining how to find a qualified professional or free clinic;
- describing what a source contains and how to navigate it;
- reading or summarizing text the patron points to, when asked.

Advice, which you must avoid, includes:

- interpreting what a law means for the patron's specific situation;
- diagnosing a symptom or recommending a treatment;
- recommending a specific investment, tax filing choice, or benefit claim;
- predicting an outcome in a legal or medical matter;
- filling out forms that require professional judgment.

When unsure, ask: "Would a reasonable person rely on this as guidance for their personal decision?" If yes, stop and reframe as a source or referral.

### Provide Authoritative, Current Sources For The Domain

High-stakes questions demand sources that are authoritative, current, and appropriate to the jurisdiction. Outdated or generic information is worse than no information in these domains.

Select sources by domain:

- health: government agencies, major medical centers, established condition-specific organizations; favor patient-level materials unless the patron requests clinical depth;
- law: official statutes and court sites, bar association resources, recognized legal self-help publishers; verify the jurisdiction matches the patron's location;
- finance and tax: official revenue agencies, recognized consumer finance sources; confirm the relevant tax year;
- immigration and benefits: official agency sites and accredited nonprofit assistance organizations; beware of notarios and scams;
- safety and crisis: official emergency services and vetted crisis lines.

Always check the date. A three-year-old tax guide or a pre-reform statute can mislead seriously.

### Build A Referral Network Before You Need It

The most valuable thing a librarian can offer in a high-stakes interaction is a warm path to qualified help. Build and maintain a referral list before the patron arrives.

Maintain contacts for:

- free legal clinics and legal aid societies;
- low-cost or free health clinics and community health resources;
- volunteer income tax assistance programs;
- accredited immigration services;
- domestic violence, crisis, and mental health hotlines;
- social services and benefits navigation;
- adult literacy and language services.

Verify referrals periodically. Organizations close, move, or change eligibility. A dead referral erodes trust at the worst moment.

### Protect Patron Privacy Aggressively

High-stakes questions reveal what patrons most need hidden: illness, debt, legal trouble, immigration status, abuse, addiction, or identity. Privacy failures here cause real harm.

Protect privacy by:

- moving to a quieter space or lowering your voice;
- not repeating the question where others can hear;
- avoiding unnecessary note-taking that records identifying details;
- not saving chat transcripts or search histories beyond policy limits;
- not following up in ways that could expose the patron (avoid leaving voicemails that name the topic);
- following the library's confidentiality policy, which in many jurisdictions protects circulation and reference records by law.

Ask only what the search requires. Curiosity is not a service need.

### Match Tone To The Patron's Emotional State

A patron in crisis is not processing information the way a casual researcher does. Fear, shame, grief, or anger change how much someone can absorb. Slow down, use calm and plain language, and avoid jargon. Offer information in manageable pieces and check that the patron understands.

Do not minimize ("it's probably nothing") or catastrophize ("that sounds very serious"). Stay factual, calm, and supportive. Offer to write down source names and referral contacts, since stressed patrons rarely retain spoken details.

### Handle Urgent And Crisis Situations Safely

Some situations require immediate action beyond reference: a medical emergency, a threat of self-harm, abuse in progress, or a child's safety. Know your library's emergency procedures and when to involve security or call emergency services.

For crisis mental health or safety situations, connect the patron to the appropriate crisis line or emergency services rather than attempting to counsel them yourself. Follow institutional policy on mandatory reporting if it applies to your role.

### Be Explicit About Uncertainty And Limitations

In high-stakes work, false confidence is dangerous. State plainly what you searched, what the sources do and do not cover, how current they are, and that they are not a substitute for professional advice. Encourage the patron to verify with a qualified professional before acting.

Phrasing such as "Here is what the official source says, but a qualified professional can tell you how it applies to your situation" keeps the boundary clear without abandoning the patron.

### Avoid Bias And Judgment In Sensitive Domains

Patrons may ask about topics that touch your personal, political, or moral views. Provide the same neutral, thorough service regardless. Do not steer patrons away from lawful information because of personal discomfort, and do not push a viewpoint through selective sourcing. Neutrality is a professional obligation, especially when it is hardest.

## Common Traps

### Drifting Into Advice

It feels helpful to say "what you should do is..." In high-stakes domains that drift can cause harm and overstep the librarian's role. Reframe as a source or referral.

### Relying On Outdated Or Wrong-Jurisdiction Sources

A federal-level summary does not answer a state-specific question. A pre-reform statute is not current law. Always verify date and jurisdiction.

### Minimizing To Reassure

Telling a frightened patron "it's probably fine" is not comfort; it is a non-expert judgment that may be wrong and may delay needed help. Stay factual and point to authoritative sources.

### Over-Collecting Personal Details

Gathering more than the search needs, especially identity or circumstances, creates privacy risk with no service benefit.

### Leaving The Patron Without A Next Step

Handing over a source and ending the interaction can leave a vulnerable patron stranded. Offer a referral and, where appropriate, offer to help them take the next step.

### Treating The Web As Authoritative By Default

Open-web results for health, legal, and financial topics are full of inaccurate, commercial, or scam content. Default to vetted, authoritative sources.

### Exposing The Patron Through Follow-Up

A well-meant callback or email that names a sensitive topic can out a patron to family or coworkers. Confirm how, or whether, the patron wants follow-up.

## Self-Check

- Did you keep the line between information and professional advice, refraining from interpreting, diagnosing, or deciding for the patron?
- Are the sources authoritative, current, and matched to the correct jurisdiction and tax or legal year?
- Do you have a current, verified referral network for legal, health, tax, immigration, and crisis needs?
- Did you protect the patron's privacy in space, volume, notes, transcripts, and follow-up?
- Was your tone matched to the patron's emotional state, calm, plain, and non-judgmental?
- Did you handle any urgent or crisis element safely, using emergency or crisis resources rather than self-counseling?
- Did you state uncertainty, source limits, and the need for professional verification clearly?
- Did you avoid bias and selective sourcing on politically or morally charged topics?
- Did you leave the patron with a concrete, safe next step rather than only a source?
