# Clarifying The Real Question

The stated question is rarely the complete question. Patrons arrive with partial vocabulary, a half-formed task, embarrassment about what they do not know, a deadline they have not mentioned, and assumptions about what the library can provide. Answering the literal first question often sends the patron away with the wrong source, the wrong scope, or a correct answer to the wrong need. The reference interview is the disciplined practice of discovering the real information need before committing to a search, and it is the single most important skill in reference service.

Use this skill before searching, before recommending a source, and before declaring an answer. The goal is to prevent the agent from jumping to a search the moment it hears keywords, from over-interrogating the patron, and from delivering a technically correct but useless answer.

## Core Rules

### Treat The First Question As A Starting Point, Not A Specification

A patron who asks for "books on taxes" may need local tax forms, a small-business deduction guide, a primer on estate tax, or a referral to a free tax-preparation clinic. A patron who asks for "something on Shakespeare" may need a quick biography, a line-by-line gloss for homework, peer-reviewed criticism for a paper, or a filmed performance. The first utterance is a signal, not a specification.

Before searching, establish:

- what the patron is ultimately trying to do with the information;
- the audience the result must serve (self, a child, a class, a client, a supervisor);
- the deadline or urgency;
- the required depth and source type;
- how much the patron already knows or has already tried.

When the request is a known-item search with full details, skip long interviews and confirm the specifics. When the request is vague, slow down.

### Match The Interview Style To The Setting And Patron

Not every interaction needs a formal interview. A quick factual lookup may need one confirming question; a complex research need may need several minutes of structured clarification. Calibrate to the channel and the person.

Adjust by:

- channel: in-person, phone, chat, email, or video each reward different question styles;
- patron confidence: a hesitant patron needs warmer, open prompts; a precise patron needs targeted confirmation;
- time pressure: an urgent need gets faster, narrower questions;
- privacy: a sensitive topic gets quieter, less exposing questions;
- language and accessibility: simplify, slow down, offer alternate formats.

Do not run the same long checklist for every patron. Over-interviewing a simple request is as much a service failure as under-interviewing a complex one.

### Lead With Open Questions, Then Narrow

Effective interviews move from open to closed. Start broad enough to let the patron describe the task in their own words, then narrow to the specifics a search requires.

A useful sequence:

- open: "Tell me more about what you're working on.";
- restatement: "So you're looking for recent statistics on... is that right?";
- scope: "Are you looking for the whole country, or a specific state?";
- format and level: "Do you need something scholarly, or more general background?";
- confirmation: "If I find X, will that be what you need?".

Restating the need in your own words is the most reliable way to catch a misunderstanding before you waste a search. Patrons will correct a restatement far more readily than they will volunteer that your interpretation is wrong.

### Use Neutral Language So You Do Not Bias The Need

The way you ask shapes the answer. Leading questions can push a patron toward a need they do not actually have, or make them feel judged for what they want.

Prefer neutral phrasing:

- "What kind of information would be most useful?" rather than "You just want a quick fact, right?";
- "Tell me more about your project" rather than "Why do you need that?";
- avoid tone that signals a question is trivial, embarrassing, or off-limits.

Neutrality matters most for topics that carry stigma or risk: health, legal trouble, finances, sexuality, immigration, or politically charged subjects. A patron who senses judgment will shut down and leave with nothing.

### Separate The Need From The Patron's Proposed Solution

Patrons often arrive with a proposed source or answer ("I need the 2020 census report"). Sometimes that is exactly right. Sometimes it is a guess about where the answer lives, and the real need is the underlying question. Confirm the underlying need before chasing the proposed source, but respect that the patron may specifically require the item they named.

Ask: "Is that report specifically required, or are you looking for the figures in it? I want to make sure I get you the right thing."

### Know When To Stop Clarifying And Start Helping

Clarification has diminishing returns. If you understand the task, the audience, the scope, and the source type, begin searching and let results confirm or correct your understanding. You can refine during the search rather than extracting every detail up front.

Stop the interview when:

- you can state the need back and the patron agrees;
- the request is a known item with complete details;
- urgency makes further questions unhelpful;
- the patron is visibly frustrated by questions.

Offer to adjust as you go: "Let me start with this; if it's not quite right, tell me and I'll shift."

### Document And Hand Off Without Exposing The Patron

When a question must be handed to another librarian, a subject specialist, or a referral, pass enough context to continue the work without exposing private details unnecessarily. Summarize the need, not the patron's identity or circumstances beyond what the next person requires.

## Common Traps

### Answering The Literal Question

The patron asks "Do you have books on diabetes?" but needs a recently updated diet guide at a lay reading level, not the clinical textbooks a literal search returns. Clarify before searching.

### The Interrogation

Firing a rigid list of questions at a simple request makes the patron feel processed rather than helped. Match the depth of the interview to the depth of the need.

### Restating Wrongly And Believing The Restatement

Restating is only useful if you treat the patron's correction as authoritative. If you "helpfully" assume you understood and search anyway, you repeat the original error with confidence.

### Leading The Patron To Your Preferred Answer

A librarian who finds government sources comfortable may steer every patron there, even when a community organization or a different format would serve better. Let the need, not your comfort, drive the source.

### Skipping The Interview Because The Keywords Seem Clear

Keyword clarity is not need clarity. "Climate change" is clear as a topic and useless as a specification. Resist the urge to search on first hearing.

### Embarrassing The Patron Into Silence

A question asked loudly, with surprise, or in front of others can end the interaction. Sensitive topics need lowered volume, private space, and matter-of-fact tone.

### Treating A Known-Item Request As An Open Topic

If a patron gives a precise title, author, and edition, do not launch a topic interview. Confirm and retrieve. Over-interviewing precise requests wastes time and signals inattention.

## Self-Check

- Did you identify the patron's ultimate task, audience, deadline, and required source type before searching?
- Did you treat the first question as a starting point and confirm the real need rather than answering literally?
- Is the interview depth matched to the request's complexity, channel, and urgency?
- Did you lead with open questions, restate the need, and then narrow to specifics?
- Is your language neutral, especially for sensitive or stigmatized topics?
- Did you separate the underlying need from the patron's proposed source when they differed?
- Did you stop clarifying once the need was clear and offer to refine during the search?
- If handing off or referring, did you pass context without exposing unnecessary personal details?
- Did you avoid both over-interviewing simple requests and under-interviewing complex ones?
