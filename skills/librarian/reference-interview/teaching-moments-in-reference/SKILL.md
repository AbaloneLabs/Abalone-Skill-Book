# Teaching Moments In Reference

Every reference transaction is also a teaching opportunity, but not every transaction should become a lesson. The patron who needs a single fact for a deadline does not want a tutorial on Boolean operators, and the patron who will return to this research repeatedly benefits enormously from learning how the answer was found. The art of the teaching moment is recognizing when a brief, well-timed bit of instruction will help the patron far more than a faster answer, and when instruction would only get in the way. Librarians tend to err in two directions: delivering answers transactionally and never building capability, or over-teaching patrons who just want the answer and feel lectured to. Good teaching in reference is light, contextual, offered rather than imposed, and tied to the actual task in front of the patron. It builds independence without abandoning the patron to figure everything out alone.

Use this skill during reference transactions when there is a chance to show the patron something transferable, when deciding how much process to reveal, and when designing reference service that builds lasting skills. The goal is to prevent the agent from either hoarding the process or over-lecturing, and to make teaching a natural, opt-in part of the interaction.

## Core Rules

### Judge Whether The Patron Wants To Learn Or Just Wants The Answer

The first decision is whether teaching serves this patron in this moment. Read the signals.

Teach when:

- the patron will repeat this kind of search (a student writing a thesis, a researcher in a field);
- the patron asks how you found something or expresses curiosity about the process;
- the answer alone will not help next time, but the method will;
- the patron has time and is not visibly rushed or distressed.

Answer directly when:

- the patron needs a single fact under time pressure;
- the patron is in crisis or distressed (teaching is inappropriate; help is what is needed);
- the question is a one-off the patron will never repeat;
- the patron has declined an offer to show the process.

Ask before launching into instruction: "Would it help if I showed you how I found that, so you can do it next time?" An opt-in offer respects autonomy and avoids the lecture trap.

### Teach The Process, Not Just The Product

When you teach, reveal the thinking that produced the answer, not just the result. The process is what transfers.

Reveal:

- which source you chose and why it fit the question;
- the terms and filters that worked, and the ones that did not;
- how you read a result record to judge relevance;
- how you refined when the first search was too broad or too narrow;
- how you would verify or cite what you found.

Narrating the process briefly, even in a minute or two, gives the patron a mental model they can reuse. Handing over only the finished answer teaches dependence.

### Keep Teaching Short, Concrete, And Just-In-Time

Reference teaching is not a class. It is a brief, contextual demonstration tied to the patron's real need. Keep it to what helps now.

Keep it tight by:

- teaching one transferable thing per interaction, not a curriculum;
- using the patron's actual topic, not a generic example;
- showing, not just telling (share a screen, point at the interface);
- stopping when the patron has what they need or signals they are full;
- offering a guide or handout for later rather than cramming in more.

A one-minute, well-chosen tip beats a ten-minute lecture every time.

### Match The Teaching To The Patron's Level

Teaching that is too basic bores; teaching that is too advanced overwhelms. Calibrate quickly to what the patron already knows.

Calibrate by:

- asking what they have already tried or which tools they know;
- watching for cues of confusion or boredom;
- building on what they know rather than restarting from zero;
- offering the next step up, not the expert's full workflow.

A graduate student who already knows their discipline's database needs a different tip than a first-year student encountering the catalog for the first time.

### Teach Transferable Skills, Not Tool-Specific Clicks

Interfaces change, and tool-specific instructions age quickly. Teach the underlying skills that transfer across tools.

Transferable skills to teach:

- decomposing a topic into concepts;
- choosing a source type by information need;
- using controlled vocabulary and synonyms;
- reading results as feedback and iterating;
- evaluating sources for authority and relevance;
- tracing citations to map a field.

Tool-specific clicks have their place, but anchor them in the transferable skill so the patron is not helpless when the interface changes.

### Use The Reference Transaction To Surface Broader Gaps

When the same confusion appears across many transactions, it signals a teaching gap that a guide, a workshop, or a course-integrated session could address. Reference teaching is real-time needs assessment.

Capture and act on patterns:

- note recurring questions or misconceptions;
- identify topics where patrons consistently struggle;
- create or improve guides, FAQs, and tutorials for those gaps;
- propose instruction sessions to departments or courses with recurring needs.

The desk is where you learn what teaching the community actually needs.

### Teach Without Condescension

Patrons often arrive feeling embarrassed about what they do not know. How you teach determines whether they leave more confident or more ashamed.

Teach respectfully by:

- normalizing the difficulty ("this database is genuinely confusing at first");
- crediting what the patron already did right;
- avoiding jargon or defining it when used;
- never implying the question is trivial or the patron should have known;
- treating the interaction as collaboration, not correction.

A patron who feels respected returns and asks more; one who feels judged disappears.

### Offer Follow-Up Paths For Deeper Learning

Some patrons want more than a moment can offer. Give them a path forward without forcing it.

Offer:

- a relevant research guide or tutorial;
- a scheduled consultation for in-depth help;
- an upcoming workshop or course session;
- the option to return with follow-up questions.

The teaching moment opens a door; the follow-up lets the patron walk through when ready.

### Document Effective Teaching For Colleagues

When you find a particularly effective way to explain a recurring concept, share it. Reference teaching improves when good explanations spread across the team.

Share through:

- a shared bank of effective explanations and analogies;
- notes on which guides or demos work for which questions;
- team training on common teaching moments.

## Common Traps

### Answering Transactionally And Never Teaching

Always delivering the answer builds dependence and wastes the chance to build lasting capability. Offer to teach when it serves the patron.

### Over-Teaching The Patron Who Just Wants The Answer

Launching into a tutorial for a rushed or one-off question feels like a lecture. Ask first and respect the answer.

### Teaching Tool Clicks Instead Of Transferable Skills

Interface-specific instructions age fast. Teach the underlying skill that survives platform changes.

### Teaching Out Of Sync With The Patron's Level

Too basic bores; too advanced overwhelms. Calibrate to what the patron already knows.

### Condescension

Implying the question is trivial or the patron should know better shames them into not returning. Teach with respect.

### Cramming A Curriculum Into A Minute

Reference teaching is one well-chosen tip, not a course. Offer follow-up paths for depth instead.

### Ignoring Recurring Gaps

When the same confusion recurs, it signals a teaching gap. Capture patterns and build guides or sessions to address them.

### Teaching With Generic Examples

A generic example is less memorable than the patron's own topic. Teach with the real search in front of you.

## Self-Check

- Did you judge whether this patron, in this moment, wants to learn or just wants the answer?
- Did you offer the teaching as opt-in rather than imposing a lesson?
- Did you reveal the process (source choice, terms, iteration, verification), not just the product?
- Was the teaching short, concrete, and tied to the patron's actual topic?
- Did you calibrate the level to what the patron already knows?
- Did you teach a transferable skill rather than only tool-specific clicks?
- Did you capture recurring confusions as signals for guides, workshops, or course-integrated instruction?
- Was the tone respectful and free of condescension, building the patron's confidence?
- Did you offer a follow-up path (guide, consultation, workshop) for patrons who want more?
- Would this patron be better equipped to do a similar search next time because of the interaction?
