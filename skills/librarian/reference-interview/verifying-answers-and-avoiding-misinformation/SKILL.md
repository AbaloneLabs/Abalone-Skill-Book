# Verifying Answers And Avoiding Misisinformation

A reference answer is not finished when it is found; it is finished when it is verified. The temptation at the desk is to deliver the first plausible result, especially under time pressure or when a patron is waiting. But the first result is often wrong, outdated, taken out of context, or scraped from an unreliable site that itself copied an error. Misinformation now travels through search engines, AI summaries, social media, and even ostensibly authoritative databases that have ingested low-quality content. A librarian who delivers an unverified answer is not saving time; they are transferring the cost to the patron, who may act on a false medical claim, cite a nonexistent source, or rely on a statistic that was never real. Verification is the discipline that separates a reference answer from a guess, and it is what makes the library's answer worth more than a search engine's.

Use this skill before delivering any nontrivial answer, before citing a fact or statistic, and whenever a result feels surprisingly easy, surprisingly extreme, or suspiciously tidy. The goal is to prevent the agent from delivering the first plausible hit, from trusting AI summaries or single sources uncritically, from presenting contested claims as settled, or from claiming more certainty than the evidence supports.

## Core Rules

### Triangulate Nontrivial Facts Across Independent Sources

A single source, however authoritative it appears, can be wrong. Triangulation means confirming a fact through at least two independent sources that did not derive from one another. This is the single most reliable guard against transmitting an error.

Triangulate by:

- checking the claim in an authoritative reference work, an official primary source, and a reputable secondary source;
- ensuring the sources are genuinely independent (two sites that copied the same Wikipedia line are one source);
- preferring primary over secondary sources for statistics, legal text, and official statements;
- noting where sources agree and where they diverge.

For routine ready-reference (a capital city, a publication date), one authoritative source may suffice. For anything consequential, health, law, statistics, contested history, triangulate.

### Trace Claims To Their Primary Source

Many facts circulate as summaries of summaries. The number shrinks, the framing shifts, and the original context is lost. Trace consequential claims back to the primary source.

Trace by asking:

- who originally produced this statistic, finding, or quotation?
- is the cited source the study itself, or an article about the study?
- does the primary source actually say what the secondary source claims?
- is the date, population, and methodology preserved or distorted in retelling?

A news headline that "a study shows X" often oversimplifies or reverses what the study found. The primary source is the authority; the retelling is a convenience that must be checked.

### Recognize And Discount AI-Generated And Scraped Content

Generative AI summaries, low-quality content farms, and search engine overviews now appear at the top of results. They are fluent, confident, and frequently wrong. They fabricate citations, conflate sources, and present invented facts with the same tone as verified ones.

Handle AI and scraped content by:

- not treating an AI summary or search overview as a source; treat it as a lead to verify;
- checking every cited source to confirm it exists and says what is claimed (AI routinely invents citations);
- preferring established reference works, official sites, and peer-reviewed sources over generated text;
- being especially wary of AI answers for statistics, citations, legal or medical claims, and recent events;
- recognizing that fluency and confidence are not evidence of accuracy.

The danger is not that AI is always wrong; it is that it is wrong unpredictably and without any signal of uncertainty.

### Apply Source Evaluation To Every Nontrivial Answer

Verification depends on evaluating the sources behind the answer. Apply a consistent evaluation framework rather than trusting by recognition or rank.

Evaluate:

- authority: who produced it, and are they qualified on this topic?
- accuracy: does it match other reliable sources and cite its own evidence?
- currency: is it current enough for a topic that changes (law, medicine, statistics, technology)?
- coverage: does it actually address the patron's specific question, or only an adjacent one?
- purpose and bias: is it informing, selling, persuading, or satirizing?
- provenance: is it the original, or a reproduction that may have been altered?

(See the related information-literacy and source-evaluation skills for fuller frameworks.)

### Handle Contested And Rapidly Changing Facts Carefully

Some facts are not settled: emerging science, ongoing legal cases, disputed historical claims, breaking news, and politically charged statistics. Presenting these as resolved misleads the patron.

Handle contested or changing facts by:

- stating that the topic is contested or evolving;
- presenting the range of credible positions, not just the one you found first;
- naming the basis for each position and who holds it;
- dating the information explicitly;
- pointing to where the patron can track updates.

For breaking news, prefer wire services and official statements over social media, and warn the patron that early reports are often incomplete or wrong.

### Match The Certainty Claimed To The Evidence

Do not present a tentative or single-sourced answer as definitive, and do not bury a strong, well-sourced answer in excessive hedging. Calibrate the language to the evidence.

Calibrate by:

- saying "according to X" to attribute a claim to its source;
- marking estimates and approximations as such;
- distinguishing "the official figure is Y" from "sources report figures ranging from Y to Z";
- admitting plainly when you could not verify a claim, rather than presenting the best available guess as fact.

Honest uncertainty is more useful than false confidence. A patron who knows an answer is uncertain can seek further; one who is told it is settled may stop looking.

### Verify Before Citing, Especially For Academic Patrons

Students and researchers who cite a source the librarian provided trust that it exists and says what was claimed. An invented or misdescribed citation can damage their work.

Before delivering a citation:

- confirm the source exists at the stated location (database, journal, shelf);
- confirm the author, title, date, and page numbers;
- confirm the source actually supports the claim being attributed to it;
- provide a stable link or full citation the patron can re-find.

### Check Dates, Editions, And Jurisdictions

A correct fact in the wrong year, edition, or jurisdiction is a wrong answer. This is especially dangerous in law, medicine, tax, and government benefits.

Check:

- the publication or update date against the patron's need for currency;
- the edition against what the patron actually holds or needs;
- the jurisdiction (a federal summary does not answer a state-specific legal question);
- the tax or reporting year for financial and statistical figures.

### Document The Verification Path

For research that matters, record not just the answer but how it was verified: which sources, which dates, where they agree or diverge. This makes the answer reproducible, defensible, and updatable, and it protects against the quiet drift of a fact that was right once but is no longer.

## Common Traps

### Delivering The First Plausible Hit

Speed feels helpful but transfers error risk to the patron. Verify nontrivial answers before delivering.

### Trusting AI Summaries And Search Overviews

Fluent generated text is not a source. Verify every claim and citation, because AI invents confidently.

### Treating Two Copies Of The Same Error As Triangulation

If both sources derived from the same origin, they are one source. Seek genuinely independent confirmation.

### Citing The Summary Instead Of The Primary Source

Secondary retellings distort. Trace consequential claims to the original study, statute, or dataset.

### Presenting Contested Claims As Settled

Emerging science and disputed topics are not resolved just because one source states a position. Present the range and the basis.

### Wrong Year, Edition, Or Jurisdiction

A correct fact in the wrong context is a wrong answer, especially in law, medicine, and tax. Check currency and jurisdiction.

### Overclaiming Certainty

Presenting a single-sourced or estimated answer as definitive misleads. Match the language to the evidence.

### Invented Or Misdescribed Citations

A citation that does not exist or does not say what is claimed can damage a patron's work. Verify before citing.

## Self-Check

- Did you triangulate nontrivial facts across genuinely independent sources before delivering the answer?
- Did you trace consequential claims back to their primary source rather than stopping at a summary?
- Did you treat AI summaries and search overviews as leads to verify, not as sources?
- Did you apply source evaluation (authority, accuracy, currency, coverage, purpose) to the sources behind the answer?
- For contested or rapidly changing facts, did you present the range of credible positions and date the information?
- Is the certainty you claimed matched to the strength of the evidence, with honest attribution and hedging?
- Did you verify citations exist, are locatable, and support the claims attributed to them?
- Did you check dates, editions, and jurisdictions, especially for legal, medical, tax, and statistical answers?
- Did you document the verification path so the answer is reproducible and updatable?
- Could you defend this answer if challenged, by showing the sources and how they were checked?
