# Remote And Digital Reference Service

Reference service no longer happens only at a desk. Patrons expect help through chat, email, text, video, social media messaging, and ticketing systems, often outside the library's open hours and from locations far from the building. Digital channels expand reach, but they strip away the cues that make in-person reference work: tone, facial expression, body language, the ability to glance at a screen together, and the easy back-and-forth that surfaces the real question. Text-only communication hides confusion, urgency, and emotion. Asynchronous channels introduce delay that can make a time-sensitive answer useless. Remote reference is real reference, but it demands deliberate techniques to compensate for what the channel removes.

Use this skill when serving patrons through any digital or remote channel. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating chat or email as a lighter version of reference, from missing hidden urgency or confusion, from overpromising turnaround, and from exposing patron data across digital systems.

## Core Rules

### Compensate For Missing Cues With Explicit Language

In person, a confused look prompts you to re-explain. Online, confusion is invisible unless you ask. Compensate by making the implicit explicit.

Techniques:

- check understanding directly: "Does that answer what you needed, or should I keep looking?";
- narrate what you are doing: "Let me search the catalog now; this may take a moment.";
- confirm the need in writing: "Just to make sure I have this right, you're looking for...";
- invite correction: "If I've got the wrong end of it, tell me and I'll adjust.";
- watch for cues that are visible: long pauses, terse replies, repeated questions, or a patron going quiet.

A patron who goes silent may have found the answer and left, or may be lost and embarrassed. A quick check-in distinguishes the two.

### Match The Interview To The Channel's Constraints

Each channel rewards a different interview style. Chat rewards short, frequent turns. Email rewards a complete first message that anticipates follow-up. Video allows something closer to in-person reference.

Adapt:

- chat: keep messages short, ask one question at a time, avoid walls of text;
- email: ask all clarifying questions in one message since each round costs hours;
- text: be concise, confirm the core need, avoid long URLs that break;
- video: use screen-sharing to search together and teach the process.

Do not paste an in-person interview script into a chat window. Reformulate for the medium.

### Set Clear Scope And Turnaround Expectations Early

Asynchronous channels create uncertainty about when, or whether, help will arrive. Set expectations immediately to reduce anxiety and repeated follow-ups.

State:

- when the patron can expect a response (within an hour during chat hours, within one business day for email);
- what you can and cannot do through the channel (e.g., cannot print, cannot access certain licensed databases off-network);
- what information you need to proceed;
- how to reach a faster or different kind of help if needed.

A patron who knows to expect a reply by morning will wait. A patron left in silence will resend the message or give up.

### Verify The Need And Identity Appropriately

Remote service raises questions about who the patron is and whether they are entitled to specific services. Verify what is necessary for the task without creating friction or invading privacy.

Verify when:

- the request involves account access, interlibrary loan, or licensed resources tied to a card or affiliation;
- you are sending materials or confirming holds;
- policy requires affiliation for a service.

Do not demand identity for general reference questions that require no account. Over-verifying creates barriers, especially for patrons who already feel unwelcome.

### Protect Privacy Across Digital Systems

Digital channels leave traces: chat logs, email threads, ticket records, shared screens, and notification previews on devices. Each is a privacy surface that in-person reference does not have.

Protect privacy by:

- following retention policy and not keeping transcripts longer than allowed;
- avoiding the collection of personal details a general question does not need;
- being cautious with shared screens that may show other patrons' information;
- warning patrons that email and chat are not fully secure for highly sensitive topics;
- not sending identifying information in subject lines or voicemails;
- using institutional, not personal, accounts and devices.

For highly sensitive topics, offer the option of an in-person or phone conversation where privacy is easier to control.

### Teach The Process, Not Just The Answer

Remote patrons often want to continue independently later. Teaching the search process through the channel builds lasting capability and reduces repeat questions.

Show:

- which database or catalog you used and why;
- the terms and filters that worked;
- how to read a result record;
- how to request or save items;
- how to get help again.

In chat, narrate briefly. In email, include a short "how to do this yourself" note. In video, share the screen and walk through the steps.

### Recognize When Remote Is Not Enough

Some needs cannot be fully met remotely: hands-on archival research, in-depth consultations, technology the patron cannot access, or situations where the channel is hiding the real need. Recognize the limit and offer a handoff.

Offer to:

- continue in person at the desk or by appointment;
- schedule a longer video consultation with a subject specialist;
- refer to another institution or service;
- switch to a channel better suited to the need.

A graceful handoff is better service than struggling through an unsuitable channel.

### Handle Concurrent And Queued Interactions Honestly

Digital reference often involves multiple patrons at once. Manage concurrency without making any patron feel abandoned.

Manage by:

- acknowledging each new patron promptly even if you cannot fully answer yet;
- setting expectations when juggling ("I'm helping someone else too; I'll be with you in a few minutes.");
- not dropping a patron silently when a more interesting question arrives;
- using canned or templated responses for common greetings and closings, customized as needed.

### Maintain Professional Tone Across Informal Channels

Chat and text feel informal, but the service is still professional. Avoid over-familiarity, sarcasm, or unprofessional shortcuts that can read as dismissive. Plain, warm, respectful language works in every channel.

## Common Traps

### Treating Digital Reference As Lighter Than In-Person

The same standards of accuracy, interview, and source quality apply. A chat answer is still a reference answer.

### Missing Hidden Urgency Or Confusion

Without cues, a patron's silence or terse reply may mask a deadline or a misunderstanding. Check in rather than assume.

### Overpromising Turnaround

Saying "I'll get right back to you" and then replying tomorrow erodes trust. Set realistic expectations up front.

### Asking Clarifying Questions One At A Time In Email

Each email round costs hours. Batch the clarifying questions so one exchange can move the work forward.

### Exposing Patron Data In Logs And Notifications

Chat transcripts, email subject lines, and shared screens can leak information to staff, family, or bystanders. Handle them as privacy-sensitive.

### Demanding Identity For Simple Questions

Requiring a card number for a general reference lookup creates needless barriers and discourages use.

### Forcing A Need Through An Unsuitable Channel

Some questions need in-person or specialist help. Recognizing the limit and handing off is service, not failure.

### Dropping A Patron When A Better Question Arrives

Concurrency is normal. Acknowledge and queue fairly rather than abandoning the first patron.

## Self-Check

- Did you compensate for missing cues by checking understanding, narrating actions, and inviting correction?
- Is the interview style matched to the channel's constraints, with batched questions for asynchronous media?
- Did you set clear turnaround and scope expectations at the start?
- Did you verify identity only where the service requires it, without creating barriers for general questions?
- Did you protect privacy across logs, transcripts, shared screens, subject lines, and notifications?
- Did you teach the search process so the patron can continue independently?
- Did you recognize when the channel was insufficient and offer an appropriate handoff?
- Did you manage concurrent interactions fairly without dropping any patron?
- Did you maintain a professional, warm tone even on informal channels?
