# Follow-Up And Closure In Reference

A reference transaction is not complete when an answer is delivered; it is complete when the patron's need is met. These are different moments, and the gap between them is where reference service most often fails silently. A librarian finds a source, hands it over, and moves to the next patron, assuming the question is resolved. The patron, meanwhile, may find the source incomprehensible, off-target, or only partially useful, and leaves with an unmet need and no path back. The discipline of follow-up and closure is the practice of verifying that the answer actually resolved the need, of keeping questions open until they are genuinely closed, and of providing a route back when the first attempt fell short. It is unglamorous work, and it is where reference service earns or loses the trust that determines whether a patron returns.

Use this skill when deciding whether a reference interaction is complete, when planning or conducting a follow-up, when managing a long-running or complex question, or when diagnosing why patrons do not return after a reference interaction. The goal is to prevent the agent from declaring closure on delivery rather than on resolution, and to keep it from abandoning patrons whose needs were only partially met.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Delivery From Resolution

Delivering a source or an answer is an event. Resolving the patron's need is an outcome. The two coincide when the answer is exactly right, but they diverge often: the source is correct but too advanced, the answer is right but incomplete, the patron needs help applying it. Treating delivery as closure closes the transaction before the need is met.

Treat resolution as the standard by:

- confirming the patron can use what was provided, not just that it was provided;
- asking whether the source answers the question or whether more is needed;
- watching for hesitation or confusion that signals the need is not yet met;
- recognizing that a correct source the patron cannot use is not a resolution.

The transaction is closed when the patron's need is met, not when the librarian's search is done.

### Verify Resolution Before Declaring Closure

Verification is the act of checking that the answer worked. It takes a moment and it prevents the silent failure of a patron leaving unsatisfied. The form of verification depends on the channel and the complexity, but the principle is constant: do not assume resolution; confirm it.

Verify by:

- asking the patron to confirm the source meets the need before closing;
- for complex questions, offering to check back after the patron has had time to use the source;
- for remote channels, where body language is absent, asking explicitly whether more help is needed;
- for high-stakes questions, scheduling a genuine follow-up rather than a cursory check.

A patron who says "that's fine, thanks" may be satisfied or may be disengaging. Calibrate the verification to the stakes and the channel.

### Set Clear Expectations For Long-Running Questions

Not every reference question is resolved in one interaction. Complex research questions, genealogy, grant-seeking, and deep subject inquiries may require the librarian to return to the patron over hours or days. Without clear expectations, the patron does not know whether to wait, to follow up, or to seek help elsewhere, and the question drifts into ambiguity.

Set expectations by:

- telling the patron when to expect an answer or an update;
- explaining what the librarian will do and what the patron should do in the meantime;
- agreeing on how follow-up will happen, email, phone, return visit;
- being honest when the question will take longer than initially hoped.

A patron who knows when to expect a response waits patiently. A patron left in silence assumes the question was forgotten.

### Keep The Question Open Until Genuinely Closed

A reference question should remain open in the librarian's mind, and where appropriate in a tracking system, until it is genuinely resolved. Closing it prematurely, because a source was sent or because time passed, abandons patrons whose needs were not met. The discipline is to resist the relief of marking something complete.

Keep questions open by:

- tracking complex or referred questions to resolution, not to first response;
- following up on questions where the initial answer was uncertain or partial;
- noting when a patron did not return for a promised follow-up and reaching out;
- distinguishing questions that are paused, awaiting patron action, from those that are closed.

A tracking system that marks a question closed on first response hides the unresolved ones and prevents follow-up.

### Provide A Route Back When The First Answer Fell Short

Patrons often need to return: the source was not quite right, the need evolved, or a new question arose from the first answer. If the interaction closed in a way that made returning feel intrusive or burdensome, the patron gives up. A good closure explicitly invites return.

Provide a route back by:

- telling the patron they can return if the source does not fully meet the need;
- making the return path easy, with a contact method or a named librarian;
- framing follow-up as expected and welcome, not as an imposition;
- for remote channels, keeping the thread open or providing a clear re-entry point.

A patron who feels they can come back will. A patron who feels the transaction is over will not, even if their need persists.

### Match Follow-Up Depth To The Stakes And The Channel

Not every question warrants a follow-up. A quick factual lookup resolved on the spot needs no follow-up; a complex research question supporting a grant deadline warrants active follow-up. The depth of follow-up should match the stakes, and over-following simple questions is as much a service failure as under-following complex ones.

Match by:

- simple, resolved questions: confirm at closure, no follow-up needed;
- moderate questions: offer a route back, follow up only if uncertainty remains;
- complex or high-stakes questions: schedule a genuine follow-up and track to resolution;
- remote channels: err toward explicit follow-up, since nonverbal cues are absent.

Calibrate the follow-up to the need, the stakes, and the medium, rather than applying one rule to all transactions.

### Document The Resolution For Continuity

When a question is resolved, the resolution should be documented in a way that supports continuity. If the patron returns later, or if another librarian picks up the question, the record should show what was provided, what worked, and what remains open. Undocumented resolutions force patrons to repeat their story and undermine trust.

Document by:

- recording the sources provided and the patron's response;
- noting what resolved the need and what did not;
- flagging any open threads or promised follow-ups;
- keeping the documentation respectful of patron privacy.

A documented resolution turns a one-time interaction into institutional knowledge that serves the patron and the next librarian.

## Common Traps

### Declaring Closure On Delivery

Handing over a source and moving on closes the transaction before the need is met. Resolution, not delivery, is the standard.

### Assuming Silence Means Satisfaction

A patron who does not return may be satisfied or may have given up. Verify resolution rather than inferring it from silence.

### No Follow-Up On Complex Questions

Complex questions that are not tracked to resolution leave patrons stranded. Keep them open and follow up.

### Unclear Expectations On Long-Running Questions

Without a stated timeline and follow-up plan, the patron does not know whether to wait or seek help elsewhere.

### Closing The Route Back

A closure that feels final discourages patrons from returning when the answer fell short. Explicitly invite return.

### Over-Following Simple Questions

Following up on every trivial lookup wastes effort and irritates patrons. Match follow-up depth to stakes.

### Undocumented Resolutions

Without a record of what was provided and what worked, returning patrons repeat their story and continuity is lost.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The transaction is treated as closed when the patron's need is met, not when the source is delivered.
- [ ] Resolution is verified before closure, calibrated to the stakes and the channel.
- [ ] Long-running questions have clear expectations for when an answer or update will come.
- [ ] Complex or referred questions are tracked to genuine resolution, not to first response.
- [ ] A clear route back is provided so patrons can return if the first answer fell short.
- [ ] Follow-up depth matches the stakes, with no over-following of simple questions or under-following of complex ones.
- [ ] Resolutions are documented in a way that supports continuity and respects patron privacy.
- [ ] No question is marked closed while uncertainty about whether the need was met remains.
- [ ] Patrons who did not return for a promised follow-up are proactively reached where possible.
- [ ] The librarian can distinguish a paused question, awaiting patron action, from a genuinely closed one.
