# Monitoring and Evaluating PDA Programs

A patron-driven acquisition program is not finished when it launches; that is when the real work begins. Without active monitoring, a PDA program silently drifts from its goal as vendor catalogs change, patron behavior shifts, and budget burns at unpredictable rates. Monitoring is what separates a program that captures genuine demand efficiently from one that exhausts its budget on low-value triggers, acquires out-of-scope titles, or serves only the patrons who learn how to game the system. The work demands regular attention to spend rate, trigger patterns, the actual titles being acquired, and honest comparison against the program's stated goals and against other acquisition methods. A PDA program that is not monitored and evaluated cannot be defended, improved, or responsibly continued.

## Core Rules

### Monitor spend rate against the budget continuously

The most immediate risk in any PDA program is budget overrun, because patron demand, not a selection calendar, drives spending. Track spend rate at least monthly, compare it against the annual cap prorated across the year, and watch for acceleration that signals the cap will be reached early. Investigate spikes, which may indicate a genuine surge in demand, a profile that is too broad, a vendor pool change, or patrons learning to trigger purchases. Maintain the auto-pause threshold and confirm it works, because a misconfigured pause lets spending continue past the cap. When spend rate diverges from plan, adjust the profile, triggers, or mediation rules mid-cycle rather than waiting for the year to collapse. Budget monitoring that happens only at year-end is too late to prevent damage.

### Analyze trigger patterns to detect drift and gaming

Trigger patterns reveal whether the program is behaving as designed. Examine triggers by subject, publisher, format, price, and patron segment, and look for anomalies. A sudden spike in one subject may mean a vendor pool change flooded the profile with new titles; a concentration of triggers from a single course or a few patrons may mean the program is subsidizing a narrow use case or being gamed; triggers on out-of-scope titles, such as textbooks or ephemera, mean the profile exclusions are leaking; and a shift in publisher distribution may signal a merger or catalog change that altered the pool. Compare patterns period over period to distinguish normal variation from drift. When anomalies appear, trace the cause and adjust the profile or triggers, because an unexamined pattern silently reshapes the collection.

### Audit the actual titles being acquired

Aggregate metrics hide what the program is really buying, so periodically review the actual titles triggered for purchase. Read through a sample of recent purchases and ask whether they fit the collection goal, whether they duplicate existing holdings, whether they represent the subjects and audiences the program was meant to serve, and whether any fall outside scope. Look for clusters of low-quality, self-published, or ephemeral titles that slipped through profile exclusions, and for high-cost titles that triggered automatically when they should have been mediated. This qualitative audit catches what metrics miss and informs profile tightening, trigger recalibration, or mediation changes. A program evaluated only through dashboards can look healthy while acquiring the wrong material.

### Evaluate cost-per-use honestly and comparatively

PDA's appeal is cost-per-use efficiency, but the metric must be computed correctly and compared fairly, or it misleads. Compute total program cost, including short-term-loan fees, purchased titles, platform fees, and staff management time, and divide by total uses. Compare cost-per-use against firm-ordered titles on the same metric, recognizing that firm orders carry selection labor that PDA shifts to configuration. Critically, examine post-purchase use, because a title triggered by two or three short-term loans and then never used again may look efficient at the trigger moment but prove a weak long-term acquisition. Track the gap between trigger-driven use and subsequent use, and use it to judge whether triggers are calibrated to lasting value or to momentary demand. Honest cost-per-use analysis often reveals that some segments of the program deliver strong value while others do not, which supports targeted adjustment rather than wholesale continuation or cancellation.

### Compare PDA outcomes against curated selection

PDA is one acquisition method among several, and its value is visible only in comparison. Compare the subjects, formats, publishers, and diversity of PDA-acquired titles against those acquired through firm orders, standing orders, and evidence-based rounds, and ask whether PDA is complementing or duplicating the curated collection. Compare use and cost-per-use across methods, and examine whether PDA is surfacing titles and demand that curated selection missed, which is its core justification, or merely acquiring what selectors would have bought anyway at higher cost. If PDA is not adding distinctive value beyond curated selection, the program's design or its budget share deserves questioning. Comparison prevents PDA from being judged on its own terms rather than against alternatives.

### Report outcomes to drive decisions

PDA reporting should drive decisions, not just describe activity. Report spend against budget, cost-per-use and post-purchase use, subject and format distribution, profile and trigger changes and their effects, titles or subjects that underperformed, and concrete recommendations for the next cycle. Present findings to the audiences who can act: selectors who should adjust profiles, leadership who control budget allocation, and governance who oversee the program. Tie each finding to a decision, whether continuing as-is, adjusting the profile, reallocating budget between PDA and curated selection, or ending the program. Reporting that does not change anything wastes the analytical effort and lets underperforming programs continue out of inertia.

### Decide explicitly whether to continue, adjust, or end

Every PDA program should face an explicit continue, adjust, or end decision on a defined cycle, rather than continuing by default. Evaluate the program against its stated goal and success metrics, weigh its value against the curated selection and alternative uses of the budget, and decide deliberately. Adjustment might mean tightening the profile, raising triggers, adding mediation, shifting budget between models, or changing vendors. Ending a program is legitimate when it no longer meets its goal, when value has declined, or when alternatives serve better, and a planned wind-down protects purchased content and patron access. The decision should be documented with the evidence and rationale, so it is defensible and informs future design. A program that continues only because no one evaluated it is a failure of management, not a sign of success.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is treating PDA as set-and-forget, so the program runs on its launch configuration long after vendor pools, patron behavior, and budgets have shifted. Another is monitoring spend only at year-end, when budget overrun has already occurred and cannot be reversed. Reading aggregate dashboards without examining trigger patterns misses drift, vendor pool changes, and gaming that silently reshape the collection. Evaluating only trigger-event use, without tracking post-purchase use, overstates efficiency and hides titles that triggered on momentary demand and then sat unused. Computing cost-per-use without including short-term-loan fees, platform fees, and staff time understates the true cost and makes PDA look better than it is. Judging PDA on its own terms, without comparing outcomes against curated selection, prevents the library from seeing whether the program adds distinctive value or merely duplicates what selectors would have bought anyway. Reporting activity rather than decisions lets underperforming programs continue out of inertia. Finally, continuing a program by default, without an explicit continue-adjust-end decision against its stated goal, is a failure of management that wastes budget and undermines the collection.

## Self-Check

- Did you monitor spend rate at least monthly against the annual cap, with auto-pause confirmed working and mid-cycle adjustment authority?
- Did you analyze trigger patterns by subject, publisher, format, price, and patron segment to detect drift, pool changes, and gaming?
- Did you audit a sample of actual titles acquired to confirm they fit the collection goal and scope, rather than relying on aggregate metrics alone?
- Did you compute cost-per-use honestly, including all fees and staff time, and compare it against firm-ordered titles on the same basis?
- Did you track post-purchase use, not just trigger events, to judge whether triggers calibrate to lasting value?
- Did you compare PDA outcomes against curated selection to determine whether the program adds distinctive value or duplicates other methods?
- Did you report outcomes tied to specific decisions, with recommendations, to the audiences who can act?
- Did you make an explicit continue, adjust, or end decision against the program's stated goal, documented with evidence and rationale?
