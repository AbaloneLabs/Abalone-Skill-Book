# Designing and Configuring PDA Programs

Designing a patron-driven acquisition program is where the library's real selection decisions get made, even though they look like technical settings. Every choice in the profile, the model, the triggers, the price caps, and the budget structure determines what patrons can ever trigger, and therefore what the collection becomes. A well-designed program captures genuine demand efficiently and predictably; a poorly designed one either blows the budget on casual browsing, fails to meet real demand, or quietly builds a collection that serves only the patrons who already know how to use it. The work is not set-and-forget configuration; it is a series of selection judgments embedded in technical parameters, and it must be approached with the same rigor and documentation as any curated collection decision.

## Core Rules

### Start from a collection goal, not a vendor product

The most common design failure is starting from what a vendor offers and working backward to a rationale. Begin instead with a concrete collection goal: what demand is the program meant to capture, what gaps does it address, what patron segments does it serve, and what does success look like. A program meant to give public-library patrons responsive access to new fiction has a fundamentally different design from one meant to let faculty trigger monographs for research, or one meant to build evidence for informed e-book selection. State the goal in writing, define the metrics that will show whether it is met, and let the goal drive every configuration choice. A program without a stated goal cannot be evaluated, because nothing defines success.

### Choose the model to match the goal

Several PDA models exist, and they serve different purposes; choosing the wrong model produces mismatched results no amount of tuning can fix. Demand-driven acquisition for e-books triggers a purchase after a threshold of short-term loans, suiting responsive breadth where demand is unpredictable. Evidence-based acquisition allows a period of use within a prepaid pool, then lets the library select which titles to keep, suiting informed selection backed by real use data. Print PDA triggers a physical purchase on patron request, suiting responsive public-library collection building. Patron-select or suggestion programs route patron requests through librarian evaluation, suiting community engagement with quality control. Approval-plan hybrids combine automatic delivery with patron-triggered elements. Match the model to the goal explicitly, and document why the chosen model fits, because the model constrains everything downstream.

### Build the profile as an explicit selection decision

The vendor profile, the pool of titles patrons can trigger, is the single most consequential design element, because patrons can only trigger purchases from what the profile includes. Treat profile construction as selection, not setup. Define the subject scope, the included and excluded publishers, the format limits, the language and audience parameters, the publication-date window, and the exclusion of textbooks, ephemera, or other out-of-scope material, and document the rationale for each. A profile that is too broad lets patrons trigger out-of-scope purchases; one that is too narrow fails to meet real demand. Review the profile against the collection-development policy to ensure it serves the same goals, and recognize that vendor pools drift as catalogs change, so the profile must be re-examined periodically, not just at launch.

### Calibrate triggers and thresholds to real demand

The trigger rule, the point at which use converts to a purchase, is where budget risk concentrates. Calibrate it deliberately using real or analogous use data rather than vendor defaults. Consider the number of short-term loans before auto-purchase, commonly two or three; the ratio of short-term-loan cost to purchase price, since fees can accumulate to near-purchase without ownership; the price cap above which titles are excluded from auto-purchase or routed to mediation; whether shallow browsing, table-of-contents views, or chapter-level use counts as a trigger; and the time window over which triggers accumulate. A trigger set too low converts casual browsing into purchases and exhausts the budget on low-value titles; a trigger set too high leaves real demand unmet. Build in mediation for high-cost or out-of-scope titles so a single trigger does not commit large funds automatically.

### Structure the budget for predictability and protection

PDA spending is inherently less predictable than firm-order spending, because patron demand, not a selection calendar, drives the spend rate, and this is the most common source of institutional anxiety about the model. Build predictability into the structure: set a firm annual budget cap; use a deposit account or prepayment so the library cannot overspend; configure auto-pause that suspends triggers when spending approaches the cap; reserve contingency for end-of-year spikes or unexpected demand; and track spend rate monthly with authority to adjust the profile mid-cycle. Decide in advance what happens when the cap is approached, whether triggers pause entirely, shift to short-term-loan-only, or move to mediated review, so the response is planned rather than improvised under pressure.

### Negotiate the contract for flexibility and portability

The vendor contract determines what the library can do with the program over its life, and terms negotiated at launch are difficult to change later. Negotiate for the right to adjust the profile and triggers without penalty, transparent and standardized usage reporting, clear short-term-loan pricing and caps, mediation rights for high-cost titles, perpetual access to purchased titles if the relationship ends, and portability of metadata and purchased content. Build in performance expectations and review points, so the library can renegotiate or exit if the program underperforms. Treat the contract as a collection-management instrument, not a procurement formality, and involve collection staff, not just purchasing, in its negotiation.

### Plan for launch, training, and communication

A well-configured program still fails if no one knows it exists or staff cannot support it. Plan the launch: ensure the discovery layer exposes PDA titles clearly so patrons can find and trigger them, train reference and instruction staff on how the program works and what patrons will see, prepare answers for predictable questions about why a title appeared or what a short-term-loan notice means, and communicate the program to the patron segments it is meant to serve. Decide how to handle the patron experience when a trigger is mediated or paused, so patrons are not left confused. Launch planning is part of design, because a program patrons cannot find or understand captures no demand.

### Build in the evaluation and review schedule at launch

Design the program from the outset to be evaluated and revised, not just launched. Schedule the first profile review within months of launch, when real use data reveals whether the configuration works; set recurring evaluation cycles, typically annual or biennial; define the metrics that will trigger a configuration change; and assign responsibility for monitoring and reporting. A program designed without a review schedule tends to run on its original configuration long after conditions change, silently drifting from its goal. Building the review into the design ensures the program stays aligned with its purpose.

## Common Traps

The most common trap is starting from a vendor product and working backward to a rationale, which produces a program configured for the vendor's convenience rather than the library's goal. Another is choosing a PDA model because it is fashionable or peer libraries use it, without checking whether it matches the collection goal, leading to mismatched results that no amount of tuning can fix. Treating the profile as a technical setting rather than a selection decision lets the vendor's catalog invisibly shape the collection, often in directions that diverge from the collection-development policy. Calibrating triggers to vendor defaults rather than real use data either converts casual browsing into purchases, exhausting the budget, or sets the threshold so high that demand goes unmet. Structuring the budget without a firm cap, auto-pause, or monthly monitoring allows unmonitored PDA to exhaust a year's budget in months, destabilizing planning. Accepting vendor contract terms without negotiating for profile flexibility, transparent reporting, mediation rights, and perpetual access locks the library into a configuration it cannot adjust and creates lock-in if the relationship ends. Launching the program without ensuring discovery-layer exposure, staff training, or patron communication means patrons never find or trigger the titles, and the program captures no demand. Finally, designing the program without a built-in evaluation and review schedule lets it run on its original configuration long after conditions, demand, or vendor pools have changed.

## Self-Check

- Did you start from a stated collection goal with defined success metrics, rather than from a vendor product?
- Did you choose the PDA model, DDA, evidence-based, print PDA, or hybrid, to match the goal, and document why it fits?
- Did you construct the profile as an explicit selection decision, with documented subject, publisher, format, language, and date parameters aligned to the collection-development policy?
- Did you calibrate triggers and thresholds using real or analogous use data, with price caps and mediation for high-cost titles?
- Did you structure the budget with a firm cap, deposit or prepayment, auto-pause, monthly monitoring, and a planned response when the cap approaches?
- Did you negotiate the contract for profile flexibility, transparent reporting, mediation rights, and perpetual access to purchased titles?
- Did you plan the launch, including discovery-layer exposure, staff training, patron communication, and handling of mediated or paused triggers?
- Did you build in an evaluation and review schedule, with defined metrics and assigned responsibility, starting within months of launch?
