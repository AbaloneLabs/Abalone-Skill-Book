# Classification Decisions

Most classification is routine, but a steady fraction of items present genuine decision problems: the subject spans two disciplines, the topic is new and not yet in the schedules, the standard placement conflicts with local collection needs, or two catalogers would reasonably choose different numbers. These decisions are where classification becomes judgment rather than lookup. The risk is that each difficult item gets handled ad hoc, fragmenting the collection and producing inconsistency that haunts later catalogers and browsers. Good classification decision-making uses a consistent framework, consults the right evidence, documents the reasoning, and recognizes when departure from the standard is justified and when it is not.

Use this skill when facing a difficult classification choice, resolving interdisciplinary or new-subject cases, deciding between Dewey and LCC or between options within a system, considering reclassification, or establishing local exception policy. The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing the first plausible number, fragmenting subjects through inconsistent choices, or departing from standards without justification.

## Core Rules

### Use A Consistent Decision Framework

Difficult classifications recur, and ad hoc choices fragment the collection. A consistent framework makes decisions repeatable and defensible.

A workable framework:

- identify the work's primary subject and discipline;
- list candidate numbers from the index or schedules;
- read scope and preference notes for each candidate;
- apply the system's preference rules;
- check the shelflist for existing arrangement;
- choose the number that best collocates and serves browsers;
- document the decision if it is non-obvious.

Apply the same framework to every difficult item. Consistency is the point.

### Determine The Primary Subject And Discipline

Most classification problems stem from unclear subject identification. Before choosing a number, define what the work is actually about and from what angle.

Ask:

- what is the central topic the work develops;
- what discipline or field does the author write from;
- who is the intended audience and what do they expect;
- if the work were split, which part is largest or most central;
- does the work treat the subject theoretically, applied, historically, or critically.

A work's primary subject is not always its title word. Read the preface, table of contents, and introduction to confirm the actual emphasis before classifying.

### Apply The System's Preference Rules

Both Dewey and LCC provide preference rules for cases where multiple numbers apply. Use them rather than personal preference.

Preference considerations:

- Dewey's preference order notes in the Manual;
- LCC's preference notes and cross-references in schedules;
- the rule of application, prefer the number for the aspect the work emphasizes;
- the rule of the first or most specific number when others are equal;
- the rule of the discipline that receives the most treatment.

Document which rule applied. Two catalogers using the same rule reach the same number.

### Check The Shelflist Before Deciding

The schedules give the theoretical right number; the shelflist shows where related works actually sit. Both matter.

Use the shelflist to:

- see where similar works are already classified;
- avoid creating a second number for an already-collocated subject;
- choose between near options based on existing arrangement;
- detect past inconsistencies that may need a policy decision;
- preserve browsing order for users.

A number correct in the abstract but isolated from related works fails the browsing test. Collocation with the existing collection often outweighs theoretical precision.

### Handle New And Emerging Subjects Deliberately

New subjects, technologies, social movements, and fields often appear before the schedules catch up. Classifying them requires judgment and documentation.

Approaches:

- find the closest existing number and classify by analogy;
- use a broader number with a local subject heading for precision;
- consult classification bulletins and other libraries' records for emerging consensus;
- propose or await schedule revisions for the topic;
- document the local decision so future items are handled consistently.

Do not invent numbers outside the system. Use the closest valid number and note the reasoning.

### Recognize When Local Departure Is Justified

Sometimes the standard placement serves the collection poorly, and a justified local departure improves access. Departure should be deliberate, documented, and exceptional.

Valid reasons for departure:

- the standard placement fragments a locally important collection;
- user browsing patterns strongly favor a different location;
- a special collection has its own arrangement scheme;
- the standard is outdated for a topic undergoing rapid change;
- consortial or shared cataloging agreements require alignment.

Invalid reasons for departure:

- personal preference for a different number;
- convenience or speed;
- misunderstanding the standard;
- avoiding the effort of building the correct number.

Document every departure with its rationale and review periodically.

### Decide Between Systems Deliberately

Some libraries use both Dewey and LCC, or transition between them. Choosing a system, or assigning an item to one, is a strategic decision.

Consider:

- the library's primary system and collection type;
- the item's fit, Dewey for browsable public collections, LCC for research depth;
- cataloging staff expertise and tools;
- interoperability with consortial partners;
- the cost and disruption of mixed or transitioning systems.

Do not assign systems item by item based on convenience. Follow a documented collection-level policy.

### Consider Reclassification Costs And Benefits

Reclassifying an existing collection or item is expensive and disruptive. Weigh benefits against costs.

Assess:

- the access problem the current classification creates;
- the scale of reclassification needed;
- the disruption to users and shelf order during the project;
- the gain in collocation and browsability;
- whether local subject headings or a finding aid could solve the problem more cheaply.

Reserve reclassification for cases where the access gain clearly justifies the cost. Partial reclassification can fragment more than it fixes.

### Document Decisions For Institutional Memory

Difficult classification decisions are reusable knowledge. Without documentation, the reasoning dies with the cataloger and inconsistency returns.

Document:

- the item or subject and the candidate numbers considered;
- the rule or evidence that decided the choice;
- any local departure and its rationale;
- cross-references to related decisions;
- the date and the standard edition used.

Maintain a decision log or local classification manual that new staff can follow.

### Balance Precision With Browsability

Classification serves both precise collocation and physical browsing. Over-precision scatters; under-precision buries. Balance them.

Practice:

- choose the most specific number that still collocates meaningfully;
- avoid numbers so narrow only one item sits there;
- avoid numbers so broad the topic is lost among neighbors;
- consider how a browser moves through the section;
- prefer the number that serves the expected user over theoretical exactness.

The shelf is a discovery tool. Classify for the user who browses, not only for the cataloger who records.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The First Plausible Number

The index or schedule often offers several candidates. Stopping at the first fragments interdisciplinary subjects.

### Ignoring Preference Rules

Both systems provide rules for multiple-number cases. Applying personal preference instead causes inconsistency.

### Skipping The Shelflist

A theoretically correct number may be isolated from related works. The shelflist reveals the real arrangement.

### Inventing Numbers For New Subjects

New topics need the closest valid number plus documentation, not invented notation outside the system.

### Departing From Standard Without Justification

Personal preference or convenience is not a valid reason to depart. Document a genuine access rationale.

### Ad Hoc Handling Of Difficult Items

Without a framework and decision log, recurring problems get different answers each time, fragmenting the collection.

### Reclassifying Without Cost-Benefit Analysis

Reclassification is expensive and disruptive. Weigh the access gain against the cost and disruption.

### Over-Precision That Scatters

Maximum specificity can isolate items. Match precision to collection size and browsing needs.

## Self-Check

- Is a consistent decision framework applied to every difficult classification?
- Is the work's primary subject and discipline identified from the content, not just the title?
- Are the system's preference rules consulted and applied, with the rule documented?
- Was the shelflist checked to ensure the number collocates with existing related works?
- For new or emerging subjects, is the closest valid number used with documented reasoning?
- Is any departure from standard practice justified by access needs and documented, not by convenience?
- Are system choices, Dewey versus LCC, made by collection policy, not item by item?
- Is reclassification weighed against cost, disruption, and alternative solutions?
- Are difficult decisions documented in a decision log or local manual for institutional memory?
- Does the chosen number balance precision with browsability for the actual user?
