# Library Of Congress Application

Library of Congress Classification is the dominant system in academic and research libraries. It is a system of twenty-one classes expressed in schedules that combine letters and numbers, and it is far more detailed and less uniformly structured than Dewey. Assigning an LCC number requires navigating schedules that differ in organization from class to class, applying internal tables that vary by schedule, and constructing a complete call number with a Cutter that collocates by author. The detail that makes LCC powerful for large research collections also makes it easy to misapply. A wrong schedule interpretation, a misread table, or a poorly constructed Cutter hides books and fragments collections. Good LCC application reads each schedule on its own terms, builds numbers step by step, and keeps the whole call number consistent and browsable.

Use this skill when assigning or building LCC numbers, constructing Cutter numbers, handling complex subjects across schedules, or establishing local LCC practice. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating LCC as uniform across classes, misapplying schedule-specific tables, building invalid Cutters, or producing call numbers that fail to collocate.

## Core Rules

### Treat Each Schedule As Its Own System

Unlike Dewey, LCC schedules are not uniform. Each class, and often each schedule within a class, has its own organization, tables, and conventions. A practice correct in one schedule may be wrong in another.

Implications:

- read the introduction and tables of the specific schedule you are using;
- note where internal tables differ from the general tables;
- check whether the schedule uses its own subdivision patterns;
- be aware that some schedules are older and less current than others;
- verify that the schedule edition is current, since LCC is revised schedule by schedule.

Do not assume that because you know class H, you know class K or class N. Open the specific schedule and follow its structure.

### Build The Number Step By Step

LCC numbers are built by combining the class letter, the subclass letters, the topic number from the schedule, and any subdivisions from internal or general tables. Each step depends on the previous.

Build sequence:

- identify the correct class and subclass from the schedule's scope;
- find the topic number within the subclass;
- apply any internal table subdivisions as the schedule instructs;
- apply general tables, such as the form or geography tables, where permitted;
- check for special instructions at the number that modify further building.

Skipping a step or applying a table out of order produces an invalid number. Work through the schedule linearly.

### Use The Correct Schedule For The Subject

LCC's detail means many subjects appear in more than one place depending on angle. A topic like "education" appears in class L, but educational aspects of medicine, law, or business appear in those classes.

Choose by:

- reading the schedule scope notes and class-here notes;
- checking the LC classification web or printed index for candidate classes;
- determining the work's primary discipline and audience;
- consulting cross-references between schedules;
- following preference notes when two schedules apply.

A medical education book for physicians is class R, while a general education book is class L. Match the schedule to the work's actual treatment.

### Apply Internal And General Tables Correctly

LCC has both general tables, used across schedules, and internal tables specific to a schedule or subclass. Confusing them is a common error.

General tables include:

- form subdivisions, for format and arrangement;
- general geographic and historical subdivisions;
- topics and subjects of general application.

Internal tables include:

- subdivisions specific to a subclass, such as those in K for law jurisdictions;
- tables defined within a schedule for its own topics.

Read the schedule to see which tables apply. A subdivision valid in one schedule may be invalid in another. When a schedule says "add as instructed under Table X," follow that table exactly.

### Construct The Cutter Number To Collocate

The Cutter number, derived from the author's name or the title, is what collocates works and arranges them alphabetically on the shelf within a class. A well-built Cutter puts the author's works together and in order.

Cutter construction:

- use the Cutter-Sanborn or LC Cutter table for the alphabetic-numeric combination;
- base the Cutter on the main entry, author name, or title if no author;
- add a work mark or date to distinguish titles by the same author;
- use a second Cutter for geographic or topical subdivision when the schedule requires;
- keep Cutter numbers consistent with existing shelflist to avoid conflicts.

Check the shelflist for the same class to avoid assigning a Cutter that conflicts with or duplicates an existing author. The Cutter must fit the collection's actual arrangement.

### Add The Date And Work Marks

After the class number and Cutter, the date and work marks complete the call number and distinguish editions and titles.

Add:

- the date of publication to distinguish editions;
- a work mark, often the first letter of the title, to distinguish titles by the same author;
- volume or copy numbers as needed;
- accession or local marks per local policy.

The date is essential for collocating editions. Without it, a browser cannot tell the 1990 edition from the 2010 revision.

### Handle Interdisciplinary And Complex Subjects

LCC handles interdisciplinary subjects through cross-references and preference notes, but the decision still requires judgment.

Approaches:

- use the schedule's preference notes when two classes apply;
- prefer the class for the work's primary discipline;
- consult the LC index and cross-references;
- for literature, follow the language and period tables in class P carefully;
- for law, follow the K schedules' jurisdictional structure precisely.

Document complex decisions in local practice. Interdisciplinary works recur, and consistent classification keeps them findable.

### Maintain Consistency With The Shelflist

The shelflist, the record of what numbers are already in use, is essential for LCC consistency. A number that looks right in the schedule may conflict with an existing arrangement.

Use the shelflist to:

- check that the Cutter does not duplicate or conflict;
- verify the number fits the existing arrangement of the topic;
- decide between near options based on where related works already sit;
- maintain alphabetical and chronological order on the shelf.

Without shelflist consultation, call numbers drift and collocation breaks.

### Keep Local Practice Documented

LCC involves many local decisions: Cutter table choice, date format, fiction treatment, oversized designation, and special collection exceptions. Document these.

Maintain:

- the Cutter table and conventions in use;
- date and work mark policies;
- treatment of multi-volume and series;
- local additions and exceptions;
- the schedule editions in use and update policy.

Documented local practice keeps a large collection consistent across catalogers and decades.

### Match Classification Depth To Collection Size

LCC's detail suits large collections, but small collections may not need full specificity. Adjust depth to the collection.

Consider:

- large research collections benefit from full schedule specificity;
- smaller academic collections may truncate or simplify;
- special collections may use local schemes better than LCC;
- mixed depth within a collection frustrates browsing.

Set a local policy for classification depth by collection type and apply it consistently.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Schedules As Uniform

Each LCC schedule has its own structure and tables. Practices correct in one may be wrong in another. Read each schedule.

### Misapplying Internal Tables

Internal tables are schedule-specific. Applying a general table where an internal one is required, or vice versa, builds invalid numbers.

### Weak Or Conflicting Cutter Numbers

A Cutter that duplicates an existing author or ignores the shelflist fragments the collection. Build Cutters against the shelflist.

### Missing The Date Or Work Mark

Without the date, editions collide; without the work mark, titles by the same author collide. Complete the call number.

### Choosing The Obvious Class Without Scope Notes

The first class that comes to mind may not be right. Read scope notes and cross-references.

### Ignoring The Shelflist

A schedule-correct number may conflict with existing arrangement. Always check the shelflist.

### Inconsistent Local Practice

Undocumented decisions on Cutters, dates, and exceptions cause drift. Maintain a local manual.

### Over-Specific Numbers In Small Collections

Full LCC specificity in a small collection scatters books. Match depth to collection size.

## Self-Check

- Is the specific schedule being used read on its own terms, with its own tables and conventions?
- Is the number built step by step, class, subclass, topic, subdivisions, in the correct order?
- Is the correct schedule chosen based on scope notes and the work's primary discipline?
- Are internal and general tables applied exactly as the schedule instructs?
- Is the Cutter number built from the correct table and checked against the shelflist?
- Are date and work marks added to distinguish editions and titles?
- Are interdisciplinary subjects resolved using preference notes and cross-references?
- Was the shelflist consulted to ensure the number fits existing arrangement?
- Is local LCC practice documented for Cutters, dates, depth, and exceptions?
- Is classification depth matched to the collection's size and browsing needs?
