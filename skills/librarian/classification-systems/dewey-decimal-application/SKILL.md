# Dewey Decimal Application

Dewey Decimal Classification is the most widely used library classification system in the world, especially in public and school libraries. Assigning a Dewey number looks like looking up a topic and copying a number, but real Dewey work involves building numbers from schedules and tables, choosing between several valid options for interdisciplinary topics, handling the system's built-in biases and gaps, and making decisions that affect where a book sits on the shelf and whether browsers find it. A poorly chosen or built number hides a book in the wrong neighborhood, fragments a subject across the collection, or perpetuates outdated categorizations. Good Dewey application follows the schedules and tables precisely, documents local practice, and recognizes the system's limits.

Use this skill when assigning or building Dewey numbers, choosing between classification options, handling complex subjects, or establishing local Dewey practice. The goal is to prevent the agent from guessing numbers, misapplying tables, fragmenting subjects, or ignoring the cultural biases embedded in the system.

## Core Rules

### Build Numbers From Schedules And Tables, Not From Memory

Dewey numbers are constructed by combining a base number from the schedules with add instructions and numbers from the seven tables. Building from memory produces errors that compound across a collection.

The build process:

- find the closest base number in the schedules for the subject;
- read the notes and add instructions at that number and its hierarchy;
- apply table numbers only where the schedule explicitly permits;
- follow the order of operations, schedule first, then tables in sequence;
- verify the built number against the schedule's examples and scope notes.

Each table, geographic areas, subdivisions, literature subdivisions, and so on, has specific application rules. Applying a table where not permitted creates an invalid number that may not sort correctly or may confuse users browsing.

### Choose The Correct Discipline First

Dewey organizes knowledge into ten main classes, and the first decision is which discipline or field the subject belongs to. A topic like "water" appears in many places depending on the angle.

Examples:

- water as a chemical substance, the 540s;
- water as a natural resource or environmental issue, the 333s or 363s;
- water treatment and engineering, the 628s;
- water in religion or ritual, the 200s;
- water law, the 340s.

Choose the discipline that reflects the book's actual treatment, not the most obvious word. Read the scope notes and class-here notes to confirm the right neighborhood before building the specific number.

### Use The Manual And Scope Notes For Options

Many subjects have more than one valid Dewey number, and the schedules provide options, often with preference notes that tell you which to choose when multiple apply.

Consult:

- the Manual notes for guidance on complex and interdisciplinary topics;
- scope notes that define what a number covers and excludes;
- class-elsewhere notes that redirect overlapping topics;
- preference tables that order options when more than one applies;
- the Relative Index to find candidate numbers, then verify in the schedules.

Do not stop at the first number the index suggests. The index lists options; the schedules and notes decide among them.

### Apply Tables Only Where Permitted

The seven Dewey tables, standard subdivisions, geographic areas, subdivisions for individual topics, and others, are powerful but restricted. Each table applies only where a schedule add note explicitly allows it.

Common errors:

- adding standard subdivisions where not permitted;
- applying geographic Table 2 to a number that has its own built-in geography;
- using Table 3 literature subdivisions for non-literature numbers;
- adding time period subdivisions from Table 1 without a schedule instruction.

Read the add notes carefully. If a schedule says add "as instructed under X," go to X and follow its instructions. Mechanical table application without schedule permission produces invalid numbers.

### Handle Interdisciplinary Topics With Preference Rules

Interdisciplinary topics, those spanning multiple disciplines, are common and are a major source of inconsistency. Dewey provides preference rules to resolve them.

Approaches:

- use the preference order given in the Manual for the topic;
- prefer the discipline that receives the most space in the work;
- prefer the number that matches the work's stated purpose or audience;
- for truly balanced works, follow the standard preference order, such as 000 over 100 over 200, unless overridden.

Document the choice in local practice. Interdisciplinary topics recur, and consistent handling keeps related works together.

### Recognize And Manage System Bias

Dewey reflects the worldview and priorities of its late-nineteenth-century origins and its subsequent revisions. Some areas carry biases that affect where and how subjects are classified.

Examples:

- religion heavily weighted toward Christianity, with other faiths in narrower ranges;
- language and literature weighted toward Western European languages;
- some cultural and indigenous topics placed in history or social problems rather than their own fields;
- geographic treatment that centers certain regions.

Manage bias by:

- following current edition revisions that address known inequities;
- consulting alternative classifications or local schemes for collections where Dewey fits poorly;
- documenting local decisions to place materials more appropriately;
- being aware that users from underrepresented groups may find their subjects poorly served.

Bias does not excuse arbitrary reclassification, but awareness informs local practice and user help.

### Keep Local Practice Consistent And Documented

Dewey application involves many judgment calls, and consistency depends on documented local practice. Without it, each cataloger fragments the collection differently.

Document:

- preferred numbers for recurring local-interest topics;
- decisions on options where the schedules offer alternatives;
- the edition of Dewey in use and the policy for edition updates;
- treatment of fiction, biography, and other commonly localized areas;
- shortened or expanded number policies for specific collections;
- exceptions for special collections.

A local classification manual turns individual judgments into consistent shelving.

### Match Number Length To Collection Needs

Full Dewey numbers can be very long, but most collections do not need maximum specificity. Number length should match browsing and shelving needs.

Considerations:

- public and school libraries often use shorter numbers for browsability;
- academic and special collections may need longer numbers for precision;
- overly long numbers crowd shelves and confuse browsers;
- overly short numbers gather unrelated books together.

Set a local policy for typical number length by collection, and truncate or expand consistently. Mixed length within a collection frustrates browsing.

### Classify For The User And The Shelf

Classification serves two masters: precise subject collocation and physical browsability. Balance both.

Practice:

- choose the number that puts the book where a browser would expect it;
- prefer the number that collocates related works already in the collection;
- avoid numbers so specific that only one book sits there;
- avoid numbers so broad that the topic is buried among unrelated works.

The shelf is a discovery interface. Classify as if helping a browser, not as if maximizing theoretical precision.

## Common Traps

### Guessing Numbers From The Index

The Relative Index suggests options; the schedules decide. Always verify in the schedules and read the notes.

### Applying Tables Without Schedule Permission

Tables are powerful but restricted. Adding a table subdivision where not permitted creates an invalid number.

### Choosing The Obvious Word Over The Right Discipline

"Water" is not one number. Choose the discipline that matches the book's actual treatment.

### Ignoring Preference Notes

When multiple numbers apply, the schedules provide preference rules. Ignoring them fragments interdisciplinary topics.

### Maximum Specificity Everywhere

Over-long numbers crowd shelves and reduce browsability. Match number length to collection needs.

### Inconsistent Local Practice

Without documented decisions, recurring topics get different numbers from different catalogers, fragmenting the collection.

### Ignoring System Bias

Dewey's biases affect underrepresented subjects. Awareness informs better local decisions and user service.

### Copying Vendor Numbers Without Review

Vendor-supplied numbers may use a different edition or local practice. Verify against your edition and local policy.

## Self-Check

- Is the number built from the schedules and tables following add instructions, not from memory?
- Is the correct discipline chosen based on the work's actual treatment, not the obvious word?
- Were the Manual, scope notes, and preference rules consulted for interdisciplinary or complex topics?
- Are table subdivisions applied only where the schedule explicitly permits?
- Is the system's cultural bias recognized and managed through current editions and local practice?
- Is local classification practice documented for consistency across staff and time?
- Is the number length matched to the collection's browsing and shelving needs?
- Does the number place the book where a browser would expect to find it?
- Are recurring local-interest topics assigned consistent numbers across the collection?
- Was a vendor-supplied number verified against the local edition and policy before use?
