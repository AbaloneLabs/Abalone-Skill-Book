# Born-Digital Access And Management

Born-digital materials, records created in digital form rather than digitized from physical originals, are now a central and growing part of what libraries and archives must manage. Email archives, office documents, websites, social media, datasets, software, and digital institutional records carry unique challenges that neither traditional cataloging nor digitization workflows were built to handle. A born-digital item depends on software, operating systems, and hardware that may no longer exist; it may contain personal, confidential, or legally restricted information embedded in ways that are hard to screen; and providing meaningful access often requires emulation, migration, or specialized viewers rather than simply displaying a file. Treating born-digital materials like scanned surrogates, or deferring their management until "later," guarantees silent loss: formats become unreadable, media degrade, and the contextual information needed to interpret files disappears. Managing born-digital materials is a discipline of early intervention, format and risk awareness, privacy review, and deliberate choices about preservation and access strategies.

Use this skill when managing, describing, or providing access to born-digital materials, establishing ingest and appraisal workflows, handling format obsolescence, and balancing preservation with access and privacy. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating born-digital materials like digitized surrogates, from deferring ingest until formats are unreadable, from neglecting privacy and restriction review, or from assuming that bit-level preservation alone constitutes access.

## Core Rules

### Intervene Early Before Media And Context Degrade

Born-digital materials degrade faster than paper. Early intervention is essential.

Intervene early by:

- capturing or transferring files from original media before it fails;
- documenting the context of creation (software, systems, creators, dates);
- capturing associated metadata and folder structures as evidence;
- avoiding actions that alter or overwrite original files;
- establishing bit-level preservation copies immediately.

Deferred intervention leads to unreadable media and lost context that cannot be recovered.

### Appraise And Select With Digital Realities In Mind

Born-digital appraisal must account for volume, format viability, and access cost, not only content value.

Appraise by considering:

- the enduring value of the content;
- the volume and the feasibility of managing it;
- the format viability and migration or emulation needs;
- the presence of confidential, personal, or legally restricted information;
- the cost and expertise required for preservation and access;
- the documentation and context available to interpret the files.

Appraisal that ignores digital realities commits the institution to unsustainable burdens.

### Capture And Document Provenance And Context

Born-digital files are meaningless without context. Capture and document provenance thoroughly.

Document:

- the creator, source, and transfer history of the materials;
- the original hardware, operating system, and software environment;
- the folder structure, file naming, and organizational logic;
- dates of creation, modification, and transfer;
- any access restrictions or donor conditions;
- the chain of custody and any processing applied.

Provenance documentation is what allows future users to interpret the files.

### Assess Format Viability And Plan For Obsolescence

Formats become obsolete. Assess viability and plan for migration or emulation.

Assess and plan:

- identify formats and their openness, documentation, and adoption;
- prioritize migration of at-risk formats to sustainable preservation formats;
- plan for emulation where original experience or software dependencies matter;
- document migration and emulation decisions and their effects;
- monitor format registries for emerging obsolescence risks.

Ignoring format viability strands files in formats no one can open.

### Establish Bit-Level Preservation And Fixity

Before access, ensure bit-level preservation. Protect the authenticity of the files.

Establish:

- secure, redundant storage of bit-level preservation copies;
- fixity values (checksums) at ingest and scheduled checks;
- write-protected preservation storage to prevent alteration;
- documented policies for remediation when fixity fails;
- environmental controls appropriate for any original media retained.

Bit-level preservation is the foundation; without it, nothing else is possible.

### Conduct Privacy And Restriction Review

Born-digital materials frequently contain personal, confidential, or legally restricted information. Review before access.

Review for:

- personal and sensitive data (PII, health, financial, credentials);
- confidential institutional or third-party information;
- legal restrictions (records laws, donor agreements, embargoes);
- third-party copyrighted content embedded in files;
- the difficulty of screening large or complex file sets.

Develop access strategies that respect restrictions, including redaction, mediated access, or embargo.

### Choose Access Strategies Deliberately

Access to born-digital materials is not automatic. Choose strategies deliberately.

Choose among:

- direct file delivery for open, stable formats;
- format migration to accessible derivatives;
- emulation to recreate the original environment and experience;
- mediated or reading-room access for restricted or complex materials;
- metadata-only access where files cannot be directly delivered;
- access copies redacted of restricted content.

Match the access strategy to the format, restrictions, and user needs.

### Describe Born-Digital Materials Appropriately

Born-digital description must capture the digital nature of the materials. Adapt descriptive practice.

Describe:

- the extent in meaningful digital terms (file counts, sizes, formats);
- the software, hardware, and environment required for access;
- access conditions, restrictions, and the access strategy provided;
- the processing and any migrations applied;
- relationships between files, folders, and higher-level aggregations;
- provenance and transfer history.

Description that ignores the digital nature misleads users about what they can access and how.

### Balance Preservation, Access, And Privacy

Born-digital management requires balancing three sometimes-competing obligations.

Balance:

- preservation (authentic, complete retention) against;
- access (usable, meaningful delivery) against;
- privacy and legal restrictions (protection of sensitive content).

There is no universal resolution; each collection requires deliberate, documented choices.

### Build Sustainable Workflows And Expertise

Born-digital management is ongoing, not a one-time project. Build sustainable workflows.

Build:

- repeatable ingest, appraisal, and processing workflows;
- staff expertise in digital forensics tools and practices;
- collaboration with IT, records management, and legal stakeholders;
- documentation of tools, decisions, and procedures;
- capacity to handle the ongoing influx of born-digital materials.

Unsustainable workflows leave born-digital materials unmanaged and at risk.

## Common Traps

### Treating Born-Digital Materials Like Digitized Surrogates

Born-digital materials have distinct preservation, access, and privacy needs.

### Deferring Ingest Until Media Fails

Early intervention is essential. Deferred ingest leads to silent loss.

### Ignoring Format Obsolescence

Formats become unreadable. Assess viability and plan migration or emulation.

### Assuming Bit-Level Preservation Equals Access

Preserving bits is foundational but does not provide usable access.

### Neglecting Privacy And Restriction Review

Born-digital materials often contain sensitive information. Review before access.

### Losing Context And Provenance

Files without context are meaningless. Document creation environment and transfer history.

### Choosing Access Strategies By Default

Access strategy should match format, restrictions, and needs, not be automatic.

### Unsustainable Or Ad Hoc Workflows

Born-digital management is ongoing. Build repeatable workflows and staff expertise.

## Self-Check

- Did you intervene early to capture files and context before media and systems degrade?
- Did you appraise with digital realities (volume, format viability, access cost, restrictions) in mind?
- Did you capture and document provenance, environment, and transfer history?
- Did you assess format viability and plan for migration or emulation of at-risk formats?
- Did you establish bit-level preservation with fixity values and scheduled checks?
- Did you conduct privacy and restriction review before providing access?
- Did you choose access strategies (delivery, migration, emulation, mediated access) deliberately?
- Did you describe the digital nature of the materials, including extent, environment, and access conditions?
- Did you balance preservation, access, and privacy obligations with documented choices?
- Did you build sustainable workflows, expertise, and cross-stakeholder collaboration?
