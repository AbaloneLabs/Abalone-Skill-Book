# Digital Collections Outreach And Use

Building a digital collection is not the same as having it used. A collection can be meticulously digitized, beautifully described, and technically flawless, and still sit largely unused if no one knows it exists, no one understands why it matters, and no one has been invited to use it in their work. Outreach is what converts a digital collection from an asset into an impact. It connects collections to the audiences who need them: researchers, teachers, students, community members, and the broader public. It also closes the feedback loop, revealing how collections are actually used, which in turn informs what to digitize next, how to describe it, and where to invest. Treating outreach as an optional add-on, or as mere marketing, leaves high-value collections invisible and squanders the investment made in creating them. Outreach is a strategic, evaluative activity that requires understanding audiences, building relationships, teaching use, handling reuse permissions, and measuring impact.

Use this skill when planning outreach for digital collections, designing instruction and engagement around digital primary sources, handling reuse and permission communications, and measuring use and impact. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating outreach as optional or as one-way promotion, from neglecting teaching and community engagement, from mishandling reuse inquiries, or from failing to measure whether the collection is actually reaching its audiences.

## Core Rules

### Know Your Audiences And Their Needs

Outreach begins with understanding who the collection is for and what they need. Different audiences require different approaches.

Identify and understand:

- **Researchers:** what disciplines and questions does the collection serve?
- **Teachers and students:** what curricular and instructional uses does it support?
- **Community members:** what local, cultural, or personal connections exist?
- **The broader public:** what stories and topics have broad appeal?
- **Creative and commercial users:** what reuse opportunities exist within rights?

Tailor outreach to each audience's language, channels, and motivations rather than broadcasting uniformly.

### Connect Collections To Research And Teaching

Research and teaching are the highest-value uses of most digital collections. Build active connections.

Connect by:

- identifying courses and curricula the collection supports;
- creating research guides and course-specific materials;
- reaching out to faculty and instructors with relevant collections;
- developing primary source assignments and learning activities;
- showcasing research and teaching that uses the collection.

Passive availability rarely reaches teachers and researchers; active connection does.

### Tell The Stories In The Collection

Facts about a collection rarely motivate use; stories do. Surface the human and historical narratives.

Tell stories by:

- highlighting individual items, people, and events with broad appeal;
- connecting collection materials to anniversaries, current events, and public conversations;
- creating exhibits, blog posts, and social media features;
- using narrative to make the collection approachable and meaningful;
- respecting accuracy and context while engaging audiences.

Storytelling bridges the gap between scholarly collections and public engagement.

### Build Community Relationships

Many collections matter deeply to specific communities. Build and sustain relationships with them.

Build relationships by:

- identifying communities connected to the collection's content;
- consulting communities on materials about them;
- inviting community knowledge and contributions;
- co-creating events, exhibits, or descriptions with community partners;
- respecting cultural protocols and sensitivities.

Community relationships transform a collection from a static holding into a living resource.

### Handle Reuse And Permissions Clearly

Users who want to reuse collection materials need clear guidance and responsive support. Provide it.

Handle reuse by:

- making rights and licensing terms clear and discoverable (see the related skill on rights metadata);
- providing contact channels for permission inquiries;
- responding promptly and helpfully to reuse requests;
- explaining what users may do under standard statements and licenses;
- documenting permissions granted for institutional records.

Friction in reuse communications suppresses lawful use and frustrates users.

### Use Multiple Channels Strategically

No single channel reaches all audiences. Use multiple channels strategically.

Use channels such as:

- the institution's website, discovery platform, and collection pages;
- social media, blogs, and newsletters;
- conferences, professional networks, and disciplinary lists;
- community events, exhibits, and partnerships;
- aggregators, indexes, and cross-institutional platforms.

Match channels to audiences and coordinate messaging across them.

### Teach With Digital Primary Sources

Instruction is a powerful form of outreach. Teach users how to work with digital primary sources.

Teach:

- how to find and navigate digital collections;
- how to read and analyze primary sources critically;
- how to cite and properly attribute digital materials;
- how to evaluate rights and reuse terms;
- how to integrate primary sources into research and assignments.

Teaching use builds lasting capacity and deepens engagement.

### Measure Use And Impact

Outreach should be evaluated. Measure use and impact to improve and to justify investment.

Measure:

- quantitative use metrics (page views, downloads, searches, referrals);
- qualitative impact (citations, research outputs, class use, media coverage);
- audience reach and growth across channels;
- reuse and rights inquiries as indicators of active engagement;
- feedback from users and community partners.

Measurement closes the loop between outreach and collection development decisions.

### Close The Feedback Loop To Collection Decisions

Outreach insights should inform collection development. Close the feedback loop.

Feed back by:

- prioritizing digitization of high-demand and high-impact materials;
- improving description and discovery where users struggle;
- addressing gaps in coverage that audiences need;
- investing in the channels and formats that reach audiences;
- using impact evidence to advocate for resources.

Outreach without feedback to collection decisions misses its strategic value.

### Plan For Sustainable Outreach

Outreach is ongoing, not one-time. Plan for sustainability.

Plan for:

- regular programming rather than one-off launches;
- staff time and expertise for engagement and instruction;
- maintaining relationships over time;
- updating outreach as collections and audiences evolve;
- integrating outreach into staff roles and workflows.

Unsustained outreach produces spikes of attention followed by neglect.

## Common Traps

### Treating Outreach As Optional Or As Marketing

Outreach converts assets into impact. It is strategic, not decorative.

### Broadcasting Uniformly Instead Of Targeting Audiences

Different audiences need different approaches. Tailor outreach to each.

### Passive Availability Without Active Connection

Researchers and teachers rarely find collections on their own. Build active connections.

### Mishandling Reuse And Permission Inquiries

Friction and slow responses suppress lawful use. Provide clear, responsive support.

### Neglecting Community Relationships

Communities connected to collection content are key audiences and partners.

### Using One Channel

No single channel reaches everyone. Use multiple channels strategically.

### Forgetting To Teach Use

Users need skills to work with primary sources. Teach finding, analysis, citation, and rights.

### Not Measuring Use And Impact

Without measurement, outreach cannot improve or justify investment.

### No Feedback Loop To Collection Decisions

Outreach insights should inform what to digitize and describe next.

## Self-Check

- Did you identify the collection's audiences and their distinct needs?
- Did you build active connections to research and teaching, not just passive availability?
- Did you surface the stories and narratives that make the collection approachable?
- Did you build and sustain relationships with connected communities, respecting protocols?
- Did you make reuse terms clear and respond helpfully to permission inquiries?
- Did you use multiple channels matched to your audiences?
- Did you teach users how to find, analyze, cite, and reuse digital primary sources?
- Did you measure both quantitative use and qualitative impact?
- Did you close the feedback loop to inform collection development decisions?
- Did you plan for sustainable, ongoing outreach integrated into staff roles?
