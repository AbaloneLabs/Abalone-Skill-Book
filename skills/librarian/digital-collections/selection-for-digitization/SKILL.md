# Selection For Digitization

Digitization is not preservation by default, and it is not appropriate for every item. Selecting what to digitize is a distinct judgment from selecting what to acquire or keep. A digitization project consumes scarce staff time, equipment, storage, and metadata labor, and once an item is digitized the institution takes on a permanent obligation to manage the digital object. Selecting the wrong materials wastes resources, creates access risks through copyright violations, or damages fragile originals through careless handling. Good selection for digitization weighs intellectual value, user demand, physical condition, copyright status, and the institution's capacity to sustain the result. It is a strategic decision, not a scanning queue.

Use this skill when planning a digitization project, selecting candidates from a collection, assessing suitability for digitization, or prioritizing among competing projects. The goal is to prevent the agent from digitizing everything indiscriminately, ignoring copyright and condition, underestimating sustainability cost, or choosing items by personal interest rather than strategic value.

## Core Rules

### Digitize For A Purpose, Not By Default

Digitization should serve a defined purpose. Digitizing "because we can" or "to preserve everything" produces unfocused projects and unsustainable backlogs. Name the purpose first.

Common purposes:

- access, to make rare or fragile materials widely available;
- research value, to enable new scholarship on high-demand materials;
- preservation, to reduce handling of fragile originals through use copies;
- exhibition or teaching, to support specific programs;
- grant or consortial mandates, to contribute to a shared digital collection;
- community or cultural heritage, to preserve and share local history.

The purpose determines selection criteria, metadata depth, and sustainability commitments. State it before selecting.

### Apply Explicit Selection Criteria

Selection criteria translate purpose into decisions. Without explicit criteria, selection drifts toward whatever is easy or interesting to the selector.

Criteria to define and rank:

- intellectual or research value of the content;
- user demand, demonstrated or anticipated;
- uniqueness or rarity, is the item available elsewhere digitally;
- physical condition, can it survive digitization and does digitization aid preservation;
- copyright and rights status, can it be made accessible;
- representativeness, does it fill a gap or represent an underrepresented voice;
- relationship to existing digital collections, does it build coherence;
- technical feasibility, size, format, special equipment needs;
- metadata availability, can it be described adequately;
- sustainability cost, storage, management, preservation burden.

Rank these for the project. A demand-driven access project ranks user demand high; a preservation project ranks condition and fragility high.

### Assess Copyright Before Committing

Copyright is the most common reason digitization projects stall or fail. Assessing rights before selection prevents investing labor in items that cannot be made accessible.

Assess:

- the date and jurisdiction of the work;
- whether it is in the public domain;
- whether the institution holds rights or has donor permission;
- whether fair use or a library exception applies;
- the status of embedded third-party content, photographs, letters, music;
- orphan works risk when the rights holder cannot be found.

Document the rights determination for each candidate. Digitizing first and resolving rights later creates risk and wasted effort.

### Evaluate Physical Condition Honestly

Digitization involves handling, sometimes intensive handling, and some materials cannot survive it without conservation first. Condition also affects image quality and cost.

Evaluate:

- brittleness, fragility, broken bindings;
- tight bindings that cannot open flat;
- surface dirt, mold, or pest damage;
- foldouts, inserts, or formats needing special cradles;
- light sensitivity of photographs or inks;
- size and weight affecting equipment needs;
- need for conservation treatment before digitization.

Factor conservation cost and time into the selection decision. A high-value but fragile item may need to wait for treatment, or may justify the cost because digitization reduces future handling.

### Prioritize Uniqueness And Gap-Filling

Digitization delivers the most value when it makes unique or rare materials accessible, or when it fills gaps in the digital record. Duplicating what is already freely available digitally wastes resources.

Prioritize:

- unique holdings not digitized elsewhere;
- local history, institutional records, and community materials;
- materials representing underrepresented voices and topics;
- items that complete or contextualize existing digital collections;
- high-demand materials that researchers repeatedly request.

Check what is already available digitally before committing. Consortial and national databases help avoid redundant digitization.

### Consider The Full Lifecycle Cost

The cost of digitization is not just scanning. The dominant costs are often metadata creation, quality control, storage, and long-term preservation. Select with the full lifecycle in mind.

Cost components:

- staff time for selection, preparation, and conservation;
- scanning or photography time and equipment;
- metadata creation and authority work;
- quality control and image review;
- storage, both access and archival copies;
- preservation actions over time, fixity, migration;
- platform hosting and maintenance.

A project that budgets only scanning will run out of money at metadata. Estimate the full cost before selecting the scope.

### Balance Access And Preservation Goals

Digitization can serve access, preservation, or both, but the two have different requirements. Conflating them produces compromises that serve neither well.

Distinguish:

- access copies, optimized for web delivery, compressed, watermarked if needed;
- preservation masters, uncompressed or lossless, full resolution, with full metadata;
- the relationship between them, derivatives generated from masters.

Select items with both goals in mind, but be clear which is primary. An access-driven project may accept lower-resolution masters; a preservation project must capture at archival quality.

### Build Coherent Collections, Not Random Items

A digitization project is more valuable when it builds a coherent collection than when it digitizes scattered items. Coherence improves discovery, scholarship, and storytelling.

Build coherence by:

- digitizing complete series or collections rather than cherry-picked items;
- providing collection-level context and description;
- linking related items across the digital collection;
- aligning with thematic or grant-driven initiatives;
- consulting users and scholars about what constitutes a useful digital collection.

Random digitization produces a digital attic. Coherent digitization produces a usable resource.

### Plan For Sustainability From The Start

Every digitized object is a permanent commitment. Selecting without a sustainability plan creates orphan digital objects that degrade or disappear.

Plan for:

- ongoing storage costs and budget responsibility;
- fixity checking and preservation actions;
- metadata maintenance and authority updates;
- platform migration as systems change;
- access management and rights review over time;
- staff responsibility for the digital collection after the project ends.

If the institution cannot commit to sustaining the result, reconsider whether to digitize at all, or seek partners who can.

### Document Selection Decisions

Selection decisions should be documented so the project is defensible, repeatable, and improvable. Documentation also supports future re-evaluation.

Document:

- the selection criteria and their ranking;
- the candidates considered and the decisions made;
- the rights assessments for selected items;
- the condition assessments and conservation needs;
- the rationale for items deferred or excluded.

Documentation turns selection from personal judgment into institutional practice.

## Common Traps

### Digitizing Everything Indiscriminately

Without criteria, projects consume resources on low-value items and create unsustainable backlogs. Select strategically.

### Ignoring Copyright Until After Scanning

Rights problems halt projects and waste labor. Assess copyright before committing resources.

### Underestimating Fragility

Some materials cannot survive handling without conservation. Assess condition and factor treatment cost.

### Redundant Digitization

Digitizing what is already freely available wastes resources. Check existing digital availability first.

### Budgeting Only Scanning

Metadata, quality control, storage, and preservation dominate cost. Estimate the full lifecycle.

### Random Item Selection

Scattered items produce a digital attic. Build coherent collections with context.

### No Sustainability Plan

Every digital object is a permanent commitment. Plan for storage, preservation, and responsibility.

### Personal Interest Driving Selection

The selector's interests are not the project's priorities. Apply documented criteria.

## Self-Check

- Is there a defined purpose for the digitization project before selection begins?
- Are selection criteria explicit, ranked, and applied consistently to candidates?
- Is copyright assessed and documented for each item before resources are committed?
- Is physical condition evaluated, including conservation needs and handling risks?
- Are unique, rare, and gap-filling materials prioritized over redundant digitization?
- Is the full lifecycle cost, including metadata, storage, and preservation, estimated?
- Are access and preservation goals distinguished, with master and derivative strategies?
- Does the project build a coherent collection with context rather than scattered items?
- Is there a sustainability plan covering storage, preservation, platform, and staff responsibility?
- Are selection decisions documented for defensibility and future re-evaluation?
