# Outreach Program Design

Outreach is how the library or archive reaches beyond its existing users to engage the broader community, and it is where good intentions most often produce ineffective or even counterproductive programs. The central failure mode is program design that starts from what the institution wants to offer or showcase rather than from what a specific community needs or wants. The result is well-attended events that reach the already-engaged, programming that reinforces existing patterns rather than expanding access, and outreach that the institution counts as success while the intended new audiences experience as irrelevant or unwelcoming. Agents tend to conflate outreach with events, to measure activity rather than impact, and to design one-size-fits-all programs for audiences treated as undifferentiated. Good outreach is strategic: it identifies specific audiences and their needs, designs engagement that serves those needs, builds the relationships that make outreach land, and measures whether it actually reached and mattered to the intended people. Outreach to underserved communities carries additional responsibility, because poorly designed outreach can waste trust and reinforce the perception that the institution is not for them.

Use this skill when designing outreach programs, planning events, building audience strategies, or measuring outreach impact. The goal is to prevent the agent from designing institution-centered outreach, measuring activity instead of impact, treating audiences as undifferentiated, or conducting outreach to underserved communities without relationship and cultural competence.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Audience And Their Needs, Not From The Institution

Effective outreach begins with a specific audience and a genuine understanding of what that audience needs, wants, and experiences. Outreach designed from the institution outward, what do we want to tell or show people, reaches those already inclined to listen and misses those the institution most wants to reach. The audience analysis must come first and must be specific.

Analyze the audience first:

- identify the specific audience for the outreach, not "the public" but a defined group with shared characteristics or needs;
- understand what that audience cares about, what barriers they face in using the institution, and what would genuinely serve them;
- learn where the audience already gathers, gets information, and trusts, rather than expecting them to come to the institution's channels;
- recognize the audience's prior relationship, or lack of relationship, with the institution, and design accordingly;
- distinguish audiences the institution already serves well from those it does not, and prioritize the underserved.

Outreach to an undefined audience reaches the default audience, which is usually the already-served.

### Align Outreach With Mission And Strategic Priorities

Outreach should serve the institution's mission and strategic priorities, not exist as activity for its own sake. Without alignment, outreach becomes a collection of events that consume resources without advancing the institution's purpose. Each outreach initiative should be traceable to a mission goal, whether access, education, community connection, or collection use.

Align outreach to mission:

- articulate the mission goal each outreach initiative serves;
- prioritize outreach that advances strategic goals over outreach that is merely popular or easy;
- be willing to decline or sunset outreach that does not serve mission or audience need;
- connect outreach to the institution's core services so engagement leads to meaningful use;
- avoid outreach that exists primarily for institutional visibility without substance.

Activity that cannot be tied to mission is a candidate for redirection, regardless of how beloved it is internally.

### Build Relationships Before Designing Programs

Outreach lands when it is built on relationships, and it fails when it is transactional. Communities, especially those historically underserved or alienated from institutions, respond to outreach from institutions they trust and ignore outreach from institutions they do not. Relationship-building is not a preliminary step to be completed; it is the medium through which outreach works.

Build relationships:

- invest time in getting to know the audience and the organizations and leaders they trust;
- partner with community organizations rather than approaching communities directly as an outsider;
- listen before proposing, and let community input shape the program;
- show up consistently, not only when the institution has a program to promote;
- be honest about the institution's limitations and history, acknowledging where trust has been eroded.

Relationships take time and cannot be shortcut; outreach designed without them often reinforces the distance it meant to close.

### Design For Accessibility And Inclusion From The Start

Outreach that is not accessible and inclusive fails part of its audience by design. Accessibility is not an add-on; it must be built into program design from the start, covering physical access, communication access, language, cost, timing, and cultural welcome. Outreach to underserved communities requires particular attention to the barriers that have kept them away.

Design for accessibility and inclusion:

- choose venues, times, and formats that work for the intended audience, including those with disabilities, caregiving responsibilities, or limited transportation;
- provide communication access, such as sign language interpretation, captioning, and materials in accessible formats;
- offer programs in the languages the audience speaks, and use plain language;
- remove cost barriers where possible, recognizing that fees exclude low-income community members;
- ensure the institution's spaces and staff are welcoming to the audience, including through cultural competence training;
- plan for the audience's needs, not the institution's convenience.

Inaccessible outreach does not just fail to reach some people; it signals that they are not the intended audience.

### Differentiate Programming By Audience And Goal

Different audiences and goals require different programming. A program that works for engaged adult learners may not work for families, for teens, for older adults, or for a community new to the institution. Treating programming as transferable across audiences produces generic events that serve no one well. Each program should be designed for a specific audience and a specific goal.

Differentiate programming:

- match format to audience and goal: workshops, performances, lectures, hands-on activities, drop-in events;
- consider depth versus reach: some goals require sustained engagement, others broad exposure;
- sequence programming so initial engagement can lead to deeper involvement;
- avoid copying successful programs without adapting them to the new audience;
- test programming with representatives of the audience before full launch.

### Measure Impact, Not Just Activity

Outreach measurement often counts what is easy to count: attendance, events held, materials distributed. These activity metrics tell whether outreach happened but not whether it mattered. Impact measurement asks whether the outreach achieved its goal: did the audience engage, learn, return, or change? Without impact measurement, the institution cannot improve its outreach or justify its investment.

Measure impact:

- define what success looks like for each initiative, tied to its audience and goal;
- collect feedback from participants, not just attendance counts;
- track indicators of deeper engagement, such as return visits, new card registrations, or follow-up use;
- use both quantitative and qualitative methods, because numbers miss the texture of impact;
- compare results to the audience and goal, and use findings to improve future outreach;
- be honest about failures, because learning from ineffective outreach is as valuable as celebrating success.

Activity metrics without impact metrics optimize for busyness rather than effectiveness.

### Sustain And Evolve Successful Outreach

Effective outreach is sustained and evolved, not run once and abandoned. Audiences build expectations and relationships through repeated engagement, and one-off programs rarely achieve lasting impact. At the same time, outreach must evolve based on what is learned, because audiences and needs change. Plan for sustainability and adaptation.

Plan for sustainability:

- resource outreach as ongoing, not as one-time events dependent on episodic funding;
- build outreach capacity in staff through training and role assignment;
- document successful programs so they can be repeated or adapted;
- review outreach regularly against impact data and retire what no longer works;
- maintain relationships with community partners across program cycles.

## Common Traps

### Institution-Centered Design

Designing outreach around what the institution wants to offer reaches the already-served and misses the underserved. This is a trap because it feels productive while reinforcing existing patterns.

### Undefined Audience

Outreach to "the public" reaches the default audience and fails to expand access. This is a trap because the lack of specificity hides who is and is not being reached.

### Activity Metrics As Success

Counting attendance and events without measuring impact optimizes for busyness. This is a trap because the institution believes outreach works when it may not.

### Transactional Outreach Without Relationships

Approaching communities with programs but no relationship produces low trust and low engagement. This is a trap because the outreach fails for reasons the institution does not see.

### Accessibility As Afterthought

Adding accessibility accommodations late or not at all excludes part of the audience. This is a trap because retrofitting is harder and the exclusion signals who is welcome.

### One-Size-Fits-All Programming

Transferring programs across audiences without adaptation produces generic events. This is a trap because no audience is well served by programming designed for everyone.

### Unsustainable Episodic Outreach

Running outreach dependent on one-time funding that disappears leaves audiences and partners unsupported. This is a trap because the withdrawal damages trust built during the program.

### Ignoring Outreach To The Already-Served

Focusing outreach on audiences already engaged while claiming to reach new communities. This is a trap because the metrics look good while the underserved remain unreached.

## Self-Check

- Does the outreach begin from a specific, defined audience and a genuine understanding of their needs and barriers?
- Is each initiative aligned to a mission and strategic goal, with non-aligned activity redirected?
- Are relationships with the audience and trusted community organizations built before and during program design?
- Is accessibility and inclusion designed in from the start, covering physical, communication, language, cost, and cultural dimensions?
- Is programming differentiated by audience and goal, rather than treated as transferable?
- Is impact measured against the audience and goal, using both quantitative and qualitative methods, not just activity counts?
- Is successful outreach sustained and resourced as ongoing, with capacity built in staff?
- Are programs reviewed against impact data and evolved or retired based on findings?
- Is outreach to underserved communities conducted with cultural competence and honesty about the institution's history and limitations?
