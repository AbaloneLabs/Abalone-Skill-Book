# Partnerships And Community Collaboration

Partnerships are how libraries and archives extend their reach, share resources, and serve communities they cannot serve alone, and they are also where institutional ambition most often outruns the relationship work that makes collaboration succeed. The central risk is partnership failure rooted in misaligned expectations, unaddressed power imbalances, and vague agreements: two or more organizations begin a collaboration with different understandings of purpose, contribution, and benefit, and the partnership erodes into resentment or collapse. Agents tend to treat partnerships as agreements to be signed rather than relationships to be built, to under-invest in clarifying expectations, and to ignore the power dynamics that make partnerships between unequal institutions inherently fraught. A partnership between a well-resourced institution and a small community organization is not a relationship of equals, and pretending it is allows the imbalance to shape outcomes invisibly. Good partnership practice is explicit about purpose, contribution, benefit, and power, and it treats the collaboration as a relationship requiring ongoing maintenance, not a contract requiring only signature.

Use this skill when establishing partnerships, negotiating collaboration agreements, designing collaborative projects, or managing partnership dynamics. The goal is to prevent the agent from forming partnerships without alignment, ignoring power imbalances, writing vague agreements, or treating partnerships as transactions rather than relationships.

## Core Rules

### Establish Shared Purpose And Mutual Benefit Before Structure

A partnership must begin with a shared understanding of what the collaborators are trying to achieve and how each benefits. Without this foundation, the structure, whether a memo of understanding or a formal contract, codifies misalignment. The purpose and benefit conversation should happen early, explicitly, and honestly, including acknowledgment of where the partners' interests diverge.

Establish shared purpose:

- articulate the shared goal in terms all partners genuinely endorse;
- identify what each partner contributes and what each gains, recognizing that benefit is not always financial;
- surface where partners' interests align and where they differ, and decide whether alignment is sufficient to proceed;
- confirm that the partnership serves each partner's mission, not just one partner's agenda;
- revisit purpose as the partnership evolves, because goals shift.

A partnership without genuine shared purpose becomes one partner's project staffed by others, which is not collaboration.

### Address Power Imbalances Explicitly

Partnerships between institutions of different size, resources, and prestige are relationships with power imbalances, and ignoring the imbalance does not eliminate it; it lets the more powerful partner set terms invisibly. A large institution partnering with a small community organization must recognize that its resources, name, and processes carry weight, and that the community partner may agree to terms from caution or need rather than genuine assent. Naming and managing the imbalance is essential to ethical partnership.

Address power imbalance:

- acknowledge the imbalance openly with partners rather than pretending equality;
- design processes that give each partner meaningful voice, including the smaller or less resourced partner;
- avoid imposing the larger institution's templates, timelines, and requirements without adaptation;
- ensure the community partner benefits substantively, not symbolically;
- recognize that the community partner holds expertise and authority the institution lacks, especially regarding their own community;
- be alert to dynamics where the institution's convenience overrides the partner's needs.

Power-balanced partnership is deliberate work; the default is for the more powerful partner's norms to prevail.

### Write Clear Agreements That Capture Real Expectations

A partnership agreement, whether a memo of understanding or a contract, should capture the real expectations of the collaboration: purpose, roles, contributions, decision-making, intellectual property, financial arrangements, duration, and exit. Vague agreements that paper over unresolved differences become the source of later conflict. The agreement should be clear enough to guide operations and resolve disputes, and it should be written collaboratively, not imposed by one partner.

Capture in the agreement:

- the shared purpose and the specific activities or deliverables;
- each partner's roles, responsibilities, and contributions, including staff time, resources, and expertise;
- decision-making processes, including who decides what and how disputes are resolved;
- financial arrangements, including funding, cost-sharing, and how money flows;
- intellectual property and credit, including ownership of outputs and how partners are credited;
- data and information sharing, including what is shared and any confidentiality;
- duration, review points, and the process for renewal, amendment, or conclusion;
- exit conditions, because partnerships end and the ending should be planned.

An agreement that cannot guide a real decision or resolve a real dispute is insufficient regardless of its formality.

### Design For Each Partner's Capacity And Constraints

Partnerships fail when they demand more than a partner can sustainably give. A collaboration designed around the well-resourced partner's capacity may overwhelm a smaller partner whose staff wear many roles. Designing for the actual capacity and constraints of each partner, including staffing, governance processes, and competing priorities, prevents the resentment that comes from overcommitment.

Design for capacity:

- understand each partner's staffing, decision-making processes, and timelines, which may differ significantly;
- scope contributions to what each partner can realistically sustain, not what they aspire to provide;
- build in flexibility for the smaller partner's constraints, such as part-time staff or volunteer governance;
- avoid timelines driven only by the larger partner's funding or reporting cycles;
- check in on capacity as the partnership progresses, because circumstances change.

Overcommitment by a smaller partner often goes unspoken until it becomes withdrawal; proactive capacity design prevents this.

### Build Governance And Communication That Sustain The Partnership

A partnership is sustained through governance and communication, not through the initial agreement. Without regular communication, shared decision-making, and a structure for addressing issues, the partnership drifts and small misunderstandings accumulate into breakdowns. Governance should be lightweight enough to be manageable but substantive enough to keep the partners aligned.

Build sustaining governance:

- establish regular communication between designated partner contacts, not just at milestones;
- create shared decision-making for issues that affect both partners, with clear escalation paths;
- document decisions and actions so that institutional memory survives staff turnover;
- hold periodic reviews of the partnership's progress, challenges, and continued alignment;
- address conflicts early and directly, rather than allowing them to fester.

Partnerships often fail not from major disputes but from accumulated small miscommunications that governance and communication can catch.

### Plan For Sustainability And For Ending

Partnerships have lifecycles, and both sustainability and conclusion should be planned. Some partnerships are time-limited by design; others are ongoing and need sustainable resourcing. In both cases, the partners should discuss how the collaboration will be sustained or concluded, including what happens to shared work, data, and relationships. Unplanned endings, where a partnership fades or collapses, leave unfinished work and damaged relationships.

Plan for sustainability and ending:

- for ongoing partnerships, identify sustainable resourcing that does not depend on episodic grants;
- for time-limited partnerships, define the endpoint and what deliverables or transitions occur;
- plan for staff turnover, which is a leading cause of partnership breakdown, through documentation and shared relationships;
- agree on what happens to shared outputs, data, and assets if the partnership ends;
- conduct a respectful conclusion that acknowledges contributions and preserves the relationship for future collaboration.

Planning the ending is not pessimism; it is recognizing that partnerships, like projects, have lifecycles that benefit from intentional management.

### Evaluate The Partnership Against Shared Goals

Partnership evaluation should assess whether the collaboration achieved its shared purpose and served each partner, using criteria the partners agreed in advance. Evaluation that measures only one partner's goals, or that counts activities rather than outcomes, misses whether the partnership worked. Honest evaluation, including of failures, strengthens future collaboration.

Evaluate against shared goals:

- assess progress against the shared purpose and each partner's benefit, as defined at the start;
- use criteria both partners endorsed, not only the larger partner's metrics;
- include qualitative feedback from both partners about what worked and what did not;
- use evaluation to improve the ongoing partnership or to inform future ones;
- be honest about disappointments, because partnerships that cannot acknowledge failure cannot learn.

## Common Traps

### Partnerships Without Shared Purpose

Collaborating without genuine alignment of purpose produces one partner's project staffed by others. This is a trap because it feels like partnership while serving only one agenda.

### Ignoring Power Imbalance

Pretending unequal partners are equal lets the more powerful partner set terms invisibly. This is a trap because the imbalance shapes outcomes while remaining undiscussed.

### Vague Agreements That Avoid Hard Questions

Agreements that paper over roles, money, IP, and exit create conflict later. This is a trap because the unresolved differences resurface under stress.

### Designing For The Powerful Partner's Capacity

Scoping collaboration around the well-resourced partner overwhelms the smaller partner. This is a trap because overcommitment leads to withdrawal and resentment.

### Communication Only At Milestones

Infrequent communication lets small misunderstandings accumulate into breakdowns. This is a trap because the partnership erodes silently.

### Unsustainable Funding Dependence

Building partnerships on episodic grants that disappear leaves partners unsupported. This is a trap because the withdrawal damages both the work and the relationship.

### Unplanned Endings

Allowing partnerships to fade without conclusion leaves unfinished work and damaged relationships. This is a trap because the ending wastes the investment and closes future collaboration.

### Evaluation By One Partner's Metrics

Measuring the partnership only against the larger partner's goals misses whether it served all partners. This is a trap because the assessment is biased toward the powerful partner.

## Self-Check

- Did the partners establish shared purpose and mutual benefit explicitly before structuring the collaboration?
- Are power imbalances between partners acknowledged and managed, with meaningful voice for the smaller or less resourced partner?
- Does the agreement capture purpose, roles, contributions, decision-making, finances, IP, data sharing, duration, and exit clearly enough to guide operations and resolve disputes?
- Is the collaboration scoped to each partner's realistic capacity and constraints, including the smaller partner's?
- Is there regular communication, shared decision-making, documentation, and periodic review to sustain the partnership?
- Is sustainability or conclusion planned, with attention to resourcing, staff turnover, and the fate of shared work?
- Does evaluation assess the partnership against shared goals and each partner's benefit, using criteria both endorsed?
- Are conflicts addressed early and directly, and is the relationship preserved through honest conclusion?
