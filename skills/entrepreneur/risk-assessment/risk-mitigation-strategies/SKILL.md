# Risk Mitigation Strategies

Once risks are identified, the question becomes what to do about each: avoid it by changing course, reduce it through controls and contingency, transfer it through insurance or contracts, or accept it consciously. Different risks call for different strategies, and the right choice depends on likelihood, impact, cost of mitigation, and the venture's risk appetite. Applying one strategy uniformly, or mitigating only reactively after harm, wastes resources on low-impact risks while leaving high-impact ones unaddressed. Sound mitigation is the discipline of matching strategy to each risk deliberately.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I mitigate this risk", "what should we do about each risk", "should we insure against this", "when do we accept a risk", or "how should an entrepreneur choose risk mitigation strategies". The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to a single mitigation approach and from neglecting the deliberate matching of strategy to risk.

## Core Rules

### Match The Strategy To The Risk

The four core strategies, avoid, reduce, transfer, and accept, fit different risks. Avoid suits high-impact risks the venture can sidestep; reduce suits risks that can be lowered through controls; transfer suits risks insurable or contractually shiftable; accept suits low-impact or low-likelihood risks where mitigation cost exceeds value. Match the strategy to each risk's profile.

### Prioritize By Likelihood And Impact

Direct mitigation effort by likelihood and impact: high-likelihood, high-impact risks demand action; low-likelihood, low-impact risks may simply be monitored. Prioritization prevents over-investing in minor risks while leaving major ones unaddressed. Equal effort across unequal risks is inefficient.

### Reduce Through Controls And Contingency

For risks to be reduced, implement controls that lower likelihood, such as process or security measures, and contingency plans that lower impact, such as backups or alternate suppliers. Reduction is often the most practical strategy for startup risks. Combine likelihood and impact reduction for compounding benefit.

### Transfer Through Insurance, Contracts, Or Partnerships

Some risks are best transferred: insurance for insurable perils like liability or property loss; contractual indemnities or caps to shift specific exposures; partnerships to share risk. Transfer makes sense when another party can bear the risk more efficiently. Understand what transfer actually covers and its limits.

### Accept Consciously, Not By Default

Accepting a risk is legitimate when mitigation cost exceeds the expected value of the harm, but it must be a conscious decision, documented and owned, not a default of inattention. Distinguish conscious acceptance from neglect. Unconscious acceptance is how surprises happen.

### Avoid By Changing Course When Impact Is Existential

For risks whose impact would be existential and that can be avoided by changing course, such as a legally untenable market or a technically infeasible feature, avoidance is the right strategy. Do not try to mitigate an existential risk that can be sidestepped. Avoidance is underused because it feels like retreat.

### Build Contingency For Fast-Moving Risks

Fast-moving risks, security breaches, key-person loss, regulatory actions, need pre-built contingency: response plans, contacts, and decision paths. Building contingency under pressure is far harder than in advance. For fast-moving risks, contingency is the core mitigation.

### Monitor Slow-Moving Risks With Triggers

Slow-moving risks, market shifts, team erosion, cash burn, are mitigated through monitoring with triggers that prompt action. Define what signal would escalate each slow-moving risk, so it is caught before it becomes acute. Monitoring with triggers prevents slow risks from becoming crises.

### Consider The Cost Of Mitigation Itself

Mitigation has costs: money, time, complexity, and distraction. Consider whether the mitigation's cost is justified by the risk reduction, because over-mitigation creates its own drag. The goal is efficient risk reduction, not zero risk.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Some mitigations, insurance, legal structures, compliance programs, involve professional assessment and personal liability. Use qualified advisors for specialized mitigation. This skill provides framing only and does not replace professional risk, legal, and insurance advice.

## Common Traps

### One Strategy Applied Uniformly

Different risks need different strategies; uniformity wastes effort and leaves gaps.

### Reactive-Only Mitigation

Addressing risks only after harm foregoes the cheaper, earlier mitigation.

### Unconscious Acceptance

Risks accepted by default of inattention become surprises.

### Over-Mitigation

Mitigation cost exceeding the risk's expected value creates unnecessary drag.

### Neglecting Contingency For Fast Risks

Improvising under pressure is far harder than pre-built contingency.

### No Triggers For Slow Risks

Slow-moving risks without monitoring triggers become crises before they are noticed.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each risk has a strategy matched to its likelihood and impact profile.
- [ ] Mitigation effort is prioritized by likelihood and impact, not applied evenly.
- [ ] Reduction combines likelihood and impact controls with contingency.
- [ ] Transfer is used where another party can bear the risk more efficiently.
- [ ] Accepted risks are conscious, documented, and owned, not defaulted.
- [ ] Existential risks that can be avoided are avoided, not merely mitigated.
- [ ] Fast-moving risks have pre-built contingency plans.
- [ ] Slow-moving risks have monitoring triggers that prompt action.
- [ ] The cost of mitigation is justified by the risk reduction.
- [ ] Specialized mitigations involve qualified advisors and professional assessment.
