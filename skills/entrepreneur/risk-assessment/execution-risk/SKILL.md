# Execution Risk

Execution risk is the risk that the team cannot build, sell, and deliver the product at the quality and speed the opportunity requires, and it is the category of risk most within a company's control but also most underestimated, because founders tend to believe in their own capability and to assume that hard work will overcome any gap. Unlike market risk, which is about whether the opportunity exists, execution risk is about whether the company can capture it, and it encompasses team capability, operational systems, technical feasibility, and the dependencies that determine whether the plan can be executed. The judgment problem is honestly assessing the team's capability and coverage against what the plan requires, identifying the operational and technical dependencies that could derail execution, and distinguishing execution risk from market and product risk so that the right gaps are addressed. Agents tend to fail by assuming capability without evidence, by conflating execution risk with product risk, and by ignoring the dependencies that make execution fragile.

Use this skill when assessing execution risk for a startup. The goal is an honest understanding of whether the team can execute the plan, so that gaps are addressed before they become fatal.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Execution Risk From Market And Product Risk

The three core risks, market, product, and execution, are distinct and require different interventions. Execution risk is about whether the team can build and deliver; market risk is about whether the opportunity exists; product risk is about whether the product solves the problem.

Distinguish by:

- execution risk: can the team build, sell, and deliver at the required quality and speed;
- market risk: does a large enough, reachable, well-timed market exist;
- product risk: does the product solve a real problem in an adoptable way.

A company struggling because it cannot execute cannot be saved by a better market or product hypothesis. Diagnose the right risk before intervening.

### Assess Team Capability Against What The Plan Requires

Execution depends on the team's capability, and the honest assessment is whether the team's skills, experience, and coverage match what the plan demands. Capability gaps are the most common execution risk, and they are often denied.

Assess capability by:

- mapping the skills and functions the plan requires over the next 18 months;
- evaluating the team's proven capability in each, not just stated confidence;
- identifying the gaps where the team lacks experience or coverage;
- planning to close the gaps through hiring, advisors, or pivots in approach.

A team that has never scaled a product will struggle to scale one, however confident it is. Assess capability against evidence, not assertion.

### Evaluate Team Coverage And Single Points Of Failure

Execution risk includes coverage risk: the risk that critical functions depend on a single person, whose absence or failure cripples the company. Single points of failure make execution fragile.

Evaluate coverage by:

- identifying the functions and knowledge that reside in only one person;
- assessing the impact of losing key people to departure or incapacity;
- building redundancy or documentation for critical functions;
- recognizing that early teams always have coverage gaps, but they must be managed.

A company where one person's departure halts execution has high execution risk. Identify and address single points of failure.

### Identify Operational Dependencies And Bottlenecks

Execution depends on operational systems and processes, and the dependencies within them, vendors, partners, infrastructure, determine whether the plan can be delivered. Unidentified dependencies become bottlenecks.

Identify dependencies by:

- mapping the operational chain from build to deliver to support;
- identifying the external dependencies, vendors, platforms, partners, that must hold;
- assessing the capacity of each link against the plan's demands;
- planning for the failure or underperformance of critical dependencies.

An operational chain is only as strong as its weakest dependency. Map and stress-test the chain.

### Assess Technical Feasibility And Risk

For products with technical complexity, execution risk includes whether the technology can be built at the required performance, cost, and reliability. Technical feasibility is an execution question, distinct from whether the market wants the product.

Assess technical feasibility by:

- identifying the hard technical problems the product must solve;
- evaluating whether they are solved, solvable with known approaches, or genuinely uncertain;
- de-risking the hardest problems early through prototyping and validation;
- being honest about technical risk rather than assuming it will work out.

A product whose core technology is unproven carries high execution risk, however strong the market. Assess and de-risk technical feasibility deliberately.

### Evaluate The Realistic Pace Of Execution

Plans assume a pace of execution, and the honest assessment is whether the team can execute at that pace given its capability, systems, and dependencies. Optimistic pacing is a common execution risk.

Evaluate pace by:

- comparing the planned pace to the team's demonstrated velocity;
- accounting for the overhead of hiring, process, and coordination as the team grows;
- recognizing that early speed often slows as complexity rises;
- building realistic timelines that reflect actual capability, not aspiration.

A plan built on an optimistic pace misses milestones and burns runway. Pace the plan to realistic execution.

### Monitor Execution Against Milestones And Adjust

Execution risk is managed through monitoring progress against milestones and adjusting when execution falls short. Static plans that are not updated against reality allow execution gaps to compound.

Monitor by:

- tracking progress against clear milestones with measurable criteria;
- diagnosing the causes when milestones are missed;
- adjusting plans, resources, or scope in response to execution reality;
- avoiding the trap of assuming missed milestones will self-correct.

Execution that is monitored and adjusted stays on track; execution that is assumed drifts into failure. Monitor actively.

### Use The Execution Assessment To Inform Investment And Hiring

An execution risk assessment informs where to invest and whom to hire, because the gaps it identifies are the highest-leverage places to add capability. An assessment that does not change investment is wasted.

Use the assessment to:

- prioritize hiring in the functions where capability gaps most threaten execution;
- invest in systems and process where operational dependencies are fragile;
- de-risk technical feasibility through focused prototyping;
- adjust the plan's pace or scope where execution cannot support the original ambition.

The execution assessment should change what the company does. Direct investment at the gaps that most threaten execution.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Execution Risk With Product Or Market Risk

Different risks require different interventions. Diagnose correctly.

### Assuming Capability Without Evidence

Confidence is not capability. Assess proven skills against plan requirements.

### Ignoring Single Points Of Failure

Critical functions dependent on one person are fragile. Build coverage.

### Unidentified Operational Dependencies

External dependencies become bottlenecks. Map and stress-test the chain.

### Assuming Technical Feasibility

Unproven technology carries high risk. De-risk the hard problems early.

### Optimistic Pacing

Plans built on unrealistic pace miss milestones. Pace to actual capability.

### Unmonitored Execution

Static plans let gaps compound. Monitor and adjust against milestones.

### Assessment Without Investment Action

An assessment that does not change hiring or investment is wasted. Act on the gaps.

- [ ] Team capability is assessed against the plan's requirements, based on evidence not assertion.
- [ ] Coverage and single points of failure are identified and managed.
- [ ] Operational dependencies and bottlenecks are mapped and stress-tested.
- [ ] Technical feasibility is assessed, with the hardest problems de-risked early.
- [ ] The plan's pace is realistic against the team's demonstrated velocity.
- [ ] Execution is monitored against milestones, with plans adjusted when milestones are missed.
- [ ] The assessment informs hiring, investment, and scope decisions.
