# Market Risk

Market risk is the risk that the market a company targets does not exist, is not large enough to support the business, cannot be reached through available channels, or is timed wrong for the product, and it is the most fundamental category of startup risk because no amount of product excellence or execution overcomes a market that is not there. A great product in a non-existent market fails; a mediocre product in a large, growing market can succeed and improve. The judgment problem is assessing the market honestly rather than optimistically, sizing it with bottom-up rigor rather than top-down fantasy, distinguishing market risk from product and execution risk so that the right problems are addressed, and recognizing when timing, the most underestimated market dimension, determines success or failure. Agents tend to fail by accepting optimistic market sizing, by conflating market risk with product risk, and by ignoring timing as a market characteristic.

Use this skill when assessing market risk for a startup or product. The goal is an honest understanding of whether the market can support the business, so that effort is directed at real risks rather than wasted on markets that cannot deliver.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Market Risk From Product And Execution Risk

The three core risks of a startup, market, product, and execution, are distinct and require different interventions, and conflating them leads to solving the wrong problem. Market risk is about whether the market exists and can be served; product risk is about whether the product solves the problem; execution risk is about whether the team can build and deliver.

Distinguish by:

- market risk: does a large enough market exist, can it be reached, is the timing right;
- product risk: does the product solve a real problem in a way users will adopt;
- execution risk: can the team build, sell, and support the product at the required quality and speed.

A company struggling because the market does not exist cannot be saved by better product or execution. Diagnose the right risk before intervening.

### Assess Whether The Market Actually Exists

The most fundamental market question is whether the market exists at all, and many startups target markets that are theoretical rather than real. A market exists when there are customers with the problem, the willingness to pay, and the ability to buy.

Assess existence by:

- finding real customers who experience the problem today, not hypothetically;
- confirming they are willing to pay to solve it, not merely interested;
- verifying they have the budget and authority to buy;
- distinguishing a problem people complain about from one they pay to solve.

A market that exists only in analysis, without real paying customers, is a theory. Validate existence through evidence of real demand.

### Size The Market Bottom-Up, Not Top-Down

Top-down market sizing, taking a large industry number and claiming a percentage, produces fantasy numbers that flatter without informing. Bottom-up sizing, counting real customers at real prices, produces a number that reflects the actual opportunity.

Size bottom-up by:

- counting the real number of potential customers in the target segment;
- multiplying by a realistic price per customer;
- accounting for the portion reachable through available channels;
- being honest about the share achievable in a relevant timeframe.

A bottom-up market that is too small to support the business is a critical finding, however much smaller it is than the top-down fantasy. Use bottom-up sizing to face reality.

### Evaluate Whether The Market Is Reachable

A market that exists and is large may still be unreachable, because the channels to acquire customers are closed, expensive, or controlled by others. Reachability determines whether the market can actually be served.

Evaluate reachability by:

- identifying the channels through which customers can be acquired;
- assessing the cost of acquisition through each channel;
- checking whether channels are open or controlled by competitors or platforms;
- determining whether the unit economics work at the achievable acquisition cost.

A large market reachable only at prohibitive cost is functionally smaller than it appears. Assess reachability alongside size.

### Assess Market Timing As A Risk Dimension

Timing is the most underestimated market dimension, and being too early or too late is a common cause of failure. A market that does not yet exist, because enabling conditions are absent, or that no longer grows, because it has matured, produces failure regardless of product quality.

Assess timing by:

- checking whether the enabling conditions, technology, infrastructure, behavior, regulation, exist now;
- determining whether the market is growing, mature, or declining;
- recognizing whether competitors have already captured the market or whether it is open;
- being honest about whether the market is ready or whether the company is ahead of its time.

A market that will exist in three years does not support a company that runs out of money in two. Assess timing as a survival question.

### Validate Demand Through Customer Behavior, Not Claims

Market risk is reduced by evidence of real demand, and the strongest evidence is customer behavior, payments, retention, and usage, not claims or survey responses. Customers who say they would buy often do not.

Validate through behavior by:

- treating pre-orders, deposits, and letters of intent as stronger evidence than survey responses;
- observing whether customers pay for current solutions to the problem;
- testing willingness to pay through actual transactions, not hypothetical questions;
- recognizing that stated intent overstates real demand substantially.

A market validated by behavior is far less risky than one validated by claims. Seek behavioral evidence of demand.

### Monitor Market Evolution And Competitive Dynamics

Markets are not static; they evolve, and a market that is attractive today may erode through competition, substitution, or disruption. Monitoring evolution prevents strategic surprise.

Monitor by:

- tracking competitive entry and its effect on pricing and share;
- watching for substitute products or business models that address the same need;
- assessing whether the market is consolidating, fragmenting, or being disrupted;
- adjusting strategy as the market evolves rather than assuming stasis.

A market assessment made at founding becomes stale. Monitor evolution and update the assessment.

### Use The Market Assessment To Inform Go Or No-Go

A market risk assessment is not academic; it informs whether to proceed, pivot, or stop. A market that is too small, unreachable, or mistimed may warrant a pivot or shutdown rather than continued investment.

Use the assessment to:

- proceed with confidence if the market exists, is large, reachable, and timed well;
- pivot if the market is flawed but an adjacent opportunity is stronger;
- reduce investment if the market risk is high and unresolvable;
- stop if the market does not exist and cannot be made to.

The market assessment should change what the company does. A finding of high market risk that does not change strategy is a wasted assessment.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Market Risk With Product Or Execution Risk

Different risks require different interventions. Diagnose correctly.

### Assuming The Market Exists Without Evidence

A theoretical market without paying customers is a theory. Validate existence.

### Top-Down Market Sizing Fantasy

Industry-percentage sizing flatters without informing. Size bottom-up.

### Ignoring Reachability

A large market at prohibitive acquisition cost is functionally small. Assess channels.

### Underestimating Timing

Being too early or too late causes failure. Assess timing as survival.

### Trusting Claims Over Behavior

Stated intent overstates demand. Validate through payments and retention.

### Static Market Assessment

Markets evolve. Monitor and update the assessment.

### Market Assessment Without Strategic Action

A finding that does not change strategy is wasted. Use it to inform go or no-go.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Market risk is distinguished from product and execution risk, with the right problem addressed.
- [ ] The market's existence is validated through real paying customers, not theory.
- [ ] Market size is calculated bottom-up from real customers and prices, not top-down percentages.
- [ ] Reachability is assessed through available channels and achievable acquisition costs.
- [ ] Timing is evaluated against enabling conditions, growth stage, and competitive presence.
- [ ] Demand is validated through customer behavior, payments and retention, not claims.
- [ ] Market evolution and competitive dynamics are monitored and the assessment updated.
- [ ] The market assessment informs whether to proceed, pivot, reduce, or stop.
- [ ] The unit economics work at the achievable acquisition cost in the reachable market.
- [ ] The market can support the business at the scale required, within the relevant timeframe.
