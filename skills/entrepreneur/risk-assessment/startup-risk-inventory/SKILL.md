# Startup Risk Inventory

Startups face risk across many dimensions, market, product, team, financial, legal, competitive, operational, technological, and founders tend to see the risks closest to them while missing others. An incomplete risk picture leads to surprises that could have been anticipated and mitigated. A risk inventory is the discipline of systematically surfacing risks across dimensions, so that none is ignored simply because no one looked for it. The goal is not to eliminate risk, which is impossible in a startup, but to see the full field and address the most material threats deliberately.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what are the risks to my startup", "how do I identify risks", "what could go wrong", "what risks should a startup plan for", or "how should an entrepreneur build a risk inventory". The goal is to prevent the agent from listing only obvious risks and from leaving material threats unidentified because no one looked across all dimensions.

## Core Rules

### Inventory Risks Across Multiple Dimensions

Surface risks systematically across dimensions: market, including size and demand; product, including fit and feasibility; team, including gaps and key-person dependence; financial, including runway and unit economics; legal and compliance; competitive; operational; technological; and founder-specific, including alignment and burnout. A multi-dimensional scan prevents blind spots.

### Distinguish Assumptions From Known Risks

Some risks are known and characterized; others are assumptions, beliefs about the world that could be wrong. Distinguish the two, because assumptions are risk that masquerades as certainty. Surfacing assumptions as risks prevents false confidence in plans built on untested beliefs.

### Assess Likelihood And Impact For Each Risk

For each risk, assess likelihood and impact, to prioritize attention and resources. A high-likelihood, high-impact risk demands action; a low-likelihood, low-impact one may simply be monitored. Prioritization prevents spreading mitigation effort evenly across unequal risks.

### Look For Concentration And Correlation

Some risks are concentrated, a single customer, channel, supplier, or founder, and some are correlated, risks that materialize together, such as a market downturn hitting revenue and fundraising simultaneously. Concentration and correlation amplify impact and must be identified, because treating risks as independent understates their combined threat.

### Identify Slow-Moving And Fast-Moving Risks

Some risks develop slowly, market shifts or team erosion, allowing time to respond; others strike fast, a key-person departure, a security breach, or a regulatory action. Distinguish the time horizons, because fast-moving risks need pre-built response plans while slow-moving risks need monitoring.

### Surface Founder And Key-Person Risks

Founders and key people are themselves sources of risk: dependence on one person, founder conflict, burnout, or loss of key relationships. Surface these honestly, even though they are uncomfortable, because key-person risks are common and consequential in early ventures.

### Revisit The Inventory Regularly

The risk picture changes as the venture evolves: some risks recede, new ones emerge with scale or new markets. Revisit the inventory regularly, at milestones and when circumstances shift. A static inventory becomes stale and misses emerging threats.

### Use The Inventory To Drive Mitigation, Not Anxiety

The purpose of the inventory is to drive deliberate mitigation and monitoring, not to paralyze. For each material risk, decide whether to mitigate, transfer, accept, or monitor, and assign ownership. An inventory without action is an exercise in worry.

### Seek Diverse Perspectives To Surface Blind Spots

Founders have blind spots, especially about risks close to their own decisions. Seek diverse perspectives, from team members, advisors, investors, and peers, to surface risks the founders cannot see. Diverse input materially improves inventory completeness.

### Distinguish Internal From External Risks

Separate risks the venture can influence, such as product quality, hiring, or burn rate, from risks largely outside its control, such as macroeconomic shifts, regulatory changes, or competitor moves. Internal risks warrant mitigation and ownership; external risks warrant monitoring, hedging, and contingency planning. Conflating the two leads to either futile attempts to control the uncontrollable or passive acceptance of risks that could actually be reduced. The distinction directs effort where the venture has leverage.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Some risks, legal, regulatory, financial, carry personal liability and require professional assessment. Use qualified counsel and advisors for specialized risk areas. This skill provides framing only and does not replace professional risk and legal advice.

## Common Traps

### Obvious Risks Only

Listing only the risks close at hand leaves material threats unidentified.

### Assumptions Mistaken For Certainty

Untested beliefs treated as facts hide the venture's biggest risks.

### Ignoring Concentration And Correlation

Treating risks as independent understates their combined threat.

### Static Inventory

A risk picture built once becomes stale as the venture evolves.

### Key-Person Risks Unspoken

Founder and key-person risks are common and consequential but uncomfortable to surface.

### Inventory Without Action

Listing risks without mitigation or ownership is worry, not risk management.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Risks are inventoried across multiple dimensions, not just the obvious ones.
- [ ] Assumptions are surfaced as risks rather than treated as certainty.
- [ ] Likelihood and impact are assessed to prioritize attention.
- [ ] Concentration and correlation across risks are identified.
- [ ] Slow-moving and fast-moving risks are distinguished for appropriate response.
- [ ] Founder and key-person risks are surfaced honestly.
- [ ] The inventory is revisited regularly as the venture evolves.
- [ ] Each material risk has a mitigation, transfer, acceptance, or monitoring decision with ownership.
- [ ] Diverse perspectives are sought to surface blind spots.
- [ ] Specialized risk areas are assessed with qualified counsel and advisors.
