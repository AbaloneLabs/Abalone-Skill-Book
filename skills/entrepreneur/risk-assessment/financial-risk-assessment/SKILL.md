# Financial Risk Assessment

Financial risk is the risk that the company runs out of cash, cannot raise the capital it needs, or operates with a financial structure that cannot sustain the business, and it is the category of risk that most directly causes startup death, because running out of cash is the one failure that cannot be recovered by better product or strategy. Financial risk is not a single number; it encompasses runway, burn sustainability, funding risk, revenue concentration, and the resilience of the financial model under stress, and each must be assessed and managed. The judgment problem is modeling financial risk under multiple scenarios rather than a single hopeful projection, distinguishing financial risk from the market and execution risks that drive it, and ensuring that financial risks are identified and mitigated before they become existential. Agents tend to fail by presenting a single base-case projection, by conflating financial risk with its underlying causes, and by treating fundraising as certain rather than as a risk to be managed.

Use this skill when assessing financial risk for a startup. The goal is an honest understanding of the financial risks that could kill the company, so that they are mitigated before they become existential.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Financial Risk From Market And Execution Risk

Financial risk is often a symptom of market or execution risk: a company burns cash because the market is smaller than expected or because execution is slow. Treating the financial symptom without addressing the underlying cause produces temporary relief, not solution.

Distinguish by:

- financial risk: can the company sustain its cash position and capital structure;
- market risk: does the opportunity exist at the scale assumed;
- execution risk: can the team build and deliver at the required pace.

If the financial risk is driven by a flawed market hypothesis or execution gap, financial mitigation alone postpones failure. Diagnose the underlying cause.

### Model Runway Under Multiple Scenarios

Runway, the months of cash available, is the most critical financial metric, and a single base-case projection hides the uncertainty that defines a startup. Modeling runway under multiple scenarios reveals the range of outcomes and the decisions each requires.

Model scenarios by:

- projecting runway under base, best, and worst cases for revenue and burn;
- including a disaster case for severe downside, such as lost customers or delayed rounds;
- identifying the triggers and actions each scenario demands;
- treating the worst case as the scenario that must be survived.

A runway model with only a base case creates false confidence. Model the range and plan for the worst case.

### Assess Burn Sustainability Against Revenue Trajectory

Burn is sustainable only if revenue or funding will cover it before cash runs out, and the honest assessment is whether the burn rate is justified by the trajectory. Burning cash faster than revenue or funding can replace it is the path to running out.

Assess sustainability by:

- comparing the burn rate to the realistic revenue trajectory;
- determining whether revenue will cover burn before runway expires;
- identifying the burn reductions available if revenue falls short;
- avoiding burn rates that require fundraising to survive, unless fundraising is highly likely.

A burn rate that depends on fundraising is a bet on capital markets. Assess whether that bet is prudent.

### Evaluate Funding Risk And The Ability To Raise

For most startups, survival depends on raising capital before runway expires, and funding risk, the risk that capital cannot be raised on acceptable terms, is a central financial risk. Treating fundraising as certain is a dangerous assumption.

Evaluate funding risk by:

- assessing whether the milestones for the next round are achievable within runway;
- evaluating market conditions for fundraising, which fluctuate;
- identifying the fallback options if the round cannot be raised;
- recognizing that raising from weakness produces punitive terms or failure.

A company that must raise to survive carries high funding risk. Assess it honestly and maintain fallback options.

### Assess Revenue Concentration Risk

Revenue concentration, dependence on a small number of customers for a large share of revenue, creates financial risk, because the loss of a major customer can be existential. Concentration is often invisible until a customer leaves.

Assess concentration by:

- calculating the share of revenue from the top customers;
- identifying whether any single customer's loss would be fatal;
- diversifying the customer base to reduce concentration;
- recognizing that early companies always have concentration, but it must be managed.

A company where one customer is 40 percent of revenue is one departure from crisis. Assess and reduce concentration.

### Stress-Test The Financial Model's Assumptions

A financial model is only as good as its assumptions, and stress-testing those assumptions reveals whether the model survives realistic downside. Assumptions that are optimistic produce models that look healthy until reality arrives.

Stress-test by:

- identifying the assumptions that most drive the model, such as growth rate and churn;
- modeling what happens if each key assumption is wrong by a realistic margin;
- checking whether the model survives the combination of several assumptions being wrong;
- being honest about which assumptions are evidence-based and which are hopeful.

A model that survives stressed assumptions is trustworthy; one that collapses is a warning. Stress-test before trusting.

### Maintain A Cash Buffer For Unforeseen Shocks

Startups face unforeseen shocks, lost deals, delayed rounds, unexpected costs, and a financial structure with no buffer turns shocks into crises. Maintaining a cash buffer is a financial risk mitigation.

Maintain a buffer by:

- targeting a runway cushion beyond the minimum needed to reach the next milestone;
- avoiding spending to the edge of runway, which removes all flexibility;
- recognizing that shocks are normal, not exceptional;
- using the buffer to absorb shocks without crisis decisions.

A company with no buffer is one shock from crisis. Maintain cushion proportional to the risk environment.

### Use The Financial Risk Assessment To Drive Decisions

A financial risk assessment is not an accounting exercise; it drives decisions about burn, hiring, fundraising, and strategy. An assessment that does not change decisions is wasted.

Use the assessment to:

- adjust burn and hiring based on runway and funding risk;
- accelerate fundraising when funding risk is high;
- diversify revenue to reduce concentration;
- cut scope or pivot when the financial model is unsustainable.

The financial risk assessment should change what the company does. Use it to make the hard decisions before they become existential.

## Common Traps

### Treating Financial Risk As Separate From Its Causes

Financial risk often reflects market or execution problems. Diagnose the underlying cause.

### Single Base-Case Runway Projection

Optimism hides uncertainty. Model multiple scenarios and plan for the worst.

### Unsustainable Burn Dependent On Fundraising

Burning faster than revenue or likely funding replaces cash is dangerous. Assess burn sustainability.

### Assuming Fundraising Is Certain

Capital markets fluctuate. Assess funding risk and maintain fallbacks.

### Ignoring Revenue Concentration

Dependence on a few customers is existential risk. Assess and diversify.

### Trusting Unstress-Tested Assumptions

Optimistic assumptions produce fragile models. Stress-test before trusting.

### No Cash Buffer

A company with no buffer is one shock from crisis. Maintain cushion.

### Assessment Without Decision Action

An assessment that does not change burn, hiring, or fundraising is wasted. Act on the findings.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Financial risk is distinguished from the market and execution risks that may drive it.
- [ ] Runway is modeled under base, best, worst, and disaster scenarios, with actions for each.
- [ ] Burn sustainability is assessed against the realistic revenue trajectory.
- [ ] Funding risk is evaluated, with milestones achievable within runway and fallback options identified.
- [ ] Revenue concentration is calculated, with diversification pursued to reduce dependence.
- [ ] The financial model's key assumptions are stress-tested under realistic downside.
- [ ] A cash buffer is maintained to absorb unforeseen shocks without crisis.
- [ ] The assessment drives decisions on burn, hiring, fundraising, and scope.
- [ ] The worst-case scenario has a defined survival path, not just a number.
- [ ] The financial structure can survive the revenue not arriving on time or the round being delayed.
