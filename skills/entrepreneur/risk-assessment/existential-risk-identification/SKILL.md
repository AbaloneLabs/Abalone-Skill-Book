# Existential Risk Identification

Most startup risks are manageable setbacks, but some are existential: threats that could end the venture entirely. Running out of cash, irreconcilable founder conflict, losing product-market fit to a shift, regulatory shutdown, catastrophic liability, loss of a critical partner or platform, or a security breach that destroys trust, these can kill a company, and they deserve disproportionate attention. Identifying existential risks is the discipline of separating the threats that could end the venture from the many risks that merely hurt, and addressing the existential ones before they materialize.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what could kill my startup", "what are the existential risks", "what threats should I worry about most", "how do I identify fatal risks", or "how should an entrepreneur find existential risks". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating all risks equally and from leaving the venture's fatal threats unidentified or under-addressed.

## Core Rules

### Separate Existential From Non-Existential Risks

Not all risks are equal. Separate risks that could end the venture from risks that merely hurt, and give the existential ones disproportionate attention. Treating all risks equally dilutes focus from the threats that actually matter. Existential risks demand priority.

### Cash Exhaustion Is The Most Common Existential Risk

Running out of cash is the most common existential threat, because without runway the venture cannot address any other problem. Monitor runway obsessively, model burn under stress, and act early to extend it. Cash is the existential risk that enables addressing all others.

### Founder Conflict Can Be Fatal

Irreconcilable conflict among founders can end a venture, through paralysis, departure, or litigation. Founder relationships are foundational, and their breakdown is existential. Monitor founder alignment and address conflict early, before it becomes irreparable.

### Losing Product-Market Fit Is Existential

For ventures that have found fit, losing it, to a market shift, a disruptive entrant, or customer attrition, is existential. Monitor fit signals continuously, and treat sustained decline as an existential threat requiring response. Fit is not permanent and its loss is fatal.

### Regulatory And Legal Shutdowns Can End The Venture

In regulated industries or where compliance is material, a regulatory or legal action can shut the venture down or impose liability that ends it. Identify these exposures and address them with professional counsel, because they are existential and often preventable. Ignoring them invites termination.

### Catastrophic Liability And Security Breaches

A catastrophic liability event, an uncapped indemnity, a major lawsuit, or a security breach that destroys customer trust, can be existential, especially for small ventures. Identify the liability and security exposures that could end the venture, and mitigate them. These risks compound with scale if unaddressed.

### Platform And Partner Dependence Can Be Fatal

Dependence on a single platform, an app store, a cloud provider, a major partner, or a dominant channel, creates existential risk if that dependency changes terms, withdraws, or competes. Identify critical dependencies and plan alternatives, because platform risk has killed many ventures.

### Key-Person Dependence Is Existential Early

In early ventures, dependence on a single founder or key person for relationships, knowledge, or execution is existential if that person departs or is incapacitated. Identify key-person dependencies and build redundancy, because their loss can end the venture.

### Model Existential Scenarios Under Stress

Model how the venture fares under stress: a downturn that cuts revenue, a round that does not close, a competitor that enters, a key customer that leaves. Stress-testing reveals which scenarios are existential and how much buffer exists. Modeling under base case only hides existential exposure.

### Address Existential Risks Before They Materialize

Existential risks are far cheaper to address before they materialize than during the crisis. For each identified existential risk, take preventive action early: extend runway, resolve founder tension, diversify dependencies, address compliance. Prevention is the only viable strategy for fatal risks.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Existential risks often carry personal liability and require professional assessment. Use qualified counsel and advisors for legal, regulatory, financial, and security threats. This skill provides framing only and does not replace professional risk and legal advice.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Risks Equally

Diluting focus from existential threats by attending to all risks evenly.

### Runway Monitored Only Under Base Case

Stress scenarios that could exhaust cash are not modeled.

### Founder Conflict Left To Fester

Unaddressed founder tension becomes irreparable and fatal.

### Platform Dependence Unmonitored

A single platform or partner change can end the venture.

### Compliance Treated As Non-Existential

In regulated industries, compliance failures can shut the venture down.

### Addressing Existential Risks Only In Crisis

Prevention is far cheaper than crisis response for fatal risks.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Existential risks are separated from non-existential and given priority.
- [ ] Cash runway is monitored obsessively and modeled under stress.
- [ ] Founder alignment is monitored and conflict addressed early.
- [ ] Product-market fit signals are monitored for sustained decline.
- [ ] Regulatory and legal shutdown exposures are identified and addressed with counsel.
- [ ] Catastrophic liability and security breach exposures are mitigated.
- [ ] Platform and partner dependencies are identified with alternatives planned.
- [ ] Key-person dependencies are identified with redundancy built.
- [ ] Existential scenarios are modeled under stress, not only base case.
- [ ] Preventive action is taken on existential risks before they materialize, with professional advice.
