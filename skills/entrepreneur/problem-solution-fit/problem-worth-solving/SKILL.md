# Problem Worth Solving

Not every problem deserves a business. Many pains are real but small, infrequent, tolerated, or already adequately served. A founder who builds for any visible frustration will spend years on a product no one urgently needs. The discipline of deciding whether a problem is worth solving is what separates a venture from a hobby. The question is not "does this problem exist" but "is this problem important enough, frequent enough, and expensive enough that a specific customer will change behavior and pay to solve it".

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "is this problem big enough to build a company around", "how do I know if a pain is worth solving", "what makes a problem valuable", or "how should an entrepreneur rank problems". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the existence of a complaint as proof of a worthwhile opportunity.

## Core Rules

### Score Severity, Not Just Existence

A problem that exists mildly is not the same as a problem that hurts. Rank problems by intensity, not by whether someone mentions them.

Useful severity dimensions:

- financial loss caused by the problem;
- time wasted or delay created;
- risk, safety, compliance, or legal exposure;
- emotional cost, frustration, or stress;
- opportunity cost of not solving it;
- visibility to the person who controls budget.

A problem that causes measurable loss or risk is far more fundable than one that is merely annoying.

### Score Frequency And Recurrence

A severe but once-a-year problem may not sustain a business; a moderate but daily problem often will. Combine severity with frequency to estimate total pain.

Ask:

- how often the problem occurs;
- whether it is episodic or chronic;
- whether it is predictable or surprising;
- whether the customer is actively solving it each time it appears.

Recurring, active problems create habit and willingness to pay.

### Check Whether The Customer Already Pays Or Works Around

The strongest signal of a worthwhile problem is that the customer is already spending money, time, or effort to reduce it. Workarounds, consultants, spreadsheets, manual processes, and competing tools all prove the pain is real and worth addressing.

If the customer does nothing today, ask why they would start now.

### Test Prioritization Against Other Pains

Customers have many problems. The question is whether this one ranks high enough to act on. Ask the customer to rank this pain against others they face, and to name what they would sacrifice to solve it.

A problem that is never prioritized above other demands will lose to those demands when budget, attention, or time is scarce.

### Separate Painkiller From Vitamin

A painkiller addresses an active, recognized pain. A vitamin is a possible future benefit that requires the customer to believe in prevention or enhancement. Painkillers sell faster and at higher urgency because the customer already feels the hurt.

Be honest about which you are building. Vitamins can succeed but require education, trust, and patience that painkillers do not.

### Confirm The Problem Is Recognized, Not Latent

Some problems are real but unrecognized; the customer suffers without naming it. Latent problems can be valuable but require education and behavior change, which raises acquisition cost and lengthens sales cycles.

Ask whether the customer already describes the problem in their own words, or whether the founder must teach them that they have it.

### Map Problem To A Reachable, Budget-Holding Segment

A worthwhile problem attached to a segment you cannot reach or whose members lack budget is still not a business. Confirm that the people who feel the pain are findable, contactable, and able to pay.

### Acknowledge Founder Judgment And Time Horizon

Some worthwhile problems are only recognizable as such over years. Founders with deep domain insight may see value others miss. This is legitimate but raises personal risk and requires longer runway. This skill does not replace professional advice for problems with regulatory, safety, or legal dimensions.

## Common Traps

### Building For A Mild Annoyance

A complaint is not a market. Severity and willingness to act must be established, not assumed.

### Assuming Frequency Equals Value

A daily trivial annoyance may still not justify a paid solution if the workaround is cheap and acceptable.

### Ignoring Existing Workarounds

If customers already cope adequately, a new solution must be dramatically better to displace the habit.

### Falling For Vocal Minorities

A loud group complaining online may be small, unrepresentative, or unwilling to pay. Verify with behavior, not noise.

### Solving A Problem No One Recognizes

Latent problems can work, but only with realistic expectations about education cost and sales cycle.

### Confusing The Founder's Pain With The Customer's

The founder may feel the problem deeply while the target customer does not, or does not enough to pay.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Severity is scored on financial, time, risk, and emotional dimensions, not merely confirmed to exist.
- [ ] Frequency and recurrence are combined with severity to estimate total pain.
- [ ] Existing spending, workarounds, and coping behavior are identified as evidence the problem is worth solving.
- [ ] The problem is prioritized by customers against their other demands.
- [ ] The venture is honestly classified as painkiller or vitamin, with matching acquisition expectations.
- [ ] Whether the problem is recognized or latent is established, with implications for education cost.
- [ ] The suffering segment is reachable and holds budget.
- [ ] Vocal complaints are verified against actual behavior and willingness to pay.
- [ ] Founder pain is distinguished from customer pain.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids declaring a problem worth solving without severity, frequency, and commitment evidence.
