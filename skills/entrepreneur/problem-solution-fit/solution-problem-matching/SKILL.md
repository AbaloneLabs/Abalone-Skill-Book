# Solution Problem Matching

A good problem and a good solution do not automatically combine into a good business. Founders often build a competent solution to the wrong problem, to a partial problem, or to the problem they imagined rather than the one customers actually pay to remove. Solution-problem matching is the discipline of checking that the thing being built maps tightly to the pain that drives purchase, and that the gap between the two is understood rather than assumed.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "does this product solve the right problem", "is my solution a good fit for the customer need", "why are customers not buying when the problem is real", or "how should an entrepreneur align product with pain". The goal is to prevent the agent from praising a solution in isolation while ignoring whether it removes the pain that matters.

## Core Rules

### Restate The Problem In The Customer's Words

Before judging fit, write the problem exactly as customers describe it, including the language they use, the moment it occurs, and the loss it causes. A solution matched to a founder's paraphrase is often matched to the wrong thing.

Capture:

- the trigger situation;
- the immediate consequence;
- the words the customer uses;
- what they have already tried;
- what outcome they actually want.

Match the solution to this restated problem, not to an abstract category.

### Identify The Core Versus Peripheral Pain

Most problems have a center and a periphery. The core pain is what drives the purchase; the peripheral pains are annoyances that surround it. A solution that addresses the periphery while leaving the core untouched will not sell, however polished it is.

Ask which specific aspect of the problem the customer would pay most to remove, and confirm the solution targets that aspect.

### Map Each Solution Element To A Pain It Removes

For every feature or capability in the proposed solution, trace it to a specific pain. If a feature cannot be tied to a pain the customer recognizes, it is either a guess or founder preference.

This mapping exposes:

- features that solve nothing the customer cares about;
- pains that no feature addresses;
- mismatches where the solution is more elaborate than the pain requires.

### Check The Solution Removes The Loss, Not Just The Symptom

Customers pay to remove loss: lost time, lost money, lost deals, lost reputation, lost sleep. A solution that soothes a symptom without removing the underlying loss may feel helpful but fail to justify its price.

Trace the solution to the measurable loss it prevents, not only to the activity it supports.

### Confirm The Solution Fits The Customer's Real Workflow

A correct solution in the wrong workflow fails. Customers adopt tools that fit how they already work, who they collaborate with, and what they are allowed to change. A solution that requires them to reorganize their day, switch systems, or break an existing integration faces adoption friction far beyond its feature merit.

Ask:

- where in the current workflow the solution enters;
- who else must cooperate;
- what habit or tool it replaces;
- what switching cost the customer faces.

### Distinguish Better From Different-Enough

A solution that is merely better at the same job may not displace an incumbent, because switching costs and habit protect the status quo. To win, the solution often needs to be different enough that the customer perceives a category change, not an incremental upgrade.

Assess whether the improvement clears the switching-cost threshold, not just whether it is technically superior.

### Validate Fit With Behavior, Not Feature Agreement

Customers may agree a feature sounds useful and still not use it. Fit is proven when customers adopt, return, and pay, not when they nod during a demo.

Watch for:

- repeated use without prompting;
- willingness to integrate the solution into real work;
- referral or internal advocacy;
- payment or deposit before full delivery.

### Acknowledge That Fit Evolves

Early fit is often narrow: a specific segment, a specific use case, a specific moment. Solution-problem matching is not a one-time check. As the product and market develop, re-test whether the solution still maps to the pain that drives the current customer base.

This skill does not replace professional advice where the solution touches regulated, safety-critical, or legally consequential outcomes.

## Common Traps

### Solving The Imagined Problem

Founders build for an elegant version of the problem and miss the messy version customers actually experience.

### Feature Rich, Pain Poor

A broad feature set can hide the fact that no single feature removes the core pain customers pay to eliminate.

### Incremental Improvement Below The Switching Threshold

Being better is not enough if the improvement does not overcome habit, integration, and switching cost.

### Confusing Activity With Outcome

A solution that supports the activity around a problem without changing the outcome leaves the loss intact.

### Assuming A Demo Reaction Means Fit

Excitement during a walkthrough rarely predicts adoption. Behavior after the founder leaves is what matters.

### Drift As The Product Grows

Fit established early can erode as features accumulate and the product serves a blur of needs instead of a sharp pain.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The problem is restated in the customer's own words, including trigger, consequence, and desired outcome.
- [ ] Core pain is distinguished from peripheral pains, and the solution targets the core.
- [ ] Every solution element is mapped to a specific recognized pain.
- [ ] The solution is traced to the loss it prevents, not only the symptom it soothes.
- [ ] Workflow fit, collaboration requirements, and switching costs are considered.
- [ ] The improvement is assessed against the switching threshold, not only against technical superiority.
- [ ] Fit is validated through adoption, repeat use, and payment, not feature agreement.
- [ ] Narrow early fit is acknowledged and re-tested as the product and market evolve.
- [ ] Features that solve no recognized customer pain are flagged for removal.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids declaring fit based on demo reactions or feature richness alone.
