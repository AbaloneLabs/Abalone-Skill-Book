# Co-Founder Agreements

A co-founder agreement is the formal structure that governs the partnership: who owns what, how decisions are made, what happens if someone leaves, and how disputes resolve. Trust between founders is essential, but trust without structure is fragile, because circumstances change, people leave, and disagreements arise that trust alone cannot resolve. The agreement exists not because founders expect conflict but because it makes the hard cases manageable if they occur. Skipping or postponing the agreement, or leaving it vague, turns foreseeable situations, a founder's departure, a dispute over direction, a change in commitment, into crises that can destroy the venture.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "do we need a co-founder agreement", "how do we structure co-founder equity", "what should our founder agreement cover", or "how should an entrepreneur formalize a co-founder relationship". The goal is to prevent the agent from relying on trust without structure and from leaving the hard cases unaddressed.

## Core Rules

### Put The Agreement In Writing Early

An agreement discussed but not written is an agreement that drifts. Put the co-founder arrangement in writing early, while alignment is high and circumstances are simple, because waiting until a hard case arises means negotiating under pressure, when interests have diverged. Early written agreements are easier and fairer than late ones.

### Use Vesting To Align Equity With Contribution

Equity should vest over time, so that founders earn their ownership through sustained contribution rather than receiving it upfront. Vesting, typically over four years with a one-year cliff, protects the venture and the remaining founders if a co-founder leaves early, because unvested equity returns to the pool. Without vesting, a departing founder can keep equity they did not earn through contribution, which is unfair and damaging.

### Address Departure And Forfeiture Explicitly

Define what happens to equity if a founder leaves, voluntarily or involuntarily: what vests, what is forfeited, what is subject to repurchase, and on what timeline. Leaving these provisions vague guarantees disputes when a departure occurs. Address the scenarios explicitly, including the painful ones, so the rules are clear before they are needed.

### Define Decision Rights And Deadlock Resolution

Specify how decisions are made: who decides what, when consensus is required, and how disagreements between equal partners, deadlock, are resolved. Deadlock between equal co-founders can paralyze the venture. Define a resolution mechanism, such as a tie-breaker, a buy-sell provision, or a defined authority, before deadlock occurs.

### Clarify Roles And Areas Of Authority

Clarify each founder's role and areas of authority, so that day-to-day decisions can be made without constant negotiation, while major decisions remain shared. Ambiguity about who owns what creates either overlap and conflict or gaps and dropped work. Clear roles let the venture move while preserving shared control of major decisions.

### Address Intellectual Property And Confidentiality

Ensure that intellectual property created for the venture is assigned to the venture, not held by individual founders, and that confidentiality obligations are clear. IP ambiguity, where a founder's work might belong to them personally, creates risk that surfaces during due diligence or disputes. Address IP assignment and confidentiality in the agreement.

### Plan For Changes In Commitment And Circumstances

Circumstances change: a founder's commitment may waver, personal situations may shift, new opportunities may arise. The agreement should anticipate changes in commitment and circumstances, including provisions for reduced involvement, leaves, or changes in role, so that the partnership can adapt without crisis.

### Handle New Equity And Dilution

As the venture grows and raises capital, equity gets diluted and new shares are issued. The agreement should address how new equity, option pools, and future rounds affect the founders' ownership, so that dilution is expected and managed rather than surprising. Align expectations about dilution before it occurs.

### Revisit The Agreement As The Venture Evolves

A founder agreement that fits the start may not fit later stages: roles shift, contributions change, new partners or investors arrive. Revisit the agreement as the venture evolves, amending it to reflect current reality, rather than letting it drift from the actual relationship. A stale agreement misgoverns the partnership.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Co-founder agreements involve equity, contracts, IP, and fiduciary obligations with significant legal implications that require professional legal advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Trust Without Structure

Relying on trust alone leaves foreseeable hard cases unmanaged.

### No Vesting

Upfront equity grants let departing founders keep ownership they did not earn.

### Vague Departure Provisions

Unaddressed departure scenarios become disputes when they occur.

### Unresolved Deadlock

Equal partners without a deadlock mechanism can paralyze the venture.

### IP Ambiguity

Unclear IP assignment creates risk during due diligence or disputes.

### Stale Agreement

An agreement that no longer reflects the relationship misgoverns it.
