# Data Privacy Basics

Data privacy has moved from a niche concern to a baseline obligation for almost every digital venture, yet many founders treat it as a footnote to be added later. Privacy laws apply from the moment a venture collects personal data, and they govern notice, consent, data subject rights, security, transfers, and vendor relationships. Retrofitting privacy under regulatory pressure is expensive and sometimes impossible, and the reputational cost of a privacy failure can exceed the legal one. Privacy basics are the discipline of building respectful, compliant data practices from the start.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what privacy laws apply to my startup", "do I need a privacy policy", "how should I handle user data", "what is data privacy compliance", or "how should an entrepreneur approach data privacy". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating privacy as deferrable and from neglecting the practices that keep the venture compliant and trustworthy.

## Core Rules

### Identify Which Privacy Laws Apply

Privacy obligations depend on who the venture's users are, where they are located, and what data is collected. Laws such as the EU's GDPR, California's CCPA/CPRA, and other regional rules apply based on user location, not the venture's location. Identify which laws apply, because assuming none do is a common and costly error.

### Provide Clear Notice Through A Privacy Policy

A privacy policy, clearly disclosing what data is collected, why, how it is used, with whom it is shared, and what rights users have, is a baseline requirement under most privacy laws. The policy must be accurate, accessible, and matched to actual practices. A policy that does not match practice is both non-compliant and deceptive.

### Obtain Meaningful Consent Where Required

Some data processing requires consent, and consent must be meaningful: informed, specific, freely given, and withdrawable. Pre-ticked boxes, bundled consents, and dark patterns do not constitute valid consent. Understand when consent is required and obtain it properly, because invalid consent is no consent.

### Honor Data Subject Rights

Privacy laws grant individuals rights over their data: access, correction, deletion, portability, and objection to processing. The venture must be able to respond to these requests within statutory timelines. Building the capability to honor these rights, even simply, is part of compliant data practice.

### Implement Reasonable Security Measures

Privacy laws require reasonable security measures appropriate to the data's sensitivity and the venture's size. Basic measures, access control, encryption, vendor management, and incident response, establish a baseline. Neglecting security is both a privacy violation and a breach risk.

### Manage Data Minimization And Retention

Collect only the data needed for stated purposes, and retain it only as long as necessary. Data minimization and retention limits reduce risk, cost, and regulatory exposure. Hoarding data "in case it is useful" creates liability without value.

### Govern Vendor And Processor Relationships

When vendors process user data on the venture's behalf, proper data processing agreements and due diligence are required. The venture remains responsible for how its vendors handle data. Neglecting vendor governance transfers risk without transferring responsibility.

### Handle Cross-Border Data Transfers Carefully

Transferring personal data across borders, especially out of regions with strict rules, triggers additional requirements. Understand the rules for cross-border transfers and use appropriate mechanisms. Ignorance here creates significant exposure for international ventures.

### Plan For Breach Response

Data breaches happen, and privacy laws often require notification within tight timelines. Have a basic breach response plan: how to detect, contain, assess, notify, and remediate. Planning under the pressure of an actual breach is far harder than planning in advance.

### Map Data Flows End To End

Privacy compliance requires understanding where personal data enters, where it is stored, who can access it, where it travels, and when it is deleted. Map the full data flow from collection through processing, sharing, and retention. Many privacy failures come from data flows the venture never fully traced, such as a vendor sub-processor or a analytics tool exfiltrating data the founder did not realize was being sent. A current data map is the foundation of accurate notice, minimization, and security decisions.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Data privacy involves jurisdiction-specific legal requirements and significant liability. Use qualified privacy counsel and, where needed, security expertise. This skill provides foundational understanding only and does not replace professional legal advice.

## Common Traps

### Assuming No Privacy Laws Apply

Obligations follow users' locations, not the venture's.

### Policy-Practice Mismatch

A privacy policy that does not match actual practices is non-compliant and deceptive.

### Invalid Consent

Pre-ticked boxes and bundled consents do not constitute valid consent.

### Data Hoarding

Retaining data "just in case" creates liability without value.

### Neglected Vendor Governance

The venture remains responsible for how its vendors handle user data.

### No Breach Response Plan

Improvising under breach pressure leads to missed notification deadlines and greater harm.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Applicable privacy laws are identified based on user locations and data types.
- [ ] A clear, accurate privacy policy is in place and matched to actual practices.
- [ ] Consent is obtained meaningfully where required.
- [ ] The venture can honor data subject rights within statutory timelines.
- [ ] Reasonable security measures appropriate to the data are implemented.
- [ ] Data minimization and retention limits are practiced.
- [ ] Vendor and processor relationships are governed with proper agreements.
- [ ] Cross-border data transfers use appropriate mechanisms.
- [ ] A basic breach response plan exists.
- [ ] Privacy obligations and practices are reviewed by qualified privacy counsel.
