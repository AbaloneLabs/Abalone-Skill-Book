# Territory, Compensation, And Quota Planning

Territory, compensation, and quota are the three levers that determine whether the sales organization's effort is pointed at the right accounts, in the right amounts, with the right incentives. Territory design decides who owns which accounts; quota decides how much each rep is expected to produce; compensation decides how reps are paid for producing it. The three must be internally consistent and aligned with the company's economics, or the sales motion works against itself. The judgment problem is that each lever is easy to set in isolation and hard to set coherently: a territory carved for fairness can leave the best accounts orphaned, a quota set from the top down can exceed what the territory can produce, and a commission plan that motivates revenue can quietly destroy margin or misalign with the company's strategic priorities.

Founders tend to miss several things here. They set quotas by dividing the company revenue target by the number of reps, which has no relationship to what a territory can actually produce and guarantees missed quotas or windfall overpayment. They design compensation by copying a template without thinking through what behavior it rewards, then are surprised when reps optimize for the plan rather than for the company. They carve territories by count or alphabet rather than by potential, creating deep inequity in earning opportunity that drives rep churn. And they treat these plans as set-once, when in fact they must be revisited as the company, market, and team scale. The harm is missed targets, rep attrition, margin erosion, and incentive structures that reward the wrong deals.

## Core Rules

### Ground Quotas In Rep Capacity And Territory Potential

A quota is a prediction of what a rep can produce in a given territory, not a division of the company's revenue target. Ground quotas in the productive capacity of a rep (how many deals they can realistically work and close in a period) and in the potential of the territory (the addressable accounts and their likely value). A quota that exceeds territory potential sets the rep up to fail and drives attrition; a quota far below potential leaves revenue uncaptured. Build the quota from the bottom up, then reconcile to the company target; if the bottom-up sum falls short of the target, the gap is a coverage or capacity problem to solve, not a quota to inflate.

### Design Territories By Potential, Not By Count

The purpose of a territory is to give each rep a fair and sufficient earning opportunity, which requires balancing potential, not count. Two territories with the same number of accounts can have vastly different potential if one contains the largest buyers. Design territories by addressable potential, weighted by account size, industry, and likelihood to buy, so that reps have comparable opportunities to hit quota. Re-balance as accounts are won, lost, or added, because a territory that was balanced at assignment drifts as the market moves. Fairness in territory design is about opportunity, not headcount.

### Align Commission Plans With The Company's Strategic Priorities

A commission plan is a set of incentives, and reps will optimize for the plan as written, not for the company's intentions. Design the plan so that the behaviors it rewards are the behaviors the company needs: reward net-new logos if growth is the priority, reward expansion if net revenue retention is the priority, reward multi-year deals if predictability is the priority. Every accelerator, clawback, threshold, and cap changes behavior; design each deliberately and trace its likely effect before publishing. The test of a commission plan is to ask, for each clause, what behavior it will produce and whether that behavior serves the company.

### Balance Motivation With Margin And Unit Economics

Commission must motivate the rep without destroying the unit economics of the deal. A plan that pays too richly erodes margin and can make low-margin segments uneconomic to sell into; a plan that pays too thinly fails to motivate and drives rep attrition. Anchor commission rates to the unit economics of the business: what margin remains after cost of goods, cost to serve, and fully loaded sales cost, and ensure the plan preserves a healthy contribution. Pay special attention to accelerators and to deals that combine discounting with high commission, because those are where margin quietly disappears.

### Revisit Plans As The Organization Scales

Territory, quota, and compensation plans that work at five reps often fail at twenty-five, because the assumptions behind them, market coverage, rep capacity, deal complexity, change. Schedule a review at each inflection of scale and at each change in strategy or market. The questions to revisit: are territories still balanced by potential, are quotas still grounded in capacity, does the commission plan still reward the company's current priorities. Plans that are not revisited become misaligned with reality and produce outcomes no one intended.

### Make The Plan Simple Enough To Be Predictable For The Rep

A commission plan that a rep cannot calculate in their head is a plan that will not motivate predictably. If a rep cannot estimate what they will earn for a given deal, they cannot choose which deal to prioritize, and the incentive fails to direct behavior. Favor simplicity: clear rates, clear thresholds, clear accelerators, and a statement the rep can simulate. Complexity that exists to handle edge cases should live in policy, not in the core earning formula. A plan that requires a spreadsheet to understand has already failed part of its purpose.

### Anticipate And Govern The Gaming Of Incentives

Any incentive system will be gamed; the question is whether the gaming is anticipated and bounded. Reps will shift deals across periods to hit accelerators, split deals to maximize payout, discount to win volume, and pursue easy deals over strategic ones if the plan rewards it. Anticipate these behaviors in the plan design: use clawbacks for churned deals, rules of engagement for split credit, and floors on deal margin. Governing gaming is not distrust; it is acknowledging that people respond rationally to the incentives they are given.

## Common Traps

### Quotas Set By Dividing The Company Target

Dividing the revenue target by headcount produces quotas unrelated to territory potential and guarantees either widespread misses or windfall overpayment. The trap is the apparent simplicity of top-down allocation. The cost is rep attrition from unattainable quotas or margin erosion from overpayment.

### Territories Carved By Count Or Alphabet

Equal account counts with unequal potential create deep inequity in earning opportunity. The trap is that count-based carving looks fair on a spreadsheet. The cost is that reps in weak territories churn while reps in rich territories underwork their advantage.

### Copied Commission Plans Rewarding The Wrong Behavior

A plan copied from another company rewards the behaviors that company needed, which may not be this company's priorities. The trap is the apparent professionalism of a standard plan. The cost is a sales motion optimized for the wrong outcomes.

### Accelerators And Discounts Compounding To Erase Margin

Rich accelerators combined with heavy discounting can produce deals that pay high commission on low margin. The trap is that each lever looks reasonable in isolation. The cost is margin that disappears on the deals that close fastest.

### Plans Never Revisited At Scale

Plans that worked at one stage persist after the assumptions behind them change. The trap is the comfort of not reopening a settled question. The cost is misaligned incentives that produce unintended outcomes as the organization grows.

### Complexity That Obscures Earning Logic

A plan too complex for a rep to predict will not direct behavior, because the rep cannot choose the higher-earning path. The trap is that complexity feels rigorous. The cost is an incentive that fails to incentivize.

### Ungoverned Gaming Of Splits, Timing, And Discounts

Without rules of engagement and clawbacks, reps rationally shift deals to maximize payout in ways that cost the company. The trap is treating gaming as a character problem rather than an incentive-design problem. The cost is revenue and margin leakage that compounds.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Quotas are built bottom-up from rep capacity and territory potential, then reconciled to the company target.
- [ ] Territories are designed by addressable potential, not by account count, and are re-balanced as the market moves.
- [ ] The commission plan explicitly rewards the company's current strategic priorities (logos, expansion, multi-year, margin).
- [ ] Commission rates preserve healthy contribution margin after cost of goods, cost to serve, and fully loaded sales cost.
- [ ] Territory, quota, and compensation plans are scheduled for review at each inflection of scale and change of strategy.
- [ ] The core earning formula is simple enough for a rep to calculate and simulate without a spreadsheet.
- [ ] Rules of engagement, clawbacks, and margin floors exist to bound anticipated gaming of the plan.
- [ ] For each clause of the plan, the behavior it will produce has been traced and judged to serve the company.
