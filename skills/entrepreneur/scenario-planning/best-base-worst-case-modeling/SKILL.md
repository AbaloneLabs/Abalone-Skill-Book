# Best, Base, And Worst Case Modeling

Best, base, and worst case modeling is the workhorse of startup financial and operational planning, structuring uncertainty into three coherent cases. Its value depends entirely on the cases being genuinely distinct and the worst case being honestly stressed, because the common failure is a worst case that is merely a mildly pessimistic base, leaving the venture unprepared for real downside. Sound three-case modeling is the discipline of building internally consistent cases that span the plausible range, with the worst case stressed enough to inform real decisions.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I build best base worst case models", "what should my worst case look like", "how different should the cases be", "how do I stress test my plan", or "how should an entrepreneur model multiple cases". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing three cases that are barely distinct and from labeling a mild downside as the worst case.

## Core Rules

### Make The Cases Genuinely Distinct

The three cases must represent genuinely different outcomes, not minor variations. The best case reflects things going very well, the base case the realistic central expectation, and the worst case a genuinely adverse outcome. Cases that differ by only a few percent provide little insight into the range of possibilities.

### Ensure Internal Consistency In Each Case

Each case must be internally consistent: its assumptions must cohere across revenue, costs, hiring, and cash. A case where revenue falls but costs keep rising without explanation is incoherent. Internal consistency makes each case a plausible, analyzable future.

### Stress The Worst Case Honestly

The worst case should reflect a genuinely adverse outcome: slow customer acquisition, high churn, delayed fundraising, cost overruns, or competitive pressure. The common failure is a worst case that is only mildly worse than base, which hides the real downside. Stress the worst case enough to reveal the venture's fragility.

### Avoid The Optimistic Base Case Trap

Base cases are often, unconsciously, optimistic, because founders believe in the venture. Calibrate the base case to realistic central expectations, validated against evidence, not aspiration. An optimistic base case makes the whole three-case exercise misleading.

### Connect The Cases To Runway And Survival

The most important output of three-case modeling is often the worst case's impact on runway and survival. Connect each case to how long the venture survives and what interventions, cost cuts, fundraising, each would require. Survival implications make the cases actionable.

### Model The Drivers That Differentiate The Cases

The cases differ because specific drivers, conversion rates, sales cycles, retention, fundraising timing, take different values. Model these drivers explicitly, so the cases are transparent and adjustable. Driver-based cases can be updated as evidence arrives.

### Use The Cases To Pre-Plan Responses

For the base and worst cases especially, pre-plan the responses: what cost cuts, pivots, or fundraising actions each would trigger, and at what thresholds. Pre-planning makes response faster and less emotional when a case materializes. Cases without pre-planned responses are incomplete.

### Revisit The Cases As Evidence Accumulates

As evidence arrives, update the cases: the base case should shift toward validated reality, and the range may narrow or widen. Revisit the cases regularly, because static cases lose relevance. Updating keeps the modeling honest and useful.

### Avoid False Precision In The Cases

Three-case modeling still operates under uncertainty, and the cases are plausible ranges, not precise forecasts. Avoid attaching false precision, because exact numbers on uncertain futures create false confidence. Use the cases to understand range and direction, not false exactness.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Financial modeling informs consequential decisions about runway, fundraising, and commitments. Use qualified financial advisors where the stakes warrant, and recognize modeling informs, not determines, choices. This skill provides framing, not financial advice.

## Common Traps

### Cases Barely Distinct

Three cases that differ by only a few percent provide little insight.

### Mild Downside Labeled As Worst Case

A worst case that is only mildly pessimistic hides the real downside.

### Optimistic Base Case

An aspirational base case makes the whole exercise misleading.

### Inconsistent Assumptions Within A Case

Revenue and costs that do not cohere make a case implausible.

### Cases Without Runway And Survival Analysis

Missing the most important output, the worst case's survival impact.

### Static Cases

Cases built once and not updated lose relevance as evidence accumulates.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The three cases are genuinely distinct, not minor variations.
- [ ] Each case is internally consistent across its assumptions.
- [ ] The worst case is honestly stressed to reveal real downside.
- [ ] The base case is calibrated to realistic, evidence-based expectations, not aspiration.
- [ ] Each case is connected to runway and survival implications.
- [ ] The drivers that differentiate the cases are modeled explicitly.
- [ ] Responses are pre-planned for the base and worst cases.
- [ ] The cases are revisited and updated as evidence accumulates.
- [ ] False precision is avoided in favor of plausible ranges.
- [ ] Consequential financial decisions draw on qualified financial advisors where warranted.
