# Contingency Planning

Contingency planning is the discipline of pre-building specific responses for the material adverse events most likely to threaten the venture, so that when one occurs, the response is fast, calm, and effective rather than improvised under pressure. Startups face a predictable set of threats, funding falling through, a key person leaving, a major customer churning, a security breach, a regulatory action, and improvising the response during the crisis costs time, money, and often the venture itself. Contingency plans convert predictable crises into managed events.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what is contingency planning", "how do I prepare for things going wrong", "what contingency plans do I need", "how do I plan for a funding shortfall", or "how should an entrepreneur prepare for adverse events". The goal is to prevent the agent from leaving the venture to improvise predictable crises and from treating contingency planning as pessimism rather than prudence.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Material Adverse Events Most Likely To Threaten The Venture

Begin by identifying the specific adverse events most likely and most threatening: funding shortfalls, key-person departures, major customer churn, security breaches, regulatory actions, competitive disruption, or founder conflict. Focus on events that are both plausible and material. Generic preparedness is less useful than specific plans for specific threats.

### Build Specific Response Plans For Each Material Event

For each material event, build a specific response plan: what actions would be taken, by whom, in what sequence, and with what resources. Specific plans are actionable; generic intentions are not. The plan should be detailed enough to execute under pressure.

### Define Triggers That Activate Each Contingency

Define the triggers that activate each contingency, the specific signals that indicate the event is materializing, such as a round not closing by a date or revenue falling below a threshold. Triggers enable timely activation rather than denial. Without triggers, contingencies are activated too late.

### Prioritize Plans By Likelihood And Impact

Not every event warrants a detailed plan. Prioritize by likelihood and impact, building detailed plans for high-likelihood, high-impact events and lighter plans or monitoring for lower ones. Prioritization focuses planning effort where it matters most.

### Address The Most Common Existential Threat First

The most common existential threat is cash exhaustion, so the first contingency plan is usually for a funding shortfall or revenue decline: what costs would be cut, what bridge would be sought, what assets would be sold. Address the existential threats first, because their contingency plans are the most valuable.

### Pre-Build The Relationships And Resources Contingencies Require

Many contingencies require relationships or resources that take time to build: bridge lenders, backup suppliers, alternative hires, legal counsel, or crisis communications support. Pre-build these during calm periods, because they cannot be assembled mid-crisis. Pre-built resources make contingencies executable.

### Assign Ownership And Decision Authority For Each Plan

Each contingency plan should have a clear owner and defined decision authority, so that activation does not require debate or delay. Ambiguity about who decides and acts costs critical time in a crisis. Assign ownership in advance.

### Rehearse Or Walk Through The Plans

Where feasible, rehearse or walk through the contingency plans, especially for fast-moving events like security breaches or key-person loss. A walk-through surfaces gaps and builds familiarity, so the plan executes more smoothly under pressure. Unrehearsed plans fail in predictable ways.

### Review And Update Plans As The Venture Evolves

Contingency plans become stale as the venture grows, the team changes, and the threat landscape shifts. Review and update them regularly, at milestones and when circumstances change. Stale plans provide false confidence.

### Separate Contingency Planning From Pessimism

Contingency planning is sometimes resisted as pessimistic or as distracting from execution. Reframe it as resilience engineering: pre-built responses let the venture act fast and calmly when threats materialize, which is the opposite of fatalism. The founder who has thought through the funding-shortfall response can pursue growth confidently, knowing the downside is covered. Contingency planning enables boldness by reducing the cost of adverse outcomes, rather than signaling doubt about the venture's prospects.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Some contingencies, legal, regulatory, security, financial, require professional support to plan and execute. Use qualified advisors for specialized contingency planning. This skill provides framing only and does not replace professional advice.

## Common Traps

### Improvising Predictable Crises

Failing to pre-plan the threats most likely to occur, then improvising under pressure.

### Generic Preparedness

Vague intentions rather than specific, actionable response plans.

### No Activation Triggers

Contingencies activated too late because no signals were defined.

### Resources Not Pre-Built

Bridge lenders, backup suppliers, and counsel cannot be assembled mid-crisis.

### Ambiguous Ownership

Lack of clear decision authority costs critical time in a crisis.

### Stale Plans

Plans that have not been updated provide false confidence.
