# Startup Scenario Planning

Startups operate under deep uncertainty, and a single base-case plan, however carefully built, leaves the venture unprepared when reality diverges. Scenario planning is the discipline of constructing several plausible alternative futures, best, base, and worst, and often additional variants, to understand the range of outcomes, the drivers that distinguish them, and the responses each would require. The goal is not prediction, which is impossible, but preparedness, so that when a scenario materializes, the venture has already thought through the response.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I plan for uncertainty", "what scenarios should I plan for", "how do I do scenario planning", "what if things go better or worse than expected", or "how should an entrepreneur plan for multiple futures". The goal is to prevent the agent from planning only a single base case and from treating uncertainty as something to be hoped away rather than planned for.

## Core Rules

### Build Multiple Plausible Scenarios, Not A Single Plan

Construct several plausible scenarios, typically including best, base, and worst cases, and sometimes additional variants around specific drivers. The scenarios should be genuinely distinct, not minor variations, to expose the range of outcomes. Multiple scenarios reveal what a single plan hides.

### Identify The Key Drivers That Differentiate Scenarios

Scenarios differ because of key drivers, market growth, fundraising success, customer acquisition cost, competitive entry, hiring speed, or regulatory change. Identify the drivers that most differentiate outcomes, because these are what to monitor and influence. Drivers connect scenarios to the real world.

### Make Scenarios Plausible And Internally Consistent

Each scenario must be plausible, not fantasy, and internally consistent, with its assumptions cohering. A best case that assumes everything goes right or a worst case that assumes everything goes wrong is not useful. Plausibility and consistency make scenarios instructive.

### Plan Responses For Each Material Scenario

For each material scenario, plan the response: what the venture would do if that future arrived, including triggers, decisions, and resource shifts. Planning responses in advance is the point, because under pressure the responses are clearer and faster. Scenarios without planned responses are intellectual exercises.

### Define Triggers That Signal Which Scenario Is Unfolding

Define the early signals, the triggers, that indicate which scenario is unfolding, such as revenue falling below a threshold or a round taking longer than expected. Triggers enable timely response rather than denial. Without triggers, scenarios are recognized too late.

### Use Scenarios To Test Decisions For Robustness

Test major decisions against the scenarios: does a decision, such as a hiring commitment or a lease, work under multiple scenarios or only the base case? Decisions robust across scenarios are safer; decisions that work only under one scenario carry hidden risk. Scenario testing exposes fragility.

### Revisit And Update Scenarios Regularly

Scenarios are not built once; they evolve as the venture and the world change. Revisit and update them regularly, at milestones and when evidence shifts. Static scenarios become stale and lose their instructive value.

### Avoid False Precision In Scenarios

Scenarios are plausible narratives, not precise forecasts. Avoid false precision, because attaching exact numbers to deeply uncertain futures creates false confidence. Use ranges and qualitative distinctions where precision is unwarranted.

### Connect Scenarios To Runway And Resources

Connect scenarios to runway and resource implications, because the worst case's cash impact is often the most consequential planning output. Knowing how long the venture survives under each scenario informs fundraising and cost decisions. Resource implications make scenarios actionable.

### Use Scenarios To Identify Early Warning Indicators

Beyond triggers for response, scenarios should identify leading indicators that signal which future is becoming more likely before it fully arrives. Leading indicators might be softening demand signals, lengthening sales cycles, churn creep, or competitor hiring patterns. Monitoring leading indicators, not just lagging metrics, gives the venture earlier visibility into which scenario is unfolding and more time to adapt. Scenario planning that monitors only lagging outcomes arrives too late to act.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Strategic Judgment

Scenario planning operates under deep uncertainty and informs consequential decisions. Seek diverse input from team, advisors, and board, and recognize that scenarios inform, not determine, choices. This skill provides framing, not prediction.

## Common Traps

### Single Base-Case Plan

Planning one outcome leaves the venture unprepared for divergence.

### Scenarios As Minor Variations

Scenarios that are not genuinely distinct fail to expose the range of outcomes.

### No Planned Responses

Scenarios without response plans are intellectual exercises, not preparedness.

### Missing Triggers

Without early signals, scenarios are recognized too late to respond.

### False Precision

Exact numbers on deeply uncertain futures create false confidence.

### Static Scenarios

Scenarios built once become stale as the venture and world change.
