# Advisory Board Design

An advisory board is a structured group of advisors assembled to help the venture on a sustained basis, distinct from individual advisors who help on specific issues. A well-designed advisory board fills gaps in the founder's knowledge and network, provides coordinated perspective across the venture's key challenges, and signals seriousness to investors and partners. A poorly designed one is a list of names that meets rarely, advises generically, and adds bureaucracy without value. Advisory board design is the discipline of defining the board's purpose, choosing members to cover the venture's real gaps, and structuring it to produce sustained, useful engagement.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should I form an advisory board", "how do I design an advisory board", "who should be on my advisory board", or "how should an entrepreneur set up an advisory board". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending advisory boards as decoration and from ignoring the purpose and structure that make them useful.

## Core Rules

### Define The Advisory Board's Purpose First

Before choosing members, define what the advisory board is for: filling domain gaps, providing stage experience, opening networks, or guiding specific strategic challenges. A board without a defined purpose becomes a generic group that advises on whatever arises, which usually means little of consequence. Purpose drives composition.

### Cover The Venture's Real Gaps

Choose members to cover the specific gaps the venture faces: a domain the founder lacks, a stage the founder has not navigated, a network the venture needs, or a perspective that balances the founder's biases. Map the gaps, then select members to fill them, rather than assembling impressive people whose strengths overlap the founder's own.

### Prefer Depth And Engagement Over Prestige

An advisory board's value comes from members who engage deeply with the venture's real challenges, not from the impressiveness of their titles. Prefer members who will invest time, understand the venture specifically, and give candid, informed guidance, over more prestigious names who contribute generic advice. Depth beats prestige for advisory work.

### Keep The Board Small And Coordinated

A small advisory board, three to five engaged members, is more useful than a large one. Large boards are hard to coordinate, dilute engagement, and tend toward generic advice. Keep the board small enough that members are genuinely engaged and that the founder can sustain meaningful relationships with each.

### Structure Engagement To Produce Value

Define how the advisory board engages: meeting cadence, the materials they receive, the questions they address, and the access they have to the venture. Structure engagement so that members can give informed, specific guidance, rather than showing up to occasional meetings with no context. Structure determines whether the board produces value or ceremony.

### Distinguish Advisory Board From Governance Board

An advisory board advises; it does not govern. Distinguish it from a board of directors, which has fiduciary authority and legal duties, and ensure members understand their role is advisory, not decision-making. Confusing the two creates confusion about authority and liability. Keep the advisory role clear.

### Compensate Consistently And Fairly

Compensate advisory board members consistently, typically through small equity grants subject to vesting, reflecting their sustained engagement. Inconsistent or unclear compensation breeds resentment among members and consumes equity without clear logic. Fair, consistent compensation signals that the engagement is real.

### Set Terms And Rotate Membership

Set terms for advisory board members, so that membership is renewed based on continued relevance and engagement rather than held indefinitely. Rotation keeps the board aligned with the venture's evolving needs and provides a graceful way to wind down relationships that no longer serve. Permanent membership drifts toward irrelevance.

### Use The Board Actively, Not Ceremonially

An advisory board adds value only if the founder actively uses it: bringing real questions, sharing real information, and engaging with the guidance received. A board used ceremonially, for appearances or occasional updates, delivers little. The founder's active engagement determines whether the board earns its equity.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Advisory boards involve equity, confidentiality, fiduciary distinctions, and contractual obligations with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Board As Decoration

A list of impressive names that meets rarely and advises generically.

### Members Covering The Founder's Strengths

Overlapping expertise that leaves the venture's real gaps unfilled.

### Prestige Over Engagement

Famous members who contribute little specific guidance.

### Too Large To Coordinate

Big boards dilute engagement and tend toward generic advice.

### Advisory Confused With Governance

Unclear roles create confusion about authority and liability.

### Ceremonial Use

A board used for appearances delivers little value for its equity.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The advisory board's purpose is defined before members are chosen.
- [ ] Members are selected to cover the venture's real gaps.
- [ ] Depth and engagement are preferred over prestige.
- [ ] The board is small enough, three to five, to stay coordinated and engaged.
- [ ] Engagement is structured to produce informed, specific guidance.
- [ ] The advisory role is distinguished from governance authority.
- [ ] Compensation is consistent and fair, with vesting.
- [ ] Terms and rotation keep membership aligned with evolving needs.
- [ ] The founder uses the board actively, not ceremonially.
- [ ] Equity, confidentiality, fiduciary, and contractual implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
