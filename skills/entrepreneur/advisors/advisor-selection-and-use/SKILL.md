# Advisor Selection And Use

Advisors are experienced people who help the founder navigate decisions, domains, and networks the venture lacks internally. Used well, they compress learning, open doors, and prevent mistakes; used poorly, they add noise, consume time, and create false confidence. The difference lies in selection and use: purposeful advisors chosen for specific needs, with clear expectations and honest engagement, versus names collected for credibility who contribute little. Advisor selection is the discipline of identifying what the venture actually needs, vetting advisors against those needs, and engaging them in ways that produce real value.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should I get advisors", "how do I choose advisors", "what advisors does my startup need", or "how should an entrepreneur use advisors". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending advisors for appearances and from treating advisor relationships as automatic value rather than as engagements to be managed.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Specific Need Before Seeking An Advisor

Advisors are useful when they address a specific need: domain expertise the venture lacks, a network the founder needs access to, experience with a stage or challenge the venture faces. Identify the need before seeking an advisor, because an advisor without a defined purpose becomes a generic sounding board that adds little. Purpose drives selection.

### Vet Advisors On Relevant Experience, Not General Prestige

An advisor's value comes from relevant experience, having navigated the specific challenge, market, or stage the venture faces, not from general prestige or fame. Vet advisors on the relevance and recency of their experience, and on whether they actually engage, rather than on the impressiveness of their title. Prestige without relevance is decoration.

### Check How Advisors Engage With Other Founders

The best predictor of how an advisor will engage is how they have engaged with other founders. Ask for references and speak with founders the advisor has worked with about responsiveness, quality of advice, and whether the advisor delivered real value. Advisors who are impressive in conversation but disengaged in practice waste the founder's time and equity.

### Define Expectations And Engagement Explicitly

Define what the founder expects from the advisor, how often they will engage, what questions they will help with, and what the advisor receives in return. Explicit expectations prevent the common pattern where an advisor signs on, takes equity, and then is never heard from. Clear engagement terms make the relationship real.

### Avoid Collecting Advisors For Credibility

A long list of impressive advisors may look good in a pitch deck, but if those advisors do not actively help, the list misleads investors and consumes equity. Avoid collecting advisors for credibility alone, because the appearance of support that is not real damages trust when discovered. Prefer a few engaged advisors over many decorative ones.

### Balance Advisor Input With The Founder's Judgment

Advisors provide input; the founder makes decisions. Balance advisor perspectives, which are valuable but partial and shaped by their own experience, with the founder's own judgment and the venture's specific context. Uncritically following advisor advice, or conversely ignoring it, both waste the relationship. Use advisors to inform decisions, not to make them.

### Beware Conflicts Of Interest

Advisors may have conflicts: they may advise competitors, hold interests that color their guidance, or steer the venture toward their own investments or networks. Be aware of potential conflicts, and weigh advice accordingly. A conflicted advisor is not necessarily useless, but their advice must be interpreted in light of their interests.

### Compensate Advisors Fairly And Consistently

Advisor compensation, typically a small equity grant subject to vesting, should reflect the engagement and be consistent across advisors with similar roles. Compensate fairly for real engagement, with vesting that protects the venture if the advisor disengages, and avoid over-generous grants to advisors who contribute little, because equity is scarce.

### Rotate Advisors As Needs Change

The advisors a venture needs at the idea stage differ from those it needs at scaling. Rotate advisors as the venture's needs change, bringing in new advisors for new challenges and winding down relationships that no longer serve, rather than holding a static advisor set that becomes irrelevant. Advisor relationships should evolve with the venture.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Advisor relationships involve equity, confidentiality, and contractual obligations with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Advisors For Credibility

Names collected for appearances mislead investors and consume equity without value.

### Prestige Over Relevance

Famous advisors without relevant experience add little to specific challenges.

### Undefined Engagement

Advisors who sign on without clear expectations often disengage and deliver nothing.

### Uncritical Acceptance Of Advice

Following advisor input without applying the founder's judgment leads to poor decisions.

### Ignoring Conflicts

Conflicted advice, taken without awareness, can steer the venture against its interests.

### Static Advisor Set

Holding advisors that no longer fit the venture's current needs wastes equity and attention.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The specific need an advisor would address is identified before seeking one.
- [ ] Advisors are vetted on relevant and recent experience, not general prestige.
- [ ] References from other founders confirm how advisors actually engage.
- [ ] Expectations and engagement terms are defined explicitly.
- [ ] Advisors are not collected for credibility alone.
- [ ] Advisor input is balanced with the founder's own judgment.
- [ ] Potential conflicts of interest are considered when weighing advice.
- [ ] Advisor compensation is fair, consistent, and subject to vesting.
- [ ] Advisors are rotated as the venture's needs change.
- [ ] Equity, confidentiality, and contractual implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
