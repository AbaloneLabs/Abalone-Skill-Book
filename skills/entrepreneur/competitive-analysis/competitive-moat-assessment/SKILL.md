# Competitive Moat Assessment

A moat is a structural advantage that protects a business from competition over time. Most early startups have no moat; they have a head start, founder effort, or novelty, all of which erode. The question is not whether a moat exists today but whether one can be built, what kind, how long it will take, and how durable it will be once built. Founders who claim defensibility without naming its source are usually claiming "we will execute better", which is not a moat. Honest moat assessment forces specificity about what will actually keep competitors from taking your customers.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "does my startup have a moat", "how defensible is my business", "what is my competitive advantage", or "how should an entrepreneur think about defensibility". The goal is to prevent the agent from accepting vague claims of advantage and from confusing temporary lead with durable protection.

## Core Rules

### Name The Specific Source Of The Moat

A moat must rest on a named structural source. Enumerate the realistic sources and identify which, if any, apply.

Common moat sources:

- network effects, where value grows with users;
- switching costs and lock-in;
- economies of scale or scope;
- proprietary data or technology;
- brand, trust, and reputation;
- regulatory licenses or patents;
- distribution advantages and exclusive relationships;
- learning curves and operational excellence;
- community or ecosystem effects.

"We will be better" is execution, not a moat. Specify the structural mechanism.

### Distinguish Head Start From Moat

A first-mover advantage, founder energy, and novelty are temporary. They buy time to build a real moat but are not themselves protection. Confusing a head start with a moat leads to complacency just as competitors arrive.

Ask: what protects us after the novelty fades and competitors copy the visible product?

### Assess Moat Strength And Durability

Not all moats are equal. Judge each by how hard it is to replicate, how long it takes to erode, and what would break it. A weak moat slows competitors briefly; a strong moat makes copying uneconomic.

For each candidate moat, ask:

- how much time and capital would a capable competitor need to replicate it;
- what technology, regulation, or behavior change could erode it;
- does it strengthen or weaken as the business scales.

### Match The Moat To The Business Model

Different businesses support different moats. Marketplaces build network effects; enterprise software builds switching costs; data businesses build proprietary data moats; regulated industries build license moats. Ensure the moat you claim is compatible with how your business actually creates and captures value.

A moat that does not fit the model is aspirational, not structural.

### Recognize That Moats Are Built, Not Declared

Early ventures rarely start with a moat. They earn one through accumulated users, data, relationships, brand, or scale over time. The strategic question is whether the current plan builds a moat as a byproduct of growth, or whether growth leaves the business as exposed as it started.

Ask what the venture accumulates that becomes harder to replicate over time.

### Watch For Moat Erosion

Moats decay. Technology shifts erode proprietary tech, regulation changes erode license moats, user behavior shifts erode network effects, and competitors find workarounds. Assess not only whether a moat exists but what would erode it and how fast.

### Evaluate Counter-Positioning And Incumbent Disruption

Sometimes a moat comes from a new business model that incumbents cannot adopt without cannibalizing themselves. Counter-positioning can protect a newcomer even without traditional scale. Assess whether your model creates a dilemma for incumbents that they cannot easily resolve.

### Be Honest About The No-Moat Case

Many viable businesses never build a strong moat and instead compete on execution, niche focus, or speed. This can work but carries different risk and valuation implications. Be honest if the answer is that no durable moat is likely, rather than inventing one to feel defensible.

### Acknowledge Risk And Professional Advice

Some moats, especially patents, regulatory licenses, and exclusive contracts, carry legal and intellectual-property implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### "We Will Execute Better" As A Moat

Execution is necessary but not structural; competitors can out-execute you.

### Confusing Head Start With Defensibility

Novelty and first-mover status erode as competitors arrive.

### Vague Claims Of Advantage

"We have great technology" or "we have a strong brand" without a structural mechanism is not a moat.

### Moat That Does Not Fit The Model

A claimed moat incompatible with how the business creates value is aspirational.

### Ignoring Erosion

Moats decay; assuming permanence invites surprise.

### Inventing A Moat For Comfort

Claiming defensibility to feel secure, when none is likely, misleads strategy and investors.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The moat is tied to a named structural source, not to execution or novelty alone.
- [ ] Head start and founder effort are distinguished from durable protection.
- [ ] Each candidate moat is assessed for strength, replication cost, and erosion risk.
- [ ] The moat is compatible with how the business creates and captures value.
- [ ] The plan builds the moat as a byproduct of growth, rather than declaring it.
- [ ] What the venture accumulates over time, becoming harder to replicate, is identified.
- [ ] Moat erosion drivers, including technology and behavior shifts, are considered.
- [ ] Counter-positioning against incumbents is evaluated where relevant.
- [ ] The no-moat case is honestly acknowledged if no durable advantage is likely.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting execution, novelty, or vague advantage as a competitive moat.
