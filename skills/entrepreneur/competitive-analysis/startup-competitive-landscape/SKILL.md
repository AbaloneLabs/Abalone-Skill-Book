# Startup Competitive Landscape

A competitive landscape is not a list of companies with similar products. It is the full set of alternatives a customer considers, including direct competitors, indirect substitutes, manual workarounds, and doing nothing. Founders who map only their obvious rivals misunderstand their real competition and are blindsided by the substitutes that actually capture their customers. A useful landscape reveals where customers currently spend, what they tolerate, where the gaps are, and what a new entrant would have to displace.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "who are my competitors", "what is the competitive landscape", "how do I analyze my competition", or "how should an entrepreneur map who they are up against". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a tidy table of look-alike companies while ignoring the substitutes that actually win the customer.

## Core Rules

### Map The Full Alternative Set, Not Just Look-Alikes

Customers choose among everything that could address their need, not only products that resemble yours. Build the landscape in layers.

Include:

- direct competitors: similar product, similar customer;
- indirect competitors: different product, same job;
- substitutes: spreadsheets, consultants, manual processes, general tools;
- in-house or build options;
- doing nothing or tolerating the pain.

The substitute layer is often where customers actually go, and it is the layer most often ignored.

### Anchor The Landscape In The Customer's Job

Group competitors by the job they are hired to do, not by product category. Two products in different categories can compete for the same job; two products in the same category can serve different jobs. A job-anchored landscape reveals real rivalry that category-based mapping hides.

### Capture How Each Alternative Wins And Where It Fails

For each alternative, record why customers choose it and where it disappoints. The gap between what customers want and what each alternative delivers is where a new entrant can win.

Note for each:

- price and pricing model;
- who it serves best and worst;
- switching cost and lock-in;
- trust, brand, and distribution advantages;
- known weaknesses and complaints.

### Assess Strength Realistically, Not Flatteringly

Underestimating competitors is comforting and dangerous; overestimating them is paralyzing. Assess each on evidence: funding, traction, customer base, distribution, talent, and momentum. Avoid both dismissing rivals as incompetent and treating every well-funded incumbent as unbeatable.

### Identify What Would Have To Be True To Win

Translate the landscape into the conditions for your entry. What specific gap must you exploit, what customer must you serve better, what switching cost must you overcome, what distribution must you access? If you cannot state the condition for winning, you do not yet understand the landscape.

### Watch For Incumbent Response Capability

A new entrant's plan must account for how incumbents could respond: price cuts, feature copying, distribution moves, exclusive deals, or acquisition of the entrant. Assess not only what incumbents do today but what they could do if threatened. A plan that assumes incumbents stay still is fragile.

### Revisit The Landscape Regularly

Landscapes shift. New entrants appear, incumbents reposition, substitutes emerge, and regulation changes. A landscape mapped once goes stale. Revisit it as you learn and as the market moves, especially before major investment or strategy decisions.

### Use The Landscape To Find White Space

The purpose of mapping is not to feel overwhelmed by competition but to locate underserved customers, unmet jobs, and gaps in the current offering set. White space, where no alternative serves a specific customer or job well, is where a new entrant has the best chance.

### Acknowledge Limits And Personal Risk

Competitive intelligence gathered from public sources has limits, and some methods of gathering it carry legal risk. Founders should rely on ethical, public, and primary research. This skill does not replace professional legal advice on competitive intelligence practices.

## Common Traps

### Mapping Only Direct Competitors

Ignoring substitutes and doing-nothing misses where customers actually go.

### Category-Based Instead Of Job-Based Grouping

Products in different categories can be real rivals; same-category products can serve different jobs.

### Dismissing Incumbents As Incompetent

Underestimating response capability leads to plans that collapse when incumbents react.

### Flattering Or Paralyzing Assessment

Both dismissing and over-awe distort strategy; realistic strength assessment is required.

### Static Landscape

A map made once goes stale; landscapes shift continuously.

### No Condition For Winning

A landscape without a stated path to victory is description, not strategy.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The landscape includes direct, indirect, substitute, in-house, and do-nothing alternatives.
- [ ] Competitors are grouped by the job they are hired to do, not only by product category.
- [ ] Each alternative's strengths, weaknesses, pricing, and switching costs are captured.
- [ ] Incumbent strength is assessed realistically on evidence, not flattered or dismissed.
- [ ] The specific condition under which the new entrant can win is stated.
- [ ] Likely incumbent responses, including price and feature moves, are considered.
- [ ] The landscape is revisited regularly, not treated as static.
- [ ] White space and underserved customers are identified as entry opportunities.
- [ ] Competitive intelligence is gathered ethically from public and primary sources.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a list of look-alike companies as a complete competitive picture.
