# Indirect Competitor Analysis

Indirect competitors are the solutions that serve the same job as your product but look nothing like it: the spreadsheet instead of the app, the consultant instead of the platform, the general tool repurposed instead of the specialized one, the manual process instead of the automated service. These rivals are dangerous precisely because they are invisible in a standard competitive scan, yet they are often where customers actually go. A founder who studies only direct competitors will be confused when customers choose a clunky substitute that "should not" win.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "who are my indirect competitors", "why do customers use a workaround instead of my product", "what substitutes am I up against", or "how should an entrepreneur find non-obvious competitors". The goal is to prevent the agent from limiting competitive analysis to look-alike companies and missing the substitutes that capture the customer.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Job, Not The Product

Indirect competitors become visible only when you define the job the customer is hiring for. Everything that helps make that progress, regardless of form, is a competitor for that job. State the job precisely, then enumerate every way customers currently make that progress.

A job-anchored view reveals rivals a product-anchored view hides.

### Enumerate Substitute Categories Systematically

Walk through the categories of substitutes customers use for the job.

Common substitute types:

- manual processes and checklists;
- spreadsheets and documents;
- consultants, agencies, and contractors;
- general-purpose tools repurposed;
- internal or homegrown solutions;
- adjacent products with overlapping capability;
- doing nothing or tolerating the pain.

For each, ask why the customer uses it and what it costs them.

### Understand Why The Substitute Persists

A clunky substitute that survives does so for reasons. It may be free, already integrated, trusted, familiar, controlled by the customer, or simply good enough. Understanding the reason for persistence reveals the real bar your solution must clear, which is often not feature superiority but displacement of habit, cost, or trust.

Ask: what does the substitute offer that makes switching unattractive?

### Measure The Switching Cost Away From The Substitute

The cost to leave a substitute is often higher than it appears, because substitutes are embedded in workflow, data, habit, and relationships. Map the switching cost: data migration, retraining, workflow redesign, relationship disruption, and perceived risk. If switching cost exceeds the benefit your solution offers, the substitute wins regardless of quality.

### Find The Substitute's Weakness That Creates Opportunity

Every substitute fails somewhere: it is slow, error-prone, expensive in labor, unscalable, invisible to management, or fragile under stress. The weakness of the dominant substitute is often the wedge for a new entrant. Identify where the substitute hurts the customer most and target that pain.

### Distinguish Active From Passive Substitution

Some customers actively choose a substitute because it fits; others passively tolerate it because nothing better exists or switching is too hard. Active substitution is harder to displace; passive substitution is the more promising entry target. Segment customers by whether they chose or settled for the substitute.

### Watch For Substitutes Becoming Direct Competitors

A general tool repurposed for your job can, over time, add features and become a direct competitor, often with the advantage of existing distribution and data. Track whether substitutes are moving toward your space, since that changes the competitive future.

### Test Displacement With Real Customers

Theorizing about substitutes is insufficient. Test whether customers will actually switch from their current substitute to your solution, observing behavior and commitment rather than stated intent. A substitute that customers praise but will not leave is a stronger rival than it appears.

### Acknowledge Limits And Personal Risk

Indirect competitor analysis informs strategy but does not guarantee displacement. Overconfidence about beating a substitute can mislead investment and runway decisions. This skill does not replace professional advice where substitutes involve regulated or safety-critical outcomes.

## Common Traps

### Ignoring The Substitute Layer

The most common competitive blind spot is the workaround customers already use.

### Assuming Feature Superiority Displaces Habit

A better product often loses to an embedded, trusted, free substitute.

### Misreading Why The Substitute Persists

If you do not understand the reason for persistence, you cannot design the displacement.

### Treating All Substitutes As Equal

Active substitution is harder to beat than passive tolerance; the entry strategy differs.

### Forgetting Substitutes Evolve

A repurposed general tool can become a direct competitor with distribution advantages.

### Theorizing Without Testing Switching

Stated willingness to switch predicts real switching poorly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The job is defined precisely, and all solutions making that progress are enumerated.
- [ ] Substitute categories, including manual, spreadsheet, consultant, general tool, and do-nothing, are covered.
- [ ] The reason each substitute persists is understood, not assumed.
- [ ] Switching cost away from the substitute is mapped, including data, habit, and workflow.
- [ ] The substitute's key weakness is identified as the entry wedge.
- [ ] Active and passive substitution are distinguished, with entry strategy matched accordingly.
- [ ] Whether substitutes are evolving toward direct competition is tracked.
- [ ] Displacement is tested with real customer behavior and commitment, not stated intent.
- [ ] The analysis covers non-obvious rivals, not only obvious look-alikes.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids declaring a market open while ignoring the substitutes customers actually use.
