# Founder Decision Frameworks

Founders make an unusually high volume of consequential decisions under time pressure and incomplete information, and the quality of those decisions shapes the venture's trajectory. Yet many founders have no explicit decision process; they default to intuition, to seeking consensus, or to avoiding the choice until forced. Different decisions warrant different processes: a reversible, low-stakes choice should be made fast by the closest person; an irreversible, high-stakes choice deserves deliberate analysis, dissent, and time. The discipline of founder decision-making is matching the process to the decision, defining who decides and how, and resisting the temptation to apply one default method to everything.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how should I make this decision", "what decision framework should I use", "how do founders make hard choices", "who should decide this", or "how should an entrepreneur decide under pressure". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a single decision style for all situations, from confusing consensus with rigor, or from treating fast intuition as universally sufficient.

## Core Rules

### Match The Process To The Decision Type

Not all decisions deserve the same process. Classify a decision first by stakes, reversibility, time sensitivity, and information availability. A reversible, low-stakes decision should be made quickly by the person closest to it; an irreversible, high-stakes decision warrants deliberate analysis, written reasoning, dissent, and often outside input. Applying heavy process to trivial choices wastes time; applying light process to consequential, irreversible choices courts disaster.

### Use The Reversibility Heuristic

A core founder heuristic is to distinguish one-way doors from two-way doors. Two-way door, reversible, decisions should be made fast, because the cost of delay exceeds the cost of being wrong and you can correct. One-way door, irreversible, decisions, such as selling the company, taking a particular investor, or shutting down, deserve maximum deliberation because you cannot walk back. Misclassifying a one-way door as two-way is a common and costly error.

### Define Decision Rights Explicitly

Ambiguity about who decides creates delay, politicking, and diluted accountability. For each decision, define who is the decider, who is consulted, and who is informed. The decider owns the outcome. Clarity of decision rights matters more than the specific choice of structure; a founder who decides alone clearly is more effective than a team that decides ambiguously together.

### Avoid Defaulting To Consensus

Consensus is one valid decision mode, but it is slow, tends toward the lowest-common-denominator choice, and is inappropriate for fast or high-stakes decisions. Reserve consensus for decisions where buy-in across parties is the main value and speed is not critical. For most founder decisions, a clear decider with input from others is faster and produces sharper choices.

### Write Down The Decision And Reasoning

For consequential decisions, write down the choice, the reasoning, the alternatives considered, the key assumptions, and what would change your mind. Writing forces clarity, surfaces hidden assumptions, creates a record for later learning, and combats the tendency to retell decisions as obviously right in hindsight. A decision journal is a high-leverage practice.

### Seek Dissent Before Deciding

For high-stakes decisions, deliberately solicit dissent and opposing views before deciding. Assign someone to argue the strongest case against the leading option. Dissent surfaces blind spots, stress-tests assumptions, and prevents the founder from confirming a premature conclusion. The goal is not to reach consensus but to decide with awareness of the strongest counterarguments.

### Separate Decision From Execution

A good decision process separates the choice from its execution. Decide what to do with rigor; then commit to execution without relitigating. Founders who keep reopening decided questions, or who blend deciding and doing, produce neither good decisions nor good execution.

### Use Time-Boxing To Force Closure

Indecision has a cost, often larger than the cost of a slightly imperfect choice. Time-box decisions: set a deadline by which the decision will be made, given the information available by then. This prevents analysis paralysis and endless information-gathering that delays action past the point of relevance.

### Calibrate Confidence And Avoid False Precision

Express decision confidence honestly, including uncertainty. False precision, presenting a guess as a confident forecast, leads to overcommitment and prevents course correction. Calibrated confidence, stating the real range of uncertainty, lets the founder set review points and update as evidence arrives.

### Decide When To Decide

Some decisions are improved by waiting for more information; others are degraded by delay because the window closes or competitors move. Explicitly decide whether to decide now or gather more information, and define what specific new information would be worth waiting for. Waiting without a defined trigger is procrastination disguised as prudence.

## Common Traps

### One Process For All Decisions

Applying the same decision style, heavy analysis or fast intuition, to everything misallocates effort and risk.

### Misclassifying One-Way Doors As Reversible

Treating irreversible decisions as easily corrected leads to insufficient deliberation on choices you cannot walk back.

### Ambiguous Decision Rights

Vague ownership produces delay, diluted accountability, and politicking instead of decisions.

### Defaulting To Consensus

Using consensus for fast or high-stakes choices produces slow, mediocre decisions.

### Undocumented Reasoning

Decisions made without written reasoning cannot be learned from and get retold as obviously right.

### No Dissent

Deciding without hearing the strongest opposing view confirms premature conclusions.

### Endless Information Gathering

Waiting indefinitely for more information, without a defined trigger, delays action past relevance.

### False Precision

Presenting uncertain judgments as confident forecasts prevents course correction.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The decision is classified by stakes, reversibility, time sensitivity, and information availability before a process is chosen.
- [ ] One-way door, irreversible, decisions receive maximum deliberation; two-way door decisions are made fast.
- [ ] Decision rights are explicit: a decider is named, with defined consulted and informed parties.
- [ ] Consensus is reserved for decisions where buy-in is the main value, not used as a default.
- [ ] Consequential decisions are written down with reasoning, alternatives, assumptions, and change-of-mind triggers.
- [ ] Dissent and opposing views are deliberately sought before high-stakes decisions.
- [ ] Decision is separated from execution; decided questions are not relitigated during execution.
- [ ] Decisions are time-boxed to force closure and avoid analysis paralysis.
- [ ] Confidence is expressed with honest calibration, including uncertainty, not false precision.
- [ ] Whether to decide now or wait is itself decided, with a defined information trigger for waiting.
