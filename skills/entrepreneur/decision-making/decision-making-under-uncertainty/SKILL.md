# Decision Making Under Uncertainty

Most consequential founder decisions must be made before the relevant information exists: whether a market will materialize, whether a product will resonate, how competitors will respond, how long runway will last. Waiting for certainty is not an option, because certainty arrives only after the window has closed. The discipline is not to eliminate uncertainty, which is impossible, but to reason well within it: think in probabilities, favor reversible moves, design decisions to generate learning, and distinguish what you know from what you assume. Founders who demand false certainty overcommit to brittle plans; founders who treat uncertainty as unknowable chaos freeze. The skilled path is structured reasoning under irreducible ambiguity.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I decide when I don't have enough information", "how should a founder make decisions under uncertainty", "how do I deal with not knowing", "how do I weigh risky options", or "how should an entrepreneur think probabilistically". The goal is to prevent the agent from offering false certainty, from recommending paralysis until information is complete, or from treating uncertainty as a reason to abandon structured reasoning.

## Core Rules

### Accept That Founding Decisions Are Made Before Certainty Exists

The defining condition of entrepreneurship is decision under uncertainty. The information needed to be certain arrives only after acting. Accept this as the baseline rather than a failure, because demanding certainty before deciding means never deciding in time. The goal is to decide well within uncertainty, not to eliminate it.

### Think In Probabilities, Not Binary Outcomes

Replace will-it-work-or-not thinking with probabilistic thinking: assign rough likelihoods to outcomes, range the scenarios, and reason about expected value. Crude probabilities are far better than false certainty or hand-waving, because they make assumptions explicit and updatable. Even approximate probabilities discipline reasoning and reveal when a bet is worth taking.

### Separate Knowns, Assumptions, And Unknowns

Explicitly distinguish what is known from evidence, what is assumed, and what is genuinely unknown. Most founder plans collapse assumptions into false facts. Listing assumptions separately lets you test the riskiest ones first and update the plan as they resolve, rather than discovering too late that a load-bearing assumption was wrong.

### Favor Reversible, Learning-Generating Decisions

When uncertain, prefer decisions that are reversible and that generate information. A small, time-boxed experiment that costs little and teaches a lot is superior to a large irreversible commitment based on a guess. Structure choices so that being wrong is cheap and informative, and so that success clarifies the path forward.

### Identify And Test The Riskiest Assumption First

Under uncertainty, not all assumptions matter equally. Identify the single riskiest assumption, the one whose falsity would invalidate the plan, and test it before all others. Testing low-risk assumptions first wastes time and money; testing the riskiest first either validates the path quickly or kills it cheaply.

### Use Scenarios And Pre-Mortems

Construct explicit scenarios, best, base, worst, and run a pre-mortem: assume the decision failed, then reason backward about why. Pre-mortems surface failure modes that optimism hides and suggest guardrails to install before deciding. Scenario thinking keeps the founder prepared for multiple futures rather than committed to one.

### Avoid False Precision And False Certainty

Two opposite errors degrade uncertain decisions. False precision assigns precise numbers to guesses that do not warrant them; false certainty treats uncertain judgments as known facts. Calibrate: express uncertainty honestly, use ranges where precision is unwarranted, and resist presenting guesses as confident forecasts.

### Decide With Reversible Commitment And Defined Review Points

Commit to a decision while preserving the ability to update it. Define what evidence would cause you to reverse or adjust, and set review points to check. This combines decisiveness with adaptability, avoiding both rigid overcommitment and chronic second-guessing.

### Beware Of Wishful Thinking And Motivated Reasoning

Founders are highly motivated to believe their venture will succeed, which systematically biases interpretation of ambiguous information toward optimism. Counter motivated reasoning with pre-committed criteria, outside perspective, and deliberate search for disconfirming evidence. The founder who only seeks confirmation will be surprised by failure.

### Distinguish Uncertainty From Risk

Risk is where probabilities are known or estimable, like a coin flip; uncertainty, sometimes called Knightian uncertainty, is where even the probabilities are unknown. Use different tools for each: expected-value reasoning for risk, and reversible experimentation, diversification, and optionality for deep uncertainty. Treating deep uncertainty as calculable risk produces false confidence.

### Recognize When Waiting Is The Right Decision

Not all uncertainty should be resolved by acting immediately. Sometimes the value of waiting for information exceeds the cost of delay, especially when information is arriving soon and the decision is reversible later. Explicitly weigh the option value of waiting against the cost of a closing window, rather than acting reflexively or waiting reflexively.

## Common Traps

### Demanding Certainty Before Deciding

Waiting for complete information means deciding too late or never; uncertainty is the baseline, not an exception.

### Binary Thinking

Treating outcomes as will-work-or-won't obscures the probabilistic structure that enables good bets.

### Collapsing Assumptions Into Facts

Treating untested assumptions as known invalidates the whole plan when one proves wrong.

### Large Irreversible Bets On Guesses

Committing heavily before testing the riskiest assumption turns uncertainty into existential risk.

### False Precision

Assigning precise probabilities to genuinely unknowable outcomes manufactures unwarranted confidence.

### Motivated Reasoning

Interpreting ambiguous information as confirmation of desired outcomes blinds the founder to trouble.

### Treating Deep Uncertainty As Calculable Risk

Applying expected-value math to situations where probabilities are unknown produces false certainty.

### Acting Or Waiting Reflexively

Neither reflexive action nor reflexive delay serves the founder; each decision to act or wait should be a deliberate choice.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The decision accepts uncertainty as the baseline rather than demanding certainty before acting.
- [ ] Outcomes are reasoned about probabilistically, with rough likelihoods and expected value, not binary thinking.
- [ ] Knowns, assumptions, and unknowns are explicitly separated, with assumptions listed for testing.
- [ ] The riskiest assumption is identified and prioritized for testing before others.
- [ ] Reversible, learning-generating decisions are favored over large irreversible commitments.
- [ ] Scenarios, including best, base, worst, and a pre-mortem, are used to surface failure modes.
- [ ] Confidence is calibrated; false precision and false certainty are both avoided.
- [ ] The decision includes defined review points and evidence that would trigger reversal or adjustment.
- [ ] Motivated reasoning is countered with pre-committed criteria and disconfirming evidence.
- [ ] Deep uncertainty is distinguished from calculable risk, with appropriate tools for each.
- [ ] Whether to act now or wait is a deliberate weighing of option value against closing window, not a reflex.
