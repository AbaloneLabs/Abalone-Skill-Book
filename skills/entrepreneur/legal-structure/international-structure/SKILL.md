# International Structure

An international corporate structure is the legal and tax architecture that spans multiple countries when a startup operates, hires, or raises capital across borders, and it is one of the most complex and error-prone areas of company building because every jurisdiction imposes its own rules on tax, employment, IP, and compliance. A structure that works domestically can create severe tax leakage, permanent establishment risk, or compliance failure when stretched across borders, and the mistakes are expensive and hard to undo. The judgment problem is designing a structure that supports the company's global operations and fundraising while managing cross-border tax, transfer pricing, IP ownership, and the permanent establishment risk that can trigger taxation in unintended places. Agents tend to fail by applying domestic logic to international situations, by underestimating transfer pricing and PE risk, and by treating international structure as an accounting detail rather than a strategic architecture.

Use this skill when designing or advising on an international corporate structure for a startup. The goal is a global structure that supports operations and fundraising without creating tax, legal, or compliance traps.

## Core Rules

### Design The Structure Around The Company's Actual Global Footprint

An international structure must reflect where the company actually operates, hires, sells, and holds IP, not a theoretical model copied from another company. A structure designed for a different footprint creates mismatches that produce tax leakage and compliance failure.

Design around the footprint by:

- mapping where the company has operations, employees, customers, and IP;
- understanding the tax and legal implications of each jurisdiction;
- structuring entities to align with where value is created and revenue is earned;
- avoiding structures that look efficient on paper but do not match reality.

A structure that does not match the footprint is a fiction that tax authorities will challenge. Design it for the actual business.

### Manage Cross-Border Tax And Transfer Pricing

When entities within a global structure transact with each other, transfer pricing rules require that the transactions be priced as if they were between independent parties, and getting this wrong creates tax exposure in multiple jurisdictions. Transfer pricing is not an accounting detail; it is a structural risk.

Manage transfer pricing by:

- documenting the intercompany arrangements and the pricing methodology;
- ensuring the pricing reflects the functions, assets, and risks of each entity;
- using defendable methodologies accepted by tax authorities;
- maintaining contemporaneous documentation to support the structure.

Transfer pricing disputes can result in double taxation and penalties. Structure and document it defensibly from the start.

### Handle Permanent Establishment Risk

A permanent establishment, a fixed place of business or dependent agent that creates a taxable presence, can trigger taxation in a country where the company did not intend to be taxed. Activities like hiring sales staff, maintaining an office, or conducting core operations in a country can create PE.

Handle PE by:

- understanding what activities create a permanent establishment in each jurisdiction;
- structuring operations and sales to avoid unintended PE where possible;
- recognizing that hiring employees in a country often creates a taxable presence;
- using proper entities or employers-of-record where PE must be managed.

Unintended PE creates tax liability and compliance burden in countries the company did not plan for. Understand what triggers it.

### Structure Subsidiaries To Match Operations And Liability

Subsidiaries isolate liability, manage local compliance, and structure operations within a jurisdiction, and their use must reflect the company's operational and risk profile. Too few subsidiaries create exposure; too many create complexity.

Structure subsidiaries by:

- establishing local entities where the company has significant operations or employees;
- using subsidiaries to isolate liability in higher-risk jurisdictions;
- balancing the compliance cost of each entity against its operational benefit;
- avoiding entities that exist only for theoretical tax advantage without substance.

Subsidiaries should serve operational and risk purposes, not just tax theory. Each entity should have substance proportionate to its role.

### Decide IP Ownership Strategically

Where intellectual property is owned within the structure affects where the profits from that IP are taxed, and IP ownership is one of the most scrutinized areas of international tax. Moving IP after it has appreciated is expensive and often scrutinized.

Decide IP ownership by:

- determining where IP will be created, funded, and exploited;
- understanding the tax implications of IP ownership in each jurisdiction;
- structuring IP ownership and licensing to reflect where value is created;
- avoiding structures that concentrate IP in low-tax jurisdictions without substance, which invite challenge.

IP ownership is a strategic decision with long-term tax consequences. Make it deliberately, with awareness of how it will be scrutinized.

### Use Local Counsel For Each Jurisdiction

International structure spans multiple legal systems, and no single lawyer can competently advise on all of them. Each jurisdiction requires local expertise, coordinated by a lead advisor who understands the global picture.

Use local counsel by:

- engaging local counsel in each jurisdiction where the company operates;
- coordinating through a lead international tax or corporate advisor;
- ensuring local advice is integrated into the global structure, not siloed;
- recognizing that international structures require specialized expertise, not generalist help.

International structure is not a DIY area. The cost of local counsel is trivial compared to the cost of a cross-border error.

### Plan For Fundraising And Exit Across Borders

The international structure must support not only operations but also fundraising and exit, because investors and acquirers evaluate the structure's complexity and risk. A structure that is efficient for operations may be unattractive to investors or complicate an acquisition.

Plan for fundraising and exit by:

- ensuring the structure is understandable and clean for investor due diligence;
- avoiding structures that create tax barriers to investment or acquisition;
- considering how the structure affects the proceeds to shareholders at exit;
- simplifying the structure where possible before fundraising or sale.

Investors and acquirers discount or avoid complex, risky structures. Design for the capital and exit events the company anticipates.

### Maintain Compliance Across All Jurisdictions

An international structure creates ongoing compliance obligations in every jurisdiction, from tax filings to employment law to data protection. Falling behind on compliance in any jurisdiction creates penalties and risk.

Maintain compliance by:

- tracking the filing and reporting obligations in each jurisdiction;
- using local accountants and counsel to manage local compliance;
- staying current on changes in law that affect the structure;
- budgeting for the ongoing compliance cost of the structure.

Compliance is not a one-time setup; it is an ongoing obligation. Under-resource it and the structure becomes a liability.

## Common Traps

### Applying Domestic Logic To International Structure

Domestic structures do not stretch across borders cleanly. Design for the global footprint.

### Ignoring Transfer Pricing

Intercompany transactions must be priced defensibly. Document the methodology.

### Unintended Permanent Establishment

Hiring or operating in a country can create taxable presence. Understand the triggers.

### Subsidiaries Without Substance

Entities that exist only for tax advantage invite challenge. Give each entity operational substance.

### Strategic IP Mistakes

Where IP is owned affects where profits are taxed. Decide deliberately and early.

### Using One Lawyer For All Jurisdictions

No single lawyer covers all legal systems. Use local counsel coordinated by a lead advisor.

### Structures That Repel Investors

Complex or risky structures discount valuations and complicate exits. Design for fundraising.

### Neglected Cross-Border Compliance

Each jurisdiction has ongoing obligations. Under-resource compliance and the structure fails.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The structure reflects the company's actual global footprint of operations, employees, customers, and IP.
- [ ] Transfer pricing is documented with defendable methodologies for all intercompany transactions.
- [ ] Permanent establishment risk is understood and managed for activities in each jurisdiction.
- [ ] Subsidiaries are established where operations or liability require, with substance proportionate to role.
- [ ] IP ownership is decided strategically, reflecting where value is created and profits are taxed.
- [ ] Local counsel is engaged in each jurisdiction, coordinated by a lead international advisor.
- [ ] The structure is clean and understandable for investor due diligence and exit scenarios.
- [ ] Ongoing compliance obligations in all jurisdictions are tracked and resourced.
- [ ] The structure avoids arrangements that invite tax authority challenge without substance.
- [ ] The global structure supports operations, fundraising, and exit without tax or legal traps.
