# Delaware C-Corp

The choice of legal entity is one of the earliest and most consequential decisions a startup makes, because it shapes taxation, fundraising, governance, and exit for the life of the company, and changing it later is expensive and disruptive. For venture-backed startups in the United States, the Delaware C-Corp is the default structure, and it is the default for specific, structural reasons that matter to investors, employees, and acquirers. The judgment problem is understanding why the Delaware C-Corp is the standard, incorporating it correctly with the governance formalities that protect the corporate veil, and avoiding the mistakes, wrong entity, foreign qualification gaps, missed 83(b) elections, that create costly problems later. Agents tend to fail by treating entity choice as a formality, by neglecting ongoing governance formalities, and by missing the time-sensitive filings that cannot be undone.

Use this skill when incorporating or advising on a Delaware C-Corp. The goal is a correctly formed and maintained corporation that supports fundraising, equity, and exit without legal surprises.

## Core Rules

### Choose Delaware C-Corp For Venture-Backed Startups

The Delaware C-Corp is the standard entity for venture-backed startups for structural reasons that matter to the ecosystem of investors, employees, and acquirers. Choosing another entity, an LLC, an S-Corp, or a state-specific corporation, can complicate or block fundraising.

Understand why C-Corp is standard:

- investors, especially venture funds, are structured to invest in C-Corps, and many cannot invest in pass-through entities like LLCs;
- preferred stock, the instrument of venture financing, is designed for C-Corp structures;
- employee equity, through options on common stock, is well-established and tax-favored in C-Corps;
- Delaware corporate law is the most developed and predictable, which investors and acquirers trust.

If the company intends to raise venture capital, the Delaware C-Corp is almost always the right choice. Choosing otherwise to save short-term tax or complexity often forces a costly conversion later.

### Incorporate With Proper Governance From Day One

Incorporation is not just filing paperwork; it is establishing the legal and governance structure that defines the corporation. Doing it incompletely creates gaps that surface during due diligence and can invalidate actions.

Incorporate properly by:

- filing the certificate of incorporation with Delaware;
- adopting bylaws, organizational consent, and initial board actions;
- issuing founder stock with proper purchase agreements and vesting;
- obtaining the employer identification number and establishing the bank account.

A corporation exists only when its governance is complete. Filing the certificate without the accompanying actions leaves the company half-formed.

### File 83(b) Elections Within 30 Days Of Stock Issuance

For founders and employees receiving restricted stock subject to vesting, the 83(b) election is a time-sensitive filing that elects to be taxed on the stock at grant rather than at vesting. Missing the 30-day deadline is an irreversible error with severe tax consequences.

File 83(b) by:

- filing the election with the IRS within 30 days of the stock grant, no exceptions for weekends or delays;
- mailing it certified mail with return receipt to prove timely filing;
- keeping proof of filing permanently, because the IRS does not confirm receipt;
- ensuring every founder and employee receiving restricted stock files.

The 83(b) deadline is absolute. Missing it means paying tax on the stock's appreciation as it vests, which can be devastating. This is the single most time-sensitive and unforgiving filing in startup formation.

### Maintain Corporate Formalities To Protect The Corporate Veil

A corporation is a separate legal entity, and that separation, the corporate veil, protects founders from personal liability only if the corporation is treated as a real entity. Neglecting formalities allows creditors to pierce the veil and reach founders' personal assets.

Maintain formalities by:

- holding and documenting regular board and shareholder meetings;
- keeping corporate records, minutes, and consents current;
- commingling no personal and corporate funds; maintain separate bank accounts;
- observing the distinction between corporate and personal transactions.

The corporate veil is preserved through ongoing formalities, not just at incorporation. Neglect them and the protection erodes.

### Foreign-Qualify In States Where The Company Operates

A Delaware corporation operating in another state must foreign-qualify, register to do business, in that state, or face penalties and loss of standing. Foreign qualification is often missed by companies that incorporate in Delaware but operate elsewhere.

Foreign-qualify by:

- registering in the state where the company has its principal office or operations;
- understanding the triggers for "doing business" in each state;
- maintaining registered agents in Delaware and in operating states;
- filing the required annual reports and paying franchise taxes.

Operating without foreign qualification creates penalties and compliance gaps. Register where you operate.

### Use Experienced Startup Counsel

Startup incorporation and governance have specific, well-established practices, and general legal help or DIY approaches create problems that surface during fundraising and due diligence. Experienced startup counsel is not optional for a venture-backed company.

Use counsel by:

- engaging a lawyer who specializes in startups and venture financing, not a generalist;
- using standard, accepted documents and structures rather than novel ones;
- having counsel review equity, contracts, and governance before fundraising;
- treating legal as an investment in avoiding costly problems, not as overhead.

The cost of startup counsel is trivial compared to the cost of a structural error that blocks a financing or kills a deal. Engage it from the start.

### Understand Delaware Franchise Tax And Annual Requirements

Delaware corporations pay an annual franchise tax and file an annual report, and the calculation method matters because the default method can produce a startlingly high bill for companies with many authorized shares. Understanding the requirements avoids surprises.

Understand requirements by:

- knowing the franchise tax calculation methods, especially the authorized shares method versus the assumed par value method;
- filing the annual report and paying the franchise tax on time;
- maintaining the registered agent in Delaware;
- understanding the consequences of falling out of good standing.

A company that falls out of good standing cannot take corporate actions cleanly. Stay current on Delaware requirements.

### Plan The Equity Structure At Incorporation

The equity structure established at incorporation, the authorized shares, the classes of stock, the option pool, shapes what the company can do later. Setting it up correctly avoids costly amendments.

Plan equity by:

- authorizing sufficient shares to cover founder stock, the option pool, and future rounds;
- establishing the option pool at incorporation, commonly 10 to 20 percent;
- using standard classes, common for founders and employees, preferred for investors;
- anticipating the structure that venture financing will require.

An equity structure set up correctly at incorporation supports fundraising without amendment. One set up carelessly requires expensive fixes.

## Common Traps

### Choosing The Wrong Entity

LLCs or S-Corps complicate venture fundraising. Choose Delaware C-Corp if you intend to raise venture capital.

### Incomplete Incorporation

Filing the certificate without bylaws, consents, and stock issuance leaves the company half-formed. Complete the governance.

### Missing The 83(b) Deadline

The 30-day deadline is absolute and irreversible. File for every restricted stock grant.

### Neglecting Corporate Formalities

Commingling funds and skipping records let creditors pierce the veil. Maintain formalities.

### Skipping Foreign Qualification

Operating in a state without registering creates penalties and compliance gaps. Foreign-qualify where you operate.

### Using Generalist Or DIY Legal

Startup structures have specific practices. Use experienced startup counsel.

### Ignoring Delaware Franchise Tax

Falling out of good standing blocks corporate actions. File and pay on time.

### Poorly Planned Equity Structure

Too few authorized shares or no option pool require costly amendments. Plan equity at incorporation.
