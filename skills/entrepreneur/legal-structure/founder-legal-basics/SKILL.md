# Founder Legal Basics

A founder need not be a lawyer, but must understand the legal foundations that govern the venture and personal exposure: the entity and its obligations, the contracts that bind the venture, the intellectual property that must be protected and assigned, the compliance obligations that apply, and the personal liabilities that can arise. Legal problems in startups often come not from complex disputes but from basics neglected early: unclear agreements, missing IP assignments, ignored compliance, personal guarantees that expose the founder. Understanding the legal basics is the discipline of knowing what must be done, what must not be done without advice, and when to seek professional help.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what legal things do I need to know", "what are the legal basics for a startup", "what legal mistakes should I avoid", or "how should an entrepreneur handle legal basics". The goal is to prevent the agent from offering specific legal advice and from letting a founder proceed without awareness of the legal risks and the need for professional counsel.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Foundational Legal Areas

A founder should understand, at a high level, the foundational legal areas that affect the venture: entity law and governance, contracts and obligations, intellectual property, employment, tax, privacy and data protection, and industry-specific regulation. The goal is awareness of where risks live and when to seek help, not mastery of the law.

### Keep Personal And Venture Liabilities Separate

Commingling personal and venture finances, contracts, or operations can pierce the liability shield of an entity, exposing the founder's personal assets to the venture's liabilities. Maintain clear separation: separate accounts, separate contracts, clear documentation, so the entity's liability protection holds. Commingling is a common and costly mistake.

### Ensure Intellectual Property Is Assigned To The Venture

Intellectual property created for the venture must be assigned to the venture, by founders, employees, and contractors. Missing or vague IP assignments create a risk that the venture does not own its core assets, which surfaces painfully during due diligence or disputes. Ensure IP assignment is clear and documented for everyone who creates IP for the venture.

### Put Material Agreements In Writing

Material agreements, with co-founders, employees, contractors, customers, vendors, and partners, should be in writing, however much trust exists. Oral agreements are hard to enforce and easy to misremember, and they create disputes when memories diverge or circumstances change. Putting agreements in writing, even simply, prevents foreseeable conflicts.

### Understand Compliance Obligations Early

Compliance obligations, tax filings, licenses, permits, industry regulations, data protection, employment law, apply from the start, not only when the venture grows. Understand the obligations that apply to the venture early, because non-compliance discovered later creates penalties, rework, and liability. Ignorance of obligations does not excuse them.

### Be Cautious With Personal Guarantees

Personal guarantees, where the founder personally backs the venture's debt or lease, expose the founder's personal assets if the venture defaults. Be cautious with personal guarantees, understand what is being guaranteed and the exposure it creates, and avoid them where possible. Personal guarantees are often requested but not always necessary.

### Know When To Seek Professional Legal Help

Founders can handle some legal basics themselves, but many matters require professional legal help: entity formation and structuring, complex contracts, equity and cap table matters, IP protection, employment issues, regulatory compliance, and any dispute. Know when a matter exceeds founder competence, and seek help early, because legal problems compound when deferred.

### Avoid Practicing Law Without A License

Templates, general guidance, and a founder's own research have limits. Drafting bespoke legal documents, interpreting complex regulations, or advising others on legal matters without a license creates risk. Use templates and research for awareness, but rely on licensed professionals for documents and advice that carry legal consequence.

### Maintain Records And Documentation

Maintain records of the venture's legal foundations: formation documents, contracts, IP assignments, compliance filings, and corporate minutes. Good records protect the venture in due diligence, disputes, and audits, and they demonstrate that the venture has been run properly. Missing records create vulnerability.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Legal matters carry significant personal liability and risk. This skill provides awareness only and does not replace professional legal advice. Founders should engage qualified legal counsel for entity formation, contracts, IP, equity, compliance, and any dispute, tailored to their specific jurisdiction and situation.

## Common Traps

### Commingling Personal And Venture Matters

Mixing finances and contracts can pierce the liability shield.

### Missing IP Assignments

The venture may not own its core assets, surfacing in due diligence.

### Oral Agreements On Material Matters

Unwritten agreements create disputes when memories diverge.

### Ignored Compliance Obligations

Non-compliance discovered late creates penalties and liability.

### Casual Personal Guarantees

Exposing personal assets without understanding the risk.

### Deferring Professional Legal Help

Legal problems compound when professional advice is delayed.

- [ ] The founder understands the foundational legal areas affecting the venture.
- [ ] Personal and venture liabilities are kept separate to preserve the liability shield.
- [ ] Intellectual property is assigned to the venture by all who create it.
- [ ] Material agreements are put in writing.
- [ ] Compliance obligations are understood early and addressed.
- [ ] Personal guarantees are approached with caution and understanding of exposure.
- [ ] The founder knows when to seek professional legal help and does so early.
- [ ] Templates and research are used for awareness, with licensed professionals for consequential matters.
- [ ] Records of legal foundations are maintained.
- [ ] The founder engages qualified legal counsel for entity, contracts, IP, equity, compliance, and disputes, recognizing this skill does not replace that advice.
