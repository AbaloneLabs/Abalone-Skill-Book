# Founder Agreements

Founder agreements are the contracts that define the relationship among co-founders, and they exist to prevent the disputes that destroy companies when expectations are misaligned and undocumented. The romantic phase of starting a company, when founders agree enthusiastically and trust implicitly, is precisely when the agreements should be written, because the disagreements that arise later, under stress, money, and diverging commitment, cannot be resolved by goodwill alone. The judgment problem is allocating equity and roles to reflect genuine contribution rather than politeness, building in the protections, vesting, cliffs, IP assignment, dispute resolution, that survive a founder's departure or conflict, and recognizing that a founder agreement left ambiguous is a dispute waiting to happen. Agents tend to fail by avoiding hard conversations, by leaving terms vague to preserve harmony, and by treating the agreement as a formality rather than the operating system of the founding relationship.

Use this skill when drafting, negotiating, or advising on founder agreements. The goal is an agreement that aligns incentives, survives conflict, and prevents the disputes that kill companies.

## Core Rules

### Write The Agreement While You Still Agree

The best time to write a founder agreement is when the founders are aligned and trust each other, because that is when terms can be discussed calmly and honestly. Waiting until conflict arises means negotiating under stress, when positions have hardened and goodwill is gone.

Write early by:

- drafting the agreement at the founding of the company, before significant work or investment;
- having the hard conversations about equity, roles, and exit while alignment exists;
- documenting decisions so they do not rely on memory or goodwill later;
- recognizing that an agreement written in conflict reflects the conflict, not the partnership.

The agreement written in harmony survives the conflict that comes later. The agreement written in conflict often cannot be written at all.

### Allocate Equity To Reflect Contribution, Not Politeness

Equity allocation among founders is the most consequential and most avoided conversation, because it forces an honest accounting of who contributes what. Equal splits are often chosen to avoid the conversation, but they create resentment when contributions diverge and misalign incentives.

Allocate by:

- reflecting relative contribution in role, time, capital, risk, and ongoing commitment;
- discussing the rationale openly so expectations are aligned;
- considering the long-term arc of each founder's role, not just the start;
- being willing to have the uncomfortable conversation rather than defaulting to equal.

Equity is the incentive structure for years of work. Polite equal splits that ignore real contribution produce resentment and misalignment.

### Use Vesting And Cliffs To Protect The Company

Vesting ensures that equity is earned over time, and a cliff ensures that a founder who leaves early does not retain a large unearned stake. Without these, an early departure leaves dead equity that blocks financing and dilutes those who remain.

Use vesting by:

- applying standard vesting, typically four years with a one-year cliff, to all founder equity;
- ensuring no founder retains unearned equity if they depart early;
- documenting the vesting schedule clearly in the agreement;
- considering acceleration provisions for specific events like acquisition, designed deliberately.

Vesting protects the company and the founders who remain. Skipping it is one of the most damaging mistakes a founding team can make.

### Define Roles, Decision Rights, And Authority

Ambiguity about who decides what produces conflict and paralysis, especially as the company grows and decisions become consequential. Defining roles and decision rights clarifies authority and prevents disputes.

Define by:

- specifying each founder's role and area of authority;
- clarifying which decisions require consensus, majority, or a single decider;
- designating a CEO or final decision-maker to break deadlocks;
- establishing how roles and authority may evolve as the company grows.

A founding team without defined decision rights descends into paralysis or conflict when disagreements arise. Define authority before you need it.

### Plan For Dispute Resolution And Deadlocks

Disputes among founders are inevitable, and the agreement must define how they are resolved, because a deadlock with no resolution mechanism can paralyze and kill the company. Hoping for goodwill is not a dispute resolution plan.

Plan by:

- defining escalation paths for disagreements that cannot be resolved at the founder level;
- establishing a mechanism for breaking deadlocks, such as a tie-breaking board member or a buy-sell provision;
- considering mediation or arbitration for serious disputes;
- avoiding structures that allow a single founder to block all action.

A deadlock with no resolution mechanism is a company-killer. Build the mechanism before the deadlock.

### Address Departure And Termination Scenarios

Founders leave, voluntarily or involuntarily, and the agreement must define what happens to their equity, role, and rights when they do. Leaving these undefined produces disputes at the worst moments.

Address departure by:

- defining what constitutes voluntary departure, termination for cause, and other scenarios;
- specifying how unvested and vested equity are treated in each case;
- establishing repurchase rights for departing founders' shares where appropriate;
- clarifying ongoing obligations, such as non-compete or non-solicitation, within legal limits.

A founder departure without a defined framework becomes a crisis. Define the scenarios in advance.

### Ensure IP Assignment And Confidentiality

All intellectual property created by founders must be assigned to the company, and confidentiality obligations must be in place, or the company's ownership of its core assets is uncertain. IP assignment gaps surface during due diligence and can block financing or acquisition.

Ensure IP by:

- having every founder sign an IP assignment agreement transferring rights to the company;
- ensuring the assignment covers all work, past and future, relevant to the company;
- including confidentiality and non-disclosure obligations;
- confirming no founder is using prior employer's IP in the company's work.

The company owns its IP only if it has been properly assigned. Gaps here are among the most damaging discoveries in due diligence.

### Use Legal Counsel, Not DIY

Founder agreements have specific legal and tax requirements, and DIY agreements create gaps that surface during disputes or due diligence. Experienced counsel is essential.

Use counsel by:

- engaging a startup lawyer to draft or review the agreement;
- ensuring the agreement is consistent with corporate law and the company's structure;
- having each founder understand the terms, ideally with independent review;
- treating the cost as an investment in preventing disputes that destroy companies.

The cost of counsel is trivial compared to the cost of a founder dispute that kills the company. Do not DIY the founding relationship.

### Revisit The Agreement As The Company Evolves

A founder agreement written at founding may need to evolve as the company grows, roles change, and new people join. Treating it as immutable ignores the reality that relationships and companies change.

Revisit by:

- reviewing the agreement at major milestones, such as fundraising or significant growth;
- amending terms through proper process when the relationship or company has evolved;
- ensuring changes are documented and consistent with corporate governance;
- recognizing that the agreement should serve the company's current reality, not just its founding state.

An agreement that does not evolve with the company becomes a constraint. Revisit it at major milestones.

## Common Traps

### Avoiding The Equity Conversation

Equal splits chosen to avoid conflict create resentment when contributions diverge. Allocate to reflect contribution.

### Skipping Vesting And Cliffs

Without vesting, early departures leave dead equity. Apply standard vesting to all founders.

### Ambiguous Decision Rights

Undefined authority produces paralysis and conflict. Define roles and decision mechanisms.

### No Dispute Resolution Mechanism

A deadlock with no resolution can kill the company. Build the mechanism in advance.

### Undefined Departure Scenarios

A departure without a framework becomes a crisis. Define equity and role treatment in advance.

### IP Assignment Gaps

Uncertain IP ownership blocks financing and acquisition. Assign all IP to the company.

### DIY Agreements

Homemade agreements create gaps. Use experienced startup counsel.

### Treating The Agreement As Immutable

Companies and relationships evolve. Revisit the agreement at major milestones.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The agreement was written while founders were aligned, not during conflict.
- [ ] Equity allocation reflects genuine contribution, role, and ongoing commitment, not polite equal splits.
- [ ] Standard vesting with a one-year cliff applies to all founder equity.
- [ ] Roles, decision rights, and a final decision-maker are clearly defined.
- [ ] A dispute resolution and deadlock-breaking mechanism is established.
- [ ] Departure and termination scenarios are defined, with equity treatment specified.
- [ ] All IP is assigned to the company, with confidentiality obligations in place.
- [ ] Experienced startup counsel drafted or reviewed the agreement.
- [ ] The agreement is revisited at major milestones and evolves with the company.
- [ ] The agreement would survive a founder departure or dispute without killing the company.
