# Entity Formation Choices

The legal entity a founder chooses, a sole proprietorship, a partnership, a limited liability company, a corporation, shapes the venture's liability exposure, taxation, ability to raise capital, and governance for its entire life. The choice is consequential and hard to undo, and it interacts with the venture's goals: a venture intending to raise venture capital needs a different entity than a lifestyle business or a professional practice. Entity formation is the discipline of choosing a structure that fits the venture's intended path, in light of liability, tax, fundraising, and jurisdictional considerations, rather than defaulting to whatever is easiest to set up.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what entity should I choose", "should I form an LLC or a corporation", "how do I structure my startup legally", or "how should an entrepreneur choose a business entity". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a default entity without considering the venture's goals and from presenting entity choice as a simple or purely administrative decision.

## Core Rules

### Match The Entity To The Venture's Intended Path

Different entities fit different paths. Ventures intending to raise venture capital, issue preferred stock, and scale toward an exit typically need a corporation, often a C-corporation in jurisdictions where investors expect it. Ventures intending to stay private, distribute profits to owners, or remain small may fit better as LLCs or partnerships. Match the entity to the intended path, because converting later can be costly and complex.

### Consider Liability Exposure

A primary reason to form an entity is to limit personal liability for the venture's obligations. Sole proprietorships and general partnerships generally expose the founder's personal assets to the venture's debts and liabilities; corporations and LLCs generally provide a liability shield. Consider the venture's liability exposure, and choose an entity that protects the founder appropriately for the risks involved.

### Understand The Tax Implications

Entity choice affects taxation: pass-through entities, such as LLCs and S-corporations, pass profits to owners' personal returns; C-corporations are taxed at the entity level and again on distributions, creating potential double taxation. The tax treatment interacts with the venture's profitability, the founders' personal situations, and the intended use of profits. Understand the tax implications, recognizing they require professional advice.

### Account For Fundraising And Investor Expectations

If the venture intends to raise outside capital, investors may expect or require a specific entity type. Venture investors, especially institutional ones, often require C-corporations for preferred stock, governance, and tax reasons. Choosing an entity that conflicts with investor expectations can complicate or block fundraising. Account for fundraising plans when choosing the entity.

### Consider The Jurisdiction Of Formation

The jurisdiction of formation affects governance, taxation, investor perception, and regulatory obligations. Some jurisdictions are favored by investors and familiar to them; others offer simplicity but may complicate fundraising or create unexpected obligations. Consider the jurisdiction in light of the venture's location, investors, and long-term plans, not only formation convenience.

### Plan For Equity And Ownership Structure

If the venture will have multiple founders, employees with equity, or outside investors, the entity must support the intended ownership structure, including classes of stock, vesting, option pools, and transfer restrictions. Some entities handle complex equity more naturally than others. Plan the ownership structure, and choose an entity that supports it.

### Address Governance And Control

Entity choice affects governance: who controls the venture, how decisions are made, and what fiduciary duties apply. Corporations have boards, officers, and shareholder rights; LLCs offer flexible operating agreements; partnerships have partner duties. Consider how the founder wants the venture governed, and choose an entity whose governance fits.

### Avoid Defaulting To The Easiest Option

The easiest entity to form, often a sole proprietorship or a simple LLC, may not be the right one for the venture's goals. Defaulting to convenience can create liability exposure, tax inefficiency, or fundraising obstacles that are expensive to fix later. Choose deliberately, in light of the venture's path, rather than for setup convenience.

### Revisit The Structure As The Venture Evolves

An entity that fits at formation may need to change as the venture evolves: raising a different type of capital, adding partners, or approaching an exit may require conversion or restructuring. Revisit the structure at major milestones, and be prepared to change it, recognizing that conversion has costs.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Entity formation involves significant legal, tax, and liability implications that require professional legal and tax advice tailored to the venture's specific situation and jurisdiction. This skill does not replace that counsel, and entity decisions should not be made without it.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To The Easiest Entity

Convenience at formation can create liability, tax, or fundraising problems later.

### Ignoring Fundraising Implications

An entity that conflicts with investor expectations can block fundraising.

### Overlooking Liability Exposure

Sole proprietorships and partnerships expose personal assets to venture liabilities.

### Misunderstanding Tax Treatment

Unexpected double taxation or inefficiency arises from the wrong entity for the situation.

### Wrong Jurisdiction

A jurisdiction chosen for convenience may complicate fundraising or governance.

### Inflexible Ownership Structure

An entity that cannot support the intended equity structure constrains the venture.
