# Scenario And Sensitivity Modeling

Startups operate under deep uncertainty, and a single forecast is almost certain to be wrong. Scenario and sensitivity modeling are the disciplines of exploring how outcomes change when assumptions vary, so the founder understands the range of plausible futures and which assumptions drive them. Scenario modeling tells coherent stories about different worlds; sensitivity modeling isolates which variables matter most. Together they prevent the trap of planning for one future and being blindsided when a key assumption breaks.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I plan for different scenarios", "what if my assumptions are wrong", "how do I stress test my model", or "how should an entrepreneur handle uncertainty in projections". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a single forecast and from treating sensitivity as a formality rather than a decision tool.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Single Forecast As One Of Many Possibilities

The base case is not the future; it is the central estimate given current assumptions, and it is almost certainly wrong in detail. Treat it as one point in a range. Planning that assumes the base case will occur sets the venture up for surprise when any assumption moves.

### Build Coherent Scenarios, Not Random Variations

Scenarios should be coherent stories about how the world could differ, not arbitrary percentage swings. A downside scenario describes a specific world: demand is slower, acquisition is more expensive, a competitor enters, funding markets close. Each scenario changes multiple related assumptions together, because that is how reality moves.

Tell stories about different worlds, then model each.

### Use Best, Base, And Worst At Minimum

At minimum, model three cases. The base case is the central estimate. The best case captures upside if key assumptions beat expectations. The worst case captures what happens if the riskiest assumptions fail. Ensure the worst case is genuinely stressful, not a mildly disappointing base case, because mild downside models hide real danger.

### Identify The Sensitivity Drivers

Run sensitivity analysis to find which assumptions most affect the outcomes that matter: runway, break-even, valuation, survival. Typically these are acquisition cost, conversion rate, retention, price, and gross margin. Knowing the drivers tells the founder where to gather evidence and where small improvements change the outcome.

### Link Scenarios To Decisions And Triggers

Scenarios are useful only if they change decisions. For each scenario, define what the founder would do: in the worst case, cut burn or pivot; in the base case, proceed; in the best case, invest aggressively. Define triggers, specific observable signals, that indicate which scenario is unfolding, so the founder can act before runway is gone.

### Model Tail Risks And Existential Outcomes

Some risks are not mild downsides but existential: a regulatory change that bans the product, a dependency on one platform that changes terms, a key customer loss. Identify tail risks that could end the venture and model or at least name them, even if their probability is low, because their impact is total.

### Avoid False Precision In Probabilities

Assigning precise probabilities to scenarios implies more knowledge than founders have. It is usually more honest to describe scenarios as plausible worlds with triggers than to claim a 23 percent chance of the worst case. Use probabilities cautiously and only where evidence supports them.

### Update Scenarios As Evidence Arrives

Scenarios decay like forecasts. As evidence arrives, update which worlds are plausible, narrow the range, and adjust the triggers and decisions. A scenario set built once and never revisited becomes stale and misleading.

### Use Scenarios To Preserve Optionality

Good scenario planning reveals where the venture is fragile and where optionality, the ability to change course, is valuable. Use the insights to preserve flexibility: avoid irreversible commitments that only make sense in the base case, and keep the ability to cut, defer, or pivot if the worst case unfolds.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Scenario outcomes carry high stakes for venture survival and founder personal risk. Tax, legal, and financial implications of modeled decisions may require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Single Forecast Planning

Planning for one future blindsides the venture when any assumption moves.

### Arbitrary Percentage Swings

Random variations are not scenarios; coherent stories about different worlds are.

### Mild Downside Mistaken For Worst Case

A mildly disappointing base case hides real danger; the worst case must be genuinely stressful.

### Sensitivity As Formality

Running sensitivity without linking it to decisions and triggers wastes the analysis.

### Ignoring Tail Risks

Low-probability existential risks are dismissed because they are unlikely, despite total impact.

### False Precision In Probabilities

Precise probabilities imply knowledge the founder does not have.
