# Startup Financial Model

A financial model is a structured set of assumptions about how a venture will make and spend money over time. Its purpose is not to predict the future precisely, which is impossible, but to make assumptions explicit, test how they interact, and support decisions about pricing, hiring, spending, and fundraising. A good model is transparent: every number traces to a stated assumption, the assumptions are challengeable, and the model reveals which levers matter most. A bad model hides assumptions, produces a single confident forecast, and collapses the moment reality differs.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I build a financial model", "what should my projections look like", "how do I forecast revenue", or "how should an entrepreneur model their finances". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a black-box forecast and from confusing precision with accuracy.

## Core Rules

### Model The Business, Not A Spreadsheet Template

A model is useful only if it reflects how the specific venture works. Model the real drivers: how customers are acquired and at what cost, how they convert and retain, how revenue is recognized, how costs scale with volume, and how working capital moves with growth. A generic template filled with numbers that do not trace to the business produces false confidence.

Start from the business mechanics, then build the spreadsheet.

### Make Every Number Trace To An Assumption

Every cell in the model should trace to a stated, challengeable assumption. Revenue is not a typed growth percentage; it is customers times price, where customers come from acquisition volume times conversion, and so on. Traceable assumptions let the founder and investors see what the model depends on and change it as evidence arrives.

### Separate Knowns, Estimates, And Guesses

Not all inputs are equal. Some are known, current costs, signed contracts, existing customers. Some are estimates based on early data, conversion rates, churn. Some are guesses, long-term growth, market response. Label each input by its evidence quality so the model's reliability is visible and attention focuses on the weakest links.

### Build From Unit Economics Upward

The most credible models build from the unit outward: the economics of a single customer, acquisition cost, revenue, retention, contribution margin, then multiplied by the volume the venture can acquire and serve. Top-down models, a percentage of a market, are easy to dismiss. Bottom-up models grounded in unit economics are credible and testable.

### Model Cash, Not Just Profit

A model that shows profit but ignores cash timing misleads about survival. Include when revenue is collected, when costs are paid, how growth consumes working capital, and how fixed commitments behave. Cash runway and the timing of cash dips matter more for early-stage decisions than accounting profit.

### Include The Full Cost Structure

Models often omit real costs: taxes, payment processing, refunds and chargebacks, hosting that scales with usage, customer support that scales with customers, and the overhead of compliance and insurance. Include the full cost structure, or the model will flatter reality and mislead decisions.

### Stress Test The Key Assumptions

Identify the assumptions to which the model is most sensitive, typically acquisition cost, conversion, retention, and price, and test what happens when each is worse than expected. The point is not to find the worst case but to see which assumptions the venture's survival depends on, so effort and evidence focus there.

### Use The Model To Support Decisions

A model earns its keep by informing decisions: whether to hire, whether to raise, what to charge, which channel to invest in. Connect the model to the choices the founder faces. A model that produces a forecast but does not change any decision is decorative.

### Rebuild And Update As Evidence Arrives

Models decay. As real data arrives, replace guesses with estimates and estimates with knowns. Update the model regularly so it reflects current understanding, not the assumptions held months ago. A stale model misleads.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Financial models inform decisions with high stakes for survival and personal risk. Tax, accounting, and regulatory implications of modeled decisions may require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Black-Box Forecast

A model whose numbers do not trace to assumptions produces false confidence and cannot be challenged.

### Precision Mistaken For Accuracy

Detailed decimals do not make a guess accurate; precision hides uncertainty.

### Top-Down Market Sizing

A percentage of a giant market is easy to dismiss and rarely credible.

### Ignoring Cash Timing

Profitable-on-paper models can still run out of money.

### Omitting Real Costs

Hidden costs flatter the model and mislead hiring, pricing, and fundraising decisions.

### Single Optimistic Forecast

One forecast without stress testing hides which assumptions survival depends on.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The model reflects the venture's real business mechanics, not a generic template.
- [ ] Every number traces to a stated, challengeable assumption.
- [ ] Inputs are labeled by evidence quality: known, estimate, or guess.
- [ ] The model builds from unit economics upward, not top-down.
- [ ] Cash timing and working capital are modeled, not only profit.
- [ ] The full cost structure is included, including scaling and overhead costs.
- [ ] Key assumptions are stress tested to reveal what survival depends on.
- [ ] The model is connected to specific decisions the founder faces.
- [ ] The model is updated as real evidence replaces assumptions.
- [ ] Tax, accounting, and regulatory implications are flagged for professional advice.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a single precise forecast as the likely future.
