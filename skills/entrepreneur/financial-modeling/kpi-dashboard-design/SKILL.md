# KPI Dashboard Design

A KPI dashboard is the set of numbers a founder watches to know whether the venture is healthy and heading in the right direction. Its purpose is to focus attention on the few metrics that reflect the real drivers of the business and to trigger decisions when they move. A good dashboard is small, honest, and tied to action; a bad dashboard is large, flattering, and decorative, full of vanity metrics that look good but reveal nothing. The discipline is choosing metrics that reflect truth, even when uncomfortable, and refusing to track numbers that merely make the venture look busy.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what metrics should I track", "how do I build a KPI dashboard", "what are the right KPIs", or "how should an entrepreneur measure their startup". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a long list of flattering metrics and from separating measurement from decisions.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Venture's Current Goal

The right metrics depend on what the venture is trying to achieve right now. An early venture validating demand watches different metrics than a venture scaling acquisition or one optimizing retention. Start from the current goal, then choose the metrics that show whether progress toward it is being made.

A dashboard that ignores the current goal tracks the wrong things.

### Choose Leading Indicators Over Lagging Ones

Lagging indicators, revenue, profit, churn, tell you what already happened. Leading indicators, pipeline, activation, engagement, tell you what is about to happen. Early ventures benefit from leading indicators because they reveal problems and opportunities before they appear in lagging results. Balance both, but weight toward leading indicators that give time to act.

### Track Metrics That Reflect The Core Driver

Every venture has a core driver, the mechanism that makes it work: activation for a product-led tool, retention for a subscription, contribution margin for a transactional business, acquisition cost for a paid-growth model. The dashboard must include the metrics that reflect that driver, because if the driver breaks, the venture fails even if other numbers look fine.

### Keep The Dashboard Small And Focused

A dashboard with thirty metrics is not a dashboard; it is a report no one reads. Keep the core dashboard to a handful of metrics, the ones that would change a decision if they moved. Detailed metrics belong in supporting reports, reviewed less often. Focus forces honesty about what matters.

### Distinguish Validating Metrics From Vanity Metrics

Validating metrics connect to the thesis: retention that proves value, unit economics that prove sustainability, acquisition that proves demand. Vanity metrics, total downloads, registered users, page views, look impressive but prove nothing because they ignore engagement and economics. Refuse to track vanity metrics as KPIs, because they flatter without informing.

### Include Cohort And Trend Views, Not Only Totals

Totals hide problems. A growing total can conceal rising churn, falling activation, or worsening unit economics. Use cohort views, how customers who joined at different times behave, and trends, how rates change over time, to see whether the venture is improving or merely accumulating.

### Tie Each Metric To A Decision And A Threshold

A metric without a decision is decoration. For each KPI, know what action it triggers and at what threshold: if activation drops below X, investigate onboarding; if CAC rises above Y, review channels. Metrics that do not change any decision should not be on the dashboard.

### Be Honest About Negative Signals

A dashboard that only tracks good news is a comfort blanket. Include metrics that can reveal trouble, even when uncomfortable: churn, refund rate, support load, cash burn. The value of measurement is to surface problems early, not to reassure.

### Review At The Right Cadence

Different metrics deserve different review cadences. Leading indicators and cash may need weekly review; lagging results and strategic metrics may need monthly. Match the cadence to the metric's volatility and the speed at which the founder can act.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

KPI decisions inform choices with high stakes for survival and personal risk. Financial and regulatory metrics may require professional interpretation. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Vanity Metrics On The Dashboard

Downloads and registered users look good but prove nothing about value or sustainability.

### Dashboard Too Large To Act On

A wall of metrics is read by no one and changes no decision.

### Totals Hiding Decay

Growing totals conceal rising churn or worsening unit economics.

### Metrics Without Decisions

Tracking numbers that do not trigger any action is decorative measurement.

### Ignoring The Core Driver

Tracking generic startup metrics that do not reflect how this specific venture works.

### Only Good-News Metrics

A dashboard that cannot show trouble provides false comfort and misses early warnings.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The dashboard starts from the venture's current goal, not a generic metric list.
- [ ] Leading indicators are weighted alongside lagging ones.
- [ ] Metrics reflecting the venture's core driver are included.
- [ ] The core dashboard is small and focused, with detail in supporting reports.
- [ ] Vanity metrics are excluded from KPIs.
- [ ] Cohort and trend views are used, not only totals.
- [ ] Each metric is tied to a decision and a threshold.
- [ ] Metrics that can reveal negative signals are included, not only good news.
- [ ] Review cadence matches each metric's volatility and actionability.
- [ ] Financial and regulatory metrics are flagged for professional interpretation.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a long list of flattering metrics disconnected from decisions.
