# Positioning Statement Crafting

A positioning statement is the internal articulation of who the product is for, what it does, why it is different, and why that matters. It is not marketing copy; it is the reference that keeps product, pricing, messaging, and sales aligned. A good positioning statement is specific enough to guide decisions and short enough to be remembered and repeated. A weak one is either so generic it could describe any company or so elaborate no one can recall it. Crafting one is an exercise in disciplined compression.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I write a positioning statement", "what should my positioning statement say", "is my positioning statement good", or "how should an entrepreneur articulate their position". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a polished paragraph that sounds strategic but cannot guide a single real decision.

## Core Rules

### Use A Clear Structure, But Do Not Worship The Template

A common structure is: for a specific customer, who has a specific need, our product is a category that provides a key benefit, unlike the primary alternative, because of a reason to believe. The structure is useful because it forces each element to be explicit, but the goal is clarity, not template compliance. Adapt the structure if it produces a misleading statement.

### Make Each Element Specific And Decision-Relevant

Every slot in the statement should be specific enough that it rules out alternatives. "For businesses" rules out nothing; "for independent specialty clinics under five providers" rules out most of the market. Specificity is what makes the statement useful for choosing customers, features, and channels.

Test each element: does naming this rule out anyone or anything? If not, it is too vague.

### Anchor The Customer In A Real Segment

The customer element should name a segment that is reachable, has the problem, and can pay. Avoid abstract archetypes that describe no real buyer. The more situational the customer description, the more the statement can guide targeting.

### State The Need Or Job, Not The Feature

The need element should describe the progress the customer wants, not the feature you provide. "Who need to reduce no-shows" is a need; "who need automated reminders" is a feature masquerading as a need. The need keeps the statement customer-anchored.

### Choose The Category Deliberately

The category element sets the frame of reference and the comparison set. Choose a category that produces favorable comparisons and accurate expectations. Be deliberate about whether you are entering an existing category, re-framing one, or attempting to create a new one, since each carries different cost.

### Make The Differentiator Defensible And Believable

The differentiator should be a structural advantage, not a claim of superiority. "Because of our proprietary claims dataset built over five years" is more believable than "because we are the best". The reason to believe should be something a skeptic would find credible.

### Keep It Short Enough To Be Remembered

A positioning statement that no one can repeat from memory will not guide anyone. Aim for a statement that fits in two or three sentences and can be spoken aloud in one breath. If it requires a paragraph, it is not yet a statement; it is a description.

### Use The Statement To Test Alignment

The statement is a reference point. Use it to check whether product decisions, pricing, messaging, and channel choices reinforce the position. If a decision contradicts the statement, either the decision or the statement must change.

### Treat The Statement As A Living Document

As the venture learns, the statement should evolve. A statement frozen at founding becomes fiction within months. Revisit it as customer understanding, competitive context, and capability change.

### Acknowledge Risk And Professional Advice

Positioning statements that imply regulated, health, or financial outcomes can create legal exposure if they overstate capability. This skill does not replace professional advice for statements in regulated domains.

## Common Traps

### Generic Statements That Describe Anyone

"For businesses that want to grow" rules out no one and guides nothing.

### Feature Disguised As Need

Stating the feature instead of the customer's desired progress keeps the statement product-centric.

### Unbelievable Differentiator

Claims of superiority without a structural reason to believe invite skepticism.

### Too Long To Remember

A paragraph-length statement will not be internalized or used.

### Template Worship Over Clarity

Forcing a template that produces a misleading statement defeats the purpose.

### Frozen Statement

A statement that never updates drifts from reality as the venture learns.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The statement uses a clear structure adapted for clarity, not template compliance.
- [ ] Each element is specific enough to rule out alternatives.
- [ ] The customer names a real, reachable, paying segment.
- [ ] The need states the customer's desired progress, not a product feature.
- [ ] The category is chosen deliberately for favorable comparisons.
- [ ] The differentiator is structural and believable to a skeptic.
- [ ] The statement is short enough to be remembered and repeated.
- [ ] The statement is used to test alignment of product, pricing, messaging, and channel.
- [ ] The statement is treated as a living document, updated as learning arrives.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a generic or overly long paragraph as a usable positioning statement.
