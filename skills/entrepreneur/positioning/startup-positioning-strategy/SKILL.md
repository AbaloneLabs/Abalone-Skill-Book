# Startup Positioning Strategy

Positioning is the place a venture occupies in the customer's mind relative to alternatives. It is not a slogan and not a feature list; it is the answer to "what kind of thing is this, and who is it for". Customers will position you whether you guide them or not, and if you leave it to chance they will often position you wrongly, as a cheaper version of an incumbent or as a confusing hybrid. Deliberate positioning strategy chooses the category, the comparison, and the association you want customers to hold, and aligns everything else to reinforce it.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how should I position my startup", "what category am I in", "how do I want customers to think of us", or "how should an entrepreneur develop positioning". The goal is to prevent the agent from leaving positioning to default and from producing positioning that contradicts the product, pricing, or channel.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Category Deliberately

Customers understand new things by analogy to categories they know. The category you choose determines what you are compared against and what expectations you inherit. Choose a category that sets favorable comparisons and accurate expectations.

Ask: if customers file us under category X, who do they compare us to, what do they expect to pay, and what do they expect us to do? Choose the category whose answers help you most.

### Define The Frame Of Reference And The Difference

Positioning has two parts: the frame of reference, what you are, and the difference, why you matter within that frame. State both. "We are a scheduling tool" is a frame without a difference; "we are the scheduling tool built for solo clinics" adds a difference that selects a customer.

A positioning without a difference is invisible; a difference without a frame is confusing.

### Align Positioning With The Customer's Existing Mental Model

Positioning works with the grain of how customers already think. A position that requires customers to learn a new mental category is expensive and slow. Where possible, anchor in a category they know and then add the differentiating twist.

Radical category creation can work but carries education cost and timing risk that must be weighed.

### Make Positioning Coherent Across Every Touchpoint

A position is credible only if everything reinforces it. Product behavior, pricing level, visual identity, sales motion, channel choice, and customer service must all express the same position. Contradictions, premium position with cheap pricing, simple position with complex onboarding, erode trust.

Audit every touchpoint against the intended position.

### Choose A Position You Can Defend

A position is only valuable if you can hold it. Defensibility comes from structural fit: a position built on a customer segment, cost structure, data, or distribution you actually command. A position claimed without the structural support to hold it will be outflanked.

Ask what stops a competitor from claiming the same position more credibly.

### Repel The Wrong Customers

Strong positioning repels as much as it attracts. A position that appeals to everyone is too vague to be owned. Deliberate positioning should make the wrong customers self-deselect, which conserves sales and support effort for the right ones.

If no one is repelled, the position is probably too broad.

### Sequence Positioning With Maturity

Early positioning can be narrow and sharp, owning a specific niche, then broaden as credibility and capability grow. Trying to position as a broad platform before owning a niche usually produces a position no one believes. Plan the sequence from sharp and narrow to broader, not the reverse.

### Test Positioning In Market, Not Only Internally

Positioning is a hypothesis about how customers will perceive you. Test it by observing which positioning generates engagement, comprehension, and commitment from the right customers. Internal preference is not evidence.

### Acknowledge Risk And Professional Advice

Positioning claims that overstate capability or imply regulated outcomes can create legal and reputational risk. This skill does not replace professional advice for positioning in regulated domains.

## Common Traps

### Leaving Positioning To Default

Customers will position you, often unfavorably, if you do not guide them.

### Position Without Difference

A frame of reference without a differentiator is invisible in a crowded market.

### Contradictory Touchpoints

Premium positioning undermined by cheap pricing, or simple positioning undermined by complex onboarding, destroys credibility.

### Positioning For Everyone

A position that attracts all repels none and owns nothing.

### Claiming A Position Without Structural Support

A position you cannot defend will be taken by a better-supported competitor.

### Premature Broadening

Positioning as a broad platform before owning a niche produces disbelief.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The category is chosen deliberately, considering the comparisons and expectations it inherits.
- [ ] Both the frame of reference and the differentiating twist are stated.
- [ ] Positioning works with the customer's existing mental model, with education cost weighed.
- [ ] Product, pricing, identity, sales motion, and channel all reinforce the same position.
- [ ] The position is defensible through structural fit, not merely claimed.
- [ ] The positioning repels the wrong customers as well as attracting the right ones.
- [ ] Positioning is sequenced from sharp and narrow to broader as credibility grows.
- [ ] Positioning is tested in market with the right customers, not only preferred internally.
- [ ] Claims are truthful and not overstated relative to capability.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a slogan or feature list as a coherent positioning strategy.
