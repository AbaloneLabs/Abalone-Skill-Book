# Bottom-Up Market Sizing

Bottom-up market sizing starts with the individual customer and builds outward. Instead of slicing a percentage from an industry report, it asks: how many real customers exist, what would they pay, how often, and over what period. The method is slower and more uncomfortable than top-down because every assumption is exposed, but it is far more useful. It reveals whether the reachable market can sustain the business and where the weakest link in the estimate lies.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I build a bottom-up market model", "is my market size defensible", "how many customers do I need", or "how should an entrepreneur size a market from the customer up". The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to top-down percentages and from hiding weak volume or price assumptions behind an impressive total.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Smallest Unit Of Value

Begin with one customer, one purchase, and one period. Define the unit economics of a single transaction before multiplying anything.

Capture:

- who is the single customer;
- what they buy;
- the price per unit;
- the billing frequency;
- the expected duration of the relationship;
- the gross margin per customer.

Only once the unit is honest should you scale it up.

### Count Real Customers, Not Abstract Segments

The volume input is where bottom-up models usually break. Count actual customers using real, citable data where possible.

Sources for customer counts:

- industry association membership lists;
- government census or business registries;
- platform or directory data;
- trade publication figures;
- primary research and interviews.

Narrow the count by the constraints that actually apply: geography, size band, industry, behavior, and reachability. A count of "all small businesses" is usually useless; a count of "independent clinics in these three regions with under five providers" is usable.

### Apply Filters Down To The Reachable Segment

Walk the count through filters until you reach the customers you can actually serve and reach.

Typical filter chain:

- total population in category;
- within your geography;
- matching segment criteria;
- with the specific problem;
- reachable through your channels;
- likely to consider switching;
- able to pay your price.

Document the conversion at each filter. The final count is your volume basis.

### Multiply By Realistic Price And Frequency

Price and frequency are the second common failure point. Use the price the segment actually pays for comparable outcomes, not the price you wish they paid.

Consider:

- current spend on alternatives;
- willingness to pay from interviews;
- price benchmarks from comparable products;
- frequency of need;
- seasonality;
- expansion or contraction over time.

Multiply volume by realistic price and frequency to reach the reachable market value.

### Sanity-Check Against Top-Down

After building the bottom-up model, compare it to a top-down estimate. If they differ by an order of magnitude, one of them is wrong. Investigate the gap rather than picking the more flattering number.

The bottom-up number is usually more trustworthy, but a large discrepancy signals a hidden assumption worth examining.

### Model A Capture Range, Not A Single Number

Express the obtainable market as a range based on capture assumptions: conservative, base, and aggressive. Tie each scenario to specific channel throughput and competitive presence rather than to optimism.

A range is more honest and more useful for planning than a single heroic figure.

### Identify The Weakest Link

Every bottom-up model has one assumption that drives most of the result. Find it. If that assumption is wrong, the whole estimate collapses. State it explicitly and consider how to test it.

Common weak links:

- the reachable customer count;
- the conversion from aware to paying;
- the realistic price;
- the retention or repeat rate;
- the channel capacity.

### Tie Sizing To Decisions

A market size is useful only if it changes a decision: whether to enter, how much to invest, which segment to target first, whether the economics can close. If the number changes nothing, the exercise was decoration.

### Acknowledge Uncertainty And Personal Risk

Bottom-up sizing reduces but does not remove uncertainty. Early ventures operate on assumptions that may be wrong; sizing should inform, not replace, judgment about founder runway and risk tolerance. This skill does not replace professional financial advice.

## Common Traps

### Defaulting To Top-Down Percentages

"One percent of a huge market" avoids the hard work of counting real customers.

### Inflating Volume With Broad Categories

Counting everyone in a category hides that most are unreachable or unqualified.

### Using Aspirational Price

The price you hope to charge rarely matches what the segment currently pays.

### Single Heroic Number

A single bottom-up figure presented without ranges or weak-link analysis looks precise but is fragile.

### Ignoring The Sanity Check

Refusing to compare against top-down leaves large discrepancies unexamined.

### Sizing Without A Decision

A number that changes no plan is effort spent on appearance, not learning.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The model starts from a single customer unit with defined price, frequency, duration, and margin.
- [ ] Customer counts come from real, citable sources, not abstract segment labels.
- [ ] Counts are filtered down to a reachable segment with documented conversion at each step.
- [ ] Price and frequency reflect what the segment actually pays, not aspiration.
- [ ] The bottom-up result is sanity-checked against a top-down estimate, and gaps are investigated.
- [ ] Obtainable market is expressed as a range tied to capture assumptions.
- [ ] The weakest-link assumption is identified and flagged for testing.
- [ ] The size is connected to a concrete decision about entry, investment, or targeting.
- [ ] Filters include geography, segment, problem, channel, switching likelihood, and price tolerance.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a single precise number as if uncertainty did not exist.
