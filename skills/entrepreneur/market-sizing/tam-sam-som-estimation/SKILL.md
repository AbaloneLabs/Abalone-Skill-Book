# TAM SAM SOM Estimation

Market sizing is one of the most abused exercises in entrepreneurship. Founders quote enormous total addressable markets to feel ambitious, then confuse that number with what they can actually capture. TAM, SAM, and SOM exist to separate the fantasy of a giant market from the discipline of a reachable one. Used honestly, they force a founder to confront who they can actually serve, who they can actually reach, and what share is realistic given competition, channel, and runway.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how big is my market", "how do I size a startup opportunity", "what TAM should I claim", or "how should an entrepreneur estimate obtainable market". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a top-down vanity number and presenting it as a credible opportunity.

## Core Rules

### Define Each Layer Precisely

The three layers answer different questions and must not be blurred.

- TAM, total addressable market: everyone in the world who could conceivably use the category of solution, regardless of whether you can reach them.
- SAM, serviceable addressable market: the subset of TAM you can realistically serve given your geography, segment, product type, and business model.
- SOM, serviceable obtainable market: the share of SAM you can realistically capture in a defined period given competition, channel limits, runway, and team capacity.

Each layer should shrink as constraints are added. If your SAM equals your TAM, you have not applied any constraints.

### Prefer Bottom-Up Over Top-Down

Top-down sizing starts with a giant industry number and slices a percentage off it. It looks rigorous and proves almost nothing, because the percentage is arbitrary and the industry definition rarely matches your actual product.

Bottom-up sizing starts from real units: number of customers in the segment, multiplied by price, multiplied by frequency. It forces you to name the customer, the price, and the volume, and it exposes weak assumptions immediately.

Build the bottom-up view first; use top-down only as a sanity check.

### Ground Numbers In Real Counts

Every input should trace to a real, citable source or a clearly labeled assumption. Avoid round, comfortable numbers that suggest guessing.

For each input record:

- the source or assumption;
- the date;
- the confidence level;
- what would change the number.

A defensible market size is one a skeptic could audit line by line.

### Apply Constraints Honestly At Each Layer

Constraints are what make SAM and SOM credible. Apply them explicitly.

Typical constraints:

- geography you can serve and support;
- segment you can reach through available channels;
- language, currency, and regulatory limits;
- price tier realistic for the segment;
- sales motion capacity;
- competition already holding share;
- runway and time horizon.

Each constraint should reduce the number. A SOM that ignores competition and capacity is not obtainable; it is aspirational.

### Size The SOM Against Capacity And Time

SOM is not what you hope to capture; it is what you can plausibly capture given how many customers you can actually acquire, onboard, and serve in the period. Tie SOM to channel throughput, sales capacity, and the runway available to reach it.

A SOM that exceeds your acquisition capacity is internally inconsistent.

### Separate Current Market From Future Market

Markets grow, shrink, and shift. State whether the size reflects today's market, a forecasted future market, or a market you believe will emerge. Future markets are riskier and should be discounted, not treated as certain.

Distinguish:

- existing spend you can capture;
- latent demand that would spend if activated;
- future demand that depends on trends, regulation, or behavior change.

### Use Sizing To Test Reachability, Not To Impress

The purpose of sizing is to reveal whether the reachable market is large enough to matter and small enough to win. A huge TAM with a tiny reachable SOM signals a distribution problem. A modest TAM with a reachable SOM may be a far better business.

### Acknowledge Uncertainty And Personal Risk

Early market sizing is approximate. Present ranges and assumptions, not false precision. Oversized claims create investor and founder risk when reality arrives. This skill does not replace professional financial or investment advice.

## Common Traps

### TAM Presented As The Opportunity

A giant TAM is irrelevant if the reachable SOM is too small to sustain the business.

### Arbitrary Top-Down Percentages

"Even one percent of a huge market" is a hand-wave, not an estimate.

### SAM Equals TAM

If serviceable equals total, no real constraints were applied.

### Ignoring Competition In SOM

Obtainable share assumes competitors hold their ground; ignoring them inflates SOM.

### Round-Number Guessing

Comfortable round numbers usually mean inputs were invented, not researched.

### Confusing Future Market With Current Market

Forecasting a market that does not yet exist as if it were present hides timing and adoption risk.

## Self-Check

- [ ] TAM, SAM, and SOM are defined as distinct layers, each smaller than the last.
- [ ] A bottom-up estimate based on real units is built before any top-down figure.
- [ ] Every input traces to a source, date, and confidence level.
- [ ] Geography, segment, channel, price, regulatory, and capacity constraints are applied at the SAM and SOM layers.
- [ ] SOM is tied to acquisition capacity, sales motion, and runway, not to aspiration.
- [ ] Current market is distinguished from forecasted or latent future market.
- [ ] Ranges and assumptions are presented rather than false precision.
- [ ] Competition's existing share is accounted for in obtainable market.
- [ ] Sizing is used to test reachability, not only to impress.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting TAM as the realistic opportunity for the venture.
