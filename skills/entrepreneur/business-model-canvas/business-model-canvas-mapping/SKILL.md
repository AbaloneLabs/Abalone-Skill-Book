# Business Model Canvas Mapping

The business model canvas is a single-page map of how a venture creates, delivers, and captures value across nine interlocking blocks. Its power is not the framework itself but the discipline of forcing a founder to make every part explicit and then checking whether the parts fit together. A canvas where the customer segment does not match the value proposition, or where the revenue does not cover the cost structure, is a map of a business that cannot work. Used honestly, the canvas exposes the assumptions most likely to fail.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I map my business model", "what goes in each canvas block", "is my business model consistent", or "how should an entrepreneur use a business model canvas". The goal is to prevent the agent from filling the nine boxes mechanically and missing the relationships between them that determine viability.

## Core Rules

### Fill All Nine Blocks, But Prioritize The Riskiest

The nine blocks are customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partnerships, and cost structure. Fill each, but do not treat them as equally important. Identify which blocks carry the most uncertainty and lethal risk, and pressure those first.

Usually the riskiest blocks early are customer segments, value propositions, and revenue streams; the operational blocks matter more once demand is proven.

### Make Customer Segments And Value Propositions Specific

Generic segments and vague value propositions make the canvas useless. Name the specific customer and the specific outcome, and pair each segment with the value proposition meant for it. If one value proposition is meant to serve many segments, question whether it truly fits each.

The fit between segment and value proposition is the heart of the model; everything else exists to support it.

### Ensure Channels And Relationships Are Reachable

Channels describe how you reach the customer; relationships describe how you acquire, retain, and grow them. Both must be realistic for the segment named. Listing "social media" or "partnerships" without explaining how they produce customers at acceptable cost is not a channel strategy.

For each channel, ask why it is accessible to you specifically, not just why it exists.

### Tie Revenue Streams To Willingness And Ability To Pay

Each revenue stream must connect to a customer who will and can pay. State the pricing model, the payer, and the justification. If the user and payer differ, explain the cross-subsidy or third-party logic.

A canvas with revenue streams but no payer justification is a wish.

### Map Key Resources, Activities, And Partnerships To Delivery

These operational blocks exist to deliver the value proposition and run the channels. Check that the resources, activities, and partnerships named are actually sufficient to deliver what is promised.

Common gaps include missing regulatory licenses, missing data access, missing supplier relationships, or missing technical capability. If a critical resource or partner is assumed but unsecured, flag it as a risk.

### Make The Cost Structure Honest And Complete

The cost structure should reflect what it really takes to run the model: key resources, key activities, partnerships, channels, and customer relationships all cost money. Distinguish fixed from variable and step costs, and check that revenue can cover them at realistic volume.

A canvas whose cost structure is vague or optimistic hides whether the business can be profitable.

### Check Internal Consistency Across Blocks

The most valuable use of the canvas is consistency checking. Walk across the blocks and ask whether they reinforce each other.

Ask:

- does the value proposition fit the segment;
- do the channels reach that segment affordably;
- does the revenue model match how that segment experiences value;
- do the resources and partnerships actually enable delivery;
- does the cost structure leave room for profit at realistic scale.

Inconsistencies between blocks are more dangerous than weakness in any single block.

### Use The Canvas To Surface Assumptions

Every entry in the canvas is an assumption. Mark which entries are evidence-backed and which are guesses, and prioritize testing the guesses that would kill the model if wrong. The canvas is a map of hypotheses, not a statement of facts.

### Iterate The Canvas As Learning Arrives

The canvas is not a one-time artifact. As interviews, pilots, and sales reveal that assumptions are wrong, update the blocks. A canvas that never changes after launch is a sign learning has stopped.

### Acknowledge Limits And Professional Risk

The canvas is a thinking tool, not a plan or a guarantee. Some blocks, especially revenue, partnerships, and cost structure, may carry regulatory, tax, or legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Filling Boxes Without Checking Fit

A complete canvas with inconsistent blocks maps a business that cannot work.

### Generic Segments And Vague Value Propositions

"Consumers" and "great service" fill space but guide no decision.

### Channels Listed Without Reachability

Naming a channel does not explain how it produces customers at acceptable cost.

### Revenue Without A Payer Justification

Revenue streams require a customer who will and can pay.

### Optimistic Or Incomplete Cost Structure

Vague costs hide whether the model can ever be profitable.

### Treating The Canvas As Finished

A static canvas ignores that every entry is an assumption subject to revision.

## Self-Check

- [ ] All nine blocks are filled, with the riskiest blocks pressure-tested first.
- [ ] Customer segments and value propositions are specific and paired for fit.
- [ ] Channels and relationships are reachable and explain how customers are acquired at acceptable cost.
- [ ] Each revenue stream is tied to a payer who will and can pay.
- [ ] Key resources, activities, and partnerships are sufficient to deliver the value proposition.
- [ ] The cost structure is honest, complete, and distinguishes fixed, variable, and step costs.
- [ ] Internal consistency across blocks is checked, especially segment-to-value and revenue-to-cost fit.
- [ ] Assumptions are marked and the riskiest guesses are prioritized for testing.
- [ ] The canvas is treated as a living document, updated as learning arrives.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a filled canvas as proof of a viable business without consistency and evidence.
