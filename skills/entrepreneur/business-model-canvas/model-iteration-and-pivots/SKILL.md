# Model Iteration And Pivots

A business model is not chosen once and defended forever; it is iterated as evidence arrives about what works and what does not. The art is distinguishing a model that needs more time from one that is structurally broken, and knowing when a small iteration is enough versus when a true pivot is required. Iterate too timidly and a dying model drains runway; pivot too eagerly and the venture never compounds learning. The discipline is to let evidence, not frustration or boredom, drive the decision.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should I change my business model", "when is it time to pivot", "how do I iterate my model", or "how should an entrepreneur decide between persevering and pivoting". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a pivot out of impatience or from defending a failing model out of sunk cost.

## Core Rules

### Define What "Working" Means Before Judging The Model

You cannot decide whether to iterate or pivot without explicit success criteria. Define, in advance, the signals that indicate the model is working: acquisition cost, conversion, retention, payback, gross margin, activation rate, referral behavior. Without these, every result gets reinterpreted subjectively.

Revisit the criteria regularly and judge the model against them, not against mood.

### Distinguish Iteration From Pivot By What Changes

Iteration improves elements within the same model: refining the segment, tuning the channel, adjusting price, sharpening the value proposition. A pivot changes a foundational assumption: a different customer, a different problem, a different revenue model, a different value-creation mechanism.

Name which is happening. An iteration that quietly accumulates into a different business is a pivot that has not been acknowledged.

### Diagnose The Specific Failure Before Changing

Before iterating or pivoting, diagnose where the model is actually breaking. A model can fail at demand, at economics, at retention, at delivery, or at distribution, and each failure points to a different response.

Ask:

- is the problem that customers do not want it;
- is the problem that they want it but will not pay enough;
- is the problem that they pay but churn;
- is the problem that acquisition is too expensive;
- is the problem that delivery is too costly;
- is the problem that the channel is unreachable.

Changing the wrong element wastes the iteration.

### Preserve Learning When Pivoting

A pivot should preserve hard-won learning, not erase it. Even a failed model teaches which segments, channels, and problems do not work. Carry that knowledge into the new model so the pivot is a reconfiguration of assets and insight, not a restart from zero.

State explicitly what is preserved: customer relationships, technical capability, domain knowledge, data, brand.

### Time The Decision Against Runway

The decision to persevere or pivot is constrained by runway. A model that might work in eighteen months cannot be pursued with six months of cash. Build the decision around how much time remains to test the next model and reach the next milestone.

Do not start a pivot too late to finish it.

### Avoid Pivot-By-Frustration And Persevere-By-Sunk-Cost

Two emotional failure modes distort the decision. Pivot-by-frustration abandons a model because the founder is tired or bored, even when evidence supports persevering. Persevere-by-sunk-cost defends a failing model because of money and time already spent, even when evidence says stop.

Counter both by anchoring the decision to pre-defined evidence criteria and runway, not to feeling.

### Test The Pivot Before Fully Committing

A pivot is itself a hypothesis. Before dismantling the current model, test the new direction with the cheapest possible experiment. Confirm that the new segment, problem, or model shows signal before abandoning what exists.

This protects against lurching from one untested model to another.

### Communicate The Pivot Clearly

A pivot affects co-founders, employees, investors, and existing customers. Communicate what is changing, what is preserved, why, and what the new success criteria are. Ambiguity erodes trust and alignment precisely when they are most needed.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Pivots consume runway and can affect legal, employment, investor, and customer obligations. Some changes, such as altering a revenue model or exiting a segment, may carry regulatory or contractual implications. This skill does not replace professional advice.

## Common Traps

### Pivot Out Of Impatience

Boredom or slow progress is not evidence the model is wrong.

### Persevere Out Of Sunk Cost

Money already spent is not a reason to keep spending on a failing model.

### Iterating The Wrong Element

Changing price when the real failure is retention, or changing features when the real failure is channel, wastes effort.

### Calling A Pivot An Iteration

Quietly drifting into a different business without acknowledging it destroys clarity and learning.

### Pivot Without Testing First

Committing fully to a new untested model can repeat the original mistake faster.

### Ignoring Runway

A pivot that cannot be completed before cash runs out is not a viable plan.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Success criteria for the current model are defined in advance and revisited.
- [ ] The change is correctly classified as iteration within the model or pivot of a foundational assumption.
- [ ] The specific point of failure, demand, economics, retention, delivery, or distribution, is diagnosed before changing.
- [ ] Learning, assets, and relationships are preserved and carried into the new model.
- [ ] The decision is timed against remaining runway, with enough time to test and reach the next milestone.
- [ ] The decision is anchored to evidence criteria, not to frustration or sunk cost.
- [ ] The new direction is tested cheaply before the current model is fully abandoned.
- [ ] The pivot is communicated clearly to co-founders, team, investors, and affected customers.
- [ ] Legal, employment, investor, and customer obligations affected by the change are considered.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a pivot or perseverance without evidence, diagnosis, and runway context.
