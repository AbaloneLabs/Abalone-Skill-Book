# Vendor Selection

Choosing a vendor is a decision the venture lives with for a long time. The wrong vendor can impose costs through poor reliability, restrictive terms, integration burden, or lock-in that makes switching painful. Vendor selection is not only about price; it is about fit with the venture's needs, reliability under the conditions that matter, terms that remain fair as the venture grows, and the ability to switch if the vendor fails or the venture's needs change. The discipline is to evaluate vendors on the dimensions that affect the venture over the full relationship, not only the signup cost.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I choose a vendor", "which provider should I use", "what should I look for in a vendor", or "how should an entrepreneur select vendors". The goal is to prevent the agent from choosing on price alone and from ignoring reliability, terms, and switching costs.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Venture's Actual Needs

Define what the venture actually needs from the vendor before evaluating options: which features are essential, what volume and scale are expected, what reliability is required, what integrations are necessary. A vendor excellent on paper but missing a critical need is the wrong choice. Let the needs drive the evaluation, not the vendor's marketing.

### Evaluate Reliability Under Real Conditions

Vendors report uptime and performance in averages that can hide problems. Evaluate reliability under the conditions that matter to the venture: peak load, edge cases, incident response, and support quality during outages. Talk to existing customers, check status histories, and test where possible. Reliability is revealed under stress, not in marketing.

### Read The Terms Before Committing

Terms of service, pricing changes, data ownership, termination conditions, and usage limits determine the real cost and risk of the relationship. Read them before committing. A vendor with attractive pricing but terms that allow unilateral changes, data lock-in, or difficult exit creates long-term risk that outweighs short-term savings.

### Assess Integration Burden And Fit

A vendor that does not integrate cleanly with the venture's existing stack imposes ongoing cost through custom work, data sync issues, and maintenance. Assess how the vendor fits the current and planned stack, the quality of APIs and documentation, and the effort required to integrate. Poor fit compounds over time.

### Estimate Switching Costs Before Choosing

Before selecting a vendor, estimate what it would cost to switch away: data migration, reintegration, retraining, and transitional risk. High switching costs create lock-in that weakens the venture's position over time. Prefer vendors with lower switching costs and data portability, especially for critical capabilities.

### Compare Total Cost, Not Headline Price

Headline price hides the full cost: usage overages, support tiers, add-ons, integration, and the cost of limitations that require workarounds. Compare total cost across vendors, including the costs of what each vendor cannot do. The cheapest headline price is often not the cheapest total cost.

### Check Vendor Stability And Trajectory

A vendor that fails or stagnates strands the ventures that depend on it. Assess the vendor's stability: funding, customer base, growth, product velocity, and market position. A stable, improving vendor is a safer long-term partner than a fragile one, even at higher price, because switching under duress is costly.

### Validate With References And Trials

Do not rely on vendor claims alone. Speak with references, especially customers similar to the venture in size and use case, and run a trial or proof of concept where possible. Real users reveal reliability, support quality, and limitations that sales conversations do not.

### Negotiate Terms For The Venture's Stage

Vendors often have flexibility on terms, especially for startups, through startup programs, discounts, or customized agreements. Negotiate pricing, usage limits, and exit terms to fit the venture's stage and growth, rather than accepting standard terms designed for larger customers.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Vendor decisions involving data, security, or critical operations may carry legal, privacy, and compliance obligations that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Choosing On Price Alone

Headline price hides overages, limitations, integration, and switching costs.

### Ignoring Reliability Under Stress

Averages hide the outages and edge cases that actually affect the venture.

### Unread Terms Creating Lock-In

Terms allowing unilateral changes or data lock-in create long-term risk.

### Poor Integration Fit

A vendor that does not fit the stack imposes ongoing custom work.

### High Switching Costs Discovered Late

Lock-in weakens the venture's position and makes exit painful.

### Relying On Vendor Claims Without Validation

Sales conversations hide limitations that references and trials reveal.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The venture's actual needs are defined before evaluating vendors.
- [ ] Reliability is evaluated under real conditions, including stress and incidents.
- [ ] Terms of service, pricing changes, data ownership, and exit conditions are read before committing.
- [ ] Integration burden and fit with the current and planned stack are assessed.
- [ ] Switching costs are estimated before the vendor is chosen.
- [ ] Total cost, including overages and limitations, is compared across vendors.
- [ ] Vendor stability and trajectory are checked.
- [ ] References and trials validate vendor claims.
- [ ] Terms are negotiated to fit the venture's stage.
- [ ] Data, security, and compliance implications are flagged for professional advice.
