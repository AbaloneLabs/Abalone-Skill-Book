# Build Versus Buy For Startups

Almost every startup stands on components and services it did not build itself: payment processing, authentication, hosting, analytics, communication. The build-versus-buy decision is which capabilities to construct in-house and which to acquire from vendors or platforms. Getting this wrong is costly in both directions: building what should be bought wastes scarce engineering time and runway, while buying what should be built surrenders the differentiation that makes the venture defensible. The right choice depends on what is core to the venture's value, the stage and runway, and the total cost and risk of each option.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should I build or buy this", "is it worth building in-house", "should we use a vendor or build it ourselves", or "how should an entrepreneur decide build versus buy". The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to building out of pride or control and from buying indiscriminately without considering integration and switching costs.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Core From Context

The central question is whether the capability is core to the venture's differentiated value or context that supports it. Core capabilities, the things that make the venture uniquely valuable, are candidates to build, because owning them is the basis of competition. Context capabilities, things every venture needs but that do not differentiate, are candidates to buy, because building them diverts effort from the core.

### Weight The Decision Heavily Toward Speed At Early Stages

Early-stage ventures have limited engineering capacity and limited runway. The default should lean toward buying, because buying is almost always faster than building, and speed to evidence matters more than ownership at this stage. Reserve building for the narrow core that defines the venture, and buy the rest until scale or strategy justifies building.

### Count The Full Cost Of Building

Building is not just the initial development cost. It includes ongoing maintenance, bug fixing, security, scaling, documentation, and the opportunity cost of the engineering time not spent on the core. A build decision that looks cheap on initial estimate often costs far more over its life. Count the full lifecycle cost, not only the build.

### Count The Full Cost Of Buying

Buying is not just the vendor's price. It includes integration effort, switching costs, dependency risk, vendor lock-in, limitations on customization, and the strategic risk of depending on a third party for something important. A buy decision that looks cheap on price can become expensive if the vendor changes terms, degrades, or fails.

### Consider The Stage And Evidence

Build-versus-buy decisions should match the venture's stage. Before product-market fit, buy aggressively to reach evidence fast; the venture cannot afford to build infrastructure for a product whose shape is still uncertain. After fit, revisit decisions: capabilities that became important to retention or differentiation may now justify building, while premature builds before fit often become waste.

### Assess Vendor Risk And Dependency

When buying, assess what happens if the vendor fails, changes pricing, degrades quality, or changes direction. For capabilities critical to the venture, vendor risk is a serious consideration: a single vendor failure can break the product. Prefer vendors with portability, reasonable switching costs, and stability, and avoid single points of failure for critical functions.

### Avoid Building Commodities

Capabilities that are commodities, available from many vendors at low cost and high quality, are almost always better bought. Authentication, payments, email, and standard analytics are examples. Building commodities diverts scarce resources from the core and rarely produces a better result than mature vendors.

### Avoid Buying The Differentiator

Capabilities that constitute the venture's differentiation should not be bought from a vendor available to all competitors, because doing so erases the differentiation. If the core value depends on a capability, build or own it, even at higher cost, because it is the source of competitive advantage.

### Revisit Decisions As The Venture Scales

A decision that was right at one stage may become wrong at another. A capability bought early may become worth building at scale, when volume makes the vendor cost high or when the capability becomes central. Conversely, a capability built early may become a commodity better replaced by a vendor. Revisit build-versus-buy decisions as scale and strategy evolve.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Build-versus-buy decisions involving data, security, or critical infrastructure may carry legal and compliance obligations that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Building Out Of Pride Or Control

Constructing commodities in-house diverts effort from the core.

### Buying The Differentiator

Sourcing the core value from a vendor available to all erases advantage.

### Counting Only Initial Build Cost

Lifecycle maintenance and opportunity cost make builds far more expensive than the estimate.

### Ignoring Switching And Dependency Costs

Vendor lock-in and integration burden make buys more expensive than the price.

### Premature Building Before Fit

Building infrastructure for a product whose shape is still uncertain produces waste.

### Never Revisiting The Decision

A choice right at one stage becomes wrong at another if not revisited.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The capability is classified as core or context before deciding.
- [ ] At early stages, the default leans toward buying for speed.
- [ ] The full lifecycle cost of building, including maintenance and opportunity cost, is counted.
- [ ] The full cost of buying, including integration, switching, and dependency risk, is counted.
- [ ] The decision matches the venture's stage and evidence level.
- [ ] Vendor risk for critical capabilities is assessed.
- [ ] Commodities are bought, not built.
- [ ] The differentiator is built or owned, not bought from a shared vendor.
- [ ] Decisions are revisited as scale and strategy evolve.
- [ ] Data, security, and compliance implications are flagged for professional advice.
