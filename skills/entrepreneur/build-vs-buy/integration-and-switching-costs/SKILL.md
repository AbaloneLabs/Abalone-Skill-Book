# Integration And Switching Costs

The cost of a vendor or system is not only what is paid to use it; it is also what it takes to adopt it and what it would take to leave it. Integration costs are paid upfront, in the effort to connect the new system to the venture's stack and workflows. Switching costs are paid later, if the venture must replace the system, in data migration, reintegration, retraining, and transitional risk. Underestimating either leads to decisions that look cheap and become expensive. A honest accounting of integration and switching costs is essential to sound build-versus-buy and vendor decisions.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how hard is it to switch vendors", "what are the integration costs", "how do I estimate migration effort", or "how should an entrepreneur account for switching costs". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating adoption as plug-and-play and from ignoring the lock-in that accumulates once a system is embedded.

## Core Rules

### Count Integration Cost Before Adopting

Integration cost is the effort to make the new system work in the venture's context: connecting APIs, migrating initial data, training the team, building workflows, and working around limitations. Estimate this cost before adopting, because a system that is expensive to integrate may not be worth its headline price. Integration cost is paid in scarce engineering and operational time.

### Count Switching Cost Before Committing

Switching cost is what it would take to replace the system later: extracting data, reintegrating replacements, retraining users, and managing the transitional period where both systems may run. Estimate switching cost before committing to a vendor, because high switching cost creates lock-in that weakens the venture's position over time. A system cheap to adopt but expensive to leave is a trap.

### Identify The Sources Of Lock-In

Lock-in accumulates from several sources: proprietary data formats that are hard to export, deep integrations that must be rebuilt, user familiarity that requires retraining, custom workflows built around the system, and contractual terms that penalize exit. Identify which sources apply to each option, because they determine how locked-in the venture becomes.

### Assess Data Portability Before Adopting

Data lock-in is among the most binding forms of lock-in. Before adopting a system, assess whether data can be exported in usable formats, at scale, without excessive effort or vendor cooperation. A system that holds the venture's data hostage makes switching costly and gives the vendor leverage over time. Prefer systems with strong data portability.

### Estimate The Transitional Risk Of Switching

Switching is not only effort; it is risk. During a migration, data can be lost or corrupted, service can be interrupted, and users can be confused or lost. Estimate the transitional risk, especially for critical systems, because it is a real cost of switching that compounds the effort. Some switches are risky enough to effectively prevent change.

### Re-Evaluate Switching Cost As The Relationship Deepens

Switching cost is not static; it grows as the venture integrates the system more deeply, accumulates data in it, and builds workflows around it. A system that was cheap to leave at adoption may become prohibitively expensive to leave after a year of use. Re-evaluate switching cost periodically to understand the lock-in the venture is accumulating.

### Prefer Lower-Lock-In Options For Critical Capabilities

For capabilities critical to the venture, prefer options with lower switching costs and stronger portability, even at higher price, because the cost of being trapped in the wrong system is high. For less critical capabilities, higher lock-in may be acceptable in exchange for price or convenience. Match the lock-in tolerance to the criticality.

### Plan The Exit Before The Entry

Before adopting a system, sketch what an exit would look like: what data would need to move, what would need reintegrating, how long it would take, and what it would cost. Planning the exit clarifies the real commitment being made and prevents the venture from drifting into lock-in without understanding it.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Integration and switching decisions involving data, contracts, or critical operations may carry legal, privacy, and compliance obligations that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Treating Adoption As Plug-And-Play

Ignoring integration effort makes a system look cheaper than it is.

### Ignoring Switching Cost Until It Is Too Late

Lock-in accumulates silently and is discovered only when exit is attempted.

### Data Lock-In Discovered Late

Proprietary formats and poor export make data effectively hostage.

### Underestimating Transitional Risk

Migrations carry risk of data loss, interruption, and user confusion.

### Static View Of Lock-In

Treating switching cost as fixed when it grows with deeper integration.

### High Lock-In For Critical Capabilities

Being trapped in the wrong system for a function central to the venture.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Integration cost is estimated before the system is adopted.
- [ ] Switching cost is estimated before the vendor is committed to.
- [ ] The sources of lock-in, data, integrations, workflows, contracts, are identified.
- [ ] Data portability and export quality are assessed before adoption.
- [ ] Transitional risk of switching, including data loss and interruption, is estimated.
- [ ] Switching cost is re-evaluated as the relationship deepens.
- [ ] Lower-lock-in options are preferred for critical capabilities.
- [ ] An exit plan is sketched before entry to clarify the real commitment.
- [ ] Data, contract, and compliance implications are flagged for professional advice.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids treating adoption as plug-and-play or ignoring accumulating lock-in.
