# Problem Interviews

A problem interview is the foundational discovery activity that determines whether a real, painful, frequent problem exists before any solution is built, and it is the interview most founders do badly. The temptation to pitch the solution is overwhelming, because founders are excited about what they have built or plan to build, and pitching feels productive while listening feels passive. But a problem interview in which the founder describes the solution has stopped being discovery and become sales, and whatever the customer says afterward is contaminated by the pitch. The judgment problem is conducting interviews that reveal the truth about the problem, its severity, its frequency, and the customer's current behavior, without leading the customer toward the answer the founder hopes to hear. Agents tend to fail by pitching solutions during discovery, by confusing polite interest with problem validation, and by over-weighting a few enthusiastic responses.

Use this skill when planning, conducting, or synthesizing problem discovery interviews. The goal is honest evidence about whether a problem worth solving exists, gathered before resources are committed to a solution.

## Core Rules

### Define The Problem Hypothesis Before Interviewing

An interview without a defined problem hypothesis wanders and produces stories but no usable evidence. State specifically what problem you believe exists, for whom, and how severe, so the interview can confirm or refute it.

Define the hypothesis by:

- naming the specific problem in concrete terms, not abstract categories;
- identifying the customer segment that experiences it;
- estimating how frequent and how severe the problem is;
- stating what evidence would confirm or refute the hypothesis.

A clear hypothesis turns the interview into a test rather than a chat. Without it, any positive-sounding response feels like validation.

### Never Pitch The Solution During Discovery

The single most important rule of problem interviews is to keep the solution out of the room. The moment a founder describes what they plan to build, the customer's responses shift from honest description of their reality to reaction to the pitch, and the data is corrupted.

Avoid pitching by:

- describing only the problem area, never your solution;
- resisting the urge to ask "would you use a product that...";
- not showing mockups, demos, or feature lists during problem discovery;
- saving solution reaction for a separate, later stage of validation.

Customers cannot reliably predict whether they would use a hypothetical product, but they can describe their current problems and behavior in detail. Stay in the territory where their answers are trustworthy.

### Focus On Past Behavior, Not Future Intent

People are reliable narrators of what they have done and unreliable predictors of what they will do. A problem interview grounded in past behavior produces strong evidence; one grounded in hypotheticals produces weak, optimistic noise.

Anchor in the past by asking:

- "Tell me about the last time you experienced this problem."
- "Walk me through what you did when it happened."
- "How often does this happen, and what triggers it?"
- "What did you do right after, and what did you do to solve or work around it?"

Avoid "would you" and "do you think you might" questions, which invite speculation. Concrete, recent, past-tense questions reveal the problem's reality.

### Assess Severity, Frequency, And Current Behavior

A problem being real is not enough; it must be worth solving, which depends on how severe it is, how often it occurs, and what the customer already does about it. These three dimensions determine whether a business can be built.

Assess each dimension:

- severity: how painful or costly is the problem when it occurs;
- frequency: how often does it happen, daily, weekly, rarely;
- current behavior: what does the customer do today, including doing nothing or using workarounds;
- willingness to pay or act: have they spent money, time, or effort trying to solve it already.

A problem that is mild, rare, and already adequately worked around is not a strong foundation, no matter how many people mention it. A problem that is severe, frequent, and poorly served is.

### Segment By Problem Intensity, Not Demographics

The customers most worth understanding are those who feel the problem most intensely, because they are the earliest adopters and the clearest signal. Segmenting by demographic averages dilutes the signal with people who barely experience the problem.

Segment by intensity:

- identify the customers for whom the problem is most severe and most frequent;
- understand what makes them different from those who experience it mildly;
- prioritize learning from the intense segment, who will be the first customers;
- avoid averaging across segments with very different problem intensity.

The beachhead market is defined by problem intensity, not by age, industry, or job title. Find the people who hurt most.

### Treat Polite Interest As Weak Evidence

Customers are socialized to be encouraging, and they will express interest in ideas and sympathy for problems even when they have no intention of acting. Polite interest is among the weakest forms of evidence.

Distinguish strong from weak evidence:

- weak: "that sounds interesting," "I might use that," "that's a real problem";
- strong: vivid description of a recent painful experience, current workarounds, money or time already spent;
- strongest: the customer is actively trying to solve the problem today, however imperfectly.

Weight evidence by behavior and specificity, not by enthusiasm. A customer who describes a painful workaround they perform weekly is giving strong signal; one who says "sure, I'd try that" is giving almost none.

### Listen For The Problem Behind The Problem

Customers often describe symptoms or surface-level frustrations rather than the underlying problem. A skilled interviewer listens for the deeper need beneath the stated complaint.

Listen deeper by:

- probing why the surface problem matters and what it prevents;
- asking about the consequence and cost of the problem;
- noticing emotional language, which often marks the real pain;
- following up on workarounds, which reveal what the customer actually needs.

The stated problem and the real problem often differ. The real problem is usually the one worth building around.

### Decide How Many Interviews Are Enough

Problem discovery does not require statistical sampling, but it does require enough interviews to reach saturation, the point where new interviews stop revealing new patterns. Stopping too early risks acting on a fluke; continuing too long wastes time.

Judge sufficiency by:

- conducting enough interviews per segment to hear recurring patterns, often around five to ten;
- continuing until you stop hearing new things;
- weighting surprising or disconfirming voices, which may indicate a segment you missed;
- accepting that qualitative discovery seeks patterns, not precision.

Saturation, not sample size, is the goal. Define what saturation looks like for this problem before you start.

## Common Traps

### Pitching The Solution During Discovery

The moment you describe the solution, the interview becomes sales and the data is corrupted. Keep the solution out of the room.

### Hypothetical And Future-Tense Questions

People cannot reliably predict their behavior. Anchor in concrete past experience.

### Confusing Polite Interest With Validation

Enthusiasm and sympathy are weak evidence. Weight behavior, specificity, and money or time already spent.

### Ignoring Severity, Frequency, And Current Behavior

A real but mild, rare, or already-solved problem is not a strong foundation. Assess all three dimensions.

### Segmenting By Demographics Instead Of Intensity

Averaging across problem intensity dilutes the signal. Find and prioritize those who hurt most.

### Stopping At The Stated Problem

The surface complaint often hides a deeper need. Probe for consequence and cost.

### Too Few Or Too Many Interviews

Stop too early and you act on a fluke; continue too long and you waste time. Aim for saturation.

### Treating Discovery As Sales

A discovery interview that seeks validation rather than truth produces confirmation, not evidence.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A specific problem hypothesis, including segment, severity, and frequency, was defined before interviewing.
- [ ] The solution was never pitched during discovery interviews.
- [ ] Questions focused on concrete past behavior, not hypothetical future intent.
- [ ] Severity, frequency, and current behavior, including workarounds, were assessed for each respondent.
- [ ] Respondents were segmented by problem intensity, and the most intense segment was prioritized.
- [ ] Polite interest was distinguished from strong behavioral evidence.
- [ ] The interviewer probed for the deeper problem behind the stated complaint.
- [ ] Enough interviews were conducted per segment to reach saturation, not an arbitrary number.
- [ ] Disconfirming and surprising voices were weighted, not only confirmatory ones.
- [ ] Conclusions distinguish strong evidence, vivid behavior and existing spend, from weak evidence, expressed interest.
