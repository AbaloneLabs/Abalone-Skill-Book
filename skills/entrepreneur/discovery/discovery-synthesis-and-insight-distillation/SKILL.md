# Discovery Synthesis And Insight Distillation

Running interviews is the visible part of customer discovery. The part that determines whether discovery produces anything useful is synthesis: the disciplined act of turning a pile of notes, recordings, and impressions into a defensible understanding of the problem, the customer, and the market. This is where most discovery efforts fail, not because the interviews were bad, but because the team heard what it hoped to hear, weighted the most vivid or most recent conversations, and declared validation on the strength of three enthusiastic quotes while ignoring seventeen ambivalent ones. Synthesis is a judgment problem, and it is biased by default.

Use this skill after discovery interviews have been conducted, when the team must decide what it learned, what to do next, and whether the evidence justifies building. The goal is to prevent the agent from converting a messy body of qualitative evidence into a confident but unsupported conclusion, and to keep it from paralyzing the team with unprocessed data that never becomes a decision.

## Core Rules

### Synthesize From The Full Body Of Evidence, Not The Memorable Subset

Human memory weights the vivid, the recent, and the confirming. Three passionate interviews will feel like overwhelming evidence; twenty quiet ones will feel like nothing. But the three passionate voices may be outliers, and the decision must rest on the whole body.

Build synthesis on the full set by:

- reviewing every interview, not just the ones that stood out;
- coding or tagging each interview against the same dimensions, such as problem severity, frequency, current behavior, and willingness to act;
- counting how many respondents exhibited each pattern, not just whether any did;
- separating the intensity of a single response from the prevalence of a pattern across respondents.

A pattern held by two of thirty respondents is a segment to investigate, not a validated problem. State findings in terms of how many and how strongly, not just that something was said.

### Separate What Customers Said From What You Conclude

Raw evidence and interpretation are different things, and conflating them is the most common synthesis error. "Eight of twelve respondents described a painful manual workaround performed weekly" is evidence. "There is a strong market for an automated solution" is a conclusion that requires additional inference about willingness to pay, switching behavior, and alternatives.

Keep the two layers distinct:

- evidence layer: what respondents actually said and did, in their words where possible;
- interpretation layer: what the team infers from that evidence, with the inference made explicit;
- assumption layer: what must additionally be true for the interpretation to hold.

When a conclusion is challenged, you should be able to trace it back through the interpretation to the specific evidence. A conclusion that cannot be traced is a belief, not a finding.

### Code For Disconfirming And Surprising Evidence

Confirmation bias drives teams to collect and remember the interviews that support the hypothesis and explain away the ones that do not. A synthesis that only assembles supporting quotes is advocacy, not discovery.

Actively look for:

- interviews that contradicted the hypothesis, and what they revealed;
- respondents who did not experience the problem, and what made them different;
- signals that pointed to a different problem or a different customer than expected;
- moments where the team's framing was corrected by the customer.

Disconfirming evidence is often the most valuable output of discovery, because it prevents building the wrong thing. A synthesis that contains no surprises probably did not listen hard enough.

### Reach For Saturation, Not Consensus

Discovery synthesis does not require unanimity. It requires saturation: the point where new interviews stop revealing new patterns. A few dissenting voices inside a saturated pattern are informative, not disqualifying.

Judge saturation by:

- whether the last several interviews introduced new themes or repeated known ones;
- whether distinct segments have each been explored to their own saturation;
- whether surprising voices were followed up to determine if they represent a missed segment.

If the team is still hearing genuinely new things, synthesis is premature. If patterns have stabilized, synthesis is due even if not every voice agrees.

### Distinguish Problem Validation From Solution Demand

A frequent synthesis error is concluding that because a problem is real, demand for a specific solution exists. These are different claims with different evidence requirements. Problem validation rests on descriptions of pain, frequency, and current behavior. Solution demand rests on willingness to change, willingness to pay, and fit with the customer's actual workflow.

Be explicit about which claim the evidence supports:

- the problem is real and painful: supported by vivid behavioral evidence;
- the problem is frequent and worth solving: supported by frequency and current spend or effort;
- customers would adopt a new solution: supported by dissatisfaction with current workarounds and switching behavior, not by hypothetical interest;
- customers would pay: supported by money or time already spent on the problem, not by stated willingness.

Each step up requires stronger evidence, and each is a separate synthesis conclusion.

### Segment Findings Rather Than Averaging Across Customers

Averaging across customers with very different problem intensity produces a misleading middle. The intense segment that represents the beachhead may be diluted by a larger mild segment that will never buy. Synthesis should preserve segment-level findings.

Segment by:

- problem intensity: who hurts most versus least;
- current behavior: who has a workaround versus who suffers silently;
- willingness to act: who has already spent money or time;
- context: what jobs, industries, or situations correlate with intensity.

Report findings per segment, and identify which segment the evidence most strongly supports targeting first.

### Convert Synthesis Into Explicit Next Decisions

Discovery that produces a summary but no decision is incomplete. The purpose of synthesis is to inform a choice: build, iterate the problem, change the segment, or abandon. State the decision the evidence points to, the confidence level, and what additional evidence would raise or lower that confidence.

For each decision, specify:

- what the team now believes and why;
- what it will do next as a result;
- what assumption remains riskiest and how it will be tested;
- what would cause the team to reverse course.

A synthesis that does not change a decision was either unnecessary or not understood.

### Document The Evidence Base For Future Reference

Discovery conclusions are used long after the interviews fade from memory, by co-founders, investors, and future team members. A synthesis that exists only in the founder's head cannot be scrutinized, updated, or trusted.

Document:

- how many interviews were conducted, with whom, and in what segment;
- the key patterns and their prevalence;
- the disconfirming evidence and how it was weighed;
- the conclusions and the decisions they drove;
- the open questions and the riskiest remaining assumptions.

This record lets later evidence update rather than overwrite the team's understanding.

## Common Traps

### Cherry-Picking The Vivid Quotes

Three passionate interviews feel like proof, but prevalence matters more than intensity. A vivid quote from one respondent is a story, not a finding.

### Conflating Evidence With Conclusion

Stating "customers want this" when the evidence only shows "customers experience this problem" skips the inference steps about demand, willingness to pay, and switching.

### Explaining Away Disconfirming Evidence

Labeling a contradicting respondent as "not our customer" after the fact is a way to preserve a favored hypothesis. Disconfirming evidence should be investigated, not dismissed.

### Declaring Validation Prematurely

Stopping synthesis the moment enough supporting evidence appears, before reviewing the full body, produces a confident but fragile conclusion.

### Averaging Across Segments

Mixing intense and mild respondents into a single average hides the beachhead segment and overstates the broader market's readiness.

### Treating Saturation As Unanimity

A few dissenting voices inside a saturated pattern are expected and informative. Waiting for universal agreement means never deciding.

### Synthesis Without A Decision

A polished summary that does not change what the team does next has consumed effort without reducing uncertainty.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The synthesis was built from the full body of interviews, not just the memorable or confirming subset.
- [ ] Evidence, interpretation, and assumptions are kept in separate layers, and each conclusion traces back to specific evidence.
- [ ] Disconfirming and surprising evidence was actively sought, coded, and investigated rather than explained away.
- [ ] Findings are reported with prevalence, how many respondents exhibited each pattern, not just that a pattern appeared.
- [ ] Saturation was assessed per segment, and synthesis was not done while new themes were still emerging.
- [ ] Problem validation, solution demand, willingness to adopt, and willingness to pay are distinguished as separate claims with separate evidence thresholds.
- [ ] Findings are segmented by problem intensity and behavior rather than averaged into a misleading middle.
- [ ] The synthesis produced an explicit next decision, a confidence level, and the riskiest remaining assumption.
- [ ] The evidence base, including counts, segments, and disconfirming voices, is documented for future scrutiny and updating.
- [ ] No conclusion rests on fewer interviews or weaker evidence than the team is treating it as resting on.
