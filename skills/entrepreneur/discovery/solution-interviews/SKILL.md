# Solution Interviews

A solution interview is the validation step that follows problem discovery, and its purpose is to test whether a specific proposed solution actually fits the problem you have validated, before committing the resources to build it. This is a distinct activity from problem discovery, with different risks and different failure modes. The danger in solution interviews is the opposite of problem interviews: where problem discovery is corrupted by pitching too early, solution validation is corrupted by pitching too hard, by seeking approval rather than truth, and by interpreting any positive reaction as a mandate to build. The judgment problem is presenting a solution concept in a way that elicits honest reaction rather than performative enthusiasm, testing specific elements of the solution against the problem, and deciding whether the evidence justifies the cost of building. Agents tend to fail by treating enthusiasm as validation, by presenting solutions so abstractly that any reaction is meaningless, and by over-building based on weak solution evidence.

Use this skill when conducting solution validation interviews, presenting concepts or prototypes to potential customers, or deciding whether to proceed to build. The goal is honest evidence about solution fit, gathered before significant build investment.

## Core Rules

### Validate The Problem Before Validating The Solution

A solution interview presupposes that the problem has already been validated through discovery. Conducting solution interviews before the problem is understood tests a solution against an unknown problem, which produces meaningless results.

Sequence correctly:

- confirm through problem discovery that a real, severe, frequent problem exists;
- understand current behavior and workarounds in detail;
- only then test whether a specific solution fits that validated problem.

If the problem is not yet validated, return to discovery. Solution interviews cannot rescue an unvalidated problem.

### Present A Concrete Solution, Not An Abstract Concept

Customers cannot react meaningfully to a vague idea. "A tool that helps you manage tasks" invites generic approval; a specific concept or prototype invites useful reaction. The solution must be concrete enough to evaluate.

Make the solution concrete by:

- describing specific functionality and how it would be used;
- using a prototype, mockup, or demo where the interaction matters;
- grounding the concept in the customer's validated problem and context;
- avoiding feature lists, which invite check-the-box reactions.

The right level of concreteness depends on what you are testing, but the customer must be able to imagine using the solution, not just nod at a category.

### Test Specific Solution Elements Against The Problem

A solution interview is most useful when it tests specific hypotheses about how the solution addresses the problem, rather than gathering general approval. Decide in advance which elements you are testing.

Test elements such as:

- does the core mechanism address the customer's actual workflow and pain;
- does the solution fit the context in which the problem occurs;
- are the proposed benefits the ones the customer actually values;
- what is missing, and what is unnecessary, from the customer's view.

General approval ("that sounds good") is weak evidence. Specific reaction to how the solution does or does not fit the problem is strong evidence.

### Avoid Leading Toward Approval

The structure of the interview can manufacture approval without intending to. Leading questions, enthusiastic presentation, and social pressure all push the customer toward positive responses regardless of their true reaction.

Avoid leading by:

- presenting the solution neutrally, without selling or defending;
- asking what concerns or objections the customer has, not just what they like;
- inviting criticism explicitly, since customers default to politeness;
- watching for hesitation, qualified praise, and non-verbal signals, not just words.

A customer who says "I guess that could work" is giving a different signal than one who says "I need this now." Learn to hear the difference.

### Distinguish Demand From Approval

The most important distinction in solution interviews is between approval, "I like it," and demand, "I would use and pay for this." Approval is cheap and common; demand is rare and is what justifies building.

Look for demand signals:

- willingness to pay or pre-order, the strongest signal;
- requests to use it now, or frustration that it does not exist yet;
- attempts to negotiate access or timeline, which indicate real desire;
- concrete description of how they would integrate it into their current behavior.

Approve of a concept is not demand. A customer who likes the idea but will not commit time, money, or behavior change is signaling weak demand, however positive their words.

### Watch For The Features-Over-Fit Trap

When presented with a solution concept, customers often respond by requesting more features, which founders eagerly note as engagement. But feature requests frequently indicate that the core solution does not fit, and the customer is trying to make it fit by adding things.

Interpret feature requests carefully:

- requests that extend the core value may indicate genuine demand;
- requests that work around the core concept may indicate the fit is wrong;
- a flood of divergent requests often signals the solution is not resonating as-is;
- ask why each feature matters to distinguish real needs from nice-to-haves.

Do not treat a feature request list as a roadmap. Understand whether requests reflect demand or dissatisfaction with the core.

### Preserve Honesty About What You Will Build

Presenting a solution concept creates an implicit promise about what the product will be. Over-promising to elicit positive reaction produces demand for a product you cannot deliver, and the resulting disappointment destroys trust.

Preserve honesty by:

- representing the solution accurately, including its current limits;
- not implying capabilities or timelines you cannot meet;
- being clear about what is concept versus what is built;
- treating pre-orders or commitments as signals to honor, not as sales to capture.

Demand validated under false pretenses does not survive contact with the real product. Honest presentation produces demand that transfers to delivery.

### Decide Whether The Evidence Justifies Building

The purpose of solution interviews is to inform the build decision, and the evidence must be weighed against the cost and risk of building. Not all positive evidence justifies full investment.

Weigh evidence against cost by:

- distinguishing strong demand signals, commitment of money or behavior, from weak approval;
- considering what a minimal build would require to test further;
- recognizing that interviews, however positive, are not the same as usage;
- planning the smallest next step that reduces risk, rather than the full build.

Solution interviews reduce the risk of building the wrong thing, but they do not eliminate it. Use the evidence to choose the next, smallest test, not necessarily to commit to the whole product.

## Common Traps

### Solution Interviews Before Problem Validation

Testing a solution against an unknown problem produces meaningless results. Validate the problem first.

### Abstract Concepts Inviting Generic Approval

Vague ideas cannot be meaningfully evaluated. Make the solution concrete.

### Seeking Approval Instead Of Fit

General approval is weak evidence. Test specific elements against the problem.

### Leading Questions Manufacturing Positive Reaction

Enthusiastic presentation and leading structure produce approval regardless of truth. Present neutrally and invite criticism.

### Confusing Approval With Demand

Liking a concept is not the same as using and paying for it. Look for commitment signals.

### Treating Feature Requests As A Roadmap

Feature requests often indicate poor core fit, not engagement. Ask why each matters.

### Over-Promising To Elicit Reaction

Demand validated under false pretenses does not survive delivery. Represent the solution honestly.

### Jumping To Full Build On Weak Evidence

Interviews reduce risk but do not eliminate it. Choose the smallest next test.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The problem was validated through discovery before solution interviews were conducted.
- [ ] The solution was presented concretely enough for the customer to imagine using it.
- [ ] Specific solution elements were tested against the validated problem, not just general approval sought.
- [ ] The solution was presented neutrally, with explicit invitation of criticism and concerns.
- [ ] Demand signals, willingness to pay, pre-order, behavior change, were distinguished from mere approval.
- [ ] Feature requests were interpreted for whether they reflect demand or poor core fit.
- [ ] The solution was represented honestly, without implying capabilities or timelines that cannot be met.
- [ ] Strong and weak evidence were distinguished in synthesis.
- [ ] The evidence was weighed against build cost, and the smallest risk-reducing next step was chosen.
- [ ] The decision to build rests on demand evidence, not on enthusiasm or approval alone.
