# Launch Failure Diagnosis And Recovery

Most launches underperform. A launch that produces no spike, or a spike that produces no retention, is the common outcome, not the exception. The danger is not the failure itself; it is the misdiagnosis. A team that attributes launch failure to the wrong cause, blaming the product when the distribution was broken, or blaming the market when the messaging was unclear, pursues the wrong recovery and often abandons a venture that could have been saved, or doubles down on one that cannot. Launch failure diagnosis is a decomposition problem: separating the distinct reasons a launch can fail, identifying which one applies, and choosing a recovery that addresses the actual cause rather than a convenient one. The convenient diagnosis is almost always "the product isn't good enough" or "the market isn't ready," because both externalize the failure and spare the team from examining its execution.

Use this skill when a launch has underperformed, when diagnosing the cause, when deciding whether to relaunch, iterate, or pivot, or when recovering momentum after a failed launch. The goal is to prevent the agent from jumping to a single-cause narrative, from abandoning recoverable ventures, and from repeating the same launch mistakes under a new banner.

## Core Rules

### Decompose Failure Into Distribution, Product, Timing, And Execution

A launch can fail for several distinct reasons, and each demands a different response. Blurring them produces a confused recovery. Decompose the failure into its components before concluding anything.

Decompose by asking:

- distribution failure: did the right users hear about the product at all;
- product failure: did users who arrived experience the core value;
- positioning failure: did users who arrived understand what the product was for;
- timing failure: did the market or readiness conditions support the launch;
- execution failure: was the launch itself run competently, with working signup, onboarding, and support.

Each component can be investigated from the launch data. Distribution failure shows as low traffic from the target segment. Product failure shows as traffic that does not activate or retain. Positioning failure shows as traffic from the wrong segment or confusion in first-session behavior. A single-cause diagnosis that does not examine all components is incomplete.

### Distinguish "No One Came" From "People Came But Did Not Stay"

These are fundamentally different failures. "No one came" is a distribution or awareness problem: the launch did not reach enough of the right users. "People came but did not stay" is a product or activation problem: users arrived but did not experience value. The recovery for each is opposite, and confusing them is catastrophic.

Distinguish by:

- if traffic was low or from the wrong segment, the problem is distribution or positioning;
- if traffic was adequate and on-target but activation and retention were low, the problem is product or onboarding;
- if activation was adequate but retention collapsed, the problem is durable value, not first impression.

A team that iterates the product when the problem was distribution burns engineering effort on the wrong fix. A team that scales distribution when the product does not retain pours fuel on a leak.

### Audit Distribution Before Condemning The Product

The most common misdiagnosis is concluding the product failed when the launch never reached its intended audience. A launch promoted to the founder's personal network, to a generic audience, or through a single saturated channel may simply not have reached the target segment. Before judging the product, audit whether distribution actually delivered the right users.

Audit distribution by:

- reviewing which channels drove traffic and what segments they reached;
- checking whether the target ICP was represented in the launch audience;
- assessing whether the messaging reached the segment through channels they attend;
- comparing traffic volume to what the segment size would predict.

If the right users never arrived, the product has not been tested. Condemning it on this basis is premature.

### Audit Activation And Retention Before Condemning The Market

When users arrived but did not stay, the question is whether they experienced the core value. If activation was low, the product may be fine but the onboarding or first-run experience failed. If activation was adequate but retention collapsed, the durable value may be insufficient. Each points to a different fix.

Audit by:

- measuring what fraction of arriving users reached activation;
- identifying where in the first-session funnel users dropped off;
- interviewing users who arrived but did not return about what blocked them;
- separating onboarding friction from value deficiencies.

A product with strong value but broken onboarding fails the launch but is easily fixed. A product with adequate onboarding but weak value requires deeper iteration.

### Avoid The Externalizing Diagnoses

Two diagnoses are tempting because they spare the team: "the market isn't ready" and "the product just isn't good enough." Both may be true, but both are overused as ways to avoid examining execution. Before accepting either, rule out the controllable causes.

Rule out controllable causes by:

- verifying distribution reached the right segment in adequate volume;
- verifying the messaging was clear and the positioning was accurate;
- verifying the launch execution, signup, onboarding, and support worked;
- verifying the launch timing did not collide with a competing event or a seasonal lull.

Only after controllable causes are examined should "market readiness" or "product quality" be accepted as the diagnosis, and even then with specified evidence.

### Decide Between Relaunch, Iterate, And Pivot Based On The Diagnosis

The recovery follows the diagnosis, not the other way around. Different diagnoses demand different responses, and the team must commit to the response that matches the cause rather than the one that feels safest.

Match response to diagnosis:

- distribution failure with a working product: fix distribution and relaunch to the right segment;
- product or onboarding failure: iterate the activation path and re-test before relaunching;
- positioning failure: revise messaging and positioning, then relaunch;
- timing failure: wait for the window or adjust the offering to the current conditions;
- fundamental value failure: consider a pivot, but only after distribution and execution are ruled out.

Relaunching without fixing the diagnosed cause repeats the failure. Pivoting when the cause was distribution abandons a viable venture.

### Preserve The Learning And The Audience From A Failed Launch

A failed launch still produces assets: a list of users who showed interest, data about what messaging resonated, and learning about where the product broke. These assets are often discarded in the discouragement of failure, but they are the foundation of recovery.

Preserve by:

- retaining and re-engaging the users who signed up, even if they did not stay;
- documenting what was learned about distribution, activation, and messaging;
- keeping the launch assets, content, and relationships for a future relaunch;
- communicating honestly with the audience that showed interest about what comes next.

A failed launch that is fully discarded wastes the investment. A failed launch whose learning and audience are preserved becomes the input to the next attempt.

### Time The Recovery, Do Not Rush Or Stall

After a failed launch, two failure modes emerge: rushing a relaunch without fixing the cause, and stalling indefinitely out of discouragement. Both waste the window in which the launch audience and momentum remain recoverable. Set a deliberate recovery timeline.

Set the timeline by:

- defining the fix required by the diagnosis and how long it takes;
- planning the relaunch or iteration within a period that preserves audience interest;
- avoiding relaunching before the diagnosed cause is addressed;
- avoiding indefinite delay that lets the audience and momentum dissipate.

A recovery that is too fast repeats the failure. A recovery that never happens converts a recoverable launch into a permanent one.

## Common Traps

### Single-Cause Narrative

Attributing failure to one convenient cause, usually the product or the market, without decomposing distribution, product, positioning, timing, and execution.

### Condemning The Product When Distribution Failed

If the right users never arrived, the product has not been tested. Audit distribution before judging the product.

### Scaling Distribution When The Product Does Not Retain

Pouring more traffic into a product that does not activate or retain wastes acquisition spend and produces a bigger failure.

### Accepting "Market Readiness" Without Ruling Out Execution

"Market isn't ready" externalizes the failure. Rule out controllable causes before accepting it.

### Relaunching Without Fixing The Cause

A relaunch that does not address the diagnosed failure repeats it under a new banner.

### Discarding The Audience And Learning

A failed launch still produces users, data, and assets. Discarding them wastes the investment.

### Rushing Or Stalling The Recovery

A recovery that is too fast repeats the failure; one that never happens lets momentum die. Time it deliberately.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The failure has been decomposed into distribution, product, positioning, timing, and execution, not attributed to a single cause.
- [ ] "No one came" is distinguished from "people came but did not stay," and each is investigated separately.
- [ ] Distribution has been audited to confirm whether the target segment actually arrived before the product is condemned.
- [ ] Activation and retention have been audited to distinguish onboarding friction from value deficiency.
- [ ] Externalizing diagnoses, "market not ready" or "product not good enough," have been deferred until controllable causes are ruled out.
- [ ] The recovery response, relaunch, iterate, or pivot, matches the diagnosed cause rather than the team's comfort.
- [ ] The audience, data, and assets from the failed launch are preserved for the recovery rather than discarded.
- [ ] A deliberate recovery timeline has been set that is neither a rushed relaunch nor an indefinite stall.
- [ ] No relaunch is planned before the diagnosed cause has been addressed.
- [ ] The team can state, with evidence, which component of the launch failed and what specifically will be different next time.
