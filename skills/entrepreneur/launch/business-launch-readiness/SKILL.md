# Business Launch Readiness

Launching a business is not the same as finishing a product, opening a website, or announcing availability. A launch means the business is ready to accept real customers, deliver the promised value, handle money, respond to problems, meet basic legal and operational obligations, and learn from the market.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should be checked before launching a business", "what should be ready before first customers", "how should a go-to-market launch be prepared", or "what should an entrepreneur verify before opening". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating launch as marketing alone.

## Core Rules

### Define Launch Scope

Not every launch is public, permanent, or broad. A launch can be a pilot, beta, soft opening, local test, private cohort, waitlist release, paid trial, or full market launch.

Define:

- who can buy or use the offer;
- geography;
- channel;
- product or service scope;
- price;
- support level;
- availability window;
- success criteria;
- stop or rollback condition.

Small launches are useful when they create learning without overcommitting.

### Confirm The Promise Can Be Delivered

Marketing should not outrun operational ability. Before launch, confirm that the business can deliver what it promises at the expected quality.

Check:

- product or service readiness;
- supply availability;
- fulfillment process;
- staffing;
- onboarding;
- customer communication;
- payment handling;
- refund or cancellation process;
- support path;
- quality control;
- delivery timing;
- issue escalation.

Do not launch a promise that depends on heroic manual work unless the launch scope is intentionally small and controlled.

### Prepare Legal, Administrative, And Trust Basics

The exact requirements vary by location and industry, but an entrepreneur should not ignore basic legal and administrative readiness.

Review:

- business registration;
- tax IDs or local equivalents;
- licenses and permits;
- business bank account;
- insurance;
- contracts or terms;
- privacy policy where data is collected;
- payment processor setup;
- bookkeeping process;
- data security basics;
- customer records;
- required disclosures.

This skill does not replace professional legal, tax, or compliance advice. It ensures the agent flags the topics before launch.

### Make The First Sales Path Concrete

"Launch and promote" is not a sales plan. The entrepreneur should know where the first customers come from and what steps they take.

Define:

- target initial segment;
- channel;
- message;
- call to action;
- landing or purchase path;
- response handling;
- follow-up;
- conversion expectation;
- owner for outreach;
- daily or weekly activity target.

If the first sales path is unclear, launch may produce silence rather than learning.

### Instrument Learning

Launch should produce evidence. Decide what will be observed before customers arrive.

Track:

- traffic or inquiries;
- conversion;
- sales conversations;
- objections;
- activation or successful use;
- support issues;
- refund or cancellation reasons;
- delivery time;
- customer satisfaction;
- repeat purchase or retention;
- unit economics.

Qualitative notes matter as much as early metrics. The first customers often reveal language, objections, missing features, and operational friction.

### Create A Response Plan For Problems

Real customers create real problems. Prepare how the business will respond before launch stress arrives.

Plan:

- who monitors incoming issues;
- how customers contact support;
- refund or cancellation policy;
- service recovery approach;
- incident escalation;
- owner for urgent fixes;
- public communication if something fails;
- data backup or recovery;
- vendor backup options.

The response does not need to be elaborate, but it should exist.

### Decide What Not To Scale Yet

Early launch should often optimize for learning, not maximum volume. Too much demand before operations stabilize can harm reputation.

Consider limiting:

- customer count;
- geography;
- product options;
- customization;
- paid marketing;
- inventory commitment;
- integrations;
- service hours.

Scale after the business can reliably deliver and learn from first customers.

## Common Traps

### Launching Because The Website Is Done

A website is only one part of launch. The business also needs delivery, support, payment, legal basics, and learning loops.

### Over-Marketing Before Operations Are Ready

Large awareness can create failure if fulfillment, support, or quality cannot keep up.

### No Clear First Customer Channel

General social posting is rarely enough. Define a reachable initial audience and specific outreach path.

### Ignoring Refunds And Complaints

Refunds, complaints, cancellations, and failed delivery are not edge cases. They are part of operating.

### Collecting Data Without Responsibility

If the business collects customer data, it needs privacy, security, access, retention, and consent thinking appropriate to the context.

### Measuring Only Revenue

Early revenue matters, but so do objections, failed conversions, support burden, delivery quality, and repeat behavior.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Launch type and scope are explicit: pilot, beta, soft launch, local test, private cohort, paid trial, or full launch.
- [ ] The business can deliver the customer promise with current staffing, supply, fulfillment, support, and quality controls.
- [ ] Registration, tax, licenses, permits, insurance, banking, contracts, privacy, payments, bookkeeping, and data security basics were reviewed where relevant.
- [ ] First customer segment, channel, message, call to action, and follow-up path are concrete.
- [ ] Launch learning metrics and qualitative signals are defined before customers arrive.
- [ ] Support, refunds, cancellations, complaints, incidents, and urgent fixes have owners and response paths.
- [ ] Launch scope is limited where needed to protect quality and learning.
- [ ] Operational manual work is acknowledged rather than hidden.
- [ ] Customer data collection has privacy, security, consent, and retention considerations.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids treating launch readiness as only product completion or marketing announcement.
