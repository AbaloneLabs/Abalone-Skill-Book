# Product Hunt Launch

A Product Hunt launch is a concentrated one-day visibility event that can drive significant traffic, early users, and credibility, but its value is not the ranking on launch day; its value is whether it produces durable acquisition, learning, and momentum that survives after the spike fades. Teams routinely mistake the launch for the outcome, pouring effort into a single day of attention and then watching the traffic disappear because nothing was built to capture it. The judgment problem is treating the launch as the visible peak of weeks of preparation, designing the funnel so launch traffic converts to retained users, and using the launch to generate assets, feedback, and relationships that compound rather than evaporate. Agents tend to fail by optimizing for the ranking rather than the funnel, by launching before the product is ready to retain the traffic, and by treating launch day as the finish line rather than the starting line.

Use this skill when planning, executing, or advising on a Product Hunt launch. The goal is a launch that produces lasting value, not a one-day spike that leaves nothing behind.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Launch As The Peak Of Preparation, Not A Standalone Event

A successful Product Hunt launch is built on weeks of preparation: building an audience, refining messaging, preparing assets, lining up supporters, and ensuring the product is ready to receive and retain traffic. The launch day is the visible peak, not the work itself.

Prepare by:

- building an email list and audience weeks before launch, not on launch day;
- refining the product messaging, tagline, and demo until they are sharp;
- preparing all assets, gallery images, video, founder comment, in advance;
- lining up supporters, hunters, and partners who will engage on launch day.

A launch prepared in a few days produces a fraction of the result of one prepared over weeks. The ranking reflects the preparation, not luck.

### Ensure The Product Is Ready To Retain Launch Traffic

Product Hunt drives curious, high-intent users who will try the product once and judge it immediately. If the product is not ready to deliver value on first use, the launch traffic bounces and the opportunity is wasted, however high the ranking.

Ensure readiness by:

- validating that the first-run experience delivers value quickly, within minutes;
- fixing the bugs and friction that would cause launch-day users to abandon;
- ensuring onboarding guides users to the core value, not into a dead end;
- having the capacity to handle the traffic spike without performance failure.

Launching an unready product to a large audience burns the first impression with the users most likely to convert. Wait until the product retains before buying the attention.

### Build A Funnel That Captures Traffic, Not Just A Listing

Launch traffic that visits once and leaves produces no durable value. The launch must be designed as a funnel that captures visitors, converts them to users, and retains them beyond the spike.

Build the funnel by:

- capturing emails from visitors who are not ready to sign up, for nurturing later;
- making the path from landing page to core value as short as possible;
- offering a reason to return, such as a sequence, a community, or ongoing value;
- measuring each stage of the funnel, from visit to signup to retention.

A launch without a funnel is a faucet that runs for a day and then stops. Design the capture and retention, not just the listing.

### Choose The Date And Timing Deliberately

The day and time of launch significantly affect the result, because they determine the competitive field and the audience available. Launching on a crowded day against major releases buries the product; launching at the wrong time misses the audience.

Choose by:

- avoiding days with major competing launches that would dominate attention;
- considering the day of the week that matches the target audience's behavior;
- launching at 12:01 AM Pacific Time to maximize the full 24-hour window;
- checking the calendar for holidays or events that would suppress engagement.

The date is a strategic choice, not a default. Pick it to give the product the best chance to be seen.

### Coordinate The Hunter And Maker Roles Effectively

Product Hunt's structure involves a hunter, who posts the product, and makers, who engage in the comments. The choice of hunter and the coordination of makers affect visibility and credibility.

Coordinate by:

- choosing a hunter with a relevant following if possible, though self-hunting is now accepted;
- ensuring makers are available and responsive throughout launch day;
- having the founding team actively engage in comments, answering questions and thanking supporters;
- using the maker comment to tell the product's story and invite engagement.

Active, authentic maker engagement is one of the strongest signals on launch day. Coordinate the team to be present and responsive.

### Manage Launch Day As A Real-Time Operation

Launch day requires active management: responding to comments, rallying supporters, monitoring the funnel, and adapting to what happens. Treating it as a passive event wastes the opportunity.

Manage by:

- having the full team available and assigned to specific roles, comments, outreach, monitoring;
- responding to every comment promptly and authentically;
- quietly rallying supporters to engage, without violating Product Hunt's guidelines against manipulation;
- monitoring the funnel in real time and fixing issues as they arise.

Launch day is a live operation. The teams that win are the ones that manage it actively, not the ones that post and wait.

### Avoid Manipulation And Respect The Platform's Norms

Product Hunt's community and algorithms penalize manipulation, and tactics that violate the platform's norms, incentivized upvotes, fake accounts, coordinated spam, can sink a launch and damage reputation. The line between rallying support and manipulation must be respected.

Respect norms by:

- rallying genuine supporters who know the product, not purchased or incentivized votes;
- avoiding asking for upvotes explicitly, which violates guidelines;
- engaging authentically in comments rather than posting promotional spam;
- focusing on real value and real users, not on gaming the ranking.

A launch won by manipulation produces no durable value and risks penalties. Play within the norms.

### Extract Durable Value Beyond Launch Day

The ranking fades, but the assets, feedback, and relationships from a launch can compound if captured. A launch that produces only a one-day spike has underperformed, however high it ranked.

Extract durable value by:

- capturing and acting on the feedback from launch-day users;
- converting launch traffic into an email list, community, or user base for ongoing engagement;
- using the launch as a credibility asset in press, partnerships, and future marketing;
- following up with the users and supporters who engaged most deeply.

The launch is a beginning, not an end. Design it to produce assets and relationships that outlast the spike.

## Common Traps

### Launching With Minimal Preparation

A launch built in days produces a fraction of the result. Prepare over weeks.

### Product Not Ready To Retain Traffic

Launching an unready product burns the first impression. Wait until it retains.

### No Funnel To Capture Traffic

Visitors who leave produce no value. Design capture and retention, not just a listing.

### Poor Date And Timing Choice

A crowded day or wrong time buries the product. Choose strategically.

### Passive Launch Day Management

Posting and waiting wastes the opportunity. Manage launch day actively.

### Manipulation That Triggers Penalties

Incentivized votes and fake engagement sink launches and damage reputation. Respect the norms.

### Treating The Ranking As The Outcome

The ranking fades. Design for durable acquisition, learning, and momentum.

### Ignoring The Feedback And Relationships

Launch-day feedback and supporters are assets. Capture and act on them.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The launch is prepared over weeks, with audience, messaging, and assets ready before launch day.
- [ ] The product delivers value on first use and is ready to retain the launch traffic.
- [ ] A funnel is designed to capture visitors, convert them, and bring them back beyond launch day.
- [ ] The launch date and timing are chosen to avoid competition and reach the target audience.
- [ ] The hunter and maker roles are coordinated, with the founding team active in comments all day.
- [ ] Launch day is managed as a real-time operation, with the team assigned to specific roles.
- [ ] All tactics respect Product Hunt's norms, with no incentivized votes or manipulation.
- [ ] Launch traffic is captured into an email list, community, or user base for ongoing engagement.
- [ ] Launch-day feedback is collected and acted on to improve the product.
- [ ] The launch is used as a credibility asset in press, partnerships, and future marketing.
