# Community-Led Launch

A community-led launch builds and nurtures a community of users and advocates before and during the launch, so that the product enters the market with a base of people who already believe, contribute, and spread the word. Unlike a press or platform launch, which buys attention for a day, a community-led launch compounds, because the community itself becomes the engine of acquisition, support, and improvement over time. The judgment problem is building genuine community before you need it, seeding it with the right founding members, designing the rituals that create belonging, and converting community energy into adoption without exploiting the people who make it possible. Agents tend to fail by treating community as a marketing channel rather than a group of people, by launching a product to an empty room, and by confusing activity with belonging.

Use this skill when planning or executing a community-led launch. The goal is a community that drives durable adoption and advocacy, not a transient crowd that disperses.

## Core Rules

### Build The Community Before The Launch, Not During It

A community-led launch requires a community that exists before the product launches, because the community is the launch. Building community in parallel with the product means that launch day arrives with people ready to adopt, advocate, and contribute.

Build before launch by:

- starting the community early, often before the product is fully built;
- gathering the people who care about the problem the product addresses;
- nurturing engagement and belonging during the build, not waiting for launch;
- treating the pre-launch community as the foundation of the launch itself.

A product launched to an empty room has no community to drive adoption. Build the room first.

### Seed With The Right Founding Members

The founding members of a community set its culture, norms, and trajectory, because new members take their cues from the existing behavior. Seeding with the wrong people, opportunistic, low-engagement, or misaligned, produces a community that never coheres.

Seed carefully by:

- recruiting founding members who genuinely care about the problem and the space;
- prioritizing quality of engagement over quantity of members;
- establishing the culture and norms through the founding group's behavior;
- being willing to curate membership early, to protect the culture.

The first members define the community. Choose them as deliberately as early hires.

### Design Rituals And Practices That Create Belonging

A community is not a group of people in a channel; it is a group of people who share practices, rituals, and identity. Designing the rituals that create belonging is what transforms a crowd into a community.

Design rituals by:

- creating regular practices, such as weekly threads, events, or challenges, that members share;
- establishing traditions and shared language that build identity;
- recognizing and celebrating member contributions to reinforce belonging;
- ensuring the rituals are sustainable and authentic, not forced.

Belonging comes from shared practice, not from a Slack invite. Design the rituals that make members feel part of something.

### Focus On Value To Members, Not Extraction From Them

A community that exists to serve the company's goals, to provide free marketing, support, or feedback, is resented and abandoned. A community that delivers value to its members, connection, learning, recognition, belonging, sustains itself.

Focus on member value by:

- ensuring the community delivers tangible value to members, not just to the company;
- giving members agency and ownership over the community's direction;
- avoiding treating the community as a free labor pool for marketing or support;
- recognizing that member value is the foundation of company value.

Members stay for what they get, not for what they give. Design the community to deliver value to them first.

### Convert Community Energy Into Adoption And Advocacy

The community's energy must be converted into product adoption and advocacy for the launch to succeed, but the conversion must be organic, not extractive. Members advocate because they believe, not because they are incentivized.

Convert energy by:

- giving the community early access and involvement in the product's development;
- making it easy and natural for members to share and invite others;
- recognizing and amplifying member advocacy rather than incentivizing it transactionally;
- ensuring the product delivers on the promise the community believed in.

Forced or incentivized advocacy feels inauthentic and undermines trust. Convert energy by giving members something worth sharing and a natural way to share it.

### Reach Critical Mass Before Expecting Self-Sustaining Growth

A community reaches a point of critical mass where it grows and sustains itself through member activity, but before that point, it requires active cultivation. Expecting self-sustaining growth too early leads to a community that stalls.

Reach critical mass by:

- actively nurturing the community until member-driven activity sustains it;
- recognizing the point at which the community can grow through member advocacy;
- continuing to invest in culture and moderation even after critical mass;
- being patient, because community building is slower than other growth channels.

A community pushed to self-sustain before it is ready collapses. Cultivate it to critical mass first.

### Moderate To Protect Culture And Safety

As a community grows, moderation becomes essential to protect the culture, safety, and norms that make it valuable. Unmoderated communities drift toward noise, toxicity, or harm that drives away the members who made them good.

Moderate by:

- establishing clear norms and enforcing them consistently;
- moderating proactively, not only reactively to harm;
- empowering trusted members to help maintain the culture;
- being willing to remove members who undermine the community, even early.

Culture is preserved through moderation. A community that will not moderate its norms loses them.

### Measure Community Health, Not Just Size

Community size is a vanity metric; community health, engagement, retention, and belonging, is what predicts whether the community drives durable value. Measuring only size leads to growth that hollows out the community.

Measure health by:

- tracking engagement, such as active members and meaningful contributions;
- tracking retention, whether members stay involved over time;
- measuring belonging and satisfaction through surveys and feedback;
- treating size as a secondary metric, valuable only if health is strong.

A large, disengaged community is worth less than a small, committed one. Measure health first.

## Common Traps

### Building Community At Launch, Not Before

A product launched to an empty room has no community. Build it first.

### Seeding With The Wrong Founding Members

The first members set the culture. Curate them deliberately.

### A Channel Instead Of A Community

A Slack invite is not a community. Design rituals that create belonging.

### Extracting Value Instead Of Delivering It

A community used as free labor is resented. Deliver value to members first.

### Incentivized Advocacy

Forced advocacy feels inauthentic. Give members something worth sharing naturally.

### Expecting Self-Sustaining Growth Too Early

A community pushed to self-sustain before critical mass collapses. Cultivate it first.

### Unmoderated Growth

Unmoderated communities drift to toxicity. Moderate to protect culture and safety.

### Measuring Size Instead Of Health

A large, disengaged community underperforms a small, committed one. Measure health.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The community is built before launch, so the product enters the market with a base of believers.
- [ ] Founding members are curated for genuine care and alignment, not just for numbers.
- [ ] Rituals and practices are designed to create belonging, not just a channel for messages.
- [ ] The community delivers tangible value to members, not just to the company.
- [ ] Advocacy is organic, driven by belief, not transactional incentives.
- [ ] The community is cultivated to critical mass before self-sustaining growth is expected.
- [ ] Norms are established and enforced through proactive moderation.
- [ ] Community health, engagement, retention, and belonging, is measured, not just size.
- [ ] Members have agency and ownership in the community's direction.
- [ ] The product delivers on the promise the community believed in at launch.
