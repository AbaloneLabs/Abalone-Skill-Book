# Killing Features Gracefully

Not every feature belongs in the product forever. Some never earned their place; others were useful once but no longer serve the core value; others add complexity and maintenance cost that outweigh their benefit. Removing features is as important to product health as adding them, but it is harder, because real users may depend on what is being removed. Killing features gracefully means choosing what to retire deliberately, migrating or compensating affected users, and communicating honestly, so that removal strengthens the product rather than alienating the customers who made it work.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should I remove this feature", "how do I sunset a feature", "how do I kill a feature without losing users", or "how should an entrepreneur retire product features". The goal is to prevent the agent from leaving bloated products untouched and from removing features abruptly in ways that destroy trust.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate Features Against The Current Core Value

Periodically review features against the core value the product now delivers. Features that no longer serve the core, that serve a small fraction of users, or that add disproportionate complexity are candidates for retirement. Removal is a tool for sharpening the product, not a sign of failure.

### Measure Usage Before Deciding

Before removing a feature, measure who uses it, how much, and what value they derive from it. A feature used by few may still be critical to those few, especially if they are valuable customers. Usage data, segmented by customer type and value, informs whether removal is safe and what migration is needed.

### Distinguish Low Usage From Low Value

A feature with low overall usage may have high value for a small but important segment. Distinguish features that are genuinely low-value from features that serve a niche whose loss would matter. Removing a niche feature can lose the niche, which may be more costly than the maintenance saved.

### Plan Migration For Affected Users

If users depend on the feature, plan how they will continue their work without it: alternative features, data export, integrations with replacement tools, or manual support during transition. Abandoning users to figure out the loss themselves damages trust and can cause churn. Migration is part of removal.

### Communicate Early And Honestly

Tell affected users well in advance that the feature is being retired, why, what alternatives exist, and what the timeline is. Honest communication, even of unwelcome news, preserves trust; silence or last-minute notice destroys it. Frame removal in terms of focus and product quality, which is usually the truth.

### Time Removals To Minimize Disruption

Choose a removal timeline that minimizes disruption: give users enough warning to migrate, avoid removing during peak usage periods, and ensure alternatives are in place before removal. A rushed removal strands users; a well-timed one lets them adapt.

### Avoid The Sunk-Cost Fallacy

The effort spent building a feature is not a reason to keep it. Features should be evaluated on their current and future value, not on what they cost to build. Keeping a low-value feature because it was expensive to build compounds the waste with ongoing maintenance.

### Watch For Hidden Dependencies

Before removing a feature, check for hidden dependencies: other features, workflows, integrations, or data that rely on it. Removing a feature with unexamined dependencies can break things the team did not realize were connected. Map dependencies before removal.

### Use Removal To Sharpen The Core

Feature removal should serve the core value: simplifying the product, reducing complexity, freeing effort for what matters. Frame each removal in terms of how it sharpens the product, and ensure the freed effort goes to higher-impact work, not just to cost reduction.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Feature removal may carry contractual obligations to customers who paid for specific capabilities, with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Never Removing Anything

Bloat accumulates from features that no longer serve the core, diluting the product.

### Abrupt Removal

Deleting a feature without warning or migration strands users and destroys trust.

### Confusing Low Usage With Low Value

Removing a niche feature that matters to valuable users loses those users.

### Sunk-Cost Justification

Keeping a feature because it was expensive to build compounds waste.

### Hidden Dependencies

Removing a feature that other things rely on breaks unexpected functionality.

### Removal Without Sharpening

Cutting features without redirecting effort to the core wastes the opportunity.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Features are periodically evaluated against the current core value.
- [ ] Usage is measured and segmented before removal is decided.
- [ ] Low usage is distinguished from low value for important segments.
- [ ] Migration paths are planned for affected users.
- [ ] Removal is communicated early and honestly with alternatives and timeline.
- [ ] Removal timing minimizes disruption to users.
- [ ] Sunk-cost fallacy is avoided in retention decisions.
- [ ] Hidden dependencies are mapped before removal.
- [ ] Removal serves to sharpen the core and freed effort goes to higher-impact work.
- [ ] Contractual obligations to customers are flagged for professional legal advice.
