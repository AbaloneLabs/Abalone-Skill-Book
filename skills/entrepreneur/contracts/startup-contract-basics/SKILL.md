# Startup Contract Basics

Contracts bind the venture to obligations, allocate risk, and define relationships with customers, vendors, partners, employees, and investors, yet founders often treat them as paperwork to skim and sign. The clauses that matter, payment terms, liability caps, termination, IP ownership, indemnification, and governing law, determine who bears risk when things go wrong, and they are routinely buried in boilerplate. Understanding contract basics is the discipline of knowing what a contract actually does, what to negotiate, and what to refuse, before the venture is bound.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should I look for in a contract", "what do these contract clauses mean", "is this agreement standard", "what contract mistakes do startups make", or "how should an entrepreneur handle contracts". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating contracts as administrative and from letting a founder sign obligations without understanding the risk allocation.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Makes A Contract Enforceable

A contract generally requires an offer, acceptance, consideration, mutual assent, and clarity of terms, and some agreements must be in writing to be enforceable. Understanding these basics prevents false confidence in informal arrangements and clarifies when a binding obligation has formed, including through conduct and electronic acceptance.

### Read And Understand The Risk Allocation

The core of a contract is risk allocation: who pays if something goes wrong, who is liable for what, and how exposure is capped. Liability caps, indemnification, warranties, and limitation of consequential damages determine where risk lands. Read for the risk allocation, because that is what matters when a dispute arises, not the friendly preamble.

### Scrutinize The Clauses That Carry Real Risk

Certain clauses carry disproportionate risk and warrant scrutiny: indemnification, which can create uncapped obligations; intellectual property ownership and licenses; termination rights and what happens on termination; payment terms and late consequences; non-compete and non-solicit restrictions; and governing law and dispute resolution. These deserve attention even when the rest seems standard.

### Negotiate The Terms That Matter

Many terms presented as standard are negotiable, especially the high-risk clauses. Focus negotiation on the terms that carry real risk or strategic importance, rather than fighting every clause. Knowing what is genuinely standard in the venture's context, and what is not, is essential to negotiating effectively.

### Ensure IP And Data Terms Protect The Venture

In customer, vendor, and partner agreements, the IP ownership, license grants, and data provisions determine whether the venture retains its core assets and complies with its obligations to users. Vague or one-sided IP or data terms can compromise the venture's position. Ensure these terms protect the venture, not just the counterparty.

### Avoid One-Sided Vendor Paper

Large vendors and customers often present one-sided agreements that shift risk onto the startup. While some leverage is unavoidable, accepting one-sided terms without understanding or attempting to adjust them can create outsized exposure. Read vendor and customer paper critically, even when it feels non-negotiable.

### Put Internal Agreements In Writing Too

Internal agreements, among co-founders, with early contractors, and with advisors, should be in writing however much trust exists. Oral internal agreements create disputes when memories diverge or circumstances change, and they are far harder to enforce. Putting them in writing prevents foreseeable conflicts.

### Track Obligations And Deadlines

A signed contract creates ongoing obligations: renewal dates, notice periods, payment milestones, and termination windows. These are easily forgotten and can auto-renew into unwanted commitments or forfeit rights. Track contractual obligations and deadlines, because ignoring them does not excuse them.

### Know When A Contract Exceeds Founder Competence

Complex agreements, with investors, acquirers, large customers, or involving significant IP or liability, exceed what a founder should handle alone. Know when an agreement's stakes warrant professional review, because the cost of counsel is small relative to the cost of a bad term.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Contracts create binding legal obligations and personal and entity liability. Use qualified legal counsel for drafting, review, and negotiation of material agreements. This skill provides foundational understanding only and does not replace professional legal advice.

## Common Traps

### Skimming And Signing

Treating contracts as paperwork leads to accepting unseen obligations and risk.

### Ignoring Risk Allocation

Liability caps and indemnification, not preambles, determine exposure in disputes.

### Accepting One-Sided Vendor Paper

Vendors and large customers shift risk onto the startup, often unnoticed.

### Vague IP And Data Terms

Weak IP or data provisions compromise the venture's core assets.

### Oral Internal Agreements

Unwritten founder and contractor deals create disputes when circumstances change.

### Forgetting Renewals And Notice Windows

Auto-renewals and missed termination windows create unwanted commitments.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The founder understands what makes a contract enforceable.
- [ ] Contracts are read for risk allocation, not skimmed.
- [ ] High-risk clauses, indemnification, IP, termination, payment, governing law, are scrutinized.
- [ ] Negotiation focuses on terms that carry real risk.
- [ ] IP and data terms protect the venture, not just the counterparty.
- [ ] One-sided vendor and customer paper is read critically.
- [ ] Internal agreements are put in writing.
- [ ] Contractual obligations and deadlines are tracked.
- [ ] Complex or high-stakes agreements are flagged for professional review.
- [ ] Material contracts are reviewed by qualified legal counsel.
