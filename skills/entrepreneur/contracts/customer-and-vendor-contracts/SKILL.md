# Customer And Vendor Contracts

Customer and vendor contracts are the everyday commercial lifeblood of a venture, defining how revenue is earned, how services and goods are supplied, who bears risk, and what happens when things go wrong. These agreements look routine, but their terms, payment timing, service levels, liability caps, IP and data ownership, and termination, shape cash flow, exposure, and the venture's asset position. Copying templates uncritically or accepting counterparties' paper without review creates silent risk that surfaces only in disputes or audits.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should be in my customer contract", "how do I review a vendor agreement", "what payment terms should I offer", "who owns the IP in this engagement", or "how should an entrepreneur handle customer and vendor contracts". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating commercial contracts as boilerplate and from ignoring the terms that shape the venture's economics and risk.

## Core Rules

### Match The Contract To The Business Model

Customer contracts should reflect how the venture actually delivers and charges: subscription, one-time, usage-based, milestone, or services arrangements each call for different terms. A contract that does not match the business model creates ambiguity, billing disputes, and revenue leakage. Align the agreement with how value is actually created and captured.

### Set Payment Terms Deliberately

Payment terms, when invoices are due, late fees, milestones, and deposits, directly affect cash flow, which is existential for an early venture. Set terms that support healthy cash flow while remaining acceptable to customers, and enforce them consistently. Defaulting to whatever the customer proposes can starve the venture of cash.

### Allocate Liability Through Caps And Carveouts

Liability in commercial contracts is governed by caps, exclusions of consequential damages, and carveouts for specific breaches. These determine the venture's maximum exposure. Set liability caps that the venture can bear, exclude indirect damages where possible, and understand the carveouts, because uncapped or poorly structured liability can threaten the venture's survival.

### Clarify IP Ownership And Licenses

In customer engagements, clarify who owns the IP created: the venture, the customer, or a license arrangement. In vendor engagements, clarify what IP or data the vendor can use and for what purpose. Vague IP terms create disputes and can compromise the venture's core assets. Be explicit about ownership, license scope, and residuals.

### Address Data Handling And Privacy

Contracts involving customer or user data must address how data is handled, stored, processed, and protected, and allocate responsibility for compliance with privacy law. Vendor agreements for data processors require specific terms. Neglecting data provisions creates regulatory and liability exposure that grows with the venture's data footprint.

### Define Service Levels And Remedies

Where the venture supplies services, define the service levels and the remedies for shortfalls, such as credits or termination rights. Where the venture buys services, ensure the vendor's commitments and remedies are adequate. Vague service levels create disputes and dissatisfied customers with no clear resolution path.

### Handle Termination And Transition Clearly

Contracts should define how they end: termination for cause, termination for convenience, notice periods, and what happens to data, IP, and obligations on termination. Transition provisions matter especially for services and data-heavy engagements. Clear termination terms prevent disputes and stranded obligations.

### Scrutinize Non-Standard And One-Sided Terms

Counterparties may insert non-standard terms: broad indemnities, auto-renewals, audit rights, exclusivity, or non-competes that constrain the venture. Scrutinize these, because they often carry more risk than the headline commercial terms. Push back where the terms are one-sided or unnecessary.

### Track Renewals, Obligations, And Compliance

Signed commercial contracts create ongoing obligations: renewals, volume commitments, audit windows, and compliance certifications. Track these to avoid unwanted auto-renewals, breached commitments, and lapsed certifications. Administrative neglect of contracts creates avoidable risk.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Customer and vendor contracts create binding obligations and liability. Use qualified legal counsel to draft, review, and negotiate material agreements, especially for large customers, critical vendors, or significant exposure. This skill provides strategic framing only and does not replace professional legal advice.

## Common Traps

### Template Mismatch

A contract that does not fit the business model creates billing disputes and revenue leakage.

### Weak Payment Terms

Defaulting to customer-friendly terms starves the venture of cash.

### Uncapped Or Poorly Structured Liability

One bad event can threaten the venture's survival.

### Vague IP Ownership

Disputes over who owns created IP can compromise core assets.

### Neglected Data Provisions

Regulatory and liability exposure grows with the data footprint.

### Overlooking One-Sided Counterparty Terms

Broad indemnities, exclusivity, and auto-renewals carry hidden risk.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Customer contracts match the venture's actual business model and billing.
- [ ] Payment terms support healthy cash flow and are enforced.
- [ ] Liability is capped at a level the venture can bear, with understood carveouts.
- [ ] IP ownership and license scope are explicit in customer and vendor engagements.
- [ ] Data handling and privacy responsibilities are addressed.
- [ ] Service levels and remedies are defined where services are supplied or bought.
- [ ] Termination and transition provisions are clear.
- [ ] Non-standard and one-sided counterparty terms are scrutinized and challenged.
- [ ] Renewals, obligations, and compliance commitments are tracked.
- [ ] Material agreements are reviewed by qualified legal counsel.
