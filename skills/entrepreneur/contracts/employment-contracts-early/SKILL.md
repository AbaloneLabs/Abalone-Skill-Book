# Employment Contracts Early

Early employment arrangements set precedents for the venture's culture, equity practices, and legal posture, yet founders often handle them informally, relying on handshake offers or copied templates. The terms that matter, at-will status, equity and vesting, confidentiality and IP assignment, non-competes and non-solicits, and termination, determine the venture's asset ownership, its flexibility, and its exposure. Early employment contracts are the discipline of protecting the venture's core assets while treating early team members fairly and staying compliant with employment law.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should be in an employment agreement", "how do I handle equity in offers", "do I need a non-compete", "what IP terms do I need for employees", or "how should an entrepreneur handle early employment contracts". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating employment paperwork as routine and from neglecting the terms that protect the venture's assets and flexibility.

## Core Rules

### Secure IP Assignment As Foundational

Every employee, from the first hire, must assign IP created in their role to the venture through a proper agreement. Missing or vague IP assignment means the venture may not own its core assets, which surfaces painfully in due diligence or disputes. IP assignment is non-negotiable and foundational, regardless of how early or trusted the hire.

### Include Confidentiality Obligations

Employees should be bound to protect the venture's confidential information during and after employment. Confidentiality obligations support trade secret protection and define what employees may not take or disclose. These clauses are standard, important, and should be present in every employment agreement.

### Structure Equity And Vesting Deliberately

Equity offers should include vesting, typically over time with a cliff, to align incentives and protect the venture if the employee departs early. Vesting terms, cliffs, acceleration on exit, and the size of grants set precedents for the whole team. Structure equity deliberately, because ad hoc grants create cap table confusion and perceived unfairness.

### Be Cautious With Non-Competes And Non-Solicits

Non-compete and non-solicit clauses restrict employees' post-employment activities, but their enforceability varies sharply by jurisdiction and they can deter talent. Use them judiciously, tailored to legitimate interests, and not as a default broad restriction. Over-broad non-competes are often unenforceable and signal a restrictive culture.

### Clarify At-Will Status Or Fixed Terms

Clarify whether employment is at-will, meaning either party can end it subject to limited exceptions, or for a fixed term. At-will status provides flexibility but should be stated clearly; fixed terms provide certainty but create renewal decisions. Ambiguity here creates disputes and unexpected obligations.

### Define Role, Duties, And Expectations

While early roles are fluid, the agreement should define the role, duties, and expectations at a level that supports accountability without over-constraining. Overly rigid role definitions stifle early-stage flexibility; vague definitions create misalignment. Aim for clarity about expectations while allowing for the fluidity early ventures require.

### Address Termination And Post-Termination Obligations

Define what happens on termination: notice or pay in lieu, return of property and materials, final equity treatment, and post-termination obligations like confidentiality and non-solicits. Clear termination terms prevent disputes and ensure assets and information are returned. Neglecting these creates exposure when departures occur.

### Comply With Employment Law Basics

Employment agreements and practices must comply with employment law: classification of employees versus contractors, wage and hour rules, anti-discrimination, and required notices. Misclassification and non-compliance create liability that scales with headcount. Understand the basics and seek help as the team grows.

### Treat Early Offers As Precedent-Setting

Early offers, on compensation, equity, titles, and terms, set expectations and precedents for the team. Inconsistent or poorly considered early offers create perceived unfairness and retention problems. Treat early offers as setting the venture's patterns, and document the rationale.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Employment agreements and practices involve jurisdiction-specific legal requirements and liability. Use qualified employment counsel for agreements and as the team grows. This skill provides strategic framing only and does not replace professional legal advice.

## Common Traps

### Missing Or Vague IP Assignment

The venture may not own its core assets, a deal-breaking discovery in due diligence.

### Informal Or Handshake Offers

Unwritten terms create disputes and perceived unfairness when circumstances change.

### Over-Broad Non-Competes

Often unenforceable and damaging to recruiting and culture.

### Ad Hoc Equity Grants

Inconsistent vesting and grant sizes create cap table confusion and resentment.

### Employee Misclassification

Treating employees as contractors creates liability that scales with headcount.

### Neglecting Termination Terms

Departures without clear terms create disputes and stranded assets.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Every employee has assigned IP to the venture through a proper agreement.
- [ ] Confidentiality obligations are included in every employment agreement.
- [ ] Equity offers include vesting with terms set deliberately, not ad hoc.
- [ ] Non-competes and non-solicits are used judiciously and tailored to legitimate interests.
- [ ] At-will or fixed-term status is stated clearly.
- [ ] Roles, duties, and expectations are defined with appropriate flexibility.
- [ ] Termination and post-termination obligations are clear.
- [ ] Employment practices comply with classification, wage, and anti-discrimination law.
- [ ] Early offers are treated as precedent-setting and documented.
- [ ] Agreements and practices are reviewed by qualified employment counsel.
