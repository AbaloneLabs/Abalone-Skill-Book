# CAC And LTV Modeling

Customer acquisition cost and lifetime value are the two numbers that determine whether a growth business can sustain itself. If it costs more to acquire a customer than that customer returns over their lifetime, the venture destroys value with every sale and can only mask the destruction with capital until the capital runs out. Modeling CAC and LTV honestly is therefore one of the most important early exercises, and also one of the most abused, because both numbers are easy to flatter with optimistic assumptions about retention, margin, and acquisition efficiency.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what is my CAC", "what is a good LTV to CAC ratio", "can I afford to spend on acquisition", or "how should an entrepreneur model unit economics". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a favorable ratio built on hopeful inputs and from ignoring the components that actually determine sustainability.

## Core Rules

### Compute CAC Fully, Not Optimistically

Customer acquisition cost is everything spent to turn a stranger into a paying customer, divided by the number of customers acquired. The common error is to include only ad spend and ignore the rest.

Include in CAC:

- paid media and campaign spend;
- content, SEO, and creative production;
- sales salaries, commissions, and tools;
- marketing team and agency costs;
- events, samples, and promotional discounts;
- onboarding and activation effort attributable to acquisition.

Use a consistent time window that matches how acquisition converts to payment.

### Compute LTV Conservatively, Not Aspirationally

Lifetime value is the gross profit a customer generates over their full relationship with the business. The common error is to use revenue instead of gross profit, and to assume infinite retention.

Build LTV from:

- average revenue per customer per period;
- gross margin, not revenue;
- expected lifespan derived from real churn;
- expansion revenue where realistic;
- subtracting ongoing cost to serve and support.

Express LTV in gross profit terms so it is comparable to CAC, which is also a gross cost.

### Derive Lifespan From Real Churn

Lifespan is the inverse of churn. A monthly churn rate implies a lifespan; do not pick a lifespan independently of measured or honestly estimated churn. Early ventures often lack long-run churn data, so use conservative assumptions and sensitivity ranges, not a flattering point estimate.

### Use Ratios As Diagnostics, Not Targets

The LTV-to-CAC ratio is a useful diagnostic, but the right ratio depends on the business. A common reference is that lifetime value should be a multiple of acquisition cost, but the exact multiple that is healthy depends on payback period, gross margin, growth ambition, and capital availability.

A high ratio can indicate under-investment in growth; a low ratio can indicate unsustainable acquisition. Interpret the ratio in context, not as a universal pass-fail.

### Prioritize Payback Period Alongside Ratio

Payback period, how many months until cumulative gross profit covers CAC, often matters more than the lifetime ratio for capital-constrained ventures. A venture with a great lifetime ratio but a two-year payback can run out of cash before the ratio pays off.

Model payback explicitly and judge it against available runway and cost of capital.

### Separate Blended From Channel-Specific Economics

Blended CAC hides that some channels are efficient and others are wasteful. Compute CAC and payback by channel and by segment, so you can see which acquisition is sustainable and which is dragging the average.

A blended ratio that looks acceptable can conceal a channel that destroys money, subsidized by one that works.

### Model Sensitivity To The Drivers That Matter

CAC and LTV rest on assumptions about conversion, churn, margin, price, and expansion. Vary the most uncertain drivers to see how the ratio changes. If a small change in churn flips the business from sustainable to unviable, churn is the risk to focus on.

Present ranges, not single heroic numbers.

### Distinguish Early Founder-Led Economics From Scalable Economics

Early customers are often acquired through founder relationships, warm intros, and disproportionate effort that does not scale. Their CAC looks artificially low. Model what CAC becomes when acquisition must rely on repeatable channels, not founder heroics.

### Acknowledge Uncertainty And Professional Risk

Early unit economics are estimates that improve with data. Overstating them to justify spending creates runway and investor risk. This skill does not replace professional financial advice, especially where economics inform fundraising or valuation.

## Common Traps

### Revenue Instead Of Gross Profit In LTV

Using revenue overstates lifetime value and produces flattering ratios.

### Ignoring Sales And Marketing Salaries In CAC

Ad spend alone understates the true cost of acquisition.

### Lifespan Unrelated To Measured Churn

Picking a lifespan by feel rather than deriving it from churn hides the real retention risk.

### Blended CAC Hiding Bad Channels

A good average can conceal channels that lose money.

### Founder-Led CAC Mistaken For Scalable CAC

Early efficiency from personal networks rarely survives the move to paid channels.

### Single Point Estimate

One optimistic number is fragile; a range with sensitivity is honest.

## Self-Check

- [ ] CAC includes all acquisition costs: media, content, sales, marketing team, tools, and promotions.
- [ ] LTV is computed in gross profit terms, not revenue, and includes cost to serve.
- [ ] Customer lifespan is derived from real or conservatively estimated churn.
- [ ] Payback period is modeled alongside the LTV-to-CAC ratio.
- [ ] CAC and payback are computed by channel and segment, not only blended.
- [ ] Sensitivity analysis varies the most uncertain drivers.
- [ ] Early founder-led economics are distinguished from scalable channel economics.
- [ ] Ratios are interpreted in context of margin, growth ambition, and capital, not as universal targets.
- [ ] Ranges and assumptions are presented rather than a single flattering point estimate.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids declaring unit economics healthy without honest CAC, LTV, churn, and payback.
