# Contribution Margin Analysis

Contribution margin is the money left from a sale after the variable costs directly tied to that sale. It is the fuel that must cover fixed costs and, eventually, produce profit. A business can show growing revenue and still lose money on every transaction, which means scaling only deepens the loss. Contribution margin analysis forces the founder to confront, at the unit level, whether each sale makes the business stronger or weaker. It is one of the clearest early warnings of an unsustainable model.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "am I making money per sale", "what is my contribution margin", "can I scale this product", or "how should an entrepreneur check per-unit profitability". The goal is to prevent the agent from confusing revenue growth with unit-level health and from ignoring the variable costs that determine whether each sale helps.

## Core Rules

### Separate Variable From Fixed Costs

Contribution margin only makes sense when variable costs, those that rise with each unit sold, are separated from fixed costs, those that stay roughly constant regardless of volume.

Variable costs typically include:

- cost of goods sold or materials;
- payment processing and transaction fees;
- direct delivery, shipping, or fulfillment;
- usage-based hosting or API costs;
- commissions tied to the sale;
- customer-specific support or onboarding;
- refunds, returns, and warranty reserves.

Fixed costs include salaries, rent, software, and base infrastructure. Mixing them distorts the per-unit picture.

### Compute Contribution Per Unit And As A Percentage

Express contribution in two ways: as an absolute amount per unit, and as a percentage of price. Both matter.

Per-unit contribution shows how much each sale contributes toward covering fixed costs. Percentage contribution shows how sensitive the business is to price discounts, cost increases, or volume changes. A thin percentage margin means small shocks erase profit.

### Include All Truly Variable Costs

The common error is to include only obvious cost of goods and miss the less visible variable costs that still scale with volume. Payment processing, support burden, fraud, returns, and usage-based infrastructure all belong in variable cost if they rise with each sale.

Undercounting variable cost inflates contribution margin and hides a losing model.

### Check Whether Contribution Covers Fixed Costs At Realistic Volume

Positive contribution per unit is necessary but not sufficient. The business must sell enough units for total contribution to cover fixed costs. Compute the break-even volume and judge whether realistic acquisition can reach it within runway.

A model with positive per-unit contribution but unreachable break-even volume is still unviable.

### Distinguish Gross Margin From Contribution Margin

Gross margin, an accounting figure, and contribution margin, a decision figure, can differ. Contribution margin includes all variable costs, some of which accounting gross margin may classify elsewhere. Use contribution margin for pricing and scaling decisions, and reconcile it with accounting gross margin for reporting.

### Model How Margin Changes With Scale

Some costs are variable at low volume but become fixed or step down at higher volume; others are fixed until a threshold then jump. Contribution margin at the first hundred customers may differ sharply from contribution at ten thousand.

Map how margin evolves:

- volume discounts on inputs;
- step costs when capacity is added;
- support burden rising non-linearly;
- infrastructure tiers;
- fulfillment economies.

A model that looks profitable at small scale can deteriorate, or improve, as volume grows.

### Test Sensitivity To Price And Cost Shocks

Vary price, variable cost, and volume to see how contribution responds. If a small cost increase or price discount erases contribution, the model is fragile. Identify the levers that most affect unit profitability.

### Use Contribution Margin For Pricing And Channel Decisions

Contribution margin tells you how much room you have to discount, pay commissions, or spend on acquisition. If a channel or partner demands a take rate that exceeds contribution, that channel destroys money regardless of the volume it brings.

### Acknowledge Uncertainty And Professional Advice

Early contribution estimates rely on assumptions about costs that may shift. Thin margins carry more risk of error. This skill does not replace professional accounting or financial advice, especially where margin informs pricing, fundraising, or tax decisions.

## Common Traps

### Revenue Growth Masking Negative Contribution

Scaling a losing unit model deepens losses rather than curing them.

### Missing Hidden Variable Costs

Processing, support, fraud, returns, and usage infrastructure erode contribution if omitted.

### Confusing Gross Margin With Contribution Margin

Accounting gross margin can exclude variable costs that matter for decisions.

### Ignoring Step Costs

Margin that holds at low volume can collapse when capacity thresholds force new spending.

### Assuming Scale Will Fix Margin

Scale can improve or worsen margin; do not assume it without modeling.

### Discounting Below Contribution

Promotional or channel discounts that push price below variable cost destroy money on every sale.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Variable and fixed costs are separated, with all volume-driven costs treated as variable.
- [ ] Contribution is expressed both per unit and as a percentage of price.
- [ ] Hidden variable costs such as processing, support, fraud, returns, and usage infrastructure are included.
- [ ] Break-even volume is computed and judged against realistic acquisition within runway.
- [ ] Contribution margin is distinguished from accounting gross margin.
- [ ] How margin changes with scale, including step costs and volume discounts, is modeled.
- [ ] Sensitivity to price, variable cost, and volume shocks is tested.
- [ ] Channel and partner take rates are checked against contribution before adoption.
- [ ] Discounts are not allowed to push price below variable cost.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids declaring the model scalable without positive contribution at realistic volume.
