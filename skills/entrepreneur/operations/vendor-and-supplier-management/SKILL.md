# Vendor And Supplier Management

Vendors and suppliers are the external parties the venture depends on to operate: hosting providers, payment processors, manufacturers, fulfillment, support tools, professional services. Managing them well is not only about choosing them; it is about overseeing the relationships over time to ensure reliability, control cost, manage dependency, and respond when problems arise. Poor vendor management surfaces as outages, unexpected costs, quality failures, and lock-in that weakens the venture. The discipline is to treat vendors as relationships to be actively managed, not as one-time purchases.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I manage my vendors", "how do I handle supplier relationships", "what should I watch with my vendors", or "how should an entrepreneur manage suppliers". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating vendor selection as the end of the work and from ignoring the ongoing oversight that determines whether vendors actually serve the venture.

## Core Rules

### Manage Vendors As Ongoing Relationships

Selecting a vendor is the beginning, not the end. Vendors require ongoing management: monitoring performance, maintaining the relationship, reviewing terms, and responding to issues. Treat vendors as relationships whose quality determines operational reliability, not as static purchases.

### Monitor Reliability And Quality Continuously

Track whether vendors deliver on their commitments: uptime, delivery times, quality, support responsiveness. Reliability revealed under stress, outages, peak demand, incidents, matters more than averages. Monitor continuously, because vendor performance can degrade over time without obvious signals until a failure occurs.

### Track Total Cost Over Time

Vendor costs change: usage-based pricing rises with volume, prices increase at renewal, overages accumulate, and add-ons become necessary. Track total cost over time, not only the headline price at signup, because the real cost often diverges from the initial quote. Regular cost review prevents drift toward unexpectedly expensive relationships.

### Manage Dependency And Lock-In

Assess how dependent the venture is on each vendor and what switching would cost. For critical vendors, manage dependency actively: maintain data portability, avoid deep proprietary lock-in where possible, and keep alternatives in view. High lock-in with a critical vendor weakens the venture's position over time.

### Maintain Clear Points Of Contact And Accountability

Know who at the vendor is responsible for the relationship and for issue resolution. Establish clear points of contact, escalation paths, and response expectations. When problems arise, the venture needs to know whom to reach and what to expect; ambiguity in accountability prolongs outages and failures.

### Review And Renegotiate At Renewal

Renewals are leverage points. Before renewal, review the vendor's performance, the venture's needs, market alternatives, and pricing. Renegotiate terms where the venture has grown or where the vendor has underperformed, rather than auto-renewing into stale terms. Renewals ignored become costs and terms that drift against the venture.

### Diversify Critical Dependencies

For capabilities critical to the venture's operation, avoid single points of failure where feasible. Diversify critical dependencies across vendors or maintain backup options, because a single vendor failure in a critical function can halt the venture. Balance diversification's cost against the risk of concentration.

### Respond To Problems With A Plan

When a vendor fails, degrades, or changes terms unfavorably, have a plan: escalate, demand remediation, activate alternatives, or transition. Responding to vendor problems requires preparation, not improvisation, because decisions made under duress are usually worse. Know the exit path for critical vendors before it is needed.

### Align Vendor Choices With Stage And Strategy

Vendor choices should align with the venture's stage and strategy, not only with cost. A vendor that fits a small venture may not fit at scale; a vendor that conflicts with the venture's positioning, data practices, or values creates strategic risk. Revisit vendor choices as the venture's stage and strategy evolve.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Vendor relationships involve contracts, data, and compliance obligations with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Selection Treated As The End

Choosing a vendor without ongoing management invites degradation and surprise.

### Ignoring Cost Drift

Total cost rising with volume and renewal goes unnoticed until it is large.

### High Lock-In With Critical Vendors

Dependency that weakens the venture's position and makes exit painful.

### Ambiguous Accountability

No clear contact or escalation prolongs outages and failures.

### Auto-Renewing Into Stale Terms

Renewals ignored become costs and terms that drift against the venture.

### Single Points Of Failure

Critical functions dependent on one vendor can halt the venture if it fails.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Vendors are managed as ongoing relationships, not one-time purchases.
- [ ] Reliability and quality are monitored continuously, including under stress.
- [ ] Total cost over time is tracked, not only the signup price.
- [ ] Dependency and lock-in are assessed and managed for critical vendors.
- [ ] Clear points of contact and escalation paths are established.
- [ ] Renewals are reviewed and renegotiated, not auto-renewed.
- [ ] Critical dependencies are diversified where feasible.
- [ ] A plan exists for responding to vendor problems and failures.
- [ ] Vendor choices align with the venture's stage and strategy.
- [ ] Contract, data, and compliance implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
