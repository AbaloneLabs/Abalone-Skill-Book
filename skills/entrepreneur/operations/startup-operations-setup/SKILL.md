# Startup Operations Setup

Operations are the systems, tools, and processes that let the venture actually function: how work is tracked, how money is managed, how customers are served, how information flows, and how the team coordinates. Early on, operations are minimal and manual, and that is correct; the venture's job is to learn what works, not to build infrastructure for a scale it has not reached. But minimal does not mean chaotic. Even early operations need enough structure to deliver reliably, keep promises to customers, and avoid the breakdowns that destroy trust. The discipline is to build just enough operation to support the current stage, and to evolve it as the venture grows.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I set up my startup operations", "what tools do I need", "how do I organize how we work", or "how should an entrepreneur set up operations". The goal is to prevent the agent from over-engineering operations prematurely and from letting the venture run on pure improvisation that breaks under load.

## Core Rules

### Match Operations To The Current Stage

Operations should match what the venture actually needs now, not what it might need at scale. Early operations are minimal, manual, and flexible; later operations add structure, automation, and specialization as volume and complexity demand. Building elaborate operations before they are needed wastes effort and creates rigidity; running at scale on improvised operations creates breakdowns.

### Build Just Enough Process To Deliver Reliably

The purpose of early operations is reliable delivery: customers get what they were promised, money is tracked, commitments are met. Build just enough process to achieve that reliability, no more. Excess process, heavy documentation, rigid workflows, slows a small team without adding reliability; insufficient process breaks promises and loses customers.

### Choose Tools That Fit The Stage, Not The Aspiration

Tools should fit the venture's current size and complexity, not its aspirational scale. Heavy enterprise tools impose setup and maintenance costs that overwhelm a small team; simple tools that work now can be replaced when they no longer suffice. Choose tools that reduce friction now, and plan to revisit as needs grow.

### Establish Financial And Legal Basics Early

Some operational basics cannot be deferred: a business bank account, basic accounting, invoicing and payment tracking, tax obligations, and core legal compliance. Establish these early, because their absence creates risk and rework. Other operational sophistication can wait; these fundamentals cannot.

### Track The Work That Matters

Even a small team needs to track what work is in progress, what is committed, and what is blocked. A simple system, a board, a list, a shared document, is enough at first; the point is shared visibility, not elaborate project management. Losing track of commitments breaks delivery and trust.

### Keep Customer-Facing Operations Reliable

Customers experience the venture through its operations: whether they get what they paid for, whether support responds, whether the product works. Keep customer-facing operations reliable even when internal operations are minimal. A venture that delivers reliably to customers can survive internal mess; one that breaks customer promises cannot.

### Document What Must Be Repeatable

Document the processes that must be repeatable and transferable: how to onboard a customer, how to handle a refund, how to deploy a release, how to close the books. Do not document everything; document what someone else would need to do the work, especially as the team grows. Undocumented critical processes become bottlenecks tied to one person.

### Avoid Premature Specialization

Early teams wear many hats, and that is efficient. Avoid creating specialized roles and rigid handoffs before volume justifies them; specialization too early creates silos and coordination overhead in a team too small to benefit. Let specialization emerge as the venture grows and as repeated pain points reveal where it is needed.

### Plan For Operational Evolution

Operations that work at five people break at twenty, and those that work at twenty break at fifty. Plan for operational evolution: recognize when the current setup is straining, and evolve it before breakdowns accumulate. Operations are not set up once; they are revisited at each stage of growth.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Operations setup involves tax, legal, employment, and compliance obligations that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Over-Engineering Operations Prematurely

Elaborate processes and tools for a scale not yet reached waste effort and create rigidity.

### Pure Improvisation

No structure breaks delivery and loses customer trust under load.

### Tools Chosen For Aspiration, Not Stage

Heavy tools overwhelm a small team with setup and maintenance.

### Deferred Financial And Legal Basics

Missing fundamentals create risk and rework that compound.

### Undocumented Critical Processes

Key work tied to one person becomes a bottleneck as the team grows.

### Premature Specialization

Rigid roles and handoffs in a small team create overhead without benefit.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Operations match the venture's current stage, not an aspirational scale.
- [ ] Just enough process exists to deliver reliably, without excess rigidity.
- [ ] Tools fit the current size and complexity and can be replaced as needs grow.
- [ ] Financial and legal basics are established early.
- [ ] The work that matters is tracked with shared visibility.
- [ ] Customer-facing operations are kept reliable.
- [ ] Processes that must be repeatable are documented for transfer.
- [ ] Specialization is deferred until volume justifies it.
- [ ] Operational evolution is planned as the venture grows.
- [ ] Tax, legal, employment, and compliance obligations are flagged for professional advice.
