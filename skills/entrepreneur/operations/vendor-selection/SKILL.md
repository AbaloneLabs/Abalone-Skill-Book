# Vendor Selection

Vendor choices made early in a company's life, for infrastructure, tools, services, and operations, shape its capabilities, costs, and constraints for years, because switching vendors once a business depends on them is expensive and disruptive. A vendor that fits at ten customers may fail at ten thousand, a cheap tool may become a cost center, and a convenient integration may become a lock-in that cannot be escaped. The judgment problem is evaluating vendors on total cost and capability rather than sticker price, assessing the lock-in and switching risk that constraints create, and choosing vendors that support the company's trajectory rather than ones that constrain it. Agents tend to fail by optimizing for the short term, by underestimating switching costs, and by ignoring the concentration risk of depending on a single vendor for a critical function.

Use this skill when selecting, evaluating, or managing vendors for a startup. The goal is vendor choices that support scalability, security, and flexibility rather than creating dependencies that constrain the company later.

## Core Rules

### Evaluate On Total Cost Of Ownership, Not Sticker Price

The listed price of a vendor is rarely the total cost. Implementation, integration, training, usage overages, support tiers, and switching costs all contribute to what the vendor actually costs over time, and a cheap tool can become expensive while a pricey one can prove economical.

Evaluate total cost by:

- modeling the cost over a realistic timeframe, including growth in usage;
- accounting for implementation, integration, and migration costs;
- including training, support, and administrative overhead;
- comparing the fully loaded cost across vendors, not just the headline price.

A vendor chosen on sticker price often costs more in total. Evaluate the full picture.

### Assess Capability Against Current And Future Needs

A vendor must meet not only the company's current needs but its trajectory, because outgrowing a vendor forces a painful migration. Assessing capability against the future, not just the present, prevents premature switching.

Assess capability by:

- defining the requirements at current scale and at anticipated future scale;
- checking whether the vendor can support the company at 10x its current size;
- evaluating the roadmap and whether the vendor invests in relevant capabilities;
- identifying the points at which the vendor would break and what migration would cost.

A vendor that fits now but fails at scale is a deferred problem with interest. Assess the trajectory.

### Evaluate Lock-In And Switching Costs Before Committing

Lock-in is the cost and difficulty of leaving a vendor, and it is often invisible at selection time but decisive later. A vendor with high lock-in gains leverage that can be used against the company on price, terms, and roadmap.

Evaluate lock-in by:

- assessing how difficult and expensive it would be to switch to an alternative;
- checking whether data is portable and exportable in usable formats;
- evaluating proprietary formats, APIs, and integrations that create dependence;
- preferring vendors with open standards and exit paths where the function is critical.

Lock-in is not always avoidable, but it must be a conscious choice, not a surprise. Evaluate it before committing.

### Negotiate Contracts That Protect The Company

Vendor contracts define the terms of the relationship, and the default terms favor the vendor. Negotiating protects the company on price, liability, data ownership, and exit.

Negotiate by:

- negotiating price, especially at higher volumes or longer commitments;
- securing favorable terms on data ownership, portability, and exit;
- ensuring SLAs and remedies for failure are defined for critical functions;
- avoiding auto-renewal traps and terms that increase cost unpredictably.

The default contract is not the final offer. Negotiate the terms that matter for a growing company.

### Manage Vendor Risk And Concentration

Depending on a single vendor for a critical function creates concentration risk: if that vendor fails, raises prices, or changes direction, the company is exposed. Managing concentration requires diversifying where it matters and monitoring vendor health.

Manage risk by:

- identifying the critical functions where a vendor failure would be severe;
- avoiding single-vendor dependence for functions where alternatives exist;
- monitoring vendor financial health, roadmap, and strategic direction;
- maintaining exit plans for critical vendors, even if not immediately executable.

Concentration risk is acceptable when conscious and managed, dangerous when invisible. Know where you depend on a single vendor.

### Prioritize Security And Compliance From The Start

Vendors that handle company or customer data introduce security and compliance risk, and a vendor breach becomes the company's breach in the eyes of customers and regulators. Security cannot be retrofitted; it must be evaluated at selection.

Prioritize security by:

- evaluating the vendor's security posture, certifications, and practices;
- ensuring the vendor meets the compliance requirements the company's customers demand;
- understanding data handling, residency, and breach notification terms;
- treating security as a selection criterion, not an afterthought.

A vendor with weak security is a liability that can become a crisis. Evaluate it before committing.

### Prefer Integration And Ecosystem Fit

Vendors do not exist in isolation; they fit into a stack of tools and processes, and a vendor that integrates poorly with the existing ecosystem creates friction and manual work. Ecosystem fit affects the total cost and capability as much as the vendor itself.

Prefer fit by:

- evaluating how well the vendor integrates with the company's existing tools;
- checking the availability of APIs, connectors, and partner ecosystem;
- assessing whether the vendor's model aligns with the company's workflows;
- avoiding vendors that create data silos or process bottlenecks.

A capable vendor that integrates poorly underperforms a slightly less capable one that fits the ecosystem. Evaluate the stack, not just the tool.

### Plan The Vendor Lifecycle, Not Just Selection

Vendor management does not end at selection; it continues through usage, renewal, and eventual replacement. Planning the lifecycle prevents vendors from drifting into neglect or lock-in.

Plan the lifecycle by:

- scheduling regular reviews of vendor performance, cost, and fit;
- tracking usage against contract terms to avoid overages or underuse;
- planning renewals with negotiation, not auto-renewal by default;
- maintaining awareness of alternatives for when switching becomes necessary.

A vendor selected and forgotten drifts toward cost and lock-in. Manage the lifecycle actively.

## Common Traps

### Optimizing For Sticker Price

Cheap tools can become expensive. Evaluate total cost of ownership.

### Ignoring Future Scale

A vendor that fits now may fail at scale. Assess capability against the trajectory.

### Underestimating Lock-In

High lock-in gives vendors leverage. Evaluate switching costs before committing.

### Accepting Default Contract Terms

Default terms favor the vendor. Negotiate price, data, and exit terms.

### Single-Vendor Concentration Risk

Depending on one vendor for a critical function is dangerous. Diversify where it matters.

### Neglecting Security And Compliance

A vendor breach is the company's breach. Evaluate security at selection.

### Poor Ecosystem Fit

A vendor that integrates poorly creates friction. Evaluate the stack, not just the tool.

### Select-And-Forget Vendor Management

Vendors drift toward cost and lock-in when neglected. Manage the lifecycle actively.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Vendors are evaluated on total cost of ownership, including implementation, usage, and switching costs.
- [ ] Capability is assessed against current and future scale, not just present needs.
- [ ] Lock-in and switching costs are evaluated before commitment, with exit paths for critical functions.
- [ ] Contracts are negotiated on price, data ownership, SLAs, and exit terms, not accepted as default.
- [ ] Concentration risk is identified and managed for critical vendor functions.
- [ ] Security and compliance are evaluated at selection, not retrofitted later.
- [ ] Vendors are chosen for ecosystem fit and integration, not just standalone capability.
- [ ] Vendor performance, cost, and fit are reviewed regularly through the lifecycle.
- [ ] Renewals are negotiated, not auto-renewed by default.
- [ ] Alternatives are maintained for critical vendors, so switching remains possible.
