# Exit Strategy Options

An exit, through acquisition, IPO, or secondary sale, is how founders, employees, and investors realize the value they have built, and the realistic exit options shape many earlier decisions, from entity structure to fundraising to hiring. Yet founders often treat exit thinking as premature or mercenary, deferring it until an offer arrives or circumstances force a sale, at which point options are narrow and leverage is low. Understanding exit options is the discipline of aligning the venture's trajectory with realistic outcomes, so that decisions throughout the venture's life preserve exit optionality and value.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what are my exit options", "should I plan to sell or IPO", "how do exits work for startups", "when should I think about an exit", or "how should an entrepreneur think about exit strategy". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating exit planning as premature and from letting the venture drift into a position with few good options.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Main Exit Options

The main exit options are acquisition, sale to another company; IPO, public offering and listing; and secondary sale, sale of shares to new investors. Each has different requirements, timelines, value implications, and likelihoods for a given venture. Understand the options and which are realistic for the venture's trajectory.

### Align Exit Thinking With The Venture's Path

Exit options depend on the venture's scale, industry, growth, and market. A venture-capital-backed, high-growth company may target IPO or large acquisition; a lifestyle or niche venture may target smaller acquisition or secondary. Align exit thinking with the realistic path, because misaligned expectations lead to poor decisions.

### Recognize That Exit Options Shape Earlier Decisions

Exit options influence entity structure, fundraising instruments, investor choice, governance, hiring, and IP strategy. Decisions made early constrain or expand exit options later. Consider exit implications in major decisions, to preserve optionality rather than foreclose it inadvertently.

### Build Optionality Rather Than Optimizing For One Exit

Because exits are uncertain and circumstances change, build optionality, a venture that could be acquired, go public, or sustain secondary sales, rather than optimizing narrowly for one outcome. Optionality preserves value if the preferred exit becomes unavailable. Over-optimizing for one exit can foreclose others.

### Acquisition Is The Most Common Startup Exit

Most successful startup exits are acquisitions, not IPOs. Understanding the acquisition landscape, likely acquirers, their criteria, and timing, helps founders position the venture and recognize opportunities. Planning around acquisition realism is more practical than assuming an IPO.

### IPO Requires Scale, Growth, And Market Conditions

IPOs require significant scale, strong growth, audited financials, governance maturity, and favorable market conditions, and they are available to a small fraction of ventures. Treat IPO as a possible but demanding outcome, not a default. Pursuing IPO readiness shapes many decisions but should reflect realistic potential.

### Secondary Sales Provide Partial Liquidity

Secondary sales, where founders or employees sell shares to new investors, provide partial liquidity without a full exit, and they have become more common. They can reduce pressure and reward the team while preserving the venture's independence. Understand secondary as a distinct option with its own dynamics.

### Time Exits To The Venture's And Market's Readiness

Exit timing depends on both the venture's readiness, scale, growth, profitability, and market conditions, including valuations and M&A activity. Forced timing, from runway pressure or investor deadlines, reduces leverage and value. Monitor readiness and market windows to time exits advantageously.

### Align Stakeholders On Exit Preferences Early

Founders, employees, and investors may have different exit preferences and timelines. Misalignment surfaces painfully during an exit process. Align stakeholders on preferences and timelines early, through governance and open conversation, to avoid conflict when an opportunity arises.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Exits involve significant legal, tax, and financial consequences and personal liability. Use qualified legal, tax, and financial advisors for exit planning and execution. This skill provides strategic framing only and does not replace professional advice.

## Common Traps

### Treating Exit Thinking As Premature

Deferring exit planning until pressure forces a rushed, low-leverage decision.

### Assuming IPO As Default

IPOs are available to a small fraction of ventures; acquisition is far more common.

### Foreclosing Options In Early Decisions

Entity, fundraising, and governance choices that limit exit options later.

### Over-Optimizing For One Exit

Narrow optimization forecloses alternatives if the preferred exit becomes unavailable.

### Forced Timing From Pressure

Runway or investor pressure forces exits at low leverage and value.

### Stakeholder Misalignment On Exit

Different preferences and timelines create conflict during the exit process.
