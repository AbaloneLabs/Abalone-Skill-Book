# IPO Versus Acquisition

IPO and acquisition are the two primary full-exit paths for venture-backed startups, and they differ profoundly in requirements, implications, timelines, and likelihood. An IPO demands scale, growth, governance maturity, and favorable markets, and it creates a public company with ongoing obligations; an acquisition transfers ownership to another company, often faster and with different value realization. Comparing them honestly is the discipline of assessing which path, if either, fits the venture's realistic potential and the stakeholders' goals, rather than defaulting to IPO as the prestige outcome or acquisition as the fallback.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should we IPO or get acquired", "what is the difference between IPO and acquisition", "what does going public require", "which exit is better", or "how should an entrepreneur compare IPO and acquisition". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating IPO as the default aspiration and from ignoring the requirements and tradeoffs that determine which path is realistic and desirable.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Requirements Of Each Path

An IPO requires significant scale, strong and predictable growth, audited financials, mature governance, public-market investor interest, and favorable market conditions. An acquisition requires a willing acquirer, strategic fit, and a venture that withstands due diligence. Understand the distinct requirements, because they determine which path is even available to a given venture.

### Assess Realistic Potential Honestly

IPOs are available to a small fraction of ventures, while acquisitions are the far more common exit. Assess the venture's realistic potential for each path honestly, based on scale, growth, industry, and market, rather than aspiration. Honest assessment prevents over-investing in an unlikely IPO path.

### Compare The Value Realization

Value realization differs: an IPO may offer higher headline value and ongoing upside but with market volatility and lockups; an acquisition may offer cleaner, faster realization, sometimes at a premium for strategic fit, but with less ongoing upside. Compare the realistic value realization, including structure, escrows, and earnouts, not just headline numbers.

### Consider The Timeline And Certainty

IPOs are long, demanding processes with market-dependent timing and significant uncertainty; acquisitions can be faster but depend on finding the right acquirer. Consider timeline and certainty, because a faster, more certain exit at moderate value may beat a long, uncertain pursuit of a higher-value IPO. Timeline and certainty matter alongside value.

### Account For Ongoing Obligations Of Being Public

An IPO is not an ending but a transition to life as a public company, with ongoing reporting, governance, short-term pressure, and public scrutiny. Account for these obligations, because they change how the company is run and the founder's role. Founders sometimes underestimate the burden of public-company life.

### Evaluate Founder And Team Goals

Founders and team members have different goals: some seek the scale and mission of a public company; others prefer the exit and reduced pressure of an acquisition. Evaluate these goals honestly, because the exit path shapes the founder's and team's future. Ignoring personal goals leads to regret.

### Recognize That The Path Is Not Fully Chosen

The exit path is influenced by the venture's performance, market conditions, and acquirer interest, not fully chosen by the founder. A venture may pursue IPO readiness and be acquired instead, or vice versa. Build optionality and respond to reality, rather than assuming full control over the path.

### Consider Investor Preferences And Timelines

Investors have their own preferences and timelines for exit, which may align with or diverge from the founders'. Consider investor preferences, because they influence board decisions and the exit process. Misalignment between founders and investors on exit path creates conflict.

### Use Each Path's Discipline To Improve The Company

The disciplines required for each path, IPO's governance and reporting maturity, acquisition's operational and legal cleanliness, improve the company regardless of which exit occurs. Use the path's discipline as a forcing function for building a better company, not only as exit preparation.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Exit path decisions involve significant legal, tax, and financial consequences and personal liability. Use qualified legal, tax, and financial advisors, and experienced banking counsel, for exit path evaluation and execution. This skill provides framing only and does not replace professional advice.

## Common Traps

### IPO As Default Aspiration

Pursuing IPO readiness without honestly assessing realistic potential.

### Ignoring Public-Company Obligations

Underestimating the burden of reporting, governance, and public scrutiny.

### Headline Value Comparison Only

Ignoring structure, escrows, earnouts, and volatility in value realization.

### Assuming Full Control Over The Path

The path depends on performance, markets, and acquirers, not only founder choice.

### Founder-Investor Misalignment

Different exit preferences and timelines create conflict in the process.

### Over-Investing In An Unlikely Path

Resources spent on IPO readiness that acquisition realism did not warrant.
