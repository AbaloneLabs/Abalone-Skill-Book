# Acquisition Readiness

An acquisition is as much a test of the venture's readiness as of its strategic fit, and acquirers scrutinize financials, legal foundations, IP, customer relationships, team, and operations in due diligence. Gaps in readiness, messy cap tables, missing IP assignments, undocumented contracts, unreliable financials, can delay or derail deals, reduce the price, or create post-closing liability. Acquisition readiness is the discipline of maintaining the venture in a state that can withstand scrutiny and command its value, built over time rather than assembled under deal pressure.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I prepare for an acquisition", "what do acquirers look for in due diligence", "is my startup ready to sell", "what is acquisition readiness", or "how should an entrepreneur prepare for being acquired". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating readiness as a pre-deal scramble and from letting preventable gaps undermine a transaction.

## Core Rules

### Maintain Readiness Continuously, Not As A Scramble

Acquisition readiness is built over time through clean records, proper documentation, and disciplined operations, not assembled in the weeks before a deal. Maintain readiness continuously, because deals arise unpredictably and scrambling under pressure surfaces gaps and costs leverage. Continuous readiness is cheaper and more effective.

### Ensure A Clean, Accurate Cap Table

Acquirers scrutinize the cap table for accuracy, completeness, and clean ownership. Missing grants, undocumented transfers, or disputes delay or derail deals. Maintain a clean, accurate, legally reconciled cap table from the start.

### Secure Complete IP Ownership And Documentation

IP is often the core asset, and acquirers verify the venture owns it cleanly, with assignments from all contributors. Missing or sloppy IP assignments are a common deal-breaker. Ensure complete IP ownership and documentation for all contributors.

### Organize Financial Records And Reporting

Acquirers examine financial records for accuracy, reliability, and coherence with the venture's story. Disorganized or unreliable financials undermine confidence and value. Maintain organized financial records and reporting that withstand scrutiny.

### Document Material Contracts And Customer Relationships

Material contracts, with customers, vendors, partners, and employees, should be documented and current, because acquirers review them for obligations, revenue concentration, and risk. Undocumented or expired contracts create uncertainty. Keep material contracts documented and organized.

### Address Compliance And Legal Exposure

Acquirers assess compliance and legal exposure, including regulatory, employment, privacy, and litigation. Unaddressed exposure creates price reductions, escrows, or deal failure. Identify and address compliance and legal exposure before due diligence.

### Prepare For Team And Key-Person Scrutiny

Acquirers assess the team and key-person dependence, because the venture's value often depends on people staying. Be prepared for scrutiny of retention, roles, and key-person risk, and consider retention arrangements. Team uncertainty reduces deal value and closure odds.

### Align Stakeholders Before The Process

Founders, investors, and key team members should be aligned on the exit, valuation expectations, and roles in the process before due diligence begins. Misalignment during the process delays or breaks deals. Align stakeholders early.

### Assemble An Advisory Team Before The Deal

Acquisition processes require legal, financial, and sometimes banking expertise. Assemble the advisory team before the process, not during, because experienced advisors prevent costly mistakes and the right time to engage them is before pressure peaks.

### Model The Deal's Economics And Terms

Understand the economics and terms that matter in an acquisition, purchase price, structure, escrows, earnouts, reps and warranties, and indemnities, and how they affect realized value. Model the deal, because headline price is not the same as value received. Ignorance of deal terms costs founders money.

### Understand The Acquirer's Strategic Logic

Readiness includes understanding why an acquirer would buy the venture at all: for talent, technology, market entry, revenue, defensive blocking, or synergies with their existing business. Different logics drive different valuations and different scrutiny focus. A talent acquisition due-diligences people and retention; a technology acquisition scrutinizes IP; a revenue acquisition scrutinizes financials and customer concentration. Align readiness emphasis with the likely acquirer logic, because generic readiness misses what a specific acquirer will actually probe.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Acquisitions involve significant legal, tax, and financial consequences and personal liability. Use qualified legal, tax, and financial advisors, and experienced M&A counsel, throughout. This skill provides framing only and does not replace professional advice.

## Common Traps

### Readiness As A Pre-Deal Scramble

Assembling readiness under pressure surfaces gaps and costs leverage.

### Messy Cap Table

Missing grants or disputes delay or derail deals.

### Missing IP Assignments

The venture not owning its core assets is a common deal-breaker.

### Disorganized Financials

Unreliable records undermine confidence and reduce value.

### Undocumented Contracts

Uncertainty about obligations and revenue concentration creates risk.

### Unaddressed Legal Exposure

Compliance and litigation exposure leads to price reductions or deal failure.
