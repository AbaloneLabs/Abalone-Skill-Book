# Acquisition Channel Testing

A startup cannot grow on all channels at once, and choosing the right channels is one of the most consequential early decisions. Acquisition channel testing is the discipline of systematically evaluating candidate channels, content, referrals, paid, partnerships, sales, to find those that can reach the target customer at a cost the economics support. The trap is either committing to a channel before testing proves it works, or dabbling across many channels without enough depth in any to learn. Sound channel testing runs focused experiments, measures cost and quality, and concentrates on what proves repeatable.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what channels should I use", "how do I test acquisition channels", "where should I acquire customers", or "how should an entrepreneur find their growth channels". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending scattered channel dabbling and from committing to a channel on assumption rather than evidence.

## Core Rules

### Test Channels Against The Target Customer

A channel is only valuable if it reaches the target customer. Before testing, confirm the channel is where the target customer's attention actually is, not where it is convenient to advertise. Testing a channel that does not reach the right audience produces meaningless results, positive or negative.

### Run Focused Experiments, Not Scattered Dabbling

Test a small number of channels at a time, with enough effort in each to produce a credible signal. Dabbling across many channels simultaneously produces shallow data in all and deep learning in none. Focus produces the evidence needed to commit or move on.

### Measure Cost Per Acquisition And Customer Quality

For each channel, measure not only whether it acquires customers but what they cost and what they are worth. A channel with low acquisition cost but high churn or low value is a trap; a channel with higher cost but better retention and LTV may be superior. Measure CAC, payback, retention, and LTV by channel, not only volume.

### Define What Success Looks Like Before Testing

Before testing a channel, define what result would justify investing further: a CAC below a threshold, a conversion rate above a level, a retention pattern that holds. Pre-defining success prevents rationalizing weak results into commitment and lets the venture move on from channels that do not meet the bar.

### Give Each Channel Enough Effort To Reveal Its Potential

Channels often perform poorly at first and improve as the message, creative, and targeting are refined. A channel abandoned after a shallow test may have been dismissed prematurely. Give each promising channel enough effort and iteration to reveal its real potential before concluding.

### Match Channels To The Venture's Economics

A channel must be sustainable within the venture's unit economics. A channel whose CAC exceeds LTV cannot fund growth, however well it acquires. Match channel choices to the economics: high-LTV products can afford expensive channels; low-LTV products need cheap or organic channels.

### Sequence Channels By Stage

Before product-market fit, channel testing is premature; the focus is on retention and value. After fit, test channels to find those that can scale. Within scaling, sequence channels by potential and ease: start with the channel most likely to work and easiest to test, then expand. Trying to scale many channels at once scatters capacity.

### Watch For Channel Decay And Saturation

Channels decay over time: audiences saturate, costs rise, competition enters, platforms change algorithms. Monitor each active channel's CAC and quality over time, and begin testing the next channel before the current one fades. Relying on a single channel indefinitely is fragile.

### Distinguish Scalable From Non-Scalable Channels

Some channels, like founder outreach or community participation, are powerful but do not scale beyond the founder's time. Others, like paid or content, scale with investment. Use non-scalable channels for learning and early customers, and build scalable channels for growth. Confusing the two leads to either stalling growth or abandoning learning channels too early.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Channel testing may involve advertising claims, data collection, and platform terms with legal and regulatory implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Scattered Channel Dabbling

Shallow effort across many channels produces no deep learning.

### Committing Before Testing

Scaling a channel on assumption burns money if the assumption is wrong.

### Measuring Volume, Not Quality

A channel that acquires high-churn users at low cost worsens economics.

### Abandoning Channels Prematurely

Dismissing a channel before enough iteration to reveal its potential.

### Channel CAC Exceeding LTV

A channel that cannot fund its own growth scales losses.

### Single-Channel Fragility

Relying on one channel that decays or changes terms endangers growth.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Channels are tested against where the target customer's attention actually is.
- [ ] A small number of channels are tested with focused effort, not scattered dabbling.
- [ ] CAC, payback, retention, and LTV are measured by channel, not only volume.
- [ ] Success criteria are defined before testing begins.
- [ ] Each promising channel receives enough effort and iteration to reveal its potential.
- [ ] Channel choices are matched to the venture's unit economics.
- [ ] Channels are sequenced by stage, with testing after product-market fit.
- [ ] Channel decay and saturation are monitored, with the next channel tested before fade.
- [ ] Scalable and non-scalable channels are distinguished and used appropriately.
- [ ] Advertising, data, and platform-term implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
