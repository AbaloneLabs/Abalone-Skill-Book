# Paid Acquisition Basics

Paid acquisition, advertising and sponsored placements, buys customer attention at a price. It is the fastest way to scale acquisition when it works and the fastest way to burn runway when it does not. The discipline of paid acquisition is to treat it as an investment governed by unit economics: spend only where the cost to acquire is less than the value of the customer acquired, measure honestly by channel and cohort, and scale only after a channel proves profitable at small scale. Paid acquisition without these disciplines becomes a faucet that spends money without knowing whether it earns any back.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should I run paid ads", "how do I do paid acquisition", "how much should I spend on ads", or "how should an entrepreneur use paid channels". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending paid spend without unit economics and from scaling spend before profitability is proven.

## Core Rules

### Ground Paid Acquisition In Unit Economics

Paid acquisition is justified only when the cost to acquire a customer is less than the value that customer brings, within an acceptable payback period. Before spending, know the venture's LTV and the CAC the channel must beat. Spending without this grounding is gambling, not marketing. If the economics do not support paid acquisition, no amount of optimization will make it sustainable.

### Prove Profitability At Small Scale Before Scaling

Scale only what has been proven profitable at small scale. Run a test budget sufficient to produce credible signal, measure the resulting CAC and customer quality, and confirm the channel is profitable before increasing spend. Scaling an unproven channel multiplies losses; scaling a proven one multiplies profit.

### Measure CAC And Quality By Channel And Cohort

Measure not only aggregate CAC but CAC by channel, campaign, and creative, and the quality, retention, and LTV, of the customers each produces. Aggregate numbers hide channels that acquire cheaply but churn quickly and channels that cost more but retain far better. Decisions require granular measurement, not blended averages.

### Watch For CAC Inflation At Scale

CAC usually rises as a channel scales: the cheapest audiences are reached first, and expanding reach reaches more expensive audiences. A CAC profitable at small spend may become unprofitable at larger spend. Model how CAC changes with scale, and do not assume small-scale economics will hold at larger budgets.

### Separate Acquisition From Activation In Measurement

Paid acquisition brings users to the door; activation determines whether they stay. A campaign that acquires users who do not activate produces churn and wasted spend, even at low CAC. Measure activation and retention of paid users, not only the acquisition cost, because users who do not activate have no value.

### Set And Enforce Spend Limits

Set clear spend limits tied to the learning goal or the profitability threshold, and enforce them. Paid platforms are designed to spend; without limits, spend drifts upward, especially when results look promising early. Discipline in spend limits prevents a promising test from becoming a runaway loss.

### Use Creative And Targeting Iteratively

Paid acquisition performance depends heavily on creative and targeting, which must be iterated. Test variations of audience, message, and format, measure their performance, and scale what works while cutting what does not. Static creative and targeting degrade as audiences tire and platforms shift.

### Beware Attribution Pitfalls

Attribution, deciding which channel or touchpoint gets credit for a conversion, is messy, especially across multiple channels and devices. Last-click attribution over-credits the final touch and under-credits earlier ones; blended attribution hides channel differences. Understand the attribution model in use and its biases, and avoid over-claiming the effect of any single channel.

### Plan For Channel Saturation And Decay

Paid channels saturate: audiences tire, costs rise, and competition enters. Monitor CAC and quality over time, and plan for the channel's decay by developing alternatives before the current channel fades. Treating any paid channel as permanent is fragile.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Paid acquisition involves advertising claims, data collection, and platform terms with legal and regulatory implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Spending Without Unit Economics

Paid acquisition without knowing LTV and the CAC to beat is gambling.

### Scaling Before Profitability Is Proven

Scaling an unproven channel multiplies losses.

### Blended Averages Hiding Channel Differences

Aggregate CAC hides cheap-but-churning and costly-but-retaining channels.

### Ignoring CAC Inflation At Scale

Small-scale economics do not hold at larger spend.

### Acquisition Without Activation

Users who do not activate have no value, however cheaply acquired.

### Attribution Over-Claiming

Last-click or blended models mislead about which channels actually work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Paid acquisition is grounded in unit economics, with LTV and the CAC to beat known.
- [ ] Profitability is proven at small scale before any scaling.
- [ ] CAC and customer quality are measured by channel, campaign, and cohort.
- [ ] CAC inflation at scale is modeled, not assumed away.
- [ ] Activation and retention of paid users are measured, not only acquisition cost.
- [ ] Spend limits tied to learning and profitability are set and enforced.
- [ ] Creative and targeting are iterated based on measured performance.
- [ ] The attribution model in use and its biases are understood.
- [ ] Channel saturation and decay are monitored, with alternatives developed.
- [ ] Advertising, data, and platform-term implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
