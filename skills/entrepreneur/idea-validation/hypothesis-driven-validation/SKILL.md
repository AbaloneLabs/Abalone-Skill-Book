# Hypothesis-Driven Validation

A hypothesis is not a goal and not a feature idea. It is a specific, falsifiable prediction about the world that, if disproven, should change what the founder does next. Hypothesis-driven validation borrows the rigor of scientific testing and applies it to the messy, fast-moving context of a startup. The value is not academic purity; it is the ability to learn faster than the runway burns, and to avoid the comforting illusion that interest equals demand.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I write a good hypothesis", "what experiment should I run", "how do I make my validation falsifiable", or "how should an entrepreneur structure a lean test". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing vague, unfalsifiable statements that can never fail and therefore never teach.

## Core Rules

### Write Hypotheses As Falsifiable Predictions

A useful hypothesis predicts a specific observable outcome and states what would prove it wrong. Vague hopes cannot fail.

A strong hypothesis names:

- the customer segment;
- the behavior or belief being predicted;
- the measurable signal;
- the threshold that counts as success;
- the timeframe;
- the action taken if it passes, fails, or is inconclusive.

Compare "users will like our app" with "at least 40 percent of interviewed independent clinic owners will describe this scheduling pain unprompted and agree to a paid pilot within two weeks". The second can fail and can guide a decision.

### Separate Descriptive From Causal Hypotheses

Descriptive hypotheses ask whether something is true: does this segment have this problem? Causal hypotheses ask whether an action changes an outcome: does offering this feature increase activation? Confusing them produces experiments that measure the wrong thing.

Choose the hypothesis type that matches the current risk. Early, descriptive hypotheses about demand and problem are usually more urgent than causal hypotheses about feature effects.

### Pick The Metric Before The Test

Define the success metric and threshold before running the experiment, not after seeing results. Pre-registration prevents the human tendency to retroactively justify whatever happened.

For each hypothesis specify:

- the primary metric;
- the minimum sample or number of signals;
- the success threshold;
- the failure threshold;
- what counts as noise.

Avoid metrics that can be spun either way after the fact.

### Choose The Smallest Experiment That Can Falsify

Bigger experiments cost more runway and delay learning. Ask what is the cheapest way this hypothesis could be disproven.

Common lightweight designs:

- problem interviews scored by unprompted mention and commitment;
- landing page with a priced call to action;
- fake-door or smoke test with honest follow-up;
- concierge delivery to a handful of customers;
- preorder or deposit that risks real money;
- sales calls that attempt to close before building.

The experiment must expose the hypothesis to genuine disconfirmation, not merely collect encouragement.

### Define The Decision Tied To Each Hypothesis

A hypothesis without a linked decision is academic. Before running the test, state what the founder will do if it passes, fails, or is inconclusive.

Decisions might be: proceed to build, kill the idea, pivot the segment, raise the price, change the channel, run a follow-up test, or stop spending. If no decision changes based on the outcome, the hypothesis is not worth testing yet.

### Run Hypotheses In Risk Order

Sequence tests so the most lethal, most uncertain beliefs are examined first. It is wasteful to refine a feature hypothesis while the core demand hypothesis is still dark.

A reasonable early sequence: problem exists and is prioritized, the segment is reachable, willingness to pay exists, the channel is affordable, the solution is usable, the unit economics close.

### Document Results Honestly

Record what was predicted, what was observed, what was learned, and what changed. Honest documentation prevents selective memory and helps co-founders and future investors see the reasoning.

Include negative evidence. A trail of disproven hypotheses is a sign of disciplined learning, not failure.

### Recognize The Limits Of Hypothesis Testing

Some important questions cannot be cleanly falsified in a startup timeframe: long-term market evolution, founder endurance, category emergence, platform dependence. Do not force a false precision onto questions that are really commitments. This skill does not replace professional advice for hypotheses involving legal, regulatory, or financial liability.

## Common Traps

### Unfalsifiable Hypotheses

Statements like "customers will find value" or "the market is ready" cannot fail and therefore cannot teach. Force specificity.

### Moving The Threshold After The Result

Redefining success once data arrives destroys the learning value of the test and invites self-deception.

### Measuring Intent Instead Of Behavior

What people say they would do predicts real behavior poorly. Prefer commitments involving time, money, reputation, or data.

### Testing Too Many Variables At Once

When several changes happen simultaneously, you cannot attribute the outcome. Vary one risky belief per test where possible.

### Treating A Passed Test As Permanent Truth

A hypothesis that passes at small scale may fail at scale, in another segment, or over time. Treat results as provisional.

### Ignoring Inconclusive Results

Inconclusive is a real outcome. Pretending it was a pass, or quietly abandoning the question, wastes the experiment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Hypotheses are written as specific, falsifiable predictions with segment, signal, threshold, and timeframe.
- [ ] Descriptive and causal hypotheses are distinguished and sequenced appropriately.
- [ ] Success and failure thresholds are defined before the experiment runs.
- [ ] Each experiment is the smallest design capable of falsifying the hypothesis.
- [ ] Every hypothesis is tied to a concrete decision that will change based on the result.
- [ ] Hypotheses are sequenced by combined lethality and uncertainty.
- [ ] Results, including negative and inconclusive ones, are documented honestly.
- [ ] Questions that are genuinely commitments are not forced into false empirical tests.
- [ ] Experiments expose the belief to real disconfirmation, not mere encouragement.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a single passed hypothesis as proof of a viable business.
