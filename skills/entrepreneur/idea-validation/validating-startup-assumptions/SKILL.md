# Validating Startup Assumptions

A business plan is built on assumptions, but most founders never write them down. They carry beliefs about who the customer is, what the problem costs, how buyers decide, what the price should be, which channel works, and whether the economics close. When these beliefs are implicit, the founder optimizes the wrong things, polishes the product, and discovers a fatal flaw only after months of work. Assumption validation is the discipline of making beliefs visible, ranking them by danger, and testing the dangerous ones cheaply before they become expensive.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what assumptions should I test", "how do I know which beliefs are risky", "what should I validate before building", or "how should an entrepreneur de-risk a startup". The goal is to prevent the agent from letting assumptions stay invisible and from treating a list of assumptions as if it were evidence.

## Core Rules

### Make Assumptions Explicit Before Testing

You cannot test what you have not named. The first step is to write down every belief the venture depends on, across the whole business, not only the product.

Capture assumptions in categories:

- customer: who has the problem, how many, how reachable;
- problem: that the pain is real, frequent, and prioritized;
- value: that the proposed solution is meaningfully better than alternatives;
- behavior: that customers will change what they do today;
- economics: that they will pay a price that covers cost and acquisition;
- channel: that buyers can be reached at an acceptable cost;
- delivery: that the product can be built and supported at the assumed cost;
- regulatory: that the model is permitted in the relevant jurisdictions;
- dependency: that partners, suppliers, data, or platforms will cooperate;
- founder: that the team can learn, sell, and persist long enough.

Write each as a single testable statement, not a vague hope.

### Rank By Kill Risk, Not By Ease

Not all assumptions matter equally. Rank them by how much damage is done if they are wrong, and by how uncertain they are.

Ask of each assumption:

- if this is false, does the business die, stumble, or barely change;
- how confident am I today, and on what evidence;
- how cheaply can I reduce this uncertainty;
- does this assumption depend on another assumption being true first.

Prioritize assumptions that are both highly uncertain and highly lethal. A confident-but-lethal assumption still deserves a cheap check. An uncertain-but-harmless assumption can wait.

### Distinguish Leap-Of-Faith From Testable Fact

Some beliefs are genuinely testable through experiments; others are commitments you must simply make and live with. Do not pretend a values-driven bet is empirically validated.

Leap-of-faith assumptions often include:

- founder endurance and opportunity cost;
- long-term market direction;
- whether a category will emerge;
- whether a platform will remain open.

Testable assumptions include demand, price tolerance, channel cost, conversion, churn, build cost, and regulatory permission. Spend experiment effort on the testable set.

### Design The Cheapest Sufficient Test

A good test reduces uncertainty enough to make a decision, at the lowest credible cost. Over-building the test wastes runway; under-building it produces ambiguous results.

For each assumption define:

- what specific result would increase confidence;
- what result would decrease or kill confidence;
- what result is inconclusive and what happens then;
- the minimum sample, signal, or commitment required;
- how to avoid a false positive from politeness or novelty.

A landing page, a concierge service, a preorder, a paid pilot, or a set of sales calls can test more than a finished product.

### Separate Demand Assumptions From Solution Assumptions

Founders often conflate "people want this outcome" with "people want this product". These are different assumptions and fail for different reasons.

Test demand assumptions first: does the customer want the outcome enough to act or pay? Only then test solution assumptions: is this specific approach acceptable, usable, and better than alternatives. Building the solution before confirming demand inverts the risk.

### Track Confidence Over Time

Validation is not a single event. Keep a living record of each assumption, its current confidence level, the evidence behind it, and the next test planned.

This prevents two failures: forgetting which assumptions are still untested, and over-celebrating one positive signal while other lethal assumptions remain dark.

### Acknowledge Founder And Time Risk

Assumption testing consumes runway and founder energy. A plan that tests everything perfectly may run out of time before acting. Balance rigor against the real constraint that learning has a deadline. This skill does not replace professional legal, financial, or regulatory advice for assumptions that carry personal liability.

## Common Traps

### Treating A List Of Assumptions As Validation

Writing assumptions down is necessary but not sufficient. Until the dangerous ones are tested with real signal, the venture is still unvalidated.

### Testing The Easy Assumptions First

Founders often validate what is cheap to confirm while avoiding the scary lethal assumption, because disproving it would end the dream.

### Confirming What Was Already Believed

Tests designed to prove the founder right produce false comfort. Design tests that could plausibly disprove the belief.

### One Signal Treated As Proof

A single positive interview, sale, or press mention does not validate a category of assumption. Look for repetition, commitment, and behavior change.

### Ignoring Dependency Chains

An assumption can look safe in isolation but collapse because an upstream assumption it relied on is false. Trace dependencies before declaring confidence.

### Forgetting The Founder Assumption

The venture also depends on the founder's endurance, capital, and fit. Treating these as outside the model hides a common cause of failure.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Assumptions are written as explicit, testable statements across customer, problem, value, economics, channel, delivery, regulatory, dependency, and founder categories.
- [ ] Assumptions are ranked by combined uncertainty and kill risk, not by ease of testing.
- [ ] Leap-of-faith commitments are distinguished from empirically testable beliefs.
- [ ] Each high-priority assumption has a defined test with explicit pass, fail, and inconclusive criteria.
- [ ] Tests are the cheapest sufficient design, not over-built products.
- [ ] Demand assumptions are tested before solution assumptions.
- [ ] Confidence is tracked over time with evidence and next steps per assumption.
- [ ] Dependency chains between assumptions are traced, not assumed away.
- [ ] Founder runway, endurance, and fit are treated as real assumptions, not ignored.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids treating a written assumption list or a single positive signal as full validation.
