# Evidence Versus Enthusiasm

Enthusiasm is cheap, contagious, and almost worthless as a predictor of whether a business will succeed. Customers say nice things. Friends cheer. Mentors encourage. The press amplifies novelty. None of this is evidence that anyone will change behavior, pay, or stay. Founders who cannot tell the difference between enthusiasm and evidence build on sand, because encouragement feels like validation while teaching them nothing about risk. This skill is about calibrating how much weight a signal deserves.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "is this signal meaningful", "does this feedback prove demand", "how much should I trust this traction", or "how should an entrepreneur read early customer reactions". The goal is to prevent the agent from mistaking warmth for proof and from letting founder optimism reinterpret politeness as commitment.

## Core Rules

### Define What Counts As Evidence

Evidence is signal that costs the other party something and that predicts future behavior. Enthusiasm is signal that costs nothing and predicts little.

Strong evidence tends to involve:

- money spent, deposited, or contractually committed;
- time invested, such as a long onboarding or data migration;
- reputation risked, such as a referral or internal advocacy;
- habit changed, such as abandoning a current tool;
- access granted, such as data, contacts, or calendar;
- repeat behavior, not a single novelty reaction.

Weak evidence includes compliments, survey interest, nodding in interviews, social likes, waitlist signups without payment, and "I would totally use that".

### Weight Signal By Cost To The Signal-Giver

The more the customer sacrifices to give the signal, the more it means. A signed contract outweighs a verbal "sounds great". A non-refundable deposit outweighs an email of interest. A customer who reorganizes a workflow outweighs one who tries a demo once.

Ask of every positive signal: what did this person actually risk, spend, or change?

### Watch For Politeness And Social Pressure

People avoid disappointing founders, especially in person, especially when the founder is enthusiastic. Politeness masquerades as validation.

Counter it by:

- asking about past behavior rather than future intent;
- requesting a small commitment and observing follow-through;
- letting silence happen and seeing if the customer fills it with the problem;
- asking what is wrong with the idea, not only what is right;
- noting whether the customer describes the problem unprompted.

If the signal only appears when the founder leads, it is enthusiasm.

### Beware Novelty And Hawthorne Effects

Early users often engage because something is new, because they are being watched, or because the founder is personally attentive. This engagement fades when novelty wears off and the founder moves on.

Distinguish first-week curiosity from sustained use. Look for behavior that persists without prompting, reminders, or founder hand-holding.

### Separate Founder Optimism From External Signal

Founders are professionally optimistic. They re-read ambiguous signals favorably and remember compliments more than objections. This is useful for endurance but dangerous for learning.

Counteract by:

- writing down objections and disconfirming data explicitly;
- having a neutral party review interview notes;
- tracking the ratio of compliments to commitments;
- defining in advance what would count as a negative result.

### Demand Repetition And Pattern

A single enthusiastic customer can be an outlier. Evidence accumulates through repetition across independent customers who do not know each other and who behave consistently.

Look for:

- the same problem described in the same words;
- the same workaround already in use;
- the same budget and decision process;
- consistent willingness to commit.

One strong story is a hypothesis; a repeating pattern is evidence.

### Distinguish Paid Traction From Organic Demand

Customers acquired through heavy subsidy, discount, or founder relationship may not represent a sustainable market. Subsidized adoption proves that free or cheap things get used; it does not prove willingness to pay at a sustaining price.

Ask whether customers would return, renew, or refer at full price and full self-service.

### Treat Absence Of Objection As Absence Of Evidence

Silence is not endorsement. A customer who does not complain may simply not care enough to engage. Do not infer demand from non-resistance.

## Common Traps

### "Everyone I Talked To Loved It"

A small, friendly, non-random sample that was asked leading questions proves almost nothing about the market.

### Confusing Waitlist Size With Demand

Free signups cost the signer nothing and convert poorly. Count only signals with cost or commitment.

### Survivor Bias In Interviews

Founders interview people who agreed to talk, who are already sympathetic, and who remember the conversation favorably.

### Overweighting A Single Marquee Customer

One logo or one passionate user can dominate the founder's mental model while hiding that no one else behaves the same way.

### Treating Press Or Awards As Validation

Recognition measures novelty or narrative appeal, not purchasing behavior or retention.

### Reinterpreting Failure As Hidden Success

When customers do not buy, founders sometimes conclude the market is "just not ready yet" rather than that demand is weak.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Evidence is defined by cost and behavioral commitment, not by warmth or stated intent.
- [ ] Each positive signal is weighted by what the customer actually risked, spent, or changed.
- [ ] Interview and feedback methods counter politeness, leading questions, and novelty effects.
- [ ] Founder optimism is balanced by written objections, neutral review, and pre-defined negative criteria.
- [ ] Repetition and pattern across independent customers are required, not a single anecdote.
- [ ] Subsidized or relationship-driven traction is distinguished from organic full-price demand.
- [ ] Silence and non-objection are not treated as endorsement.
- [ ] Waitlists, likes, compliments, and press are classified as weak evidence.
- [ ] Disconfirming data is recorded rather than explained away.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids upgrading enthusiasm into proof of a viable business.
