# PMF Signals And Measurement

Product-market fit is the point where a product satisfies a real need well enough that customers use it, keep using it, and tell others. It is not a feeling and not a launch; it is a pattern of evidence. Measuring it badly is dangerous, because acting as if fit exists when it does not leads to premature scaling that burns runway and reputation, while missing fit that does exist leads to under-investment that cedes the market. The discipline is to know which signals actually indicate fit, to measure them honestly, and to resist declaring fit on weak evidence.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "do I have product-market fit", "how do I measure product-market fit", "what are the signs of PMF", or "how should an entrepreneur know they have fit". The goal is to prevent the agent from declaring fit on vanity metrics or early enthusiasm and from confusing usage with retention.

## Core Rules

### Treat Product-Market Fit As A Pattern, Not A Moment

Fit is not a single event or a single metric crossing a threshold. It is a sustained pattern: customers come, they stay, they derive value, and some bring others. Look for a consistent pattern across multiple signals over time, not a spike or a single survey score. A pattern that holds across weeks and cohorts is far stronger evidence than a momentary peak.

### Retention Is The Core Signal

The single most reliable signal of fit is retention: do the customers who start using the product keep using it, at a rate that does not decay to near zero? Plot retention curves by cohort. A flattening curve, where users stabilize at a meaningful level rather than churn to nothing, indicates the product holds value for a segment. A curve that decays to zero indicates the product is not holding anyone.

### Distinguish Active Usage From Registration

Sign-ups and downloads are not fit; they measure top-of-funnel interest. Fit is about whether those who arrive stay and engage. Measure active usage, defined by the action that reflects real value for the product, not merely logging in. A growing registered-user count with flat or falling active usage is a warning, not a victory.

### Use The "Very Disappointed" Survey Cautiously

The question, "how would you feel if you could no longer use this product," with a target of around 40 percent answering "very disappointed," is a useful signal, but it is not conclusive. It can be biased by who responds, by survey timing, and by enthusiasm that does not translate to retention. Use it alongside behavioral signals, not as a substitute for them.

### Segment The Fit Signal

Fit often exists for a specific segment before it exists broadly. Segment retention, usage, and survey responses by customer type, source, and use case. Finding a segment with strong fit, even while the overall base is weak, is valuable: it tells the venture where to focus. Averages hide segment-level fit.

### Watch For Organic Growth And Word Of Mouth

When customers start bringing other customers, through referral, word of mouth, or organic search, it is a strong signal that the product delivers value worth sharing. Organic growth that does not depend on paid acquisition suggests the product is satisfying a real need. Track the share of acquisition that is organic.

### Separate Fit From Free Or Discounted Usage

Usage driven by free access or heavy discounts does not prove willingness to pay, and therefore does not prove fit in a commercial sense. Test whether users value the product enough to pay, at a price that supports unit economics. Free usage is a weaker signal than paid retention.

### Avoid Declaring Fit On Early Enthusiasm

Early users are often unusually forgiving, curious, or aligned with the founder. Their enthusiasm can look like fit but fade as the product reaches less friendly users. Wait for the pattern to hold across cohorts that include less ideal customers before declaring fit.

### Distinguish Fit From A Feature, Not The Product

Sometimes users love one feature of a product while ignoring the rest. This may indicate fit for a narrower product than the one envisioned, not fit for the full vision. Investigate which specific value drives retention; it may point to a different product than the one being built.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Acting on fit claims carries stakes for scaling decisions and personal risk. Where claims inform fundraising or hiring, professional advice may be needed. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Declaring Fit On Vanity Metrics

Sign-ups and downloads measure interest, not fit.

### Retention Curve That Decays To Zero

A product that holds no one is not at fit, regardless of acquisition.

### Early-User Enthusiasm Mistaken For Fit

Friendly early users fade; fit must hold across less ideal cohorts.

### Averages Hiding Segment Fit

Overall weak retention can conceal a segment with strong fit.

### Free Usage Mistaken For Willingness To Pay

Discounted usage does not prove commercial fit.

### Loving One Feature, Ignoring The Rest

Fit for a feature may point to a different product than the vision.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Fit is assessed as a sustained pattern across signals, not a single moment or metric.
- [ ] Retention curves by cohort are the core evidence, and they flatten at a meaningful level.
- [ ] Active usage, defined by value-reflecting actions, is measured, not only registration.
- [ ] Survey signals are used alongside behavioral data, not as a substitute.
- [ ] Fit signals are segmented by customer type, source, and use case.
- [ ] Organic growth and word of mouth are tracked as supporting signals.
- [ ] Willingness to pay is tested, not assumed from free or discounted usage.
- [ ] Fit is not declared on early-user enthusiasm before less friendly cohorts are reached.
- [ ] The specific value driving retention is investigated, in case it points to a different product.
- [ ] Scaling decisions based on fit claims are flagged for professional advice where relevant.
