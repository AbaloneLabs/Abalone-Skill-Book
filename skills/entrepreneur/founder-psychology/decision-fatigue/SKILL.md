# Decision Fatigue

Decision fatigue is the deterioration of decision quality that results from making many decisions over time, as cognitive resources deplete with each choice, and it is especially dangerous for founders because the role demands a volume and stakes of decisions that few other roles face. Unlike burnout, which is a chronic condition, decision fatigue is an acute depletion that accumulates within a day and across periods of high decision density, and its effect is to push the founder toward worse decisions precisely on the consequential choices that arrive late in the day or after a long sequence. The judgment problem is recognizing that decision quality is a finite resource, building systems that conserve it for what matters, and structuring the founder's day and processes so that consequential decisions receive full cognitive capacity rather than depleted reserves. Agents tend to fail by treating all decisions as equal, by recommending more decisions rather than fewer, and by ignoring the sequencing that determines whether important choices get fresh or fatigued judgment.

Use this skill when recognizing, preventing, or advising on decision fatigue. The goal is decisions made with full capacity on what matters, not a high volume of decisions made with degrading quality.

## Core Rules

### Recognize Decision Quality As A Finite Resource

Decision-making draws on finite cognitive resources, and each decision depletes them, so that decision quality declines over a sequence of choices. This is not a matter of willpower or dedication; it is a physiological reality that cannot be overcome by effort.

Recognize by:

- understanding that the brain tires from deciding, not just from thinking;
- accepting that more decisions mean lower average quality, regardless of intention;
- tracking when your own decision quality declines, often late in the day or after dense sequences;
- treating decision capacity as a resource to be managed, not an unlimited faucet.

A founder who treats decisions as free produces a high volume of low-quality decisions. Recognize the finitude and manage it.

### Distinguish Decision Fatigue From Burnout

Decision fatigue and burnout are related but distinct, and confusing them leads to wrong interventions. Decision fatigue is acute depletion from decision volume; burnout is chronic exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced efficacy from prolonged stress.

Distinguish by:

- decision fatigue accumulates within a day or period and recovers with rest and reduced decisions;
- burnout is chronic and persists despite rest, involving cynicism and detachment;
- decision fatigue affects decision quality specifically; burnout affects overall function;
- decision fatigue is managed by reducing and sequencing decisions; burnout requires deeper intervention.

A founder with decision fatigue needs fewer and better-timed decisions; one with burnout needs structural and recovery intervention. Diagnose correctly.

### Reduce The Number Of Low-Value Decisions

Most decisions a founder faces are low-value and do not warrant cognitive expenditure. Reducing these, through defaults, delegation, and systems, conserves decision capacity for what matters.

Reduce by:

- establishing defaults and policies that make routine decisions automatic;
- delegating decisions to the people closest to the work, with clear authority;
- eliminating decisions that do not need to be made at all;
- building systems and processes that handle recurring choices without founder involvement.

Every low-value decision a founder makes steals capacity from a high-value one. Eliminate and delegate ruthlessly.

### Prioritize And Sequence Consequential Decisions

Because decision quality declines over a sequence, the order in which decisions are made matters. Consequential decisions should be made early, with fresh capacity, and low-value decisions deferred or eliminated.

Prioritize and sequence by:

- making the most consequential decisions early in the day, when capacity is fresh;
- tackling one major decision at a time, rather than stacking many;
- deferring reversible or low-stakes decisions to lower-capacity periods;
- avoiding important decisions late in the day or after dense decision sequences.

A consequential decision made at the end of a long day is made with depleted judgment. Sequence the important ones first.

### Build Systems That Handle Recurring Decisions

Many founder decisions are recurring, and building systems, policies, or frameworks that handle them removes them from the founder's cognitive load. A system that makes a decision once, through a policy, is more scalable than deciding each time.

Build systems by:

- creating policies for recurring decisions, such as hiring criteria or spending thresholds;
- using frameworks that guide decisions consistently without founder involvement;
- documenting decision criteria so others can decide within them;
- reviewing and refining the systems rather than deciding each instance.

A policy that handles a class of decisions conserves capacity forever after. Build systems for what recurs.

### Delegate Decisions To The Lowest Competent Level

Centralizing decisions in the founder creates both a bottleneck and a fatigue source. Delegating to the lowest competent level, with clear authority and context, improves both speed and decision capacity.

Delegate by:

- pushing decisions to the people closest to the relevant information;
- providing clear authority and decision rights so delegation is real;
- giving context and criteria so delegated decisions align with the company's goals;
- resisting the urge to reverse or second-guess delegated decisions, which undermines delegation.

Delegation is not abdication; it is structured empowerment that conserves the founder's capacity and develops the team. Push decisions down.

### Recognize The Signals Of Fatigue And Pause

Decision fatigue produces recognizable signals, and recognizing them allows the founder to pause rather than make a depleted decision. Pushing through fatigue produces decisions that are later regretted.

Recognize signals by:

- noticing impulsivity, irritability, or defaulting to the easiest option;
- recognizing when analysis is giving way to fatigue-driven shortcuts;
- pausing before consequential decisions made in a depleted state;
- deferring decisions when fatigue signals appear, where possible.

A decision deferred to a fresh state is often better than one made under fatigue. Recognize the signals and pause.

### Protect Recovery To Restore Decision Capacity

Decision capacity recovers through rest, sleep, and disconnection, and protecting recovery is essential to maintaining decision quality over time. A founder who does not recover degrades decision quality chronically.

Protect recovery by:

- protecting sleep as a non-negotiable, because it restores cognitive function;
- taking genuine breaks and disconnection that allow mental recovery;
- avoiding decision density during periods of low capacity;
- recognizing that recovery is an investment in decision quality, not a luxury.

A rested founder makes better decisions than an exhausted one. Protect recovery as a strategic practice.

## Common Traps

### Treating Decisions As Free

More decisions mean lower quality. Manage decision capacity as finite.

### Confusing Fatigue With Burnout

Decision fatigue is acute; burnout is chronic. Diagnose and intervene correctly.

### Making Every Decision Oneself

Low-value decisions steal capacity from high-value ones. Eliminate and delegate.

### Consequential Decisions Late In The Day

Important choices made with depleted judgment are worse. Sequence them early.

### Deciding Recurring Issues Each Time

Systems and policies handle classes of decisions. Build them for what recurs.

### Centralizing Decisions

Centralization creates bottlenecks and fatigue. Delegate to the lowest competent level.

### Pushing Through Fatigue Signals

Fatigue-driven decisions are later regretted. Pause and defer when depleted.

### Neglecting Recovery

Without rest, decision quality degrades chronically. Protect recovery.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Decision quality is recognized as a finite resource that depletes with use, not an unlimited faucet.
- [ ] Decision fatigue is distinguished from burnout, with appropriate interventions for each.
- [ ] Low-value decisions are reduced through defaults, delegation, and elimination.
- [ ] Consequential decisions are sequenced early in the day and in low-density periods.
- [ ] Systems and policies handle recurring decisions, removing them from the founder's load.
- [ ] Decisions are delegated to the lowest competent level, with clear authority and context.
- [ ] Signals of fatigue, impulsivity, shortcuts, are recognized and trigger pausing or deferring.
- [ ] Recovery, sleep, breaks, disconnection, is protected to restore decision capacity.
- [ ] The founder's day is structured to conserve capacity for what matters most.
- [ ] The volume of founder-made decisions is deliberately lower than the volume of decisions made.
