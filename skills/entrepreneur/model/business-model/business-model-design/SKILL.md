# Business Model Design

A business model explains how a venture creates value, delivers it, captures enough value to survive, and repeats the process. A product can be loved but economically weak. A business can generate revenue but lose money on every customer. A founder must understand the economic mechanism before scaling effort.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should be considered in a business model", "how should this startup make money", "is this revenue model viable", or "what should an entrepreneur check before scaling". The goal is to prevent the agent from describing only the product and ignoring acquisition, pricing, costs, margin, retention, and operational complexity.

## Core Rules

### Map Value Creation And Value Capture

First state what value is created and who captures it.

Ask:

- What problem is solved?
- Who benefits directly?
- Who pays?
- Why would they pay now?
- What outcome justifies the price?
- Does the business capture value through sales, subscription, usage, transaction fees, services, marketplace take rate, licensing, advertising, data, financing, or another model?

If the user and payer differ, the model must explain why the payer benefits enough to fund the user experience.

### Understand The Revenue Engine

Revenue should be described by mechanism, not only by optimism.

Define:

- pricing unit;
- price level;
- billing frequency;
- sales cycle;
- contract length;
- expansion path;
- churn risk;
- payment timing;
- refunds or chargebacks;
- seasonality;
- concentration by customer or channel.

Do not assume revenue grows smoothly. Early sales can be founder-led, custom, discounted, or non-repeatable.

### Calculate Unit Economics Early

A business model needs approximate unit economics even before perfect data exists.

Check:

- gross margin;
- cost to serve;
- customer acquisition cost;
- payback period;
- lifetime value assumptions;
- retention;
- support cost;
- onboarding cost;
- payment processing;
- delivery, hosting, inventory, labor, or fulfillment costs;
- returns, warranties, fraud, or bad debt.

Unit economics do not need false precision. They need honest assumptions and sensitivity analysis.

### Distinguish Fixed, Variable, And Step Costs

Some costs scale with each customer or order. Others stay fixed until a threshold, then jump. The business model should account for both.

Examples:

- software hosting may scale gradually;
- customer support may require another hire after volume grows;
- warehouses and equipment create step costs;
- compliance and insurance may rise with geography or industry;
- enterprise sales may need account management;
- marketplace liquidity may require subsidies.

Ignoring step costs can make a model appear profitable until it grows.

### Design Acquisition And Distribution

Distribution is part of the business model. A product that cannot reach customers affordably is not a strong business.

Define:

- acquisition channels;
- why those channels are accessible;
- expected conversion path;
- sales motion;
- referral or word-of-mouth mechanics;
- partner dependence;
- paid marketing assumptions;
- channel conflict;
- brand and trust requirements.

Do not write "social media", "SEO", or "partnerships" as a plan without explaining how they produce customers at acceptable cost.

### Consider Retention And Repeat Behavior

A business can acquire customers and still fail if they do not return, renew, reorder, or expand.

Ask:

- Why does the customer keep using or buying?
- What habit, integration, data, relationship, inventory, or outcome creates retention?
- What causes churn?
- How quickly does value appear?
- Does the customer need ongoing support?
- Is repeat use natural or forced?

Retention quality affects pricing, acquisition spend, valuation, and operational load.

### Evaluate Strategic Defensibility

Defensibility is not only patents or secrecy. It may come from brand, network effects, switching costs, data advantages, distribution, operational excellence, regulatory licenses, supplier relationships, community, or speed of learning.

Ask what prevents another capable competitor from copying the visible product and taking the customers. If the answer is "we will execute better", specify where that execution advantage comes from.

## Common Traps

### Revenue Without Profitability

Revenue growth can hide bad margins, expensive acquisition, high churn, or heavy operations. Check the path to sustainable economics.

### Free Users Without A Payer

Free adoption may create value, but the model must eventually identify who pays and why.

### Marketplace Chicken-And-Egg

Marketplaces need supply and demand. The model should explain which side starts first, what incentive solves liquidity, and how quality is controlled.

### Underpricing To Avoid Sales Difficulty

Low pricing may reduce objection but can destroy margin, positioning, and ability to support customers. Pricing should reflect value, cost, and acquisition model.

### Ignoring Working Capital

Inventory, receivables, supplier terms, refunds, and payment timing can make a profitable model cash-hungry.

### Assuming Scale Fixes Everything

Scale can improve economics, but it can also reveal support, fraud, quality, hiring, infrastructure, or compliance problems.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The model clearly states who uses, who pays, and why payment is justified.
- [ ] Revenue mechanism, pricing unit, billing frequency, sales cycle, and payment timing are explicit.
- [ ] Unit economics include gross margin, cost to serve, acquisition cost, retention, support, and payback assumptions.
- [ ] Fixed, variable, and step costs are distinguished.
- [ ] Distribution channels and customer acquisition path are specific and plausible.
- [ ] Retention, repeat behavior, churn causes, and expansion paths are considered.
- [ ] Working capital, refunds, payment processing, inventory, receivables, or fulfillment timing are reviewed where relevant.
- [ ] Defensibility is explained beyond generic "better execution".
- [ ] Sensitivity to pricing, conversion, churn, margin, and acquisition cost is considered.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids calling the venture viable without a credible value capture mechanism.
