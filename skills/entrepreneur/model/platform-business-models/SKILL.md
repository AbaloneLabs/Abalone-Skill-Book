# Platform Business Models

A platform business does not produce the value it delivers; it enables others to produce and exchange it, and that single difference changes almost everything about how the business must be designed, launched, and defended. A linear business makes a product and sells it; a platform business connects producers and consumers and must get both sides on board before either finds value. This creates the famous chicken-and-egg problem: producers will not join without consumers, and consumers will not join without producers. It also creates the potential for network effects, where value compounds as more participants join, which is the strategic prize that makes platforms attractive and the reason so many fail trying. The judgment problem is recognizing when a platform model is actually appropriate, solving the cold-start problem, designing governance that keeps the platform valuable, and building defensibility through network effects rather than features. Agents tend to fail by calling any multi-sided business a platform, by underestimating the cold-start problem, and by assuming network effects will appear automatically.

Use this skill when designing, evaluating, or investing in a platform business: marketplaces, ecosystems, developer platforms, or any model where the business mediates value exchange between participants. The goal is a clear-eyed assessment of whether a platform model fits and how to make it work.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish A Platform From A Linear Product Business

The first and most important judgment is whether the business is genuinely a platform or a linear product described in platform language. The distinction determines strategy, metrics, and risks.

A platform business:

- enables third parties to create value that the platform mediates;
- derives its value from interactions between participants, not from its own production;
- faces multi-sided demand and must balance supply and demand;
- can benefit from network effects as participation grows.

A linear business:

- produces the product or service it sells;
- faces one-sided demand, its customers;
- competes on the quality and efficiency of its own production;
- does not inherently benefit from network effects.

Calling a product business a platform does not make it one, and applying platform strategy to a linear business wastes effort and misleads investors.

### Solve The Chicken-And-Egg Cold-Start Problem

Every platform faces the cold-start problem: the platform has no value to one side without the other. How this is solved determines whether the platform ever reaches viability. This is the single hardest problem in platform businesses and the most common point of failure.

Cold-start strategies include:

- subsidizing one side, often the side whose participation creates value for the other;
- starting with a single, narrow use case or geography to concentrate both sides;
- bringing one side on board first through a standalone tool or content that does not require the other;
- seeding supply by initially acting as the producer before opening to third parties;
- leveraging an existing network, audience, or asset to bootstrap one side.

A platform launch without a credible cold-start strategy will stall, because neither side has reason to join an empty platform. The strategy must be designed, not hoped for.

### Identify Which Side To Subsidize And Which To Monetize

Platforms typically subsidize the side whose participation generates the most value for the other side, and monetize the side that captures that value and has willingness to pay. Getting this backwards strangles the platform.

Decide subsidy and monetization by:

- identifying which side is more price-sensitive and which has more willingness to pay;
- subsidizing the side whose growth most increases value for the other, often supply;
- recognizing that the monetizing side must perceive enough value to pay, which requires the subsidized side to be present;
- being willing to lose money on one side deliberately to grow the network.

A platform that tries to monetize both sides equally from day one often fails to reach critical mass on either.

### Design For Network Effects, But Do Not Assume Them

Network effects, where each new participant increases value for existing ones, are the strategic prize of platforms, but they are not automatic. They depend on the structure of the platform and the nature of the interactions.

Foster network effects by:

- designing interactions that genuinely improve with more participants, such as more matches, more content, more liquidity;
- reducing friction in finding and transacting between participants;
- encouraging the behaviors that create value for others, such as reviews, contributions, and reputation;
- recognizing whether effects are local, same geography or category, or global.

Not every platform benefits from network effects. Some are aggregators with scale advantages but no compounding network value. Be honest about which you are building.

### Design Governance, Rules, And Trust Mechanisms

A platform without governance degrades, because participants will pursue their own interests, sometimes at the expense of others and the platform. Spam, fraud, low-quality supply, and abuse destroy value faster than growth can create it.

Govern by:

- setting clear rules for participation and behavior on both sides;
- building trust mechanisms, such as reviews, verification, ratings, and dispute resolution;
- monitoring for and removing bad actors and low-quality contributions;
- balancing openness, which grows the network, with quality control, which preserves value.

Governance is not a feature added later; it is core to the platform's long-term value. Platforms that neglect it lose their best participants to the consequences of the worst.

### Manage Multi-Homing And Lock-In

Participants can usually use multiple platforms, called multi-homing, and high multi-homing weakens network effects and pricing power. Platforms must decide how to reduce multi-homing or accept it.

Consider:

- whether participants can easily use competing platforms simultaneously;
- what creates switching costs or lock-in, such as reputation, data, or integration;
- whether lock-in is earned through value or imposed through friction, with very different consequences;
- the risk that aggressive lock-in drives participants to alternatives.

Earned lock-in, through accumulated value and integration, sustains the platform. Imposed lock-in breeds resentment and invites disruption.

### Watch For Disintermediation

Platforms face disintermediation, where participants who found each other through the platform transact directly to avoid fees. This is a structural risk that depends on the nature of the transaction.

Reduce disintermediation by:

- providing ongoing value that justifies the platform's role, such as trust, payments, or tools;
- ensuring the fee is proportionate to the value the platform adds;
- recognizing that high-value, repeat transactions are most at risk of going direct;
- designing the platform to remain essential throughout the relationship, not just at discovery.

A platform that is only useful for the first connection will be bypassed thereafter. Remain essential throughout the transaction lifecycle.

### Assess Whether The Market Can Support A Platform

Not every market can support a viable platform, even with good execution. Market structure determines whether a platform can reach liquidity and defend itself.

Assess market viability by:

- whether the market is fragmented enough on both sides that participants need a mediator;
- whether transaction friction is high enough that a platform adds real value;
- whether the market is large enough to support network effects and a business;
- whether network effects are strong enough to create a defensible winner, or whether the market stays fragmented.

A market that is too consolidated, too small, or too low-friction may not support a platform, however well executed. Assess the market before committing to the model.

## Common Traps

### Calling A Product Business A Platform

Multi-sided does not mean platform. Distinguish genuine platforms from linear businesses in platform clothing.

### No Credible Cold-Start Strategy

An empty platform attracts no one. Design how to solve chicken-and-egg before launch.

### Monetizing Both Sides Equally Early

Charging both sides from day one often prevents reaching critical mass. Subsidize strategically.

### Assuming Network Effects Will Appear

Network effects depend on design and market structure. Engineer for them; do not assume them.

### Neglecting Governance And Trust

Without rules and trust mechanisms, bad actors degrade the platform faster than growth builds it.

### Ignoring Multi-Homing

If participants easily use competitors, network effects and pricing power weaken. Address lock-in honestly.

### Vulnerability To Disintermediation

Platforms useful only at discovery get bypassed for repeat transactions. Stay essential throughout.

### Assuming Any Market Supports A Platform

Market structure determines platform viability. Assess fragmentation, friction, and size before committing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The business is genuinely a platform mediating third-party value, not a linear product in platform language.
- [ ] A credible cold-start strategy solves the chicken-and-egg problem for both sides.
- [ ] The side to subsidize and the side to monetize are chosen based on value generation and willingness to pay.
- [ ] Network effects are designed for and honestly assessed, not assumed to appear automatically.
- [ ] Governance, rules, trust mechanisms, and quality control are core to the design, not afterthoughts.
- [ ] Multi-homing risk is assessed, and lock-in is earned through value rather than imposed through friction.
- [ ] Disintermediation risk is addressed by remaining essential throughout the transaction lifecycle.
- [ ] Market structure, fragmentation, friction, and size are assessed for platform viability.
- [ ] The platform's defensibility rests on network effects and accumulated value, not on features alone.
- [ ] The model is honest about whether network effects are local or global and strong enough to win.
