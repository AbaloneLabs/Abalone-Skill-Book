# MVP Definition And Scope

A minimum viable product is the smallest thing that lets the venture learn whether its riskiest assumption is true. Its purpose is not to ship a small version of the final product; it is to test a belief with the least effort that produces credible evidence. The two failure modes are opposites: building too much, which wastes time and money testing things that did not need testing, and building too little, which produces evidence too weak to decide. Good MVP scoping identifies the single riskiest assumption, defines what evidence would confirm or refute it, and builds only what is needed to generate that evidence.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should my MVP include", "how do I scope my MVP", "what is a minimum viable product", or "how should an entrepreneur define their MVP". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating the MVP as a trimmed-down full product and from including features that do not serve a specific learning goal.

## Core Rules

### Define The MVP By The Assumption It Tests

An MVP is not defined by features; it is defined by the assumption it is built to test. Start by identifying the riskiest untested assumption, usually about demand or value: will the target customer pay for a solution to this problem? The MVP is then the smallest thing that can generate evidence about that assumption. If you cannot state the assumption the MVP tests, the scope is wrong.

### Distinguish Minimal From Incomplete

Minimal means no more than is needed to produce credible evidence. Incomplete means missing something required for the evidence to be meaningful. An MVP that is incomplete produces noise; an MVP that is not minimal wastes effort. The discipline is to remove everything that does not serve the learning goal while keeping everything that does.

### Identify The Riskiest Assumption, Not The Easiest One

Founders often test the assumptions they can easily test rather than the ones that matter most. The MVP should target the assumption whose failure would kill or reshape the venture: usually that customers want the solution enough to use or pay for it. Testing easy assumptions first delays confronting the risky one and can produce false confidence.

### Define The Evidence The MVP Must Produce

Before building, define what evidence would confirm or refute the assumption: a number of sign-ups, a percentage that activate, a willingness to pay, a retention rate. Without a defined evidence standard, the MVP produces ambiguous results that founders interpret optimistically. Define the bar for evidence in advance.

### Separate Learning MVPs From Selling MVPs

Some MVPs exist to learn whether the problem is real; others exist to learn whether the solution and business model work. Confusing the two leads to scope errors. A learning MVP can be a landing page, a concierge service, or a prototype; a selling MVP must actually deliver value and transact. Be clear which kind is being built and scope accordingly.

### Resist Building The Full Product In Disguise

The temptation to add "just one more feature" turns an MVP into a full product delivered late. Every feature should be justified by the learning it enables. If a feature does not change the evidence the MVP produces, it does not belong in the MVP. Defer it to a later version.

### Choose The Cheapest Form That Produces Credible Evidence

MVPs need not be software. A landing page, a manual service, a video, a fake door test, or a spreadsheet can test an assumption at far lower cost than building product. Choose the cheapest form that produces evidence credible enough to decide. Credibility matters: evidence the founder will rationalize away is not worth gathering.

### Plan The Decision After The MVP

An MVP without a planned decision is an experiment without a purpose. Before building, state what the venture will do if the evidence confirms the assumption, and what it will do if it refutes it. If both outcomes lead to the same action, the MVP is not needed. The decision must be real and committed in advance.

### Time-Box The MVP

MVPs expand to fill available time. Set a fixed time box for building and testing the MVP, and ship whatever is ready when the box ends. A time box forces scope discipline and prevents the MVP from becoming a long build that delays learning.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

MVP decisions carry stakes for venture direction and personal risk. Where the MVP involves customer data, payments, or regulatory exposure, professional advice may be needed. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### MVP As Trimmed-Down Full Product

Including features that do not serve a learning goal wastes effort and delays evidence.

### Testing The Easiest Assumption

Avoiding the riskiest assumption produces false confidence and delays the real test.

### Ambiguous Evidence Standards

Without a defined bar, founders interpret MVP results optimistically.

### Building When A Cheaper Form Would Suffice

Software is often overkill for testing an assumption a landing page or manual service could test.

### No Decision Planned

An MVP whose outcomes lead to the same action is an experiment without purpose.

### Scope Creep

"Just one more feature" turns an MVP into a late, full product.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The MVP is defined by the specific assumption it is built to test.
- [ ] The scope is minimal but not incomplete: everything needed for credible evidence is included.
- [ ] The MVP targets the riskiest assumption, not the easiest one to test.
- [ ] The evidence standard that would confirm or refute the assumption is defined in advance.
- [ ] The MVP type, learning versus selling, is clear and scope matches it.
- [ ] Every feature is justified by the learning it enables; non-learning features are deferred.
- [ ] The cheapest credible form of MVP is chosen, not necessarily software.
- [ ] The decision after the MVP is planned and committed for both confirm and refute outcomes.
- [ ] The MVP is time-boxed to force scope discipline.
- [ ] Data, payment, and regulatory exposures are flagged for professional advice.
