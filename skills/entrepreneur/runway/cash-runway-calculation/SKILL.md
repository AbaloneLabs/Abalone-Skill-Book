# Cash Runway Calculation

Cash runway is how many months a venture can survive before it runs out of money, given current cash and expected net cash burn. It is one of the most important numbers a founder tracks, because running out of cash ends the venture regardless of how good the strategy is. Yet runway is frequently miscalculated: founders use accounting profit instead of cash, ignore the timing of when money arrives and leaves, omit personal financial needs, and plan only for the base case. An honest runway calculation forces these factors into the open and reveals how much real time the venture has to reach its next milestone.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how much runway do I have", "how do I calculate runway", "how long will my cash last", or "how should an entrepreneur figure out their runway". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a single optimistic number and from confusing accounting profit with cash survival.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Runway From Cash, Not From Profit

Runway is a cash concept. A venture can be profitable on paper and still run out of cash because customers pay late, inventory is bought early, or growth consumes working capital. Calculate runway from the cash balance and the expected net cash outflow per month, not from the income statement.

Track actual cash in the bank and realistic monthly net cash burn.

### Model Cash Timing, Not Only Monthly Average

Money does not arrive and leave evenly. Some expenses are paid upfront, some revenue arrives late, and some costs are lumpy. Model the month-by-month cash position, not just an average burn, to catch the months where cash dips lowest even if the average looks fine.

Include:

- when customers pay versus when revenue is recognized;
- when inventory, deposits, and equipment must be paid for;
- payment processor holds and delays;
- tax payment dates;
- loan and debt service dates;
- payroll cycles and bonuses.

### Build Base, Downside, And Survival Cases

A single runway number is fragile. Build at least three scenarios.

- base case: realistic expected revenue and spend;
- downside case: slower revenue, higher costs, delayed milestones;
- survival case: minimum viable spend if assumptions fail badly.

For each, compute the months of runway remaining and the decision point at which the venture must raise, cut, pivot, or stop.

### Separate Fixed Commitments From Flexible Spend

Some spending is locked in, leases, payroll, contracts, debt, and subscriptions; some is flexible, marketing, contractors, events, and discretionary tools. Runway calculations should distinguish these, because flexible spend is what can be cut to extend runway when revenue disappoints.

Know how much burn is committed versus controllable.

### Include Founder Personal Runway

Business runway and personal runway are linked. A founder who cannot cover personal living expenses will be forced into rushed decisions or will leave the venture before it has time to succeed. Include the founder's personal financial needs, including living expenses, insurance, and obligations, in the overall runway picture.

Do not treat founder time as free if the founder has real personal financial constraints.

### Account For Receivables And Working Capital

Revenue that is earned but not yet collected does not pay bills. Inventory bought but not yet sold ties up cash. Refunds, chargebacks, and bad debt reduce realized cash. Runway must account for the working capital the business consumes as it grows, because growth often accelerates cash burn even when it is profitable on paper.

### Plan The Milestone Runway Must Reach

Runway is not valuable in itself; it is the time bought to reach a milestone that justifies more funding or sustainability. Define the milestone the runway must buy: validating demand, reaching break-even, proving repeatable acquisition, closing a key partnership. If runway does not map to a milestone, spending drifts.

### Revisit Runway As Conditions Change

Runway is not static. Revenue, costs, commitments, and personal needs all change. Recalculate runway regularly, especially after major changes in spend, revenue, or plans, so decisions are based on current reality rather than a stale estimate.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Runway calculations carry high stakes: errors can end the venture or trap the founder in personal financial risk. Tax, debt, and commitment implications may require professional advice. This skill does not replace professional financial or accounting counsel.

## Common Traps

### Profit Mistaken For Cash

Accounting profit hides cash timing problems that determine survival.

### Single Optimistic Number

One base-case runway figure ignores the downside and survival cases that actually govern risk.

### Ignoring Working Capital

Growth can accelerate cash burn even in a profitable-on-paper business.

### Omitting Founder Personal Needs

A founder who cannot cover living costs will be forced into decisions that harm the venture.

### Stale Estimate

A runway number computed once and never revisited drifts from reality.

### Runway Without A Milestone

Time bought without a milestone to reach is spending without direction.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Runway is calculated from cash balance and net cash burn, not from accounting profit.
- [ ] Month-by-month cash timing is modeled, including collections, payments, inventory, and taxes.
- [ ] Base, downside, and survival cases are built, each with a decision point.
- [ ] Fixed commitments are distinguished from flexible, cuttable spend.
- [ ] Founder personal runway and living expenses are included in the picture.
- [ ] Receivables, working capital, refunds, and bad debt are accounted for.
- [ ] The milestone the runway must buy is explicitly defined.
- [ ] Runway is recalculated regularly as conditions change.
- [ ] Tax, debt, and commitment implications are considered.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a single optimistic runway figure as the real survival horizon.
