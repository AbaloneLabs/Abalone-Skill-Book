# Runway Extension Strategies

When runway shortens, whether because revenue is slower than expected or costs are higher, a founder must extend it. Extension is not a single lever; it is a combination of cutting costs, accelerating or restructuring revenue, deferring commitments, negotiating terms, and sometimes raising capital. Each lever has tradeoffs: cutting too deep can cripple delivery, deferring can create future cliffs, and raising under pressure is expensive. The discipline is choosing the combination that extends runway enough to reach the next milestone without damaging the venture's ability to succeed.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I extend my runway", "what can I cut to make cash last", "how do I buy more time", or "how should an entrepreneur make their money last longer". The goal is to prevent the agent from reaching for a single lever, usually fundraising, and from cutting so deep that the venture cannot deliver.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose Why Runway Is Short Before Acting

Runway shortens for different reasons, and the right response depends on the cause. Revenue slower than expected suggests a demand or sales problem. Costs higher than expected suggest an operational or commitment problem. A milestone slipping suggests a planning or execution problem. Diagnose the cause before choosing levers, because extending runway without fixing the cause only delays the same failure.

### Rank Levers By Reversibility And Damage

Extension levers differ in how reversible they are and how much they damage the venture. Prefer reversible, low-damage levers first.

Typical order:

- cut discretionary and cosmetic spend;
- defer non-critical projects and purchases;
- negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers;
- accelerate collection of receivables;
- restructure or defer fixed commitments;
- reduce variable costs tied to growth experiments;
- reduce team or compensation, with care;
- raise capital or take loans as a last resort under pressure.

Each step up this list is more damaging and harder to reverse.

### Cut Cosmetic And Discretionary Spend First

The first cuts should be spend that produces neither learning nor delivery: vanity marketing, unnecessary tools, premium offices, non-essential travel, and projects not tied to the current milestone. These cuts extend runway with little damage to the venture's ability to succeed.

### Defer Before Cutting Capacity

Where possible, defer rather than destroy. Deferring a project preserves the team and the option to resume; cutting a team member removes capacity that is expensive to rebuild. Defer non-critical roadmap, events, and purchases before reducing the people who deliver the core.

### Negotiate Terms To Shift Cash Timing

Runway can be extended without cutting spend by shifting when cash leaves. Negotiate longer payment terms with suppliers, shorter terms with customers, payment plans for large obligations, and grace periods on contracts. These shifts extend runway without reducing the underlying commitments, though they may store up future pressure.

### Accelerate Revenue Carefully

Bringing revenue forward can extend runway, but the methods matter. Offering steep discounts to close deals faster can damage pricing and unit economics. Prioritizing faster-closing segments or prepayment options is usually safer than discounting. Ensure revenue acceleration does not mortgage future economics to buy present time.

### Preserve Core Delivery Capacity

Cuts that impair the venture's ability to deliver or learn defeat the purpose of extension. Protect the capacity needed to reach the next milestone: the core product team, the active acquisition channel, and the delivery function. Cutting these extends runway into a venture that can no longer succeed.

### Raise Capital As A Last Resort Under Pressure

Raising capital under runway pressure is expensive and disempowering. Investors sense urgency, valuations suffer, and terms worsen. Treat fundraising as a runway extension lever of last resort, used only when other levers are exhausted and the milestone is within reach. Fundraising from strength, before pressure, is always preferable.

### Plan The Milestone The Extension Must Reach

Extension is only valuable if it buys time to reach a milestone that changes the venture's position: proving demand, reaching break-even, closing a key deal. Define the milestone and confirm the extension is sufficient to reach it. Extending runway without a milestone is postponing the same failure.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Runway extension decisions carry high stakes for venture survival, team welfare, and founder personal risk. Employment, contract, debt, and tax implications may require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Reaching For Fundraising First

Raising under pressure is expensive and weakens the founder's position.

### Cutting So Deep The Venture Cannot Deliver

Cuts that impair core capacity extend runway into a venture that can no longer succeed.

### Extending Without Fixing The Cause

Buying time without diagnosing why runway shortened only delays the same failure.

### Discounting To Accelerate Revenue

Steep discounts damage pricing and unit economics to buy present time.

### Ignoring Reversibility

Destroying capacity that is expensive to rebuild, when deferral would have sufficed.

### Extension Without A Milestone

Buying time without a milestone to reach postpones failure rather than preventing it.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The cause of the runway shortfall is diagnosed before levers are chosen.
- [ ] Levers are ranked by reversibility and damage, with low-damage cuts first.
- [ ] Cosmetic and discretionary spend is cut before capacity.
- [ ] Deferral is preferred over destruction of capacity where possible.
- [ ] Payment terms are negotiated to shift cash timing where feasible.
- [ ] Revenue acceleration avoids damaging pricing and unit economics.
- [ ] Core delivery and learning capacity is protected through the cuts.
- [ ] Fundraising is treated as a last resort under pressure, not a first lever.
- [ ] The milestone the extension must reach is defined and confirmed reachable.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a single lever or cuts so deep the venture cannot deliver.
