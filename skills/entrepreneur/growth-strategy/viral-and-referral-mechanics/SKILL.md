# Viral And Referral Mechanics

Viral and referral growth happens when existing users bring in new users, creating a loop where acquisition compounds without proportional spending. It is powerful when it works and widely misunderstood. True virality comes from the product's nature: using it naturally involves others, or sharing it delivers value to both sender and recipient. Forced or incentivized referral, where users share for rewards rather than because the product invites it, tends to attract low-quality users who churn and damage unit economics. Designing viral mechanics means understanding what would make sharing genuine, measuring the loop honestly, and resisting the temptation to buy the appearance of virality.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I make my product viral", "should I add a referral program", "how do I get users to share", or "how should an entrepreneur design referral mechanics". The goal is to prevent the agent from confusing incentivized sharing with genuine virality and from designing referral programs that acquire the wrong users.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Inherent Virality From Incentivized Referral

Inherent virality comes from the product's use naturally involving others: a collaboration tool requires inviting colleagues; a payment tool requires sending to others; a content tool creates shareable output. Incentivized referral pays or rewards users to share a product that use does not naturally involve. Inherent virality compounds sustainably; incentivized referral often acquires low-quality users who leave when the reward stops.

### Build On Genuine Sharing Value

The strongest referral mechanics create value for both the sender and the recipient: the sender gains by bringing in someone they want to use the product with, and the recipient gains by discovering something useful. Design mechanics where sharing is in the sender's interest for reasons beyond the reward, because that sharing persists where incentivized sharing fades.

### Model The Viral Coefficient Honestly

The viral coefficient, the number of new users each existing user brings in, determines whether growth compounds. A coefficient above one means growth accelerates; below one means referral slows but does not stop. Model the coefficient from real behavior, accounting for conversion of invitations and time lag, not from optimistic assumptions. A coefficient that looks above one under friendly assumptions is usually below one in reality.

### Measure The Full Loop, Not Only Sharing

Sharing is one step; the loop includes share, recipient sees, recipient converts, new user shares. Measure conversion at each step, because the bottleneck may be anywhere. A product where users share freely but recipients do not convert has a broken loop, not virality.

### Watch The Quality Of Referred Users

Referred users can differ from organic users: they may be more loyal, because they come through a trusted contact, or less loyal, if they joined for a reward. Measure retention and value of referred users separately, because a referral program that acquires high-churn users worsens economics even as it grows the top of the funnel.

### Avoid Referral Programs That Distort The Product

Referral mechanics that intrude on the core experience, nagging prompts, forced invitations, social spam, degrade the product for existing users and can drive churn. Design mechanics that fit naturally and can be ignored, so that the pursuit of virality does not damage retention.

### Account For Channel And Audience Fit

Referral works differently across audiences and channels. Consumer products with social sharing spread differently than B2B tools where the recipient is a colleague. Design mechanics for the specific audience and the channels they actually use, not for generic sharing buttons.

### Time Referral To After Value Is Delivered

Users refer products they have found valuable, not products they just signed up for. Time referral prompts and mechanics to after the user has experienced the core value, because referrals before value are premature and ineffective. Let the value drive the share.

### Recognize The Limits Of Virality

Virality is rare and often temporary. Most products are not inherently viral, and forcing virality onto a non-viral product produces gimmicks. Be honest about whether the product's nature supports viral growth, and do not treat virality as a default strategy.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Referral programs may involve promotional terms, payments, and data sharing with legal and regulatory implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Incentivized Referral Mistaken For Virality

Reward-driven sharing acquires low-quality users who churn when rewards stop.

### Optimistic Viral Coefficient

Modeling from friendly assumptions hides a coefficient below one.

### Measuring Sharing, Not The Full Loop

High sharing with low conversion is a broken loop, not virality.

### High-Churn Referred Users

Referral that acquires users who leave quickly worsens unit economics.

### Mechanics That Degrade The Product

Nagging prompts and forced invitations drive churn among existing users.

### Assuming Virality As Default

Forcing virality onto a non-viral product produces gimmicks, not growth.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Inherent virality is distinguished from incentivized referral.
- [ ] Mechanics create value for both sender and recipient beyond rewards.
- [ ] The viral coefficient is modeled from real behavior, including conversion and time lag.
- [ ] The full loop, share, see, convert, share again, is measured, not only sharing.
- [ ] Retention and value of referred users are measured separately from organic users.
- [ ] Mechanics fit naturally and can be ignored, without degrading the core experience.
- [ ] Mechanics are designed for the specific audience and channels they use.
- [ ] Referral prompts are timed to after the user experiences core value.
- [ ] Whether the product's nature supports virality is honestly assessed.
- [ ] Promotional, payment, and data implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
