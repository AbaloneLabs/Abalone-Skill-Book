# Startup Growth Engines

A growth engine is the repeatable mechanism by which a venture acquires and retains customers at a sustainable cost. It is not a collection of tactics; it is the underlying model that makes growth work for a specific product and market. Different products support different engines: a product with inherent sharing supports viral growth; a product with clear ROI supports sales-led growth; a low-friction tool supports product-led growth. Choosing the wrong engine, or trying to run several at once before any works, scatters effort and prevents any single engine from compounding.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I grow my startup", "what growth strategy should I use", "how do I get more users", or "how should an entrepreneur design their growth model". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a grab bag of tactics and from ignoring the fit between the product's nature and the growth engine.

## Core Rules

### Match The Engine To The Product's Nature

Growth engines depend on characteristics of the product. Viral growth requires inherent sharing or network effects; paid growth requires unit economics that support acquisition cost; content and SEO growth require search demand and expertise; sales-led growth requires high contract value; product-led growth requires low friction and clear self-serve value. Choose an engine the product's nature supports, not one borrowed from an unrelated company.

### Focus On One Engine Until It Works

Running multiple engines simultaneously before any works dilutes effort and prevents compounding. Focus on one engine, the one best suited to the product and stage, until it produces repeatable, measurable growth. Only then consider adding a second engine. Premature diversification produces mediocrity across all.

### Define The Engine's Core Loop

Each engine has a core loop: the repeated sequence of actions that produces growth. For viral growth, the loop is use, share, new user, use. For content, it is publish, rank, acquire, convert. For paid, it is spend, acquire, convert, monetize, reinvest. Define the loop for the chosen engine and optimize its steps, because growth comes from strengthening the loop, not from isolated tactics.

### Ensure Unit Economics Support The Engine

An engine only sustains growth if its unit economics work: the cost to acquire through that engine is less than the value of the customer acquired, within an acceptable payback period. Measure the engine's CAC and LTV, and confirm the loop reinvests profitably. An engine with broken economics burns money faster as it scales.

### Measure The Engine's Leading Indicators

Each engine has leading indicators that predict whether it will compound: viral coefficient for viral, conversion and rank for content, CAC and payback for paid, activation for product-led. Measure the engine's specific leading indicators, not only lagging revenue, because they reveal whether the engine is healthy before results appear.

### Avoid Copying Tactics From Unrelated Models

Tactics that work for one engine or product often fail for another. A viral tactic fails in a sales-led product; an enterprise sales process fails in a low-friction tool. Do not copy tactics from companies with different engines; understand the engine and choose tactics that serve it.

### Sequence The Engine To The Stage

The right engine changes with stage. Before product-market fit, growth investment is premature; the focus is on retention and value. After fit, the engine that matches the product's nature can be invested in. Premature growth investment before fit scales a leaky bucket and burns runway.

### Watch For Engine Decay And Saturation

Growth engines decay: viral products lose novelty, paid channels saturate and rise in cost, content faces rising competition, sales markets get tapped. Monitor the engine's leading indicators for decay, and begin developing the next engine before the current one fades. Relying on a single engine forever is fragile.

### Distinguish Growth From Leakage

Apparent growth can mask leakage if churn is high. An engine that acquires users who leave quickly produces vanity growth, not sustainable growth. Measure net growth, accounting for churn, and ensure the engine's customers retain enough to justify the acquisition cost.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Growth decisions involve spending commitments and claims that carry stakes for venture survival and personal risk. Advertising, data, and contractual implications may require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Grab Bag Of Tactics Without An Engine

Scattered tactics without a coherent loop produce activity without compounding growth.

### Wrong Engine For The Product

Forcing a sales engine onto a low-friction tool, or viral tactics onto an enterprise product, fails.

### Multiple Engines Before Any Works

Premature diversification prevents any single engine from compounding.

### Broken Unit Economics

An engine whose CAC exceeds LTV burns faster as it scales.

### Copying Unrelated Companies

Tactics from a different engine or product fail when transplanted.

### Growth Masking Leakage

Acquiring fast while churning fast produces vanity, not sustainable, growth.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The chosen engine matches the product's nature and characteristics.
- [ ] The venture focuses on one engine until it produces repeatable growth.
- [ ] The engine's core loop is defined and its steps are optimized.
- [ ] Unit economics, CAC, LTV, and payback, support the engine.
- [ ] The engine's specific leading indicators are measured, not only lagging revenue.
- [ ] Tactics are chosen to serve the engine, not copied from unrelated companies.
- [ ] The engine is sequenced to the stage, with growth investment after fit.
- [ ] Engine decay and saturation are monitored, with the next engine developed before fade.
- [ ] Net growth accounting for churn is measured, not gross acquisition.
- [ ] Advertising, data, and contractual implications are flagged for professional advice.
