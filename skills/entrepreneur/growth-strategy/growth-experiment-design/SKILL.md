# Growth Experiment Design

Growth through experimentation is the discipline of treating acquisition, activation, and retention as hypotheses to be tested, measured, and iterated, rather than as opinions to be asserted. Well-designed experiments turn growth from guesswork into evidence: each test isolates a change, measures its effect on a defined metric, and produces a decision. Poorly designed experiments produce noise: confounded variables, biased samples, and metrics that measure the wrong thing, leading to confident decisions based on misleading signals. The quality of growth experimentation determines whether the venture learns what works or merely accumulates activity.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I run growth experiments", "how do I test my growth ideas", "what should I A/B test", or "how should an entrepreneur experiment with growth". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending uncontrolled or mis-measured experiments and from confusing motion with learning.

## Core Rules

### Frame Each Experiment As A Hypothesis

Each experiment should test a specific hypothesis: "changing X will improve metric Y by at least Z." Define the hypothesis, the change, the metric, and the expected effect before running the test. Experiments without hypotheses produce data without insight, because there is no defined question to answer.

### Isolate One Variable At A Time

To attribute an effect to a cause, change one variable at a time. Changing multiple things simultaneously confounds the results: an improvement could come from any of the changes, and the venture cannot learn which mattered. Isolate variables, or use a designed multi-factor approach that can separate effects.

### Choose A Metric That Reflects The Hypothesis

The metric measured should directly reflect the hypothesis being tested. Testing a headline change should measure click-through or conversion, not total visits. Testing an onboarding change should measure activation, not sign-ups. A mismatched metric produces results that do not answer the question the experiment was meant to ask.

### Ensure The Sample Is Large Enough And Unbiased

Experiments need enough sample to distinguish a real effect from noise, and the sample must be unbiased, representative of the population the change will affect. Too-small samples produce false positives and negatives; biased samples, such as only weekday users or only one segment, produce results that do not generalize. Check sample size and composition before trusting results.

### Run Experiments Long Enough For Signal

Short experiments capture noise and novelty effects; the true effect often emerges only over time, especially for retention. Run experiments long enough to see the effect on the metric that matters, including lagging effects, or risk acting on transient signals that fade.

### Define The Decision Before The Result

Before running the experiment, define what will happen if the result is positive, negative, or inconclusive. Pre-defining the decision prevents cherry-picking favorable results and rationalizing unfavorable ones. If the experiment cannot change a decision, it is not worth running.

### Beware Novelty And Seasonality Effects

Changes often produce a short-term bump from novelty, users reacting to something new, that fades as the novelty wears off. Seasonality and external events can also distort results. Distinguish sustained effects from transient ones by measuring over time and comparing to baselines.

### Watch For False Positives And Multiple Testing

Running many experiments increases the chance of finding a "significant" result by accident. Multiple testing without correction produces false positives that lead the venture to scale changes that do not actually work. Account for multiple testing and require replication of surprising results before acting.

### Measure Effects On The Growth Loop, Not Only Local Metrics

A change that improves a local metric, such as click-through, may harm the broader loop, such as retention or LTV, if it attracts the wrong users or sets wrong expectations. Measure effects on the growth loop and unit economics, not only on the local metric the change targets.

### Document And Share Results

Record each experiment's hypothesis, design, result, and decision, and share them with the team. Documentation accumulates knowledge, prevents repeating tests, and builds a library of what works and what does not. Undocumented experiments force the venture to relearn the same lessons.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Growth experiments may involve advertising claims, user data, and promotional terms with legal and regulatory implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Confounded Variables

Changing multiple things at once prevents attributing the effect to its cause.

### Mismatched Metric

Measuring a metric that does not reflect the hypothesis answers the wrong question.

### Too-Small Or Biased Sample

Small or unrepresentative samples produce results that do not generalize.

### Short Experiments Capturing Novelty

Transient bumps fade; sustained effects emerge only over time.

### No Pre-Defined Decision

Without a decision rule, favorable results are kept and unfavorable ones rationalized.

### Local Optimization Harming The Loop

A local improvement that hurts retention or economics makes growth worse.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each experiment is framed as a hypothesis with a defined change, metric, and expected effect.
- [ ] Variables are isolated so effects can be attributed to causes.
- [ ] The metric measured directly reflects the hypothesis being tested.
- [ ] Sample size and composition are checked for adequacy and lack of bias.
- [ ] Experiments run long enough to capture sustained effects, not novelty.
- [ ] The decision for positive, negative, and inconclusive results is defined before running.
- [ ] Novelty and seasonality effects are distinguished from sustained effects.
- [ ] Multiple testing is accounted for, and surprising results are replicated.
- [ ] Effects on the growth loop and unit economics are measured, not only local metrics.
- [ ] Experiments are documented and shared to accumulate knowledge.
- [ ] Advertising, data, and promotional implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
