# Bridge Financing

A bridge round is capital raised to carry a company from its current state to the next priced round or milestone, and it occupies a genuinely ambiguous strategic position. Used well, a bridge buys the time needed to hit the milestones that justify a strong priced round, turning a moment of weakness into a stronger negotiating position. Used poorly, a bridge burns capital without changing the underlying situation, dilutes founders through harsh terms, and merely delays a failure that was already coming. The judgment problem is recognizing when a bridge is the right move versus when it papers over an unresolved problem, structuring it so that the cost is proportionate to the time and risk, and ensuring the capital actually reaches the milestone that changes the company's position. Agents tend to fail by treating bridges as easy money, by accepting terms whose cost they do not fully understand, and by raising a bridge without a credible plan to reach the next round.

Use this skill when evaluating, structuring, or accepting bridge financing, or when advising a founder on whether to bridge. The goal is a bridge that genuinely improves the company's position, not one that delays an inevitable outcome at high cost.

## Core Rules

### Decide Whether A Bridge Is The Right Move Versus A Priced Round

The first question is whether the company needs a bridge at all, or whether it should raise a priced round, cut burn, or face the underlying issue. A bridge is appropriate only when more time at the current trajectory will materially improve the company's position.

A bridge may be right when:

- the company is close to milestones that would justify a much higher valuation in a priced round;
- market conditions make a priced round temporarily impossible or punitive;
- the company needs a small amount of capital to reach profitability or a clear inflection point;
- existing investors are willing to bridge on reasonable terms to protect their investment.

A bridge is likely wrong when:

- the underlying business has not advanced and more time will not change the valuation;
- the company is avoiding a necessary down round or strategic reckoning;
- the bridge terms are so harsh they damage the cap table without fixing the problem;
- there is no credible plan to reach a priced round even after the bridge.

The honest test is whether the bridge changes the company's negotiating position, or merely postpones the same negotiation with less runway.

### Ensure The Bridge Reaches A Credible Milestone

A bridge without a clear milestone is capital spent without a destination. The milestone must be specific, achievable within the bridge runway, and sufficient to change the company's position for the next round.

Define the milestone by:

- identifying what achievement would justify a priced round at a defensible valuation;
- confirming the milestone is reachable with the bridge capital and the current team;
- ensuring the milestone produces evidence, metrics, or traction that investors will credit;
- building in a margin of safety, because milestones slip.

A bridge that funds vague "growth" without a defined outcome often reaches the end of its runway in the same position, now with less capital and harsher terms. The milestone is the point of the bridge.

### Understand The Cost: Discounts, Valuation Caps, And Warrants

Bridge financing is rarely free money. Bridges are typically structured as convertible notes or SAFEs with terms that compensate investors for the risk, and those terms have real costs that founders often underestimate.

Understand each cost:

- discounts: the bridge investor converts at a discount to the next round price, commonly 20 percent, diluting founders more than a straight round;
- valuation caps: if the next round's valuation exceeds the cap, bridge investors convert at the cap, capturing upside that would otherwise go to founders;
- warrants: additional equity granted to bridge investors as a fee, on top of conversion, further diluting;
- interest and maturity: notes accrue interest and have a maturity date that can trigger repayment or conversion under pressure.

Model the fully diluted ownership after bridge conversion under various next-round scenarios. A bridge that looks small can dilute founders heavily if the next round is strong, because caps and discounts convert favorably for the bridge investor.

### Manage The Signal A Bridge Sends To The Market

A bridge sends a signal to the market, and that signal must be managed deliberately. A bridge from existing investors signals confidence and protection; a bridge shopped widely because no priced round is possible signals distress.

Manage the signal by:

- preferring insider bridges from existing investors when possible, which signal confidence;
- framing the bridge around a specific milestone and a planned priced round, not as open-ended survival;
- being honest with the market about why a bridge rather than a round, because the truth shapes expectations;
- recognizing that a bridge followed by a weak round reinforces a distress narrative.

The narrative around the bridge matters as much as the capital. A bridge that reads as strength supports the next round; one that reads as desperation undermines it.

### Negotiate Terms Proportionate To Risk And Time

Bridge terms should reflect the risk and the short duration of the capital, not the terms of a full round. Harsh terms on a short bridge extract disproportionate cost.

Negotiate by:

- ensuring discounts and caps reflect the bridge's short duration and the investor's risk;
- resisting warrants and fees that extract value beyond the conversion economics;
- setting a maturity that allows time to reach the milestone without pressure;
- avoiding terms that give bridge investors control or preferences beyond their capital.

A bridge investor taking round-like economics on short-term capital is extracting more than the risk justifies. Terms should match the instrument.

### Avoid The Bridge-To-Nowhere Trap

The most dangerous pattern is the serial bridge: a company raises a bridge, fails to reach the milestone, raises another bridge on worse terms, and repeats until the cap table is destroyed and the company fails anyway. Each bridge postpones the reckoning at increasing cost.

Avoid the trap by:

- being honest about whether the milestone is reachable before raising the bridge;
- cutting burn aggressively so the bridge runway extends to the milestone;
- having a clear plan for what happens if the milestone is missed, including shutdown or sale;
- refusing to raise a second bridge unless the underlying situation has genuinely changed.

A bridge should be a one-time intervention that changes the company's trajectory, not a recurring pattern that delays the inevitable. If the first bridge does not reach the milestone, the problem is strategic, not financial.

### Consider Alternatives: Cutting Burn, Down Round, Or Strategic Sale

A bridge is one option among several, and the alternatives may serve the company better. Cutting burn extends runway without dilution; a down round raises real capital at a lower valuation; a strategic sale realizes value before failure.

Consider alternatives by:

- cutting burn to reach the milestone with existing capital, avoiding dilution entirely;
- accepting a down round if it raises sufficient capital on clear terms, rather than a harsh bridge;
- exploring a strategic sale or acqui-hire if the company cannot reach viability;
- weighing the dilution and risk of a bridge against these alternatives.

A bridge is not always the least-bad option. Sometimes the honest path is a down round, a sale, or a shutdown, and a bridge merely makes the eventual outcome worse.

### Keep Existing Investors Aligned

A bridge often involves existing investors, and their alignment determines whether the bridge supports the company or extracts from it. Misaligned insiders can use a bridge to gain control or favorable terms at the founders' expense.

Keep alignment by:

- ensuring insider bridge terms are fair and not opportunistic;
- involving more than one investor where possible to prevent a single party from gaining leverage;
- documenting the rationale and terms clearly for all shareholders;
- recognizing when insider terms signal that investors have lost confidence and are protecting downside.

Existing investors bridging the company can be a sign of support or a sign that they are positioning for control. Read the terms, not just the participation.

## Common Traps

### Bridging When The Underlying Problem Is Unresolved

A bridge cannot fix a strategic or product problem. It only buys time, which is useful only if the time is used to change the position.

### No Credible Milestone

A bridge without a specific, achievable milestone reaches the end of its runway in the same position, with less capital and worse terms.

### Underestimating The Cost Of Terms

Discounts, caps, and warrants can dilute founders heavily, especially if the next round is strong. Model the fully diluted outcome.

### Ignoring The Market Signal

A bridge reads as either confidence or distress. Manage the narrative, because it shapes the next round.

### Terms Disproportionate To The Instrument

Round-like economics on short-term capital extract more than the risk justifies. Match terms to the bridge.

### The Serial Bridge Trap

Repeated bridges on worsening terms destroy the cap table and delay an inevitable failure. Intervene strategically, not repeatedly.

### Overlooking Alternatives

Cutting burn, a down round, or a sale may serve the company better than a bridge. Weigh all options.

### Misaligned Insider Terms

Insider bridges can be support or opportunism. Read the terms, not just the participation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A bridge was chosen over a priced round, burn cut, or sale only because more time will materially improve the company's position.
- [ ] A specific, achievable milestone justifies the bridge, with margin for slippage.
- [ ] The fully diluted cost of discounts, caps, warrants, and interest is modeled under various next-round scenarios.
- [ ] The bridge is framed around a milestone and planned round, and the market signal is managed.
- [ ] Terms are proportionate to the bridge's short duration and risk, not round-like.
- [ ] There is a credible plan to reach a priced round after the bridge, and a clear path if the milestone is missed.
- [ ] The bridge is a one-time intervention, not the start of a serial bridge pattern.
- [ ] Alternatives, cutting burn, down round, or sale, were weighed against the bridge.
- [ ] Existing investor terms are fair and not opportunistic, with alignment documented.
- [ ] The bridge genuinely changes the company's negotiating position rather than delaying the same outcome.
