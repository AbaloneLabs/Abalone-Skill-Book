# Financial Runway Scenarios

Runway is the number of months a company can operate before it runs out of cash, and it is the single most consequential operational number a startup manages, because running out of cash is the one failure that cannot be recovered by better product or strategy. Yet runway is not a single fixed number; it is a function of burn rate and revenue, both of which change, and the assumptions behind any runway calculation determine whether it is trustworthy or dangerously optimistic. The judgment problem is modeling runway under multiple scenarios rather than a single hopeful projection, stress-testing the assumptions that drive it, and using runway planning to make decisions about burn, hiring, and fundraising before the cash is gone. Agents tend to fail by presenting a single base-case runway as if it were certain, by excluding the time needed to raise from the runway calculation, and by treating revenue assumptions as more reliable than they are.

Use this skill when building, interpreting, or advising on financial runway scenarios for a startup. The goal is runway planning that supports real decisions under uncertainty, rather than a single number that creates false confidence.

## Core Rules

### Model Multiple Scenarios, Not A Single Projection

A single runway number, however carefully calculated, hides the uncertainty that defines a startup. Revenue slips, costs rise, and the base case rarely matches reality. Scenario modeling reveals the range of outcomes and the decisions each demands.

Model at minimum:

- base case: the most likely revenue and burn trajectory;
- best case: upside if revenue accelerates or costs fall;
- worst case: downside if revenue slips, costs rise, or both;
- disaster case: severe downside, including lost customers or delayed rounds.

The value is not in predicting which scenario occurs but in knowing the decisions each requires. A company that has planned the worst case can act on it; one that planned only the base case is caught by surprise.

### Include The Time Needed To Raise In The Runway Calculation

A common and dangerous error is calculating runway to zero cash, as if the company can raise the moment it runs out. In reality, fundraising takes months, and raising from a position of desperation yields terrible terms or fails entirely.

Account for raise time by:

- subtracting the time needed to raise, commonly three to six months, from the runway;
- treating the effective runway as the point by which fundraising must begin, not the point of zero cash;
- planning to start raising when runway still allows negotiation from strength;
- recognizing that a weak position compresses timelines and worsens terms.

The real runway is the months before you must start raising, not the months before cash hits zero. A company that waits until cash is low has already lost negotiating leverage.

### Stress-Test The Revenue Assumptions

Revenue is the most optimistic and most wrong assumption in most runway models. Founders project growth that assumes everything goes right, and runway calculations built on that optimism collapse when reality arrives.

Stress-test revenue by:

- modeling what happens if sales close later than expected;
- modeling churn and contraction, not just new revenue;
- questioning whether the pipeline will convert at the assumed rate;
- distinguishing committed revenue from forecasted revenue.

A runway model that treats forecasted revenue as certain is not a plan; it is a hope. Build the worst case on the revenue you would bet on, not the revenue you would wish for.

### Separate Fixed And Variable Burn

Not all burn is equal. Fixed costs, salaries, rent, software, persist regardless of revenue, while variable costs scale with activity. Understanding the composition of burn reveals the levers available when runway tightens.

Analyze burn by:

- identifying fixed costs that are hard to reduce quickly;
- identifying variable costs that can be cut in response to revenue shortfalls;
- knowing which cuts preserve the core business and which cripple it;
- modeling the burn reduction achievable under various cost-cutting scenarios.

A company that knows its fixed floor and its variable levers can respond to a revenue shortfall decisively. One that does not cuts blindly and often destructively.

### Plan The Burn Decisions Before They Are Needed

When runway tightens, decisions must be made fast, and decisions made under pressure are worse than decisions made in advance. Pre-planning the burn reductions and the triggers for them allows rapid, rational response.

Pre-plan by:

- defining the runway thresholds that trigger specific actions, such as hiring freezes or cuts;
- deciding in advance what would be cut and what would be protected;
- establishing the revenue or cash milestones that change the plan;
- communicating the framework to the team so execution is fast when needed.

A company with pre-planned triggers cuts burn in a week; one without spends a month debating while runway burns. Plan the decisions before the crisis.

### Treat Hiring As A Runway Decision

Every hire extends or shortens runway, because compensation is usually the largest burn line. Hiring decisions are therefore runway decisions, and treating them as separate produces runway surprises.

Incorporate hiring by:

- modeling the runway impact of planned hires before making them;
- distinguishing critical hires that accelerate revenue from those that add cost without near-term return;
- timing hires to revenue or funding milestones rather than to ambition;
- being willing to delay non-critical hires when runway is tight.

A hiring plan that ignores runway is a plan to run out of cash. Every offer letter is a runway commitment.

### Update Runway Scenarios Regularly

Runway is not a number calculated once; it changes monthly with burn and revenue, and a stale runway model is a liability. Regular updating keeps decisions grounded in current reality.

Update by:

- revisiting the scenarios monthly with actual burn and revenue figures;
- adjusting projections based on what the latest data reveals;
- re-testing the assumptions that have proven wrong;
- communicating runway status to the team and investors appropriately.

A runway model updated monthly informs decisions; one updated at fundraising is a backward-looking artifact. Keep it current.

### Use Runway Planning To Drive Fundraising Timing

Runway planning is not an accounting exercise; it is the basis for deciding when to raise, how much to raise, and when to cut burn. The scenarios should drive these decisions proactively.

Use scenarios to decide:

- when to begin raising, accounting for raise time and negotiating strength;
- how much to raise to reach the milestones that justify the next round or profitability;
- whether to cut burn to extend runway or raise to accelerate growth;
- what runway cushion is required to operate without constant fundraising anxiety.

Runway planning exists to inform action. A model that does not change decisions is not worth building.

## Common Traps

### A Single Base-Case Runway Presented As Certain

Optimism hides uncertainty. Model multiple scenarios to reveal the range of outcomes.

### Runway Calculated To Zero Cash

Fundraising takes months. The real runway ends when raising must begin, not when cash hits zero.

### Revenue Assumed To Convert As Forecasted

Pipeline slips, churn happens. Stress-test revenue against realistic outcomes.

### Treating All Burn As Equal

Fixed and variable costs differ in reducibility. Know the levers before you need them.

### Deciding Under Pressure

Burn cuts made in crisis are worse than those planned in advance. Pre-plan triggers and actions.

### Hiring Without Runway Impact

Every hire is a runway commitment. Model hiring against runway, not just ambition.

### Stale Runway Models

Runway changes monthly. Update scenarios regularly or the model misleads.

### Runway Planning Without Decisions

A model that does not change fundraising, burn, or hiring decisions is not worth building.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Multiple scenarios, base, best, worst, and disaster, are modeled, not a single projection.
- [ ] The time needed to raise is subtracted from runway, so fundraising begins from strength.
- [ ] Revenue assumptions are stress-tested for slippage, churn, and conversion rate.
- [ ] Fixed and variable burn are separated, and the available cost-cutting levers are known.
- [ ] Burn reduction decisions and their triggers are planned in advance, not deferred to crisis.
- [ ] Hiring is modeled against runway, with critical and non-critical hires distinguished.
- [ ] Runway scenarios are updated monthly with actual figures and revised projections.
- [ ] Runway planning drives decisions on fundraising timing, amount, and burn cuts.
- [ ] The worst case has a defined response, not just a number.
- [ ] The runway model would survive the revenue not arriving on time.
