# Cap Table Management

A cap table is the definitive record of who owns what percentage of a company, and it is one of the few documents whose accuracy and cleanliness directly affect whether a company can be funded, acquired, or governed without crisis. A cap table is not a spreadsheet to fill in once; it is a living instrument that changes with every grant, round, and conversion, and small errors or casual decisions early on compound into severe problems later. Misallocated equity, broken vesting, undocumented promises, and excessive early dilution can poison founder incentives, block financing, and kill acquisitions. The judgment problem is building a cap table that is fair, clean, and defensible from day one, managing dilution deliberately across rounds, and recognizing that equity is the company's most expensive currency, spent on people and capital that determine its fate. Agents tend to fail by treating equity allocation casually, by failing to model future dilution, and by letting the cap table drift into a state that repels investors and acquirers.

Use this skill when creating, managing, or explaining a cap table: allocating founder equity, issuing grants, modeling dilution, setting vesting, or preparing for a round or exit. The goal is a cap table that is accurate, fair, and attractive to the investors and talent the company needs.

## Core Rules

### Build A Clean Cap Table From Day One

The cap table should be established formally at incorporation, not assembled retroactively from memory and emails. Reconstructing ownership after the fact produces disputes, errors, and terms no one can prove.

Establish cleanly by:

- documenting every share, grant, and transfer in a single authoritative record;
- keeping all agreements, board approvals, and stock purchase paperwork consistent with the cap table;
- using cap table management software or a disciplined spreadsheet, not scattered documents;
- reconciling the cap table against legal documents regularly.

A clean cap table is a signal of operational maturity that investors and acquirers check immediately. A messy one is a red flag that delays or kills deals.

### Allocate Founder Equity Deliberately, Not Casually

How founders split equity at the start shapes incentives and relationships for years, and casual splits, equal shares for everyone regardless of role, or splits that ignore ongoing contribution, create resentment and misalignment later.

Allocate deliberately by:

- reflecting relative contribution, role, risk, and ongoing commitment, not just equal division;
- discussing and documenting the rationale so expectations are aligned;
- considering future contributions and the long-term arc of each founder's role;
- revisiting only through structured processes, never through ad hoc renegotiation.

Founder equity is not a gesture of partnership; it is the incentive structure that determines whether the founders stay aligned through years of hard work. Get it right early, because fixing it later is painful.

### Use Vesting To Protect The Company And Align Long-Term Incentives

Vesting, typically four years with a one-year cliff, ensures that equity is earned over time and that a founder or employee who leaves early does not retain a large unearned stake. Without vesting, early departures leave dead equity that blocks financing and dilutes those who remain.

Apply vesting by:

- using a standard schedule, commonly four years with a one-year cliff, for founders and employees;
- ensuring all equity, including founder equity, is subject to vesting;
- documenting vesting terms clearly and consistently;
- considering acceleration provisions for specific events like acquisition, designed deliberately.

Vesting protects the company from the consequences of early departure and aligns equity with ongoing contribution. Skipping it is one of the most damaging cap table mistakes.

### Model Dilution Across Future Rounds Before Accepting Investment

Every investment round dilutes existing shareholders, and founders who do not model future dilution are often surprised to find their ownership far smaller than expected after several rounds. Dilution must be understood and planned, not discovered after the fact.

Model dilution by:

- projecting ownership through anticipated rounds, including option pool creation and expansion;
- understanding how much ownership founders will retain at exit under various scenarios;
- recognizing that the option pool often comes out of the pre-money valuation, diluting founders;
- balancing the capital needed against the dilution acceptable.

A founder who models dilution negotiates from understanding. One who does not may accept terms that leave them with little ownership and little incentive at the company's most valuable moment.

### Manage The Option Pool Strategically

The employee option pool is the currency used to attract and retain talent, and its size and management affect both hiring capacity and dilution. Too small a pool constrains hiring; too large a pool dilutes founders unnecessarily.

Manage the pool by:

- sizing the pool to cover hiring needs until the next round, with some buffer;
- understanding who bears the dilution when the pool is created or expanded, often founders;
- tracking granted versus available options to avoid running out mid-hiring;
- refreshing grants for top performers strategically to retain them without excessive dilution.

The option pool is a strategic resource. Manage it as deliberately as cash, because it is the company's primary tool for building the team.

### Keep The Cap Table Attractive To Investors And Acquirers

Investors and acquirers scrutinize the cap table for problems that signal risk or complicate deals. A cap table with red flags can block financing or reduce valuation, however good the business.

Keep it attractive by:

- avoiding excessive early dilution that leaves founders with too little ownership to stay motivated;
- preventing dead equity from departed founders or employees without vested shares;
- minimizing complex preferences, bridges, and convertible notes that stack up before rounds;
- documenting everything cleanly so due diligence proceeds smoothly.

The cap table is part of the company's story to investors. A clean, well-structured table signals competence; a messy one signals risk.

### Document Every Equity Decision And Promise

Undocumented equity promises are a recurring source of founder disputes and cap table crises. A verbal promise of equity to an early contributor, if not documented, becomes a dispute that can derail financing or trigger litigation.

Document by:

- putting every grant, promise, and agreement in writing, reviewed by counsel;
- ensuring board approval for all equity actions;
- avoiding informal or contingent promises that are not reflected in the cap table;
- communicating clearly what was and was not granted, to prevent misunderstanding.

The cap table is the source of truth, and anything not in it does not exist, however strongly someone remembers a promise. Document everything.

### Use Professional Help For Complex Transactions

Cap table complexity rises sharply with convertible notes, SAFEs, preferred stock, and down rounds, and the consequences of error are severe. These are not areas for amateur spreadsheet work.

Engage professionals by:

- using experienced startup counsel for equity structuring and documentation;
- using cap table software or services for ongoing management;
- getting professional review before rounds, exits, or complex transactions;
- not treating legal and financial review as optional costs to skip.

The cost of professional help is trivial compared to the cost of a cap table error that blocks a financing or kills an acquisition.

## Common Traps

### Assembling The Cap Table Retroactively

Reconstructing ownership from memory produces disputes and errors. Build it formally from day one.

### Casual Founder Equity Splits

Equal or unconsidered splits create resentment and misalignment. Reflect contribution and ongoing role.

### Skipping Vesting

Without vesting, early departures leave dead equity that blocks financing and dilutes those who remain.

### Not Modeling Future Dilution

Founders discover too late that rounds and option pools leave them with little ownership. Model it in advance.

### Mismanaging The Option Pool

Too small constrains hiring; too large over-dilutes. Size and track the pool strategically.

### Red Flags That Repel Investors

Excessive early dilution, dead equity, and stacked preferences signal risk and reduce valuation.

### Undocumented Equity Promises

Verbal promises become disputes. Document every grant and agreement in writing.

### DIY Complex Transactions

Convertibles, preferred stock, and down rounds require professional help. Errors are costly.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The cap table was established formally at incorporation and is reconciled regularly with legal documents.
- [ ] Founder equity reflects deliberate allocation based on contribution, role, and ongoing commitment.
- [ ] All equity, including founder equity, is subject to vesting, typically four years with a one-year cliff.
- [ ] Future dilution is modeled across anticipated rounds, including option pool creation.
- [ ] The option pool is sized to hiring needs and tracked to avoid running out.
- [ ] The cap table is clean of red flags: no excessive early dilution, dead equity, or stacked complexity.
- [ ] Every equity decision and promise is documented in writing and board-approved.
- [ ] Complex transactions use experienced startup counsel and cap table software.
- [ ] Founder ownership under various exit scenarios is understood and acceptable.
- [ ] The cap table would survive investor or acquirer due diligence without surprises.
