# Sales Process For Startups

A sales process is the defined sequence of stages a prospect moves through from first contact to closed deal, and the actions that move them between stages. For a startup, building a sales process is what turns founder-led sales, which depend on the founder's personal effort, into a repeatable motion that others can execute. The process must fit the venture's contract size and sales motion: a low-friction self-serve product needs almost no process, while a high-value enterprise sale needs a structured multi-stage process. Mismatching the process to the motion creates either bureaucracy that slows simple sales or chaos that loses complex ones.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I build a sales process", "what sales stages should I use", "how do I make sales repeatable", or "how should an entrepreneur structure their sales". The goal is to prevent the agent from imposing a generic enterprise process on a simple sale and from leaving complex sales unstructured.

## Core Rules

### Match The Process To The Sales Motion

The sales process should fit how the product is actually bought. A self-serve product needs minimal process, focused on activation and payment. A low-touch sale needs a light process with clear stages. A high-touch enterprise sale needs a structured process with discovery, evaluation, and procurement stages. Do not impose more process than the motion requires, and do not leave a complex motion without structure.

### Define The Stages That Reflect Real Buying

Define sales stages that reflect where the prospect actually is in their buying process, not internal milestones. Typical stages include qualified, where the prospect fits and has the problem; engaged, where there is active conversation; evaluating, where the prospect is assessing fit; and closing, where terms are being finalized. Stages should correspond to observable prospect behavior, not to hopeful milestones.

### Specify The Exit Criteria For Each Stage

For each stage, define what must be true for a deal to advance to the next: a confirmed meeting, a shared evaluation, a verbal commitment. Exit criteria prevent deals from stalling in a stage without progress and make the pipeline honest. A deal that has not met the exit criteria has not advanced, however optimistic the forecast.

### Document The Actions That Move Deals Forward

For each stage transition, document the actions that move a deal forward: what to send, what to ask, what to confirm. This documentation turns the founder's personal skill into a transferable playbook. Without documented actions, the process is a diagram that no one can execute.

### Track The Pipeline Honestly

Track the pipeline with the defined stages and exit criteria, and resist inflating forecasts by advancing deals that have not met the criteria. An honest pipeline reveals where deals stall and which stages need attention; an inflated one hides problems until the quarter ends. Discipline in pipeline management is what makes the process useful.

### Measure Conversion And Time At Each Stage

Measure the conversion rate and the average time deals spend at each stage. These metrics reveal the health of the process: where deals leak, where they stall, and which stages are bottlenecks. A process without measurement cannot be improved, because no one knows where it breaks.

### Build The Process From What Already Works

Build the sales process from what the founder has already observed to work in early sales, not from a generic template. The process should codify real successful patterns, not impose theoretical stages. A process that does not match how deals actually close will be ignored.

### Iterate The Process As The Motion Matures

The sales process is not fixed; it evolves as the product, market, and motion mature. Early versions are rough and revised often; later versions stabilize but still adapt to new segments or competitive shifts. Treat the process as a living document, improved through what each deal reveals.

### Avoid Process For Its Own Sake

Process exists to make sales repeatable and improvable, not to create bureaucracy. If a stage, a form, or a step does not help close deals or reveal information, remove it. Excess process slows sales and frustrates both sellers and buyers, especially in smaller deals.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Sales processes involve contracts, representations, and commitments with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Generic Enterprise Process On A Simple Sale

Imposing multi-stage bureaucracy on a low-friction product slows deals.

### No Process For A Complex Sale

Leaving a high-value motion unstructured loses deals to chaos.

### Stages That Reflect Internal Milestones

Stages that do not match prospect behavior produce dishonest pipelines.

### No Exit Criteria

Deals stall in stages without measurable progress, inflating forecasts.

### Process That Does Not Match How Deals Close

A theoretical process ignored by the team codifies nothing real.

### Process As Bureaucracy

Stages and forms that do not help close deals slow sales and frustrate buyers.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The process matches the venture's contract size and sales motion.
- [ ] Stages reflect the prospect's real buying position, with observable exit criteria.
- [ ] Exit criteria for each stage are defined and enforced.
- [ ] The actions that move deals between stages are documented.
- [ ] The pipeline is tracked honestly against the defined stages.
- [ ] Conversion and time at each stage are measured to find bottlenecks.
- [ ] The process is built from observed successful patterns, not generic templates.
- [ ] The process is iterated as the motion matures.
- [ ] Process that does not help close deals or reveal information is removed.
- [ ] Contract and representation implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
