# Pitch Deck And Narrative

A pitch deck is a compressed argument that an opportunity is real, large, and reachable, and that this team can capture it. Its core is not design but narrative: a coherent story that connects the problem, the solution, the evidence, the market, the model, and the use of funds into a case an investor can evaluate. Weak decks either bury the argument in features and design, or overstate evidence and hide risk. A strong deck leads with the thesis, supports it with honest evidence, acknowledges the main risks, and makes the use of funds concrete.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I make a pitch deck", "what should my deck say", "how do I pitch investors", or "how should an entrepreneur build their investor narrative". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a feature list disguised as a story and from overstating evidence or hiding risk.

## Core Rules

### Lead With The Thesis, Not The Features

Investors fund theses, not feature catalogs. Open with the core argument: what important truth the founder believes, what problem it creates, and why now is the time to solve it. The product and features support the thesis; they are not the thesis. A deck that opens with features asks the investor to infer the argument, which they will not.

State the thesis clearly and early, then support it.

### Build The Narrative As A Connected Story

A deck is a sequence where each slide sets up the next. Problem leads to solution, solution leads to market, market leads to model, model leads to traction, traction leads to the ask. Each slide should answer the question the previous slide raised. A deck that is a pile of independent slides reads as disconnected and unconvincing.

Map the argument flow before designing slides.

### Support Claims With Honest Evidence

Every major claim, market size, demand, retention, growth, defensibility, should be backed by evidence the investor can check. Where evidence is early, say so. Where assumptions are unproven, mark them. Honest, specific evidence builds trust; vague or overstated claims destroy it. Investors discount decks that overstate, and the loss of credibility spreads to the parts that are true.

Distinguish proven results from projections and assumptions.

### Make The Problem And Customer Specific

Abstract problems and generic markets are unconvincing. Name a specific customer, a specific pain, and a specific moment when the problem occurs. Concrete problems are believable; abstract ones are forgettable. Show that the founder has seen the problem up close, not read about it.

### Quantify The Market From The Bottom Up

Top-down market sizes, a percentage of a giant number, are easy to dismiss. Bottom-up sizing, number of customers times realistic price, grounded in a specific segment and acquisition plan, is credible. Show how the market estimate connects to the customers the venture can actually reach.

### Show Traction As Momentum, Not Vanity

Traction is evidence the thesis is working: growing usage, paying customers, retention, unit economics, or repeatable acquisition. Vanity metrics, downloads, registered users, social followers, without engagement or revenue, do not prove the thesis. Lead with the metrics that validate the core argument and be honest about what they do and do not show.

### Present The Model And Unit Economics

Investors want to know how the venture makes money and whether the economics improve with scale. Show the revenue model, the key unit economics, and the path to better margins or lower acquisition costs. Avoid presenting a model that only works at massive scale with no evidence the levers will move.

### Make The Ask And Use Of Funds Concrete

The ask should state how much is being raised, what milestone it buys, and how the funds will be used to reach it. Vague asks signal unclear thinking. Concrete use of funds, with the milestone it reaches, shows discipline and gives investors a way to judge whether the capital is sufficient.

### Acknowledge The Main Risks

Every venture has risks. Naming the principal risks, competition, execution, regulation, timing, shows judgment and builds trust. Hiding risks invites investors to find them and assume the worst. Acknowledging them, with mitigation, is more persuasive than pretending they do not exist.

### Keep The Deck Short And Scannable

Investors review many decks. Keep the core deck short, typically ten to fifteen slides, with each slide carrying one idea. Dense slides are skipped. Additional detail belongs in an appendix or backup, available on request.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Pitching involves legal representations about the business. Claims in a deck can carry liability, and securities laws govern how offers to invest are made. This skill does not replace professional legal advice on pitch content and fundraising compliance.

## Common Traps

### Feature List Disguised As Story

A deck of features asks the investor to infer the argument, which they will not.

### Overstating Evidence

Inflated traction or market size destroys credibility and spreads to true claims.

### Disconnected Slides

A pile of independent slides reads as unconvincing compared to a connected narrative.

### Vanity Metrics

Downloads and followers without engagement or revenue do not validate the thesis.

### Hiding Risks

Pretending risks do not exist invites investors to find them and assume the worst.

### Vague Ask

An unclear amount or use of funds signals unclear thinking and weakens the case.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The deck leads with a clear thesis, not a feature catalog.
- [ ] Slides form a connected narrative where each sets up the next.
- [ ] Major claims are supported by honest, specific evidence.
- [ ] The problem and customer are concrete and specific, not abstract.
- [ ] Market size is built bottom-up and connected to reachable customers.
- [ ] Traction uses validating metrics, not vanity numbers.
- [ ] The revenue model and unit economics are presented with a path to improvement.
- [ ] The ask and use of funds are concrete and tied to a milestone.
- [ ] The principal risks are acknowledged with mitigation.
- [ ] The deck is short, scannable, and avoids dense slides.
- [ ] Claims are honest and avoid legal misrepresentation; securities compliance is flagged for professional advice.
