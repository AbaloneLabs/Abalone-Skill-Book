# Compensation And Equity Early

Early hires are compensated through a mix of cash salary and equity, and getting the mix right is consequential for the venture's finances, fairness, and ability to retain people. Cash is scarce in early ventures, so equity fills the gap, offering hires a stake in the upside they help create. But equity is complex: it dilutes founders, carries vesting and tax implications, and must be distributed fairly across hires or it breeds resentment. Early compensation design is the discipline of balancing cash constraints, fairness, retention, and the venture's risk, in a way that is defensible and sustainable.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how much should I pay early hires", "how much equity should I give", "how do I set compensation for my startup", or "how should an entrepreneur compensate early employees". The goal is to prevent the agent from setting compensation arbitrarily and from ignoring the long-term implications of equity, fairness, and dilution.

## Core Rules

### Balance Cash And Equity Against The Venture's Constraints

Early compensation is a balance: cash the venture can afford, equity that fills the gap and aligns the hire with the upside. The right balance depends on the venture's cash position, the hire's risk tolerance, and market norms. Be honest about what the venture can pay in cash, and use equity to make the total package competitive, not to underpay people who need cash.

### Use Equity To Align Interests, Not Only To Save Cash

Equity's deepest purpose is alignment: giving hires a stake in the venture's success so their incentives point toward long-term value, not only short-term pay. Use equity for alignment, not merely as a substitute for cash, because hires who understand and value equity are invested in the venture's outcome in a way cash-only hires are not.

### Understand Vesting And Its Purpose

Equity grants should vest over time, typically over four years with a one-year cliff, so that equity is earned through continued contribution rather than granted upfront. Vesting protects the venture and the team: it ensures equity goes to those who contribute over time, and it provides a mechanism when hires leave early. Understand vesting and apply it consistently.

### Be Consistent And Fair Across Hires

Inconsistent equity grants across similar hires breed resentment and damage trust. Establish a framework for equity by role, stage, and contribution, and apply it consistently, documenting the reasoning behind each grant. Fairness does not mean identical grants, but it means defensible and consistent logic that the team can understand.

### Account For Dilution Over Time

Every equity grant dilutes founders and existing holders. Model how the cap table evolves as the team grows and as future rounds occur, so that early grants do not leave founders with too little, and so that the option pool remains sufficient for future hires. Dilution is inevitable, but unmanaged dilution creates problems later.

### Be Honest About Equity's Value And Risk

Equity in an early venture is high-risk and likely worthless; most startups do not produce a liquidity event, and equity can be diluted to little through later rounds. Be honest with hires about this risk, rather than selling equity as if it were certain value. Honesty builds trust; overpromising on equity's value breeds resentment if the outcome disappoints.

### Set Cash Salary To Meet Needs And Market

Cash salary should meet the hire's needs and reflect market norms adjusted for the venture's stage and risk, not be minimized to the point the hire struggles. A hire who cannot meet personal financial needs will be distracted or leave, however much equity they hold. Set cash salary to sustain the hire, within what the venture can afford.

### Consider The Tax Implications Of Equity

Equity compensation carries tax implications for both the venture and the hire, varying by jurisdiction and equity type. Understand the basics, and ensure hires understand the tax consequences of their grants, because surprises create resentment and financial strain. Tax treatment is a reason to seek professional advice.

### Review And Adjust The Framework As The Venture Grows

The compensation and equity framework that fits a venture's first hires will not fit later stages. As the venture grows, raises capital, and hires more senior roles, review and adjust the framework, including the option pool and grant sizes. A static framework becomes wrong as the venture evolves.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Compensation and equity involve tax, securities, employment, and contractual obligations with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Equity Used Only To Underpay

Using equity to justify cash below what hires need leads to distraction and attrition.

### Inconsistent Grants

Inequity across similar hires breeds resentment and damages trust.

### Ignoring Dilution

Unmodeled dilution leaves founders with too little and depletes the option pool.

### Overpromising Equity's Value

Selling equity as certain value breeds resentment if the outcome disappoints.

### Cash Too Low To Sustain Hires

Hires who cannot meet needs are distracted or leave, despite equity.

### Static Framework

A framework that fits early hires becomes wrong as the venture grows.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Cash and equity are balanced against the venture's constraints and the hire's needs.
- [ ] Equity is used for alignment, not only to substitute for cash.
- [ ] Vesting is understood and applied consistently.
- [ ] Equity grants are consistent and fair across similar hires, with documented reasoning.
- [ ] Dilution over time is modeled, including future rounds and the option pool.
- [ ] The risk and likely value of equity is communicated honestly to hires.
- [ ] Cash salary meets needs and reflects market norms adjusted for stage and risk.
- [ ] Tax implications of equity are understood and communicated.
- [ ] The compensation framework is reviewed and adjusted as the venture grows.
- [ ] Tax, securities, employment, and contractual implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
