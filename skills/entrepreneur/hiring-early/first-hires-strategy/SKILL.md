# First Hires Strategy

The first hires are unlike any that follow. They shape the product, the culture, and the venture's trajectory, and they are made when the venture has the least to offer, uncertain prospects, limited pay, and high risk. First-hire strategy is the discipline of choosing these foundational people deliberately: what profile fits, what tradeoffs to accept, what to offer, and what to demand. Getting the first hires right compounds for years; getting them wrong creates damage that is expensive to repair, because early hires set norms and carry institutional knowledge that later hires inherit.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "who should my first hires be", "how do I hire my first employees", "what should I look for in early hires", or "how should an entrepreneur make their first hires". The goal is to prevent the agent from applying large-company hiring patterns to early hires and from underweighting the cultural and strategic weight of the first people.

## Core Rules

### Treat First Hires As Foundational, Not Incremental

The first hires shape the venture disproportionately: they set the culture, establish how work is done, and carry knowledge that becomes institutional. Treat them as foundational decisions, not as incremental capacity. The cost of a wrong first hire is not only the replacement cost but the cultural and strategic damage done while they were there.

### Hire For Stage Fit And Adaptability

First hires must fit the venture's stage: comfortable with ambiguity, willing to span functions, and able to adapt as the venture changes direction. Candidates who thrive in structured, specialized environments often struggle in early ventures, however strong their credentials. Hire for stage fit and adaptability alongside skill, because the venture will change around the hire.

### Prioritize Self-Sufficiency And Range

Early ventures need people who can take ownership of broad areas with little direction, figure things out, and deliver across a range of tasks. Self-sufficient generalists who can span functions are more valuable early than narrow specialists who need structure and support. Prioritize range and ownership in the first hires.

### Accept Calculated Tradeoffs In Profile

First hires involve tradeoffs: experience versus potential, specialization versus range, proven track record versus adaptability. No candidate offers everything, and the venture cannot attract every strength. Make the tradeoffs deliberately, knowing which qualities are essential and which can be developed, rather than searching for an impossible ideal.

### Offer Equity And Mission, Not Only Salary

Early ventures usually cannot match the salary of established companies, but they can offer equity, mission, autonomy, and growth that larger companies cannot. Be honest about the compensation reality, and recruit people motivated by what the venture uniquely offers. Hires who join only for salary leave when a better salary appears; hires who join for mission and equity stay through difficulty.

### Vet For Culture And Values, Rigorously

The first hires define the culture, so vet them for alignment with the values and norms the founder wants to establish, as rigorously as for skill. A first hire who is culturally misaligned sets a norm that later hires inherit. Reference checks, trial work, and behavioral interviews should probe culture and values, not only capability.

### Avoid Hiring Friends Without Rigor

Hiring friends or former colleagues is tempting and sometimes right, but it must be subject to the same rigor as any hire, with clear expectations and honest assessment of fit. Hiring friends without rigor, or avoiding hard conversations because of the relationship, creates teams shaped by familiarity rather than fit, and makes necessary partings painful.

### Define The Role, But Expect It To Evolve

Define the role's purpose and the problem it solves, but expect the role to evolve as the venture learns. Early hires should be comfortable with roles that shift, because the venture's needs change. Hire people who can grow with the role, rather than candidates suited only to a narrow, fixed definition.

### Onboard The First Hires Deeply

First hires need deep onboarding into the venture's context, strategy, and the founder's thinking, because they will make many decisions independently and shape what follows. Invest in onboarding, even when time is scarce, because under-onboarded first hires make misaligned decisions that compound. Onboarding is an investment in the venture's coherence.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

First hires involve employment, equity, immigration, and contractual obligations with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Large-Company Hiring Patterns Applied Early

Structured, specialized hiring profiles misfit the ambiguity of early ventures.

### Foundational Weight Underweighted

Treating first hires as incremental capacity ignores their cultural and strategic impact.

### Overweighting Credentials Over Stage Fit

Strong-on-paper candidates who need structure struggle in early ventures.

### Salary-Only Recruitment

Hires motivated only by salary leave when better pay appears.

### Insufficient Culture Vetting

A culturally misaligned first hire sets norms that later hires inherit.

### Hiring Friends Without Rigor

Familiarity-based hiring without honest assessment creates fit problems.

## Self-Check

- [ ] First hires are treated as foundational, not incremental, decisions.
- [ ] Hires are chosen for stage fit and adaptability alongside skill.
- [ ] Self-sufficiency and range are prioritized in the profile.
- [ ] Tradeoffs in profile are made deliberately, with essentials distinguished from developables.
- [ ] Equity, mission, and growth are offered honestly alongside salary.
- [ ] Culture and values are vetted as rigorously as skill.
- [ ] Hiring friends is subject to the same rigor as any hire.
- [ ] Roles are defined but expected to evolve, with hires who can grow.
- [ ] First hires are onboarded deeply into context and strategy.
- [ ] Employment, equity, immigration, and contractual implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
