# Strategy Testing And Validation

A strategy that reads well in a document and survives no contact with reality is a story, not a strategy. Startup strategy is a set of integrated bets about where to play and how to win, and like any bet, it can be tested before it is fully committed. The discipline of strategy testing is the act of subjecting a formulated strategy to adversarial scrutiny, identifying the assumptions on which it depends, and validating or refuting those assumptions before the company has invested years and capital into executing a plan that may rest on a false foundation. The failure mode is treating strategy as a statement of intent that the team commits to and then executes, rather than as a hypothesis that must earn its commitment through testing. Teams fall in love with their strategy, defend it against challenge, and discover only after failure that a pre-mortem would have revealed the fatal flaw.

Use this skill after a strategy has been formulated and before it has been fully committed, when stress-testing a strategic plan, when running a pre-mortem or red-team review, or when diagnosing why a coherent strategy failed in execution. The goal is to prevent the agent from committing to an untested strategy, from defending it against legitimate challenge, and from confusing coherence on paper with viability in practice.

## Core Rules

### Treat Strategy As A Hypothesis, Not A Decision

A strategy is a set of beliefs about the market, the customer, the competition, and the company's ability to win. Each of those beliefs may be wrong. Treating strategy as a settled decision commits the company to executing regardless of evidence, while treating it as a hypothesis builds in the expectation that it will be tested and may be revised.

Adopt the hypothesis stance by:

- stating the strategy's core bets explicitly, not as background but as testable claims;
- identifying which bets are most uncertain and most consequential;
- defining what evidence would confirm or refute each bet;
- setting review points at which the strategy is re-examined against accumulated evidence.

A strategy that cannot be wrong cannot be tested, and a strategy that cannot be tested is a belief.

### Run A Pre-Mortem Before Committing

A pre-mortem is the exercise of imagining that the strategy has been executed for a year and has failed, then working backward to explain why. It surfaces the failure modes the team is suppressing because it is excited about the strategy. A pre-mortem is most valuable before commitment, when the findings can still change the plan.

Run a pre-mortem by:

- assembling the team and assuming the strategy has failed;
- generating plausible reasons for the failure, especially ones the team has been avoiding;
- ranking the reasons by likelihood and consequence;
- designing tests or mitigations for the most serious ones;
- revising the strategy to address the vulnerabilities revealed.

A strategy that survives a rigorous pre-mortem is more robust than one that was never challenged. A strategy whose advocates refuse to pre-mortem it is fragile by definition.

### Identify And Test The Load-Bearing Assumptions

Every strategy rests on a few assumptions that, if false, invalidate the whole. These are the load-bearing assumptions, and they are often the ones the team is most confident about and therefore least inclined to examine. Surfacing and testing them is the core of strategy validation.

Identify load-bearing assumptions by:

- asking, for the strategy to work, what must be true about the customer, the market, the competition, and our capability;
- distinguishing assumptions with supporting evidence from assumptions held on faith;
- ranking assumptions by how much damage their falsity would cause;
- designing the cheapest test that could refute each high-risk assumption.

A strategy whose load-bearing assumptions have never been tested is a gamble disguised as a plan.

### Red-Team The Strategy From The Competitor's Perspective

A strategy that looks strong from the inside may look weak from the competitor's perspective. Red-teaming is the exercise of adopting the adversary's viewpoint and asking how the strategy would be attacked. It reveals vulnerabilities that the strategy's authors, invested in its success, cannot see.

Red-team by:

- assigning someone, ideally external or skeptical, to argue the competitor's case;
- asking how an incumbent or fast follower would neutralize the strategy;
- asking what a well-funded attacker would do to break the positioning;
- taking the criticisms seriously and designing responses or revisions.

A strategy that has never been red-teamed has been validated only by its advocates.

### Distinguish Strategic Coherence From Strategic Viability

A strategy can be internally coherent, with choices that fit together, and still fail because the choices rest on false beliefs about the world. Coherence is necessary but not sufficient. Viability requires that the coherent choices also match reality. Teams often confuse the two, treating a well-argued strategy as proven.

Test viability separately by:

- checking each strategic choice against external evidence, not just internal logic;
- asking whether the customer, market, and competitive assumptions hold;
- looking for disconfirming evidence, not just confirming anecdotes;
- accepting that coherence is the team's work and viability is the world's verdict.

A coherent strategy built on a false market assumption fails as surely as an incoherent one.

### Define Kill Criteria And Review Points In Advance

A strategy should have explicit criteria for when it is not working and review points at which those criteria are examined. Without them, a failing strategy drifts, propped up by hope and sunk cost, until the company runs out of resources. Kill criteria force honesty.

Define by:

- setting measurable indicators that the strategy is succeeding, such as segment penetration, retention, or unit economics;
- setting thresholds below which the strategy is considered failing;
- scheduling review points at which the indicators are examined honestly;
- committing in advance to the actions that follow a failing review, such as revision or pivot.

A strategy without kill criteria cannot fail; it can only run out of money.

### Separate Strategy Failure From Execution Failure

When a strategy fails, the question is whether the strategy was wrong or whether it was executed poorly. These demand different responses: a wrong strategy must be revised, while poor execution must be fixed. Confusing them leads to abandoning viable strategies or doubling down on broken ones.

Distinguish by:

- comparing what was executed to what the strategy specified;
- checking whether the strategic assumptions held even if the outcome failed;
- asking whether better execution would have produced the intended result;
- separating the quality of the plan from the quality of its implementation.

A strategy that was never actually executed cannot be judged failed; it can only be judged unexecuted.

### Validate The Strategy Against The Company's Actual Capabilities

A strategy that requires capabilities the company does not have and cannot build is not viable regardless of its market logic. Many strategies fail because they assume the company can execute at a level it cannot sustain. Capability validation is as important as market validation.

Validate by:

- listing the capabilities the strategy requires, in product, sales, operations, and capital;
- assessing honestly whether the company has or can build each;
- identifying capability gaps and whether they can be closed in the relevant timeframe;
- revising the strategy where the capability gap is structural.

A strategy that ignores capability constraints is aspirational, not executable.

## Common Traps

### Committing To An Untested Strategy

Treating strategy as a decision rather than a hypothesis commits the company to executing regardless of evidence. Test before committing.

### Refusing To Pre-Mortem

A strategy whose advocates will not imagine its failure is fragile. Pre-mortems surface the flaws the team is suppressing.

### Untested Load-Bearing Assumptions

The assumptions that matter most are often the most confidently held and least examined. Test the ones whose falsity would be fatal.

### Validating Only From The Inside

A strategy that looks strong to its authors may be weak to a competitor. Red-team from the adversary's perspective.

### Confusing Coherence With Viability

Internal logical consistency does not prove the strategy matches reality. Coherence is necessary but not sufficient.

### No Kill Criteria

A strategy without explicit failure thresholds and review points drifts until resources are exhausted. Define when it is not working.

### Blaming Execution For Strategy Failure

A wrong strategy fails regardless of execution quality. Distinguish strategic error from implementation error before deciding the response.

### Ignoring Capability Constraints

A strategy that requires capabilities the company cannot build is aspirational, not executable. Validate against actual capabilities.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The strategy is treated as a hypothesis with explicit, testable bets rather than as a settled decision.
- [ ] A pre-mortem was conducted before commitment, and the vulnerabilities it revealed were addressed.
- [ ] The load-bearing assumptions were identified, ranked by consequence, and tested with the cheapest refutation possible.
- [ ] The strategy was red-teamed from a competitor's or adversary's perspective, and the criticisms were taken seriously.
- [ ] Strategic coherence is distinguished from strategic viability, and both are validated separately.
- [ ] Kill criteria and review points are defined in advance, with committed actions following a failing review.
- [ ] Strategy failure and execution failure are distinguished when diagnosing why a plan did not produce its intended result.
- [ ] The strategy has been validated against the company's actual capabilities, and capability gaps are addressed or the strategy revised.
- [ ] No load-bearing assumption is being held on faith alone without a plan to test it.
- [ ] The team can articulate what evidence would cause it to revise or abandon the strategy, and is not defending it against legitimate challenge.
