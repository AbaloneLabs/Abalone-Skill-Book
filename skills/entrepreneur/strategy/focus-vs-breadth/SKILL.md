# Focus Versus Breadth

A central tension in startup strategy is between focus, concentrating resources on a narrow wedge to win decisively, and breadth, spreading across segments, features, or markets to capture more and reduce dependence. Both have merits and risks: focus creates depth but risks dependence on one bet; breadth creates optionality but risks spreading thin and winning nowhere. The right balance depends on the venture's stage, resources, and evidence, and it shifts over time. Choosing between focus and breadth is the discipline of matching scope to what the venture can actually execute and defend.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "should we focus or diversify", "how narrow should our target be", "should we add more features or markets", "when should a startup expand", or "how should an entrepreneur balance focus and breadth". The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to either premature expansion or rigid over-concentration and from ignoring how the right scope depends on stage and evidence.

## Core Rules

### Default To Focus Early, Especially Pre-Fit

Early-stage ventures, especially before product-market fit, should generally focus narrowly: on one customer segment, one problem, one wedge. Focus concentrates scarce resources on finding fit, and breadth before fit dilutes effort across bets none of which gets enough attention. Default to focus until evidence justifies expansion.

### Use A Wedge To Win, Then Expand

A common sound pattern is to win a wedge, a narrow segment or use case where the venture can be decisively best, and then expand from that beachhead into adjacent segments. The wedge creates a foothold and proof; expansion builds on it. This sequencing concentrates effort while preserving a growth path.

### Recognize When Breadth Reduces Risk

Breadth can reduce risk by avoiding dependence on a single customer, segment, channel, or feature. When one bet carries concentration risk, such as a single dominant customer, diversification is prudent. Recognize when concentration has become risky and breadth reduces exposure.

### Avoid Premature Expansion

Expanding before the wedge is won, before fit is clear or the beachhead is defensible, is a common error that spreads resources thin and wins nowhere. Resist the pull of apparent opportunity in adjacent areas until the core is secure. Premature expansion trades depth for breadth prematurely.

### Avoid Over-Concentration That Creates Fragility

Conversely, excessive concentration can create fragility: dependence on one customer, one channel, one feature, or one market exposes the venture to shocks. Monitor concentration, and broaden when a single point of failure threatens survival. Over-concentration trades robustness for depth beyond what is wise.

### Match Scope To Resources And Execution Capacity

Scope must match what the venture can actually execute with its resources and team. A scope that exceeds execution capacity produces shallow efforts everywhere. Match ambition to capacity, and expand scope only as capacity grows.

### Let Evidence Drive Expansion Decisions

Expansion should be driven by evidence, demand signals, retention, and unit economics in the target area, not by ambition or restlessness. Expand where evidence shows a real opportunity, and avoid expanding on hope. Evidence-driven expansion is disciplined; ambition-driven expansion is gambling.

### Consider The Cost Of Context Switching

Pursuing multiple segments, features, or markets imposes context-switching costs on a small team, reducing effectiveness in each. Factor these costs into breadth decisions, because the apparent gains from breadth often shrink once switching costs are counted. Focus has hidden value in reducing switching.

### Sequence Focus And Breadth Over The Venture's Life

The right balance shifts over time: focus to find fit, broaden as the wedge is won, refocus when breadth dilutes, and so on. Think of focus and breadth as a sequence over the venture's life, not a one-time choice. Dynamic scope management is healthier than a static decision.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Strategic Judgment

Focus-versus-breadth decisions carry real risk and benefit from diverse input and experienced advisors. This skill provides strategic framing; for consequential scope commitments, seek advisor and board input and validate with evidence.

## Common Traps

### Premature Expansion

Broadening before the wedge is won spreads resources thin and wins nowhere.

### Over-Concentration

Dependence on one customer, channel, or feature creates fragility.

### Ambition-Driven Expansion

Expanding on hope rather than evidence is gambling, not strategy.

### Ignoring Context-Switching Costs

Apparent breadth gains shrink once switching costs on a small team are counted.

### Static Scope Decision

Treating focus or breadth as a one-time choice rather than a shifting balance.

### Scope Exceeding Execution Capacity

Ambition uncalibrated to the team's capacity produces shallow efforts everywhere.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Early-stage scope defaults to focus until evidence justifies expansion.
- [ ] A wedge is identified to win before expanding to adjacent areas.
- [ ] Concentration risk is monitored, and breadth is used to reduce fragility.
- [ ] Expansion is resisted until the core wedge is secure.
- [ ] Over-concentration that creates single points of failure is avoided.
- [ ] Scope matches the venture's resources and execution capacity.
- [ ] Expansion decisions are driven by evidence, not ambition or restlessness.
- [ ] Context-switching costs are factored into breadth decisions.
- [ ] Focus and breadth are sequenced dynamically over the venture's life.
- [ ] Consequential scope commitments draw on advisor and board input and evidence.
