# Moat Building

A moat is a structural feature that allows a company to defend its profits and market position against competition over time, and it is the difference between a business that compounds value and one whose advantages are competed away. Most startups have no moat at founding; they have a temporary wedge, a feature, a timing advantage, or an insight that attracts customers but that competitors can replicate. The judgment problem is distinguishing a real moat from a temporary advantage, understanding which sources of defensibility are available to a given business, and sequencing moat-building so that the company invests in durable advantage rather than mistaking early traction for permanent strength. Agents tend to fail by assuming novelty is a moat, by conflating growth with defensibility, and by prescribing moats that the business cannot actually build.

Use this skill when analyzing, designing, or advising on competitive moats for a startup. The goal is an honest assessment of defensibility and a deliberate plan to build durable advantage where it is achievable.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish A Real Moat From A Temporary Advantage

Most early advantages are temporary, not structural. A clever feature, a first-mover position, a viral launch, or a charismatic founder can drive early growth, but none of these defend profits against determined competition. A real moat is structural: it makes it hard for competitors to replicate the position even when they understand it.

Distinguish by asking:

- can a well-funded competitor copy this advantage within one to two years;
- does the advantage compound with scale, or does it erode;
- is the advantage tied to the company's structure, or to a moment in time;
- would the advantage survive the founder's departure or a market shift.

A feature is not a moat. First-mover status is rarely a moat. Network effects, switching costs, scale economies, and brand are moats because they are structural and compounding.

### Understand The Canonical Sources Of Defensibility

The well-established sources of competitive moat are limited and specific, and each applies to different business models. Knowing them allows an honest assessment of what a given company can build.

The canonical moats include:

- network effects: value grows as users or participants increase, making the leader increasingly hard to displace;
- switching costs: the cost, effort, or risk of moving to a competitor keeps customers locked in;
- scale economies: unit cost advantages that come from scale, allowing the leader to underprice competitors;
- brand and trust: durable customer preference and recognition that commands premium and loyalty;
- intellectual property: patents, trade secrets, or proprietary technology that competitors cannot legally or practically replicate;
- regulatory or structural barriers: licenses, compliance, or capital requirements that block entry.

Few companies build all of these. The task is to identify which are achievable for the specific business and invest in those.

### Assess Whether The Business Model Can Support A Moat

Not every business can build a durable moat, and pretending otherwise wastes effort. A services business, a commodity product, or a feature-without-network-effect may have no path to structural defensibility, and the honest assessment is that defensibility must come from elsewhere, such as brand, scale, or a pivot in model.

Assess by:

- identifying which canonical moats are structurally available to the business;
- being honest about which are not achievable, rather than forcing a narrative;
- considering whether the business model itself must evolve to unlock defensibility;
- recognizing that some valuable businesses operate without strong moats and compete on execution.

An honest moat assessment is more valuable than a flattering one. A business with no moat can still succeed on execution, but it should not be told it is defensible when it is not.

### Sequence Moat-Building Against The Company's Stage

Moats are not built on day one; they are built as the company reaches the scale and position that make them achievable. A seed-stage company cannot build scale economies, but it can begin building network effects or switching costs. Sequencing matters.

Sequence by:

- identifying the moat the company ultimately aims to build;
- determining what must be true, in terms of scale, data, or integration, for that moat to activate;
- building the foundations of the moat early, even before it is fully active;
- avoiding premature investment in moats that require scale the company does not yet have.

A moat built out of sequence wastes resources. Build the foundation early, and let the moat activate as the company reaches the necessary scale.

### Recognize That Network Effects Are Powerful But Rare

Network effects are among the strongest moats, because the leader becomes increasingly hard to displace as the network grows, but they are also rare and often misidentified. A product having users is not a network effect; a network effect exists when each new user increases the value for existing users.

Recognize network effects by:

- distinguishing true network effects, where value grows with users, from scale effects, where cost falls with volume;
- identifying whether the network effect is direct, indirect, or data-based;
- understanding the conditions under which the network effect activates and strengthens;
- being honest about whether the business model produces a network effect at all.

Misidentifying a network effect leads to investing in growth that does not compound defensibility. Confirm the mechanism before betting on it.

### Beware Moats That Depend On The Founder Or A Moment

Some advantages are tied to a founder's relationships, charisma, or a specific market moment, and these are not moats because they do not transfer and do not persist. A business whose defensibility depends on the founder is fragile.

Beware by:

- distinguishing founder-dependent advantages from structural ones;
- building systems, brand, and relationships that transfer beyond the founder;
- recognizing that timing advantages erode as competitors enter;
- planning for the defensibility that will exist when the founder is less central.

A moat must survive the founder's reduced involvement and the passage of time. If it does not, it is not a moat.

### Defend The Moat Actively, Not Passively

A moat is not a static defense; competitors will attack it, and it must be reinforced and extended over time. A company that builds a moat and then assumes it is permanent loses it to determined competition.

Defend actively by:

- investing in the moat's strength even after it is established;
- extending the moat into adjacent areas as the market evolves;
- monitoring competitive threats to the moat and responding;
- recognizing that moats erode without reinforcement.

Moats are defended through ongoing investment, not assumed through existence. Treat defensibility as an active practice.

### Use The Moat Assessment To Inform Strategy

A moat assessment is not an academic exercise; it informs strategy, pricing, investment, and even whether to proceed. A company with no path to a moat must compete on execution and cost; one with a strong moat can invest in growth and premium.

Use the assessment to:

- prioritize investment in the areas that build durable advantage;
- set pricing that reflects, or builds, defensibility;
- decide whether to pursue scale, integration, or network growth based on the moat;
- inform fundraising and exit strategy based on the defensibility of the business.

The moat assessment should change what the company does. If it does not, it was not a real assessment.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Novelty Is A Moat

A clever idea or feature attracts customers but does not defend profits. Novelty is temporary.

### Conflating Growth With Defensibility

Fast growth without a structural moat is competed away. Growth is not defensibility.

### Prescribing Moats The Business Cannot Build

Not every business can build every moat. Assess honestly what is achievable.

### Building Moats Out Of Sequence

Scale-dependent moats cannot be built at seed stage. Sequence moat-building to the company's stage.

### Misidentifying Network Effects

Having users is not a network effect. Confirm the mechanism before betting on it.

### Founder-Dependent Defensibility

Advantages tied to the founder do not transfer. Build structural moats.

### Passive Moat Assumption

Moats erode without reinforcement. Defend them actively.

### Moat Assessment Without Strategic Action

An assessment that does not change strategy is not useful. Use it to inform investment and pricing.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The company's advantages are honestly distinguished between temporary wedges and structural moats.
- [ ] The canonical moats relevant to the business are identified, with honest assessment of achievability.
- [ ] The business model is assessed for whether it can support a durable moat, without forcing a narrative.
- [ ] Moat-building is sequenced against the company's stage, with foundations built early and activation at scale.
- [ ] Network effects, if claimed, are confirmed as true mechanisms where value grows with users, not just scale.
- [ ] Defensibility does not depend solely on the founder or a market moment.
- [ ] The moat is defended and extended actively, not assumed to be permanent.
- [ ] The moat assessment informs investment, pricing, and strategic priorities.
- [ ] Competitors' ability to replicate the advantage within one to two years is honestly assessed.
- [ ] The company is investing in durable advantage, not mistaking early traction for permanent strength.
