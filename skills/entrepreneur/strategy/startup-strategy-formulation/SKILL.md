# Startup Strategy Formulation

Strategy is the integrated set of choices that defines where a venture will play, how it will win, and what it will deliberately not do. Founders often confuse strategy with vision, goals, or a feature roadmap, producing documents that inspire but do not guide hard choices. A sound startup strategy is specific enough to make some opportunities wrong, coherent across its elements, and grounded in the venture's actual market, customer, and competitive reality. Formulating strategy is the discipline of making integrated choices under uncertainty, not articulating aspirations.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what is my startup's strategy", "how do I create a strategy", "where should we play and how should we win", "what should our strategic priorities be", or "how should an entrepreneur formulate strategy". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing vision statements or goal lists and from ignoring the integrated, choice-making nature of real strategy.

## Core Rules

### Define Where To Play And How To Win

Strategy begins with two core questions: where to play, which customers, segments, geographies, and channels, and how to win, the advantage that lets the venture win there. A strategy that answers only one is incomplete. Define both explicitly, because the pairing is what makes a strategy coherent.

### Make Choices That Render Some Opportunities Wrong

A real strategy makes choices that foreclose alternatives: it says no to some customers, channels, or features. If a strategy is compatible with pursuing anything, it is not a strategy. Test the strategy by asking what it rules out; if nothing, it needs sharpening.

### Ground Strategy In Market, Customer, And Competitive Reality

Strategy must be grounded in the actual market size and dynamics, real customer needs, and the competitive landscape. A strategy built on assumptions rather than evidence is a wish. Ground the choices in validated reality, and revise as that reality clarifies.

### Ensure Coherence Across The Elements

The elements of strategy, target customer, value proposition, competitive advantage, go-to-market, and business model, must reinforce each other. Incoherence, a premium product with a low-price channel, or a mass-market target with a niche feature set, undermines execution. Check that the elements fit together as a system.

### Distinguish Strategy From Vision, Goals, And Roadmaps

Vision describes the desired future; goals set targets; roadmaps sequence work; strategy defines the integrated choices that make the vision achievable. Conflating these produces inspiring but non-actionable documents. Keep strategy distinct as the choice-making layer beneath vision and above execution.

### Account For The Venture's Stage And Resources

Strategy must fit the venture's stage and resources: an early-stage strategy centers on finding fit and a wedge; a growth-stage strategy centers on scaling what works. A strategy that ignores stage and resources is unrealistic. Calibrate ambition to what the venture can actually execute.

### Identify And Build On A Defensible Advantage

Strategy should identify the advantage the venture will build and defend: network effects, scale, brand, switching costs, data, IP, or distribution. Without a defensible advantage, success invites imitation that erodes returns. Make the advantage explicit and plan how it compounds.

### Plan For Competitive Response

Competitors respond to a venture's moves, and strategy must anticipate how. A strategy that assumes competitors stand still fails when they react. Consider how incumbents and new entrants will respond, and build that into the plan.

### Revisit Strategy As Reality Evolves

Strategy is not set once; it evolves as the market, customers, and competitive landscape clarify. Revisit the strategy at major milestones and when evidence contradicts assumptions. Static strategy becomes wrong strategy as the world changes.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Strategy formulation benefits from diverse input, advisors, and sometimes professional facilitation, especially for high-stakes directions. This skill provides strategic framing; for consequential strategic commitments, seek experienced advisors and validate with the board.

## Common Traps

### Vision Mistaken For Strategy

Aspirational statements that do not guide hard choices are not strategy.

### Strategy Compatible With Anything

If nothing is ruled out, the strategy is not sharp enough to guide decisions.

### Unvalidated Assumptions

Strategy built on untested market or customer beliefs is a wish.

### Incoherent Elements

Target, value prop, channel, and model that do not reinforce each other undermine execution.

### Ignoring Stage And Resources

Ambition uncalibrated to what the venture can execute is unrealistic.

### No Defensible Advantage

Success without defensibility invites imitation that erodes returns.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The strategy defines both where to play and how to win.
- [ ] The choices render some opportunities wrong; the strategy is not compatible with anything.
- [ ] The strategy is grounded in validated market, customer, and competitive reality.
- [ ] The elements, target, value prop, advantage, channel, model, are coherent as a system.
- [ ] Strategy is distinguished from vision, goals, and roadmaps.
- [ ] The strategy fits the venture's stage and resources.
- [ ] A defensible advantage is identified with a plan for it to compound.
- [ ] Competitive response is anticipated and built into the plan.
- [ ] The strategy is revisited as reality evolves.
- [ ] Consequential strategic commitments draw on diverse input and experienced advisors.
