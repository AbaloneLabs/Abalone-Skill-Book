# Startup Market Research

Market research for a startup is not the same as corporate market research. A large company researches to optimize within a known market; a startup researches to discover whether a market exists at all, who is in it, and whether it can be reached. The danger is two-sided: doing no research means building blind, while doing too much research means burning runway on reports that produce comfortable slides but no decisions. Good startup research is fast, decision-oriented, and biased toward primary signal from real customers.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what market research should I do", "how do I research my market", "do I have enough market information", or "how should an entrepreneur research a market before building". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending exhaustive desk research that delays action or from skipping research entirely and building on assumption.

## Core Rules

### Start From A Specific Decision

Research without a decision attached is tourism. Before researching, name the specific question that must be answered and the decision that hangs on it. Is the market big enough to enter? Is the segment reachable? Will customers pay? Is the channel affordable? Each question drives different research.

If you cannot name the decision the research informs, the research is not yet well-scoped.

### Balance Primary And Secondary Research

Secondary research, using existing reports, data, and publications, is fast and cheap and is good for sizing, context, and competitive landscape. Primary research, talking to and observing real customers, is slower and more expensive but is the only way to learn problem language, workflow, budget, and willingness to pay.

Use secondary research to frame the market and primary research to test the specific assumptions that determine viability. Neither alone is enough.

### Prioritize The Riskiest Unknowns

Not all research questions matter equally. Rank research by how lethal the unknown is if it turns out wrong, and by how uncertain it currently is. Research the combination of high-lethal and high-uncertain first.

Typical early lethal unknowns: does the segment have the problem, is it reachable, will it pay, can it be served profitably, is the channel affordable, does regulation permit the model.

### Time-Box Research To Runway

Research consumes runway. A research plan that takes six months is a plan to spend six months of runway before acting. Scope research to the time and money available, and accept that some questions will remain partially answered. Perfect research is the enemy of a venture that must ship before the money runs out.

### Triangulate Rather Than Trust Single Sources

Any single source, report, dataset, or interview can mislead. Triangulate important conclusions across multiple independent sources and methods. If three different approaches converge, confidence rises; if they diverge, the gap is itself a finding worth investigating.

### Distinguish Fact From Forecast From Opinion

Market reports mix historical fact, analyst forecast, and opinion. Treat each differently. Historical data is most reliable but may be outdated; forecasts embed analyst assumptions that can be wrong; opinion is weak evidence. Read sources critically and note what kind of claim each rests on.

### Capture Source Quality And Recency

Record for each source what it is, who produced it, when, and what interest they may have. A vendor-sponsored report, an analyst forecast, and government data deserve different weight. Recency matters in fast-moving markets; a two-year-old figure may already be obsolete.

### Translate Research Into Hypotheses To Test

Research should produce testable hypotheses, not a folder of reports. Convert each important finding into a hypothesis about customer behavior, willingness to pay, or channel cost, and plan the cheapest test of it. Research that does not lead to a test or a decision has not earned its cost.

### Watch For Founder Bias In Interpretation

Founders read research favorably, remembering confirming data and explaining away disconfirming data. Counter this by writing down disconfirming findings explicitly and having a neutral party review the conclusions.

### Acknowledge Limits And Personal Risk

Research informs but does not guarantee. Markets shift, and research can be wrong. Over-investing in research delays action and consumes runway; this is a personal risk for the founder. This skill does not replace professional advice for markets with regulatory, legal, or financial sensitivity.

## Common Traps

### Research As Procrastination

Endless desk research feels productive but can be a way to avoid the risk of building and testing.

### Trusting A Single Slick Report

One analyst forecast, especially a vendor-sponsored one, can anchor a false market view.

### Skipping Primary Research

Secondary data cannot reveal problem language, workflow, or willingness to pay; only customers can.

### Researching The Wrong Question

Elegant research into market size is useless if the lethal unknown is channel affordability.

### Reading Research Favorably

Confirmation bias turns ambiguous data into apparent proof.

### No Decision Attached

Research that changes no plan is effort spent on comfort, not learning.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each research effort is tied to a specific decision the founder must make.
- [ ] Primary and secondary research are balanced, with primary used to test viability assumptions.
- [ ] Research is prioritized by combined lethality and uncertainty of the unknown.
- [ ] Research is time-boxed to available runway and money.
- [ ] Important conclusions are triangulated across independent sources and methods.
- [ ] Facts, forecasts, and opinions are distinguished, with source quality and recency recorded.
- [ ] Findings are translated into testable hypotheses with planned cheap tests.
- [ ] Disconfirming data is recorded explicitly and reviewed for bias.
- [ ] Research is scoped to the startup's real constraints, not to corporate-style completeness.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a folder of reports as sufficient market understanding without decisions and tests.
