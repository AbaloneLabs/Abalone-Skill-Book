# Secondary Data For Startups

Secondary data is information that already exists: industry reports, government statistics, trade association data, analyst forecasts, public filings, and published research. For a resource-constrained startup, it is the fastest way to frame a market, size an opportunity, and understand context before spending on primary research. But secondary data is also stale, generic, sometimes biased, and often produced for a different purpose than the founder's question. Used uncritically, it produces confident-looking slides built on shaky ground.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "where do I find market data", "is this report reliable", "how do I use secondary research", or "how should an entrepreneur use existing market data". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating any published number as ground truth and from overpaying for reports that add comfort but not decision value.

## Core Rules

### Match The Data To The Specific Question

Secondary data is most useful when it directly informs a decision. Before searching, state the specific question: how big is the reachable segment, how fast is it growing, what do customers currently spend, who are the incumbents. Then seek data that speaks to that question, not data that simply looks impressive.

Generic industry reports rarely answer a startup's specific segment question precisely.

### Evaluate Source Quality And Bias

Not all sources are equal. Judge each by who produced it, why, when, and what interest they have.

Consider:

- government and statistical data: usually neutral but coarse and dated;
- trade associations: credible on member counts but may overstate health;
- analyst firms: polished but expensive and forecast-dependent;
- vendor-sponsored reports: may serve the sponsor's narrative;
- press and blogs: fast but often unsourced or sensational;
- academic research: rigorous but may be narrow or old.

Weight sources by credibility, independence, and relevance, not by production quality.

### Check Recency And Velocity

Markets move. A figure from three years ago may already misrepresent today's reality, especially in fast-changing categories. Record the date of every data point and prefer the most recent credible source. Where the market is moving quickly, treat older data as a lower bound on confidence.

### Prefer Granular Over Aggregate

Aggregate industry figures hide the segment-level reality a startup actually targets. A growing industry can contain a shrinking segment, and vice versa. Seek the most granular data available: by segment, geography, company size, or behavior, and beware of conclusions drawn from too-coarse aggregates.

### Triangulate Across Independent Sources

A single source can be wrong or biased. For important conclusions, find at least two independent sources using different methods. Convergence increases confidence; divergence is a signal to investigate the discrepancy rather than pick the favorable number.

### Separate Historical Fact From Forecast

Historical data describes what happened; forecasts describe what someone expects. Forecasts embed assumptions that may not hold. Treat forecasts as scenarios, not facts, and note the assumptions behind them. A forecast is only as good as the model and the assumptions that produced it.

### Adjust For Definition Mismatches

Reports define categories differently. One report's "small business" may be under fifty employees; another's may be under five hundred. Before comparing figures across sources, confirm the definitions align, or the comparison is meaningless.

### Convert Data Into Decisions And Tests

Secondary data should inform a decision or generate a hypothesis to test with primary research. If a dataset changes no decision and produces no testable hypothesis, it has not earned its cost. End each research session by stating what was learned, what decision it informs, and what should now be tested with customers.

### Avoid Overpaying For Comfort

Expensive reports can feel authoritative but often repackage public data with polish. Before buying, check whether the same conclusion is reachable from free or low-cost sources. Pay for unique, decision-critical data, not for the feeling of rigor.

### Acknowledge Limits And Personal Risk

Secondary data is a starting point, not a substitute for primary customer signal. Over-reliance on reports can create false confidence that misleads investment and runway decisions. This skill does not replace professional advice for data used in regulated or financial decisions.

## Common Traps

### Treating Any Published Number As Fact

Reports mix fact, forecast, and opinion; uncritical use builds on sand.

### Stale Data In Fast Markets

Old figures can badly misrepresent current reality in quickly moving categories.

### Aggregate Hiding Segment Reality

Industry growth can mask the segment shrinkage that dooms a focused entrant.

### Single-Source Confidence

One source, especially a biased one, can anchor a false market view.

### Definition Mismatches

Comparing figures across sources with different category definitions produces nonsense.

### Paying For Comfort

Expensive reports that repackage public data add feeling of rigor, not decision value.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each data search is tied to a specific decision-relevant question.
- [ ] Source quality, independence, bias, and recency are evaluated and recorded.
- [ ] The most granular available data is preferred over coarse aggregates.
- [ ] Important conclusions are triangulated across independent sources and methods.
- [ ] Historical fact is distinguished from forecast, with forecast assumptions noted.
- [ ] Category definitions are checked before comparing figures across sources.
- [ ] Findings are converted into decisions or testable hypotheses, not just filed.
- [ ] Paid reports are justified by unique decision-critical content, not by comfort.
- [ ] Recency is weighted appropriately for the speed of the market.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting secondary data as sufficient without primary validation of the riskiest assumptions.
