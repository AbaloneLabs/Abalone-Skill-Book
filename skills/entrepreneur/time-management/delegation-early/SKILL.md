# Delegation Early

Delegation is often treated as a late-stage luxury, something to do once the company is bigger and there is money for help. In practice, failing to delegate early is one of the most common growth limiters in a startup: the founder becomes the bottleneck for every decision, the venture cannot scale past what one person can hold, and the team never develops the capability the company needs. Delegation is not abdication; it is the deliberate transfer of authority and work to others with the right context, support, and accountability. Building the habit early, even when it feels slower than doing it yourself, is what creates the capacity for the venture to grow beyond the founder.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should I delegate", "how do I delegate as a founder", "when should I start delegating", "how do I let go of control", or "why am I the bottleneck". The goal is to prevent the agent from validating the instinct to hold onto everything until the company is bigger, or from treating delegation as simply handing off tasks without the context, authority, and accountability that make it work.

## Core Rules

### Treat Delegation As A Growth Mechanism, Not A Convenience

Delegation is how the venture builds capacity beyond the founder. Each piece of work delegated creates room for the founder to focus on higher-leverage activities and develops a team member's capability. Framing delegation as an investment in capacity, rather than a cost or convenience, changes when and how aggressively you do it. The right time to start delegating is earlier than feels comfortable.

### Delegate Outcomes, Not Tasks

Effective delegation transfers ownership of an outcome, not just the execution of a task. Handing someone a task to execute exactly as you would is not delegation; it is using them as an extension of your hands. Delegating an outcome, with the authority to decide how to achieve it, develops judgment and frees the founder from the details. Specify the result and the constraints, then let the owner own the how.

### Provide Context, Authority, And Accountability Together

Delegation fails when any of three elements is missing: context, why the work matters and the background; authority, the power to make the relevant decisions; and accountability, how success is measured and reviewed. Delegating responsibility without authority creates frustration; delegating without context produces wrong decisions; delegating without accountability creates drift. All three must travel together.

### Start With The Reversible And The Teachable

Begin delegating work that is reversible, so mistakes are recoverable, and teachable, so the process can be transferred. This builds trust on both sides and develops the team member's capability without risking the venture. Reserve for yourself, for now, the irreversible, high-stakes, and uniquely founder-judgment work, and delegate the rest progressively as trust and capability grow.

### Accept The Short-Term Cost Of Slower And Worse

Early delegation is slower and produces worse results than doing it yourself, because the other person is learning. Accept this short-term cost as the price of long-term capacity. Refusing to pay it keeps the founder as the permanent bottleneck and the team permanently dependent. The cost is real and temporary; the capacity gain compounds.

### Build Systems And Documentation That Make Delegation Possible

Delegation is far easier when the work is systematized: documented processes, defined standards, checklists, and shared context reduce the friction of handoff and the risk of error. Invest in lightweight systems early, not as bureaucracy but as the infrastructure that lets work move off the founder. Undocumented work that lives only in the founder's head cannot be delegated.

### Resist The Control And Quality Trap

The most common barrier to delegation is the belief that no one will do it as well as the founder, which is often true initially and irrelevant strategically. If you only keep work you do best, you never build anyone else's capability and you remain the ceiling. Accept that early delegated work will be imperfect and prioritize growth of capability over short-term quality.

### Diagnose Reluctance Honestly

Reluctance to delegate has several distinct sources: fear of losing control, lack of trust, perceived cost, identity tied to being the doer, or genuine unclear process. Diagnose which is operating, because the remedies differ. Fear and identity require internal work; unclear process requires systematization; lack of trust requires hiring or development. Treating all reluctance as the same prevents progress.

### Create Feedback Loops And Review Points

Delegation without review is abdication. Establish lightweight feedback loops: regular check-ins, defined metrics, and review points where the delegatee gets feedback and the founder catches drift early. The goal is enough oversight to catch problems without so much that you have not actually delegated. Calibrate oversight down as trust and competence grow.

### Delegate Decision Authority, Not Just Work

The highest-leverage delegation is of decision authority, not just execution. As the team matures, delegate the power to decide within a domain, with clear guardrails, so the founder is no longer the decision bottleneck. This is harder and scarier than delegating tasks, but it is what truly scales the venture beyond one person.

## Common Traps

### Waiting Until The Company Is Bigger

Deferring delegation until you can afford help ensures the company never gets big enough to afford help, because the founder remains the bottleneck.

### Delegating Tasks, Not Outcomes

Handing off execution while retaining all decisions develops no capability and frees no real attention.

### Missing Authority Or Accountability

Delegating responsibility without the power to decide or the metrics to be held to creates frustration and drift.

### Refusing The Short-Term Quality Cost

Insisting delegated work match the founder's quality immediately prevents capability from ever developing.

### Work Living Only In The Founder's Head

Undocumented, unsystematized work cannot be effectively delegated and keeps the founder indispensable.

### Control And Identity Resistance

Holding work because being the doer is tied to identity or control keeps the founder as the permanent ceiling.

### Abdication Masquerading As Delegation

Handing off work with no context, oversight, or review is not delegation; it is abandonment that produces predictable failures.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Delegation is framed as investment in venture capacity, not a convenience, and started earlier than feels comfortable.
- [ ] Outcomes, not just tasks, are delegated, with the authority to decide the how.
- [ ] Context, authority, and accountability are transferred together for each delegation.
- [ ] Delegation begins with reversible, teachable work and expands as trust and capability grow.
- [ ] The short-term cost of slower, worse early delegated work is accepted as the price of long-term capacity.
- [ ] Lightweight systems and documentation exist to reduce handoff friction and error risk.
- [ ] The control and quality trap is recognized; capability growth is prioritized over short-term perfection.
- [ ] Reluctance to delegate is diagnosed by source, fear, trust, process, identity, and addressed accordingly.
- [ ] Feedback loops and review points provide oversight without negating the delegation.
- [ ] Decision authority, not only execution, is progressively delegated as the team matures to remove the founder as decision bottleneck.
