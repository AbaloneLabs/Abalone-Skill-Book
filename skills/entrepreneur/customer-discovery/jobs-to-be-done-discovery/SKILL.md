# Jobs To Be Done Discovery

Customers do not buy products; they hire them to make progress in a particular circumstance. This is the core of jobs-to-be-done thinking, and it reframes discovery away from features and demographics toward the underlying motivation. Two products that look identical can serve different jobs, and two customers with identical demographics can hire the same product for different reasons. Understanding the job clarifies what you are really competing with, why people switch, and what would make them switch to you.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what job is my product hired for", "why do customers really buy this", "how do I use jobs-to-be-done for my startup", or "how should an entrepreneur discover customer motivation". The goal is to prevent the agent from describing products by their features while missing the progress the customer is trying to make.

## Core Rules

### Frame The Job As Progress, Not Feature

A job is the progress a person is trying to make in a specific circumstance, not a task list. State the job from the customer's perspective, beginning with their situation and desired outcome.

Compare "users need a scheduling tool" with "when a clinic owner is juggling patient no-shows and staff schedules, help me keep the calendar full without burning hours on phone calls". The second is a job; the first is a category.

### Capture The Circumstance, Not Just The Outcome

Jobs are situational. The same person can have different jobs in different contexts. Specify when and where the struggle happens, what just happened before, and what is at stake.

Circumstance includes:

- the triggering event;
- the time pressure;
- who else is involved;
- what is at risk;
- what the person has already tried.

The circumstance often reveals the real job more than the requested feature does.

### Distinguish Functional, Emotional, And Social Jobs

People hire products for more than function. They also hire them to feel a certain way and to be perceived a certain way by others.

For each job consider:

- functional: what practical progress is sought;
- emotional: how does the person want to feel during and after;
- social: how do they want to be seen by peers, bosses, family, or customers.

Ignoring emotional and social jobs explains many purchases that look irrational on function alone.

### Identify What The Customer Is Hiring Now

The competition for a job is not only similar products. It is everything else the customer could hire to make the same progress, including spreadsheets, manual work, doing nothing, hiring a person, or repurposing a general tool.

Mapping current hires reveals the real switching cost and the bar your solution must clear.

### Understand The Push, Pull, Anxiety, And Inertia Of Switching

People switch jobs-to-be-done for identifiable forces. Use them to diagnose why customers do or do not adopt.

- push: dissatisfaction with the current way;
- pull: attraction of the new way;
- anxiety: uncertainty about the new way;
- inertia: habit, sunk cost, and integration holding the current way in place.

For switching to happen, push and pull must exceed anxiety and inertia. A great product can still fail if inertia is strong.

### Discover Jobs Through Struggle Stories

The richest job data comes from stories of recent struggle or switching. Ask customers to walk through the last time they faced the circumstance, what they did, and how they decided.

Look for:

- the moment they decided the current way was no longer acceptable;
- what they considered and rejected;
- what finally made them act;
- what would have stopped them.

### Avoid Aggregating Jobs Too Early

A product can serve multiple jobs, but early ventures win by serving one job exceptionally well. Identify the dominant job before trying to serve secondary ones, because serving several jobs averagely usually serves none well.

### Connect The Job To Willingness To Pay

The value of a job is related to the stakes. A job tied to revenue, risk, reputation, or significant time loss supports higher price and faster purchase than a job tied to mild convenience. Confirm the job you target is one customers will pay to make progress on.

### Acknowledge Limits And Personal Risk

Jobs-to-be-done is a lens, not a complete strategy. It must combine with reachability, economics, and founder fit. This skill does not replace professional advice where the job touches regulated or safety-critical outcomes.

## Common Traps

### Describing Features As Jobs

"Send messages faster" is a feature; "stay close to a remote team without micromanaging" is a job. Confusing them hides motivation.

### Ignoring Emotional And Social Jobs

Purchases that look irrational often make sense once emotional and social progress is included.

### Assuming The Obvious Competitor

The real competition for a job may be a spreadsheet, a person, or doing nothing.

### Treating One Job As Universal

Different customers hire the same product for different jobs; assuming one universal job flattens the market.

### Underestimating Inertia

Even strong push and pull lose to habit and switching cost if inertia is ignored.

### Over-Aggregating Jobs To Please Everyone

Serving many jobs averagely usually defeats serving one job remarkably.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The job is framed as progress in a specific circumstance, not as a feature or category.
- [ ] The triggering circumstance, time pressure, stakeholders, and stakes are captured.
- [ ] Functional, emotional, and social dimensions of the job are considered.
- [ ] Current hires, including non-product alternatives, are identified as the real competition.
- [ ] Push, pull, anxiety, and inertia forces are analyzed for the switching decision.
- [ ] Jobs are discovered through recent struggle and switching stories, not hypothetical questions.
- [ ] A dominant job is chosen for early focus before secondary jobs.
- [ ] The job is connected to willingness to pay through the stakes involved.
- [ ] Inertia and switching cost are weighed, not assumed away.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting features or demographics as the customer's real motivation.
