# Customer Interview Techniques

Customer interviews are the most common and most mishandled validation tool. Done well, they reveal problems, workarounds, budgets, and decision processes that no survey could surface. Done poorly, they manufacture false encouragement: customers say what the founder wants to hear, and the founder hears what they hoped to hear. The difference is almost entirely in technique. The goal of an interview is not to confirm the idea but to discover how the customer actually thinks and behaves.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what should I ask in a customer interview", "how do I run a discovery interview", "why are my interviews not giving me useful signal", or "how should an entrepreneur interview potential customers". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing leading question scripts that will only generate politeness.

## Core Rules

### Interview For Past Behavior, Not Future Intent

People are poor predictors of their own future actions and generous with hypothetical approval. Anchor the interview in concrete, recent, lived experience.

Ask about:

- the last time this problem happened;
- what they did immediately before and after;
- what tools, people, or workarounds they used;
- how much time or money it cost;
- who else was involved.

"What would you do" invites fantasy; "what did you do last Tuesday" invites truth.

### Ask Open Questions And Then Stay Quiet

Leading or yes/no questions tell you nothing. Open questions, followed by silence, force the customer to fill the space with their own framing.

Strong openers include:

- walk me through the last time this happened;
- what was the hardest part;
- why was that hard;
- what did you try first;
- what do you wish had happened instead.

Resist the urge to rescue the customer from silence; the silence is where signal lives.

### Avoid Pitching The Solution

The moment you describe your idea, the interview shifts from discovery to sales, and politeness takes over. Customers will rarely tell a founder their baby is ugly.

If you must mention the concept, do it last, keep it abstract, and ask what would have to be true for them to adopt it, and what would stop them.

### Listen For The Customer's Language

The words customers use are data. If they describe the problem differently than your pitch, their words are the marketing copy, the feature names, and the positioning. Note exact phrases, because they reveal how the customer thinks about the pain.

### Probe For Specifics And Numbers

Vague answers hide the truth. Convert generalities into specifics.

- "often" becomes "how many times last month";
- "expensive" becomes "how much did it cost";
- "hard" becomes "what did you do because it was hard";
- "we" becomes "who exactly, and who decided".

Numbers expose whether the pain is real or rhetorical.

### Capture Commitments, Not Just Opinions

End each interview by testing willingness to act, because opinion is cheap and action is evidence.

Possible commitments:

- a follow-up meeting;
- a pilot or paid trial;
- an introduction to a colleague or budget holder;
- permission to share data or recordings;
- a preorder or deposit.

The number of interviews that yield commitment matters more than the number that yield praise.

### Separate The User, Buyer, And Decision Maker

The person you interview may not be the person who pays or decides. Map who feels the pain, who controls budget, and who must approve. Interview each role, because their problems and language differ.

### Document Immediately And Honestly

Memory edits interviews favorably within hours. Write or record the conversation, capture verbatim quotes, and note objections as carefully as compliments. A neutral reviewer should be able to read your notes and reach their own conclusion.

### Sample Beyond The Friendly

Friends, former colleagues, and people who reply to warm intros are biased toward encouragement. Push for colder contacts, reluctant interviewees, and people with no reason to please you.

### Recognize Limits And Personal Risk

Interviews inform direction but do not replace professional advice for regulated, safety-critical, or legally consequential decisions. Founder time spent interviewing is also runway spent; balance depth against the deadline to act.

## Common Traps

### Pitching Then Asking For Reaction

Once the idea is on the table, the customer's answers are contaminated by politeness and novelty.

### Hypothetical Questions

"What would you pay" and "would you use this" produce optimistic fiction, not behavior.

### Filling The Silence

Founders hate awkward pauses and rescue customers, cutting off the most honest moments.

### Counting Compliments As Signal

Praise is cheap. Unless it is paired with commitment or behavior change, it teaches nothing.

### Interviewing Only The Convenient

Friendly, available, similar-to-founder respondents create an echo chamber.

### Leading Questions Disguised As Open Ones

"Wouldn't it be great if..." and "Do you also find it frustrating when..." are yes-manufacturing machines.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Questions anchor in recent, specific past behavior rather than hypothetical future intent.
- [ ] Questions are open, and the script includes deliberate silence after each.
- [ ] The solution is not pitched until the end, if at all, and only abstractly.
- [ ] Exact customer language and phrases are captured verbatim.
- [ ] Generalities are converted into specific numbers, frequencies, and costs.
- [ ] Each interview ends with a concrete commitment request, not only an opinion poll.
- [ ] User, buyer, and decision-maker roles are distinguished and interviewed separately where relevant.
- [ ] Notes are recorded immediately, including objections and disconfirming data.
- [ ] The sample reaches beyond friendly and convenient contacts.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids treating praise or hypothetical interest as validated demand.
