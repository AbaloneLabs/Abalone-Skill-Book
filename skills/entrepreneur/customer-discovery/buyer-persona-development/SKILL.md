# Buyer Persona Development

A buyer persona is not a demographic sketch or a marketing decoration. It is a working model of the specific person whose problem you solve, who controls the budget, and whom you must reach, persuade, and serve. Vague personas produce vague messaging, wasted acquisition spend, and a product that serves an imaginary average customer. A useful persona sharpens every downstream decision: what to build, what to say, where to show up, and what to charge.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "who is my target customer", "how do I write a buyer persona", "which segment should I target first", or "how should an entrepreneur define their ideal customer". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing shallow persona cards that look professional but cannot guide a single real decision.

## Core Rules

### Build Personas From Evidence, Not Imagination

A persona invented at a whiteboard is a guess. A persona built from interviews, observed behavior, real language, and actual purchase patterns is a tool. Ground every attribute in something a real customer said or did, and label assumptions as assumptions.

### Separate The Personas That Matter Early

Early ventures cannot serve everyone. Identify the one or two personas whose pain is sharpest, who are reachable, and who can pay. Targeting more personas early dilutes messaging, product focus, and learning speed.

Choose a primary persona and defer the rest until the primary is validated.

### Distinguish User, Buyer, And Influencer

In many businesses the person who uses the product is not the person who pays or decides. Each role is a different persona with different pains, language, and motives.

Define for each relevant role:

- what they personally stand to gain or lose;
- what language they use;
- what evidence they trust;
- what risk they perceive;
- what authority they hold over the purchase.

### Capture Behavior And Context, Not Only Demographics

Age, title, and location are weak predictors of purchase. Behavior and context are strong.

Include:

- current workflow and tools;
- where they spend time and attention;
- what triggers the problem;
- what they already pay for;
- who they trust and consult;
- what a typical day looks like around the pain.

The more situational detail, the more the persona can guide messaging and channel.

### Capture Motivations, Fears, And Success Criteria

People buy to achieve outcomes and to avoid risks. Name what success looks like for the persona and what failure or embarrassment they fear.

Useful motivators include saving time, reducing risk, looking good internally, hitting a target, avoiding blame, freeing up staff, or unlocking revenue. Useful fears include wasted spend, disruption, looking foolish, vendor lock-in, and compliance exposure.

### Make The Persona Actionable

A persona earns its place only if it changes a decision. Test each persona by asking: does this change what I build, what I say, where I advertise, or what I charge? If the answer is no, the persona is too generic.

Link persona attributes to specific decisions: channel choice, messaging angle, pricing model, feature priority, sales motion.

### Define Reachability

A persona you cannot find or contact is theoretical. Specify where this persona is reachable: which communities, events, publications, search behavior, platforms, or referral paths. If reachability is unclear, acquisition will stall regardless of how well the persona is otherwise defined.

### Keep Personas Living Documents

Early personas are hypotheses. Update them as interviews, sales calls, and usage data reveal that the real customer differs from the assumed one. A persona frozen at launch becomes a fiction within months.

### Acknowledge Founder Bias And Personal Risk

Founders often build personas that resemble themselves or their network, which can blind them to the actual buying audience. Test the persona against cold, independent customers. This skill does not replace professional advice where targeting involves regulated or protected categories.

## Common Traps

### Demographic-Only Personas

Age and title do not predict pain, budget, or behavior; they only fill template fields.

### The Imaginary Average Customer

Blending many segments into one persona produces someone who represents no real buyer.

### Personas That Change No Decisions

If a persona cannot guide messaging, channel, pricing, or features, it is decoration.

### Ignoring The Buyer Role

Solving the user's pain while ignoring who pays leads to loved-but-unbought products.

### Over-Targeting Early

Pursuing many personas at once fragments focus and slows learning.

### Never Updating The Persona

A persona that matched the first five interviews may not match the fiftieth customer.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Persona attributes are grounded in interviews and observed behavior, with assumptions labeled.
- [ ] A primary persona is chosen for early focus, with others deferred.
- [ ] User, buyer, and influencer roles are distinguished where they differ.
- [ ] Behavior, workflow, context, and reachability are captured, not only demographics.
- [ ] Motivations, fears, and success criteria are explicit.
- [ ] Each persona is tested against whether it changes a concrete build, message, channel, or pricing decision.
- [ ] Reachability via specific communities, channels, or referral paths is defined.
- [ ] Personas are treated as living hypotheses and updated with new evidence.
- [ ] Founder bias toward self-resembling personas is checked against cold customers.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a demographic sketch as a decision-driving persona.
