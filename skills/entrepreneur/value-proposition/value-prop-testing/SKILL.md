# Value Proposition Testing

A value proposition is a hypothesis, and like any hypothesis it must survive contact with the market before it is trusted. Founders often fall in love with a phrasing internally, build messaging around it, and only discover months later that customers found it confusing, unbelievable, or irrelevant. Testing converts opinion into evidence: it reveals which promise generates attention, comprehension, objection, and commitment from the specific customers you want. The goal is not to find the cleverest words but to find the promise that moves real behavior.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I test my value proposition", "which message works best", "is my value prop resonating", or "how should an entrepreneur validate their messaging". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating internal preference or click counts as proof that a proposition works.

## Core Rules

### Define What "Works" Means Before Testing

A proposition can generate clicks but not comprehension, comprehension but not trust, trust but not commitment. Decide in advance which outcome defines success for the current stage.

Possible success criteria:

- the right customer self-identifies and engages;
- the customer can accurately restate the promise;
- the customer proceeds to a next step, such as a demo, trial, or deposit;
- the customer refers someone else;
- objections shift from confusion to specific concerns.

Clicks alone are a weak signal; they measure curiosity, not fit.

### Test With The Right Customers, Not Any Respondents

A proposition tested on the wrong audience produces misleading confidence. Ensure respondents match the target persona in role, segment, situation, and budget authority. Testing on friends, generic survey panels, or adjacent audiences teaches little about the customers who must actually buy.

Document who was tested and confirm they resemble the real buyer.

### Separate Comprehension From Appeal

A proposition can be appealing yet misunderstood, or well understood yet unappealing. Test both.

Ask respondents to restate the promise in their own words. If their restatement drifts from your intent, the proposition is unclear regardless of whether they liked it. Confusion is a more important signal than mild approval.

### Measure Behavior And Commitment, Not Only Preference

Asking which proposition people prefer invites politeness and aesthetic judgment. Stronger tests observe whether a proposition causes a next action.

Behavior-based tests include:

- which variant drives more signups, deposits, or demo requests;
- which variant reduces bounce or raises time on page;
- which variant generates more qualified inbound questions;
- which variant produces referrals;
- which variant survives a sales conversation toward close.

Prefer tests where the customer risks something: time, data, or money.

### Test One Variable At A Time

If you change the promise, the audience, the format, and the channel simultaneously, you cannot attribute the result. Vary one element per test, holding the rest constant, so the outcome is interpretable.

### Include Objection And Confusion Signals

The most valuable testing output is often what confuses or repels customers. Track which propositions generate "I do not understand", "that sounds too good to be true", or "that is not my problem". These signals reveal whether the proposition is matched to the customer's reality.

### Run Enough Exposure To Distinguish Signal From Noise

A handful of reactions is anecdote, not evidence. Expose each variant to enough of the right customers that a pattern can emerge. Define a minimum sample before declaring a winner, and beware of declaring victory on early noise.

### Test Across Channels And Contexts

A proposition that wins in a survey may lose in a search ad or a sales call, because context changes how messages land. Test in the channel where the proposition will actually live, not only in a controlled setting.

### Watch For Novelty And Founder Effects

Early positive reactions can reflect novelty, personal founder charisma, or the halo of attention rather than the proposition itself. Look for whether the proposition still works when the founder is absent and the novelty fades.

### Acknowledge Uncertainty And Personal Risk

Testing reduces but does not eliminate uncertainty about messaging. Over-rotating on a single test can mislead; ignoring tests in favor of founder intuition raises the risk of building on a misunderstood promise. This skill does not replace professional advice for propositions in regulated domains.

## Common Traps

### Declaring A Winner On Clicks Alone

Clicks measure curiosity, not comprehension, trust, or purchase intent.

### Testing On The Wrong Audience

Generic respondents approve almost anything; they are not your buyer.

### Confusing Appeal With Comprehension

A liked-but-misunderstood proposition fails in the field.

### Changing Everything At Once

Multi-variable changes make results unattributable.

### Trusting Founder Preference

Internal conviction about wording is not evidence of market resonance.

### Overreading A Small Sample

A few reactions can be pure noise dressed up as a pattern.

### Ignoring Channel Context

A proposition that tests well in a survey may flop in the real channel.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Success criteria are defined before testing, distinguishing comprehension, appeal, and commitment.
- [ ] Respondents match the target persona in role, segment, situation, and authority.
- [ ] Comprehension is tested by asking respondents to restate the promise in their own words.
- [ ] Behavior and commitment signals are measured, not only stated preference.
- [ ] One variable is changed per test so results remain attributable.
- [ ] Objection, confusion, and disbelief signals are captured and analyzed.
- [ ] Each variant is exposed to a pre-defined minimum sample before a winner is declared.
- [ ] Testing occurs in the channel and context where the proposition will actually be used.
- [ ] Novelty and founder-charisma effects are considered when interpreting early results.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids declaring a proposition proven based on clicks, preference, or small samples alone.
