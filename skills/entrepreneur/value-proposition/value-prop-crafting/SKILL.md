# Value Proposition Crafting

A value proposition is the promise that makes a customer choose you over doing nothing or choosing someone else. Most value propositions fail not because they are poorly written but because they are generic, unbelievable, or matched to a pain the customer does not actually prioritize. Crafting one is not a copywriting exercise; it is the act of compressing everything you know about the customer, the problem, the alternative, and your advantage into a single credible promise. A weak value proposition quietly sabotages every channel, because no amount of traffic fixes an offer no one wants.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what is my value proposition", "how do I write a value prop", "is my messaging working", or "how should an entrepreneur describe what they offer". The goal is to prevent the agent from producing polished but empty slogans that sound confident and commit to nothing.

## Core Rules

### Anchor The Proposition In The Customer's Pain

A value proposition that begins with the product is already backwards. Begin with the specific customer, the specific circumstance, and the specific outcome they want. The product is the means; the progress is the point.

State explicitly:

- who the customer is;
- what situation they are in;
- what outcome they want;
- what currently stops them;
- how you change that.

If you cannot name the pain in the customer's words, the proposition is a guess.

### Promise A Specific Outcome, Not A Category

Generic promises like "we help businesses grow" or "we make work easier" commit to nothing and distinguish no one. A strong proposition names a concrete, verifiable outcome.

Compare "we help teams collaborate" with "we help distributed clinics cut no-shows by reminding patients in their preferred language". The second is specific, falsifiable, and memorable.

### Make The Promise Credible And Verifiable

A proposition the customer does not believe is worse than no proposition, because it triggers skepticism. Support the promise with evidence the customer's context will trust.

Credibility tools:

- a concrete mechanism, not magic;
- a measurable outcome with a timeframe;
- a relevant proof point, testimonial, or pilot result;
- transparency about what it does and does not do.

Avoid superlatives you cannot defend; they erode trust.

### Distinguish Benefit From Feature

Customers buy benefits, not features. A feature is what the product does; a benefit is what the customer gains. Translate every feature into the outcome it produces for the specific persona.

"We use machine learning" is a feature; "you catch fraudulent charges before they settle" is a benefit. Lead with the benefit; let the feature support it.

### Differentiate Against The Real Alternative

The alternative is rarely a direct competitor; it is whatever the customer does today, including nothing. The proposition must explain why switching to you is worth the effort, risk, and cost relative to that alternative.

Name the comparison honestly and show where you are meaningfully better, not merely different.

### Match The Proposition To The Buyer's Priorities

The same product can carry different propositions for different buyers. The user cares about ease; the buyer cares about cost and risk; the executive cares about outcomes and optics. Craft the proposition for the person whose decision you need, using the language and evidence they trust.

### Keep It Short Enough To Survive Contact

A value proposition must survive being spoken aloud, read in three seconds, and repeated by someone who does not work for you. If it requires a paragraph, it is not yet a proposition; it is a description.

Aim for one sentence that a stranger could repeat after hearing it once.

### Test The Proposition Against Real Reactions

Value propositions are hypotheses. Test them by watching which versions generate engagement, questions, objections, and commitment. The market's reaction is the truth; internal preference is not.

Track which propositions move the customers you actually want, not merely which ones generate clicks.

### Acknowledge Founder Bias And Personal Risk

Founders fall in love with clever phrasing that obscures the real offer. A proposition that overpromises creates customer and legal risk; one that underpromises starves the business. This skill does not replace professional advice for claims involving regulated outcomes, health, finance, or safety.

## Common Traps

### Category Slogans

"We are the Uber for X" borrows familiarity but commits to nothing specific.

### Feature Lists Disguised As Promises

Bullet points of capabilities do not tell the customer what they gain.

### Unbelievable Superlatives

"The best", "the only", "revolutionary" trigger skepticism unless rigorously supported.

### Ignoring The Real Alternative

Comparing only to direct competitors misses that doing nothing is usually the strongest incumbent.

### One Proposition For Everyone

A single generic promise cannot move users, buyers, and executives at once.

### Cleverness Over Clarity

Witty phrasing that obscures the offer wins awards and loses customers.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The proposition is anchored in a named customer, circumstance, and desired outcome.
- [ ] The promise is specific and verifiable, not a generic category claim.
- [ ] Credibility is supported by mechanism, measurable outcome, or relevant proof.
- [ ] Features are translated into benefits the customer cares about.
- [ ] The proposition differentiates against the real alternative, including doing nothing.
- [ ] Separate propositions exist for user, buyer, and decision-maker where they differ.
- [ ] The core proposition is short enough to be spoken, read in seconds, and repeated.
- [ ] Propositions are tested against real engagement and commitment, not internal preference.
- [ ] Superlatives are either supported or removed.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a clever slogan as a decision-driving value proposition.
