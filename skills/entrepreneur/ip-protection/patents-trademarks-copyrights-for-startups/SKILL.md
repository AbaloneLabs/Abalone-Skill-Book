# Patents, Trademarks, And Copyrights For Startups

Patents, trademarks, and copyrights protect fundamentally different things, arise differently, last for different durations, and cost different amounts, yet founders frequently conflate them or choose the wrong tool for the asset. A patent protects functional inventions, a trademark protects brand identifiers, and a copyright protects original creative expression; using one where another applies wastes money and fails to protect the asset. Understanding the distinctions is foundational to making sound IP decisions with limited startup resources.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what is the difference between a patent and a trademark", "should I copyright or patent my software", "how do I protect my logo", "what does a trademark cover", or "how should an entrepreneur choose among IP types". The goal is to prevent the agent from conflating protection types and from recommending the wrong tool for a given asset.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Each Protection Type Covers

Patents protect functional inventions, processes, machines, and compositions of matter. Trademarks protect brand identifiers, names, logos, slogans, that distinguish the source of goods or services. Copyrights protect original creative expression, software code, written works, art, music. Knowing what each covers is the first step to choosing correctly.

### Match The Tool To The Specific Asset

Choose the protection type that matches the specific asset being protected: a brand name calls for a trademark, not a patent; a novel mechanism calls for a patent; software code calls for copyright, with patents considered separately for any novel functional aspects. Matching the tool to the asset is the core decision.

### Understand How Each Arises

Some rights arise automatically, while others require application and examination. Copyrights arise upon fixation of an original work, though registration strengthens enforcement. Trademark rights can arise through use, though registration strengthens them. Patents require a formal application and examination before any right exists. Understanding how rights arise prevents false confidence in unprotected assets.

### Consider Cost And Duration Tradeoffs

The three types differ sharply in cost and duration. Patents are expensive to file, prosecute, and maintain, and last a limited term. Trademarks are comparatively affordable and can last indefinitely with use and renewal. Copyrights are inexpensive and last a long time. Weigh cost and duration against the asset's value and the venture's resources.

### Recognize The Disclosure Tradeoff

Patents require public disclosure of the invention in exchange for the right to exclude. This means competitors can read the patent and design around it, and the disclosure is permanent even if the patent lapses. Copyrights and trademarks do not require disclosing secret know-how. Factor the disclosure tradeoff into the patent decision.

### Account For Software's Special Complexity

Software can implicate all three types: code is copyrightable, novel functional aspects may be patentable in some jurisdictions, and the brand is trademarkable. Software patents are subject to specific eligibility rules that vary by jurisdiction and are often contested. Treat software IP as a specialized area requiring expert guidance, not generic assumptions.

### Clear Names And Brands Before Use

Before adopting a brand, clear it to ensure it does not infringe existing trademarks, because rebranding after launch is costly. Clearance searches are cheaper than litigation or forced rebrands. This is a practical, high-value step often skipped by founders.

### Register Where It Matters

Registration is not always required but materially strengthens enforcement: copyright registration enables statutory damages and fees; trademark registration provides nationwide rights and notice; patents require examination. Register where the asset's value and the venture's markets justify the cost, and not everywhere by default.

### Plan Maintenance And Renewals

IP rights often require ongoing maintenance: patent maintenance fees, trademark renewal filings, and copyright is largely maintenance-free but requires monitoring for infringement. Plan for the ongoing cost and administrative burden, because lapsed rights cannot always be revived.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Choosing among protection types, filing, and enforcement involve jurisdiction-specific legal complexity. Use qualified IP counsel for filings, clearance, and strategy. This skill provides foundational understanding only and does not replace professional legal advice.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Protection Types

Using the wrong tool for an asset wastes money and fails to protect it.

### Assuming Rights Exist Without Registration

False confidence in unregistered patents or weak trademarks creates exposure.

### Ignoring The Patent Disclosure Tradeoff

Disclosing an invention publicly and irreversibly for a patent that may never pay off.

### Skipping Brand Clearance

Adopting a name without clearing it leads to costly forced rebrands or litigation.

### Treating Software IP As Simple

Software's mix of copyright, patent eligibility, and brand issues requires specialized guidance.

### Overlooking Maintenance Obligations

Lapsed rights from missed fees or renewals cannot always be recovered.
