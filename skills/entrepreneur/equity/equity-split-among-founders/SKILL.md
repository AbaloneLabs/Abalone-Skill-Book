# Equity Split Among Founders

The equity split among co-founders is one of the earliest and most consequential decisions a venture makes, and it is often done casually, with a default equal split that fails to reflect differing contributions, roles, time commitments, and risk. A poorly considered split breeds resentment as the venture evolves and contributions diverge, and it is hard to undo without conflict. Sound equity splitting is the discipline of aligning ownership with expected long-term contributions and risk, documented clearly, with vesting to protect the venture if a founder departs.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how should we split equity", "is an equal founder split fair", "how do I divide shares among co-founders", "what factors should drive the equity split", or "how should an entrepreneur handle founder equity". The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to an equal split without analysis and from ignoring the factors that make a split sustainable or divisive.

## Core Rules

### Align Equity With Long-Term Contribution, Not Past Work

Equity represents ownership of the venture's long-term future, so the split should reflect expected long-term contributions, not merely who had the idea first or who did early work. A split weighted to past contributions misaligns ownership from the years of work ahead. Focus on who will build the company over time.

### Consider The Key Factors Deliberately

The key factors in a split include role and responsibilities, time commitment, full-time versus part-time, prior contributions, capital contributed, opportunity cost, and risk taken. Consider these deliberately rather than defaulting to equal shares. Different ventures weight these factors differently, and the weighting should be a conscious decision.

### Question The Default Equal Split

An equal split is simple and feels fair, but it is appropriate only when founders' contributions, roles, and commitments are genuinely comparable. Where they diverge, an equal split can create resentment, as the founder contributing more comes to feel under-recognized. Question the default and split based on reality.

### Use Vesting To Protect The Venture

Founder equity should vest over time, typically with a cliff, so that a founder who departs early does not retain a large unearned stake. Vesting protects the venture and the remaining founders, and it is expected by investors. Without vesting, an early departure leaves dead equity that complicates fundraising and cap table management.

### Address The Idea Versus Execution Distinction

Ideas alone are worth little; execution is worth most. A founder who contributed the idea but executes less should generally hold less equity than those who build the company. Avoid over-weighting the idea, because the market rewards execution, and over-rewarding the idea breeds resentment among builders.

### Have The Hard Conversation Early

The equity conversation is uncomfortable, but deferring it makes it harder, because contributions and emotions accumulate. Have the conversation early, when the venture is abstract and relationships are intact, and document the outcome. Early clarity prevents later conflict.

### Document The Split And The Reasoning

Document the split, the factors considered, and the reasoning, in a founders' agreement and the cap table. Documentation prevents disputes when memories diverge and demonstrates diligence to investors. An undocumented split is a source of future conflict.

### Revisit Only With Care And Agreement

Once set and documented, the split should be stable, but circumstances can warrant adjustment, such as a founder's role changing materially. Revisit only with all founders' agreement and clear documentation, because unilateral changes destroy trust. Stability with a deliberate revision path is healthier than constant renegotiation.

### Account For Future Dilution And The Option Pool

The founder split exists alongside the future employee option pool and investor dilution. Consider how the split interacts with the option pool and future rounds, because a split that leaves no room for talent or investors constrains the venture. The split is one part of a larger cap table picture.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Founder equity splits involve legal, tax, and relational consequences. Use qualified legal counsel to document the split and vesting. This skill provides strategic framing only and does not replace professional legal and tax advice.

## Common Traps

### Default Equal Split Without Analysis

Equal shares that ignore divergent contributions breed resentment.

### Over-Weighting The Idea

Rewarding the originator disproportionately undervalues the execution that creates value.

### No Vesting

An early departure leaves dead equity that complicates fundraising and cap table management.

### Deferring The Conversation

Delay makes the split harder as contributions and emotions accumulate.

### Undocumented Splits

Unwritten agreements create disputes when memories diverge.

### Ignoring Future Dilution And The Option Pool

A split that leaves no room for talent or investors constrains the venture.
