# Equity Compensation For Employees

Equity compensation allows a resource-constrained startup to attract and retain talent that it could not afford at market cash salaries, and it aligns employees with the venture's long-term success. But equity grants are easily mishandled: ad hoc sizes create perceived unfairness, unclear vesting creates confusion, and an unplanned option pool creates cap table problems. Sound equity compensation is the discipline of granting competitively, consistently, and deliberately, in service of attracting talent while protecting the cap table.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how much equity should I give employees", "how does vesting work", "how do I set up an option pool", "what is a fair equity grant", or "how should an entrepreneur compensate employees with equity". The goal is to prevent the agent from making ad hoc grants and from neglecting the consistency and cap table discipline that healthy equity compensation requires.

## Core Rules

### Use Equity To Bridge The Cash Gap

Equity's primary early purpose is to let the venture hire talent it cannot afford at market cash, by offering ownership in lieu of full salary. Frame equity compensation around this purpose, and be honest about the tradeoff, because employees accept lower cash for meaningful upside. Equity is not a perk; it is compensation.

### Set Grant Sizes Competitively And Consistently

Grant sizes should be competitive with market norms for the role, stage, and venture type, and consistent across comparable roles. Research benchmarks, set ranges by role and seniority, and apply them consistently to avoid perceived unfairness. Ad hoc grants create resentment and cap table confusion.

### Use Standard Vesting Structures

Standard vesting, typically four years with a one-year cliff, aligns employees with long-term contribution and protects the venture if someone departs early. Use standard structures unless there is a specific reason to deviate, because non-standard vesting creates administrative complexity and perceived unfairness.

### Plan The Option Pool Deliberately

The employee option pool should be planned to cover expected hires through the next funding milestone, sized in light of investor expectations and cap table impact. An undersized pool forces painful top-ups that dilute founders; an oversized pool dilutes unnecessarily. Plan the pool as part of cap table strategy, not reactively.

### Communicate The Value And Risk Honestly

Equity compensation should be communicated honestly: the potential upside, the dilution risk, the vesting mechanics, and the possibility that the equity becomes worthless. Over-selling equity as guaranteed wealth damages trust and creates disputes if the outcome disappoints. Honest communication builds durable alignment.

### Document Grants Properly

Every grant must be documented with proper option agreements, board approval, and cap table entries. Sloppy documentation creates disputes, tax problems, and due diligence issues. Proper documentation is foundational, regardless of how early or small the grant.

### Consider Tax Implications And Election Deadlines

Equity compensation has tax implications, and certain elections, such as early exercise or specific filings, have strict deadlines that affect tax treatment. Understand the basics and ensure employees are informed, because missed elections create avoidable tax costs. Tax treatment requires professional advice.

### Distinguish Employees From Contractors And Advisors

Employees, contractors, and advisors are compensated differently in equity: employees typically receive options with standard vesting; contractors and advisors often receive different instruments or vesting. Distinguish the categories and use appropriate structures, because conflating them creates errors.

### Revisit The Strategy As The Venture Scales

Equity compensation strategy evolves with scale: early grants are larger in percentage but riskier; later grants are smaller in percentage but less risky. Revisit the strategy at funding milestones, because what is competitive and appropriate changes with stage. Static strategy becomes misaligned over time.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Equity compensation involves legal, tax, and cap table consequences. Use qualified legal and tax counsel to set up plans, document grants, and advise on elections. This skill provides strategic framing only and does not replace professional legal and tax advice.

## Common Traps

### Ad Hoc Grant Sizes

Inconsistent grants create perceived unfairness and cap table confusion.

### Undersized Option Pool

Forces painful top-ups that over-dilute founders.

### Over-Selling Equity As Guaranteed Wealth

Damages trust if the outcome disappoints.

### Sloppy Grant Documentation

Creates disputes, tax problems, and due diligence issues.

### Missed Tax Election Deadlines

Avoidable tax costs arise from missed filings.

### Conflating Employees, Contractors, And Advisors

Different categories require different equity structures.
