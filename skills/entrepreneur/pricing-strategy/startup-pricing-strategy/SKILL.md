# Startup Pricing Strategy

Price is one of the most powerful and most neglected levers a startup has. It signals positioning, shapes who buys, determines unit economics, and constrains every other decision about acquisition, retention, and growth. Yet founders often set prices by copying competitors, by guessing, or by choosing a round number that feels safe, and then never test them. Good pricing is a deliberate choice grounded in the value delivered, the customer's willingness to pay, the venture's economics, and the positioning it wants to occupy. It is not found; it is designed and then tested.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how should I price my product", "what pricing model should I use", "how do I set my price", or "how should an entrepreneur figure out pricing". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a price copied from competitors or chosen by feel, and from separating pricing from value, economics, and positioning.

## Core Rules

### Treat Price As A Strategic Lever, Not A Round Number

Price is not a detail to fill in at the end. It determines who the customer is, what the venture can afford to spend on acquisition, how the product is perceived, and whether the business works. Treat pricing as a strategic decision that shapes the venture, not an arbitrary figure chosen for comfort.

### Ground Price In Value Delivered

The strongest basis for price is the value the product creates for the customer: money saved, revenue gained, time recovered, risk reduced. Estimate that value, then set price to capture a fair share of it. Pricing far below value leaves money on the table and attracts price-sensitive customers; pricing far above value without evidence invites churn and resentment.

### Understand Willingness To Pay By Segment

Different customers have different willingness to pay, based on the value they receive and their constraints. Segment customers and understand what each segment will pay, rather than applying one price to all. Segmented pricing, tiers, packages, and enterprise contracts let the venture capture value from different segments without leaving money on the table.

### Choose A Pricing Model That Fits The Value

The pricing model, subscription, usage, per-seat, one-time, freemium, outcome-based, should fit how the customer receives value. A product used continuously fits subscription; a product used in bursts fits usage; a product tied to headcount fits per-seat. A mismatched model creates friction, churn, and perverse incentives.

### Ensure Price Supports Unit Economics

Price must cover the cost to acquire, serve, and support the customer, with a margin that funds growth. If the price cannot support healthy unit economics at the current acquisition cost, either the price is too low, the acquisition is too expensive, or the segment is wrong. Do not set price in isolation from economics.

### Let Price Reinforce Positioning

Price signals positioning. A high price signals premium and selects for customers who value quality and support; a low price signals value and selects for price-sensitive customers. Choose a price consistent with the positioning the venture wants to occupy, because the customers who arrive are shaped by the price they saw.

### Test Prices Rather Than Assert Them

Prices are hypotheses. Test them: vary price by segment or cohort, observe conversion and retention, and measure the effect on revenue and economics, not only on conversion. A price that lowers conversion but raises revenue per customer may be better. Let evidence, not opinion, settle pricing debates.

### Account For Price As A Commitment

Prices are sticky. A low launch price sets an anchor that is hard to raise, and discounts become expected. Consider the long-term implications of the initial price and the discounting patterns, because customers remember and resist changes. Plan pricing transitions, such as grandfathering, deliberately.

### Avoid Racing To The Bottom On Price

Competing on the lowest price is a fragile strategy for a startup, because it selects price-sensitive customers, erodes margins, and invites a race larger competitors can always win. Compete on value, differentiation, and fit for a segment, and use price to reflect that value, not to undercut.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Pricing decisions carry stakes for venture survival and personal risk. Tax, regulatory, and contractual implications of pricing models may require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Copying Competitor Prices

Competitors' prices reflect their economics and positioning, not yours.

### Price Set By Feel

A round number chosen for comfort ignores value, willingness to pay, and economics.

### Price Isolated From Unit Economics

A price that cannot support acquisition and serving costs breaks the business.

### Underpricing To Win Customers

Low prices attract price-sensitive customers, erode margins, and set a low anchor.

### Never Testing Prices

Treating the initial price as final leaves money on the table or repels the right customers.

### Sticky Low Anchors

Launch discounts and low prices become expected and are hard to raise.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Price is treated as a strategic lever, not an arbitrary round number.
- [ ] Price is grounded in the value delivered to the customer.
- [ ] Willingness to pay is understood by segment, not assumed uniform.
- [ ] The pricing model fits how the customer receives value.
- [ ] Price supports healthy unit economics at the current acquisition cost.
- [ ] Price reinforces the positioning the venture wants to occupy.
- [ ] Prices are tested against revenue and economics, not only conversion.
- [ ] Long-term price stickiness and discount patterns are considered.
- [ ] The strategy avoids racing to the bottom on price.
- [ ] Tax, regulatory, and contractual implications are flagged for professional advice.
