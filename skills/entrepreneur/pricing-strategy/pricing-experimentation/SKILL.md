# Pricing Experimentation

Because prices are hypotheses, they should be tested. Pricing experimentation is the discipline of varying price to learn what the market will bear and how price affects conversion, revenue, retention, and unit economics. Done well, it turns pricing from opinion into evidence. Done poorly, it produces misleading signals: tests that bias results, anchor customers to low prices, or measure the wrong outcome and lead the venture to underprice or overprice with false confidence.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "how do I test my pricing", "should I raise my price", "how do I run a pricing experiment", or "how should an entrepreneur experiment with price". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending biased or simplistic tests and from confusing short-term conversion effects with long-term revenue and retention.

## Core Rules

### Test Pricing As Hypotheses, Not Opinions

Frame each pricing test as a hypothesis: "customers in segment X will accept price Y without a drop in conversion below Z." Define what outcome would confirm or refute the hypothesis before running the test. Testing without a hypothesis produces data without insight.

### Measure Revenue And Economics, Not Only Conversion

The most common pricing mistake is to measure conversion alone. A higher price often lowers conversion but raises revenue per customer and improves unit economics, making it the better choice. Measure the outcomes that matter: revenue, contribution margin, retention, and lifetime value, not just how many people clicked buy.

### Use Cohorts To Avoid Contamination

When testing prices, avoid contaminating results by showing different prices to the same customers or by letting customers compare prices. Use separate cohorts, segments, or time periods, and ensure the test design does not let customers self-select into the cheaper option in a way that biases results.

### Watch For Anchoring Effects

Once customers see a low price, that price becomes an anchor against which future prices are judged. A test that shows a low price can create expectations that make later price increases feel like betrayal. Consider anchoring effects when designing tests, and avoid public price tests that train customers to expect discounts.

### Run Tests Long Enough For Signal

Pricing effects, especially on retention and lifetime value, take time to appear. A test measured over days may show conversion effects but miss retention effects that emerge over weeks or months. Run tests long enough to capture the outcomes that matter, or risk choosing a price that looks good early and fails later.

### Segment By Willingness To Pay

Different segments have different willingness to pay. Test pricing within segments, not only across the whole base, because an average can hide that one segment accepts a higher price while another rejects it. Segmented pricing tests reveal where value and willingness to pay differ.

### Distinguish Price Sensitivity From Product Fit

A price that converts poorly may indicate the price is too high, or it may indicate the product does not deliver enough value at any price. Before concluding price is the problem, check whether the value proposition and product fit support the price. Raising fit raises willingness to pay.

### Account For Self-Selection Bias

Customers who respond to a discount or a low price may differ systematically from those who pay full price: more price-sensitive, less loyal, higher churn. A test that attracts more customers at a low price may be attracting the wrong customers, worsening long-term economics even as short-term conversion rises.

### Document And Iterate

Record each test, its hypothesis, design, results, and decision. Pricing knowledge accumulates across tests; without documentation, the venture repeats mistakes and cannot learn what the market will bear. Iterate pricing as evidence accumulates and as the product and market evolve.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Pricing experiments can carry legal and contractual risks, such as price discrimination rules and existing customer agreements. This skill does not replace professional legal advice on pricing practices.

## Common Traps

### Measuring Only Conversion

A higher price that lowers conversion but raises revenue is mistaken for a failure.

### Anchoring Customers Low

Public low-price tests train customers to expect discounts and resist increases.

### Contaminated Test Design

Letting customers see or compare prices biases results.

### Short Tests Missing Retention

Tests too brief to show retention effects choose prices that fail later.

### Confusing Price Sensitivity With Weak Fit

Lowering price to compensate for weak product fit masks the real problem.

### Self-Selection Bias

Low prices attract price-sensitive, high-churn customers that worsen economics.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each pricing test is framed as a hypothesis with a defined confirm-or-refute outcome.
- [ ] The test measures revenue, economics, and retention, not only conversion.
- [ ] Cohorts are separated to avoid contamination and customer comparison of prices.
- [ ] Anchoring effects are considered, and public discount tests are avoided.
- [ ] Tests run long enough to capture retention and lifetime value effects.
- [ ] Pricing is tested within segments, not only across the whole base.
- [ ] Price sensitivity is distinguished from weak product fit.
- [ ] Self-selection bias from low prices is accounted for.
- [ ] Tests are documented so pricing knowledge accumulates.
- [ ] Price discrimination and contractual risks are flagged for professional legal advice.
