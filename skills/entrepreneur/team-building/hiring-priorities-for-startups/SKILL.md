# Hiring Priorities For Startups

A startup cannot hire everyone it needs at once, so the order of hiring matters as much as the choices themselves. Hiring priorities are the discipline of sequencing hires against the venture's biggest constraints, risks, and milestones, so that each hire unlocks the most leverage at the time it is made. Hiring out of order, bringing on a role before the constraint it addresses is the bottleneck, or after the constraint has already caused damage, wastes scarce resources. Sound hiring priorities identify what is currently blocking the venture and hire to relieve that blockage, in sequence.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "who should I hire next", "what roles should I prioritize", "in what order should I hire", or "how should an entrepreneur sequence their hiring". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending hires by convenience or familiarity and from sequencing roles without reference to the venture's actual constraints.

## Core Rules

### Hire Against The Current Constraint

The next hire should relieve the venture's current biggest constraint: the bottleneck that most limits progress, whether in building, selling, delivering, or operating. Identify the constraint, then hire to relieve it. Hiring that does not address the current constraint adds capacity without unlocking progress.

### Sequence Hires To Milestones

Sequence hires so that each arrives in time to support the next milestone, with enough lead time for ramp-up. Hiring too late means the milestone slips waiting for capacity; hiring too early means paying for capacity before the work exists to use it. Plan hiring against the milestone roadmap, not reactively.

### Distinguish Leverage Hires From Capacity Hires

Some hires create leverage, unlocking work that multiplies the team's output, such as a key engineer who unblocks product development or a first sales hire who opens a channel. Others add capacity, doing more of work that already works. Prioritize leverage hires early, because they unlock disproportionate progress; capacity hires can follow once the leverage is in place.

### Avoid Hiring For Convenience Or Familiarity

It is tempting to hire people the founder knows or roles that are easy to fill, but convenience hires often do not address the constraint and create teams shaped by familiarity rather than need. Hire against the venture's priorities, even when it is harder, because convenient hires that do not relieve constraints waste resources.

### Account For Ramp-Up Time

A hire does not contribute immediately; there is a ramp-up period during which the new person learns and becomes productive. Account for ramp-up when planning hiring timing, because a hire made when the need is urgent arrives too late to help with that need. Hire ahead of the need, with ramp-up in mind.

### Balance Builders, Sellers, And Supporters

A team needs builders who create the product, sellers who bring in customers, and supporters who keep operations and customers running. Imbalance, too many builders and no sellers, or builders without support, creates bottlenecks even with a full headcount. Balance the mix against the venture's current needs.

### Avoid Premature Hiring Before Evidence

Hiring before the evidence justifies it, before demand is proven or before the work exists, burns runway and creates a team that must be managed rather than work that must be done. Let evidence, not optimism, drive hiring, because premature hires are expensive to undo.

### Consider The Cost Of Management Overhead

Each hire adds management overhead: someone must onboard, direct, and develop them. Beyond a certain size, the founder cannot manage everyone directly, and management hires become necessary. Account for the management overhead each hire creates, and plan for when management capacity itself becomes a constraint.

### Re-Prioritize As Constraints Shift

The binding constraint shifts as the venture grows: building may be the bottleneck, then selling, then delivery, then operations. Re-prioritize hiring as constraints shift, hiring for the new bottleneck rather than continuing to hire for the old one. Static hiring plans optimize for a past constraint.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Hiring involves employment, equity, immigration, and contractual obligations with legal implications that require professional advice. This skill does not replace that counsel.

## Common Traps

### Hiring That Does Not Address The Constraint

Adding capacity that does not relieve the bottleneck wastes resources.

### Convenience And Familiarity Hires

Roles shaped by who is easy to find rather than what the venture needs.

### Ignoring Ramp-Up Time

Hiring when the need is urgent arrives too late to help.

### Premature Hiring

Growing the team before evidence justifies it burns runway.

### Imbalanced Builder-Seller-Supporter Mix

A full headcount with bottlenecks because one function is missing.

### Static Hiring Plan

Continuing to hire for a past constraint after it has shifted.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The next hire relieves the venture's current biggest constraint.
- [ ] Hires are sequenced to milestones, with ramp-up time accounted for.
- [ ] Leverage hires are prioritized over capacity hires early.
- [ ] Hires are chosen against priorities, not convenience or familiarity.
- [ ] Ramp-up time is factored into hiring timing.
- [ ] The builder-seller-supporter mix is balanced against current needs.
- [ ] Hiring is driven by evidence, not optimism.
- [ ] Management overhead of each hire is accounted for.
- [ ] Hiring priorities are re-evaluated as constraints shift.
- [ ] Employment, equity, immigration, and contractual implications are flagged for professional legal advice.
