# Launch Timing And Sequencing

A launch is not a single event; it is a sequence of controlled releases, each tuned to what the venture is ready to learn and deliver. Timing a launch too early, before the product can deliver or the business can respond, creates reputation damage. Timing it too late, after over-polishing, wastes runway and cedes the market. Sequencing a launch, through private pilots, beta cohorts, soft launches, and full release, lets the venture learn from small audiences before exposing itself to large ones. The discipline is matching each stage of release to the venture's readiness and learning goals.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "when should I launch", "how should I sequence my launch", "should I do a beta first", or "how should an entrepreneur time and stage a launch". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating launch as a single big-bang date and from confusing a finished product with launch readiness.

## Core Rules

### Define What Each Launch Stage Is For

Different launch stages serve different purposes. A private pilot tests delivery with a handful of trusted customers. A beta tests the product and onboarding with a slightly larger, forgiving cohort. A soft launch tests acquisition and operations with a real but limited audience. A full launch commits to broad availability. Define what each stage must prove before moving to the next.

### Match Release Scope To Readiness

Each stage should release only what the venture can deliver, support, and learn from. Releasing too broadly before operations can cope creates failures that damage reputation. Releasing too narrowly after readiness wastes learning speed. Calibrate scope to current capability.

Ask at each stage: can we deliver, support, respond to problems, and learn from this audience.

### Sequence From Small And Forgiving To Large And Demanding

Move from audiences that are small, known, and forgiving to audiences that are large, unknown, and demanding. Early cohorts should be close enough to help debug and tolerant enough to survive rough edges. Later audiences expect polish and will judge failures harshly.

Plan the cohort progression: who is first, who is next, and what evidence justifies each expansion.

### Time Against Market Opportunity And Competition

Launch timing is not only about internal readiness; it is also about the market. A seasonal demand window, a competitor's move, a regulatory change, or a category moment can shift the right time to launch. Weigh internal readiness against external opportunity and competitive pressure.

Do not delay so long that a window closes, but do not rush so fast that delivery fails.

### Define Stage Transitions By Evidence, Not By Calendar

Move from one launch stage to the next based on evidence, not on a fixed date. Define what metrics and qualitative signals must be met to expand: delivery quality, support burden, conversion, retention, activation. Without evidence-based transitions, launches expand on hope and fail at scale.

### Instrument Each Stage For Learning

Every launch stage should produce learning that improves the next. Instrument what is observed: where customers drop, what they complain about, what they love, what breaks. The point of a staged launch is to fix problems while the audience is small, not to discover them at full scale.

### Prepare The Response Path Before Each Stage

Each stage will surface problems. Before expanding, prepare how the venture will respond: who handles support, how refunds or fixes work, how incidents escalate, how customers are communicated with. A launch stage without a response plan turns small problems into public failures.

### Avoid The Big-Bang Trap

The big-bang launch, releasing everything to everyone at once, maximizes exposure but also maximizes risk and minimizes learning speed. It is rarely the right choice for an early venture. Prefer staged release unless there is a specific reason broad simultaneous release is required.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

Launch timing consumes runway and founder energy, and some launches, especially in regulated industries, carry compliance and liability implications. This skill does not replace professional advice.

## Common Traps

### The Single Big-Bang Launch

Releasing to everyone at once maximizes risk and minimizes learning.

### Launching Before Delivery Can Cope

Broad release before operations can support it creates reputation damage.

### Delaying For Over-Polish

Waiting for perfection wastes runway and cedes market windows.

### Calendar-Based Stage Transitions

Expanding on a fixed date rather than on evidence leads to failure at scale.

### No Response Plan

Launch stages without a support and incident response plan turn small problems public.

### Ignoring Market Timing

Internal readiness alone, without market window and competition, mistimes the launch.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each launch stage has a defined purpose and proof requirement before the next.
- [ ] Release scope is matched to what the venture can deliver, support, and learn from.
- [ ] Audiences are sequenced from small and forgiving to large and demanding.
- [ ] Internal readiness is weighed against market opportunity and competitive timing.
- [ ] Stage transitions are governed by evidence and metrics, not by fixed dates.
- [ ] Each stage is instrumented to produce learning that improves the next.
- [ ] A support, refund, fix, and incident response path is prepared before each expansion.
- [ ] A big-bang launch is avoided unless specifically justified.
- [ ] Regulated or liability-bearing launch implications are considered.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids recommending a single launch date without sequencing, readiness, and market context.
