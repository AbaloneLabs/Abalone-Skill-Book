# GTM Strategy Design

A go-to-market strategy is the plan for how a venture reaches its target customers, convinces them, closes them, and delivers value, in a way that is repeatable and economic. It is not a marketing plan and not a launch announcement. A coherent GTM aligns the target segment, the value proposition, the channel, the pricing, the sales motion, and the delivery into a single system where each part reinforces the others. An incoherent GTM, where the channel cannot reach the segment or the pricing cannot cover the sales motion, fails even when the product is good.

Use this skill before answering broad questions such as "what is my go-to-market strategy", "how do I reach my first customers", "how should I plan my GTM", or "how should an entrepreneur design how they go to market". The goal is to prevent the agent from listing channels without coherence and from treating GTM as marketing rather than as an aligned system.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Target Segment And Work Backward

GTM begins with a specific, reachable segment, not with channels. Define who the first customers are, where they spend attention, how they buy, and what they trust. Only then choose channels and motions that fit how those customers actually decide.

A GTM designed around a channel rather than around the customer usually misses the customer.

### Align Segment, Value Proposition, Channel, Pricing, And Motion

The components of GTM must fit together. The segment must be reachable through the channel; the value proposition must resonate with the segment; the pricing must cover the cost of the motion; the motion must match how the segment buys.

Check each pair:

- segment to channel: can you reach them this way;
- value prop to segment: does it speak to their need;
- pricing to motion: does the price support the sales cost;
- motion to buying behavior: does it fit how they decide.

A single mismatch can break the whole system.

### Match The Sales Motion To Deal Size And Complexity

Different segments and price points require different motions. Low-price, self-serve products need product-led or content-led acquisition. Mid-market products need inside sales and marketing. High-price enterprise products need direct sales and relationship building. Choosing a motion that does not match the deal size and complexity makes acquisition uneconomic or impossible.

### Choose Channels You Can Access And Afford

Channels must be both accessible to the venture and affordable given the unit economics. A channel that requires scale the venture lacks, or that costs more to acquire than the customer returns, is not viable regardless of its reach.

For each channel, estimate reachable volume, conversion, acquisition cost, and payback before committing.

### Design For A Repeatable Motion, Not Founder Heroics

Early customers often come from founder relationships, which is fine for proof but not for scale. A GTM strategy must describe a repeatable motion that does not depend on the founder's personal network. If the only acquisition path is founder-led, the venture has not yet found a GTM.

Plan the transition from founder-led to repeatable channel as an explicit step.

### Sequence GTM With Maturity

GTM evolves with the venture. Early GTM optimizes for learning and proof with a narrow segment and a single channel. As proof accumulates, GTM expands to additional channels and segments. Plan the sequence rather than attempting a full multi-channel GTM from day one.

### Instrument The Funnel End To End

A GTM strategy requires measurement to know what is working. Instrument the full funnel: awareness, engagement, qualification, conversion, activation, retention, and expansion. Without instrumentation, GTM becomes guesswork and spend without learning.

Define the metrics that matter at each stage and the targets that indicate health.

### Plan For Delivery And Retention, Not Only Acquisition

GTM does not end at the sale. The plan must include how customers are onboarded, activated, supported, and retained, because acquisition without retention destroys unit economics. A GTM that optimizes only the top of the funnel produces churned customers and wasted spend.

### Acknowledge Personal Risk And Professional Advice

GTM execution consumes runway and founder energy, and some channels and motions, such as regulated advertising or cross-border sales, carry compliance implications. This skill does not replace professional advice.

## Common Traps

### Channel-First Instead Of Customer-First GTM

Choosing channels before defining the segment usually misses the customer.

### Motion Mismatched To Deal Size

Enterprise sales motion for a low-price product, or self-serve for a complex enterprise deal, makes acquisition uneconomic.

### Founder Heroics Mistaken For A Motion

Founder-led acquisition proves demand but does not scale; it is not a GTM strategy.

### Full Multi-Channel GTM From Day One

Attempting every channel at once dilutes focus and proves nothing.

### Optimizing Only Acquisition

Acquisition without onboarding, activation, and retention destroys unit economics.

### Uninstrumented Funnel

Without measurement, GTM spend produces no learning.

## Self-Check

- [ ] GTM starts from a specific, reachable segment and works backward to channels.
- [ ] Segment, value proposition, channel, pricing, and sales motion are aligned and mutually reinforcing.
- [ ] The sales motion matches the deal size, complexity, and buying behavior.
- [ ] Channels are accessible to the venture and affordable given unit economics.
- [ ] The motion is repeatable and does not depend solely on founder relationships.
- [ ] GTM is sequenced, with a narrow first motion expanding as proof accumulates.
- [ ] The full funnel is instrumented with metrics and targets at each stage.
- [ ] Onboarding, activation, support, and retention are included, not only acquisition.
- [ ] The transition from founder-led to repeatable acquisition is planned.
- [ ] The conclusion avoids presenting a channel list as a coherent go-to-market strategy.
