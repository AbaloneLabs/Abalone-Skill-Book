# Partnership Identification And Due Diligence

Partnerships are relationships through which a venture accesses distribution, capability, credibility, or cost structure it could not build alone. They are among the most seductive moves available to a founder, because a marquee partner feels like validation and a channel partner feels like growth without the cost of building a sales motion. The judgment problem is that most partnerships consume more attention than they return, because the work of identifying the right partner, establishing genuine mutual value, and diligencing reliability is skipped in favor of the excitement of signing. A venture that says yes to too many partnerships fragments its focus, accumulates obligations it cannot service, and discovers too late that the partner's incentives were never aligned with its own.

Founders tend to miss several things here. They pursue partnerships for the press release rather than for the economic or strategic outcome, then find the relationship produces nothing after the announcement. They fail to distinguish partnership types, treating a channel partner, a technology integration, a co-marketing arrangement, and a strategic alliance as if they required the same structure and yielded the same value. They assess fit from their own side only, ignoring whether the partner has a real reason to invest in the relationship. And they under-diligence reliability, signing with partners whose incentives, stability, or execution capacity make the partnership a liability. The harm is wasted founder attention, distracting obligations, and occasionally entanglements that constrain the company's future options.

## Core Rules

### Start From A Specific Gap, Not From A Partner

The right starting point for a partnership is a specific gap the venture cannot close alone: a distribution channel it cannot access, a capability it should not build, a credibility signal it lacks, a cost it cannot bear. Define the gap and the outcome required before identifying partners, so that partner selection is driven by fit to the gap rather than by who is available or flattering. Partnerships pursued because a partner is interested, rather than because a gap exists, almost always consume more than they return. The discipline is to name the strategic need first and let it filter candidates.

### Distinguish Partnership Types And Match Structure To Type

Partnerships fall into broadly different types, each with different value, structure, and risk. Strategic partnerships align the venture with a larger player for credibility, access, or co-development. Channel partnerships put the venture's product in front of audiences the partner owns, typically for a share of revenue. Technology partnerships integrate products to create joint value, requiring engineering investment and ongoing maintenance. Co-marketing partnerships combine audiences for mutual reach at low cost. Confusing these types, or applying the structure of one to the needs of another, produces relationships that fail to deliver because they were built for the wrong purpose. Identify the type first, then match the structure.

### Assess Mutual Value From Both Sides

A partnership endures only if both parties derive real value, measured in their own terms, not the venture's. Before pursuing, articulate specifically what the partner gets: revenue, capability, competitive positioning, access to a segment, a story to tell. If the answer is vague or one-sided, the partnership will be sustained only by the venture's effort and will collapse when that effort wanes. The strongest partnerships are those where the partner's incentive to invest is as clear and as strong as the venture's. Assess mutual value honestly, and walk away from relationships that depend on the partner's generosity.

### Diligence Reliability And Alignment Before Committing

Enthusiasm at signing does not predict execution over years. Diligence the partner's reliability: do they honor commitments, do they have the execution capacity to deliver their side, are they financially stable, is their strategy likely to remain aligned with yours, and what happens to the partnership if their priorities or leadership change. Talk to others who have partnered with them. The cost of diligence is small; the cost of an entangled, underperforming, or adversarial partnership is large and recurring. Treat the partnership as a long-term commitment and diligence it accordingly.

### Prioritize Ruthlessly Against Core Focus

Every partnership consumes founder and team attention, and attention is the scarcest resource a venture has. Prioritize partnerships against the core focus: does this partnership advance the venture's most important strategic outcome, or does it distract from it? A partnership that is interesting but not central is a distraction dressed as opportunity. Maintain a high bar, and be willing to decline or defer partnerships that do not clearly serve the core, even when they are flattering. The ventures that build effective partnership portfolios are the ones that say no far more often than they say yes.

### Define Success Criteria Before Signing

Before committing, define what success looks like and how it will be measured: revenue generated, customers accessed, capability delivered, credibility earned, within what timeframe. Defining success criteria in advance does two things: it forces honesty about whether the partnership is worth the effort, and it creates the basis for later evaluation and exit. Partnerships without defined success criteria drift, consume attention indefinitely, and become impossible to evaluate or wind down because no one agreed on what they were for.

### Sequence Partnerships To Capacity

A venture can only service a small number of partnerships well. Sequencing matters: pursue the partnership that addresses the most important gap first, service it to the point of measurable return, and only then add the next. Pursuing many partnerships in parallel ensures that none receives the attention required to succeed and that all underperform. Treat partnership capacity as a constrained resource and allocate it deliberately, not opportunistically.

## Common Traps

### Partnerships For The Press Release

Pursuing a partnership for the announcement rather than the outcome produces a relationship that delivers nothing after the launch. The trap is that the announcement feels like progress and validation. The cost is attention and obligations with no return, and credibility eroded when the partnership quietly produces nothing.

### Confusing Partnership Types

Applying the structure of one partnership type to the needs of another, for example a channel arrangement for what should be a technology integration, produces a relationship built for the wrong purpose. The trap is that all partnerships feel similar at the outset. The cost is a structure that cannot deliver the intended value.

### One-Sided Value Dependent On The Partner's Generosity

If the partner's benefit is unclear, the partnership survives only on the venture's effort. The trap is optimism about the partner's goodwill. The cost is a relationship that collapses the moment the venture stops driving it.

### Under-Diligenced Reliability

Signing without diligence on the partner's execution capacity, stability, and strategic alignment risks entanglement with a partner who cannot or will not deliver. The trap is the excitement of the relationship. The cost is an underperforming or adversarial partnership that is expensive to exit.

### Distraction From Core Focus

Interesting but non-central partnerships fragment attention from the work that determines the venture's success. The trap is that each partnership looks individually justifiable. The cost is cumulative distraction that slows the core.

### No Defined Success Criteria

Without agreed success criteria, partnerships drift, consume attention indefinitely, and become impossible to evaluate or exit. The trap is the discomfort of defining failure conditions up front. The cost is relationships that persist long after they have stopped delivering.

### Too Many Partnerships In Parallel

Pursuing many partnerships at once ensures none receives the attention to succeed. The trap is the apparent efficiency of parallel pursuit. The cost is a portfolio of underperforming relationships rather than one or two that deliver.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each partnership is pursued to fill a specific, named gap the venture cannot close alone, not because a partner was available.
- [ ] The partnership type (strategic, channel, technology, co-marketing) is identified and the structure matched to it.
- [ ] The value to the partner is articulated specifically and is as clear and strong as the value to the venture.
- [ ] Reliability and strategic alignment have been diligenced, including reference checks with other partners.
- [ ] The partnership is prioritized against core focus, with a willingness to decline non-central opportunities.
- [ ] Success criteria, with measures and timeframes, are defined before signing.
- [ ] Partnerships are sequenced to the venture's capacity to service them, not pursued in parallel beyond capacity.
- [ ] The venture says no to partnerships more often than yes, reserving commitment for those that clearly serve the core.
