# Insurance Selection And Coverage Gaps

Travel insurance is often discussed too late and too generally. Travelers may assume their credit card, health plan, supplier policy, or basic travel policy covers every disruption, only to discover exclusions after illness, weather, baggage loss, medical evacuation, or cancellation. Agents often say "buy travel insurance" without helping the traveler identify what risks need coverage or where gaps may remain. Strong insurance guidance helps travelers ask the right questions while avoiding overconfident coverage promises.

Use this skill when discussing travel insurance, medical coverage, cancellation exposure, evacuation, baggage, adventure activities, or coverage gaps.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Trip's Actual Financial And Health Risks

Insurance needs depend on trip cost, nonrefundable exposure, traveler health, destination medical infrastructure, activity risk, weather season, supplier reliability, and tolerance for loss. A domestic weekend with refundable hotel has different needs from a cruise, safari, ski trip, expedition, international family trip, or medical-sensitive journey.

List the risks that matter: cancellation, interruption, medical care, evacuation, baggage, delay, missed connection, rental car, adventure sports, supplier default, political unrest, hurricanes, or work cancellation. Then evaluate coverage types against those risks.

### Separate Supplier Terms From Insurance Coverage

Refundable supplier terms and insurance coverage are different. A hotel refund policy may help before arrival but not after interruption. Airline credits are not cash refunds. Cruise or tour penalties may be strict. Insurance may consider supplier credits before reimbursing.

Map what each supplier refunds or credits, then identify what remains exposed. Insurance should be evaluated against remaining risk, not assumed to replace every nonrefundable term.

### Check Medical And Evacuation Needs Carefully

Domestic health insurance may not cover international care, out-of-network treatment, evacuation, or upfront payment. Some destinations have limited medical facilities, remote access, or costly evacuation. Travelers with chronic conditions, pregnancy, older age, adventure activities, or remote itineraries need extra attention.

Encourage travelers to verify health coverage and consider medical travel coverage or evacuation benefits. Do not provide medical or insurance legal advice beyond recommending policy review and qualified sources.

### Watch Time-Sensitive Benefits

Some policies offer benefits only if purchased within a window after initial trip deposit, such as preexisting condition waivers or cancel-for-any-reason options, depending on policy. Travelers who wait may lose options.

Flag timing early, especially before large deposits or final payment. The planner should not interpret eligibility definitively unless qualified, but should make the deadline risk visible.

### Identify Activity And Destination Exclusions

Adventure sports, skiing, diving, trekking, motorbikes, altitude, alcohol-related incidents, unattended belongings, epidemics, known storms, war, sanctioned countries, and government advisories may be excluded or require upgrades. Rental car coverage may exclude certain countries or vehicle types.

Compare the policy to the itinerary. If the traveler plans activities beyond ordinary tourism, coverage must be checked specifically. Do not assume "travel insurance" covers adventure.

### Compare Credit Card Coverage Realistically

Premium credit cards may include trip delay, cancellation, rental car, baggage, or evacuation-related benefits, but coverage can be limited by payment method, trip length, covered reasons, dollar limits, companions, and documentation. Some cards are secondary coverage.

Ask the traveler to review their card benefits and limitations. Card coverage can be useful, but it is rarely a universal substitute for travel insurance.

### Preserve Documentation For Claims

Insurance claims require evidence: policy, booking confirmations, proof of payment, supplier terms, cancellation notices, medical documentation, delay notices, receipts, police reports, baggage reports, and proof of refunds or credits. Documentation should be gathered during disruption, not weeks later.

Tell travelers to keep receipts and written confirmations. A covered event can still be denied if evidence is missing.

### Communicate Uncertainty Clearly

Only the insurer can approve a claim. The planner can point out likely issues, encourage review, and help organize documents, but should not guarantee coverage. Use cautious wording and direct travelers to policy documents, licensed insurance professionals, or insurer support for definitive interpretation.

This protects the traveler from false confidence and the planner from overpromising.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Insurance Covers Any Cancellation

Policies cover specific reasons and exclusions. Disliking the trip or changing plans may not be covered.

### Buying After The Risk Is Known

Known storms, illness, strikes, or advisories may not be covered if insurance is purchased too late.

### Ignoring Medical Evacuation

Remote or international trips may need evacuation coverage, not just trip cancellation.

### Relying Blindly On Credit Cards

Card benefits have limits, exclusions, and payment requirements. Verify.

### Missing Adventure Exclusions

Skiing, diving, trekking, motorbikes, and altitude may need specific coverage.

### Treating Supplier Credits As Full Recovery

Credits can reduce insurance reimbursement and may be less useful than cash.

### Forgetting Documentation

Claims fail when travelers lack receipts, notices, reports, or proof of loss.

### Overpromising Claim Outcomes

Only policy terms and insurer review determine coverage. Avoid guarantees.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are trip-specific financial, medical, activity, destination, and disruption risks identified?
- [ ] Are supplier refund and credit terms separated from insurance coverage?
- [ ] Are medical care, evacuation, remote destination, chronic condition, pregnancy, and age-related needs considered?
- [ ] Are time-sensitive benefits such as preexisting condition waiver or cancel-for-any-reason flagged for review?
- [ ] Are activity, destination, advisory, weather, alcohol, vehicle, and adventure exclusions checked against the itinerary?
- [ ] Are credit card benefits reviewed realistically for limits, covered reasons, payment requirements, and exclusions?
- [ ] Are claim documentation needs explained before disruption occurs?
- [ ] Is coverage uncertainty communicated without guarantees?
- [ ] Are travelers directed to policy documents, insurer, or licensed professionals for definitive advice?
- [ ] Would the traveler understand what remains uninsured or uncertain?
