# Advisor Fees Scope And Client Service Model

Professional travel advice needs a clear service model. Without it, clients may expect unlimited research, instant responses, fee-free changes, full disruption support, or advocacy for bookings the advisor did not control. Agents often produce attractive trip ideas without defining what the advisor is being paid to do, how supplier commissions affect the relationship, what is included in planning, and what happens after booking. A strong advisor model protects the client, the advisor, and supplier relationships by making scope, fees, authority, and service expectations explicit.

Use this skill when preparing a travel proposal, agency service agreement, fee explanation, client onboarding, or post-booking support boundary.

## Core Rules

### Define The Service Scope In Plain Operational Terms

State what the advisor will do: destination consultation, itinerary design, hotel sourcing, flights, tours, dining, transfers, insurance guidance, supplier booking, document reminders, final itinerary, in-trip support, change handling, and post-trip follow-up. Also state what is excluded: immigration legal advice, medical advice, tax advice, 24/7 emergency rescue, bookings made independently, passport processing, or unlimited revisions.

Scope should match trip complexity. A weekend hotel booking does not need the same service model as a multi-country family trip, honeymoon, safari, cruise, incentive group, or destination wedding. The more complex the trip, the more important it is to define who owns each component.

### Explain Fees And Commissions Transparently

Travel advisors may earn planning fees, service fees, supplier commissions, markups, referral fees, or a combination. Clients do not need every supplier contract detail, but they should understand what they pay directly, what may be compensated by suppliers, when fees are due, whether fees are refundable, and what triggers additional fees.

Transparency prevents suspicion and helps clients value the work. If a planning fee covers research and design but not repeated redesign after a major brief change, say so. If an airline ticketing fee applies because air servicing is labor-intensive and low-commission, explain the reason.

### Separate Advice, Booking, And Servicing Authority

An advisor can advise on a component without booking it. They can book through some suppliers but not others. They can service reservations they ticket or control, but may have limited authority over client-booked reservations, OTA bookings, direct bookings, award tickets, or supplier-controlled changes.

Clarify which components the advisor will book, which the client will book, and what support applies to each. If the client books independently after receiving advice, define whether the advisor will support changes or disruptions for those components and whether extra fees apply.

### Set Revision And Decision Expectations

Trip planning can expand endlessly if decision rights and revision limits are unclear. Define the number or type of revisions included, what counts as a material change, how quickly the client must respond to holds or quotes, and when availability or price can change.

Use staged approvals: brief approval, concept approval, shortlist approval, booking approval, final itinerary review. This helps clients make decisions at the right level of detail and prevents repeated full redesigns after work has been completed.

### Establish Communication And Response Standards

Clients may expect immediate replies, especially for expensive or emotional trips. Advisors should define normal response times, urgent channels, business hours, in-trip support process, and emergency limitations. If true 24/7 support is not included, state what travelers should do in emergencies and who to contact first.

Communication standards should be realistic and client-centered. For high-touch luxury or group travel, more proactive communication may be appropriate. For simple planning, standard business-hour support may be sufficient.

### Clarify Risk, Insurance, And Supplier Responsibility

Advisors can help clients understand travel risks and supplier terms, but they do not control weather, airline operations, border decisions, government rules, supplier performance, strikes, illness, or traveler behavior. The service model should explain that suppliers provide the contracted services and that terms, cancellation penalties, and insurance coverage matter.

Recommend travel insurance awareness and encourage clients to review coverage. Avoid guaranteeing coverage or legal outcomes. For high-risk travel, state where the client should seek qualified advice, such as medical, legal, or immigration counsel.

### Protect Client Data And Payment Handling

Travel planning involves sensitive data: passport details, birthdates, payment cards, loyalty numbers, health needs, dietary restrictions, family information, and travel patterns. Define secure collection, storage, sharing, and deletion practices as appropriate. Do not casually ask for or store payment details if a safer process exists.

Clarify who charges the client: advisor, agency, supplier, tour operator, cruise line, hotel, or consolidator. Payment transparency reduces dispute risk and helps clients recognize legitimate charges.

### Design The Post-Booking Service Model

After booking, clients need confirmations, deadline reminders, final documents, changes, supplier reconfirmation, and disruption support. Define what the advisor monitors and what the traveler must monitor. For example, the advisor may track major schedule changes for tickets issued by the agency but may not monitor client-booked flights.

Set expectations for change fees, cancellation handling, refunds, credits, claim documentation, and post-trip feedback. Post-booking service is often where advisor value is most visible, but only if responsibility is clear.

## Common Traps

### Giving Free Full Planning Before Scope Is Set

Extensive research without fee, scope, or commitment can train clients to treat planning labor as free and can create confusion over what is owed.

### Hiding Compensation Model

Clients may distrust recommendations if fees and commissions are opaque. Transparent explanation improves trust.

### Supporting Uncontrolled Bookings As If Controlled

Advisors may not be able to change or advocate effectively for reservations booked directly by the client or through an OTA. Boundaries must be clear.

### Allowing Unlimited Revisions

Unlimited revisions turn planning into open-ended labor and delay booking until availability disappears. Define revision rules.

### Overpromising Emergency Support

Unless the agency truly provides 24/7 support, do not imply that the advisor can solve every in-trip emergency immediately.

### Treating Supplier Terms As Fine Print

Cancellation, deposit, refund, and change terms affect real money. Clients need to understand major exposure before approval.

### Collecting Sensitive Data Casually

Passport and payment information should be handled carefully. Unnecessary collection creates privacy and security risk.

### Failing To Define Post-Trip Responsibilities

Refunds, credits, claims, complaints, and feedback can linger after travel. Define what support is included and what requires additional work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is advisor scope defined in operational terms with clear inclusions and exclusions?
- [ ] Are planning fees, service fees, commissions, markups, due dates, refundability, and extra-fee triggers explained?
- [ ] Are advice, booking authority, and servicing responsibility separated by trip component?
- [ ] Are client-booked or third-party-booked components handled with clear support boundaries?
- [ ] Are revision limits, approval stages, quote validity, and decision deadlines defined?
- [ ] Are communication standards, urgent support process, and emergency limitations clear?
- [ ] Are supplier responsibility, cancellation terms, travel risk, and insurance awareness explained without overpromising?
- [ ] Are client data and payment handling practices appropriate for sensitivity?
- [ ] Is the post-booking support model clear for changes, documents, disruption, refunds, and claims?
- [ ] Would the client understand what they are paying for and what remains their responsibility?
