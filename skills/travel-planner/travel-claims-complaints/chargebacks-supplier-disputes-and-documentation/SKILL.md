# Chargebacks Supplier Disputes And Documentation

Travel disputes are evidence problems before they are persuasion problems. Flights, hotels, tours, cruises, car rentals, and platforms may each have different terms, payment flows, and service records. Agents often jump to emotional complaint language or advise chargebacks without checking the contract, timeline, and evidence. This skill helps the agent organize facts, preserve options, and avoid overclaiming legal or financial outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Classify the dispute accurately

Determine whether the issue is non-delivery, partial delivery, service quality, billing error, duplicate charge, cancellation refund, schedule change, denied boarding, hotel overbooking, voucher rejection, damage claim, insurance claim, or dissatisfaction. Each category has different evidence and escalation path.

Do not treat every disappointment as a chargeback. If the supplier delivered the contracted service poorly, the route may be complaint or goodwill rather than payment reversal. If the service was not provided, evidence burden differs.

### Reconstruct the contract and payment flow

Identify who sold the service, who charged the card, who operated the service, which terms applied, and whether an OTA, wholesaler, advisor, supplier, tour operator, or credit card portal was involved. The merchant of record may differ from the supplier the traveler met.

Collect booking confirmations, terms, cancellation policy, receipts, ticket numbers, vouchers, supplier locators, payment statements, emails, and screenshots from before the dispute. Payment flow determines who can refund and who must answer a chargeback.

### Build a dated evidence file

Create a timeline with dates, times, names, locations, confirmation numbers, promised service, actual service, communications, attempted resolutions, and financial amounts. Add photos, videos, call logs, chat transcripts, emails, boarding passes, hotel folios, police reports, medical notes if relevant, and replacement purchase receipts.

Evidence should be factual and concise. Emotional narrative can support the story, but disputes are stronger when documents prove the claim.

### Check deadlines and sequence escalation

Refund requests, supplier complaints, chargebacks, insurance claims, and legal remedies have deadlines. Credit card disputes may require trying to resolve with the merchant first. Insurance may require prompt notice. Suppliers may require written claims within a short window.

Do not delay while waiting for informal promises. Record promised response dates and escalate when missed. But avoid filing conflicting claims that undermine each other without understanding consequences.

### Use precise, proportionate remedy requests

Ask for the remedy that matches the failure: full refund, partial refund, fee reversal, credit, replacement service, reimbursement, points restoration, apology, correction, or goodwill. Overbroad demands can weaken credibility.

Calculate amounts clearly. Separate prepaid amounts, unused nights, taxes, fees, replacement costs, transportation, meals, and consequential losses. Do not include speculative damages unless a qualified advisor says they are claimable.

### Preserve chargeback credibility

Chargebacks can be useful for clear non-delivery, unauthorized charges, duplicate billing, or merchant refusal to honor terms. They can also be denied if documentation is weak, terms allowed the charge, or the traveler used the service. Chargebacks may affect supplier relationships or future accounts.

Do not guarantee chargeback success. Encourage travelers to follow card issuer instructions and provide accurate evidence. Misrepresenting a dispute is risky and unethical.

### Coordinate insurance and supplier claims

Travel insurance claims may require supplier denial, cancellation proof, medical documentation, police reports, delay certificates, baggage reports, or receipts. Supplier refunds and insurance reimbursements should not duplicate the same loss.

The agent can help organize documents but should not interpret policy coverage beyond general awareness unless qualified. Direct travelers to insurer terms and claims channels.

### Keep communication professional

Complaints should be specific, chronological, and calm. Identify booking details, what was promised, what happened, evidence attached, remedy requested, and response deadline. Avoid insults, threats, or exaggerated claims.

Professional tone helps escalation staff act. It also creates a clean record if the dispute moves to card issuer, regulator, insurer, or legal advisor.

## Common Traps

- Advising a chargeback before identifying merchant, supplier, terms, and service category.
- Relying on anger instead of a dated evidence file.
- Missing claim, refund, insurance, or card dispute deadlines.
- Asking for a full refund when a partial remedy matches the documented failure.
- Forgetting screenshots of terms before webpages change.
- Confusing OTA confirmation, supplier locator, voucher, and payment receipt.
- Filing supplier, insurance, and chargeback claims inconsistently; promising legal, insurance, or chargeback outcomes beyond the agent's role
- Including speculative or unsupported losses that distract from the main claim; letting phone promises go undocumented

## Self-Check

- Is the dispute classified by non-delivery, partial delivery, quality, billing, cancellation, voucher, damage, or dissatisfaction?
- Have merchant of record, seller, operator, booking channel, payment flow, and terms been identified?
- Is there a dated evidence file with confirmations, receipts, screenshots, communications, photos, and replacement costs?
- Are supplier, insurance, chargeback, and complaint deadlines tracked?
- Is the requested remedy precise, proportional, and calculated clearly?
- Has chargeback advice avoided guarantees and followed card issuer requirements?
- Are insurance and supplier claims coordinated to avoid duplicate or inconsistent recovery?
- Is the complaint communication professional, factual, chronological, and evidence-based?
