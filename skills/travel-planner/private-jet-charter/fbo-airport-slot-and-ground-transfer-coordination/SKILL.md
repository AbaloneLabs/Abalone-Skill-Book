# FBO Airport Slot And Ground Transfer Coordination

Private flight success depends heavily on what happens around the aircraft. The right airport, FBO, slot, customs arrangement, ground transfer, luggage plan, and arrival instructions can make private aviation feel seamless. The wrong choices can erase the benefit of charter through delays, curfews, access restrictions, or confused drivers. Agents should treat FBO and ground coordination as core itinerary design, not as supplier detail.

## Core Rules

### Choose airports by mission, not map distance alone

Private aircraft may access smaller airports, but airport choice depends on runway suitability, customs, operating hours, curfews, slots, parking, fuel, weather alternates, FBO quality, ground distance, road reliability, and destination access. The closest airport may not be usable or best.

Compare airport options with the operator. For ski resorts, islands, events, remote estates, and city centers, ground transfer time and weather reliability may matter as much as flight time. Do not promise a specific airport until the operator confirms feasibility.

### Confirm FBO and passenger experience

FBOs vary in lounges, privacy, vehicle access, customs facilities, pet handling, mobility support, luggage help, catering, restrooms, meeting rooms, and operating hours. High-touch travelers may expect discretion and quick movement; families may need space, snacks, and bathrooms.

Provide clear arrival instructions: FBO name, address, entrance, parking, ID needed, arrival time, contact number, and whether passengers can be driven aircraft-side. Many travelers do not know that private aviation uses different terminals.

### Manage slots, PPR, curfews, and permits

Some airports require slots, prior permission required, parking reservations, noise restrictions, curfews, overflight permits, landing permits, or special event controls. Peak events, holidays, and congested airports can restrict access. International flights add customs and immigration requirements.

Track which constraints are confirmed and which remain pending. If a slot or permit is not secured, the itinerary should not treat timing as guaranteed.

### Coordinate customs and international arrival

Private international travel may require passenger details, passports, visas, customs declarations, agriculture rules, pet documents, weapons restrictions, cash declarations, and advance passenger information. Customs availability may determine airport choice or schedule.

Travel planners should not provide immigration legal advice, but they must ensure official requirements are checked and operator instructions are followed. A private aircraft does not remove border controls.

### Align ground transfers with aircraft timing

Drivers need FBO address, tail or flight reference if appropriate, passenger names, luggage, vehicle type, child seats, pets, mobility equipment, security protocol, and contact chain. Private flight times can shift due to weather, crew, slot, or passenger timing, so transfer providers must monitor updates.

Plan pickup location precisely. At some FBOs, drivers wait landside; at others, they may approach aircraft with permission. Misunderstanding this can delay high-value travelers.

### Handle luggage, pets, and special equipment

Private aviation has luggage limits based on aircraft type and mission. Large skis, golf clubs, medical devices, musical instruments, strollers, pets, and fragile items need confirmation. Ground vehicles must also fit the luggage.

Do not let a luxurious aircraft choice hide a baggage problem. Ask early and communicate dimensions and weights to the operator where required.

### Plan for weather and airport alternates

Weather can change airport choice, departure time, route, and ground transfer. Fog, crosswinds, thunderstorms, snow, icing, smoke, and mountain weather may force alternate airports. If an alternate is used, ground transfer distance may change significantly.

Have a plan for alternate airport ground movement when the destination is time-sensitive. For remote areas, the alternate may be hours away.

### Keep passenger instructions simple and secure

Final instructions should be concise: arrival time, FBO address, ID, baggage limits, contact, parking, driver plan, customs documents, and contingency contact. Avoid sending unnecessary tail numbers, passenger details, or private addresses to broad lists.

For high-profile travelers, coordinate communication through agreed channels and limit itinerary visibility.

## Common Traps

- Choosing the closest airport before confirming runway, customs, FBO, curfew, and weather suitability.
- Assuming all FBOs offer the same privacy, vehicle access, and passenger support.
- Treating slots, PPR, parking, and permits as automatic.
- Forgetting customs and immigration requirements for private international flights.
- Sending drivers to the main terminal instead of the FBO.
- Failing to update ground transfers when aircraft timing changes.
- Discovering luggage, ski, golf, pet, or medical equipment limits too late.
- Having no plan if weather forces an alternate airport.
- Overcomplicating passenger instructions with operational details they do not need.
- Sharing sensitive tail, passenger, or address details too broadly.

## Self-Check

- Has the operator confirmed airport feasibility, runway, weather, customs, hours, curfew, parking, and FBO suitability?
- Are FBO address, entrance, parking, arrival time, ID needs, contact, and vehicle access clear to passengers?
- Are slots, PPR, permits, parking, and special event controls confirmed or marked pending?
- Are customs, immigration, passport, visa, pet, agriculture, and passenger information requirements being handled through official/operator guidance?
- Do drivers have the correct FBO, timing updates, passenger count, luggage, child seats, pets, mobility needs, and contact chain?
- Are luggage and special equipment compatible with aircraft and ground vehicle?
- Is there an alternate-airport ground transfer plan for weather-sensitive destinations?
- Are passenger instructions simple, secure, and limited to need-to-know details?
