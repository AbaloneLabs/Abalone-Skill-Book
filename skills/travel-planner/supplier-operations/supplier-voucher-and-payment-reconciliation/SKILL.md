# Supplier Voucher And Payment Reconciliation

Travel can fail at the point of service when payment and confirmation records do not match. A traveler may arrive with an agency voucher the hotel cannot find, a tour may expect payment onsite, a transfer may have the wrong date, or a DMC may not have received final balance. Agents often assume that a booked service is ready because it appears in an itinerary. Strong supplier operations reconcile confirmations, vouchers, payment status, and traveler documents before departure so the traveler is not asked to solve back-office problems on the road.

Use this skill when preparing final documents, checking prepaid services, reconciling supplier payments, or servicing changed bookings.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Booking, Confirmation, Voucher, And Payment

A booking request is not the same as supplier confirmation. A confirmation is not always proof of payment. A voucher may be required for service even when a booking exists. Payment may be deposit, prepaid, balance due, pay locally, agency account, virtual card, or direct supplier charge.

For each service, identify status: requested, held, confirmed, ticketed, vouchered, deposit paid, fully prepaid, balance due, pay locally, cancelled, refunded, or pending change. Do not send final documents with ambiguous status.

### Reconcile Supplier Details Against The Itinerary

Compare each voucher or confirmation to the traveler itinerary: traveler names, dates, times, pickup location, drop-off location, room type, meal plan, activity, guide language, vehicle type, number of guests, inclusions, exclusions, emergency contact, cancellation terms, and supplier reference.

Small mismatches matter. A transfer date off by one day, wrong hotel name, missing child seat, or incorrect passenger count can create service failure. Reconcile after every change.

### Verify Payment And Balance Due

Travelers should know what has been paid and what must be paid locally. Suppliers should know whether they are prepaid. Confusion can lead to duplicate charges, denied service, or awkward disputes at check-in.

Track payment method, amount, currency, payer, due date, receipt, invoice, and balance. For DMCs, villas, tours, or cruises, confirm final payment was received and applied. For hotels using virtual cards or agency billing, ensure the property understands payment arrangement.

### Confirm Vouchers Are Usable By The Traveler

A voucher should contain enough information for the traveler and supplier: supplier name, service date, service description, confirmation number, traveler names, inclusions, meeting point, emergency contact, local payment due, and cancellation notes. It should be readable offline.

Avoid sending internal supplier invoices or documents with confidential net rates, commissions, or irrelevant back-office notes. Traveler documents should be clean and service-ready.

### Handle Net Rates, Commissions, And Confidential Pricing Carefully

Travel trade documents may include net rates, commission, markup, supplier incentives, or agency payment details that should not be shown to the traveler. At the same time, the traveler needs enough proof to receive service.

Use client-facing vouchers or confirmations where available. If a supplier document exposes confidential pricing, create a traveler-safe summary while preserving internal records.

### Reconfirm Critical Services Before Departure

For high-stakes services, reconfirm with suppliers shortly before travel: first-night hotel, late arrival, airport transfer, cruise transfer, private guide, mobility equipment, safari camp, remote lodge, special meal, or prepaid activity. Confirm date, time, names, payment, and emergency contact.

This is especially important after schedule changes, supplier changes, or long gaps between booking and travel. Reconfirmation catches system drift.

### Track Refunds, Credits, And Reissued Vouchers After Changes

When a booking changes, old vouchers may remain in circulation. Cancelled services may need refunds or credits. New services may require new vouchers and payments. The traveler should not carry both old and new instructions without clarity.

Void or mark superseded documents. Track financial changes separately from operational changes. Ensure refunded or credited amounts reconcile to supplier records.

### Preserve Internal Audit Trail

Keep supplier invoices, receipts, confirmations, vouchers, payment proof, refund records, and correspondence organized. If a supplier disputes payment or a traveler is charged again, the advisor needs evidence quickly.

Good reconciliation protects traveler experience, advisor accountability, and financial control.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Itinerary Equals Confirmation

An itinerary can list services that are not confirmed, paid, or vouchered. Verify status.

### Sending Documents With Hidden Net Rates

Trade documents may reveal confidential pricing. Use traveler-safe vouchers.

### Missing Pay-Locally Balances

Travelers can be surprised by taxes, resort fees, activity balances, or local cash payments. State them clearly.

### Forgetting Virtual Card Issues

Hotels may not see or process virtual card payment correctly. Confirm billing instructions.

### Letting Old Vouchers Survive Changes

Old documents can send travelers to wrong places or create duplicate charges. Supersede clearly.

### Ignoring Time Zone Or Date Errors

International travel and overnight flights create date mistakes. Reconcile carefully.

### Not Reconfirming Critical Services

High-stakes first services deserve a final check before departure.

### Weak Payment Evidence

Without receipts and correspondence, duplicate charges or denied service are harder to resolve.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is each service status clear: requested, held, confirmed, ticketed, vouchered, paid, balance due, cancelled, refunded, or pending?
- [ ] Do vouchers match the itinerary for names, dates, times, locations, guest count, inclusions, and contacts?
- [ ] Are payment amount, currency, payer, method, receipt, invoice, due date, and balance tracked?
- [ ] Are traveler vouchers usable offline and free of confidential net rates or internal notes?
- [ ] Are local payments, taxes, fees, deposits, and balances clearly shown to the traveler?
- [ ] Are critical hotels, transfers, guides, tours, lodges, and special services reconfirmed before departure?
- [ ] Are old vouchers voided or marked superseded after changes?
- [ ] Are refunds, credits, new payments, and reissued vouchers reconciled?
- [ ] Is internal evidence organized for supplier disputes or duplicate charges?
- [ ] Could the traveler present the documents and receive service without explaining the back office?
