# Trip Scope And Success Criteria

An itinerary can be detailed and still miss the trip. Before designing routes, hotels, and activities, the agent needs to understand what success means for this traveler in this moment. A retirement trip, school visit, honeymoon, conference, religious pilgrimage, medical trip, and low-cost backpacking route have different definitions of success. Agents often jump into recommendations while leaving purpose, constraints, budget, decision process, and acceptable tradeoffs implicit. Scope prevents attractive but misaligned plans.

## Core Rules

### Define the primary purpose of the trip

Ask what the trip must accomplish. The answer may be rest, celebration, family connection, cultural learning, business productivity, recovery, adventure, obligation, status, grief support, scouting relocation, or simply being away. Secondary goals matter, but the primary purpose should guide tradeoffs.

If a trip has multiple purposes, rank them. A conference trip with a spouse may need productivity first and leisure second. A family reunion may need accessibility and togetherness before sightseeing. A honeymoon may need privacy and service reliability before maximizing activities.

### Establish non-negotiables and flexible variables

Non-negotiables include dates, budget ceiling, passport rules, mobility access, medical needs, child gear, dietary restrictions, flight duration, room configuration, safety, religious observance, pet requirements, or must-attend events. Flexible variables may include destination, hotel style, activity mix, departure airport, exact neighborhood, or trip length.

Write non-negotiables in operational terms. "Nice hotel" is not a non-negotiable; "quiet room with elevator access near the conference venue under 15 minutes by taxi" is actionable.

### Set budget as a planning tool, not a guess

Clarify total budget, per-person budget, excluded costs, payment timing, refund tolerance, upgrade flexibility, currency, and whether the traveler prefers one all-in price or component choices. Ask what happens if the desired trip cannot fit the target budget.

Budget should shape scope. If the budget cannot support the desired destination, season, or comfort level safely, reduce nights, change dates, choose fewer cities, adjust lodging, or select a different destination. Do not hide mismatch until booking.

### Define success criteria that can be tested

Success criteria should let the agent judge whether a proposal fits. Examples include "no one changes hotels more than twice," "children have pool time every afternoon," "arrive one day before the cruise," "avoid red-eye flights," "two accessible bathrooms are confirmed," or "keep daily walking under three miles."

Vague goals like "memorable" or "authentic" need translation. Ask what those words mean in activities, pace, lodging, food, guides, crowds, language, and comfort. The proposal should be evaluated against the translated criteria.

### Clarify planning authority and service scope

Determine whether the agent is brainstorming, making a shortlist, producing a full itinerary, booking components, servicing changes, or only advising. Clarify whether flights, insurance, visas, dining, tours, transfers, special requests, and in-trip support are in scope.

If the traveler will book independently, make that boundary visible. A plan that assumes advisor servicing but is booked through unrelated channels may fail during disruption.

### Identify risk tolerance and recovery expectations

Some travelers accept tight connections, nonrefundable rates, basic rooms, remote transfers, uncertain weather, or self-guided logistics. Others need reliability, buffers, service support, and flexibility. Risk tolerance should be tied to trip stakes, not personality alone.

High-stakes occasions, cruises, medical appointments, school start dates, safaris, weddings, and business meetings need more conservative planning. State where extra buffers or refundable options are protecting the trip.

### Mark unknowns and research obligations

Early scope often includes unknowns: visa rules, weather, closures, festival dates, supplier availability, accessibility verification, budget realism, safety, or medical requirements. List unknowns and define what evidence will resolve them.

Do not pretend the proposal is final if key facts are unverified. A strong scope identifies what must be checked before quoting or booking.

### Define completion before starting detailed design

Agree what a finished deliverable looks like: destination recommendation, comparative shortlist, day-by-day itinerary, booking-ready quote, final operating document, emergency plan, or supplier handoff. This prevents overwork and underdelivery.

Set expected revision path and decision deadlines. Availability and prices change, so the scope should explain when the traveler must approve holds, deposits, or tickets.

## Common Traps

- Starting with destinations before knowing what the trip must accomplish.
- Treating all stated preferences as equal rather than ranking primary purpose and constraints.
- Using vague terms like luxury, authentic, relaxed, or family-friendly without operational definition.
- Accepting a budget that cannot support the requested season, destination, lodging, or risk tolerance.
- Designing a full itinerary when the client only approved brainstorming or a shortlist.
- Forgetting excluded costs such as meals, transfers, resort fees, bags, tips, visas, and insurance.
- Assuming the traveler wants maximum activities rather than success against their purpose.
- Ignoring risk tolerance until proposing nonrefundable or fragile components.
- Calling a plan complete while key documents, availability, accessibility, or local rules are unverified.
- Failing to define revision limits and decision deadlines before availability changes.

## Self-Check

- Is the primary trip purpose clear and ranked against secondary goals?
- Are non-negotiables written in operational terms rather than vague preferences?
- Is the budget defined by total, per person, exclusions, payment timing, refund tolerance, and flexibility?
- Are success criteria specific enough to test the proposal?
- Is planning scope clear for brainstorming, itinerary design, booking, servicing, documents, and support?
- Does risk tolerance match trip stakes, deadlines, and traveler capability?
- Are unknowns, research obligations, and evidence requirements listed before booking?
- Is the final deliverable defined with revision path and decision deadlines?
- Would a different agent understand what not to optimize because it is outside scope?
