# Client Intake And Trip Proposal

Professional travel planning starts before destinations and hotels. A weak intake produces weak proposals, surprise costs, scope creep, and mismatched expectations. Agents may rush to produce an itinerary before clarifying budget authority, traveler constraints, decision timeline, service scope, booking responsibility, and what "good" means for the client.

Use this skill when acting as a travel planner, advisor, concierge, or agency assistant preparing client questions, proposals, quotes, or option comparisons. The goal is to turn a request into a decision-ready brief and a proposal the client can approve.

## Core Rules

### Qualify The Request Before Designing

Gather enough information to know whether the trip is feasible and what kind of work is required.

Clarify:

- destination or openness to suggestions;
- dates and flexibility;
- number and type of travelers;
- purpose and desired feeling;
- budget range and inclusions;
- must-haves and dealbreakers;
- accessibility, dietary, medical, or documentation constraints;
- booking timeline;
- decision maker;
- whether the planner books or only advises.

If a key constraint is unknown, state assumptions in the proposal.

### Identify Budget Authority And Scope

Budget confusion causes rework. Determine who approves the spend and what is included.

Define:

- total budget;
- whether flights are included;
- lodging standard;
- activity and dining level;
- fees, insurance, and transfers;
- service fee or planning fee if applicable;
- payment deadlines;
- refund and cancellation expectations.

Do not design luxury components for a moderate budget unless presenting a deliberate stretch option.

### Present Options By Tradeoff

Clients need choices that differ meaningfully.

Good option sets compare:

- lower cost versus higher convenience;
- fewer bases versus more coverage;
- central lodging versus larger space;
- guided structure versus independent flexibility;
- refundable versus nonrefundable;
- relaxed pace versus ambitious itinerary.

Avoid giving three versions that only differ by hotel name without explaining the decision logic.

### Make Assumptions And Exclusions Visible

A proposal should not hide uncertainty.

State:

- prices subject to availability;
- what is not included;
- what requires current verification;
- passport, visa, or health assumptions;
- cancellation terms not yet confirmed;
- optional items;
- decisions still needed from the client.

This prevents the client from treating a planning draft as a guaranteed quote.

### Use Proposal Structure That Supports Approval

A proposal should help the client decide quickly.

Include:

- summary of trip concept;
- why the plan fits the brief;
- high-level itinerary;
- key inclusions;
- estimated cost or range;
- major tradeoffs;
- booking urgency;
- risks or verification items;
- next steps and decision deadline.

Do not bury critical cost or risk information in footnotes.

### Set Change And Communication Expectations

Clients may expect unlimited revisions or instant responses unless boundaries are clear.

Define:

- number or type of revisions;
- response times;
- what changes may affect price;
- when availability can no longer be held;
- who contacts suppliers;
- how approvals are documented;
- what happens after booking.

This is especially important when prices change quickly.

### Keep The Proposal Honest

Sales language should not overpromise. Avoid guaranteeing weather, upgrades, entry, safety, or supplier performance. Highlight strengths and tradeoffs.

Good proposals build trust by showing what is excellent, what is uncertain, and where the client has a decision.

### Match Proposal Depth To Commitment Stage

Not every client needs a fully built itinerary immediately. Early proposals should often compare direction, budget, and feasibility. Later proposals should become more specific about hotels, routing, activities, and terms. Excess detail too early can waste work and create false expectations; too little detail near booking can hide risk.

Use a staged approach:

- concept stage: trip style, destination logic, rough budget, major risks;
- shortlist stage: route, lodging areas, sample hotels, pace, and key tradeoffs;
- booking stage: current availability, terms, deposits, deadlines, and decision points;
- final stage: confirmations, vouchers, contacts, and traveler instructions.

This sequencing helps the client approve the right level of detail at the right time.

### Capture What Would Make The Trip A Failure

Clients often state what they want, but not what they fear. Ask or infer the failure conditions: too rushed, too expensive, unsafe, too touristy, bad food, poor sleep, too many transfers, not enough downtime, disappointing room, or missed special occasion. A proposal should actively avoid those failure conditions, not merely add attractive features.

## Common Traps

### Skipping Intake To Impress The Client

A fast itinerary can look responsive but miss the real brief.

### Presenting Too Many Options

Too many choices shift planning labor back to the client.

### Hiding Budget Gaps

If the desired trip exceeds budget, say so and offer tradeoffs.

### Treating Estimates As Holds

Prices are not secured until booking or hold confirmation.

### Forgetting Decision Rights

Groups need a decision maker or the proposal may stall.

### Overselling Certainty

Travel has availability, weather, supplier, and entry uncertainty.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Intake captures destination, dates, travelers, purpose, budget, constraints, timeline, and decision maker.
- [ ] Budget scope and inclusions are explicit.
- [ ] Options differ by meaningful tradeoffs, not cosmetic details.
- [ ] Assumptions, exclusions, verification items, and price volatility are visible.
- [ ] Proposal structure supports approval with summary, fit, itinerary, cost, risks, and next steps.
- [ ] Revision, communication, approval, and booking expectations are clear.
- [ ] The proposal does not guarantee weather, entry, upgrades, safety, or supplier performance.
- [ ] Budget gaps are addressed with concrete alternatives.
- [ ] The client knows what decision is required and by when.
- [ ] The proposal is decision-ready rather than merely attractive.
