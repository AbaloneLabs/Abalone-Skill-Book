# Recovery Accommodation And Aftercare Logistics

Recovery travel is not normal lodging with extra rest time. After a procedure, illness, injury, dental work, fertility treatment, or wellness intervention, the traveler may have mobility limits, pain, medication schedules, wound care, diet restrictions, infection risk, fatigue, privacy needs, and uncertain clearance to travel. Agents often choose comfortable hotels without checking the physical and operational needs of recovery. This skill helps the planner support aftercare logistics while leaving clinical decisions to qualified professionals.

## Core Rules

### Start With Clinician-Provided Restrictions

Ask what the clinician has specified: observation period, follow-up appointments, wound care, medication timing, diet, bathing, lifting, walking, stairs, driving, flying, compression garments, sleep position, infection precautions, emergency symptoms, and required companion. Do not create these rules yourself. If instructions are missing, tell the traveler to obtain them before finalizing travel.

Translate restrictions into logistics. If stairs are limited, lodging needs elevator access. If refrigeration is required, the room needs a fridge. If frequent follow-up is needed, lodging should be near the clinic. If rest is critical, avoid noisy nightlife districts. If the traveler cannot lift luggage, arrange porterage or companion help.

### Choose Lodging For Recovery Function

Recovery lodging should be evaluated for elevator, step-free access, room size, bathroom type, walk-in shower or tub suitability, bed height, quietness, blackout, climate control, refrigerator, microwave or kitchenette, laundry, room service or grocery delivery, proximity to clinic and pharmacy, 24-hour front desk, and ability to extend stay.

Vacation rentals may offer kitchen and space but lack front desk support, elevator reliability, daily cleaning, medical assistance, or flexible extension. Hotels may offer support but small rooms and restaurant dependence. Choose based on recovery needs, not only comfort or price. Avoid describing lodging as medically suitable unless the relevant features are verified.

### Plan Transport With Mobility And Comfort In Mind

Post-treatment transport may require private car, larger vehicle, minimal walking, wheelchair assistance, help with luggage, avoidance of bumpy roads, frequent stops, or direct routing. Public transit may be inappropriate even if normally efficient. Airport assistance, priority boarding, and extra connection time may be needed.

Confirm whether the traveler can sit for long periods, climb into vehicles, manage stairs, carry bags, or travel alone. Refer clinical clearance questions to the treating clinician. For return flights, consider schedule, layovers, airport walking distance, seat comfort, and contingency if clearance is delayed.

### Protect Medication, Supplies, And Food Access

Recovery may require prescriptions, dressings, ice packs, compression garments, special diet, hydration, stool softeners, pain management, antibiotics, or medical devices. The planner should help identify logistical access: pharmacy location, delivery, refrigeration, carry-on placement, translation of prescription names, and customs rules for medication.

Food planning matters. Post-procedure travelers may need soft foods, low-sodium meals, high-protein options, no alcohol, bland meals, or scheduled eating with medication. Do not assume hotel restaurants can accommodate. Arrange groceries or simple meals where needed.

### Build A Delayed-Return And Emergency Plan

Recovery timelines can change. The itinerary should allow extension if the traveler is not cleared to fly, needs an extra follow-up, develops a complication, or misses a transport connection. Check changeable fares, lodging extension possibility, insurance limitations, visa stay limits, and companion availability.

Emergency planning should include clinic after-hours number, local emergency number, nearest hospital, travel insurer assistance, companion contact, translation support, and copies of discharge instructions. The planner should not triage symptoms, but should make sure the traveler knows whom to contact.

### Respect Privacy And Emotional Load

Recovery can be private, painful, or emotionally intense. Do not expose procedure details in supplier notes unless necessary. Build a quiet schedule and avoid social obligations unless the traveler wants them. For fertility, cosmetic, dental, or sensitive procedures, privacy and discretion may be as important as logistics.

Companions need planning too. They may need lodging, meals, workspace, transport, emotional bandwidth, and instructions on what they are expected to do. A companion who is unprepared can become another stress point.

## Common Traps

- Choosing lodging by star rating instead of verified recovery features.
- Assuming the traveler can use stairs, carry luggage, sit in a small car, or walk long airport distances after treatment.
- Scheduling sightseeing, dining, or transfers without clinician clearance.
- Forgetting refrigerator, pharmacy, soft food, wound-care supplies, laundry, or room-service needs.
- Booking rigid return flights when clearance to travel may change.
- Treating vacation rentals as automatically better for recovery without checking support and access.
- Sharing procedure details broadly with hotels, drivers, or group members.
- Ignoring the companion's role, fatigue, and logistics.
- Providing clinical guidance instead of asking the traveler to obtain medical instructions.

## Self-Check

- Are clinician-provided restrictions obtained or flagged as required before final planning?
- Are restrictions translated into lodging, transport, meal, medication, and schedule needs?
- Are elevator, step-free access, bathroom, bed, quiet, fridge, food, laundry, and extension options verified?
- Is lodging close enough to clinic, pharmacy, and emergency care for the recovery plan?
- Are transport, airport assistance, luggage help, connection time, and vehicle comfort planned around mobility limits?
- Are medications, supplies, prescriptions, refrigeration, customs rules, and pharmacy access considered?
- Are food and hydration needs practical for the room and neighborhood?
- Is there a delayed-return plan covering flights, lodging, visa stay, insurance, and companion availability?
- Are clinic after-hours, emergency, hospital, insurer, and translation contacts available?
- Is privacy protected while companion expectations are clear?
