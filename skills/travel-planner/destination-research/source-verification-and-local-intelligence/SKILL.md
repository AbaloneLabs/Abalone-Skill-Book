# Source Verification And Local Intelligence

Destination research is full of stale, sponsored, copied, and context-free advice. A restaurant list may be old, a transit route may have changed, a neighborhood may be safe by day but difficult late at night, and an "official-looking" page may be a reseller. Agents often blend memory, search results, and travel blogs without explaining confidence or checking the source that actually controls the fact.

Use this skill before making destination-specific claims, validating local logistics, recommending neighborhoods, relying on opening hours, citing safety or entry information, or turning research into a travel plan. The goal is to make the agent source-sensitive without requiring exhaustive research for every low-stakes suggestion.

## Core Rules

### Match Source Strength To Decision Risk

Not every fact needs the same evidence. A casual cafe suggestion can rely on recent reviews and map checks. A visa rule, ferry cancellation policy, safety alert, or permit requirement needs official or provider confirmation.

Use stronger sources for:

- entry, visa, transit, and customs requirements;
- health, safety, weather, strikes, and emergency conditions;
- official attraction hours and timed-entry policies;
- transport schedules, last departures, and service disruptions;
- lodging legality, fees, and cancellation terms;
- park permits, trail closures, and restricted areas;
- insurance, cruise, airline, and tour operator terms.

Use softer sources for:

- atmosphere, crowd feel, food quality, neighborhood vibe, and subjective fit;
- recent traveler friction;
- practical tips such as where queues form or when a place feels crowded.

The source should be strong enough for the consequence of being wrong.

### Prefer Primary Sources For Controlled Facts

Controlled facts are facts owned by a specific authority or provider. Use the source that controls the fact when the plan depends on it.

Examples:

- a museum's own site for hours and closure days;
- a rail operator for schedules and strike updates;
- a park authority for permits and trail closures;
- an embassy or government site for entry requirements;
- an airline for baggage and check-in rules;
- a hotel or booking contract for cancellation terms;
- an official event organizer for dates and access rules.

Third-party summaries can help find issues, but they should not be the final authority for high-stakes facts.

### Check Recency And Season

Travel information expires. A blog from three years ago may still describe a monument accurately but fail on prices, transport, safety, ticketing, or crowd management. Reviews from peak season may not describe winter. A pandemic-era rule may no longer apply.

Check:

- publication or update date;
- whether comments or reviews show recent changes;
- season of the visit;
- whether the source covers the same traveler type;
- whether the destination has recently changed rules, hours, access, or ownership;
- whether construction, road work, strikes, or local events affect the plan.

If recency is weak, mark the fact as requiring verification before booking or departure.

### Use Multiple Source Types For Local Fit

Local intelligence is richer when different source types agree or explain different angles.

Combine:

- official sites for rules and schedules;
- maps for geography, transit, walking time, and nearby services;
- recent reviews for practical friction;
- local news for closures, strikes, weather, and events;
- forums for traveler-specific scenarios;
- guidebooks for durable context;
- local tourism boards for broad orientation;
- specialist blogs for niche activities, with sponsorship caution.

Do not average all sources equally. Understand what each source is good at.

### Watch For Sponsorship And Affiliate Bias

Travel content is often monetized through hotels, tours, credit cards, luggage, insurance, and affiliate booking links. Sponsored content may still be useful, but the agent should treat rankings and "best" claims cautiously.

Warning signs:

- every recommendation links to a commissionable booking;
- disadvantages are absent;
- superlatives are unsupported;
- the same few attractions appear across copied articles;
- "hidden gem" language describes heavily marketed places;
- luxury, card, or tour recommendations are not tied to traveler needs.

Use sponsored content for ideas, not as sole evidence.

### Preserve Uncertainty In The Output

When a fact is not verified, say so. A good travel answer can still be useful if it separates confirmed facts from assumptions.

Use phrases such as:

- "verify current hours before going";
- "this is route logic, not a live schedule check";
- "official entry requirements should be checked for your nationality";
- "recent reviews suggest, but provider confirmation is needed";
- "if this tour is central to the trip, confirm availability before booking flights."

This protects the user from treating planning-level research as booking-ready truth.

### Avoid Over-Researching Low-Stakes Choices

Source discipline should not paralyze planning. For low-stakes recommendations, give a short list of good-fit options and explain how the traveler should confirm details. Reserve deep verification for decisions that affect booking, safety, budget, access, or the trip's main purpose.

## Common Traps

### Treating Search Rank As Reliability

Top search results may be optimized, sponsored, old, or generic. Ranking is not authority.

### Confusing Reviews With Fit

High ratings do not mean a place fits the traveler, budget, diet, accessibility, or location.

### Using A Blog For Official Rules

Blogs can explain experience, but official rules need official sources.

### Ignoring Source Date

Travel changed rapidly in many places. Old details can survive online long after they are wrong.

### Presenting Unverified Details As Bookable

Specific times, prices, and rules can mislead if not checked against current provider sources.

### Losing Practical Local Clues

Official sites may be correct but incomplete about queues, crowd feel, confusing entrances, or transfer friction. Recent traveler reports can add practical context.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Source strength matches the consequence of being wrong.
- [ ] Primary sources are used or required for controlled facts such as entry, schedules, permits, hours, and policies.
- [ ] Recency, season, traveler type, and local changes are considered.
- [ ] Local fit uses multiple source types rather than search rank alone.
- [ ] Sponsored, affiliate, copied, or generic content is treated cautiously.
- [ ] Confirmed facts, assumptions, and items requiring verification are separated.
- [ ] Low-stakes recommendations are not over-researched at the cost of useful planning.
- [ ] Recent reviews or forums are used for practical friction without replacing official rules.
- [ ] Destination-specific claims do not rely only on model memory.
- [ ] The final recommendation tells the traveler what must be checked before booking or departure.
