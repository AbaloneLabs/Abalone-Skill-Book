# Event Lead And Campaign Follow Up Discipline

Events and campaigns create fragile interest. A candidate may remember a conversation, a role, a speaker, or a promise for only a short time. Agents often collect leads and delay follow-up until context is stale, then send generic messages that ignore what happened. This skill helps the agent turn event and campaign leads into respectful, prioritized recruiting action.

## Core Rules

### Capture context at the point of contact

Record what was discussed, role interest, level, location, graduation date, skills, follow-up promise, consent, and urgency while the conversation is fresh. A badge scan or attendee list alone is rarely enough.

Follow-up quality depends on context quality.

### Prioritize leads by fit and intent

Not every event lead needs the same follow-up. Segment hot prospects, role-ready candidates, future-fit candidates, general interest, students, referrals, and low-fit contacts. Match response speed and content to the segment.

High-fit candidates should not wait behind generic campaign processing.

### Follow up quickly and specifically

Reference the event, conversation, topic, role, or interest. Provide a clear next step: apply, schedule screen, join talent community, attend follow-up session, send portfolio, or watch for future roles.

Generic "thanks for stopping by" messages waste the moment.

### Respect consent and channel rules

Event attendance does not always equal consent for ongoing recruiting campaigns. Follow opt-in, badge-scan disclosure, unsubscribe, and regional data rules. If consent is unclear, use the approved path.

Recruiting enthusiasm does not override privacy.

### Avoid duplicate or conflicting contact

Multiple recruiters may meet the same candidate at an event or campaign. Deduplicate records, assign ownership, and coordinate messages. Candidates should not receive conflicting role suggestions or repeated introductions.

Events can create data chaos without ownership.

### Convert promises into tasks

If a recruiter promised to send a role, introduce a manager, review a portfolio, or follow up after graduation, create a task with owner and due date. Verbal promises are easy to lose after busy events.

Candidate trust depends on small follow-through.

### Measure conversion by quality and close low-fit leads respectfully

Track event leads to responses, screens, interviews, offers, hires, future pipeline, and opt-outs. Attendance and scan count are not enough. Learn which events and campaigns produce qualified engagement.

Marketing activity should inform recruiting strategy.

If a lead is not aligned, avoid indefinite nurture without relevance. Provide appropriate closure, general resources, or opt-in choice. Not every contact should become a long-term CRM record.

Respectful non-pursuit protects brand.

### Coordinate event promises with campaign automation and review source quality after the campaign

If candidates were told they would receive a specific role, recruiter note, portfolio review, or interview link, do not drop them into a generic nurture sequence first. Automation should support the human promise, not erase it.

Event follow-up fails when campaign systems ignore conversation context.

After follow-up, compare leads by event, session, community, source, and message. Some events produce brand awareness but few qualified candidates; others produce fewer but stronger relationships. Use evidence to decide whether to repeat, change, or stop the activity.

Lead follow-up should improve future event strategy.

### Protect community trust and assign follow-up ownership before the event

Some events are community spaces, not recruiting databases. Follow community expectations about outreach, sponsor access, and list use. If the company received attendee data as a sponsor, confirm what contact is permitted.

Misusing community access can damage relationships beyond one campaign.

Decide who owns lead upload, dedupe, segmentation, first message, recruiter handoff, and reporting before leads arrive. Post-event confusion causes delay and duplicate contact.

Event follow-up should be designed before the booth opens.

## Common Traps

- Collecting badge scans with no conversation context.
- Following up weeks later after interest has gone cold.
- Sending identical generic messages to all leads; treating event attendance as consent for indefinite marketing
- Letting multiple recruiters contact the same lead; forgetting promised follow-up from the event
- Measuring event success by booth traffic only; adding low-fit leads to CRM forever
- Ignoring candidate urgency or competing timelines; failing to update source attribution and campaign data
- Sending generic automation that contradicts specific event promises; repeating events without reviewing lead quality and conversion
- Treating community event access as permission for unrestricted recruiting outreach; waiting until after the event to assign follow-up ownership

## Self-Check

- Is lead context captured beyond name and email?
- Are leads prioritized by fit, intent, and urgency?
- Is follow-up timely and specific to the interaction?
- Are consent, opt-out, and regional rules followed?
- Are duplicate records and recruiter ownership resolved?
- Are promised follow-ups converted into tasks?
- Are event outcomes measured by qualified progression?; are low-fit leads closed or given opt-in choices respectfully?
- Is campaign source data accurate?; would the candidate feel the follow-up remembered the interaction?
- Are human event promises preserved in follow-up automation?; are event and campaign sources reviewed for quality, not just lead count?
- Are community and sponsor contact permissions respected?; is follow-up ownership assigned before the event or campaign launch?
