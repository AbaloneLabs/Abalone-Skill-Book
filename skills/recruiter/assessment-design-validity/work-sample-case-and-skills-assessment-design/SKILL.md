# Work Sample Case And Skills Assessment Design

Assessments can improve hiring signal, but they can also waste candidate time, leak unpaid work, create accessibility barriers, and test the wrong skills. Recruiters often inherit take-home assignments or case studies without asking whether they are job-relevant, calibrated, scored consistently, and proportionate. This skill helps the recruiter design assessments that produce useful evidence while respecting candidates.

## Core Rules

### Tie Assessment To Job Tasks

The assessment should sample real work: debugging, sales discovery, writing, prioritization, design critique, data analysis, customer response, leadership judgment, or operational planning. Avoid puzzles or trivia unless they truly predict performance.

If the task is not job-relevant, it should not drive selection.

### Define What The Assessment Measures

Clarify whether the exercise measures technical skill, problem structuring, communication, judgment, collaboration, speed, creativity, or domain reasoning. One task cannot measure everything.

Scoring should match the intended signal.

### Keep Burden Proportionate

Assessment time should be appropriate for role level, candidate stage, and market norms. Long unpaid take-homes can exclude caregivers, disabled candidates, hourly workers, and employed candidates with limited time.

Respect candidate time as part of employer brand.

### Avoid Unpaid Business Work

Case studies should not ask candidates to solve active company problems in ways the company can use commercially without compensation or consent. Use fictionalized or historical scenarios where possible.

Assessment must not become free consulting.

### Provide Clear Instructions And Constraints

Candidates need expectations, time limits, allowed tools, submission format, evaluation criteria, confidentiality, and whether AI or outside resources are allowed. Ambiguous instructions measure guesswork.

Clarity improves fairness and signal quality.

### Design For Accessibility

Offer reasonable accommodations, accessible formats, flexible scheduling where appropriate, and alternatives for disability-related barriers. Avoid unnecessary speed tests, sensory demands, or platform constraints.

Assessment design should measure skill, not ability to navigate barriers.

### Use Structured Scoring

Rubrics should define strong, acceptable, and weak evidence. Reviewers should be trained and calibrated. Unstructured assessment review invites bias and inconsistent standards.

Score evidence, not polish alone.

### Protect Confidentiality And Candidate Work

Clarify who will see submissions, how long they are retained, and whether they will be reused. Candidates may share sensitive prior work or proprietary examples.

Treat candidate work product as confidential hiring material.

### Validate And Iterate

Track pass rates, candidate feedback, adverse impact, predictive value, drop-off, and interviewer usefulness. Remove or redesign assessments that do not improve decisions.

An assessment should earn its place in the process.

### Decide Whether The Assessment Is Screening Or Confirmation

An assessment used early in the funnel should be short, accessible, and focused on must-have evidence. An assessment used late can be deeper because the candidate and company have mutual investment. Confusing these purposes leads to excessive early burden or shallow late evaluation.

Recruiters should ask what decision the assessment supports and whether the same decision could be made with less burden at that stage.

### Protect Comparability Across Candidates

Assessments become unfair when candidates receive different prompts, time limits, hints, tools, or reviewer expectations. Some flexibility is necessary for accommodations, but ordinary process variation should be controlled. If candidates can choose different difficulty levels, the scoring rubric must account for that choice.

Comparability matters because assessment evidence often carries disproportionate weight in debrief.

### Decide How Results Will Be Used Before Launch

The team should know whether the assessment is a pass-fail gate, one input among several, a discussion artifact for a later interview, or a way to identify training needs after hire. That decision changes the burden candidates can reasonably be asked to carry and the precision required in scoring. A lightweight discussion exercise should not silently become the main rejection mechanism.

Define how to handle partial completion, unfamiliar tools, strong reasoning with a wrong final answer, and candidates who ask clarifying questions. For many roles, the quality of assumptions, prioritization, tradeoff explanation, and stakeholder framing may matter more than a polished deliverable. If those dimensions matter, make them visible in the rubric before the first candidate completes the work.

## Common Traps

- Designing an assessment without deciding whether it is a gate, evidence source, or interview aid.
- Penalizing candidates for asking clarifying questions when the job itself rewards clarification.

- Using puzzles or trivia unrelated to the job.
- Designing one exercise to measure too many things.
- Creating long take-homes for early-stage candidates.
- Asking candidates to solve real company problems for free.
- Giving vague instructions and then scoring interpretation.
- Ignoring accessibility and accommodation needs.
- Reviewing submissions without a rubric.
- Rewarding polish, privilege, or free time over capability.
- Sharing candidate work too broadly.
- Keeping assessments because managers like them despite weak signal.
- Failing to monitor adverse impact and drop-off.
- Using a late-stage depth assessment as an early-stage screen.
- Letting candidates receive different instructions, hints, or scoring expectations.

## Self-Check

- Is the assessment's decision weight explicit before candidates complete it?
- Does the rubric credit reasoning, assumptions, and prioritization when those are job-relevant?

- Is the assessment directly tied to job tasks?
- Is the intended signal clearly defined?
- Is candidate time burden proportionate?
- Does the task avoid unpaid usable business work?
- Are instructions, constraints, and evaluation criteria clear?
- Are accommodations and accessible formats available?
- Is scoring structured and calibrated?
- Is candidate work protected confidentially?
- Are pass rates, feedback, adverse impact, and predictive value monitored?
- Would the assessment improve hiring decisions enough to justify its burden?
- Is the assessment appropriate for the stage where it is used?
- Are prompt, time, tools, and scoring comparable across candidates?
