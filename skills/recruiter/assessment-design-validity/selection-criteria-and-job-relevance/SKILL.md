# Selection Criteria And Job Relevance

Selection criteria turn a job need into decisions about people. If criteria are vague, inflated, or unrelated to the work, recruiters screen out capable candidates, create bias risk, and make interview feedback inconsistent. Agents often copy requirements from old job descriptions or manager preferences without testing job relevance. This skill helps the recruiter define criteria that are evidence-based, fair, and usable throughout the hiring process.

## Core Rules

### Start With Work Outcomes

Define what the person must accomplish in the first 6 to 12 months. Outcomes may include building systems, selling into a market, managing a team, handling cases, serving customers, reducing risk, or operating equipment.

Criteria should flow from work outcomes, not from idealized candidate profiles.

### Separate Must-Have From Trainable

Must-haves should be genuinely required at start. Trainable skills, domain knowledge, tools, or credentials should not become barriers unless ramp time is impossible.

Overstated must-haves shrink the pool and reduce diversity.

### Test Credential Requirements

Degrees, certifications, licenses, years of experience, brand-name employers, and specific tools may be valid or may be proxies. Ask what capability the credential proves and whether other evidence could prove it.

Credential inflation is a common selection error.

### Define Evidence For Each Criterion

For each criterion, identify acceptable evidence: work sample, portfolio, interview answer, certification, reference, employment history, test, or practical demonstration. Criteria that cannot be evidenced consistently should be revised.

Selection standards need observable proof.

### Avoid Personality And Culture Vagueness

Terms like "scrappy," "polished," "executive presence," "culture fit," or "strong communicator" invite bias unless translated into job behaviors. Define the behavior and context.

Use values and behaviors, not vibes.

### Consider Adjacent And Nontraditional Backgrounds

Candidates may have transferable skills from different industries, military service, caregiving, education, freelancing, community leadership, or internal roles. Criteria should allow evidence from varied paths.

Job relevance should broaden evidence, not lower standards.

### Align Screening And Interview Criteria

Resume screen, recruiter screen, technical assessment, panel interview, and final debrief should use the same core criteria. Misalignment causes candidates to pass one stage and fail another for unstated reasons.

Criteria should be visible across the process.

### Review For Adverse Impact Risk

Requirements that disproportionately exclude protected or underrepresented groups should be job-related and necessary. If a less exclusionary criterion works, use it.

Fair criteria reduce legal and ethical risk.

### Recalibrate With Evidence

If the funnel produces too few qualified candidates, inconsistent feedback, or strong performers rejected by criteria, revisit the criteria. Do not simply lower the bar; test whether the bar measures the job.

Criteria should improve with market and performance evidence.

### Use Negative Criteria Sparingly

Hiring teams often define criteria by what failed in the last hire: "no job hoppers," "must have startup grit," "no enterprise-only people," or "must be hands-on." These may reflect real lessons, but they can also encode overcorrections and bias. Convert negative criteria into job-relevant evidence. For example, instead of rejecting "enterprise-only" backgrounds, define the need to make decisions with ambiguity and limited support.

Negative screens should be tested against actual performance requirements before they become filters.

### Decide What Evidence Is Sufficient

Criteria should include a threshold for sufficiency. A candidate may show enough evidence through one strong project, several adjacent experiences, or a certification plus practical examples. Without sufficiency standards, interviewers keep asking for more evidence from some candidates than others.

This is especially important for candidates from nontraditional backgrounds whose evidence may not match the hiring team's familiar pattern.

### Preserve A Narrow Door For Equivalency

Strong criteria define what matters without locking the team into one biography. When a requirement can be met through adjacent industry experience, military service, caregiving leadership, open-source work, academic projects, internal stretch assignments, or nontraditional training, name those equivalents before review starts. This keeps flexibility from becoming ad hoc favoritism.

Equivalency does not mean lowering the bar. It means stating which outcomes, complexity, independence, domain exposure, or judgment signals are genuinely necessary. If the role needs regulated-device launch experience, the substitute may be another high-control product environment. If it needs stakeholder influence, a candidate may show it through community, operations, or customer escalation work. The recruiter should help the hiring team decide these boundaries explicitly.

## Common Traps

- Treating a familiar career path as the only acceptable proof of ability.
- Adding equivalency language after seeing a favored candidate rather than defining it before screening.

- Copying criteria from old requisitions without job analysis.
- Turning nice-to-haves into must-haves.
- Requiring degrees or years of experience as lazy proxies.
- Using vague culture language to reject candidates.
- Applying different criteria at different stages.
- Ignoring transferable skills and nontraditional evidence.
- Failing to define what good evidence looks like.
- Treating manager preference as job necessity.
- Keeping criteria that create adverse impact without justification.
- Recalibrating only after the funnel fails.
- Confusing high standards with inflated requirements.
- Turning lessons from one bad hire into broad exclusion rules.
- Requiring more proof from nontraditional candidates because sufficiency was not defined.

## Self-Check

- Are acceptable equivalent forms of evidence defined before candidate review?
- Would a nontraditional but capable candidate understand how to demonstrate fit?

- Are criteria tied to concrete work outcomes?
- Are must-haves distinguished from trainable skills?
- Are credential requirements justified?
- Is evidence defined for each criterion?
- Are vague personality terms translated into job behaviors?
- Are nontraditional and adjacent backgrounds considered?
- Are screening and interview criteria aligned?
- Is adverse impact risk reviewed?
- Are criteria recalibrated using funnel and performance evidence?
- Would two trained interviewers apply the criteria consistently?
- Are negative criteria translated into job-relevant positive evidence?
- Is the threshold for sufficient evidence clear?
