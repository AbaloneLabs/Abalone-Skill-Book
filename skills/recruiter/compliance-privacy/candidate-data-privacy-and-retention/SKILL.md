# Candidate Data Privacy And Retention

Recruiting creates a large trail of personal data: resumes, contact details, compensation expectations, interview notes, assessment results, work authorization information, references, background checks, demographic data, and sometimes sensitive disclosures. Agents often treat candidate data as ordinary workflow content and miss privacy, retention, consent, access control, and note-quality obligations. This skill helps the agent handle candidate data as controlled information.

## Core Rules

### Collect only what the process needs

Before asking for data, know why it is needed and which system should hold it. Avoid collecting unnecessary ID documents, birth dates, personal addresses, salary history, health details, family information, or sensitive demographic information unless required and approved.

Overcollection increases privacy risk and can introduce bias into evaluation.

### Use approved systems of record

Candidate data should live in approved ATS, HRIS, assessment, background-check, or sourcing systems. Avoid storing resumes, notes, contact lists, compensation details, or feedback in personal drives, local downloads, broad chat channels, or uncontrolled spreadsheets unless policy allows.

Convenient side lists often become privacy and retention problems.

### Control access by need to know

Share candidate information only with people involved in sourcing, evaluation, coordination, approval, compliance, or onboarding. Interviewers need role-relevant materials, not every private note. Agencies and vendors need only approved data for their work.

Do not forward candidate profiles widely because someone might be interested.

### Write notes as if candidates may request them

Recruiting notes should be factual, job-related, respectful, and free of protected or irrelevant information. Avoid comments about age, appearance, family, health, nationality, accent stereotypes, personality labels, or speculation.

Notes can be discoverable, auditable, or subject to access requests depending on jurisdiction and policy.

### Handle consent and notice requirements

Some jurisdictions or tools require candidate notice or consent for data processing, sourcing outreach, talent communities, background checks, assessments, AI tools, recording, or cross-border transfer. Follow company policy and route uncertainty to privacy, legal, or HR.

Do not assume public profile data can be used without limits.

### Separate sensitive data from ordinary evaluation

Work authorization, disability accommodation, demographic self-identification, background checks, and medical or protected disclosures may have special storage and access rules. Keep them out of general interviewer notes unless policy directs otherwise.

Sensitive data should not influence ordinary hiring evaluation except where legally and job-relevantly required.

### Respect retention and deletion rules

Candidate data may need to be retained for compliance, audit, reporting, or legal defense, but not forever. Follow retention schedules, deletion requests, talent community opt-outs, and local requirements. Do not keep shadow copies after the system of record deletes data.

Retention discipline is part of trust.

### Review recruiting analytics carefully

Funnel metrics, source analysis, diversity reporting, and AI scoring may use candidate data. Ensure data is aggregated, authorized, accurate, and not used for discriminatory targeting or unauthorized monitoring.

Analytics should improve process fairness and effectiveness without exposing individuals unnecessarily.

### Control AI and automation use and limit data shared in collaborative work

If AI tools summarize resumes, score candidates, draft outreach, transcribe interviews, or analyze sentiment, confirm the tool is approved and candidates receive any required notice. Review outputs for error, bias, hallucination, and inappropriate inference from protected or sensitive data.

Do not paste candidate materials into unapproved AI tools. Convenience does not override privacy and procurement controls.

Recruiting often happens in Slack, email, spreadsheets, agency portals, and hiring-manager documents. Share links to approved records where possible instead of copying full resumes or notes. Redact sensitive details when a narrow excerpt is enough.

Every duplicate copy makes retention and deletion harder.

## Common Traps

- Collecting sensitive information because it might be useful later.
- Keeping candidate spreadsheets outside approved systems.
- Sharing resumes and feedback in broad channels.
- Writing subjective or protected-class comments in ATS notes.
- Assuming public profiles have no privacy limits; adding candidates to talent communities without proper consent or notice
- Mixing accommodation, background, or demographic data into general interview notes; ignoring deletion requests or retaining shadow copies
- Sending candidate data to agencies or vendors without approved terms; using recruiting analytics without checking data scope, accuracy, and authorization
- Pasting resumes, notes, or interview transcripts into unapproved AI tools; copying full candidate records into collaboration tools when a controlled link or excerpt would suffice

## Self-Check

- Is every data element collected for a defined recruiting purpose?
- Is candidate data stored in approved systems rather than personal or uncontrolled locations?
- Is access limited to people with a recruiting need to know?
- Are notes factual, job-related, respectful, and free of sensitive speculation?
- Are consent, notice, recording, AI, sourcing, and cross-border rules followed where relevant?
- Is sensitive data separated and protected according to policy?
- Are retention, deletion, and opt-out requirements respected?
- Are vendors, agencies, and tools approved for the data shared?
- Are analytics aggregated and authorized?; would the candidate and privacy reviewer consider the data handling defensible?
- Are AI and automation tools approved, noticed where required, and checked for error or bias?; are duplicate copies in collaboration tools minimized or controlled?
