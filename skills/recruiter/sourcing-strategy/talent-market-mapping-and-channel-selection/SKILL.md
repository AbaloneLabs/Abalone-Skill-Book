# Talent Market Mapping And Channel Selection

Sourcing strategy is a market hypothesis, not a list of places to post a job. The recruiter must understand where qualified people are likely to be, how they describe themselves, what motivates them, and which channels can reach them credibly. Agents often default to generic job boards or LinkedIn keyword searches and miss adjacent titles, non-obvious communities, compensation realities, geographic supply, and source diversity. This skill helps the agent choose channels based on the talent market and role constraints.

## Core Rules

### Map capabilities before titles

Start with the core capabilities from the success profile, then identify titles, adjacent roles, industries, projects, certifications, tools, communities, and career paths that may signal those capabilities. Titles vary widely by company size and industry.

Do not search only for the hiring manager's preferred title. A strong candidate may be called operations lead, program manager, implementation consultant, solutions engineer, chief of staff, or customer success manager depending on context.

### Segment the market

Break the market into segments: direct competitors, adjacent industries, larger companies, smaller companies, agencies, consultancies, startups, public sector, remote talent, local talent, returners, internal candidates, alumni, and referral networks. Each segment has different motivations, compensation expectations, and ramp risks.

Market segmentation helps the recruiter test where quality and response are strongest instead of treating all candidates as one pool.

### Choose channels by candidate behavior

Different candidates respond to different channels: passive professionals may need personalized outreach; frontline workers may use local boards, text, walk-ins, community groups, or referrals; early-career talent may rely on campus, internships, and social channels; executives may require networks and research.

Do not assume a channel is weak because it is unfamiliar to the recruiter. The question is whether the target candidates actually use it and trust it.

### Balance proven channels with source diversity

Referrals and familiar networks can be high-converting but may reproduce homogeneity. Job boards can create volume but weak fit. Agencies can accelerate niche searches but add cost and control issues. Communities can improve relevance but require respectful engagement.

Build a channel mix that supports both quality and breadth. If the slate lacks diversity of background, geography, industry, or demographic representation where legally and ethically relevant, revisit channels early.

### Test search strings and signal quality

For outbound sourcing, test keywords, titles, companies, skills, and exclusions. Review whether results actually show the success profile. If not, refine the hypothesis. Do not equate a large result count with a strong market.

Good sourcing uses evidence from profiles, response rates, screen pass rates, and hiring manager calibration to improve the map.

### Consider employer value proposition by segment

A channel may reach candidates, but the message must fit their motivations. Candidates may care about mission, compensation, flexibility, learning, stability, impact, technology, manager quality, equity, location, or career path. Different segments require different value propositions.

Sourcing strategy should include why someone in each segment would consider the role, not just how to find them.

### Watch channel compliance and representation risks

Some channels create legal or fairness issues: excluding public posting where required, targeting only protected characteristics, scraping data against platform terms, using personal data improperly, or relying on closed networks that limit opportunity. Route uncertain practices through recruiting leadership, HR, legal, or privacy owners.

The recruiter should expand reach without violating fair hiring or data rules.

### Set feedback loops by channel

Track source volume, qualified rate, response rate, screen pass rate, interview pass rate, offer rate, acceptance, time, cost, and slate composition. Channels should be judged by downstream signal, not vanity metrics.

If a channel produces many applicants but few qualified screens, adjust posting, targeting, or effort allocation.

## Common Traps

- Searching only the exact title requested by the hiring manager.
- Treating LinkedIn or a major job board as the whole market.
- Ignoring adjacent industries or roles that share the needed capability.
- Overusing referrals without checking slate breadth and fairness.
- Choosing channels based on recruiter habit rather than candidate behavior.
- Measuring success by profile count or applicant volume instead of quality progression.
- Using generic employer value proposition across all candidate segments; missing local, community, campus, professional association, or niche technical sources
- Creating compliance or privacy risk through careless data collection or targeting; waiting until late-stage pipeline failure to revisit channel strategy

## Self-Check

- Are capabilities mapped to multiple possible titles, industries, companies, and career paths?
- Is the talent market segmented instead of treated as one pool?
- Are channels chosen based on where target candidates actually engage?
- Does the channel mix balance quality, speed, cost, and source diversity?
- Have search strings and profile results been tested against the success profile?
- Is the value proposition tailored by candidate segment?
- Are public posting, privacy, platform, and fair hiring constraints checked?
- Are channel metrics tied to downstream quality and progression?
- Is there a plan to revise channels after early market evidence?
- Would the strategy still work if the obvious source produces too few qualified candidates?
