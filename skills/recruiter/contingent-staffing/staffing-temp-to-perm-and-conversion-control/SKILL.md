# Staffing Temp To Perm And Conversion Control

Temporary and contract-to-hire arrangements can help teams test fit and meet urgent needs, but they can also create confusion about employment status, conversion promises, performance criteria, and agency fees. Agents often sell "temp-to-perm" informally and miss approval, fairness, worker communication, and vendor terms. This skill helps the agent manage staffing conversions with controlled expectations.

## Core Rules

### Define conversion possibility before assignment

Clarify whether conversion is expected, possible, unlikely, or not allowed. Know timing, fees, approval, headcount, performance criteria, and manager decision rights. Communicate accurately to the worker and vendor.

Do not use conversion possibility as bait when no approved path exists.

### Keep employment status clear

Temporary workers may be employed by an agency or vendor, not the company. Explain who handles pay, benefits, timekeeping, attendance, issues, and HR concerns. Managers should know the boundaries.

Confusion creates co-employment and trust problems.

### Establish performance feedback process

If conversion depends on performance, define how feedback is collected, who evaluates, what criteria apply, and when decisions happen. Temporary workers deserve a fair view of expectations.

Do not wait until assignment end to say they were not meeting the bar.

### Coordinate vendor terms

Conversion fees, minimum hours, notice, replacement guarantees, billing, and candidate ownership should be known before conversion discussions. Vendor terms can affect timing and cost.

Avoid offer delays caused by late contract review.

### Compare conversion candidates fairly

A temporary worker may have on-the-job evidence, while external candidates may bring different experience. Decide whether the process will include interviews, references, or direct conversion, and apply criteria consistently.

Familiarity should not automatically decide.

### Protect worker communication

Temporary workers often live with uncertainty. Provide clear updates about assignment length, conversion status, decision timing, and next steps within vendor and policy rules.

Silence can lead strong workers to leave for stable offers.

### Plan transition to employee status and learn from conversion outcomes

Conversion may require new offer, background check, benefits enrollment, payroll change, equipment, access changes, tenure treatment, and onboarding. Coordinate timing to avoid pay or access gaps.

Conversion is not just a title change.

Track conversion rate, performance, retention, manager satisfaction, vendor quality, and worker experience. Use this to decide whether temp-to-perm is working as intended.

The model should be evaluated, not assumed.

### Avoid creating a shadow probation system and coordinate with workforce planning

Temp-to-perm can become a way to test workers without the protections, clarity, or support of an employee probation process. Confirm that expectations, evaluation, and conversion criteria are fair and disclosed.

The worker should understand what is being assessed and what conversion depends on.

If many temporary workers convert, the organization may need a planned talent pipeline, training capacity, compensation strategy, and vendor cost model. If few convert, the model may be misleading candidates or failing managers.

Conversion patterns should inform workforce strategy.

### Manage conversion communication with the vendor and reassess role fit before conversion

The vendor may need notice, release approval, fee calculation, worker communication boundaries, and final billing before conversion. Do not bypass the vendor unless contract and policy allow it. Poor coordination can create disputes and damage future supply.

The worker should receive a clear message from the right party at the right time.

A temp worker may succeed in a narrow temporary assignment but not in the broader permanent role. Confirm whether the employee role adds duties, schedule, customer exposure, leadership, compliance, or performance expectations.

Conversion should be based on the job being offered, not only the job already done.

## Common Traps

- Describing roles as temp-to-perm without approved conversion path.
- Letting workers think the company is their employer when the agency is.
- Giving no performance feedback until conversion denial; discovering conversion fees only after the manager wants to hire
- Converting based only on familiarity rather than criteria; leaving workers uncertain about assignment end and decision timing
- Failing to coordinate payroll and benefits transition; treating conversion as a shortcut around fair hiring process
- Ignoring vendor quality after conversions occur; letting strong workers leave because decisions are slow
- Using temp-to-perm as an unclear shadow probation system; ignoring conversion patterns when planning workforce strategy
- Bypassing vendor conversion steps and creating contract disputes; converting based on temporary assignment success without checking permanent role scope

## Self-Check

- Is conversion status clear before assignment starts?
- Are employment-status boundaries communicated?
- Is performance feedback tied to conversion criteria and timing?
- Are vendor fees and terms known before conversion discussions?
- Is the worker compared fairly against role criteria?
- Are updates provided within vendor and policy boundaries?
- Are offer, payroll, benefits, access, and onboarding transitions planned?; are conversion outcomes tracked?
- Is temp-to-perm used for a valid business reason?; would the worker understand their status and realistic path?
- Are conversion criteria disclosed enough to be fair?; are conversion rates reviewed as workforce planning input?
- Are vendor notice, fees, and communication boundaries coordinated?; is the permanent role scope reassessed before conversion?
