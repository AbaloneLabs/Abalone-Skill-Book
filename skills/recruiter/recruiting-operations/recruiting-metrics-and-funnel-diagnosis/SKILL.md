# Recruiting Metrics And Funnel Diagnosis

Recruiting metrics are useful only when they explain decisions and bottlenecks. A faster time-to-fill can hide bad hires, a large pipeline can hide poor quality, and high interview volume can indicate weak screening. Agents often report numbers without diagnosing the system behind them. This skill helps the agent use metrics to understand where recruiting is healthy, stuck, unfair, or misaligned.

## Core Rules

### Define the question before selecting metrics

Decide whether the goal is to diagnose speed, quality, source effectiveness, candidate experience, diversity, manager responsiveness, offer competitiveness, or recruiter workload. Different questions require different metrics.

Do not start with a dashboard and search for meaning. Metrics should answer an operational question.

### Use funnel conversion by stage

Review applicants or sourced profiles to screens, screens to submissions, submissions to interviews, interviews to finalists, offers to accepts, accepts to starts, and starts to retention where available. Stage conversion shows where the process loses candidates or quality.

Overall totals are rarely enough. A role may have high volume but weak screen pass, or strong finalists but poor offer acceptance.

### Segment before drawing conclusions

Break metrics by role type, level, location, source, recruiter, hiring manager, stage, candidate type, and time period. Averages can hide high-volume roles, executive searches, seasonal spikes, or one manager's bottleneck.

Segmentation helps avoid blaming the whole process for a localized issue.

### Pair speed with quality and experience

Time-to-fill, time-in-stage, and recruiter activity should be interpreted with quality of hire, interview pass rate, offer acceptance, candidate withdrawal, new-hire retention, and candidate feedback where available. Speed without quality can create downstream cost.

Do not optimize recruiters to push candidates faster if hiring managers are not ready or assessment quality is weak.

### Treat source metrics as downstream signals

Source success should include qualified rate, interview rate, offer rate, acceptance, retention, cost, and slate breadth, not just applicant count or response rate. A source that generates fewer candidates may produce stronger hires.

Avoid investing in channels because they look busy.

### Watch for bias and fairness signals

Stage drop-off patterns, interviewer pass rates, referral advantage, source concentration, and disposition reasons can reveal process bias or inconsistent criteria. Where demographic data is available and lawful to analyze, use the approved process.

Fairness metrics should trigger inquiry, not unsupported conclusions.

### Separate recruiter performance from system constraints

Recruiters influence many metrics, but hiring manager delays, compensation limits, brand weakness, market scarcity, approvals, and interviewer behavior also shape outcomes. Diagnose ownership before assigning blame.

Metrics should clarify where intervention is needed.

### Turn diagnosis into actions

A funnel review should end with actions: recalibrate criteria, change sourcing channels, revise postings, improve outreach, train interviewers, enforce feedback SLAs, adjust compensation, simplify process, or pause low-readiness roles.

Reporting without decisions creates dashboard theater.

### Define time windows and cohorts carefully and separate lagging and leading indicators

Recruiting metrics can change depending on whether the cohort is applications created, candidates entering a stage, requisitions opened, offers extended, or hires started. Time windows can include seasonal peaks, hiring freezes, layoffs, budget cycles, or campaign launches.

Do not compare periods or teams without checking whether the cohort definition is the same. A misleading cohort can make a healthy process look broken or hide a serious bottleneck.

Time-to-fill, acceptance rate, and new-hire retention are lagging indicators. Outreach response, manager review time, screen pass rate, interview feedback timeliness, and offer approval age can warn earlier. Use leading indicators to intervene before candidates are lost.

If the team only reviews lagging metrics, every lesson arrives after the damage.

## Common Traps

- Treating time-to-fill as the only measure of recruiting health.
- Reporting applicant volume as pipeline quality.
- Using averages without segmenting by role type, source, level, or hiring manager.
- Blaming recruiters for delays caused by feedback or approval bottlenecks; ignoring candidate withdrawals and offer declines as process signals
- Measuring source success only at top of funnel; overlooking bias signals in stage conversion and disposition patterns
- Presenting metrics from dirty ATS data without caveats; optimizing activity volume rather than hiring outcomes
- Ending metric reviews without operational decisions; comparing teams or periods with different cohort definitions
- Waiting for lagging metrics when leading indicators already show risk; treating metric movement as meaningful without checking sample size

## Self-Check

- Is there a clear operational question behind the metric review?
- Are funnel conversions reviewed by stage?
- Are metrics segmented enough to avoid misleading averages?
- Are speed metrics paired with quality, retention, and candidate experience signals?
- Are source metrics evaluated downstream, not only by volume?
- Are fairness and bias patterns reviewed through approved data methods?
- Are recruiter-controlled issues separated from manager, compensation, approval, and market constraints?
- Are data quality limitations stated?; does the diagnosis lead to concrete actions and owners?
- Would the metrics change how the next search is run?; are cohorts and time windows defined consistently?
- Are leading indicators used before problems become final outcomes?; is sample size large enough to support the conclusion?
