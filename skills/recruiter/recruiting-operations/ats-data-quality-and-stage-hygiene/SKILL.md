# ATS Data Quality And Stage Hygiene

The applicant tracking system is not just an administrative database. It is the source of truth for candidate status, decision history, compliance records, funnel health, and recruiter follow-up. Agents often treat ATS updates as cleanup after recruiting work and miss that bad data creates bad decisions: stale pipelines, false conversion rates, lost candidates, weak audits, and duplicated outreach. This skill helps the agent maintain recruiting data as operational infrastructure.

## Core Rules

### Keep candidate stage truthful

Candidate stage should reflect the real process state: applied, sourced, contacted, responded, screened, submitted, hiring manager review, interview, offer, hired, rejected, withdrawn, nurture, or hold. Do not leave candidates in active stages because a decision feels uncomfortable.

Stage truth protects follow-up, reporting, and candidate experience. A stale active candidate is often a ghosted candidate.

### Use disposition reasons consistently

Disposition reasons should be job-related and specific enough for reporting: lacks required skill, compensation mismatch, location constraint, withdrew, accepted other offer, no response, role closed, duplicate, failed required check, or not selected after interview. Use the approved taxonomy.

Do not overuse vague categories such as "other" or "not fit." They hide funnel problems and weaken compliance records.

### Document decision evidence in the right place

Feedback, screening notes, interview scorecards, offer approvals, and rejection reasons should be recorded in approved fields or linked systems. Keep notes factual and job-related. Do not store sensitive data or informal opinions in random fields to make them easier to find.

The next recruiter should understand what happened without asking for private context.

### Control duplicate and merged profiles

Duplicate profiles can create repeated outreach, conflicting status, lost consent, broken reporting, and candidate confusion. Before contacting or advancing a candidate, check for existing records, prior applications, do-not-contact status, agency ownership, internal status, and past interview notes where policy permits.

Merging records should preserve decision history and privacy restrictions.

### Maintain requisition and pipeline status

Requisitions should show whether they are open, paused, filled, canceled, evergreen, confidential, exploratory, or pending approval. Candidate status should match requisition status. If a role pauses, decide what candidates should be told and how they are coded.

Open requisitions with no real activity distort workload and time-to-fill reporting.

### Protect data access and privacy

ATS records may include compensation expectations, interview notes, contact details, demographic self-ID, accommodation routing, and background-check status. Use permissions correctly and avoid exporting data unless needed and approved.

Data hygiene includes minimizing uncontrolled copies.

### Audit before reporting

Before sharing funnel metrics, confirm stage definitions, date fields, source attribution, duplicate handling, rejection categories, and requisition status. Reports based on messy data can lead leaders to fix the wrong problem.

If data quality is uncertain, state the limitation instead of presenting false precision.

### Build hygiene into routine work

Set regular habits: daily follow-up queue, aging candidate review, missing feedback check, stale stage cleanup, duplicate review, and closed-role disposition. Data quality should not depend on occasional heroic cleanup.

Healthy ATS discipline reduces cognitive load and prevents candidate neglect.

### Preserve source attribution and ownership and treat missing data as a process signal

Source attribution affects channel decisions, referral credit, agency ownership, compliance reporting, and recruiter workload analysis. Record whether the candidate came from inbound application, outbound sourcing, referral, agency, event, campus, internal mobility, talent community, or previous pipeline. If multiple sources exist, follow policy for primary source.

Do not overwrite source history for convenience. Bad attribution causes the team to invest in the wrong channels and can create disputes with agencies or employees.

Missing scorecards, blank disposition reasons, incomplete compensation fields, or unclosed candidates are not only data defects. They point to unclear ownership, overloaded recruiters, weak manager discipline, or tools that do not match the process.

When hygiene problems recur, fix the workflow rather than only cleaning records.

## Common Traps

- Leaving rejected or withdrawn candidates in active stages.
- Using vague disposition reasons that cannot guide funnel improvement.
- Recording subjective or sensitive comments in ATS notes.
- Contacting candidates without checking duplicate records or prior status.
- Keeping paused or canceled requisitions open for reporting convenience; exporting candidate lists into uncontrolled spreadsheets
- Reporting funnel metrics without checking source and stage accuracy; treating ATS cleanup as low-priority administrative work
- Losing agency, referral, or internal candidate history during profile merge; failing to update candidates when stage changes internally
- Overwriting source attribution and distorting channel decisions; treating repeated missing fields as individual forgetfulness rather than process failure

## Self-Check

- Does each candidate stage match the real process state?
- Are disposition reasons specific, approved, and job-related?
- Are notes factual, respectful, and stored in approved fields?
- Have duplicates, prior applications, agency ownership, and do-not-contact status been checked?
- Does requisition status match real hiring activity?
- Are paused or closed role candidates coded and communicated appropriately?
- Is sensitive candidate data protected by access controls and minimized exports?
- Are reports audited for stage, source, date, and duplicate accuracy?
- Is there a routine hygiene cadence for stale records and missing feedback?; could another recruiter reconstruct the decision history from the system?
- Is source attribution preserved according to policy?; are recurring missing-data patterns reviewed as workflow problems?
