# Compensation Range Intake And Market Alignment

Compensation misalignment breaks searches. A role can have clear requirements and strong sourcing, but if the pay range does not match level, market, location, scarcity, and candidate expectations, recruiting becomes wasted effort. Recruiters do not own compensation philosophy, but they must surface mismatch early and prevent hidden pay issues from becoming offer-stage failures. This skill helps the recruiter calibrate compensation realities before candidates invest in the process.

## Core Rules

### Confirm The Approved Range

Clarify base, bonus, equity, commission, sign-on, benefits, location differential, and any flexibility. Know whether the range is approved, aspirational, confidential, or pending finance and compensation review.

Do not launch a search on a range that cannot support an offer.

### Match Range To Level And Scope

Compensation should reflect role level, scope, impact, management responsibility, scarcity, and internal bands. If the manager wants senior impact at mid-level pay, the search strategy must change.

Pay mismatch is often a leveling problem.

### Compare Market Reality

Use compensation data, candidate conversations, recent declines, agency feedback, competitor intelligence, and accepted-offer data. Market data should be current and relevant to location, skill, and company stage.

Do not rely only on stale surveys or manager memory.

### Address Location Strategy

Remote, hybrid, multi-location, cost-of-labor, cost-of-living, and geographic pay policies affect range. A national remote search with local pay assumptions may fail or create equity concerns.

Location decisions should be tied to compensation strategy.

### Discuss Candidate Expectations Early

Where legally allowed, understand candidate expectations, current competing offers, must-have compensation structure, and risk tolerance. Avoid anchoring or prohibited salary-history practices.

Early expectation alignment prevents late-stage surprises.

### Include Total Rewards Without Hiding Base Issues

Benefits, equity upside, flexibility, mission, growth, and learning can matter, but they rarely fully overcome a weak base range for candidates with clear market alternatives.

Sell total rewards honestly without using them to dismiss pay gaps.

### Watch Internal Equity

Increasing range for external candidates can create compression or inequity with current employees. Compensation exceptions need review and documentation.

Hiring competitiveness should not create unmanaged retention risk.

### Recalibrate Search Strategy

If range is below market, adjust level, requirements, location, seniority, sourcing pool, role scope, or timeline. Continuing the same search with lower pay is not a strategy.

Compensation data should change decisions.

### Document Compensation Assumptions

Record approved range, flexibility, market concerns, candidate feedback, and manager decisions. Documentation helps explain why searches stall or offers fail.

Compensation ambiguity should be visible, not hidden in recruiter frustration.

### Force An Early Tradeoff Decision

When the approved range does not match the target market, do not let the team proceed as if sourcing effort will solve the problem. Bring the hiring manager, compensation partner, finance owner, and recruiter into a concrete tradeoff: raise or approve flexibility, reduce scope, change level, change location, lengthen timeline, accept a less scarce background, or pause the search. A recruiter can waste weeks generating candidates who were never realistically closeable.

The tradeoff should be explicit enough to guide candidate conversations. If the team chooses to keep the range low, the recruiter needs permission to target a different pool and to explain the opportunity honestly. If the team chooses higher pay, internal equity and approval path must be handled before finalists are in motion.

### Plan Disclosure And Candidate Messaging

Compensation alignment includes how and when the range is communicated. The recruiter should understand applicable pay-transparency rules, company policy, and candidate expectations before outreach and screening. Messaging should distinguish approved range, likely offer zone, variable pay, equity uncertainty, location adjustments, and negotiable components.

Avoid vague statements such as "competitive" when the range is known to be below market. Candidates can tolerate constraints better than surprises. Clear messaging also helps the recruiter interpret declines: a candidate who exits after hearing the range provides useful market data, while a candidate who exits at offer stage may indicate poor upfront calibration.

## Common Traps

- Treating sourcing volume as a remedy for a range that cannot close the target market.
- Saying "competitive compensation" when the approved range is materially below candidate alternatives.

- Starting sourcing before the range is approved.
- Seeking senior scope at junior or mid-level pay.
- Using stale market data.
- Ignoring location pay policy until offer stage.
- Asking prohibited salary-history questions.
- Selling equity or mission to avoid discussing base mismatch.
- Creating external offers that compress internal employees.
- Continuing a failed search without changing range or scope.
- Hiding compensation concerns to avoid manager conflict.
- Treating candidate expectations as greed rather than market signal.
- Forgetting variable compensation risk for sales or bonus-heavy roles.

## Self-Check

- If range and market are misaligned, has the team chosen a concrete tradeoff rather than continuing unchanged?
- Is candidate-facing compensation messaging lawful, specific enough, and consistent with the approved range?

- Is the compensation range approved and complete?
- Does range match level and scope?
- Is market data current and relevant?
- Is location strategy reflected in pay?
- Are candidate expectations discussed lawfully and early?
- Is total rewards messaging honest?
- Are internal equity and compression risks considered?
- Has search strategy changed if range is weak?
- Are compensation assumptions documented?
- Would a finalist be unlikely to discover a pay mismatch only at offer stage?
