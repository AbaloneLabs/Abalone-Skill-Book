# Interviewer Calibration And Question Quality

Interview quality depends on interviewer discipline. Even a well-designed process fails when interviewers improvise irrelevant questions, apply different bars, overvalue confidence, ask legally risky questions, or ignore the signal they were assigned. Agents often focus on scheduling the panel and miss whether interviewers are prepared to gather comparable evidence. This skill helps the agent make interviews more reliable and fair.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate the bar before candidates arrive

Interviewers should understand the success profile, role context, level, must-haves, acceptable tradeoffs, and the specific signal they own. Discuss examples of strong, acceptable, and weak evidence. Clarify what should not be evaluated in their stage.

Do not assume senior employees know how to interview. Experience in the job does not automatically produce reliable assessment.

### Use structured questions with room for follow-up

Prepare questions tied to the target capability. Behavioral questions should ask for real examples, context, actions, tradeoffs, and results. Technical questions should reflect job-relevant problems and allow evaluation of reasoning, not only memorization.

Structure does not mean robotic interviewing. It means candidates are assessed against comparable evidence while interviewers can probe details.

### Avoid prohibited and irrelevant topics

Interviewers should not ask about age, family, marital status, pregnancy, disability, health, religion, citizenship beyond authorized work questions, national origin, protected leave, salary history where restricted, or other protected topics. They should avoid social chatter that drifts into these areas.

If a candidate volunteers sensitive information, redirect to job-relevant requirements and avoid recording unnecessary details.

### Watch for bias in signal interpretation

Common biases include similarity bias, pedigree bias, halo effect, confidence bias, accent bias, affinity from shared hobbies, skepticism toward nontraditional paths, and overreaction to communication style. Interviewers should evaluate evidence against the scorecard, not comfort with the person.

Calibration should include reminders that "culture fit" must be behaviorally defined.

### Prevent duplicated or missing coverage

Each interviewer should know what others are covering. Duplicated questioning wastes time and overweights repeated signals. Missing coverage leaves the final decision dependent on assumptions.

If an interviewer wants to explore another area, they should note it as secondary and avoid turning the whole interview away from assigned signal.

### Require evidence-rich feedback

Feedback should include examples, observed behavior, answer quality, evidence strength, concerns, and recommendation. Vague comments such as "not senior enough," "great energy," or "not a fit" should be challenged.

Feedback should distinguish skill gaps from communication style, interview anxiety, missing context, or unclear question design.

### Protect candidate dignity

Interviewers should be on time, prepared, respectful, and clear about purpose. Stress interviews, trick questions, dismissive tone, or adversarial behavior can harm employer brand and distort signal. Candidates do not perform their best when the process feels hostile.

The recruiter should treat interviewer behavior as part of process quality.

### Close calibration loops

Review pass rates, feedback quality, candidate complaints, interviewer disagreement, and post-hire performance where available. If an interviewer consistently rejects strong candidates or writes weak feedback, intervene.

Interview calibration is ongoing, not a one-time training document.

### Prepare interviewers for candidate questions and handle panel disagreement constructively

Interviewers often become informal sellers of the role. They should know what they can accurately say about team goals, manager style, working norms, challenges, compensation boundaries, remote policy, growth path, and process timing. If they do not know, they should route the question rather than guess.

Unprepared answers can contradict recruiter messaging or create promises the company cannot honor. Calibration should include both assessment questions and candidate-facing facts.

Disagreement can be useful when it reveals different evidence, but it becomes harmful when interviewers defend impressions. Ask each interviewer to point to observed behavior and assigned signal. If disagreement comes from different bars, recalibrate before using it against the candidate.

The recruiter should not force consensus by averaging vague opinions.

## Common Traps

- Assuming interviewers understand the bar because they work in the function.
- Letting interviewers ask favorite questions unrelated to the role.
- Asking legally risky questions during casual conversation.
- Treating confidence, polish, accent, or shared background as capability.
- Duplicating the same assessment across multiple interviews; allowing vague feedback to drive decisions
- Penalizing candidates for not guessing what an unclear question wanted; using hostile or trick-question formats that create poor signal and poor experience
- Failing to correct interviewers who ignore scorecards; treating culture fit as personal comfort rather than defined behaviors
- Letting interviewers improvise answers about role terms, growth, or flexibility; resolving panel disagreement by hierarchy instead of evidence

## Self-Check

- Do interviewers understand role context, level, must-haves, tradeoffs, and assigned signal?
- Are questions tied to job-relevant capabilities?
- Are follow-ups designed to gather evidence, not to lead the candidate?
- Are prohibited and irrelevant topics clearly avoided?
- Are bias risks named and tied to scorecard discipline?
- Is each interviewer covering distinct signal?
- Does feedback require examples and evidence, not vague impressions?
- Is candidate dignity protected through preparation, punctuality, and respectful tone?
- Are weak interviewer patterns monitored and corrected?; would the interview notes support a fair decision audit?
- Are interviewers prepared to answer candidate questions accurately or route them?; is disagreement resolved through evidence and bar calibration?
