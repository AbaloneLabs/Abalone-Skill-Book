# Interview Plan And Signal Design

An interview process should be an evidence system. Each stage needs a reason, a target signal, an evaluator, and a decision it can inform. Agents often add interviews because a process template exists or a stakeholder wants to meet the candidate, then miss duplicated coverage, missing must-haves, candidate fatigue, and weak comparability. This skill helps the agent design interview loops that are focused, fair, and decision-useful.

## Core Rules

### Start from the success profile

Map each must-have and major differentiator to an assessment method. Decide whether the signal is best collected through recruiter screen, hiring manager interview, technical assessment, portfolio review, work sample, behavioral interview, peer interview, executive conversation, reference, or compliance check.

Do not create interview stages before knowing what evidence is needed. A loop that does not cover the success profile will still feel thorough while leaving the decision exposed.

### Give every stage a distinct purpose

Each interview should answer a specific question: can the candidate perform the core work, operate at the required level, collaborate with key stakeholders, handle ambiguity, meet technical standards, lead people, or accept the role constraints. Remove stages that repeat the same broad conversation.

Redundancy is costly. It slows hiring, frustrates candidates, and increases the chance that subjective impressions overpower evidence.

### Balance depth with candidate burden

A process should be long enough to reduce hiring risk and short enough to respect candidate time. Senior, technical, regulated, or high-impact roles may need deeper assessment. High-volume or competitive roles may need faster loops.

Do not add a stage simply because stakeholders are curious. If an interviewer cannot name the signal they own, they should not be in the loop.

### Decide sequence deliberately

Put high-disqualification checks early: work authorization where permitted, compensation range, location, core technical screen, license, schedule, or role constraints. Put expensive or time-consuming exercises only after enough mutual fit exists.

The sequence should reduce wasted effort for both sides. A candidate should not complete a lengthy work sample before learning that compensation or location is impossible.

### Define evidence standards before interviews happen

For each stage, define what strong, acceptable, weak, and concerning evidence looks like. Provide structured questions, scorecards, and evaluation anchors. This helps interviewers compare candidates fairly and prevents post-hoc criteria.

If the team cannot define the bar, the interview plan is not ready.

### Include role selling without contaminating assessment

Interviews should also help candidates understand the opportunity. Decide which interviewer explains team culture, manager style, growth path, product, technical environment, mission, or challenges. Selling should be honest and timed so it does not replace assessment.

Candidates should leave with accurate information and a sense that the process is organized.

### Account for accommodations and accessibility

Interview design should allow reasonable accommodations, time-zone fairness, accessible tools, clear instructions, and alternatives where appropriate. Avoid assessment formats that create unnecessary barriers unrelated to job performance.

If an exercise requires unpaid labor, special equipment, long uninterrupted time, or inaccessible tooling, review whether it is necessary and fair.

### Build decision checkpoints

Define what happens after each stage: advance, reject, gather missing evidence, recalibrate, or pause. Avoid automatically moving candidates through a full loop when an early stage creates decisive evidence.

Decision checkpoints keep the process from becoming a conveyor belt.

### Review fairness across candidate types

Check whether the plan treats inbound, sourced, referral, internal, remote, local, senior, early-career, and nontraditional candidates consistently enough for fair comparison. Some variation is legitimate, but candidates for the same role should not face materially different bars because of source or familiarity.

If internal or referral candidates skip stages, document why and confirm the missing signal is available elsewhere. Process shortcuts can create resentment and weak evidence.

## Common Traps

- Copying a standard interview loop without mapping it to the role.
- Letting every stakeholder ask the same broad background questions.
- Missing a must-have signal until the final stage.
- Adding interviews to satisfy curiosity rather than decision need.
- Requiring lengthy work samples before basic fit and constraints are confirmed.
- Using scorecards with vague labels but no evidence anchors; designing exercises that create accessibility, caregiving, equipment, or time barriers unrelated to the job
- Allowing selling conversations to replace actual assessment; moving candidates through all stages even after decisive reject or advance signals
- Confusing process length with evaluation quality; letting referrals or internal candidates bypass stages without preserving equivalent evidence

## Self-Check

- Is every must-have and major differentiator mapped to an assessment method?
- Does each interview stage have a distinct signal and decision purpose?
- Are redundant interviews removed or narrowed?
- Is the process length proportional to role risk and candidate market reality?
- Are disqualifying constraints checked early enough?
- Are scorecards and evidence anchors defined before interviews begin?
- Does the plan include honest role selling without weakening assessment?
- Are accessibility and accommodation needs considered in format, timing, and tools?
- Are decision checkpoints defined after each stage?
- Would a reviewer understand why each stage exists?; are process variations across candidate sources documented and evidence-equivalent?
