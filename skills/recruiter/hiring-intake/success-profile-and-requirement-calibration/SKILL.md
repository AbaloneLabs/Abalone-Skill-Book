# Success Profile And Requirement Calibration

A success profile is the bridge between business need and candidate evaluation. Without it, recruiters screen for familiar titles, interviewers ask inconsistent questions, and hiring managers reject candidates using criteria they never stated. Agents often treat requirements as a list of skills instead of a hypothesis about what someone must do successfully in context. This skill helps the agent create criteria that are job-relevant, evaluable, and adjustable when market evidence disproves assumptions.

## Core Rules

### Define success in outcomes and context

Describe what the person must accomplish in the first 3, 6, and 12 months. Include operating context: team maturity, manager support, ambiguity, customer exposure, tools, pace, travel, stakeholder complexity, compliance environment, and performance expectations.

Requirements should flow from these outcomes. A candidate with an impressive background may still fail if the role requires different context tolerance, collaboration style, or ramp conditions.

### Separate capability from proxy

Common proxies include years of experience, specific company pedigree, school, degree, title, industry, tool name, or career path. Some proxies may be useful signals, but they are not the capability itself. Identify the underlying capability: enterprise selling, distributed systems debugging, regulated documentation, stakeholder negotiation, high-volume scheduling, or frontline leadership.

Overreliance on proxies narrows the pool and often reproduces bias. Use proxies only when they predict job performance and cannot be replaced by better evidence.

### Define minimum bar and differentiators

Clarify the minimum acceptable level for each core capability and what makes a candidate exceptional. This prevents interviewers from treating every positive signal as required. It also helps recruiters shortlist strong but nontraditional candidates.

For example, "has led a payroll implementation for 5,000 employees" may be a differentiator, while "can manage complex cross-functional implementation risks" may be the true must-have.

### Calibrate with real candidate examples

Use a small set of profiles to test criteria. Review candidates who clearly pass, clearly fail, and sit near the boundary. Ask which evidence matters and which rejection reasons are weak or inconsistent.

Calibration should happen early. Waiting until the team has rejected many candidates wastes market time and may reveal that the success profile was unrealistic.

### Make tradeoffs explicit

Few candidates satisfy every preference. Decide which tradeoffs are acceptable: deeper domain expertise with less management experience, stronger execution with less strategy, high potential with longer ramp, remote flexibility versus location, compensation versus seniority, or culture add versus familiar background.

If tradeoffs are not explicit, each interviewer may optimize for a different ideal.

### Build structured evaluation from the profile

Map each capability to an assessment method. Resume review may show exposure, but interviews or work samples may be needed for judgment, communication, problem solving, leadership, or technical depth. Avoid assessing the same easy signal repeatedly while ignoring harder must-haves.

The success profile should shape the interview plan, scorecard, sourcing targets, and recruiter screen.

### Revisit requirements when market evidence conflicts

If the pool is too small, compensation is mismatched, outreach response is poor, or calibrated candidates are repeatedly rejected, revisit assumptions. The answer may be to raise compensation, change level, relax a proxy, split the role, adjust location, or accept a ramp plan.

Do not keep searching for a profile the market evidence says is unlikely.

### Guard against culture-fit misuse

If the profile includes culture, define it behaviorally: collaboration across functions, customer empathy, comfort with ambiguity, feedback habits, ethical judgment, or ownership. Avoid vague "fit" language that can hide similarity bias.

Strong profiles include culture contribution, not just conformity to the existing team.

### Distinguish current need from future fantasy

Hiring teams often combine today's urgent workload with future leadership hopes and create a profile no one can fairly evaluate. Separate what the role must do immediately from what the person could grow into later. If future growth potential matters, define the signals and support required.

This prevents rejecting candidates who can solve the real job because they do not match an imagined future version of the role.

## Common Traps

- Defining success as a list of credentials rather than role outcomes.
- Treating years of experience or brand-name employers as proof of capability.
- Calling every preference a must-have and creating a unicorn profile.
- Failing to agree on what "senior" or "strategic" means in observable terms.
- Letting interviewers add new criteria after meeting candidates.
- Rejecting nontraditional candidates without checking whether the concern is job-relevant; repeating the same assessment across interviews while missing key capabilities
- Ignoring market evidence that compensation, location, or requirements are unrealistic; using culture fit as a vague reason to reject candidates
- Never defining acceptable tradeoffs before candidate review; combining immediate execution needs with future leadership hopes until the profile becomes unrealistic

## Self-Check

- Are 3, 6, and 12 month success outcomes defined?
- Are requirements tied to the operating context and real work conditions?
- Are proxies separated from the underlying capabilities they are meant to signal?
- Is the minimum bar distinct from differentiators and preferences?
- Have real candidate examples been used to calibrate the boundary?
- Are acceptable tradeoffs explicit?
- Does the interview plan assess each core capability with an appropriate method?
- Are interviewers prevented from adding unstated criteria late in the process?
- Is market evidence used to revise unrealistic requirements?
- Is culture described behaviorally rather than as vague fit?; are current job needs separated from future potential expectations?
