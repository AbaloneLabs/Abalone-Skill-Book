# Hiring Demand And Role Intake

Recruiting intake is not an administrative kickoff. It defines the business problem the hire should solve, the evidence candidates will be judged against, and the tradeoffs the recruiter must manage. Agents often jump from "we need a person" to job posting or sourcing, then miss unresolved demand, conflicting stakeholder expectations, budget uncertainty, team capacity, and hidden bias in the role definition. This skill helps the agent slow down enough to make the search operationally usable.

## Core Rules

### Define the hiring problem before defining the person

Start by identifying why the role exists. Is it a backfill, growth hire, replacement for underperformance, new capability, capacity relief, geographic expansion, succession plan, or urgent project need? The reason changes the search strategy and assessment criteria.

Do not accept "we need someone senior" or "we need a strong operator" as a hiring problem. Translate the request into work outcomes: what will be better six months after the hire, what work is currently blocked, what risk exists if no one is hired, and what tradeoffs the team has already tried.

### Confirm approval, budget, level, and decision ownership

Before search begins, confirm the requisition approval state, compensation band, location constraints, employment type, level, reporting line, headcount plan, and decision maker. If approval is pending, label the search as exploratory and avoid creating candidate expectations that the team cannot honor.

Recruiting credibility is damaged when candidates move through a process for a role that is not funded, is later downleveled, or has no aligned decision owner.

### Separate must-haves from preferences

Hiring managers often list every trait of the previous incumbent, ideal future leader, and preferred background. Force a distinction between required capabilities, trainable skills, preferred experiences, and nice-to-have signals. Ask which requirement would make the team reject an otherwise strong candidate and why.

Overloaded requirements shrink the market and increase bias. A requirement should be tied to job performance, legal eligibility, safety, regulation, customer need, or ramp speed.

### Identify stakeholder misalignment early

The hiring manager, skip-level leader, team peers, interviewers, finance, HR, and recruiter may hold different assumptions about seniority, scope, compensation, remote flexibility, urgency, and evaluation criteria. Surface these conflicts during intake instead of discovering them after interviews.

If stakeholders disagree on the problem, the recruiter should not solve it by sending more candidates. The search needs a reset or decision meeting.

### Build the search from real constraints

Document constraints that materially shape candidate availability: location, schedule, travel, language, security clearance, licensure, immigration sponsorship, compensation, hybrid policy, start date, industry restrictions, noncompete sensitivity, and team interview capacity.

Do not bury constraints in recruiter notes. They should be visible in search strategy, outreach, screening, and candidate communication.

### Define evidence and evaluation before sourcing

For each must-have, decide how evidence will be collected: resume signal, portfolio, recruiter screen, technical interview, case exercise, work sample, reference, certification, or structured behavioral question. If no one can describe how to evaluate a requirement, the requirement is probably unclear.

This prevents candidates from being rejected by gut feel or late-stage surprise criteria.

### Decide the initial market hypothesis

A good intake ends with a market hypothesis: target titles, adjacent titles, industries, companies, seniority, compensation range, locations, channels, likely objections, and expected scarcity. Treat it as a starting point to test, not as a permanent truth.

If the market hypothesis is weak, plan an early calibration review after a small candidate sample.

### Set operating expectations

Agree on response times, interview availability, feedback deadlines, slate review cadence, escalation path, and what will trigger a search reset. A recruiter cannot run a healthy search if managers take two weeks to review resumes or interviewers miss debriefs.

The intake should create commitments from the hiring team as well as from recruiting.

## Common Traps

- Starting sourcing because a requisition exists, even though approval, budget, level, or decision ownership is unclear.
- Treating a backfill as automatically identical to the previous employee instead of reassessing current team needs.
- Letting preferred pedigree, school, company, or title become an unstated must-have.
- Listing too many requirements without tying them to job outcomes.
- Ignoring compensation or location constraints until candidates are already engaged.
- Failing to define how each requirement will be evaluated.
- Allowing different stakeholders to hold different versions of the role; promising urgency without securing interview availability and feedback discipline
- Treating a poor early candidate response as candidate quality failure before testing whether the role definition is unrealistic; omitting risk if the hire is delayed or not made, which weakens prioritization

## Self-Check

- Is the business reason for the hire explicit and tied to work outcomes?
- Are approval, budget, level, reporting line, location, and decision ownership confirmed or labeled as pending?
- Are must-haves separated from preferences and trainable skills?
- Is each requirement tied to job performance, regulation, safety, customer need, or ramp speed?
- Have stakeholder disagreements about scope, level, compensation, location, and urgency been surfaced?
- Are real constraints visible in the search strategy and candidate communication plan?
- Is there an evaluation method for each must-have?
- Does the intake include an initial market hypothesis and calibration plan?
- Are hiring manager and interviewer operating commitments defined?
- Would the recruiter know what evidence should cause a search reset?
