# Job Posting And Employer Value Proposition

A job posting is both a sourcing tool and a promise. It tells candidates what the company values, who should apply, what the work really involves, and whether the opportunity is worth attention. Agents often produce generic postings full of requirements and brand slogans, then miss clarity, inclusion, legal claims, and candidate decision factors. This skill helps the agent write postings that attract the right candidates without misleading them.

## Core Rules

### Lead with the real work and impact

Explain what the person will do, who they will work with, what outcomes matter, and why the role exists. Candidates need more than a title and company description. They need to understand the work context.

Avoid vague statements such as "fast-paced environment" or "rockstar needed" unless translated into concrete conditions.

### Align requirements with the success profile

Separate must-have qualifications from preferred experience. Keep requirements tied to job performance. Remove unnecessary degrees, years, tools, industry background, or pedigree where equivalent evidence can work.

Overloaded postings discourage qualified candidates and can reduce slate breadth.

### Make the value proposition specific

Candidates evaluate scope, learning, manager quality, flexibility, mission, compensation, stability, technology, autonomy, benefits, team maturity, and growth path. Name the parts that are true and distinctive for this role.

Do not rely on generic employer claims that could apply to any company.

### Be honest about constraints and challenges

If the role requires travel, onsite presence, on-call work, high ambiguity, transformation, regulated work, customer escalation, or fast growth, say so in a balanced way. Candidates should opt into the real role.

Hiding challenges may increase applicants but reduce acceptance and retention.

### Use inclusive and accessible language

Avoid jargon, gender-coded terms, unnecessary physical requirements, elitist signals, age-coded phrases, and insider language. Make the posting readable and accessible. Encourage candidates who meet core requirements, not only those who match every preference.

Inclusive language is not cosmetic; it changes who sees themselves as eligible.

### Handle pay, location, and flexibility accurately

State compensation range, location, remote/hybrid expectations, sponsorship, travel, schedule, and employment type according to policy and legal requirements. Do not advertise flexibility that the manager will not honor.

Misleading practical details create candidate distrust and compliance risk.

### Coordinate with hiring manager and approvals

The posting should reflect the agreed role, level, compensation, and process. Review with hiring manager, HR, compensation, legal, or employer brand where required. Public claims can become evidence of expectations.

Do not publish a role narrative that internal stakeholders cannot support.

### Use posting performance as feedback

Track applicant volume, qualified rate, source quality, candidate questions, drop-off, and demographic or source patterns where available. If the posting attracts wrong-fit candidates or discourages qualified ones, revise it.

A posting is a testable hypothesis, not a static artifact.

### Align posting language with funnel stage reality and avoid internal jargon and inflated titles

If the posting promises collaboration, speed, flexibility, mentorship, or high autonomy, the interview process should reinforce those claims. A posting that says the company values candidate time but requires six uncoordinated interviews creates credibility loss.

Review whether the candidate experience, interview questions, and offer conversation will support the value proposition. The posting should not be the most polished version of a process the company cannot deliver.

Internal terminology, leveling codes, product acronyms, and team nicknames may be meaningless to candidates. Titles that are inflated for attraction can also create compensation, expectation, and internal equity issues.

Use language candidates can understand and that internal systems can honor.

## Common Traps

- Writing a company advertisement instead of a role-specific opportunity.
- Listing every preferred trait as a requirement.
- Using vague or exclusionary language such as rockstar, ninja, young, native speaker, or culture fit.
- Hiding travel, onsite, schedule, or ambiguity constraints; advertising pay or flexibility that differs from manager reality
- Copying an old posting without checking current role needs; requiring degrees or years without job-related justification
- Making claims about growth, promotion, or mission that cannot be supported; ignoring applicant quality data after posting
- Treating the posting as separate from interview and offer expectations; advertising a candidate experience that the interview process contradicts
- Using internal jargon or inflated titles that create expectation mismatch; reusing old postings without checking whether the role, manager, or work model changed

## Self-Check

- Does the posting explain real work, outcomes, team context, and role purpose?
- Are must-haves separated from preferences?
- Are requirements job-related and not unnecessarily exclusionary?
- Is the value proposition specific and true for this role?
- Are challenges, travel, schedule, onsite, and ambiguity described honestly?
- Is language inclusive, readable, and accessible?
- Are compensation, location, flexibility, sponsorship, and employment type accurate and compliant?
- Have required internal owners reviewed public claims?; are posting performance and applicant quality tracked?
- Would a new hire recognize the job from the posting after starting?; does the interview and offer process reinforce the posting's value proposition?
- Are titles and terms understandable externally and supportable internally?; has reused posting copy been checked against the current role reality?
