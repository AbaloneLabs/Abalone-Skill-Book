# Skills Supply Demand And Location Strategy

Location strategy can make or break a search. A company may insist on a hub where the skill is scarce, use compensation from one market while sourcing in another, or open remote search without understanding legal and time-zone constraints. Recruiters need to advise where talent exists and how supply-demand dynamics affect pay, timeline, and process. This skill helps the recruiter turn market availability into location and search strategy.

## Core Rules

### Define Skill Scarcity

Some skills are common generally but scarce at the required level, industry, clearance, language, tool, or location. Scarcity should be specific.

Do not call a role hard to fill without naming the scarce element.

### Compare Demand Competition

Demand comes from competitors, adjacent industries, startups, government, consultancies, and remote employers. High demand affects compensation, response rates, and counteroffers.

Talent supply should always be evaluated against competing demand.

### Map Location Pools

Identify where relevant talent works now, where they are willing to move, and where remote work is legally and operationally possible. Commute patterns and relocation appetite matter.

Location strategy should be evidence-based.

### Align Pay With Search Geography

If the company sources nationally but pays local rates, candidates may disengage. If it pays premium rates in low-cost markets, internal equity issues can arise.

Compensation and geography must be designed together.

### Evaluate Remote Feasibility

Remote hiring depends on collaboration style, time zones, tax and employment setup, data security, equipment, manager capability, and team norms. Remote can widen supply but add complexity.

Remote should be a deliberate operating model, not only a sourcing fix.

### Consider Relocation Realistically

Relocation depends on family, housing costs, schools, immigration, partner employment, cultural fit, relocation assistance, and candidate life stage. For many roles, relocation appetite is limited.

Do not build a search on relocation assumptions without evidence.

### Use Location To Recalibrate Requirements

If talent is scarce in the preferred location, options include changing level, skills, remote policy, compensation, training, or timeline. Location data should change tradeoff decisions.

Search strategy should not ignore supply constraints.

### Include Diversity Effects

Location choices can expand or narrow diversity. Some hubs may have homogeneous networks; remote can broaden access but may exclude people lacking home office resources or stable broadband.

Talent geography affects representation.

### Monitor Market Changes

Layoffs, new employers, immigration changes, remote policy shifts, and economic cycles can change supply quickly. Keep location assumptions current.

Market intelligence has a shelf life.

### Compare Scarcity Against Role Flexibility

Skill scarcity is not meaningful in isolation. The recruiter should compare supply against how flexible the role is on seniority, adjacent tools, domain experience, location, remote policy, compensation, visa support, travel, and ramp time. A scarce skill may become searchable if the team accepts adjacent experience; a plentiful skill may still be unreachable if location or pay is too restrictive.

This analysis should pressure-test must-haves. If the team requires three rare skills in one person, the recruiter should ask whether the job can be split, sequenced, supported by training, or redesigned around a core skill plus learnable components. Location strategy should follow that conversation, not precede it.

### Use Multiple Signals And Name Confidence

Supply-demand analysis can be distorted by profile keyword inflation, stale public data, hidden passive talent, inflated job postings, and duplicate candidates. Combine sources such as ATS history, sourced profile counts, response rates, compensation data, agency feedback, hiring outcomes, community signals, and competitor movement.

State confidence plainly. A large pool with uncertain keyword accuracy should not be presented as abundant talent. A small but highly responsive community may be more viable than a large uninterested market. Naming confidence helps leaders decide whether to test, invest, or redesign.

### Recommend A Test Market When Evidence Is Thin

When supply data is uncertain, propose a bounded test: a short sourcing sprint, response-rate benchmark, compensation probe, agency sanity check, or two-location comparison. A test market prevents leaders from betting the whole plan on weak data while still creating evidence quickly enough to guide hiring strategy.

## Common Traps

- Calling a skill scarce without comparing scarcity to possible flexibility in role design.
- Presenting keyword counts as talent supply without checking accuracy, response, and market interest.

- Treating skill scarcity as generic.
- Looking at supply without demand competition.
- Insisting on a location with little relevant talent.
- Searching nationally with local compensation assumptions.
- Calling a role remote without operational readiness.
- Overestimating relocation willingness.
- Refusing to recalibrate requirements despite location evidence.
- Ignoring diversity implications of hub strategy.
- Using stale market data after layoffs or remote policy shifts.
- Treating time zones as minor scheduling details.
- Assuming candidates will move for brand alone.

## Self-Check

- If evidence is thin, is there a bounded test market or sourcing experiment to improve confidence?

- Does the supply-demand view test which requirements, locations, or compensation assumptions can flex?
- Are data sources, confidence level, and known distortions stated clearly?

- Is the scarce skill element identified?
- Is demand competition understood?
- Are talent pools mapped by geography and willingness?
- Is pay aligned with search location?
- Is remote feasibility operationally real?
- Are relocation assumptions evidence-based?
- Are requirements recalibrated using location data?
- Are diversity effects considered?
- Are market changes monitored?
- Would the chosen location strategy produce enough qualified candidates at the right price?
