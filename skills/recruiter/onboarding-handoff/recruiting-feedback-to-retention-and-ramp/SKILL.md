# Recruiting Feedback To Retention And Ramp

Recruiting quality is not proven at offer acceptance. A hire who starts and quickly struggles, regrets the role, or leaves early may reveal problems in success profile, assessment, role selling, compensation, onboarding, or manager alignment. Agents often stop learning after the requisition closes and miss the feedback loop between hiring and retention. This skill helps the agent connect recruiting decisions to actual new-hire outcomes.

## Core Rules

### Define what post-hire success means

Quality of hire can include ramp speed, manager satisfaction, new-hire experience, retention, performance milestones, productivity, culture contribution, customer impact, and whether the hire solved the business problem. Decide which signals are appropriate for the role and available without unfair surveillance.

Do not reduce quality to whether the role was filled.

### Compare outcomes to the success profile

After the hire ramps, review whether the original must-haves predicted success. Did the candidate struggle in areas that were not assessed? Were some requirements unnecessary? Did a nontraditional strength matter more than expected?

This improves future requirement calibration.

### Check promise accuracy

Ask whether the job matched what recruiting and hiring managers communicated: scope, manager style, flexibility, workload, growth, compensation, travel, team maturity, and challenges. If promises were inaccurate, identify where the message drifted.

Retention problems often begin as expectation problems.

### Learn from early attrition without blame

If a hire leaves early or struggles, examine role design, onboarding, manager readiness, assessment gaps, compensation mismatch, relocation stress, team culture, and candidate motivation. Avoid blaming the candidate by default.

The goal is to improve the system, not defend the hire decision.

### Capture hiring manager feedback carefully

Managers may praise or criticize recruiting based on outcome, but feedback should be specific: candidate strengths, gaps, ramp surprises, process accuracy, and interview signal quality. Avoid using manager dissatisfaction as proof that recruiting failed without evidence.

Post-hire review should be balanced and job-related.

### Protect new-hire privacy and dignity

Recruiting does not need access to every performance issue. Use aggregated or role-relevant feedback where possible. Sensitive employee relations, health, accommodation, or disciplinary matters should stay with appropriate owners.

Learning loops must respect the new hire as an employee, not a candidate artifact.

### Feed lessons into future searches

Update success profiles, screen questions, interview scorecards, job postings, candidate messaging, sourcing strategy, and onboarding handoff based on evidence. If no artifact changes, the learning may not stick.

Close the loop with recruiters and hiring managers who will run similar searches.

### Balance speed metrics with retention signals

A search that filled quickly but produced poor ramp may not be a recruiting success. A longer search that produced durable performance may be worth the time. Interpret recruiting metrics with post-hire outcomes when available.

This prevents optimizing for short-term closure over long-term hiring quality.

### Include candidate-side expectation data and distinguish selection error from onboarding or management error

The new hire's early experience should be compared with what they said they wanted during screening and closing: growth, flexibility, manager support, technical challenge, stability, mission, compensation, or learning. If the role fails on the candidate's stated priorities, the issue may be expectation management or role reality.

This data should be handled respectfully and shared only at the level needed for process improvement.

A struggling hire may reflect weak candidate assessment, but it may also reflect poor onboarding, unclear goals, manager absence, team conflict, or changed role conditions. Diagnose before changing the recruiting bar.

Otherwise the team may overcorrect sourcing or interviews when the actual issue happens after start.

## Common Traps

- Treating offer acceptance or start date as the end of recruiting quality.
- Measuring quality of hire only through manager opinion.
- Ignoring whether requirements predicted actual performance.
- Failing to check if the role matched candidate-facing promises.
- Blaming the candidate for early attrition without reviewing role, manager, and process factors; pulling sensitive employee performance details into recruiting notes
- Learning lessons informally but not updating scorecards or postings; optimizing time-to-fill while ignoring ramp or retention
- Treating nontraditional hires' struggles as proof that broader sourcing was wrong; ignoring successful hires who disproved old assumptions
- Ignoring the candidate's stated decision criteria when reviewing early retention; blaming selection when onboarding or manager support caused the ramp problem

## Self-Check

- Are post-hire success signals defined for the role?
- Is quality of hire reviewed beyond acceptance and start?
- Are outcomes compared against the original success profile?
- Did recruiting and manager messaging accurately describe the role?
- Are early attrition or ramp issues reviewed systemically, not just personally?
- Is hiring manager feedback specific and evidence-based?
- Is new-hire privacy protected in the learning process?
- Are lessons reflected in future requirements, screens, interviews, postings, and handoffs?
- Are speed metrics balanced with ramp and retention signals?; did the hire solve the business problem the requisition was opened to address?
- Are new-hire outcomes compared with candidate-side expectations captured during recruiting?; is the team distinguishing selection errors from onboarding and management failures?
