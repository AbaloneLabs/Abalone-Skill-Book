# Offer To Onboarding Handoff Readiness

The handoff from recruiting to onboarding is where candidate promise becomes employee reality. A candidate may accept because of role scope, manager trust, flexibility, compensation, relocation support, or timing, and those details can be lost if the handoff is treated as paperwork. Agents often assume acceptance closes recruiting work and miss start readiness, contingencies, system setup, and relationship context. This skill helps the agent make the transition controlled and human.

## Core Rules

### Confirm offer terms are final and consistent

Before handoff, verify signed offer, start date, title, level, compensation, work location, work model, manager, department, employment type, contingencies, and any approved exceptions. Ensure ATS, HRIS, offer letter, and manager understanding match.

Inconsistent terms at onboarding damage trust immediately.

### Identify remaining contingencies

Background checks, references, work authorization, immigration, license verification, medical or safety requirements, relocation, and required documents may still be pending. Know who owns each item and whether the candidate can start before completion.

Do not let onboarding proceed on assumptions if policy requires completion first.

### Transfer only useful candidate context

Share information that helps onboarding: preferred name, pronouns if voluntarily shared and appropriate, start logistics, relocation timing, accessibility needs through proper channels, decision factors, manager commitments, and candidate questions. Avoid unnecessary interview opinions or sensitive data.

The handoff should preserve trust without spreading private information.

### Coordinate operational readiness

Check equipment, accounts, workspace, badge, payroll, benefits, training schedule, first-week agenda, manager welcome, and required forms. Recruiting may not own these tasks, but should verify handoff owners exist.

A new hire who starts without tools or clarity may question the decision.

### Keep the candidate informed through transition

Tell the candidate who will contact them next, what documents are needed, what timeline to expect, and who to contact with questions. If ownership transfers from recruiter to onboarding, make that explicit.

Do not let the candidate experience a sudden silence after signing.

### Preserve commitments made during recruiting

If the hiring manager promised a project focus, flexibility arrangement, relocation support, training, or title clarification, ensure the right owner knows. Approved commitments should not disappear because they were discussed verbally.

If a commitment was not approved, resolve it before start.

### Plan for start-date changes

Start dates may shift due to notice, relocation, verification, immigration, illness, or business needs. Define who approves changes and who updates payroll, IT, manager, onboarding, and candidate.

Start-date drift without coordination creates operational and pay errors.

### Close recruiting loop after start

After the hire starts, confirm handoff completion and capture lessons about process accuracy, candidate expectations, and first-week readiness. Use feedback to improve offer and onboarding transitions.

Recruiting quality should be judged partly by whether the hire's first experience matches the promise.

### Align handoff timing with candidate notice period and confirm ownership of exceptions

Some new hires have long notice periods, relocation windows, immigration timelines, or personal constraints. Decide when onboarding contact should begin, how often to update the candidate, and which documents should wait until contingencies clear.

Too little contact creates anxiety; too much administrative demand during notice can overwhelm the candidate or conflict with current-employer obligations.

Exceptions such as delayed start, remote-first setup, relocation, sign-on conditions, accessibility support, alternative equipment, or manager-approved schedule flexibility need named owners. If exceptions are hidden in offer conversations, onboarding teams may treat the new hire as standard.

The recruiter should make approved exceptions visible without exposing unnecessary private details.

## Common Traps

- Assuming signed offer means all terms and systems are aligned.
- Forgetting pending contingencies before onboarding begins.
- Sharing unnecessary interview notes or sensitive candidate information.
- Letting recruiter communication stop before onboarding has contacted the candidate; losing verbal commitments made during closing
- Failing to coordinate equipment, accounts, payroll, or first-week schedule; allowing start-date changes without informing all owners
- Treating relocation, immigration, or accommodation details as generic onboarding tasks; not verifying that the hiring manager is ready to welcome the new hire
- Missing feedback on whether recruiting promises matched onboarding reality; starting onboarding contact too late or too aggressively during notice period
- Letting approved offer exceptions disappear in standard onboarding workflows; assuming onboarding knows candidate commitments that were only discussed verbally

## Self-Check

- Are signed offer terms consistent across ATS, HRIS, letter, manager, and onboarding?
- Are contingencies identified with owners and start implications?
- Is only necessary candidate context transferred?
- Are equipment, access, payroll, workspace, and first-week plans owned?
- Does the candidate know who contacts them next and when?
- Are approved recruiting commitments preserved in handoff?
- Are start-date changes controlled and communicated?; are sensitive accommodation, immigration, or background details routed correctly?
- Is the hiring manager prepared for day one?; is post-start feedback used to improve recruiting handoff quality?
- Is onboarding contact timed appropriately for notice, relocation, immigration, or personal constraints?; are approved exceptions assigned to owners in the handoff?
- Are verbal commitments converted into approved handoff notes or resolved before start?; is the candidate told which owner now handles each onboarding question?
