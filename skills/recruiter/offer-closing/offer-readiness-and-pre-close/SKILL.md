# Offer Readiness And Pre-Close

An offer should not be a hopeful guess. By the time an offer is made, the recruiter should understand the candidate's motivations, constraints, compensation expectations, competing processes, start timing, and unresolved concerns, while the company should have approval, level, package, and hiring decision alignment. Agents often treat offer creation as the next administrative step after a positive debrief and miss preventable decline risk. This skill helps the agent prepare offers that are accurate, timely, and likely to be accepted for the right reasons.

## Core Rules

### Confirm the candidate's decision criteria

Before offer approval, review what matters to the candidate: compensation, title, scope, manager, growth, flexibility, location, mission, stability, team, benefits, visa support, start date, and family or relocation constraints where the candidate chooses to share them. Capture what would make them hesitate or decline.

Do not assume enthusiasm after interviews means acceptance. Candidates can like the team and still decline because one decision factor is unresolved.

### Verify compensation expectations and constraints

Confirm current expectations, currency, base, bonus, equity, benefits, relocation, sign-on, notice period, and competing offer range where the candidate is willing to share. Compare against approved band and internal equity guidance.

If expectations are outside range, resolve the gap before formal offer whenever possible. A surprise low offer can permanently damage trust.

### Align internal approvals before promising

Confirm level, title, compensation, start date, location, work model, reporting line, immigration support, relocation, sign-on, equity, and any exceptions. Know who approves each part and what documentation is required.

Do not hint that a package is approved when it is still pending. Candidate-facing confidence should match internal reality.

### Surface unresolved candidate concerns

Ask what information the candidate still needs to decide. Concerns may involve manager style, team stability, role scope, travel, remote policy, product direction, financial health, workload, career path, or interview experience. Route concerns to the right person before offer if needed.

Unaddressed concerns often reappear as negotiation friction or last-minute decline.

### Assess competing processes and urgency

Know whether the candidate has competing offers, final interviews, deadlines, counteroffer risk, relocation constraints, or personal timing issues. Use this to plan offer speed and communication, not to pressure the candidate dishonestly.

If the company cannot move quickly, communicate realistic timing and maintain engagement.

### Prepare the hiring manager for closing

The hiring manager may need to reinforce role impact, team fit, growth path, and why the candidate was chosen. Align on what the manager should say and what should remain with recruiting or HR, such as compensation mechanics.

Closing should be coordinated. Mixed messages from recruiter and manager create doubt.

### Decide whether to make verbal offer, written offer, or more discussion

Sometimes a verbal offer is appropriate after alignment. Sometimes the candidate needs another conversation, approval is incomplete, or terms are too uncertain. Choose the next step based on readiness, not excitement.

Do not send written terms while major questions remain unresolved unless the process requires it and risks are clear.

### Keep pre-close ethical

Pre-close is not manipulation. Avoid false deadlines, fake scarcity, exaggerated certainty, or pressure to accept without review. The goal is mutual clarity and informed commitment.

A candidate who accepts under pressure may still withdraw or start with damaged trust.

### Confirm role reality after final interviews

Before offer, check whether anything changed during the process: team structure, manager, reporting line, title, level, location policy, travel, compensation band, start date, project priority, or hiring urgency. Candidates should not accept based on an outdated version of the role.

If the process revealed concerns about role scope or manager expectations, resolve them before the offer. Misalignment at offer stage becomes early attrition risk.

## Common Traps

- Making an offer because the debrief was positive without checking candidate decision criteria.
- Discovering compensation mismatch only after the written offer.
- Suggesting terms are approved before approval is complete.
- Ignoring unresolved concerns because the candidate seemed enthusiastic.
- Treating competing offers as a reason for pressure rather than planning.
- Letting recruiter and hiring manager communicate different role expectations; sending a written offer while title, location, start date, or work model is still ambiguous
- Using false urgency or emotional pressure to secure acceptance; failing to prepare a closing narrative tied to the candidate's motivations
- Underestimating relocation, visa, notice period, or personal timing constraints; making an offer based on role details that changed during the process

## Self-Check

- Are the candidate's decision criteria and likely objections documented?
- Are compensation expectations compared with approved range and internal equity guidance?
- Are level, title, package, location, work model, start date, and exceptions approved or clearly pending?
- Are unresolved candidate concerns identified and routed before offer?
- Are competing processes and timing constraints understood?
- Is the hiring manager aligned on closing message and boundaries?
- Is the next step appropriate: more discussion, verbal offer, or written offer?
- Are candidate-facing statements accurate relative to internal approval status?
- Is the pre-close respectful and free of false pressure?
- Would a decline reason, if it happens, surprise the recruiter?; are final role details current and consistent with what the candidate believes?
