# Compensation Negotiation And Approval Control

Compensation negotiation is a trust, equity, and governance moment. Recruiters must help candidates understand the package while protecting approval rules, internal equity, legal constraints, and accurate expectations. Agents often focus on closing the candidate and miss unauthorized promises, inconsistent treatment, pay compression, salary-history restrictions, and downstream resentment. This skill helps the agent negotiate with clarity and control.

## Core Rules

### Know the approved compensation architecture

Understand the role's level, band, geographic differential, base range, bonus target, equity range, sign-on rules, relocation policy, benefits, commission plan, and exception approval path. Know which parts are flexible and which are fixed.

Do not negotiate from intuition. The recruiter should know the authorized space before responding to a counter request.

### Discuss expectations lawfully and consistently

Follow local rules about salary history, pay transparency, and required range disclosure. Ask for expectations only where allowed and useful. Provide range information according to policy. Apply the same communication standard across candidates.

Do not pressure candidates to disclose current pay where restricted or irrelevant. Current pay can reproduce inequity.

### Separate candidate preference from approval authority

A candidate may request more base, higher title, remote work, sign-on, equity, relocation, visa support, or deadline change. Capture the request accurately and route it. Do not imply approval until the right owner approves.

Language matters: "I can take that back for review" is different from "we can probably do that."

### Evaluate internal equity and compression risk

Increasing an offer may affect employees in the same level, team, geography, or protected class. Coordinate with compensation, HR, and hiring leadership where required. A higher offer may be justified by skill scarcity or level, but the rationale should be documented.

Closing one candidate should not create unmanaged equity or retention problems.

### Trade across package elements deliberately

Candidates may value base, bonus, equity, sign-on, relocation, flexibility, title, development, or start date differently. Some elements are one-time, some recurring, some symbolic, and some governed tightly. Make tradeoffs explicit and approved.

Avoid solving every concern with base salary if another approved element addresses the candidate's actual need.

### Keep negotiation facts clean

Document candidate requests, company responses, approval owners, final terms, and rationale. Avoid subjective comments about the candidate's personal circumstances, negotiation style, or perceived need.

Clean records protect fairness and prevent later confusion about what was promised.

### Preserve relationship while holding boundaries

If the company cannot meet a request, explain the boundary respectfully and offer the best accurate package. Do not make the candidate feel punished for negotiating. Negotiation behavior should not become a character judgment unless it violates clear professional boundaries.

The goal is an informed decision, not a power contest.

### Confirm final understanding before acceptance

Before acceptance, make sure the candidate understands base, variable pay, equity vesting, benefits timing, relocation, sign-on conditions, start date, contingencies, and expiration. Route detailed benefits, tax, legal, or financial questions to appropriate owners.

Do not let a candidate accept based on misunderstanding of a complex package.

### Control negotiation cadence and deadlines and coordinate changes across all documents

Set a clear timeline for candidate response, internal review, revised terms, and written confirmation. If approvals take longer than expected, update the candidate instead of letting silence feel like negotiation tactics. If an offer expires, confirm whether the deadline is policy, business need, or flexible.

Artificial pressure damages trust, but open-ended negotiation can also block workforce planning. Use honest deadlines and documented ownership.

When terms change, update the verbal summary, written offer, compensation worksheet, approval record, ATS, HRIS handoff, relocation documents, and hiring manager understanding. A negotiated change that appears in one place but not another creates disputes.

The final package should be internally consistent before the candidate signs.

## Common Traps

- Quoting compensation flexibility without knowing approval limits.
- Asking about salary history where prohibited or using it to anchor unfairly.
- Treating negotiation as a reason to judge candidate loyalty or personality.
- Increasing offers without checking internal equity or level consistency.
- Promising title, remote work, sign-on, or equity before approval; focusing only on base salary when candidate concerns involve risk, relocation, or timing
- Failing to document counter requests and final approved rationale; giving different information to candidates in similar situations
- Letting approval delays create candidate distrust; allowing candidates to accept without understanding variable pay, equity, contingencies, or conditions
- Using vague or artificial deadlines that feel like pressure tactics; changing terms verbally without updating written documents and systems

## Self-Check

- Is the approved compensation range and flexibility understood before negotiation?
- Are pay transparency, salary history, and local legal rules followed?
- Are candidate requests captured accurately without implying unapproved commitments?
- Are exception approvals routed to the correct owners?
- Is internal equity, compression, geography, and level consistency considered?
- Are package tradeoffs deliberate and tied to candidate priorities?
- Are negotiation records factual and appropriate?
- Are boundaries communicated respectfully without penalizing negotiation?
- Does the candidate understand the full package and contingencies before acceptance?; would compensation, HR, hiring leadership, and the candidate agree on what was promised?
- Are negotiation timelines, offer deadlines, and approval owners explicit?; are all documents and systems updated consistently after term changes?
