# Resume Screening And Evidence Quality

Resume screening is an evidence interpretation task, not a keyword match. A resume is an incomplete, self-presented record shaped by culture, coaching, industry norms, language ability, and access to opportunity. Agents often overvalue familiar employers, titles, formatting, and exact keywords while missing transferable capability, context, scale, progression, and evidence gaps. This skill helps the agent screen consistently against job-relevant criteria.

## Core Rules

### Screen against calibrated must-haves

Use the success profile and must-have criteria as the screen standard. Separate evidence that clearly meets a requirement, evidence that is uncertain and needs screening, and evidence that is absent. Do not reject for preferences unless the search strategy explicitly requires them.

If the criteria are not calibrated, pause and clarify. Resume screening cannot fix unclear requirements.

### Interpret context, not just labels

Titles and company names vary by industry, geography, company size, and career path. Look for scope, outcomes, complexity, customers, team size, budget, tools, domain, regulatory environment, and progression. A smaller-company manager may have broader responsibility than a big-company specialist with a more recognizable title.

Do not equate prestige with performance. Use brand names as context, not proof.

### Identify transferable evidence

Candidates may show relevant capability through adjacent roles, volunteer leadership, military service, caregiving return, freelance work, open-source work, projects, certifications, internal mobility, or industry transitions. Transferability matters when the core capability matches even if the exact background differs.

The agent should ask whether the person has done work with similar complexity, not whether they have followed the most familiar path.

### Avoid keyword and formatting bias

Applicant tracking systems and human reviewers can over-reward resumes optimized for keywords or polished formatting. Strong candidates may use different terminology, especially across industries, countries, languages, or career levels.

Do not reject solely because the exact tool or acronym is missing if related evidence suggests the skill may exist. Mark it for screen verification.

### Treat gaps and moves as questions, not automatic defects

Employment gaps, short tenures, career changes, contract work, layoffs, relocations, and return-to-work patterns require care. Some may be relevant to availability or role fit; many are not. Avoid assumptions about health, family, age, commitment, or performance.

If a pattern affects the role, ask a job-relevant question during screening rather than speculating.

### Compare evidence consistently

Use the same bar for all candidates. If one candidate is advanced despite a missing requirement because of strong adjacent evidence, apply the same reasoning to others. If one candidate is rejected for lack of scale, define what scale means.

Inconsistent screening creates bias and makes hiring manager calibration harder.

### Document decision-useful notes

Notes should explain why the candidate advances, needs clarification, or is rejected based on job-related evidence. Avoid subjective comments about personality, age, background, family, appearance, nationality, or other protected or irrelevant traits.

Good notes help later reviewers audit fairness and understand search calibration.

### Recognize when screening should become a call

If the resume has partial evidence for core capabilities, unusual but plausible background, unclear scope, or strong potential with gaps, a recruiter screen may be better than rejection. Screening calls are costly, but so is discarding qualified candidates due to incomplete documents.

Use calls to resolve important uncertainty, not to compensate for every weak resume.

### Handle applicant materials consistently across formats

Some candidates submit portfolios, LinkedIn profiles, GitHub pages, publications, military records, internal profiles, or short-form applications instead of traditional resumes. Decide which materials are official for review and which are supplemental. Apply the same job-related evidence standard across formats.

Do not reward candidates only because they know how to package themselves in the dominant resume style. Strong evidence may appear in project descriptions, public work, shipped products, or measurable outcomes outside a conventional CV.

## Common Traps

- Rejecting candidates because they lack the exact title while showing equivalent work.
- Advancing candidates mainly because of prestigious employers or schools.
- Treating missing keywords as missing skills.
- Penalizing employment gaps, career breaks, or nontraditional paths without job relevance.
- Overlooking candidates from adjacent industries who have the same underlying capability.
- Writing subjective or legally risky screening notes; applying different standards to referral candidates and inbound applicants
- Rejecting candidates for preferences that were never agreed as must-haves; using resume polish as a proxy for job performance
- Failing to mark uncertain but important evidence for screen-call verification; treating nontraditional application materials as inferior without reviewing their job-relevant evidence

## Self-Check

- Is the resume reviewed against calibrated must-haves rather than vague impressions?
- Are title, company, school, and format treated as context rather than proof?
- Has the agent looked for scope, complexity, outcomes, tools, domain, and progression?
- Are transferable capabilities from adjacent or nontraditional paths considered?
- Are gaps, short tenures, and transitions handled as job-relevant questions rather than assumptions?
- Is the same evidence bar applied across referrals, inbound applicants, and sourced candidates?
- Are notes objective, job-related, and appropriate for audit?
- Are uncertain but important signals routed to screening instead of automatic rejection?
- Are preferences separated from disqualifiers?
- Would the decision be defensible to the hiring manager, candidate, HR, and compliance reviewer?; are portfolios, profiles, internal records, and nontraditional materials reviewed with the same evidence standard?
