# Recruiter Screen Call And Fit Assessment

The recruiter screen is often the first human conversation in the hiring process. It should verify must-haves, clarify uncertainty, understand motivation, set expectations, and protect candidate trust. Agents often treat the screen as a casual chat or checklist and miss evidence quality, fairness, candidate selling, compensation alignment, and prohibited questions. This skill helps the agent run a screen that is structured enough for quality and human enough for engagement.

## Core Rules

### Begin with purpose and expectations

Open by explaining the role of the screen, expected duration, topics, and next steps. Confirm the candidate has time and knows the conversation is exploratory unless otherwise stated. This reduces confusion and builds trust.

Do not imply the candidate is further along than they are. Be clear whether the conversation is for qualification, mutual fit, or market exploration.

### Verify must-haves with job-relevant questions

Focus on the calibrated requirements that could not be confirmed from the resume: scope, tools, domain, work authorization if policy permits, location, schedule, travel, compensation expectations, start timing, language, license, clearance, or other job-related constraints.

Avoid questions about age, family, marital status, health, disability, religion, citizenship beyond work authorization, or other protected topics. If uncertain, route to HR guidance.

### Probe for evidence, not rehearsed claims

Ask candidates to describe specific work: what they owned, scale, constraints, stakeholders, decisions, outcomes, and lessons. Follow up on vague claims with "what was your role" and "how did you measure success."

The goal is not to conduct the full interview, but to collect enough evidence to decide whether the next stage is justified.

### Understand motivation and decision criteria

Ask why the candidate is open to change, what they want in the next role, what would make them decline, and what timing or constraints matter. Motivation helps with closing and prevents process waste.

Do not use motivation as a vague culture-fit filter. Tie it to role alignment, retention risk, and candidate experience.

### Represent the role honestly

Share role scope, team context, process, location, compensation policy, work model, travel, known challenges, and uncertainties at the appropriate level. If a role is messy, high-change, or ambiguous, say so. Candidates should opt into the real opportunity.

Overselling creates offer declines, early attrition, and reputation damage.

### Capture compensation and logistics early

Discuss compensation expectations, currency, variable pay, equity, relocation, notice period, visa or work authorization process where relevant, schedule, and remote constraints according to local law and company policy. Be transparent about known range or process when allowed.

Avoid moving candidates forward when there is a clear, unresolvable mismatch unless leadership explicitly approves a path.

### Maintain consistency across candidates

Use a structured guide for core questions while allowing tailored follow-up. Ask all candidates the same essential eligibility and fit questions. This supports fairness and makes comparisons meaningful.

Do not give referral candidates easier screens or ask nontraditional candidates extra skeptical questions beyond job relevance.

### Manage candidate questions as part of assessment

Candidates are also evaluating the employer. Answer questions about team, process, compensation, flexibility, growth, manager style, and risks honestly within policy. If the recruiter does not know, commit to finding out rather than guessing.

Candidate questions can reveal decision criteria, concerns, and competing opportunities. Capture them as closing intelligence, not as interruptions to the recruiter's script.

### Close with next steps and evidence-quality notes

End by explaining timeline, next stage, what the candidate should expect, and when they will hear back. Document evidence, open questions, concerns, compensation/logistics, motivation, and recommendation.

Notes should distinguish verified facts, candidate statements, recruiter judgment, and unresolved questions.

## Common Traps

- Running an unstructured conversation and later relying on memory.
- Asking prohibited or irrelevant personal questions.
- Overselling the role or hiding major challenges to keep the candidate interested.
- Treating compensation mismatch as something to solve at the offer stage.
- Advancing candidates because they are personable despite weak must-have evidence.
- Rejecting candidates because their motivation sounds unfamiliar but is not job-relevant; asking different standards of proof for different candidate backgrounds
- Failing to capture candidate decision criteria and likely objections; guessing answers to candidate questions instead of verifying role or policy details
- Leaving candidates unclear about next steps or timing; writing notes that are subjective, vague, or inappropriate for audit

## Self-Check

- Did the screen open with purpose, duration, and process expectations?
- Were must-haves and key uncertainties verified with job-relevant questions?
- Were prohibited or sensitive topics avoided?
- Did the agent probe specific evidence rather than accept broad claims?
- Are candidate motivations and decision criteria documented without becoming vague culture-fit judgments?
- Was the role represented honestly, including constraints and known challenges?
- Are compensation, logistics, availability, and work model alignment checked early?
- Were core questions applied consistently across candidates?
- Were candidate questions answered honestly or routed for follow-up instead of guessed?
- Do notes separate facts, candidate statements, recruiter assessment, and open questions?; does the candidate know the next step and expected timing?
