# Feedback Discipline And Role Reset

Hiring manager feedback is the control system for a search. If feedback is late, vague, inconsistent, or based on changing criteria, the recruiter cannot improve the pipeline and candidates lose momentum. Agents often respond to rejection by sourcing more, rather than asking whether the search needs a reset. This skill helps the agent use feedback quality to decide when to continue, recalibrate, pause, or redesign the role.

## Core Rules

### Require evidence-based feedback

Feedback should state whether the candidate meets must-haves, where evidence is weak, what concern is decisive, and whether the concern changes the search strategy. Vague comments such as "not quite right," "too junior," "not enough ownership," or "not a fit" need clarification.

Do not accept feedback that cannot guide sourcing, screening, or candidate communication.

### Track feedback patterns, not only single decisions

One rejection may be candidate-specific. Repeated rejections for the same reason may reveal a requirement problem, compensation mismatch, sourcing channel issue, interviewer bar drift, or unrealistic profile. Track rejection reasons by stage and source.

Pattern recognition prevents the recruiter from treating a broken search as a productivity problem.

### Identify moving-target requirements

If the manager rejects candidates for criteria not discussed at intake, ask whether the role definition has changed. New criteria may be valid, but they require a reset: update success profile, sourcing map, interview plan, candidate messaging, and stakeholder expectations.

Do not quietly absorb new criteria into the search while candidates already in process are judged by a different bar.

### Use data in reset conversations

Bring evidence: profiles reviewed, outreach response, screen pass rate, rejection reasons, compensation objections, time in stage, candidate withdrawals, source mix, and market examples. Data shifts the conversation from blame to decision.

The recruiter should present options: adjust requirements, raise compensation, change level, expand location, add training/ramp, pause search, or accept longer timeline.

### Protect candidates during recalibration

When a role resets, candidates in process need fair handling. Decide whether they are still viable under the new profile, should be transferred to another role, rejected with closure, or paused with explanation. Avoid evaluating candidates against criteria they never opted into.

A role reset is internal; candidates should not pay for confusion through silence.

### Escalate chronic feedback failure

If the manager repeatedly misses feedback deadlines, changes criteria, rejects without evidence, or delays decisions until candidates withdraw, escalate to recruiting leadership, HR, or the business owner. Use facts and impact, not frustration.

The goal is to unblock hiring, not to win an argument.

### Distinguish role reset from search optimization

Not every issue requires a reset. Low outreach response may need message changes. Weak screen pass may need sourcing refinement. Late-stage rejection may need interview calibration. A reset is needed when the underlying role, bar, or constraints are wrong.

Choose the intervention that matches the evidence.

### Document the new agreement

After reset, update intake notes, scorecards, job posting, outreach, compensation assumptions, interview plan, and candidate status. If documentation is not updated, the team will drift back to the old search.

Reset is complete only when operations reflect the new decision.

### Decide what to do with already rejected candidates

A reset may reveal that some earlier rejections were based on criteria that no longer apply. Review whether any silver medalists, held candidates, or rejected candidates should be reconsidered. This must be handled carefully to avoid confusing candidates or appearing disorganized.

If re-engagement is appropriate, explain the renewed context honestly without implying the first decision was careless. If not, record why the prior decision still stands.

## Common Traps

- Sourcing more candidates in response to vague rejection feedback.
- Allowing late criteria changes without updating the role profile.
- Treating slow manager feedback as normal while candidates withdraw.
- Using anecdotes instead of funnel data in reset conversations.
- Pausing candidates without communication during internal recalibration.
- Escalating emotionally instead of showing business impact; resetting the whole role when only one channel or screen question is weak
- Updating recruiter strategy but not job posting, scorecards, or interviewer guidance; letting the manager compare candidates to an imagined perfect person
- Ignoring candidate withdrawal as a signal of process or role problems; forgetting to review held or recently rejected candidates after the role criteria change

## Self-Check

- Is hiring manager feedback specific enough to improve the search?
- Are rejection reasons tracked by pattern, stage, and source?
- Have late or new criteria been identified and treated as possible role changes?
- Is reset discussion supported by funnel, market, and candidate data?
- Are candidates already in process handled fairly during recalibration?
- Are chronic feedback failures escalated with evidence and impact?
- Is the intervention matched to the actual problem: sourcing, screening, interview, compensation, or role design?
- Are updated criteria documented across all recruiting artifacts?
- Are feedback deadlines and decision ownership recommitted after reset?
- Would another recruiter understand the new search without oral history?; has the team decided whether previously rejected or held candidates should be reconsidered?
