# Work Authorization Sponsorship And Immigration Routing

Work authorization and sponsorship are high-stakes recruiting topics. A careless question can create discrimination risk, and a careless promise can derail a candidate's life plans. Agents often try to be helpful and over-answer immigration questions, or avoid the topic until offer stage. This skill helps the agent handle authorization and sponsorship as a policy-controlled feasibility issue.

## Core Rules

### Use approved work authorization questions

Ask only approved questions about whether the candidate is authorized to work and whether they will require sponsorship now or in the future. Do not ask about citizenship, nationality, birthplace, visa details, family status, or immigration history beyond policy.

If the candidate volunteers sensitive details, redirect to the approved process.

### Confirm sponsorship policy early

Know whether the company sponsors for this role, level, location, employment type, and timing. Some roles may be ineligible because of budget, legal entity, security, contract, or business constraints.

Do not let candidates progress deeply if sponsorship is impossible and sponsorship is required.

### Route immigration questions to experts

Recruiters should not give legal advice about visa eligibility, timelines, portability, dependents, permanent residency, or country-specific rules. Route to immigration counsel, HR mobility, legal, or approved vendor.

Helpful guessing can cause serious harm.

### Protect candidate privacy

Authorization status can be sensitive. Share only with people who need it for hiring feasibility, legal review, or onboarding. Do not discuss visa status casually with interviewers.

Interviewers usually need to evaluate job fit, not immigration details.

### Align sponsorship timing with hiring process

Immigration review may affect offer timing, start date, relocation, background checks, and onboarding. Build time for review before making commitments. If timing is uncertain, communicate carefully.

Candidate expectations should match process reality.

### Avoid discriminatory assumptions

Do not infer sponsorship needs from name, accent, school, location, ethnicity, or nationality. Apply approved questions consistently. Candidates with domestic-looking profiles may need sponsorship; international-looking profiles may not.

Consistency protects fairness.

### Document feasibility decisions appropriately and revisit if role or location changes

Record authorization and sponsorship information in approved fields only. Use standard disposition if sponsorship cannot be supported. Avoid informal notes with sensitive details or speculation.

Records should support compliance without spreading private data.

Sponsorship feasibility may change if the role moves location, level, entity, employment type, or start date. A previously feasible candidate may become infeasible, or vice versa. Recheck before offer changes.

Immigration feasibility is tied to concrete role terms.

### Separate recruiting feasibility from legal eligibility and coordinate offer expiration and immigration timing

The recruiter can identify whether sponsorship appears supported by company policy. Only qualified immigration owners should assess legal eligibility, petition strategy, or probability. Avoid saying a case is easy, guaranteed, or impossible unless that decision comes from the proper owner.

Candidates may make life decisions based on perceived immigration feasibility.

Standard offer deadlines may not work when immigration review is pending. Decide whether the offer can be extended, conditional, or delayed, and who explains that to the candidate. Do not pressure candidates to accept terms they cannot evaluate because sponsorship details are unresolved.

Immigration uncertainty should be managed explicitly.

### Track authorization changes during process and coordinate sponsorship cost and business approval

Candidate authorization status can change because of graduation, layoffs, employer sponsorship, visa expiry, relocation, marriage, country move, or policy change. For long processes, reconfirm through approved channels before offer and start.

Do not rely on early-screen information if months have passed or role terms changed.

Sponsorship may involve legal fees, filing fees, timelines, travel, premium processing, or business-case approval. Confirm who owns cost approval and whether it affects offer timing. Do not let hiring managers assume sponsorship is free or automatic.

Feasibility includes budget and process, not only legal possibility.

## Common Traps

- Asking about citizenship or nationality instead of approved authorization questions.
- Waiting until offer stage to discover sponsorship is required.
- Promising sponsorship before policy or legal review; letting interviewers discuss visa details unnecessarily
- Inferring work authorization from accent, name, or education; giving immigration advice to be helpful
- Recording sensitive visa details in broad notes; ignoring start-date impact from immigration timelines
- Treating sponsorship policy as identical across roles and countries; forgetting to recheck feasibility after role terms change
- Presenting recruiter judgment as immigration eligibility advice; applying standard offer deadlines when immigration review is unresolved
- Relying on outdated authorization information in long processes; ignoring sponsorship cost approval and business-case ownership

## Self-Check

- Are only approved work authorization questions used?
- Is sponsorship policy confirmed for this role, location, and employment type?
- Are immigration questions routed to qualified owners?
- Is candidate privacy protected from unnecessary sharing?
- Is sponsorship timing built into offer and start planning?
- Are assumptions based on name, accent, or nationality avoided?
- Is information documented only in approved fields?; are disposition reasons compliant if sponsorship cannot be supported?
- Is feasibility rechecked when role terms change?; would the candidate and legal owner agree no unauthorized advice was given?
- Is company sponsorship feasibility separated from legal eligibility assessment?; are offer timing and immigration review timing coordinated?
- Is authorization status reconfirmed when process or role terms change?; are sponsorship costs and approval owners identified?
