# AI Assisted Screening And Human Review

AI tools can summarize candidate materials and surface matches, but they can also hallucinate, amplify bias, hide criteria, and make weak evidence look authoritative. Agents often treat AI ranking as efficient truth and miss the need for human review, approved use, candidate notice, and job-related validation. This skill helps the agent use AI in recruiting without outsourcing judgment.

## Core Rules

### Confirm tool approval and allowed use

Before using AI for screening, summarization, ranking, outreach, or interview notes, confirm the tool is approved for candidate data and the use case. Some jurisdictions or company policies require notice, audit, or restrictions.

Do not paste resumes or interview notes into unapproved AI tools.

### Keep criteria job-related and visible

AI matching should be based on calibrated job criteria, not hidden proxies. Know what inputs the tool uses and whether recruiters can explain why a candidate was advanced or reviewed.

If criteria cannot be understood, use the tool cautiously and route concerns.

### Treat AI output as a draft

AI summaries can omit context, infer incorrectly, exaggerate fit, or miss transferable evidence. Human reviewers should check source materials before rejecting or advancing based on AI output.

No candidate should be rejected solely because an opaque summary looked weak.

### Watch bias and proxy risk

AI may overvalue familiar titles, schools, employers, keywords, uninterrupted career paths, or polished writing. It may undervalue nontraditional backgrounds, career breaks, adjacent skills, or different terminology.

Bias review is needed even if the tool appears neutral.

### Document human decision rationale

If AI assists review, record the human job-related reason for action. Do not document only an AI score. Human rationale supports audit, candidate inquiries, and process learning.

Automation should support evidence, not replace it.

### Protect sensitive data

Candidate materials may include protected disclosures, accommodation information, demographic data, compensation expectations, or background details. Ensure AI tools do not ingest, infer, or expose sensitive information beyond approved use.

Sensitive data should not become hidden model input.

### Monitor outcomes over time and be transparent within policy

Track pass-through, rejection patterns, source impact, candidate complaints, and error examples. Compare AI-assisted decisions with human review quality. If the tool consistently misses certain profiles, adjust or stop use.

AI governance requires ongoing monitoring.

Where required or appropriate, disclose automated assistance and provide candidates with process information. Recruiters should know what they can say when candidates ask how screening works.

Transparency protects trust and compliance.

### Define what humans must review and preserve appeal or correction paths where required

Specify when humans review AI output: before rejection, before shortlist, when confidence is low, when candidate background is nontraditional, when accommodation or sensitive data appears, or when the AI conflicts with recruiter judgment. Do not leave human review to informal preference.

Human review should be a designed control, not a vague assurance.

Some processes may require candidates to correct data, request reconsideration, or understand automated decision assistance. Know the policy and jurisdiction. Even where not required, serious error reports should have an owner.

AI use increases the need for accountable correction.

### Test AI summaries against source documents and avoid sensitive inference

Sample AI summaries and rankings against actual resumes, profiles, and notes. Look for omitted transferable experience, invented claims, wrong dates, misunderstood job titles, or overemphasis on keywords. Document recurring errors and adjust use.

An AI tool that saves time but changes the evidence cannot be trusted without controls.

AI may infer age, gender, ethnicity, health, disability, nationality, or family status from names, schools, gaps, language, or activities. Ensure prompts and workflows do not ask for or use those inferences.

The recruiter should not accept sensitive inferences even when the model volunteers them.

## Common Traps

- Using unapproved AI tools with candidate data.
- Rejecting candidates based on AI summary without source review.
- Treating AI scores as objective truth; allowing hidden criteria to shape hiring decisions
- Ignoring bias against nontraditional profiles and career gaps; documenting only AI output instead of human rationale
- Feeding sensitive disclosures into AI workflows; failing to monitor outcomes after tool rollout
- Assuming vendor claims prove compliance; being unable to answer candidate questions about automated screening
- Saying humans review AI output without defining when and how; having no path to correct AI or data errors
- Failing to sample AI summaries against source documents; allowing sensitive inferred traits to influence screening

## Self-Check

- Is the AI tool approved for candidate data and this use case?
- Are candidates given notice where required?
- Are matching criteria job-related and understandable?
- Is source material reviewed before rejection or advancement?
- Are bias and proxy risks monitored?
- Is human decision rationale documented?
- Is sensitive data protected from inappropriate AI use?; are outcomes and errors reviewed over time?
- Are recruiters prepared to explain the process within policy?; would the decision be defensible without the AI score?
- Are human-review triggers defined and followed?; is there an owner for correction, reconsideration, or error reports?
- Are AI outputs periodically checked against source materials?; are sensitive inferences excluded from prompts, outputs, and decisions?
