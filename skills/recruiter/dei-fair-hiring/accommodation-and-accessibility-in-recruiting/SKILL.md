# Accommodation And Accessibility In Recruiting

Recruiting processes should assess job-relevant capability, not a candidate's ability to navigate inaccessible tools, rigid schedules, or unexamined assessment formats. Accommodation requests can be sensitive because they may involve disability, health, religion, caregiving, pregnancy, or other protected contexts. Agents often try to be helpful but ask too much private information, improvise inconsistent changes, or confuse access with lower standards. This skill helps the agent route and manage accommodations with care.

## Core Rules

### Provide a clear path to request accommodation

Candidates should know how to request accommodations for interviews, assessments, travel, communication, or scheduling. The path should be visible in process instructions where policy supports it. Recruiters should know who owns review and approval.

Do not wait for candidates to disclose personal details in a stressful moment.

### Ask for functional need, not diagnosis

The recruiter usually needs to know what adjustment is needed: captioning, extra time, accessible room, break, alternative format, screen-reader compatible materials, interpreter, schedule constraint, written questions, or travel support. Avoid asking for diagnosis, medical history, medication, or personal details beyond the approved process.

Privacy discipline protects the candidate and the organization.

### Route through the correct owner

Accommodation decisions may require HR, legal, accessibility, occupational health, or recruiting operations depending on policy and jurisdiction. The recruiter should facilitate, not independently adjudicate complex eligibility questions.

If a request is straightforward and policy allows immediate action, still document it appropriately and apply it consistently.

### Preserve the evaluation standard while adjusting access

An accommodation should remove unnecessary barriers, not change the essential job criteria. For example, extra time on a written exercise may be appropriate if speed is not essential. If speed under pressure is job-critical, the assessment design may need careful review.

The agent should distinguish access adjustment from criterion change.

### Review assessment formats for unnecessary barriers

Take-home exercises, timed tests, video interviews, whiteboard sessions, panel interviews, onsite visits, and asynchronous tools can create barriers. Ask whether each format is truly needed and whether alternatives can collect the same signal.

Accessibility should be considered before a candidate requests it, not only as an exception.

### Protect confidentiality

Only share accommodation details with people who need to implement them. Interviewers may need to know timing or format changes, not the reason. Do not discuss a candidate's disability or personal situation with the panel.

Candidate trust can be lost quickly if private information spreads.

### Prevent retaliation or stigma

Candidates should not be penalized for requesting accommodation. Monitor comments that frame the request as inconvenience, lack of commitment, or inability to do the job. If interviewers express concern, redirect to job-relevant evidence and policy.

The process should evaluate performance with the approved adjustment.

### Document process and follow-up

Record the request, routing, approved adjustment, implementation owner, and any issues, following retention and privacy rules. After the interview, check whether the accommodation worked if appropriate.

Documentation helps improve future accessibility and protects consistency.

### Handle timing without penalizing the candidate and prepare interviewers only with necessary instructions

Accommodation requests may require extra coordination, but the candidate should not be disadvantaged because the process needs time to respond. Communicate expected timing, pause deadlines if appropriate, and make sure interview slots or application windows are not lost because of internal delay.

If an assessment deadline cannot fairly accommodate the request, escalate before the deadline passes.

Interviewers may need to know that a candidate receives extra time, a different format, captioning, breaks, or written prompts. They usually do not need to know why. Provide implementation instructions and remind interviewers to assess against the same job criteria.

This protects confidentiality and reduces stigma.

## Common Traps

- Asking candidates to disclose diagnosis or medical history to justify scheduling or format needs.
- Treating accommodation as special favor rather than access to fair evaluation.
- Letting each recruiter improvise different responses to similar requests.
- Sharing the reason for accommodation with interviewers who only need logistics.
- Assuming a candidate who requests accommodation cannot meet role requirements; refusing alternatives without checking whether the original assessment format is essential
- Forgetting accessibility in tools, documents, video platforms, onsite rooms, and travel; penalizing candidates for process friction created by inaccessible design
- Failing to document routing and implementation; treating accommodation requests as delays instead of part of process quality
- Letting accommodation coordination consume the candidate's interview window or deadline; explaining the reason for an accommodation to interviewers who only need instructions

## Self-Check

- Is there a clear accommodation request path for candidates?
- Has the agent asked for functional need rather than diagnosis or unnecessary private detail?
- Is the request routed through the correct HR, legal, accessibility, or recruiting owner?
- Does the adjustment preserve job-relevant evaluation while removing unnecessary barriers?
- Are assessment formats reviewed for accessibility and alternatives?
- Is candidate information shared only with those who need it?
- Are interviewers prevented from stigmatizing or penalizing the request?
- Is the approved adjustment implemented consistently?
- Are records handled according to privacy and retention rules?; is feedback gathered when an accessibility issue reveals a process defect?
- Are deadlines, interview slots, and candidate status protected while requests are handled?; do interviewers receive only the implementation details they need?
