# Agency Search Brief And Market Control

External agencies can extend reach, but they also represent the employer in the market. A weak brief leads to weak candidates, off-brand outreach, uncontrolled promises, and duplicate contact. Agents often hand agencies a job description and expect results, then blame candidate quality. This skill helps the agent use agency partners with clear criteria, boundaries, and market control.

## Core Rules

### Decide why agency support is needed

Clarify whether the agency is needed for niche expertise, confidential search, speed, geography, temporary staffing, executive access, or recruiting capacity. The reason shapes partner selection, fee tolerance, and process expectations.

Do not outsource a role just because internal alignment is weak. Agencies cannot fix unclear requirements.

### Provide a real search brief

The brief should include business problem, success profile, must-haves, preferences, compensation range, location, work model, selling points, disqualifiers, process, timeline, and candidate communication boundaries. Include examples of good and poor fit.

A job description is not a search brief.

### Control market messaging

Agencies should know what they can say about company, role, compensation, remote work, urgency, sponsorship, and process. Review outreach tone and claims if needed. Candidate expectations created by an agency become the employer's problem.

Do not allow agencies to oversell or hide constraints to generate submissions.

### Set submission quality standards

Define what must accompany each candidate: resume, summary, evidence against criteria, compensation expectations, availability, location, motivation, source, and open concerns. Reject low-context submissions quickly.

Agencies should not be rewarded for volume without fit.

### Coordinate market coverage

Decide which companies, geographies, channels, and candidates the agency may contact. Avoid duplicate outreach from internal recruiters and multiple agencies. For sensitive searches, track contact permissions.

Uncoordinated market activity damages brand and candidate trust.

### Define communication and feedback cadence

Agree on submission review time, feedback format, pipeline updates, calibration calls, and escalation. Agencies need timely feedback to improve, but feedback should be evidence-based.

Silence turns agency work into guessing.

### Protect data and compliance and recalibrate or stop quickly

Agencies handle candidate data, compensation, and sometimes sensitive information. Confirm contracts, data processing, privacy, retention, equal opportunity, and background-check boundaries.

Vendor speed should not bypass compliance.

If agency submissions miss repeatedly, review whether the brief, market, agency capability, or internal criteria are wrong. Pause low-quality submissions instead of letting the vendor flood the process.

Agency management requires active control.

### Define candidate ownership and exclusivity before launch and share feedback that teaches the market

Before the agency starts, confirm whether the search is exclusive, contingent, retained, role-limited, geography-limited, or time-limited. Define candidate ownership rules, duplicate handling, and whether the agency can contact candidates already known to the company.

Ambiguity at launch becomes fee conflict later.

When rejecting agency submissions, explain whether the gap was must-have skill, level, motivation, compensation, location, availability, or role misunderstanding. Good feedback helps the agency refine market search instead of guessing.

Vague rejection wastes both vendor and internal time.

### Require agency evidence, not sales summaries and review agency outreach before market launch

Agency submissions should show what the recruiter learned: why the candidate is interested, which must-haves are met, where evidence is thin, compensation expectations, availability, and any concerns. A polished paragraph that merely sells the candidate is not enough.

The internal team needs decision-useful evidence, not agency enthusiasm.

For sensitive or hard-to-fill roles, sample the agency's outreach language before it goes live. Check accuracy, tone, role constraints, compensation language, confidentiality, and opt-out behavior. Agencies may use templates that do not match the employer's standards.

Prevention is easier than repairing market damage.

## Common Traps

- Sending only a job description and expecting calibrated candidates.
- Using agencies to avoid hard internal role-alignment work.
- Letting agencies make unapproved claims about pay, remote work, or urgency; rewarding candidate volume instead of evidence quality
- Allowing duplicate outreach across agencies and internal recruiters; providing vague feedback that cannot improve search
- Ignoring privacy and data-processing requirements; letting agency submissions age without review
- Continuing with a poor-fit vendor out of habit; failing to tell agencies which candidate segments are off-limits
- Starting agency search without ownership, exclusivity, and duplicate rules; rejecting agency candidates without feedback that improves future submissions
- Accepting agency sales summaries without evidence against criteria; letting agency outreach go live without checking accuracy for sensitive roles

## Self-Check

- Is the reason for agency support explicit?
- Does the agency have a real search brief, not only a job description?
- Are candidate-facing claims and outreach boundaries clear?
- Are submission quality requirements defined?
- Is market coverage coordinated to avoid duplicate or sensitive contact?
- Is feedback cadence agreed and evidence-based?
- Are privacy, contracts, and compliance obligations covered?; are poor submissions recalibrated or stopped quickly?
- Are agencies representing the employer accurately?; would a candidate contacted by the agency hear the same role truth as from internal recruiting?
- Are ownership, exclusivity, and duplicate rules defined before launch?; does rejection feedback help the agency recalibrate the market?
- Do submissions include evidence, concerns, motivation, and logistics?; has agency outreach language been reviewed where role risk justifies it?
