# Candidate Ownership Fee And Duplication Risk

Agency recruiting creates ownership questions: who introduced the candidate, when, under what terms, and for which role. These questions can become expensive and can distract from candidate experience. Agents often treat ownership as an administrative issue and miss contract rules, duplicate records, candidate consent, referral conflicts, and process delays. This skill helps the agent handle ownership disputes without losing control of the hire.

## Core Rules

### Check existing candidate history before accepting submissions

Before accepting an agency submission, check ATS history, prior applications, referrals, talent community records, internal sourcing, and other agency submissions according to policy. Candidate history affects fee eligibility and communication ownership.

Do not let agencies create ownership over candidates already known to the company if the contract says otherwise.

### Follow contract terms, not informal claims

Ownership may depend on introduction date, active consideration, role match, candidate consent, duplicate submission window, or fee schedule. Use the vendor agreement and procurement or legal guidance when uncertain.

Agency confidence is not the same as contractual entitlement.

### Confirm candidate consent and awareness

Candidates should know an agency is submitting them and for which role or company unless a permitted confidential process applies. Blind submissions can create privacy and trust issues.

Do not move forward with a candidate whose submission status is unclear.

### Resolve duplicates quickly

If two sources submit the same candidate, decide ownership before interviews progress if possible. Keep candidate communication clear and avoid making them mediate between sources.

Ownership disputes should not leave candidates waiting without updates.

### Separate candidate evaluation from fee dispute

Evaluate the candidate against role criteria even if ownership is unresolved, but do not make commitments that create contract risk. Coordinate with recruiting operations, procurement, or legal.

The candidate should not be penalized for vendor confusion.

### Document source decisions

Record submission date, source, candidate consent, prior history, contract rule applied, and decision. This protects future roles and fee discussions.

Weak documentation invites repeat disputes.

### Manage referral conflicts carefully and avoid incentives for low-quality speculative submission

Employee referral and agency submissions can overlap. Follow policy for referral bonus, agency fee, and candidate ownership. Avoid sharing private candidate details with referrers while resolving the issue.

Fairness to employees and vendors depends on clear rules.

If agencies submit broadly to capture ownership, tighten brief rules, require evidence, or pause the partner. Candidate ownership should reward real search work, not database dumping.

Vendor behavior follows the incentives the company permits.

### Keep candidate communication neutral during disputes and review ownership before offer approval

If ownership is disputed, communicate process timing to the candidate without blaming vendors or exposing contract details. The candidate should know their application is being handled, not feel like a fee object.

Internal resolution should happen behind the scenes with procurement, legal, or recruiting operations.

Before extending an offer to an agency-touched candidate, confirm fee status, guarantee terms, invoice requirements, and source record. Late discovery can delay offer or create unexpected cost.

Offer-stage surprises make the company look disorganized.

### Handle candidate preference in source disputes and prevent duplicate outreach before it happens

Sometimes the candidate prefers to work through one agency or directly with the company. Candidate preference matters for communication, but contract ownership may still control fee questions. Separate candidate relationship management from commercial resolution.

Do not ask the candidate to interpret vendor contracts or choose who gets paid.

Use shared target lists, do-not-contact records, agency coverage rules, and regular pipeline reviews. Duplicate outreach is easier to prevent than explain to a candidate who receives three messages for the same role.

Market coordination is part of fee control and brand control.

## Common Traps

- Accepting agency submissions without checking ATS history.
- Letting agency assertions override contract terms.
- Moving candidates forward when consent to submission is unclear; allowing duplicate-source disputes to stall candidate communication
- Rejecting or delaying a strong candidate because vendors disagree; failing to document ownership decisions
- Sharing referral or agency dispute details too broadly; paying fees for candidates already in process due to poor records
- Letting agencies submit weak speculative profiles to claim ownership; resolving ownership only after offer stage
- Discussing vendor disputes with candidates in a way that undermines trust; discovering unresolved fee status only after offer approval starts
- Asking candidates to resolve agency-versus-company ownership questions; failing to coordinate target lists before duplicate outreach occurs

## Self-Check

- Was candidate history checked before accepting the submission?
- Are ownership rules based on contract and policy?
- Is candidate consent to agency submission clear?
- Are duplicate submissions resolved quickly with candidate experience protected?
- Is candidate evaluation separated from fee dispute where possible?
- Are source decisions documented with dates and rationale?
- Are referral and agency conflicts handled according to policy?; are privacy boundaries maintained during disputes?
- Is vendor behavior monitored for speculative ownership capture?; would procurement, legal, agency, employee, and candidate understand the decision?
- Is candidate communication neutral while ownership is resolved?; is fee status confirmed before offer approval?
- Is candidate preference separated from contract ownership resolution?; are duplicate-contact controls in place before outreach?
