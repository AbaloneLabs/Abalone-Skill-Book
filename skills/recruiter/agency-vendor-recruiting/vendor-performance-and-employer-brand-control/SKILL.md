# Vendor Performance And Employer Brand Control

Recruiting vendors act in the employer's name. Their outreach, screening, promises, and follow-up affect candidate trust even when internal recruiters never speak to the candidate. Agents often judge agencies by number of submissions or hires and miss brand damage, compliance risk, candidate complaints, and market noise. This skill helps the agent govern vendor performance as part of recruiting quality.

## Core Rules

### Measure quality beyond submissions

Track qualified submission rate, interview pass, offer rate, acceptance, retention where available, candidate feedback, speed, diversity of source, compliance adherence, and hiring manager satisfaction. Submission volume alone can reward poor behavior.

A vendor who floods weak profiles creates internal cost.

### Review candidate-facing behavior

Ask candidates how the agency described the role, compensation, location, process, and company. Review outreach samples where appropriate. Agencies should not use misleading urgency, hide constraints, or pressure candidates.

Candidate complaints about vendors are employer-brand signals.

### Monitor compliance and data handling

Vendors may process candidate data, conduct outreach, use tools, or store notes. Confirm they follow privacy, consent, equal opportunity, retention, and platform rules. Require escalation for sensitive issues.

Outsourcing does not outsource accountability.

### Calibrate regularly

Hold check-ins to review brief accuracy, pipeline, rejection reasons, market feedback, compensation objections, and candidate experience. Good vendors improve with feedback; poor vendors repeat the same misses.

Calibration should be specific and evidence-based.

### Control market saturation

Multiple vendors contacting the same market can create spam, duplicate submissions, and rumors. Limit coverage, coordinate target segments, and pause partners if the market is being over-contacted.

Brand control includes restraint.

### Watch cost and value

Fees, retainers, temp margins, conversion fees, replacement guarantees, and internal review time all matter. A vendor may be worth high cost for niche or executive roles, but not for roles internal teams can fill well.

Evaluate total value, not only fee percentage.

### Escalate or exit when needed and capture market intelligence

If a vendor misrepresents roles, violates process, submits poor candidates, mishandles data, or damages candidate trust, escalate quickly. Define corrective action, pause submissions, or terminate according to contract.

Do not tolerate brand risk because a role is hard.

Vendors may provide valuable market feedback: compensation, competitor hiring, candidate objections, availability, and reputation. Distinguish evidence from sales claims and feed useful intelligence back into search strategy.

Vendor governance should harvest learning, not only police behavior.

### Review vendor incentives and behavior together and include vendors in candidate experience audits

Contingent, retained, hourly, success-fee, and RPO models create different incentives. A contingent agency may prioritize speed and volume; a retained search partner may provide deeper mapping; an RPO may optimize process throughput. Evaluate behavior in light of the model.

If incentives drive poor behavior, change the engagement structure or controls.

Candidate NPS, complaints, withdrawal reasons, and review-site comments should include agency-touched candidates. Otherwise vendor-caused issues disappear from internal dashboards.

The employer owns the experience even when the vendor causes the defect.

### Define minimum brand standards and compare vendor performance against internal alternatives

Vendors should meet standards for response time, truthful messaging, privacy, respectful rejection, opt-out handling, accessibility routing, and escalation. Put these expectations in onboarding and performance reviews, not only informal conversations.

Brand standards let the company address behavior before it becomes a public complaint.

Some vendors look useful because roles are hard, but internal teams might perform better with better intake, compensation, or sourcing tools. Periodically compare vendor outcomes against internal benchmarks and total cost.

The question is not whether the vendor fills roles, but whether it is the right way to fill them.

## Common Traps

- Measuring vendors by submission count only.
- Ignoring candidate complaints about agency behavior.
- Allowing agencies to oversell compensation, flexibility, or urgency; assuming vendor data handling is compliant because there is a contract
- Letting multiple vendors saturate the same talent market; continuing a vendor relationship because of habit despite poor quality
- Ignoring internal time spent reviewing weak submissions; treating hard roles as permission for brand-damaging outreach
- Accepting vendor market claims without evidence; failing to document vendor issues and corrective actions
- Ignoring how fee model shapes vendor behavior; excluding agency-touched candidates from candidate experience audits
- Managing vendor brand standards only informally; renewing vendors without comparing outcomes to internal alternatives

## Self-Check

- Are vendor metrics tied to downstream quality and candidate experience?
- Is candidate-facing messaging reviewed or sampled?
- Are privacy, consent, and compliance obligations monitored?
- Is calibration regular and evidence-based?
- Is market coverage coordinated to avoid saturation?
- Are cost and internal review burden considered?; are serious vendor issues escalated or paused quickly?
- Is market intelligence separated from vendor sales claims?; are corrective actions documented?
- Would candidates contacted by vendors experience the employer brand accurately?; are vendor incentives considered when diagnosing behavior?
- Are agency-touched candidates included in experience and complaint reviews?; are minimum candidate-facing brand standards documented?
- Is vendor value compared with internal recruiting alternatives?; are vendor-caused candidate harms visible in renewal decisions?
