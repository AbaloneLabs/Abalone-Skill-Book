# Competitive Positioning

Positioning only has meaning relative to alternatives. Competitive positioning is the deliberate choice of which alternatives to compete against, which frame of reference to claim, where to differentiate, and where to concede. It fails when it pretends the brand has no competitors, when it competes on the same axes as everyone else, when it claims a category the audience does not recognize, or when it ignores the switching costs and habits that keep customers with the alternative.

Use this skill before defining competitive positioning, building perceptual maps, choosing a category, or reviewing a positioning strategy. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing positioning that is internally satisfying but competitively naive.

## Core Rules

### Name The Real Alternative

Customers always have an alternative, even if it is doing nothing. Identify the real competitor before positioning.

Identify:

- direct competitors offering the same category;
- adjacent or substitute solutions, including manual workarounds and spreadsheets;
- the status quo or inertia, often the strongest competitor;
- internal teams or build-versus-buy alternatives in B2B;
- the next best option the customer would actually choose.

Saying "we have no competitors" usually means you have not identified the real one. Positioning against a phantom competitor misses the one that actually blocks you.

### Choose The Frame Of Reference Deliberately

The category you claim sets up the comparison. Choose it strategically, not by default.

Choose between:

- competing in an established category, which aids comprehension but invites direct comparison;
- creating a new category, which reduces comparison but increases explanation burden;
- repositioning against an adjacent category to change the comparison;
- positioning by use case, audience, or outcome rather than category.

Each choice has tradeoffs. A new category feels distinctive but forces you to educate the market. An established category is familiar but crowded. Decide which problem you are solving.

### Map The Competitive Landscape

Understand where competitors are strong and weak, and where open space exists. Use perceptual mapping to find opportunity.

Map:

- the dimensions customers actually use to compare, such as price, ease, power, or trust;
- where each competitor sits on those dimensions;
- where the market is crowded and where space is open;
- where your brand currently sits in the audience's mind, not your own;
- where you could move to occupy defensible space.

Mapping on dimensions no customer cares about produces a map that looks strategic but guides nothing. Map on real buying dimensions.

### Differentiate On Dimensions That Matter

Differentiation only helps if the audience values it. Choose axes where you can win and customers care.

Differentiate on:

- a dimension customers use to decide, not one only you care about;
- a difference that is genuine and hard to copy;
- a difference you can prove, not just claim;
- a difference that is large enough to change choice;
- a difference that is sustainable over time.

Being different on something no one values is not positioning; it is irrelevance. Being different on something easily copied is temporary.

### Account For Switching Costs And Habits

Even superior positioning loses to inertia if switching is hard. Positioning must address the cost of change.

Consider:

- what customers have invested in the current solution, data, integrations, habits;
- the risk and effort of switching;
- the time to realize value after switching;
- how to reduce switching cost or risk;
- how to position the cost of staying versus switching.

A positioning that ignores switching costs overstates how easily customers will move. Address the real barrier.

### Align Positioning With Proof And Delivery

Positioning must be supportable by what you can prove and deliver. Claims that exceed reality collapse on contact.

Ensure:

- the positioning reflects genuine product or service strengths;
- proof exists for the differentiating claims;
- sales, support, and product can deliver on the promise;
- the positioning does not overstate relative to actual capability;
- weaknesses implied by the positioning are addressed.

Positioning that promises what the product cannot deliver creates the exact gap that drives churn and bad reviews.

### Decide Where To Concede

No positioning wins on every dimension. Deliberately choose where to concede rather than pretending to be best at everything.

Concede:

- dimensions where you cannot credibly lead;
- segments you will not serve well;
- comparisons where the competitor genuinely wins;
- tradeoffs that follow from your differentiation.

Brands that claim to be best at everything are trusted at nothing. Honest concession on less-important dimensions strengthens credibility on the ones that matter.

## Common Traps

### Claiming No Competitors

Ignoring the real alternative, often inertia or a workaround, leaves positioning aimed at nothing.

### Competing On Crowded Axes

Differentiating on the same dimensions as everyone else makes the brand interchangeable.

### New Category Without Education

Creating a category without the budget and patience to teach the market leaves audiences confused.

### Ignoring Switching Costs

Positioning that assumes customers switch easily overlooks the inertia that actually blocks growth.

### Positioning On Dimensions No One Values

Being different on something customers do not care about does not change choices.

### Overclaiming Relative Capability

Positioning that exceeds what the product delivers collapses when customers experience the gap.

### Best At Everything

Claiming superiority on all dimensions destroys credibility and trust.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The real alternative, including inertia and workarounds, is explicitly named.
- [ ] The frame of reference or category is chosen deliberately with its tradeoffs understood.
- [ ] The competitive landscape is mapped on dimensions customers actually use to decide.
- [ ] Differentiation is on dimensions the audience values, that are genuine, provable, and hard to copy.
- [ ] Switching costs, habits, and risk are addressed in the positioning.
- [ ] Positioning is aligned with proof and with what the product can actually deliver.
- [ ] Deliberate concessions are made on dimensions where the brand cannot credibly lead.
- [ ] The positioning could not be claimed with equal credibility by the named competitors.
- [ ] Weaknesses implied by the positioning are acknowledged and addressed.
- [ ] The strategy is tested against how the audience currently perceives the brand, not only internal aspiration.
