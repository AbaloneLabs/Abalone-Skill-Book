# Messaging For Segments And Personas

Generic messaging aimed at an average customer reaches no one in particular. Different segments have different situations, pains, objections, languages, and decision processes, and messaging that ignores these differences underperforms in every segment. Segment messaging fails when personas are invented from assumptions rather than research, when they are so numerous that no one can act on them, when messaging is tailored in surface words but not in substance, or when the buying committee is treated as a single person.

Use this skill before building personas, adapting messaging by segment, addressing a buying committee, or auditing whether messaging fits its audience. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing segment messaging that changes adjectives but not understanding.

## Core Rules

### Build Personas From Research, Not Stereotype

Personas only help if they reflect real people. Build them from evidence, not imagination.

Ground personas in:

- interviews and conversations with real customers and prospects;
- sales and support feedback about real questions and objections;
- behavioral data about what different segments actually do;
- win-loss analysis of why different segments buy or not;
- the language real customers use, not marketing language.

A persona invented in a workshop without customer contact is fiction that misguides messaging. Research makes personas useful; assumption makes them dangerous.

### Focus On A Manageable Number Of Segments

Too many personas paralyze messaging. Prioritize the segments that matter most.

Prioritize by:

- segments with the highest value or strategic importance;
- segments that are sufficiently different to need distinct messaging;
- segments that are reachable and addressable;
- the few segments that drive most of the outcome.

Three to five well-researched segments usually beat fifteen shallow ones. Depth on the important beats coverage of everything.

### Tailor Substance, Not Just Words

Real segment messaging changes the argument, proof, and offer, not just the adjectives. Surface adaptation fools no one.

Adapt:

- the situation and trigger that matters to that segment;
- the primary pain or desire the message leads with;
- the proof and evidence that segment finds credible;
- the objections that segment raises;
- the call to action and next step appropriate to their readiness.

Changing "for enterprises" to "for startups" while keeping the same message is not segmentation. The substance must fit the segment.

### Address The Full Buying Committee

In considered purchases, especially B2B, multiple roles influence the decision. Message to the committee, not one person.

Map:

- the user, who cares about ease and outcomes;
- the buyer, who cares about cost and risk;
- the technical or security reviewer, who cares about fit and safety;
- the executive sponsor, who cares about strategic value;
- the champion who advocates internally.

Each role has different questions and needs different proof. Messaging aimed only at the user often loses the buyer; messaging aimed only at the executive loses the champion.

### Match Message To Stage And Readiness

Segments also differ by where they are in the journey. Adapt by stage as well as by identity.

Adapt by stage:

- unaware, focus on the problem and its cost;
- problem-aware, focus on options and approaches;
- solution-aware, focus on differentiation and proof;
- evaluating, focus on comparison, risk, and migration;
- customer, focus on adoption, expansion, and advocacy.

The same segment needs different messages at different stages. A single message per segment ignores this dimension.

### Use The Segment's Language And Frame

People recognize themselves when you use their language. Mirror their vocabulary and mental models.

Adopt:

- the terms and frameworks the segment uses to describe their world;
- the examples and references that fit their context;
- the channels and formats they trust;
- the proof types they find credible, data, stories, or peer reference;
- the tone that fits their culture and sophistication.

Messaging that uses the company's internal jargon instead of the customer's language signals it is not really for them.

### Validate Segment Messaging With The Segment

Assumptions about segments are often wrong. Test messaging with real members of each segment.

Validate by:

- sharing drafts with customers or prospects from the segment;
- testing different messages and measuring response;
- gathering sales and support feedback on what resonates;
- checking win rates by segment and message;
- revising personas and messaging as you learn.

Segment messaging that is never tested against the segment is a guess. Validation turns it into a strategy.

## Common Traps

### Personas From Assumptions

Inventing personas without research produces stereotypes that misguide messaging.

### Too Many Personas

A dozen shallow personas paralyze teams and prevent focus on the segments that matter.

### Surface Adaptation Only

Changing words without changing substance produces messaging every segment sees through.

### Ignoring The Buying Committee

Messaging to one role in a multi-role decision loses the others who can block the purchase.

### One Message Per Segment

Ignoring stage and readiness within a segment misses the journey dimension of relevance.

### Internal Jargon

Using the company's language instead of the customer's signals the message is not really for them.

### Untested Assumptions

Segment messaging never validated with the segment remains a guess that may be wrong.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Personas are built from research with real customers, not workshop assumptions.
- [ ] The number of priority segments is manageable, focused on value and genuine difference.
- [ ] Messaging adapts substance, including pain, proof, objection, and offer, not just words.
- [ ] The full buying committee is addressed with role-appropriate messages and proof.
- [ ] Messages adapt by stage and readiness within each segment, not only by identity.
- [ ] The segment's own language, examples, and credible proof types are used.
- [ ] Segment messaging is validated with real members of each segment.
- [ ] Sales, support, and win-loss feedback inform persona and message refinement.
- [ ] Win rates and response are measured by segment and message, not only in aggregate.
- [ ] Personas and messaging are revised as learning accumulates, not frozen after creation.
