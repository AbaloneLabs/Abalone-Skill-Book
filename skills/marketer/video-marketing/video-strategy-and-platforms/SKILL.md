# Video Strategy And Platforms

Video is not one channel; it is a family of channels with different audiences, algorithms, formats, and jobs. The most common failure is to treat video as a single asset that can be posted everywhere unchanged. A horizontal YouTube explainer is not a TikTok, a LinkedIn thought-leadership clip is not a Reel, and a sound-off feed video is not a sound-on storytelling piece. Strategy fails when video is chosen because it is popular rather than because it solves a funnel job, when formats are mismatched to platform expectations, or when a single hero asset is expected to perform across platforms that reward opposite things.

Use this skill before planning a video program, selecting platforms, deciding format and length, allocating video budget across the funnel, or reviewing whether a video plan is coherent. The goal is to prevent the agent from defaulting to "make a video" without specifying what job the video does, for whom, on which platform, and in what native shape.

## Core Rules

### Define The Funnel Job Before The Format

Video can serve awareness, consideration, or conversion, but a single video rarely serves all three well. Decide the job first.

Map each video to a stage:

- awareness: reach, recall, introduction to a problem or brand;
- consideration: explanation, demonstration, proof, comparison, trust;
- conversion: offer, urgency, proof, and a clear next step;
- retention and advocacy: onboarding, education, customer stories, community.

Ask which job this asset must do. A brand-awareness video that ends in a hard discount ask will underperform at both recall and conversion. Match the creative intent to one primary job and measure it against stage-appropriate metrics, not against the metrics of a different stage.

### Respect Platform-Native Formats

Each platform rewards a specific shape, pace, and social contract. Posting the same file everywhere ignores the format the algorithm and audience expect.

Platform expectations to plan for:

- YouTube: horizontal 16:9, search and recommendation driven, sound-on, tolerates longer form, rewards titles, thumbnails, and retention;
- TikTok: vertical 9:16, sound-on, trend and audio driven, rewards hooks in the first seconds and completion;
- Instagram Reels: vertical 9:16, sound-on, rewards visual hook and trend alignment, competes with a fast feed;
- LinkedIn: square 1:1 or vertical 9:16, often sound-off in-feed, captions essential, rewards professional relevance and first-frame clarity;
- in-stream and connected TV: horizontal, sound-on, higher production tolerance, mid-funnel reach.

Choose the platform because the audience and the funnel job fit, then produce the native format for that platform. Do not produce one format and hope it adapts.

### Design For Sound-Off First Where Feeds Demand It

A large share of mobile feed video is watched with sound off, especially on LinkedIn and during autoplay in public. A video that depends on a voiceover to make sense will lose these viewers in the first two seconds.

Build sound-off resilience:

- captions or burned subtitles on every feed video;
- on-screen text that carries the core message in the first frame;
- visuals that communicate the hook without audio;
- a first frame that signals what the video is about instantly.

Sound-on platforms like TikTok and YouTube reward audio, but even there captions expand reach and accessibility. Treat captions and readable first frames as default, not optional.

### Match Length To Platform And Purpose

Length is a platform contract, not a creative preference. The wrong length fights the algorithm and the audience's expectation.

Length guidance:

- feed shorts (TikTok, Reels, Shorts): 15 to 60 seconds, with the strongest hook in the first three;
- YouTube search and explainer: 4 to 12 minutes when the topic earns it, shorter when it does not;
- YouTube consideration and thought leadership: 8 to 20 minutes for audiences that opted in;
- in-feed social and LinkedIn: 30 to 90 seconds for most messages;
- connected TV and pre-roll: 6, 15, or 30 seconds depending on placement.

Ask whether the topic earns the length. Padding a 60-second idea into 8 minutes destroys retention. Cutting a 6-minute explanation into 60 seconds destroys clarity.

### Treat Aspect Ratio As A Production Decision, Not A Post-Production Fix

Reformatting a horizontal master into vertical crops loses framing, eyeline, and graphics. Plan multi-format capture at the shoot.

Decide at production:

- which aspect ratios you will ship (16:9, 9:16, 1:1);
- safe zones for each platform so captions and UI do not cover key content;
- framing that survives cropping, especially for talking heads and product shots;
- graphics and lower thirds placed inside all target safe zones.

Retrofitting crops in post is where text gets cut off, faces get decapitated, and calls to action disappear behind platform UI. Plan the deliverable shapes before the camera rolls.

### Sequence Video Across The Funnel

A video program compounds when assets feed each other. Plan the system, not isolated videos.

Sequence deliberately:

- a hero long-form piece that earns attention and trust;
- cut-downs and shorts repurposed for feed discovery;
- paid amplification of the best-performing organic cuts;
- retargeting of engaged viewers with the next-step offer;
- customer-story and proof videos for consideration and sales enablement.

A single video rarely carries a program. A library with clear funnel roles, repurposing paths, and amplification steps does.

## Common Traps

### One Asset Posted Everywhere

Posting an identical horizontal file to TikTok, LinkedIn, and YouTube ignores each platform's native contract and signals low effort to the audience and the algorithm.

### Choosing Video Because It Is Trendy

Video is expensive to produce well. Choosing it without a funnel job, audience, or measurement plan burns budget on assets that cannot be justified.

### Sound-Dependent Feed Videos

Feed videos that require audio to make sense lose the sound-off majority in the first two seconds and never recover retention.

### Wrong Length For The Platform

A 90-second message stretched to 8 minutes kills retention; an 8-minute explanation crammed into 60 seconds kills comprehension. Length must match the platform contract and the topic's earned attention.

### Measuring Awareness Video With Conversion Metrics

Judging a recall video by click-through or a conversion video by view count applies the wrong yardstick and leads to killing assets that were doing their actual job.

### Ignoring The First Frame

Algorithms and audiences decide in under two seconds. A slow build, a logo splash, or an unclear opening causes the viewer to scroll before the message lands.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each video has a defined funnel job (awareness, consideration, conversion, retention) and stage-appropriate metrics.
- [ ] Platform choices are driven by audience and funnel fit, not popularity, and each has a native format plan.
- [ ] Feed videos are designed sound-off first, with captions, readable on-screen text, and a clear first frame.
- [ ] Length matches the platform contract and the topic's earned attention, with the strongest hook in the first three seconds.
- [ ] Aspect ratios and safe zones were planned at production, not retrofitted as crops in post.
- [ ] The video program sequences hero content, cut-downs, paid amplification, retargeting, and proof assets across the funnel.
- [ ] No single asset is expected to perform unchanged across platforms with opposite format expectations.
- [ ] Measurement matches the stage: recall for awareness, retention and watch time for consideration, clicks and conversion for lower-funnel video.
