# Data Visualization And Reporting

A report's job is to drive a decision, not to display data. Reports fail when they bury insight under dozens of charts, when they use chart types that distort the comparison, when they present numbers without context or commentary, or when they hide bad results behind averages and vanity metrics. A good report makes the answer obvious and the action clear.

Use this skill before building a dashboard, writing a performance report, choosing visualizations, or presenting results. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing reports that look thorough but communicate nothing.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Question, Not The Data

Every report should answer a specific question for a specific audience. Build from the question outward.

Clarify:

- the primary question the report answers;
- the audience and their decision context;
- the one number or chart that answers it;
- the supporting context needed;
- the action the report should prompt.

A report that starts from available data becomes a data dump. A report that starts from a question becomes a decision tool.

### Choose Chart Types That Show The Right Comparison

The chart type determines what the eye can compare. Match type to message.

Match:

- trend over time to line charts;
- comparison across categories to bar charts;
- part of a whole to stacked or proportional charts, used carefully;
- correlation to scatter plots;
- distribution to histograms;
- funnel flow to funnel charts.

Never use a pie chart for more than a few slices, never use a line chart for unrelated categories, and never use 3D effects that distort comparison.

### Provide Context For Every Number

A number alone is meaningless. Context makes it actionable.

Provide:

- the target or benchmark it is compared against;
- the trend over time, not just the current point;
- the prior period and year-over-year comparison;
- the segment breakdown that explains the average;
- the statistical significance where relevant.

"Sessions were 50,000" informs nothing. "Sessions were 50,000, up 12 percent versus target, driven by organic, with paid flat" informs action.

### Lead With The Answer And Insight

Decision-makers read the top first. Put the conclusion there, not the data.

Structure:

- the headline answer to the primary question;
- the key insight explaining why;
- the supporting chart or table;
- the recommended action;
- the detail in an appendix for those who want it.

Burying the answer on chart seventeen guarantees it will not drive action. Lead with insight, support with data.

### Avoid Distortion And Misleading Visualization

Visual choices can mislead even with accurate data. Avoid distortion.

Avoid:

- truncated axes that exaggerate change;
- inconsistent scales across compared charts;
- dual axes that imply false correlation;
- area or 3D effects that distort perception;
- color choices that imply meaning where none exists.

The goal is honest communication. A chart that exaggerates to make a point undermines trust once noticed.

### Segment To Reveal, Average To Hide

Averages hide the segments that explain the result. Break down where it matters.

Break down by:

- channel, campaign, or source;
- segment, persona, or cohort;
- geography or device;
- new versus returning;
- time period to reveal seasonality.

A flat average can hide a winning segment and a losing one within the same number. Segmentation reveals where to double down and where to fix.

### Write Commentary That Interprets, Not Just Describes

A report with charts but no interpretation is unfinished. Add the human analysis.

Commentary should:

- state what happened in plain language;
- explain the likely why, with evidence;
- flag what is surprising or concerning;
- distinguish signal from noise;
- recommend the next action.

Charts describe; commentary interprets and directs. Without it, the reader must do the analysis themselves, and many will not.

## Common Traps

### Data Dump Dashboards

Dozens of charts with no narrative inform nothing and overwhelm the reader.

### Wrong Chart Type

Using a pie for many categories or a line for unrelated categories distorts the message.

### Numbers Without Context

A figure with no target, trend, or comparison is unactionable.

### Burying The Answer

Hiding the conclusion deep in the report ensures it drives no decision.

### Distorting Axes

Truncated or inconsistent axes exaggerate change and undermine trust.

### Averages Hiding Segments

A single average can mask a winning and a losing segment at once.

### Charts Without Commentary

Visuals without interpretation leave the reader to guess the meaning.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The report is built around a primary question and audience decision, not available data.
- [ ] Chart types match the message, such as line for trend and bar for comparison.
- [ ] Every key number has context of target, trend, prior period, and relevant segmentation.
- [ ] The report leads with the answer and insight, with detail in support.
- [ ] Visualizations avoid distortion from truncated axes, dual axes, and 3D effects.
- [ ] Averages are broken down by segment where the breakdown changes the interpretation.
- [ ] Commentary interprets what happened and why, and recommends action.
- [ ] Surprising or concerning results are flagged rather than buried.
- [ ] Signal is distinguished from noise, with significance noted where relevant.
- [ ] The report prompts a clear next action rather than ending on data.
