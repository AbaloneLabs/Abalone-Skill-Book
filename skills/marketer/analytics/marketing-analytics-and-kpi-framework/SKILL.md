# Marketing Analytics And KPI Framework

Measurement shapes behavior. Teams optimize for what is measured, so the wrong metrics produce the wrong work. Analytics fails when it tracks vanity metrics that go up regardless of effort, when it confuses correlation with causation, when it attributes outcomes to the last click and starves earlier efforts, or when it produces dashboards full of numbers that inform no decision. Good analytics connects activity to outcome to decision.

Use this skill before defining metrics, building dashboards, setting KPI targets, or reviewing a measurement plan. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing measurement that looks rigorous but drives wrong behavior.

## Core Rules

### Start From Decisions, Then Choose Metrics

Metrics exist to inform decisions. Choose them by working backward from what you will decide.

For each metric clarify:

- the decision it informs;
- who uses it and how often;
- what action a change would trigger;
- the threshold that signals action;
- whether a better metric exists.

A metric that does not inform a decision is noise. A dashboard of such metrics wastes attention and invites misoptimization.

### Distinguish Vanity, Activity, And Outcome Metrics

Not all metrics deserve equal attention. Classify them honestly.

Classify:

- vanity metrics that rise regardless of effort, such as total visits or raw social followers;
- activity metrics that measure what marketing did, such as emails sent or campaigns launched;
- outcome metrics that measure what resulted, such as qualified leads, pipeline, revenue, retention;
- leading indicators that predict outcomes versus lagging ones that confirm them.

Track activity to manage effort, but evaluate success on outcomes. Vanity metrics belong in context only, never as headline KPIs.

### Build A Connected Funnel From Activity To Revenue

Isolated metrics hide the relationship between effort and result. Connect them into a model.

Connect:

- reach and engagement to leads generated;
- leads to qualified opportunities;
- opportunities to pipeline and win rate;
- pipeline to revenue and CAC;
- revenue to retention, LTV, and payback.

A connected funnel lets you see where effort converts to outcome and where it leaks. It also lets you model the impact of changes before committing spend.

### Choose Attribution Honestly, Knowing Its Limits

No attribution model is perfectly correct. Choose one consciously and know its bias.

Consider:

- last-click over-credits the final touch and starves awareness;
- first-click over-credits discovery and ignores conversion drivers;
- multi-touch spreads credit but can dilute real contribution;
- data-driven models need sufficient clean data to trust;
- every model is a lens, not the truth.

State which model you use and why, and review results under more than one model for important decisions. Never present one model's output as objective truth.

### Separate Correlation From Causation

Two metrics moving together does not mean one caused the other. Be careful in interpretation.

Guard against:

- seasonality and external factors driving both;
- a third variable causing both;
- coincidence in small samples;
- reverse causation;
- survivorship bias in who you measure.

Use controlled comparisons, holdout groups, and incrementality testing where the decision is large enough to warrant rigor.

### Set Targets That Are Meaningful, Not Arbitrary

A metric without a target is just a number. Targets make metrics actionable.

Set targets by:

- baselining current performance;
- benchmarking against history and peers where credible;
- tying targets to the business outcome required;
- separating achievable stretch from fantasy;
- reviewing and revising as conditions change.

A target disconnected from the business outcome invites gaming. A target tied to required revenue or growth focuses effort.

### Make Dashboards Decision-Driven, Not Data-Dumped

A dashboard should answer questions, not display everything. Design for the decision.

Design:

- one primary question per dashboard view;
- the few metrics that answer it;
- context such as target, trend, and comparison;
- the action threshold made visible;
- segmentation that reveals the why.

A dashboard with fifty charts answers nothing. A dashboard with three charts and a clear target drives action.

## Common Traps

### Vanity Metrics As Headlines

Tracking raw totals that rise regardless of effort creates false confidence and misdirects work.

### Last-Click Attribution As Truth

Crediting only the final touch starves the awareness and nurturing work that made it possible.

### Correlation As Causation

Acting on two metrics moving together without proving causation leads to confident wrong decisions.

### Metrics Without Decisions

Dashboards full of numbers that inform no action waste attention and invite misoptimization.

### Arbitrary Targets

Targets pulled from thin air, not tied to business outcomes, get gamed or ignored.

### Activity Mistaken For Outcome

Celebrating emails sent or campaigns launched rather than results achieved rewards motion over progress.

### Single-Model Certainty

Presenting one attribution model's output as objective truth hides its bias.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Every metric is tied to a specific decision, user, action threshold, and review cadence.
- [ ] Vanity, activity, and outcome metrics are classified, with outcomes as headline KPIs.
- [ ] Metrics are connected in a funnel model from activity to revenue and retention.
- [ ] The attribution model is chosen consciously with its bias stated and checked against alternatives.
- [ ] Correlation is distinguished from causation, with incrementality or holdouts used for major decisions.
- [ ] Targets are based on baselines, benchmarks, and required business outcomes, not arbitrary numbers.
- [ ] Dashboards are designed around primary questions with context and action thresholds visible.
- [ ] Leading and lagging indicators are both tracked appropriately.
- [ ] The framework resists gaming, for example by tying incentives to outcomes rather than activity.
- [ ] The measurement plan is reviewed and revised as strategy and conditions change.
