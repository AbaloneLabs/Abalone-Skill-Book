# Community Health And Moderation

A community's health is not visible in its membership number; it is visible in the ratio of members who contribute, the sentiment of their interactions, and the sustainability of the people who hold it together. Health fails when vanity metrics mask declining participation, when moderation policy is reactive instead of preventive, when toxic behavior is tolerated because removing it feels costly, or when the small group of moderators and superusers carrying the community burns out and leaves. Governance is the part of community work that is invisible when it works and catastrophic when it fails: a healthy community can be destroyed in days by a governance vacuum, and rebuilding trust after a moderation failure is far harder than maintaining it.

Use this skill before defining health metrics, writing moderation policy, handling toxicity, or scaling governance. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating moderation as a cleanup task and governance as an afterthought, when both are the load-bearing structure of a durable community.

## Core Rules

### Measure Health With Participation, Not Headcount

Membership count hides decline. A community can grow in members while quietly dying in participation. Health metrics must capture actual engagement.

Track meaningful health metrics:

- DAU and MAU, and the DAU/MAU ratio that shows stickiness;
- contribution ratio, the percentage of members who post or reply in a period;
- response rate and response time, whether questions get answered;
- new-member activation, whether newcomers post within their first days;
- retention curves, whether members return over weeks and months;
- sentiment, the tone and emotional health of conversations.

A rising membership with a falling contribution ratio is a community in decline, not in growth. Read the metrics that reveal relationships, not the ones that flatter.

### Write Moderation Policy As Prevention, Not Punishment

Moderation policy sets expectations before conflict, so that enforcement is consistent rather than arbitrary. Reactive moderation produces inconsistent, contested decisions.

Build preventive policy:

- publish clear community guidelines that state expected behavior, not just prohibited behavior;
- define what happens at each level of violation, with predictable consequences;
- explain the appeal path so members trust the process is fair;
- make the policy visible and referenced, not buried;
- apply it consistently regardless of a member's status or popularity.

Consistency is the core of perceived fairness. A policy applied unevenly destroys trust faster than having no policy at all.

### Handle Toxicity Early And Visibly

Tolerated toxicity drives out the members a community most wants to keep. Handling it decisively, early, and visibly protects the culture.

Address toxicity effectively:

- act on the first signs, not after a pattern is entrenched;
- be transparent about enforcement so the community sees standards are real;
- distinguish between heated disagreement and harassment or abuse;
- protect targeted members and follow up with them;
- avoid the trap of platforming bad behavior under the guise of open debate.

The members who leave first when toxicity is tolerated are usually the high-value, high-empathy contributors. The members who remain are often those who tolerate or perpetuate the toxicity. This inverts the community's quality over time.

### Prevent Moderator And Superuser Burnout

Communities are sustained by a small number of people who carry disproportionate load. Burning them out is the most common silent cause of community collapse.

Protect the people who hold the community:

- allocate real paid hours or meaningful recognition to moderators;
- distribute load across a team rather than relying on one or two people;
- rotate responsibilities and build in breaks;
- provide private support spaces for moderators to decompress and coordinate;
- recognize emotional labor and provide escalation paths for hard cases.

Unpaid, unsupported moderators carrying a community eventually quit, often suddenly, and the community discovers too late how much depended on them.

### Scale Governance With The Community

As a community grows, governance that worked at small scale breaks. Plan the evolution deliberately.

Evolve governance:

- move from founder-led moderation to a moderator team as scale demands;
- introduce trusted member roles, such as veterans or community leads;
- create sub-spaces or chapters to keep governance local and intimate;
- document decisions and precedents so governance is consistent across moderators;
- periodically review guidelines as the community's needs and risks change.

Governance that does not scale with the community produces either chaos, when control is too loose, or resentment, when control is too tight and centralized.

### Monitor Sentiment As A Leading Indicator

Sentiment declines before participation declines. Tracking the emotional health of the community catches problems early.

Monitor sentiment:

- read conversations regularly, not just metrics dashboards;
- run periodic member surveys on satisfaction and belonging;
- watch for rising conflict, cynicism, or disengagement in tone;
- track how newcomers describe their first experiences;
- treat a sentiment drop as a signal to investigate causes, not an anomaly to ignore.

A community can look healthy on activity metrics while its members are becoming disillusioned. Sentiment is the early warning system.

## Common Traps

### Membership Count Masking Decline

Growing headcount while contribution ratio and sentiment fall hides a community that is hollowing out.

### Reactive, Inconsistent Moderation

Handling problems only when they erupt, and applying rules unevenly, destroys the perceived fairness that holds trust.

### Tolerated Toxicity

Allowing bad behavior to persist drives out the high-value, empathetic members first, inverting community quality over time.

### Moderator Burnout

Overloading a small group of unpaid or unsupported moderators leads to sudden departures and silent collapse.

### Governance That Does Not Scale

Keeping small-community governance as the community grows produces either chaos from loose control or resentment from tight centralization.

### Ignoring Sentiment

Treating activity metrics as sufficient while ignoring declining tone and belonging causes problems to be discovered only after members have left.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Health is measured with DAU/MAU, contribution ratio, response rate, activation, retention, and sentiment, not just membership.
- [ ] Moderation policy is preventive, published, consistent, and includes a fair appeal path.
- [ ] Toxic behavior is addressed early and visibly, with targeted members protected.
- [ ] Moderators and superusers have real hours, recognition, load distribution, and decompression support.
- [ ] Governance evolves with scale through trusted roles, sub-spaces, and documented precedents.
- [ ] Sentiment is monitored as a leading indicator through regular reading, surveys, and tone analysis.
- [ ] A declining contribution ratio is read as decline, not offset by rising membership.
- [ ] Guidelines are periodically reviewed as community needs and risks change.
