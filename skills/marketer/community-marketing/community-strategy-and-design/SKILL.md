# Community Strategy And Design

A community is not an audience, a user base, or a content following. An audience listens to you; a community talks to each other, and that peer-to-peer value is the entire point. Community strategy fails when a community is launched to serve the brand's need for engagement rather than the members' need for each other, when the platform is chosen by familiarity instead of by member behavior, or when success is measured in membership count rather than in the relationships and value the space actually creates. A community built without a clear purpose becomes a ghost town with a large number, or a support burden the brand never intended to carry.

Use this skill before deciding to build a community, choosing a platform, defining purpose and metrics, or distinguishing a community strategy from an audience strategy. The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a community because communities are valuable in the abstract, without confronting whether members have a genuine reason to gather and to return.

## Core Rules

### Decide Why A Community Is Worth Building

A community is a long-term, high-maintenance commitment. It must be built for a reason that justifies that cost, not because community is fashionable.

Clarify the strategic purpose:

- support: members help each other solve problems, reducing support load;
- feedback: members shape the product, giving the brand signal it cannot get elsewhere;
- advocacy: members champion the brand, expanding reach and trust;
- product-led growth: members derive value from each other's presence, deepening retention;
- belonging: members find identity and connection around a shared interest or identity.

State the primary purpose explicitly. A community trying to be all of these at once usually serves none well. The purpose determines platform, metrics, moderation, and staffing.

### Distinguish Community From Audience

This is the foundational distinction. Conflating the two produces strategies that build followers, not communities.

Audience:

- one-to-many, the brand broadcasts and members consume;
- value flows from brand to member;
- success is reach and consumption;
- the brand owns the relationship.

Community:

- many-to-many, members create value for each other;
- value flows between members, with the brand as host;
- success is member-to-member interaction and relationships;
- members own a share of the experience.

If removing the brand's content would leave nothing, it is an audience, not a community. A real community has member-generated value that survives without constant brand output.

### Choose Platform By Member Behavior, Not Familiarity

Platform choice shapes who joins, how they behave, and whether the community lives. Choosing a platform because the team knows it, rather than because members already gather there, is a common error.

Platform considerations:

- Discord: real-time, chat-first, strong for gaming, developer, and highly engaged niches; demanding to moderate;
- Slack: professional, channel-based, familiar to B2B and work audiences; can feel like work;
- forums and dedicated platforms: asynchronous, searchable, durable knowledge; slower but deep;
- social groups, such as Facebook or LinkedIn groups: low friction, ride existing habits; less control and weaker identity;
- in-product or owned community: deepest integration with the product; highest build cost.

Go where the members already are or where their behavior naturally fits. Forcing a real-time chat audience onto a forum, or a deep-knowledge audience onto ephemeral chat, fights member nature.

### Apply A Purpose Framework Like SPICE

A purpose model keeps strategy honest by forcing explicit choices about what the community is for. SPICE is one useful frame.

SPICE dimensions:

- sense of belonging: do members feel seen and connected?
- influence: can members shape the community and the product?
- participation: are members actively contributing, not just lurking?
- connection: are members forming relationships with each other?
- empowerment: do members gain capability or status through the community?

Use such a model to diagnose gaps. A community with high membership but low participation has a participation problem, not a growth problem. Name the real weakness rather than chasing vanity metrics.

### Define Success Metrics That Reflect Community Value

Membership count is the most misleading community metric. It measures sign-ups, not value.

Define metrics by purpose:

- active engagement: daily and monthly active members, contribution ratio;
- interaction depth: posts, replies, threads, and member-to-member responses;
- retention: whether members return over weeks and months;
- sentiment: the tone and health of conversations;
- outcome metrics tied to purpose: support deflection, product feedback shipped, advocacy generated, retention lift.

A community of ten thousand silent members is weaker than a community of five hundred who help each other weekly. Measure the relationships and value created, not the door count.

### Commit To Staffing And Moderation From Day One

A community is not self-running at launch. It requires people, time, and policy from the start.

Plan for:

- a community manager or team with real hours allocated;
- moderation coverage appropriate to the platform and size;
- community guidelines published before launch;
- a path for escalating conflict, abuse, and safety issues;
- ongoing content seeding and member recognition.

Communities launched without staffing become abandoned spaces or, worse, unsafe ones. Budget the human cost before the launch cost.

## Common Traps

### Community Built For The Brand, Not Members

Launching a community to serve the brand's engagement needs, when members have no reason to gather with each other, produces a ghost town.

### Conflating Community With Audience

Treating followers or subscribers as a community, when value only flows from brand to member, means the peer-to-peer value never develops.

### Platform Chosen By Team Familiarity

Picking Discord or Slack because the team knows it, rather than where members already gather, fights member behavior and suppresses participation.

### Membership Count As The Success Metric

Counting sign-ups measures the door, not the room. Silent members create no community value regardless of number.

### No Staffing Or Moderation Plan

Launching without allocated community management and moderation creates abandoned or unsafe spaces that damage the brand.

### Purpose Spread Too Thin

Trying to make the community serve support, feedback, advocacy, and growth simultaneously usually serves none well and confuses members.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The community has a single primary strategic purpose that justifies the long-term commitment.
- [ ] The design builds member-to-member value, distinguishing it from a one-to-many audience strategy.
- [ ] Platform choice is driven by where members already gather and how they behave, not team familiarity.
- [ ] A purpose framework such as SPICE is used to diagnose participation, belonging, and influence gaps.
- [ ] Success metrics measure active engagement, contribution ratio, retention, and sentiment, not just membership count.
- [ ] Staffing, moderation, guidelines, and escalation paths are planned and budgeted before launch.
- [ ] The community would still create value for members if the brand stopped posting content.
- [ ] Outcome metrics tie back to the stated purpose, such as support deflection or retention lift.
