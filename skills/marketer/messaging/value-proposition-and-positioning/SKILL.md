# Value Proposition And Positioning

Positioning decides what the offer means in the customer's mind and why it is the right choice among alternatives. A value proposition explains the specific value a customer should expect. Messaging fails when it describes features, slogans, or internal aspirations without connecting to customer context, competing alternatives, proof, and believable outcomes.

Use this skill before writing landing pages, ads, campaign copy, sales narratives, product pages, launch messaging, category explanations, or brand claims. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing polished language that is vague, undifferentiated, unprovable, or misaligned with the audience.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Customer's Situation

Messaging should begin from the customer's world, not the company's internal roadmap.

Clarify:

- who the message is for;
- what situation triggers interest;
- what problem, desire, risk, or job matters;
- what they currently use or believe;
- what language they use;
- what outcome they want;
- what would make them skeptical.

If the customer does not recognize the situation, the message will feel generic or irrelevant.

### Name The Competitive Alternative

Positioning only has meaning relative to alternatives. The alternative may be a competitor, spreadsheet, manual service, existing habit, internal team, cheaper product, premium product, or doing nothing.

Ask:

- What would the customer do without this offer?
- Why is that alternative insufficient?
- Where is it stronger?
- What switching cost exists?
- What comparison frame should the marketer accept or change?

Do not say "we have no competitors". That usually means the real competitor is inertia or an existing workaround.

### Make The Promise Specific

A value proposition should say what changes for the customer. Avoid empty language like "better", "simple", "powerful", "innovative", or "next-generation" unless supported by specific meaning.

Specific promises may involve:

- saving time;
- reducing risk;
- increasing revenue;
- improving accuracy;
- making collaboration easier;
- lowering cost;
- increasing confidence;
- reducing manual work;
- improving access;
- creating a better experience.

The promise should be plausible and concrete enough for the customer to evaluate.

### Connect Features To Benefits And Proof

Features are what the product or service has. Benefits are why the customer cares. Proof is why the customer should believe the claim.

For each major message, connect:

- feature or capability;
- customer benefit;
- evidence or proof;
- limitation or condition where relevant.

Proof can include demos, customer evidence, data, certifications, case studies, expert review, product behavior, guarantees, or transparent explanation. Do not make claims stronger than the proof supports.

### Choose The Positioning Level

Messaging can position the offer at several levels:

- category: what kind of thing it is;
- use case: when to use it;
- audience: who it is for;
- outcome: what it helps achieve;
- alternative: what it replaces;
- philosophy: why its approach is different.

Choose the level that reduces confusion and supports buying. A new category may feel exciting but can increase explanation burden. A familiar category can help comprehension but invite direct comparison.

### Handle Objections Honestly

Good messaging anticipates skepticism. It should not hide every tradeoff.

Common objections:

- too expensive;
- too risky;
- too hard to switch;
- not trusted;
- not for my industry;
- too complex;
- too limited;
- already using something else;
- unclear return on investment.

Address important objections with proof, explanation, scope clarity, or a lower-friction next step. Avoid pretending objections do not exist.

### Maintain Message Consistency Across Touchpoints

Customers may see an ad, landing page, email, sales deck, pricing page, onboarding flow, and support article. The core promise should stay consistent even if expression changes by channel.

Check that the message does not shift from "save time" in ads to "enterprise control" on the page to "lowest price" in sales unless those messages are deliberately segmented.

## Common Traps

### Feature Lists Without Customer Meaning

Listing capabilities may impress insiders but fail to explain why a customer should care.

### Vague Superlatives

"Best", "fastest", "easiest", "trusted", and "AI-powered" are weak unless defined and supported.

### Copying Competitor Language

Using the same category phrases as competitors can make the brand invisible. Use customer language, but clarify the distinct angle.

### Overclaiming Results

Promising outcomes that depend on customer effort, context, or uncertain conditions can mislead and create support problems.

### Ignoring The Buying Committee

In B2B or considered purchases, users, buyers, security, finance, legal, and executives may each need different proof.

### Changing Message Every Campaign

Testing variations is useful. Constantly changing the core story prevents learning and brand memory.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The message starts from a specific customer situation, problem, desire, or job.
- [ ] The competitive alternative or current workaround is named.
- [ ] The value promise is concrete and customer-observable.
- [ ] Features are connected to benefits and proof.
- [ ] Claims are not stronger than the available evidence.
- [ ] The positioning level is deliberate: category, use case, audience, outcome, alternative, or philosophy.
- [ ] Major objections are addressed honestly.
- [ ] The message distinguishes the offer without relying on vague superlatives.
- [ ] Touchpoints preserve a consistent core promise while adapting to channel and segment.
- [ ] The copy avoids polished but empty language that customers cannot evaluate.
