# Guerrilla And Stunt Strategy

Guerrilla marketing trades money for an idea powerful enough to earn attention. That trade is also its central risk: when the idea is not strong enough to carry the budget, there is no media spend to compensate, and the campaign simply does not exist in the audience's mind. Guerrilla and stunt strategy fails when the format is chosen because it is cheap rather than because the idea is genuinely shareable, when success is assumed to depend on virality that cannot be engineered, when earned media is treated as guaranteed rather than as a fragile dependency, or when a stunt grabs attention in a way that is off-brand, offensive, or legally reckless. A stunt that goes wrong does not just fail; it becomes the story.

Use this skill before planning a guerrilla campaign or stunt, deciding whether the approach fits, assessing virality and earned-media risk, or evaluating brand alignment. The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending a stunt because stunts can be cheap and exciting, without confronting that the entire model collapses if the idea is not exceptional.

## Core Rules

### Decide Whether Guerrilla Actually Fits

Guerrilla is not a universal tactic; it fits specific situations and fails in others. Choosing it for the wrong reasons wastes the opportunity and the budget.

Guerrilla fits when:

- the budget is low relative to the attention goal, so the idea must do the heavy lifting;
- the concept is highly shareable, surprising, or emotionally resonant;
- there is strong local or niche relevance that makes the stunt feel earned, not random;
- the brand can tolerate unpredictability and the absence of guaranteed reach;
- the idea has a clear angle that media or audiences would want to talk about.

Guerrilla does not fit when guaranteed reach is required, when the brand cannot afford reputational risk, when the idea is merely clever but not shareable, or when a conventional campaign would achieve the goal more reliably. Be honest about which situation you are in.

### Make The Idea Carry The Budget

In guerrilla, the concept is the media spend. If the idea is not exceptional, there is nothing to amplify, and the budget is wasted regardless of how small it was.

Pressure-test the idea:

- could the idea be described in one sentence that makes a stranger want to see it?
- is there a genuine element of surprise, delight, provocation, or relevance?
- would someone share it voluntarily, and why?
- is it tied to a real insight about the audience or the moment?
- does it have a visual or narrative hook that travels in a photo, clip, or headline?

If the idea needs a long explanation to seem interesting, it is not strong enough to carry a guerrilla budget. Keep refining until the concept is self-evidently worth attention.

### Treat Earned Media As A Dependency, Not A Guarantee

Guerrilla often depends on earned media, the press, social shares, and word of mouth, to deliver reach. That dependency is fragile and must be planned for, not assumed.

Plan for earned media realistically:

- identify the specific outlets, creators, and communities likely to pick it up;
- give the stunt a clear, shareable visual or narrative angle that travels;
- build relationships and seeding before the stunt, not cold pitches after;
- have a distribution plan to capture and amplify the moment digitally;
- prepare for the likely outcome that earned media underdelivers, and decide what paid amplification can rescue.

A stunt with no seeding, no relationships, and no amplification plan is relying on luck. Earned media rewards preparation as much as it rewards a great idea.

### Assess Virality Risk Honestly

Virality is the dream and the danger. The same qualities that make a stunt spread can also make it spread for the wrong reasons.

Evaluate virality risk:

- could the stunt be misinterpreted, mocked, or seen as offensive?
- does it touch sensitive topics, locations, or communities where backlash is likely?
- is there a version of the story where the brand is the villain?
- what is the worst plausible interpretation, and could the brand survive it?
- does the stunt respect the public space and the people in it?

Virality is not controllable. A stunt designed to be provocative must be stress-tested against its worst-case interpretation, because that interpretation is the one most likely to spread.

### Ensure Brand Alignment And Relevance

A stunt that earns attention but does not connect to the brand is a wasted spectacle. Audiences remember the stunt and forget who did it.

Check alignment:

- does the stunt connect logically to the brand, product, or message?
- is the brand's role integral, or could any brand have done it?
- does the tone match the brand's identity and values?
- is there local or cultural relevance that makes it feel authentic?
- will the association be clear in the photos, clips, and headlines that travel?

If the stunt works as entertainment but not as brand communication, it has failed its purpose. The brand must be inseparable from the idea.

### Define Success Beyond Going Viral

"Going viral" is not a strategy or a metric. Define what the stunt is actually accountable for.

Define success:

- the primary outcome, such as awareness, sign-ups, sales, or press coverage;
- realistic reach and engagement targets, not hopes;
- how the stunt connects to a funnel, landing page, or follow-up;
- the measurement approach for earned and shared media;
- the threshold for success and the threshold for cutting losses.

A stunt without defined success criteria is judged by feelings afterward, which leads to either false victory claims or unjustified abandonment of a promising approach.

## Common Traps

### Choosing Guerrilla Because It Is Cheap

Selecting guerrilla for cost alone, when the idea is not exceptional, guarantees a campaign that simply does not get noticed.

### Assuming Virality Can Be Engineered

Treating virality as a predictable outcome, rather than a fragile and uncontrollable result, sets up campaigns for disappointment and risk.

### Earned Media Treated As Guaranteed

Relying on press and shares without seeding, relationships, or amplification leaves reach entirely to luck.

### Attention Without Brand Connection

A stunt that entertains but does not clearly connect to the brand produces spectacle that audiences remember without remembering who did it.

### Ignoring Backlash Risk

Provocative stunts that are not stress-tested against their worst interpretation often spread for the wrong reasons and damage the brand.

### Success Defined As Going Viral

Treating virality as the goal, rather than defining real outcomes, makes the campaign unjudgeable and unimprovable.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Guerrilla was chosen because the idea and situation fit, not merely because the budget is low.
- [ ] The idea has been pressure-tested to confirm it can carry the budget without paid media compensation.
- [ ] Earned media is planned with specific outlets, seeding, relationships, and a digital amplification path.
- [ ] Virality risk has been stress-tested against the worst plausible interpretation and backlash scenarios.
- [ ] The stunt connects logically and inseparably to the brand, product, or message.
- [ ] Success is defined by real outcomes and measurement, not by the hope of going viral.
- [ ] The stunt respects the public space, local relevance, and the people it involves.
- [ ] A paid amplification backup exists in case earned media underdelivers.
