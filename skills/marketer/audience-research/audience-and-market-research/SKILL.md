# Audience And Market Research

Research is how marketing avoids guessing about the people it serves. It fails when it asks leading questions, when it samples only the customers who already love the brand, when it confuses what people say with what they do, when it reports findings without synthesizing insight, or when it treats one method as sufficient. Bad research is worse than no research because it lends false confidence to wrong decisions.

Use this skill before designing research, conducting interviews or surveys, sizing a market, or synthesizing findings. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing research that looks rigorous but produces misleading or unactionable conclusions.

## Core Rules

### Start From A Specific Decision The Research Must Inform

Research without a decision to inform becomes an expensive fishing trip. Define the question first.

Clarify:

- the specific decision the research will inform;
- what you need to know to make that decision;
- what would change the decision if you learned it;
- the level of confidence required;
- the deadline by which the decision must be made.

Research scoped to a decision produces actionable insight. Research scoped to "understand our customers" produces a report no one uses.

### Triangulate Methods, Do Not Rely On One

Each research method has blind spots. Combine methods to see the whole picture.

Combine:

- qualitative interviews for depth, motivation, and language;
- surveys for breadth, quantification, and segmentation;
- behavioral data for what people actually do, not what they say;
- sales and support conversations for real objections and questions;
- competitive and market data for context.

People routinely misreport their own behavior in surveys. Behavioral data reveals what they actually do. Interviews explain why. Use all three.

### Avoid Leading And Biased Questions

How you ask determines what you hear. Design questions that do not predetermine the answer.

Ensure:

- neutral wording that does not imply the desired answer;
- no double-barreled questions asking two things at once;
- no jargon or assumptions the respondent may not share;
- balanced scales and answer options;
- open questions before prompted ones.

A question like "How much do you love our product?" manufactures a positive answer. A neutral question like "How would you describe your experience?" earns a real one.

### Sample Beyond The Already-Convinced

Researching only happy customers or engaged users produces a flattering but false picture.

Sample:

- prospects who did not buy, not just customers;
- churned or at-risk customers, not just retained ones;
- the broader market, not just your existing audience;
- diverse segments, not the loudest or most accessible;
- non-users who represent the opportunity.

Listening only to people who already chose you hides why others did not, which is usually the more valuable insight.

### Separate What People Say From What They Do

Stated preference and revealed preference often differ. Treat both with appropriate skepticism.

Interpret carefully:

- survey and interview claims as intentions, not predictions;
- behavioral and usage data as evidence of actual choice;
- the gap between the two as itself an insight;
- purchase history as stronger evidence than stated future intent;
- context that explains why stated and revealed differ.

People say they will pay more for quality and then buy on price. Believe behavior over assertion when they conflict.

### Synthesize Findings Into Insight, Not Raw Data

A research report full of charts but no insight is not useful. Synthesize into what it means and what to do.

Synthesize:

- the key insights that matter for the decision;
- the patterns across methods and segments;
- the surprises that challenge assumptions;
- the implications for strategy and messaging;
- the open questions that remain.

Data describes; insight explains and directs. A good synthesis turns research into a clear point of view and recommended action.

### Acknowledge Limits And Uncertainty

Research has limits. Be honest about what it can and cannot support.

Acknowledge:

- sample size and composition limits;
- the difference between correlation and causation;
- how the method shapes the findings;
- how recent and how generalizable the data is;
- what remains unknown.

Overstating research certainty misleads decisions. Stating limits honestly lets decision-makers weigh the evidence appropriately.

## Common Traps

### Research Without A Decision

Generic "customer understanding" research produces reports that inform nothing.

### Leading Questions

Biased question wording manufactures the answer the researcher wanted to hear.

### Sampling The Already-Convinced

Researching only happy customers hides why others chose competitors or nothing.

### Believing Stated Over Revealed Behavior

Treating survey claims as predictions ignores that people often do not do what they say.

### Single-Method Confidence

Relying on only surveys or only interviews misses what other methods would reveal.

### Data Without Synthesis

A report of raw findings without insight and implication is not actionable.

### Overstating Certainty

Presenting limited research as definitive misleads decisions that deserve appropriate uncertainty.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The research is scoped to a specific decision and what would change it.
- [ ] Multiple methods are triangulated, including qualitative, quantitative, and behavioral.
- [ ] Questions are neutral, non-leading, and free of jargon and double-barreled structure.
- [ ] Sampling includes non-customers, churned users, and the broader market, not only the convinced.
- [ ] Stated preference is interpreted alongside revealed behavior, with conflicts treated as insight.
- [ ] Findings are synthesized into key insights, patterns, and implications, not just raw data.
- [ ] The synthesis addresses surprises that challenge existing assumptions.
- [ ] Limits of sample size, composition, recency, and generalizability are stated honestly.
- [ ] Correlation is not presented as causation.
- [ ] The research concludes with a clear point of view and recommended action for the decision.
