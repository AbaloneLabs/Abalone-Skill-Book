# Competitive Intelligence And Market Sizing

Strategy requires an honest understanding of the market, how big it is, how fast it grows, and who competes in it. Market sizing estimates the opportunity; competitive intelligence maps the players and their strategies. Both are easy to do badly: sizing inflates the opportunity through optimistic assumptions, and competitive intelligence devolves into feature lists that miss strategy. The judgment problem is that these analyses inform investment, entry, and positioning decisions worth far more than the analysis cost, and their errors compound into strategic mistakes. The most common failure is sizing the market to justify a desired conclusion rather than to discover the truth, or tracking competitors' surface features while missing their underlying strategy and momentum. The skill is sizing markets with grounded assumptions and reading competitors strategically rather than superficially.

Use this skill before sizing a market, before entering a new market, before conducting competitive analysis, or before making investment decisions based on market opportunity. The goal is to prevent the agent from inflating market size through optimism, from confusing feature tracking with strategic intelligence, or from presenting estimates as facts.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Distinguish TAM, SAM, And SOM Honestly

Total addressable market (TAM) is the entire opportunity; serviceable addressable market (SAM) is the portion the business can reach; serviceable obtainable market (SOM) is the realistic share captureable. Conflating these, especially presenting TAM as the opportunity, inflates expectations. Each must be defined and defended separately.

- [ ] Define TAM, SAM, and SOM separately and clearly.
- [ ] Base each on grounded assumptions about reach, fit, and capture.
- [ ] Avoid presenting TAM as the realistic opportunity.
- [ ] Show the logic from TAM to SOM transparently.

### [ ] Ground Sizing In Bottom-Up And Top-Down Estimates

Bottom-up sizing builds from units, prices, and customers; top-down sizing starts from industry totals and narrows. Each has weaknesses; triangulating both reveals a more credible estimate than either alone. A single method hides its assumptions.

- [ ] Build a bottom-up estimate from customer counts and value.
- [ ] Cross-check with a top-down estimate from industry data.
- [ ] Reconcile discrepancies between the two methods.
- [ ] Present the range and assumptions, not a single precise number.

### [ ] Use Credible, Sourced Data

Market sizing is only as credible as its inputs. Industry reports, government data, primary research, and disclosed financials vary in reliability. Citing weak or unsourced data undermines the analysis. Source and scrutinize every input.

- [ ] Use primary sources where possible.
- [ ] Scrutinize the methodology behind third-party reports.
- [ ] Cite sources for every key assumption.
- [ ] Flag estimates that rest on weak data.

### [ ] Model Growth And Trajectory, Not Just Size

A market's current size matters less than its trajectory. A large but shrinking market is a trap; a small but fast-growing market is an opportunity. Sizing must include growth rate, drivers, and stage, not just a static snapshot.

- [ ] Estimate growth rate and project the market over the relevant horizon.
- [ ] Identify the drivers of growth and the risks to it.
- [ ] Assess the market's stage: emerging, growing, mature, declining.
- [ ] Weight trajectory alongside current size in decisions.

### [ ] Analyze Competitors Strategically, Not Just Feature Lists

Competitive intelligence that catalogs features misses strategy. The goal is to understand each competitor's strategy, positioning, strengths, weaknesses, and momentum, enough to predict their moves and find gaps. Feature lists are data; strategic analysis is intelligence.

- [ ] Analyze each competitor's positioning, target, and differentiation.
- [ ] Assess their strengths, weaknesses, and likely strategic moves.
- [ ] Look at their momentum: growth, funding, product velocity, customer sentiment.
- [ ] Identify gaps and opportunities the competitive map reveals.

### [ ] Include Indirect And Emerging Competitors

The most dangerous competitors are often not the direct look-alikes but the indirect alternatives and emerging entrants. A spreadsheet replacing a SaaS tool, a startup below the radar, or an adjacent player expanding in are real threats. The competitive set must be broad.

- [ ] Include indirect alternatives and "do nothing" options.
- [ ] Watch for emerging entrants and adjacent players.
- [ ] Monitor for disruption from outside the traditional set.
- [ ] Revisit the competitive set regularly; it shifts faster than analyses.

### [ ] Use Primary And Open-Source Intelligence

Published reports are starting points, but the richest intelligence comes from primary sources: customer interviews, win-loss analysis, competitor product trials, job postings, and public disclosures. Combining open-source intelligence with primary research produces a fuller picture than reports alone.

- [ ] Conduct win-loss analysis to learn how competitors win and lose.
- [ ] Trial competitor products and analyze their experience.
- [ ] Monitor competitor hiring, funding, and public communications.
- [ ] Interview customers about their evaluation of alternatives.

### [ ] Assess Competitive Moats And Sustainability

Understanding how defensible each competitor's position is, their moats, predicts how the market will evolve. A competitor with a strong network effect or switching cost is harder to displace than one competing on features. Moat analysis informs where to attack and where to avoid.

- [ ] Identify each major competitor's moat: network, data, brand, switching cost.
- [ ] Assess how sustainable each moat is.
- [ ] Find competitors whose position is weak or eroding.
- [ ] Avoid frontal attacks on strongly moated positions.

### [ ] Connect Intelligence To Strategic Decisions

Competitive intelligence and market sizing earn their value by informing decisions: where to enter, how to position, what to build, whom to acquire. Intelligence that does not reach decision-makers or change decisions is wasted. Connect analysis explicitly to the decisions at hand.

- [ ] Frame intelligence around the strategic decisions it informs.
- [ ] Deliver intelligence to decision-makers in time to act.
- [ ] Translate analysis into implications and options.
- [ ] Track whether intelligence changed decisions and outcomes.

### [ ] Maintain Intelligence As A Living Discipline

Markets and competitors change continuously. A one-time analysis goes stale quickly. Competitive intelligence and market sizing must be maintained as living disciplines, updated regularly and refreshed as conditions change.

- [ ] Update market sizing on a regular cadence and when conditions shift.
- [ ] Monitor competitors continuously, not just at planning time.
- [ ] Refresh intelligence as new data and entrants emerge.
- [ ] Treat intelligence as ongoing, not project-based.

## Common Traps

### [ ] TAM As The Opportunity

Presenting total addressable market as the realistic captureable opportunity.

### [ ] Optimistic Sizing

Inflating market size through hopeful assumptions to justify a desired conclusion.

### [ ] Single-Method Sizing

Relying on one method whose assumptions remain hidden and untested.

### [ ] Feature Tracking As Intelligence

Cataloging competitor features without understanding their strategy.

### [ ] Narrow Competitive Set

Missing indirect, emerging, and adjacent competitors that pose real threats.

### [ ] Reports Without Primary Research

Relying on published reports without primary and open-source intelligence.

### [ ] Ignoring Moats

Attacking strongly defended positions or missing weakly defended opportunities.

### [ ] One-Time Analysis

Letting intelligence go stale by treating it as a project rather than a discipline.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Are TAM, SAM, and SOM defined and defended separately, without conflating them?
- [ ] Is sizing triangulated between bottom-up and top-down methods with transparent assumptions?
- [ ] Is the analysis grounded in credible, sourced, and scrutinized data?
- [ ] Does sizing model growth, drivers, and market stage, not just current size?
- [ ] Does competitive analysis address strategy and momentum, not just features?
- [ ] Does the competitive set include indirect, emerging, and adjacent competitors?
- [ ] Does intelligence combine primary and open-source research, not just published reports?
- [ ] Are competitor moats assessed for sustainability and attackability?
- [ ] Is intelligence connected explicitly to the strategic decisions it informs?
- [ ] Are market sizing and competitive intelligence maintained as living, updated disciplines?
