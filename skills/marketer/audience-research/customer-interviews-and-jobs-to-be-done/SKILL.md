# Customer Interviews And Jobs To Be Done

Customer interviews are the richest source of real insight, but they are also the easiest to corrupt. They fail when they ask customers to explain their own motivations, which people are bad at, when they lead the witness toward the answer the interviewer wants, when they interview only recent or happy customers, or when they report quotes as insight without understanding the underlying need. Jobs-to-be-done framing helps because it focuses on the progress a person is trying to make, not their demographic or their stated preference.

Use this skill before planning interviews, writing guides, conducting conversations, or synthesizing qualitative findings. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing interviews that confirm what the team already believed.

## Core Rules

### Focus On Specific Past Behavior, Not Hypothetical Future

People cannot reliably predict what they will do, but they can describe what they did. Anchor interviews in real events.

Ask about:

- the last time they faced this situation or problem;
- the specific steps they took and why;
- what they considered and rejected;
- what almost stopped them;
- what happened after.

Hypothetical questions like "would you use this feature?" produce unreliable answers. Concrete recollections of real decisions produce real insight.

### Ask About The Job, Not The Person

Jobs-to-be-done frames the customer as someone trying to make progress in a situation. Focus on the job, not the demographic.

Uncover:

- the progress the person is trying to make;
- the situation that created the need;
- the outcome they hoped for;
- the forces that held them back, habit, anxiety, or cost;
- the push that made them seek something new.

A job is stable across demographics and solutions. Understanding the job explains why people hire a product, including yours or a competitor's.

### Map The Forces Of Progress

When someone switches to or from a solution, specific forces are at work. Surface them in the interview.

Explore:

- the push of their current situation that created dissatisfaction;
- the pull of the new solution's promise;
- the anxiety or uncertainty about the new;
- the habit or allegiance to the current way.

These four forces explain switching. A new solution wins when push and pull overcome anxiety and habit. Ignoring these forces misses why adoption stalls.

### Avoid Leading And Self-Justifying Questions

The interviewer's hypotheses can corrupt the interview. Ask neutral, open questions that let the customer surprise you.

Avoid:

- questions that imply the desired answer;
- asking customers to explain their own psychology;
- pitching your solution and then asking if they like it;
- yes-or-no questions where a story is needed;
- reacting in ways that reward agreeable answers.

Ask "tell me about the last time you..." and then listen. The goal is to learn what you did not know, not to confirm what you already believed.

### Interview Across The Full Switching Spectrum

Insight comes from the edges, not the average. Interview people at different points of the journey.

Include:

- recent buyers, while memory is fresh;
- people who considered you and chose a competitor;
- people who chose the status quo or a workaround;
- people who churned or stopped using the product;
- people early in considering the problem.

Happy recent customers tell a flattering story. Those who chose differently or left tell the story that reveals the real forces.

### Probe For The Underlying Need, Not The Stated Want

Customers often describe solutions, not needs. Probe beneath the surface request.

Probe by:

- asking why behind each stated reason;
- exploring what they were trying to accomplish, not what they asked for;
- distinguishing the functional, emotional, and social dimensions of the job;
- noticing what they do not say or avoid;
- looking for workarounds that reveal unmet need.

A customer asking for a faster horse may need faster transportation. The stated want is a clue to the underlying job, not the job itself.

### Synthesize Patterns Into Testable Insight

Individual interviews are anecdotes. Synthesize across interviews to find patterns worth acting on.

Synthesize:

- recurring jobs, forces, and barriers across interviews;
- segments that share a situation and job;
- language and framing the audience actually uses;
- surprises that challenge internal assumptions;
- hypotheses worth testing quantitatively.

Report patterns and the confidence behind them, not cherry-picked quotes that support a predetermined conclusion.

## Common Traps

### Asking Customers To Explain Themselves

People are poor at explaining their own motivations; asking them to produces confident wrong answers.

### Hypothetical Questions

Future-tense questions about what people would do produce unreliable, optimistic answers.

### Leading The Witness

Questions that imply the desired answer manufacture the conclusion the interviewer wanted.

### Interviewing Only Happy Customers

Sampling only the convinced hides the forces that drive others to competitors or inertia.

### Reporting Quotes As Insight

Cherry-picked quotes that confirm assumptions are not synthesis; patterns across interviews are.

### Pitching Then Asking

Presenting your solution and asking for reaction produces polite, useless feedback.

### Confusing Wants With Jobs

Taking the stated solution request literally misses the underlying progress the customer seeks.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Questions anchor in specific past behavior and real decisions, not hypothetical futures.
- [ ] The interview frames the customer around the job or progress they are trying to make.
- [ ] The four forces of push, pull, anxiety, and habit are explored for switching decisions.
- [ ] Questions are neutral and open, designed to let the customer surprise the interviewer.
- [ ] The interviewer avoids pitching the solution or asking customers to justify their psychology.
- [ ] Interviewees span the full spectrum, including non-buyers, churned users, and status-quo choosers.
- [ ] Probing reaches the underlying need beneath the stated want or solution request.
- [ ] Synthesis identifies recurring patterns, segments, and language across interviews.
- [ ] Surprises that challenge internal assumptions are surfaced and taken seriously.
- [ ] Findings are reported as patterns with stated confidence, not cherry-picked quotes.
