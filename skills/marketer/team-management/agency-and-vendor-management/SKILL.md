# Agency And Vendor Management

Agencies and vendors extend marketing's capacity, but they extend its problems too. Relationships fail when the scope is vague, when expectations are not aligned, when the agency is managed by neglect or by micromanagement, when performance is not measured against clear outcomes, or when the relationship serves the vendor's revenue more than the client's results. Good vendor management is a partnership with clear briefs, shared goals, and honest accountability.

Use this skill before selecting an agency, writing a brief or scope, setting KPIs, or reviewing a vendor relationship. The goal is to prevent the agent from spending on vendors without clear accountability for results.

## Core Rules

### Select For Fit And Evidence, Not Pitch Polish

Agencies pitch well; that does not mean they deliver. Select on fit and evidence.

Select by:

- relevant experience and proven results, not just credentials;
- cultural and working-style fit;
- the actual team that will do the work, not just the pitch team;
- references from clients with similar needs;
- transparency about capabilities and limits.

The pitch team rarely does the work. Evaluate who will actually serve the account and whether they have delivered for similar clients.

### Write Briefs And Scopes That Define Outcome

Vague scopes produce vague results. Define the outcome and the work clearly.

Define:

- the business outcome the engagement must drive;
- the specific deliverables and scope of work;
- the success metrics and how they are measured;
- the timeline, milestones, and review points;
- the inputs and access the agency needs from you.

A brief that says "improve our marketing" invites drift. A brief that defines the outcome, scope, and metrics enables accountability.

### Align On Goals, Not Just Activity

Agencies can stay busy without delivering results. Align on goals.

Align on:

- the business or marketing outcome, not just outputs;
- the metrics that define success;
- the leading indicators of progress;
- the boundaries of the agency's responsibility;
- how performance will be evaluated.

An agency measured on activity will produce activity. An agency measured on outcome will work toward results.

### Manage The Relationship Actively

Neglect and micromanagement both fail. Manage with engagement and trust.

Manage by:

- a regular cadence of communication and review;
- clear feedback, both positive and corrective;
- shared visibility into performance and work;
- respect for the agency's expertise;
- timely decisions and responses to unblock work.

An agency left alone drifts toward its own priorities. An agency micromanaged cannot apply its expertise. Active, respectful management gets the best of the partnership.

### Define KPIs And SLAs That Drive Accountability

Without clear metrics, performance is subjective. Define and track them.

Define:

- the outcome KPIs tied to the engagement goal;
- the operational SLAs such as turnaround and quality;
- the reporting cadence and format;
- the consequences of missing and exceeding targets;
- the data sources and definitions behind the metrics.

Subjective evaluation invites dispute. Clear KPIs and SLAs create shared accountability and honest performance conversation.

### Evaluate Performance Honestly And Regularly

Relationships degrade without honest evaluation. Evaluate on a cadence.

Evaluate:

- performance against the agreed KPIs and SLAs;
- the quality and strategic value of the work;
- the working relationship and communication;
- the ROI of the engagement versus alternatives;
- whether to continue, expand, restructure, or end.

Agencies rarely self-correct without feedback. Regular, honest evaluation keeps the relationship productive or surfaces the need to change.

### Protect Strategic Judgment In-House

Agencies execute and advise; the strategic judgment should stay in-house.

Keep in-house:

- the core strategy and prioritization;
- the brand and positioning decisions;
- the budget allocation decisions;
- the interpretation of what results mean;
- the accountability for business outcomes.

Outsourcing execution leverages expertise; outsourcing strategic judgment abdicates the core of marketing. Keep the judgment that matters in-house.

## Common Traps

### Pitch Team Over Working Team

Selecting on the pitch when the day-to-day team differs leads to disappointment.

### Vague Scope And Brief

Undefined outcome and scope produce drift and unaccountable work.

### Activity Over Outcome

Measuring the agency on activity rather than results keeps them busy without delivering.

### Neglect Or Micromanagement

Either extreme fails; the relationship needs active, respectful engagement.

### No Clear KPIs

Subjective performance evaluation invites dispute and lets underperformance hide.

### No Honest Evaluation

Relationships without regular, candid evaluation degrade or persist past their value.

### Outsourcing Strategy

Delegating strategic judgment to the vendor removes core capability and accountability.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Selection is based on relevant experience, the working team, and references, not pitch polish.
- [ ] The brief and scope define the business outcome, deliverables, metrics, and timeline.
- [ ] Goals are aligned on outcomes, with the agency accountable for results, not just activity.
- [ ] The relationship is managed actively with regular cadence, feedback, and shared visibility.
- [ ] KPIs and SLAs are defined, tracked, and tied to accountability.
- [ ] Performance is evaluated honestly and regularly against agreed metrics.
- [ ] Core strategic judgment and budget decisions remain in-house.
- [ ] The ROI of the engagement is assessed against alternatives.
- [ ] The working relationship and communication are reviewed, not just the output.
- [ ] The decision to continue, expand, restructure, or end is made deliberately, not by inertia.
