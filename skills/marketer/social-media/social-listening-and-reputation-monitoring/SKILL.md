# Social Listening And Reputation Monitoring

Conversations about a brand happen whether the brand participates or not, across reviews, forums, social platforms, and media. Social listening is the practice of systematically monitoring those conversations to understand sentiment, catch issues early, find opportunities, and inform strategy. Reputation monitoring is the early warning system that detects crises before they escalate and tracks the health of the brand over time. The judgment problem is that the volume of signal is overwhelming, most of it noise, and distinguishing meaningful shifts from chatter requires deliberate setup and interpretation. The most common failure is either ignoring listening entirely, leaving the brand blind to crises, or drowning in dashboards that report vanity metrics without insight. The skill is building a listening system that surfaces actionable signal and governing when and how the brand responds.

Use this skill before setting up social listening, before designing reputation monitoring, before responding to a crisis or negative conversation, or when the brand is surprised by sentiment it did not see coming. The goal is to prevent the agent from being blind to conversation, from drowning in noise, or from responding to online discussion in ways that escalate rather than resolve.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Listen Across The Full Conversation Landscape

Brand conversation happens far beyond the brand's own channels: review sites, forums, Reddit, news, blogs, video comments, and competitor mentions. Listening only on owned channels misses most of the conversation. The system must cover the full landscape where the audience talks.

- [ ] Map the platforms and spaces where the audience discusses the brand and category.
- [ ] Monitor review sites, forums, and news, not just owned social channels.
- [ ] Include indirect mentions, misspellings, and category terms.
- [ ] Watch competitor mentions for comparative context.

### [ ] Distinguish Signal From Noise In Volume

Listening tools produce overwhelming volume, most of it irrelevant. Without filtering and prioritization, the team drowns in mentions and misses what matters. Signal is defined by relevance, sentiment shift, influence, and anomaly, not by raw volume.

- [ ] Define what counts as actionable signal for the brand.
- [ ] Filter out spam, bots, and irrelevant mentions.
- [ ] Prioritize by influence, sentiment, and anomaly, not just volume.
- [ ] Use alerts for meaningful shifts rather than monitoring every mention.

### [ ] Track Sentiment And Its Change Over Time

A single sentiment score is meaningless; what matters is the trend. Is sentiment improving or declining? Did a specific event cause a shift? Tracking sentiment over time, and correlating it with events, reveals the health of the brand and the impact of actions.

- [ ] Track sentiment over time, not as a single snapshot.
- [ ] Correlate sentiment shifts with events, launches, or news.
- [ ] Segment sentiment by topic, product, or audience.
- [ ] Use sentiment trend, not absolute score, as the signal.

### [ ] Set Up Crisis Detection And Escalation

Crises often begin as small signals before escalating. A listening system must detect anomalies, spikes in negative sentiment or volume, and escalate them quickly to the right responders. Without crisis detection, the brand finds out about problems from journalists or viral posts.

- [ ] Define thresholds for volume and sentiment that trigger escalation.
- [ ] Set up real-time alerts for anomalies.
- [ ] Define an escalation path with roles and response times.
- [ ] Test the crisis detection and escalation process before a real crisis.

### [ ] Decide When To Respond And When To Observe

Not every mention warrants a brand response. Responding to every comment is unsustainable and can escalate minor issues; never responding misses opportunities and leaves crises unaddressed. Clear criteria for when to respond, observe, or escalate govern the brand's engagement.

- [ ] Define criteria for when the brand responds, observes, or escalates.
- [ ] Respond to genuine questions, complaints, and opportunities.
- [ ] Avoid feeding trolls or escalating minor negativity.
- [ ] Escalate serious issues through the crisis path, not ad hoc replies.

### [ ] Respond Authentically And Quickly In Public

When the brand responds publicly, authenticity and speed matter. Canned, legalistic responses escalate frustration; human, timely responses defuse it. The response style must reflect the brand while meeting the audience's expectation of genuine engagement.

- [ ] Respond promptly, within defined service levels.
- [ ] Use a human, authentic voice, not corporate boilerplate.
- [ ] Acknowledge concerns genuinely before explaining or defending.
- [ ] Move complex issues to private channels when appropriate.

### [ ] Feed Listening Insights Into Strategy And Product

Listening is not just for defense; it is a source of insight. Customer complaints reveal product issues, competitive mentions reveal positioning gaps, and category conversation reveals trends. Feeding these insights into strategy and product turns listening from a cost into an asset.

- [ ] Synthesize listening insights regularly for strategy and product teams.
- [ ] Identify recurring complaints as product or process improvements.
- [ ] Spot category trends and competitive shifts early.
- [ ] Close the loop by acting on insights and communicating back.

### [ ] Monitor Competitors And The Category

Listening should extend beyond the brand to competitors and the category. Competitor sentiment, complaints, and launches reveal opportunities and threats. Category conversation reveals trends the brand should engage with. Listening only to oneself misses the strategic context.

- [ ] Monitor competitor mentions, sentiment, and launches.
- [ ] Track category and industry conversation for trends.
- [ ] Identify gaps and opportunities from competitor weaknesses.
- [ ] Use competitive listening to inform positioning and messaging.

### [ ] Measure Reputation Health, Not Just Mentions

Reputation health is broader than mention volume or sentiment. It includes share of voice, brand perception on key attributes, trust indicators, and review ratings. A reputation dashboard should track the metrics that reflect how the brand is genuinely perceived.

- [ ] Track share of voice relative to competitors.
- [ ] Measure perception on key brand attributes, not just sentiment.
- [ ] Monitor trust indicators like review ratings and recommendations.
- [ ] Combine quantitative and qualitative signals for a full picture.

### [ ] Respect Privacy And Ethics In Listening

Listening collects public conversation, but ethical boundaries apply. Do not collect private data, do not deceive about the brand's identity, and respect individuals' expectations. Ethical listening protects the brand's reputation and avoids legal risk.

- [ ] Listen only to public conversation, not private data.
- [ ] Be transparent about brand identity when engaging.
- [ ] Avoid deceptive practices like astroturfing or fake reviews.
- [ ] Respect individuals' privacy and consent.

## Common Traps

### [ ] Owned-Channel-Only Listening

Monitoring only the brand's own channels and missing the wider conversation.

### [ ] Drowning In Volume

Reporting mention counts without filtering for actionable signal.

### [ ] Snapshot Sentiment

Treating a single sentiment score as meaningful without tracking trend.

### [ ] No Crisis Detection

Finding out about crises from outside rather than from the listening system.

### [ ] Respond To Everything

Engaging every mention, escalating minor issues and exhausting the team.

### [ ] Canned Responses

Corporate boilerplate that escalates frustration rather than defusing it.

### [ ] Listening Without Action

Collecting insights that never reach strategy or product teams.

### [ ] Brand-Only Blindness

Listening to the brand but not competitors or the category.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Does listening cover the full landscape of platforms, reviews, forums, and news?
- [ ] Is signal distinguished from noise through filtering and prioritization?
- [ ] Is sentiment tracked as a trend over time and correlated with events?
- [ ] Are crisis detection thresholds and escalation paths defined and tested?
- [ ] Are there clear criteria for when to respond, observe, or escalate?
- [ ] Are public responses authentic, human, and timely?
- [ ] Are listening insights fed into strategy and product decisions?
- [ ] Are competitors and the category monitored, not just the brand?
- [ ] Does the reputation dashboard track share of voice, perception, and trust, not just mentions?
- [ ] Are privacy and ethics respected in what is collected and how the brand engages?
