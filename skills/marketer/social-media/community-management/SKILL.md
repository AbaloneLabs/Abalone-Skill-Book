# Community Management

A community is not an audience you broadcast to; it is a group of people who connect with each other around a shared interest, purpose, or identity, with the brand as host. Community management fails when it confuses a follower count with a community, when it over-moderates into sterility or under-moderates into toxicity, when it measures activity instead of belonging, or when it extracts value from members without giving any back.

Use this skill before launching a community, writing guidelines, planning moderation, defining community metrics, or diagnosing why a community is inactive or unhealthy. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating community as a content channel rather than a relationship-building discipline.

## Core Rules

### Define The Community's Purpose And Member Value

A community needs a reason to exist that members care about, independent of the brand. Clarify the shared purpose first.

Define:

- the shared interest, identity, or goal that unites members;
- what members get from participating, such as help, belonging, access, or recognition;
- what the brand gets, and be honest that it is not the members' primary motivation;
- the boundary of who the community is for and who it is not for.

Without a member-centered purpose, a community is just a brand audience that does not need to talk to each other.

### Build For Member-To-Member Connection

The defining feature of a community is that members interact with each other, not only with the brand. Design for that.

Foster connection:

- spaces and rituals that encourage members to help and respond to each other;
- prompts, questions, and traditions that spark conversation;
- surfacing and celebrating member contributions;
- introductions and onboarding that help newcomers find their place;
- subgroups or threads for specific interests and segments.

If removing the brand left a group that still wanted to talk, it is a community. If not, it is an audience.

### Set And Enforce Clear Guidelines

Healthy communities need boundaries that are explicit, fair, and consistently applied. Ambiguy breeds both toxicity and resentment.

Establish:

- clear rules on respect, spam, self-promotion, and prohibited content;
- the consequences for violations and how they escalate;
- consistent enforcement regardless of member status;
- transparency about moderation decisions where appropriate;
- a process for appeals and edge cases.

Inconsistent moderation, especially favoring high-status members, destroys trust faster than almost anything else.

### Moderate With Care And Consistency

Moderation shapes culture. Both over- and under-moderation kill communities.

Balance:

- removing genuine harm, spam, and abuse promptly;
- allowing disagreement and discomfort that stays within guidelines;
- not silencing criticism of the brand when it is constructive;
- explaining moderation actions to preserve trust;
- empowering trusted members or moderators to help at scale.

A sterile community with no debate is boring; a toxic one drives good members away. Aim for safe, lively, and fair.

### Onboard And Integrate Newcomers

Most communities die not from toxicity but from the cold-start problem for new members. Design the entry.

Design onboarding:

- a welcome that orients newcomers to purpose, norms, and how to start;
- easy first interactions that build confidence;
- introductions or buddy systems for new members;
- surfacing the most valuable recent content and people;
- a path from lurker to participant to contributor.

If newcomers arrive and see no clear way in, they leave and never return. The first experience determines whether they stay.

### Recognize And Elevate Contributors

A small number of members usually create most of the value. Identify, support, and recognize them.

Nurture contributors:

- identify the members who help, answer, and welcome others;
- give them recognition, status, or access;
- recruit trusted members into moderator or leader roles;
- protect them from burnout and entitlement;
- ensure recognition does not create cliques that exclude others.

Healthy communities run on the unpaid generosity of a core group. Care for that group deliberately.

### Measure Health By Belonging And Value, Not Activity

Activity metrics are easy but misleading. A community can be busy and unhealthy.

Measure:

- member-to-member interaction, not just brand-to-member;
- return participation and retention of members;
- the quality and helpfulness of contributions;
- whether members report belonging, learning, or getting value;
- sentiment and conflict resolution, not just post volume;
- guardrails such as churn, complaints, or toxic incidents.

A community that posts a lot but where members feel ignored or unsafe is not healthy, regardless of the activity dashboard.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Audience With Community

A large following where members do not interact with each other is an audience, not a community.

### Over-Moderation Into Sterility

Removing all disagreement makes a community boring and drives away the engaged members who made it lively.

### Inconsistent Enforcement

Applying rules differently to high-status members destroys the fairness that communities depend on.

### Brand-Centric Purpose

A community that exists to serve the brand's goals, with no independent member value, fails to earn participation.

### No Onboarding Path

New members who cannot figure out how to start leave immediately and never return.

### Ignoring The Core Contributors

Failing to recognize and protect the small group that creates most value leads to their burnout and departure.

### Activity As Health

Measuring posts and members rather than belonging and value hides whether the community actually helps anyone.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The community has a member-centered purpose that explains why people participate independent of the brand.
- [ ] Design encourages member-to-member connection, not only brand-to-member broadcast.
- [ ] Guidelines are clear, fair, and consistently enforced regardless of member status.
- [ ] Moderation removes harm while allowing constructive disagreement and criticism.
- [ ] Onboarding helps newcomers find their place and make a first contribution.
- [ ] Core contributors are identified, recognized, and protected from burnout.
- [ ] Recognition does not create exclusive cliques that alienate other members.
- [ ] Health is measured by belonging, value, retention, and member-to-member interaction, not activity volume.
- [ ] Sentiment, conflict, and complaints are tracked as guardrails, not ignored.
- [ ] The community would still have reason to exist if the brand stepped back, confirming it is a real community.
