# Loyalty Program Design

A loyalty program is not a discount scheme dressed up as a club. It is an engineered system of incentives meant to shift customer behavior over time in exchange for value the customer perceives as worth the engagement. Programs fail when they reward customers who would have bought anyway, when the reward structure is economically unsustainable, when rewards feel irrelevant or unreachable, when redemption friction destroys perceived value, or when the program becomes a liability that the finance team eventually forces the company to devalue.

Use this skill before designing, relaunching, or auditing a loyalty or membership program. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a program that looks generous but changes no behavior, or one that looks profitable on paper but collapses when customers actually redeem.

## Core Rules

### Start With The Behavior You Want To Change

A loyalty program exists to change behavior. Define the specific behavior before choosing a structure.

Identify:

- the customer behavior you want more of (frequency, basket size, category breadth, retention, referrals, data sharing, app usage);
- the gap between current behavior and target behavior;
- which customers are worth investing in;
- whether the desired behavior is achievable and sustainable;
- how you will know the program caused the change.

If the program rewards behavior that would happen without it, it is a margin giveaway, not a loyalty strategy. The first test of any program is incremental lift: would these purchases, these visits, these referrals have occurred anyway?

### Choose The Right Structure For The Job

Each program archetype changes behavior differently. Match structure to objective.

Common structures and what they do:

- **Points programs** reward incremental transactions and are good for frequency and basket size, but they create liability and complexity.
- **Tiered programs** reward cumulative spend and status, good for concentrating value on the best customers and driving aspiration, but they can alienate the middle and create gaming.
- **Paid membership** exchanges an upfront fee for ongoing benefits, good for commitment and predictable revenue, but it only works if benefits are genuinely worth the fee and used.
- **Mission or community programs** reward engagement, contribution, or values alignment, good for brands with strong identity, but hard to tie to transactional outcomes.

Do not pick a structure because competitors use it. Pick it because it targets the behavior you identified and fits your economics and audience.

### Model The Economics Before Launching

A loyalty program is a financial product as much as a marketing one. The economics must work under realistic redemption assumptions.

Model:

- earn rate and burn rate (how fast points accrue and redeem);
- breakage (the percentage of issued value never redeemed);
- realistic redemption rate, not optimistic breakage;
- cost of rewards as a percentage of revenue;
- incremental margin from changed behavior;
- liability growth on the balance sheet;
- tax and accounting treatment of deferred revenue and points;
- sensitivity to a surge in redemptions.

Breakage is the silent killer. Programs that look profitable often rely on high breakage, and the moment customers learn to redeem, or the moment regulation or reputation forces expiration transparency, the economics invert. Design for the world where customers actually use the program.

### Make Rewards Relevant And Attainable

A reward the customer does not want, or cannot reach, generates no behavior change and may generate resentment.

Ensure rewards:

- are relevant to the audience's actual desires, not what the company finds cheap to give;
- have a clear and attainable path, not an unreachable asymptote;
- feel meaningful relative to the effort required;
- include both near-term small rewards and aspirational larger ones;
- avoid expiration rules that feel punitive or deceptive.

Test perceived value, not just cost. A low-cost reward that feels valuable beats an expensive reward that feels like a consolation prize. Conversely, a reward that is trivially easy to earn can train customers to wait for discounts rather than pay full price.

### Decide What Status Means

If the program has tiers, status must carry real meaning. Empty status breeds cynicism.

Status should provide:

- genuine differentiated benefits, not just a different colored card;
- recognition that the customer values, such as access, service, or priority;
- a reason to maintain, not just achieve, the tier;
- clear and fair qualification criteria;
- a soft landing or grace period that avoids abrupt demotion shock.

Status that is easy to game or that resets harshly creates churn at the boundary. Design tier transitions thoughtfully.

### Avoid Cannibalizing Full-Price Behavior

The most common loyalty failure is training customers who would pay full price to instead wait for rewards, discounts, or redemption. The program should pull behavior forward or deepen engagement, not replace organic purchases with subsidized ones.

Guard against cannibalization by:

- rewarding behaviors that expand the relationship, not just transactions;
- avoiding rewards that function as standing discounts;
- measuring incremental revenue net of reward cost;
- segmenting analysis by whether customers were already loyal before joining.

### Plan For The Lifecycle Of The Program

Programs decay. What excites at launch becomes table stakes, then clutter.

Plan for:

- periodic refresh of rewards and benefits;
- communication that keeps the program top of mind;
- data collection and use that improves personalization;
- sunset or evolution of underperforming rewards;
- measurement of program health over years, not quarters.

## Common Traps

### Rewarding The Already Loyal

The easiest customers to enroll are your best customers, who needed no incentive. If the program mostly pays out to them, it is a cost with no incremental return. Always measure incremental behavior, not total behavior of members.

### Designing For Optimistic Breakage

Planning the program around the assumption that most points will expire unused creates a program that is profitable only when it fails its customers. When redemption rises, the liability surfaces and the company is forced to devalue, destroying trust.

### Rewards No One Wants

A catalog full of low-value, irrelevant, or hard-to-reach rewards signals that the company does not understand its customers and is protecting margin, not building loyalty.

### Complexity That Hides Value

Convoluted earn rules, confusing tier math, and opaque redemption paths make the program feel untrustworthy. Customers disengage from what they cannot predict.

### Status With No Real Benefit

A tier that offers nothing but a label teaches customers that status is meaningless and that the company does not value their loyalty.

### Standing Discount Disguised As Loyalty

If the program effectively lowers the price for everyone who joins, it is a price cut, not a loyalty program. It erodes margin and trains customers to never pay full price.

### Liability That Grows Unchecked

Points issued are a promise on the balance sheet. Programs that issue faster than they redeem build a liability that eventually forces painful devaluation.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The program defines a specific behavior it intends to change, not just engagement metrics.
- [ ] The structure (points, tiers, paid, mission) was chosen because it targets that behavior, not because competitors use it.
- [ ] Economics are modeled under realistic redemption rates, not optimistic breakage assumptions.
- [ ] Breakage assumptions are conservative and the program is still viable if customers redeem as intended.
- [ ] Rewards are relevant, attainable, and meaningful relative to effort, with both near-term and aspirational options.
- [ ] Status tiers carry genuine differentiated benefits, not just labels.
- [ ] The program is measured on incremental behavior, separating already-loyal customers from newly-influenced ones.
- [ ] The program avoids functioning as a standing discount that cannibalizes full-price purchases.
- [ ] Points liability, accounting treatment, and sensitivity to redemption surges are understood.
- [ ] A lifecycle plan exists for refreshing rewards, communication, and measurement beyond launch.
