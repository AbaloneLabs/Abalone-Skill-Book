# Go To Market Strategy And Launch

A go-to-market (GTM) strategy is the plan for reaching a target market, delivering value, and converting demand into revenue for a specific product or market entry. It is not a marketing campaign and not a feature release; it is the coordinated system of audience, positioning, channels, pricing, sales motion, and success handoff that determines whether a product finds and retains customers. The judgment problem is that GTM spans functions that often optimize locally, and a launch that is brilliant in marketing but misaligned with sales or product produces churn and wasted spend. The most common failure is treating GTM as a launch event rather than as a sustained system, or as a marketing plan rather than a cross-functional commitment. The skill is designing a GTM strategy that is coherent across functions, sequenced realistically, and measured on outcomes that matter.

Use this skill before planning a product launch or market entry, before defining a GTM motion, before sequencing channels and segments, or when a launch underperformed despite strong marketing. The goal is to prevent the agent from conflating GTM with marketing, from launching before product and sales are ready, or from choosing channels without a coherent customer journey.

## Core Rules

### [ ] Treat GTM As A Cross-Functional System, Not A Marketing Plan

GTM is not owned by marketing alone. It requires alignment across product, marketing, sales, customer success, pricing, and support. A launch where marketing drives demand that sales cannot convert, or that product cannot support, wastes the effort. GTM planning must convene all functions and define their interdependencies.

- [ ] Map every function that touches the customer journey and define each one's role.
- [ ] Confirm product readiness, including reliability and supportability, before launch.
- [ ] Confirm sales and success readiness, including training and collateral.
- [ ] Define a single GTM owner accountable for cross-functional coherence.

### [ ] Define The Target Segment And ICP Precisely

A GTM strategy aimed at a fuzzy audience wastes budget and produces weak messaging. The ideal customer profile (ICP) must be specific enough that channels, messaging, and sales motion can be tailored to it. "SMB" or "developers" is a starting point, not an ICP.

- [ ] Define the ICP with firmographic, demographic, and behavioral specificity.
- [ ] Prioritize segments by fit, accessibility, and value, not just size.
- [ ] Align messaging, channels, and sales motion to the prioritized segment.
- [ ] Resist launching to all segments at once; sequence by readiness and fit.

### [ ] Choose A Sales Motion That Matches The Product

The GTM motion, product-led growth, sales-led, channel-led, self-serve, must match the product's complexity, price, and buyer behavior. A complex enterprise product cannot be self-serve; a simple low-priced product cannot support a field sales motion. Mismatch between motion and product burns money.

- [ ] Match the motion to ACV, complexity, and buyer journey length.
- [ ] Align marketing channels to the chosen motion, e.g., product-led trials or sales-led ABM.
- [ ] Ensure pricing and packaging support the motion.
- [ ] Revisit the motion as the product and market mature.

### [ ] Sequence The Launch Realistically

A launch is not a single event but a sequence: beta, early access, general availability, expansion. Sequencing allows learning, proof points, and refinement before full scale. Launching everything everywhere at once maximizes risk and minimizes learning.

- [ ] Define launch phases and the goal of each, e.g., learn, prove, scale.
- [ ] Use early phases to gather proof points, testimonials, and case studies.
- [ ] Scale channels only after the conversion mechanics are proven.
- [ ] Build a launch readiness checklist with go/no-go criteria per phase.

### [ ] Align Channels To The Customer Journey

Channels must map to the stages of the buyer journey: awareness, consideration, evaluation, purchase, onboarding, expansion. A channel that drives awareness but cannot support evaluation, or vice versa, creates gaps. The channel mix should form a coherent journey, not a list of tactics.

- [ ] Map each channel to the journey stage it serves.
- [ ] Ensure no journey stage is unsupported by any channel.
- [ ] Align messaging across channels so the story is coherent.
- [ ] Measure channels on the metrics appropriate to their stage, not all on revenue.

### [ ] Define Pricing And Packaging As Part Of GTM

Pricing is a core GTM decision, not a finance afterthought. It signals positioning, determines the addressable market, shapes the sales motion, and affects adoption. Launching without finalized, tested pricing creates confusion and lost revenue.

- [ ] Finalize pricing and packaging before launch, tested with real buyers.
- [ ] Ensure pricing is consistent with positioning and the sales motion.
- [ ] Plan for pricing changes and how to communicate them.
- [ ] Align packaging with how the target buyer wants to buy.

### [ ] Build The Success And Expansion Handoff

GTM does not end at purchase; it extends through onboarding, activation, and expansion. A launch that drives acquisition but neglects onboarding produces churn that erodes the launch. The success motion must be designed as part of GTM, not added after.

- [ ] Define the activation milestone that predicts retention.
- [ ] Design onboarding to get customers to value quickly.
- [ ] Plan expansion plays for upsell and cross-sell.
- [ ] Feed retention and expansion data back into GTM targeting.

### [ ] Measure GTM On Outcomes, Not Outputs

GTM success is measured on business outcomes: pipeline, conversion, retention, revenue, not on outputs like ads run or events held. Measuring outputs rewards activity; measuring outcomes rewards effectiveness. Define the metrics that prove the GTM is working before launch.

- [ ] Define the primary GTM success metric, e.g., qualified pipeline or net revenue retention.
- [ ] Define leading indicators for each journey stage.
- [ ] Instrument measurement before launch so data is available from day one.
- [ ] Review outcomes regularly and reallocate budget toward what works.

### [ ] Plan For Post-Launch Sustainment

A launch is the beginning of a product's life in market, not the end. Many launches spike and fade because post-launch sustainment, ongoing demand gen, optimization, and iteration, is unplanned. GTM must include the plan for the months after launch.

- [ ] Budget for sustained demand generation beyond the launch spike.
- [ ] Plan iteration based on early launch data.
- [ ] Assign owners for post-launch optimization.
- [ ] Define the criteria for declaring the launch a success or pivoting.

## Common Traps

### [ ] GTM As A Marketing Plan

Treating GTM as marketing's responsibility while sales, product, and success operate independently.

### [ ] Fuzzy ICP

Launching to a broad or vague audience, producing weak messaging and wasted spend.

### [ ] Motion-Product Mismatch

Choosing a sales motion that the product's complexity and price cannot support.

### [ ] Big Bang Launch

Launching everywhere at once, maximizing risk and minimizing learning.

### [ ] Channel List Without Journey

A set of channels that do not form a coherent path from awareness to expansion.

### [ ] Pricing As Afterthought

Launching without finalized, tested pricing, creating confusion and lost revenue.

### [ ] Acquisition Without Onboarding

Driving signups without an activation and retention motion, producing churn.

### [ ] Output Metrics

Measuring activity rather than pipeline, conversion, and revenue.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is GTM planned as a cross-functional system with product, sales, marketing, and success aligned?
- [ ] Is the ICP defined precisely enough to tailor messaging, channels, and motion?
- [ ] Does the sales motion match the product's complexity, price, and buyer behavior?
- [ ] Is the launch sequenced into phases with clear goals and go/no-go criteria?
- [ ] Do channels map to a coherent customer journey with no unsupported stages?
- [ ] Is pricing finalized, tested, and consistent with positioning before launch?
- [ ] Is the success and expansion handoff designed as part of GTM, not added after?
- [ ] Are GTM metrics defined as outcomes, with leading indicators per stage?
- [ ] Is there a plan and budget for post-launch sustainment and iteration?
- [ ] Is there a single owner accountable for cross-functional GTM coherence?
