# SaaS Pricing And Packaging

Pricing is the single most powerful and most neglected lever in SaaS. It is not a number pulled from a spreadsheet; it is a statement of positioning, a constraint on who buys, and the mechanism that determines whether the business compounds. Pricing fails when it is copied from competitors without understanding value, when the model does not match how customers consume value, when free and paid tiers are gated by guesswork, or when price increases are avoided until margin collapses. Packaging fails when it obscures the choice, when tiers are designed for internal simplicity rather than buyer clarity, or when discounts become the default response to any objection.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how should we price our SaaS", "should we use per-seat or usage pricing", "what features go in which tier", "should we offer annual billing", or "how do we raise prices without losing customers". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating pricing as a tactical config decision instead of a strategic positioning and economics decision.

## Core Rules

### Choose A Pricing Model That Matches Value Consumption

The pricing model must align with how customers experience and consume value. A mismatch creates friction, churn, and leaving money on the table.

Evaluate models against the product:

- per-seat pricing fits collaboration and team tools where value scales with the number of users.
- usage pricing fits infrastructure, API, and consumption products where value scales with volume processed.
- tiered flat pricing fits products with distinct capability levels where buyers prefer predictable cost.
- value-based pricing ties price to a measurable outcome the customer cares about, such as revenue generated or cost saved.

Ask which model makes the customer's bill grow naturally as their success grows. A team tool on usage pricing feels unpredictable and penalizing. An infrastructure product on per-seat pricing feels disconnected from value. The model should make expansion feel earned, not taxed.

### Treat Pricing As Positioning

Price signals who the product is for. A low price attracts price-sensitive, high-support customers and signals a commodity. A high price signals seriousness, filters for committed buyers, and funds the product and service quality that justifies it.

Consider:

- does the price attract the customer profile the business can serve profitably?
- does the price signal the intended market tier (consumer, SMB, mid-market, enterprise)?
- does the price fund the roadmap, support, and sales motion the positioning promises?
- would a dramatically different price change who buys more than how many buy?

Pricing below cost to win volume often attracts the worst-fit customers and destroys the margin needed to improve the product. The agent should test whether the price is a positioning decision disguised as a math decision.

### Design Tiers Around Buyer Decisions, Not Internal Logic

Good packaging makes the buyer's choice easy. Bad packaging makes the buyer do arithmetic to figure out what they get.

Design tiers by:

- anchoring each tier to a buyer persona or use case, not to an internal feature inventory;
- using clear, comparable value metrics across tiers;
- making the upgrade path obvious and value-justified;
- limiting the number of tiers to what a buyer can compare at a glance;
- naming tiers by value or audience, not by metal colors or arbitrary labels.

Ask whether a buyer can, in under a minute, identify which tier is for them and what they gain by moving up. If they cannot, the packaging is working against conversion.

### Gate Features By Value And Willingness To Pay

Feature gating determines the free-to-paid and tier-to-tier upgrade logic. Poor gating either gives away so much that no one upgrades, or walls off so much that free users never experience value.

Gate by asking:

- does the feature let a free user experience the core value that drives retention?
- does the gated feature address a need that emerges only after the user is successful?
- is the gated feature tied to willingness to pay, such as scale, advanced workflows, or compliance?
- does gating create a natural, value-justified reason to upgrade?

The free or entry tier should deliver enough value to create habit and retention. The paid tiers should gate the capabilities that successful, growing users naturally need.

### Decide Annual Versus Monthly Deliberately

Billing period is a cash, retention, and commitment decision, not just a discount choice.

Weigh:

- annual billing improves cash flow, reduces churn friction, and signals commitment, but requires confidence in retention.
- monthly billing lowers commitment barriers and suits cautious buyers or variable usage, but increases churn exposure.
- the annual discount should reflect real retention value and cash benefit, not be a default 20% off.

Forcing annual on a product with unproven retention risks collecting prepayments for customers who will demand refunds. The agent should align billing period with the confidence in delivered value and the customer's budgeting reality.

### Plan Price Increases As A Managed Process

Avoiding price increases erodes margin over time, especially with inflation and rising costs. But increases done badly trigger churn and resentment.

Manage increases by:

- grandfathering existing customers for a defined period to honor the original value exchange;
- communicating the change with clear reasoning tied to added value;
- timing increases to coincide with product improvements or renewal cycles;
- segmenting impact so the largest, most at-risk accounts are handled individually;
- measuring the net effect of increased revenue against any churn, not just the churn alone.

A price increase that loses a few low-fit customers while improving margin can be net positive. The agent should evaluate the portfolio effect, not panic at any single cancellation.

### Use Discounts Strategically, Not Reflexively

Discounts are a tool, not a default. Unmanaged discounting trains buyers to negotiate, erodes the price's positioning signal, and hides the real willingness to pay.

Govern discounts by:

- defining standard discount thresholds and the approval they require;
- tying discounts to commitments such as annual prepay or multi-year terms;
- tracking discounting patterns by rep, segment, and deal size;
- avoiding discounts as the first response to any objection;
- preserving list price integrity so the published price remains credible.

### Validate Pricing With Evidence

Pricing assumptions must be tested. The right price is found through customer evidence, not internal debate.

Validate by:

- interviewing customers about value and willingness to pay;
- analyzing conversion and expansion by price point and cohort;
- testing price changes in controlled segments;
- monitoring win/loss reasons for price-related patterns;
- comparing against alternatives the customer considers, not just direct competitors.

## Common Traps

### Copying Competitor Pricing

Competitors may have different costs, buyers, and value delivery. Copying their price imports their constraints without their context.

### Pricing Model Mismatched To Value

Per-seat pricing on a consumption product, or usage pricing on a team tool, creates friction that suppresses both adoption and expansion.

### Tiers Designed For Internal Simplicity

Packaging built around feature inventories or engineering convenience confuses buyers and suppresses conversion.

### Free Tier That Cannibalizes Paid

Giving away the full core value removes the upgrade reason and attracts support-heavy non-payers.

### Discounting As Default Objection Handling

Reflexive discounting erodes margin, trains buyers to negotiate, and destroys the price's positioning signal.

### Avoiding Price Increases Until Margin Collapses

Delaying increases compounds margin erosion and makes the eventual increase larger and more disruptive.

### Pricing Below Cost To Win Volume

Low prices attract high-support, low-fit customers and starve the product of the margin needed to improve.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The pricing model matches how customers consume and grow value, not just internal preference.
- [ ] Price is evaluated as a positioning signal that attracts the right buyer profile, not only a revenue lever.
- [ ] Tiers are designed around buyer decisions and comparable value metrics, not internal feature lists.
- [ ] Feature gating preserves core value in the free tier while creating a natural upgrade reason.
- [ ] Annual versus monthly billing reflects retention confidence and cash needs, not a default discount.
- [ ] Price increases are planned with grandfathering, communication, and net portfolio impact measured.
- [ ] Discounts are governed by thresholds and tied to commitments, not used as default objection handling.
- [ ] Pricing decisions are validated with customer evidence, conversion data, and win/loss analysis.
- [ ] The published list price is protected from erosion through unmanaged discounting.
- [ ] The pricing could not be replaced by a competitor's number without changing who buys.
