# SaaS Growth And Acquisition

SaaS growth is not "run more ads and watch signups rise." It is the deliberate alignment of how a product is sold, how users experience value, and how the unit economics of acquisition compound. Growth fails when teams copy a growth model that does not fit their product, when activation is treated as a marketing metric instead of a product experience, when acquisition channels are chosen for volume rather than payback, or when virality is assumed rather than engineered. The most common failure is importing the surface tactics of a successful company without understanding the underlying growth engine.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "should we go product-led or sales-led", "should we offer freemium or a free trial", "what is our activation metric", "how do we reduce CAC", or "how do we build virality into the product". The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending generic growth tactics that ignore the product's actual motion, value delivery, and economics.

## Core Rules

### Choose The Growth Motion To Fit The Product

Growth motion is not a preference; it is a consequence of the product, buyer, and value delivery. Product-led growth (PLG) works when a single user can derive value independently, when value compounds with usage, and when the product can be self-served. Sales-led growth works when the buyer differs from the user, when value requires integration or change management, when the deal size justifies a sales motion, or when procurement and security review gate adoption.

Ask:

- Can one person sign up and reach value without help?
- Is the buyer the same as the user?
- Does value increase with usage and teammates, or is it delivered in a configured deployment?
- Is the contract value high enough to support a sales cost?
- Does adoption require IT, security, or executive sign-off?

A collaboration tool with viral team invites fits PLG. An enterprise data platform that requires integration, migration, and security review fits sales-led. Forcing the wrong motion creates friction: self-serve on a product that needs configuration produces churn, while sales outreach on a self-evident product produces annoyed users and bloated CAC.

### Match The Entry Model To The Growth Motion

The entry model, freemium versus free trial versus demo, must align with the motion and value delivery.

- Freemium keeps a useful tier free forever. It works when the free product creates ongoing habit, when free users refer paid users, or when free usage generates expansion. It fails when the free tier satisfies the need so fully that no one upgrades, or when it attracts support-heavy users who never pay.
- Free trial gives full access for a limited time. It works when value is felt quickly and the decision can be made within the window. It fails when time-to-value exceeds the trial, when the trial ends before the user integrates the product into their workflow, or when trial conversion is pressured rather than earned.
- Demo or contact-sales gates access. It works for high-consideration, high-value products where the buyer needs a guided evaluation. It fails when used for products that could self-serve, adding friction and losing users who would have converted on their own.

Ask whether the entry model creates a path to value and a natural reason to upgrade, or whether it is chosen because competitors use it.

### Define Activation As A Behavioral Threshold

Activation is the moment a user has experienced the core value of the product, not the moment they signed up or clicked around. A vague activation definition produces vanity metrics and hides the real funnel problem.

Define activation by:

- identifying the specific action or set of actions that correlate with retention;
- validating the correlation with data, not assumption;
- measuring time-to-activation, not just activation rate;
- segmenting activation by persona, use case, and acquisition source.

A strong activation metric is specific, measurable, and predictive of retention. "User created a project" is weak if retained users also invite teammates and connect a data source. The agent should push for the minimal behavior set that predicts a retained, expanding customer.

### Engineer Time-To-Value Downward

Time-to-value (TTV) is the delay between signup and the first experience of real value. Long TTV is the single largest driver of trial failure and early churn. Reducing TTV is a product and onboarding problem, not a marketing copy problem.

Reduce TTV by:

- removing setup steps that are not essential to the first value moment;
- providing templates, sample data, or guided first runs;
- defaulting to the fastest path to the core workflow;
- measuring where users drop off before value;
- distinguishing must-do setup from nice-to-have configuration.

Ask whether every step in onboarding is necessary for the first value experience, or whether it is there for completeness, compliance, or the team's comfort.

### Evaluate Acquisition Channels On Payback, Not Cost

Cheap acquisition is not good acquisition. The right evaluation is CAC payback, the time it takes for gross profit from a customer to recover acquisition cost, combined with contribution margin and retention.

Evaluate channels by:

- CAC by channel and cohort;
- payback period by channel;
- conversion from signup to activated to paid;
- retention and expansion by acquisition source;
- scalability ceiling of each channel.

A channel with low CAC but poor retention and no expansion can destroy value. A channel with higher CAC but strong retention and expansion can be the foundation of compounding growth. The agent should resist optimizing for CAC in isolation.

### Design Virality And Network Effects Deliberately

Virality and network effects are powerful but frequently misunderstood. Virality is when existing users bring in new users through invites, sharing, or collaboration. Network effects are when the product becomes more valuable as more participants join. They are not automatic and not interchangeable.

Engineer virality by:

- making collaboration inherently multi-user, so inviting others is part of the core workflow;
- ensuring the invited user receives value, not just an obligation;
- building shareable artifacts that travel outside the product;
- measuring the viral coefficient and the cycle time.

Distinguish true network effects from scale effects. A marketplace or communications product has network effects. A single-user tool with a referral button does not. Do not claim network effects where only virality or brand exists.

### Align Acquisition With Retention And Expansion

Acquisition quality is only visible in the downstream cohort. Growth strategy must connect acquisition to retention and expansion, not treat them as separate functions.

Connect by:

- acquiring users who fit the ideal retention and expansion profile;
- measuring cohort retention by acquisition source;
- feeding retention and expansion insights back into targeting and messaging;
- avoiding acquisition that looks good in signup volume but churns quickly.

## Common Traps

### Copying A Growth Model Without The Underlying Engine

Teams copy Slack's freemium or Salesforce's sales motion without the product, buyer, or economics that make those models work. The tactics fail because the engine is absent.

### Freemium That Cannibalizes Paid

A free tier that fully satisfies the need removes the reason to upgrade and attracts support-heavy non-payers. The free tier must leave a real, valuable reason to move to paid.

### Activation Defined As Signups

Treating signups or logins as activation hides the real funnel. Users who never reach value churn regardless of signup volume.

### Optimizing CAC In Isolation

Low CAC from low-intent sources produces poor retention and weak payback. Acquisition efficiency must include downstream cohort quality.

### Assuming Virality Will Emerge

Adding a referral button does not create virality. Virality requires the product's core workflow to naturally involve inviting or sharing with others.

### Ignoring Time-To-Value

A trial or freemium model with long TTV converts poorly regardless of acquisition quality. TTV is often the hidden bottleneck.

### Treating Growth As Marketing-Only

In SaaS, growth is a product, pricing, and go-to-market problem. Marketing alone cannot fix a product whose motion, activation, or TTV is broken.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The growth motion (product-led or sales-led) matches the product's value delivery, buyer, and contract value.
- [ ] The entry model (freemium, free trial, demo) creates a path to value and a natural upgrade reason.
- [ ] Activation is defined as a specific behavioral threshold validated against retention data.
- [ ] Time-to-value is measured and actively reduced, with non-essential onboarding steps removed.
- [ ] Acquisition channels are evaluated on CAC payback and cohort retention, not cost alone.
- [ ] Virality and network effects are engineered into the core workflow, not assumed or bolted on.
- [ ] Free tier value is balanced so it does not cannibalize paid conversion.
- [ ] Acquisition quality is judged by downstream retention and expansion, not signup volume.
- [ ] Growth strategy connects acquisition, activation, retention, and expansion as one system.
- [ ] The chosen model is justified by the product's reality, not by copying a famous competitor.
