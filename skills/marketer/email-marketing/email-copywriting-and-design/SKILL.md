# Email Copywriting And Design

Email creative must earn the open, hold attention, and drive action, often on a small screen, in a crowded inbox, within seconds. It fails when subject lines are vague or misleading, when the message buries the point, when design breaks on mobile or in different clients, when there are too many competing calls to action, or when the email looks great in a designer's tool but breaks in the recipient's inbox.

Use this skill before writing email copy, designing templates, crafting subject lines, or reviewing email creative. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing email that is polished in isolation but fails in the real inbox environment.

## Core Rules

### Earn The Open Honestly

The subject line and preview text decide whether the email is opened. They must earn attention without deceiving.

Craft:

- a subject line that is specific and relevant to the recipient;
- preview text that complements rather than repeats the subject;
- no misleading or clickbait subject lines that betray the content;
- alignment between the subject promise and the email's actual content;
- relevance to the segment and moment.

A misleading subject line may win an open but loses trust and increases unsubscribes and complaints when the content disappoints.

### Lead With The Point

Recipients scan. The most important message and action must be visible immediately, not buried below filler.

Structure:

- the key value or point in the first screen, above the fold;
- a clear, single primary call to action;
- supporting detail below for those who want more;
- no lengthy preamble or generic greeting that delays value;
- scannable layout with short paragraphs and clear hierarchy.

If the recipient must read three paragraphs to understand why they received the email, most will not.

### Write For The Inbox Context

Email is read fast, often on mobile, often while distracted. Write for that reality.

Write:

- concisely, because attention is short;
- in a voice that fits the relationship and brand;
- with concrete benefits and specifics, not vague claims;
- with a call to action that is obvious and easy to tap;
- with proof or reassurance appropriate to the ask.

Email is not a white paper. Density that works in a guide fails in an inbox.

### Design For Mobile And Real Clients

Most email is opened on mobile, and email clients render inconsistently. Design for the worst case, not the designer's screen.

Ensure:

- responsive design that works on small screens;
- legible font sizes and tap targets for touch;
- graceful degradation when images do not load, with alt text;
- testing across major clients and devices, not just one;
- a clear, readable text version for those who block images or use plain text.

An email that looks perfect in a design tool but breaks in Outlook or on a phone loses most of its impact.

### Use One Primary Call To Action

Multiple competing calls to action dilute focus and reduce action. Decide what the email is for.

Apply:

- one primary action the email is designed to drive;
- secondary actions, if any, clearly subordinate;
- a button or link that is obvious and tappable;
- a destination page that fulfills the email's promise;
- consistency between the email ask and the landing experience.

An email with five equal buttons asks the recipient to do nothing in particular, and that is usually what they do.

### Respect Accessibility And Inbox Norms

Email should be usable by everyone and should not trigger spam filters through careless choices.

Respect:

- accessibility, with alt text, color contrast, and readable structure;
- plain-text alternatives for screen readers and image-blocking clients;
- no spam-triggering patterns like all-caps, excessive punctuation, or deceptive content;
- unsubscribe that is visible and working, not hidden;
- a recognizable sender name and address.

Accessibility and deliverability are not optional refinements; they determine whether the email reaches and can be used by the recipient.

### Test And Iterate On Creative

Email creative benefits from testing because small changes move results. Build testing into the process.

Test:

- subject lines and preview text, the highest-leverage test;
- send time and day for the audience;
- calls to action, wording, placement, and design;
- content length and structure;
- one variable at a time to learn clearly.

Testing randomly teaches nothing. Test with a hypothesis and apply what is learned to future sends.

## Common Traps

### Misleading Subject Lines

Tricking recipients into opening betrays trust and drives unsubscribes and spam complaints.

### Burying The Point

Hiding the key message or action below filler guarantees most recipients never see it.

### Too Many Calls To Action

Competing buttons ask the recipient to do nothing and reduce conversion on all of them.

### Design That Breaks On Mobile Or In Clients

Beautiful designs that fail on phones or in Outlook lose the majority of opens.

### Image-Dependent Layouts

Emails that are blank when images do not load lose recipients who block images by default.

### Vague, Claim-Heavy Copy

Empty superlatives without specifics fail to persuade and can create compliance risk.

### Testing Without Hypothesis

Random A/B tests without a learning goal produce noise instead of insight.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Subject lines and preview text earn the open honestly and align with the email's content.
- [ ] The key message and primary call to action are visible above the fold.
- [ ] Copy is concise, specific, and written for fast mobile reading.
- [ ] Design is responsive, tested across major clients and devices, and degrades gracefully.
- [ ] Images have alt text and the email is readable when images are blocked.
- [ ] There is one clear primary call to action with subordinate secondary actions at most.
- [ ] The landing page fulfills the email's promise and ask.
- [ ] Accessibility, contrast, alt text, and plain-text alternatives are addressed.
- [ ] No spam-triggering patterns, and unsubscribe is visible and working.
- [ ] Creative is tested with hypotheses on subject lines, timing, and calls to action, with learnings applied.
