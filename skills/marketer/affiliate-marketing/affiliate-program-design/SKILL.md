# Affiliate Program Design

An affiliate program is a commission-based distribution channel, not a passive growth hack. It pays partners only when they drive a defined outcome, which sounds risk-free but is not: poorly designed programs pay for sales that would have happened anyway, attract partners who exploit attribution loopholes, create compliance and fraud exposure, and quietly erode margin and brand control. Designing the program means engineering the incentives, the rules, and the economics so that partners are rewarded for genuine incremental value, not for intercepting demand the brand already created.

Use this skill before launching, restructuring, or auditing an affiliate program. The goal is to prevent the agent from setting attractive commissions and a long cookie window without modeling who actually gets paid, for what, and at whose expense.

## Core Rules

### Define The Action You Are Willing To Pay For

Commission follows the defined action. Vague or misaligned actions pay for the wrong outcomes.

Choose and specify:

- the conversion event (sale, lead, sign-up, qualified action);
- whether payment is on first sale only or recurring;
- whether returns, refunds, and chargebacks are netted out;
- whether the action must be net-new or can be any sale;
- the qualifying conditions that make a commission valid.

Pay only for outcomes that represent genuine incremental value. Paying for any sale, including those the brand would have captured organically, turns the program into a tax on existing demand.

### Choose The Commission Structure Deliberately

Commission structure shapes partner behavior. Each model has different incentives and risks.

Common structures:

- **Cost per sale (CPA on sale)** pays a fixed amount or percentage per transaction; simple and predictable, but it can over-reward low-effort interception.
- **Revenue share** pays a percentage of revenue, aligning partner upside with basket size and value; good for subscriptions but creates ongoing liability.
- **Tiered commissions** increase rates as partners hit volume thresholds; motivates top partners but can incent volume over quality.
- **Performance or quality tiers** adjust rate by outcome quality, such as new-customer versus returning-customer sales; most protective of margin but more complex.

Match structure to what you want partners to optimize. A flat high rate rewards everyone equally, including low-quality and intercepting partners.

### Set The Cookie Window With Intent

The cookie window determines how long after a click a partner can claim attribution. It is a margin and fairness lever, not a setting to copy from a template.

Consider:

- the typical consideration window for your product;
- whether last-click or multi-touch attribution applies;
- how the window interacts with brand bidding and coupon sites;
- the risk of long windows capturing sales driven by other channels;
- fairness to partners whose content genuinely influenced the sale.

A very long cookie window on last-click attribution over-credits the last touch and under-credits the channels and partners that built awareness. A very short window under-credits partners who drive considered purchases. Choose deliberately and document the reasoning.

### Write Terms And Conditions That Protect The Program

Affiliate terms are the contract that prevents abuse. Vague terms invite disputes and exploitation.

Cover at least:

- allowed and prohibited promotional methods;
- brand bidding, trademark, and search restrictions;
- coupon and cashback rules, including requiring an actual discount;
- domain, subdomain, and typosquatting restrictions;
- email and SMS compliance requirements;
- disclosure and FTC obligations;
- prohibition of cookie stuffing, forced clicks, and toolbars;
- grounds for termination and clawback;
- payout thresholds, schedule, and methods;
- changes to terms and notice periods.

Enforce terms consistently. Selective enforcement teaches partners that the rules are negotiable.

### Model The Full Economics

A program that looks affordable on a per-sale basis can be expensive once all costs surface.

Model:

- commission as a percentage of attributed revenue;
- cannibalization of organic and paid search sales;
- network or platform fees;
- fraud and chargeback losses;
- management and monitoring overhead;
- incremental margin on affiliate-driven sales versus non-incremental;
- impact on blended customer acquisition cost.

The true cost of affiliate sales includes the sales you would have captured anyway but now pay commission on. Incrementality testing, not gross attributed revenue, reveals whether the program pays for itself.

### Structure Partner Tiers To Reward Quality

Not all partners deserve the same terms. Tiers let you concentrate reward and support on partners who drive real value.

Tier by:

- volume and consistency of quality sales;
- new-customer contribution;
- adherence to terms and brand guidelines;
- content quality and audience fit;
- longevity and reliability.

Avoid tiers that reward raw volume alone, which can incent low-quality partners to chase thresholds through questionable tactics.

### Decide Network Versus In-House

Running through an affiliate network versus building in-house involves tradeoffs in reach, control, cost, and fraud exposure.

Networks provide:

- access to many pre-vetted partners;
- tracking, payment, and dispute infrastructure;
- fraud monitoring tools;
- but higher fees, less direct relationships, and less control.

In-house provides:

- direct partner relationships and custom terms;
- lower per-transaction fees;
- full control over data and rules;
- but requires building tracking, payment, vetting, and fraud systems.

The choice depends on scale, internal capability, and how much control matters. Many programs start on a network and bring top partners in-house over time.

## Common Traps

### Paying For Non-Incremental Sales

The most common and most expensive trap. Partners capture attribution for sales driven by brand search, direct traffic, or other channels, and the program pays commission on demand it did not create.

### Default Cookie Windows That Over-Credit Last Click

A long last-click window handed to coupon and cashback sites lets them intercept customers at the point of purchase and claim commission for adding no value.

### Attractive Commissions Without Terms

High commissions without clear, enforced terms attract opportunistic and fraudulent partners who exploit the gaps.

### Tiering On Volume Alone

Rewarding partners purely for volume can incent low-quality traffic, brand bidding, and aggressive tactics that hit thresholds but damage brand and margin.

### Ignoring Cannibalization

Counting gross affiliate revenue without netting out sales cannibalized from organic and paid channels hides the true cost of the program.

### Vague Or Unenforced Terms

Terms that exist on paper but are not monitored or enforced signal to bad actors that the rules are optional.

### Underestimating Fraud Exposure

Cookie stuffing, brand bidding, fake leads, and trademark abuse can silently drain budget. Programs without active monitoring discover fraud only during audits.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The commissionable action is precisely defined, including returns, refunds, and qualifying conditions.
- [ ] The commission structure (CPA, rev-share, tiered, quality-adjusted) was chosen to shape partner behavior deliberately.
- [ ] The cookie window is set with intent, considering consideration period, attribution model, and interception risk.
- [ ] Terms and conditions cover promotion methods, brand bidding, coupons, domains, compliance, clawbacks, and termination.
- [ ] Terms are monitored and enforced consistently, not selectively.
- [ ] Full economics are modeled including cannibalization, network fees, fraud, and management overhead.
- [ ] Incrementality is tested, not assumed, so the program is evaluated on net-new value.
- [ ] Partner tiers reward quality and new-customer contribution, not raw volume alone.
- [ ] The network-versus-in-house decision is justified by scale, capability, and control needs.
- [ ] Payout thresholds, schedules, and methods are defined and sustainable.
