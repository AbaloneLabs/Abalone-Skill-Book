# Retail Channel And Distribution

Where a brand sells is a strategic decision that shapes its economics, its brand perception, its customer relationships, and its future flexibility. Channel choice is not a logistics question; it is a positioning question answered in the language of access. The work fails when distribution is chased wherever it is available without considering fit, when channel economics are modeled only at the gross margin level, when direct and wholesale channels are allowed to undercut and cannibalize each other, or when the choice between exclusivity and breadth is made for short-term volume rather than long-term brand health.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "which retail channels should we use", "should we sell DTC or wholesale", "how do we manage channel conflict", or "should distribution be exclusive or broad". The goal is to prevent the agent from treating distribution as a sales target to be maximized, when in fact the wrong distribution can damage a brand faster than no distribution at all.

## Core Rules

### Treat Channel Choice As Positioning, Not Logistics

Every channel signals something about the brand and reaches a different shopper in a different mindset. Choosing channels is choosing what the brand means and to whom.

Evaluate each channel against:

- the shopper it reaches and the mindset they bring;
- the brand signal the channel sends: premium, value, convenience, authority;
- the control the brand retains over presentation, service, data, and price;
- the cost structure and unit economics of selling through it;
- the competitive set the brand sits within on that channel;
- the growth trajectory and strategic importance of the channel;
- the fit between the channel's operations and the product's requirements.

A brand distributed everywhere is not necessarily stronger; it may simply be diluted, competing on price in channels that erode its meaning.

### Model Channel Economics Beyond Gross Margin

Channel profitability is not the wholesale price minus cost of goods. It is the full economic picture of serving that channel, and channels that look similar can be radically different in true profit.

Model the full economics:

- gross margin at the channel's price point;
- trade spend, slotting, returns, and chargebacks;
- logistics, packaging, and minimum order requirements;
- marketing and sell-through support required to make the channel work;
- payment terms, working capital, and bad debt risk;
- customer acquisition cost where the brand must drive traffic;
- lifetime value and data ownership where the channel allows direct relationships;
- the cost of channel conflict and cannibalization elsewhere.

A channel that adds volume while destroying blended margin or cannibalizing a more profitable one can be value-negative despite looking like growth.

### Match Channel Mix To Brand Stage And Category

The right channel mix depends on the brand's maturity, the category's norms, and the strategic goal. The same channel can be ideal at one stage and harmful at another.

Sequence channel decisions by:

- launch stage: controlled channels that protect positioning and gather learning;
- growth stage: channels that scale reach without compromising economics;
- maturity stage: optimization and pruning of channels that no longer earn their place;
- category norms: where shoppers expect to find and buy this kind of product;
- competitive entry: whether distribution itself is a barrier or an invitation;
- data and learning needs: channels that teach the brand about its customer;
- service and fulfillment capability: whether the brand can meet the channel's demands.

Adding channels faster than operations and brand coherence can support produces a fragile distribution footprint that collapses under strain.

### Manage Channel Conflict Deliberately

Where multiple channels meet the same shopper, conflict is inevitable unless it is designed for. Unmanaged conflict turns partners into adversaries and customers into the losers.

Address conflict through:

- differentiated assortments by channel where possible;
- price management that prevents visible undercutting;
- clear rules for who owns the customer relationship and data;
- distinct value propositions so channels compete on different terms;
- MAP or similar pricing policies where legal and enforceable;
- transparent communication with partners about channel strategy;
- monitoring of gray market, unauthorized resellers, and marketplace listings.

Pretending conflict will not occur is how brands lose wholesale partners overnight when a cheaper DTC or marketplace price appears.

### Decide Exclusive Versus Broad Distribution Strategically

Exclusivity and breadth are not good and bad; they are tools for different objectives. The choice should follow strategy, not opportunism.

Choose exclusivity when:

- brand equity depends on scarcity, prestige, or curation;
- the partner adds service, education, or authority the brand cannot provide alone;
- controlling the experience matters more than total reach;
- the category rewards depth of relationship over width of distribution.

Choose broad distribution when:

- convenience and availability are the core value proposition;
- the category is competitive and shoppers will substitute if absent;
- scale economies in production and logistics matter;
- brand awareness is high enough that broad presence reinforces rather than dilutes.

Switching between strategies has a cost: broad-to-exclusive is hard to reclaim scarcity, and exclusive-to-broad can cheapen a carefully built premium position.

### Evaluate Retailer Fit Before Pursuing Distribution

Not every retailer that will take the product should carry it. Poor-fit distribution wastes effort and can harm the brand.

Assess fit on:

- shopper overlap with the brand's target audience;
- category role and performance at that retailer;
- service and presentation standards;
- margin and terms the retailer can sustain;
- brand adjacency: what the product sits next to on shelf or page;
- the retailer's growth trajectory and strategic direction;
- operational reliability in ordering, fulfillment, and payment;
- data sharing and collaboration willingness.

A strong brand in the wrong retailer underperforms and teaches the retailer the product does not sell, which is hard to reverse.

### Plan For Channel Evolution

Channels are not static. They rise, mature, and decline, and the brand's footprint must evolve with them.

Plan for evolution by:

- reviewing channel performance and contribution regularly;
- exiting channels that no longer fit or profit before they drag the brand;
- testing emerging channels early without overcommitting;
- protecting the ability to move with shoppers as they shift;
- avoiding over-dependence on a single channel or partner;
- building capabilities, such as DTC or marketplace expertise, ahead of need.

Brands locked into a declining channel because they never built alternatives lose the shopper even when they keep the product.

## Common Traps

### Maximizing Distribution Wherever Available

Treating distribution points as a score to be maximized fills the wrong shelves with the wrong shoppers and dilutes brand meaning.

### Gross Margin As The Only Economic Lens

Ignoring trade spend, returns, logistics, working capital, and cannibalization makes a channel look profitable that is actually destroying blended value.

### Unmanaged Channel Conflict

Allowing DTC, wholesale, and marketplace to undercut each other destroys partner trust and trains shoppers to chase the lowest price.

### Exclusivity Or Breadth Chosen For Short-Term Volume

Picking the convenient option rather than the strategic one erodes prestige or forfeits availability in ways that are expensive to reverse.

### Ignoring Retailer Fit

Pursuing any retailer that will list the product teaches weak retailers the product does not sell and burns relationships with better ones.

### Over-Dependence On One Channel

Relying on a single channel or partner leaves the brand exposed to that channel's decline, policy changes, or negotiation leverage.

### Adding Channels Faster Than Operations Can Support

Scaling distribution before fulfillment, service, and brand coherence can keep up produces broken experiences that undo the expansion.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Channel choices are evaluated as positioning decisions, considering shopper, brand signal, control, economics, and competitive context.
- [ ] Channel economics are modeled fully, including trade spend, returns, logistics, working capital, cannibalization, and data ownership.
- [ ] The channel mix matches the brand's stage, category norms, and strategic goal, sequenced to protect positioning and learning.
- [ ] Channel conflict is managed through assortment, pricing, value propositions, and partner communication, not ignored.
- [ ] The exclusive-versus-broad decision follows strategy and brand equity, not short-term volume opportunity.
- [ ] Retailer fit is assessed on shopper overlap, category role, adjacency, standards, and collaboration before pursuing distribution.
- [ ] Channel performance is reviewed regularly, with exits, tests, and capability building planned for evolution.
- [ ] No single channel or partner represents dangerous over-dependence.
- [ ] Cannibalization between channels is measured and accounted for in growth claims.
- [ ] Distribution expansion is gated by operational and brand-coherence readiness, not by availability alone.
