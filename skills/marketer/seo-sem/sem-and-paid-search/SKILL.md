# SEM And Paid Search

Paid search captures demand that already exists. It is powerful because it reaches people in the moment of intent, but it is also one of the most efficient ways to waste money, because every click costs and because intent is not always what it appears. SEM fails when it bids on broad or irrelevant terms, when it sends traffic to pages that do not match the query, when it ignores Quality Score and auction dynamics, or when it optimizes for clicks instead of qualified outcomes.

Use this skill before building a paid search account, restructuring campaigns, writing ad copy, setting bids, or auditing search spend efficiency. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating paid search as a simple buy-button for traffic rather than a discipline of intent matching and auction economics.

## Core Rules

### Match Bids To Intent And Value

Not every query with your keyword is worth bidding on. Separate high-value intent from low-value curiosity.

Segment by intent:

- brand terms, where searchers know you, high intent and usually high ROI;
- competitor terms, where searchers compare, lower conversion but strategic;
- category or generic terms, where searchers research, broad and expensive;
- transactional modifiers, such as buy, price, or hire, that signal readiness;
- negative intent, such as free, job, or download, that should be excluded.

Bid aggressively where intent and value are high and conversion is likely. Bid cautiously or exclude where intent is weak or value is low, regardless of volume.

### Send Traffic To A Matching Destination

The landing experience must answer the query and fulfill the ad's promise. Mismatch is the most common cause of wasted spend.

Ensure alignment:

- the ad copy matches the keyword and query;
- the landing page matches the ad's promise and the searcher's intent;
- the page delivers the information or action the searcher expected;
- the offer and next step fit the readiness stage;
- the page is fast, mobile-friendly, and free of friction.

Sending research-intent traffic to a hard-sell page, or transactional traffic to a generic homepage, wastes the click you paid for.

### Structure The Account For Control And Learning

Account structure determines what you can control and what you can learn. Sloppy structure mixes intent and prevents optimization.

Structure by:

- campaigns separated by intent, product, or audience;
- ad groups tightly themed around a single intent;
- keywords, ads, and landing pages aligned within each ad group;
- budget control at the level where decisions matter;
- clean separation of brand, competitor, and generic performance.

When an ad group mixes high- and low-intent keywords, the performance data blends and you cannot optimize either well.

### Manage Auction Economics, Not Just Position

Paid search is an auction. Winning the top position at any cost destroys profitability. Understand what you are actually paying for.

Track:

- cost per qualified action, not just cost per click or impression;
- the relationship between bid, Quality Score, and actual cost;
- conversion rate and value by keyword and match type;
- the diminishing returns of higher positions;
- the break-even CPA or target ROAS that keeps spend profitable.

A higher position that doubles cost for a small conversion lift is usually a bad trade. Optimize for profit and volume, not rank.

### Use Match Types And Negatives Deliberately

Match types and negative keywords are the primary tools for controlling who you pay to reach. Use them with intent.

Apply:

- exact and phrase match for high-confidence, high-value terms;
- broad match only with close monitoring and strong negatives;
- negative keywords to exclude irrelevant, low-value, or off-target queries;
- regular search-term review to find new negatives and opportunities;
- audience and geo exclusions to avoid wasted spend.

Unchecked broad match and weak negatives are the fastest way to spend budget on the wrong people.

### Write Copy That Earns The Right Click

Ad copy should attract the right searcher and repel the wrong one. The goal is qualified clicks, not maximum clicks.

Write to:

- match the query and intent directly;
- communicate the specific value and differentiator;
- set expectations the landing page can fulfill;
- include a clear, stage-appropriate call to action;
- use ad assets and extensions to increase relevance and real estate.

An ad that overpromises to win clicks sets up a landing-page mismatch and wastes the spend. Honest, specific copy improves both click quality and Quality Score.

### Separate Brand, Competitor, And Generic Performance

These three buckets have completely different economics and should never be evaluated together.

Report separately:

- brand campaigns, which are usually high ROI but partly cannibalize organic;
- competitor campaigns, which are strategic but lower converting;
- generic or category campaigns, which build consideration but are expensive;
- remarketing and audience layers that cut across them.

Blending them hides the real performance of each and leads to misallocated budget.

## Common Traps

### Bidding Broad Without Negatives

Broad match without strong negatives spends budget on irrelevant queries that look related but have no intent.

### Optimizing For Clicks Not Outcomes

Maximizing click-through rate or traffic volume can increase spend on low-quality clicks that never convert.

### Landing Page Mismatch

Sending traffic to a page that does not match the query or ad promise guarantees wasted spend.

### Ignoring Quality Score

Low Quality Score raises costs and lowers position, making efficient volume impossible regardless of bid.

### Blending Brand And Generic

Reporting brand and generic together inflates apparent performance and hides how generic terms really perform.

### Chasing Position Over Profit

Paying whatever it takes for the top spot often destroys the unit economics that made the channel worthwhile.

### Set And Forget

Paid search decays as competitors, queries, and costs shift. Accounts that are not actively managed drift toward waste.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Bids are differentiated by intent and business value, with brand, competitor, and generic separated.
- [ ] Every keyword sends traffic to a landing page that matches the query and ad promise.
- [ ] The account is structured into tightly themed ad groups that allow clean optimization.
- [ ] Decisions are based on cost per qualified action and ROAS, not clicks or position alone.
- [ ] Match types and negative keywords are actively managed through regular search-term review.
- [ ] Ad copy attracts qualified clicks and repels irrelevant ones, with expectations the page can fulfill.
- [ ] Quality Score and its drivers of relevance, experience, and expected click-through are monitored.
- [ ] Brand, competitor, and generic performance are reported separately, not blended.
- [ ] The break-even CPA or target ROAS is known and used to keep spend profitable.
- [ ] The account is actively managed and refreshed rather than set and forgotten.
