# Ecommerce Storefront And Conversion

An ecommerce storefront is not a catalog of products; it is a sequence of decisions the shopper is guided through, where every extra second, ambiguity, or missing signal costs revenue. Conversion fails when product detail pages (PDPs) answer internal questions instead of shopper questions, when search returns irrelevant results, when checkout demands information or accounts that shoppers refuse to give, when trust is assumed rather than earned, or when mobile is treated as a smaller version of desktop rather than a different context. Most lost revenue is not lost at the ad; it is lost in the silent friction between interest and completed purchase.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do we improve our PDPs", "why is our checkout conversion low", "how do we improve site search", "how do we build trust on the site", or "how do we optimize for mobile". The goal is to prevent the agent from suggesting cosmetic changes while the real conversion killers are structural friction and missing information.

## Core Rules

### Optimize The PDP Around Shopper Questions

The product detail page is where the purchase decision is made or lost. A strong PDP answers the questions a shopper has in the order they have them, not the questions the merchandiser finds easy to fill in.

Build PDPs by addressing:

- what the product is and what it does, stated in the first viewport;
- whether it fits or suits the shopper's specific situation (size, compatibility, use case);
- why this option over alternatives, with differentiators made concrete;
- what it actually looks like, through imagery and video that show real use;
- whether other buyers trust it, through reviews and ratings;
- what it costs, when it arrives, and what the return path is.

Ask whether a shopper who has never seen the product could confidently buy it after one scroll. If key decision information is buried below the fold or absent, the PDP is leaking conversions regardless of traffic quality.

### Make Search And Merchandising Serve Intent

On-site search is where high-intent shoppers go, and it is often the worst-performing part of a store. Returning "no results", returning irrelevant results, or ignoring synonyms and intent destroys conversion from the most ready-to-buy visitors.

Improve search and merchandising by:

- handling synonyms, misspellings, and natural language queries;
- returning relevant results even when the exact term is absent;
- merchandising results by relevance, margin, and inventory, not just alphabetical order;
- offering filters and facets that match how shoppers actually narrow choice;
- surfacing alternatives when a product is out of stock.

A shopper who searches has declared intent. Failing them at search wastes the most valuable traffic on the site.

### Remove Friction From Cart And Checkout

Checkout is where the most committed shoppers are lost. Every unnecessary field, forced account creation, surprise cost, or slow step is a point of abandonment.

Reduce checkout friction by:

- allowing guest checkout and never forcing account creation before purchase;
- minimizing form fields to what is strictly required;
- showing all costs, including shipping and taxes, as early as possible;
- offering fast payment options that skip manual entry;
- removing distractions and navigation that pull shoppers out of the flow;
- making error states clear, specific, and easy to correct.

Ask whether every step and field in checkout exists to serve the shopper or to serve internal data collection. Surprise fees and forced accounts are among the largest preventable causes of abandonment.

### Earn Trust Explicitly

Online shoppers cannot touch the product or meet the seller, so trust must be constructed from signals. Trust is not assumed by the brand; it is built by evidence on the page.

Build trust with:

- visible reviews and ratings, including critical ones, presented honestly;
- clear return, refund, and shipping policies stated before purchase;
- security and payment credibility signals;
- accurate delivery estimates;
- responsive customer service contact options;
- consistency between the promise on the ad and the experience on the page.

Hiding or deleting negative reviews destroys trust more than the reviews themselves. Shoppers expect to see some criticism; its absence reads as manipulation.

### Treat Mobile As A Different Context

Mobile is not a smaller desktop. Shoppers on mobile have different attention, different input methods, and different constraints. Treating mobile as a scaled-down desktop produces a poor experience on the majority of traffic.

Optimize mobile by:

- prioritizing thumb-reachable actions and large tap targets;
- minimizing typing through autofill, stored payment, and account data;
- ensuring imagery and text are legible without zooming;
- reducing page weight and requests for slower connections;
- testing the full purchase flow on real devices, not just resized browsers.

### Prioritize Site Speed As A Conversion Feature

Speed is not a technical nicety; it is a conversion lever. Slow pages increase abandonment at every step, and the effect is largest on mobile and at checkout.

Improve speed by:

- measuring load and interaction time on real devices and connections;
- reducing image, script, and third-party weight;
- prioritizing above-the-fold rendering;
- removing unused trackers and tags that delay interaction;
- treating speed regressions as conversion bugs, not backend concerns.

### Use Imagery And Video To Reduce Uncertainty

Shoppers buy when uncertainty is low. Imagery and video reduce uncertainty about fit, scale, material, and use in ways text cannot.

Use visuals by:

- showing the product in real use and at real scale;
- including multiple angles, close-ups, and context shots;
- using video for products where motion, fit, or assembly matters;
- ensuring visuals load quickly and are mobile-optimized;
- avoiding stock imagery that misrepresents the actual product.

## Common Traps

### PDP Built Around Internal Data, Not Shopper Questions

Specs and feature lists that answer the merchandiser's questions, not the shopper's, leave the actual decision unanswered.

### Search That Fails High-Intent Shoppers

"No results" or irrelevant results waste the most ready-to-buy traffic and send them to competitors.

### Forced Account Creation At Checkout

Requiring an account before purchase is one of the largest preventable causes of abandonment.

### Surprise Costs Late In The Flow

Shipping and taxes revealed at the final step trigger abandonment after the shopper has invested effort.

### Deleting Or Hiding Negative Reviews

Removing criticism reads as manipulation and erodes the trust that reviews are meant to build.

### Mobile Treated As Scaled-Down Desktop

Ignoring thumb reach, typing burden, and connection speed produces a poor experience on the majority of traffic.

### Speed Treated As A Backend Concern

Slow pages silently increase abandonment at every step, especially on mobile and at checkout.

## Self-Check

- [ ] PDPs answer shopper decision questions in priority order, not internal data fields.
- [ ] On-site search handles synonyms, misspellings, and intent, and surfaces relevant alternatives.
- [ ] Checkout allows guest purchase and removes non-essential fields and distractions.
- [ ] All costs, including shipping and taxes, are shown as early as possible.
- [ ] Trust signals, including honest reviews and clear policies, are visible before purchase.
- [ ] Mobile is optimized for thumb reach, minimal typing, and real connection speeds.
- [ ] Site speed is measured on real devices and treated as a conversion lever, not a backend metric.
- [ ] Imagery and video reduce uncertainty about fit, scale, and real use.
- [ ] Negative reviews are presented honestly rather than hidden or deleted.
- [ ] The full purchase flow has been tested on real mobile devices, not just resized browsers.
