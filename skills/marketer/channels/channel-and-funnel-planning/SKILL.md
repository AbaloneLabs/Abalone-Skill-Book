# Channel And Funnel Planning

A channel plan is not a list of places to post. A funnel is not a decorative diagram. The marketer must decide how a specific audience will discover the offer, understand it, trust it, take the next step, convert, and continue receiving value. Channel and funnel planning fails when each tactic is optimized separately without understanding the customer journey.

Use this skill before choosing channels, planning acquisition, designing landing paths, reviewing conversion flows, or connecting marketing to sales, product, or customer success. The goal is to prevent the agent from recommending disconnected tactics.

## Core Rules

### Match Channel To Audience Behavior

Start with where the audience already pays attention and what mindset they have there.

Ask:

- where they search;
- what communities they trust;
- what media they consume;
- who influences them;
- what events or moments trigger need;
- whether they prefer self-serve or sales-assisted buying;
- whether the purchase is urgent, considered, emotional, technical, or regulated.

Do not choose channels because they are fashionable. Choose channels because they fit audience behavior and purchase context.

### Assign Each Channel A Role

Channels can serve different roles:

- awareness;
- education;
- credibility;
- comparison;
- lead capture;
- conversion;
- retargeting;
- retention;
- advocacy;
- partner leverage.

One channel rarely does everything. Search may capture intent. Social may build familiarity. Email may nurture. Events may build trust. Sales may close. Product onboarding may activate. State the role before judging performance.

### Design The Next Step

Every touchpoint should have a clear next step appropriate to the audience's stage. The next step may be reading, subscribing, downloading, requesting a demo, trying the product, buying, booking, sharing, or returning.

Check:

- is the next step obvious;
- is it too much commitment for the current stage;
- does the landing experience match the promise;
- is the form length appropriate;
- does the follow-up happen quickly;
- is the handoff clear;
- can the user recover if interrupted.

The funnel often breaks between interest and next action.

### Identify Friction And Trust Gaps

Conversion problems are not always traffic problems. Prospects may hesitate because of unclear pricing, weak proof, risk, missing comparison, lack of authority, poor page speed, complex forms, confusing trial, or sales delay.

Map friction by stage:

- awareness: message not relevant;
- consideration: value unclear;
- evaluation: proof missing;
- conversion: form or pricing friction;
- activation: product fails to deliver quick value;
- retention: expectation mismatch.

Fix the stage that actually leaks, not the stage that is easiest to measure.

### Connect Marketing And Sales

For sales-assisted funnels, marketing should not stop at lead generation. Define qualification, routing, follow-up expectations, sales enablement, feedback loop, and lead quality criteria.

Clarify:

- what counts as a qualified lead;
- which data sales needs;
- response time expectation;
- ownership of nurture;
- how rejected leads are classified;
- how win/loss feedback returns to marketing;
- how content supports objections.

Volume without quality can damage sales trust.

### Consider Retention And Expansion

Acquisition channels shape customer expectations. A channel that brings poorly matched customers may increase churn, refunds, support burden, or low activation.

Include:

- onboarding path;
- expectation set by ads and pages;
- retention signal;
- customer education;
- referral or advocacy;
- upsell or expansion path;
- support load.

Funnel planning should not end at conversion.

### Measure By Stage And Segment

Aggregate funnel metrics can hide the real issue. Break down by channel, audience, campaign, landing page, device, geography, stage, and lead quality where useful.

Track:

- reach;
- click or visit quality;
- conversion rate;
- cost per qualified action;
- lead quality;
- activation;
- retention;
- revenue or margin;
- payback period;
- guardrails such as complaints, unsubscribes, refunds, or churn.

Attribution is imperfect. Use it to learn, not to pretend every dollar has exact causality.

## Common Traps

### Channel Hopping Too Quickly

Channels take learning. Abandoning a channel after weak first execution may prevent learning. But continuing without a clear hypothesis also wastes money.

### Optimizing For Top-Of-Funnel Metrics

Cheap traffic can produce poor customers. Judge channels by the role they are meant to play and downstream quality.

### Landing Page Mismatch

If the ad promises one thing and the landing page emphasizes another, users lose trust.

### Ignoring Slow-Burn Channels

SEO, community, partnerships, brand, and content may not show immediate conversion but can build durable acquisition if aligned with strategy.

### Too Many Channels Too Early

Small teams can dilute effort by trying every channel. Focus enough to learn.

### No Feedback From Sales Or Product

Marketing data alone may miss lead quality, onboarding failure, support burden, and customer mismatch.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Channel choices are based on audience behavior, trust sources, buying context, and trigger moments.
- [ ] Each channel has a defined role in awareness, education, credibility, conversion, retention, or advocacy.
- [ ] Each touchpoint has an appropriate next step for the audience's stage.
- [ ] Landing pages, forms, follow-up, and handoffs match the promise made by the channel.
- [ ] Friction and trust gaps are mapped by funnel stage.
- [ ] Sales-assisted funnels define qualification, routing, response time, feedback, and lead quality.
- [ ] Retention, onboarding, support burden, and customer fit are considered after conversion.
- [ ] Metrics are segmented by stage, channel, audience, and quality rather than only aggregate volume.
- [ ] Attribution uncertainty is acknowledged.
- [ ] The plan avoids recommending disconnected tactics without a coherent customer journey.
