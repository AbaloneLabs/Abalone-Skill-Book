# Advertising Claims And Ethics

Marketing can persuade without deceiving. A marketer must check whether claims are truthful, clear, evidence-backed, fairly qualified, and consistent with what customers actually experience. This is not only a legal concern; misleading marketing damages trust, increases refunds and complaints, creates support burden, and weakens long-term brand equity.

Use this skill before publishing ads, landing pages, product claims, endorsements, influencer posts, testimonials, reviews, pricing pages, comparison claims, guarantees, health or financial claims, environmental claims, urgency claims, or promotional offers. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing persuasive copy that overstates, hides, or manipulates.

## Core Rules

### Identify Every Claim

Claims are not only sentences with numbers. A claim can be express or implied through headline, image, comparison, testimonial, design, omission, or context.

Look for claims about:

- performance;
- price;
- savings;
- speed;
- ease;
- safety;
- health;
- income;
- environmental impact;
- popularity;
- scarcity;
- availability;
- guarantee;
- customer satisfaction;
- compatibility;
- security or privacy.

If a reasonable customer would take away a message, treat it as a claim even if the words are indirect.

### Require Evidence Before Publishing

Claims should be backed before they run, not justified afterward. The strength of evidence should match the strength and risk of the claim.

Ask:

- What exactly is being claimed?
- What evidence supports it?
- Is the evidence current?
- Does it apply to the advertised audience and context?
- Are limitations disclosed clearly?
- Would a typical customer experience this result?
- Does the claim require scientific, technical, financial, or legal substantiation?

Do not let "marketing language" become an excuse for unsupported factual claims.

### Make Disclosures Clear And Close

Disclosures should help customers understand the claim before they act. They should not hide material conditions in tiny text, distant footnotes, hover-only elements, or separate pages.

Review:

- price conditions;
- subscription renewal;
- trial limits;
- shipping or fees;
- eligibility;
- typical results;
- material connections;
- sponsored content;
- affiliate links;
- data use;
- important limitations.

Disclosures cannot usually fix a headline that directly contradicts reality. If the main message is misleading, rewrite it.

### Treat Testimonials And Endorsements Carefully

Testimonials, reviews, influencer posts, and endorsements can imply that results are typical or independent. They need honesty and disclosure.

Check:

- the endorsement reflects genuine experience;
- material connections are disclosed;
- compensation, free product, employment, family relationship, or affiliate incentives are visible;
- atypical results are not presented as normal without context;
- reviews are not fake, suppressed selectively, or manipulated;
- the endorser is not making claims the marketer cannot support.

Influencer content should not hide the commercial relationship.

### Avoid Manipulative Urgency And Scarcity

Urgency can be legitimate when it is true. It becomes deceptive when countdowns, stock claims, deadlines, limited seats, or "only today" messages are fabricated or recycled.

Ask:

- Is the scarcity real?
- Is the deadline real?
- What happens after the timer ends?
- Are conditions clear?
- Does the design pressure users into a decision they cannot evaluate?

Urgency should inform, not trap.

### Align Claims With Product Reality

Marketing should match actual delivery. If onboarding, support, pricing, cancellation, product behavior, or customer success cannot deliver the promise, the claim creates downstream damage.

Before publishing, confirm:

- product can perform as described;
- sales understands limits;
- support can answer questions;
- pricing and terms match copy;
- screenshots are current;
- examples are representative;
- customer success can deliver promised outcomes.

### Consider Sensitive Audiences And High-Stakes Claims

Claims involving health, money, employment, children, education, safety, privacy, security, housing, credit, or vulnerable users need stronger care. The harm from misleading claims is higher, and additional rules may apply.

If the campaign touches high-stakes areas, involve appropriate legal, compliance, medical, financial, or subject-matter review.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Puffery Covers Everything

General praise may be acceptable, but specific factual claims need support. "Best for reducing tax risk" or "secure by default" is not mere style if customers rely on it.

### Hiding Conditions In Fine Print

Important conditions should be noticeable and understandable. Fine print that contradicts the main claim is a warning sign.

### Cherry-Picking Reviews

Featuring only unusually positive results can mislead if customers infer typical experience.

### Using Old Data

Claims based on outdated benchmarks, old pricing, old screenshots, or past customer results can become misleading.

### Letting Design Mislead

Visual hierarchy, button labels, defaults, and comparison tables can imply things copy does not say directly.

### Copying Competitor Claims

Competitors may be wrong or operating under different evidence. Your claim still needs support.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Express and implied claims are identified across text, visuals, layout, testimonials, comparisons, and omissions.
- [ ] Each factual claim has evidence before publication.
- [ ] Evidence matches the audience, context, timeframe, and strength of the claim.
- [ ] Disclosures are clear, close to the claim, visible before action, and not contradicted by the main message.
- [ ] Testimonials, reviews, endorsements, affiliate relationships, sponsorships, and influencer connections are honest and disclosed.
- [ ] Scarcity, urgency, discounts, trials, guarantees, and pricing conditions are real and clearly explained.
- [ ] Product, support, sales, onboarding, pricing, screenshots, and terms match the marketing promise.
- [ ] High-stakes claims involving health, money, safety, privacy, children, education, credit, or security receive appropriate review.
- [ ] The copy avoids manipulating users through hidden conditions, misleading design, or unsupported fear.
- [ ] The final message protects long-term trust, not only short-term conversion.
