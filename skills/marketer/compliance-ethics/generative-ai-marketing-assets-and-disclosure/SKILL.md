# Generative AI Marketing Assets And Disclosure

Generative AI lets a marketing team produce images, video, voice, copy, and entire synthetic personas at a speed and volume that was impossible a few years ago. That power is exactly why it is a judgment problem. An AI-generated face used as a testimonial, a synthetic voice reading a customer quote, an AI image of a product that does not look like the real thing, an AI influencer with no disclosure — each of these can drive short-term engagement while quietly destroying the trust that marketing exists to build. The technology makes fabrication cheap; the discipline of using it honestly is what separates a credible brand from one that gets caught manufacturing reality.

The core difficulty is that generative output sits on a spectrum, and the right treatment depends on what the asset implies about reality. An AI-generated abstract background is low risk because it implies nothing factual. An AI-generated "customer" giving a testimonial is high risk because it implies a real person had a real experience — which is a fabrication. A photorealistic AI image of a product can misrepresent what customers will receive. A synthetic voice cloned from a real person raises consent and identity questions. Agents tend to treat all AI output as "just content" and apply a single rule (disclose everything, or disclose nothing), when the honest answer depends on what the asset is being used to claim or imply, and what a reasonable person would believe they are seeing or hearing.

This skill is about the marketing judgment of producing and publishing generative-AI assets responsibly: when disclosure is required, how to disclose effectively, how to avoid misrepresentation, and how to manage the rights, consent, and brand-trust risks that synthetic media introduces. It is not about prompt engineering or tool selection. It complements the advertising-claims skill (truthfulness of claims) and the data-privacy skill (consent and personal data), focusing specifically on the problems generative AI creates.

## Core Rules

### Classify Each Asset By What It Implies About Reality

The right treatment of an AI-generated asset depends on what a reasonable viewer would infer from it. Before publishing, classify the asset along this spectrum, because the classification drives the disclosure and review decision:

- **Purely decorative / abstract.** AI-generated backgrounds, textures, patterns, illustrations clearly not depicting reality. Low misrepresentation risk; minimal disclosure burden.
- **Depicting a product, place, or outcome.** AI images or video showing what the product looks like, what results it produces, or a location. Higher risk: if the depiction differs from reality, it misleads. Must match the actual product, or be clearly marked as illustrative.
- **Depicting people (synthetic personas, AI models, AI influencers).** Faces, bodies, voices, or full personas that do not represent real, consenting individuals. High risk: viewers assume the people are real, so testimonials, endorsements, or "customer stories" from synthetic people are fabrications.
- **Cloning or modifying a real person.** Using a real person's likeness, voice, or identity via AI (a cloned voice, a face swap, a digital double). Highest risk: consent, identity, and rights questions dominate, and misrepresentation can defame or deceive.

An asset that moves up this spectrum carries more obligation, not less. A decorative AI texture and an AI "customer" testimonial are not the same problem and must not get the same treatment.

### Disclose When The Asset Implies Something That Is Not Real

The purpose of disclosure is to prevent a reasonable person from being misled about what they are seeing or hearing. Disclosure is required when the AI-generated nature of the asset, if hidden, would cause a viewer to believe something false — that a synthetic person is real, that an AI voice is a genuine customer, that a generated image is a photograph of the actual product, that an AI influencer is a human being.

- **Synthetic people and personas must be disclosed.** If a face, voice, or persona is AI-generated and presented as a person, the audience must be able to tell. An AI influencer, an AI "customer," an AI spokesperson — each implies a real person and must be labeled.
- **Product depictions must match reality or be marked illustrative.** If an AI image shows the product, what customers receive must match it. If it cannot match (a concept, a rendering, an enhanced version), mark it clearly as illustrative or a concept, not as the real product.
- **Cloned or modified real people require consent and usually disclosure.** Using a real person's voice or likeness via AI requires that person's consent, and the audience often must be told the depiction is AI-generated or modified.

Disclosure is not a punishment for using AI; it is the mechanism that keeps the audience's beliefs aligned with reality. When in doubt about whether an asset misleads, the safer choice is to disclose.

### Make Disclosure Effective, Not Bury It

A disclosure that a reasonable person does not see or understand is not a disclosure. Effective disclosure is clear, prominent, and understandable at the moment the impression is formed:

- **Place it where the impression is formed.** A disclosure buried in a caption on an image-heavy platform, in a video description nobody reads, or in fine print below the fold does not reach the viewer while they are forming the belief. Put it on or near the asset, visible in the same view.
- **Use plain, specific language.** "AI-generated," "synthetic image," "this persona is not a real person," "voice generated with AI" are clearer than vague terms like "enhanced" or "digital." Name what is artificial.
- **Match the medium.** A video needs on-screen or spoken disclosure; an image needs a visible label; audio needs a spoken disclosure. A text disclosure does not help a viewer watching a muted video.
- **Do not contradict the disclosure elsewhere.** If the asset is labeled AI-generated in one place but presented as authentic elsewhere (a synthetic testimonial quoted as a real customer review), the contradiction defeats the disclosure.

The test is whether a typical viewer, encountering the asset as it is normally consumed, would understand what is real and what is not.

### Never Use Synthetic People For Testimonials, Endorsements, Or Reviews

The clearest line: do not present AI-generated people as real customers giving genuine testimonials, endorsements, or reviews. A testimonial's entire value is that a real person had a real experience and chose to share it. A synthetic testimonial fabricates the person, the experience, or both, and no disclosure fully repairs it — a label saying "this customer is AI-generated" simply reveals that the testimonial is invented, which means it is not a testimonial at all. The same applies to AI-generated reviews, ratings, or social proof. If the experience is not real, do not present it as social proof; generate honest, clearly illustrative content instead, or use genuine customer voices with permission.

### Match AI Product Depictions To Reality

An AI image or video of a product is a claim about what the product looks like and does. If customers order based on the depiction and receive something different, the asset misled them, regardless of intent. For product depictions:

- **The depiction must match the shipped product** in appearance, scale, color, features, and results, or the differences must be immaterial.
- **Enhancements that change expectations are risky.** AI that makes food look more appetizing, a physical product more flawless, or results more dramatic than typical creates a gap between expectation and reality that drives complaints, returns, and trust loss.
- **Mark concepts and renderings clearly.** If the image is a concept, a prototype, an illustration of an intended outcome, or a stylized rendering, label it as such rather than presenting it as a photograph of the real thing.

The rule is the same as for any product imagery: it must not overstate. AI just makes overstating dangerously easy.

### Secure Rights And Consent Before Using Real Likeness Or Voice

Generative AI makes it trivial to clone a real person's face or voice, and that triviality is precisely why the consent bar must be explicit. Before using AI to replicate or modify a real person's identity:

- **Get explicit consent** from the person whose likeness, voice, or identity is being used, for the specific use and scope. Consent to appear in one campaign is not consent to be cloned into another.
- **Respect the rights of third parties** whose work the model may have been trained on or whose style is being mimicked. An AI asset that reproduces a living artist's distinctive style, a celebrity's likeness, or copyrighted characters carries rights risk that does not vanish because a model generated it.
- **Document the provenance and permissions.** Know what model generated the asset, what inputs were used, and what rights clearances exist, so you can answer if the asset is challenged.

"An AI generated it" is not a defense to using someone's identity or work without permission. The ease of generation increases, not decreases, the need to confirm you have the right to publish.

### Set A Policy Before Production, Not After A Problem

Ad-hoc decisions made per asset lead to inconsistency and gaps. Establish a team or brand policy for generative AI in marketing before assets pile up, covering:

- **What requires disclosure** and how disclosure is executed per channel and medium.
- **What is prohibited** (synthetic testimonials, cloned likeness without consent, product depictions that misrepresent).
- **Who reviews and approves** AI assets before publication, and what the review checks (misrepresentation, rights, disclosure, brand fit).
- **How AI assets are labeled and archived** for provenance, so the team can later identify which assets were AI-generated and under what terms.

A policy turns a series of risky one-off judgments into a consistent, reviewable practice. Without one, each asset is an independent gamble, and the gaps only surface as incidents.

### Treat Authenticity As A Long-Term Asset, Not A Constraint

The temptation with generative AI is to optimize each asset for engagement and treat disclosure and accuracy as friction. The reframe is that audience trust is the asset marketing is protecting, and synthetic media that misleads trades long-term trust for short-term metrics. Once an audience discovers that a brand's people, testimonials, or product images were fabricated, the credibility loss extends to every future claim. The disciplined posture is to use generative AI where it adds genuine value (speed, scale, creative range, accessibility) without ever letting it manufacture the reality the brand is asking people to trust.

## Common Traps

### Treating All AI Output As "Just Content"

Applying a single rule — disclose everything, or disclose nothing — to all AI assets, regardless of what each implies. A decorative AI background and an AI "customer" testimonial are not the same risk and need different treatment. The fix is to classify each asset by what it implies about reality and apply disclosure and review accordingly.

### The Undisclosed AI Influencer Or Synthetic Persona

Building an AI-generated persona and presenting it as a real person — an AI influencer, an AI spokesperson, an AI "customer" — without disclosure, so the audience forms a parasocial relationship with and trust in someone who does not exist. The fix is to disclose synthetic personas clearly and to never use them for testimonials or endorsements, where the entire value is real human experience.

### Disclosure That Nobody Sees

Placing the AI disclosure in a caption, a video description, or fine print that the typical viewer never encounters while forming their impression, then claiming the asset was disclosed. The fix is to place disclosure where the impression is formed — on the image, on-screen or spoken in the video — using plain language, matched to the medium.

### AI Product Imagery That Overstates

Using AI to make a product look better, bigger, more flawless, or more effective than it is, creating a gap between the depiction and what customers receive. The fix is to ensure AI product depictions match reality, and to label concepts, renderings, and illustrative depictions clearly rather than presenting them as photographs.

### Cloning A Real Person's Voice Or Face Without Consent

Using AI to replicate a real person's likeness or voice because the tool makes it easy, without securing explicit consent for the specific use. The fix is to treat any use of a real person's identity via AI as requiring explicit, scoped consent and rights clearance, regardless of how the asset was produced.

### Synthetic Testimonials And Reviews

Generating AI "customers" to deliver testimonials, reviews, or ratings, treating disclosure as sufficient. The fix is to recognize that a synthetic testimonial is a fabrication, not a testimonial, and that disclosure merely reveals the fabrication — use genuine customer voices with permission, or clearly illustrative content, instead.

### No Policy, So Every Asset Is A Gamble

Letting each team member make independent disclosure and rights decisions per asset, producing inconsistent treatment and uncovered risks. The fix is a written policy covering disclosure triggers, prohibitions, review and approval, and provenance archiving, so AI use is consistent and reviewable.

### Trusting The Output Without Reviewing For Misrepresentation

Publishishing AI-generated assets directly because they look good, without reviewing whether they misrepresent the product, misuse a likeness, reproduce protected work, or require disclosure. The fix is a human review step before publication that checks misrepresentation, rights, disclosure, and brand fit — the same review any asset would get, applied to assets that are uniquely easy to get wrong.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each AI-generated asset was classified by what it implies about reality (decorative, product depiction, synthetic person, cloned real person), and the disclosure and review decision matches that classification.
- [ ] Disclosure is present wherever the AI nature, if hidden, would mislead a reasonable viewer — especially for synthetic personas, AI voices, and AI-generated people presented as real.
- [ ] Disclosure is effective: placed where the impression is formed, in plain specific language, matched to the medium, and not contradicted elsewhere.
- [ ] No synthetic people are used as testimonials, endorsements, or reviews; genuine customer voices are used with permission, or content is clearly illustrative.
- [ ] AI product depictions match the real product in appearance and results, or are clearly labeled as concepts, renderings, or illustrative.
- [ ] Any use of a real person's likeness or voice via AI has explicit, scoped consent, and third-party rights (style, likeness, copyrighted material) were checked.
- [ ] A written policy governs generative AI use: disclosure triggers, prohibitions, review and approval ownership, and provenance archiving — so treatment is consistent, not ad hoc.
- [ ] A human review before publication checked each asset for misrepresentation, rights, disclosure, and brand fit, rather than publishing AI output on trust.
- [ ] The decision protects long-term audience trust, recognizing that fabricated reality trades credibility for short-term engagement.
