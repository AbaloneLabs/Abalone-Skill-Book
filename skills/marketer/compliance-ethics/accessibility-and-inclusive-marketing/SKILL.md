# Accessibility And Inclusive Marketing

Marketing that excludes is marketing that fails its audience and its business. It fails when content is inaccessible to people with disabilities, when imagery and language exclude or stereotype, when accessibility is treated as a compliance checkbox rather than a design principle, or when inclusion is performative rather than genuine. Accessibility and inclusion expand reach, build trust, and reflect respect for the full audience. They are not optional refinements; they are part of whether the work is complete.

Use this skill before creating content, designing experiences, selecting imagery, or writing copy. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing marketing that silently excludes audiences through avoidable barriers or bias.

## Core Rules

### Treat Accessibility As A Requirement, Not A Refinement

Accessibility is not a nice-to-have for after launch. It is a requirement of the work.

Treat as required:

- digital content usable by people with disabilities;
- alternatives for visual and audio content;
- compatibility with assistive technology;
- sufficient contrast, readable type, and clear structure;
- keyboard and screen-reader navigability.

Inaccessible content excludes a significant audience and may violate legal obligations. Build accessibility in from the start, not as a retrofit.

### Provide Alternatives For Non-Text Content

Visual and audio content excludes without alternatives. Provide them.

Provide:

- alt text for meaningful images;
- captions and transcripts for audio and video;
- audio descriptions for visual-only video content;
- text alternatives for infographics and complex visuals;
- descriptive link text, not bare URLs or "click here."

A video without captions excludes the deaf and hard-of-hearing community. An image without alt text excludes screen-reader users. Alternatives are how inclusion happens.

### Write And Design For Cognitive Accessibility

Accessibility includes cognitive and neurological considerations. Design for them.

Design for:

- plain language and clear structure;
- predictable navigation and consistent patterns;
- sufficient time for reading and interaction;
- minimal distraction and avoidance of seizure-triggering patterns;
- clear error prevention and recovery.

Complex jargon, chaotic layouts, and time pressure exclude people with cognitive, learning, or attention differences. Clarity and predictability include them.

### Use Inclusive Language And Avoid Stereotypes

Language shapes who feels welcome. Use inclusive, respectful language.

Use:

- gender-neutral language where appropriate;
- people-first or identity-first language per community preference;
- culturally respectful and non-appropriative terms;
- language that avoids stereotypes and assumptions;
- the terms communities use for themselves.

Language that stereotypes or excludes signals that some audiences are not welcome or not understood. Inclusive language invites the full audience.

### Represent The Full Audience In Imagery

Imagery communicates who the brand is for. Represent the audience authentically.

Represent by:

- diverse imagery across race, gender, age, ability, and body;
- authentic representation, not tokenism;
- avoiding stereotypes and cliches;
- imagery that reflects real people and real situations;
- sensitivity to cultural and religious considerations.

Imagery that always shows the same demographic tells others they are not the audience. Authentic representation expands who sees themselves in the brand.

### Test With Real Users, Including Those With Disabilities

Assumptions about accessibility and inclusion are unreliable. Test with real users.

Test with:

- users who rely on assistive technology;
- users from the communities being represented;
- automated accessibility tools as a first check, not a substitute;
- real devices and real conditions;
- feedback channels that reach diverse users.

Automated tools catch a fraction of issues. Real users, including those with disabilities, reveal what tools cannot. Testing closes the gap between intention and reality.

### Make Inclusion Genuine, Not Performative

Token representation and surface inclusion are detected and resented. Be genuine.

Be genuine by:

- reflecting inclusion in the organization, not just the marketing;
- sustaining representation over time, not just during heritage months;
- consulting the communities being represented;
- backing representation with real commitment and action;
- avoiding appropriation and exploitation of culture.

Performative inclusion that is not backed by reality damages trust when exposed. Genuine inclusion is consistent, consulted, and sincere.

## Common Traps

### Accessibility As Checkbox

Treating accessibility as a compliance task rather than a design principle excludes users.

### Missing Alternatives

Visual and audio content without captions, transcripts, or alt text excludes audiences.

### Cognitive Exclusion

Complex language, chaotic layouts, and time pressure exclude people with cognitive differences.

### Stereotyping Language And Imagery

Stereotypes and assumptions signal that some audiences are not welcome or understood.

### Token Representation

Performative diversity without authenticity is detected and damages trust.

### Automated-Tool-Only Testing

Relying on automated checks without real user testing misses real barriers.

### Inclusion Only In Marketing

Representation in ads that the organization does not reflect is exposed as performative.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Accessibility is treated as a requirement built in from the start, not a retrofit.
- [ ] Non-text content has alternatives such as alt text, captions, transcripts, and descriptions.
- [ ] Language and design support cognitive accessibility through clarity and predictability.
- [ ] Language is inclusive, respectful, and free of stereotypes and assumptions.
- [ ] Imagery represents the full audience authentically, without tokenism.
- [ ] Content is tested with real users, including those who use assistive technology.
- [ ] Automated accessibility tools are used as a first check, not a substitute for user testing.
- [ ] Inclusion is genuine and sustained, backed by organizational reality, not just marketing.
- [ ] Communities being represented are consulted, and appropriation is avoided.
- [ ] Representation and accessibility are consistent over time, not limited to specific occasions.
