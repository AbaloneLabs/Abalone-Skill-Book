# Data Privacy And Consent

Privacy is both a legal obligation and a trust decision. Marketing fails on privacy when it collects data first and considers compliance later, when consent is buried or manufactured, when data is used in ways customers did not expect, or when tracking persists after customers have asked it to stop. The harm is compounded because privacy failures are discovered by customers, regulators, or the press, not by the team that created them. Good privacy practice collects only what is needed, obtains genuine consent, and treats customer data as a trust to protect.

Use this skill before designing data collection, building consent flows, setting up tracking, or using customer data for personalization or sharing. The goal is to prevent the agent from creating privacy exposure that destroys trust and invites legal liability.

## Core Rules

### Collect Only What You Need, And Justify It

Data collection carries obligation and risk. Collect only what serves a defined purpose.

Collect by:

- identifying the specific purpose each data point serves;
- removing data that is collected without a clear use;
- minimizing sensitivity by avoiding what is not essential;
- documenting the legal basis for each collection;
- retaining data only as long as needed.

Collecting data "because we might need it" accumulates risk without value. Purpose-driven, minimized collection is both compliant and trustworthy.

### Obtain Genuine Consent, Not Manufactured Consent

Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous. Do not manufacture it.

Ensure consent:

- is opt-in for sensitive and non-essential processing, not pre-ticked;
- is specific to each purpose, not bundled;
- is informed with clear, plain disclosure;
- is as easy to withdraw as to give;
- is not coerced by gating essential service.

Consent obtained through dark patterns or bundling is not genuine and does not withstand scrutiny. Honest consent protects both the customer and the company.

### Honor Preferences And Deletion Requests

Customers have the right to control their data, including stopping its use and deleting it. Honor these.

Honor:

- opt-out and unsubscribe requests promptly and completely;
- deletion and access requests within legal timelines;
- preference changes across all systems and channels;
- the intent of the request, not just the letter;
- the propagation of changes to partners and vendors.

A deletion request honored in one system but not another is a failure. Preferences must propagate across the full data ecosystem.

### Be Transparent About How Data Is Used

Customers deserve to know how their data is used. Be transparent.

Be transparent by:

- providing clear, accessible privacy notices;
- disclosing tracking, cookies, and sharing in plain language;
- explaining personalization and how it works;
- being honest about data shared with partners;
- avoiding buried disclosures customers will not find.

Transparency buried in legalese is not transparency. Clear disclosure lets customers make informed choices and builds trust.

### Manage Cookies And Tracking Responsibly

Tracking technologies carry privacy obligations. Manage them deliberately.

Manage:

- cookie consent banners that obtain real consent;
- the categorization of essential versus non-essential cookies;
- the removal of non-essential cookies before consent;
- the impact of browser changes and identifier deprecation;
- vendor and third-party tags that set cookies.

A cookie banner that loads all cookies regardless of consent is non-compliant. Responsible cookie management respects choice and adapts to a changing tracking landscape.

### Vet Data Sharing With Partners And Vendors

Data shared with partners and vendors is still your responsibility. Vet and govern it.

Govern:

- what data is shared and for what purpose;
- the partner's privacy and security practices;
- the contractual obligations and permitted uses;
- the customer's consent for the sharing;
- the audit and review of partner compliance.

Sharing data with a vendor does not transfer the obligation. The company remains accountable for how customer data is used, wherever it flows.

### Design For Privacy From The Start

Privacy is not a layer added before launch. Design it in from the start.

Design by:

- considering privacy at the planning of any data-using feature;
- involving legal and privacy early, not at the end;
- building consent and preference management into the system;
- defaulting to the most privacy-protective setting;
- testing the privacy experience with real users.

Privacy added late is expensive, incomplete, and risky. Privacy by design is cheaper, cleaner, and more trustworthy.

## Common Traps

### Collect First, Comply Later

Accumulating data without purpose creates risk and obligation without value.

### Manufactured Consent

Pre-ticked boxes, bundling, and dark patterns produce consent that does not withstand scrutiny.

### Ignoring Deletion And Preference Requests

Failing to propagate opt-out and deletion across systems violates trust and law.

### Buried Disclosures

Transparency hidden in legalese is not transparency and erodes trust when discovered.

### Non-Compliant Cookie Practices

Loading non-essential cookies before consent or ignoring banner choices is non-compliant.

### Ungoverned Data Sharing

Sharing data with vendors without vetting transfers risk without transferring accountability.

### Privacy As Afterthought

Adding privacy late is expensive and incomplete; it must be designed in from the start.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Each data point collected serves a defined purpose, with a documented legal basis.
- [ ] Consent is genuine, opt-in for non-essential processing, specific, informed, and easy to withdraw.
- [ ] Opt-out, unsubscribe, deletion, and access requests are honored promptly across all systems.
- [ ] Privacy notices and disclosures are clear, accessible, and in plain language.
- [ ] Cookie and tracking practices obtain real consent and respect user choices.
- [ ] Data sharing with partners and vendors is vetted, contracted, and consented.
- [ ] Privacy is designed in from the start of any data-using feature or campaign.
- [ ] Sensitive data is minimized and given heightened protection.
- [ ] Data retention is limited to what is needed, with disposal of expired data.
- [ ] The privacy experience is tested with real users, not just reviewed by legal.
