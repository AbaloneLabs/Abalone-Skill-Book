# B2B Demand Generation

B2B demand generation is the disciplined creation of qualified interest in a complex, multi-stakeholder purchase. It is not the generation of form fills, and it collapses when marketers confuse activity with outcomes. The work fails when demand gen is reduced to gated-asset lead capture that floods sales with unqualified names, when MQL and SQL definitions are fuzzy or unagreed between marketing and sales, when programs target individual leads rather than the buying committee that actually decides, or when the pursuit of volume erodes the quality that determines whether pipeline ever closes.

Use this skill before answering questions such as "how do we build a B2B demand gen engine", "what should we gate versus leave open", "how do we define MQL and SQL", or "should we run broad demand or account-based marketing". The goal is to prevent the agent from optimizing for lead count dashboards while the real engine of revenue, qualified pipeline, starves.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Demand Generation From Lead Generation

Lead generation captures contact details; demand generation creates genuine commercial interest. Confusing the two produces large databases of people who never buy.

Understand the difference:

- lead gen optimizes for form submissions and contact data;
- demand gen optimizes for awareness, consideration, and qualified intent;
- lead gen can be manufactured with gimmicks; demand gen must be earned with relevance;
- lead gen fills the top of a leaky funnel; demand gen builds the conviction that closes deals;
- lead gen is measured by count; demand gen is measured by pipeline, win rate, and revenue.

State explicitly which you are building. Most healthy programs do both, but the demand engine must lead, or the leads will never convert.

### Design Content Gating As A Strategic Choice

Gating is not a default; it is a tradeoff between data capture and reach. The wrong gating strategy starves demand in order to fill a database.

Decide gate strategy per asset:

- gate when the asset signals high buying intent and justifies sales follow-up;
- leave open when the goal is reach, education, and category creation;
- consider the friction cost: how many valuable readers you lose per lead captured;
- avoid gating commodity content that adds no value, which trains audiences to avoid you;
- align the gate's ask to the value: a deep benchmark can ask more than a checklist;
- use progressive profiling instead of re-asking the same questions;
- respect privacy and consent, because non-compliant data is a liability, not an asset.

A program that gates everything captures few leads and little demand. A program that gates nothing builds reach but no pipeline. The art is matching the gate to the intent signal.

### Define MQL And SQL With Sales Agreement

The handoff between marketing and sales is where most demand programs break. Fuzzy or unilateral stage definitions guarantee friction and wasted effort.

Define stages jointly:

- MQL: the threshold at which a lead shows enough fit and intent for marketing to route;
- SQL: the threshold at which sales accepts the lead as worth active pursuit;
- the specific behaviors, scores, and attributes that trigger each stage;
- the service-level agreement for follow-up time and attempts;
- the feedback loop when sales rejects leads, so definitions can be refined;
- the difference between a contact and an account-level qualification;
- the disqualification criteria, because saying no fast preserves sales capacity.

Definitions written by marketing alone and imposed on sales will be ignored. Definitions agreed and reviewed jointly become the operating system of pipeline.

### Market To The Buying Committee, Not The Individual

B2B purchases are made by committees, not lone buyers. Programs that target and score only one contact per account misread the real decision.

Account for committee dynamics:

- map the roles: champion, economic buyer, technical evaluator, end user, blocker, executive sponsor;
- understand that different roles need different evidence and messages;
- score engagement at the account level, not only the individual lead;
- recognize that a single hot contact may have no authority, while quiet execs decide;
- build content for each role's questions and risks;
- track committee coverage: how many roles at the target account are engaged;
- coordinate sales and marketing to surround the account rather than chase one person.

A lead scored as sales-ready because one person downloaded a lot may represent no real purchase intent. Committee-level engagement is the truer signal.

### Choose Account-Based Versus Broad Demand Deliberately

Account-based marketing and broad demand generation are complementary, not interchangeable. Each suits different segments and objectives.

Match the approach:

- one-to-one ABM for a tiny number of high-value accounts with bespoke programs;
- one-to-few ABM for clusters of accounts sharing an industry or pain;
- one-to-many ABM for targeted lists at scale with personalized layers;
- broad demand for category creation, reach, and filling the top of funnel;
- ABM where the deal size justifies the investment and the ICP is narrow;
- broad demand where the market is large and self-serve or lower-touch sales apply.

Forcing every account into ABM wastes money on accounts that will never buy large. Forcing enterprise deals through broad demand loses the personalization that closes them.

### Balance Lead Quality Against Volume

Volume is seductive because it fills dashboards. Quality is what produces revenue. The two must be balanced against the cost of sales capacity.

Manage the balance:

- set quality thresholds, not just volume targets;
- track lead-to-opportunity and opportunity-to-win rates by source;
- cut sources that produce volume but no pipeline, however cheap the leads;
- invest more in sources that produce fewer but higher-converting leads;
- consider the cost of sales time spent on bad leads, which is often hidden;
- use scoring and qualification to protect sales capacity for real opportunities;
- review the mix regularly as channels and buyer behavior shift.

A program celebrated for lead volume while opportunity creation stalls is failing in disguise. Revenue and pipeline are the true scoreboards.

### Instrument The Funnel End To End

Demand gen only improves when the whole funnel is visible and measured. Blind spots hide where pipeline leaks.

Instrument:

- reach and engagement by channel and segment;
- conversion from visitor to lead to MQL to SQL to opportunity to closed-won;
- velocity: time spent and drop-off at each stage;
- source attribution with a defensible model, not last-touch only;
- cohort analysis of leads by entry channel and their eventual revenue;
- the cost per qualified opportunity, not just cost per lead;
- the contribution of marketing to pipeline and revenue over realistic time horizons.

B2B cycles are long, so measure with patience and cohorts. Judging a demand program in its first weeks by closed revenue will always look like failure.

## Common Traps

### Equating Lead Count With Demand

Large lead volumes with no pipeline create a false sense of success and burn sales capacity chasing names that never convert.

### Gating Everything For Data

Gating commodity content captures few leads and little reach, training the audience to avoid your assets and starving real demand.

### MQL And SQL Defined In Isolation

Stage definitions created by marketing without sales agreement guarantee a broken handoff and mutual blame.

### Targeting Individuals, Not Committees

Scoring and pursuing one contact per account misses the real decision dynamics and overweights low-authority engagement.

### Wrong Approach For The Segment

Running expensive ABM on accounts that will never justify it, or forcing large deals through broad demand, misallocates budget and loses deals.

### Volume Over Quality

Optimizing the dashboard for lead count while opportunity creation and win rate decline is failure dressed as growth.

### Last-Touch Attribution In Long Cycles

Creditting only the final touch ignores the demand built earlier and leads to cutting the very programs that created the pipeline.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The program explicitly distinguishes demand generation from lead generation and prioritizes qualified interest over raw capture.
- [ ] Content gating is decided per asset based on intent signal and value, balancing data capture against reach, with privacy respected.
- [ ] MQL and SQL definitions are agreed jointly with sales, with clear triggers, SLAs, feedback loops, and disqualification criteria.
- [ ] Programs target and score at the account and committee level, with content tailored to each buying role.
- [ ] The account-based versus broad demand choice matches deal size, ICP narrowness, and sales motion for each segment.
- [ ] Lead quality thresholds and source-level conversion rates are tracked, and volume-only sources are cut.
- [ ] The funnel is instrumented end to end with velocity, cohort, and multi-touch attribution over realistic B2B time horizons.
- [ ] Success is measured by pipeline and revenue contribution, not lead count alone.
- [ ] Sales capacity cost of bad leads is considered in source evaluation.
- [ ] The program is reviewed against revenue outcomes, not activity metrics, with patience for long sales cycles.
