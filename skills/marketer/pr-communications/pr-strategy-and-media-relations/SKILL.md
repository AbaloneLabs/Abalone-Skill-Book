# PR Strategy And Media Relations

Public relations fails quietly. Press releases go unread, pitches are ignored, and coverage, when it comes, does not move the audiences that matter. PR fails when it pitches the company instead of a story, when it treats journalists as a distribution channel rather than people with audiences to serve, when it chases volume of coverage over coverage that influences the right readers, or when it has no strategy beyond "get us in the press." Good PR earns attention by being genuinely useful to journalists and their audiences.

Use this skill before developing a PR strategy, building media outreach, pitching stories, or planning an announcement. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing outreach that journalists ignore and coverage that informs no decision.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Audience And Outcome, Not The Coverage

PR exists to influence an audience toward an outcome. Define that before chasing press.

Define:

- the audience whose perception or action matters;
- the outcome the coverage should drive;
- the publications and journalists that reach them;
- the message that must land;
- how success will be measured beyond clip count.

Coverage in the wrong outlet, read by the wrong audience, accomplishes nothing. Start from whom you must reach and what they must conclude.

### Pitch Stories, Not The Company

Journalists write stories their readers care about, not promotions of your company. Pitch accordingly.

Pitch:

- a story with news value, conflict, data, or human interest;
- a trend or insight the journalist's readers need;
- access to sources, customers, or exclusive data;
- a genuine angle, not a disguised product release;
- relevance to the journalist's beat and past work.

A pitch that says "we launched a product" is an ad the journalist did not ask for. A pitch that offers a story their readers want earns coverage.

### Build Relationships Before You Need Them

Media relations is relational, not transactional. Invest before the ask.

Build by:

- reading and engaging with journalists' work over time;
- offering useful sources and data without always asking for coverage;
- respecting their deadlines and editorial independence;
- being a reliable, honest, and responsive resource;
- understanding their beat and what they cover.

A pitch from a stranger asking for a favor is easy to ignore. A pitch from a trusted source who has been helpful earns attention.

### Target Precisely, Not Broadcast

A media list built by blasting every address you can find produces spam and resentment. Target precisely.

Target:

- journalists who actually cover your space;
- outlets your audience reads and trusts;
- the specific angle each journalist would find relevant;
- the right timing for each outlet;
- exclusives or embargoes where they add value.

A precise pitch to ten relevant journalists beats a blast to a thousand who do not cover the topic. Precision respects the journalist and improves results.

### Respect Editorial Independence And Truth

Journalists are not your megaphone. Respect their independence and tell the truth.

Respect:

- that they will write their own perspective, not your script;
- that you cannot buy or trade for coverage;
- that honesty about limitations builds credibility;
- that exaggeration or spin destroys trust permanently;
- that off-the-record is a real commitment.

Treating journalists as a channel to be managed burns relationships. Treating them as independent professionals with audiences to serve earns lasting trust.

### Prepare For The Announcement, Not Just The Pitch

When coverage comes, the company must be ready. Prepare the full response.

Prepare:

- the landing experience for readers who click through;
- the sales and support teams for inbound interest;
- the executives for follow-up questions;
- the data and proof behind every claim;
- the monitoring of coverage and response to errors.

Coverage that drives interest the company is not ready to handle wastes the opportunity. Prepare the downstream before the pitch goes out.

### Measure Influence, Not Just Clips

Clip count flatters. Measure whether coverage influenced the audience and outcome.

Measure:

- whether coverage reached the target audience;
- whether it conveyed the intended message;
- whether it drove measurable outcomes such as traffic, leads, or perception shift;
- the share of voice versus competitors;
- the quality and credibility of the coverage.

A hundred clips in outlets no one reads is worth less than one in the publication your buyers trust. Measure influence, not volume.

## Common Traps

### Pitching The Company Not A Story

Asking journalists to promote your company rather than offering a story their readers want.

### Transactional Outreach

Asking for coverage from strangers without investing in relationships.

### Broadcast Blasting

Sending generic pitches to every journalist on a list produces spam and resentment.

### Treating Journalists As A Channel

Expecting journalists to deliver your script destroys trust and editorial goodwill.

### Exaggeration And Spin

Overstating claims for coverage destroys credibility when discovered.

### Clip Count As Success

Measuring volume of coverage rather than whether it influenced the right audience.

### Unprepared For Inbound

Coverage that drives interest the company cannot handle wastes the opportunity.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The PR strategy starts from a target audience and outcome, not coverage volume.
- [ ] Pitches offer stories with news value, not promotions of the company.
- [ ] Media relationships are built before they are needed, with genuine helpfulness.
- [ ] Outreach is precisely targeted to journalists who cover the space and reach the audience.
- [ ] Editorial independence and truth are respected, with no spin or bought coverage.
- [ ] The company is prepared to handle inbound interest when coverage appears.
- [ ] Success is measured by influence on the audience, not just clip count.
- [ ] Exclusives and embargoes are used where they add value to journalists.
- [ ] Coverage is monitored for accuracy and errors are addressed professionally.
- [ ] Claims in pitches are backed by data and proof the company can support.
