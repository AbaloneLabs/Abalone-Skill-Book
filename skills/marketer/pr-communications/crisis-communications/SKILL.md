# Crisis Communications

A crisis is when trust is most at risk and most recoverable. Crisis communications fails when the response is slow, when it minimizes or deflects responsibility, when it speaks in legal language instead of human language, when internal and external messages conflict, or when the focus is on protecting the company's image rather than addressing the harm. Good crisis response is fast, honest, empathetic, and focused on making things right, and it is prepared before the crisis occurs.

Use this skill before preparing a crisis plan, drafting a crisis response, coordinating stakeholders under pressure, or reviewing a crisis communication. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a response that deepens the damage through delay, denial, or defensiveness.

## Core Rules

### Prepare The Framework Before The Crisis

Crises do not wait for planning. Prepare the response framework in advance.

Prepare:

- a crisis response team with clear roles;
- escalation paths and decision authority;
- holding statements and templates ready to adapt;
- monitoring for early detection of issues;
- a single source of truth for approved messaging.

Organizations that improvise under pressure respond slowly and inconsistently. Preparation enables speed and coherence when they matter most.

### Respond Fast, Even Before All Facts Are Known

Speed shapes perception. Acknowledge and commit to investigating before all details are clear.

Respond by:

- acknowledging the issue promptly;
- stating what is known and what is being verified;
- committing to transparency as facts emerge;
- expressing concern for those affected;
- avoiding silence that fills with speculation.

Silence or delay is interpreted as indifference or guilt. A fast, honest acknowledgment, even with incomplete facts, builds trust and controls the narrative.

### Lead With Empathy And Responsibility

People judge whether you care before they judge the facts. Lead with empathy and ownership.

Lead with:

- genuine concern for those affected;
- responsibility rather than deflection;
- what you are doing to address the harm;
- what you are doing to prevent recurrence;
- human language, not legal hedging.

A response that minimizes, blames others, or hides behind lawyers signals that the company cares more about itself than those harmed. Empathy and responsibility preserve trust.

### Speak To Those Affected, Not Just The Public

The people most harmed deserve direct, priority communication. Do not address only the broader public.

Address:

- affected customers or individuals directly and first;
- employees who need to understand and represent the response;
- partners and stakeholders who need coordination;
- regulators or authorities where required;
- the public with a consistent message.

Speaking past the affected to the broader audience compounds the harm. Those most affected set the tone for whether trust is recoverable.

### Coordinate One Consistent Voice

Conflicting messages deepen a crisis. Coordinate a single, consistent voice.

Coordinate:

- one approved set of facts and messages;
- alignment across executives, PR, legal, and support;
- consistent statements across all channels;
- a single designated spokesperson where possible;
- regular updates as the situation evolves.

When the CEO, the press release, and the support team say different things, trust collapses. Consistency requires coordination before speaking.

### Be Transparent About What Happened And What Changes

Trust is rebuilt by transparency and change, not by moving on. Explain and commit.

Explain:

- what happened, in plain language;
- how it happened, as far as is known;
- the impact on those affected;
- what you are doing to fix it now;
- what you are changing to prevent recurrence.

Vague statements that avoid detail read as evasion. Specific transparency and concrete commitments rebuild trust over time.

### Monitor, Adapt, And Follow Through

A crisis evolves, and so must the response. Monitor and adapt.

Monitor:

- how the response is being received;
- new facts and developments;
- the concerns and questions being raised;
- the spread and sentiment of the conversation;
- whether commitments are being fulfilled.

A static response becomes out of touch. Continuous monitoring and adaptation, plus follow-through on commitments, determine whether recovery is real.

## Common Traps

### No Preparation

Improvising under pressure produces slow, inconsistent, and damaging responses.

### Silence And Delay

Waiting for full facts before saying anything is read as indifference or guilt.

### Minimizing Or Deflecting

Downplaying harm or blaming others signals the company cares only about itself.

### Legal Language Over Human Language

Hedged, lawyerly statements fail to connect with people's emotions and concerns.

### Conflicting Messages

When different voices say different things, trust collapses faster than from the crisis itself.

### Vague Non-Apologies

Statements that avoid responsibility or detail read as evasion and deepen distrust.

### Announce And Move On

Failing to follow through on commitments destroys the trust the response tried to build.

## Self-Check

- [ ] A crisis response framework, team, and templates are prepared before a crisis occurs.
- [ ] The initial response is fast, acknowledging the issue and committing to transparency.
- [ ] The response leads with empathy and responsibility, not deflection or minimization.
- [ ] Those most affected are addressed directly and first, not only the broader public.
- [ ] One consistent, coordinated voice is maintained across all channels and stakeholders.
- [ ] Statements use human language, not legal hedging that distances the company.
- [ ] Transparency explains what happened, the impact, and what is changing.
- [ ] The response is monitored and adapted as facts and sentiment evolve.
- [ ] Commitments to affected parties are tracked and fulfilled, not just announced.
- [ ] The focus is on addressing harm and rebuilding trust, not only protecting image.
