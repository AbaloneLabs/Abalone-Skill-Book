# Co-Marketing And Campaigns

Co-marketing multiplies reach by combining two brands' audiences, but it also multiplies the ways a campaign can fail. It fails when the two sides have different goals, when the joint value proposition is muddled, when one side does most of the work, when promotion is uncoordinated, or when the customer experience is inconsistent across the two brands. Good co-marketing requires a clear shared goal, a coherent joint message, and disciplined coordination.

Use this skill before planning a co-marketing campaign, defining a joint offer, splitting responsibilities, or reviewing a co-marketing plan. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing co-marketing that consumes effort from both sides and produces less than either would alone.

## Core Rules

### Agree On A Shared Goal And Metric First

Two partners with different goals pull in different directions. Align before building.

Align on:

- the specific outcome both sides want;
- the primary metric that defines success;
- how each side benefits and measures;
- the audience the campaign targets;
- the timeline and key milestones.

A campaign where one side wants leads and the other wants awareness serves neither well. A shared goal focuses the joint effort.

### Craft A Coherent Joint Value Proposition

The offer must make sense as a combination, not as two separate pitches stapled together.

Craft:

- a value proposition that is greater together than apart;
- a clear story for why the two complement each other;
- messaging that serves the customer's need, not each brand's ego;
- consistent language across both brands' materials;
- a single primary call to action.

A joint campaign that pitches two unrelated products confuses the audience. A coherent joint offer creates value the customer could not get from either alone.

### Split Responsibilities And Costs Fairly

Co-marketing fails when one side does the work and the other takes the credit. Define contributions clearly.

Define:

- who creates what content and assets;
- who promotes through which channels;
- how costs are split;
- who owns which metrics and reporting;
- how leads and value are shared.

Unfair or unclear splits breed resentment and underperformance. Fair, explicit agreements sustain the collaboration.

### Coordinate Launch And Promotion

Two brands promoting on different timelines and messages dilutes the campaign. Coordinate.

Coordinate:

- a shared launch date and countdown;
- aligned messaging and creative;
- coordinated promotion across both audiences;
- a shared hub or landing experience;
- consistent follow-up and nurture.

Fragmented promotion reaches each audience separately and loses the compounding effect. Coordinated launch and promotion amplify both sides.

### Maintain Brand Consistency Across Both Sides

The customer should experience one coherent campaign, not two inconsistent ones.

Maintain:

- shared visual and messaging standards;
- approval processes for both brands' materials;
- consistency in tone and claims;
- alignment on data and proof points;
- a unified customer journey across touchpoints.

Inconsistent branding or claims across partners confuses the audience and undermines trust. Treat the campaign as one experience.

### Design Lead Sharing And Attribution

Co-marketing generates shared value, including leads. Define how they are handled.

Define:

- how leads are captured and attributed;
- consent and data sharing terms;
- how leads are routed and nurtured by each side;
- the deduplication and ownership rules;
- the reporting both sides receive.

Ambiguous lead handling causes disputes and compliance risk. Clear terms protect both sides and the customer.

### Measure Joint Outcome And Individual Contribution

A co-marketing campaign should be evaluated both as a whole and by what each side gained.

Measure:

- the joint outcome against the shared goal;
- each side's contribution and benefit;
- the cost and effort each side invested;
- the incremental value versus going alone;
- what to repeat or change next time.

Measurement that only credits one side, or that ignores the joint result, misleads future collaboration. Measure both views honestly, and share the data transparently so both partners trust the numbers and can decide together what to repeat, scale, or abandon.

## Common Traps

### Misaligned Goals

Two partners pursuing different outcomes pull the campaign apart.

### Muddled Joint Value Proposition

Two unrelated pitches stapled together confuse the audience.

### Unequal Effort

One side doing the work while the other takes credit breeds resentment.

### Uncoordinated Promotion

Fragmented launch and messaging dilute the compounding effect.

### Inconsistent Brand Experience

Different branding and claims across partners undermine trust.

### Ambiguous Lead Handling

Unclear lead sharing and attribution cause disputes and compliance risk.

### One-Sided Measurement

Crediting only one side or ignoring the joint result misleads future collaboration.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Both sides agree on a shared goal and primary metric before building the campaign.
- [ ] The joint value proposition is coherent and greater together than apart.
- [ ] Responsibilities, costs, and lead sharing are defined fairly and explicitly.
- [ ] Launch and promotion are coordinated across both audiences and channels.
- [ ] Brand consistency is maintained across both sides' materials and touchpoints.
- [ ] Lead capture, attribution, consent, and routing are defined and compliant.
- [ ] The campaign is measured both as a joint outcome and by individual contribution.
- [ ] The incremental value versus each side going alone is understood.
- [ ] Approval processes ensure consistent claims and data across both brands.
- [ ] Learnings are captured to improve or decide on future co-marketing.
