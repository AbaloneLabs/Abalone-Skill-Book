# Partnership Strategy And Selection

Partnerships promise leverage, but they often deliver logos without value. They fail when the strategy collects partner brands for announcement value rather than business outcome, when fit is assessed on size or prestige rather than complementary capability, when the partnership serves one side's goals, or when no one owns the work of making the partnership produce. Good partnerships are built on mutual value, clear goals, and the operational commitment to realize them.

Use this skill before defining a partnership strategy, evaluating partners, structuring a program, or reviewing partnership selection. The goal is to prevent the agent from pursuing partnerships that look impressive in a press release and produce nothing.

## Core Rules

### Start From The Business Outcome The Partnership Must Drive

A partnership is a means to an outcome. Define the outcome first.

Define:

- the specific business goal the partnership serves;
- how the partnership advances it better than alternatives;
- the measurable result expected;
- the timeline over which value will appear;
- what would make the partnership worth the effort.

Partnerships without a defined outcome become logo collections. Partnerships tied to a goal, such as entering a market, reaching an audience, or filling a capability gap, produce value.

### Select For Complementary Value, Not Prestige

A partner's brand is not enough. Select for what each side brings to the other.

Assess:

- complementary audience or capability;
- genuine value exchange, not one-sided extraction;
- alignment of customer and market;
- operational ability to execute together;
- cultural and strategic fit.

A prestigious partner with no complementary value produces an announcement and nothing more. A less famous partner with real fit produces results.

### Define The Partnership Type And Structure

Partnerships take many forms, each with different mechanics. Choose and structure deliberately.

Types include:

- co-marketing for shared audience reach;
- technology integration for combined product value;
- reseller or channel for distribution;
- referral and affiliate for lead flow;
- strategic alliance for joint go-to-market.

The type determines the structure, the commitments, and how value is created and measured. A vague "partnership" with no defined structure drifts.

### Ensure Mutual Value And Incentive Alignment

A partnership lasts only if both sides gain. Design for mutual value.

Ensure:

- each side gets something it values;
- incentives are aligned, not conflicting;
- the value exchange is balanced over time;
- neither side bears disproportionate cost or risk;
- the relationship survives personnel changes.

A partnership that serves one side's goals erodes when the other side stops investing. Mutual value makes the partnership self-sustaining.

### Assess Operational Fit And Commitment

Strategy without execution fails. Assess whether both sides can and will execute.

Assess:

- operational capability to integrate or co-sell;
- bandwidth and priority within each organization;
- clarity of ownership on both sides;
- the processes and systems needed to collaborate;
- the realistic timeline for results.

A partner that is strategically perfect but operationally absent produces nothing. Operational fit and committed ownership matter as much as strategy.

### Plan For The Long Term, Not The Launch

Partnerships are relationships, not events. Plan beyond the announcement.

Plan:

- the roadmap of joint activity over time;
- the cadence of collaboration and review;
- how the partnership evolves as both sides grow;
- the governance and decision-making structure;
- the exit conditions if the partnership underperforms.

The launch is the easiest part. The ongoing work of maintaining and growing the partnership is where value is created or lost.

### Define Measurement And Review From The Start

If value will be measured, define it before the partnership begins.

Define:

- the metrics tied to the partnership goal;
- the data sharing and attribution approach;
- the review cadence and ownership;
- the thresholds for success, concern, and exit;
- the cost of the partnership against its value.

Partnerships measured post-hoc against vague goals always look fine and produce little. Measurement designed up front keeps both sides accountable.

## Common Traps

### Logo Collection

Pursuing partners for announcement prestige rather than business outcome.

### Prestige Over Fit

Selecting partners for brand fame rather than complementary value.

### One-Sided Value

Partnerships that serve one side's goals and erode when the other disengages.

### Vague Partnership Structure

A "partnership" with no defined type, commitments, or mechanics drifts into nothing.

### Ignoring Operational Fit

Strategic perfection with no operational commitment produces no results.

### Launch-Only Thinking

Treating the announcement as the goal and neglecting the ongoing relationship.

### No Measurement Design

Measuring vague goals after the fact lets underperformance hide.

## Self-Check

- [ ] The partnership is tied to a specific, measurable business outcome.
- [ ] Partners are selected for complementary value and fit, not prestige or size.
- [ ] The partnership type and structure are defined with clear commitments.
- [ ] Value exchange is mutual and incentives are aligned over time.
- [ ] Operational fit and committed ownership are assessed on both sides.
- [ ] The plan extends beyond launch to ongoing collaboration and governance.
- [ ] Measurement is designed before the partnership begins, tied to the goal.
- [ ] The cost of the partnership is weighed against its expected value.
- [ ] Exit conditions are defined in case the partnership underperforms.
- [ ] The partnership is reviewed on a regular cadence, not just at launch.
