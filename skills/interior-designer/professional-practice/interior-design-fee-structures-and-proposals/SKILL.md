# Interior Design Fee Structures and Proposals

Pricing interior design work is a business judgment problem, not a calculation exercise. The trap is that designers underprice by estimating hours without accounting for revision cycles, undocumented scope creep, and the unpaid advisory time that clients expect, then discover mid-project that the fee does not cover the work. A weak fee proposal also fails to define what is included and excluded, inviting disputes when the client assumes additional services are free. The judgment is to match the fee structure to the project type and risk, price the full scope including likely revisions, and write the proposal so that scope boundaries are enforceable.

## Core Rules

### Match the Fee Structure to the Project Type and Risk

Different fee structures suit different projects, and choosing the wrong one transfers risk to the designer or the client. A fixed (lump sum) fee suits projects with a well-defined scope and low uncertainty, such as a residential renovation with specified finishes; it gives the client cost certainty but transfers scope-creep risk to the designer, so it requires a tight scope definition. An hourly fee suits projects with uncertain scope, such as advisory work or open-ended redesign; it is fair but makes the client anxious about runaway costs, so it requires regular reporting and a not-to-exceed cap. A cost-plus (markup on goods and furniture) fee suits procurement-heavy work but creates a perception conflict if the client suspects the designer is over-specifying to increase markup; disclose the markup transparently. A percentage-of-construction fee suits large commercial work but is now disfavored in many jurisdictions because it incentivizes overbuilding; check whether the project type and contract permit it. Do not default to one structure; select based on the project's uncertainty, the client's risk tolerance, and what the scope rewards.

### Price the Full Scope, Including Revision Cycles and Unpaid Time

The most common underpricing error is estimating only the hours for the first-pass deliverable and ignoring the revision cycles, coordination meetings, and advisory calls that consume real time. A design package is rarely delivered once; clients request two to four revision rounds, and each round reopens specification, drawing updates, and re-coordination with contractors. Estimate the number of likely revision cycles from the project type and the client's decision style, and include those hours in the fee. Include the unpaid time that clients expect: the initial consultation, the sourcing trips, the site visits during construction, the responses to contractor questions (RFIs), and the punch list. A fee that prices only the drawings will not cover the work, and the designer absorbs the difference. Build a contingency (typically 10 to 15 percent) into the fee for the work that is foreseeable but not yet defined.

### Define Included and Excluded Services Explicitly

The proposal must list what is included and, equally important, what is excluded. Included services typically cover schematic design, design development, construction documents, finish and fixture selection, and a defined number of site visits. Excluded services—items the client often assumes are included—must be named: structural engineering, mechanical and electrical engineering, permitting fees, surveying, geotechnical work, art and accessories beyond a defined allowance, move-in coordination, and post-occupancy adjustments. When an excluded service is requested later, the proposal's exclusion list is the basis for an additional-services invoice. Without an explicit exclusion list, the client assumes everything is included and resists paying for additional services, eroding the fee.

### Establish Additional-Services and Scope-Change Mechanisms

Scope changes are normal, not exceptional, in interior design. The proposal should define the mechanism for additional services: how a scope change is requested, how it is priced (hourly rate or a quoted fee), how it is approved (written sign-off before work begins), and how it is invoiced. Establishing this mechanism before the project starts prevents the awkward negotiation of payment after the work is done. The client who approved a scope change verbally and later disputes the invoice is a common loss; a written additional-services authorization, signed before the work, is the protection. Make the additional-services rate explicit in the proposal so that the client knows the cost of changes from the outset.

### Structure Payment Milestones to Cash Flow, Not to Completion

Payment tied only to completion leaves the designer financing the client's project. Structure payments as milestones that track the work: a retainer or deposit at signing (typically 15 to 25 percent), payments at schematic design approval, design development approval, and construction document issuance, and a final payment at substantial completion rather than final completion. The final payment should not be so large (e.g., 50 percent) that the designer is dependent on the client's final goodwill; 10 to 15 percent is typical. Tie each milestone payment to a deliverable and an approval, so that payment follows the client's acceptance of work, not the designer's assertion that work is done. A payment schedule weighted toward the end of the project is a cash-flow risk and a collection risk.

### Document the Fee Basis So It Is Defensible

The proposal should show how the fee was derived, not just the number. For a fixed fee, itemize the scope phases and the hours or the deliverables that the fee covers, so that when the client requests a reduction, the designer can show what scope must be removed to reduce the fee. For an hourly fee, show the blended or individual rates and the estimated hours by phase. A fee presented as a single number is hard to defend and easy to negotiate down; a fee presented as a priced scope is defensible because the client sees that reducing the fee requires reducing the scope. This documentation also protects the designer if the client later claims the fee was excessive or that the scope was misunderstood.

## Common Traps

### Underpricing by Estimating Only First-Pass Hours

The trap is estimating the hours to produce the drawings once and ignoring the two to four revision cycles, the coordination meetings, and the site visits. The harm is a fee that covers 60 percent of the work, with the designer absorbing the rest as unpaid overtime. The discipline is to estimate the full cycle, including revisions and unpaid advisory time, and to build in a contingency.

### Failing to Define Excluded Services

The trap is listing what is included but not what is excluded, assuming the client understands the boundaries. The harm is the client assuming engineering, permitting, and accessories are included, then resisting when these are billed as additional services. The discipline is to list excluded services explicitly, so that any later request is clearly additional and billable.

### Accepting Verbal Scope Changes Without Written Authorization

The trap is agreeing to a client's verbal request for a change ("just move that wall") and doing the work without a written additional-services authorization. The harm is the client later disputing the invoice because there is no record of approval. The discipline is to require written authorization for any scope change before the work begins, with the cost stated.

### Weighting the Final Payment Too Heavily

The trap is structuring 40 to 50 percent of the fee as a final payment at project end, to make the proposal look smaller up front. The harm is that the designer finances the project and risks non-payment if the client becomes dissatisfied or financially stressed near completion. The discipline is to structure milestone payments that track the work, with a modest final payment.

### Presenting the Fee as a Single Number

The trap is presenting the fee as one figure without showing the priced scope, making it easy for the client to negotiate down without understanding what is being reduced. The harm is a reduced fee for the same scope, eroding margin. The discipline is to present the fee as a priced scope, so that reducing the fee visibly requires reducing the scope.

## Self-Check

- Is the fee structure (fixed, hourly, cost-plus, percentage) matched to the project's uncertainty and the client's risk tolerance, rather than defaulted to one type?
- Does the fee estimate include revision cycles, coordination meetings, site visits, and unpaid advisory time, with a contingency of 10 to 15 percent?
- Does the proposal list excluded services (engineering, permitting, surveying, accessories, move-in) explicitly, so that later requests are clearly additional?
- Is there a defined additional-services mechanism, with the rate, the request process, and the requirement for written authorization before work begins?
- Is the payment schedule structured as milestones tied to deliverables and approvals, with a final payment of 10 to 15 percent rather than a large lump at completion?
- Is the fee basis documented as a priced scope, so that the client sees what the fee covers and what must be removed to reduce it?
