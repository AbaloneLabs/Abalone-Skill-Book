# Interior Design Licensure, Liability, and Insurance

Licensure, liability, and insurance define the legal boundary of what an interior designer may do, what they are responsible for, and how they are protected when something goes wrong. The judgment problem is that the regulatory landscape for interior design is fragmented—practice acts, title acts, and unregulated jurisdictions differ on whether a designer may stamp drawings, file permits, or call themselves a "registered interior designer"—and a designer who assumes uniform rules may practice outside their legal scope or fail to carry the protection their exposure requires. The discipline is to know the jurisdiction's rules, to practice within the certified scope, and to carry insurance matched to the actual liability, not to the premium.

## Core Rules

### Know the Jurisdiction's Regulatory Model Before Practicing

Interior design regulation falls into three models, and the designer must know which applies in each jurisdiction where they practice. A practice act prohibits uncertified persons from performing the defined scope of interior design work (often including nonstructural, nonseismic elements and permit filing); practicing without certification in a practice-act jurisdiction is illegal. A title act restricts only the use of the title "registered" or "certified interior designer" but does not restrict the work; anyone may do the work, but only certified persons may use the title. An unregulated jurisdiction imposes no certification requirement. The designer must determine, for each project's location, whether a practice act, title act, or no regulation applies, and must not assume that rules from one jurisdiction carry to another. A designer who stamps drawings or files permits in a practice-act jurisdiction without certification exposes themselves to fines, injunction, and unenforceable contracts.

### Obtain and Maintain Appropriate Certification (NCIDQ and State Registration)

The NCIDQ (National Council for Interior Design Qualification) examination is the standard credential recognized by most regulated jurisdictions, combined with education and experience requirements. A designer practicing in a regulated jurisdiction should obtain NCIDQ certification and the corresponding state or provincial registration, and must maintain it through continuing education units (CEUs), which are audited. Lapses in registration are not benign; practicing with a lapsed registration in a regulated jurisdiction is practicing without certification. Track CEU deadlines, renewal dates, and jurisdiction-specific requirements (some require additional exams or specific coursework). For designers working across borders, determine whether reciprocity or comity applies, or whether a separate registration is required. Do not represent certification status inaccurately in marketing or proposals; misrepresentation is a disciplinary offense and, in some jurisdictions, a consumer protection violation.

### Carry Professional Liability Insurance Matched to the Exposure

Professional liability insurance (errors and omissions, or E&O) covers claims arising from the designer's professional acts—design errors, specification failures, omissions in the documents—distinct from general liability, which covers bodily injury and property damage. The designer should carry E&O insurance with limits matched to the project exposure: residential work may be adequately covered at lower limits, while commercial and public work may require higher limits (often contractually specified by the client). Determine whether the policy is claims-made (covers claims made during the policy period, requiring tail coverage if the designer leaves practice) or occurrence-based, and understand the retroactive date, which excludes claims from acts before that date. A gap in coverage—a lapse between policies, or a missing tail when winding down—leaves historical exposure uninsured. Do not assume a project's general liability covers professional errors; it typically excludes them.

### Understand the Legal Scope of Practice and Its Boundaries

The legal scope of interior design typically includes space planning, finish and fixture selection, nonstructural elements, furniture and equipment layout, and the preparation of documents for these elements. It typically excludes structural design (load-bearing walls, foundations), mechanical and electrical engineering, plumbing design, and any work requiring a professional engineer or architect's stamp. A designer who crosses into structural or engineering work—specifying a load-bearing beam, redesigning a plumbing riser—is practicing outside their scope and may be practicing engineering or architecture without a license, which is illegal and uninsured. When a project requires work outside the interior design scope, the designer must engage or recommend a licensed architect or engineer; the designer coordinates but does not perform that work. The boundary is not a suggestion; it is a legal and insurance line.

### Document Decisions and Communications to Defend Against Claims

Most professional liability claims arise not from catastrophic design failure but from disputes over scope, approvals, and communications—claims that the designer approved a change they did not approve, or that the client was not warned of a risk. The defense against these claims is documentation. Maintain a project record: the contract, the scope, the phase approvals (in writing), the correspondence (especially email), the meeting notes, the requests for information (RFIs) and responses, the submittals and approvals, the change orders, and the site visit reports. Document warnings given to the client (about budget, about schedule, about a contractor's deviation) in writing, with the client's acknowledgment. A claim that surfaces two years after project completion is defended from the record; a designer without a record defends from memory and loses. Treat documentation as risk management, not as paperwork.

### Manage Conflicts of Interest and Disclosure Obligations

Conflicts of interest in interior design arise most commonly from financial relationships with vendors, showrooms, and contractors—from whose goods the designer receives a markup, a commission, or a kickback. The designer must disclose these relationships to the client, in writing, before the engagement or before the relevant procurement. Undisclosed markups are a breach of fiduciary duty and, in some jurisdictions, a consumer protection violation. The disclosure should state whether the designer marks up goods, at what rate, and whether the client may purchase directly. A related conflict is accepting referral fees from contractors or vendors; these should be disclosed or refused. The discipline is to disclose proactively, because a conflict disclosed and consented to is manageable, while a conflict discovered later is a breach of trust and a claim.

## Common Traps

### Practicing in a Jurisdiction Without Knowing Its Regulatory Model

The trap is assuming that because the designer is certified in one jurisdiction, they may practice in another, or that no certification is needed anywhere. The harm is practicing illegally in a practice-act jurisdiction, with fines, injunction, and unenforceable contracts. The discipline is to determine the regulatory model (practice act, title act, unregulated) for each project location before practicing.

### Letting Certification Lapse and Continuing to Practice

The trap is missing a CEU deadline or a renewal date and continuing to practice as a registered designer. The harm is practicing without valid certification, which is illegal in regulated jurisdictions and voids the professional standing. The discipline is to track renewal dates and CEU requirements and to maintain certification continuously.

### Carrying Only General Liability and Assuming It Covers Design Errors

The trap is carrying general liability insurance (for bodily injury and property damage) and assuming it covers professional design errors. The harm is an uninsured professional liability claim, because general liability typically excludes professional acts. The discipline is to carry separate professional liability (E&O) insurance with limits matched to the project exposure.

### Crossing Into Structural or Engineering Work

The trap is specifying a structural element or redesigning a mechanical system to be helpful, crossing the legal scope of interior design. The harm is practicing engineering or architecture without a license, which is illegal and uninsured. The discipline is to engage a licensed architect or engineer for work outside the interior design scope.

### Failing to Document Approvals, Warnings, and Communications

The trap is relying on verbal approvals and undocumented communications, assuming the relationship will remain good. The harm is an undefendable claim when the relationship sours, because the record does not exist. The discipline is to document approvals, warnings, and key communications in writing, treating documentation as risk management.

## Self-Check

- Has the regulatory model (practice act, title act, or unregulated) been determined for each jurisdiction where the designer practices, and is the designer certified where required?
- Is NCIDQ certification and state/provincial registration current, with CEU requirements met and renewal dates tracked?
- Does the designer carry professional liability (E&O) insurance, separate from general liability, with limits matched to the project exposure, and is the policy type (claims-made, occurrence) and retroactive date understood?
- Is the designer practicing strictly within the legal scope of interior design, engaging licensed architects or engineers for structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing work?
- Is a project record maintained (contract, approvals, correspondence, RFIs, change orders, site reports, warnings) sufficient to defend a claim that surfaces years later?
- Are financial relationships with vendors, showrooms, and contractors disclosed to the client in writing before procurement, and are referral fees disclosed or refused?
