# Interior Design Contracts and Scope of Service

The interior design contract defines what the designer owes the client, what the client owes the designer, and what happens when the project diverges from plan. The judgment problem is that designers often work from generic templates that do not match the actual scope, leaving gaps where the client expects services the designer did not agree to, or where the designer is exposed to liability the contract did not allocate. A weak contract also fails to distinguish the designer's role from the contractor's, creating ambiguity about who is responsible for construction means, methods, and site safety. The discipline is to define the scope as deliverables, allocate responsibility clearly, and write terms that survive the project's inevitable changes.

## Core Rules

### Define the Scope as Deliverables and Phases, Not as Outcomes

A scope defined as outcomes ("design a beautiful living room") is unenforceable because outcomes are subjective. Define the scope as deliverables: the schematic design package (floor plans, elevations, one perspective), the design development package (reflected plans, finish plans, fixture selections), the construction documents (dimensioned drawings, specifications, schedules), and the procurement services (furniture, fixtures, and equipment sourcing and purchasing). Tie each phase to an approval gate, so that the project does not advance until the client approves the prior phase in writing. A scope defined as deliverables is measurable, billable, and defensible; a scope defined as outcomes invites the client to demand unlimited revisions until the subjective outcome is "achieved."

### Distinguish the Designer's Role from the Contractor's Explicitly

The most dangerous ambiguity in an interior design contract is the boundary between the designer's responsibility and the contractor's. The designer is responsible for the design—the drawings, the specifications, and the design intent—but not for the construction means, methods, sequences, techniques, or site safety, which are the contractor's sole responsibility. The contract must state this explicitly: the designer is not the constructor, does not supervise the construction, and is not responsible for the contractor's acts or omissions or for jobsite safety. Without this clause, a client injured by a construction defect or a worker harmed on site may claim the designer supervised the work and share the liability. The designer observes the work for conformance with the design intent; the contractor controls the site.

### Allocate Permitting and Code Compliance Responsibility Clearly

Permitting and code compliance are often a gray zone. The contract should state who is responsible for obtaining the permit (often the contractor or the owner, not the designer), who is responsible for code compliance of the design (the designer, for the design elements within their scope), and who is responsible for code compliance of the construction (the contractor). The designer is responsible for designing to code—fire ratings, egress, accessibility, electrical and plumbing layouts that meet code—but is not responsible for the building official's interpretation or for the contractor's deviations from the documents. If the designer is to file the permit or provide construction administration, state it as an included service; if not, state it as excluded. Ambiguity here leads to the designer being blamed for permit delays or code violations that were the contractor's or the owner's responsibility.

### Establish the Document Control and Approval Process

The contract should establish how design decisions are made and documented: who approves each phase, in what form (written, email, signed), and what happens if the client does not respond within a defined period. A common trap is the client who gives verbal approval and later denies it, forcing rework; the contract should require written approval (email is usually sufficient) to advance each phase. Establish that the designer's documents are the controlling instruments and that the client's verbal instructions are not binding until confirmed in writing. Establish a mechanism for resolving disputes over design decisions—a designated client decision-maker, not a committee—to prevent the designer from being caught between conflicting client instructions.

### Address Ownership of Documents and Intellectual Property

The contract should state who owns the design documents. The default, absent a clause, is that the designer retains ownership (copyright) of the drawings and specifications and licenses them to the client for use on this project only; the client may not reuse them for another project or transfer them to another designer without permission. Some clients (especially commercial) demand ownership transfer; this is negotiable but should be priced as a separate fee, because it transfers the designer's intellectual property. The contract should also address the designer's right to photograph and publish the completed project, which is valuable for marketing; reserve this right unless the client objects in writing. Do not leave ownership ambiguous, because disputes over document reuse and publication rights are common and costly.

### Include Termination, Suspension, and Dispute Resolution Terms

The contract should address what happens if the project is suspended or terminated. A termination-for-convenience clause lets either party end the engagement with notice, with the designer paid for work completed and costs incurred. A termination-for-cause clause addresses breach, with a cure period. On termination, the client is entitled to the documents prepared to date, but the designer is paid for them including a reasonable profit on unperformed work if the termination is for convenience. Include a dispute resolution mechanism—mediation, then arbitration, or litigation in a defined venue—to avoid the cost and unpredictability of a default lawsuit in an unfavorable jurisdiction. These terms are insurance against the project going wrong; their absence leaves the designer exposed.

## Common Traps

### Using a Generic Template That Does Not Match the Scope

The trap is using a downloaded or inherited contract template without tailoring it to the actual scope, leaving services the designer intends to perform unlisted or excluded services not excluded. The harm is a contract that does not reflect the engagement, creating disputes over what was agreed. The discipline is to tailor the scope, deliverables, and exclusions to the specific project.

### Failing to Distinguish Designer from Contractor Responsibility

The trap is omitting the clause that the designer is not responsible for construction means, methods, or site safety. The harm is exposure to construction-related liability if the client or a worker claims the designer supervised the work. The discipline is to state explicitly that the designer observes for design conformance and does not control the site or the contractor.

### Leaving Permitting and Code Compliance Ambiguous

The trap is not stating who obtains the permit and who is responsible for code compliance of the design versus the construction. The harm is the designer being blamed for permit delays or code violations. The discipline is to allocate permitting and code responsibilities explicitly in the contract.

### Giving Away Document Ownership by Default

The trap is omitting an intellectual property clause, allowing the client to claim ownership by default or to reuse the documents with another designer. The harm is loss of the designer's intellectual property and marketing material. The discipline is to state that the designer retains copyright and licenses the documents for this project only, with transfer priced separately if requested.

### Omitting Termination and Dispute Resolution Terms

The trap is assuming the project will go well and omitting termination and dispute terms. The harm is an expensive, unpredictable lawsuit in an unfavorable venue if the relationship breaks down. The discipline is to include termination-for-convenience and for-cause clauses and a defined dispute resolution mechanism.

## Self-Check

- Is the scope defined as deliverables and phases (schematic, design development, construction documents, procurement) with approval gates, rather than as subjective outcomes?
- Does the contract explicitly state that the designer is not responsible for construction means, methods, sequences, or site safety, which are the contractor's sole responsibility?
- Is permitting and code compliance responsibility allocated clearly—who obtains the permit, who is responsible for design code compliance, who for construction compliance?
- Is there a document control and approval process requiring written approval to advance each phase, and a designated client decision-maker?
- Does the contract address ownership of documents (designer retains copyright, licenses for this project) and the designer's right to photograph and publish?
- Are termination-for-convenience and for-cause clauses, payment on termination, and a dispute resolution mechanism (mediation/arbitration/venue) included?
