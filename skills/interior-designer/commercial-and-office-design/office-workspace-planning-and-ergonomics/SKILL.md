# Office Workspace Planning and Ergonomics

The modern office is a compromise between collaboration and concentration, and the failure mode is optimizing for one at the expense of the other — an open plan that maximizes interaction but makes focused work impossible, or a fully enclosed plan that provides quiet but isolates and reduces collaboration. The judgment problem is that office work is not one activity but a range of modes (focused individual work, collaborative team work, informal conversation, formal meetings, phone and video calls, rest and socializing), and a workspace that supports only one mode fails the others, and the most common failure is the undifferentiated open plan that assumes everyone does collaborative work all day. The deeper trap is that ergonomics is treated as a furniture specification (an adjustable chair and desk) rather than as a layout and culture decision, when the real ergonomic drivers are movement, posture variation, and visual and acoustic comfort, which the layout determines. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether an office workspace supports the full range of work modes and the health of the occupants.

## Core Rules

### Provide a Range of Work Settings, Not a Single Uniform Plan

Modern office work requires multiple settings: focused workstations (quiet, individual), collaborative areas (team tables, project rooms), informal meeting spaces (huddle rooms, lounges), formal meeting rooms (conference, presentation), phone and video call rooms (acoustically private), and social and rest spaces (kitchen, lounge, wellness room). The rule is to provide a portfolio of settings matched to the work modes, sized to the team's actual activities, rather than a single uniform open plan. The decision criterion is to analyze the work modes (what percentage of time is spent on each) and to allocate the floor area accordingly. A single-setting office forces all work into a mode that fits some tasks and fails others.

### Design Workstations for Ergonomic Adjustability and Posture Variation

The workstation — desk, chair, monitor, keyboard — must support ergonomic adjustability and posture variation. This means: height-adjustable (sit-stand) desks that allow the user to alternate between sitting and standing through the day; task chairs with adjustable seat height, depth, lumbar, and armrests; monitor arms that position screens at eye level and arm's length; and keyboard trays that position the keyboard at elbow height. The rule is to specify adjustability as the default, not the upgrade, and to train users on adjustment, because an adjustable chair left at its default is functionally a fixed chair. Posture variation (alternating sit and stand) is more important than any single "correct" posture.

### Manage Acoustic Comfort for Both Focus and Collaboration

Acoustics are the most common complaint in open offices, and the failure is that conversation (collaboration) and concentration (focus) are acoustically incompatible in the same space. The rule is to zone the office acoustically: locate collaborative areas (team tables, phone zones) away from focused work areas, use sound-absorbing materials (acoustic ceiling clouds, wall panels, floor coverings) to reduce reverberation, provide enclosed phone and huddle rooms for calls and conversations that would disturb others, and consider sound masking (white noise systems) for open areas. The decision criterion is to separate the loud activities from the quiet ones and to absorb and mask the sound that remains.

### Plan Circulation and Sightlines for Both Efficiency and Privacy

Office circulation must be efficient (short paths between related workstations and meeting rooms) and must manage sightlines for visual privacy and distraction control. The rule is to plan primary circulation paths that are direct and generous (minimum 36 inches, 48-60 for main aisles), to orient workstations so that screens and desks are not directly exposed to passing traffic (which is distracting), and to use partial screens, plants, and furniture to create visual buffering without full enclosure. The trap is the long open aisle with desks lining both sides, which exposes every screen to every passerby and creates a distracting, surveilled feeling.

### Provide Adequate Meeting and Collaboration Space Sized to the Team

Meeting space is chronically under-provided in offices, and the result is meetings held in open areas (disturbing focused workers) or in inadequate rooms (too small, no technology, no privacy). The rule is to calculate the meeting room requirement from the team size and the meeting patterns (how many concurrent meetings, of what size, with what technology), and to provide a mix of room sizes (two-person huddle rooms, four-to-six-person project rooms, eight-to-twelve-person conference rooms) with appropriate technology (displays, video conferencing, whiteboards). The decision criterion is to size the meeting portfolio to the peak concurrent demand, not to the average.

### Design for Health: Light, Air, Movement, and Biophilia

Office health factors — daylight access, air quality, opportunities for movement, and connection to nature — affect both wellbeing and productivity, and they must be designed in, not left to chance. The rule is to maximize daylight access (locate workstations within 30 feet of windows, use glass walls for perimeter offices), to ensure adequate ventilation and air quality, to provide opportunities for movement (stairs, walking paths, standing-height counters, wellness rooms), and to incorporate biophilic elements (plants, natural materials, views to nature). A office that ignores these factors produces fatigue, discomfort, and reduced performance.

## Common Traps

### The Undifferentiated Open Plan

The designer specifies a uniform open plan with rows of benching workstations, assuming it supports collaboration and efficiency, and discovers that focused work is impossible due to noise and distraction, and that collaboration happens in meeting rooms anyway. The mechanism of failure is that a single setting was provided for multiple work modes. The defense is to provide a portfolio of settings matched to the work modes.

### Adjustable Furniture Left at Default

The designer specifies sit-stand desks and ergonomic chairs but provides no training, and the users leave the furniture at its default (seated, unadjusted), continuing to experience strain. The mechanism of failure is that adjustability was treated as the solution rather than as a tool that requires use. The defense is to train users on adjustment and to default the settings correctly at installation.

### Acoustic Zones Mixed

The designer places the team collaboration table adjacent to the focused workstations, and the conversation disrupts the focused work, while the focused workers' keyboard noise distracts the collaborators. The mechanism of failure is that loud and quiet activities were colocated. The defense is to zone the office acoustically and to separate incompatible activities.

### Meeting Space Under-Provisioned

The designer provides a single large conference room and no huddle rooms, and the team holds two-person meetings in the open (disturbing others) or cannot find a private space for a call. The mechanism of failure is that the meeting portfolio was sized to one mode, not to the range. The defense is to calculate the concurrent meeting demand and to provide a mix of room sizes.

### Screens Exposed to Circulation

The designer lines the main aisle with desks facing the aisle, and every passerby sees every screen, creating distraction and a surveilled feeling. The mechanism of failure is that sightlines were not managed. The defense is to orient workstations away from the main circulation and to use screens and plants for visual buffering.

## Self-Check

- Did I analyze the team's work modes and provide a portfolio of settings (focus, collaboration, meeting, phone, social) sized to the actual activities?
- Did I specify height-adjustable desks and ergonomic task chairs with full adjustability, and plan for user training on adjustment?
- Did I zone the office acoustically, separating loud collaborative activities from quiet focused work, with absorption, masking, and enclosed rooms?
- Did I plan circulation that is direct and generous, with workstations oriented away from passing traffic and sightlines managed for privacy?
- Did I calculate the concurrent meeting demand and provide a mix of room sizes with appropriate technology?
- Did I design for health (daylight access, air quality, movement opportunities, biophilia) rather than treating these as optional?
- Did I verify that the layout supports the full range of work modes, not just collaboration or just focused work?
