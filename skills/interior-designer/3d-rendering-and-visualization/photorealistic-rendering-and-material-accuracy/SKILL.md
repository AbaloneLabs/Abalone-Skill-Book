# Photorealistic Rendering And Material Accuracy

Photorealistic renderings are the primary tool for communicating interior design intent to clients, and their power to persuade is exactly why they are dangerous. The judgment problem is that renderings are judged by whether they look beautiful, when their value depends on whether they are accurate — whether the materials, lighting, scale, and atmosphere shown will match the built result. An agent who produces a stunning rendering with idealized lighting, impossible material reflections, or compressed scale delivers an image the client approves and a built space that disappoints. The agent's job is to build renderings that are both compelling and honest, calibrate materials and lighting to real-world behavior, represent scale and proportion accurately, and use the rendering to reveal rather than flatter the design.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate Materials To Real-World Appearance And Scale

Materials in renderings must match their real-world appearance: the wood grain scale, the stone reflectance, the metal roughness, the fabric texture. Calibrate material maps to the correct physical scale — a wood grain tile pattern at the wrong scale reads false — and set reflectance and roughness to realistic values, not values chosen to flatter. An idealized material that looks better in rendering than in reality sets up client disappointment.

### Represent Lighting Honestly, Including Its Limitations

Rendering lighting can be set to idealized conditions — perfect balance, no glare, flattering shadows — that the built space cannot achieve. Represent the lighting design honestly, including the actual fixture types, their real distribution, and the limitations of the scheme, and show the space under the lighting conditions it will actually experience. A rendering lit like a magazine but built with standard downlights misleads the client about the atmosphere.

### Maintain Accurate Scale, Proportion, And Camera Perspective

Camera settings in 3D software can distort scale and proportion — wide lenses stretch rooms, low angles exaggerate height — and these distortions mislead the client about the space's feel. Use camera lenses and heights that represent human viewing accurately, verify dimensions against the model, and avoid the temptation to widen or raise the view to make the space feel larger. A rendering that makes a small room feel spacious sets up an inevitable disappointment.

### Show Realistic Furniture, Accessories, And Entourage

Furniture, art, accessories, and human figures in renderings set scale and life, but idealized or generic entourage misrepresents the design. Use furniture that matches the actual specification or realistic equivalents, populate with appropriately scaled figures, and avoid over-styling that suggests a finish level or lifestyle beyond the project. Over-styled renderings sell an aspiration the design does not deliver.

### Represent The Design At The Right Level Of Finish

Renderings should represent the design at the finish level actually specified, not an upgraded or idealized version. Use the specified materials, the actual lighting, and the real furniture quality, and avoid substituting premium materials or idealized conditions to make the rendering more attractive. A rendering that shows premium stone when the spec calls for tile misleads the client about both appearance and budget.

### Use Renderings To Test And Reveal, Not Only To Sell

Beyond selling the design, renderings are tools to test it — to reveal dark corners, material conflicts, scale problems, and circulation issues before construction. Use rendering iterations to identify and fix design problems, and share honest renderings of problem areas with the team. A rendering used only to flatter misses its diagnostic value.

### Communicate What The Rendering Represents And Its Limits

Clients interpret renderings as promises, so communicate what the rendering represents — design intent, material direction, lighting concept — and what it does not — exact color match, precise finish, final accessory selection. Label renderings with their purpose and note where materials or lighting are conceptual. An unlabeled rendering becomes a contract the design cannot fulfill.

## Common Traps

### Idealized Lighting That The Built Space Cannot Achieve

A rendering lit with perfect balance and flattering shadows, using lighting the design does not include, sells an atmosphere the built space will not deliver. The trap is that the rendering looks beautiful while the reality disappoints. Represent the actual lighting honestly.

### Distorted Scale From Camera Settings

Wide lenses and low camera angles stretch rooms and exaggerate height, making spaces feel larger or grander than they are. The trap is that the client approves the feel of the space as rendered while the built space feels smaller. Use accurate perspective.

### Upgraded Materials In The Rendering

Substituting premium stone, richer wood, or finer fabric in the rendering than the spec provides misleads about both appearance and budget. The trap is that the rendering looks richer while the built result looks cheaper by comparison. Render the specified materials.

### Over-Styling That Suggests A Higher Finish Level

Excessive accessories, idealized art, and aspirational styling suggest a finish level or lifestyle beyond the project, setting expectations the design cannot meet. The trap is that the styling sells the image while misrepresenting the design. Use realistic entourage.

## Self-Check

- Are material maps calibrated to real-world scale, reflectance, and roughness?
- Is the lighting represented honestly, including actual fixtures, distribution, and limitations?
- Are camera lenses and heights set for accurate human viewing, without scale distortion?
- Does the furniture and entourage match the specification or realistic equivalents?
- Does the rendering represent the design at the specified finish level, not an upgraded version?
- Are renderings used to test and reveal design problems, not only to sell the design?
- Is the client told what the rendering represents and where it is conceptual?
