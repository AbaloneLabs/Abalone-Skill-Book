# VR AR And Immersive Presentation Design

Immersive technologies — VR walkthroughs, AR overlays, 360 panoramas — offer powerful design communication, and their power to convince is matched by their power to mislead. The judgment problem is that immersive experiences feel real to the viewer, so their inaccuracies — wrong scale perception, idealized lighting, missing construction reality — are experienced as truth rather than representation. An agent who delivers a VR walkthrough that makes a tight room feel spacious, or that omits the structural column the design must work around, creates a visceral conviction that the built space then betrays. The agent's job is to calibrate the immersive experience for accurate scale and perception, design the interaction and navigation for comfort, represent the design honestly including its constraints, and use immersion to reveal rather than persuade.

## Core Rules

### Calibrate Scale And Eye Height For Accurate Perception

VR experiences can distort scale perception — spaces feel larger or smaller than they are depending on eye height, field of view, and navigation speed. Calibrate the virtual eye height to actual human height, set the field of view to match the headset, and verify that the perceived scale matches the modeled dimensions. A VR experience that makes a 2.4-meter ceiling feel generous sets up disappointment when the built ceiling feels lower.

### Design Navigation For Comfort And No Motion Sickness

VR navigation that moves the viewer through space can induce motion sickness, particularly with artificial locomotion, and the interaction design must minimize this. Use teleportation, snap turning, or fixed viewpoints where possible, limit smooth locomotion, and test with susceptible users. A VR presentation that nauseates the client damages the design communication rather than enhancing it.

### Represent Lighting And Materials Consistently With Reality

Immersive rendering must represent lighting and materials as they will actually appear, not idealized, because the viewer experiences them as real. Calibrate the virtual lighting to the design's actual fixture performance, represent materials at their real reflectance and scale, and avoid idealized conditions. An immersive experience lit more beautifully than the design delivers a false conviction about the atmosphere.

### Show Design Constraints, Not Only The Ideal View

Immersive walkthroughs can be choreographed to show ideal views and hide constraints — structural columns, low beams, tight clearances — that the occupant will encounter. Show the full space including its constraints, so the client experiences the real conditions, not a curated path. A walkthrough that hides the column in the middle of the room creates a conviction that the space is open, which the built reality contradicts.

### Use AR To Overlay Design On Real Conditions

Augmented reality overlays the design on the real space, allowing the client to see proposed changes in context, and this is powerful for renovation and fixture placement. Calibrate the AR overlay to the real geometry, anchor it accurately, and use it to test scale, clearances, and visual relationships in the actual space. AR that is poorly anchored or scaled misleads about fit and proportion.

### Design The Interaction For The Audience's Technical Comfort

VR and AR interactions must be designed for the client's technical comfort, not the designer's. Provide guided experiences, simple controls, and support for first-time users, and avoid requiring the client to master complex interfaces. A client struggling with the headset or controls is focused on the technology, not the design.

### Connect The Immersive Experience To Buildable Documentation

The immersive model must connect to the buildable documentation — the same geometry, materials, and dimensions that drive the construction drawings — so the experience represents what will be built. Avoid maintaining a separate "presentation model" that diverges from the construction model, because the divergence creates a gap between experience and reality. The model the client experiences should be the model that is built.

## Common Traps

### Scale Distortion That Misleads About Space

VR eye height, field of view, or navigation speed that distorts scale makes spaces feel larger or smaller than they are, creating false convictions. The trap is that the experience feels real while the scale is wrong. Calibrate to actual human dimensions.

### Hiding Constraints Behind Curated Views

A walkthrough that shows ideal views and omits columns, beams, or tight clearances creates a conviction of spaciousness the built space contradicts. The trap is that the curated path seems comprehensive while hiding reality. Show the full space.

### Idealized Immersive Lighting

Immersive rendering lit more beautifully than the design's actual fixtures creates a false impression of the atmosphere. The trap is that the experience feels real while the lighting is idealized. Calibrate to actual performance.

### Divergence Between Presentation And Construction Models

A presentation model maintained separately from the construction model drifts, so the immersive experience diverges from what is built. The trap is that both models seem authoritative while they disagree. Use one connected model.

## Self-Check

- Is the virtual eye height, field of view, and navigation calibrated for accurate scale perception?
- Is navigation designed to avoid motion sickness, with tested comfort for susceptible users?
- Are lighting and materials in the immersive experience calibrated to real-world performance?
- Does the walkthrough show design constraints — columns, beams, clearances — not only ideal views?
- Is AR overlay calibrated and anchored to the real geometry for accurate fit and proportion testing?
- Is the interaction designed for the client's technical comfort with guided, simple controls?
- Is the immersive model connected to the buildable documentation, not a separate presentation model?
