# Finish Schedules and Furniture Schedules

Finish schedules and furniture schedules are the bridge between the design intent (shown on the plans) and the product specification (documented in the specifications). They consolidate repetitive data—every floor finish, every wall finish, every furniture piece—into a tabular form keyed to the plans, so that the contractor and the installer can see, at a glance, what goes where. The judgment problem is that schedules are often treated as lists to be filled in at the end, when in fact they are working documents that must be accurate, complete, coordinated with the plans and the specifications, and maintained through revisions. A schedule with errors (a finish keyed to the wrong room, a furniture piece with the wrong dimensions) propagates into the field as incorrect installation.

## Core Rules

### Key Every Schedule Entry to the Plan Unambiguously

The schedule's value depends on its key to the plan. Key every finish to a room or space with a unique room number or name, shown on both the plan and the schedule; key every furniture piece to a location with a furniture tag on the furniture plan. Use a consistent keying system (room numbers like 101, 102; furniture tags like F-01, F-02) and confirm that every key on the plan appears in the schedule and every schedule entry appears on the plan. Ambiguous keys (a finish keyed to "office" when there are five offices) guarantee errors. The key is the link that makes the schedule usable; without it, the schedule is just a list.

### Document Complete Product Data for Each Entry

Each schedule entry must document the complete product data: the manufacturer, the model or product name, the color or pattern, the size or dimension, the material, the finish, and any performance data (abrasion rating for upholstery, fire classification for finishes, lumens for lighting). For finishes, include the product name, color name and number, and the manufacturer; for furniture, include the manufacturer, model, dimensions, finish, fabric, and any options. Complete data allows the contractor to procure the correct item, allows substitutions to be evaluated against the specified performance, and provides the owner a record for maintenance and replacement. A schedule with only a product name and color is insufficient for procurement and for substitution control.

### Coordinate the Schedule with the Specifications

The schedule and the specification must agree. The schedule lists the specific product for each location; the specification defines the performance, installation, and quality requirements for the product category. The schedule references the specification section (for example, a finish schedule entry references Section 09 30 00 for tile, with the product listed in the schedule and the installation specified in the section). Confirm that the products listed in the schedule meet the performance requirements in the specification, and that the specification does not list a different product than the schedule. Conflicts between the schedule and the specification create disputes and change orders; coordination eliminates them.

### Include Quantities, Units, and Waste Factors Where Applicable

For finishes, include the quantity in the appropriate unit (square feet for flooring and wall coverings, linear feet for trim) with a waste factor appropriate to the material and the installation pattern (typically 5 to 10 percent for standard installations, 10 to 15 percent for diagonal or pattern installations, more for natural stone with matching). For furniture, include the quantity per location and the total quantity. Quantities allow the contractor to price the work accurately and allow the designer to verify that the schedule covers the complete scope. Omit quantities, and the contractor estimates them, often incorrectly, leading to shortages or overages. Document the waste factor and its basis, because waste factors vary by material and pattern.

### Maintain the Schedule Through Revisions with Version Control

Schedules change through the project as finishes and furniture are revised, value-engineered, or substituted. Maintain the schedule with version control: each revision is dated, the changes are noted, and superseded versions are archived. Use revision clouds on the plans to show where the schedule changed, and update the key on the plan to match the revised schedule. Communicate revisions to the contractor and the vendors in writing, because an outdated schedule drives incorrect procurement and installation. A schedule that is not maintained through revisions becomes unreliable, and the team reverts to the drawings and to memory, both of which are error-prone.

### Provide the Schedule to the Owner as a Maintenance Record

At project closeout, the finish and furniture schedules are part of the owner's maintenance record, used for replacement, repair, and future renovation. Provide the schedules in a usable format (a spreadsheet or database, not just the drawing set), with the complete product data, the vendors, the purchase dates, and any maintenance instructions. For finishes, include cleaning and maintenance recommendations; for furniture, include the manufacturer's care instructions and the fabric cleaning codes. The schedule as a maintenance record extends the design's value beyond construction and supports the owner's stewardship of the investment.

## Common Traps

### Keying Finishes or Furniture Without Unique Room or Tag Numbers

The trap is keying a finish to a room name ("Office") or a furniture piece to a generic tag ("Sofa") when multiple instances exist. The mechanism of harm is that the contractor or installer applies the wrong finish or places the wrong furniture, because the key is ambiguous. The discipline is to use unique room numbers and furniture tags, shown on both the plan and the schedule.

### Listing Only Product Name and Color Without Performance Data

The trap is that the schedule lists the product name and color but omits the performance data (abrasion rating, fire classification, dimensions, fabric), because the data is "in the spec." The mechanism of harm is that substitutions are evaluated on name and color, not on performance, and that procurement lacks the data to order correctly. The discipline is to document complete product data in the schedule, referencing the specification for installation and quality.

### Conflicts Between the Schedule and the Specification

The trap is that the schedule lists one product and the specification lists another, because they are maintained independently. The mechanism of harm is a dispute over which governs and a change order to resolve it. The discipline is to coordinate the schedule and the specification at every issue and to confirm the products agree.

### Omitting Quantities and Waste Factors

The trap is that the schedule lists the product and location but not the quantity, leaving the contractor to estimate. The mechanism of harm is a shortage (the contractor orders too little, delaying installation) or an overage (the contractor orders too much, inflating cost). The discipline is to include quantities with waste factors appropriate to the material and pattern.

### Failing to Update the Schedule Through Revisions

The trap is that the schedule is issued at construction documents and not updated as finishes and furniture are revised, so the field team works from an outdated schedule. The mechanism of harm is incorrect procurement and installation, discovered too late to correct easily. The discipline is to maintain the schedule with version control, to update the plans to match, and to communicate revisions in writing.

## Self-Check

- Is every schedule entry keyed to the plan with a unique room number or furniture tag, shown on both the plan and the schedule?
- Does each entry document complete product data (manufacturer, model, color, size, material, finish, performance) sufficient for procurement and substitution control?
- Is the schedule coordinated with the specifications, with products agreeing and the schedule referencing the specification sections for installation and quality?
- Are quantities included in the appropriate units, with waste factors documented and appropriate to the material and installation pattern?
- Is the schedule maintained through revisions with version control, dated changes, revision clouds on the plans, and written communication to the team?
- At closeout, is the schedule provided to the owner in a usable format with complete product data, vendors, and maintenance instructions?
- Do the schedule entries meet the performance requirements in the specifications (abrasion, fire classification, dimensions), so substitutions preserve the intent?
