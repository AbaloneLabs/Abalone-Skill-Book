# Drawing Set Coordination and Completeness

A construction drawing set is the primary instruction to the contractor, and its quality determines whether the project is built as designed or becomes a series of field interpretations and change orders. The judgment problem is that drawing sets are produced under deadline pressure by multiple team members (designer, architect, engineers, consultants), and coordination between them is often assumed rather than verified. The result is a set with conflicts (the lighting plan shows fixtures where the furniture plan shows cabinets; the reflected ceiling plan shows a soffit where the mechanical plan shows a duct), gaps (a condition that is neither drawn nor detailed), and inconsistencies (the same element dimensioned differently on different sheets). Coordination and completeness are achieved through a disciplined quality control process, not through individual diligence.

## Core Rules

### Organize the Drawing Set by a Recognized Sheet Sequence

Organize the drawing set by a recognized sequence so that the contractor, the client, and the reviewers can navigate it. A typical interior drawing set: cover sheet, demolition plan, partition plan (or floor plan), reflected ceiling plan, finish plan, furniture (or FF&E) plan, power and communications plan, lighting plan, details (wall sections, casework, specialty), and schedules (doors, windows, finishes, plumbing fixtures, lighting, furniture). Number the sheets consistently (A-101 for architectural, E-101 for electrical, I-101 for interior) and maintain a sheet index on the cover. Consistent organization reduces the risk that a reviewer misses a sheet and that a condition goes undocumented.

### Coordinate Across Disciplines: Architecture, Structure, Mechanical, Electrical, FF&E

Coordination across disciplines is the core of drawing set quality. The interior plans must agree with the architectural plans (walls, doors, openings), the structural plans (load-bearing walls, structure below partitions), the mechanical plans (ducts, diffusers, equipment locations), the electrical plans (power, lighting, communications), and the FF&E plans (furniture, equipment, clearance). Conflicts are common: a lighting fixture centered over a furniture piece on the furniture plan but offset on the lighting plan; a diffuser in the ceiling where the furniture plan shows a tall cabinet; a wall on the partition plan that the electrical plan omits. Hold coordination meetings at each milestone and use overlay review (digital or physical) to identify conflicts before issuing.

### Provide Details for Every Non-Standard Condition

Standard conditions (a typical partition, a standard door) may be noted without detail, but every non-standard condition requires a detail: a custom millwork piece, a transition between two floor finishes, a ceiling soffit, a wall panel system, a specialty installation. The detail shows the geometry, the materials, the fastening, and the relationship to adjacent elements, at a scale large enough to read (typically 1 1/2 or 3 inches per foot for casework; 3/4 inch per foot for wall sections). Review the plans for conditions that are not detailed: a wall that terminates in an unusual way, a ceiling that changes height, a built-in that intersects a window. Every non-detailed condition is a condition the contractor will interpret, often differently than intended.

### Dimension Clearly and Consistently, Without Redundancy or Omission

Dimensions govern what is built. Dimension clearly from a defined reference (a building grid, a wall line, a centerline), consistently across sheets (the same reference on the plan and the detail), and without redundancy (dimensioning the same condition on multiple sheets, which can conflict) or omission (a condition that is drawn but not dimensioned). For interior work, dimension partition locations, openings, built-ins, and fixture centers, with overall dimensions for verification. Do not rely on the drawing scale for dimensions; scale dimensions are approximate and lead to errors. Review the dimensions for internal consistency (do the parts sum to the whole) and for coordination with the FF&E plan (does the furniture fit the dimensioned space).

### Include Complete Schedules for Doors, Finishes, Lighting, and Furniture

Schedules consolidate the specification data for repetitive elements (doors, finishes, lighting, plumbing fixtures, furniture) into a tabular form keyed to the plans. A complete schedule includes the mark (the key on the plan), the quantity, the product or material, the size, the finish, the fire rating (where applicable), the manufacturer and model, and notes. Review the schedules against the plans: every mark on the plan must appear in the schedule, and every schedule entry must appear on the plan. Incomplete schedules (a door on the plan that is not in the schedule, or a schedule entry that is not on the plan) create gaps that the contractor fills by assumption or by question, both of which are risks.

### Perform a Quality Control Review Before Every Issue

Before every issue (schematic design, design development, construction documents), perform a quality control review of the complete drawing set. The review checks: Are all sheets present and correctly numbered? Do the plans agree across disciplines? Are all non-standard conditions detailed? Are dimensions clear, consistent, and complete? Do schedules match the plans? Are notes consistent and not conflicting? The review is best performed by a reviewer who did not produce the drawings, because the producer reads what they intended, not what is drawn. Document the review comments and their resolution. A disciplined QC review catches conflicts and gaps before they reach the contractor, where they become change orders and disputes.

## Common Traps

### Issuing Without Cross-Discipline Coordination

The trap is that each discipline issues its own sheets without overlay review, assuming they will fit together. The mechanism of harm is conflicts discovered in the field (a duct where a light belongs, a wall where a cabinet belongs) that require rework. The discipline is to hold coordination meetings and overlay reviews at each milestone and to document conflict resolution.

### Detailing Standard Conditions but Omitting Non-Standard Ones

The trap is that standard conditions are detailed (because details exist) but non-standard conditions are not (because they require custom details), so the contractor interprets the non-standard condition. The mechanism of harm is a built condition that does not match the design intent. The discipline is to review the plans for non-standard conditions and to detail each one.

### Dimensioning the Same Condition Differently on Different Sheets

The trap is that the same condition is dimensioned on the floor plan, the furniture plan, and the detail, and the dimensions conflict. The mechanism of harm is a dispute over which dimension governs and a change order to resolve it. The discipline is to dimension each condition once, on the most appropriate sheet, and to reference that dimension elsewhere.

### Incomplete Schedules That Do Not Match the Plans

The trap is that schedules omit marks that appear on the plans or include marks that do not appear, because the schedules and plans are updated independently. The mechanism of harm is a gap that the contractor fills by assumption or by question. The discipline is to review the schedules against the plans at every issue and to confirm every mark appears in both.

### Skipping the Quality Control Review Under Deadline Pressure

The trap is that the QC review is skipped or compressed to meet an issue date, because the team is confident in the drawings. The mechanism of harm is conflicts and gaps that a review would have caught, discovered in the field. The discipline is to schedule the QC review as a non-negotiable part of every issue and to assign a reviewer who did not produce the drawings.

## Self-Check

- Is the drawing set organized by a recognized sheet sequence, with consistent numbering and a sheet index on the cover?
- Have the plans been coordinated across disciplines (architecture, structure, mechanical, electrical, FF&E) through overlay review at each milestone?
- Is every non-standard condition (custom millwork, transitions, soffits, intersections) detailed at a readable scale?
- Are dimensions clear, consistent, and complete, dimensioned once from a defined reference, without redundancy or omission?
- Do the schedules (doors, finishes, lighting, furniture, plumbing) match the plans, with every mark in both?
- Has a quality control review been performed by a reviewer who did not produce the drawings, with comments documented and resolved?
- Are notes consistent across sheets, without conflicting instructions, and is a hierarchy established for resolving conflicts between drawings and specifications?
