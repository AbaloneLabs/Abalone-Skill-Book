# Interior Specification Writing

Specifications are the written instructions that accompany the drawings and define the materials, products, workmanship, and quality standards of the project. The judgment problem is that specifications are often treated as a paperwork exercise—a list of products attached to the drawings—when in fact they are a legal and technical document that governs what is built and installed. A weak specification leaves the quality to the contractor's discretion, invites substitutions that erode the design, and fails to enforce the performance the design requires. A strong specification defines the product, the performance, the installation, and the substitution criteria with enough precision that the contractor can build the design as intended and that substitutions preserve the intent.

## Core Rules

### Structure the Specification by a Recognized System (MasterFormat or UDK)

Specifications must be structured by a recognized system so that trades, contractors, and architects can find and reference each section. The Construction Specifications Institute's MasterFormat is the standard for North American construction: Division 06 (Wood, Plastics, Composites), Division 09 (Finishes), Division 11 (Equipment), Division 12 (Furnishings), Division 22 (Plumbing), Division 26 (Electrical), and Division 28 (Electronic Safety and Security). For furniture and soft goods, a furniture schedule with specifications may supplement or replace Division 12 sections. Structure each specification by the three-part section format: Part 1 General (administrative, submittals, quality assurance), Part 2 Products (materials, fabrication, manufacturer), Part 3 Execution (installation, field quality, adjustment). The structure is the framework that makes the specification usable.

### Specify by the Method Appropriate to the Element: Proprietary, Performance, or Descriptive

Specifications use three methods, often in combination. Proprietary specification names the manufacturer and product (and may list equals or approved equals), suitable for items where a specific product is required for the design. Performance specification defines the required performance (abrasion, acoustical, fire, structural) and allows the contractor to propose a product that meets it, suitable for items where the performance matters more than the specific product. Descriptive specification describes the material and fabrication in detail without naming a product, suitable for custom work. Choose the method by the element: proprietary for specified furniture and fixtures, performance for generic materials and assemblies, descriptive for custom millwork and upholstery. State the method and the basis-of-design clearly, so the contractor knows what is required and how substitutions will be evaluated.

### Coordinate Specifications with Drawings: No Conflicts, No Gaps

The drawings and the specifications must agree and must together define the complete scope. The drawings show location, quantity, and geometry; the specifications define the product, quality, and installation. Conflicts arise when the drawings show one product and the specifications another, or when the drawings show an element that the specifications do not define. Gaps arise when an element is neither drawn nor specified. Review the drawings and specifications together for conflicts and gaps before issuing, and resolve discrepancies by a clear hierarchy (specifications govern over drawings for quality and product; drawings govern for quantity and location). A note in the General Conditions or the General Requirements establishes the hierarchy. Conflicts and gaps become change orders and disputes during construction.

### Include Submittals, Samples, and Mock-Ups as Quality Assurance

Specifications must require the submittals that allow the designer to verify quality before and during installation: product data, shop drawings, samples, and mock-ups. Require submittals for each specified product, with the submittal requirements in Part 1 of the section. Require samples for finishes, textiles, and materials where color, texture, or quality must be verified; require shop drawings for custom millwork, casework, and stone; require mock-ups for assemblies that are repeated or critical (a millwork panel, a tile wall, a painted finish). Specify that the mock-up is the standard for the work, and that work must match the approved mock-up. Submittals, samples, and mock-ups are the designer's tools for enforcing quality; without them, the specification is unenforceable.

### Define Installation, Workmanship, and Acceptance Criteria

Specifications must define not only the product but the installation: the substrate preparation, the installation method, the workmanship standard, and the acceptance criteria. Reference industry standards (ANSI, ASTM, the Tile Council of America's Handbook, the Architectural Woodwork Standards) for installation and workmanship, and specify the grade or level (Premium Grade, Custom Grade for casework; Level 4 or 5 for drywall finish). Define the acceptance criteria: tolerances, alignment, cleanliness, and the conditions under which the work will be rejected. Without installation and acceptance criteria, the contractor's standard applies, which may be lower than the design requires, and disputes over quality become subjective.

### Control Substitutions Through a Defined Process

Substitutions are inevitable, and the specification must control them. Define the substitution process: when substitutions may be proposed (before bidding, or during a defined substitution period), what documentation is required (product data, performance comparison, price comparison), and the criteria for approval (equal performance, quality, and design intent). Require that substitutions be submitted in writing and approved before installation; do not allow after-the-fact substitutions. Evaluate substitutions against the specification's performance and quality requirements and against the design intent, not just against the product name. Document the approval or rejection with the rationale, because the substitution record is part of the project's quality control and protects the designer and the client.

## Common Traps

### Listing Products Without Performance or Installation Criteria

The trap is that the specification lists the product (manufacturer, model, color) but omits the performance requirements, the installation method, and the acceptance criteria. The mechanism of harm is that the contractor installs the product to a lower standard than intended, or that a substitution is evaluated only on product equivalence, not on performance. The discipline is to specify the product, the performance, the installation, and the acceptance criteria together.

### Conflicts Between Drawings and Specifications

The trap is that the drawings show one product or assembly and the specifications describe another, often because they are prepared by different team members at different times. The mechanism of harm is a dispute during construction over which governs, and a change order to resolve the discrepancy. The discipline is to review drawings and specifications together for conflicts and gaps before issuing and to establish a clear hierarchy for resolution.

### Omitting Submittals, Samples, and Mock-Ups

The trap is that the specification does not require submittals or samples, so the designer has no opportunity to verify quality before installation. The mechanism of harm is that the installed work does not meet the design intent, and the correction requires removal and reinstallation. The discipline is to require submittals, samples, and mock-ups for specified products and to make the approved submittal or mock-up the standard for the work.

### Allowing Open-Ended or After-the-Fact Substitutions

The trap is that the specification allows substitutions at any time or does not define the process, so the contractor substitutes freely during construction. The mechanism of harm is the accumulation of substitutions that erode the design intent and the quality, without review. The discipline is to define the substitution process, to require written proposals and approval before installation, and to document the approval with the rationale.

### Specifying Custom Work Without Fabrication or Workmanship Standards

The trap is that custom millwork, upholstery, or stone is specified by description without reference to industry standards (Architectural Woodwork Standards, ANSI) or to a grade or level. The mechanism of harm is that the custom work is fabricated to the fabricator's standard, which may be lower than the design requires, and disputes over quality become subjective. The discipline is to reference the applicable standard and to specify the grade or level.

## Self-Check

- Is the specification structured by MasterFormat (or recognized system), with each section in the three-part format (General, Products, Execution)?
- Is each element specified by the appropriate method (proprietary, performance, descriptive), with the basis-of-design and substitution criteria stated?
- Are drawings and specifications reviewed together for conflicts and gaps, with a clear hierarchy for resolution?
- Are submittals, samples, and mock-ups required in Part 1, with the approved submittal or mock-up as the standard for the work?
- Are installation methods, workmanship standards (ANSI, AWS, ASTM), and acceptance criteria (tolerances, grades, levels) specified?
- Is the substitution process defined, with written proposals, approval criteria, and documentation required before installation?
- For custom work, are fabrication standards (Architectural Woodwork Standards, grade or level) referenced, so quality is objective, not subjective?
