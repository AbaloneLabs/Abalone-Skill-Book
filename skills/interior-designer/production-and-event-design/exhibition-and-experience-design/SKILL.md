# Exhibition and Experience Design

Exhibition and experience design structures a visitor's journey through content, where the environment, the narrative, and the interaction combine to produce understanding or emotion. The judgment problem is that designers often approach exhibitions as interiors to be filled with content, when the visitor's experience is shaped by the sequence, the dwell time, the reading level, and the physical and cognitive load of navigating the space. A weak exhibition overwhelms visitors (too much text, too many objects, no hierarchy), underwhelms them (sparse, uninterpreted, forgettable), or loses them (confusing circulation, no clear path, dead ends). The discipline is to design the narrative arc and the visitor flow as one system, to calibrate content density to attention, and to design for the range of visitors who will encounter the work.

## Core Rules

### Structure the Narrative Arc and the Visitor Flow as One System

An exhibition tells a story, and the visitor's physical movement through the space should follow the story's arc. Structure the narrative (introduction, rising tension, climax, resolution, or thematic sequence) and map it to the circulation (entry, sequence of galleries or zones, culmination, exit) so that the visitor encounters the content in the intended order. Design the circulation to reinforce the narrative: a linear path for a chronological story, a branching path for thematic exploration, a central hub for a visitor-determined sequence. Provide clear wayfinding (numbering, color coding, floor markings) so that visitors can follow the intended sequence or, if the design allows choice, can navigate confidently. A narrative without a corresponding flow produces a disconnected experience; a flow without a narrative produces a space the visitor wanders without understanding. Design the two together, testing the sequence by walking it.

### Calibrate Content Density to Visitor Attention and Dwell Time

Visitors have finite attention and finite time, and exhibitions that exceed either lose their audience. Calibrate the content density—the number of objects, the length of text, the number of interactives—to the expected dwell time and the visitor's reading tolerance. A common failure is the "label-heavy" exhibition, where visitors read the first label, skim the second, and read no more, missing the interpretation. Use hierarchy to manage density: a headline message (readable at a glance, establishing the theme), a secondary message (a paragraph, for engaged visitors), and a deep layer (a catalog, audio guide, or digital resource, for the deeply interested). Distinguish the "must-see" content (the core narrative, the signature objects) from the "nice-to-see" content (supporting material, tangential stories), and ensure that a visitor who engages only the must-see layer leaves with the essential message. A well-calibrated exhibition respects the visitor's attention; a poorly calibrated one squanders it.

### Design for the Range of Visitors and Accessibility

Exhibitions serve a range of visitors: adults and children, experts and novices, fluent readers and language learners, visitors with mobility, visual, auditory, or cognitive differences. Design for this range through multiple modalities: text for readers, audio for listeners, tactile and interactive elements for kinesthetic learners, visual and object-based content for those who engage through looking. Ensure physical accessibility (wheelchair-turning radius, reachable label heights, accessible routes with no steps or thresholds, seating at intervals), sensory accessibility (adjustable lighting for photosensitive visitors, quiet zones or times for neurodivergent visitors, captioning and transcripts for audio-visual content), and cognitive accessibility (plain language summaries, multilingual labels where the audience demands, consistent navigation conventions). Accessibility is not an add-on; it is a design parameter that, when integrated, improves the experience for all visitors, not only those with identified needs.

### Balance Object Conservation with Visitor Access

Exhibitions that display original objects—artworks, artifacts, specimens—must balance the visitor's access (the desire to see, approach, and engage) with the object's conservation (the requirement to preserve it from light, humidity, handling, and theft). Coordinate with conservators on the environmental requirements: light levels (often 50 lux or lower for light-sensitive materials like textiles and works on paper), ultraviolet filtration, temperature and humidity ranges, and duration of display (with rotation for sensitive materials). Design cases, barriers, and distance to protect objects without alienating visitors: a case that is too distant or too reflective defeats the viewing; a barrier that is too low invites handling. Integrate security (alarms, guards, sightlines) unobtrusively, because overt security degrades the experience. The balance is not conservation versus access but conservation enabling sustained access—an object preserved today is an object that can be exhibited tomorrow.

### Design Interactivity with Purpose and Durability

Interactives—touchscreens, mechanical devices, immersive environments, digital experiences—can deepen engagement, but they are often the weakest element of an exhibition, because they are designed for novelty rather than purpose and built for permanence rather than the abuse of public use. Design each interactive with a clear purpose: what understanding or emotion does it produce that the static content cannot? An interactive without a purpose is a distraction that competes with the content. Design for durability and maintenance: public interactives are subjected to heavy use, spills, and misuse, and a broken interactive is worse than none (it signals neglect). Specify robust mechanisms, accessible components for repair, and a maintenance plan with the institution. Test interactives with actual visitors before launch, because designers' assumptions about how people will use them are consistently wrong. A purposeful, durable, tested interactive enhances the exhibition; a purposeless, fragile, untested one degrades it.

### Integrate Digital and Physical Layers Coherently

Many exhibitions integrate digital layers—audio guides, augmented reality, apps, projections—alongside the physical content. Integrate these layers coherently, so that they complement rather than compete with the physical experience. An audio guide that narrates over the same content as the label divides the visitor's attention; an augmented reality layer that adds context the physical content lacks enriches it. Design the digital layers to be optional (not all visitors will use them) and to not gate essential content behind a device or app that may fail, run out of battery, or exclude visitors without smartphones. Coordinate the digital and physical design teams, because the layers must be designed together—an AR layer designed after the physical exhibition is complete is usually bolted on and incoherent. The digital should extend the physical, not replace or duplicate it.

## Common Traps

### Designing the Space Without Structuring the Narrative and Flow

The trap is designing the galleries as interiors and filling them with content, without structuring the narrative arc and mapping it to the circulation. The harm is a disconnected experience where visitors wander without understanding the story. The discipline is to design the narrative and the flow as one system, testing the sequence by walking it.

### Overwhelming Visitors with Content Density

The trap is including too much text, too many objects, and too many interactives, exceeding the visitor's attention and dwell time. The harm is visitors who disengage, reading no labels and missing the interpretation. The discipline is to calibrate density to attention, using hierarchy to distinguish must-see from nice-to-see content.

### Treating Accessibility as an Add-On

The trap is designing the exhibition for a default visitor and adding accessibility (ramps, large print, captioning) as an afterthought. The harm is a compromised experience for visitors with differences and a fragmented design. The discipline is to design for the range of visitors through multiple modalities from the outset, integrating physical, sensory, and cognitive accessibility.

### Sacrificing Conservation for Access or Vice Versa

The trap is prioritizing visitor access at the expense of object conservation (over-lighting, allowing handling) or conservation at the expense of access (objects too distant or dimly lit to engage). The harm is damaged objects or disengaged visitors. The discipline is to balance conservation and access, coordinating with conservators on environmental requirements and designing cases and barriers that protect without alienating.

### Designing Interactives for Novelty Without Purpose or Durability

The trap is including interactives because they are engaging or impressive, without a clear interpretive purpose or robust construction. The harm is interactives that distract from the content and that break under public use, signaling neglect. The discipline is to design each interactive with a purpose, to specify durable and maintainable construction, and to test with actual visitors.

## Self-Check

- Is the narrative arc structured and mapped to the circulation, so that the visitor's physical movement follows the story, with clear wayfinding for the intended sequence?
- Is content density calibrated to expected dwell time and reading tolerance, with hierarchy distinguishing must-see from nice-to-see content?
- Does the design serve the range of visitors through multiple modalities (text, audio, tactile, visual) and integrated physical, sensory, and cognitive accessibility?
- Are object conservation requirements (light levels, UV filtration, temperature, humidity, display duration) coordinated with conservators, with cases and barriers that protect without alienating?
- Are interactives designed with a clear interpretive purpose, robust and maintainable construction, and tested with actual visitors before launch?
- Are digital layers (audio guides, AR, apps) integrated coherently with the physical content, complementing rather than competing, optional rather than gating essential content?
