# Kitchen and Back of House Coordination

The back of house is where a restaurant is actually made, and a beautiful dining room attached to a broken kitchen will fail no matter how good the design looks up front. The judgment problem is that designers and operators often treat the kitchen as the contractor's problem, specify it loosely, and discover too late that the cooking line cannot handle peak volume, that the dish pit contaminates the prep area, or that servers and cooks collide at the pass. A commercial kitchen is a precision industrial environment governed by heat, grease, water, sanitation codes, and the relentless pace of service, and the interior designer is responsible for coordinating its relationship to the front of house even when a kitchen consultant details the equipment. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether the back of house actually supports the menu and the volume, not merely whether it fits in the remaining space.

## Core Rules

### Size the Kitchen to the Menu and the Peak Volume, Not the Floor Plan Leftover

The kitchen is not the space left over after the dining room is laid out; it is determined by the menu, the cooking methods, the number of covers, and the prep strategy. A complex menu cooked to order needs more line and prep area than a limited assembly menu; a high-volume concept needs wider aisles and duplicate stations to avoid bottlenecks. The rule is to derive the kitchen area and station count from the menu and the peak service volume first, then fit the dining room into what remains. Inverting this sequence is the single most common cause of restaurant failure.

### Design the Pass as the Hinge Between Kitchen and Service

The kitchen pass, where finished plates are checked and handed to servers, is the most critical interface in the entire restaurant. It must be wide enough for peak ticket volume, positioned so servers can reach it without entering the cooking line, and located so the expediter can see both the kitchen and the dining room. The rule is to treat the pass as a designed element, not a gap between equipment, and to ensure its width, height, and sightlines support the rhythm of service. A pass that is too small, poorly located, or blind to the room creates chaos at exactly the moment the restaurant is busiest.

### Separate Clean and Dirty Flows to Protect Food Safety

The flow of clean food, plates, and service items must not cross the flow of dirty dishes, trash, and waste. The rule is to design the dish pit and trash removal on a path that does not return through prep and cooking areas, and to ensure that incoming deliveries and outgoing waste use separate routes. This is not merely a code issue; cross-contamination paths are how health inspections fail and how foodborne illness originates. The decision criterion is to trace every item, raw ingredient to served plate and served plate to cleaned dish, and confirm no dirty flow contaminates a clean flow.

### Provide Adequate Walk-In, Dry Storage, and Prep for the Delivery Cadence

Storage sizing depends on the delivery cadence the operator can sustain. A restaurant in a location with daily deliveries needs less storage than one relying on weekly deliveries; a menu built on fresh, made-in-house components needs more prep area than one built on purchased components. The rule is to size walk-in refrigeration, dry storage, and prep tables to the actual supply chain and production strategy, not to a generic percentage of floor area. Undersized storage forces daily emergency deliveries and crowded, unsafe prep.

### Coordinate Grease, Ventilation, and Plumbing Early

Commercial kitchens generate heat, grease, moisture, and waste that the building must be engineered to handle. Type I hoods over grease-producing equipment, make-up air, grease waste plumbing, and floor drains must be coordinated with the architectural and MEP design from the earliest schematic phase, not added later. The rule is to map every piece of grease-producing and water-using equipment, confirm the hood and utility coverage, and verify the building can support the exhaust and make-up air loads. Late discovery of ventilation or plumbing limits forces expensive redesign or menu restriction.

### Plan Staff Circulation, Changing, and Break Space

Back of house is also a workplace, and staff need a path to enter, change, store belongings, take breaks, and use restrooms without passing through the cooking line or the dining room. The rule is to provide dedicated staff entry, locker or changing area, and staff restroom that meet code and dignity standards. The trap is treating staff support as optional because the kitchen is tight, which produces a kitchen where staff store coats with food and take breaks in the dish pit.

## Common Traps

### Treating the Kitchen as the Leftover Space

The designer lays out the dining room to maximize covers and gives the kitchen whatever rectangle remains, assuming the kitchen consultant will make it work. The harm is that the cooking line, prep, and storage cannot support the menu or the volume, and the restaurant cannot execute its concept at peak. The mechanism of failure is optimizing the visible, revenue-attributed front of house while starving the invisible back of house that actually produces the product.

### Undersizing the Pass for Peak Ticket Volume

The pass is sized for the average ticket flow and works fine until the Friday night rush, when tickets stack up, plates wait, and food quality collapses as dishes sit. The harm is that the restaurant performs well on slow nights and fails precisely when it is fullest and most visible. The false signal is that the pass works during testing, because tests rarely simulate the peak ticket volume that reveals the bottleneck.

### Crossing Dirty and Clean Paths

The dish pit is placed wherever it fit, and the route from dining room to dish and from dish back to the line cuts through the prep area. The harm is chronic contamination risk, failed health inspections, and the real possibility of foodborne illness. The mechanism is that the designer optimized circulation distance without tracing the sanitary logic of clean and dirty flows, so the layout is efficient and unsafe simultaneously.

### Ignoring Ventilation Until Equipment Is Selected

The kitchen is laid out and equipment selected before anyone checks whether the building's exhaust and make-up air capacity can support the hood load. The harm is a forced reduction in cooking equipment, a switch to lower-grease methods, or an expensive mechanical retrofit, any of which compromises the menu. The defense is to coordinate hoods, exhaust, and make-up air in parallel with the equipment plan from the earliest schematic design.

### Forgetting Staff Support Space

In a tight kitchen, lockers, changing area, and staff restroom are the first things cut, because they produce no revenue. The harm is that staff store personal items in food storage areas, change in restrooms, and experience the workplace as degrading, which drives turnover in an industry already plagued by it. The mechanism is treating staff space as overhead rather than as the infrastructure that sustains the labor the restaurant depends on.

## Self-Check

- Did I derive the kitchen area and station count from the menu, cooking methods, and peak cover volume before fitting the dining room, rather than giving the kitchen the leftover space?
- Did I design the pass as a deliberate element with adequate width, sightlines to both kitchen and dining room, and server access that does not enter the cooking line?
- Did I trace clean and dirty flows, from raw ingredient to served plate and from served plate to cleaned dish, to confirm no contamination path?
- Did I size walk-in, dry storage, and prep to the delivery cadence and the make-versus-buy production strategy, not to a generic floor-area percentage?
- Did I coordinate Type I hoods, exhaust, make-up air, grease waste, and floor drains with the MEP design from the earliest schematic phase?
- Did I provide dedicated staff entry, changing and locker space, and staff restroom that meet code and dignity standards?
- Did I verify the building can support the kitchen's exhaust and make-up air loads before finalizing the equipment plan?
