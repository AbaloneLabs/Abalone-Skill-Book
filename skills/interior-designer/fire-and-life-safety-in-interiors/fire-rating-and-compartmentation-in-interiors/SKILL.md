# Fire Rating And Compartmentation In Interiors

Fire-rated assemblies compartment the building to contain fire and smoke, and their integrity depends on details that are easy to overlook. The judgment problem is that fire ratings are treated as a wall or floor property, when they are actually assembly properties that depend on the continuity of the rating through penetrations, joints, openings, and transitions. An agent who specifies a two-hour wall but allows unprotected penetrations, unrated doors, or a ceiling that stops short of the deck delivers a wall that looks rated and is not. The agent's job is to identify where rated assemblies are required, specify the complete rated assembly, protect every penetration and opening, and coordinate the rated construction so the compartment is continuous.

## Core Rules

### Identify Required Fire Ratings From Building Code And Type

Fire ratings are required by the building code based on construction type, occupancy, and the location of the assembly — exit stairs and corridors, occupancy separations, shaft enclosures, and floor and wall assemblies. Identify every required rating from the code and the architect's basis of design before detailing interiors, because interior work often intersects rated assemblies. Assuming a wall is non-rated when it is required to be rated leads to non-compliant construction.

### Specify The Complete Rated Assembly, Not Just The Wall

A fire-rated assembly includes the wall or floor, the framing, the boards or units, the joints, and the penetrations, all tested together as a system. Specify the complete listed assembly, including the number and type of boards, fastening, and joint treatment, because substituting any component voids the listing. A wall built with the right board but the wrong fastening or joint treatment does not achieve the rated performance.

### Protect Every Penetration With Listed Fire-Stopping

Every penetration of a rated assembly — electrical boxes, pipes, ducts, cables — must be protected with a listed fire-stopping system matching the penetration type and the assembly. Specify the fire-stopping product and detail, and require documentation that the installed system matches a listed configuration. Unprotected penetrations void the rating and provide a path for fire and smoke spread.

### Coordinate Rated Doors, Frames, And Glazing

Openings in rated walls require rated doors, frames, and glazing as a listed assembly, with a rating matching the wall, and the hardware must be listed. Coordinate the door and frame specification with the wall rating, and verify that vision panels use rated glazing within the area limits. A non-rated door or oversized vision panel in a rated wall voids the compartment.

### Maintain Rating Continuity At Ceilings And Floors

A rated wall must extend continuously from floor to the rated floor or roof above, not stop at a suspended ceiling, unless the ceiling is part of the rated assembly. Verify that rated walls extend through ceiling plenums and that the rating is continuous at intersections with floors and exterior walls. A wall that stops at a non-rated ceiling allows fire to spread through the plenum, defeating the compartment.

### Address Rated Corridors And Exit Enclosures

Corridors and exit stairs are often required to be fire-rated, and the rating extends to all walls, doors, and penetrations along the path. Verify the corridor or enclosure rating, and ensure that every door, window, and penetration along the path maintains the rating. A rated corridor with an unrated door or unprotected penetration fails the enclosure.

### Coordinate Dampers In Rated Duct Penetrations

Where ductwork penetrates a rated assembly, fire dampers, smoke dampers, or combination dampers are required, and their type and installation must match the assembly and the system design. Coordinate the damper requirement with the mechanical design, and verify access for damper inspection and reset. A duct penetration without the required damper voids the rating and provides a path for fire and smoke.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Rating As A Wall Property, Not An Assembly

Specifying "two-hour wall" without the complete listed assembly — boards, fastening, joints — produces a wall that may not achieve the rating. The trap is that the wall looks substantial while the construction does not match a tested assembly. Specify the complete listed assembly.

### Unprotected Penetrations Voiding The Rating

Electrical boxes, pipes, and cables through a rated wall without listed fire-stopping provide paths for fire and smoke, voiding the compartment. The trap is that the penetrations look minor while defeating the rating. Protect every penetration with listed fire-stopping.

### Rated Wall Stopping At A Non-Rated Ceiling

A wall that stops at a suspended ceiling instead of extending to the rated floor above allows fire to spread through the plenum. The trap is that the wall appears continuous in the room while the rating is broken above the ceiling. Extend rated walls through the plenum.

### Unrated Doors Or Glazing In Rated Walls

A non-rated door, oversized vision panel, or non-rated glazing in a rated wall voids the compartment at the opening. The trap is that the opening looks similar to a rated one while lacking the listing. Coordinate rated doors, frames, and glazing.

## Self-Check

- Are all required fire ratings identified from the building code and construction type before detailing?
- Is each rated assembly specified as a complete listed system, including boards, fastening, and joints?
- Is every penetration protected with a listed fire-stopping system matching the assembly?
- Are doors, frames, and glazing in rated walls specified as rated assemblies matching the wall?
- Do rated walls extend continuously through ceilings and plenums to the rated floor or roof above?
- Are rated corridors and exit enclosures verified at every wall, door, and penetration?
- Are fire and smoke dampers coordinated at duct penetrations, with access for inspection?
