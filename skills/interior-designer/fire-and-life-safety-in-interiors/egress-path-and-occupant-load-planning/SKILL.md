# Egress Path And Occupant Load Planning

Egress is the life-safety foundation of every interior, and it is governed by quantitative code requirements that leave no room for aesthetic preference. The judgment problem is that egress is treated as a code-compliance overlay applied late, when in fact the occupant load determines the required exit capacity, door widths, and corridor dimensions that shape the entire plan. An agent who lays out a beautiful space and then checks egress may discover that the furniture layout blocks an exit, a corridor is too narrow for the occupant load, or the travel distance exceeds the limit, forcing costly redesign. The agent's job is to establish the occupant load and egress capacity early, design the paths as part of the layout, and verify every egress dimension and door condition against the code throughout the process.

## Core Rules

### Calculate Occupant Load Early To Drive The Design

The occupant load, calculated from the use and floor area per code factors, determines the required number and width of exits, the corridor width, and the door hardware. Calculate this load at the start of design, because it sets dimensional requirements that shape the plan. Discovering late that the occupant load requires an additional exit or wider corridors forces fundamental redesign.

### Provide The Required Number And Width Of Exits

Codes specify the minimum number of exits — typically two for most occupancies, more for high loads — and the minimum clear width per occupant served. Verify that the plan provides the required number of exits and that each door and corridor provides the required clear width, measured at the narrowest point including hardware projections. An undersized exit or a missing second exit fails the code and endangers occupants.

### Verify Maximum Travel Distance To An Exit

Codes limit the maximum distance from any point to an exit, varying by occupancy and sprinkler status, and exceeding this distance requires an additional exit or rearrangement. Measure the travel distance along the actual path, including around furniture and partitions, not as a straight line. A plan that looks well-distributed can exceed travel distance in a remote corner, requiring redesign.

### Keep Egress Paths Clear Of Obstructions And Furniture

Egress paths must remain clear of furniture, displays, and fixtures that narrow the required width or obstruct passage, and this must be maintained for the life of the space, not just at completion. Design the furniture layout to respect the egress width, and communicate to the client that the paths must remain clear. A display or cabinet placed in a corridor after occupancy can render a compliant space non-compliant.

### Coordinate Door Hardware For Egress Operation

Egress doors must open with one motion, without a key or special knowledge, and panic hardware may be required based on occupancy and load. Verify that every egress door's hardware allows single-motion egress, and that panic hardware is provided where the load triggers it. A locked or complex egress door can trap occupants in an emergency.

### Address Common Path Of Travel And Dead Ends

Codes limit the length of a common path of travel — the distance before a occupant has a choice of two exits — and the length and configuration of dead-end corridors. Verify these conditions in the plan, because a long dead-end or common path can require an additional exit or corridor reconfiguration. These conditions are easy to overlook in complex plans.

### Coordinate Egress With Fire-Rated Assemblies And Areas Of Refuge

Egress paths often pass through fire-rated corridors and stair enclosures, and the doors, walls, and penetrations must maintain the required ratings. In some occupancies, areas of refuge or accessible means of egress are required. Coordinate the egress path with the rated assemblies and any required refuge or accessible egress features.

## Common Traps

### Treating Egress As A Late Code Overlay

Calculating egress after the plan is fixed often reveals insufficient exit width, excessive travel distance, or blocked paths that require redesign. The trap is that the plan looks complete while failing the fundamental life-safety requirements. Establish occupant load and egress capacity at the start.

### Measuring Travel Distance As A Straight Line

Travel distance is measured along the actual path a person walks, around furniture, partitions, and fixtures, not as a straight line. The trap is that the straight-line distance is within the limit while the actual path exceeds it. Measure the real path.

### Furniture Or Displays Blocking Egress Width

A compliant plan can be rendered non-compliant by furniture, displays, or fixtures placed in the egress path after completion. The trap is that the design was compliant while the furnished or occupied space is not. Design the furniture to respect egress and communicate the requirement.

### Egress Doors With Complex Or Locked Hardware

An egress door with a deadbolt, double-cylinder lock, or hardware requiring two motions traps occupants in an emergency. The trap is that the hardware provides desirable security while failing life-safety. Verify single-motion egress at every exit door.

## Self-Check

- Is the occupant load calculated early and used to drive exit count and width requirements?
- Does the plan provide the required number of exits and clear width at every point?
- Is the travel distance measured along the actual path and within the code limit?
- Are egress paths kept clear of furniture, displays, and fixtures for the required width?
- Does every egress door allow single-motion exit, with panic hardware where required?
- Are common path of travel and dead-end conditions verified against code limits?
- Is the egress path coordinated with fire-rated assemblies and any required refuge or accessible egress?
