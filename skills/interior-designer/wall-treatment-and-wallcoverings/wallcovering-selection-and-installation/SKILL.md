# Wallcovering Selection And Installation

Wallcoverings deliver pattern, texture, and material richness that paint cannot, but their specification is where appearance and performance most often diverge. The judgment problem is that wallcoverings are selected from small samples that conceal the realities of large-scale installation: pattern repeat across a room, seam visibility on textured materials, adhesion to the specific substrate, and compliance with fire codes in commercial use. An agent can specify a beautiful grasscloth that shows every seam and cannot be cleaned, a vinyl that fails fire code, or a pattern whose repeat lands awkwardly around windows and doors. The agent's job is to select wallcoverings by performance and installation behavior as much as by appearance, plan the layout to control seams and pattern, and prepare the substrate so the covering performs.

## Core Rules

### Verify Fire Code Classification For The Occupancy

Commercial and public interiors require wallcoverings that meet specific fire-performance classifications — typically a Class A or documented flame spread rating — and residential occupancies have their own requirements. Verify the wallcovering's tested classification against the code requirement before specifying, because a non-compliant covering will fail inspection and require removal. The classification depends on the adhesive and substrate as a system, not just the face material, so specify the assembly.

### Plan Pattern Repeat And Layout Before Ordering Quantity

Wallcoverings with a pattern repeat require more material than the wall area, because each strip must align to the repeat, generating waste. Calculate the repeat drop, plan the strip layout around openings and corners, and order the quantity including the repeat waste plus a reserve. A pattern laid out without planning produces mismatched seams at the most visible points and insufficient material to complete the job from one dye lot.

### Anticipate Seam Visibility On Natural And Textured Materials

Natural materials — grasscloth, linen, cork, unbacked paper — vary between strips and show seams inherently, which can be a deliberate texture or an unacceptable flaw depending on the design intent. Understand the material's seam behavior before specifying, and set client expectations for natural variation. Specifying grasscloth expecting a seamless field produces disappointment; specifying it for its panel character produces satisfaction.

### Specify Substrate Preparation And Primer

Wallcoverings require a smooth, primed, and sized substrate; imperfections telegraph through thin coverings, and poor priming causes adhesion failure. Specify the wall preparation — skim coating, sanding, primer, and sizing — appropriate to the covering weight and adhesive. A premium wallcovering installed over an unprepared wall shows every defect and may peel at the seams.

### Control Dye Lot And Order Sufficient Reserve

Wallcoverings vary between dye lots, and completing a project from multiple lots produces visible color bands at the seams. Order the entire project quantity from a single dye lot, calculated with pattern repeat waste, plus a reserve of 10 to 15 percent for future repairs. Running short and reordering almost always means a different lot and visible mismatch.

### Match Adhesive And Backing To The Covering And Substrate

Different wallcoverings require specific adhesives and backings: heavy vinyl needs a heavy-duty clear adhesive, fabric-backed vinyl uses specific primers, unbacked papers need wheat paste, and some specialty coverings require non-woven backings. Specify the adhesive system matched to the covering and substrate, because the wrong adhesive causes bubbling, seam failure, or staining. The adhesive is part of the specification, not a contractor choice.

### Plan For Maintenance, Cleaning, And Repair

Wallcoverings in high-traffic and wet areas face abrasion, staining, and impact, and their cleanability varies enormously — vinyl is washable, paper and fabric are not. Match the covering's cleanability to the location, and plan the repair strategy, because damaged wallcoverings are difficult to patch invisibly and often require replacing full strips. Retain reserve material for repairs.

## Common Traps

### Specifying A Wallcovering Without Verifying Fire Classification

A beautiful covering that lacks the required fire classification will fail commercial inspection and must be removed, or worse, will be installed and discovered after occupancy. The trap is that the sample does not indicate the classification, and the assumption is that all commercial coverings comply. Verify the tested assembly classification before specifying.

### Ignoring Pattern Repeat In Quantity And Layout

Ordering wall area quantity without accounting for pattern repeat leaves insufficient material, and reordering produces a different dye lot with visible mismatch. The trap is that the math looks correct for flat wall area while ignoring the waste each repeat generates. Calculate repeat drop and order from a single lot with reserve.

### Expecting Natural Materials To Appear Seamless

Grasscloth, hand-printed papers, and natural weaves vary strip to strip and show seams; specifying them expecting a uniform field produces a result the client perceives as defective. The trap is that the small sample does not reveal the variation at scale. Set expectations for natural variation or choose a manufactured material.

### Under-Preparing The Substrate

Wallcoverings, especially thin ones, telegraph every substrate imperfection — dents, ridges, paint buildup — and poor priming causes peeling. The trap is that the wall "looks smooth" to the eye but reveals every flaw once covered. Specify and budget for the substrate preparation the covering requires.

## Self-Check

- Is the wallcovering's fire classification verified against the occupancy's code requirement as an assembly?
- Is pattern repeat calculated into the quantity, with strip layout planned around openings?
- Is the seam behavior of natural and textured materials understood and communicated?
- Is substrate preparation — skim, sand, prime, size — specified for the covering type?
- Is the full quantity ordered from a single dye lot with 10 to 15 percent reserve?
- Is the adhesive and backing system specified to match the covering and substrate?
- Is cleanability matched to the location, with reserve material retained for repairs?
