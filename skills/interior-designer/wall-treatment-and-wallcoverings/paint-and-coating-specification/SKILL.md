# Paint And Coating Specification

Paint appears simple because it is ubiquitous, and that apparent simplicity is exactly where specifications go wrong. The judgment problem is that "paint the walls white" hides a stack of decisions — primer type, sheen, substrate preparation, VOC class, color formulation, touch-up behavior — each of which can cause visible failure. An agent who specifies only color and sheen delivers a result dependent on the contractor's defaults, which may produce poor hide, visible touch-ups, burnishing in corridors, or peeling in wet areas. The agent's job is to specify the coating system as a whole, match sheen and chemistry to the substrate and use, prepare the substrate properly, and control color so that the painted surface performs and reads as intended across the whole project.

## Core Rules

### Specify The Full Coating System, Not Just Topcoat Color

A paint specification is a system: primer matched to substrate and topcoat, intermediate coats for hide and build, and topcoat matched to the use. Specify each layer, because omitting the primer or mismatching it to the topcoat chemistry causes adhesion failure, poor hide, and uneven finish. A topcoat-only specification leaves the system to the contractor, who will choose the cheapest compatible products, often with inferior hide and durability.

### Match Sheen To The Use And Wear Pattern

Sheen determines both appearance and performance: higher sheens are more durable and washable but highlight substrate imperfections and create glare; lower sheens hide imperfections and reduce glare but are less washable and burnish under rubbing. Match sheen to the wall's use — higher sheen in high-traffic and wet areas, lower sheen on broad wall planes and ceilings — and do not default to a single sheen across the project. A flat paint in a corridor burnishes where it is touched; a high-gloss paint on an uneven wall highlights every bump.

### Control Color Across Batches And Application Methods

Paint color varies between batches, between application methods (spray versus roller), and between wet and dry states. Specify that all paint of a single color come from a single batch where possible, require field-mixed box blending of multiple batches, and approve a field-applied mockup under the actual lighting. Color that reads correct on a chip can shift noticeably across a large wall or under different light sources, and batch variation produces visible patches.

### Prepare The Substrate To The Coating's Requirement

Paint reveals, rather than hides, substrate condition: cracks telegraph, joints show, and uneven texture is accentuated by gloss. Specify substrate preparation — priming of porous substrates, skimming of drywall joints, sanding of existing finishes, repair of cracks — to the level the coating requires. High-performance and high-gloss coatings demand near-perfect substrate preparation, and specifying them without prep guarantees a flawed finish.

### Verify Adhesion And Compatibility With Existing Finishes

In renovation, new coatings must adhere to existing paint, wallcoverings, or substrates, and incompatibility causes peeling, cracking, or lifting. Test adhesion of the proposed system to the existing surface, and specify the appropriate primer or sealer for the substrate. Applying latex over an incompatible existing finish, or coating over a contaminated surface, produces failure that appears within weeks.

### Specify VOC Class And Emissions For Occupant Health

Paint emissions affect indoor air quality, especially in occupied spaces and sensitive environments like healthcare and schools. Specify low- or zero-VOC and low-emission coatings certified by a recognized standard, and require ventilation during and after application. Standard commercial paints off-gas for weeks, affecting occupants and triggering requirements in green building certifications.

### Plan For Touch-Up And Long-Term Maintenance

Walls get scratched, marked, and patched, and the paint must touch up cleanly or the wall requires full repainting. Specify coatings with good touch-up behavior — typically lower sheens and formulations designed for field repair — and retain the batch and color formulation for future maintenance. A paint that cannot be touched up invisibly forces full-wall repainting for minor damage.

## Common Traps

### Specifying Only Color And Sheen

Leaving primer, substrate prep, and product grade to the contractor produces inconsistent results dependent on defaults. The trap is that the color is "specified" while the system that delivers it is undefined, so the finished wall may have poor hide, uneven sheen, or short life. Specify the full system.

### Using The Wrong Sheen For The Wear Condition

A flat paint in a high-touch corridor burnishes to a glossy patch wherever it is rubbed; a gloss paint on an uneven wall highlights every imperfection. The trap is that the sheen was chosen for appearance without considering wear or substrate condition. Match sheen to both the use and the wall flatness.

### Ignoring Batch Variation And Lighting Shifts

Paint mixed in multiple batches can vary in tone, and color perceived under studio lighting shifts under the project's actual lamps. The trap is that the chip looked correct, masking batch and metamerism issues that appear only on the installed wall. Box-blend batches and approve a field mockup under real lighting.

### Applying New Coatings Over Incompatible Existing Finishes

Repainting over an existing oil or contaminated surface without a compatible primer causes the new coat to peel or crack within weeks. The trap is that the wall looks fine immediately after painting, hiding the adhesion failure. Test adhesion and specify the correct primer for existing substrates.

## Self-Check

- Does the specification include primer, intermediate, and topcoat matched to substrate and use?
- Is sheen matched to the wear pattern and substrate flatness of each surface?
- Is color controlled across batches with box-blending and a field-applied mockup under real lighting?
- Is substrate preparation specified to the level the coating requires?
- Has adhesion to existing finishes been tested, with compatible primers specified?
- Is the VOC class and emissions standard specified for occupant health?
- Are touch-up behavior and retained batch formulation planned for long-term maintenance?
