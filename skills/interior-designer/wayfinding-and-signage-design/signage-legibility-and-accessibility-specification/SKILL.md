# Signage Legibility And Accessibility Specification

Wayfinding signage that cannot be read fails its only purpose, and accessibility-compliant signage that cannot be understood fails equally. The judgment problem is that legibility and accessibility are treated as checkbox compliance — minimum type sizes, minimum contrast, tactile letters present — when they are interdependent judgments about viewing distance, lighting, user vision, language, and cognitive load. An agent can meet every code minimum and still produce signage that stressed, visually impaired, or non-native users cannot parse. The agent's job is to specify signage that is genuinely legible under real viewing conditions, accessible to the full range of users, and integrated with the wayfinding strategy rather than bolted on as a compliance layer.

## Core Rules

### Size Type To Viewing Distance, Not To A Minimum

Legible type size depends on the distance from which the sign is read. A sign read at three meters needs larger type than one read at one meter, and a directional sign read while moving needs larger type still. Calculate type height against the actual viewing distance and the user's likely speed, not against a generic minimum. Signs sized to a code minimum but read from across a large lobby are illegible in practice.

### Specify Contrast As A Measured Ratio, Not An Impression

Sign legibility depends on the luminance contrast between text and background, which must be specified as a measured ratio (typically 70 percent or higher) and verified, not judged by eye. Dark text on a dark-toned "light" background, or glossy surfaces that reflect light, can fail the ratio while looking acceptable in a sample. Specify matte finishes to avoid glare, and verify contrast under the actual lighting condition.

### Coordinate Tactile And Braille Signage With Code And Ergonomics

Tactile (raised character) and braille signage is required at specific locations — typically permanent room identification — and must meet precise specifications for character height, relief depth, braille cell dimensions, and mounting location and height. Locate tactile signs on the latch side of doors, within reach ranges, and clear of door swings. Non-compliant tactile signage fails inspection and, more importantly, fails the visually impaired users it is meant to serve.

### Design For Multilingual And Pictographic Clarity

In diverse or international contexts, signage must serve users who do not read the primary language. Use internationally recognized pictograms alongside text, keep text minimal, and where multilingual text is necessary, establish a clear hierarchy (primary language larger or first). Do not rely on text alone where a significant user population reads another language. Pictograms must be standardized and unambiguous, not custom illustrations that require interpretation.

### Avoid Glare, Reflection, And Backlight Interference

Glossy sign faces, glass-mounted signage, and backlit panels can produce glare that obscures text, especially for users with low vision or under bright ceiling lighting. Specify matte or anti-glare finishes, test signage under the actual lighting, and avoid placing readable signage behind glass without accounting for reflection. A sign that is legible in a studio sample can be unreadable in a glazed lobby.

### Integrate Accessibility Signage With The Wayfinding System

Accessible signage is not a separate system bolted onto the main wayfinding; it must be integrated so that all users navigate one coherent system. Tactile and braille signs should appear at the same decision and identification points as visual signs, and accessible routes should be signed as part of the primary wayfinding, not relegated to a secondary "accessible" path. Separate-but-equal accessible signage signals that some users are secondary.

### Verify Mounting Heights, Locations, And Clear Floor Space

Accessible signage is only useful if a user can reach and approach it. Verify mounting heights within reach ranges, locate signs clear of door swings and protrusions, and ensure a clear floor space for a wheelchair user to approach. A correctly specified sign mounted too high, behind a door swing, or without approach space fails the users it serves and may fail inspection.

## Common Traps

### Meeting Code Minimums While Remaining Illegible

Code minimums for type size and contrast are floors, not targets; signage that merely meets them can be unreadable at the actual viewing distance or under the actual lighting. The trap is that the signage passes compliance review while failing users. Always size and verify against real conditions, not against the minimum.

### Specifying Contrast By Eye Rather Than By Ratio

Designers often judge contrast visually and accept combinations that look distinct in a sample but fail the measured ratio, especially dark-on-dark or tone-on-tone schemes. The trap is that the failure appears only in the field under real lighting. Specify the contrast ratio and require matte finishes, then verify.

### Treating Tactile And Braille As A Compliance Checkbox

Adding raised letters and braille without regard to location, height, or character specifications produces signage that is technically present but functionally useless to visually impaired users. The trap is that it passes a casual inspection while failing the users who depend on it. Follow the full specification: character dimensions, relief, braille cell, location, and height.

### Relying On Text Alone In Multilingual Contexts

Signage that depends on reading a specific language excludes every user who does not read it, which in airports, hospitals, and tourist venues is a large population. The trap is that the signage is perfectly clear to the designer and client, who share the language, while failing visitors. Use standardized pictograms and keep text minimal and hierarchical.

## Self-Check

- Is type size calculated against the actual viewing distance and user speed, not a generic minimum?
- Is contrast specified as a measured ratio with matte finishes, and verified under real lighting?
- Do tactile and braille signs meet character, relief, braille cell, location, and height specifications?
- Are pictograms and multilingual hierarchy used where users may not read the primary language?
- Are glare, reflection, and backlighting accounted for in finish and mounting specifications?
- Is accessible signage integrated into the primary wayfinding system, not segregated?
- Are mounting heights, door-swing clearance, and wheelchair approach space verified for every accessible sign?
