# Occupant Feedback and Environmental Comfort Assessment

Environmental comfort—thermal, visual, acoustic, and air quality—is where design intent most often diverges from occupant experience. The judgment problem is that comfort is both objective (measurable light, temperature, decibel levels) and subjective (perceived warmth, glare sensitivity, distraction), and a comfort assessment that measures only the physical conditions misses the perceptual gap that drives complaints. A weak assessment also treats all complaints equally, when a single chronic complaint (the meeting room that is always too hot) often signals a systemic design or operations failure that affects many. The discipline is to measure the physical conditions against the design targets and standards, to capture the perceptual experience that explains the gap, and to distinguish systemic issues from individual preferences.

## Core Rules

### Measure Physical Conditions Against Design Targets and Standards

The first step in comfort assessment is to measure the actual environmental conditions and compare them to the design targets and the applicable standards. For thermal: air and radiant temperature, humidity, and air velocity, compared to ASHRAE 55 or equivalent comfort standards and to the design setpoints. For lighting: illuminance (lux) at the workplane, daylight factor, and glare (uniformity, direct glare), compared to IES or EN recommendations and to the design calculations. For acoustics: background noise level (dB), reverberation time (RT60), and speech intelligibility (privacy), compared to the design criteria and to standards like ANSI S12.60 for classrooms or Building Bulletin 93. For air quality: CO2, VOCs, and ventilation rates, compared to ASHRAE 62.1 or equivalent. The comparison reveals whether the space performs as designed; a space that meets targets but still generates complaints points to a perceptual or operational issue, while a space that misses targets points to a design or commissioning failure.

### Capture the Perceptual Experience to Explain the Gap

Physical measurements alone cannot explain why occupants are uncomfortable, because comfort is perceptual. Use a structured comfort survey (such as the BUS Methodology, the CBE Occupant Satisfaction survey, or a tailored instrument) to capture perceived thermal, lighting, acoustic, and air quality satisfaction, along with perceived control, and the sources of dissatisfaction. Cross-reference the survey with the physical measurements: the meeting room that measures within thermal comfort range but is perceived as too hot may have a radiant temperature or air distribution problem that the air temperature sensor misses. The perceptual data also identifies the sources of dissatisfaction that measurements cannot—the glare from a specific window at a specific time, the noise from a specific neighbor—that point to targeted corrections. Do not dismiss complaints that contradict the measurements; the contradiction is the finding.

### Distinguish Systemic Issues from Individual Preferences

Not every complaint indicates a design failure. Distinguish systemic issues—affecting many occupants, recurring, linked to a design or operations decision—from individual preferences that reflect personal comfort differences. A systemic issue (the open office is too loud for 60 percent of occupants) indicates a design or operations problem requiring intervention; an individual preference (one person is cold at a temperature 80 percent find comfortable) may be addressed with personal control (a task light, a localized heater within safety limits) but does not indicate a design failure. Analyze the distribution of responses, not just the average: a bimodal distribution (some very satisfied, some very dissatisfied) may indicate that the space serves one work mode well and another poorly, a design insight the average conceals. Prioritize systemic issues for correction, because they affect the most occupants and have the clearest design linkage.

### Assess the Role of Personal Control and Operability

Personal control—over lighting, temperature, blinds, and acoustic privacy—is one of the strongest predictors of comfort satisfaction, often more powerful than the absolute conditions. An occupant who can adjust their task light, open a window, or close a blind tolerates a wider range of conditions than one who cannot. Assess whether the design provides operable and accessible controls: do the blinds work and are they reachable, does the task lighting allow adjustment, are the thermostat controls responsive or locked, are there spaces (phone booths, quiet rooms) that provide acoustic control? A common finding is that controls exist but are locked, broken, or unintuitive, neutralizing the design intent. The assessment should test operability, not just presence, because a non-functional control is worse than no control (it generates frustration). Where personal control is limited by the design (open plan, centralized HVAC), the assessment should identify whether compensating strategies (zoning, accessible quiet spaces) are adequate.

### Diagnose the Source: Design, Commissioning, or Operations

A comfort problem has one of three sources, and the diagnosis determines the correction. A design source: the glazing ratio causes overheating, the open plan density exceeds the acoustical treatment's capacity, the HVAC is undersized. A commissioning source: the systems were designed correctly but were not balanced, programmed, or set up correctly, so they underperform (the most common source of post-occupancy comfort problems). An operations source: the facilities staff operate the systems outside the design parameters (locked thermostats, overridden schedules, disabled sensors) or the occupants misuse the controls. The assessment should diagnose the source through the combination of physical measurement, document review (the design intent, the commissioning report), and operational observation (how the systems are actually run). A correction aimed at the wrong source—redesigning a system that was merely mis-commissioned—wastes money and fails to solve the problem.

### Connect Findings to Correctable Design and Operational Changes

The assessment should conclude with correctable actions, distinguished by source and cost. Design corrections (adding shading, increasing acoustical absorption) are typically more expensive and may require renovation; commissioning corrections (rebalancing the HVAC, reprogramming the lighting controls) are typically less expensive and high-impact; operational corrections (unlocking thermostats, training facilities staff, adjusting schedules) are the least expensive and often the quickest. Prioritize the corrections by impact (how many occupants affected) and cost, and present them to the client as a ranked list. A comfort assessment that diagnoses but does not recommend correction leaves the client with the problem; one that recommends without diagnosing the source risks the wrong correction.

## Common Traps

### Measuring Only Physical Conditions and Ignoring Perception

The trap is measuring temperature, lux, and decibels and reporting compliance with standards, while ignoring that occupants are dissatisfied. The harm is a false conclusion that the space performs well, missing the perceptual or operational gap. The discipline is to capture perceived comfort alongside physical measurement and to investigate contradictions.

### Treating All Complaints as Design Failures

The trap is treating every complaint as evidence of a design problem, when some reflect individual preferences or operational misuse. The harm is over-investment in corrections for individual preferences and misattributed blame to the design. The discipline is to distinguish systemic issues from individual preferences through response distribution analysis.

### Ignoring Personal Control and Operability

The trap is assessing the designed conditions without testing whether the controls (blinds, thermostats, task lights) are operable and accessible. The harm is missing that the design's comfort strategy depends on controls that are locked, broken, or unintuitive. The discipline is to test operability, not just presence, and to assess compensating strategies where control is limited.

### Misdiagnosing the Source of the Problem

The trap is assuming a comfort problem is a design failure when it is a commissioning or operations failure, leading to expensive redesign that does not solve the problem. The harm is wasted money and persistent discomfort. The discipline is to diagnose the source (design, commissioning, operations) through measurement, document review, and operational observation before recommending correction.

### Diagnosing Without Recommending Correction

The trap is delivering a comfort report that identifies problems and sources but no correctable actions. The harm is a client left with the problem and no path forward, discrediting the assessment. The discipline is to connect findings to ranked, source-specific, cost-aware corrections.

## Self-Check

- Are physical conditions (thermal, lighting, acoustic, air quality) measured and compared to design targets and applicable standards (ASHRAE, IES, ANSI)?
- Is perceived comfort captured through a structured survey and cross-referenced with physical measurements to explain contradictions?
- Are systemic issues (affecting many, recurring) distinguished from individual preferences through analysis of response distributions?
- Is personal control assessed not just for presence but for operability and accessibility (working blinds, responsive thermostats, intuitive controls)?
- Is the source of each comfort problem diagnosed as design, commissioning, or operations, through measurement, document review, and operational observation?
- Does the assessment conclude with ranked, source-specific, cost-aware corrections prioritized by occupant impact?
