# Post-Occupancy Evaluation Methodology

A post-occupancy evaluation (POE) is the structured assessment of a completed and occupied interior against its design intent and the occupants' actual experience. The judgment problem is that POEs are often treated as satisfaction surveys—a quick questionnaire asking if people "like" the space—when their value lies in connecting design decisions to measurable outcomes: does the open plan actually support collaboration, does the daylighting strategy actually reduce lighting complaints, does the acoustical treatment actually meet privacy needs. A weak POE collects opinions without tying them to design moves, producing findings that are unactionable. The discipline is to design the evaluation around hypotheses linking design decisions to outcomes, to use mixed methods, and to time the evaluation to capture settled occupancy rather than novelty effects.

## Core Rules

### Frame the POE Around the Design Intent and Hypotheses

A POE without a design intent to evaluate against produces undirected data. Before designing the evaluation, recover the project's design intent and the hypotheses it embedded: the open plan was intended to increase collaboration and reduce email volume; the daylighting strategy was intended to reduce lighting complaints and energy use; the acoustical pods were intended to provide confidential call space. Frame the evaluation questions around these hypotheses: did the design move produce the intended outcome? This framing connects findings to design decisions and makes the POE actionable for the client and for the designer's future work. A POE that asks only "do you like the space?" cannot answer whether the design worked; a POE that asks "has the open plan changed how you collaborate?" can.

### Use Mixed Methods to Triangulate Findings

No single method captures the full picture of how an interior performs. A strong POE combines: structured surveys (broad, quantitative, comparable across respondents), walkthroughs and behavioral observation (what people actually do, not what they report), focus groups or interviews (depth, the why behind the patterns), environmental monitoring (light levels, temperature, humidity, noise, measured against design targets and standards), and document analysis (the design documents, the original program, the budget). Triangulation—finding the same pattern across methods—strengthens the finding; a survey result that is contradicted by observation is a signal to investigate, not to dismiss either source. Do not rely on a survey alone; reported behavior and actual behavior diverge, and the divergence is often the most informative finding.

### Time the Evaluation to Capture Settled Occupancy

Occupants' responses to a new space are distorted by novelty (early enthusiasm or early resistance) and by disruption (the stress of moving). A POE conducted immediately after move-in captures these distortions, not the space's true performance. Wait until occupancy has settled—typically three to six months for routine workplaces, longer for complex environments like healthcare or education where seasonal cycles matter—so that occupants have adapted to the space and their responses reflect routine use. For environments with seasonal variation (daylighting, heating, cooling), conduct the evaluation across or after a full seasonal cycle, because a summer-only evaluation will miss winter daylighting and heating performance. A POE timed to a milestone (the client's request, the warranty period) rather than to settled occupancy produces findings that do not reflect the space's steady-state performance.

### Select Indicators That Are Measurable and Linked to Design

The indicators measured should be those that the design can plausibly affect and that the client cares about. For workplace interiors: collaboration frequency and mode, perceived productivity, environmental satisfaction (lighting, thermal, acoustic, spatial), absenteeism, and space utilization (measured by sensors or observation). For healthcare: patient and staff satisfaction, wayfinding success, perceived privacy, and clinical outcomes where attributable. For education: student engagement, teacher satisfaction, and learning outcomes where attributable. For residential: occupant satisfaction, maintenance issues, and energy performance. Avoid indicators that are not linked to design (general job satisfaction is affected by many factors unrelated to the interior) or that are not measurable with available methods. Select indicators that allow comparison to benchmarks—industry standards, pre-occupancy baselines, or comparable projects—because a number without a reference is hard to interpret.

### Address Privacy, Consent, and the Ethics of Observation

POEs collect data about people, and the collection must respect privacy and consent. Surveys should be voluntary and, where the population is small, anonymous or aggregated to prevent identification of individuals. Behavioral observation should be disclosed—occupants should know they are being observed and why—and should not capture identifiable individuals without consent. Environmental monitoring measures the space, not the people, but camera-based occupancy sensors that capture images require particular care. Focus group and interview data should be anonymized in reporting. Obtain any required institutional review board (IRB) or ethics approval for research in regulated settings (healthcare, education involving students). The ethics of POE matter not only for compliance but for the validity of the data; occupants who feel surveilled or coerced give guarded, unreliable responses.

### Structure Findings to Connect Design Decisions to Outcomes

The POE report should not be a list of complaints and compliments. It should connect design decisions to outcomes: the open plan increased reported collaboration by a measured amount but increased acoustic complaints, suggesting that the acoustical treatment was under-specified for the resulting density; the daylighting strategy met illuminance targets in perimeter zones but not in the core, suggesting a refinement to the lighting control strategy. For each finding, state the design decision, the measured outcome, the strength of the connection, and the implication for this project (corrective action) and for future projects (design guidance). Separate findings that are strongly supported (triangulated, measured) from those that are suggestive (single-method, reported) so that the client can act with appropriate confidence. A POE report that the client cannot act on is a wasted evaluation.

## Common Traps

### Conducting a Satisfaction Survey and Calling It a POE

The trap is distributing a "do you like the space?" questionnaire and reporting the scores as an evaluation. The harm is findings that are not connected to design decisions and are therefore unactionable, discrediting the POE as a practice. The discipline is to frame the evaluation around the design intent and hypotheses, connecting findings to design moves.

### Relying on a Single Method

The trap is using only a survey, or only observation, and treating the single-source findings as conclusive. The harm is missing the divergence between reported and actual behavior, or the context that only depth methods provide. The discipline is to use mixed methods and to triangulate findings across them.

### Evaluating Too Soon After Move-In

The trap is conducting the POE in the first weeks after occupancy, capturing novelty and disruption rather than settled use. The harm is findings that do not reflect the space's true performance and that may reverse as occupants adapt. The discipline is to wait three to six months (longer for seasonal or complex environments) for settled occupancy.

### Measuring Indicators Not Linked to Design

The trap is measuring broad outcomes (general satisfaction, productivity) that are affected by many factors unrelated to the interior, and attributing changes to the design. The harm is spurious conclusions and overclaimed design impact. The discipline is to select indicators the design can plausibly affect and to compare against benchmarks or baselines.

### Reporting Complaints Without Connecting to Design

The trap is listing occupant complaints and compliments without tying them to the design decisions that caused them. The harm is a report the client cannot act on, because the cause and the corrective design move are not identified. The discipline is to structure findings as design-decision-to-outcome connections with implications for action.

## Self-Check

- Is the POE framed around the project's design intent and the hypotheses linking design decisions to outcomes, rather than as a general satisfaction survey?
- Does the evaluation use mixed methods (surveys, observation, focus groups, environmental monitoring, document analysis) to triangulate findings?
- Is the evaluation timed to settled occupancy (three to six months or more, across relevant seasonal cycles), not to the move-in date or an arbitrary milestone?
- Are the indicators selected measurable, linked to design decisions, and comparable to benchmarks or pre-occupancy baselines?
- Are privacy, consent, and ethics addressed—voluntary and anonymous surveys, disclosed observation, anonymized reporting, and required IRB or ethics approval where applicable?
- Does the report connect each finding to a design decision, with the measured outcome, the strength of the connection, and the implication for corrective action and future design?
