# POE Findings and Design Iteration

The value of a post-occupancy evaluation is realized only when its findings change future design. The judgment problem is that POE findings are often delivered as a project-specific report, filed, and forgotten—so the designer repeats the same mistakes and never accumulates the evidence that would let them design with confidence rather than habit. A weak feedback loop also fails to distinguish project-specific findings (particular to this client or site) from transferable lessons (applicable across projects), so the designer either over-generalizes an anomaly or discards a pattern as one-off. The discipline is to extract transferable lessons, to feed them into the design process as evidence, and to build a cumulative knowledge base that strengthens each subsequent project.

## Core Rules

### Separate Project-Specific Findings from Transferable Lessons

Not every POE finding should change future design. Some findings are project-specific: this client's organizational culture made the open plan fail; this site's orientation made the daylighting strategy underperform; this budget forced a specification that did not last. These findings inform corrections to this project and the relationship with this client, but they should not be generalized into design rules. Other findings are transferable: the acoustical pods were used more than predicted across three projects, suggesting a design pattern; the lighting control strategy consistently underperforms when not commissioned, suggesting a process change. Distinguish the two by asking: is this finding explained by a project-specific condition, or does it recur across projects and conditions? Generalize only the transferable lessons, and record the project-specific conditions that explain the anomalies, so that future projects with similar conditions are forewarned.

### Translate Findings into Actionable Design Guidance

A finding ("occupants in the open plan reported acoustic distraction") is not yet design guidance. Translate it into an actionable move: "for open plans above a density threshold, provide acoustical phone booths at a ratio of one per ten seats, and specify sound-absorbing finishes on ceilings and partitions to an NRC of 0.9 or higher." The translation connects the finding to a design decision, a specification, and a quantitative threshold. Without this translation, the finding is an observation that the designer may or may not act on; with it, the finding becomes a design rule that can be applied and tested. Maintain a library of these translated lessons, indexed by topic (daylighting, acoustics, circulation, space allocation), so that they are retrievable when a relevant design decision arises.

### Feed Lessons into the Design Process at Decision Points

Lessons learned are useful only if they are retrieved and applied when a relevant design decision is made. Build the feedback into the design process: at schematic design, when space-planning decisions are made, review the lessons on space allocation and adjacency; at design development, when finishes and systems are specified, review the lessons on material performance and system commissioning; at construction documents, review the lessons on specification clarity and constructability. This requires the lessons to be organized and accessible, not buried in project reports. A practice that conducts POEs but does not retrieve the lessons at decision points gains no benefit from the evaluation; the retrieval mechanism is as important as the evaluation itself.

### Use POE Evidence to Justify Design Decisions to Clients

POE evidence is a powerful tool for justifying design decisions to clients, especially decisions that cost more or deviate from convention. A client reluctant to invest in acoustical treatment can be shown evidence from prior projects that acoustic complaints are the leading source of post-occupancy dissatisfaction and that the treatment reduces them measurably. A client skeptical of daylighting controls can be shown evidence that commissioned controls reduce energy and complaints. This evidence-based justification is more persuasive than professional opinion alone, because it shows that the decision is grounded in measured outcomes, not preference. Build the evidence base across projects, and use it in proposals and presentations to differentiate the practice and to support decisions that the client might otherwise resist.

### Build a Cumulative Knowledge Base Across Projects

A single POE produces a finding; a series of POEs produces a knowledge base. Aggregate findings across projects to identify patterns that no single project reveals: which design moves consistently perform well, which consistently underperform, which conditions predict success or failure. This cumulative knowledge base is the practice's intellectual capital—it is what distinguishes an evidence-based practice from one that relies on habit and convention. Structure the knowledge base to allow comparison across projects (consistent indicators, consistent methods) and to control for project-specific conditions. Over time, the knowledge base allows the designer to state, with evidence, that a given design move produces a given outcome under given conditions—a claim that few practices can make and that clients increasingly demand.

### Acknowledge the Limits of POE Evidence

POE evidence is valuable but not absolute, and overstating it undermines credibility. A POE establishes correlation (the design move and the outcome occurred together) more often than causation (the design move caused the outcome), because many factors beyond design affect outcomes. Acknowledge the confounding factors—organizational change, seasonal variation, occupant turnover—and present the evidence with appropriate confidence: "across three projects, the acoustical booths were heavily used and correlated with reduced acoustic complaints," not "acoustical booths solve acoustic problems." Distinguish findings strongly supported by multiple projects and methods from those that are suggestive from a single project. Honest limitation acknowledgment strengthens the evidence's credibility and prevents the overclaiming that, when exposed, discredits the entire knowledge base.

## Common Traps

### Generalizing a Project-Specific Finding into a Design Rule

The trap is treating a finding explained by a project-specific condition (a particular client culture, a particular site) as a universal lesson. The harm is a design rule that fails when applied to different conditions, discrediting the evidence base. The discipline is to distinguish project-specific findings from transferable lessons and to generalize only the latter.

### Filing the POE Report Without Extracting Lessons

The trap is delivering the POE report, filing it, and never extracting or applying the lessons. The harm is repeated mistakes and no accumulated evidence, negating the value of the evaluation. The discipline is to translate findings into actionable design guidance and to maintain a retrievable lessons library.

### Conducting POEs Without a Retrieval Mechanism

The trap is building a lessons library that is never consulted at design decision points. The harm is lessons that exist but are not applied, so the design process does not improve. The discipline is to build feedback into the design process at schematic design, design development, and construction documents.

### Overclaiming Causation from Correlation

The trap is stating that a design move caused an outcome when the POE established only correlation, ignoring confounding factors. The harm is overclaimed evidence that, when challenged, undermines the practice's credibility. The discipline is to acknowledge confounders and to present evidence with appropriate confidence, distinguishing strong from suggestive findings.

### Treating POE as a One-Off Rather Than a Cumulative Practice

The trap is conducting a POE as a discrete project deliverable rather than as part of a cumulative knowledge-building practice. The harm is isolated findings that never aggregate into the patterns that distinguish evidence-based design. The discipline is to structure POEs for cross-project comparison and to build a cumulative knowledge base over time.

## Self-Check

- Are project-specific findings (explained by particular client, site, or budget conditions) distinguished from transferable lessons that recur across projects?
- Are findings translated into actionable design guidance with specific moves, specifications, and quantitative thresholds, rather than left as observations?
- Is there a retrieval mechanism that surfaces relevant lessons at design decision points (schematic, design development, construction documents)?
- Is POE evidence used to justify design decisions to clients, especially costly or unconventional ones, with measured outcomes rather than opinion alone?
- Is a cumulative knowledge base structured across projects, with consistent indicators and methods, to identify patterns that single projects cannot?
- Are the limits of the evidence acknowledged—correlation versus causation, confounding factors, strength of support—so that claims are made with appropriate confidence?
