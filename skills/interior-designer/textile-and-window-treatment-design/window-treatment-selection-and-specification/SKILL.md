# Window Treatment Selection and Specification

Window treatments sit at the intersection of daylight control, thermal performance, acoustics, privacy, and aesthetics, and they are among the most operationally complex elements in a room. The judgment problem is that designers select treatments by appearance and opacity, then discover during installation that the stackback consumes the wall, the motorization requires power that was not roughed-in, the treatment blocks an operable window or door, or the fabric fails under UV exposure. Window treatments must be specified as integrated systems—fabric, hardware, controls, and structure—coordinated with the architecture, the lighting, and the user's operation, not selected as a decorative afterthought.

## Core Rules

### Match the Treatment Type to the Functional Requirement

Different treatment types solve different problems. Sheer drapery diffuses light and provides daytime privacy without blocking the view; blackout shades or drapery eliminate light for sleeping or media; cellular shades add insulation (R-value) at the window; louvered blinds or shutters allow adjustable light and privacy control; solar shades reduce glare and heat gain while preserving a view. Define the functional requirement—light control, glare control, thermal insulation, privacy, UV protection, acoustics—before selecting the type, because no single treatment optimizes all functions. In many rooms, a layered solution (a sheer plus a blackout, or a blind plus drapery) is required to meet all functions.

### Calculate Stackback and Clear Opening Before Sizing

Stackback is the space a treatment occupies when fully open, and it determines whether the window or door is usable and whether the treatment consumes the wall. Drapery stackback is roughly 25 to 33 percent of the covered width; roller and roman shades stack at the head; vertical blinds stack to one or both sides. Calculate the stackback and confirm it clears the window opening, the door swing, and adjacent furniture or art. For sliding doors, confirm the stack does not block the traffic path. For treatments mounted above the window, confirm the stack height clears the ceiling or header. Designing the treatment without stackback calculations produces installations where the treatment, when open, blocks the very window it was meant to dress.

### Specify Motorization and Controls Early for Power Rough-In

Motorized treatments—roller shades, drapery tracks, and automated blinds—require power and control infrastructure that must be roughed-in during construction. Specify the motor type (line voltage, low voltage, or battery), the power location (outlet in the head, junction box, or battery wand), and the control system (wall switch, remote, app, or integration with a home automation or lighting control system) before construction documents. Coordinate with the electrical engineer for power and with the low-voltage or integration contractor for control. Retrofitting motorization after finishes is expensive and often impossible without exposed wiring. The treatment specification must include the motor, the power, and the control as a system.

### Coordinate Treatment Mounting with Architecture and Structure

Window treatments mount to the wall, ceiling, window frame, or inside the window reveal, and each mounting condition has structural and architectural implications. A heavy drapery or a motorized shade requires secure anchoring into blocking or structure, not drywall. An outside mount requires clearance above and beside the window; an inside mount requires sufficient depth in the reveal. Coordinate the mounting with the window frame, the header, the ceiling height, and adjacent millwork. For treatments in specialty windows (arched, angled, skylights, French doors), confirm the treatment is available in the required configuration and that the mounting is feasible. The reflected ceiling plan and elevations must show the treatment mounting, not just a note.

### Specify Fabric for Performance, Durability, and Code

Fabric selection for window treatments must address performance as well as aesthetics. UV exposure degrades fabrics—specify solution-dyed or UV-stabilized fabrics for south- and west-facing windows, and confirm colorfastness. Blackout fabrics must be verified for light blockage at the edges and seams, not just the center. Solar screen fabrics are rated by openness factor (1 to 10 percent) that trades view and light against glare and heat reduction. In commercial and hospitality applications, fabrics must meet flame-resistance requirements (NFPA 701 for vertical treatments, California Technical Bulletin 117 for upholstery components); document the test report. In humid environments (bathrooms, coastal), specify mildew-resistant fabrics and hardware. The specification must state the performance requirements alongside the color and pattern.

### Address Daylight, Glare, and Thermal Performance as a System

Window treatments interact with the building's daylighting, glare control, and thermal performance. A treatment that blocks all daylight increases electric lighting energy; a treatment that admits glare makes workstations unusable; a treatment with low insulation value increases heating and cooling loads. Coordinate the treatment with the daylighting strategy: use sheer or solar shades where daylight is desired with glare control; use blackout where darkness is required; use cellular or insulated shades where thermal performance is critical. In commercial projects, coordinate with the mechanical engineer, because window treatments affect the cooling load calculation. Automated treatments that respond to sun sensors can optimize daylight and thermal performance, but only if the automation is integrated into the design.

## Common Traps

### Selecting by Appearance and Opacity Without Functional Analysis

The trap is choosing a treatment because it looks right in the design, then discovering it does not solve the room's actual problem—glare on a screen, heat gain, lack of blackout for sleep. The mechanism of harm is a beautiful treatment that the client replaces because it does not function. The discipline is to define the functional requirement first and to select the treatment type to meet it, layering treatments where a single type cannot.

### Ignoring Stackback and Sizing the Treatment to the Window Width

The trap is sizing the treatment to the window width without accounting for stackback, so that when the treatment is open it covers part of the window or the door opening. The mechanism of harm is reduced daylight, blocked views, or a door that cannot be used, and a client who feels the treatment is in the way. The discipline is to calculate stackback and to size the treatment, including the stack, to clear the opening.

### Specifying Motorization Without Power and Control Rough-In

The trap is specifying motorized shades or drapery on the finish schedule without coordinating power and control, assuming the electrician or the contractor will figure it out. The mechanism of harm is that power is not roughed-in to the treatment location, and the retrofit requires exposed wiring, a battery wand that the client must recharge, or abandonment of the motorization. The discipline is to specify the motor, power location, and control system before construction documents and to coordinate with the electrical and low-voltage trades.

### Mounting into Drywall Without Structural Blocking

The trap is specifying a heavy drapery or a motorized shade without coordinating structural blocking in the wall or ceiling framing. The mechanism of harm is that the treatment is installed into drywall anchors that cannot hold the weight, and the hardware pulls out under load or under motor vibration. The discipline is to specify blocking on the framing drawings and to verify it during framing inspection.

### Specifying Fabric Without UV, Code, or Durability Verification

The trap is selecting a fabric for color and pattern without confirming UV stability, flame resistance, or durability for the application. The mechanism of harm is fading or degradation on sunny exposures, a code violation in commercial work, or mildew in humid environments. The discipline is to specify the performance requirements—UV stability, flame resistance (with test report), abrasion, and mildew resistance—alongside the aesthetic criteria.

## Self-Check

- Is the treatment type selected to meet the functional requirement (light, glare, thermal, privacy, UV), with layered treatments where a single type is insufficient?
- Is stackback calculated and shown, confirming the treatment clears the window opening, door swing, traffic path, and adjacent elements when open?
- For motorized treatments, are the motor type, power location, and control system specified and coordinated with electrical and low-voltage rough-in before construction documents?
- Is the mounting condition (inside, outside, ceiling, wall) coordinated with structure, blocking, window frame depth, and ceiling height, and shown on the drawings?
- Does the fabric specification include performance requirements (UV stability, openness factor for solar shades, blackout verification, flame resistance with test report, mildew resistance) alongside color and pattern?
- Is the treatment coordinated with the daylighting, glare, and thermal strategy, and with the mechanical cooling load in commercial projects?
- For specialty windows (arched, angled, skylight, French door), is the treatment configuration confirmed available and the mounting confirmed feasible?
