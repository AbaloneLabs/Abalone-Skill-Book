# Soft Goods and Upholstered Furniture Specification

Upholstered furniture and soft goods—sofas, chairs, ottomans, headboards, bedding, pillows, and cushions—are where comfort, durability, and craft converge. The judgment problem is that the visible element (the fabric) is only the surface of a system that includes the frame, the suspension, the cushion, the fill, and the application method. Designers who specify by style and fabric and trust the workroom for the rest produce furniture that looks right but sags, compresses, or fails within years. Soft goods specification requires decisions about every layer of the construction, because each layer governs a different aspect of comfort, support, and longevity, and a weak layer compromises the whole.

## Core Rules

### Specify Frame Construction for Strength and Longevity

The frame is the skeleton of upholstered furniture, and its material, joinery, and reinforcement determine whether the piece holds its shape over decades. Specify hardwood frames (kiln-dried maple, oak, alder) with mortise-and-tenon, doweled, or double-doweled joinery, corner-blocked and glued, for high-quality residential and contract use. Avoid softwood, particleboard, or stapled joinery for anything beyond occasional use. For contract and hospitality, specify frames that meet durability standards and that carry a manufacturer's warranty appropriate to the use. Document the frame specification in the furniture schedule or the custom specification, because the frame is invisible once upholstered and a weak frame cannot be corrected after the fact.

### Select Suspension System by Support and Use

The suspension (the seat support under the cushion) governs the firmness, resilience, and longevity of the seat. Options include eight-way hand-tied springs (the traditional premium, offering even, resilient support); sinuous (no-sag) wire springs (common, cost-effective, suitable for moderate use); web suspension (elastic or woven webbing, suitable for light use or backs); and coil springs with a grid top. Match the suspension to the use: eight-way tied or heavy coil for premium residential and contract seating; sinuous for moderate residential; web suspension only for light use. Document the suspension in the specification, because it is a primary determinant of comfort and longevity and is invisible once the piece is upholstered.

### Coordinate Cushion and Fill Materials with Comfort and Performance

Cushion construction determines the initial feel and the long-term performance of the seat. Options include high-resiliency (HR) polyurethane foam in various densities and firmnesses, wrapped or capped with fiber or down for softness; spring-down units (coil springs in a foam casing wrapped in down or fiber) for premium plush support; and all-down or all-fiber fills for a soft, sink-in feel that requires regular fluffing. Specify the foam density (1.8 to 2.5+ pounds per cubic foot for contract and premium residential) and the indentation force deflection (IFD) for firmness. Recognize that softer fills compress and require maintenance; higher-density foams retain shape longer. For contract use, specify foams and fills that meet flame-resistance standards (CAL 117 or 133). Document the cushion construction, because the cover fabric does not reveal what is inside.

### Detail the Upholstery Application Method and Pattern Matching

The way fabric is applied to the frame affects appearance, pattern alignment, and longevity. Specify pattern matching at seams (the repeat must align across cushions, borders, and frame), the pattern direction (railroaded or up-the-roll), the placement of pattern motifs (centered on backs and seats), and the treatment of self-welt, contrast welt, or no welt. Specify the seam placement to avoid bulk at corners and to preserve pattern. For tailored upholstery, specify a tight, hand-applied finish with no ripples; for slipcovers, specify the fit and the closure method. Document the application method on the cutting order and the specification, because the workroom's default may not match the design intent.

### Specify Bedding, Pillows, and Cushions by Fill and Cover

Soft goods beyond upholstery—bedding, decorative pillows, cushions, and throws—require fill and cover specifications. For pillows and cushions, specify the fill (down, down-and-feather blend, synthetic down alternative, or solid foam) and the fill weight or density; for bedding, specify the thread count, fiber content, and construction of sheets, and the fill power and construction of duvets and comforters. Down fill power (600 to 800+) indicates loft and insulation; higher fill power is warmer and more resilient. Coordinate the fill with the cover (a down-proof ticking for down fills to prevent leakage). For contract and healthcare, specify antimicrobial, moisture-barrier, and flame-resistant fills and covers. Document the specifications so the owner can replace or augment the goods consistently.

### Coordinate Flame Resistance and Contract Performance Requirements

In commercial, hospitality, healthcare, and other regulated occupancies, upholstered furniture must meet flame-resistance standards as a complete assembly, not just as a fabric. California Technical Bulletin 133 (CAL 133) tests the complete upholstered furniture assembly for fire performance in public occupancies; CAL 117 tests components. Verify that the specified furniture, as built with the specified fabric and foam, carries a CAL 133 certificate for the applicable jurisdiction, because not all fabric-foam combinations pass. Document the certificate in the specification. For healthcare, specify antimicrobial and moisture-barrier constructions. Do not assume a fabric that passes CAL 117 makes the furniture CAL 133 compliant; the assembly must be tested.

## Common Traps

### Specifying by Style and Fabric, Trusting the Workroom for the Rest

The trap is that the visible elements—style and fabric—dominate the specification, and the frame, suspension, and cushion are left to the workroom's default. The mechanism of harm is a piece that looks right but sags, compresses, or breaks within a few years, because the default construction is built to a price, not to a performance target. The discipline is to specify every layer of the construction and to document it, so the workroom builds to the specification.

### Using Low-Density Foam for High-Use or Contract Seating

The trap is specifying foam by firmness (IFD) without specifying density, resulting in a cushion that feels right initially but compresses permanently under heavy use. The mechanism of harm is sagging, bottoming-out seats within months in contract or family use. The discipline is to specify both density and IFD, with higher densities (2.0+ lb/cu ft) for contract and premium residential use.

### Assuming a CAL 117 Fabric Makes Furniture CAL 133 Compliant

The trap is that a fabric passes CAL 117 (a component test), so the designer assumes the furniture passes CAL 133 (an assembly test). The mechanism of harm is that the complete assembly—fabric, foam, frame, and lining—does not pass CAL 133, and the furniture cannot be used in the regulated occupancy without a certificate. The discipline is to verify the CAL 133 certificate for the specific fabric-foam-frame combination and to document it.

### Ignoring Pattern Repeat, Direction, and Motif Placement

The trap is specifying a patterned fabric without documenting the repeat, direction, and motif placement, then discovering that the finished piece has mismatched seams, off-center motifs, or a pattern running the wrong direction. The mechanism of harm is a piece that looks wrong and must be re-cut and re-upholstered, wasting fabric and labor. The discipline is to document the repeat, direction, and motif placement and to calculate the yield before cutting.

### Specifying Soft Fills Without Maintenance Communication

The trap is specifying down or all-fiber fills for their luxurious feel without communicating the maintenance requirement (regular fluffing, periodic redistribution) to the owner. The mechanism of harm is a cushion that flattens and looks worn because the owner does not maintain it, leading to a complaint that the furniture is defective. The discipline is to specify the fill appropriate to the owner's maintenance tolerance and to document the maintenance requirement in the owner's manual or closeout documents.

## Self-Check

- Is the frame construction (hardwood species, joinery, corner blocking) specified and documented for strength and longevity?
- Is the suspension system (eight-way tied, coil, sinuous, web) selected by support and use, and documented in the specification?
- Is the cushion construction specified by density and IFD (for foam) or fill type and weight (for down/fiber), appropriate to the use and maintenance tolerance?
- Are pattern repeat, direction, motif placement, and application method (welt, seams, tailored vs. slipcover) documented on the cutting order and specification?
- Are bedding, pillows, and cushions specified by fill (type, weight, fill power) and cover (thread count, fiber, down-proof ticking), with contract or healthcare requirements as applicable?
- For regulated occupancies, is the CAL 133 (or applicable) certificate verified for the complete fabric-foam-frame assembly and documented in the specification?
- Are maintenance requirements (fluffing, rotation, cleaning) documented for the owner, especially for soft fills and delicate covers?
