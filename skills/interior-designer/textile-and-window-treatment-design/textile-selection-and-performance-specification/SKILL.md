# Textile Selection and Performance Specification

Textiles are among the most tactile and visible elements in an interior, and they are also among the most performance-sensitive. Upholstery must resist abrasion, staining, and fading; drapery must drape and resist UV; wall coverings must meet flame-resistance codes. The judgment problem is that textiles are selected primarily for pattern, color, and hand, and the performance specification—abrasion rating, colorfastness, flame resistance, maintenance—is treated as a verification step rather than a selection filter. A fabric that is beautiful but fails in service—pilling, fading, tearing, or violating a flame code—becomes a liability and a reupholstering cost. Textile selection must balance aesthetics with quantified performance criteria appropriate to the use, and the specification must document the criteria so substitutions preserve performance.

## Core Rules

### Define the Performance Requirement by Application Before Selecting Fabric

Different applications demand different performance. Residential upholstery in a family room requires moderate abrasion resistance (15,000 to 30,000 double rubs Wyzenbeek or Martindale) and stain resistance; contract and hospitality upholstery requires high abrasion (50,000 to 100,000+ double rubs), heavy stain resistance, and flame resistance; drapery requires drape, UV colorfastness, and minimal stretching; wall covering requires abrasion, cleanability, and flame resistance. Define the application's performance requirement as a set of target values before reviewing fabrics, and filter candidate fabrics by whether they meet the targets. Do not reverse this order, because a beautiful fabric that fails the target is a wasted selection.

### Specify Abrasion Resistance by the Correct Test and Method

Abrasion resistance is the primary durability metric for upholstery, and it is measured by the Wyzenbeek test (common in the US, reporting double rubs) or the Martindale test (common in Europe, reporting rubs). The tests are not directly comparable; a Wyzenbeek rating cannot be converted to Martindale by a formula. Specify the test method and the minimum value: residential upholstery typically 15,000 Wyzenbeek, contract 30,000 to 50,000, heavy contract and hospitality 50,000 to 100,000+. Recognize that abrasion resistance is one factor; a high-rub fabric with poor seam strength or colorfastness still fails in service. Specify abrasion alongside the other performance criteria, not in isolation.

### Verify Flame Resistance for Commercial and Hospitality Applications

In commercial, hospitality, healthcare, and other regulated occupancies, textiles must meet flame-resistance requirements, typically NFPA 701 for vertical applications (drapery, vertical panels) and California Technical Bulletin 117 (CAL 117) or 133 for upholstery. In some jurisdictions and high-occupancy Assembly uses, stricter standards apply (CAL 133 for upholstered furniture in public spaces, NFPA 260 for upholstery components). Verify the fabric's test report for the applicable standard, and document the listing or certificate in the specification. Do not accept a manufacturer's verbal claim of "flame resistant"; require the test report. A non-compliant fabric in a regulated occupancy is a code violation that must be removed and replaced.

### Coordinate Fiber Content with Durability, Maintenance, and Environment

Fiber content determines a textile's durability, cleanability, fading, and suitability for the environment. Solution-dyed acrylic and polyester offer excellent UV colorfastness for sunny exposures; wool offers natural flame resistance, resilience, and appearance retention; nylon offers high abrasion resistance for heavy-use upholstery; cotton and linen offer a natural hand but lower durability and staining; olefin offers stain and moisture resistance for outdoor or humid use. Match the fiber to the environment: avoid cotton and linen in high-sun or high-humidity locations; avoid untreated wool where moths are a risk; specify solution-dyed synthetics for sunny and high-traffic applications. Document the fiber content in the specification, because substitutions may change the performance.

### Specify Cleaning, Maintenance, and Finish Requirements

A textile's serviceability depends on its cleaning and maintenance requirements, which must be communicated to the owner and the facility staff. Specify the cleaning method (water-based, solvent, dry-clean only) using the cleaning code (W, S, W/S, X) and any special finish requirements (stain-resistant finish, antimicrobial treatment, moisture barrier). For contract and healthcare applications, specify fabrics that withstand disinfectant cleaning without color loss or degradation, and document the approved cleaners. A fabric that requires dry cleaning in a healthcare setting is unserviceable; a fabric that cannot withstand the facility's disinfectant protocol will degrade. Coordinate the maintenance specification with the owner's cleaning program.

### Document Pattern Repeat, Width, and Direction for Yield and Layout

Textile selection is not complete until the pattern repeat, fabric width, and pattern direction (railroaded or up-the-roll) are documented, because these determine the yield and the layout of cut pieces. A large pattern repeat increases the yardage required and the cost; a narrow fabric width increases seams; a one-way pattern prevents efficient layout. Calculate the yield including the repeat, and confirm the pattern direction is feasible for the upholstered piece or the drapery panel. Document the repeat, width, and direction in the specification and on the cutting order, so the workroom or upholsterer can produce the piece as designed and so substitutions preserve the layout.

## Common Traps

### Selecting Fabric by Pattern and Color, Then Checking Performance

The trap is falling in love with a fabric's appearance and verifying performance afterward, only to find it fails the abrasion, flame, or colorfastness target. The mechanism of harm is a reselection under time pressure, often to a less suitable fabric, or a costly reupholstering after the original fails in service. The discipline is to define the performance targets first and to filter candidate fabrics by performance before aesthetic selection.

### Confusing Wyzenbeek and Martindale Ratings

The trap is comparing a Wyzenbeek rating to a Martindale rating, or converting between them, leading to an under-specified fabric. The mechanism of harm is a fabric that meets the stated number but fails in service because the test method differs. The discipline is to specify the test method and to compare only like-tested fabrics, recognizing that the methods stress the fabric differently.

### Accepting Verbal Flame-Resistance Claims Without a Test Report

The trap is that a manufacturer or sales representative states a fabric is "flame resistant" or "meets CAL 117," and the designer specifies it without the test report. The mechanism of harm is that the claim is unsupported or the test method differs, and the fabric fails plan review or a field audit, requiring removal. The discipline is to require the test report or certificate and to document it in the specification.

### Ignoring Fiber Content and Environment Compatibility

The trap is selecting a fiber for hand or appearance without considering the environment—UV exposure, humidity, traffic, moths—and discovering degradation after installation. The mechanism of harm is fading, mildew, pilling, or moth damage that requires replacement. The discipline is to match the fiber to the environment and to document the fiber content so substitutions preserve the performance.

### Omitting Pattern Repeat and Direction from the Specification

The trap is specifying a fabric by name and color without the pattern repeat, width, or direction, then discovering during fabrication that the yield is much higher than estimated or the pattern cannot be laid out as shown. The mechanism of harm is a cost overrun, a delayed delivery, or a finished piece with mismatched pattern. The discipline is to document the repeat, width, and direction and to calculate the yield before committing to the fabric.

## Self-Check

- Is the performance requirement (abrasion, colorfastness, flame resistance, maintenance) defined by application and used as a selection filter before aesthetic selection?
- Is abrasion resistance specified by the correct test method (Wyzenbeek or Martindale) with a minimum value appropriate to the use, without cross-method comparison?
- For commercial, hospitality, healthcare, and regulated occupancies, is the flame-resistance test report (NFPA 701, CAL 117/133, or applicable standard) documented in the specification?
- Is the fiber content matched to the environment (UV, humidity, traffic, moths) and documented for substitution control?
- Are the cleaning code, approved cleaners, and any special finishes (stain resistant, antimicrobial, moisture barrier) specified and coordinated with the owner's maintenance program?
- Are pattern repeat, fabric width, and pattern direction documented, and is the yield calculated, including the repeat, before the fabric is committed?
- Does the specification include enough performance and physical data that a substitution preserves the original fabric's function, not just its appearance?
