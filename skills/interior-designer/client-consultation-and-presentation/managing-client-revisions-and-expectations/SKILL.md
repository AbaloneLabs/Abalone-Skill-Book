# Managing Client Revisions and Expectations

Revisions are an inherent part of interior design—the client sees the design develop and responds to it—but unmanaged revisions destroy projects. The judgment problem is that designers often accept revisions without assessing or communicating their impact, because they want to please the client and because the impact is not always obvious in the moment. A small change ("let's move that wall two feet") cascades into structural, mechanical, and finish revisions; a series of small changes accumulates into scope creep that blows the budget and the schedule. Managing revisions requires a disciplined process: assess the impact, communicate it, document the decision, and control the scope. The designer who manages revisions well serves the client better than the designer who accepts every request.

## Core Rules

### Assess the Impact of Every Revision Before Responding

When the client requests a revision, do not respond immediately. Assess the impact: what does the change affect (the floor plan, the structure, the mechanical, the finishes, the furniture, the specifications), what is the cost (design time, construction cost, procurement cost), what is the schedule impact (re-design time, revised lead times, delayed occupancy), and what is the effect on the design intent (does the change support or erode the design). The assessment may take minutes (for a finish change) or days (for a plan change). Communicate to the client that you will assess the impact and respond, rather than agreeing on the spot. An unassessed revision is a blank check the designer writes on their own budget and schedule.

### Communicate the Cost, Schedule, and Design Impact Honestly

Once the impact is assessed, communicate it honestly to the client, including the cost (design fee, construction cost, procurement cost), the schedule (days or weeks of delay), and the effect on the design intent. Frame the communication as a decision for the client: "This change will cost $X, delay the project by Y weeks, and will [improve / compromise / not affect] the design intent; do you want to proceed?" Do not minimize the impact to avoid conflict, because the impact will surface later as a budget overrun or a schedule slip, and the client will hold the designer responsible. Honest communication allows the client to make an informed decision and preserves the designer's credibility.

### Establish a Revision Process in the Owner-Designer Agreement

The owner-designer agreement should establish the revision process: how many rounds of revision are included in the fee, how additional revisions are billed, how changes to scope are documented and priced, and how the client requests changes (in writing, to a single point of contact). A defined process prevents the informal accumulation of revisions ("just a quick change") that consumes the designer's time without compensation and that erodes the design through unmanaged iteration. Reference the process at the first revision request, so the client understands the framework. A revision process is not adversarial; it is the structure that allows revisions to be managed professionally.

### Document Every Revision and Decision in Writing

Document every revision and the client's decision in writing: what was requested, what the impact was, what the client decided, and what the effect on the project is. Use a revision log, a change order, or a memo, and reference the documentation in the project correspondence. Written documentation prevents misunderstandings ("I thought that was included"), provides the record for the project, and protects the designer if the revision's impact is later disputed. A verbal revision, undocumented, becomes a dispute when the bill or the schedule reflects it. The discipline of documentation is the designer's protection and the project's memory.

### Distinguish Between Design Development and Scope Change

Not every revision is a scope change. Design development is the expected iteration within the agreed scope: refining the plan, selecting materials, adjusting details as the design resolves. Scope change is a change to what was agreed: a new space, a different function, a revised program. Design development is included in the fee; scope change is not. Distinguish between the two, and when a revision crosses from development to scope change, identify it, communicate it, and document it as a change with its cost and schedule impact. Failing to distinguish allows scope creep to consume the fee and the schedule without acknowledgment.

### Protect the Design Intent Through the Revision Process

Revisions can erode the design intent, one small change at a time, until the design is a compromise of compromises. Protect the design intent by evaluating each revision against it: does the change support the intent, compromise it, or erode it? When a revision erodes the intent, say so, and offer an alternative that achieves the client's goal while preserving the intent. The designer's responsibility is not only to execute the client's requests but to advocate for the design's quality, because the client hired the designer for their judgment, not only for their labor. A designer who accepts every revision without advocacy produces a degraded design and a client who wonders why the result is mediocre.

## Common Traps

### Agreeing to Revisions on the Spot Without Impact Assessment

The trap is agreeing to a revision in the meeting, to please the client and to keep the project moving, without assessing the cost, schedule, and design impact. The mechanism of harm is an impact that surfaces later as a budget overrun or a schedule slip, for which the designer is held responsible. The discipline is to assess before responding and to communicate the impact honestly.

### Minimizing the Impact to Avoid Conflict

The trap is downplaying the cost or schedule impact to avoid a difficult conversation, because the designer wants to please the client. The mechanism of harm is an impact that surfaces later, eroding the client's trust and the designer's credibility. The discipline is to communicate the impact honestly, framed as a decision for the client.

### Accepting Revisions Without a Defined Process or Compensation

The trap is accepting revisions informally, without a process or compensation, because each seems small. The mechanism of harm is the accumulation of revisions that consume the fee and the schedule without acknowledgment, eroding the designer's margin and the project's quality. The discipline is to establish a revision process in the agreement and to reference it at the first request.

### Failing to Document Revisions and Decisions

The trap is accepting revisions verbally and not documenting them, relying on memory and correspondence. The mechanism of harm is a misunderstanding or a dispute when the impact surfaces, with no record to resolve it. The discipline is to document every revision and decision in writing.

### Letting Scope Creep Erode the Design Intent

The trap is accepting revisions that erode the design intent, one small change at a time, without advocating for the design. The mechanism of harm is a final design that is a compromise of compromises, mediocre and unrecognizable from the approved concept. The discipline is to evaluate each revision against the design intent, to advocate for the design, and to offer alternatives that preserve the intent.

## Self-Check

- Is the impact of every revision (cost, schedule, design intent) assessed before responding to the client?
- Is the impact communicated honestly, framed as a decision for the client, without minimizing to avoid conflict?
- Does the owner-designer agreement establish a revision process (included rounds, billing for additional, change documentation)?
- Is every revision and decision documented in writing (revision log, change order, memo), referenced in correspondence?
- Is the distinction between design development (within scope) and scope change (beyond scope) made explicit, with scope changes documented and priced?
- Is each revision evaluated against the design intent, with the designer advocating for the design and offering alternatives that preserve the intent?
- Is the cumulative effect of revisions on the budget and schedule tracked and communicated to the client at each milestone?
