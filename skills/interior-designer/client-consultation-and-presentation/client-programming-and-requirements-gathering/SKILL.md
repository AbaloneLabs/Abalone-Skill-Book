# Client Programming and Requirements Gathering

Programming is the research and documentation phase that precedes design, in which the designer gathers and structures the client's functional, aesthetic, operational, and financial requirements into a design program. The judgment problem is that programming is often abbreviated or skipped—designers move quickly to concepts and plans—when in fact the program is the foundation against which every design decision is evaluated. A design built on an incomplete or assumed program solves the wrong problem, misses critical requirements, and requires redesign when the gaps surface. Rigorous programming uncovers requirements the client could not articulate, reconciles conflicting stakeholder needs, and produces a documented program that guides the design and protects the designer from scope creep.

## Core Rules

### Structure the Programming Interview Around Functional, Aesthetic, and Operational Domains

A complete program covers functional requirements (what activities occur, how many people, what equipment, what storage), aesthetic requirements (what style, mood, image, materials, colors the client wants), operational requirements (how the space is maintained, who manages it, what hours, what security), and financial requirements (what budget, what schedule, what priorities). Structure the programming interview to cover each domain, with specific questions for each, rather than an open conversation. Use questionnaires, tours of the client's current space, and review of the client's operations to gather data the client may not think to mention. Document the interview, because the program is the record of what the client asked for, and it resolves later disputes about scope.

### Quantify Requirements: People, Equipment, Storage, Hours, Budget

Qualitative requirements ("we need a big kitchen," "we want it to feel open") are insufficient for design. Quantify each requirement: how many people use the kitchen simultaneously, what appliances and equipment, how much storage (cabinets, pantry, refrigerator), what hours of use, what budget. Quantification converts vague desires into design criteria that can be tested: a kitchen for two cooks with a 36-inch range, a pantry, and seating for four is a designable problem; "a big kitchen" is not. Where the client cannot quantify, the designer proposes values based on the client's description and confirms them, because the designer's assumption must be documented and agreed.

### Identify and Reconcile Stakeholder Conflicts

In residential work, spouses, partners, and family members may have conflicting requirements; in commercial work, the owner, the users, the facilities staff, and the brand team may conflict. Identify the stakeholders and their requirements, and surface conflicts during programming, not during design. Reconcile conflicts through structured discussion: present the conflict, the trade-offs, and the options, and facilitate a decision. Document the decision and the rationale, because unresolved conflicts resurface during design as changes and disputes. The designer's role in programming includes facilitating stakeholder alignment, not just gathering a list.

### Document the Program as a Structured, Measurable Document

The program is not a set of interview notes; it is a structured document that organizes the requirements into categories (spaces, activities, equipment, storage, aesthetics, budget, schedule) with measurable criteria for each. For each space, document the activities, the users, the equipment, the storage, the area requirement, the adjacencies, and the special requirements. For the project, document the overall goals, the budget, the schedule, the priorities, and the constraints (codes, accessibility, brand standards). The program is the reference document for design decisions and for scope management; a design that does not meet the program is a design that needs revision or a program that needs a documented change.

### Establish Priorities and Trade-Offs Before Design

Clients want everything, and budgets and space are finite. Establish priorities during programming: which requirements are essential, which are important, which are desirable, and which are optional. Use a prioritization exercise (rank-order, must-have versus nice-to-have, budget allocation) to make the trade-offs explicit before design. Priorities guide the design (invest in the essentials first) and guide value engineering (cut the optional before the essential). Document the priorities, because they are the basis for design decisions and for budget reconciliation throughout the project.

### Confirm the Program with the Client in Writing Before Designing

Before beginning design, present the program to the client for written confirmation. The confirmation is the agreement that the program accurately captures the requirements, the priorities, and the constraints, and that the design will be evaluated against it. A program confirmed in writing prevents scope creep (the client adds requirements during design without a change order), resolves disputes (the designer can reference the program), and aligns expectations (the client sees, before design, what they have asked for). If the program changes during design, document the change as a program amendment with its effect on scope, budget, and schedule.

## Common Traps

### Skipping or Abbreviating Programming to Start Design Sooner

The trap is that programming feels slow and design feels productive, so the designer abbreviates programming to start sketching. The mechanism of harm is that the design solves an incomplete or assumed problem, and the gaps surface during design or construction as changes, disputes, and redesign. The discipline is to complete the program before design and to confirm it in writing.

### Accepting Qualitative Requirements Without Quantification

The trap is documenting "we need a big family room" without quantifying the activities, the users, and the equipment. The mechanism of harm is a family room that is the wrong size or configuration for the actual use, discovered after construction. The discipline is to quantify each requirement into measurable design criteria.

### Failing to Identify and Reconcile Stakeholder Conflicts

The trap is gathering requirements from one stakeholder (the person who hired the designer) without identifying others (the spouse, the users, the facilities staff) whose requirements may conflict. The mechanism of harm is a conflict that surfaces during design or after construction, requiring redesign or producing a space that does not serve all users. The discipline is to identify all stakeholders, gather their requirements, and reconcile conflicts during programming.

### Treating the Program as Interview Notes Rather Than a Structured Document

The trap is that the program is a set of notes, not a structured document, so it is not usable as a design reference or a scope-management tool. The mechanism of harm is that requirements are forgotten, misinterpreted, or changed without documentation. The discipline is to structure the program into categories with measurable criteria and to use it as the reference throughout the project.

### Designing Without Confirmed Priorities and Trade-Offs

The trap is that the designer assumes all requirements are equal, invests in the wrong ones, and discovers at budget reconciliation that the essentials are underfunded. The mechanism of harm is value engineering that cuts the essential because the optional was designed first. The discipline is to establish priorities during programming and to design to them.

## Self-Check

- Does the programming interview cover functional, aesthetic, operational, and financial domains, with specific questions for each?
- Is each requirement quantified (people, equipment, storage, hours, budget) into measurable design criteria, with the designer's assumptions documented where the client could not quantify?
- Are all stakeholders identified, their requirements gathered, and conflicts reconciled and documented during programming?
- Is the program a structured document with categories (spaces, activities, equipment, aesthetics, budget, schedule) and measurable criteria, not just interview notes?
- Are priorities and trade-offs established and documented before design, guiding design investment and value engineering?
- Is the program confirmed by the client in writing before design begins, with subsequent changes documented as program amendments?
- Does the program include the constraints (codes, accessibility, brand standards, schedule) that will govern the design?
