# Faucet And Fitting Specification

Faucets and fittings are the most handled plumbing elements and the most common source of user complaint, because their specification depends on precise dimensional and functional coordination that catalogs obscure. The judgment problem is that faucets are selected by appearance and flow rate, while the factors that determine satisfaction — spout reach relative to basin, splash behavior, valve operation force, rough-in compatibility, and scald protection — are interdependent and only reveal themselves at the sink. An agent who specifies a beautiful faucet whose reach splashes off a shallow basin, or a shower valve without scald protection, delivers a fixture that looks right and performs wrong. The agent's job is to coordinate the fitting with its basin and user, specify the correct valve and rough-in, ensure scald safety and accessibility, and match the finish and trim across the space.

## Core Rules

### Match Spout Reach And Height To The Basin

A faucet's spout reach and height determine where the water lands in the basin, and a mismatch produces splashing outside the basin or a weak stream against the back wall. Coordinate the spout reach so the stream lands centered or forward in the basin, and the height so there is clearance for hands and vessels but not so high that water splashes. A faucet chosen without basin coordination splashes, wasting water and wetting the counter.

### Specify The Correct Valve Type For The Use

Faucets use different valve types — single-handle, two-handle, thermostatic, pressure-balance, sensor — each suited to different uses. Residential lavatories suit single-handle or two-handle; showers require pressure-balance or thermostatic mixing for scald protection; commercial restrooms benefit from sensor or metering faucets for hygiene and water control. Specify the valve type that serves the use, because the wrong valve compromises safety, hygiene, or convenience.

### Require Scald Protection In Showers And Baths

Showers and bathtubs require pressure-balance or thermostatic mixing valves that prevent scalding when pressure fluctuates, and this is a code requirement in most jurisdictions. Verify that every shower and bath fitting includes the required scald protection, and specify the valve and rough-in accordingly. A shower without scald protection can deliver dangerously hot water when a toilet flushes, a serious injury risk.

### Coordinate Rough-In With The Selected Fitting

Faucets and fittings require specific rough-in — supply line spacing, deck or wall mounting, valve body depth — that must match the selected trim. Coordinate the rough-in with the fitting during design, because changing the fitting after rough-in is expensive. Wall-mount faucets require rough-in in the wall before tile; deck-mount faucets require holes in the counter or sink; sensor faucets require power. Each fitting dictates its rough-in.

### Verify Accessibility And Operation Force

Accessible faucets must be operable with one hand without tight grasping or twisting, and lever, blade, or sensor controls meet this; round knob handles often do not. Verify that accessible installations have compliant controls and that the faucet reach and clearance suit a seated or standing user at the basin. A faucet that is mechanically compliant but requires two hands or forceful twisting fails accessibility.

### Match Flow Rate And Aeration To The Basin

Flow rate and aeration affect both water efficiency and splash behavior: a high-flow faucet in a shallow basin splashes, while a low-flow aerated faucet may feel weak if poorly designed. Select a flow rate that balances efficiency with adequate wetting, and verify that the aerator produces a coherent stream rather than a spray that splashes. Efficiency that degrades the user experience leads to workarounds.

### Coordinate Finish, Trim, And Accessories

Faucets, shower trims, controls, and accessories should share finish and design language within a space and ideally across the project. Coordinate the finish family — chrome, brushed nickel, brass, matte black — and specify matching accessories in the same finish. Mixed or unmatched finishes undermine the designed impression, and discontinued finishes create maintenance problems.

## Common Traps

### Spout Reach That Splashes Off The Basin

A faucet whose reach is too long or too short for the basin lands the stream on the counter or the back wall, splashing water outside the basin. The trap is that the faucet looks proportional in a catalog while its reach is wrong for the specific basin. Coordinate reach and basin together.

### Showers Without Scald Protection

A shower fitted with a standard mixing valve, without pressure-balance or thermostatic protection, can deliver scalding water when cold pressure drops. The trap is that the fitting looks identical to a compliant one while lacking the safety feature. Require scald protection on every shower and bath.

### Fitting-Rough-In Mismatches

A faucet selected without coordinating the supply spacing, mounting, or valve depth cannot be installed on the existing or planned rough-in. The trap is that the fitting is specified while the plumbing behind the wall is left generic. Coordinate fitting and rough-in during design.

### Knob Handles That Fail Accessibility

Round or cross knob handles require grasping and twisting, failing the one-hand, no-tight-grip accessibility requirement, even on an otherwise accessible installation. The trap is that the faucet is in an accessible location while the handle is not compliant. Specify lever, blade, or sensor controls for accessible installations.

## Self-Check

- Is the spout reach and height coordinated so the stream lands correctly in the basin without splashing?
- Is the valve type — single-handle, two-handle, thermostatic, sensor — matched to the use?
- Do all showers and baths include pressure-balance or thermostatic scald protection?
- Is the rough-in coordinated with the selected fitting's mounting, supply, and valve depth?
- Are accessible fittings operable with one hand without tight grasping or twisting?
- Is the flow rate and aeration balanced for efficiency without splash or weak stream?
- Are finish, trim, and accessories coordinated across the space and project?
