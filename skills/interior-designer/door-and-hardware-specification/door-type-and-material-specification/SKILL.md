# Door Type And Material Specification

Doors are the most touched and most regulated elements in an interior, and their specification is where function, code, and craft converge. The judgment problem is that doors are often selected by appearance and a generic "solid core" note, when the actual requirements — fire rating, acoustic class, traffic duty, vision panel code, hardware preparation, and swing clearance — determine which door is correct for each opening. An agent who specifies a door without mapping its fire rating, STC, and use can deliver a beautiful door that fails inspection, leaks sound into a private room, or fails within a year of heavy use. The agent's job is to specify each door by its full functional and code profile, match material and core to the duty, coordinate vision panels and hardware, and verify that the assembly meets every requirement of the opening.

## Core Rules

### Match Core Type To Duty, Acoustics, And Fire Rating

Door cores range from hollow and particleboard through structural composite and mineral, and the core determines weight, acoustic performance, fire rating, and durability. Hollow-core doors suit light residential duty; solid and structural composite cores serve commercial traffic; mineral cores are required for fire-rated doors. Specify the core to the door's duty, STC requirement, and fire rating, because a door that looks identical can perform completely differently based on its core.

### Verify Fire Rating For Rated Openings

Openings in fire-rated walls require fire-rated doors, frames, and hardware as a listed assembly, with a rating matching the wall (20, 45, 60, 90 minutes). Specify the door and frame as a listed pair from one manufacturer, and verify that vision panels, hinges, and closers are part of the listing. A fire-rated door with non-listed hardware or an oversized vision panel voids the rating and fails inspection.

### Coordinate Vision Panels With Code And Privacy

Code requires vision panels in some doors — typically in educational and assembly occupancies — to prevent opening into a person, and their size and location are regulated. In other doors, vision panels compromise privacy or security. Coordinate vision panel requirement, size, location, and fire-rated glazing with the code and the room's function. A door that needs a vision panel for code and lacks one, or that has one where privacy is required, is mis-specified.

### Specify STC Rating For Acoustic Separation

Where doors separate acoustically sensitive spaces — offices, exam rooms, studios, bedrooms — the door's STC rating and its seals determine the actual sound isolation. A high-STC wall with a low-STC door fails the assembly, because sound leaks through the door. Specify the door STC and the perimeter seals, drop seals, and threshold to match the wall's acoustic intent.

### Select Door Material And Finish For Traffic And Wear

Door faces — wood veneer, high-pressure laminate, paint grade, stainless steel, glass — must withstand the traffic and wear of the location. High-traffic and abuse-prone openings need durable faces and edge protection; residential and low-traffic openings can use finer veneers. Match the face material and edge construction to the wear expectation, because a delicate veneer on a corridor door will damage within months.

### Coordinate Door Swing, Clearance, And Approach

A door's swing direction and clearance affect function, accessibility, and code compliance: doors must not swing into required egress width, accessible doors need approach clearance and lever hardware, and doors must clear furniture and fixtures. Verify the swing in plan against egress, accessibility approach requirements, and the furnished layout. A door that swings into a narrow corridor or blocks a fixture is a functional failure discovered at occupancy.

### Plan Hardware Preparation And Reinforcement

Door hardware — hinges, locks, closers, stops, panic devices — requires specific preparation and internal reinforcement in the door and frame. Coordinate the hardware schedule with the door specification so that reinforcement, mortises, and preps are factory-installed. Field-prepped hardware on an unreinforced door fails under use and voids warranties.

## Common Traps

### Specifying Doors By Appearance Without Core Or Rating

A door that looks correct but has the wrong core fails acoustically, wears quickly, or lacks the required fire rating. The trap is that doors of identical appearance can have completely different performance based on the hidden core. Specify the core and rating explicitly for every door.

### Fire-Rated Doors With Non-Listed Hardware Or Glazing

A fire-rated door is part of a listed assembly; non-listed hinges, an oversized vision panel, or the wrong closer voids the rating. The trap is that the door carries a rating label while the assembly does not comply, a failure discovered at inspection. Specify the complete listed assembly.

### Vision Panels Missing Where Required Or Present Where Harmful

Code-required vision panels omitted for aesthetics fail inspection, while vision panels added for appearance compromise privacy in sensitive rooms. The trap is that vision panels are treated as a style choice rather than a code and function decision. Verify the requirement per opening and coordinate size and glazing.

### Ignoring Door Swing And Accessibility Clearance

A door that swings into a corridor reduces egress width, and a door without approach clearance fails accessibility. The trap is that the swing looks fine in a furniture plan while violating egress or accessibility dimensions. Verify every swing against egress width and accessible approach requirements.

## Self-Check

- Is the door core specified to match duty, acoustic class, and fire rating for each opening?
- Are fire-rated doors, frames, and hardware specified as a complete listed assembly?
- Are vision panels coordinated with code requirements, size limits, rated glazing, and privacy needs?
- Is the door STC and seal system specified to match the wall's acoustic separation?
- Are face material and edge construction matched to the traffic and wear of the location?
- Is every door swing verified against egress width, accessible approach, and furniture clearance?
- Is hardware preparation and reinforcement coordinated with the door specification?
