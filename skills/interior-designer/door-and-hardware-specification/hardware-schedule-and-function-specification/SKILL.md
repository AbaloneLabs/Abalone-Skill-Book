# Hardware Schedule And Function Specification

A hardware schedule is the document that makes doors actually work, and its complexity is where most hardware failures originate. The judgment problem is that hardware is specified opening-by-opening without a coherent function logic, producing locks that do not match the use, closers that fail life-safety, panic hardware where none is required, and accessibility hardware that is incomplete. An agent who treats the hardware schedule as a list of products rather than a function system delivers openings that fail code, frustrate users, and require expensive correction. The agent's job is to develop the hardware schedule from a function logic, verify each opening against egress, accessibility, fire, and security requirements, and coordinate the hardware with the door and frame so the assembly performs as a system.

## Core Rules

### Develop The Schedule From A Function Logic, Not Product List

Each opening has a function — entry, office, storage, restroom, egress, secure — and the hardware follows from that function: what locks, what latches, what closes, what stops. Build the schedule by function first, assigning hardware sets to function types, then tailor per opening. A schedule built product-by-product without function logic produces inconsistent and incorrect hardware across similar openings.

### Verify Egress And Life-Safety Hardware For Every Exit Door

Exit doors require hardware that allows egress with one motion, without keys or special knowledge, and panic hardware may be required based on occupancy and load. Verify every egress path's hardware against the code: no deadbolts requiring two operations, no key-required egress, and panic devices where the occupancy load triggers them. A single locked or complex exit door can fail life-safety and trap occupants in an emergency.

### Coordinate Accessibility Hardware And Clearances

Accessible doors require lever or ADA-compliant hardware, proper mounting heights, operating force limits, and approach clearance. Verify that every accessible opening has compliant hardware, that closers are adjusted to allowable opening force, and that threshold heights and door maneuvering clearance are met. Incomplete accessibility hardware — a lever handle with an inaccessible closer force — fails the requirement and the user.

### Match Lock Function To The Use And Security Need

Lock functions — passage, privacy, entry, classroom, storeroom, dormitory — determine how the door operates and secures, and the wrong function frustrates users or compromises security. A storeroom function (always locked, key to enter) on an office traps occupants; a passage function on a storage room provides no security. Match the lock function to the actual use and security requirement of each opening.

### Specify Closers For Function, Force, And Code

Door closers control the door's closing and are required on fire-rated doors and many egress doors, but they must be sized to the door and adjusted to allowable opening force for accessibility. Specify closer size to the door width and wind condition, verify the opening force meets accessibility limits, and require delayed action or backcheck where traffic or wind demands. An undersized closer fails to latch a fire door; an oversized closer exceeds accessible opening force.

### Coordinate Hardware With Fire-Rated Assemblies

Fire-rated doors require listed hardware — hinges, closers, locks, and panic devices — and some hardware is restricted on rated doors (no spring hinges, no electromagnetic locks without fail-safe). Verify that every piece of hardware on a rated opening is listed and compatible, because non-listed hardware voids the rating. The hardware schedule must cross-reference the fire rating of each opening.

### Detail Stops, Silencers, And Protection Plates

Door stops, silencers, kick plates, and armor plates protect the door, the wall, and the hardware from damage, and their omission causes rapid deterioration in high-traffic openings. Specify wall or floor stops at every door to prevent handle damage, silencers at frames to reduce noise, and protection plates where traffic and cart use demand them. Omitting these shortens door life and increases maintenance.

## Common Traps

### Building The Schedule Product-By-Product

Specifying hardware opening-by-opening without a function logic produces inconsistent sets, where similar openings get different hardware and dissimilar openings get the same. The trap is that each line item seems reasonable while the schedule lacks coherence. Build from function types.

### Egress Hardware That Requires Two Motions Or A Key

A deadbolt or double-cylinder lock on an egress door requires two operations or a key to exit, violating life-safety code. The trap is that the hardware provides desirable security while trapping occupants in an emergency. Verify single-motion egress on every exit path.

### Accessibility Hardware That Is Incomplete

A lever handle alone does not make a door accessible; the closer force, threshold height, and approach clearance must also comply. The trap is that the visible hardware looks compliant while the operating force or clearance fails. Verify the full accessibility condition.

### Non-Listed Hardware On Fire-Rated Doors

Standard hinges, closers, or locks on a fire-rated door void the assembly's rating, a failure that may not surface until inspection or a fire. The trap is that the hardware looks identical to listed hardware. Cross-reference every rated opening's hardware to listed products.

## Self-Check

- Is the hardware schedule built from function types, with consistent sets across similar openings?
- Does every egress door allow single-motion exit without a key, with panic hardware where required?
- Is accessibility hardware complete — lever, height, closer force, threshold, and approach clearance?
- Is the lock function matched to the use and security need of each opening?
- Are closers sized to the door and adjusted to accessible opening force?
- Is every piece of hardware on fire-rated openings listed and compatible with the rating?
- Are stops, silencers, and protection plates specified for traffic protection?
