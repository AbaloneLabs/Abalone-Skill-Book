# Access Control And Security Hardware Integration

Electrified and access-controlled doors add a layer of complexity that mechanical hardware does not, and this is where security and life-safety most often conflict. The judgment problem is that access control is specified by security consultants focused on controlling entry, while egress, fire code, and fail-safe behavior are the designer's responsibility, and the two must be reconciled at the opening. An electrified lock that controls entry but traps occupants in a fire, a maglock without required egress hardware, or a secure door that fails unlocked during a power loss are all consequences of treating access control as a security add-on rather than an integrated opening assembly. The agent's job is to coordinate the electrified hardware with egress and fire requirements, verify fail-safe behavior, plan power and wiring paths, and detail the opening so security and life-safety coexist.

## Core Rules

### Reconcile Access Control With Egress And Life-Safety

Every door on an egress path must allow occupant exit at all times, regardless of access control state, typically with one motion and no special knowledge. Electrified hardware that controls entry must not impede egress: request-to-exit devices, motion sensors, or mechanical egress hardware must allow exit even if the access system fails. Verify that no access-controlled door traps occupants, because this is both a code violation and a lethal hazard.

### Specify Fail-Safe Versus Fail-Secure Behavior Correctly

Electrified locks behave differently when power is lost: fail-safe locks unlock (allowing egress), while fail-secure locks remain locked. Egress and fire doors require fail-safe behavior so occupants can exit during a power loss or fire; secure storage doors may use fail-secure to protect contents. Specify the correct behavior per opening based on its egress role, and connect fire alarm interfaces so that fail-safe doors release on alarm.

### Coordinate Power Supply, Wiring Paths, And Backup

Electrified hardware requires power — typically low-voltage — delivered through the door or frame, and the power supply, wiring path, and backup must be planned. Coordinate power supplies and their locations, route wiring through the frame or via electric hinges, and provide backup for life-safety openings so they function during outages. Electrified hardware without a coordinated power plan cannot be installed or fails when power is interrupted.

### Integrate With Fire Alarm And Building Systems

Electrified hardware on fire-rated doors and egress paths must interface with the fire alarm system so that doors release, close, or latch as required during a fire. Coordinate the interface — alarm release, door position, smoke detection — with the fire alarm design, and verify that the hardware is listed for fire applications. An electrified opening that does not respond correctly to a fire alarm compromises compartmentation and egress.

### Verify Listing For Fire-Rated Electrified Openings

Electrified hardware on fire-rated doors must be listed for fire applications, and not all electrified locks, strikes, and maglocks carry this listing. Verify that every device on a rated opening is listed, and that the assembly — door, frame, hardware, power transfer — maintains the rating. Non-listed electrified hardware on a fire door voids the rating and fails inspection.

### Coordinate With Security System Architecture

Access control is part of a building-wide security system — controllers, readers, servers, monitoring — and the door hardware must integrate with that architecture. Coordinate reader locations, device types, and communication with the security system design, and verify that the hardware supports the intended credential and monitoring scheme. Hardware specified without security system coordination may not integrate or may require costly rework.

### Plan For Maintenance, Diagnostics, And Service

Electrified hardware requires periodic service — battery replacement, sensor adjustment, firmware updates — and the design must allow access for maintenance. Locate power supplies and controllers accessibly, provide diagnostic indication, and ensure that service does not require dismantling the door. Electrified openings that cannot be serviced fail unpredictably and compromise both security and life-safety.

## Common Traps

### Access Control That Impedes Egress

An electrified lock that controls both entry and exit, or a maglock without mechanical egress hardware, can trap occupants during a power loss or system failure. The trap is that the security design focused on entry control while the egress path was overlooked. Verify single-motion egress at every access-controlled opening.

### Wrong Fail-Safe Or Fail-Secure Behavior

A fail-secure lock on an egress door remains locked during a power loss, trapping occupants, while a fail-safe lock on a secure storage door unlocks and compromises security. The trap is that the behavior was not matched to the opening's role. Specify fail-safe for egress and fire doors, fail-secure only where contents protection dominates.

### Electrified Hardware Without Coordinated Power

Electrified locks, readers, and sensors require power delivered through the door, and without coordinated wiring paths and power supplies the hardware cannot be installed. The trap is that the hardware is specified while the power infrastructure is left to the electrical contractor, producing incompatible or incomplete installations. Plan power and wiring as part of the opening design.

### Non-Listed Electrified Hardware On Fire Doors

Electrified hardware that is not listed for fire applications, installed on a fire-rated door, voids the rating and fails inspection. The trap is that the hardware looks identical to listed products and the listing is assumed. Verify listing for every device on a rated opening.

## Self-Check

- Does every access-controlled door allow single-motion egress at all times, regardless of system state?
- Is fail-safe behavior specified for egress and fire doors, and fail-secure only for contents protection?
- Are power supplies, wiring paths, and backup coordinated for every electrified opening?
- Is the fire alarm interface coordinated so doors release, close, or latch correctly during a fire?
- Is every electrified device on a fire-rated opening listed for fire applications?
- Is the hardware integrated with the building security system architecture and credential scheme?
- Are power supplies, controllers, and devices accessible for maintenance and diagnostics?
