# Interior Project Scheduling and Phasing

A project schedule is the time map of the project, and it governs whether the design is realized on time and in coordination—or whether trades collide, lead times are missed, and occupancy is delayed. The judgment problem is that schedules are often built as lists of phases ("design, then construction, then install") without the detail and the critical-path analysis that reveal dependencies and risks. A schedule that does not account for the custom millwork lead time, for the sequencing of finishes (paint before flooring, flooring before cabinets), or for the inspection sequence will slip, and the slips compound. Rigorous scheduling identifies the critical path, builds in buffers, sequences the trades, and is tracked against milestones.

## Core Rules

### Build the Schedule Around the Critical Path, Not the Phases

The critical path is the longest sequence of dependent activities that determines the project's minimum duration. In an interior project, the critical path often runs through design completion, permitting, demolition, rough construction (framing, mechanical, electrical, plumbing), inspections, drywall, millwork fabrication and installation, finishes, and FF&E installation. Identify the critical path by listing every activity, its duration, and its dependencies (what must finish before it starts), then calculating the longest path. The critical path is where schedule risk concentrates: a delay on the critical path delays the project; a delay off the critical path may not. Manage the critical path aggressively and build buffers off it. A schedule without critical-path analysis is a wish, not a plan.

### Sequence Trades to Avoid Rework and Damage

Trade sequencing governs quality as well as schedule. The general sequence is demolition, rough construction (framing, rough mechanical, electrical, plumbing), inspections, insulation and drywall, paint, finish flooring, cabinetry and millwork, trim, fixtures and appliances, and final FF&E installation. Deviations cause rework and damage: flooring installed before paint is splattered; cabinets installed before flooring creates a toe-inset problem; fixtures installed before finishes are scratched. Document the sequence in the schedule and enforce it in the field, because contractors optimize their own trade's schedule, not the project's. Coordinate especially around hand-offs: the drywall cannot close until inspections pass; the paint cannot start until drywall is finished; the flooring cannot start until paint is done.

### Coordinate Long-Lead Items with the Construction Schedule

Long-lead items—custom millwork (12 to 20 weeks), custom upholstery (12 to 16 weeks), imported tile or stone (8 to 16 weeks), specialty fixtures (8 to 14 weeks)—must be ordered early enough to deliver when the construction reaches the point of installation. Identify long-lead items at the start of scheduling, confirm their lead times in writing, and place the orders at the calculated latest start date, which may be before construction begins. Coordinate the order dates with the client's deposit approvals and with the design completion (the item must be specified before it can be ordered). A long-lead item ordered late delays the installation and the occupancy; a long-lead item ordered before the design is confirmed risks rework if the design changes.

### Build Buffers for Inspections, Approvals, and Unforeseen Conditions

Schedules that assume everything goes as planned always slip. Build buffers for the known risks: permit and review approvals (which depend on the AHJ and may take weeks longer than estimated), inspections (which must be scheduled and may fail, requiring rework), concealed conditions in renovation (asbestos, structural surprises, water damage), and weather (for work that affects the exterior or the climate control). Add contingency time to the critical path (5 to 15 percent for new construction, 15 to 25 percent for renovation) and locate the buffers before the milestones they protect. Communicate the buffers to the client as part of the schedule, not as hidden padding, so the client understands the risk and the plan to manage it.

### Track Progress Against Milestones and Update the Schedule

A schedule is a living document, tracked against milestones and updated as conditions change. Define milestones (design complete, permit approved, demolition complete, drywall complete, millwork installed, FF&E installed, occupancy) and track actual dates against planned dates weekly. When an activity slips, update the downstream schedule, recalculate the critical path, and communicate the impact to the client and the team. Do not let the schedule drift untracked, because a small slip unrecognized becomes a large delay at occupancy. Use a scheduling tool (Gantt chart, project management software) that shows dependencies and critical path, not just a list of dates. The schedule is only useful if it is maintained and used to drive decisions.

### Coordinate Occupancy, Move-In, and Commissioning

The end of construction is not the end of the project. Occupancy requires a punch list (the walk-through to identify incomplete or defective work), commissioning of systems (testing and balancing mechanical, programming lighting and controls, training the owner's staff), cleaning, and the move-in. Schedule these activities after construction substantial completion and before final occupancy, and coordinate them with the client's move-in plans. A project that is "complete" but not commissioned or cleaned is not occupiable, and the gap between substantial completion and occupancy is often underestimated. Schedule the closeout activities explicitly, and coordinate the FF&E installation, the punch list resolution, and the move-in to avoid conflicts.

## Common Traps

### Building the Schedule as Phases Without Critical-Path Analysis

The trap is listing the phases (design, construction, install) with estimated durations, without mapping dependencies and calculating the critical path. The mechanism of harm is that the schedule's true minimum duration is longer than estimated, and the slip is discovered late. The discipline is to map dependencies, calculate the critical path, and manage it.

### Sequencing Trades by Availability Rather Than by Quality Logic

The trap is sequencing trades by who is available when, rather than by the quality logic (paint before flooring, flooring before cabinets). The mechanism of harm is rework and damage: splattered flooring, scratched fixtures, inset problems. The discipline is to define the quality sequence and to enforce it, coordinating trade availability to the sequence.

### Ordering Long-Lead Items Late

The trap is waiting to order long-lead items until construction reaches the installation point, by which time the lead time delays the project. The mechanism of harm is a delayed installation and occupancy. The discipline is to identify long-lead items at scheduling, confirm lead times, and order at the calculated latest start date, even if before construction.

### Assuming Everything Goes as Planned Without Buffers

The trap is building a schedule with no buffers, assuming permits, inspections, and conditions go as planned. The mechanism of harm is that the inevitable delay has no absorption, and the project slips at every risk. The discipline is to build buffers for the known risks and to communicate them as part of the schedule.

### Letting the Schedule Drift Untracked

The trap is building a schedule and not tracking it against milestones, so slips accumulate unrecognized until occupancy. The mechanism of harm is a large delay discovered late, with no opportunity to mitigate. The discipline is to track progress weekly, update the schedule, recalculate the critical path, and communicate impacts.

## Self-Check

- Is the schedule built around the critical path, with activities, durations, and dependencies mapped, and the longest path identified?
- Is the trade sequence defined by quality logic (demolition, rough, drywall, paint, flooring, cabinetry, trim, fixtures, FF&E) and enforced in the field?
- Are long-lead items identified, lead times confirmed in writing, and order dates calculated to deliver at the installation point?
- Are buffers built in for permits, inspections, concealed conditions, and weather, with contingency on the critical path communicated to the client?
- Is progress tracked against milestones weekly, with the schedule updated, the critical path recalculated, and impacts communicated?
- Are closeout activities (punch list, commissioning, cleaning, move-in) scheduled after substantial completion and coordinated with occupancy?
- Does the scheduling tool show dependencies and critical path, not just a list of dates, and is it used to drive decisions?
