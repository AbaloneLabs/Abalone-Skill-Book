# Contract Administration and RFI Management

Contract administration is the management of the construction contract during the building phase, and it is where the design intent meets the reality of construction. The judgment problem is that designers often treat administration as paperwork—responding to RFIs and reviewing submittals—when in fact it is the active defense of the design intent and the primary risk-management activity of the project. Every RFI is a question about the design's intent or its constructability; every submittal is a proposed interpretation of the specification; every change order is a modification of scope, cost, or schedule. Weak administration allows the design to be built differently than intended, allows substitutions to erode quality, and allows disputes to accumulate into claims. Rigorous administration enforces the documents, clarifies intent promptly, and documents every decision.

## Core Rules

### Respond to RFIs Promptly, Completely, and in Writing

Requests for Information (RFIs) are the contractor's questions about the documents, and they must be answered promptly, completely, and in writing. A prompt response (typically within 7 to 10 days, or per the contract) prevents delay; a complete response answers the question and addresses the related implications (does the answer affect other details, other trades, the schedule); a written response creates the record. Do not answer verbally or by field direction without documentation, because undocumented answers become disputes. If the RFI reveals a document error or omission, correct it by a formal clarification or a sketch, not just by the RFI response. Track RFIs in a log, with the question, the response, the date, and the effect on scope or cost.

### Review Submittals and Shop Drawings Against the Specifications

Submittals (product data, samples, shop drawings) are the contractor's and vendor's proposed interpretation of the specifications, and they must be reviewed against the documents before approval. Review for conformance: does the submitted product meet the specified performance, the specified quality, and the design intent? Review for completeness: does the submittal include all the required information (dimensions, finishes, fabrication details)? Review for coordination: does the shop drawing agree with the architectural, structural, and mechanical conditions? Stamp the submittal with a clear disposition (approved, approved as noted, revise and resubmit, rejected), with notes explaining the disposition. Do not approve a submittal that does not conform, because the approval is the designer's acceptance of the product as meeting the specification. Track submittals in a log, with the submittal, the disposition, the date, and the effect on procurement.

### Issue Clarifications, Sketches, and Change Orders Through a Defined Process

Clarifications, sketches (SKs), and change orders are the mechanisms for modifying or supplementing the documents during construction, and they must be issued through a defined process. A clarification or SK supplements the documents (answering a question, providing a detail); a change order modifies the contract (scope, cost, or schedule). Issue each in writing, numbered and dated, referencing the affected documents, and distribute to the contractor, the client, and the project record. Do not allow informal field direction to modify the work without documentation, because informal direction becomes a dispute over scope and cost. The defined process ensures that every modification is documented, reviewed for its effect on scope and cost, and approved by the client before it is built.

### Document Every Field Decision and Direction

Every field decision and direction must be documented, because memory and verbal direction are unreliable and become disputes. Document field decisions in a field report or a project memorandum, with the date, the participants, the decision, and the rationale. Document verbal direction in a follow-up written confirmation (an email or a memo) to the contractor and the client. Document site observations in field reports, with photographs and notes on the progress, the conformance to the documents, and any issues observed. The documentation is the project's memory and the designer's protection; a project without documentation is a project at risk of disputes the designer cannot defend.

### Manage the Change Order Process to Control Scope and Cost

Change orders modify the contract scope, cost, or schedule, and they must be managed to control the project's budget and timeline. Require the contractor to propose change orders in writing, with the scope, the cost (with backup), and the schedule impact documented. Review the proposal for validity (is the change required by a document error, a client request, or an unforeseen condition?), for cost (is the pricing reasonable and supported), and for schedule (is the delay justified). Negotiate or reject unreasonable proposals. Obtain the client's written approval before authorizing the change. Track change orders in a log, with the cumulative effect on the contract sum and schedule, and report the cumulative effect to the client at each milestone. Unmanaged change orders erode the budget and the schedule; managed change orders control them.

### Coordinate Communication Through a Single Point of Contact

Project communication must be coordinated through a single point of contact for each party (the designer for the design team, the contractor for the trades, the client for the owner's decisions), to prevent conflicting direction and confusion. Direct communication between the designer and the trades (bypassing the contractor) undermines the contractor's management and creates conflicting direction; direct communication between the client and the trades (bypassing both designer and contractor) creates chaos. Establish the communication protocol in the project agreements and enforce it. The single-point protocol does not prevent collaboration; it ensures that direction is consistent, documented, and accountable.

## Common Traps

### Responding to RFIs Verbally or Without Documentation

The trap is answering an RFI in the field or by phone, without written documentation, to keep the work moving. The mechanism of harm is a dispute when the verbal answer is implemented differently than intended, with no record to resolve it. The discipline is to respond in writing, with the response tracked in the RFI log.

### Approving Submittals Without Conformance Review

The trap is stamping submittals "approved" without detailed review against the specifications, to keep procurement moving. The mechanism of harm is a product that does not meet the specification, installed and accepted, eroding the quality and the design intent. The discipline is to review each submittal for conformance and to stamp with a clear disposition and notes.

### Allowing Informal Field Direction to Modify the Work

The trap is giving direction in the field ("go ahead and move that outlet") without a clarification, SK, or change order. The mechanism of harm is a modification that is undocumented, that may conflict with other conditions, and that becomes a scope or cost dispute. The discipline is to document every modification through the defined process.

### Failing to Document Field Decisions and Observations

The trap is making field decisions and observations without documenting them, relying on memory and verbal communication. The mechanism of harm is a dispute the designer cannot defend, because there is no record of the decision or the observation. The discipline is to document every field decision and observation in writing, with photographs.

### Accepting Change Orders Without Validity, Cost, or Schedule Review

The trap is accepting the contractor's change order proposals without reviewing their validity, cost, or schedule impact, to avoid conflict. The mechanism of harm is budget and schedule erosion by change orders that are not justified or not reasonably priced. The discipline is to review each proposal for validity, cost, and schedule, to negotiate or reject unreasonable proposals, and to obtain the client's approval before authorizing.

## Self-Check

- Are RFIs responded to promptly (within the contract timeframe), completely (addressing implications), and in writing, tracked in a log?
- Are submittals and shop drawings reviewed for conformance to the specifications, completeness, and coordination, with a clear disposition stamped and notes?
- Are clarifications, sketches, and change orders issued through a defined, numbered, dated process, distributed to all parties?
- Are field decisions, direction, and observations documented in writing (field reports, memos, follow-up confirmations), with photographs?
- Are change orders reviewed for validity, cost (with backup), and schedule impact, negotiated or rejected as appropriate, and approved by the client before authorization?
- Is communication coordinated through a single point of contact for each party, with the protocol established in the agreements and enforced?
- Are RFI, submittal, and change order logs maintained and reported to the client at each milestone, with cumulative scope and cost tracked?
