# Procurement Scheduling and Lead-Time Management

Procurement scheduling is the process of sequencing the ordering, production, shipping, and installation of furniture, fixtures, and equipment so that they arrive when the project is ready for them—neither too early (requiring storage and risking damage) nor too late (delaying occupancy). The judgment problem is that designers treat procurement as ordering from a schedule, when in fact it is a logistics problem governed by lead times, production schedules, shipping windows, and site readiness, all of which interact. A single late custom piece can delay occupancy; a warehouse full of early furniture incurs storage fees and damage risk. Procurement must be planned as a schedule with critical paths, buffers, and tracking, not as a series of purchase orders.

## Core Rules

### Identify Lead Times for Every Item Before Committing to the Schedule

Lead time—the period from order to delivery—varies enormously: stock production items may ship in 2 to 4 weeks; semi-custom items in 8 to 12 weeks; custom upholstery and case goods in 12 to 16 weeks; custom millwork and imported items in 16 to 26 weeks or more. Identify the lead time for every item in the FF&E schedule before building the procurement schedule, by obtaining quotes and lead-time confirmations from the vendors. The longest lead-time item is the critical path that governs the schedule; if a custom dining table takes 20 weeks, the procurement schedule must start 20 weeks before the required delivery, regardless of how quickly other items can be ordered. Do not assume standard lead times; confirm them in writing, because lead times change with the vendor's production load.

### Build the Procurement Schedule Backward from the Required Occupancy Date

Build the procurement schedule backward from the required occupancy or installation date, not forward from the order date. Determine when the site must be ready for installation (construction complete, finishes cured, climate controlled, secure), then when each item must deliver to meet the installation, then when each item must ship to deliver, then when each item must be ordered to ship, accounting for its lead time. The backward schedule reveals the latest order date for each item and the total procurement duration, which may require starting orders before construction is complete. Present the procurement schedule to the client early, because it may require deposits and commitments before the client expects.

### Coordinate Custom and Production Items on a Single Critical Path

Custom items (upholstery built to specification, case goods made to order, millwork) and production items (catalog furniture, stock fixtures) have different lead times and different risks. Custom items cannot be expedited significantly and carry risk of production delays; production items are more predictable but may be out of stock or backordered. Place all items on a single procurement schedule with their lead times and order dates, and identify the critical path (the longest sequence that governs the overall schedule). Manage the critical path aggressively: confirm the custom vendor's production slot at order, and confirm production item availability before relying on the catalog lead time. A single delayed custom item on the critical path delays the whole installation.

### Include Receiving, Storage, and Installation in the Schedule

Delivery is not the end of procurement. The schedule must include receiving (inspecting items for damage and completeness on arrival), storage (if the site is not ready, in a climate-controlled, secure warehouse), and installation (delivering to the site, assembling, placing, and styling). Receiving and inspection protect against discovering damage after the installer has left; storage protects items from site conditions (dust, humidity, theft) that can damage them; installation requires site readiness (construction complete, climate controlled, clean). Schedule the receiving, storage, and installation as distinct phases with their own durations and responsibilities, and coordinate them with the construction schedule. Furniture delivered to an unready site is furniture at risk.

### Track Orders, Production Milestones, and Shipments Actively

Procurement is not a fire-and-forget activity. Track each order through its milestones: order acknowledgment, production start, production completion, ship date, and delivery date. For custom items, request production progress updates and photos at key milestones. For shipments, track the carrier and the tracking number, and confirm delivery. Maintain a procurement tracker (a spreadsheet or project management tool) with each item's status, updated weekly, and communicate status to the client and the project team. Active tracking surfaces delays early, when they can be mitigated (expediting, sourcing an alternative, adjusting the installation schedule), rather than at the delivery date, when they are crises.

### Plan for Delays, Damage, and Backorders with Buffers and Alternates

Procurement is subject to delays (production, shipping, customs), damage (in production, in transit, in receiving), and backorders (discontinued items, stockouts). Build buffers into the schedule: order earlier than the latest date where possible, to absorb delays; specify a receiving and inspection process that catches damage before installation; identify alternate products for critical items, so a backorder can be resolved without delaying the project. For critical-path custom items, consider ordering a backup or confirming the vendor's capacity before relying on the lead time. Communicate the risk and the mitigation to the client, so delays are understood as inherent to procurement, not as failures.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Standard Lead Times Without Vendor Confirmation

The trap is using standard or remembered lead times (production furniture 6 weeks, custom 12 weeks) without confirming with the vendor at quote. The mechanism of harm is that the actual lead time is longer, the item delivers late, and the installation or occupancy is delayed. The discipline is to confirm lead times in writing at quote and to build the schedule from confirmed lead times.

### Building the Schedule Forward from the Order Date

The trap is ordering when ready and scheduling delivery based on the lead time, without working backward from the required occupancy date. The mechanism of harm is that the procurement duration exceeds the available time, and the delay is discovered too late to mitigate. The discipline is to build the schedule backward from occupancy and to start orders early enough to meet it.

### Delivering Furniture to an Unready Site

The trap is delivering furniture to the site because the lead time elapsed, even though construction is not complete, because the schedule was not coordinated with construction. The mechanism of harm is furniture damaged by dust, humidity, or construction activity, or furniture requiring reinstallation after the site is ready. The discipline is to coordinate the delivery and installation with the construction schedule and to use storage when the site is not ready.

### Failing to Inspect on Receiving and Discovering Damage at Installation

The trap is accepting delivery without inspecting for damage and completeness, then discovering damage at installation when the installer has left. The mechanism of harm is a delayed installation while the damaged item is replaced, and a dispute over responsibility. The discipline is to inspect on receiving, to document damage with photos and notes, and to file claims immediately.

### Treating Procurement as Order-and-Forget

The trap is placing orders and assuming delivery will follow, without tracking milestones. The mechanism of harm is a delay or backorder discovered at the delivery date, when it is too late to mitigate. The discipline is to track each order through milestones, to maintain a procurement tracker, and to communicate status weekly.

## Self-Check

- Are lead times for every item confirmed in writing with the vendors before the procurement schedule is built?
- Is the procurement schedule built backward from the required occupancy or installation date, with order dates calculated to meet delivery?
- Are custom and production items on a single critical-path schedule, with the longest lead-time item governing the start of ordering?
- Are receiving, storage, and installation included as distinct phases, coordinated with the construction schedule and site readiness?
- Is a procurement tracker maintained with order milestones (acknowledgment, production, ship, deliver), updated weekly and communicated to the team?
- Are buffers and alternate products planned for delays, damage, and backorders, with risks communicated to the client?
- Is the receiving and inspection process defined, with damage documented and claimed immediately, before installation?
