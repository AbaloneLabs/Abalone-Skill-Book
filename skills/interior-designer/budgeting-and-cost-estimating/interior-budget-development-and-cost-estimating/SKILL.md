# Interior Budget Development and Cost Estimating

Budget development is the process of translating a design intent into a defensible cost estimate, and it is the point at which many projects lose alignment with the client's resources. The judgment problem is that budgets are often developed optimistically (to win the project or please the client), aggregated loosely (a lump sum without breakdown), or deferred until the design is complete (when overruns require redesign). A budget that is developed without rigor becomes a fiction that the project collides with later, forcing value engineering that strips the design of its intent. Rigorous budget development breaks the project into categories, estimates each from current data, includes appropriate contingency, and is reconciled to the design at every phase.

## Core Rules

### Separate Construction (Hard) Costs from FF&E (Soft) Costs Early

Interior project costs divide into construction (hard) costs—demolition, framing, drywall, millwork, electrical, plumbing, mechanical, finishes—and FF&E (soft) costs—furniture, fixtures, equipment, accessories, art, and window treatments. These two categories have different cost structures, different procurement paths, and different contingency requirements. Estimate them separately and track them separately, because confusion between them produces budgets that undercount one or the other. Within construction, break the estimate into the Construction Specifications Institute (CSI) divisions or a similar structure; within FF&E, break it into furniture, textiles, accessories, art, and shipping and installation. The breakdown is the basis for tracking and for value engineering.

### Estimate from Current, Local, and Specific Data

Cost estimates are only as good as their data. Use current unit costs (updated at least annually, and verified against recent bids), local to the project's market (costs vary significantly by region), and specific to the design (the actual material, fixture, or assembly, not a generic allowance). For construction, use unit costs per square foot or per assembly, verified against recent comparable projects or a cost estimator's data. For FF&E, use actual product pricing from the specified vendors, including freight and installation. Avoid allowances for undefined items, because allowances are almost always exceeded; if an item is undefined, define it before budgeting, or carry a clearly labeled, sized allowance.

### Include Contingency Appropriate to the Phase and Risk

Contingency is the reserve for unknowns, and its size depends on the phase and the project's risk. Design contingency (during design, for items not yet specified) is typically 15 to 25 percent; construction contingency (during construction, for unforeseen conditions and changes) is 5 to 15 percent for new work and 15 to 25 percent for renovation, where concealed conditions are common. FF&E contingency is typically 5 to 10 percent. Reduce the design contingency as the design is specified, but never eliminate the construction contingency. Document the contingency and its basis, and do not let the contingency be consumed by scope creep rather than reserved for true unknowns.

### Reconcile the Budget to the Design at Every Phase

The budget is not a one-time estimate; it is a living document reconciled to the design at every phase. At schematic design, estimate from the concept and confirm feasibility against the client's budget. At design development, estimate from the developing specifications and identify gaps. At construction documents, estimate from the detailed scope and confirm the bid aligns. At each phase, if the estimate exceeds the budget, make a deliberate decision: reduce scope, change specifications, or increase the budget. Do not defer the reconciliation, because the later the gap is discovered, the more expensive and disruptive the correction. The reconciliation is a design decision, documented and communicated to the client.

### Track Procurement, Freight, Installation, and Tax Separately

The purchase price of a sofa is not the budget cost of the sofa. FF&E budgets must include the vendor's procurement or designer discount, the freight (often 8 to 15 percent of the product cost), the installation (receiving, assembly, placement, often 5 to 10 percent), the sales tax (applicable in most jurisdictions), and the designer's markup or fee. Construction estimates must include the general conditions, the contractor's overhead and profit, permits, and bonds. Estimate each of these separately and aggregate them explicitly, because omitting freight, installation, or tax is a common cause of budget overruns that surface at the end of the project. The fully loaded cost, not the product price, is the budget number.

### Present Budget Options and Value Engineering as Design Decisions

When the budget and the design diverge, present the client with options, not a single edict. Frame value engineering as a design decision: "We can reduce the kitchen budget by 15 percent by changing the cabinetry to a semi-custom line, or by reducing the pantry; which preserves the design intent for you?" Offer trade-offs that preserve the design's priorities (the focal elements, the user experience) while reducing cost in lower-priority areas. Avoid across-the-board cuts, which erode the design uniformly, and avoid unilateral cuts, which surprise the client. Document the options and the client's decision, because the decision shapes the design and the budget going forward.

## Common Traps

### Developing the Budget Optimistically to Win or Please

The trap is that an optimistic budget secures the project or pleases the client in the short term, so the designer underestimates or omits categories. The mechanism of harm is that the actual costs exceed the budget, the client loses trust, and the design is value-engineered under duress, often losing its intent. The discipline is to estimate rigorously, to include appropriate contingency, and to communicate honestly, even if the budget exceeds the client's initial expectation.

### Aggregating Costs Without Breakdown

The trap is presenting a lump-sum budget without a breakdown by category, which is easier but hides the assumptions. The mechanism of harm is that the client cannot evaluate the budget, the designer cannot track it, and overruns in one category are masked by underruns in another until the total is exceeded. The discipline is to break the budget into construction (by division) and FF&E (by category) and to track each.

### Using Allowances Instead of Specific Estimates

The trap is carrying allowances for undefined items (lighting allowance, appliance allowance) because the items are not yet selected. The mechanism of harm is that the allowance is almost always lower than the actual selection, and the overrun surfaces late. The discipline is to define items before budgeting, or to carry clearly labeled, sized allowances with the understanding that they will be reconciled when the item is selected.

### Omitting Freight, Installation, and Tax from FF&E Budgets

The trap is budgeting the product price and forgetting that freight, installation, and tax add 15 to 30 percent. The mechanism of harm is a budget that appears sufficient but is exceeded when the fully loaded costs are tallied. The discipline is to estimate freight, installation, and tax separately and to aggregate them into the fully loaded budget.

### Deferring Budget Reconciliation Until Construction Documents

The trap is that the budget is developed at schematic design and not reconciled until the documents are complete, when the gap is discovered. The mechanism of harm is a large, late discrepancy that requires expensive redesign or painful value engineering. The discipline is to reconcile at every phase and to make design adjustments early, when they are inexpensive.

## Self-Check

- Are construction (hard) and FF&E (soft) costs estimated and tracked separately, with construction broken by CSI division and FF&E by category?
- Are unit costs current, local, and specific to the design, verified against recent bids or a cost estimator's data?
- Is contingency included appropriate to the phase and risk (design 15 to 25 percent, construction 5 to 25 percent, FF&E 5 to 10 percent), and documented?
- Is the budget reconciled to the design at every phase, with gaps addressed by deliberate scope, specification, or budget decisions?
- Are procurement, freight, installation, tax, overhead, and profit estimated separately and aggregated into the fully loaded cost?
- When budget and design diverge, are options and value engineering presented as design decisions, with the client's choice documented?
- Are allowances clearly labeled and sized, and reconciled when the item is selected, rather than carried as hidden under-budgeting?
