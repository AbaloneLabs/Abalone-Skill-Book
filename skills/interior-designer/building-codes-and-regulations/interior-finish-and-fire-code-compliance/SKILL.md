# Interior Finish and Fire Code Compliance

Interior finishes—wall coverings, ceiling tiles, flooring, trim, and decorative materials—are governed by fire performance requirements that vary by occupancy, by location within the building (exit, corridor, room), and by whether the building is sprinklered. The judgment problem is that finishes are selected primarily for aesthetics, durability, and cost, and the fire performance classification is checked later as a paperwork item—if it is checked at all. But a beautiful finish with the wrong fire classification is a code violation that must be removed and replaced, and in the worst case it is the finish that ignites and spreads a fire. Interior finish compliance is a selection criterion, not an afterthought, and it must be applied at the same time as the aesthetic and performance criteria.

## Core Rules

### Classify Finishes by Occupancy and Location Using the Correct Standard

Interior finishes are classified by flame spread and smoke development using ASTM E84 (the Steiner tunnel test), reported as Class A, B, or C for wall and ceiling finishes, and by critical radiant flux for flooring. The required class depends on occupancy group and location: exits and exit access corridors require the most stringent class (typically Class A or B), rooms require less (Class B or C), and the requirements are reduced in sprinklered buildings. Determine the required class for each finish location before selecting products, and verify each candidate product's classification against the required class. A product's classification is documented by a test report or listing; do not accept a manufacturer's verbal claim.

### Apply the Interior Finish Requirements to All Finish Layers

The code regulates the finish itself, but also the assembly and the substrate. A Class A wall covering applied over a combustible substrate or with a combustible adhesive may not achieve the required performance, because the adhesive or substrate contributes to flame spread. Confirm the tested assembly, including substrate and adhesive, matches the intended installation. For ceiling finishes, confirm the ceiling membrane is part of a listed assembly where required; a lay-in ceiling tile in a rated ceiling assembly must be the listed tile, not a substitute. For flooring, confirm the radiant flux classification includes the underlayment and adhesive, because these affect performance.

### Verify Finish Requirements in Exits, Corridors, and Concealed Spaces

The most stringent finish requirements apply to exits (exit stair enclosures, exit passageways) and exit access corridors, because these are the paths occupants use to escape. Confirm finishes in these locations meet the required class without exception. In concealed spaces—above ceilings, in plenums, in crawl spaces—finishes and materials are regulated for smoke and flame because fire in a concealed space spreads undetected. Confirm that cables, wires, and ducts in plenums meet plenum-rated requirements (NFPA 90A, NFPA 262), and that exposed materials in concealed spaces meet the applicable requirements.

### Coordinate Finish Selection with Sprinkler Status

Sprinklered buildings receive reduced interior finish requirements in many cases: a Class B finish may be allowed where Class A would be required in an unsprinklered building. Confirm whether the building is sprinklered before applying finish requirements, and document the sprinkler status as a design basis. If the sprinkler system is value-engineered out or not installed, the finish requirements revert to the unsprinklered standard, which may require replacing finishes already installed. Coordinate finish selections with the fire protection engineer and the owner to avoid this rework.

### Document Each Finish's Classification in the Specification

The specification must state the required fire classification for each finish and the product's tested classification, with the test standard and the testing laboratory or listing agency. A finish schedule that lists only the product name and color is insufficient for plan review and for field substitution. Include the test report number or listing number so the contractor can verify that a proposed substitution meets the same classification. Without this documentation, substitutions during construction can quietly replace a compliant finish with a non-compliant one. The specification is the control document for finish compliance.

### Address Decorative Materials, Artwork, and Acoustical Treatments

Decorative materials—curtains, banners, artificial foliage, artwork, acoustical panels—are regulated as interior finish or interior decoration and must meet flame-resistance requirements, especially in Assembly occupancies. Confirm that textiles are flame-retardant treated or inherently flame resistant, and that the treatment is durable (not washed out). Acoustical wall panels and ceiling clouds must carry a classification. In high-density Assembly occupancies (theaters, nightclubs, restaurants), the quantity of decorative material is restricted and its flame resistance is critical, because these materials have been the fuel in fatal fires. Do not assume decorative equals exempt.

## Common Traps

### Selecting Finishes for Aesthetics First and Checking Fire Class Later

The trap is that finishes are chosen in design development for look and feel, and the fire classification is verified in construction documents—if at all. The mechanism of harm is that a selected finish does not meet the required class for its location, and the correction requires reselecting and redrawing after the design is approved. The discipline is to determine the required class for each location at the start of finish selection and to filter candidate products by classification before aesthetic selection.

### Accepting Manufacturer Claims Without a Test Report or Listing

Manufacturers may state a finish is "Class A" or "fire rated" without providing the test report or listing that documents the classification. The trap is that the claim seems authoritative, so the designer specifies the product. The mechanism of harm is that the AHJ or the contractor's substitution request reveals the product is not listed or the test assembly differs from the intended installation, and the finish fails compliance. The discipline is to require the test report or listing number in the specification and to verify it before approval.

### Ignoring the Substrate, Adhesive, and Assembly in Finish Performance

A finish's classification is based on a tested assembly that includes substrate and adhesive. The trap is that designers specify the finish product but allow any substrate or adhesive, assuming the classification transfers. The mechanism of harm is that a combustible adhesive or substrate increases flame spread, and the installed assembly does not achieve the tested performance. The discipline is to specify the tested assembly, including substrate and adhesive, or to require a field mock-up with the intended assembly tested.

### Assuming Decorative Materials and Artwork Are Exempt

Curtains, banners, artwork, and acoustical panels are regulated finishes, but designers often treat them as decoration outside the code. The trap is that these materials add fuel load and flame spread, especially in Assembly occupancies. The mechanism of harm is a beautiful but non-compliant and unsafe interior, and in the worst case a fire that spreads via decorative materials. The discipline is to classify and document all decorative materials and to specify flame-retardant treatments or inherently flame-resistant products.

### Applying Sprinkler Credits Without Confirming the System

Reduced finish requirements in sprinklered buildings are a credit, not a default. The trap is that designers assume the building is sprinklered and apply the reduced requirements. The mechanism of harm is that the sprinkler system is not installed or is value-engineered out, and the finishes must be upgraded to the unsprinklered standard after installation. The discipline is to confirm the sprinkler system in the design basis and to coordinate its scope with the owner before relying on sprinkler credits.

## Self-Check

- Is the required finish class (A, B, or C) determined for each location based on occupancy, location (exit, corridor, room), and sprinkler status?
- Does each specified finish carry a documented classification from a test report or listing (ASTM E84 for walls and ceilings, critical radiant flux for flooring)?
- Are substrates, adhesives, and assemblies specified to match the tested condition, or is a field mock-up required?
- Do finishes in exits, exit access corridors, and concealed spaces meet the most stringent required class without exception?
- Is the sprinkler status documented as a design basis, and are finish requirements adjusted if the system is removed?
- Are decorative materials, artwork, textiles, and acoustical treatments classified and documented as regulated finishes?
- Does the finish schedule and specification state the required class, the product's classification, and the test report or listing number for every regulated finish?
