# Art Selection and Placement in Interiors

Art is often the element that makes a room feel complete, yet it is frequently the last thing considered and the most casually placed. The judgment problem is that designers treat art as decoration to fill empty walls rather than as a spatial and experiential element that interacts with scale, sightlines, lighting, and traffic. Art that is too small disappears; art that is too large overwhelms; art placed too high floats above the furniture; art lit by the wrong fixture glares or fades. Successful art selection and placement require the same rigor as furniture layout: the art must be sized to the wall and the furniture, placed at the correct viewing height, lit for visibility and conservation, and coordinated with the architecture and the user's eye level.

## Core Rules

### Size Art to the Wall and the Furniture, Not to the Image

The most common error is art that is too small for its wall or its furniture. A piece of art above a sofa should fill roughly two-thirds to three-quarters of the sofa's width; a piece on a blank wall should relate to the wall's scale, not float in its center. Calculate the art size relative to the furniture or the wall area before selecting, and if a single piece is too large or costly, plan a grouping (a salon hang or a grid) that achieves the same coverage. A small piece on a large wall reads as an afterthought; an oversized piece on a small wall dominates the room. Size is a relational decision, not an intrinsic property of the artwork.

### Place Art at the Correct Viewing Height Relative to Use

Eye level for standing viewing is approximately 57 to 60 inches to the center of the artwork (museum standard), but interior placement must account for use. Above a sofa or console, the bottom of the art should sit 6 to 10 inches above the furniture, so the art relates to the furniture rather than floating. In a dining room or seated area, lower the center to a seated eye level (approximately 50 to 54 inches). In a hallway or stairwell, follow the standing standard but adjust for the stair slope. Do not place all art at one uniform height regardless of context; the height must respond to whether the viewer is standing, seated, or moving.

### Plan the Composition: Single Piece, Pair, Triptych, or Salon Hang

The composition of art on a wall is a design decision. A single large piece creates a focal point; a pair or triptych creates rhythm and balance; a salon hang (a dense, overlapping arrangement of varied works) creates richness and personal narrative; a grid creates order and formality. Match the composition to the wall and the room: a salon hang suits a traditional or eclectic room with high ceilings; a grid suits a modern or formal space; a single piece suits a focal wall. Plan the composition on the elevation drawing, with dimensions and spacing, before installation. A salon hang requires a layout plan with each piece's position; a random installation reads as clutter.

### Coordinate Framing with the Art, the Architecture, and Conservation

Framing protects the art, presents it, and integrates it with the architecture. Select the frame material, profile, and finish to complement the art (not compete with it) and the interior (not clash with trim and furniture). Use matting to create visual breathing room and to separate the art from the glazing. Specify conservation-grade materials—rag or alpha-cellulose mat board, UV-filtering glazing, reversible hinging—for anything of value or sentimental importance, because acidic materials and UV light degrade art over time. For mirrors and large works, coordinate the weight with the hanging hardware and the wall structure. Document the framing specification so the framer produces the intended result.

### Light Art for Visibility, Color Rendering, and Conservation

Art lighting is a discipline of its own. Specify fixtures that illuminate the art evenly without hot spots or glare, with a beam angle matched to the art size and a throw distance matched to the ceiling height. Use adjustable recessed accents, track-mounted accents, or picture lights, and locate them at the correct angle (approximately 30 degrees from vertical, or steeper for larger art) to minimize glare and shadows. Specify the light source for color rendering (CRI 90+ for accurate color) and for conservation (low UV, low heat; LED is preferred). For valuable or light-sensitive art (works on paper, photographs, textiles), control the lux level and the exposure duration to prevent fading. Coordinate art lighting with the lighting design and the electrical rough-in.

### Curate for Coherence, Narrative, and the Client's Voice

A collection of art should cohere, whether through style, period, color, medium, or theme—or through the deliberate, curated contrast of difference. Understand the client's taste, collection, and narrative before selecting or placing art; art that the client loves and that tells their story is more successful than art that is merely on-trend. In hospitality and commercial work, curate art that supports the brand narrative and the local context. Avoid the generic "hotel art" that signals no point of view. Document the curation rationale so the client and future designers understand the intent and can maintain or extend it.

## Common Traps

### Sizing Art Too Small for the Wall or Furniture

The trap is selecting art by the image or the price, without relating it to the wall or furniture scale, resulting in a piece that looks lost. The mechanism of harm is a room that feels incomplete and a client who adds more small pieces, compounding the clutter without solving the scale problem. The discipline is to calculate the art size relative to the furniture or wall before selecting and to plan a grouping where a single piece is insufficient.

### Placing All Art at a Uniform Standing Eye Level

The trap is applying the 57- to 60-inch museum standard to every piece regardless of context, so that art above a sofa floats above the furniture and art in a dining room sits too high for seated viewing. The mechanism of harm is a disconnect between the art and the furniture and the viewer. The discipline is to adjust the height to the use: relate art above furniture to the furniture, and lower art in seated areas to seated eye level.

### Installing a Salon Hang Without a Layout Plan

The trap is that a salon hang looks casual, so designers assume it can be installed casually. The mechanism of harm is a random, unbalanced arrangement that reads as clutter rather than curation. The discipline is to plan the salon hang on paper, with each piece's size and position, and to establish a frame (the overall boundary of the arrangement) and a rhythm (the spacing and the relationship of pieces) before installation.

### Framing with Non-Conservation Materials

The trap is using standard mat board and regular glass for valuable or sentimental art to save cost, then discovering years later that the mat has acid-burned the art and the light has faded it. The mechanism of harm is irreversible damage to the art. The discipline is to specify conservation-grade materials (rag or alpha-cellulose mat, UV-filtering glazing, reversible hinging) for anything of value, and to communicate the conservation rationale to the client.

### Lighting Art with the Wrong Fixture, Angle, or Source

The trap is aiming a general recessed light at the art, or using a picture light with an incandescent bulb that generates heat and UV. The mechanism of harm is glare, uneven illumination, color distortion, or fading of the art. The discipline is to specify art lighting with the correct beam angle, throw, color rendering (CRI 90+), and conservation properties (low UV, low heat), located at the correct angle, and to coordinate the lighting with the electrical rough-in.

## Self-Check

- Is the art sized to fill roughly two-thirds to three-quarters of the furniture below it, or to relate to the wall scale, rather than floating in the wall?
- Is the art placed at a height that relates to the furniture below it and to the viewer's eye level (standing, seated, or moving)?
- Is the composition (single, pair, triptych, salon, grid) planned on the elevation, with dimensions and spacing, before installation?
- Is the framing specified with conservation-grade materials (rag or alpha-cellulose mat, UV-filtering glazing, reversible hinging) for valuable or sentimental art?
- Is the art lighting specified with the correct beam angle, throw, CRI 90+, and low UV/heat, located at the correct angle, and coordinated with electrical rough-in?
- Does the curation cohere through style, period, color, medium, theme, or curated contrast, and does it reflect the client's voice or the brand narrative?
- For light-sensitive art (works on paper, photographs, textiles), are lux level and exposure duration controlled to prevent fading?
