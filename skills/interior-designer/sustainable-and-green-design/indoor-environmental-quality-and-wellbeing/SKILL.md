# Indoor Environmental Quality and Wellbeing

Indoor environmental quality (IEQ) — the air, the thermal comfort, the acoustics, the light, and the connection to nature — determines the health, comfort, and productivity of the people who occupy the space, and unlike energy or water, IEQ is the sustainability dimension the occupant experiences directly and constantly. The judgment problem is that IEQ is often subordinated to energy efficiency (a tightly sealed, energy-efficient building with inadequate ventilation that degrades air quality), to first cost (cheap materials that off-gas), or to aesthetics (a beautiful all-hard-surface space with terrible acoustics), and the trade-offs are made silently, harming the occupants who cannot diagnose why they feel unwell or unproductive in the space. The deeper trap is that IEQ is a system — the materials, the ventilation, the thermal control, the acoustics, the daylight all interact — and optimizing one dimension while degrading another produces a space that is "green" in one attribute and unhealthy in another. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether an interior genuinely supports the health and wellbeing of its occupants.

## Core Rules

### Specify Low-Emitting Materials Across All Product Categories

Interior materials — paints, adhesives, sealants, panel products, flooring, insulation, finishes — off-gas volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and other pollutants for months or years after installation, and the cumulative effect on indoor air quality is significant. The rule is to specify low- or zero-VOC products across all categories, verified by third-party certifications (GREENGUARD, FloorScore, GREENGUARD Gold for sensitive occupancies like schools and healthcare), to prefer solid wood or no-added-formaldehyde panels over standard composite panels, and to require the certifications in the specifications, not just to request "low-VOC." The decision criterion is to treat material emissions as a health specification, with documentation, not as a marketing claim.

### Ensure Adequate Ventilation and Air Quality Monitoring

Even with low-emitting materials, occupied spaces require adequate ventilation to dilute and remove the pollutants generated by occupants (carbon dioxide, bioeffluents), by the equipment (printers, copiers), and by the cleaning and maintenance products. The rule is to verify the ventilation design meets or exceeds the ASHRAE 62.1 standard (for commercial) or 62.2 (for residential), to specify demand-controlled ventilation (CO2 sensors that increase fresh air when occupancy rises), and to consider air quality monitoring (PM2.5, CO2, VOC sensors) that alerts occupants and operators to degraded air. A tightly sealed energy-efficient building without adequate ventilation traps pollutants and degrades health.

### Design Thermal Comfort for the Range of Occupants and Activities

Thermal comfort is subjective and varies by individual (metabolism, clothing, activity), and a single thermostat setting cannot satisfy everyone, so the design must provide both a good baseline and individual control. The rule is to design the HVAC for even distribution (no hot or cold spots), to provide individual or zone controls (thermostats per zone, operable windows where feasible, personal comfort systems like desk fans or heated chairs), and to manage the radiant environment (cold windows in winter, hot glass in summer, addressed through glazing and shading). The decision criterion is to ask whether the typical occupant can adjust their thermal environment, not just whether the average temperature is correct.

### Manage Acoustics for Both Concentration and Communication

Acoustic comfort supports both concentration (the absence of distracting noise) and communication (the ability to hear and be heard), and these can conflict — a quiet space is good for concentration but may lack the masking that makes conversation private, while a lively space suits collaboration but disturbs focused work. The rule is to zone the space acoustically (quiet zones for focus, lively zones for collaboration), to use absorptive materials (acoustic ceiling, wall panels, flooring, furnishings) to control reverberation, to provide acoustic privacy (sound-rated partitions, speech masking) where confidentiality matters, and to design the background sound level (not too quiet, not too loud) for the function. The decision criterion is to design the acoustic environment for the activity, not to default to whatever results.

### Integrate Daylight and Electric Light for Circadian Health

Light affects not only vision but circadian rhythm, mood, and alertness, and the lighting design must support the occupant's biological needs as well as the visual tasks. The rule is to maximize daylight access (locate workstations near windows, use light shelves and reflective surfaces to distribute daylight deep into the space), to design the electric lighting to supplement the daylight and to support the circadian rhythm (brighter, cooler light by day; warmer, dimmer light in the evening), and to provide individual control (task lighting, dimming) so the occupant can adjust. The decision criterion is to ask whether the lighting supports the occupant's health and rhythm, not just whether the illuminance target is met.

### Incorporate Biophilic Design for Psychological Wellbeing

Biophilia — the human connection to nature — is a documented wellbeing factor, and biophilic design (direct nature, indirect nature, and the nature of the space) reduces stress, improves focus, and supports healing. The rule is to incorporate direct nature (plants, water features, views to nature), indirect nature (natural materials, nature-based patterns and colors, nature imagery), and the nature of the space (prospect and refuge, organized complexity, risk-free mystery) into the design. The decision criterion is to treat biophilia as a design requirement supported by evidence, not as a decorative accent, and to integrate it substantively rather than as a token plant.

## Common Traps

### Energy Efficiency at the Expense of Air Quality

The designer or the engineer tightens the building envelope and reduces the ventilation to save energy, and the indoor air quality degrades as pollutants accumulate, producing the "sick building" effect (headaches, fatigue, irritation) that harms the occupants. The mechanism of failure is that energy was optimized over IEQ. The defense is to maintain adequate ventilation (ASHRAE 62.1/62.2) even in energy-efficient designs and to use heat or energy recovery to offset the energy cost.

### "Low-VOC" Without Third-Party Verification

The designer specifies "low-VOC" products based on manufacturer claims, and the actual emissions are higher than claimed or the product off-gasses other pollutants, degrading the air quality. The mechanism of failure is that the claim was not verified. The defense is to require third-party certifications (GREENGUARD, FloorScore) with documentation.

### Thermal Comfort Designed to the Average

The designer sets a single thermostat and a single setpoint for the whole space, and the occupants who run hot or cold are uncomfortable with no recourse. The mechanism of failure is that individual variation was not considered. The defense is to provide zone or individual controls and to manage the radiant environment.

### Acoustics Defaulted to the Hard-Surface Look

The designer specifies hard surfaces (concrete, glass, bare gypsum) for a modern aesthetic, and the space is reverberant and noisy, degrading concentration and communication. The mechanism of failure is that acoustics were not designed. The defense is to incorporate absorptive materials and to design the acoustic environment for the function.

### Biophilia as a Token Plant

The designer adds a few plants or a nature photograph as a biophilic gesture, and the connection to nature is superficial, delivering none of the documented wellbeing benefits. The mechanism of failure is that biophilia was treated as decoration rather than as a design strategy. The defense is to integrate biophilia substantively (views, patterns, materials, spatial qualities).

## Self-Check

- Did I specify low-emitting materials (zero-VOC paints, no-added-formaldehyde panels, GREENGUARD/FloorScore certified) across all categories with third-party verification?
- Did I verify the ventilation meets ASHRAE 62.1/62.2, with demand-controlled ventilation and air quality monitoring where appropriate?
- Did I design thermal comfort for the range of occupants, with zone or individual controls and managed radiant conditions?
- Did I zone the space acoustically and use absorptive materials, sound-rated assemblies, and masking for both concentration and communication?
- Did I integrate daylight and electric light to support circadian health, with individual lighting control?
- Did I incorporate biophilic design substantively (direct nature, indirect nature, the nature of the space) rather than as a token gesture?
- Did I treat IEQ as a system where air, thermal, acoustic, light, and biophilic dimensions are balanced, not optimized at each other's expense?
