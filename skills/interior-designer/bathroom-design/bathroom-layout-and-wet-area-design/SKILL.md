# Bathroom Layout and Wet-Area Design

A bathroom is the most functionally demanding room per square foot in a house, because it concentrates plumbing, water control, privacy, ventilation, storage, and grooming tasks into a small space where failures are not merely inconvenient but destructive — water that reaches where it should not causes rot, mold, and structural damage. The judgment problem is that bathroom layout is often driven by plumbing convenience and fixture symmetry rather than by the daily grooming routine and the waterproofing logic, producing bathrooms where the shower leaks into the wall, the vanity is too close to the toilet, the door swings into the user, or the storage is inadequate for the actual products and linens. The deeper trap is that wet-area design is invisible in plan — the waterproofing, the slope, the drain location, the curb — and these invisible details determine whether the bathroom lasts decades or fails in years. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether a bathroom layout and wet area function and endure.

## Core Rules

### Size Fixture Clearances for Comfort and Code, Not for Fit

Bathroom fixtures have minimum clearances that must be respected for comfort and for code: 30 inches of clear space in front of the toilet (and 21 inches from the toilet centerline to any obstruction), 30 inches between the vanity centerline and the toilet or wall, 36 inches of clear space in front of the vanity, and a minimum shower interior of 36x36 inches (30x30 absolute code minimum, but too small for comfort). The decision criterion is to lay out the fixtures to the comfortable clearance, not the code minimum, because a bathroom sized to the minimum feels cramped and is hard to clean. The trap is fitting all the fixtures into a small footprint by shaving clearances, producing a bathroom where two people cannot use it simultaneously and where the door hits the user.

### Design the Wet Area for Containment, Slope, and Drainage

The shower or wet area must contain water, slope to the drain, and drain fast enough to prevent pooling. This means: a curb or a level threshold with a linear drain (for barrier-free), properly sloped floors (1/4 inch per foot to the drain, 1/8 inch minimum), a waterproof membrane (sheet membrane or liquid-applied) that extends up the walls and across the curb, proper drain type (a three-piece clamping drain for traditional pan, a linear drain for barrier-free), and a door or glass enclosure that contains splash. The rule is to design the wet area as a waterproof system — membrane, slope, drain, enclosure — not as a tile surface over a hopeful substrate, because tile and grout are not waterproof; the membrane is.

### Position the Toilet for Privacy and Access

The toilet should be located for privacy (not visible from the doorway or from an open plan beyond), for access (not in a tight alcove that is hard to clean and uncomfortable to use), and for rough-in compatibility (the wall drain location dictates the toilet position). The decision criterion is to place the toilet on the most private wall, away from the door, with the required clearances, and to verify the rough-in location against the fixture. A toilet placed in the open or in a tight corner is a daily discomfort and a privacy failure.

### Design the Vanity for the Grooming Routine and Storage

The vanity is where the daily grooming occurs, and it must support that routine: adequate counter space for products, storage for daily items within reach (drawers, not just doors and shelves), task lighting at the face (sconces at eye level, not just overhead), an outlet for appliances, and a mirror placed for the user's height. For a shared vanity, provide two sinks with adequate separation (at least 36 inches between centerlines) and separate storage. The rule is to design the vanity around the morning and evening routine, not around a standard double-sink cabinet, because a vanity that does not support the routine becomes a daily frustration.

### Plan Ventilation to Remove Moisture at the Source

Bathroom moisture — from showers and tubs — must be removed at the source by an exhaust fan sized to the room (typically 1 CFM per square foot of floor area, minimum 50 CFM) and ducted to the exterior, not to the attic or the soffit. The fan should be located in the shower or near it (where the moisture is generated), should be quiet enough that the user actually turns it on (1.5 sones or less), and should run for 20-30 minutes after the shower (via a timer or a humidity sensor). The rule is to treat ventilation as a moisture-control system, not as an optional fan, because inadequate ventilation causes mold, peeling paint, and rot.

### Provide Storage for Linens, Products, and Cleaning Supplies

Bathrooms accumulate linens (towels, washcloths, bath mats), grooming products, medications, cleaning supplies, and often a hamper, and the storage must be planned for these categories. The rule is to allocate dedicated storage: a linen cabinet or closet for towels and bulk items, vanity drawers for daily products, a medicine cabinet for medications, a location for the hamper, and storage for cleaning supplies (often outside the wet area). A bathroom with inadequate storage becomes cluttered within weeks, and the clutter lands on the vanity and the tub deck where it is both unsightly and in the way.

## Common Traps

### Sizing Fixtures to the Code Minimum

The designer fits the toilet, vanity, and shower into a small bathroom by using the code minimum clearances, and the result is a bathroom where the user's knees touch the vanity, the door hits the user, and the shower is too small to wash comfortably. The mechanism of failure is that the minimum was treated as the target. The defense is to size to the comfortable clearance, accepting a smaller vanity or a relocated fixture to preserve the clearances.

### Tile and Grout Treated as Waterproof

The designer specifies a beautiful tile shower assuming the tile and grout contain the water, and the underlying substrate (often drywall or cement board without a membrane) gets wet, rots, and fails within years. The mechanism of failure is that tile and grout are decorative, not waterproof; the waterproofing is the membrane beneath. The defense is to specify a continuous waterproof membrane (sheet or liquid) beneath the tile, properly lapped and sealed at all penetrations.

### Inadequate Slope to the Drain

The shower floor is built with a minimal or inconsistent slope, and water pools in the corners or near the curb rather than draining, leading to standing water, soap scum accumulation, and eventual leakage at the curb. The mechanism of failure is that the slope was not built to the standard (1/4 inch per foot). The defense is to verify the slope during construction and to use a pre-sloped pan or a linear drain system that ensures consistent drainage.

### The Exhaust Fan Ducted to the Attic

The designer or contractor ducts the bathroom exhaust to the attic or the soffit rather than to the exterior, and the warm moist air condenses in the attic, causing mold, wood rot, and ice dams in cold climates. The mechanism of failure is that the duct termination was not specified or verified. The defense is to specify exterior termination (through the roof or wall, with a proper cap) and to verify it at installation.

### Vanity Lighting From Above Only

The designer places a single light fixture above the mirror, and the user's face is lit from above with deep shadows under the eyes and nose, making grooming difficult. The mechanism of failure is that the lighting was placed for ceiling convenience rather than for face lighting. The defense is to specify sconces at eye level on either side of the mirror (or a wide fixture above with diffused light) that illuminate the face from the front and sides.

## Self-Check

- Did I size fixture clearances to the comfortable dimension (30 inches in front of toilet, 36 inches in front of vanity, 36x36 shower minimum), not the code minimum?
- Did I design the wet area as a waterproof system (membrane, slope, drain, enclosure) rather than relying on tile and grout to contain water?
- Did I position the toilet for privacy and access, away from the door, with the required clearances and a verified rough-in?
- Did I design the vanity for the grooming routine (counter space, drawer storage, face-level task lighting, outlet, mirror at user height)?
- Did I size and locate the exhaust fan to remove moisture at the source (in or near the shower, ducted to the exterior, with a timer or humidity sensor)?
- Did I allocate dedicated storage for linens, daily products, medications, the hamper, and cleaning supplies?
- Did I verify the slope to the drain (1/4 inch per foot) and the waterproofing membrane extent during the wet-area design?
