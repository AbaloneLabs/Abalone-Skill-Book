# Structural And MEP Coordination In Renovation

Renovation interiors fail most often at the seams between new systems and existing fabric. The judgment problem is that designers draw the new interior as a finished vision and treat structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing coordination as a downstream technical detail, when in renovation these systems are the primary design constraints. Existing structure cannot move, existing chases have limited capacity, and new systems must thread through a building that was never planned for them. The agent's job is to coordinate these systems as part of the design, resolve conflicts in three dimensions before construction, and sequence demolition and installation so that the building's function and integrity are protected.

## Core Rules

### Map Existing Structure, Chases, And Utilities First

Before designing new systems, map the existing structure, mechanical chases, plumbing stacks, electrical service, and the capacity of each. Existing buildings have limited vertical space, fixed structural grid lines, and utility entry points that cannot be casually relocated. Designing new systems without this map produces designs that cannot be built without major structural work. The existing utility infrastructure is the starting constraint, not an afterthought.

### Resolve System Conflicts In Three Dimensions

Structural beams, ductwork, plumbing drains, sprinkler lines, lighting, and ceiling profiles all occupy the same overhead space. Coordinate them in section and reflected ceiling plans, not just in plan view. Identify the deepest constraint (usually the structural beam or the largest duct) and build the ceiling and lighting strategy around it. Conflicts discovered in the field force compromises that lower ceiling heights, misalign fixtures, or require ugly soffits.

### Design For Limited Vertical Space

Renovations often have less floor-to-structure height than new construction, and new systems must fit within it. Prioritize low-profile systems — flexible ducts, shallow fixtures, concealed sprinklers, in-floor or perimeter distribution — and accept that some ceilings must be lowered or furred. Do not promise ceiling heights that the structure and systems cannot deliver. Model the worst-case section early to confirm the achievable ceiling profile.

### Sequence Demolition To Protect Occupied And Adjacent Areas

In renovations, demolition affects occupied or adjacent spaces through noise, dust, vibration, and utility shutdowns. Sequence demolition to isolate work zones, maintain egress, and schedule noisy or utility-interrupting work during permitted hours. Coordinate shutoffs of power, water, and fire systems with the building's operation, because unplanned shutdowns can close a business or violate life-safety requirements.

### Coordinate New System Penetrations With Existing Structure

New plumbing, ductwork, and electrical runs often require penetrations through existing structural elements. Each penetration must be reviewed for structural impact — notching a joist or drilling a beam without engineering review can compromise the structure. Coordinate every penetration with structural assessment and document approved locations. Uncoordinated field penetrations are a leading cause of structural damage in renovation.

### Plan For Phasing And Temporary Systems

Renovations of occupied buildings require phasing: moving occupants, providing temporary egress and life-safety systems, and maintaining partial utility service during work. Design the phasing plan alongside the design, because the cost and feasibility of phasing often reshape the scope. A design that ignores phasing may be unbuildable without vacating the entire building, which the client may not accept.

### Verify Utility Capacity For The New Program

The new program may demand more electrical service, water, drainage, or ventilation than the existing utilities can deliver. Confirm service capacity early and budget for upgrades — new transformers, larger service entrances, additional risers — as a distinct scope. Discovering inadequate capacity during construction halts work and forces expensive emergency upgrades.

## Common Traps

### Designing The Interior Before Coordinating Systems

Drawing a clean ceiling and lighting plan, then discovering that ductwork and structure require a lower ceiling or a soffit, forces redesign that compromises the design. The trap is that the visual design looks resolved while the system coordination is unresolved, and the conflict surfaces during construction when redesign is expensive. Coordinate systems during design, not after.

### Ignoring The Deepest Overhead Constraint

Designers often set ceiling heights based on the average condition, then discover that a structural beam or main duct drops below the intended ceiling line, forcing an awkward bulkhead. The trap is that the average hides the worst case, which governs. Always design to the deepest constraint so the ceiling profile is consistent and intentional.

### Allowing Uncoordinated Field Penetrations

When contractors route plumbing and electrical without coordinated drawings, they cut through structure wherever convenient, causing damage that is hidden but real. The trap is that the work proceeds quickly and looks fine on the surface while the structural integrity is quietly compromised. Require coordinated penetration drawings and engineering review for every structural penetration.

### Underestimating Utility Capacity Needs

A new kitchen, additional bathrooms, or a denser occupancy can exceed the existing electrical, water, or drainage capacity. The trap is that capacity is invisible until the system is loaded, at which point breakers trip, drains back up, or pressure drops. Verify capacity against the new program's demand during design, not after occupancy.

## Self-Check

- Have existing structure, chases, and utilities been mapped before new systems are designed?
- Are structural, ductwork, plumbing, sprinkler, lighting, and ceiling conflicts resolved in section?
- Has the worst-case overhead section been modeled to confirm achievable ceiling heights?
- Does the demolition sequence protect occupied and adjacent areas and maintain egress and life-safety?
- Is every new structural penetration reviewed and documented with engineering approval?
- Is a phasing plan with temporary systems designed alongside the renovation scope?
- Has existing utility capacity been verified against the new program's demand?
