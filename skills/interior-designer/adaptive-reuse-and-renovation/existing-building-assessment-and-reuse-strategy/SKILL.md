# Existing Building Assessment And Reuse Strategy

Adaptive reuse begins with a judgment problem that determines everything downstream: what in the existing building is an asset to preserve, what is a liability to remove, and what is a constraint to design around. Agents often approach existing buildings with a fresh-design mindset, treating the structure as a blank container, which destroys the character that justified reuse and triggers costly code and structural surprises. The agent's job is to read the building as a layered record of its history, assess its real condition and capacity, identify the regulatory and hazard constraints that will govern the work, and form a reuse strategy that protects value while enabling the new program.

## Core Rules

### Document Existing Conditions Before Designing

Before any design decision, capture a thorough record of the existing building: measured drawings, structural systems, material conditions, openings, levels, and anomalies. Existing drawings are rarely accurate; field-measure the critical dimensions. Photograph every condition that will be altered, because hidden conditions uncovered during demolition will be disputed. A complete existing-conditions record is the foundation for every later decision and the primary defense against change orders.

### Identify Historical Fabric Worth Preserving

Not everything old is worth saving, but the features that give the building its identity — original fenestration patterns, structural elements, decorative trim, materials, and spatial volumes — are usually the reason reuse was chosen. Identify these assets early and design around them rather than through them. Removing character-defining features to simplify the new program destroys the value proposition of reuse and often violates preservation requirements. Distinguish structural and historical significance from mere age.

### Assess Structural Capacity For The New Program

Existing structures were designed for their original use and may not support the new loads, spans, or openings. Engage structural assessment early: live-load capacity, floor flatness, seismic and wind capacity, and the feasibility of new penetrations. Do not assume a heavy timber or masonry building can absorb a new use without reinforcement. Structural surprises are the most common cause of reuse projects becoming financially unviable.

### Identify Hazardous Materials And Environmental Risk

Older buildings routinely contain lead paint, asbestos, mold, contaminated soils, and other hazardous materials whose abatement drives cost and schedule. Commission hazardous materials surveys before finalizing scope, and budget for abatement as a distinct line item. Discovering asbestos or lead mid-construction halts work and triggers regulated removal procedures that can delay a project by weeks and inflate cost dramatically.

### Map The Code Triggers That The Renovation Activates

Renovations and changes of use trigger code compliance requirements that new construction avoids. A change of occupancy classification, substantial alteration thresholds, seismic upgrade triggers, accessibility path requirements, and energy-code compliance for altered systems can all be activated by work that seems cosmetic. Map these triggers against the proposed scope before design is fixed, because they may force structural, life-safety, or accessibility work far beyond the visible renovation.

### Separate Preservation From Restoration From Transformation

Clarify the treatment philosophy for each part of the building: preserve (maintain as-is), restore (return to a prior state), rehabilitate (alter for new use while retaining character), or transform (radically change). Mixed philosophies across a project create incoherent results and conflicting specifications. State the treatment intent per area so the design and construction team apply consistent standards.

### Sequence Investigation, Abatement, And Construction

Reuse projects have a discovery-driven sequence: investigation, then abatement of hazards, then demolition, then design refinement based on actual conditions, then construction. Trying to compress this into a linear design-bid-build schedule guarantees that hidden conditions emerge during construction when they are most expensive. Build contingency and investigation phases into the schedule and budget from the start.

## Common Traps

### Treating The Existing Building As A Blank Container

Designing a new interior as if the shell were neutral ignores the structural, dimensional, and character constraints that define reuse. The trap is that the design looks clean on paper but collides with reality during construction — a beam where a doorway was planned, a floor that cannot take the new load, a window pattern that the new layout fights. Design with the building, not against it.

### Trusting Existing Drawings Without Field Verification

Old drawings rarely match as-built conditions: dimensions drift, undocumented alterations accumulate, and original design intent differs from what was built. The trap is that designing from unverified drawings produces a beautiful set of documents that fail in the field, generating change orders and conflict. Always field-measure the conditions that govern the design.

### Underestimating Code Triggers From Change Of Use

A change from warehouse to residential, or office to assembly, activates occupancy-based code requirements including egress capacity, fire ratings, accessibility, plumbing counts, and seismic upgrades. The trap is that these triggers are invisible in a visual walk-through and emerge only during permit review, when the design is already fixed. Map code triggers during assessment, not during permitting.

### Destroying Character-Defining Features For Convenience

Removing an original ceiling height, an industrial window pattern, or an exposed structure to simplify mechanical routing saves short-term effort but erases the character that made reuse attractive and often violates preservation agreements. The harm is a generic interior that could exist in any new building, defeating the purpose of reuse. Protect character features as design constraints, not obstacles.

## Self-Check

- Is there a complete, field-verified existing-conditions record before design begins?
- Have character-defining historical features been identified and protected as design constraints?
- Has structural capacity been assessed against the new program's loads and openings?
- Have hazardous materials surveys been commissioned and abatement budgeted as a distinct line item?
- Have code triggers from change of use and substantial alteration been mapped before design is fixed?
- Is a consistent treatment philosophy (preserve, restore, rehabilitate, transform) stated for each area?
- Does the schedule include investigation, abatement, and design-refinement phases for hidden conditions?
