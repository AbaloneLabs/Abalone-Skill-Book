# Upholstery and Case-Good Specification

Furniture is specified by its appearance in a catalog, but its quality and longevity are determined by construction details that are invisible in the photograph — the frame joinery, the spring type, the foam density, the fabric abrasion rating, the case-good drawer suspension, the finish durability. The judgment problem is that two sofas that look identical can differ by a factor of ten in price and by a factor of ten in service life, and the difference is entirely in the construction that the designer must know how to specify and the client must be willing to pay for. The deeper trap is that furniture is often specified by model number alone, leaving the construction to the manufacturer's default, which in a price-competitive market means the cheapest construction that meets the catalog claim. A specification that does not state the frame, the suspension, the foam, and the fabric rating is not a specification; it is a wish. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether upholstered furniture and case goods will perform and last, or fail prematurely.

## Core Rules

### Specify the Frame Material and Joinery for Uphstered Furniture

The frame is the skeleton of upholstered furniture, and its material and joinery determine whether the piece stays square and stable over decades or loosens and sags within years. The standard for quality is kiln-dried hardwood (maple, oak, alder, beech) with joinery that includes mortise-and-tenon, double dowels, or corner blocks reinforced with screws and glue — not staples, not butt joints, not softwood. The rule is to specify the frame material (kiln-dried hardwood), the joinery method (mortise-and-tenon or dowel with corner blocks), and to require that the frame carry a lifetime warranty against structural failure. A frame specified only as "wood" or "hardwood" leaves the construction to the manufacturer's cheapest option.

### Specify the Suspension System

The suspension — the springs or webbing that support the seat — determines the sit and the longevity of the cushion support. Quality options include eight-way hand-tied coil springs (traditional, durable, expensive), sinuous (no-sag) wire springs (common, acceptable when heavy gauge and closely spaced), and elastic webbing (acceptable for lighter-use pieces). The rule is to specify the suspension type and gauge, and to be suspicious of unspecified suspension, which often means the lightest, cheapest option. The suspension and the foam work together; a great foam over a cheap suspension will still sag.

### Specify Foam Density and Indentation Force Deflection

Cushion quality is determined by the foam's density (pounds per cubic foot, correlating with longevity) and its indentation force deflection or IFD (the firmness, correlating with feel). High-quality seat foam is 1.8 to 2.5 density; lower density foams (1.5 and below) flatten and lose their shape within a few years. The rule is to specify the foam density (minimum 1.8 for residential, 2.0+ for commercial), the IFD for the desired firmness, and to consider a wrap (down, fiber, or a softer foam layer) for surface softness over a firm core. Cushions specified only as "high-density foam" or "premium foam" are unspecified and will be whatever the manufacturer defaults to.

### Specify Fabric by Abrasion, Colorfastness, and Cleanability Ratings

Upholstery fabric performance is measured by the Martindale or Wyzenbeek abrasion test (double rubs to wear through), colorfastness to light and to crocking, and cleanability (the ability to remove stains without damage). Residential fabrics should meet 15,000+ double rubs; commercial contract fabrics 30,000-50,000+ depending on traffic. The rule is to specify the abrasion rating appropriate to the use, to verify colorfastness ratings for sun-exposed locations, and to prefer cleanable fabrics (solution-dyed acrylics, treated performance fabrics, or washable slipcovers) for family and hospitality use. A fabric chosen for pattern and color without performance ratings will wear through or stain permanently in service.

### Specify Case-Good Joinery, Drawers, and Finish

For case goods (tables, dressers, cabinets, storage pieces), quality is determined by the wood joinery (dovetailed or mortise-and-tenon drawers, not stapled; solid wood or veneer over plywood, not over particleboard), the drawer suspension (full-extension ball-bearing slides, not partial-extension or epoxy), and the finish (catalyzed conversion varnish or lacquer that resists water and alcohol, not a simple oil or wax). The rule is to specify the case construction (solid wood or plywood veneer, not particleboard), the drawer joinery (dovetailed) and suspension (full-extension undermount slides), and the finish type and durability. Case goods specified by appearance alone will have stapled drawers, particleboard cases, and fragile finishes.

### Require Submittals and Samples Before Production

Furniture specifications are only as good as the verification that the manufacturer meets them, and the verification requires submittals — fabric samples for color and hand, foam specs, frame construction details, finish samples — before production begins. The rule is to require submittals for any non-trivial furniture order, to review them against the specification, and to reject substitutions that do not meet the construction and performance requirements. Without submittals, the manufacturer ships whatever they have in stock, and the specification is aspirational. Submittals are the mechanism that makes the specification real.

## Common Traps

### Specifying by Model Number Alone

The designer selects a sofa by model number from a catalog, trusting the manufacturer's construction, and the piece arrives with a softwood frame, stapled joints, low-density foam, and a fabric that fails in a year. The mechanism of failure is that the model number specified the appearance but not the construction, and the manufacturer's default construction was the cheapest that met the catalog image. The defense is to specify the frame, suspension, foam, and fabric ratings explicitly, not just the model.

### Ignoring Foam Density and Assuming "Good" Foam

The designer specifies "high-density foam" or accepts the manufacturer's claim of "premium cushions," and the foam flattens and loses its shape within two years. The mechanism of failure is that "high-density" and "premium" are marketing terms, not specifications, and the actual density may be 1.5 or lower, which is inadequate for seat cushions. The defense is to specify the density numerically (1.8+ residential, 2.0+ commercial) and to require the foam spec in the submittal.

### Choosing Fabric for Pattern Without Performance Ratings

The designer and client fall in love with a fabric's pattern and color and specify it for a family room sofa without checking the abrasion rating or cleanability. Within a year, the fabric pills, wears through at the arms, and stains permanently. The mechanism of failure is that appearance was the criterion and performance was assumed. The defense is to require the Martindale or Wyzenbeek rating and to verify cleanability for the intended use before specifying.

### Case Goods With Stapled Drawers and Particleboard Cases

The designer specifies a dresser or console by appearance, and it arrives with stapled drawer joints, partial-extension slides, and a case of veneer over particleboard that sags and swells with humidity. The mechanism of failure is that the construction was not specified and the manufacturer defaulted to the cheapest. The defense is to specify dovetailed drawers, full-extension slides, and solid wood or plywood veneer cases.

### Skipping Submittals to Save Time

Under schedule pressure, the designer skips the submittal review and approves the order from the catalog, and the furniture arrives with substitutions — a different fabric, a lighter foam, a cheaper frame — that do not meet the design intent. The mechanism of failure is that the specification was never verified against the production. The defense is to require submittals and to build the review time into the schedule, because correcting a wrong shipment after the fact costs far more time than the submittal review.

## Self-Check

- Did I specify the frame material (kiln-dried hardwood) and joinery (mortise-and-tenon or dowel with corner blocks) for upholstered pieces, not just the model number?
- Did I specify the suspension type (eight-way hand-tied, heavy-gauge sinuous, or webbing) appropriate to the use?
- Did I specify the foam density numerically (1.8+ residential, 2.0+ commercial) and the IFD for the desired firmness?
- Did I specify the fabric abrasion rating (Martindale or Wyzenbeek) appropriate to the use, and verify colorfastness and cleanability?
- For case goods, did I specify the case material (solid or plywood veneer, not particleboard), drawer joinery (dovetailed), suspension (full-extension), and finish type?
- Did I require submittals (fabric, foam, frame, finish samples) before production and review them against the specification?
- Did I translate the construction differences into price and longevity tradeoffs the client can understand before they choose on appearance?
