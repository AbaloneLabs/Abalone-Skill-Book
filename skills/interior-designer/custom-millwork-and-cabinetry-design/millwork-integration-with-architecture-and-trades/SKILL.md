# Millwork Integration with Architecture and Trades

Custom millwork does not exist in isolation; it is a built element installed within an architecture of walls, floors, ceilings, and systems, and its success depends on the integration with all of them. The judgment problem is that millwork is often detailed as a freestanding object—drawn in elevation and section, with its own dimensions—without the coordination of the surrounding architecture and trades that determines whether it fits and functions. A millwork piece that conflicts with a duct, a switch, or a baseboard; that is dimensioned without the wall's plumbness; or that is installed before the floor finish is a millwork piece that requires field modification or remaking. Integration is a design discipline, not an installation hope, and it must be coordinated across the drawings and the trades.

## Core Rules

### Coordinate Millwork with Wall, Floor, and Ceiling Conditions

Millwork meets the architecture at every surface, and each interface must be detailed. At the wall, account for plumbness (no wall is plumb) with scribe moldings, filler panels, or adjustable reveals; account for wall openings (windows, doors, outlets) that the millwork must fit around or integrate. At the floor, account for levelness (no floor is level) with adjustable legs, shims, or a recessed toe; account for the floor finish thickness, because the millwork must sit on the finished floor, not the subfloor, in most cases. At the ceiling, account for flatness (no ceiling is flat) with a scribed crown, a reveal, or a gap; account for ceiling heights and for the millwork's height relative to the ceiling. Measure the actual conditions before fabricating, because the drawings represent the ideal, not the reality.

### Coordinate Electrical, Lighting, and Low-Voltage Rough-In

Millwork integrates electrical (outlets, switches), lighting (under-cabinet, in-cabinet, toe-kick), and low-voltage (audiovisual, communications, controls) systems, and the rough-in must be coordinated before the walls are closed. Show the outlet and switch locations on the millwork drawings, coordinated with the cabinet layout (an outlet behind a cabinet door, not behind a drawer; a switch at the cabinet edge, not behind a door swing). Show the lighting rough-in (junction boxes, drivers) at the millwork, coordinated with the lighting design. Show the low-voltage rough-in (data, audiovisual) at the millwork, coordinated with the technology design. Coordinate the rough-in with the electrical engineer and the low-voltage contractor, because the engineer's default locations will not match the millwork. A millwork piece installed without rough-in coordination requires surface-mounted wiring or remaking.

### Coordinate Plumbing Rough-In for Millwork with Sinks and Fixtures

Millwork that incorporates sinks, faucets, or plumbing fixtures (vanities, wet bars, laundry cabinets, kitchen sinks) requires plumbing rough-in coordinated with the cabinet layout. Show the supply and waste locations on the millwork drawings, coordinated with the sink or fixture location and with the cabinet interior (the waste cannot exit through a drawer; the supply cannot interfere with a shelf). Coordinate the rough-in with the plumber and the mechanical engineer, because the plumber's default locations will not match the cabinet. Coordinate the fixture selection (the sink, the faucet) with the cabinet and the countertop, because the fixture dimensions drive the cabinet and the cutout. A millwork piece installed without plumbing coordination requires re-routing pipes or remaking.

### Resolve Conflicts with Mechanical Ducts, Diffusers, and Equipment

Millwork often occupies space that mechanical systems also claim: a duct in the soffit above the cabinets, a diffuser in the ceiling where the tall cabinet reaches, a return in the cabinet toe kick, a rooftop unit's penetration above the built-in. Identify the mechanical conditions at the millwork location and resolve conflicts in the design: lower the cabinet, relocate the diffuser, frame the soffit to the cabinet, integrate the return. Coordinate with the mechanical engineer, because the engineer's design will not account for the millwork unless the millwork is shown on the reflected ceiling plan and the sections. A millwork piece installed in conflict with a duct or diffuser requires field modification of the millwork or the mechanical, both expensive.

### Sequence Millwork Fabrication and Installation with Construction

Millwork fabrication is a long-lead activity (8 to 16 weeks or more), and installation must be sequenced with the construction. Fabrication cannot begin until the design is finalized and the shop drawings are approved; installation cannot begin until the site is ready (walls painted, flooring installed, rough-ins complete). Sequence the design finalization and the shop drawing approval to the fabrication lead time, so the millwork delivers when the site is ready. Sequence the installation after the flooring and the paint, and before the trim and the backsplash, so the millwork integrates cleanly. Coordinate the installation with the countertop template and installation (the countertop cannot be templated until the cabinets are installed and leveled), because the countertop is another long-lead item that follows the millwork. A millwork schedule not sequenced with construction delays the project.

### Conduct a Field Measure Before Fabrication

Before fabricating custom millwork, conduct a field measure of the actual conditions: the wall plumbness, the floor levelness, the ceiling height, the opening dimensions, the locations of rough-ins and openings. The field measure corrects the drawings' ideal dimensions to the site's real dimensions, and it is the basis for the shop drawings. Do not fabricate from the design drawings without a field measure, because the drawings represent the ideal, not the reality, and the difference is discovered at installation, when correction is expensive. The field measure is the responsibility of the millwork fabricator, but the designer should confirm it is done and should review the shop drawings against it. A field measure is the insurance that the millwork fits.

## Common Traps

### Detailing Millwork as a Freestanding Object Without Architectural Coordination

The trap is detailing the millwork in elevation and section, with its own dimensions, without showing the surrounding wall, floor, and ceiling conditions. The mechanism of harm is a millwork piece that does not fit the actual conditions (an unplumb wall, an unlevel floor, an uneven ceiling), requiring field modification. The discipline is to detail the millwork in context, with the architectural interfaces shown and the site conditions measured.

### Installing Millwork Without Electrical and Plumbing Rough-In Coordination

The trap is installing the millwork and discovering that the outlets, switches, or plumbing are in the wrong locations, because the rough-in was not coordinated with the cabinet layout. The mechanism of harm is surface-mounted wiring, re-routed pipes, or remaking the cabinet. The discipline is to show the rough-in on the millwork drawings and to coordinate with the trades before the walls are closed.

### Fabricating Without a Field Measure

The trap is fabricating the millwork from the design drawings, without measuring the actual site conditions. The mechanism of harm is a millwork piece that does not fit the site (too wide for the opening, too tall for the ceiling, misaligned with the openings), requiring remaking. The discipline is to conduct a field measure before fabrication and to base the shop drawings on it.

### Ignoring Mechanical Conflicts Until Installation

The trap is not checking the millwork location against the mechanical design (ducts, diffusers, returns), discovering the conflict at installation. The mechanism of harm is field modification of the millwork or the mechanical, both expensive and both disruptive. The discipline is to identify mechanical conditions at the millwork location during design and to resolve conflicts in the drawings.

### Sequencing Millwork Installation Out of Order

The trap is installing the millwork before the flooring or the paint, or after the trim and the backsplash, because the schedule was not sequenced. The mechanism of harm is a millwork piece that sits on the subfloor (creating a toe-inset problem when the flooring is added), is splattered by paint, or conflicts with the trim. The discipline is to sequence the installation after flooring and paint, and before trim and backsplash, and to coordinate the countertop template after the millwork is installed.

## Self-Check

- Are the wall, floor, and ceiling interfaces detailed, with scribing, leveling, and finish coordination, based on measured site conditions?
- Are electrical, lighting, and low-voltage rough-ins shown on the millwork drawings and coordinated with the trades before the walls are closed?
- Is plumbing rough-in coordinated with sinks and fixtures, with supply and waste locations shown on the millwork drawings?
- Are mechanical conflicts (ducts, diffusers, returns, penetrations) identified and resolved in the drawings, coordinated with the mechanical engineer?
- Is millwork fabrication sequenced to the lead time, and installation sequenced after flooring and paint, before trim and backsplash, with the countertop templated after installation?
- Is a field measure conducted before fabrication, and are the shop drawings based on the measured conditions?
- Are the shop drawings reviewed against the design, the specifications, the site conditions, and the trade rough-ins before fabrication?
