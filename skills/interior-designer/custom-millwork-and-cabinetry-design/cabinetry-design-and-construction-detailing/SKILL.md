# Cabinetry Design and Construction Detailing

Custom cabinetry is among the most detailed and most consequential elements of an interior, because it is built to fit specific conditions, to serve specific functions, and to last for decades. The judgment problem is that cabinetry is often designed by elevation (the visible face) without the construction and installation detailing that determines whether the cabinet fits, functions, and endures. A cabinet that looks right in elevation but is dimensioned without accounting for the appliance, the floor slope, the wall plumbness, or the hardware clearance will not fit or function in the field. Custom cabinetry requires detailing at the level of construction: the carcass, the joinery, the hardware, the installation, and the coordination with site conditions and adjacent elements.

## Core Rules

### Design from the Inside Out: Function, Storage, and Ergonomics Before Face

Cabinetry serves a function (storage, workspace, display, concealment), and the function governs the interior layout before the face. Design the interior first: what is stored (dimensions, weight, access frequency), what clearances are required (for appliances, for access), what ergonomics apply (counter height, reach depth, drawer versus door). Then design the face (the doors, drawers, panels, and trim) to express the interior layout and the design aesthetic. Reversing this order—a face designed for symmetry or style, with the interior fitted in afterward—produces cabinets that look right but function poorly (a symmetrical face with an awkward interior, a drawer too shallow for its contents). Document the interior layout (the storage, the clearances, the ergonomics) as part of the design, not just the elevation.

### Specify Materials, Construction, and Grade by the Architectural Woodwork Standards

Custom cabinetry materials and construction are governed by the Architectural Woodwork Standards (AWS), published by the Woodwork Institute (WI) and the Architectural Woodwork Manufacturers Association of Canada (AWMAC). The AWS defines grades (Premium, Custom, Economy) with specific requirements for materials (panel products, solid wood, veneer), construction (joinery, assembly, tolerances), and finishing. Specify the grade and the standard in the specification and on the drawings, because the grade defines the quality objectively and resolves disputes. For the visible faces, specify the wood species, the veneer cut and match (book match, slip match), and the finish (stain, clear coat, conversion varnish). For the carcass and interiors, specify the panel product (plywood, particleboard, MDF) and the exposure (domestic, industrial). Do not leave materials and construction to the fabricator's default; specify them.

### Detail Joinery, Assembly, and Tolerances for Quality and Durability

Cabinetry joinery and assembly determine the cabinet's structural quality and durability. Detail the joinery: dovetailed or doweled drawer construction (not stapled), mortise-and-tenon or cope-and-stick door frames (not mitered with staples), dadoed and rabbetted carcass joints with confirmat screws or cam locks (not butt joints with staples). Specify the tolerances (the AWS grade defines them): panel flatness, door and drawer alignment, gaps and reveals. Detail the back panel (a structural back for tall cabinets, a floating back for wide cabinets), the corner bracing, and the hanging and anchoring. The detailing is what distinguishes a Premium-grade cabinet from an Economy-grade cabinet, and it is invisible once installed, so it must be specified and verified at the shop drawing stage, not accepted on faith.

### Coordinate Dimensions with Appliances, Plumbing, and Site Conditions

Cabinet dimensions must coordinate with the appliances, the plumbing fixtures, and the site conditions, all of which have specific requirements. Obtain the appliance and fixture installation manuals and coordinate the cabinet openings, clearances, and rough-ins: a refrigerator requires a specific opening width, side clearance, and air space; a cooktop requires a specific cutout and side clearance; a sink requires a specific cabinet width and false front; a dishwasher requires a specific opening and adjacent clearance. Measure the site conditions (wall plumbness, floor levelness, ceiling flatness) and detail the cabinet to accommodate them (scribe moldings, adjustable legs, filler panels), because no wall is plumb and no floor is level. A cabinet dimensioned without site measurement and appliance coordination will not fit; coordinate before fabrication.

### Specify Hardware for Function, Durability, and Accessibility

Cabinet hardware—hinges, slides, knobs, pulls, locks, and specialty hardware (pull-out shelves, lazy Susans, trash pull-outs)—governs the cabinet's function and durability. Specify full-extension, soft-close drawer slides (not partial-extension or roller slides) for accessible and durable storage; specify self-closing, adjustable hinges (concealed or exposed per the design) for doors. Specify the pulls and knobs for ergonomics and accessibility (lever-style, easy to grip, located for reach), and confirm the hardware locations do not conflict with the door or drawer edges. For accessible cabinetry, specify hardware operable with a closed fist and located within reach range. The hardware is a significant cost and quality component; do not leave it to the fabricator's allowance, because the allowance will be filled with the cheapest compliant product.

### Detail the Installation, Anchoring, and Integration with Architecture

Cabinet installation is the final coordination of the cabinet with the architecture, and it must be detailed, not assumed. Detail the anchoring (to blocking in the wall framing, not to drywall), the leveling (with adjustable legs or shims, on an uneven floor), the scribing (to walls, ceilings, and adjacent elements), and the integration with trim, countertops, and backsplashes. Detail the seams between cabinets (the reveals, the alignment), the toe kick (the height and depth for ergonomics and accessibility), and the lighting (under-cabinet, in-cabinet, toe-kick) with the electrical rough-in. Coordinate the installation sequence with the construction (cabinets after flooring, before trim and backsplash), because a cabinet installed out of sequence creates conflicts. The installation detail is where the cabinet meets the building, and it is where most field problems occur.

## Common Traps

### Designing by Elevation Without Interior or Construction Detailing

The trap is designing the cabinet face for style and symmetry, without detailing the interior layout, the construction, or the installation. The mechanism of harm is a cabinet that looks right but functions poorly, fits badly, or fails structurally. The discipline is to design from the inside out and to detail the construction and the installation.

### Leaving Materials and Grade to the Fabricator's Default

The trap is specifying the cabinet by elevation and dimensions, leaving the materials, the construction, and the grade to the fabricator. The mechanism of harm is a cabinet built to the fabricator's default (often Economy grade with stapled joinery and particleboard), which does not meet the design's quality intent. The discipline is to specify the grade (per the AWS), the materials, and the construction, and to verify them at the shop drawing stage.

### Dimensioning Without Appliance Manuals and Site Measurement

The trap is dimensioning the cabinet from the design, without obtaining the appliance installation manuals and measuring the site conditions. The mechanism of harm is a cabinet that does not fit the appliance or the site, requiring field modifications or remaking. The discipline is to obtain the appliance manuals, to measure the site, and to detail the cabinet to both.

### Specifying Hardware by Allowance Rather Than by Product

The trap is specifying the hardware as an allowance, filled by the fabricator with the cheapest compliant product. The mechanism of harm is hardware that is not durable, not accessible, or not the design intent, discovered at installation. The discipline is to specify the hardware by product (slides, hinges, pulls) and to confirm the specification at the shop drawing stage.

### Failing to Detail the Installation and Anchoring

The trap is assuming the cabinet will be installed correctly without detailing the anchoring, leveling, scribing, and integration. The mechanism of harm is a cabinet installed to drywall without blocking, unleveled on an uneven floor, or un-scribed to a wavy wall, resulting in a cabinet that is unstable, misaligned, or gaps at the architecture. The discipline is to detail the installation, including the blocking (coordinated with framing), the leveling, the scribing, and the integration with trim and countertops.

## Self-Check

- Is the cabinet designed from the inside out, with the interior layout (storage, clearances, ergonomics) documented before the face?
- Is the grade (Premium, Custom, Economy per the AWS), the materials, and the construction specified, and verified at shop drawings?
- Are joinery (dovetailed drawers, mortise-and-tenon doors, dadoed carcass), tolerances, and structural detailing specified per the AWS grade?
- Are dimensions coordinated with appliance and fixture installation manuals and with measured site conditions (plumbness, levelness)?
- Is the hardware (slides, hinges, pulls) specified by product for function, durability, and accessibility, not by allowance?
- Is the installation detailed (anchoring to blocking, leveling, scribing, integration with trim, countertops, lighting), and sequenced with construction?
- Are the shop drawings reviewed against the design, the specifications, and the site conditions before fabrication?
