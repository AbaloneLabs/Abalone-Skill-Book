# Material Emissions And VOC Specification

Interior materials emit volatile organic compounds and other pollutants long after installation, and these emissions are a primary determinant of indoor air quality. The judgment problem is that materials are specified by appearance, durability, and cost, while their emissions — formaldehyde from composite wood, VOCs from paints and adhesives, plasticizers from resilient flooring — are invisible in a sample and emerge as health effects over time. An agent who specifies standard commercial materials in a school, healthcare, or tight building delivers a space that looks correct and degrades occupant health. The agent's job is to understand the emission sources in each material category, specify low-emission products certified to recognized standards, coordinate emissions across the whole material palette, and verify that the specification protects the occupants it serves.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Major Emission Sources By Material Category

Each material category has characteristic emission sources: composite wood products emit formaldehyde; paints, adhesives, and sealants emit VOCs; resilient flooring emits plasticizers; insulation and panels emit various compounds. Identify the major sources in each category specified, so the specification targets the real pollutants rather than generic "low-VOC" claims. A specification that addresses paint but ignores composite wood leaves a major formaldehyde source.

### Specify Products Certified To Recognized Emissions Standards

Emissions performance is verified by recognized standards and certifications — GREENGUARD, CDPH Standard Method, FloorScore, GREENGUARD Gold for sensitive environments — that test chamber emissions under defined conditions. Specify products certified to the standard appropriate to the environment, rather than accepting manufacturer self-claims. A product labeled "low-VOC" without certification may emit well above the thresholds that protect health.

### Require Higher Standards For Sensitive Environments

Schools, healthcare, childcare, and tight buildings with limited ventilation require stricter emissions limits than general commercial space, because occupants are more vulnerable or exposure is more concentrated. Specify the more stringent standards — GREENGUARD Gold or equivalent — for these environments, and verify every material category meets the higher bar. General-commercial limits in a school or clinic allow emissions that affect vulnerable occupants.

### Coordinate Emissions Across The Whole Material Palette

Indoor air quality results from the combined emissions of every material in the space, not any single material, so a few low-emission products among standard-emitting ones do not deliver healthy air. Coordinate the emissions specification across the entire palette — flooring, walls, ceilings, paint, adhesives, sealants, cabinetry, furniture — so the cumulative load is controlled. A low-VOC paint over formaldehyde-emitting cabinetry delivers little benefit.

### Address Adhesives And Sealants, Not Just Finishes

Adhesives and sealants — flooring adhesive, wallcovering paste, caulk, joint sealant — are high-emission materials that are often overlooked because they are hidden. Specify low-emission adhesives and sealants across every application, because a low-emission flooring installed with a high-VOC adhesive emits at the adhesive's rate. The hidden materials contribute as much to the emission load as the visible finishes.

### Specify Composite Wood With Low Formaldehyde

Composite wood products — particleboard, MDF, plywood — are major formaldehyde sources, and their emissions vary by resin type and certification. Specify ultra-low or no-added-formaldehyde composite wood, or products certified to CARB Phase 2 or TSCA Title VI at minimum, and require third-party certification. Standard composite wood in cabinetry or paneling can dominate the formaldehyde load in a space.

### Plan For Flush-Out Or Bake-Out Before Occupancy

Even low-emission materials release elevated compounds immediately after installation, and a flush-out period of ventilation before occupancy, or a bake-out with heat and ventilation, can reduce the initial load. Plan the schedule to allow flush-out, especially in tight buildings and sensitive environments. Occupying immediately after finish installation exposes occupants to peak emissions.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Generic Low-VOC Claims Without Certification

A product labeled "low-VOC" without third-party certification may emit well above health-protective thresholds, because the claim is unverified. The trap is that the label suggests safety while the emissions are unknown. Require recognized certification.

### Addressing Finishes But Ignoring Hidden Materials

Specifying low-VOC paint and flooring while leaving adhesives, sealants, and composite wood at standard emissions leaves major sources unaddressed. The trap is that the visible materials look healthy while the hidden ones dominate the load. Specify emissions across all categories.

### Using General-Commercial Limits In Sensitive Environments

Standards adequate for general office space allow emissions that affect children, patients, or other vulnerable occupants in schools and healthcare. The trap is that the limits are "compliant" while insufficient for the population. Apply stricter standards to sensitive environments.

### Occupying Immediately After Finish Installation

Peak emissions occur in the days and weeks after installation, and occupying during this period exposes occupants to the highest concentrations. The trap is that the schedule rewards speed while delivering peak exposure. Plan a flush-out period.

## Self-Check

- Are the major emission sources identified for each material category specified?
- Are products certified to recognized emissions standards rather than accepted on self-claims?
- Are stricter standards specified for schools, healthcare, and other sensitive environments?
- Is the emissions specification coordinated across the entire material palette, not a few products?
- Are adhesives, sealants, and hidden materials specified for low emissions, not just finishes?
- Is composite wood specified as ultra-low or no-added-formaldehyde with third-party certification?
- Is a flush-out or bake-out period planned before occupancy, especially for tight or sensitive buildings?
