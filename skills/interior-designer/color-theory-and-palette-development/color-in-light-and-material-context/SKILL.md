# Color in Light and Material Context

A color specified in the studio is not the color that will exist in the room, because color is not a fixed property of a surface — it is the light reflected from that surface into the eye, and that light changes with every variable: the color temperature of the source, the spectral content of the lamp, the time of day, the angle of incidence, the adjacent materials, and the age of the surface. The judgment problem is that designers routinely specify color as if it were stable, validating choices under studio lighting that bears no resemblance to the installed condition, and then are surprised when a gray turns purple at dusk, when two matched materials diverge under LED, or when a north-facing room makes every warm color look muddy. The deeper trap is metamerism — the phenomenon where two colors match under one light and mismatch under another — which can silently destroy a carefully coordinated palette at the moment the lighting changes. This skill covers the decisions that determine whether colors survive the transition from specification to installation across real lighting and material conditions.

## Core Rules

### Specify the Lighting Before or Concurrently With the Color

Because color is light-dependent, the lighting must be defined before the color can be responsibly chosen. The color temperature (2700K warm, 3000K neutral warm, 3500K neutral, 4000K cool, 5000K daylight), the CRI (color rendering index, ideally 90+ for residential and hospitality), and the spectral distribution of the source all determine how the specified color will appear. The decision criterion is to never specify a wall, fabric, or finish color without knowing the light source it will be viewed under, and to test the color under that exact source. A color chosen without reference to its lighting is a guess.

### Test Colors in the Actual Space at the Actual Time of Use

Showroom and studio lighting flatter colors uniformly; real rooms do not. A north-facing room receives cool, bluish daylight that shifts warm colors toward muddy and grays towards blue; a south-facing room receives warm light that enriches warm tones and can wash out cool ones; a west-facing room shifts dramatically through the day from cool morning to golden afternoon. The rule is to paint large sample boards (at least 2x3 feet) and place them on the actual walls, viewing them at the hours the space is most used, before specifying. A single visit at noon does not capture the room's color behavior across the day.

### Identify and Manage Metamerism Between Materials

Metamerism occurs when two color samples match under one light source but not another, because their spectral reflectance curves differ even though their perceived color is the same under a reference source. This is the hidden killer of coordinated palettes: the paint matches the fabric under the studio's halogen, but under the room's LED the paint reads green and the fabric reads gray. The defense is to source materials intended to be viewed together and to test them together under the actual lighting, and to prefer materials with similar pigment chemistry where a close match is critical. Where a perfect match is required (a painted door to match a fabric), request a physical match under the specified light, not a numerical one.

### Account for Reflected Color From Adjacent Surfaces

Every surface reflects colored light onto its neighbors. A large green wall reflects green light onto a white ceiling and onto human skin, shifting their apparent color. A warm wood floor reflects warm light upward onto walls and furniture. A bright accent rug casts its color onto nearby legs and baseboards. The rule is to map the major reflective surfaces and predict their color contribution to adjacent elements, and to test the palette with all major materials in proximity. Ignoring reflected color produces rooms where elements look subtly wrong next to each other with no diagnosable cause.

### Predict How Colors Will Age and Soil

Colors do not stay as specified. Paint yellows slightly with age and amber varnish; fabrics soil and shift toward gray or yellow; wood darkens and ambers, sometimes dramatically; stone and concrete develop patina. A palette that is perfect on installation may look tired or mismatched in two years as the elements age at different rates. The decision criterion is to ask how each material will look in five years, not just on day one, and to choose colors that age gracefully together. Light colors that show soil, and high-contrast palettes where one element yellows and the other does not, are common sources of premature aging complaints.

### Use the Color Rendering Index and Spectral Data, Not Just Kelvin

The color temperature (Kelvin) of a light source tells you the warmth or coolness of the white light, but it does not tell you how accurately the source renders the colors of objects. Two 3000K lamps can render a red completely differently because their spectral content differs. The CRI (color rendering index) is a partial measure, and for critical color work the TM-30 spectral data (fidelity index and gamut index) gives a fuller picture. The rule is to specify high-CRI (90+) sources for spaces where color matters — residential, retail, hospitality, art — and to request TM-30 data for critical applications. A low-CRI lamp makes every color look slightly dead and makes palette coordination impossible.

## Common Traps

### Specifying Color Under Studio Lighting and Assuming It Transfers

The designer selects all colors under the bright, neutral, high-CRI lighting of the studio or showroom, where every color reads true and balanced. The harm is that the same colors, in a dim residential room with 2700K LED, shift dramatically — the cool gray turns lavender, the crisp white turns creamy, the accent that sang goes flat. The mechanism of failure is that the color was validated in a light condition that does not exist in the actual space, and the false signal is that each swatch "looked right" at selection. The defense is mandatory in-situ testing under the specified lighting.

### Ignoring the North-Face Muddy Shift

North-facing rooms receive cool, indirect daylight with a bluish cast that systematically shifts warm colors toward muddy and makes neutrals read cooler than expected. A designer who does not account for this specifies a warm gray that reads cold and flat, or a cheerful yellow that reads greenish and dull. The trap is assuming daylight is daylight, when in fact orientation dramatically changes the spectral content of the daylight entering the room. The defense is to test specifically in north-facing rooms and to choose colors with built-in warmth to compensate for the cool incident light.

### Assuming Two Materials That Match Under One Light Will Match Under Another

The designer matches a paint color to a fabric swatch under the studio's daylight-balanced lamps and specifies both. At installation, under the room's warm LED, the paint reads greenish and the fabric reads pinkish, and the "matched" pair looks mismatched. The mechanism is metamerism — different pigment chemistries producing the same perceived color under one reference light but diverging under another. The false signal is that the match "worked" in the studio. The defense is to test the match under the actual installed lighting and to request physical matches, not numerical ones, for critical pairings.

### Forgetting That Reflected Color Changes the Whole Room

The designer specifies a clean white ceiling, a green feature wall, and neutral furniture, and is surprised that the ceiling and the furniture both take on a greenish cast from the reflected light of the wall. The harm is that the "neutral" elements are no longer neutral in context, and the palette reads off. The trap is thinking of each surface color in isolation rather than as a contributor to the ambient color of the room. The defense is to test the full palette together in the actual space and to anticipate major reflective contributions from large colored surfaces.

### Choosing Colors That Look Good New but Age Poorly Together

A high-contrast palette of bright white and deep navy looks striking on installation, but the white yellows with age and UV while the navy stays relatively stable, and in two years the crisp contrast is gone, replaced by a yellowed-off-white against navy that reads tired. The mechanism of failure is that the designer optimized for the day-one photograph rather than for the year-five reality, and the materials age at different rates. The defense is to ask how each element will look after aging and soiling, and to choose palettes where the aging is either graceful or synchronized.

## Self-Check

- Did I define the lighting (color temperature, CRI, and where critical the TM-30 data) before or concurrently with specifying colors?
- Did I test key colors on large sample boards in the actual space, at the hours of peak use, before specifying?
- Did I identify materials that must match and test them together under the actual lighting to catch metamerism?
- Did I account for the orientation of the room (north, south, east, west) and the resulting daylight color shift?
- Did I map the major reflective surfaces and predict their color contribution to adjacent elements and to skin tones?
- Did I specify high-CRI (90+) sources for spaces where color rendering matters?
- Did I consider how each material will age, soil, and shift over five years, and choose colors that age gracefully together?
