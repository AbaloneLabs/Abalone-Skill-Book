# Room Acoustics and Reverberation Control

Room acoustics is the behavior of sound within a single enclosed space—how long sound persists (reverberation), how evenly it distributes, and how clearly speech or music is perceived. It is distinct from sound isolation (keeping sound between spaces) and from mechanical noise control. The judgment problem is that designers conflate "acoustics" with "add absorptive panels," when in fact the correct treatment depends on the room's use, volume, and shape, and the wrong treatment degrades the space as much as none. A highly absorptive restaurant becomes a dead, uncomfortable room; a reflective classroom destroys speech intelligibility. The right acoustical treatment is use-specific and quantitatively derived, not a generic ceiling tile selection.

## Core Rules

### Define the Acoustical Goal by Room Use Before Selecting Treatment

Different uses require different acoustical environments. Classrooms and conference rooms require low reverberation and high speech intelligibility (reverberation time, RT60, below 0.6 to 0.8 seconds). Restaurants require moderate absorption to reduce clatter and raise speech privacy between tables, but not so much that the room feels dead. Auditoriums and concert halls require a longer, carefully shaped reverberation (1.5 to 2.2 seconds) that supports music. Open offices require absorption to reduce distraction and masking to increase privacy. Define the acoustical goal—intelligibility, privacy, musical support, or noise reduction—as a measurable target before selecting materials, because the target determines the treatment type and quantity.

### Calculate Reverberation Time from Room Volume and Absorption

Reverberation time (RT60) is the time for sound to decay by 60 decibels, and it is the primary metric for room acoustics. Estimate it using the Sabine equation: RT60 = 0.05 × V / A, where V is room volume in cubic feet and A is total absorption in sabins. Total absorption is the sum of each surface area times its absorption coefficient at the relevant frequency. Calculate RT60 at 500 and 1000 Hz (or octave bands) and compare to the target for the use. Do not guess; the calculation reveals whether the room has too much or too little absorption and where to add it. For classrooms, ANSI S12.60 specifies maximum RT60 of 0.6 seconds; for many assembly spaces, design standards or codes specify targets.

### Distribute Absorptive Materials Strategically, Not Uniformly

Where absorption is placed matters as much as how much. For speech intelligibility, place absorption on the ceiling (the primary reflecting surface for seated listeners) and on the upper rear walls to prevent late reflections and echo. For music, preserve some reflective surfaces to support early reflections and reverberation. Avoid placing all absorption on one surface, which creates an unbalanced field. In offices, a highly absorptive ceiling (NRC 0.70 or higher) over the open plan is the primary treatment; wall panels address specific reflections. Distribute absorption to control the problematic reflections identified by geometry and use, not to achieve a uniform absorption coefficient.

### Address Reflection, Echo, and Flutter with Geometry and Treatment

Hard, parallel surfaces create flutter echo—a repeating reflection that smears sound. Concave surfaces focus sound into hot spots and dead zones. Large, hard rear walls create slap-back echo. Identify these geometric problems in the room shape: parallel hard walls, domed or curved ceilings, large unbroken rear walls. Correct flutter with absorptive or diffusive panels on one of the parallel surfaces; correct focusing by avoiding concave geometry or treating it with absorption or diffusion; correct slap-back with absorption on the rear wall. Geometry and treatment work together; a well-shaped room needs less treatment, and a poorly shaped room cannot be fully corrected by treatment alone.

### Coordinate Acoustical Treatment with Lighting, HVAC, and Structure

Acoustical treatment occupies surfaces and volumes that other systems also claim. A suspended acoustical ceiling competes with lighting, sprinklers, HVAC diffusers, and the plenum. Wall panels compete with art, switches, and wainscoting. Baffles and clouds compete with lighting and structure. Coordinate the layout so that acoustical treatment is not displaced by other systems: confirm the ceiling grid layout allows continuous absorptive tile, confirm wall panel locations are not blocked by furniture or art, confirm baffles do not block lighting. The acoustical design must be integrated into the reflected ceiling plan and the elevation drawings, not added as a note.

### Verify Speech Intelligibility and Privacy Metrics for Critical Spaces

For classrooms, conference rooms, and open offices, verify speech intelligibility or privacy metrics, not just RT60. Speech Transmission Index (STI) or Articulation Loss of Consonants (%ALcons) measure intelligibility directly; Privacy Index or Articulation Index measure privacy. In open offices, ASTM E1130 and E1110 provide metrics and methods. These metrics account for background noise (from HVAC and activity) as well as reverberation, because intelligibility depends on signal-to-noise ratio. A room with good RT60 but a noisy HVAC system has poor intelligibility. Coordinate the acoustical treatment with the mechanical noise control and the activity noise to meet the intelligibility or privacy target.

## Common Traps

### Applying Uniform Absorption Without Analyzing the Room's Use

The trap is specifying absorptive ceiling tile throughout, assuming more absorption is always better. The mechanism of harm is that a restaurant or hospitality lounge becomes acoustically dead and uncomfortable, or a music room loses the reverberation that supports the performance. The false signal is that the room is "acoustically treated." The discipline is to define the acoustical goal by use and to calculate the required absorption, not to apply a generic treatment.

### Ignoring the Effect of Furnishings and Occupants on Absorption

Furnishings, finishes, and occupants contribute absorption that is often omitted from the calculation. The trap is calculating absorption from architectural surfaces alone and over-treating the room, because the occupied and furnished condition has more absorption than the empty condition. The mechanism of harm is a room that is too dead when occupied, or a design that adds unnecessary absorption at cost. The discipline is to include furnishings and occupants in the Sabine calculation, using standard absorption coefficients, and to evaluate both the empty and occupied conditions.

### Treating Only the Ceiling and Ignoring Wall Reflections

The ceiling is the primary absorptive surface, but in rooms with hard walls and high ceilings, wall reflections dominate the late sound field and cause echo and intelligibility loss. The trap is treating the ceiling and assuming the acoustics are addressed. The mechanism of harm is a room that still has echo and poor intelligibility despite a fully absorptive ceiling, because the wall reflections are untreated. The discipline is to analyze the reflection paths and to treat walls where late reflections reach listeners.

### Confusing Reverberation Control with Sound Isolation

Reverberation control (within a room) and sound isolation (between rooms) are different problems with different solutions. The trap is specifying absorptive panels to solve a sound isolation problem—the panels reduce reverberation within the room but do almost nothing to stop sound transmitting to the adjacent room. The mechanism of harm is a client complaint that "we added acoustical panels and we still hear everything next door," because isolation requires mass, airtight construction, and structural decoupling, not absorption. The discipline is to diagnose whether the problem is reverberation or isolation and to apply the correct solution.

### Assuming an Acoustical Ceiling Tile's NRC Rating Tells the Whole Story

NRC (Noise Reduction Coefficient) is an average of absorption at 250, 500, 1000, and 2000 Hz, but rooms often have problems at other frequencies. The trap is selecting tile by NRC alone. The mechanism of harm is that a tile with good NRC absorbs speech frequencies but not low-frequency HVAC rumble, or vice versa, and the room's specific problem persists. The discipline is to review the absorption coefficients at octave bands relevant to the problem (speech, music, mechanical noise) and to select materials accordingly.

## Self-Check

- Is the acoustical goal defined as a measurable target (RT60, STI, Privacy Index) appropriate to the room's use, before treatment selection?
- Is reverberation time calculated from room volume and absorption (Sabine equation) at relevant frequencies, including the contribution of furnishings and occupants?
- Is absorption distributed strategically to control the identified reflections, rather than applied uniformly?
- Have geometric problems (parallel hard walls, concave surfaces, large rear walls) been identified and corrected with treatment or diffusion?
- Is the acoustical treatment coordinated with lighting, HVAC, sprinklers, and structure on the reflected ceiling plan and elevations?
- For critical spaces, have speech intelligibility or privacy metrics been verified, accounting for background noise as well as reverberation?
- Has the problem been correctly diagnosed as reverberation control (within-room) versus sound isolation (between-room), with the correct solution applied?
