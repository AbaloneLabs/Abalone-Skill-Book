# HVAC and Mechanical Noise Control in Interiors

Mechanical systems are the dominant source of background noise in most interior spaces, and that background noise governs speech intelligibility, speech privacy, concentration, and comfort. The judgment problem is that mechanical noise control is invisible on the drawings—it appears as notes and coordination, not as a visible design element—and it is often treated as the mechanical engineer's responsibility, when in fact the interior designer sets the acoustical targets, locates noise-sensitive spaces, and coordinates the architectural conditions that determine whether the mechanical design succeeds. A conference room with a quiet STC wall and a noisy diffuser is not a private conference room; a bedroom below a rooftop unit is not a restful bedroom. Mechanical noise control must be designed, not assumed.

## Core Rules

### Set Background Noise Targets (NC or RC) by Space Use

Background noise from mechanical systems is rated by Noise Criterion (NC) or Room Criterion (RC) curves, which describe the allowable noise level across frequency bands. Set the NC or RC target by space use at the start of design: NC 25 to 30 for concert halls and recording studios; NC 30 to 35 for private offices, conference rooms, and bedrooms; NC 35 to 40 for open offices and classrooms; NC 40 to 45 for lobbies and retail. Document the target in the design criteria and in the mechanical performance specification, so the mechanical engineer designs to it. Without a stated target, the engineer defaults to thermal and spatial efficiency, which often produces NC levels 5 to 15 points above the acoustical ideal.

### Locate Mechanical Equipment Away from Noise-Sensitive Spaces

The most effective noise control is source-location. Locate air handlers, rooftop units, pumps, chillers, and fan-coil units away from bedrooms, conference rooms, classrooms, and other quiet spaces. Do not place a rooftop unit above a bedroom, a fan-coil unit in a conference room ceiling, or a pump room adjacent to a private office. Where proximity is unavoidable, specify vibration isolation, massive enclosures, and duct silencers. Coordinate equipment location with the mechanical engineer and the structural engineer early, because relocation after design development is costly. The interior designer must advocate for the acoustical location during space planning, not accept the engineer's default location.

### Control Air Velocity in Ducts and at Diffusers to Reduce Turbulent Noise

Air moving through ducts and diffusers generates turbulent noise that increases with velocity. Specify maximum duct velocities for the acoustical target: low velocity (800 to 1200 fpm in main ducts, 400 to 600 fpm at terminals) for quiet spaces like conference rooms and bedrooms; moderate velocity for offices; higher velocity for industrial or retail. Specify low-noise diffusers and registers with published NC ratings, and select them so that their self-noise is below the room NC target. Coordinate with the mechanical engineer, who may size ducts for thermal efficiency and pressure drop without regard to velocity noise. The interior designer must state the velocity and diffuser-noise limits in the mechanical performance specification.

### Specify Vibration Isolation for Equipment and Structure-Borne Paths

Mechanical equipment transmits vibration into the structure, which radiates as noise in remote spaces—sometimes far from the equipment. Specify vibration isolation for all rotating equipment: spring isolators for air handlers and pumps (with a static deflection appropriate to the equipment and the structure), neoprene pads for small fans and pumps, and flexible connectors for ductwork and piping connected to the equipment. Coordinate the isolation with the structural engineer, because the structure must support the equipment and the isolators, and because lightweight structures may require special isolation. Structure-borne noise is difficult to correct after construction, so specify it during design and verify it during installation.

### Address Duct Crosstalk and Plenum Returns Between Spaces

Ducts and plenum returns connect spaces acoustically as well as thermally, transmitting speech and noise between rooms via the ductwork or the shared ceiling plenum. Duct crosstalk occurs when a single duct serves two rooms with short, unlined runs between them; plenum return occurs when air returns through a shared ceiling plenum rather than dedicated ducts. Address duct crosstalk with lined ducts, duct silencers, or separate duct runs to noise-sensitive spaces; address plenum return by using dedicated return ducts to sensitive spaces or by adding transfer ducts with lined interiors. A conference room with a plenum return to an open office has no speech privacy, regardless of the wall STC. Identify return paths in the mechanical design and coordinate acoustical treatment.

### Coordinate Acoustical Requirements with the Mechanical Engineer in Writing

Mechanical noise control is a shared responsibility, and the interface between the interior designer's acoustical targets and the engineer's mechanical design must be documented. Provide the mechanical engineer with the NC or RC targets, the velocity limits, the vibration isolation requirements, and the noise-sensitive adjacencies in writing, as design criteria. Review the mechanical design against these criteria at schematic design, design development, and construction documents. Do not assume the engineer will meet unstated acoustical requirements; state them, review for compliance, and resolve conflicts before construction. The specification is the control document: include the acoustical performance requirements in the mechanical sections.

## Common Traps

### Omitting a Stated NC or RC Target and Assuming the Engineer Will Design Quietly

The trap is that mechanical engineers design to thermal and spatial efficiency, not acoustics, and without a stated target the resulting NC level is often 5 to 15 points above ideal. The mechanism of harm is a space that is thermally comfortable but acoustically uncomfortable—too loud for conversation, too noisy for concentration, or too disruptive for sleep. The discipline is to set and document the NC or RC target in the design criteria and the mechanical specification.

### Placing Rooftop or Closet Equipment Above or Adjacent to Quiet Spaces

The trap is that equipment location is chosen for duct routing efficiency or structural convenience, placing a rooftop unit above a bedroom or a fan-coil closet next to a conference room. The mechanism of harm is structure-borne vibration and airborne noise that dominate the space's background noise, exceeding the NC target and making the space unusable for its intended purpose. The discipline is to locate equipment away from quiet spaces during space planning and to coordinate the location with the mechanical and structural engineers.

### Sizing Ducts and Diffusers for Thermal Efficiency Without Velocity Limits

The trap is that the mechanical engineer sizes ducts for the lowest cost and pressure drop, resulting in high velocities and turbulent noise at diffusers. The mechanism of harm is a whistling, rumbling diffuser that exceeds the NC target and is distracting or annoying. The discipline is to specify maximum duct velocities and diffuser NC ratings in the mechanical performance specification and to review the mechanical design for compliance.

### Ignoring Plenum Return and Duct Crosstalk Between Spaces

The trap is that the wall between two rooms is rated for STC, so the designer believes speech privacy is achieved. The mechanism of harm is that sound transmits via the shared ceiling plenum or the duct, bypassing the wall entirely, and privacy is lost. The discipline is to identify return paths in the mechanical design and to specify dedicated returns, transfer ducts, or lined ducts for noise-sensitive spaces.

### Treating Mechanical Noise Control as the Engineer's Sole Responsibility

The trap is that the interior designer assumes the mechanical engineer handles acoustics, and the engineer assumes the designer handles it, so no one owns it. The mechanism of harm is that acoustical targets are never stated, never reviewed, and never met, and the result is discovered at occupancy. The discipline is for the interior designer to set the acoustical criteria, communicate them in writing, review the mechanical design, and document the requirements in the specification.

## Self-Check

- Is an NC or RC background noise target documented in the design criteria and mechanical specification for each space, appropriate to its use?
- Is mechanical equipment located away from noise-sensitive spaces, with vibration isolation specified where proximity is unavoidable?
- Are maximum duct velocities and diffuser NC ratings specified in the mechanical performance specification, and has the mechanical design been reviewed for compliance?
- Is vibration isolation specified for all rotating equipment, coordinated with the structural engineer?
- Have duct crosstalk and plenum return paths been identified and treated with lined ducts, silencers, or dedicated returns for noise-sensitive spaces?
- Are the acoustical requirements communicated to the mechanical engineer in writing and documented in the mechanical specification sections?
- Has the mechanical design been reviewed against the acoustical criteria at each design phase, with conflicts resolved before construction?
