# Sound Isolation and Noise Control Between Spaces

Sound isolation is the prevention of sound transmission between spaces, and it is governed by mass, structural discontinuity, and airtightness. It is the most common acoustical complaint in residential, hospitality, and office design: "I can hear my neighbor." The judgment problem is that sound isolation fails not in the specified assembly but in the details: a high-STC wall defeated by a back-to-back electrical outlet, a rated floor assembly defeated by a rigid partition connection, a quiet room defeated by a noisy diffuser. Designers who specify a Sound Transmission Class (STC) rating and stop there produce assemblies that test well in the lab and fail in the field, because field performance depends on flanking paths, penetrations, and construction quality that the rating does not capture.

## Core Rules

### Select the Assembly by Required STC or IIC, Not by Familiarity

Sound Transmission Class (STC) rates airborne sound isolation of walls and floors; Impact Insulation Class (IIC) rates impact sound isolation of floor-ceiling assemblies. Select the assembly to meet the required rating for the adjacency: residential unit-to-unit typically requires STC 50 to 60 (code minimum 45, FHA, or stricter local); office-to-office requires STC 45 to 55 for speech privacy; hospitality requires STC 50 to 55 plus IIC 50 to 60. Use published tested assemblies (ASTM E90 lab data) and select the one that meets the rating with margin, because field performance (ASTM E336) runs 5 to 10 points below lab ratings. Do not assume a familiar assembly meets the rating; verify the test report.

### Detail for Airtightness and Eliminate Gaps

Air is the medium of sound, so an airtight assembly is an acoustical assembly. Every gap, crack, and penetration leaks sound disproportionately: a 1 percent gap in a wall can reduce its effective STC by several points. Seal all penetrations with acoustical sealant, stagger electrical boxes on opposite sides of the wall by at least 24 inches and place them in different stud cavities, avoid back-to-back boxes, and seal around boxes. Do not run plumbing or ductwork through a rated wall between units without acoustical treatment. Detail the head-of-wall, base-of-wall, and perimeter joints with sealant or gaskets. Airtightness is the detail that separates a rated assembly from a leaky one.

### Eliminate Flanking Paths That Bypass the Assembly

Flanking is sound transmission via paths other than the direct assembly: through a continuous exterior wall, a continuous ceiling plenum, a structural connection, a corridor, or a window. Flanking often dominates the actual sound transmission, so even a perfect wall fails if the flanking path is untreated. Identify flanking paths in the design: continuous exterior curtain walls that bridge between units, shared corridor ceilings, structural slabs that transmit impact, continuous soffits. Address each with structural discontinuity (resilient channels, isolation clips, acoustical expansion joints), continuous construction breaks, or additional layers. A field STC test (ASTM E336) measures the composite performance including flanking, so design for the field rating, not the lab rating.

### Address Impact Noise with Discontinuous Floor Construction

Impact noise (footsteps, dropped objects, chair movement) is rated by IIC and is the dominant complaint in multifamily and hospitality floor-ceiling assemblies. The most effective control is structural discontinuity: a concrete slab with a floated floor (a gypsum or concrete topping on a resilient isolation mat) or a wood frame assembly with resilient channels or isolation clips and multiple layers of gypsum. Carpet with pad adds IIC points; hard surface flooring reduces them. A common failure is installing hard surface flooring (wood, tile, LVT) over a wood frame assembly without an underlayment rated for impact, which drops IIC below code minimums. Specify the floor-ceiling assembly as a system, including the underlayment, and verify the IIC test report for the specific assembly.

### Control Mechanical and Structure-Borne Noise at the Source

Mechanical equipment—air handlers, pumps, chillers, fans—is a major noise source, and structure-borne noise transmits through the building via rigid connections. Specify vibration isolation for equipment (spring isolators, neoprene pads) and flexible connectors for ductwork and piping. Locate equipment away from noise-sensitive spaces; do not place a rooftop unit above a bedroom or conference room. Size ductwork and diffusers for low air velocity to reduce turbulent noise, and add duct liner or silencers where required. Coordinate mechanical noise control with the mechanical engineer, because the engineer optimizes for thermal performance and may not account for acoustical velocity limits. Background noise from mechanical systems is measured in NC or RC ratings; confirm the design meets the NC target for the space use.

### Coordinate Sound Isolation with Fire-Rating and Structure

Sound-isolating assemblies are often also fire-rated assemblies and structural elements, and the three requirements interact. A resilient channel that improves STC may not be compatible with a fire-rated assembly unless the combined assembly is tested. Additional gypsum layers that improve STC add weight that affects structure. Penetrations that are fire-stopped may still leak sound unless acoustically sealed. Coordinate the assembly so that it meets all three requirements with a single tested configuration, and document the assembly as a system. Do not let the fire-rating detail or the structural detail compromise the acoustical detail without a documented, tested alternative.

## Common Traps

### Specifying STC Without Detailing Penetrations and Flanking

The trap is that an STC rating on a partition schedule seems to guarantee isolation, so the designer moves on. The mechanism of harm is that the field performance is 5 to 15 points below the lab rating because of unsealed penetrations, back-to-back boxes, and flanking paths, and the client complains after move-in. The discipline is to detail every penetration, seal, and flanking path, and to specify the field performance target, not just the lab STC.

### Assuming Absorption Solves Isolation

The trap is adding absorptive panels inside a room to stop sound from reaching the adjacent room. The mechanism of harm is that absorption reduces reverberation within the source room (a small benefit) but does almost nothing to stop sound transmitting through the assembly, which requires mass and discontinuity. The false signal is that the source room sounds quieter. The discipline is to diagnose isolation as a between-room problem requiring mass, airtightness, and decoupling, not an absorption problem.

### Installing Hard Surface Flooring Without an Impact Underlayment

The trap is that hard surface flooring is desirable in residential and hospitality units, so designers specify it over wood-frame or thin concrete assemblies without an underlayment rated for impact. The mechanism of harm is that IIC drops below code minimums and below the client's expectation, and the impact noise (footsteps, chairs) transmits clearly to the unit below. The correction is expensive: removing the flooring and adding an underlayment, or adding a ceiling below. The discipline is to specify the floor-ceiling assembly as a system, including the underlayment, and to verify the IIC test report.

### Ignoring Continuous Exterior Walls and Curtain Walls as Flanking Paths

A demising wall between two units that meets at a continuous exterior curtain wall creates a flanking path: sound travels through the exterior wall from one unit to the other. The trap is that the demising wall is rated, so the designer believes the isolation is achieved. The mechanism of harm is that the continuous exterior wall transmits sound and the field STC is far below the rating. The discipline is to break the exterior wall or to extend the demising wall construction to the structure, sealing all joints, and to evaluate flanking in the design.

### Treating Mechanical Noise as an Afterthought

Mechanical equipment is a major noise source, but designers often assume the mechanical engineer will handle acoustics. The trap is that the engineer optimizes for thermal performance and space, not acoustics, and the result is noisy diffusers, rumbling air handlers, and structure-borne vibration. The mechanism of harm is a space that meets the thermal design but exceeds the NC target and is uncomfortable. The discipline is to coordinate mechanical noise control, specify velocity limits and vibration isolation, and confirm the NC rating as a design requirement.

## Self-Check

- Is the wall, floor, or ceiling assembly selected from a tested source (ASTM E90) to meet the required STC or IIC with margin for field conditions?
- Are all penetrations detailed for airtightness (acoustical sealant, staggered electrical boxes, sealed perimeter joints)?
- Have flanking paths (continuous exterior walls, plenums, corridors, structural connections) been identified and addressed with discontinuity or breaks?
- For floor-ceiling assemblies, is impact noise (IIC) addressed with a discontinuous construction (floated floor, resilient channels, rated underlayment), especially under hard surface flooring?
- Is mechanical equipment vibration-isolated, located away from sensitive spaces, and sized for acoustical velocity limits, with an NC target documented?
- Is the sound-isolating assembly coordinated with fire-rating and structural requirements as a single tested system?
- Is the field performance target (ASTM E336) specified, not just the lab STC, recognizing that flanking and construction quality reduce field ratings?
