# Restoration and Preservation Considerations

Restoration and preservation work on historic interiors is governed by an established ethical and technical framework—most notably the Secretary of the Interior's Standards for the Treatment of Historic Properties—that prioritizes the retention of historic fabric and the reversibility of interventions. The judgment problem is that interior designers, trained to create, often approach historic interiors as renovation projects, replacing and rebuilding rather than preserving and repairing. This destroys the historic fabric that gives the interior its significance and can violate preservation easements, tax credit requirements, or landmark regulations. Restoration and preservation require a different mindset: diagnose before treating, repair before replacing, document before intervening, and coordinate with preservation authorities before acting.

## Core Rules

### Determine the Regulatory and Standards Framework Before Designing

Historic interiors may be subject to federal, state, or local regulation: National Register listing, state or local landmark designation, preservation easements, historic tax credits, or review by a historic district commission. Each framework imposes different standards and review processes. The Secretary of the Interior's Standards define four treatments—preservation, rehabilitation, restoration, and reconstruction—each with different rules for retaining and replacing historic fabric. Determine the applicable framework and treatment before designing, because the framework governs what you can and cannot do. A design that ignores the framework may be rejected by the reviewing authority, requiring redesign, or may proceed and violate the regulation, creating liability.

### Document Existing Conditions and Historic Significance

Before any intervention, document the existing interior: the floor plan, the architectural features (moldings, trim, staircases, fireplaces), the finishes (paint, wallpaper, flooring), and the fixtures. Research the building's history to identify the period of significance—the period that the preservation effort aims to protect—and the character-defining features that contribute to that significance. Documentation includes photography, measured drawings, finish analysis (paint and wallpaper chronology), and material identification. This documentation is the basis for the treatment plan and the record of what exists before intervention. Without documentation, interventions cannot be evaluated for their effect on historic fabric, and lost features cannot be replicated.

### Apply the Standards' Treatment Hierarchy: Retain, Repair, Replace in Kind

The preservation hierarchy prioritizes retention of historic fabric above all. Retain and preserve character-defining features in place; if a feature is deteriorated, repair it rather than replacing it; if repair is not possible, replace in kind (matching the original material, design, and detailing); only if in-kind replacement is not feasible, substitute a compatible material, documented and reversible. This hierarchy is the opposite of renovation, which often defaults to replacement. Evaluate each feature against the hierarchy: is it character-defining? Is it deteriorated? Can it be repaired? Document the evaluation and the decision. The goal is maximum retention of original fabric, because original fabric carries the historic significance that reproductions do not.

### Match Historic Materials, Profiles, and Finishes Exactly

When repair or replacement is required, match the historic material, profile, and finish exactly. Run custom molding profiles to match existing; source matching brick, stone, or wood species; replicate plaster with traditional three-coat work; match paint colors through analysis of the historic finish layers (cross-section microscopy) and the period palette; replicate wallpaper through custom printing or sourcing from historic pattern archives. Do not substitute "similar" materials or profiles, because the difference is visible and erodes the historic character. For features that cannot be exactly matched (extinct wood species, discontinued fixtures), document the substitution and its rationale, and ensure the substitution is reversible.

### Make Modern Interventions Reversible and Visually Compatible

Modern interventions—new mechanical systems, accessibility modifications, structural reinforcement, new uses within the historic shell—must be designed to be reversible and visually compatible. Reversibility means the intervention can be removed without damaging the historic fabric: a new partition on a shimmed curb rather than bolted to the floor; a new duct in a soffit that can be dismantled; a new fixture on a mounting plate that does not penetrate the historic trim. Visual compatibility means the intervention does not obscure, compete with, or falsely imitate the historic character: a new element reads as new (differentiated) but harmonious (compatible) with the historic, per the Standards. Document the intervention's reversibility and compatibility for the reviewing authority and for future stewards.

### Coordinate with Preservation Authorities and Specialists

Preservation work requires coordination with reviewing authorities (historic commissions, SHPOs, landmark staff) and with specialists: preservation architects, architectural historians, conservators, and craftspeople with historic trade skills. Engage the reviewing authority early, before design, to confirm the treatment framework and the review process. Engage specialists for tasks beyond the interior designer's expertise: a conservator for the treatment of historic finishes or artworks, a plaster specialist for ornamental plaster, a woodworker for run moldings. Do not attempt preservation work with general contractors unfamiliar with historic buildings, because standard construction practices can destroy historic fabric. The team's qualifications are part of the preservation plan.

## Common Traps

### Treating a Historic Interior as a Renovation Project

The trap is approaching a historic interior with a renovation mindset—replacing, rebuilding, modernizing—rather than a preservation mindset. The mechanism of harm is the irreversible loss of historic fabric (original moldings, trim, plaster, flooring) that gives the interior its significance, and the violation of preservation standards or regulations. The discipline is to adopt the preservation hierarchy (retain, repair, replace in kind) and to evaluate every feature against it before intervening.

### Intervening Without Documenting Existing Conditions

The trap is beginning demolition or construction without documenting the existing interior, so that features removed or altered cannot be replicated or studied. The mechanism of harm is the loss of the historic record and the inability to evaluate the intervention's effect. The discipline is to photograph, measure, and analyze the existing interior before any intervention, and to archive the documentation.

### Substituting "Similar" Materials Instead of Matching Exactly

The trap is substituting a "similar" material, profile, or finish because the exact match is unavailable or costly. The mechanism of harm is a visible difference that erodes the historic character and that a knowledgeable viewer or reviewing authority identifies as non-compliant. The discipline is to match exactly through custom fabrication, sourcing, or specialist replication, and to document substitutions only when exact matching is infeasible.

### Making Irreversible Interventions in Historic Fabric

The trap is bolting, nailing, or adhering new elements to historic fabric in a way that cannot be removed without damage. The mechanism of harm is permanent damage to the historic fabric, violating the reversibility principle of the Standards. The discipline is to design interventions to be reversible: shimmed, clipped, or mounted on plates that do not penetrate the historic material.

### Proceeding Without Reviewing Authority Approval

The trap is proceeding with design or construction without the reviewing authority's approval, assuming the work is minor or that approval will follow. The mechanism of harm is a stop-work order, a requirement to undo completed work, or the denial of permits and tax credits. The discipline is to engage the reviewing authority early, to confirm the treatment framework and review process, and to obtain written approval before proceeding.

## Self-Check

- Is the regulatory and standards framework (National Register, landmark, easement, tax credit, Secretary's Standards treatment) identified before design begins?
- Are existing conditions documented (photography, measured drawings, finish analysis, material identification) and the period of significance and character-defining features identified?
- Is the preservation hierarchy (retain, repair, replace in kind, substitute reversible) applied to every feature, with decisions documented?
- Are historic materials, profiles, and finishes matched exactly through custom fabrication or specialist replication, with substitutions documented only when infeasible?
- Are modern interventions designed to be reversible and visually compatible (differentiated but harmonious), with the reversibility documented?
- Are reviewing authorities engaged early and specialists (conservators, preservation architects, historic trades) engaged for tasks beyond the interior designer's expertise?
- Is the project team qualified for preservation work, avoiding standard construction practices that destroy historic fabric?
