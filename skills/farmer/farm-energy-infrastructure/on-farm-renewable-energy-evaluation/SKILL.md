# On Farm Renewable Energy Evaluation

On Farm Renewable Energy Evaluation is a farm-management judgment problem inside load assessment, technology and site matching, ownership-versus-lease structure, interconnection and incentive rules, and the tension between long payback and energy cost, resilience, and marketing value. The agent is usually being asked for a recommendation before all local evidence is visible, while utility rates, incentive programs, contractor availability, and the farm's cash position are already moving. The task is not to recite generic renewable-energy advice; it is to decide what evidence is strong enough, what can still wait, and what action would create the least regret if conditions change.

A renewable-energy decision converts advice into capital and field behavior over decades. A technically sound answer can still fail if it ignores the actual load profile and time-of-use, roof or ground structural capacity, shading and orientation, interconnection limits and standby or demand charges, roof warranty and re-roofing timing, the difference between owning and leasing, and the incentive and tax rules that make or break the economics. Use this skill to slow the decision down enough to expose hidden constraints, then make the recommendation practical, auditable, and appropriately conservative when electrical safety, structural integrity, legal compliance, environmental exposure, or major financial loss is involved.

## Core Rules

### Name The Decision, Load, And Objective
Start by stating exactly what decision on farm renewable energy evaluation requires: the load or facility served (irrigation pump, milking parlor, cold storage, greenhouse, shop, residence, or the whole meter); the objective (reduce energy cost, hedge rate increases, gain resilience and backup power, meet a buyer or certification requirement, diversify income through export); and the consequence of doing nothing. Many weak recommendations skip this framing and drift into generic advice. The correct system for offsetting a daytime irrigation load differs from one meant to keep a milking parlor or cold store running through an outage. If the decision is a large capital outlay, tied to a lease or easement on the land, or dependent on an incentive with a sunset, require stronger evidence and clearer ownership before recommending action.

### Size The System To The Real Load And Rate Structure
Renewable economics follow the match between generation and consumption, not the panel nameplate. Start with at least a year of utility bills and, where possible, interval data, and assess the load by season, time of day, and time-of-use or demand-charge structure. A solar array that exports at a low feed-in or net-metering credit while the farm buys power at a high peak rate underperforms one that offsets on-site consumption. For biogas or wind, assess the fuel supply (manure volume and bedding) or the wind resource with measured data, not assumption. Size the system to the load, the rate, and the incentive, and state the assumed capacity factor, degradation rate, and consumption-match explicitly.

### Match The Technology To The Site And The Resource
Technology selection should follow from the resource and the site, not from a contractor's preference. Solar needs unshaded south-facing (in the northern hemisphere) area, suitable roof structure and remaining roof life, or ground area that does not take prime ground out of production; wind needs a measured resource, tower height above turbulence, and setback from neighbors and airspace; biogas needs a steady manure or substrate stream, process heat, and a use or export path for the gas or electricity; biomass needs a fuel supply and handling. Check orientation, shading, structural capacity, soil and foundation conditions, and any aviation, setback, or zoning constraint before recommending a technology. A ground-mounted array on prime farmland, or a wind tower too close to a neighbor, trades one farm asset for another.

### Scrutinize Ownership Versus Lease And Power-Purchase Structures
The deal structure changes the return and the risk as much as the hardware. Compare owning the system (upfront cost, depreciation, tax credit, full benefit and full risk) against a lease or power-purchase agreement (little or no upfront, fixed or escalating per-kWh price, system owned by a third party, often with a long term and an easement or lien on the land). Read the contract for the term, the escalator, the buyout and removal provisions, the production guarantee and what happens if the system underperforms, the insurance and liability assignment, and the effect on selling or financing the land. A lease or PPA that looks cheap per kWh can encumber the property for 20 years and complicate a future sale or succession; an owned system can be a strong asset if the incentives and tax position support it. Do not compare only the monthly payment.

### Verify Interconnection, Incentives, And Tax Treatment Before Counting On Them
The economics of on-farm renewable energy depend on rules that vary by utility, state, and year: net-metering or net-billing terms and caps, interconnection study and cost, standby or demand charges that can erase savings, the investment tax credit and its eligibility and timing, depreciation and the farm's tax appetite, state or utility rebates and their sunset dates, and renewable-energy-certificate ownership. Confirm the interconnection feasibility and queue with the utility, the incentive availability with the program administrator, and the tax treatment with a qualified adviser before relying on them in the payback calculation. An array sized above a net-metering cap, or a tax credit the farm cannot use because it has no tax liability, turns a positive model negative.

### Plan For Resilience Realistically
If the objective includes backup power, specify what renewable energy alone will and will not do: most grid-tied solar inverters shut down in an outage for safety unless the system includes battery storage and islanding capability, and battery sizing must match the critical load and the outage duration, not the daily generation. Name the critical loads (milking, water, ventilation, refrigeration), the autonomy required, the fuel for any backup generator, and the transfer and islanding hardware. Do not imply that a grid-tied array provides outage resilience without storage and islanding, because a farmer who relies on that assumption during an outage can lose animals or product.

### Protect Safety, Structure, And Insurance
Renewable-energy guidance should name the electrical and structural safety requirements: licensed electrical work, permit and inspection, rapid-shutdown and labeling, grounding and lightning protection, roof loading and re-roof sequencing (do not mount panels on a roof near end of life), fire access and setback, and notification of the property insurer. Confirm that the installation will be to code, permitted, and insured, and that the roof or foundation can carry the load. An uninsured or unpermitted installation can void coverage and complicate a sale.

### Make The Work Executable By The Farm That Exists
Convert the recommendation into capital, contractor selection, timing, interconnection, incentive paperwork, and operations and maintenance. Check whether the farm has the roof or ground, the tax appetite for credits, the cash or financing, a reputable and licensed installer, and the capacity to monitor and maintain the system. If execution depends on a narrow incentive window or an interconnection queue, name the backup: phased installation, a different ownership structure, or waiting for the next incentive cycle. A plan that assumes instant installation and a perfect incentive is not a plan for a farm.

### Preserve The Evidence Trail
For on farm renewable energy evaluation, record the load data, the system design and specifications, the production estimate and assumptions, the contract and easement terms, the interconnection agreement, the incentive and tax-credit documentation, the permits and inspections, the insurance endorsement, and the ongoing production monitoring. Records protect the farm in performance disputes, incentive recapture, property sale, insurance claims, and tax filing. If the recommendation cannot say what should be recorded, it probably has not defined the decision clearly enough.

## Common Traps

### Sizing To The Panel Instead Of The Load And Rate
A system sized to available roof or ground area, or to a sales target, rather than to the consumption profile and the time-of-use and demand structure, can export at low value and leave demand charges untouched. The trap is optimizing installed capacity instead of bill savings.

### Owning Or Leasing Without Reading The Contract
A lease or power-purchase agreement can lock the land into a 20-year term with an escalator, a removal obligation, and a lien or easement that complicates sale or finance. An owned system can waste a tax credit the farm cannot absorb. The trap is comparing the monthly payment instead of the full contract and tax position.

### Counting Incentives That Are Not Confirmed
Net-metering caps, interconnection costs, standby charges, rebate sunsets, and tax-credit eligibility vary and change. A payback model that assumes incentives without confirming availability and terms can turn positive economics negative after the system is built.

### Implying Resilience Without Storage
A grid-tied array without battery storage and islanding provides no outage protection, yet the resilience claim is often made. A farmer who counts on it during an outage can lose refrigerated product, milk, or animals. The trap is conflating bill savings with backup power.

### Taking Prime Land Or An End-Of-Life Roof
A ground array on prime farmland or a roof array on a roof near replacement trades one asset for another, and a roof that must be replaced under the panels wipes out years of savings. Check land productivity and remaining roof life before siting.

### Treating Local Professional Judgment As Optional
Renewable energy has many decisions where utility tariffs, interconnection rules, zoning, structural loading, incentive law, and tax treatment change the answer. The trap is presenting confident advice when the right next step is to involve a licensed electrician, structural engineer, tax adviser, utility interconnection group, or incentive program administrator.

### Hiding Uncertainty Behind A Confident Tone
Energy decisions often need action under uncertainty, but uncertainty should be named. State what is known, what is inferred, what must be checked locally, and what would change the recommendation. Overconfidence can push a farmer into a long-lived, hard-to-reverse capital commitment.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is the exact on-farm renewable energy decision tied to a specific load, facility, and meter, and a named objective (cost, hedge, resilience, marketing), rather than described as generic advice?
- [ ] Is the system sized to at least a year of real load data and the time-of-use and demand-charge structure, with capacity factor, degradation, and consumption-match stated?
- [ ] Is the technology matched to a verified resource and to site conditions (shading, orientation, structure, soil, zoning, setback)?
- [ ] Does the ownership-versus-lease-or-PPA comparison read the full contract: term, escalator, buyout, removal, production guarantee, insurance, and effect on sale or finance?
- [ ] Are interconnection feasibility, net-metering or net-billing caps, standby charges, tax-credit eligibility, and incentive sunset confirmed with the utility, program, and tax adviser before the payback is relied on?
- [ ] If resilience is an objective, are critical loads, battery sizing, autonomy, islanding hardware, and generator backup specified, without implying outage protection from a grid-tied array alone?
- [ ] Does the safety and insurance plan cover licensed electrical work, permit and inspection, rapid shutdown, grounding, roof or foundation loading, fire access, and insurer notification?
- [ ] Have capital, financing, tax appetite, contractor reputation and licensing, incentive timing, and operations-and-maintenance capacity been tested against the recommendation?
