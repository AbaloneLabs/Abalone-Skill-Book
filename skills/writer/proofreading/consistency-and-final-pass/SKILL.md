# Consistency And Final Pass

The final consistency pass is the last check before publication. Its job is not to improve the prose or catch new ideas, but to verify that the piece is internally consistent and conforms to its required standards. This is where mismatches hide: a term defined one way and used another, a number that contradicts itself across sections, a cross-reference that points to the wrong place, a heading style that shifts halfway through, or a citation format that drifts from the house style.

Agents tend to skip or rush the consistency pass because the prose feels finished and the issues seem trivial. The harm is that inconsistencies are exactly what readers and editors notice in an otherwise polished piece, and they erode trust precisely because they look like carelessness. A reader who finds a number stated two ways, or a term capitalized inconsistently, begins to doubt the rest of the work. The consistency pass is low-glamour but high-credibility.

This skill covers the judgment for the final pass: what categories of consistency to check, how to verify defined terms and cross-references, how to handle numbers and formatting, how to apply house style without strangling voice, and how to know the piece is genuinely ready. The agent has freedom in style choices where no standard is imposed, but must verify every category where a standard exists.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Final Pass As Verification, Not Revision

The final pass is a verification pass, not a revision pass. Its goal is to confirm consistency and conformance, not to improve prose or develop ideas. If you find yourself rewriting sentences or reconsidering structure, you have left the final pass and re-entered revision, which means the piece was not actually ready.

Keep the pass in verification mode:

- check against standards rather than against preferences;
- flag inconsistencies rather than improving style;
- if a substantive problem appears, decide whether to fix it minimally or return to revision;
- do not introduce new material or new phrasings that create fresh inconsistency.

A final pass that turns into revision never finishes cleanly. Verify; do not revise.

### Check Defined Terms And Terminology

Terms defined or introduced in the piece must be used consistently throughout. A term defined as "customer success manager" on first use should not become "account manager" later unless a deliberate distinction is made. Terminology drift confuses readers about whether two terms refer to the same thing.

Check terminology:

- is each key term defined once and used consistently;
- are abbreviations introduced on first use and used thereafter;
- is the same concept named the same way across sections;
- are technical terms used at a consistent level for the audience;
- does any term shift meaning partway through.

Build a quick terminology list from the first uses, then scan the rest against it. Inconsistencies are easy to miss in sequence but obvious when checked against a list.

### Verify Numbers, Dates, And Quantities

Numbers are high-risk because errors and inconsistencies in them directly undermine credibility. A statistic stated one way in the introduction and another in the body, or a date that contradicts a timeline, signals inaccuracy even if the prose is flawless.

Verify every number:

- does each figure match wherever it appears;
- are units consistent (millions versus billions, percentages versus decimals);
- do dates align with any timeline or sequence in the piece;
- are totals and subtotals internally consistent;
- are approximations flagged consistently (about, roughly, approximately).

Cross-check numbers against their source where possible. A number that cannot be traced is a risk.

### Check Cross-References And Internal Pointers

Cross-references, internal pointers (see above, as discussed in Section 3), and links must point to the right place. After any restructuring, references that were correct may now be wrong, pointing to deleted sections or renumbered headings.

Check references:

- do "see above" and "see below" pointers point to the intended content;
- do section or figure numbers match the actual structure;
- do links resolve to the correct destination;
- do references to tables, figures, or appendices match their labels;
- has any restructuring left a reference pointing to deleted or moved material.

Restructuring is the most common cause of broken references. Always re-check them after any structural change.

### Verify Formatting And Visual Consistency

Formatting consistency is part of credibility. Heading styles, list formats, emphasis (bold versus italics), spacing, and indentation should be uniform unless a deliberate distinction is intended. Inconsistent formatting looks careless even when the content is sound.

Check formatting:

- are heading levels styled consistently throughout;
- are lists formatted the same way (bullets versus numbers, punctuation);
- is emphasis used consistently for the same kind of element;
- are block quotes, code blocks, or callouts styled uniformly;
- is spacing and indentation consistent.

A style drift partway through, such as headings switching from Title Case to sentence case, is a common and visible error.

### Apply House Style Where It Exists

If a house style, style guide, or publication standard applies, the final pass verifies conformance. This includes capitalization rules, hyphenation, number formatting (spell out versus numerals), citation format, and preferred terminology. House style is not optional where it is mandated.

Apply house style by:

- identifying the governing style guide or standards;
- checking capitalization, hyphenation, and number formatting against it;
- verifying citation or reference format conforms;
- checking preferred terminology and spelling (US versus UK, brand names);
- confirming any required elements (abstract, metadata, disclaimers) are present.

Where no house style is imposed, choose a consistent internal standard and apply it uniformly. Internal consistency is the minimum even without an external guide.

### Check Capitalization And Proper Nouns

Capitalization errors are common and visible. Proper nouns, titles, headings, and defined terms must be capitalized consistently. A name capitalized one way in one place and another elsewhere looks sloppy.

Check capitalization:

- are proper nouns and brand names spelled and capitalized correctly;
- are headings consistently capitalized (Title Case or sentence case, pick one);
- are defined terms capitalized consistently if that convention is used;
- are job titles, departments, and organizations capitalized per the relevant style;
- is the capitalization consistent with the chosen or house style.

Brand and organization names are especially error-prone because their official capitalization may be non-obvious. Verify against the source.

### Confirm Required Elements And Metadata

The final pass confirms that all required elements are present and correct. Depending on the publication type, this may include a title, subtitle, author attribution, date, abstract, tags, alt text, captions, citations, and disclaimers. Missing elements are a publication failure even when the prose is perfect.

Confirm:

- the title and any subtitle are final and correct;
- author attribution, date, and metadata are accurate;
- abstract, summary, or lede is present if required;
- captions, alt text, and image descriptions are present and correct;
- citations, references, and footnotes are complete and formatted;
- any required disclaimers, disclosures, or legal elements are included.

### Do A Fresh Full Read At The End

After all category checks, do one fresh full read of the piece as a reader, not as an editor. This catches anything the category-by-category checks missed: a tonal inconsistency, a leftover placeholder, a duplicated paragraph, or an error that does not fit any single category. The fresh read is the final safety net.

Read for the whole experience, not for any single category. If the fresh read finds nothing, the piece is ready. If it finds something, decide whether it is a quick fix or a reason to return to revision.

## Common Traps

### Turning The Final Pass Into Revision

Starting to improve prose during the consistency pass means the piece was not ready and the pass will not finish cleanly. Verify; do not revise.

### Missing Terminology Drift

A term used one way early and another late confuses readers. Check terminology against a list of first uses rather than trusting memory.

### Numbers That Contradict Across Sections

A figure stated differently in two places destroys credibility. Cross-check every number wherever it appears.

### Broken Cross-References After Restructuring

Any structural change can break pointers and links. Always re-check references after restructuring, even minor moves.

### Formatting Drift Partway Through

Heading style, list format, or emphasis that shifts mid-piece looks careless. Verify formatting is uniform throughout.

### Ignoring House Style

Where a style guide applies, conformance is not optional. Verify capitalization, hyphenation, numbers, and citations against the governing standard.

### Skipping The Fresh Full Read

Category checks miss things that do not fit a single category. A final fresh read as a reader catches leftovers and whole-piece issues.

## Self-Check

Before treating the final consistency pass as complete, verify:

- The pass stayed in verification mode and did not drift into revision.
- Defined terms and terminology are consistent throughout, checked against a first-uses list.
- Every number, date, and quantity is consistent wherever it appears and traceable to a source.
- Cross-references, internal pointers, and links point to the correct, current destinations.
- Formatting, headings, lists, emphasis, spacing, and indentation are uniform throughout.
- House style or the chosen internal standard is applied consistently, including capitalization and hyphenation.
- Proper nouns, brand names, and titles are spelled and capitalized correctly per the relevant style.
- All required elements, title, metadata, captions, alt text, citations, disclaimers, are present and correct.
- No leftover placeholders, duplicated paragraphs, or editing artifacts remain; a fresh full read as a reader found no remaining inconsistencies or whole-piece problems
- The piece conforms to its required standards without having its voice strangled by over-application; the draft is internally consistent and externally conformant, and ready for publication
