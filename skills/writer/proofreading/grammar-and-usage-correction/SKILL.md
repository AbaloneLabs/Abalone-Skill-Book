# Grammar And Usage Correction

Grammar and usage correction is the proofreading-adjacent pass that fixes errors in agreement, tense, pronoun reference, parallel structure, and word usage. Unlike typo detection, which hunts for surface mistakes, this pass checks whether the sentences are grammatically sound and whether words are used correctly. The difficulty is not finding the errors alone; it is correcting them without over-correcting into stiff, over-formal prose that loses the writer's voice.

Agents tend to fail in one of two directions. Some under-correct, leaving agreement errors and misused words that undermine credibility. Others over-correct, applying prescriptive rules mechanically and turning lively prose into lifeless, formally correct but wooden sentences. The harm of under-correction is visible error that readers notice. The harm of over-correction is prose that is grammatically defensible but drained of rhythm, voice, and naturalness, which readers experience as dull even if they cannot name why.

This skill covers the judgment for grammar and usage correction: how to find the real errors that matter, how to distinguish genuine errors from acceptable variation, how to correct without stiffening the prose, and how to handle contested usage where authorities disagree. The agent has freedom in style choices, but must correct errors that impede clarity or credibility while preserving the prose's naturalness.

## Core Rules

### Correct For Clarity And Credibility, Not For Pedantry

The purpose of grammar correction is to serve the reader: clarity, credibility, and smooth reading. It is not to enforce every prescriptive rule regardless of effect. Some rules, when applied mechanically, make prose worse. Judge each correction by whether it helps the reader, not by whether it satisfies a rule.

Before correcting, ask:

- does the error impede clarity or cause misreading;
- does the error undermine the writer's credibility with this audience;
- is the "rule" a genuine standard or a contested preference;
- would the correction improve the sentence or merely make it more formal.

Correct errors that affect clarity or credibility. Leave acceptable variation alone. The reader is the standard, not the rulebook.

### Fix Agreement Errors Reliably

Agreement errors, subject-verb, pronoun-antecedent, and number, are among the most visible and credibility-damaging mistakes. They are also easy to miss because the subject and verb are often separated by intervening words. Check agreement deliberately.

Common agreement problems:

- subject and verb separated by a phrase that distracts from the true subject;
- collective nouns that can be singular or plural depending on meaning;
- indefinite pronouns (each, every, none) that take singular verbs;
- pronouns whose antecedent is unclear or plural when the pronoun is singular;
- compound subjects joined by or/nor where the verb matches the nearer subject.

For each verb and pronoun, identify the true subject or antecedent and confirm the match. Intervening phrases are the most common cause of agreement errors.

### Check Tense Consistency And Sequence

Tense errors often arise from drifting between tenses within a passage or mishandling the sequence of tenses in complex sentences. The reader experiences tense inconsistency as confusion about when things happened.

Check:

- is the narrative or analytical tense consistent within a passage;
- do shifts in tense reflect real shifts in time, or accidental drift;
- in reported speech or thought, is the tense sequence correct;
- are perfect tenses used correctly to show prior action.

Unintentional tense shifts are common in drafts that move between narration and commentary. Confirm each passage holds a consistent temporal frame unless a deliberate shift is intended.

### Resolve Pronoun Reference Ambiguity

Pronouns are efficient, but they cause errors when their antecedent is unclear, missing, or ambiguous. The reader who cannot tell what a pronoun refers to has to backtrack, which breaks flow. Pronoun reference is a clarity issue as much as a grammar issue.

Check each pronoun:

- is there a clear, single antecedent;
- could the pronoun refer to more than one noun;
- does the pronoun refer to a noun that appears only in the writer's mind, not the text;
- does "this" or "that" refer to a whole previous sentence or idea rather than a noun.

When reference is ambiguous, either repeat the noun or restructure so the antecedent is clear. Vague "this" and "that" (this means that...) often need a noun added (this approach means that...).

### Maintain Parallel Structure

Parallel structure requires that items in a series, pairs, or lists share the same grammatical form. Broken parallelism is a common error that makes sentences feel off even when the reader cannot name the problem. It is especially common in lists, headings, and bulleted series.

Check parallelism in:

- items in a list or series (all nouns, all verbs, all gerunds, all infinitives);
- paired conjunctions (both X and Y, either X or Y, not only X but also Y);
- headings and bullets within a single list;
- comparisons (as X as Y, more X than Y).

When items do not match in form, revise so they do. Parallel structure is invisible when correct and jarring when broken.

### Correct Usage Without Over-Correcting

Usage errors involve using a word in a way that is wrong or contested: less versus fewer, between versus among, which versus that, who versus whom, lay versus lie, affect versus effect. Some of these are clear errors; others are contested or changing. Correct the clear errors, but do not impose contested preferences where the common usage is acceptable.

Approach contested usage by:

- checking whether the usage genuinely confuses readers or merely annoys pedants;
- considering the audience and register, formal prose may warrant the traditional form;
- preferring clarity over rule-following when the two conflict;
- not "correcting" a usage that is widely accepted just because a prescriptive rule forbids it.

The goal is prose that readers trust, not prose that satisfies every disputed rule.

### Preserve Voice And Naturalness When Correcting

Grammar correction can stiffen prose when applied mechanically. A sentence corrected into formal correctness may lose the rhythm or directness that made it work. Always read the corrected sentence in context and confirm it still sounds natural.

Guard against over-correction by:

- not converting contractions to full forms unless the register demands it;
- not replacing who with whom where whom sounds stilted to the audience;
- not splitting every split infinitive if the unsplit version reads worse;
- not turning active, direct sentences into passive, formal ones to satisfy a rule.

A correction that makes the sentence grammatically perfect but lifeless is a bad trade. Correct toward clarity and credibility while keeping the prose alive.

### Match Correction Depth To Register

The level of grammatical formality should match the register. Formal prose, academic, legal, official, warrants stricter adherence to traditional grammar. Conversational or creative prose can use sentence fragments, contractions, and informal structures where they serve voice and rhythm.

Calibrate correction to register:

- formal prose: apply traditional grammar strictly;
- general nonfiction: apply standard grammar but allow natural contractions and directness;
- conversational or creative prose: allow deliberate fragments and informal usage that serves voice.

Do not impose formal-register rules on conversational prose, or conversational looseness on formal prose. Match the correction to the register the piece requires.

## Common Traps

### Over-Correcting Into Stiffness

Applying every prescriptive rule mechanically produces formally correct but lifeless prose. Correct toward clarity and credibility, not toward maximum formality.

### Leaving Agreement Errors Hidden By Intervening Words

Subject-verb agreement errors often hide because phrases separate the subject and verb. Check each verb against its true subject, ignoring intervening words.

### Drifting Tense

Unintentional shifts between past and present, or mishandled tense sequence, confuse the reader about timing. Hold a consistent tense within each passage.

### Ambiguous Pronoun Reference

Pronouns without a clear antecedent force the reader to backtrack. Make every pronoun's reference unambiguous.

### Broken Parallelism

Items in a list or pair that do not share grammatical form feel wrong to the reader. Revise for parallel structure, especially in lists and headings.

### Imposing Contested Usage Preferences

Some usage rules are disputed or changing. Correcting widely accepted usage to satisfy a disputed rule annoys readers without aiding clarity. Correct clear errors; leave acceptable variation.

### Mismatching Correction To Register

Applying formal-grammar rules to conversational prose, or conversational looseness to formal prose, misfits the register. Calibrate correction to the piece's level.

## Self-Check

Before treating the grammar and usage pass as complete, verify:

- Each correction serves clarity or credibility, not mere rule-following.
- Subject-verb and pronoun-antecedent agreement was checked, including cases with intervening phrases.
- Tense is consistent within each passage, with shifts only where deliberately intended.
- Every pronoun has a clear, single antecedent, and vague this/that references were resolved.
- Parallel structure holds in all lists, pairs, headings, and comparisons.
- Clear usage errors were corrected, while contested preferences were not imposed where common usage is acceptable.
- Corrections preserved the prose's voice and naturalness rather than stiffening it.
- Correction depth matched the register, formal for formal prose, natural for conversational.
- No contraction, fragment, or informal structure was removed where it served voice and register; the corrected sentences were read in context and still sound alive, not wooden
- No agreement, tense, or reference error remains that a careful reader would notice; the prose is grammatically sound without having been over-corrected into lifelessness
