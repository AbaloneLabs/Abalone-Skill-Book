# Error And Typo Detection

Proofreading for typos and mechanical errors is the last line of defense before a piece reaches the reader. It is a different task from revision or line editing: its only job is to catch errors that survived every earlier pass. The central difficulty is that the brain corrects errors automatically as it reads for meaning, so a normal read-through finds almost nothing. Effective proofreading requires methods that defeat the brain's auto-correction and force attention onto the surface of the text.

Agents tend to treat proofreading as a quick final read and then declare the draft clean. The harm is that obvious errors ship: transposed letters, doubled words, missing small words, homophone confusions, and broken formatting that the writer's eye glided over a dozen times. These errors do not usually destroy meaning, but they damage credibility, because readers interpret them as evidence of carelessness about the whole piece.

This skill covers the judgment for effective error detection: how to manufacture the fresh eyes that catch typos, which techniques actually surface errors versus which feel thorough but find little, how to handle the proofreading pass without contaminating it with revision, and how to know when the pass is genuinely complete. The agent has freedom in technique, but must use at least one method that disrupts normal reading.

## Core Rules

### Proofread Last, After All Revision Is Done

Proofreading is the final pass. If any revision, line editing, or restructuring remains, proofreading is premature, because those changes will introduce new errors that the proofreading pass will not catch. Proofread only when the text is otherwise frozen.

The discipline is to resist proofreading earlier. Catching a typo mid-revision feels productive, but it does not substitute for a dedicated final pass, and it distracts from the revision work. Note errors you notice during revision, but save the systematic proofread for the end.

### Defeat The Brain's Auto-Correction

The reason normal reading misses typos is that the brain reads for meaning and silently corrects surface errors. You see "the" even when the text says "teh"; you skip doubled words like "the the"; you supply missing small words. Effective proofreading uses methods that disrupt this automatic correction and force attention onto the surface.

Methods that defeat auto-correction:

- read the text backward, word by word, so meaning cannot carry you along;
- read each sentence in isolation, covering the surrounding text;
- change the font, size, or format so the text looks unfamiliar;
- print the draft and mark errors by hand;
- read aloud slowly, including every word and punctuation mark;
- use text-to-speech to hear the text at speech pace.

No single method catches everything. Combine at least two, because different methods surface different error types.

### Read At Proofreading Speed, Not Reading Speed

Proofreading requires a much slower pace than normal reading. At reading speed, the brain skims and auto-corrects. At proofreading speed, you examine each word and mark. The most common reason a proofread finds nothing is that it was done too fast.

Slow down deliberately:

- point at each word with a cursor or finger;
- pause at each sentence boundary;
- read punctuation as well as words;
- stop at anything that looks slightly off and examine it.

If you can read the draft at normal speed during a proofread, you are not proofreading; you are reading.

### Use Multiple Techniques For Different Error Types

Different errors hide in different ways and need different techniques. A single method, however good, leaves blind spots. Layer techniques to cover the range.

Technique-to-error mapping:

- reading backward: catches spelling errors and doubled words;
- reading aloud: catches missing words, broken rhythm, and homophone confusions;
- format change: catches formatting errors and inconsistent spacing;
- isolation: catches errors hidden by context;
- text-to-speech: catches errors the eye forgives but the ear catches.

Run the pass with at least two techniques, ideally targeting the error types most likely in this draft.

### Proofread In A Single, Focused Mode

Proofreading requires a different attention from revision. Do not mix proofreading with line editing or restructuring during the same pass. If you start rewriting a sentence, you leave proofreading mode and enter revision mode, and the proofread becomes incomplete.

Rules for focused proofreading:

- mark errors; do not fix them immediately if fixing breaks focus;
- if you notice a substantive problem, note it for a revision pass and continue proofreading;
- keep the pass about error detection, not improvement;
- take breaks, because proofreading attention fatigues quickly.

A proofread contaminated by revision is neither a complete proofread nor a complete revision.

### Check The High-Risk Error Zones

Some parts of a text carry more risk than others. Errors in titles, headings, names, numbers, and the first and last sentences do disproportionate damage because they are the most visible and the most trusted. Allocate extra attention to these zones.

High-risk zones to check carefully:

- the title and any headings;
- the first and last sentence of the piece;
- proper names, titles, and attributions;
- numbers, dates, and units;
- URLs, email addresses, and links;
- captions and callouts.

An error in a heading undermines the whole piece more than an error buried mid-paragraph. Proofread these as if they were separate, higher-stakes documents.

### Verify Homophones And Common Confusions

Homophones and commonly confused words are the errors most likely to survive spell-check, because they are real words used wrongly. Spell-check will not flag them. They require deliberate attention.

Common confusions to check deliberately:

- their, there, they're; your, you're; its, it's;
- affect, effect; principle, principal; complement, compliment;
- then, than; loose, lose; lead, led;
- words that sound similar and are easily swapped in context.

Scan for these specifically. Because spell-check passes them, only a human (or a targeted check) catches them.

### Check Formatting And Mechanical Elements

Proofreading includes mechanical and formatting elements, not just words. Broken formatting signals carelessness as much as a typo does. Check the elements that are easy to overlook when reading for words.

Mechanical checks:

- consistent heading styles and hierarchy;
- consistent spacing, indentation, and alignment;
- correct and consistent punctuation (dashes, quotes, apostrophes);
- complete and correctly formatted lists;
- no broken links, images, or cross-references;
- consistent capitalization, especially in headings and proper nouns.

## Common Traps

### Proofreading At Reading Speed

A fast proofread finds little because the brain auto-corrects. Proofreading must be slow and deliberate, examining each word.

### Relying On A Single Read-Through

One normal read-through, even a careful one, misses errors that survive auto-correction. Layer multiple techniques to cover different error types.

### Proofreading Before Revision Is Done

Proofreading a draft that will still be revised wastes the pass, because new errors will appear. Proofread only when the text is frozen.

### Mixing Proofreading With Revision

Starting to rewrite during a proofread leaves both tasks incomplete. Mark errors and substantive problems separately; keep the pass focused.

### Trusting Spell-Check Alone

Spell-check catches non-words but passes homophones, doubled words, and correctly spelled wrong words. It is a first filter, not a proofread.

### Skipping The High-Risk Zones

Errors in titles, headings, names, and numbers do disproportionate damage. These zones need extra attention, not less.

### Declaring Clean After One Pass

A single proofread rarely catches everything. Expect to find errors on a second pass with a different technique. Humility about completeness prevents shipping obvious mistakes.

## Self-Check

Before treating the proofreading pass as complete, verify:

- The proofread was done only after all revision and line editing was finished.
- At least one method that defeats auto-correction was used, not just a normal read.
- The pass was done at proofreading speed, examining each word, not reading speed.
- Multiple techniques were layered to cover different error types.
- The pass stayed in proofreading mode and did not drift into revision.
- High-risk zones, title, headings, names, numbers, first and last sentences, were checked with extra care.
- Homophones and commonly confused words were scanned for deliberately.
- Formatting and mechanical elements, spacing, punctuation, lists, links, were checked.
- Doubled words, missing small words, and transposed letters were specifically hunted; the draft was read aloud or played back at speech pace to catch what the eye forgives
- A second pass with a different technique found no new errors; no known error type was left to spell-check alone, since spell-check passes real-word errors
