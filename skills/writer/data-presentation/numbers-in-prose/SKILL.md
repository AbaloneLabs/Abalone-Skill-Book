# Numbers In Prose

Numbers in prose are harder than they look. A figure dropped into a sentence without context is meaningless; a figure precise to four decimals is unreadable; a figure rounded the wrong way distorts the truth; a figure without a unit invites catastrophic misreading. Writers often treat numbers as self-explanatory because they feel objective, but a number only communicates when the writer has done the work of scaling, rounding, contextualizing, and presenting it in a form the reader can actually hold. Otherwise the number sits on the page, precise and inert, persuading no one of anything.

Presenting numbers is a judgment problem because the right form depends on the reader, the argument, and the precision the claim requires. The same figure might need to be exact in a technical report, rounded in an essay, and scaled to a human rate in a narrative. An agent that pastes figures in their raw form tends to produce prose that is either numbingly precise or deceptively rounded, and that fails to make the data mean anything to the reader. The craft is translating numbers into prose that communicates their significance without distorting their value.

Use this skill when incorporating figures into prose, when deciding how to round or scale, when contextualizing a number for readers, or when choosing between numerals and spelled-out forms. The goal is prose in which every number is legible, meaningful, and honest, carrying exactly the weight the argument needs.

## Core Rules

### Contextualize Every Number

A number without context is a factoid: technically true, rhetorically empty. "The company served 400,000 customers" means nothing until the reader knows whether that is large or small, growing or shrinking, typical or exceptional. Context is what turns a number into meaning, and providing it is the writer's job, not the reader's. Never present a figure without giving the reader a way to judge its significance.

Contextualize by:

- providing a baseline, prior value, or benchmark for comparison;
- giving the denominator or rate that puts the raw number in proportion;
- noting the trend or direction if the figure is a point in time;
- comparing to a familiar reference that gives the magnitude shape.

"The company served 400,000 customers, twice the figure of three years ago and roughly the population of Minneapolis" communicates; "The company served 400,000 customers" does not. The context is not optional decoration; it is the meaning.

### Round To The Precision The Argument Requires

Precision is not always virtue. A figure precise beyond what the argument needs, or beyond what the reader can grasp, obscures rather than clarifies. Round to the level of precision that serves the claim, and note the rounding where it matters. The goal is the tightest representation that preserves the meaning, not the most decimal places the source offered.

Round by:

- matching precision to the claim: "about 12 percent" when the argument needs approximation, "12.3 percent" only when the tenths matter;
- rounding large figures to readable forms, such as 1.2 million rather than 1,234,567;
- avoiding false precision that implies rigor the source does not have;
- noting when a figure is an estimate or approximation rather than a measurement.

Never round in a direction that changes the claim. Rounding for readability is honest; rounding to make a figure look larger or smaller than it is, is distortion. The test is whether the rounded figure supports the same claim as the precise one.

### Scale Numbers To Human Terms

Large numbers are abstract; human-scale numbers are graspable. A national figure in the billions becomes comprehensible when scaled to a per-person or per-household rate; an annual total becomes vivid as a daily or hourly figure. Scaling translates magnitude into terms the reader can picture, which is what makes the number do work in the argument.

Scale by:

- converting totals to per-capita, per-household, or per-unit rates where comparison matters;
- breaking annual figures into daily, hourly, or per-minute equivalents for vividness;
- comparing aggregates to familiar quantities, such as populations or distances;
- choosing the scale that makes the magnitude legible without exaggerating it.

Scaling is a judgment, not a formula. The right scale is the one that lets the reader understand the magnitude honestly; the wrong scale can minimize or inflate. Test whether the scaled figure gives an accurate impression of the original.

### Choose Numerals Or Spelled-Out Forms Consistently

The choice between numerals and spelled-out numbers affects readability, and conventions exist to keep prose consistent. Small whole numbers are often spelled out in prose; larger numbers and all measurements use numerals; numbers beginning a sentence are spelled out. Follow a consistent convention so the reader is not distracted by shifting forms.

Apply consistently:

- spell out small whole numbers in non-technical prose, following your style guide's threshold;
- use numerals for larger numbers, measurements, percentages, and dates;
- never begin a sentence with a numeral; reword or spell it out;
- keep forms consistent within a sentence and paragraph, not mixed arbitrarily.

Consistency matters more than the specific rule chosen. A paragraph that spells out "seven" but uses "42" in the next clause reads as careless. Pick a convention and apply it throughout, or follow the style guide the piece uses.

### State The Unit And Basis Explicitly

A number without a unit is ambiguous, and ambiguity in figures can be catastrophic. "400,000" could be people, dollars, units, or households; "12" could be percent, points, or thousands. State the unit explicitly, and confirm the basis, such as the year, the population, or the measurement method, so the reader interprets the figure correctly.

State explicitly:

- the unit: people, dollars, households, tons, percent, percentage points;
- the basis: the year, the population, the geographic scope;
- the currency and whether figures are adjusted for inflation across time comparisons;
- whether a percentage is a share of a total or a change from a prior value.

Confusing percent with percentage points, or nominal with inflation-adjusted dollars, are common and serious errors. Make the unit and basis unambiguous so the reader cannot misinterpret the figure.

### Make Numbers Serve The Sentence, Not Dominate It

Prose choked with figures becomes a list, not an argument. Numbers should appear inside sentences that carry meaning, not as a sequence of data points the reader must assemble. Integrate figures into the grammar of your claims, and avoid stringing statistics together without connective interpretation. The number should serve the sentence; the sentence should not be a delivery vehicle for the number.

Integrate by:

- embedding figures inside claims, not appending them: "Revenue, which fell 14 percent, has not recovered";
- following figures with interpretation, not another figure;
- limiting the density of numbers per sentence so the reader can absorb each;
- using prose to state the meaning, with the figure as support.

A sentence with three statistics and no interpretation is harder to read and less persuasive than a sentence with one statistic and a clear claim. Density defeats comprehension; select the figure that matters and let the prose do the rest.

### Preserve Honesty Through Presentation

How a number is presented can persuade beyond what it supports, and the writer is responsible for the impression the presentation creates. A figure framed to shock, a comparison chosen to flatter, or a precision that implies false certainty all distort through presentation even when the underlying number is correct. Present numbers so the reader's impression matches the data's honest meaning.

Preserve honesty by:

- avoiding framing that exaggerates, such as "a staggering" before a modest figure;
- choosing comparisons that illuminate rather than flatter the number;
- not implying certainty through precision the source does not warrant;
- presenting unfavorable and favorable figures with the same standards.

The line between emphasis and distortion is a judgment. A figure presented to make the reader feel more strongly than the data warrants is a form of overclaim, and a careful reader will notice the mismatch between the number and the rhetoric around it.

## Common Traps

### The Number Without Context

A bare figure means nothing without a baseline or comparison. Contextualize so the reader can judge significance.

### Precision That Obscures

Figures precise beyond the argument's need, or beyond the source's rigor, confuse and imply false certainty. Round to the precision that serves the claim.

### Magnitude The Reader Cannot Picture

Large aggregates are abstract; scale to per-person or per-time rates to make them graspable.

### Missing Or Ambiguous Units

A number without a clear unit and basis invites misreading. State units, basis, currency, and adjustment explicitly.

### Density That Defeats Comprehension

Stringing statistics without interpretation turns prose into a list. Integrate figures into claims and follow them with meaning.

### Framing That Persuades Beyond The Data

Adjectives and comparisons that exaggerate a figure's significance distort through presentation. Match the rhetoric to what the number actually shows.

## Self-Check

Before treating the presentation of numbers in prose as complete, verify:

- Every figure is contextualized with a baseline, comparison, or rate that makes it meaningful.
- Numbers are rounded to the precision the argument requires, without distorting the claim.
- Large figures are scaled to human terms the reader can picture.
- Numerals and spelled-out forms follow a consistent convention throughout.
- Units, basis, currency, and inflation adjustment are stated explicitly where relevant.
- Percent is not confused with percentage points, nor nominal with adjusted figures.
- Numbers are integrated into sentences rather than strung together as a list.
- Each figure is followed by interpretation, not merely another figure.
- Framing and adjectives do not exaggerate a figure's significance beyond what it shows; precision in the prose does not exceed what the source actually supports
- No figure is rounded in a direction that changes the claim it supports; a reader encountering each number would understand both its value and its significance
