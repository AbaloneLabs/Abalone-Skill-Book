# Quotation And Paraphrase Craft

Representing another writer's words is a craft with real stakes. Quote too much and your voice disappears under a parade of other people's sentences; paraphrase carelessly and you distort the source while claiming its authority. The choice between quoting and paraphrasing is not stylistic preference. It is a judgment about what kind of fidelity the argument needs: fidelity to the source's exact language, or fidelity to its meaning rendered in your own. Get this wrong and the reader either drowns in quotation or is misled by paraphrase that has drifted from what the source actually said.

The craft is a judgment problem because both failure modes are invisible to the writer in the moment. A paraphrase that has subtly shifted emphasis feels faithful while you write it; a quotation that runs too long feels thorough rather than bloated. An agent that treats quotation and paraphrase as interchangeable containers for source material tends to produce drafts where quotations are dropped without framing, paraphrases quietly change meaning, and the boundary between the source's thought and the writer's becomes blurred. The result is prose that is technically cited but intellectually unreliable.

Use this skill when drafting or revising any passage that draws on sources, when deciding how to represent a source's words, when writing signal phrases and attributions, or when checking whether a paraphrase is faithful. The goal is to represent each source with the kind of fidelity its role in your argument demands, and to keep the boundary between your voice and the source's clean.

## Core Rules

### Quote When The Wording Itself Is The Evidence

Direct quotation is justified when the source's specific language is doing work your paraphrase cannot do. Quote when the exact words matter: a contested phrase under dispute, a vivid or striking formulation, a primary source whose wording is the historical artifact, a technical definition that must be precise, or a statement whose force depends on its idiom. In these cases, the wording is the evidence, and changing it would lose what makes the source valuable.

Quote when:

- the source's phrasing is vivid, memorable, or rhetorically powerful in a way your prose would flatten;
- the exact wording is under dispute or being analyzed;
- the source is a primary document, law, regulation, or canonical text where wording carries legal or interpretive weight;
- precision is essential and paraphrase would introduce ambiguity;
- the source's voice or persona is itself part of what you are showing.

When none of these apply, paraphrase. Quoting material whose content matters but whose wording does not surrenders your sentence to another voice for no gain and pads the prose. The default is paraphrase; quotation is the exception earned by the nature of the material.

### Paraphrase When The Meaning Is What Matters

Paraphrase is the right choice when you need the source's substance but its specific language adds nothing. Most evidence in most arguments falls into this category: findings, arguments, claims, descriptions, and explanations whose content you need but whose wording is incidental. Paraphrasing lets you compress, integrate into your own sentence structure, and keep your voice in command while still attributing the idea.

A faithful paraphrase must:

- preserve the source's meaning without adding, omitting, or shifting emphasis;
- render the idea in genuinely your own sentence structure and vocabulary, not a light rewording;
- attribute the source clearly so the reader knows the idea is not yours;
- avoid introducing your interpretation as if it were the source's claim.

The danger of paraphrase is drift: small changes in wording that accumulate into a shift in meaning. A paraphrase that softens a source's hedged claim into certainty, or that generalizes a specific finding into a broad one, has become inaccurate even if no individual word is wrong. Check every paraphrase against the source for fidelity of meaning, not just wording.

### Use Signal Phrases That Show The Source's Role

A signal phrase does more than attribute; it tells the reader how the source functions in your argument. "Smith argues," "Smith concedes," "Smith documents," and "Smith speculates" attribute the same source but frame its contribution differently. Choose the verb that accurately captures what the source is doing, so the reader understands the weight to give it.

Useful signal verbs and their force:

- "argues," "contends," "maintains" for positions the source is advancing;
- "finds," "shows," "demonstrates" for evidence the source presents;
- "concedes," "acknowledges," "admits" for points the source grants against its own case;
- "speculates," "suggests," "hypothesizes" for claims the source does not fully establish;
- "documents," "records," "reports" for descriptive or observational material;
- "warns," "cautions" for prescriptive or cautionary claims.

Avoid weak verbs like "says" or "states" that carry no information about the source's role, and avoid verbs that overstate, like "proves" for what a source only suggests. The signal phrase is a precision instrument; calibrate it to what the source actually does.

### Match The Quotation Length To Its Purpose

Quotations should be as long as necessary and as short as possible. A quotation that runs a full paragraph when a single sentence carries the point buries the relevant language under surrounding material. A quotation trimmed so aggressively that it loses its context distorts the source. Match the length to the work the quotation must do.

Length guidance:

- quote a phrase or clause when a specific term or formulation is the point;
- quote a full sentence when the complete thought is needed for accuracy;
- quote a passage only when the surrounding context shapes the meaning or the force depends on development;
- use ellipses to trim irrelevant material, but never in a way that changes the meaning;
- use brackets to clarify or adjust, but never to insert your own claim into the source's words.

Every ellipsis and bracket is a place where meaning can shift. Review each one to confirm it preserves what the source actually said. A quotation edited to say something easier to argue is no longer a quotation.

### Keep The Boundary Between Source And Writer Clean

The reader must always be able to tell which words and ideas are the source's and which are yours. Blurring this boundary, whether through sloppy attribution, unmarked paraphrase, or quotation marks placed inconsistently, undermines the credibility of the whole piece. The boundary is maintained through consistent mechanical conventions and through prose that distinguishes levels of voice.

Maintain the boundary by:

- placing quotation marks around any wording taken directly from the source, even a distinctive phrase;
- attributing every paraphrased idea, not just direct quotations;
- marking where your interpretation begins after a quotation, so the reader knows the source's words have ended;
- using consistent citation format so attributions are unambiguous;
- never letting a source's distinctive phrasing appear in your prose without quotation marks.

Distinctive phrasing is the clearest trap: a memorable line from a source slips into your paraphrase without marks, and the boundary breaks. When in doubt, quote. The cost of an unnecessary quotation mark is far lower than the cost of presenting another's words as your own.

### Summarize When The Gist Suffices

Sometimes you do not need a source's specific claim or wording but only its general conclusion or position. Summarizing compresses a source's overall stance into a sentence or two, letting you cite it for context or background without developing its details. Summary is appropriate when the source is supporting rather than load-bearing.

Summarize when:

- you are establishing the landscape of a debate and need only each source's position;
- a source provides background that the reader needs in outline, not detail;
- multiple sources converge on a point and you can group them rather than quote each;
- the source is cited to show awareness, not to carry an argument.

Summary still requires attribution and fidelity. A summary that distorts a source's position to make it easier to dismiss or easier to support is dishonest compression. Summarize fairly even when summarizing briefly.

### Introduce Context The Reader Needs To Judge The Source

A quotation or paraphrase carries more weight when the reader knows who is speaking and why they should listen. Introduce enough context, such as the source's expertise, stake, or position in a debate, for the reader to assess the claim. A bare name with no context forces the reader to take the source on trust, which informed readers will not do.

Provide context by:

- noting the source's relevant expertise or role when it is not obvious;
- indicating the source's stake or position when it affects their claim;
- situating the source within a debate when their position matters to how the reader weighs it;
- flagging when a source is being cited for its data versus its opinion.

Do not over-contextualize; a brief clause is often enough. But never leave a source as a naked name. The reader's ability to judge the evidence depends on knowing whose evidence it is.

## Common Traps

### Paraphrase That Drifts Into Distortion

Small wording changes accumulate until a hedged claim becomes certain or a specific finding becomes general. Check every paraphrase against the source for fidelity of meaning, not just wording.

### Over-Quoting Material That Should Be Paraphrased

Quoting at length when only the content matters bloats prose and surrenders sentences to other voices. Quote only when the source's language itself is the evidence.

### Weak Or Overstated Signal Verbs

"Says" carries no information about the source's role; "proves" overstates what most sources do. Calibrate the signal verb to what the source actually does.

### Trimming Quotations Until They Change Meaning

Ellipses and brackets that remove qualifying clauses or insert clarifying claims can shift a source's meaning. Review every edit to confirm it preserves what was said.

### Distinctive Phrasing Borrowed Without Marks

A memorable line slipping into paraphrase without quotation marks breaks the boundary between source and writer. When phrasing is distinctive, quote it.

### Summary That Distorts To Serve The Argument

Compressing a source's position to make it easier to dismiss or support is dishonest even when brief. Summarize fairly, especially when summarizing briefly.

## Self-Check

Before treating the quotation and paraphrase craft as complete, verify:

- Direct quotation is reserved for cases where the source's exact wording is the evidence.
- Paraphrase is used when meaning matters more than wording, and each paraphrase preserves the source's meaning without drift.
- Signal phrases use verbs that accurately capture each source's role rather than weak or overstated defaults.
- Quotation length matches purpose, with ellipses and brackets reviewed for preserved meaning.
- The boundary between source wording and your own prose is clean, with distinctive phrasing quoted.
- Every paraphrased idea is attributed, not only direct quotations.
- Summary, where used, represents sources fairly rather than distorting them for convenience.
- Context is provided so the reader can judge each source's authority and stake.
- No quotation is edited in a way that changes what the source actually said; the reader can always tell which words and ideas belong to the source and which to you
- Verbs of attribution do not overstate or understate the source's actual claim; the choice between quoting and paraphrasing follows the nature of the material, not habit or convenience
