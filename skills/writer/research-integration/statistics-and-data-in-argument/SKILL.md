# Statistics And Data In Argument

Numbers feel authoritative, which is exactly why they are dangerous in argument. A statistic dropped into prose carries an aura of proof that the number itself rarely earns. Writers reach for a figure to settle a question, when most figures can only illustrate, suggest scale, or shift a probability. The gap between what a number shows and what a writer claims it shows is where most data-driven arguments fail, and the failure is usually invisible to the writer because the number felt decisive in the moment.

Using statistics responsibly is a judgment problem because the same figure can support radically different claims depending on what it measures, what it omits, and what it is compared against. An agent that treats data as self-evident tends to overclaim, presenting correlation as causation, a sample as a population, or a single metric as a verdict. The reader who knows the difference dismisses the whole argument, and the reader who does not is misled. The craft is making each number do only the work it can actually bear.

Use this skill when incorporating statistics, studies, or quantitative evidence into a draft, when interpreting what a figure shows, when calibrating the strength of a data-backed claim, or when revising prose that leans too hard on numbers. The goal is to use data honestly: to let it carry the weight it can carry and no more, and to make its meaning legible to the reader.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish What A Number Proves From What It Illustrates

Most statistics in argument do one of three things, and confusing them is the central error. A number may prove a narrow factual point, illustrate a pattern or scale, or suggest a relationship worth investigating. Only the first carries the force of proof, and it applies to a much narrower claim than writers assume. A figure showing enrollment fell 14 percent proves that enrollment fell 14 percent; it does not prove why, or that the fall is unusual, or that it will continue.

Separate the functions:

- proof: the number directly establishes a specific, bounded factual claim;
- illustration: the number gives concrete shape or scale to a point made on other grounds;
- suggestion: the number indicates a possible relationship that would need more evidence to establish.

State which function a statistic is performing. If you write "this proves," the number must actually prove; if it only illustrates or suggests, say so. The verb you attach to a number is a claim about its strength, and overstating it is the most common form of statistical dishonesty.

### Calibrate The Claim To The Evidence's Strength

Every statistical claim has a strength, and that strength must match the evidence behind it. A claim of causation requires far more than a claim of correlation; a claim about a population requires more than a sample; a claim of trend requires more than two data points. Match the claim's force to what the evidence can support, and hedge where the evidence falls short.

Calibrate by asking:

- is the claim descriptive, comparative, correlational, or causal, and does the evidence support that level;
- is the figure from a sample, and if so, how representative and how large;
- does the evidence show association, mechanism, or both;
- could the same data support a different conclusion;
- what would a knowledgeable skeptic say is overstated.

Use hedging language calibrated to the gap: "suggests," "is associated with," "appears to," "may," when the evidence is suggestive; "indicates," "supports the view that," when it is stronger; reserve "proves," "demonstrates," "causes," for evidence that genuinely establishes the claim. Hedging is not weakness; it is accuracy. Overclaiming is what makes data-driven arguments collapse under scrutiny.

### Establish The Comparison The Number Needs

A single number rarely means anything on its own. Fourteen percent is large or small only against a comparison: a baseline, a trend, a peer group, a historical norm. Presenting a statistic without the comparison it needs leaves the reader unable to judge whether it matters. The comparison is what turns a number into evidence.

Provide comparison through:

- a baseline showing what the figure is measured against;
- a trend showing direction and rate of change over time;
- a peer or benchmark group showing whether the figure is high, low, or typical;
- a denominator or rate that puts the raw number in proportion.

Without comparison, a statistic is a factoid: technically true, rhetorically useless, and often misleading. Always ask what the reader would need to know to judge whether this number is significant, and provide it. A number without context can imply more than it says.

### Name What The Statistic Measures And What It Omits

Every metric is a reduction. It measures one thing by ignoring others, and the things it ignores can be exactly what determines whether it supports your claim. A test score measures performance on the test, not learning; a revenue figure measures money in, not profit or sustainability; a survey measures what respondents say, not what they do. Naming the metric's scope protects against overclaim.

Clarify measurement by:

- stating exactly what was counted, surveyed, or measured;
- noting what the metric excludes or aggregates;
- flagging when the metric is a proxy for something else;
- acknowledging known weaknesses in how the data was collected.

A statistic whose limitations are named is more persuasive than one presented as seamless, because the reader trusts the writer who understands the data's boundaries. A statistic presented as airtight invites the skeptic to find the seam and dismiss the whole claim.

### Avoid The Correlation To Causation Leap

The most common statistical error in argument is moving from "these two things vary together" to "one causes the other." Correlation can reflect causation, but it can also reflect a common cause, reverse causation, coincidence, or selection bias. Asserting causation from correlation alone is an overclaim that informed readers will catch immediately.

Guard against the leap by:

- asking whether a plausible mechanism connects the two variables;
- checking for confounders that could drive both;
- considering whether the direction of causation could be reversed;
- looking for whether the relationship holds across different contexts;
- citing evidence beyond the correlation, such as experiment or mechanism, before claiming causation.

If only correlational evidence is available, say the variables are associated or related, not that one causes the other. Honest limitation strengthens the argument; a causal claim that the evidence cannot support weakens everything around it.

### Put The Number In Prose The Reader Can Hold

A statistic buried in technical precision can be as useless as one that is vague. Readers cannot hold a number they cannot picture. Translate figures into forms the reader can grasp: round where precision exceeds the argument's need, scale to human terms, and frame the magnitude in concrete reference. The goal is comprehension, not approximation that distorts.

Make numbers graspable by:

- rounding to the precision the argument requires, and noting the rounding;
- scaling large figures to relatable units, such as per-person or per-day rates;
- comparing magnitudes to something familiar ("roughly the population of ...");
- placing the number in a sentence that states its meaning, not just its value.

Balance precision against legibility. A figure precise to four decimals that no one can picture is weaker than a rounded figure the reader actually understands. But never round in a direction that changes the claim; rounding is for comprehension, not for spinning the result.

### Acknowledge Uncertainty And Source Quality

Statistics come with uncertainty, and pretending otherwise is a form of overclaim. A survey has a margin of error; a study has confidence intervals; a model has assumptions. Failing to acknowledge uncertainty presents a fragile finding as robust and invites collapse when a knowledgeable reader checks. Name the uncertainty, and weigh the source's quality.

Acknowledge by:

- noting margins of error or confidence intervals when they affect the claim;
- flagging when a figure is an estimate, a projection, or a model output rather than a measurement;
- indicating the source's methodology quality and independence;
- distinguishing peer-reviewed findings from press releases, advocacy data, or vendor statistics.

A claim that acknowledges its uncertainty is more durable than one that hides it. Readers trust writers who are honest about the limits of their evidence, and they distrust writers who present every figure as settled fact.

## Common Traps

### Treating Illustration As Proof

A number that gives scale or shape to a point is not the same as one that establishes the point. Match the verb to the function; do not let an illustrative figure carry the force of proof.

### The Correlation To Causation Leap

Asserting causation from correlational evidence alone overclaims and is easily demolished. Require mechanism or experimental evidence before claiming causation.

### The Number Without A Comparison

A bare statistic implies more or less than it should because the reader has no baseline. Always provide the comparison that lets the reader judge significance.

### Precision That Obscures Meaning

Figures precise beyond the argument's need, or beyond what the reader can picture, fail to communicate. Round and scale for comprehension without distorting the claim.

### Ignoring What The Metric Omits

A measure of one thing presented as a measure of another overclaims. Name what the statistic actually counts and what it leaves out.

### Hiding Uncertainty To Sound Decisive

Suppressing margins of error or model assumptions makes a fragile finding look robust and collapses under scrutiny. Acknowledge uncertainty to strengthen credibility.

## Self-Check

Before treating the use of statistics as complete, verify:

- Each statistic's function (proof, illustration, suggestion) is stated and matches the claim's force.
- Claims are calibrated to evidence strength, with hedging where the evidence is suggestive rather than conclusive.
- Every key statistic is accompanied by the comparison, baseline, or context needed to judge its significance.
- What each metric measures and omits is named, so the reader is not misled about scope.
- No causal claim rests on correlational evidence alone, without mechanism or experimental support.
- Numbers are presented in a form the reader can picture, with rounding that does not distort the claim.
- Uncertainty, margins of error, and source quality are acknowledged where they affect the argument.
- The verb attached to each number does not overstate what the evidence shows.
- A knowledgeable skeptic would not find the central statistical claims overstated; figures are attributed to identifiable sources with methodology the reader could assess
- The same data could not easily support a contradictory conclusion that you have ignored; precision serves comprehension rather than implying false rigor
