# Weaving Evidence Into Prose

Research only becomes an argument when it is woven into prose. A draft can be thoroughly sourced and still fail, because evidence that is dropped in rather than woven in reads as decoration. The reader meets a string of quotations, a pile of statistics, or a tour of experts, and the writer's own line of thought disappears underneath the material it was meant to support. The piece becomes a collage of other voices with no governing voice, and the reader cannot tell what the writer actually believes or why the evidence matters.

Weaving is a judgment problem because the goal is not to include evidence but to make evidence do work. Each piece of research must earn its place by advancing the argument, and the prose around it must connect the evidence to the claim it supports. An agent that treats integration as insertion tends to produce drafts where sources interrupt the flow, where quotations speak for themselves, and where the relationship between evidence and claim is left for the reader to infer. The result is well-cited but inert.

Use this skill when drafting or revising a researched piece, when moving from notes to prose, when evidence feels pasted in or the argument keeps disappearing behind its sources, or when deciding how much framing a quotation needs. The goal is prose where evidence and argument are inseparable, each source entering only where it carries weight and leaving once its work is done.

## Core Rules

### Make Evidence Serve The Sentence Before It And After It

Every piece of evidence should be anchored to the claim it supports and connected to the claim that follows. Evidence that floats between sentences, with no clear relationship to either side, is a patch. The reader should be able to point to the sentence the evidence proves and the sentence the evidence enables.

Anchor evidence by:

- stating the claim the evidence will support before introducing it;
- presenting the evidence in the most economical form that carries its force;
- explaining or interpreting the evidence immediately after, rather than moving on;
- drawing the implication that connects the evidence to the next move in the argument.

A useful pattern is claim, evidence, interpretation, implication. The interpretation is not optional. A quotation or statistic does not speak for itself; the writer must say what it shows and why it matters. Without interpretation, evidence is merely displayed.

### Choose The Tightest Form That Carries The Meaning

Evidence comes in a range of forms, from the direct quotation to the brief paraphrase to the passing allusion. The form should be the tightest one that preserves the meaning the argument needs. Defaulting to the long quotation when a paraphrase would do, or to the full statistic when a rounded figure would carry the point, bloats the prose and hides the argument under its sources.

Match form to need:

- direct quotation, when the wording itself is the evidence, as with a vivid statement, a contested phrase, or a primary source;
- close paraphrase, when the content matters but the wording does not;
- summary, when the gist of a source is enough and the detail would distract;
- passing reference, when a source is cited for authority without needing its substance developed.

Quoting at length is justified when the source's language is doing work your prose cannot do: it is especially precise, especially vivid, or especially contested. Otherwise, paraphrase and move on. The reader came for your argument, not a relay of others' sentences.

### Frame Every Quotation With Your Own Prose

A quotation dropped into a paragraph without a frame is a gap in the argument. The reader hits the quotation, reads someone else's words, and then the argument resumes as if nothing happened. The quotation must be introduced by prose that sets up what it will show and followed by prose that extracts what it showed.

Frame quotations with:

- a signal phrase that attributes the source and previews its role ("As the lead investigator put it, ...");
- context the reader needs to understand why this source matters here;
- a follow-up sentence that interprets the quotation rather than restating it.

Avoid the "hit and run": a quotation introduced, dropped, and abandoned. The sentences immediately before and after a quotation should be yours, doing the work of connecting the source to your argument. If you cannot write a meaningful sentence after the quotation, the quotation is not earning its place.

### Integrate Sources Into Sentences, Not After Them

The strongest integration weaves a source's substance into the grammar of your own sentence, rather than appending a citation to a complete thought. "Productivity fell 12 percent in the first quarter (Lee, 2023)" integrates the evidence; "A study found something interesting. (Lee, 2023)" does not. The source should appear inside the claim it supports, not as a parenthetical afterthought.

Integration techniques:

- name the source inside the sentence ("Lee's 2023 analysis found a 12 percent drop");
- embed the specific finding as part of your assertion ("Productivity, which fell 12 percent in the first quarter, has not recovered");
- use the source's evidence as the subject of your sentence ("The 12 percent drop Lee documented exceeds every prior estimate").

When a source is woven into the sentence, the citation becomes a courtesy rather than the load-bearing element. The prose reads as argument; the citation confirms the authority. When the source hangs off the end of the sentence, the prose reads as a claim with a footnote, which is weaker.

### Distribute Evidence Across The Argument, Not Concentrated In Patches

Evidence should appear where each claim needs support, distributed across the piece, not banked in a single section labeled "evidence" or "research shows." A draft that front-loads all its sources and then argues without them, or that argues for pages and then dumps its evidence at the end, has not integrated research; it has stored it.

Distribute by:

- matching evidence to claims at the point each claim is made;
- resisting the urge to create a "background" or "literature" section that holds all the sources;
- letting some claims rest on the strength of prior evidence already introduced;
- recognizing when a new claim requires fresh support even late in the piece.

The reader should encounter evidence as a continuous texture, not as a wall in the middle. If a section has no sources, ask whether it is making claims that need them; if a section is nothing but sources, ask whether it is making an argument at all.

### Let The Writer's Voice Remain Audible

A well-integrated piece keeps the writer's voice in command. The sources support, complicate, and illustrate, but the writer's thesis, transitions, and conclusions are the spine. If you remove every quotation and citation, the skeleton of the argument should still be visible and coherent. If removing the sources leaves nothing, the piece has no writer, only a compiler.

Keep voice audible by:

- opening and closing each paragraph with your own claim or implication, not a source's words;
- using sources inside your sentences rather than letting sources carry whole sentences;
- stating your synthesis explicitly rather than implying it through juxtaposition of sources;
- making the transitions between sources your own, showing how they relate.

The test is whether the reader could describe what you argue, not just what you cited. Evidence serves the voice; the voice does not serve the evidence.

### Vary The Rhythm Of Evidence And Argument

Prose that integrates evidence well varies its rhythm. Some sentences make claims; some introduce sources; some interpret; some step back and reflect. Prose that uses the same pattern in every sentence, such as claim-cite-claim-cite, becomes mechanical and fatiguing. Vary the texture so the reader feels movement.

Vary by:

- alternating short evidence-claim sentences with longer interpretive passages;
- placing a vivid quotation against surrounding paraphrase for contrast;
- letting some paragraphs run on argument alone, then grounding the next paragraph in evidence;
- using a striking statistic as a hinge, then developing its meaning.

The rhythm should feel deliberate, not random. Variation prevents the reader from predicting where the next citation will land and tuning out the evidence as background noise.

### Cut Evidence That Does Not Carry Weight

Integration includes the discipline of removal. A source gathered but not load-bearing should not appear in the draft merely because it was found. Every piece of evidence should be doing work that no other evidence is doing; if two sources make the same point, keep the stronger and cut the other. Padding the prose with research dilutes the argument it was meant to support.

Cut when:

- the source merely corroborates a point already established;
- the evidence is interesting but does not advance the current claim;
- the quotation repeats what your paraphrase already said;
- the statistic is precise beyond what the argument requires.

A leaner, more selective use of evidence reads as more authoritative, not less. The reader trusts a writer who uses only what matters over one who deploys everything gathered.

## Common Traps

### The Patchwork Quote Dump

Stringing quotations together with minimal connective prose produces a collage, not an argument. The reader loses the writer's voice under a parade of sources. Frame each quotation, interpret it, and let your argument govern the sequence.

### The Unframed Dropped Quotation

A quotation placed between two sentences with no introduction or follow-up creates a gap the reader must bridge alone. Always bracket quotations with your own prose that sets up and extracts their meaning.

### Evidence Concentrated In One Section

Banking all sources in a background or literature section, then arguing without them, leaves later claims unsupported and earlier claims buried. Distribute evidence to the claims that need it, wherever they fall.

### Over-Quoting What Should Be Paraphrased

Quoting at length when the content, not the wording, is what matters bloats the prose and surrenders the sentence to another voice. Quote only when the source's language itself is the evidence.

### Letting Sources Speak For Themselves

Presenting a quotation or statistic without interpretation assumes the reader will draw the intended conclusion. They often will not. Say what the evidence shows and why it matters.

### Citation As Substitution For Integration

Appending a parenthetical citation to an unsupported claim does not integrate evidence; it tags a claim. The source's substance should appear inside the sentence, not only in the citation.

## Self-Check

Before treating the integration of evidence as complete, verify:

- Every piece of evidence is anchored to a specific claim and followed by interpretation.
- Quotations are framed by your own prose both before and after, with no dropped or abandoned quotes.
- The form of each source (quotation, paraphrase, summary, allusion) is the tightest one that carries the needed meaning.
- Sources are woven into the grammar of sentences rather than appended as afterthoughts.
- Evidence is distributed across the argument at the points claims are made, not banked in one section.
- The writer's voice remains audible and governs the sequence, with the skeleton of the argument visible if sources were removed.
- No quotation is used where a paraphrase would carry the same meaning more economically.
- The rhythm of evidence and argument varies rather than repeating a mechanical claim-cite pattern.
- Evidence that merely corroborates or does not advance a claim has been cut; each source is doing work no other source is doing, with redundancy removed
- Interpretation follows evidence rather than leaving the reader to infer the connection; the reader could describe the argument, not merely list the sources cited
