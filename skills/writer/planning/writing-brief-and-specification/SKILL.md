# Writing Brief And Specification

A brief is the contract a piece of writing must satisfy. Whether the writer is producing it for themselves or receiving it from a client, editor, or stakeholder, the brief defines what the finished piece is supposed to do, for whom, under what constraints, and how success will be judged. Without a clear brief, drafting becomes guesswork: the writer produces something fluent and plausible, only to discover at review that it answered the wrong question, addressed the wrong reader, missed a mandatory constraint, or omitted material a stakeholder considered essential.

The judgment problem is that agents tend to treat briefs as administrative formality rather than as the load-bearing document they are. They accept vague instructions, fill in the blanks with assumptions, and start drafting before the ambiguities are resolved. The result is rework, missed requirements, and friction at the point where fixing direction is most expensive. A brief that leaves the objective fuzzy, the audience undefined, or the success criteria unstated is not a brief; it is an invitation to produce off-target work. The real work is to make the brief specific enough to guide drafting and to treat it as a living contract that is revisited, not filed and forgotten.

Use this skill when preparing to write on commission, when creating a self-directed brief before drafting, when a provided brief is vague or incomplete, when stakeholders disagree about what the piece should do, or when a piece has drifted from its original intent and the brief needs revision. The goal is to make the brief a precise, shared, and revisable agreement that prevents wasted drafts.

## Core Rules

### Define The Objective As A Specific Change, Not A Topic

The core of any brief is the objective: what the finished piece should cause or enable. A topic is not an objective. "Write about onboarding" is a topic; "help new engineers reach their first merged pull request within two weeks" is an objective. The objective should name the change the piece is meant to create in the reader, the organization, or the world.

Specify the objective by asking:

- What should the reader know, feel, decide, or do after reading?
- What problem does the piece solve, and for whom?
- What does success look like in concrete, observable terms?
- What would failure look like, and what causes it?

Write the objective as a single sentence that a stranger could act on. If the objective can only be stated as "cover this topic," the brief is not yet ready to draft from.

### Specify The Audience Precisely Enough To Guide Choices

The brief must define the reader with enough specificity to determine vocabulary, depth, tone, evidence, and length. "General audience" is rarely specific enough. The audience definition should distinguish the primary reader from secondary readers and name what the primary reader already knows, lacks, and cares about.

Capture for the primary reader:

- their role, expertise, and context;
- what they already know and what they misunderstand;
- what pressure or goal brought them to the piece;
- what evidence and tone they will trust;
- how much time and attention they will give;
- what decisions or actions the piece should enable for them.

Secondary readers, such as reviewers, legal, or a broader public, should be named separately with their specific needs, so they can be served deliberately rather than accidentally.

### Fix The Constraints Before Drafting, Not During

Constraints discovered late cause rework. The brief should surface every hard and soft constraint before drafting begins, so the writer can design within them from the start rather than retrofit them afterward.

Constraints to capture:

- length or word count, and whether it is a hard limit or a guide;
- format and structure expectations;
- tone, voice, and register, including house style;
- required sources, citations, or evidence types;
- mandatory inclusions and prohibited content;
- legal, compliance, disclosure, or sensitivity requirements;
- deadline and milestone schedule;
- review and approval chain;
- platform, distribution, and technical requirements.

Distinguish hard constraints, which cannot be violated, from soft constraints, which can be negotiated. When a constraint conflicts with the objective, surface the conflict in the brief rather than silently choosing one over the other.

### State The Success Criteria In Verifiable Terms

A brief is only as good as its definition of done. Success criteria should be specific enough that a reviewer can determine whether the piece meets them without relying on subjective impression. Vague criteria such as "make it engaging" or "ensure quality" produce unresolvable review debates.

Write success criteria that can be checked:

- Does the piece enable the specific reader action named in the objective?
- Does it meet every hard constraint on length, format, sources, and compliance?
- Does it cover every mandatory inclusion and avoid every prohibition?
- Does it match the specified tone and register for the specified audience?
- Does it satisfy the stakeholder and approval requirements?
- Can a reviewer confirm each criterion without guessing the writer's intent?

When stakeholders hold different implicit definitions of success, the brief is where those differences get reconciled into one shared standard.

### Treat The Brief As A Contract With Stakeholders

For commissioned work, the brief is an agreement between the writer and the commissioning party. It should be explicit enough that disagreements at review can be resolved by reference to the brief rather than by renegotiation. A brief that lives only in someone's head is a source of conflict; a brief that is written, shared, and acknowledged is a tool for alignment.

Make the brief contractual by:

- writing it down and circulating it before drafting;
- confirming that all stakeholders agree on objective, audience, and constraints;
- recording decisions where stakeholders disagreed and how they were resolved;
- naming who has authority to request changes and who has final approval;
- defining the scope of revisions the brief includes, and what counts as out of scope.

When a stakeholder requests a change that contradicts the brief, update the brief rather than silently absorbing the change, so the contract stays honest.

### Make A Self-Directed Brief As Rigorous As A Commissioned One

Writers writing for themselves often skip the brief, assuming they know what they want. This is a mistake. A self-directed piece without a written brief drifts just as easily as a commissioned one, because the writer's intentions are no more immune to ambiguity than a client's. Writing the brief forces the same discipline: a specific objective, a defined reader, fixed constraints, and verifiable success criteria.

For self-directed work, the brief also serves a second purpose: it reveals whether the idea is worth writing. If you cannot write a credible brief, the idea may not yet be ready. The brief is a test of the piece's viability before any prose is invested.

### Revise The Brief When The Piece Evolves

Briefs are not immutable. As drafting proceeds, the writer often discovers that the real subject, the right angle, or the achievable scope differs from what was planned. Refusing to update the brief produces a piece that serves an outdated contract. The discipline is to revise the brief deliberately when the piece evolves, rather than letting the draft drift while the brief stays frozen.

Update the brief when:

- drafting reveals the objective was misstated or unachievable;
- the strongest material falls outside the planned scope;
- the audience needs differ from what was assumed;
- a constraint proves incompatible with the objective;
- stakeholders change their requirements.

When the brief changes, communicate the change to anyone relying on the original, and update the objective, scope, and success criteria together so they remain consistent.

### Capture The Brief In A Consistent Shape

A brief should be structured so that nothing important is omitted and so that it can be scanned quickly. Use a consistent set of fields so that writers and stakeholders know where to look for each kind of information.

A workable brief includes:

- the objective, stated as a specific change;
- the primary and secondary audiences;
- the tone, voice, and register;
- the length and format constraints;
- the required and prohibited content;
- the sources and evidence requirements;
- the success criteria, in verifiable terms;
- the deadline, milestones, and approval chain;
- open questions and unresolved decisions.

Leaving a field blank is acceptable temporarily, but every blank is a risk that should be closed before drafting or flagged as a known ambiguity.

## Common Traps

### Accepting A Vague Brief And Filling Gaps With Assumptions

When a brief leaves the objective, audience, or constraints undefined, the writer inevitably fills the gaps with guesses. Those guesses surface as rework at review. Resolve ambiguities in the brief, not in the draft.

### Treating The Brief As Paperwork Rather Than Guidance

A brief written to satisfy process, then ignored during drafting, provides no protection. The brief must be consulted as the piece develops, not filed and forgotten.

### Letting Stakeholders Hold Different Implicit Briefs

When each stakeholder carries a different mental version of the brief, review becomes a collision of unspoken expectations. Write the brief down and confirm shared agreement before drafting.

### Confusing Topic With Objective

A brief that specifies a topic but not the change the piece should create leaves the writer to infer the purpose. Topics do not guide choices; objectives do.

### Refusing To Revise The Brief When The Piece Changes

Holding a frozen brief while the draft evolves produces a piece that satisfies an outdated contract. Update the brief deliberately when the work reveals a better direction.

### Overloading The Brief With Conflicting Objectives

A brief that tries to serve too many objectives, audiences, and stakeholders at once produces a piece that satisfies none of them. Identify the primary objective and reader, and subordinate the rest.

### Leaving Success Criteria Subjective

Criteria such as "make it compelling" cannot be verified and invite endless revision. Define success in terms a reviewer can check against the actual output.

## Self-Check

Before treating the brief as sufficient to draft, verify:

- The objective is stated as a specific change the piece should create, not merely a topic.
- The primary audience is defined precisely enough to determine vocabulary, depth, tone, evidence, and length.
- Every hard and soft constraint is captured, with conflicts between constraints surfaced rather than hidden.
- Success criteria are written in verifiable terms a reviewer can check without guessing.
- The brief has been written down, shared, and acknowledged by all stakeholders for commissioned work.
- For self-directed work, the brief is as rigorous as a commissioned one and has tested whether the idea is ready.
- The brief is treated as revisable, and a process exists for updating it when the piece evolves.
- Open questions and unresolved decisions are flagged rather than silently assumed.
- The brief does not overload the piece with conflicting objectives or too many primary audiences.
- A writer receiving this brief could draft toward it without needing to guess the objective, audience, constraints, or definition of done.
