# Research And Evidence Planning

Research planning is not gathering everything available on a topic. It is deciding, before a single source is opened, which claims in the planned piece actually need support, what kind of support each one needs, where that support will come from, and how much is enough. A piece can be well-written and still fail because its strongest claims float without anchors, or because it drowns in evidence that never connects to a point. The reverse failure is just as common: a writer over-researches out of anxiety, collecting far more than the piece can use, and then either pads the draft or never finishes it.

The judgment problem is that agents tend to treat research as a background task rather than a design decision. They search broadly, bookmark liberally, and assume that more sources mean a stronger piece. But evidence has no value until it is matched to a specific claim at a specific strength. A statistic that proves scale does nothing for a claim about causation. A vivid personal story does nothing for a claim about prevalence. An expert opinion does nothing for a claim that requires measurement. Planning research means mapping each claim to the evidence type it actually requires, then gathering only what the map demands.

Use this skill before drafting, when a piece makes claims that need support, when deciding how much research is enough, when balancing lived experience with external authority, or when planning how evidence will enter and sit inside the prose. The goal is to prevent both under-researched pieces that make unsupported assertions and over-researched pieces that bury their argument under undigested material.

## Core Rules

### Map Claims To Evidence Requirements Before Researching

Before gathering anything, list the claims the piece will make and classify each by the kind of support it demands. Different claim types require different evidence types, and mismatch is the most common research failure.

Classify each claim:

- A factual claim (something happened, something exists) requires verification from a reliable record or primary source.
- A quantitative claim (how many, how often, how much) requires data, a study, or an authoritative statistic with a known method.
- A causal claim (X causes Y) requires evidence of mechanism, controlled comparison, or expert analysis that addresses confounds.
- An evaluative claim (this is good, bad, better, worse) requires criteria, comparison, and often expert or experiential judgment.
- A normative claim (we should do this) requires both evidence about consequences and a stated value or principle.
- An experiential claim (this is what it felt like) requires the writer's honest experience, sometimes corroborated.

Write the claim-evidence map as a list before researching. For each claim, note: what type of claim is this, what evidence type would satisfy a careful reader, and what would happen to the piece if that evidence cannot be found. If a central claim cannot be supported, the piece's thesis may need to change before research begins.

### Match Evidence Quantity To Claim Strength, Not To Available Material

The amount of evidence a claim needs is determined by how strong the claim is and how skeptical the reader will be, not by how much material exists. A modest, well-qualified claim may need one solid source. A sweeping, contrarian, or high-stakes claim needs proportionally more: multiple independent sources, attention to counterexamples, and explicit handling of uncertainty.

Calibrate by asking:

- How surprising or controversial is this claim to the target reader?
- What does the reader lose if the claim is wrong?
- Is the claim universal, typical, or about a specific case?
- Would a single counterexample damage it, or is it statistical?

Do not pad a qualified claim with excess evidence; that buries the point and signals insecurity. Do not under-support a strong claim; that reads as assertion. The goal is enough evidence that a reasonable skeptic is moved, and no more.

### Distinguish Source Types And Their Authority Limits

Not all sources do the same work. A piece that cites only one type of source often looks stronger than it is. Know what each source type can and cannot establish.

Common source types and their limits:

- Primary sources (documents, records, original data) establish what is in the record but not what it means.
- Peer-reviewed studies establish findings under method but may not generalize to the writer's specific claim.
- Expert commentary interprets evidence but carries the expert's assumptions and conflicts of interest.
- Journalism reports events and quotes but varies widely in rigor and may be secondhand.
- Institutional reports aggregate data but reflect institutional priorities.
- Personal experience establishes lived truth but not prevalence or causation.

For load-bearing claims, prefer independent corroboration across source types rather than multiple sources that all derive from the same origin. A claim supported by a study, an expert interpretation, and a primary record is far stronger than one supported by three articles that all cite the same study.

### Balance Personal Experience And External Evidence Deliberately

Many pieces blend the writer's experience with external evidence, and the balance is a design choice, not an accident. Experience gives a piece intimacy, specificity, and credibility of witness. External evidence gives it generalizability, scale, and authority beyond one life. Confusing their roles weakens both.

Decide for the piece, and for each major claim:

- Is this a claim where the writer's experience is the evidence, or where experience illustrates a broader pattern?
- Does the reader need external evidence to trust that the experience is not merely idiosyncratic?
- Is the writer claiming expertise, and if so, is that expertise established or asserted?
- Where experience and evidence seem to conflict, which does the piece privilege and why?

A memoir or personal essay may rest almost entirely on experience, but it should not silently generalize from one life to everyone. An analytical piece may use a personal anecdote as a doorway, but it should not let the anecdote stand as proof. Name the role each kind of evidence plays so the reader can weigh it correctly.

### Plan The Integration Strategy Before Drafting

How evidence enters the prose is as important as whether it exists. Evidence that is dropped in without integration reads as a citation dump; evidence that is woven into the argument reads as reasoning. Decide the integration approach per source during planning, not during drafting.

Integration choices include:

- direct quotation when the source's exact wording matters or carries authority;
- paraphrase when the idea matters more than the wording;
- summary when a body of work must be compressed;
- data presentation when numbers carry the argument;
- narrative use when a source becomes a character or scene;
- footnoted support when the point is made in prose and the source is backup.

Plan which claims will be foregrounded with visible evidence and which will be supported quietly. Not every claim needs a visible citation; over-citing low-stakes claims makes the prose feel defensive. Reserve visible support for claims a skeptic would challenge.

### Set A Research Boundary To Avoid Over-Researching

Research has diminishing returns, and unbounded research is a leading cause of unfinished pieces. Set a stopping condition before beginning: a time limit, a source count, or a completeness criterion tied to the claim-evidence map. Stop when every load-bearing claim has adequate support, even if more material exists.

Define the boundary by asking:

- Once every claim on the map is supported, is further research adding value or feeding anxiety?
- Is the writer researching to avoid the harder work of writing?
- Has research begun to repeat itself, surfacing the same points in different sources?
- Is the piece's deadline compatible with the research scope?

A piece with adequate support and a finished draft beats a piece with exhaustive research and no draft. Treat research as serving the piece, not the writer's sense of thoroughness.

### Plan For Gaps And Counter-Evidence

Strong research planning anticipates what the reader will object to and what the evidence cannot show. Identify the counter-claims, the confounds, and the gaps before drafting, and decide how the piece will handle each. Ignoring counter-evidence does not make it disappear; it makes the piece look naive or dishonest when the reader supplies it themselves.

For each major claim, ask:

- What is the strongest objection a skeptic would raise?
- What evidence exists against this claim, and is it serious?
- What does the evidence not establish that the claim might imply?
- Where are the boundaries of what is known?

Handling gaps honestly, by acknowledging limits or narrowing the claim, usually strengthens a piece more than pretending the gaps do not exist.

## Common Traps

### Researching The Topic Instead Of The Claims

Gathering general background on a topic produces a pile of material with no connection to the piece's specific claims. Topic-level research feels productive but rarely yields the targeted support a claim needs. Research claims, not topics.

### Treating More Sources As Stronger Evidence

Stacking sources does not strengthen a claim if they all derive from the same origin or do not address the claim's actual type. Three articles citing one study are one study. Quantity without independence is an illusion of rigor.

### Using The Wrong Evidence Type For The Claim

A vivid anecdote cannot establish prevalence. A statistic cannot establish what something felt like. An expert opinion cannot establish a measurement. Mismatched evidence feels supportive to the writer but reads as irrelevant or misleading to a careful reader.

### Letting Personal Experience Stand As General Proof

One person's experience is real and valid as experience, but it does not prove a pattern. Generalizing silently from a single life invites the reader to dismiss the whole piece when they find one exception. Either narrow the claim or bring external evidence for the general case.

### Over-Citing To Signal Rigor

Attaching a source to every sentence, including low-stakes and uncontested ones, makes the prose read as defensive and buries the argument. Visible support should appear where a skeptic would challenge, not everywhere as decoration.

### Researching To Avoid Writing

Open-ended research is a common form of procrastination disguised as productivity. If research keeps expanding without producing a draft, the boundary was never set. Define a stopping condition and honor it.

### Ignoring Counter-Evidence Hoping The Reader Will Too

Suppressing or skipping evidence against the claim does not protect the argument; it hands the skeptical reader a reason to distrust the writer. Acknowledging and addressing counter-evidence is usually more persuasive than pretending it does not exist.

## Self-Check

Before treating the research plan as sufficient to draft, verify:

- Every load-bearing claim is classified by type and matched to the evidence type that type requires.
- Evidence quantity is calibrated to claim strength and reader skepticism, not to how much material happens to exist.
- Sources are varied in type and independent in origin where claims are strong or contested.
- The balance between personal experience and external evidence is a deliberate choice, and experience is not silently generalized into proof.
- An integration strategy exists for each source: quote, paraphrase, summary, data, narrative, or footnote.
- A research stopping condition is defined and respected; research serves the piece rather than feeding avoidance.
- The strongest counter-claims and counter-evidence have been identified, and the piece has a plan to address or acknowledge them.
- Gaps in the evidence are named rather than concealed, and claims are narrowed where support is thin.
- No claim depends on the wrong evidence type; no central assertion floats without the support a careful reader would demand.
- The plan could be explained to a skeptical editor in a few sentences: here is what each major claim needs, here is where it comes from, and here is why it is enough.
