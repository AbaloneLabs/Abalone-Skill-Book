# Pre-Draft Feasibility And Resource Assessment

Not every idea worth writing can be written, at least not as first conceived. A piece may require interviews the writer cannot secure, data that is not publicly available, expertise the writer does not have, a word count too short for the argument's complexity, or a deadline too tight for the research required. Assessing feasibility before committing to a draft is the difference between a project that ships and one that stalls at 60 percent when the writer discovers a missing resource they cannot obtain. Feasibility assessment is unglamorous and frequently skipped, because the excitement of an idea feels like enough to carry the project. But an idea without the resources to execute it is not a project; it is a wish, and committing to it produces missed deadlines, compromised quality, or abandoned drafts.

Agents tend to miss that feasibility is a pre-commitment gate, that resource gaps discovered late are more costly than those discovered early, that scope can be adjusted to fit constraints rather than constraints ignored to fit scope, and that abandoning or revising an infeasible project early is a better outcome than shipping a compromised version late. The harm is a writer who commits to a project, invests weeks, and then discovers a fatal resource gap that forces a pivot, a downgrade, or an abandonment that wastes the investment.

Use this skill when assessing whether a writing project is feasible, evaluating resource availability, judging scope against constraints, or deciding whether to proceed, revise, or abandon. The goal is to make a deliberate go-or-no-go decision before investing drafting effort, so that committed projects have the resources they need to succeed.

## Core Rules

### Assess Feasibility Before Committing To The Draft

Feasibility assessment is a pre-commitment gate. Before investing significant drafting effort, evaluate whether the project can be completed to the required quality within the available constraints. This assessment is separate from idea quality: an idea can be excellent and infeasible, or modest and feasible. Both assessments matter, and feasibility must pass before commitment.

Conduct the feasibility assessment by identifying the resources the project requires and checking whether each is available. The assessment should be honest, not optimistic: if a resource is uncertain, treat it as a risk, not as available. Document the assessment and the decision (proceed, proceed with revisions, defer, or abandon) so the reasoning is explicit and can be revisited if circumstances change. A project that begins without a feasibility assessment is a project that may discover its fatal gap at 60 percent completion.

### Identify The Specific Resources Each Project Type Requires

Different types of writing require different resources, and the feasibility assessment must identify the specific resources the project at hand needs. A generic "do I have what I need" is insufficient; the assessment must be specific to the project type.

Identify resources by category. Research access: do you need interviews, archives, datasets, or documents that require permission or payment? Expertise: do you need domain knowledge you do not have, and can you obtain it through research, consultation, or collaboration? Evidence: do you need data, studies, or examples to support claims, and are they available and credible? Time: does the deadline allow for the research, drafting, and revision the project requires? Length: does the word count allow the argument or story to be developed at the required depth? Access to people or places: do you need to observe, visit, or experience something that requires logistics or permission? For each category, identify the specific resource and assess its availability. A project that requires a resource in a category where availability is uncertain or absent is a project with a feasibility risk.

### Judge Scope Against Deadline And Word Count Constraints

Scope and constraints must fit together. A scope that requires deep research and a complex argument cannot fit a short word count or a tight deadline without compromising quality. The feasibility assessment must test whether the scope, as conceived, fits within the deadline and word count, and if not, whether the scope can be adjusted to fit.

Test the fit by estimating the effort each scope element requires: how many interviews, how much research, how many words of argument development, how many revision passes. Compare the total effort to the available time and length. If the scope exceeds the constraints, options include narrowing the scope (fewer subtopics, a sharper angle, a smaller case study), extending the deadline, increasing the word count, or reducing the depth. Do not assume the constraints will stretch to fit the scope; they rarely do. Adjust the scope to fit the constraints, and if the adjusted scope is no longer worth writing, abandon or defer the project.

### Evaluate Research Access Before Promising Evidence-Based Claims

A piece that makes evidence-based claims is only as feasible as the evidence it can access. Promising a data-driven analysis without confirming the data is available, or an interview-based piece without confirming the interviews, is committing to a project whose foundation may not exist.

Before committing, confirm access to the specific evidence the piece requires. For data: is it publicly available, purchasable, or accessible through a source you can approach? For interviews: have the subjects agreed or is agreement likely, and is there a fallback if key subjects decline? For documents or archives: can you obtain access within the timeline? If access is uncertain, either secure it before committing, plan the piece around available evidence (adjusting claims to match what you can support), or abandon the evidence-dependent framing for one that does not require the unavailable resource. Do not draft claims that depend on evidence you have not confirmed you can obtain.

### Assess Expertise Gaps Honestly

A writer does not need to be an expert on a subject to write about it well, but they do need enough expertise to avoid errors, engage seriously with the subject, and add value beyond what a reader could find from a quick search. The feasibility assessment must honestly evaluate the writer's expertise relative to the project's demands.

Identify the expertise the project requires: does it require deep technical knowledge, legal precision, medical accuracy, or domain-specific judgment? Assess the writer's current expertise and the gap. If the gap is bridgeable through research, consultation, or expert review, the project is feasible with those resources planned. If the gap is too large to bridge within the constraints, the project is infeasible as conceived. Options include collaborating with an expert, narrowing the scope to the writer's area of competence, or abandoning the project. Do not commit to a project that requires expertise the writer cannot obtain; the result will contain errors that undermine credibility.

### Plan For Resource Risks And Fallbacks

Even when resources appear available, there is risk: interviews get canceled, data turns out to be incomplete, experts are too busy to review, deadlines get compressed. A feasibility assessment that treats resources as guaranteed is fragile. Plan for risks and fallbacks.

For each critical resource, identify the risk (what could go wrong) and the fallback (what you will do if it does). If a key interview is canceled, is there an alternative source? If the data is incomplete, can the analysis be adjusted? If the expert reviewer is unavailable, is there another path to verification? A project with fallbacks for its critical resources is resilient; one without fallbacks is brittle, and a single resource failure can derail it. If a critical resource has no fallback, that is a feasibility risk that may warrant revising or abandoning the project.

### Decide: Proceed, Revise, Defer, Or Abandon

The feasibility assessment culminates in a decision: proceed as conceived, proceed with revisions, defer until resources are available, or abandon. This decision should be explicit and documented, not implicit.

Proceed when all critical resources are available, the scope fits the constraints, and the risks have fallbacks. Proceed with revisions when the project is feasible with adjustments to scope, angle, or method; document the revisions. Defer when the project is worth doing but a critical resource is temporarily unavailable; set a condition for revisiting. Abandon when the project is infeasible without compromises that would make it not worth doing; the idea may return in a different form when resources change. Treat abandonment not as failure but as a decision that frees resources for feasible projects. A writer who abandons infeasible projects early invests their effort where it produces completed work.

## Common Traps

### Committing To A Project Without Assessing Feasibility

Skipping the feasibility assessment leads to discovering fatal resource gaps late, after significant investment. Assess before committing.

### Treating Uncertain Resources As Available

Optimism about resource availability produces commitments that cannot be met. Treat uncertain resources as risks, not as available, and plan fallbacks.

### Fitting Scope To Constraints By Ignoring Constraints

Assuming the deadline or word count will stretch to fit the scope rarely works. Adjust scope to fit constraints, or adjust constraints if possible.

### Promising Evidence-Based Claims Without Confirming Evidence Access

A piece that depends on unavailable evidence has no foundation. Confirm access to data, interviews, and documents before committing to claims that require them.

### Overestimating Expertise Or Underestimating The Expertise Required

Committing to a project that requires expertise the writer cannot bridge produces errors and undermines credibility. Assess the gap honestly and plan how to bridge it.

### No Fallbacks For Critical Resources

A project whose critical resources have no fallback is brittle. A single resource failure can derail the entire project. Plan fallbacks for each critical resource.

### Treating Abandonment As Failure Rather Than Decision

Holding onto an infeasible project wastes effort that could produce completed work elsewhere. Abandoning early is a decision that frees resources for feasible projects.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has a feasibility assessment been conducted before committing significant drafting effort, identifying required resources and checking availability?
- [ ] Have the specific resources required by the project type been identified by category (research access, expertise, evidence, time, length, access to people or places)?
- [ ] Has the scope been tested against the deadline and word count, with scope adjusted to fit constraints rather than constraints assumed to stretch?
- [ ] Has access to specific evidence (data, interviews, documents) been confirmed before committing to evidence-based claims?
- [ ] Has the writer's expertise been honestly assessed relative to the project's demands, with a plan to bridge gaps through research, consultation, or collaboration?
- [ ] Have risks and fallbacks been identified for each critical resource, so the project is resilient to single-point failures?
- [ ] Has an explicit decision been made and documented: proceed, proceed with revisions, defer, or abandon?
- [ ] If the project was revised or abandoned, is the reasoning documented so the idea can be revisited when resources change?
