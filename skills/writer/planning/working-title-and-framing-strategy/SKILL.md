# Working Title And Framing Strategy

A working title is not a label applied at the end. It is a framing decision made before or during drafting that shapes everything that follows: what the writer includes, what they exclude, what tone they adopt, and what promise they make to the reader. The frame a writer chooses determines whether the reader engages, what they expect, and whether the piece satisfies or betrays that expectation. A piece about the same subject can be framed as an exposé, a how-to, a personal essay, a data analysis, or a polemic, and each frame produces a fundamentally different piece with different obligations. An agent who drafts without a working frame produces content that drifts, because without a frame there is no criterion for what belongs and what does not. An agent who frames dishonestly, promising one thing and delivering another, erodes reader trust regardless of the writing quality.

Agents tend to miss that framing is a pre-draft decision with structural consequences, that a frame creates obligations the piece must meet, that titles set expectations that the content must satisfy, and that a frame chosen too late requires restructuring rather than retitling. The harm is a piece that reads as unfocused because it was never framed, or a piece that disappoints because its title promised more than the content delivers.

Use this skill when choosing a working title, deciding how to frame a piece, testing whether a frame serves the content, or revising a frame as the draft evolves. The goal is to make framing a deliberate decision that aligns the title, the promise, and the content, rather than an afterthought that produces drift or disappointment.

## Core Rules

### Choose A Working Title Before Drafting, Not After

A working title is a drafting tool, not just a publishing artifact. It forces the writer to commit to a frame before they start, which gives every drafting decision a criterion: does this serve the frame? Drafting without a working title produces content that accumulates interesting material without a unifying purpose, because nothing excludes anything.

Write a working title that captures the frame in a single phrase or sentence. It does not need to be the final title; it needs to be specific enough to guide drafting. "Thoughts on productivity" is not a working title; it is a topic. "Why the productivity advice that works for morning people fails everyone else" is a working title, because it commits to an angle, an audience, and a promise. Revise the working title as the draft reveals what the piece is actually about, but always have one. A draft without a working frame is a draft without a spine.

### Understand That The Frame Creates Obligations

A frame is a promise. Framing a piece as a definitive guide creates the obligation to be comprehensive and authoritative. Framing it as a personal essay creates the obligation to be honest and vulnerable. Framing it as a data-driven analysis creates the obligation to present evidence and acknowledge limitations. Framing it as a contrarian take creates the obligation to engage seriously with the mainstream view before overturning it. Each frame carries obligations that the piece must meet, or the reader feels cheated.

Before committing to a frame, identify its obligations. Ask: what does this frame promise the reader? What must the piece deliver to satisfy that promise? What would make the piece feel like it broke the frame's contract? If the writer cannot meet the frame's obligations with the material and expertise available, the frame is wrong. Choose a frame whose obligations the piece can actually fulfill, rather than a more ambitious frame that the content cannot support. A modest frame fully met is better than an ambitious frame half-met.

### Test The Frame Against The Content, Not Just The Subject

A frame must fit the content the writer actually has, not the content they wish they had. A common failure is choosing an ambitious frame based on the subject's potential, then producing content that does not live up to it. The piece promises a deep investigation but delivers surface-level observations. The piece promises a practical guide but delivers vague advice. The gap between frame and content is where reader disappointment lives.

Test the frame against the actual content at multiple stages. After outlining: does the planned content support the frame's promise? After drafting: does the written content deliver what the title implies? Before publishing: would a reader who came for the title feel satisfied by the content? If the answer is no at any stage, either upgrade the content to meet the frame or downgrade the frame to match the content. Do not publish a frame that the content does not support; the mismatch is the first thing the reader notices.

### Match The Frame To The Audience's Entry Point

Different audiences approach the same subject from different entry points, and the frame must meet them where they are. A frame that assumes expertise alienates beginners. A frame that assumes ignorance bores experts. A frame that assumes agreement alienates skeptics. The frame is the reader's first interaction with the piece, and if it does not match their entry point, they leave before the content has a chance.

Identify the target audience's entry point: what do they already know, what do they believe, what do they want, and what would make them engage? Choose a frame that connects to that entry point. For a general audience, frame around a question or tension they already feel. For an expert audience, frame around a specific gap or debate in the field. For a skeptical audience, frame around shared ground before introducing the contrarian claim. The frame is not just about the subject; it is about the relationship between the subject and the reader.

### Use The Frame To Decide What Belongs And What Does Not

The frame is the primary exclusion tool. Once the frame is set, every piece of potential content can be tested against it: does this serve the frame? Material that is interesting but does not serve the frame belongs in a different piece. Without a frame, the writer has no exclusion criterion, and the piece accumulates tangents that dilute its focus.

Apply the frame test ruthlessly during drafting and revision. When a section, anecdote, or argument does not serve the frame, cut it or move it, even if it is well-written. The discipline of exclusion is what produces a focused piece, and the frame is what makes exclusion possible. A piece that includes everything related to the subject serves no reader well; a piece that includes only what serves the frame serves its reader completely.

### Revise The Frame When The Draft Reveals A Better One

Sometimes the drafting process reveals that the piece is actually about something different or more interesting than the original frame. The writer starts with one frame and discovers, through drafting, that the real insight lies elsewhere. This is not a failure; it is the writing process working. But it requires revising the frame, not ignoring the discovery.

When the draft diverges from the frame, assess whether the divergence is a better frame or a tangent. If the draft has discovered a sharper, more interesting angle than the original frame, revise the frame to match and restructure the piece to serve the new frame. If the divergence is a tangent that does not serve any frame, cut it. The key is to keep the frame and the content aligned: when one changes, the other must change too. A piece whose frame and content have drifted apart reads as unfocused, regardless of the quality of either.

### Distinguish The Working Frame From The Published Title

The working frame guides drafting; the published title attracts readers. They serve different purposes and may look different. The working frame can be long, specific, and internal, because its job is to guide the writer. The published title must be concise, compelling, and honest, because its job is to attract the reader and set accurate expectations.

When moving from working frame to published title, preserve the frame's promise while making the title attractive. Do not let the pursuit of a compelling title distort the frame's honesty. A clickbait title that promises more than the content delivers may attract clicks but loses trust. A title that accurately reflects the content but is dull may not attract readers. Aim for a title that is both honest and compelling, and when they conflict, prioritize honesty, because a trusted reader returns and a clicked-and-disappointed reader does not.

## Common Traps

### Drafting Without A Working Title Or Frame

Drafting without a frame produces unfocused content that accumulates material without a unifying purpose. Always have a working title before or early in drafting.

### Choosing A Frame Whose Obligations The Content Cannot Meet

An ambitious frame creates obligations the content cannot fulfill, producing disappointment. Choose a frame whose obligations the available material and expertise can support.

### Keeping The Frame When The Draft Has Found A Better One

When drafting reveals a sharper angle, revise the frame rather than forcing the content back into the original frame. Keep frame and content aligned.

### Promising More Than The Content Delivers

A title or frame that promises more than the content delivers attracts attention but loses trust. Test the frame against the content before publishing.

### Forgetting That The Frame Is An Exclusion Tool

Without a frame, there is no criterion for exclusion, and the piece accumulates tangents. Use the frame to decide what does not belong.

### Mismatching The Frame To The Audience's Entry Point

A frame that does not meet the audience where they are, in knowledge, belief, or desire, loses them before the content begins. Match the frame to the entry point.

### Letting Click Appeal Override Frame Honesty

A compelling but dishonest title may win clicks but loses returning readers. When appeal and honesty conflict, prioritize honesty.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is there a working title or frame committed before or early in drafting, specific enough to guide what belongs and what does not?
- [ ] Have the frame's obligations been identified, and can the available material and expertise meet them?
- [ ] Has the frame been tested against the actual content at outline, draft, and pre-publish stages to confirm the content delivers the frame's promise?
- [ ] Does the frame match the target audience's entry point in knowledge, belief, and desire?
- [ ] Has the frame been used as an exclusion tool, with material that does not serve it cut or moved?
- [ ] If the draft diverged from the original frame, has the frame been revised to match the discovered angle, or has the divergence been cut as a tangent?
- [ ] Does the published title preserve the frame's promise while being compelling, with honesty prioritized when appeal and honesty conflict?
- [ ] Would a reader who came for the title feel satisfied by the content, or would they feel the frame promised something the piece did not deliver?
