# Title Meta And Snippet Craft

The title is the first thing a reader sees, and often the only thing they see before deciding whether to read further. In search results, social feeds, and link previews, the title and the snippet stand alone, competing for attention among many alternatives. A reader decides in seconds whether the piece is worth their time, based on a title, a one-line description, and a URL. Writing these elements as afterthoughts, after the body is finished, wastes the work the body took to produce. Titles and snippets are not labels; they are promises, and they determine whether the promise ever reaches a reader.

The judgment problem is that a title serves two masters. It must attract discovery, signaling to search algorithms and scanners what the piece is about, and it must promise honestly what the piece delivers. The two pressures pull toward opposite failures. Chasing the click produces titles that overpromise and bodies that underdeliver, eroding trust once the reader arrives. Chasing accuracy produces titles so dry that no one clicks, leaving good content unread. The discipline is a title and snippet that are both discoverable and honest, that earn the click by promising exactly what the piece provides. This skill prevents the harm of clickbait and the waste of undiscovered content.

Use this skill when writing titles, meta descriptions, snippets, social cards, or link previews; when balancing search optimization with honesty; or when revising titles that underperform. The agent has freedom to write compelling titles, but must never promise in the title what the body does not deliver.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Title As A Promise, Not A Label

A title is not a category for the piece; it is a contract with the reader. It tells the reader what they will get in exchange for their attention. A title that promises more than the piece delivers betrays the reader; a title that promises less undersells the piece. The right title names the value the piece actually provides, in words the reader recognizes.

Craft the title as a promise by asking:

- What will the reader have after reading this piece?
- Does the title state that value accurately?
- Is the promise specific enough to be meaningful?
- Can the body deliver what the title implies?

If the body cannot deliver the title's promise, change the title or change the body. A title and body that disagree destroy trust.

### Write For Both The Reader And The Search

A title must work for the human deciding whether to click and for the algorithm deciding whether to show the piece. The two are not enemies: a title that clearly names the topic and value serves both. The failure is optimizing for one at the expense of the other. Pure SEO titles read as robotic; pure literary titles may be invisible to search.

Serve both by:

- including the primary keyword where it reads naturally;
- leading with the value or topic the reader is looking for;
- keeping the title clear before it is clever;
- placing the most important words early, before truncation cuts them;
- ensuring the title makes sense out of context, as in a search result.

Clarity serves readers and search together. Cleverness that obscures clarity serves neither.

### Front-Load The Important Words

In search results and link previews, titles are often truncated, especially on mobile. Words near the end may never be seen. Front-loading the most important words, the topic, and the value ensures the reader gets the essential meaning even if the title is cut. This is not keyword stuffing; it is prioritizing the information the reader needs to decide.

Front-load by:

- putting the primary topic or keyword near the start;
- placing brand or less essential information later;
- keeping the title short enough that truncation is less likely;
- ensuring the first few words make sense on their own;
- avoiding leading with filler ("The ultimate guide to...") that pushes value away.

If the title is cut after five words, those five words should still convey the piece's subject.

### Write Meta Descriptions That Earn The Click Honestly

The meta description is the snippet of text that appears under the title in search results. It is a sales pitch in miniature: it must convince the reader that the piece is worth their time. The temptation is to overpromise, to write a description more compelling than the piece. The discipline is a description that is compelling and accurate, that earns the click by representing the piece truthfully.

Write honest meta descriptions by:

- summarizing what the reader will actually get;
- including the primary keyword naturally;
- stating the value or benefit clearly;
- avoiding hype that the body cannot support;
- keeping within length limits so the description is not truncated mid-thought.

A description that earns a click through honesty brings a reader who stays. A description that earns a click through hype brings a reader who leaves.

### Match The Snippet To The Intent

The snippet, whether the meta description or the text the search engine extracts, should match the intent behind the reader's search. A reader with an informational intent wants to see that the piece will explain; a reader with a transactional intent wants to see that the piece will help them act. A snippet that signals the wrong intent attracts the wrong reader or fails the right one.

Match snippet to intent by:

- confirming the piece addresses the reader's likely goal;
- using language that signals the content type ("guide," "comparison," "steps");
- reflecting the depth and angle the reader expects;
- avoiding a snippet that implies a different scope than the piece covers.

The snippet is the reader's first evidence of whether the piece is for them. Make it accurate.

### Respect Length And Truncation Realities

Titles and snippets have practical length limits determined by where they appear. Search engines truncate titles around a certain character count; meta descriptions are cut after a similar limit; social previews have their own constraints. Writing without regard for these limits means the reader sees a cut-off version that may lose its meaning. Design for the truncated form.

Design for truncation by:

- keeping titles within common display limits;
- front-loading essential meaning so cuts are survivable;
- writing meta descriptions that work even if slightly truncated;
- checking how the title and description appear in actual previews;
- not relying on words at the end to carry the meaning.

The form the reader actually sees is the truncated form. Write for that form.

### Avoid Clickbait And Misleading Hooks

Clickbait is a title or snippet that promises more than the piece delivers, engineered to earn the click regardless of whether the piece satisfies. It works in the short term and destroys trust in the long term. Readers who arrive at a piece that does not match its title leave, and their leaving signals to algorithms that the piece did not help. Honest hooks outperform clickbait over time.

Avoid clickbait by:

- not promising secrets, shocks, or transformations the piece does not provide;
- not withholding the answer to force a click ("you won't believe...");
- not inflating stakes beyond what the content supports;
- ensuring the title's emotional register matches the piece's actual register;
- treating the click as the start of a relationship, not a one-time extraction.

The click earned by honesty brings a reader who may return. The click earned by deception brings a reader who learns not to trust you.

### Keep Titles And Snippets Consistent With The Body

The title and snippet set expectations; the body must meet them. Inconsistency between the promise and the delivery is the fastest way to lose a reader. After writing the body, return to the title and description and confirm they still match. Drafts evolve, and a title written at the start may no longer fit the finished piece.

Verify consistency by:

- re-reading the title after the body is complete;
- confirming the body delivers what the title promises;
- checking that the snippet accurately reflects the piece's actual content;
- updating the title if the piece shifted during writing;
- ensuring no claim in the title is unsupported in the body.

The title is the promise; the body is the proof. They must agree.

## Common Traps

### Overpromising In The Title

A title that promises more than the body delivers betrays the reader and signals low quality to algorithms. Promise what you can provide.

### Writing Titles Only For Search

SEO-optimized titles that read as robotic repel human readers. Write for clarity first; clarity serves search too.

### Burying The Important Words

Truncation cuts the end of titles. If the essential meaning is at the end, readers never see it. Front-load.

### Clickbait Hooks That Mislead

Hooks engineered only for the click bring readers who leave. Honesty earns the click and the reader.

### Ignoring Truncation Limits

Titles and descriptions that exceed display limits are cut, often losing their meaning. Design for the truncated form.

### Snippet That Implies A Different Intent

A snippet signaling the wrong intent attracts the wrong reader. Match the snippet to the reader's likely goal.

### Forgetting To Reconcile Title And Body

Drafts evolve. A title written first may not fit the finished piece. Reconcile title and body at the end.

## Self-Check

Before treating the title and snippet as complete, verify:

- The title is a promise the body actually delivers, not a label or a lure.
- The title serves both the human reader and search, with clarity before cleverness.
- The most important words are front-loaded so truncation does not destroy meaning.
- The meta description earns the click honestly without overpromising.
- The snippet matches the intent a reader searching for this content would have.
- Length and truncation limits have been respected, and the truncated form still works.
- No clickbait or misleading hook promises more than the piece provides.
- The title's emotional register matches the piece's actual register; the title and snippet have been reconciled with the finished body
- No claim in the title is unsupported in the body; the title would make sense and earn a click when seen alone in a search result
- The description reflects the piece's actual scope and value; you have treated the click as the start of a reader relationship, not a one-time extraction
