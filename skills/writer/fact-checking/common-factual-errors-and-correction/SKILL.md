# Common Factual Errors And Correction

Most factual errors are not exotic. They are a small set of recurring mistakes: a date off by a year, a name misspelled or misattributed, a number with the wrong unit or a transposed digit, a quotation stripped of its context, a title that has changed since the source was written. These errors feel too obvious to check, which is exactly why they survive into print. The writer assumes that anything so simple must be right, and the reader, who catches them instantly, draws a disproportionate conclusion: if the writer got the date wrong, what else did they get wrong?

Factual error is a judgment problem because the cost of small mistakes is not proportional to their size. A single wrong digit in a statistic, or a misspelled name in a profile, can do more credibility damage than a flaw in the argument, because it is verifiable and visible. An agent that treats small facts as self-checking tends to publish errors that a careful reader flags in minutes, and each one erodes the trust the rest of the piece worked to build. The craft is knowing where these errors cluster, checking them systematically, and correcting them in a way that preserves credibility when they slip through.

Use this skill when checking a draft for factual errors, when correcting errors found in your own or others' work, when handling quotations and attributions carefully, or when weighing whether a small mistake threatens the piece's credibility. The goal is to catch the predictable errors before publication and to handle corrections, when needed, in a way that maintains rather than destroys trust.

## Core Rules

### Know Where Factual Errors Cluster

Factual errors are not random; they cluster in predictable categories. Knowing the clusters lets you check systematically rather than hoping to catch errors by feel. The same fact types fail across pieces and across writers, because they are the ones that feel obvious and therefore go unchecked. Target the clusters deliberately.

Common error clusters:

- dates, including day-month confusion, calendar differences, and off-by-one years;
- names, including spellings, transliterations, and attributions of who did or said what;
- numbers, including transposed digits, wrong units, and magnitude errors (thousands versus millions);
- titles and affiliations, which change over time and may be out of date in the source;
- places, including contested names, historical names, and geographic precision;
- quotations, including wording drift and lost context.

Build a checklist from these clusters and apply it to every draft. Most corrections will come from these categories, because most errors live there.

### Check Dates Against The Original Record

Dates fail in ways that feel impossible to get wrong, which is why they survive. A year off, a day and month transposed, a calendar misread, a date taken from a secondary source that rounded or approximated, all produce errors a reader catches at a glance. Check every date against the original record, not against memory or a secondary summary.

Date checks:

- confirm the full date, day, month, and year, against the primary source;
- watch for day-month order differences across regional conventions;
- confirm the calendar or era for historical or non-Gregorian dates;
- distinguish the date of an event from the date of a report about it;
- confirm ranges and approximations are labeled as such, not presented as exact.

A date presented as exact must be exact. If the source only gives "early 2023," do not write "January 2023" unless you have confirmed it. Approximation disguised as precision is a common and avoidable error.

### Verify Names, Spellings, And Attributions

Names are where credibility damage is sharpest, because a misspelled or misattributed name is a personal slight the subject and their community will notice immediately. Verify the spelling of every name against an authoritative source, confirm the correct attribution of actions and statements, and confirm current titles and affiliations. Do not rely on memory or on how the name appeared in a secondary source.

Name and attribution checks:

- confirm spellings against the subject's own publications, institutional page, or authoritative reference;
- confirm transliterations against the subject's preferred form, not an arbitrary convention;
- confirm who actually said or did what, distinguishing speakers in a dialogue or co-authors of a work;
- confirm titles and affiliations are current, since they change;
- confirm the correct person when names are common or shared with more famous figures.

Misattributing a quotation, or naming the wrong person in a profile, is among the most damaging small errors, because it is both verifiable and personal. Check names as if each one could prompt a correction request, because it can.

### Scrutinize Numbers For Magnitude And Units

Numbers fail at the level of magnitude and units more often than at the level of exact value. A figure that should be millions written as billions, a percentage presented as a raw count, a currency or unit mismatched across a comparison, these produce claims that are wildly wrong even when the digits look precise. Check the magnitude and units of every number, not just the digits.

Number checks:

- confirm the unit (people, households, dollars, percent) matches what the claim requires;
- confirm the magnitude (thousands, millions, billions) is correct;
- confirm the denominator for any rate or percentage;
- confirm the currency and adjust for inflation where comparisons span time;
- confirm that figures being compared use the same basis and time frame.

A number that is precisely wrong is worse than one that is approximately right, because precision lends false confidence. Verify that the precision in the draft is backed by precision in the source, and that the magnitude and units are what the argument assumes.

### Preserve Quotation Context And Wording

Quotations fail in two ways: the wording drifts, or the context is stripped until the meaning shifts. A quotation with a word changed, a clause dropped, or a punctuation mark altered can carry a different meaning, and a quotation lifted from its surrounding context can imply the opposite of what was intended. Treat quotation accuracy as both a wording problem and a context problem.

Quotation checks:

- confirm every word, including punctuation, against the primary source or recording;
- confirm that ellipses and brackets do not change the meaning;
- confirm the speaker, occasion, and date of the statement;
- read enough surrounding context to confirm the meaning is preserved;
- confirm that paraphrased framing around the quote is accurate.

A quotation that is word-perfect but contextually misleading is a factual error, and often a more serious one than a misquote, because it is harder to detect and more damaging to fairness. Check both wording and context.

### Correct Errors Transparently And Promptly

When an error is found, how it is corrected affects credibility as much as the error itself. A correction that is hidden, minimized, or delayed signals that the writer or publication cares more about appearance than accuracy. A correction that is clear, prompt, and proportionate signals the opposite and can preserve, even strengthen, trust. Handle corrections as part of the craft, not as damage control.

Correct by:

- acknowledging the error clearly, stating what was wrong and what is correct;
- correcting promptly once the error is confirmed, without delay or minimization;
- explaining the significance, distinguishing trivial slips from substantive errors;
- placing the correction where readers will see it, not buried;
- preserving the correction record so the change is traceable.

Readers do not expect infallibility; they expect honesty. A visible, prompt correction of a small error often does less credibility damage than the error itself would have done if left standing, because it demonstrates the standard the writer holds themselves to.

### Weigh The Credibility Cost Of Each Error

Not all errors carry the same cost, and part of the craft is recognizing which errors threaten the piece and which are trivial. An error in a load-bearing claim, an error about a living person, and an error that supports the argument's direction all do more damage than a slip in incidental color. Weigh each error's cost and prioritize both checking and correction accordingly.

Weigh by asking:

- does the error affect a load-bearing claim or incidental detail;
- does it concern a person, institution, or contested matter where stakes are high;
- does the error conveniently support the argument, which suggests bias rather than slip;
- would a reader who notices it lose trust in the whole piece or just the detail.

Errors that cluster, or that consistently point in the argument's favor, are especially damaging because they suggest a pattern rather than isolated mistakes. Treat patterned errors as a signal to re-examine the whole draft, not just the individual facts.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Simple Facts Are Self-Checking

Dates, names, and units feel too obvious to check, which is why they survive. Check the obvious facts systematically, because that is where errors hide.

### Precision Disguising Magnitude Errors

A number precise to several digits but wrong in magnitude (millions versus billions) carries false confidence. Verify units and magnitude, not just digits.

### Quotations Right In Wording, Wrong In Context

A word-perfect quote stripped of context can invert meaning. Check context and attribution, not only the words.

### Out-Of-Date Titles And Affiliations

Sources record titles at a moment in time; people move. Confirm current titles and affiliations rather than trusting the source's snapshot.

### Hidden Or Delayed Corrections

Minimizing or burying a correction damages credibility more than the error. Correct clearly, promptly, and visibly.

### Ignoring Patterned Errors

Errors that consistently support the argument suggest bias, not slips. Treat clustering errors as a signal to re-examine the whole piece.

## Self-Check

Before treating the factual error check and correction process as complete, verify:

- Every date has been confirmed against the original record, including day, month, and year.
- Every name is spelled correctly and attributed to the right person, with current titles confirmed.
- Every number's magnitude and units match the claim, not just the digits.
- Every quotation is word-perfect and preserves the original context and meaning.
- Titles and affiliations have been checked for currency against authoritative sources.
- A systematic checklist of common error clusters has been applied to the draft.
- Errors found have been corrected transparently, promptly, and where readers can see them.
- The credibility cost of each error has been weighed, with priority on load-bearing and personal claims.
- Patterned errors that consistently favor the argument have triggered a full re-examination; approximations are labeled as such rather than presented as exact
- No precision in the draft exceeds what the source actually supports; a reader who checked any fact in the piece would find it accurate as stated
