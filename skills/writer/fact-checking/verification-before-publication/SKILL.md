# Verification Before Publication

A draft can be beautifully written, well-argued, and rigorously sourced, and still contain a fact that is wrong. Verification is the discipline that catches those errors before a reader does, and it is distinct from research. Research gathers material to build the argument; verification tests the material the argument already rests on, treating every load-bearing fact as suspect until it survives a second look. Skipping verification, or treating it as a formality, is how careful writers publish avoidable errors that erode the trust their craft built.

Verification is a judgment problem because not every claim requires the same standard. A passing historical allusion, a load-bearing statistic, a direct quotation, and a biographical detail each carry different risks and need different checks. An agent that applies one verification level to everything either wastes effort on trivial claims or, more dangerously, under-checks the claims that matter most. The craft is matching the verification method and rigor to the claim's stakes, and knowing which facts must be confirmed by a second, independent method before the piece can ship.

Use this skill when preparing a draft for publication, when deciding how thoroughly to check a given claim, when running a fact-check pass, or when determining what a second verifier should confirm. The goal is a piece in which every load-bearing fact has been tested against an independent source, and no avoidable error reaches the reader.

## Core Rules

### Treat Verification As Distinct From Research

Research and verification answer different questions. Research asks "what evidence supports this claim?" Verification asks "is this specific fact actually true, confirmed independently of how I first found it?" Conflating the two leaves writers trusting facts because they found them once, in one place, often the place they will cite. Verification requires stepping outside the source of the claim and confirming it holds up.

Distinguish the two by:

- treating research as building the argument and verification as stress-testing it;
- checking facts against sources independent of where you first encountered them;
- not accepting a fact merely because a source you trust asserted it;
- building a separate verification pass after drafting, not folding it into research.

A fact that appears in your draft has passed research but not yet verification. The verification pass is what earns it the right to stay.

### Set The Verification Standard By Claim Stakes

Not every claim deserves the same scrutiny. The standard should scale with the stakes: a load-bearing factual claim, a defamatory or legally risky assertion, a surprising statistic, and a direct quotation all demand rigorous verification, while incidental color and common-knowledge background need less. Calibrate effort to risk so that verification time goes where errors would do the most damage.

Calibrate by asking:

- would an error here undermine the central argument or the writer's credibility;
- is the claim about a living person, an institution, or a contested event, raising reputational or legal stakes;
- is the claim surprising, counterintuitive, or likely to be challenged;
- is the claim specific and falsifiable, such as a date, number, or named attribution;
- is the claim incidental, where an error would be minor and easily corrected.

Concentrate verification on the high-stakes end. A wrong date in a throwaway clause is a small embarrassment; a wrong attribution in a central claim is a catastrophe. Know which is which and check accordingly.

### Require A Second Method For Load-Bearing Facts

The strongest verification confirms a fact through a second, independent method, not by re-reading the same source. If you found a statistic in a press release, confirm it in the underlying study or an independent database. If you found a quotation in a secondary account, confirm it in the primary transcript. A fact that survives a different route is far more reliable than one checked only against its origin.

Apply second-method checks by:

- tracing statistics back to their original source rather than the report that cited them;
- confirming quotations against primary recordings or transcripts, not secondary retellings;
- checking dates, names, and titles against an independent reference, not the source that asserted them;
- verifying causal or surprising claims against expert sources who did not make the original assertion.

If a second method contradicts the first, the contradiction is itself a finding that must be resolved before publication. Do not publish a load-bearing fact that two methods disagree on without explaining the discrepancy or choosing the stronger evidence.

### Verify The Specifics, Not Just The Gist

Verification often fails at the level of detail. A writer confirms that "the general claim is true" and assumes the specific figure, date, or name must therefore be right, when the specifics are exactly where errors hide. Verify the exact number, the exact date, the exact name and title, the exact wording of a quotation, not merely the surrounding claim.

Verify specifics including:

- exact figures and units, including whether millions were meant rather than billions;
- exact dates, including day, month, and year, and the calendar or era being used;
- exact names, spellings, titles, and affiliations of people and institutions;
- exact wording of quotations, including punctuation that can change meaning;
- exact attributions of who said or did what, not just the general event.

The devil is in the precision. A claim that "enrollment fell significantly" verified as true does not verify that "enrollment fell 14 percent." Check the specific assertion in the draft, word for word, against the independent source.

### Check Quotations Against Their Original Context

Quotations are especially error-prone because they can be right in wording but wrong in meaning. A quotation lifted from its context can imply the opposite of what the speaker meant, or attribute a statement to the wrong occasion, or conflate a question with an answer. Verifying a quotation means checking not only that the words are exact but that they appear in the context and with the attribution the draft implies.

Check quotations by:

- confirming the exact wording against the primary recording or transcript;
- confirming the speaker, the occasion, and the date of the statement;
- reading enough surrounding context to confirm the meaning is not inverted;
- confirming that paraphrased framing around the quote is accurate.

A quotation that is word-perfect but contextually misleading is still an error, and often a worse one than a misquote, because it is harder to detect and more damaging to fairness. Treat context as part of the quotation's accuracy.

### Verify Names, Dates, Numbers, And Titles Mechanically

Certain fact types fail in predictable, mechanical ways and should be checked systematically. Names get misspelled or misattributed; dates drift by a day, a year, or a calendar; numbers transpose digits or shift units; titles and affiliations go out of date. These are the errors a reader catches instantly and a writer misses because they feel too obvious to check. Check them anyway, deliberately.

Mechanical checks:

- confirm every name's spelling against an authoritative source, not memory;
- confirm every date against the original record, watching for day-month confusion;
- confirm every number's value and unit against the source, including magnitude;
- confirm current titles and affiliations, which may have changed since the source was written;
- confirm place names, especially transliterations and historically contested names.

These checks are unglamorous but high-yield. Most post-publication corrections are for exactly these fact types, and they are the easiest to get right with a systematic pass.

### Build A Verification Pass Into The Final Stage

Verification should be a defined stage, not something that happens incidentally during editing. Build a pass that walks through the draft claim by claim, identifies the load-bearing and high-stakes facts, and checks each against an independent source. Treat the pass as the last gate before publication, with the power to hold the piece until a fact is confirmed.

Structure the pass by:

- listing every factual claim in the draft, prioritized by stakes;
- marking each as verified, unverifiable, or in dispute, with the source of verification;
- resolving disputes or revising claims that cannot be verified;
- flagging any claim that rests on a single, uncorroborated source for editorial review.

A piece that has not passed a structured verification gate is not ready to publish, no matter how well written. The pass is what converts a draft into a defensible publication.

## Common Traps

### Conflating Research With Verification

Trusting a fact because you found it once, in the source you will cite, skips independent confirmation. Treat verification as a separate stress test after research.

### One Standard Applied To All Claims

Checking everything equally wastes effort, and checking nothing rigorously endangers load-bearing facts. Scale verification to the stakes of each claim.

### Verifying The Gist But Not The Specifics

Confirming the general claim while the exact figure, date, or name is wrong. Verify the precise assertion in the draft, word for word.

### Single-Source Load-Bearing Facts

Relying on one source for a high-stakes claim leaves it uncorroborated. Require a second, independent method for facts the argument depends on.

### Quotations Right In Wording, Wrong In Context

A word-perfect quote lifted from misleading context is still an error. Check the surrounding context and attribution, not just the words.

### Skipping Mechanical Checks On Names And Numbers

Obvious-feeling facts like spellings, dates, and units are exactly what readers catch and writers miss. Check them systematically anyway.

### No Defined Verification Stage

Verification folded into editing gets skipped under deadline. Build a structured pass as the last gate before publication.

## Self-Check

Before treating verification as complete, verify:

- Every load-bearing factual claim has been checked independently of where it was first found.
- Verification standards are scaled to claim stakes, with the most effort on high-risk facts.
- A second, independent method has confirmed each load-bearing statistic, quotation, and attribution.
- Specifics (exact figures, dates, names, titles) have been checked, not just the general claim.
- Quotations are verified for context and attribution, not only for exact wording.
- Names, dates, numbers, and titles have been checked mechanically against authoritative sources.
- Claims about living people, institutions, or contested events have received heightened scrutiny.
- Surprising or counterintuitive claims are corroborated before publication.
- A structured verification pass has walked the draft claim by claim; each claim is marked verified, unverifiable, or in dispute, with disputes resolved or revised
- No load-bearing fact rests on a single uncorroborated source without editorial awareness; the piece has passed a defined verification gate before being approved for publication
