# Paragraph Unity And Topic Control

A paragraph is a unit of thought, not a unit of length. Its job is to make one main move and to make it completely enough that the reader can hold it before moving on. Unity is the principle that everything in the paragraph serves that single move. When unity holds, the reader feels the paragraph click into place. When unity breaks, the reader senses drift without being able to name it, and attention leaks.

The common failure is to treat paragraphs as containers that fill until they look full. Sentences get added because they seem related, and the paragraph slowly tries to do two or three jobs at once. The topic sentence, if there is one, no longer governs the whole paragraph. The reader reaches the end uncertain what the paragraph was for. This is a problem of control, not of grammar.

Use this skill when drafting or revising paragraphs, when writing or evaluating topic sentences, when a paragraph feels scattered, or when deciding whether to split a paragraph. The agent has freedom to break conventions for effect, but should always be able to state, in one sentence, what each paragraph is doing.

## Core Rules

### Give Each Paragraph One Main Move

A paragraph should advance a single idea, claim, scene, step, or piece of evidence. If you cannot state the paragraph's move in one sentence, it is probably doing too much. The move might be to define, to argue, to illustrate, to qualify, to narrate, to compare, or to conclude, but it should be one move.

Test each paragraph by asking:

- What is this paragraph adding that the previous one did not?
- Could I summarize it in a single sentence?
- Does every sentence serve that summary, or do some belong elsewhere?

When a paragraph tries to make a claim, give evidence, introduce a counterargument, and transition to a new topic, it has become several paragraphs stitched together. Split it.

### Use Topic Sentences To Declare The Move

A topic sentence declares what the paragraph is about and often what it will do. It gives the reader a frame before the supporting material arrives. The topic sentence usually appears first, but it can appear second, after an orienting hook, or even last, when the paragraph builds toward a revelation.

A strong topic sentence:

- names the paragraph's subject and stance;
- promises a specific development, not just a topic label;
- connects to the previous paragraph's move;
- is narrow enough to complete in one paragraph.

Weak topic sentences merely announce a theme ("There are several factors...") without committing to a claim. Commit, so the paragraph has something to fulfill.

### Keep Every Sentence Under The Topic Sentence's Governance

Once the topic sentence declares the move, every following sentence should serve it. Sentences that drift into a related but distinct idea break unity. This does not mean every sentence must repeat the topic; it means every sentence must advance, support, qualify, or complete the declared move.

Watch for:

- sentences that introduce a new claim mid-paragraph;
- evidence that actually supports a different point;
- tangential context that belongs in another paragraph;
- a second topic sentence hiding inside the paragraph.

When you find a sentence that does not serve the move, move it to its own paragraph or cut it.

### Recognize When A Paragraph Is Doing Too Much

A paragraph doing too much sends recognizable signals. It feels long without feeling complete. It contains more than one claim that could stand alone. It introduces a new term, example, or counterargument halfway through. The reader cannot say, at the end, what the paragraph concluded.

Common overload patterns:

- claim plus full counterargument plus rebuttal in one block;
- two examples that actually illustrate different points;
- a definition followed by an unrelated application;
- a narrative beat followed by analytical commentary that belongs separately.

When you detect overload, split along the fault line. Give each move its own paragraph so each can be developed properly.

### Control The Relationship Between Adjacent Paragraphs

Unity within a paragraph is necessary but not sufficient. Each paragraph must also relate clearly to its neighbors. The reader should understand why this paragraph follows the previous one and precedes the next. Without that, even unified paragraphs feel like a list.

Strengthen inter-paragraph unity by:

- echoing the previous paragraph's ending in the next topic sentence;
- using transitions that name the logical relationship;
- ordering paragraphs by a clear spine (cause, contrast, sequence, escalation);
- avoiding two consecutive paragraphs that make the same move.

If two paragraphs make the same point, combine them or differentiate their moves.

### Let The Paragraph End With Forward Motion

The last sentence of a paragraph is a stress position. It should not merely stop; it should complete the move and often point forward. A strong ending carries the reader into the next paragraph, creates emphasis, or leaves a residue the reader holds.

Weak endings:

- trail off with a vague summary;
- introduce new material that belongs in the next paragraph;
- repeat the topic sentence verbatim;
- end on a subordinate afterthought.

Revise endings so they complete the paragraph's job and set up what follows.

### Revise Paragraphs As Units, Not As Sentences

When revising, read paragraphs as wholes before line-editing sentences. Ask whether the paragraph is doing one job, whether the topic sentence governs it, and whether the order of sentences advances the move. Only after the paragraph is unified should you polish individual sentences. Polishing sentences inside a paragraph that lacks unity wastes effort and makes the underlying drift harder to detect.

## Common Traps

### The Container Paragraph

Filling a paragraph until it looks long enough, regardless of whether the sentences belong together, produces drift. Length is not the measure; unity is.

### The Hidden Second Topic Sentence

A paragraph that seems unified may contain a second claim buried mid-paragraph that quietly takes over. If you can find two topic sentences, you have two paragraphs.

### Topic Sentence As Label

"There are many reasons for this" is a label, not a move. A real topic sentence commits to a specific development the paragraph then fulfills.

### Tangent Disguised As Context

Writers often add background that feels relevant but does not serve the paragraph's move. If context does not advance the declared job, it belongs elsewhere.

### Repeating The Same Move Across Paragraphs

Several paragraphs that each make the same point in different words create the feeling of stalled thought. Differentiate the moves or combine the paragraphs.

### Ending By Stopping

A paragraph that simply runs out of sentences gives the reader no emphasis and no forward motion. End with intention.

## Self-Check

Before treating paragraph unity as achieved, verify:

- Each paragraph makes one main move that can be stated in a single sentence.
- Each paragraph has a topic sentence that declares a specific development.
- Every sentence in the paragraph serves the topic sentence's declared move.
- No paragraph contains a hidden second topic sentence or competing claim.
- Overloaded paragraphs have been split along their fault lines.
- Adjacent paragraphs relate through clear logical or verbal connection.
- No two consecutive paragraphs make the same move in different words.
- Paragraph endings complete the move and create forward motion.
- Context and background within a paragraph actually serve its job; the paragraph reads as a unified unit, not as a container of loosely related sentences
- Revision addressed paragraph-level unity before sentence-level polish; a reader could outline the piece from the topic sentences alone
