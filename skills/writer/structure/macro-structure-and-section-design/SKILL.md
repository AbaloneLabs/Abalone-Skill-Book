# Macro Structure And Section Design

Macro structure is the architecture beneath a long piece. It is the decision about what the major sections are, what order they appear in, and what work each one does for the reader. Once a piece grows beyond a few paragraphs, structure stops being something the writer feels and becomes something the reader either follows or fights. A weak macro structure produces sections that are individually competent but collectively exhausting: the reader finishes each part understanding it, yet cannot say why the parts came in that order or where the whole thing is going.

The judgment problem here is that agents tend to treat sectioning as filing. They sort material by type (background, methods, results, discussion) or by topic, then assume the order is fine because each bucket is complete. But macro structure is not filing. It is an argument about sequence. The same material, ordered differently, makes a different claim about what matters and how the reader should think.

Use this skill when planning long-form essays, reports, white papers, book chapters, multipart articles, guides, proposals, or any piece where the division into sections is itself a design decision. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a draft whose parts never cohere into a directed journey.

## Core Rules

### Choose One Organizing Principle And Make It Carry The Piece

Every long piece is held together by an organizing principle: the logic that decides what comes next. Common principles include chronological progression, problem-to-cause-to-solution, claim-to-evidence-to-implication, simple-to-complex, known-to-uncertain, question-to-investigation-to-answer, comparison-of-options, and before-and-after transformation.

Choose one principle deliberately and let it govern the major sections. Mixing principles accidentally is the most common structural failure. A piece that begins as problem-solution, then drifts into chronological history, then switches to a list of options, leaves the reader recalibrating the reading mode at every boundary. If you must combine principles, do it in a hierarchy: let one govern the whole, and let the other operate within sections.

Write the organizing principle in one sentence before designing sections. If you cannot name it, the structure is not yet designed.

### Design Sections As Reader Movements, Not Topic Buckets

A section is not a container for a topic. It is a movement the reader makes. Each section should change what the reader understands, believes, feels, or is prepared to do. Before assigning a section a heading, state its job: what state is the reader in when they enter, and what state should they be in when they leave?

Ask of every section:

- What does this section add that no other section adds?
- What must the reader already know to enter it?
- What does it set up for later sections?
- What would be lost if it were cut or merged?

Avoid "background," "context," and "overview" as section purposes unless you can specify the movement. A background section that merely deposits facts is a junk drawer. Reframe it as "establish the stakes the reader needs before the problem lands" or "supply the minimum vocabulary the argument requires."

### Sequence For Dependency, Not For Completeness

The order of sections should follow what the reader needs to understand each next section, not the order in which you discovered or assembled the material. Treat each section as having prerequisites. A section that introduces a contested claim should come after the section that establishes the shared facts it contests. A section that offers a recommendation should come after the sections that justify it.

Build a dependency map before locking the sequence. For each section, list which other sections must precede it. If you find a section whose prerequisites are scattered, either move those prerequisites earlier or move the section later. Cycles and forward references are sometimes unavoidable, but each one is a cost the reader pays; make them rare and intentional.

### Decide Section Weight By Argumentative Load

Not every section deserves equal length. A common error is to give each topic roughly the same space, producing a flat piece with no center of gravity. Instead, allocate length by how much work a section does in the overall argument.

Sections that carry the core claim, the pivotal evidence, or the emotional turn deserve more room. Sections that orient, define, or qualify deserve less. A piece whose longest section is the literature review and whose shortest is the actual recommendation has inverted its priorities. Audit the draft by measuring where the words go and asking whether that distribution matches where the stakes are.

### Build A Skeleton You Can Read In Headings Alone

A strong macro structure is legible from its section headings alone. If a reader scans only the headings, they should be able to reconstruct the argument's shape and direction. This is the single best test of macro structure, and it should be applied before drafting fills the sections with prose.

Check the heading sequence:

- Does each heading name a move, not just a topic?
- Does the sequence read as a progression rather than a list?
- Could a stranger predict, from the headings, what the piece concludes?
- Are there two headings that promise the same job, signaling redundancy?

If the headings read as a table of contents for a filing cabinet, the structure is wrong. Revise until they read as a sentence-stripped version of the argument.

### Design The Joints Between Major Sections

Where two sections meet, the reader must reorient. Long pieces fail at section boundaries more often than within sections. Each major section should open by connecting to where the reader just was and signaling where they are going. This is not ornament; it is navigation.

For each boundary, decide: does the next section continue, complicate, apply, contrast, escalate, or resolve the previous one? State that relationship in the first sentence of the new section or the last sentence of the previous one. If the relationship is hard to name, the section order may be wrong.

### Use Part And Chapter Breaks To Reset The Reader

In very long pieces, group sections into parts, and use part breaks to mark genuine shifts in mode, time, scale, or stakes. A part break tells the reader to set down one set of questions and pick up another. Use them sparingly: too many breaks fragment momentum, while too few bury real shifts inside continuous prose.

A part break should mark a change the reader can feel: a move from diagnosis to prescription, from history to analysis, from the general to the specific case, from setup to payoff. If two parts do not differ in mode or stakes, they should not be separate parts.

### Make The Structure Visible Without Being Mechanical

Readers benefit from knowing the shape of a long piece, but they resent being lectured about it. You may signal structure through a brief roadmap, through section headings that name moves, or through occasional orientation sentences. Avoid over-signposting: "In this section we will discuss X, then in the next section we will discuss Y" repeated at every boundary becomes noise.

Give the reader just enough map to know where they are without making them feel they are reading an outline instead of a piece of writing.

### Match Structure To Genre Obligations

Different genres carry structural expectations that the reader uses as scaffolding. A scientific report, a policy memo, a personal essay, a how-to guide, and a narrative nonfiction chapter each have conventional macro shapes. Deviating is allowed and often valuable, but deviation should be a choice made with awareness of the expectation you are breaking and why.

When you break a genre convention, do it to serve the reader, not to signal cleverness. An unconventional structure that confuses the reader is a failure even if it is interesting.

## Common Traps

### Filing Instead Of Structuring

Sorting material into topical buckets and calling the result a structure produces a piece that is complete but directionless. The test is whether the section order could be shuffled without loss; if it could, you have filing, not structure.

### The Flat Piece With No Center Of Gravity

When every section gets equal length and equal emphasis, the reader cannot tell what matters. The piece reads as a tour rather than an argument or a journey. Allocate space and emphasis to the load-bearing sections.

### Front-Loading Everything The Writer Knows

Writers often begin with full context, full background, and full definitions before the reader has any reason to care. The reader wades through orientation with no stake in it. Introduce background at the moment it becomes necessary, not at the moment the writer happens to know it.

### Sections That Repeat The Same Move

Two sections that both "explain the problem" or both "review the evidence" signal that one is redundant or that the structure has not actually advanced. Each section should make a move no other section makes.

### Hidden Dependencies That Force Rereading

When a section references a concept defined three sections later, or assumes a conclusion established in a section that comes after it, the reader is forced to read forward blind or reread backward. Map dependencies and eliminate forward references that are not strictly necessary.

### Mistaking Length For Depth

A long section is not automatically a deep one. Padding a section to match the perceived importance of its topic produces repetition and digression. Depth comes from developing the move fully, not from restating it at greater length.

## Self-Check

Before treating the macro structure as ready, verify:

- The organizing principle is named in one sentence and governs the whole piece.
- Each major section has a stated reader movement, not merely a topic.
- The section sequence follows reader dependency, not writer convenience.
- Section lengths reflect argumentative load, with the load-bearing sections given the most room.
- The headings alone reconstruct the argument's shape and direction.
- Every section boundary has a defined relationship between what came before and what comes after.
- Part or chapter breaks mark genuine shifts in mode, time, scale, or stakes; no two sections perform the same job
- Background and context appear when needed, not dumped at the front; no section depends on material introduced later without an intentional, flagged forward reference
- The structure respects or deliberately breaks genre conventions for a stated reason; a reader scanning only the headings could predict where the piece is going
- The longest sections are the ones doing the most argumentative or emotional work; the structure could not be shuffled into a different order without loss of meaning
