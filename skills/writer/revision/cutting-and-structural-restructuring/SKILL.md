# Cutting And Structural Restructuring

Cutting is the highest-leverage and most resisted form of revision. A draft becomes itself not by accumulation but by subtraction: removing what does not serve the piece so what remains carries more weight. Restructuring is cutting's partner, the willingness to move whole sections, invert order, or rebuild the spine when the original arrangement no longer serves the meaning.

Agents tend to add during revision and resist subtracting. Addition feels like progress; cutting feels like loss. But a draft that keeps everything usually serves nothing well. The harm of insufficient cutting is a piece that feels padded, repetitive, or unfocused, where the strong material is buried under material the writer could not bear to remove. The harm of timid restructuring is a piece whose parts are individually fine but whose order is accidental, so the reader never feels the shape of the thought.

This skill covers the judgment required to cut decisively and restructure bravely: how to evaluate material by function, how to handle the emotional resistance to deletion, how to test a new structure before committing, and how to preserve cut material so deletion feels safe. The agent has wide freedom to reorganize, but must cut and move based on the piece's actual job, not on attachment.

## Core Rules

### Cut By Function, Not By Quality

A passage should stay only if it serves the piece's controlling purpose. Quality is not sufficient reason to keep material. A beautiful paragraph that does not advance the argument, develop the scene, or fulfill the reader contract is a burden.

Test each section and passage against function:

- Does it advance the controlling question or promise?
- Does it prepare, complicate, or resolve something the reader needs?
- Would its absence be felt as a gap, or merely as fewer words?
- Does it duplicate work already done elsewhere?
- Does it belong to a different piece?

If a passage fails these tests, it should go regardless of how well written it is. Function is the only honest criterion.

### Cut The Largest Units First

Begin cutting at the largest scale and move downward: sections before paragraphs, paragraphs before sentences, sentences before words. Cutting one section can eliminate the need to cut twenty sentences within it.

Large-scale cuts are higher leverage and clarify the piece fastest. They also reduce the emotional cost, because removing one section is one decision, while surgically trimming it sentence by sentence is many small losses that never resolve the underlying problem. When in doubt about whether to trim or remove, remove.

### Relocate Before You Delete, Delete Before You Compress

Before deleting a strong passage, ask whether it belongs elsewhere. A passage that weakens one section might strengthen another, or might belong in a different piece entirely. Moving material often solves problems that cutting or rewriting cannot.

Use a cuttings file: a separate document where deleted material lives. This is not a graveyard but a resource. Knowing that cut material is preserved makes decisive cutting emotionally possible, because nothing is truly lost. Compressing a passage (turning a paragraph into a sentence) should come only after you have confirmed it belongs where it is.

### Restructure When Order Is The Problem

Sometimes no amount of cutting fixes a draft because the problem is sequence. The reader is given the wrong information at the wrong time, or the sections answer questions in an order that prevents understanding. Restructuring changes the spine without necessarily changing the content.

Signals that restructuring is needed:

- the strongest insight appears too early or too late;
- the reader needs background that arrives only after the claim that requires it;
- two sections make more sense in reverse order;
- the ending depends on material the reader has forgotten;
- the draft front-loads context the reader does not yet care about.

When restructuring, test the new order against the reader's cognitive path: what does the reader know at each point, and is that enough to carry the next section?

### Test A Restructure Before Committing

Large restructures are risky. Before rewriting transitions and polishing a new order, validate it cheaply. Write the new structure as a list of section jobs in the proposed order, and read only that list to see if the line of thought holds. If the skeleton works, the prose can follow.

Cheaper tests first:

- outline the new order as one-line section summaries;
- check that each section prepares the next;
- confirm the opening and ending still connect through the new middle;
- identify which transitions must be rewritten and which survive.

Only after the skeleton is confirmed should you invest in rewriting the connective prose. Testing the skeleton prevents expensive rework when a new order turns out to be worse.

### Kill Darlings Deliberately

A "darling" is a passage the writer loves for its own sake, not for its service to the piece: a clever line, a vivid image, a hard-won paragraph, a show of research. Darlings are dangerous because their quality masks their irrelevance.

To kill a darling, first move it to the cuttings file rather than destroying it. Then ask whether the piece is clearer, faster, or more focused without it. Usually it is. The piece is not a showcase for the writer's best sentences; it is an experience for the reader. A darling that earns its place by function may stay. A darling kept only because it is admired weakens the whole.

### Preserve Meaning Through Reorganization

When you move or cut large units, the meaning of what remains can shift. A claim that was qualified by a later paragraph may now read as unqualified. An example that depended on context may now confuse. Restructuring is not finished when the blocks are in a new order; it is finished when each remaining block still makes sense in its new position.

After any major restructure, read the affected sections in sequence and check that:

- every claim still has the support it needs nearby;
- no remaining reference points to deleted material;
- pronouns, definitions, and terms still resolve correctly;
- the emotional or logical arc still builds as intended.

### Cut Proportionally To The Whole

Cutting should respect proportion. If the body is overwritten but the opening and ending are thin, cutting only the body leaves the high-leverage parts underdeveloped. Diagnose where the draft is bloated and where it is starved, and redistribute. Sometimes the right move is to cut a long middle section and expand a rushed ending with the recovered attention.

## Common Traps

### Keeping Material Because It Was Hard To Write

Effort invested in a passage is a sunk cost. The reader never sees the effort and owes it nothing. Keeping hard-won material that does not serve the piece punishes the reader for the writer's labor.

### Trimming Instead Of Removing

Surgically shaving words from a section that should not exist prolongs the problem and rarely produces a clean result. If a section lacks a job, remove it; do not compress it into a smaller version of the same irrelevance.

### Cutting Out Of Frustration Rather Than Judgment

Cutting can become a mood rather than a decision. Aggressive cutting driven by fatigue or self-doubt can remove material the piece needs. Cut against a clear functional test, not against how you feel in the moment.

### Fear Of Losing The Draft

Writers under-cut because they fear losing material permanently. A cuttings file eliminates this fear. Make recoverability a precondition of bold cutting.

### Restructuring Without Testing The Skeleton

Moving sections and immediately rewriting all the prose commits effort before the new order is validated. If the order is wrong, the rewrite is wasted. Always test the skeleton first.

### Preserving The Original Order Out Of Inertia

The order in which material was drafted is rarely the best order for the reader. Treat the draft's sequence as provisional. Inertia is not a reason to keep an arrangement.

### Keeping A Darling That Clashes

A clever or beautiful passage that clashes with the piece's tone or stance does damage out of proportion to its size. One tonally wrong darling can make the surrounding prose feel unstable.

## Self-Check

Before treating cutting and restructuring as complete, verify:

- Every remaining section and passage passes a functional test tied to the controlling purpose.
- Cutting was done at the largest useful scale first, not sentence by sentence.
- Deleted material is preserved in a cuttings file, making the cuts recoverable.
- Material was relocated before being deleted where relocation served the piece.
- Any restructure was tested as a one-line skeleton before prose was rewritten.
- The new order follows the reader's cognitive path, not the writer's drafting path.
- Remaining claims still have nearby support after cuts and moves.
- No remaining text references, depends on, or points to deleted material.
- Darlings were evaluated by function, and kept only if they earn their place; cutting respected proportion: high-leverage parts are not starved while low-leverage parts are merely trimmed
- The piece reads as more focused, not just shorter; the restructured draft was read in full sequence to confirm the arc still holds
