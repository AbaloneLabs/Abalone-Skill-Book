# Line Editing And Sentence Level Revision

Line editing is the revision pass that works at the level of the sentence. It comes after structure is settled and before proofreading begins. Its job is not to decide what the piece says, that is structural work, nor to catch mechanical errors, that is proofreading. Its job is to make each sentence do its work as well as it can: clear, precise, well-paced, free of clutter, carrying the right emphasis, and faithful to the voice. A piece can be structurally sound and still fail at the line level, reading as flabby, monotonous, or vague. A piece can be mechanically correct and still read badly, because correctness is not the same as life.

The common failure is to confuse line editing with proofreading, treating it as a hunt for errors rather than a craft pass. The line editor is not looking for what is wrong in a grammatical sense but for what is weak: the sentence that says what it means but says it limply, the rhythm that drags, the word that is almost right, the clause that adds nothing, the emphasis that lands on the wrong syllable. A second failure is to over-edit, tightening every sentence until the prose goes flat and metallic, sacrificing breath and variation for compression. Line editing is surgery, and like surgery it requires judgment about when to cut, when to reconstruct, and when to leave well enough alone.

Use this skill when doing a dedicated line-level pass, when a draft is structurally sound but reads weakly at the sentence level, when deciding whether a sentence works or needs revision, or when tightening prose without flattening voice. The agent has wide latitude in style, but the priorities of clarity, precision, rhythm, and restraint are not optional.

## Core Rules

### Line Edit Only After Structure Is Set

Line editing presupposes that the larger decisions are settled. If the structure is still changing, a sentence may be cut or rewritten wholesale next week, and any line-level investment in it is wasted. Worse, polish creates attachment: a beautifully line-edited paragraph becomes harder to cut even when it should go. Do the line pass when the piece's shape, argument, and section order are locked, and the remaining work is genuinely at the sentence level. If you find yourself line-editing a passage whose place in the piece is uncertain, stop and resolve the structural question first.

Confirm before line editing that:

- the section belongs in the piece and in this position;
- the paragraph's purpose is clear;
- the content is staying, not provisional;
- the voice and stance are settled for this section.

If any of these is unstable, line editing is premature.

### Read For Rhythm Before Reading For Words

The most common line-level weakness is not a wrong word but a wrong rhythm. A sentence can be grammatically correct and precisely worded and still fail because it drags, or because every sentence in a paragraph has the same shape, or because the emphasis falls on a minor word instead of the load-bearing one. Rhythm is what makes prose feel alive or dead, and it is felt before it is analyzed. Read the passage aloud, or under the breath, and listen for where the prose stumbles, where it monotonizes, and where it fails to land.

Listen for:

- sentences that all begin the same way, producing a droning effect;
- sentences that all run the same length, producing monotony;
- clauses that interrupt the main thrust and dissipate the force;
- endings that fall away weakly instead of landing;
- places where the prose should speed up or slow down and does not.

Fix rhythm first, because a rhythmic fix often reveals the word-level problems, while a word-level fix in a bad rhythm often does not save the sentence.

### Tighten By Removing What Adds Nothing

Most drafts carry redundancy: the same idea stated twice, a clause that repeats what the previous sentence established, an adverb that duplicates the verb's meaning, a hedge that weakens without adding nuance, an adjective that the noun already implies. Tightening is the art of removing what adds nothing so that what remains carries more force. The goal is not the shortest possible sentence but the one in which every word earns its place.

Common redundancies to cut:

- adverbs that repeat the verb, such as "shouted loudly";
- adjectives that the noun implies, such as "unexpected surprise";
- clauses that restate the previous sentence;
- hedges that weaken without qualifying, such as "somewhat" or "rather" used reflexively;
- throat-clearing openers that delay the point;
- transitions that the logic already makes obvious.

Tightening is not minimalism for its own sake. It is removing what does not serve so that what does can be felt.

### Choose The Precise Word Over The Approximate One

Word-level precision is where line editing does its most visible work. The approximate word is close enough to convey meaning but not exact enough to convey the writer's actual perception. The precise word lands; the approximate word almost lands. Line editing is the pass where approximate words get replaced with the right ones, where "walked" becomes "limped" or "strode" or "wandered" depending on what actually happened, where "important" becomes the specific value at stake.

Principles for word precision:

- prefer the specific concrete word to the general abstract one where concreteness serves;
- prefer the active verb to the noun-plus-verb construction, "decided" over "made a decision";
- prefer the ordinary exact word to the fancy approximate one; precision is not ornament;
- watch for words that repeat unintentionally within a passage and vary them;
- keep precision within the voice; a precise word that breaks the voice is the wrong choice.

Precision serves clarity and vividness. It should never become a display of vocabulary.

### Preserve Voice While Sharpening

Line editing can flatten voice if it is done mechanically. The impulse to tighten and clarify can strip away the texture, rhythm, and characteristic choices that make the voice recognizable. A sentence that is perfectly clear and perfectly tight but sounds like anyone wrote it has lost something. The line editor must sharpen the sentence while preserving the choices that constitute the voice, even when those choices are not the most efficient.

To preserve voice:

- identify the voice's characteristic choices before editing, such as a preference for long accumulating sentences or for stark short ones;
- treat those choices as load-bearing and edit within them, not against them;
- recognize that some inefficiency is voice; a voice that digests ideas slowly may need the longer sentence;
- when tightening would change the voice, ask whether the tightening serves the piece or merely the editor's preference;
- accept that a slightly less efficient sentence may be the right one if it preserves the voice's presence.

The test is whether the edited sentence is more itself, not merely more compressed.

### Know When A Sentence Works And Should Be Left Alone

Not every sentence needs revision. A common line-editing failure is to revise sentences that already work, driven by the momentum of editing rather than by a real weakness. Over-editing produces prose that feels worked-over, self-conscious, and uniform. Part of line-level judgment is recognizing the sentence that does its job and leaving it, even if it is not how you would have written it. The best line editing is invisible; the prose feels natural, not improved.

A sentence works when:

- it carries its meaning clearly and with the right force;
- its rhythm fits the passage;
- its words are precise enough for the purpose;
- it preserves the voice;
- nothing in it distracts or obstructs.

When a sentence meets these, leave it. Editing it further risks making it worse.

### Match Emphasis To Importance

Line editing is where emphasis gets tuned. Within a sentence, the most important idea should fall where the reader's attention peaks, often the end. Within a paragraph, the most important sentence should occupy the position of greatest force. Line editing reorders clauses and sentences so that the weight falls where the meaning is heaviest. A sentence whose emphasis lands on a minor clause buries its own point.

Tune emphasis by:

- moving the load-bearing idea to the end of the sentence, where readers naturally place weight;
- cutting the clauses that compete with the main point for attention;
- using a short sentence after long ones to land a point with force;
- varying sentence length so that the important sentences stand out;
- ensuring paragraph endings carry the thought the reader should retain.

### Line Edit In A Single Dedicated Pass

Line editing works best as a dedicated pass with a single lens, reading for the sentence rather than for structure or mechanics. Mixing line editing with proofreading or structural revision dilutes both, because each requires a different reading speed and attention. Read the whole piece, or a whole section, with only the sentence in view. Note other problems, structural gaps, factual questions, mechanical errors, for later passes, but keep the line pass focused on the sentence.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Line Editing With Proofreading

Treating the line pass as a hunt for grammatical errors misses the real work. Line editing is about strength and craft, not correctness. Correct but weak sentences are the line editor's main subject.

### Over-Tightening Into Flatness

Compressing every sentence to its minimum produces prose that reads as efficient but lifeless. Variation, breath, and texture are part of voice. Tighten what adds nothing; keep what gives life.

### Line Editing Before Structure Is Set

Polishing sentences that may be cut is wasted effort and creates attachment that resists necessary cuts. Confirm structure before investing in the line.

### Revising Sentences That Already Work

The momentum of editing can lead to revising sentences that need no revision. Over-editing produces self-conscious, uniform prose. Recognize the sentence that works and leave it.

### Prioritizing Precision Over Voice

Replacing a voice-characteristic word with a more precise one can sharpen meaning while flattening voice. Precision must serve the voice, not override it.

### Ignoring Rhythm And Fixing Only Words

Word-level fixes in a bad rhythm often fail to save the sentence. Rhythm is felt first and should usually be addressed first.

### Monotonizing Sentence Structure

Tightening every sentence to the same shape produces monotony. Variation in length and structure is what makes prose readable and emphatic.

## Self-Check

Before treating the line-level pass as complete, verify:

- The pass was done only after structure was settled, with no provisional passages receiving line-level investment.
- Rhythm was read for, aloud or under the breath, before word-level fixes were applied.
- Redundancy was removed so that remaining words carry force, without stripping texture that serves the voice.
- Words were chosen for precision, with approximate words replaced by exact ones within the voice's register.
- Voice was preserved; characteristic choices were treated as load-bearing and edited within, not against.
- Sentences that already work were left alone rather than revised for the sake of editing.
- Emphasis falls on the load-bearing ideas, at sentence and paragraph level.
- The pass was dedicated to the sentence, with other problems noted for later passes.
- The prose reads as natural and alive, not as worked-over or uniformly compressed.
- No sentence was tightened past the point where it lost its breath or its voice.
