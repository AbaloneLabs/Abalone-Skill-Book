# Revision Strategy And Pass Planning

Revision is not a single act of improvement. It is a sequence of distinct passes, each with a different lens and a different tolerance for change. The most common failure is not poor editing but disordered editing: polishing sentences that belong to a section about to be cut, or agonizing over a comma while the argument still lacks a spine. Strategy is the discipline of doing the right kind of work at the right time.

Agents tend to under-plan revision because drafting already feels like progress and "just improving it" feels sufficient. But undirected revision produces a draft that is locally better and globally unchanged. The harm is rework: every sentence polished before a structural decision is a sentence that may need to be rewritten or deleted, and the writer's emotional investment in that polish makes the necessary cut harder.

This skill covers how to plan the revision process itself: how to diagnose what a draft needs, sequence passes from largest to smallest leverage, allocate effort, and know when a pass is genuinely complete. The agent has freedom to choose the number and naming of passes, but the ordering principle is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose Before You Edit

Before changing anything, read the draft as a reader and form a diagnosis. Ask what the draft is actually doing versus what it is supposed to do, where it is strongest, where it loses the reader, and what kind of revision each weakness requires.

Produce a short revision map before editing:

- the draft's current purpose and audience as written (not as intended);
- the two or three largest problems, ranked by leverage;
- which problems are structural, which are developmental, which are line-level, which are mechanical;
- sections that may need to be cut, moved, or rewritten wholesale;
- a rough guess at how many passes the draft needs.

A diagnosis turns revision from reactive tinkering into a plan. Without it, the agent drifts toward the easiest fixes and leaves the highest-leverage problems untouched.

### Revise From Largest Leverage To Smallest

Sequence passes so that each one makes decisions the later passes depend on. If you line-edit a section and then delete it, the line-edit was wasted. Work in roughly this order:

- developmental and structural revision (purpose, premise, argument, section order);
- scene and evidence revision (what material stays, what gets added, what gets cut);
- paragraph and movement revision (how sections connect and progress);
- voice and tone revision (stance, consistency, emphasis);
- line revision (sentence clarity, rhythm, emphasis);
- word-level revision (diction, precision, filler);
- proofreading (grammar, mechanics, consistency).

This ordering is the single most important rule. Large changes invalidate small ones, so do large changes first. Small changes rarely invalidate large ones.

### Give Each Pass A Single Lens

A pass works best when it looks for one class of problem. A pass that tries to fix structure, voice, and commas simultaneously will do all three poorly, because each problem requires a different reading speed and a different attention.

Define each pass by its question:

- Structural pass: does each section earn its place and appear in the right order?
- Evidence pass: is every claim supported and every piece of evidence used?
- Voice pass: is the stance consistent and the tone appropriate?
- Line pass: is each sentence clear, and does it carry the right emphasis?
- Proofreading pass: are there mechanical errors?

When you notice a different problem during a pass, note it and return to it in its own pass. Do not context-switch mid-pass unless the problem blocks the current pass.

### Match Pass Depth To Draft Stage

A first draft needs heavy developmental revision and almost no line editing. A late draft needs the opposite. Calibrate how deep each pass goes based on how settled the larger decisions are.

If the structure is still unstable, do not spend a pass choosing between synonyms; the sentence may not survive. If the structure is locked and the argument is sound, a deep line pass is exactly right. Misjudging stage is a major source of wasted effort and of premature polish that resists later change.

### Budget Effort By Leverage, Not By Length

A short introduction that frames the whole piece deserves more revision time per word than a long middle section that simply supplies evidence. Allocate effort by how much a passage affects the reader's overall experience, not by how many words it contains.

High-leverage targets usually include:

- the title and opening, which set the reader contract;
- the controlling claim or thesis;
- section openings and endings, which carry movement;
- the final paragraph, which the reader remembers most;
- any passage where the reader is likely to get confused or skeptical.

Spend disproportionately on these. A perfectly polished body paragraph buried in the middle adds less than a slightly improved opening.

### Separate Generation From Evaluation

Each revision pass has two sub-modes: generating options and evaluating them. Conflating them produces timid revision. When revising a weak passage, first produce two or three alternative versions without judging them, then evaluate which best serves the piece.

This separation matters most for high-stakes passages. Generating under evaluation suppresses bold restructurings. Evaluating during generation produces premature commitment. Keep the modes distinct within a pass.

### Preserve A Recoverable Draft History

Before a major pass, keep the previous version recoverable. Structural revision is destructive: you may cut a section that later turns out to belong elsewhere, or rewrite an opening that was better than the replacement.

Recoverability is not nostalgia. It is what allows decisive cutting and rewriting, because nothing is truly lost. Without it, writers hedge and preserve weak material out of fear. Version the draft at the start of each significant pass so you can compare and, if necessary, roll back.

### Know When A Pass Is Complete

A pass is complete when its question is answered for the whole draft, not when you feel tired of it. Define completion criteria per pass:

- structural pass: every section has a clear job and appears in the right order;
- evidence pass: every claim is matched to appropriate support;
- line pass: every sentence has been read once for clarity and emphasis;
- proofreading pass: the draft has been read in a fresh-eyes mode at least once.

Moving to the next pass before the current one is complete leaves holes that compound. Resist the urge to "move on" because the easy parts are done.

## Common Traps

### Polishing Before Restructuring

The most expensive trap. Sentence-level polish on a section that will be cut or moved is pure waste, and worse, it creates attachment that makes the cut harder. Always confirm structure before investing in prose.

### Treating Revision As One Undifferentiated Pass

Reading through and "fixing things as I notice them" feels productive but skips the highest-leverage problems, which require deliberate attention. Undifferentiated revision improves the visible surface and leaves the buried structure weak.

### Skipping The Diagnosis

Jumping straight into editing without a revision map leads to fixing what is easy rather than what matters. The diagnosis is what aligns effort with leverage; skipping it is the root cause of disordered revision.

### Too Many Passes, Too Shallow

Planning eight passes and skimming each is worse than planning four and doing each thoroughly. Each pass needs enough depth to actually resolve its class of problem. Depth beats pass count.

### Editing In The Wrong Mode

Trying to generate new material while in a critical proofreading mindset produces stiff, cautious prose. Trying to proofread while in a generative mindset misses errors. Match your mental mode to the pass, and do not mix them.

### Mistaking Movement For Progress

Changing words and rearranging commas feels like work but may not improve the reader's experience. Measure progress against the diagnosis and the pass's completion criteria, not against the number of edits made.

### Never Declaring A Draft Done

Endless revision without a stopping rule produces diminishing returns and erodes confidence. Define what "done" means for the draft's purpose and audience, and respect it.

## Self-Check

Before treating the revision plan as complete, verify:

- A written diagnosis exists and identifies the highest-leverage problems, not just the easiest ones.
- Passes are sequenced from largest leverage (structural) to smallest (mechanical), with no inversion.
- Each pass has a single, clearly defined lens and a stated completion criterion.
- Pass depth is calibrated to draft stage: heavy developmental work early, heavy line work late.
- Effort is budgeted by leverage, with disproportionate attention to title, opening, claim, and ending.
- Generation and evaluation are kept separate within high-stakes passes.
- The previous draft version is recoverable before each destructive pass.
- No pass was abandoned early because the easy parts were finished.
- The plan distinguishes structural, developmental, line, and proofreading work rather than merging them; the number of passes matches the draft's needs, not an arbitrary count
- A stopping rule exists so the draft can actually be declared done; the plan would still make sense if the agent returned to it after a break
