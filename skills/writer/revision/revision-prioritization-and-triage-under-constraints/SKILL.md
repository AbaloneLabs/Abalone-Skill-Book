# Revision Prioritization And Triage Under Constraints

Revision in the abstract is infinite: a piece can always be improved, and a sufficiently attentive editor can find something to change in any draft. But revision in practice is constrained by time, word count, deadline, and the diminishing returns of further effort. The skill of revision prioritization is deciding which issues to fix first, which to defer, and which to accept, given the constraints of the situation. A writer who revises everything equally under a tight deadline revises nothing well; a writer who triages effectively ships a stronger piece because the highest-impact issues receive the attention they deserve while the lower-impact issues are accepted or deferred. Triage is not a compromise or a failure of thoroughness; it is the discipline of allocating finite revision effort to maximize the piece's quality.

Agents tend to miss that revision must be prioritized by impact, that structural issues must be fixed before line-level issues or the line work is wasted, that not all feedback deserves equal weight, and that "good enough to ship" is a legitimate and necessary judgment rather than a surrender. The harm is a revision that polishes prose while the structure is broken, that addresses every piece of feedback equally and loses the piece's voice, or that never ships because it is never finished.

Use this skill when revising under constraints, triaging feedback, deciding revision priorities, or judging when a draft is good enough. The goal is to allocate revision effort to maximize quality under real constraints, shipping the strongest piece the situation allows.

## Core Rules

### Revise Top-Down: Structure Before Sentence

Revision has a natural order, from the highest level to the lowest, because changes at higher levels invalidate work at lower levels. If you revise sentences and then discover the section must be cut, the sentence revision was wasted. If you revise paragraphs and then discover the argument must be restructured, the paragraph revision was wasted. Always revise from the top down: structure first, then section and paragraph, then sentence and word.

The order is: first address the piece's purpose, thesis, and overall structure (does it deliver on its promise? are the sections in the right order? is anything missing or redundant?). Then address section-level logic and paragraph coherence (does each section serve the structure? does each paragraph build the section?). Then address sentence-level clarity, rhythm, and precision. Only at the end address word choice, grammar, and mechanics. Under time constraints, you may not reach the lowest levels, and that is correct: a structurally sound piece with imperfect sentences is stronger than a perfectly polished piece with a broken structure. Prioritize the level where the most impactful problems live, and work downward as time allows.

### Triage Issues By Impact On The Reader

Not all revision issues are equal. Some issues, if unfixed, will cause the reader to misunderstand, disengage, or reject the piece. Other issues, if unfixed, will slightly diminish a piece that is otherwise effective. Triage issues by their impact on the reader's experience, addressing high-impact issues first and accepting or deferring low-impact issues.

High-impact issues include: a broken structure that confuses the reader, a missing thesis that leaves the reader without a point, a factual error that undermines credibility, a tone mismatch that alienates the audience, and a missing key argument that leaves the reader unconvinced. These must be fixed regardless of time constraints, because an unfixed high-impact issue sinks the piece. Medium-impact issues include: a weak transition, a redundant paragraph, an imprecise word, an uneven pace. These should be fixed if time allows. Low-impact issues include: a stylistic preference, a minor word choice, a formatting nuance. These can be accepted or deferred. Classify each identified issue by impact and revise in impact order, so the highest-impact issues are guaranteed attention even if time runs out.

### Weight Feedback By Source And Alignment, Not By Volume

When receiving feedback from multiple sources, the temptation is to address every note equally, treating each as a required fix. This produces a piece that satisfies every commenter's preference but loses its coherence and voice. Feedback must be weighted by the source's authority, their alignment with the target audience, and the consistency of the note across sources, not by the mere fact that it was given.

Weight feedback by: consistency (if multiple independent readers flag the same issue, it is likely real; if one reader flags something no one else noticed, it may be idiosyncratic), audience alignment (a reader who represents the target audience's perspective has more authority on audience reaction than one who does not), expertise (an expert's feedback on factual accuracy outweighs a lay reader's), and the nature of the note (a note about confusion or disbelief is more weighty than a note about preference). Address consistent, audience-aligned, expertise-backed notes first. Push back on or discard notes that are idiosyncratic, preference-based, or misaligned with the piece's purpose. The goal is not to satisfy all feedback but to use feedback to identify real problems, then solve those problems in the way that best serves the piece.

### Distinguish Revisable Problems From Acceptable Imperfections

Not every imperfection is a revisable problem. Some imperfections are acceptable: they slightly diminish the piece but fixing them would cost more than the improvement is worth, or the "fix" would introduce new problems. The discipline is distinguishing imperfections that must be revised from those that can be accepted, given the constraints.

An imperfection is revisable if it affects the reader's comprehension, credibility, or engagement. An imperfection is acceptable if it is a matter of preference, if the fix would distort the piece's voice or structure, or if the revision effort exceeds the improvement value. For example, a sentence that is slightly awkward but comprehensible may be acceptable if revising it would require restructuring a paragraph that otherwise works. A factual error is never acceptable; a stylistic preference is often acceptable. Make this judgment consciously rather than defaulting to either "fix everything" (which never finishes) or "ship it as is" (which misses real problems). Document the accepted imperfections so they are conscious choices rather than oversights.

### Sequence Revision In Passes To Maintain Focus

Revision is more effective when conducted in focused passes, each addressing a single type of issue, rather than trying to fix everything in a single read-through. A single-pass revision scatters attention across structure, sentences, and words, addressing none deeply. A multi-pass revision allows full attention on one level per pass, catching more issues at each level.

Sequence passes from top to bottom: a structural pass (purpose, thesis, section order, completeness), a logical pass (argument flow, evidence, transitions), a paragraph pass (coherence, topic sentences, development), a sentence pass (clarity, rhythm, variety, precision), and a proofreading pass (grammar, mechanics, typos). Under time constraints, compress the passes but maintain the order: a quick structural pass, a combined logical-and-paragraph pass, and a sentence-and-proofreading pass. The key is that each pass has a defined focus, so the reviser is not trying to catch everything at once. Multi-pass revision feels slower but catches more, because focused attention sees what scattered attention misses.

### Judge "Good Enough To Ship" Against The Purpose And Audience

The question "is it good enough?" is not answerable in the abstract; it is answerable only against the piece's purpose and audience. Good enough for a blog post is not good enough for a legal contract. Good enough for a sympathetic audience is not good enough for a skeptical one. Judge completion against the specific standards the piece must meet.

Ask: does the piece achieve its purpose for its audience? Does it deliver the information, persuasion, or experience the reader came for? Are the high-impact issues resolved? Are the factual claims accurate and supported? Is the structure clear and the prose comprehensible? If yes, the piece is good enough to ship, even if medium and low-impact issues remain. The standard is not perfection but adequacy to the purpose. A piece that meets its purpose, with high-impact issues resolved, is complete, even if a further revision pass could improve it. Holding a piece to a standard of perfection, beyond what the purpose requires, is a form of procrastination that prevents shipping. Ship the piece that meets its purpose, and reserve further revision for pieces where the purpose demands higher quality.

### Recognize Diminishing Returns And Stop

Revision has diminishing returns: the first pass yields large improvements, the second pass yields smaller improvements, and subsequent passes yield increasingly marginal changes while consuming equal time. Recognizing the point of diminishing returns, and stopping there, is essential to efficient revision.

Track the yield of each revision pass. If a pass is finding and fixing significant issues, continue. If a pass is finding only minor issues or restyling sentences without meaningful improvement, the returns have diminished and further revision is not the best use of time. This is especially true under deadline: the hour spent on a fifth revision pass could be better spent on a new piece, on rest, or on a different task. Stopping at diminishing returns is not laziness; it is the recognition that revision effort, like any resource, should be allocated where it yields the most value. The piece that has been revised past the point of diminishing returns is not meaningfully better than the piece that stopped there, and the time spent is lost to other work.

## Common Traps

### Revising Bottom-Up: Polishing Sentences Before Fixing Structure

Line-level revision of a structurally broken piece is wasted when the structure changes. Revise top-down.

### Treating All Feedback As Equally Weighty

Addressing every note equally produces a piece that satisfies commenters but loses coherence. Weight feedback by consistency, audience alignment, and expertise.

### Trying To Fix Everything And Never Shipping

Perfection is not the standard; adequacy to purpose is. A piece that meets its purpose with high-impact issues resolved is good enough to ship.

### Single-Pass Revision That Scatters Attention

Trying to fix structure, paragraphs, and sentences in one read-through catches less than focused multi-pass revision. Sequence passes by level.

### Defaulting To "Fix Everything" Or "Ship As Is"

Both defaults abdicate judgment. Distinguish revisable problems from acceptable imperfections and decide consciously.

### Confusing Preference Notes With Problem Notes

A preference (how the reader would write it) is not a problem (what is wrong with the piece). Address problems; consider preferences but do not treat them as required.

### Revising Past The Point Of Diminishing Returns

A fifth pass finding only minor issues is not worth the time. Recognize diminishing returns and allocate effort to higher-value work.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Is revision sequenced top-down (structure, then section/paragraph, then sentence/word), so that line-level work is not invalidated by structural changes?
- [ ] Have identified issues been triaged by impact on the reader, with high-impact issues (broken structure, missing thesis, factual errors) addressed first regardless of time constraints?
- [ ] Has feedback been weighted by consistency across sources, audience alignment, and expertise, rather than addressed equally, with idiosyncratic or preference-based notes set aside?
- [ ] Have revisable problems been distinguished from acceptable imperfections, with the distinction made consciously and documented rather than defaulted?
- [ ] Is revision conducted in focused passes by level (structural, logical, paragraph, sentence, proofreading), maintaining attention on one type of issue per pass?
- [ ] Is "good enough to ship" judged against the piece's specific purpose and audience, with the standard being adequacy to purpose rather than perfection?
- [ ] Has the point of diminishing returns been recognized, with revision stopped when passes yield only marginal improvements?
- [ ] Under constraints, are the highest-impact issues guaranteed attention, even if lower levels receive less polish?
