# Revision Readiness And Completion Judgment

Two of the hardest judgments in writing are knowing when a draft is ready to be revised and knowing when a revision is complete. These are not mechanical determinations; they are judgments about the state of the work, the writer's readiness to see it clearly, and the balance between improvement and overworking. A draft that is revised before it is ready suffers, because the writer is still in drafting mode, protective of the words they just produced, and unable to see the draft's problems objectively. A revision that never ends suffers, because the writer cannot distinguish productive improvement from anxious tinkering, and the piece is either never shipped or shipped after being worked past its best state. The skill is judging readiness, the moment a draft is cool enough to revise productively, and completion, the moment a revision has done its work and further effort will not improve the piece.

Agents tend to miss that readiness requires distance from the draft, that completion is a judgment rather than a feeling, that anxious tinkering mimics productive revision but degrades the piece, and that the inability to finish is often a symptom of uncertainty about the piece's purpose rather than insufficient revision. The harm is a revision that is too early and too protective, or a revision that never ends and polishes the life out of the piece.

Use this skill when deciding if a draft is ready to revise, judging if a revision is complete, distinguishing productive revision from tinkering, or deciding when to stop and ship. The goal is to revise at the right time and stop at the right time, shipping a piece that has been improved but not overworked.

## Core Rules

### Let The Draft Cool Before Revising

A freshly written draft is too hot to revise. The writer is still in the generative mode that produced it, emotionally attached to the words as written, and unable to see the draft as a reader rather than as the author. Revising a hot draft produces protective revision: the writer defends the existing words rather than evaluating them, makes small changes rather than structural ones, and cannot see the draft's real problems because they are too close to its creation.

Let the draft cool by creating distance between drafting and revision. The distance can be temporal (a day, a week, long enough that the writer has forgotten the exact words and can read them fresh), contextual (working on something else in between, so the writer's attention has moved), or modal (reading the draft in a different format, like print instead of screen, or reading aloud, which changes perception). The goal of cooling is to shift from writer's perspective to reader's perspective, to see the draft as something received rather than something produced. A cool draft reveals its problems; a hot draft conceals them. If you must revise a hot draft (under deadline), at least change context: read it in a different format, or read it aloud, to force a shift in perspective. But whenever possible, let the draft cool before revising, because the distance is what enables objectivity.

### Diagnose Readiness By The Writer's Ability To See Problems

A draft is ready to revise when the writer can see its problems, not just its intentions. The test of readiness is whether the writer, reading the draft, can identify what is not working without immediately defending it. If the writer reads the draft and thinks "this is exactly what I meant, it's fine," the draft is not ready (or the writer is not ready). If the writer reads the draft and thinks "this section doesn't earn its conclusion, and this transition is confusing," the draft is ready.

Readiness is a property of the writer's relationship to the draft, not just the draft's quality. A draft can be structurally complete but not ready to revise because the writer is too attached. A draft can be rough but ready to revise because the writer can see its problems clearly. Diagnose readiness by self-honesty: can you identify three specific problems in the draft right now? If yes, you are ready to revise. If you can only see the draft's virtues, or if every problem you identify you immediately explain away, you need more distance. Do not confuse the desire to revise (eagerness to improve) with readiness to revise (ability to see what needs improving).

### Define Completion Criteria Before Judging Completion

"Is it done?" is unanswerable without criteria. Completion is not a feeling of satisfaction (which may never arrive) or the absence of anything to change (which never arrives, because a piece can always be changed). Completion is the fulfillment of defined criteria: the conditions the piece must meet to serve its purpose. Define these criteria before judging completion, so the judgment is against standards rather than against an elusive feeling of "done."

Completion criteria may include: the piece delivers its thesis or purpose, the structure is clear and logical, the key arguments are supported, the high-impact issues identified in revision are resolved, the prose is clear and comprehensible, and the factual claims are accurate. Define criteria specific to the piece and its purpose: a persuasive essay must be convincing; a procedure must be followable; a narrative must deliver its emotional arc. Judge completion against these criteria, not against a feeling. If the criteria are met, the piece is complete, even if the writer feels it could be better. If the criteria are not met, the piece is not complete, even if the writer feels tired of it. Criteria make completion a judgment rather than a mood.

### Distinguish Productive Revision From Anxious Tinkering

Productive revision improves the piece: it resolves problems, clarifies meaning, strengthens structure, and sharpens prose. Anxious tinkering mimics revision but does not improve the piece: it restyles sentences that already work, swaps synonyms without purpose, and adjusts what is already adequate. The writer engaged in anxious tinkering feels busy and productive but is not improving the piece, and may be degrading it by replacing strong, direct prose with overworked, self-conscious prose.

Distinguish the two by the nature of the changes. Productive revision addresses identified problems: "this transition is confusing, I need to clarify it." Anxious tinkering addresses preferences: "I wonder if this word is better than that word." Productive revision is motivated by the piece's needs; anxious tinkering is motivated by the writer's anxiety (fear of judgment, perfectionism, inability to let go). To distinguish, ask of each change: what problem does this solve? If the change solves an identified problem, it is productive. If the change is a preference or a "let me see if I can make it better" without an identified problem, it is likely tinkering. Productive revision has a stopping point (when the problems are solved); anxious tinkering does not (because there are always more preferences to indulge).

### Recognize The Symptoms Of Overworking

A piece that has been revised past its best state shows symptoms of overworking: the prose becomes self-conscious and mannered, replacing direct expression with elaborate construction; the voice becomes homogenized, losing its distinctiveness through repeated smoothing; the energy diminishes, as spontaneous vitality is polished into correctness; and the writer begins to second-guess choices that were originally confident. Overworking does not improve the piece; it degrades it, producing a piece that is correct but lifeless.

Recognize overworking by watching for these symptoms. If the prose is becoming more elaborate rather than more direct, the revision is overworking. If the voice is flattening, losing its texture, the revision is overworking. If you are changing things back and forth (revising a sentence, then revising it back to the original), you are in the tinkering zone. When you recognize overworking, stop. The piece was likely better at an earlier revision state, and further effort is degrading rather than improving. If possible, compare the current version to an earlier draft: if the earlier draft has more energy and directness, restore it and revise only the specific problems, preserving the vitality that overworking eroded.

### Use External Readers To Test Completion

The writer is the worst judge of their own completion, because they cannot read the piece as a first-time reader. They know what they meant, so they cannot tell if the meaning is clear; they know the structure, so they cannot tell if it is confusing. External readers, who encounter the piece fresh, can test completion by reporting whether the piece works for them.

Use external readers strategically to test specific completion criteria. Give them the piece and ask targeted questions: "Could you identify the main point?" "Was there a moment you felt confused or wanted to stop reading?" "Did the conclusion feel earned?" Their answers test whether the piece meets its criteria for a fresh reader. If readers consistently report confusion at the same point, the piece is not complete. If readers understand, engage, and are satisfied, the piece is likely complete. Do not ask readers "is it done?" (they cannot judge your standards), but ask them about their experience, from which you can judge whether the piece meets its completion criteria. External readers break the writer's solipsism and provide the reader-perspective data that completion judgment requires.

### Separate Completion From Confidence

A piece can be complete without the writer feeling confident about it, and a writer can feel confident about a piece that is not complete. Completion and confidence are independent: completion is a judgment against criteria, confidence is an emotion about the work. Do not wait for confidence to declare completion, because confidence may never arrive, and do not assume confidence means completion, because confidence can be misplaced.

Judge completion against criteria, not against confidence. If the criteria are met, the piece is complete, even if you feel uncertain. The uncertainty is often about the piece's reception (will the reader like it?), which is not within the writer's control and is not a completion criterion. Ship the complete piece and let the reception be what it will be. Conversely, if you feel confident but the criteria are not met (a key argument is unsupported, a section is confusing), the piece is not complete regardless of your confidence. Use criteria to override both false confidence and paralyzing uncertainty, judging completion by standards rather than feelings.

## Common Traps

### Revising A Hot Draft

A freshly written draft is defended rather than evaluated. Let the draft cool to gain the reader's perspective.

### Confusing Eagerness To Revise With Readiness To Revise

Eagerness is the desire to improve; readiness is the ability to see problems. Diagnose readiness by whether you can identify specific problems without defending them.

### Judging Completion By Feeling Rather Than Criteria

"Does it feel done?" is unanswerable. Define completion criteria and judge against them.

### Anxious Tinkering Disguised As Productive Revision

Tinkering restyles what works without solving problems. Ask of each change: what problem does this solve?

### Overworking The Prose Into Lifelessness

Self-conscious, mannered prose and flattened voice are symptoms of overworking. Recognize them and stop, or restore an earlier, more vital draft.

### Waiting For Confidence Before Shipping

Confidence is an emotion, not a completion standard. Ship the piece that meets its criteria, regardless of how you feel about it.

### Assuming The Writer Can Judge Completion Alone

The writer cannot read as a first-time reader. Use external readers to test whether the piece works for a fresh audience.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Has the draft been allowed to cool before revision, through temporal, contextual, or modal distance, so the writer can see it as a reader rather than as the author?
- [ ] Has readiness been diagnosed by the writer's ability to identify specific problems without defending them, rather than by eagerness to revise?
- [ ] Have completion criteria been defined specific to the piece's purpose (thesis delivered, structure clear, arguments supported, high-impact issues resolved), against which completion is judged?
- [ ] Has each revision change been tested for whether it solves an identified problem (productive) or indulges a preference (anxious tinkering)?
- [ ] Are symptoms of overworking (self-conscious prose, flattened voice, back-and-forth changes) recognized, and is revision stopped or an earlier draft restored when they appear?
- [ ] Have external readers been used to test specific completion criteria, asking about their experience rather than asking "is it done?"
- [ ] Is completion judged against criteria rather than against confidence, with the piece shipped when criteria are met regardless of the writer's feelings?
- [ ] Is the distinction between completion (criteria met) and confidence (emotion about the work) maintained, so that neither false confidence nor paralyzing uncertainty overrides the criteria-based judgment?
