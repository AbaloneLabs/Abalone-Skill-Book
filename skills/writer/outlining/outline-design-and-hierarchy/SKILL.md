# Outline Design And Hierarchy

An outline is a logic test, not a typing aid. Its real value is that it lets you see the skeleton of a piece before you have invested in prose, when rearranging, cutting, and rethinking are cheap. A draft is expensive to restructure; an outline is cheap. The agent who skips outlining, or who outlines only by listing topics, forfeits the chance to find structural failures early and instead discovers them after hundreds of words are already written.

The judgment problem is that agents tend to outline by topic rather than by move. They produce a list of headings ("Background," "Analysis," "Conclusion") and call it an outline, but such a list cannot be tested for logic because it contains no logic. A real outline encodes the argument's progression: each point follows from the previous one, each level of detail carries a defined purpose, and the whole can be read as a compressed version of the piece. The hierarchy is not decoration; it is the structure of the reasoning.

Use this skill when planning any piece whose logic must hold: essays, reports, arguments, guides, proposals, talks, and long-form articles. The goal is to prevent the agent from drafting on a foundation whose weaknesses a ten-minute outline would have exposed.

## Core Rules

### Outline Moves, Not Topics

The single most important rule: each outline point should state a move, not name a topic. "Background" is a topic. "Establish that the current system silently penalizes new users" is a move. A move tells the reader what change occurs at that point; a topic only tells the reader what the section is about.

Test each point by asking what the reader understands after it that they did not understand before. If the answer is "more information about X," the point is a topic. Rewrite it as the specific claim, transition, or development it performs. Outlines built from moves can be read as arguments; outlines built from topics cannot.

### Choose The Right Level Of Detail For The Task

Outlines serve different purposes at different granularities. Match the level of detail to what you are testing. A high-level outline (major sections only) tests the macro structure and the organizing principle. A mid-level outline (sections plus their key points) tests whether each section has a coherent internal progression. A detailed outline (down to paragraph moves or example placement) tests whether the argument actually holds at the sentence level.

Do not over-detail early. A premature detailed outline freezes decisions before the macro logic is settled. Start coarse, confirm the spine, then drill down only where the logic is fragile or contested.

### Make Hierarchy Carry Meaning

Hierarchy is not just indentation. Each level of an outline should represent a distinct kind of decision. A common and useful convention:

- Level one: the major moves or sections of the piece.
- Level two: the sub-moves within each section.
- Level three: the evidence, examples, or specific claims that support each sub-move.

Keep the meaning of each level consistent across the whole outline. If level two means "sub-argument" in one section and "example" in another, the hierarchy stops communicating structure and becomes noise. Decide what each level means and hold to it.

### Use The Outline To Test Logic Before Drafting

The point of outlining is to find failures while they are cheap to fix. Once you have a move-based outline, run logic tests on it before writing prose:

- Read only the level-one points. Do they form a coherent progression?
- For each point, ask: does this follow from the previous point, or does it assume something not yet established?
- Identify any point that depends on a later point. These are forward references that will confuse readers.
- Look for two points that make the same move. One is likely redundant.
- Check whether removing any single point breaks the chain. If none are load-bearing, the outline is a list, not a structure.

Fix the outline until it passes these tests. Drafting on a tested outline is dramatically faster and produces fewer structural rewrites.

### Pick A Format That Matches The Piece

Different formats suit different kinds of work. Choose deliberately rather than defaulting.

- Alphanumeric outlines (I, A, 1, a) suit formal, hierarchical arguments where levels matter.
- Bullet hierarchies suit flexible planning and rapid revision.
- Sentence outlines, where each point is a full sentence stating a claim, suit arguments because they force precision and expose gaps.
- Topic outlines, where each point is a phrase, suit quick capture but hide logical weakness.
- Index-card or sticky-note outlines suit rearrangement-heavy work like narrative and long-form nonfiction.

Sentence outlines are the most demanding and the most diagnostic. If you cannot write each point as a complete sentence, you do not yet know what the point is.

### Ensure Each Level Earns Its Depth

Every sub-point should be necessary to its parent, and every parent should require its sub-points. A section with one lonely sub-point probably does not need the sub-level. A section with ten sub-points probably needs to be split into two sections. Watch for hierarchy that exists only because the writer had more to say, not because the structure requires it.

Audit depth by collapsing the outline: hide the lowest level and read the level above. If the higher level still makes complete sense, the hidden level may be detail rather than structure. If the higher level becomes vague or unsupported, the hidden level is load-bearing.

### Make The Outline Reversible Into A Summary

A well-designed outline should be collapsible into a summary and expandable back into full structure. If you delete the lowest level, the remaining points should read as a coherent shorter piece. If you delete the next level, what remains should still be a recognizable skeleton. This reversibility is the hallmark of a hierarchy that genuinely carries meaning rather than one that merely sorts material.

If collapsing a level destroys coherence, that level was doing structural work that the higher level fails to signal. Either promote the work or make the higher level carry it explicitly.

### Outline The Counter-Structure Too

For argumentative pieces, outline not only your own case but the objections and alternatives a reader would raise. Building a counter-structure forces you to find the weakest joints in your own argument before a hostile reader does. Note where each objection belongs, where it should be answered, and whether the answer requires restructuring.

## Common Traps

### The Topic List Disguised As An Outline

An outline of headings without moves cannot be tested for logic and provides no protection against structural failure. It looks like planning but delivers none of its value.

### Over-Detailing Before The Spine Is Set

Spending effort on fine-grained points before the macro logic is confirmed wastes work, because changes at the top invalidate the detail below. Confirm the spine first.

### Inconsistent Hierarchy

When indentation depth means different things in different sections, the outline stops communicating structure. Readers, including the writer returning later, cannot trust the levels.

### Treating The Outline As A Contract Never To Be Revised

An outline is a hypothesis about structure, not a vow. Discoveries during drafting should update it. An outline treated as immutable becomes a cage that distorts the emerging piece.

### Outline Points That Cannot Be Sentences

If a point resists being written as a complete sentence, the writer does not yet know what move it performs. Phrase outlines hide this ignorance; sentence outlines expose it.

### Hiding Logical Gaps Behind Detail

Piling sub-points under a weak parent point can create the illusion of support where none exists. Test by reading parents alone, without their children.

## Self-Check

Before treating the outline as ready for drafting, verify:

- Each outline point states a move, not merely a topic.
- The level of detail matches what is being tested and is not prematurely fine-grained.
- Each level of hierarchy has a consistent meaning across the whole outline.
- Reading only the top level reveals a coherent progression.
- No point depends on material introduced later without an intentional, flagged forward reference.
- No two points perform the same move.
- Removing any single point would weaken the chain, confirming the outline is structural, not a list.
- The format chosen matches the kind of piece and the kind of testing needed; each level earns its depth; no level is a lone child or an overstuffed parent
- Collapsing the outline to a higher level still yields a coherent shorter piece; for argumentative work, major objections and their placement are outlined
- The outline could be read aloud as a compressed version of the piece's logic; any point that cannot be written as a complete sentence has been revised until it can
