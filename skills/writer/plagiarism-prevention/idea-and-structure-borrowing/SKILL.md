# Idea And Structure Borrowing

Plagiarism is often imagined as copied words, but ideas and structures can be stolen too, and this form of borrowing is harder to see and easier to excuse. A writer who would never paste another's sentences may still lift an argument's sequence, a framework's categories, a metaphor that organizes a field, or an original interpretation, and present these as their own thinking. Because no words match, the writer feels safe. But the intellectual contribution, the part that took real thought to produce, has been taken without credit. Idea and structure plagiarism is the invisible majority of plagiarism, and agents who reason only about wording will miss it entirely.

The judgment problem is that influence is unavoidable and, in moderation, desirable. Every writer builds on prior thought, and the line between legitimate influence and theft is genuinely hard to draw. Common knowledge needs no attribution; a distinctive intellectual contribution does. A genre's conventional structure is free to use; a specific author's original arrangement may not be. An agent that draws these lines carelessly tends either to over-attribute trivia, cluttering prose with unnecessary citations, or to under-attribute real contributions, passing off others' thinking as its own. The craft is recognizing what is genuinely original in a source, the part that cost the author real work, and ensuring that contribution is credited while freely using what belongs to the shared commons.

Use this skill when synthesizing ideas from multiple sources, adapting a framework or model, following another writer's analytical structure, or deciding whether a concept is common knowledge or attributable. The goal is borrowing that is honest about what came from where.

## Core Rules

### Credit The Intellectual Contribution, Not Just The Words

The part of a source that deserves attribution is its intellectual contribution: the original idea, interpretation, framework, argument, or synthesis that the author created through effort. Words are only the surface. If you take the underlying thinking and re-express it in entirely new prose without credit, you have still taken the contribution. The test is not whether your sentences resemble the source but whether your thinking does.

Ask, for each idea in your draft:

- Did this idea, distinction, or framing originate with a specific source?
- Is it the source's distinctive contribution, or something it merely reported?
- Could a reader reasonably assume this is my original thinking?
- If the source had not existed, would I have arrived at this point?

When the answer points to a source's contribution, attribute it, even if your wording is wholly your own.

### Distinguish Common Knowledge From Attributable Ideas

Common knowledge is information so widely established in a field or culture that no single source can claim it; it needs no attribution. Attributable ideas are those traceable to a specific originator whose contribution should be credited. The trouble is that the boundary shifts with audience and field, and writers often misjudge it.

Common knowledge typically includes:

- established historical facts and dates in general circulation;
- basic definitions found across many reference works;
- widely known scientific consensus presented at an introductory level;
- folk sayings, common metaphors, and cultural references in general use.

Attributable ideas typically include:

- a specific author's original argument, interpretation, or theory;
- a coined term, named framework, or distinctive model;
- a novel finding, statistic, or dataset;
- a specific author's selection, arrangement, or synthesis of material.

When uncertain whether something is common knowledge, attribute. The cost of citing an unnecessary source is small; the cost of stealing a real contribution is large.

### Recognize When Structure Itself Is A Contribution

Sometimes the structure of a piece, the order and relationship of its parts, is the author's real invention. A framework that divides a problem into four categories, a method that sequences steps in a particular way, or an argument that builds through a distinctive progression may be the intellectual heart of the source. Copying that structure while changing every word can still be plagiarism of the contribution.

Test structural borrowing by asking:

- Is this structure conventional for the genre, or was it the author's original choice?
- Does the structure carry the source's analytical logic, or merely its topic?
- Would following this structure reproduce the source's reasoning, not just its subject?
- Is the arrangement itself what makes the source valuable?

When the structure is the contribution, name its origin rather than silently adopting it.

### Trace Influence Back To Its Source

Influence often arrives indirectly: an idea encountered in a secondary source that itself drew on a primary one, a framework absorbed from a teacher who learned it from its originator. Honest attribution follows the idea back to its origin where feasible, rather than crediting the most recent or convenient source. Citing the textbook that summarized a theorist, when you could cite the theorist, can obscure the real contribution and shortchange the originator.

Practices:

- when a secondary source sparks an idea, trace it to the primary where you can;
- credit the originator of a concept, not only the popularizer;
- when you cannot locate the origin, acknowledge the chain you do know;
- do not let convenience determine attribution.

Generosity in tracing influence is a mark of intellectual honesty.

### Separate Genre Convention From Original Arrangement

Not every structural similarity is theft. Genres carry conventions: a lab report follows a standard format, a how-to article sequences steps predictably, a news story leads with the most important facts. Using these conventions is not borrowing; it is participating in a shared form. Plagiarism of structure begins where the arrangement is original to a specific author and not dictated by convention.

Distinguish:

- genre-mandated structure, which is free to use;
- logical structure dictated by the subject matter, which is largely shared;
- an author's original, non-obvious arrangement, which deserves credit;
- a distinctive framing or lens that organizes the material in a new way.

When the structure flows naturally from the topic or the form, no attribution is needed. When it reflects a specific author's creative choice, it does.

### Acknowledge Direct And Indirect Influence Honestly

Some of the hardest cases are not direct borrowing but deep influence: a writer whose work shaped how you think about a whole domain, a book that reorganized your mental model, a mentor whose framework underlies your approach. You may not be quoting or even paraphrasing them, but your thinking is theirs in a way that matters. Honest writers acknowledge this kind of influence rather than presenting derivative thinking as self-generated.

This does not require citing every influence on every piece. It requires honesty about the major shapers of the ideas you present as your own, especially when a reader would otherwise assume originality.

### When In Doubt, Over-Attribute

The asymmetry of attribution favors generosity. An unnecessary citation is a small clutter; an uncredited contribution is a theft. When you are unsure whether an idea is common knowledge, whether a structure is original, or whether a source shaped your thinking enough to warrant a mention, err toward attributing. Writers respected for originality are often those most generous in crediting their sources, because confidence in one's own contribution does not require denying others'.

## Common Traps

### Treating Rewording As A Cure For Idea Theft

Changing every word while preserving the source's argument, framework, or interpretation feels safe because no text matches. But the intellectual contribution is unchanged, and presenting it as original is still plagiarism. The fix is attributing the idea, not just varying the language.

### Assuming Anything Without A Named Author Is Free

Writers sometimes treat material found without a clear byline, in a wiki, a lecture, or casual conversation, as unowned and free to claim. But ideas have originators even when the byline is missing, and the absence of an obvious author does not make a distinctive contribution common knowledge.

### Confusing Synthesis With Originality

Gathering ideas from several sources and arranging them in one place can feel like original work, and sometimes it is. But if the ideas and their relationships all come from sources, the synthesis is derivative and the sources deserve credit. True original synthesis adds a new connection or insight, not just a new arrangement.

### Borrowing A Framework And Calling It Inspiration

"Inspired by" is sometimes used to soften what is really direct adoption of a framework or model. Inspiration that leads to your own distinct work is fine; inspiration that reproduces another's structure under a new label is uncredited borrowing. Name the framework you are using.

### Over-Attributing Trivia To Appear Thorough

Citing common knowledge to seem rigorous clutters prose and devalues the citations that matter. Not every established fact needs a source. Reserve attribution for genuine contributions, and trust the reader to recognize shared knowledge.

### Forgetting The Originator Behind The Popularizer

A famous popularizer can overshadow the originator of an idea, and citing only the well-known version shortchanges the real contributor. When you know the origin, credit it, even if you first encountered the idea elsewhere.

## Self-Check

Before treating borrowed ideas and structures as properly handled, verify:

- Each distinctive idea, framework, or interpretation in the piece is traced to its origin and credited.
- Material treated as common knowledge is genuinely established in general circulation, not a specific contribution.
- No original structural arrangement from a source has been reproduced without attribution.
- Genre conventions and subject-mandated structures are used freely, while author-specific arrangements are credited.
- Influence has been traced back to originators where feasible, not stopped at the most convenient source.
- Major shapers of your thinking are acknowledged where a reader would otherwise assume originality.
- Synthesis that draws on sources is credited, and only genuinely new connections are presented as original.
- Coined terms, named models, and distinctive frameworks are attributed to their creators.
- "Inspired by" is not used to disguise direct adoption of another's structure; trivia is not over-cited in a way that clutters the piece and devalues real citations
- A reader could trace each non-obvious idea in the piece to its source; you have not presented another's intellectual contribution as your own thinking, even with wholly new wording
