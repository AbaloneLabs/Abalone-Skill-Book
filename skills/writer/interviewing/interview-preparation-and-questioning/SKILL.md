# Interview Preparation And Questioning

An interview fails long before it begins, in the preparation the writer skipped. The most common interview problems are not bad luck or difficult subjects; they are the predictable result of walking in without a clear purpose, without enough background, or with questions designed to fill time rather than to elicit something the writer could not have gotten otherwise. The writer who improvises comes away with vague generalities the subject has already given a hundred times, while the writer who prepares comes away with the specific, surprising, and usable material that makes a piece worth reading.

Interview preparation is a judgment problem because the goal is not to ask everything but to ask the right things, and that depends on what the piece needs, what the subject knows, and what the subject is likely to resist. An agent that treats the interview as a conversation to be managed in the moment tends to produce interviews that drift, miss the load-bearing questions, and fail to follow up on the moments where the real material appeared. The craft is designing questions that serve a defined purpose, building the rapport that lets the subject answer honestly, and knowing what the interview must accomplish before it ends.

Use this skill when preparing to interview a source, when designing a question list, when deciding between open and closed questions, when planning to build rapport, or when diagnosing why an interview produced thin material. The goal is an interview that is purposeful enough to yield material no other method could, and humane enough that the subject speaks genuinely rather than performing.

## Core Rules

### Define The Interview's Purpose Before Designing Questions

Every interview should have a purpose sharper than "learn about the subject." The purpose determines what the interview must accomplish, which determines the questions, the order, the length, and the kind of rapport required. Without a defined purpose, the interview becomes a general conversation that yields general material, and the writer leaves with nothing they could not have found in published sources.

Define the purpose by asking:

- what specific information, perspective, or quote does the piece need that only this person can provide;
- what is the single most important thing to learn from this interview;
- what would make this interview a success, and what would make it a failure;
- how does this interview fit the larger piece, and what gap does it fill.

Write the purpose in one sentence before drafting any questions. If the purpose is vague, the questions will be vaguer. A sharp purpose lets you cut questions that do not serve it, no matter how interesting they seem.

### Research The Subject And The Topic Thoroughly

Asking a subject something they have already answered publicly, or something basic research would have covered, wastes the interview and signals disrespect for the subject's time. Research the subject's background, prior statements, work, and public positions, and research the topic enough to ask informed questions. The interview should reach for what is not already known, not rehash what is available.

Research before the interview by:

- reading the subject's published work, prior interviews, and public statements;
- understanding their role, expertise, stake, and likely positions;
- learning the topic well enough to follow unexpected answers and ask informed follow-ups;
- identifying what they have not addressed, where they have shifted, or what is contested.

Thorough research lets you ask questions that surprise the subject and reach new ground. It also builds credibility: subjects engage more honestly with interviewers who have done the work, and they shut down with those who have not.

### Choose Open And Closed Questions Deliberately

Open and closed questions do different work, and the choice should be deliberate. Open questions, which cannot be answered briefly, invite the subject to think, narrate, and reveal; closed questions, which can be answered specifically, confirm facts, pin down details, and test precision. Most interviews need both, in a deliberate sequence.

Use each type by design:

- open questions to explore, such as "how did you come to that view?" or "what was that like?";
- closed questions to confirm, such as "was that in 2019?" or "how many people were in the room?";
- open questions early, to build rapport and elicit material you did not anticipate;
- closed questions later, to pin down specifics the open answers raised.

Avoid the common failure of leading closed questions dressed as open ones, such as "don't you think that was a mistake?" A real open question does not presuppose the answer. Design questions that let the subject surprise you, or you will only hear what you expected.

### Sequence Questions To Build Trust Before Difficulty

The order of questions affects what the subject will share. Beginning with hostile, probing, or technical questions before trust is established makes subjects defensive and shuts down honesty. Beginning with questions that let the subject speak about what they know and care about builds rapport and earns the latitude to ask harder questions later. Sequence with the relationship in mind.

Sequence by:

- opening with questions the subject can answer confidently and that show you have prepared;
- moving to the substantive material once the subject is engaged and comfortable;
- saving the most difficult, sensitive, or confrontational questions for after rapport is built;
- ending with room for the subject to add what you did not think to ask.

This is not manipulation; it is respect for how trust forms. A subject who feels respected and heard will answer harder questions more fully than one who was challenged before any relationship existed.

### Design Questions That Reach Specific Material

Vague questions yield vague answers. "Tell me about your work" produces a rehearsed summary; "walk me through the decision to shut down the project, and where you disagreed with the team" produces specific, usable material. Design questions that reach the concrete: specific decisions, moments, people, numbers, and feelings. Specificity in the question produces specificity in the answer.

Reach specifics by:

- asking about particular moments rather than general experiences;
- asking for the reasoning behind a decision, not just the decision itself;
- asking what the subject saw, heard, or felt at a specific point;
- asking for concrete examples when the subject speaks in abstractions;
- asking what surprised them, what they would do differently, what they disagreed with.

When a subject gives a general answer, follow with a request for a specific instance. The specific instance is almost always more useful than the generalization, and it is where vivid, quotable material lives.

### Plan For Follow-Ups, Not Just A Script

An interview is not a script to be read; it is a conversation to be guided. The most valuable material often comes from following up on something the subject said that you did not anticipate, which a rigid question list would skip past. Prepare questions, but hold them loosely enough to follow the subject where the real material is.

Plan for follow-ups by:

- treating the question list as a map of territory to cover, not a sequence to recite;
- listening for surprises, hesitations, contradictions, and emotional shifts, and following them;
- preparing follow-up probes for likely answers, especially evasions or generalities;
- leaving room in the time budget for unexpected threads to develop.

The writer who reads questions in order, regardless of answers, conducts a survey. The writer who listens and follows conducts an interview. The difference in material quality is large.

### Build Rapport As A Condition Of Honesty

Rapport is not a courtesy; it is the condition under which subjects speak honestly rather than performing a public version of themselves. People reveal more to an interviewer they trust, respect, and feel understood by, and they retreat into safe generalities with one they do not. Build rapport deliberately, through preparation, genuine curiosity, respect, and attention.

Build rapport by:

- demonstrating you have done your research and respect the subject's time and work;
- listening actively, showing the subject's answers are being received and considered;
- matching the subject's register and pace where appropriate, without flattery;
- being honest about the interview's purpose and how the material will be used;
- treating the subject as a person, not just a source of quotes.

Rapport is not friendship, and it is not manipulation. It is the professional trust that lets an interview move past rehearsed answers into genuine reflection, and it must be built honestly to be sustained.

## Common Traps

### No Defined Purpose

An interview without a sharp purpose yields general material available elsewhere. Define what the interview must accomplish before designing questions.

### Asking What Research Could Answer

Questions about basic background waste the interview and signal disrespect. Research first; ask for what only this person can provide.

### Leading Questions Disguised As Open Ones

Questions that presuppose an answer produce the answer you expected, not the truth. Design questions that let the subject surprise you.

### Difficult Questions Before Rapport

Probing or confrontational questions early in the interview make subjects defensive. Build trust before asking the hardest things.

### Vague Questions Yielding Vague Answers

General questions produce rehearsed summaries. Ask for specific moments, decisions, and examples to reach usable material.

### Reading A Script Instead Of Listening

Following a rigid question list skips the unexpected material. Hold the list loosely and follow up on surprises.

## Self-Check

Before treating interview preparation as complete, verify:

- The interview has a defined purpose, stated in one sentence, that the piece needs.
- The subject and topic have been researched thoroughly enough to ask informed questions.
- Open and closed questions are chosen deliberately, with open questions reaching for new material.
- Questions are sequenced to build trust before difficulty, not to confront early.
- Questions are designed to reach specific moments, decisions, and examples, not generalities.
- The question list is treated as a map, with room planned for follow-ups on unexpected answers.
- Rapport-building is part of the plan, through preparation, attention, and honesty about purpose.
- No question asks what basic research could have answered.
- Leading questions have been revised to allow genuine, surprising answers; the most important questions are identified, so they are reached before time runs out
- The interview's success criteria are clear enough to know when the purpose has been met; the subject will leave feeling respected and heard, not merely mined for quotes
