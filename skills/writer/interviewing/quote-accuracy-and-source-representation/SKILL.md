# Quote Accuracy And Source Representation

A quotation is a transfer of trust. The reader believes the words between the quotation marks were actually said, by the person named, in the context implied, and that responsibility falls entirely on the writer. When a quote is trimmed until it changes meaning, attributed to the wrong moment, lifted from a context that inverted it, or surrounded by framing that steers the reader toward a conclusion the speaker did not reach, the writer has not just made an error; they have borrowed a person's authority under false pretenses. The subject is misrepresented, the reader is misled, and the writer's credibility, once exposed, collapses along with the quote.

Source representation is a judgment problem because fairness is not the same as neutrality, and accuracy is not the same as completeness. A writer can quote every word correctly and still distort a source through selection, framing, or omission, and the line between sharp editing and unfair distortion is a judgment that must be made deliberately for every quote. An agent that treats quotation as mechanical extraction tends to produce representations that are technically accurate in wording but misleading in meaning, which is the most damaging form of misquotation because it is the hardest to detect and defend. The craft is representing each source's words and meaning honestly, even when the honest representation is less convenient than a sharper one.

Use this skill when writing up interview material, when selecting and framing quotations, when deciding how to represent a source fairly, when handling on- and off-record terms, or when weighing whether a use of a quote is ethical. The goal is to use sources' words in a way they would recognize as fair, even if they disagreed with the piece's conclusions.

## Core Rules

### Quote In Context, Not Just In Wording

A quotation's meaning depends on its context, and a quote stripped of context can imply the opposite of what the speaker meant. Accuracy of wording is necessary but not sufficient; the writer must also preserve the context that gives the words their meaning. A word-perfect quote placed in a framing that changes its significance is a distortion, and often a worse one than an outright misquote.

Preserve context by:

- retaining the surrounding conditions, qualifiers, and caveats the speaker attached;
- not presenting a conditional or hypothetical statement as a flat assertion;
- not separating a quote from the question or discussion that prompted it in a way that shifts meaning;
- providing enough of the occasion that the reader understands when and why it was said.

Before using a quote, ask whether the speaker would recognize their meaning in how you have presented it. If they would feel their words were turned against their intent, the context has been broken, regardless of the wording.

### Preserve Meaning Through Selection And Trimming

Selecting which words to quote and where to trim is where distortion most often enters, because every cut changes emphasis. Trimming a qualifier, dropping a hedge, or selecting the most striking phrase while omitting the surrounding caution can turn a measured statement into a sweeping one. Selection is inevitable, but it must preserve the speaker's meaning, not just their most quotable words.

Preserve meaning by:

- retaining qualifiers, hedges, and limitations that shape the claim;
- not trimming a quote until it says something stronger or simpler than the original;
- using ellipses only where the omitted material does not affect meaning;
- choosing representative quotes over cherry-picked striking ones when they diverge.

The temptation is to use the vivid line and drop the boring caveat, but the caveat is often where the meaning lives. A quote edited for impact at the cost of accuracy is a distortion, and the speaker, on reading it, will recognize the betrayal.

### Attribute Quotes To The Right Speaker And Occasion

A quotation must be attributed to the person who said it, on the occasion it was said, with the conditions that governed it. Misattribution, whether to the wrong person in a conversation, the wrong event, or the wrong on-the-record status, is a serious failure because it borrows authority under false labels. Confirm who spoke, when, and under what terms before publishing any quote.

Attribute accurately by:

- confirming the speaker, especially in group interviews or transcripts with multiple voices;
- confirming the occasion, date, and setting of the statement;
- confirming the on-the-record status and any conditions the speaker set;
- not implying a public statement was made when it was given in a private interview, or vice versa.

Attribution is part of the quote's accuracy. A quote correctly worded but attributed to the wrong occasion or status misleads the reader about its weight and provenance, and exposes the writer to legitimate complaint.

### Frame Quotes Without Distorting Them

The framing around a quote, the prose that introduces and follows it, shapes how the reader receives it. Framing can contextualize honestly or steer dishonestly, and the line is a judgment. A frame that tells the reader what to think before the quote, or that extracts a conclusion the speaker did not reach, distorts even when the quote itself is intact.

Frame honestly by:

- introducing the quote with enough context that the reader understands its occasion and purpose;
- not telegraphing a judgment that pre-disposes the reader before the quote appears;
- following the quote with interpretation that is clearly yours, not presented as the speaker's;
- not juxtaposing the quote with material that implies a meaning the speaker did not intend.

The writer is allowed to interpret, argue with, and draw conclusions from a quote. What the writer is not allowed to do is disguise their interpretation as the speaker's meaning. Keep the boundary between what the source said and what you make of it visible.

### Honor On-Record, Background, And Off-Record Terms

The terms under which a source spoke govern how their words can be used, and violating those terms is both an ethical failure and a practical one that burns sources and invites disputes. Be clear about whether material is on the record, on background, on deep background, or off the record, and use it only as agreed. When terms are ambiguous, clarify before publishing.

Honor terms by:

- confirming the on-the-record status of material before the interview or at the start;
- using background material only as agreed, without identifying the source beyond the terms;
- never publishing off-record material, even paraphrased, without explicit permission;
- returning to the source to renegotiate terms when material is too valuable to leave unused.

The writer's reputation among sources depends on honoring these terms absolutely. One violation, even for a strong quote, can close off a network of sources and damage the writer's ability to report in the future.

### Represent Disagreement And Nuance Fairly

A source's full view often includes qualifications, disagreements, or complexities that a single quote cannot capture. Reducing a nuanced source to a single line that supports your argument, while omitting their qualifications or contrary statements, misrepresents them even if the line is accurately quoted. Represent the source's meaning as a whole, not just its most usable fragment.

Represent fairly by:

- including the source's qualifications or hedges where they affect the claim;
- not omitting a source's disagreement with your framing while quoting their agreement;
- representing uncertainty the source expressed, rather than flattening it into certainty;
- giving the source's view as a coherent position, not a collection of extractable lines.

Fairness does not require abandoning your argument or giving the source veto over the piece. It requires that the source would recognize their meaning as represented, even if they disagreed with how you used it.

### Allow Sources To Clarify, Not To Control

Offering sources a chance to clarify their quotes, especially on technical or sensitive material, improves accuracy and fairness. But clarification is not the same as ceding control: the source can correct genuine errors and clarify meaning, but they do not gain editorial control over the piece or the right to retract on-the-record statements they regret. Hold the line between accuracy and control.

Allow clarification by:

- offering to check quotes for accuracy, especially on technical or high-stakes material;
- correcting genuine errors of fact or wording the source identifies;
- not allowing sources to rewrite quotes they simply wish they had phrased differently;
- being clear about what is a correction (an error) versus a retraction (a change of mind).

A source who clarifies a genuine misquote is helping the piece; a source who tries to retract a statement they made on the record is testing the writer's independence. Distinguish the two and respond differently.

## Common Traps

### Word-Perfect Quotes In Broken Context

A quote accurate in wording but stripped of context can invert meaning. Preserve the context that gives the words their significance.

### Trimming Until The Meaning Shifts

Dropping qualifiers and hedges to sharpen a quote distorts it. Retain the language that shapes the claim.

### Misattribution Of Speaker Or Occasion

A quote labeled to the wrong person, event, or on-record status borrows authority falsely. Confirm who, when, and under what terms.

### Framing That Pre-Determines The Reader's Reading

Prose around a quote that tells the reader what to think distorts even an intact quote. Interpret clearly as your own, not as the speaker's meaning.

### Violating On- And Off-Record Terms

Using material against the terms agreed is an ethical failure that burns sources. Honor the status of every piece of material.

### Cherry-Picking A Source's Convenient Lines

Quoting agreement while omitting a source's qualifications or disagreement misrepresents their whole view. Represent their meaning coherently.

### Ceding Editorial Control Through "Clarification"

Allowing sources to rewrite quotes they regret surrenders independence. Clarify errors; do not retract on-the-record statements.

## Self-Check

Before treating quote accuracy and source representation as complete, verify:

- Every quote preserves the context that gives the words their meaning, not just the wording.
- Selection and trimming retain qualifiers and hedges rather than sharpening the claim.
- Each quote is attributed to the correct speaker, occasion, and on-record status.
- Framing around quotes contextualizes honestly without pre-determining the reader's reading.
- The writer's interpretation is distinguished from the source's meaning.
- On-record, background, and off-record terms are honored exactly as agreed.
- Sources' qualifications, disagreements, and nuances are represented, not just convenient lines.
- Sources would recognize their meaning as represented, even if they disagreed with its use.
- Clarification was offered for technical or sensitive material, without ceding editorial control; no quote has been edited, trimmed, or framed in a way that changes what the speaker meant
- The ethical obligations to sources, especially vulnerable ones, have been met; the piece could withstand the subject reading their own quotes and framing
