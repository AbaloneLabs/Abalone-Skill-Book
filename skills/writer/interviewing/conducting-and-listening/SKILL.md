# Conducting And Listening

Preparation gets the writer to the interview; conducting is what happens once it begins, and it is where most interviews are won or lost. The skills are not the ones that show on a question list. They are listening closely enough to hear what was almost said, following up on the hesitation that signaled real material, capturing wording accurately enough that a quote survives verification, and holding the conversation steady when a subject becomes evasive, hostile, or emotional. A perfectly prepared interview can still fail in the conducting, because the writer was too busy thinking of the next question to hear the current answer.

Conducting is a judgment problem because the writer must hold several things at once: the question list, the purpose, the subject's comfort, the time, and the surprising material emerging in real time. An agent that treats conducting as reciting questions tends to miss the moments that matter, accept vague answers, and capture quotes imprecisely, all of which surface later as thin material and unverifiable attributions. The craft is listening actively, following the subject's lead toward the real material, and recording accurately enough that what was said can be used and defended.

Use this skill when conducting an interview, when deciding how to follow up on answers, when capturing quotes in real time, or when handling a subject who is difficult, evasive, or emotional. The goal is an interview in which the subject is heard, the material is captured accurately, and the conversation reaches what a script alone could not.

## Core Rules

### Listen More Than You Speak

The interview's value comes from the subject's words, not the interviewer's. A writer who fills silences, over-explains questions, or talks to demonstrate understanding crowds out the material the interview exists to gather. Speak enough to guide, prompt, and follow up; be quiet enough that the subject has room to think and speak at length. The ratio should favor the subject heavily.

Listen actively by:

- asking the question, then letting the subject answer without interruption;
- tolerating silence, which often prompts the subject to continue into deeper material;
- resisting the urge to fill pauses or to affirm every sentence with talk;
- giving the subject your full attention rather than reading ahead to the next question.

Silence is a tool. A subject who pauses is often about to say the more honest or more specific thing, and an interviewer who jumps in cuts it off. Let the silence hold, and the follow-up often comes unprompted.

### Follow Up On What Was Almost Said

The most valuable material often lives in what the subject almost said: the hesitation, the partial answer, the deflection, the word chosen and then replaced. A prepared question list will not catch these; only active listening will. Follow up on the signals that the real answer is nearby, even when it means leaving the planned sequence.

Follow up on:

- hesitations, qualifiers, and sudden vagueness, which often mark sensitive ground;
- contradictions with what the subject said earlier or with known facts;
- emotional shifts, where the subject's tone or affect changes;
- answers that stopped just short of the specific moment or name;
- deflections, where the subject answered a different question than the one asked.

A gentle follow-up, such as "can you say more about that?" or "what did you mean when you said it was complicated?" often opens the ground a direct question would have closed. The follow-up is where interviewing becomes a craft rather than a survey.

### Capture Quotes Accurately In Real Time

A quote that is not captured accurately at the time is lost or, worse, reconstructed inaccurately later. Memory of wording degrades within minutes, and a quote written from memory is often subtly wrong in ways that fail verification and distort meaning. Capture exact wording as the subject speaks, whether by transcription, careful note-taking, or recording, and mark clearly what is verbatim versus paraphrase.

Capture accurately by:

- recording when possible and permitted, as the most reliable capture method;
- taking verbatim notes of any phrase likely to be quoted, with quotation marks;
- noting the speaker, the context, and any nonverbal cues that shape meaning;
- distinguishing in your notes between exact words, close paraphrase, and your interpretation;
- confirming key quotes with the subject before leaving, when accuracy is uncertain.

A quote is a precision artifact. If you are not certain of the exact wording, do not present it as a direct quote; paraphrase and attribute. The cost of a misquote, to fairness and credibility, is far higher than the cost of paraphrasing what you could not capture exactly.

### Pin Down Vague Or Evasive Answers

Subjects often answer vaguely, whether from caution, habit, or evasion, and accepting the vague answer leaves the interview thin. A vague answer is an invitation to follow up, not a stopping point. Press gently but firmly for the specific: the number, the name, the moment, the reasoning behind the generalization. The specific is almost always more useful and more honest.

Pin down by:

- asking for a specific example when the subject speaks in abstractions;
- asking for the number or date when the subject gives a rough characterization;
- asking who, specifically, when the subject says "some people" or "the team";
- politely restating the question when the subject answers a different one;
- noting the evasion rather than letting it pass, sometimes by returning to it later.

Pressing does not mean hostility. A calm, specific follow-up, asked respectfully, often gets the real answer where a confrontational challenge would harden the evasion. The goal is precision, not combat.

### Handle Sensitive And Emotional Material With Care

Interviews sometimes reach grief, trauma, conflict, or personal stakes, and how the writer handles these moments affects both the material and the subject. Pushing too hard causes harm and shuts down honesty; retreating too soon leaves important material unspoken. Handle sensitive ground with respect for the subject, clarity about why the question matters, and attention to the subject's cues.

Handle sensitivity by:

- explaining why the difficult question is necessary before asking it;
- letting the subject set the pace on emotional material, without abandoning the question;
- acknowledging the weight of what the subject is sharing rather than moving past it briskly;
- being prepared to pause, take a break, or return to easier ground if the subject needs it;
- never exploiting vulnerability for vividness at the subject's expense.

Care is not avoidance. The difficult question may still need to be asked; it is asked in a way that respects the subject's humanity. A subject who feels respected through difficulty speaks more honestly than one who feels mined.

### Manage Time Without Losing The Material

Interviews run on finite time, and the writer must cover the purpose without rushing past the material that matters. Time management is a real-time judgment: when to follow an unexpected thread, when to move on, and how to ensure the most important questions are reached before the clock runs out. Manage time actively, but in service of the material, not against it.

Manage time by:

- identifying the must-ask questions and ensuring they are reached;
- following valuable unexpected threads while watching the clock;
- moving on from unproductive lines without being rude;
- leaving time at the end for the subject to add what you did not ask;
- being honest with the subject about time constraints when they arise.

The failure is not running out of time; it is reaching the end without the load-bearing questions asked. Know which questions cannot be sacrificed, and protect time for them even when interesting material competes.

### Keep The Subject Oriented To Purpose And Use

The subject deserves to understand what the interview is for and how the material will be used, both for ethical reasons and because clarity produces better answers. A subject who knows the purpose can steer toward relevant material; a subject who knows the use can speak with appropriate candor. Keep the subject oriented without over-promising or under-explaining.

Orient the subject by:

- restating the purpose at the start and confirming consent to the interview's scope;
- being honest about whether the piece is on the record, on background, or anonymous;
- explaining where and how quotes will appear, and any review or fact-check process;
- not implying promises about tone, framing, or outcome that you cannot keep.

Honesty about use protects the subject and the writer. A subject misled about how their words will appear is a credibility and ethical failure, and often a source of later dispute. Clarity up front prevents both.

## Common Traps

### Talking Over The Subject's Answers

Filling silence or affirming constantly crowds out the material. Listen more than you speak, and let pauses do their work.

### Missing The Follow-Up Signal

Hesitations, vagueness, and emotional shifts mark where the real material is. Follow up on these rather than moving to the next prepared question.

### Capturing Quotes Imprecisely

Memory degrades quickly, and reconstructed quotes are often wrong. Capture exact wording in real time or paraphrase rather than misquoting.

### Accepting Vague Answers

Generalities leave the interview thin. Press gently for the specific example, number, or name.

### Pushing Too Hard Or Retreating Too Soon On Sensitivity

Both extremes harm the material and the subject. Ask difficult questions with respect and attention to the subject's cues.

### Losing Must-Ask Questions To Time

Following interesting threads is fine, but not if the load-bearing questions go unasked. Protect time for what the piece needs.

### Misleading The Subject About Use

Implying promises about framing or anonymity that you cannot keep is an ethical failure. Be honest about purpose and use from the start.

## Self-Check

Before treating the conducting of the interview as complete, verify:

- The subject spoke substantially more than the interviewer, with silences tolerated rather than filled.
- Follow-ups were made on hesitations, vagueness, contradictions, and emotional shifts.
- Quotes were captured accurately in real time, with verbatim wording marked distinctly from paraphrase.
- Vague or evasive answers were followed up with requests for specifics.
- Sensitive and emotional material was handled with care, without abandoning necessary questions.
- The most important questions were reached before time ran out.
- The subject was kept oriented to the interview's purpose and how material will be used.
- On-the-record status and any anonymity were clearly understood by both sides.
- No quote is presented as verbatim unless it was captured exactly or confirmed; the interview reached material a prepared script alone would not have produced
- The subject was treated with respect throughout, including difficult moments; enough was captured that the piece's needs from this interview are met or clearly identified as gaps
