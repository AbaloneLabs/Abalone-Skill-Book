# Goal Setting And Success Criteria

A piece of writing without a defined goal is judged only by whether it exists and reads well, which is a standard weak enough to approve almost anything. Without success criteria decided in advance, "is it good?" becomes a matter of taste argued after the work is done, when it is too late to redirect. Strong writing starts from a concrete answer to what change the piece must create and how that change could be recognized, so that effort is spent producing the right effect rather than polishing the wrong one.

Goal-setting for writing is a judgment problem because the obvious goals are usually the wrong ones. "Inform the reader," "sound professional," and "cover the topic" are not goals; they are descriptions of activity. A real goal names the reader, the desired change, and the evidence that the change happened. An agent that skips this tends to produce drafts that are complete and competent yet purposeless, because no one defined what success would even look like.

Use this skill when starting a piece, when a brief is vague, when revising a draft whose purpose has drifted, or when evaluating whether a finished piece succeeded. The goal is to make "done" and "good" decidable before the writing is finished, not to bureaucratize creativity.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish Activity Goals From Outcome Goals

Most writing briefs confuse what the writer will do with what the piece must achieve. Activity goals describe effort ("write a guide on onboarding"); outcome goals describe effect ("reduce the number of support tickets from new users in their first week"). Only outcome goals can tell you whether the piece worked.

Separate the two explicitly:

- activity: what the writer produces (a draft, a post, a report);
- output: what the reader receives (information, a framework, an experience);
- outcome: what changes in the reader or world (a decision, a behavior, a feeling, a reduced error rate).

Aim to define the outcome, then work backward to the output and activity that can produce it. If you can only name the activity, the goal is not yet set.

### Define The Reader Change The Piece Must Create

The core of a writing goal is the change it creates in a specific reader. Without a defined change, the piece has no spine and no test. State the change as concretely as the subject allows.

Specify:

- who changes (which reader, specifically);
- from what state to what state (belief, understanding, feeling, behavior, decision);
- what evidence would show the change happened;
- what the reader can do, decide, or feel after that they could not before.

"After reading, a hiring manager can name three risks in this candidate profile they would have missed" is a goal. "Cover the topic of hiring risk" is not. The first is testable; the second is not.

### Make Success Criteria Observable Before They Are Inspirational

Success criteria must be checkable against reality, not aspirational. Vague criteria like "engage the reader" or "build trust" cannot be evaluated and therefore cannot guide decisions during drafting. Translate aspiration into observable signals.

Observable criteria types:

- behavioral: the reader takes a specific action (clicks, signs up, follows the steps, shares);
- comprehension: the reader can accurately paraphrase or apply the key claim;
- decision: the reader makes the choice the piece was designed to inform;
- emotional: the reader reports or signals a specific feeling or shift;
- avoidance: the reader stops doing something or avoids a specific error;
- relational: trust, alignment, or credibility measurably increases.

Some criteria are hard to measure directly, but they should still be imaginable. "A skeptical expert would concede the piece is fair" is observable in principle even if you cannot survey every expert.

### Set Criteria For Failure, Not Just Success

Knowing what success looks like is half the work; knowing what failure looks like is the other half. Failure criteria protect against the piece that technically meets the brief but misses the point. Define what would make the piece unacceptable even if it reads well.

Failure might include:

- the reader finishes but cannot state the main takeaway;
- the piece is accurate but does not change the target reader's view or action;
- the tone alienates the primary reader even though the content is correct;
- the piece overpromises and underdelivers, damaging trust;
- a stakeholder is satisfied but the actual reader is not moved;
- the piece could be swapped for a generic competitor without loss.

Failure criteria force honesty. If you cannot imagine how the piece could fail, you have not thought hard enough about what success requires.

### Align Goals With The Real Constraint Set

Goals that ignore constraints are fantasies. A piece's achievable goal depends on length limits, deadline, available evidence, access to sources, platform norms, and the reader's realistic attention. Setting a goal the constraints make impossible guarantees disappointment and encourages faking success.

Map constraints against goals:

- does the length allow the depth the outcome needs;
- does the deadline allow the research the claim requires;
- does the evidence available support the strength of the goal;
- does the platform's norms allow the tone or structure the goal implies;
- does the reader's attention allow the complexity the outcome needs.

When a goal and a constraint conflict, either reduce the goal, change the constraint, or split the work. Do not pretend the constraint does not exist.

### Distinguish The Writer's Goal From The Reader's Goal

The writer may want to persuade, sell, build authority, or document, while the reader wants to decide, solve, understand, or be entertained. These goals can align or conflict. Misalignment produces pieces that serve the writer's need while frustrating the reader's, which ultimately defeats both.

Check alignment:

- does achieving the writer's goal require the reader to get what they came for;
- is the writer's goal hidden inside language that pretends to serve the reader;
- would the reader feel manipulated if the writer's true goal were visible;
- can both goals be served, or must one be subordinated.

Sustainable writing serves the reader's goal as the route to the writer's goal. When the writer's goal must override the reader's, do it openly rather than disguising it.

### Use Criteria To Drive Revision Priorities

Success criteria are not only for final evaluation; they are revision tools. When a draft is imperfect in many ways, the criteria tell you which imperfections matter. Fix what blocks the goal first; polish what does not affect the goal last.

Apply criteria during revision:

- which criterion is most at risk in the current draft;
- which sections most directly serve the outcome goal;
- what would a reader who failed to change point to as the reason;
- is the gap a content gap, a clarity gap, a tone gap, or a structure gap.

Without criteria, revision becomes endless polishing of sentences that may not matter. With criteria, revision becomes targeted work on the few things that determine success.

### Revisit Goals When The Draft Reveals A Better One

First-set goals are often wrong, just like first plans. As the draft develops, the real goal may emerge: a stronger claim, a more important reader, a deeper outcome. Clinging to the original goal when a better one appears wastes the discovery. Re-goal deliberately when the evidence supports it.

Signs a goal should change:

- the strongest material serves a different outcome than planned;
- the planned goal cannot be achieved with the available evidence;
- the draft keeps reaching toward a more important reader;
- the original goal was set by assumption, not by the brief or the reader's need.

When re-goal-setting, update the criteria, the brief, and the framing together so the piece stays coherent. A draft with a changed goal and an unchanged introduction is incoherent.

## Common Traps

### Accepting Activity As A Goal

"Write the report" is an activity, not an outcome. Pieces judged only by completion tend to exist without achieving anything. Always push from activity to outcome.

### Vague Criteria That Cannot Be Checked

"Make it engaging" or "ensure quality" cannot be evaluated and therefore cannot guide work. If a criterion cannot be observed or imagined concretely, it is decoration.

### Setting Goals The Constraints Forbid

Ambitious goals that ignore length, deadline, evidence, or platform produce either failure or faked success. Align goals with the real constraint set or change the constraints.

### Confusing The Writer's Goal With The Reader's

Pieces that serve the writer's need while ignoring the reader's ultimately fail both. The reader's goal is usually the route to the writer's, not an obstacle to it.

### No Failure Criteria

Without defined failure modes, a piece can read well and still miss the point, and no one will notice until the outcome fails to materialize. Define what would make the piece unacceptable.

### Keeping The Original Goal After The Draft Shifted

When the draft reveals a stronger goal, freezing the original wastes the discovery. Re-goal openly and update the framing to match.

## Self-Check

Before treating the goal and criteria as settled, verify:

- The goal is an outcome (a reader change), not merely an activity or output.
- The reader change is specified: who changes, from what to what, with what evidence.
- Success criteria are observable or at least imaginable, not purely aspirational.
- Failure criteria are defined, so the piece can be rejected for missing the point even if it reads well.
- Goals are aligned with the real constraints of length, deadline, evidence, platform, and attention.
- The writer's goal and the reader's goal are distinguished, and their alignment is honest.
- Criteria are being used to prioritize revision, not only for final judgment; the goal could not be satisfied by a generic competitor piece; it is specific to this work
- You have revisited the goal after drafting and updated it if a stronger one emerged; the introduction, structure, and ending still serve the current goal, not a stale one
- Stakeholder approval is not mistaken for the actual reader outcome; the criteria distinguish between a piece that is complete and a piece that succeeded
- Where measurement is impossible, the criterion is still concrete enough to imagine checking; the piece has a defined reason to exist beyond "cover this topic."
