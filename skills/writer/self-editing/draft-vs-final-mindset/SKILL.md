# Draft Vs Final Mindset

Writing well requires two incompatible mental modes. The generative mode produces material: it is expansive, tolerant, forward-moving, and willing to write badly in order to write at all. The editorial mode improves material: it is critical, reductive, precise, and willing to cut. Both are necessary, but they cannot operate simultaneously without crippling each other. The discipline of writing is knowing which mode you are in, committing to it fully, and switching deliberately rather than drifting.

Agents tend to conflate the modes. The most common failure is premature editing: critiquing each sentence as it is written, which strangles generation and produces cautious, thin drafts that never discover their real subject. The opposite failure is perpetual drafting: generating endlessly without ever switching to the critical mode, producing volume without shape. The harm in both cases is wasted effort and a draft that is neither bold enough nor clean enough.

This skill covers the judgment of when to be in which mode, how to commit fully to the current mode, how to switch deliberately, and how to resist the pull of the wrong mode at the wrong time. The agent has freedom in process details, but must keep the two modes distinct and switch between them consciously.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Modes As Mutually Exclusive

You cannot fully generate and fully edit at once. Generation requires suspending judgment; editing requires applying it. Attempting both produces a compromised version of each: generation that is too cautious to discover anything, and editing that is too distracted to be thorough.

Commit to one mode at a time. When generating, forbid yourself from fixing, judging, or even rereading for quality. When editing, forbid yourself from generating new directions unless the edit absolutely requires it, and even then mark the gap and return to it in a generative session. The modes are tools; use one at a time.

### Generate Without Judgment

The generative mode's job is to produce material to work with, not to produce finished prose. A messy first draft that reaches the end is more valuable than a perfect first paragraph that goes nowhere. Judgment during generation is the enemy of discovery, because the best moves are often the ones that look risky or wrong until the surrounding material exists.

To protect generation:

- do not reread what you just wrote for quality;
- do not stop to find the perfect word; mark it and keep moving;
- do not delete; cross out or leave a placeholder;
- do not revise the opening before you know the ending;
- permit repetition, vagueness, and excess; revision exists to fix these.

The generative mode trusts that the editorial mode will clean up later. Generation that tries to be final is generation that never finds its real subject.

### Edit Without Mercy

The editorial mode's job is to serve the reader, not the writer's feelings about what was written. Once you switch to editing, the draft is material, not achievement. Cut, restructure, and rewrite as the piece requires, without preserving passages out of loyalty to the effort of writing them.

To commit fully to editing:

- treat the draft as an object, not as yourself;
- cut generously, knowing cut material is recoverable;
- question every section's right to exist;
- do not generate new material to "rescue" a weak passage unless it serves the piece;
- prefer the harder, better change to the easier, safer one.

Editing that is gentle out of respect for the drafting self produces a draft that is polite but weak.

### Switch Modes Deliberately, Not By Drift

Mode switches should be decisions, not accidents. Drifting into editing while drafting, or into generating while editing, breaks the concentration each mode requires and produces work that is neither bold nor clean. Decide in advance which mode a session is in, and honor that decision.

Use explicit signals to mark the switch:

- a different document, file, or view for drafting versus editing;
- a stated intention at the start of a session ("this is a generative pass");
- a checkpoint where generation stops and editing begins;
- a rule that you do not edit until the draft reaches a defined milestone (a complete first draft, a full outline, a set target).

Deliberate switching prevents the slow bleed where one mode contaminates the other.

### Sequence Modes By Draft Stage

The right mode depends on the draft's stage. Early stages need generation: outlining, freewriting, drafting without revision. Middle stages alternate: structural revision may require generating new sections, which then need editing. Late stages are almost entirely editorial: line editing, cutting, and proofreading.

A rough sequence:

- generate the first draft fully before editing it;
- edit structurally before line editing;
- generate new material only in targeted bursts once the structure is settled;
- reserve the final stages for purely editorial passes.

Misjudging the stage produces the characteristic failures: line-editing a first draft (wasted, because structure will change), or endlessly generating new material in a late draft (avoiding the necessary cutting).

### Resist Premature Perfectionism

The strongest pull toward the wrong mode is perfectionism during generation. It feels responsible to make each sentence right as you write it, but it is the single biggest enemy of strong drafts. Premature polish locks in choices before the writer knows what the piece is about, and it makes later structural change painful because polished sentences feel too good to cut.

Counter premature perfectionism by:

- setting a generation target (word count, section count, or completion) before allowing any editing;
- writing placeholders ("[find better word]", "[example needed]") instead of stopping;
- accepting that the first draft is allowed to be bad;
- remembering that no reader sees the first draft; only the final one matters.

Perfectionism is an editorial virtue applied at the wrong time. In generation, it is a vice.

### Know When To Stop Generating

Perpetual drafting is the opposite failure. Some writers never switch to editing because generation feels safer and more pleasant than judgment. The result is sprawling drafts that never converge on a shape. Generation must have a stopping rule.

A draft is ready to leave generative mode when:

- it reaches a defined end, even roughly;
- the main sections exist, even if disordered;
- the controlling idea is discoverable in the material;
- further generation would add volume but not understanding.

Once these hold, switch to editing. Additional generation past this point is avoidance.

### Use The Right Mode For The Right Problem

Some problems are generative and some are editorial, and applying the wrong mode wastes effort. A missing section is a generative problem: you cannot edit your way into material that does not exist. A bloated section is an editorial problem: you cannot generate your way out of excess. Diagnose the problem type before choosing the mode.

Generative problems: missing evidence, thin scenes, underdeveloped ideas, absent counterarguments. Editorial problems: weak structure, redundancy, unclear sentences, poor rhythm, errors. Match the mode to the problem, or you will work hard in the wrong direction.

## Common Traps

### Editing While Drafting

The classic failure. Fixing sentences as you write them slows generation, suppresses discovery, and locks in choices prematurely. Forbid editing during a generative session.

### Never Leaving Generative Mode

Endless drafting without editing produces volume without shape. Generation must end and editing must begin. A stopping rule prevents avoidance disguised as productivity.

### Polishing A First Draft

Line-editing or word-polishing a first draft is wasted effort, because structural revision will likely change or delete the polished passages. Defer polish until the structure is settled.

### Treating The Draft As The Self

Editing feels personal when the draft is treated as an expression of self rather than as material. This makes necessary cuts feel like rejection and weakens the editorial mode. Separate the work from the self.

### Switching Modes Unconsciously

Drifting into the wrong mode mid-session breaks concentration. Use explicit signals and rules to keep the current mode stable until a deliberate switch.

### Applying The Wrong Mode To The Problem

Trying to edit a generative problem (missing material) produces nothing. Trying to generate through an editorial problem (excess, disorder) produces more mess. Diagnose first, then choose the mode.

### Confusing Caution With Quality

Cautious, over-edited prose often reads as stiff and lifeless. Quality comes from bold generation followed by ruthless editing, not from timid generation followed by gentle editing.

## Self-Check

Before treating the mode management as sound, verify:

- Generation and editing were treated as mutually exclusive modes within each session.
- The generative mode was protected from judgment: no rereading for quality, no premature fixing.
- The editorial mode was committed and unsentimental: cuts and rewrites were made as the piece required.
- Mode switches were deliberate decisions, not drift.
- The session's mode was stated or signaled before work began.
- Modes were sequenced by draft stage: generation early, structural editing next, line editing late.
- Premature perfectionism was resisted during generation, using targets and placeholders.
- Generation had a stopping rule, and the draft was moved to editing once it was met.
- Each problem was matched to the correct mode rather than forced through the wrong one; the first draft was allowed to be imperfect, with no reader expected to see it
- The final draft reflects both bold generation and thorough editing, not one without the other; no session tried to do both modes at once and ended up doing neither well
