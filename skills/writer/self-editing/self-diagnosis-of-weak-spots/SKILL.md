# Self-Diagnosis Of Weak Spots

Every writer has recurring flaws. Some overwrite openings, some bury the main point, some lean on abstract nouns, some repeat the same sentence rhythm, some over-explain, some under-evidence. These patterns are invisible to the writer in the moment of drafting because they feel natural, but they are highly visible to readers and editors. Self-diagnosis is the practice of finding your own patterns and converting them into a revision checklist that catches them reliably.

Agents tend to revise each draft as if it were unique, fixing whatever stands out that day. This treats symptoms but not the disease. The harm of undiagnosed patterns is that the same weaknesses recur draft after draft, costing the same rework and producing the same reader friction, because the writer never names the pattern or builds a habit to counter it. A writer who knows their own weak spots edits faster and more surely, because the checklist does the noticing for them.

This skill covers how to diagnose your own recurring flaws, how to distinguish true patterns from one-off mistakes, how to build a personal revision checklist from those patterns, and how to keep that checklist honest over time. The agent has freedom in the form of the checklist, but must ground every entry in observed evidence from real drafts.

## Core Rules

### Gather Evidence Before Diagnosing

A pattern requires more than one instance. Before declaring a weakness, collect evidence across multiple drafts. One overwritten opening is a mistake; five overwritten openings across five pieces is a pattern worth a checklist entry.

Build the diagnosis from real data:

- review three to five finished drafts, not just the current one;
- note any feedback that repeats across different readers and pieces;
- track which edits you make most often during revision;
- list the passages you are least confident about and look for common features.

A diagnosis grounded in evidence is trustworthy. A diagnosis from a single bad draft is likely an overreaction. Distinguish the chronic from the acute before building habits around it.

### Separate Pattern From Incident

Not every flaw is a pattern. Some are caused by the specific demands of one piece: a technical topic that forced jargon, a tight deadline that produced rushing, a complex argument that resisted structure. These are incidents, not habits.

Ask whether the flaw appears:

- across different topics and genres, or only this one;
- under normal conditions, or only under pressure;
- in places you draft freely, or only where you struggled;
- in early drafts that you later fixed, or surviving into final drafts.

True patterns survive across contexts and into the final draft. Incidents are context-specific and often resolved in revision. Build checklist entries for patterns; handle incidents case by case.

### Name The Flaw Precisely

A vague checklist entry ("be clearer") is useless because it cannot be checked. A precise entry ("I tend to write openings longer than they need to be; check whether the first paragraph can lose its first two sentences") is operational. Precision turns a feeling into a detectable condition.

For each pattern, specify:

- where it tends to appear (opening, transitions, endings, evidence sections);
- what it looks like concretely (overlong sentences, abstract nouns, hedging language);
- what question detects it ("Could the reader state the main point after the first paragraph?");
- what the fix direction is (cut, concretize, commit, reorder).

A checklist entry is only useful if you can act on it without further interpretation.

### Build A Tiered Personal Checklist

Not all weak spots matter equally. Organize the checklist by leverage, so the highest-impact patterns get attention first. A flat list encourages checking the easy items and skipping the hard ones.

Suggested tiers:

- structural and developmental patterns (buried points, weak spines, missing evidence);
- voice and stance patterns (tonal drift, over-hedging, false authority);
- paragraph and movement patterns (weak transitions, stalled repetition);
- sentence patterns (uniform length, nominalization, filler openers);
- mechanical patterns (specific recurring errors).

Place the patterns that most damage the reader experience at the top. Revise the checklist order as your skills change.

### Attach A Detection Question To Each Entry

Each checklist item should end with a question you can answer yes or no about the current draft. A question forces a real check rather than a vague scan.

Examples:

- "Does the main point appear early enough that a skimmer would catch it?"
- "Is there at least one concrete example or scene per major abstraction?"
- "Do more than three sentences in a row share the same length?"
- "Does every paragraph's first sentence orient the reader to its job?"

If you cannot frame the pattern as a yes/no question, the entry is too vague. Refine it until it is checkable.

### Update The Checklist From Each Revision

A checklist is a living document. After each revision, note which patterns you caught and which surprised you. Add newly noticed patterns; retire entries for flaws you have genuinely outgrown. A checklist that never changes is either perfect (unlikely) or unused.

Review the checklist periodically:

- which entries fire on most drafts (keep and prioritize);
- which entries rarely fire (demote or retire);
- which new flaws appeared that have no entry yet (add);
- which entries are too vague to act on (sharpen or split).

The checklist should reflect your current writing, not your writing from a year ago.

### Use The Checklist As A Pass, Not A Replacement For Reading

The checklist is a structured pass layered on top of normal revision, not a substitute for reading the draft as a reader. Run the checklist after your developmental and structural passes, when the larger decisions are settled and the patterns can be detected in stable text.

The sequence: diagnose the draft as a reader, revise structurally, then run the personal checklist as a targeted sweep for your known patterns, then proofread. The checklist catches what your reading eye is blind to because the flaws feel natural to you.

### Distinguish Style From Flaw

Some recurring features are voice, not weakness. A writer who naturally uses long, accumulative sentences may be exercising a stylistic strength, not suffering a flaw. Do not convert every recurring feature into something to eliminate.

Test whether a feature is flaw or style:

- does it serve the reader, or only the writer's habit;
- does it appear by choice in the right places, or compulsively everywhere;
- does it survive when you try alternatives, or does the alternative read better;
- do trusted readers experience it as a strength or a friction.

Keep stylistic features; build checklist entries only for features that consistently weaken the reader's experience.

## Common Traps

### Diagnosing From A Single Draft

One draft can make any flaw feel like a pattern. Building habits from a single instance over-corrects and may suppress a stylistic choice that was right for that piece. Require evidence across drafts.

### Vague Checklist Entries

Entries like "improve flow" or "be concise" cannot be checked and so are ignored. Every entry must be a precise, answerable question tied to a concrete feature.

### Treating Every Recurring Feature As A Flaw

Voice consists partly of recurring features. Aggressively eliminating everything that repeats flattens the writing into generic competence. Distinguish habit-that-serves from habit-that-harms.

### Never Retiring Stale Entries

A checklist that only grows becomes noise. Entries for flaws you have outgrown dilute attention from current patterns. Prune regularly.

### Running The Checklist Instead Of Reading

The checklist catches known patterns but cannot find novel problems or judge overall shape. It complements reading as a reader; it does not replace it.

### Ignoring Feedback Because It Conflicts With Self-Image

Repeated feedback that stings often points at a real pattern. The instinct to dismiss it as the reader's failure is usually wrong. Treat consistent external signals as evidence, even when uncomfortable.

### Over-Fitting The Checklist To One Genre

Patterns may differ across genres. A pattern in essays may not appear in technical docs. Keep entries labeled by where they apply, and do not assume one checklist fits every kind of writing.

## Self-Check

Before treating the self-diagnosis as complete, verify:

- The diagnosis is based on evidence from at least three drafts, not a single piece.
- Each declared pattern is distinguished from one-off incidents tied to specific conditions.
- Every checklist entry names the flaw precisely, with where it appears and what it looks like.
- Each entry has a concrete yes/no detection question, not a vague aspiration.
- The checklist is tiered by leverage, with the most damaging patterns first.
- Entries are ordered and pruned so the list reflects current writing, not old habits.
- The checklist is run as a targeted pass after structural revision, not as a replacement for reading.
- Recurring features were tested for style-versus-flaw before being targeted for elimination.
- Stale entries for outgrown flaws have been retired; new flaws noticed in the latest revision have been added with precise detection questions
- The checklist is specific enough that another writer could not use it unchanged; it reflects your patterns; running the checklist on the current draft would actually catch the patterns it lists
