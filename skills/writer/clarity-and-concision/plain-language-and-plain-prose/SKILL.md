# Plain Language And Plain Prose

Plain language is writing the reader can understand on the first reading, without specialist knowledge, and without re-reading. It is not simplified thinking, nor is it writing down to the reader. It is the discipline of expressing complex ideas in the clearest form they will bear. The harm plain language prevents is real: instructions people cannot follow, contracts they cannot evaluate, policies they cannot comply with, and arguments they cannot assess. When prose is needlessly obscure, the reader pays the cost.

The common failure is to confuse plainness with simplicity of thought, or to resist plainness as a threat to authority. Writers fear that clear prose will seem unsophisticated, so they reach for jargon, nominalizations, and inflated diction that signal expertise while obscuring meaning. The opposite failure is to flatten genuinely complex ideas past the point where accuracy survives. Plain language is not the lowest common denominator; it is the clearest expression the content and audience allow.

Use this skill when writing for broad audiences, when revising jargon-heavy or inflated drafts, when applying plain language principles to instructions or policy, or when deciding whether plainness serves the piece. The agent has freedom to use technical language when the audience needs it, but should never make prose harder than the content requires.

## Core Rules

### Match Clarity To Audience Need

Plainness is calibrated to the reader. A specialist audience may need precise technical terms that would confuse a general reader. A general audience needs ordinary words and explained terms. The question is not "is this plain enough" in the abstract, but "can this reader understand and use this on the first reading." Match clarity to who is reading and why.

Assess:

- Who is the reader, and what must they do with this text?
- Do they share the vocabulary?
- Are they reading under pressure, scanning, or studying?
- What happens if they misunderstand?

Raise clarity where misunderstanding carries cost. Calibrate, do not generalize.

### Prefer Ordinary Words When They Carry The Meaning

When an ordinary word and a technical word carry the same meaning for the reader, choose the ordinary one. "Use" instead of "utilize." "Help" instead of "facilitate." "End" instead of "terminate." Ordinary words are recognized faster and with less effort, leaving more attention for the content. Reserve technical terms for when they carry a distinction ordinary words cannot.

Choose ordinary words when:

- the technical term adds no precision the reader needs;
- the ordinary word is unambiguous in context;
- the audience is general or mixed;
- the goal is comprehension rather than signaling expertise.

Do not choose ordinary words when doing so collapses a necessary distinction.

### Define Necessary Technical Terms

Some content requires specialized vocabulary. Plain language does not forbid it; it requires that necessary terms be defined or introduced so the reader can follow. A term that is essential but undefined excludes the reader. Define on first use, in plain words, and use the term consistently thereafter.

When introducing terms:

- define on first appearance, not in a glossary the reader may skip;
- use the same term throughout, avoiding synonym drift;
- give a concrete example alongside the definition;
- check that the definition uses vocabulary the reader already has.

Plainness and precision coexist when terms are introduced with care.

### Write Short, Direct Sentences As A Default

Long sentences increase cognitive load and the chance of misreading. Plain language favors shorter sentences with clear subjects and verbs, breaking complex ideas into steps the reader can hold. This does not mean every sentence must be short; it means the default is direct, and length is added only when the idea requires it.

Strengthen sentence clarity by:

- keeping subject and verb close;
- putting the main clause first;
- limiting embedded clauses and parentheticals;
- breaking sentences that carry more than one main idea;
- using lists for series, conditions, or steps.

The reader should rarely need to re-read a sentence to parse it.

### Use Structure To Reduce Cognitive Load

Plain language is structural, not just lexical. Headings, bullet lists, numbered steps, and short paragraphs help the reader find and hold information. A wall of text is hard to read no matter how plain the words. Structure turns dense content into navigable content.

Use structure to:

- break procedures into numbered steps;
- separate conditions from actions;
- group related points under clear headings;
- use lists for parallel items;
- front-load the answer before the explanation.

Structure is part of clarity, not a concession to it.

### Prefer Active Voice And Named Actors

Active voice, with named actors as subjects, is usually clearer than passive voice. It tells the reader who does what, which matters in instructions, policies, and arguments. Passive voice has legitimate uses, but defaulting to it in the name of formality often obscures agency and increases word count.

Use active voice when:

- the reader needs to know who acts;
- giving instructions or stating responsibilities;
- establishing causation or accountability.

Use passive only when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately de-emphasized.

### Avoid Inflation And Bureaucratic Phrasing

Inflated and bureaucratic phrasing is the enemy of plain language. "Implement a strategic initiative to optimize operational efficiencies" means "improve how we work," but it costs the reader effort to decode and signals distance rather than clarity. Plain language names the action in words the reader uses.

Cut:

- nominalizations that hide verbs;
- long verb phrases where one verb works;
- abstract collectives ("stakeholders," "the general public") when specific actors can be named;
- hedges and qualifiers that soften without adding accuracy.

Plainness often means trusting the reader with direct statements.

### Know When Plainness Is Not The Goal

Plain language is a strong default, but not every piece should be maximally plain. Literary prose may use complexity for effect. Specialist communication among experts may use dense technical language efficiently. Persuasive writing may use rhythm and figure that resist simplification. The judgment is whether the complexity serves the reader and purpose, or whether it only serves the writer's comfort or ego.

Plainness may yield when:

- the audience shares the specialized vocabulary and reads faster with it;
- complexity creates a deliberate aesthetic or rhetorical effect;
- simplifying would distort the meaning;
- the genre expects and rewards density.

Even then, complexity should be chosen, not accidental.

### Test With A Real Reader When Stakes Are High

For high-stakes prose, instructions, legal summaries, or public-facing policy, test clarity with a representative reader. If they cannot understand or act on the text on the first reading, revise. The writer is a poor judge of their own clarity because they already know the meaning. External testing reveals what plain language principles predict but cannot guarantee.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Plainness With Dumbing Down

Plain language expresses complex ideas clearly; it does not remove the complexity. Do not simplify past the point of accuracy.

### Using Jargon To Signal Expertise

Technical terms that the reader does not need exclude and obscure. Use jargon only when it carries necessary precision.

### Bureaucratic Inflation

"Optimize operational efficiencies" hides a simple action behind abstraction. Name the action plainly.

### Over-Simplifying Necessary Distinctions

Collapsing fine distinctions to achieve plainness can mislead. Preserve distinctions the reader needs, even at the cost of length.

### Defaulting To Passive Voice

Passive voice used for formality hides agency and adds words. Use it deliberately, not by default.

### Assuming The Writer Can Judge Their Own Clarity

Writers already know the meaning, so their prose feels clear to them. Test with a real reader when clarity matters.

## Self-Check

Before treating plain language work as complete, verify:

- The prose can be understood by the intended reader on the first reading.
- Ordinary words are used where they carry the meaning without loss.
- Necessary technical terms are defined on first use and used consistently.
- Sentences are short and direct as a default, with length added only when needed.
- Structure, such as headings, lists, and steps, reduces cognitive load.
- Active voice and named actors make agency clear where it matters.
- Inflation and bureaucratic phrasing have been cut.
- Complexity that remains is chosen to serve the reader, not accidental.
- Necessary distinctions have not been collapsed in the name of simplicity; the reader could act on or use this text without specialist help
- High-stakes prose has been tested or checked against a representative reader; plainness has improved clarity without reducing the accuracy or depth of the content
