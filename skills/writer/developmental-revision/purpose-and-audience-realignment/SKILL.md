# Purpose And Audience Realignment

Drafts drift. A piece begun to persuade a skeptical executive may, by the end, have become an explainer for newcomers. A post intended to comfort may have turned argumentative. An essay aimed at specialists may have acquired a general-audience opening that no longer fits the technical body. This drift is normal, because drafting discovers what a piece is really about, but it leaves the draft serving a mismatched purpose or audience unless revision explicitly realigns the whole.

Agents tend to notice local problems, a confusing opening, a tonal clash, a section that feels off, without recognizing that the cause is a shifted purpose or audience. The harm of unreconciled drift is a draft that tries to be two things for two readers and succeeds at neither: an opening that promises one job and a body that does another, evidence calibrated for one audience and tone calibrated for another. The reader feels the mismatch even when each part is individually fine.

This skill covers the judgment for realignment: how to detect that purpose or audience has shifted, how to decide which version of the purpose is the real one, how to realign the whole piece consistently, and how to handle the case where the draft must serve a genuinely mixed audience. The agent has freedom to choose the final purpose and audience, but must make the whole draft serve a single coherent job.

## Core Rules

### Detect Drift Before Repairing Symptoms

Before fixing a confusing opening or a tonal clash, ask whether the cause is a shifted purpose or audience. Local fixes to a global mismatch produce a patchwork. Detect the drift first, then realign globally.

Signals of drift:

- the opening promises one thing and the body delivers another;
- the tone shifts partway through without reason;
- the evidence is calibrated for experts but the framing assumes novices;
- the ending answers a different question than the opening asked;
- different sections assume different reader knowledge levels;
- the draft tries to do two jobs, such as explain and persuade, without reconciling them.

When several of these appear together, the cause is almost always purpose or audience drift, not isolated craft errors.

### Re-State The Actual Purpose Explicitly

Name what the draft is actually doing now, not what it was planned to do. Drafting often reveals that the real job differs from the intended one. Realignment begins by stating the actual purpose in one sentence, then deciding whether to accept it or redirect the draft back to the original.

State the purpose concretely:

- what should the reader understand, believe, decide, or do after reading;
- what is the single most important takeaway;
- what reader action or feeling defines success;
- what is this piece not trying to do.

If the actual purpose differs from the planned one, choose deliberately. Accepting the drift is often correct, because the draft discovered a better job. But the choice must be conscious, not accidental.

### Define The Actual Audience Explicitly

Name the reader the draft is now actually addressing. Audience drift is subtler than purpose drift: the writer may have started for one reader and gradually written for another as the material developed. Realignment requires a single primary audience, even if secondary audiences exist.

Define the audience concretely:

- who is the primary reader, in specific terms;
- what do they already know, and what must be explained;
- what do they want, fear, or resist;
- how much time and attention will they give;
- what register, evidence, and examples suit them.

A draft cannot be optimized for everyone. Commit to a primary reader and calibrate everything to that reader. Mixed audiences require explicit layering, not vague compromise.

### Reconcile Purpose And Audience With Each Other

Purpose and audience must fit each other. A purpose that requires deep expertise cannot be served by a general audience, and vice versa. After stating both, check whether they are compatible. If the purpose demands a reader the current audience cannot be, one of them must change.

Compatibility checks:

- does the audience have the background the purpose requires;
- does the purpose match what the audience wants or needs;
- is the evidence and register suited to both;
- if the audience were truly the primary one, would this still be the right purpose.

When purpose and audience conflict, decide which is fixed by the context (a deadline, a publication, a client) and adjust the other to match.

### Realign The Whole Draft, Not Just The Opening

Once the purpose and audience are settled, realign every part of the draft to them, not just the most visible mismatch. The opening and ending are the most common drift points, but evidence, examples, register, and section order must all serve the reconciled job.

Realign systematically:

- the opening must promise the actual purpose to the actual audience;
- the structure must serve the actual purpose's logic;
- the evidence and examples must suit the actual audience's knowledge;
- the register and tone must fit the actual audience's expectations;
- the ending must fulfill the actual purpose, not the original one.

Partial realignment leaves the draft feeling inconsistent. The whole piece must move together.

### Calibrate Depth And Register To The Chosen Audience

A realigned audience requires realigned depth and register. A draft realigned from specialists to general readers must lose unexplained jargon and add orientation. A draft realigned from general readers to specialists must lose over-explanation and add precision. Mismatched depth is a frequent residual of drift.

For the chosen audience, check:

- is every term defined or assumed at the right level;
- is the evidence technical enough or too technical;
- is the register formal, conversational, or instructional as appropriate;
- are the examples recognizable to this reader or alienating.

Depth and register are audience-dependent; what is right for one reader is wrong for another.

### Handle Mixed Audiences By Layering, Not Averaging

Sometimes a draft must genuinely serve more than one audience, such as a report read by both executives and technicians. Do not average the treatment, which serves neither well. Instead, layer: give the primary audience the main line, and provide for secondary audiences through clearly signaled side paths.

Layering techniques:

- an executive summary up front, detail in the body;
- sidebars or clearly marked sections for specialist detail;
- inline definitions for novices that specialists can skip;
- a structure that lets each reader find their level quickly.

Averaging produces prose that is too deep for the novice and too shallow for the expert. Layering serves both.

### Test The Realignment Against The Reader Contract

The reader contract is the implicit promise made by the title, opening, and framing. After realignment, verify that the contract matches the actual piece. A realigned draft whose opening still promises the old job misleads the reader.

Check:

- does the title still fit the realigned purpose;
- does the opening prepare the realigned audience;
- does the framing set up the realigned promise;
- would a reader drawn by the title and opening feel the body delivered.

## Common Traps

### Fixing Symptoms Of Drift As Local Errors

A confusing opening or tonal clash is often a symptom of purpose or audience drift. Treating it as a local craft error patches one spot while the global mismatch remains. Detect drift before repairing symptoms.

### Refusing To Accept The Draft's Real Purpose

Drafting discovers purpose. Clinging to the original plan when the draft has found a better job produces a piece fighting itself. Accept the drift when it is an improvement.

### Trying To Serve Everyone

A draft optimized for no specific reader serves no one well. Commit to a primary audience and calibrate to it. Mixed audiences need layering, not averaging.

### Realignment That Stops At The Opening

Rewriting the opening to match the new purpose while leaving the body calibrated to the old audience produces inconsistency. Realign the whole draft, including evidence, register, and ending.

### Keeping Evidence Calibrated To The Old Audience

Audience realignment requires recalibrating depth and examples. Keeping specialist evidence in a general-audience piece, or vice versa, leaves the residual mismatch visible.

### Confusing Purpose Expansion With Purpose Drift

Sometimes a piece legitimately broadens its scope. Expansion is fine when intentional and reconciled; drift is the problem when unintentional. Distinguish a deliberate widening from an accidental slide.

### Averaging Mixed Audiences

Averaging depth and register to split the difference between two audiences serves neither. Layer the treatment so each reader finds their level.

## Self-Check

Before treating purpose and audience realignment as complete, verify:

- Drift was detected as a global cause before local symptoms were repaired.
- The actual purpose is stated in one concrete sentence, distinct from the original plan.
- The actual audience is defined concretely, with a single primary reader committed to.
- Purpose and audience were checked for compatibility, and one was adjusted if they conflicted.
- Every part of the draft, not just the opening, was realigned to the reconciled job.
- Depth and register were recalibrated to the chosen audience's knowledge and expectations.
- Mixed audiences, if present, are handled by layering rather than averaging.
- The title, opening, and framing form a reader contract that matches the realigned piece.
- The ending fulfills the realigned purpose, not the original one; evidence and examples suit the realigned audience's level and interests
- No section still serves the old purpose or old audience; a reader drawn by the realigned title and opening would feel the body delivered on the promise
