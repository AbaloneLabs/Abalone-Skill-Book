# Structural And Conceptual Revision

Developmental revision is the deepest layer of revision. It does not improve sentences or paragraphs; it reconsiders what the piece is, what it claims, and how its parts relate. This is where the highest-leverage changes live: changing the controlling idea, reordering the spine, redefining the argument, or discovering that the draft's real subject is not the one it started with. Everything downstream, line editing and proofreading, depends on these decisions being right.

Agents tend to skip developmental revision because it is uncomfortable and expensive. It requires questioning assumptions that felt settled during drafting, and it can invalidate large amounts of existing work. The harm of skipping it is a piece that is well-polished at the sentence level but fundamentally weak: an argument that does not quite hold, a structure that does not quite serve the reader, a premise that was never examined. No amount of line editing fixes a wrong controlling idea.

This skill covers the judgment for big-picture revision: how to identify when the premise or structure itself is the problem, how to reconsider the controlling idea, how to evaluate and rebuild the spine, and how to make high-leverage changes without losing what the draft got right. The agent has broad freedom to reshape, but must ground every major change in a clear diagnosis of what the current shape fails to do.

## Core Rules

### Question The Premise Before The Prose

Before revising structure, question the premise itself. A draft built on an unexamined or wrong premise cannot be saved by rearrangement. Developmental revision begins by asking whether the piece is answering the right question, making the right claim, and taking the right stance.

Ask of the whole draft:

- What is the controlling question, and is it the question the reader actually needs answered?
- What is the central claim, and is it true, defensible, and appropriately scoped?
- Has the drafting revealed that the real subject is different from the planned one?
- Is the premise too broad, too narrow, or aimed at the wrong tension?
- Does the draft's strongest insight match its stated premise, or has it drifted?

If the premise is wrong, no structural fix suffices. Redefine the controlling question and claim first, then rebuild structure to serve them.

### Find The Draft's Real Center

First drafts often discover their real subject late. The strongest claim, image, or insight may appear in the middle, buried under material written toward an earlier plan. Developmental revision's first task is to find that center and decide whether the piece should be rebuilt around it.

Look for the center in:

- the paragraph where the draft becomes most alive or certain;
- the claim that the rest of the material keeps supporting or circling;
- the objection or tension the draft cannot stop returning to;
- the consequence or image that carries the most weight;
- the sentence that would hurt most to cut.

If the real center differs from the planned premise, the developmental decision is whether to restate the premise to match the center, or to cut the center and return to the plan. Usually the center is right and the plan was provisional.

### Evaluate The Spine Against The Reader's Path

The spine is the underlying logic that orders the piece. Developmental revision tests whether the current spine serves the reader's cognitive and emotional path. A spine can be internally consistent yet wrong for the reader, delivering information in an order that prevents understanding.

Test the spine by asking, at each point:

- What does the reader know now?
- Is that enough to understand and accept the next section?
- Where is the reader asked to hold unresolved complexity without knowing why?
- Does the order build tension, trust, or understanding, or does it dissipate them?
- Would a different spine (chronological, problem-solution, claim-evidence, misconception-correction) serve this material better?

If the reader's path is broken, the spine must change, even if each section is individually sound.

### Distinguish Structural Problems From Surface Problems

A common error is treating a structural problem with surface fixes. If readers are confused, the cause may be a missing transition (surface) or a wrong order (structural). If the argument feels weak, the cause may be unclear sentences (surface) or an unsupported claim (structural). Diagnose the layer before choosing the fix.

Structural problems show up as:

- readers who understand each section but miss the overall point;
- sections that are clear alone but confusing in sequence;
- an argument that feels unsatisfying even when the prose is clean;
- repetition that no line edit can remove because the sections genuinely overlap;
- an ending that does not land because the structure did not prepare it.

Surface fixes to structural problems are rework. Confirm the layer before editing.

### Rebuild The Skeleton Before The Prose

Once a structural change is decided, rebuild the skeleton before rewriting any prose. Write the new structure as a list of section jobs in the new order, and read that list to confirm the line of thought holds. Only when the skeleton works should you invest in rewriting the connective prose.

The skeleton test:

- list each section as one sentence describing its job;
- read the list in order; does the thought progress logically and emotionally;
- does each section prepare the next;
- do the opening and ending still connect through the new middle;
- which sections must be rewritten, moved, cut, or newly generated.

Rebuilding prose before confirming the skeleton guarantees rework when the new order turns out to be flawed.

### Make The Highest-Leverage Change First

When multiple developmental changes are needed, sequence them by leverage. The change that affects the most of the draft should come first, because it may render other planned changes unnecessary or different. Changing the controlling idea may dissolve several structural problems at once.

Leverage ordering:

- premise and controlling question first;
- central claim and its scope next;
- spine and section order next;
- section jobs and evidence allocation last.

Small developmental changes made before large ones are often invalidated. Start at the top of the leverage ladder.

### Preserve What The Draft Got Right

Developmental revision is destructive, but not everything should be destroyed. Before rebuilding, identify what the draft got right: a strong opening image, a well-supported sub-claim, a vivid example, a correct stance. Preserve these even as the surrounding structure changes, relocating them if needed.

A developmental revision that discards everything and starts over wastes the discovery that drafting produced. The goal is to keep the genuine insights and rebuild the architecture around them, not to erase the draft.

### Reconcile Parts After A Conceptual Shift

When the controlling idea or premise changes, every part of the draft must be reconciled to the new concept. Sections that supported the old premise may now undermine the new one. Examples that illustrated the old claim may now be irrelevant. A conceptual shift is not complete until the whole draft consistently serves the new idea.

After any conceptual change, check:

- does the opening still set up the right promise;
- does each section's job still serve the new controlling question;
- do the examples and evidence support the new claim;
- does the ending fulfill the new premise rather than the old one.

## Common Traps

### Polishing Instead Of Rethinking

The most common avoidance. Line-editing feels like progress but leaves the premise and structure unexamined. If the piece feels unsatisfying despite clean prose, the problem is developmental, and no amount of polish will fix it.

### Keeping A Wrong Premise Out Of Sunk Cost

Rethinking a premise can invalidate hours of work, which makes it tempting to preserve a flawed plan. But shipping a piece on a wrong premise wastes all the downstream effort too. The sunk cost of drafting is not a reason to keep a bad foundation.

### Rearranging Without Diagnosing

Moving sections around without a diagnosis of why the current order fails produces a different arrangement that is equally weak. Diagnose the reader's path before choosing a new spine.

### Confusing A Strong Section With A Correct Structure

A vivid section can mask a broken structure. The piece may feel strong because one part is excellent, while the overall argument still does not hold. Judge the structure on the whole, not on the best part.

### Changing The Spine But Not The Transitions

A new order requires rewritten connective prose. Keeping the old transitions in a new order produces non sequiturs and confusion. Structural change is not finished until the connective tissue is rebuilt.

### Over-Revising Into Caution

Developmental revision can drift toward making the piece safer and more conventional, trimming the bold claim into a bland one. High-leverage revision should sharpen the claim, not soften it out of fear.

### Losing The Draft's Discovery

Starting over completely discards the insights drafting produced. Preserve the genuine discoveries even when rebuilding the architecture around them.

## Self-Check

Before treating developmental revision as complete, verify:

- The premise and controlling question were questioned, not assumed, and still match the draft's real subject.
- The draft's real center was identified and the piece is built around it, not around a stale plan.
- The spine was tested against the reader's cognitive and emotional path, not just internal consistency.
- Structural problems were distinguished from surface problems before fixes were chosen.
- Any structural rebuild was tested as a one-line skeleton before prose was rewritten.
- Developmental changes were sequenced by leverage, with premise and claim before spine and sections.
- What the draft got right was preserved and relocated, not discarded in the rebuild.
- After any conceptual shift, every section was reconciled to the new controlling idea; the central claim is true, defensible, and appropriately scoped
- The opening sets up the right promise for the revised piece; the ending fulfills the revised premise rather than the original one
- The piece would still hold together if reduced to its section jobs, with no prose; no amount of remaining line editing could fix a problem the developmental pass left unresolved
