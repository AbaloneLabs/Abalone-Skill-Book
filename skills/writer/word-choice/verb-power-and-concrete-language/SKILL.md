# Verb Power And Concrete Language

Strong prose runs on verbs and nouns. Verbs carry action and energy; nouns carry the things the reader can see, count, or name. When verbs are weak and nouns are abstract, the writer patches the gap with adjectives and adverbs, and the prose becomes heavy without becoming vivid. The reader works harder and sees less. Power in prose comes from letting verbs and nouns do the work they are built to do.

The common failure is to build sentences around weak verbs and abstract nouns, then compensate with modifiers. "She made a quick decision" uses a weak verb ("made"), an abstract noun ("decision"), and a modifier ("quick") to convey what one strong verb could carry: "She decided quickly," or better, a verb that encodes the speed. "Is indicative of" replaces "indicates." "Conducted an investigation" replaces "investigated." Each substitution drains energy and adds words. The reader feels the weight without the force.

Use this skill when strengthening sentences, replacing weak verbs and abstract nouns, cutting unnecessary modifiers, or making prose more vivid without inflating it. The agent has freedom to use modifiers and abstractions when they serve meaning, but should let verbs and nouns carry the primary weight.

## Core Rules

### Let Verbs Carry The Action

The verb is the engine of the sentence. A strong specific verb encodes the action and often its manner, speed, or force, reducing the need for modifiers. "He walked heavily" becomes "he trudged." "She spoke quietly" becomes "she murmured." The strong verb does in one word what a weak verb plus adverb does in two.

To strengthen verbs:

- replace "make a decision" with "decide";
- replace "is indicative of" with "indicates";
- replace "give assistance to" with "assist" or "help";
- replace "conduct an investigation" with "investigate";
- replace "have a discussion about" with "discuss."

When a sentence's main verb is a form of "to be" or a vague verb like "make," "do," "have," or "perform," look for the real action hiding in a nearby noun.

### Prefer Concrete Nouns Over Abstract Ones

Concrete nouns name things the reader can perceive: objects, people, places, actions. Abstract nouns name concepts: situation, process, development, factor. Concrete nouns create images; abstract nouns create categories. Prose built on concrete nouns lets the reader see; prose built on abstractions makes the reader infer.

Move toward concreteness by:

- naming the actor instead of "individuals";
- naming the object instead of "items";
- naming the place instead of "location";
- naming the action instead of "activity";
- replacing "the implementation of the policy" with "implementing the policy," or better, naming who did what.

Abstraction is not forbidden, but each abstraction should be earned by surrounding concrete detail.

### Reduce Reliance On Adjectives And Adverbs

Modifiers are support, not structure. When a sentence needs many adjectives and adverbs to convey meaning, the verb and noun are usually too weak. Strengthen the verb or noun and watch the modifiers become unnecessary. "He ran very fast" becomes "he sprinted." "A big, important, significant change" becomes "an upheaval."

Use modifiers when they:

- add genuine specificity the verb or noun cannot encode;
- create necessary qualification or bounds;
- contribute to rhythm or emphasis.

Cut modifiers that merely intensify ("very," "really," "extremely") or that restate what the noun already implies ("past history," "future plans").

### Eliminate Nominalizations That Hide Verbs

A nominalization turns a verb into a noun, often adding a weak verb to carry it. "They made an evaluation of the proposal" hides the verb "evaluate" inside the noun "evaluation" and props it up with "made." "They evaluated the proposal" restores the verb and cuts the scaffolding. Nominalizations are a major source of wordy, lifeless prose.

Common nominalization patterns to revise:

- "make a decision" to "decide";
- "reach a conclusion" to "conclude";
- "perform an analysis" to "analyze";
- "achieve a reduction" to "reduce";
- "the creation of" to "creating" or "to create."

Keep nominalizations only when the noun form is the true subject or when the discipline requires it.

### Use Active Voice To Show Agency

Active voice puts the actor in the subject position and the action in the verb: "The committee approved the budget." Passive voice hides the actor: "The budget was approved." Active voice is usually stronger because it shows agency and reduces word count. Passive voice is appropriate when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately de-emphasized, but it should be a choice, not a default.

Strengthen agency by:

- naming the actor as the subject;
- using active verbs unless passive serves a purpose;
- avoiding passive constructions that evade responsibility;
- checking whether "by" phrases reveal a hidden actor who should be the subject.

When the actor matters, let the reader see them.

### Choose Specific Verbs Over General Ones

General verbs like "is," "has," "make," "go," "get," and "do" carry little information. Specific verbs carry manner, intent, or result: "stated" versus "said," "strode" versus "walked," "secured" versus "got." The specific verb makes the sentence more precise and more vivid at once.

Choose verbs that encode:

- manner (trudged, strode, stumbled);
- intent (persuaded, coerced, urged);
- result (shattered, repaired, restored);
- speed or force (lunged, drifted, slammed).

Avoid the thesaurus trap: choose the specific verb that is accurate, not merely unusual. A precise ordinary verb beats a showy wrong one.

### Ground Abstractions In Concrete Detail

Some ideas must be expressed abstractly, but abstractions become vivid only when grounded in concrete detail. "The company faced financial difficulty" is abstract; "The company missed payroll for three months" is concrete and more powerful. Pair each important abstraction with a concrete example, image, or consequence the reader can perceive.

Ground abstractions with:

- a specific example or case;
- a measurable consequence;
- a sensory or material detail;
- a named actor and action;
- a scene the reader can picture.

Abstraction stacked on abstraction feels weighty and means little; abstraction grounded in concrete detail carries both meaning and force.

### Cut Empty Modifiers And Intensifiers

Empty modifiers add words without adding meaning. "Very," "really," "quite," "rather," "basically," "essentially," and "actually" rarely change the truth of a sentence. They often signal that the writer has not committed to the claim or has not found the strong word. Cut them, and if the sentence feels weak, strengthen the verb or noun rather than restoring the modifier.

Keep a modifier only if removing it changes the meaning. If "very important" and "important" mean the same in context, cut "very." If "important" is too weak, find the precise word, such as "decisive" or "essential."

## Common Traps

### Weak Verb Plus Adverb

"He ran quickly" uses a weak verb propped by an adverb. Choose the strong verb that encodes the manner.

### Nominalization Stacks

"Conducted an evaluation of the implementation" hides verbs in nouns and adds scaffolding. Restore the verbs.

### Modifier Overload

Piling adjectives on a weak noun signals the noun is wrong. Choose a stronger noun and cut the modifiers.

### Passive By Default

Using passive voice to sound formal hides agency and drains energy. Choose passive deliberately, not habitually.

### Showy Verbs Over Accurate Ones

Reaching for an unusual verb from the thesaurus can introduce inaccuracy. Precision beats novelty.

### Abstraction Without Grounding

Stacked abstractions feel important and mean little. Ground each key abstraction in concrete detail.

## Self-Check

Before treating verb power and concrete language as achieved, verify:

- Main verbs carry the action rather than relying on weak verbs plus adverbs.
- Concrete nouns predominate over abstract ones, or abstractions are grounded in detail.
- Adjectives and adverbs add genuine specificity rather than propping up weak verbs and nouns.
- Nominalizations have been converted back to verbs where possible.
- Active voice shows agency unless passive serves a clear purpose.
- Verbs are specific and accurate, not merely unusual.
- Empty intensifiers like very and really have been cut.
- Key abstractions are paired with concrete examples, images, or consequences.
- No sentence relies on a stack of modifiers to convey what one strong verb could; agency is visible where the actor matters
- The prose feels energetic rather than heavy and lifeless; cutting modifiers has strengthened, not weakened, the meaning
