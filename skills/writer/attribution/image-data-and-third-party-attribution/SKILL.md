# Image Data And Third Party Attribution

Text is not the only material a writer borrows, and the non-text material, images, datasets, charts, code, illustrations, audio, and video, carries its own attribution and licensing rules that are stricter and more easily broken than those for words. A writer who would never paste a paragraph without credit will often drop in a photograph, a chart, or a dataset with a vague "source:" label or no label at all, assuming that because the material is online it is free to use. It is not. Most images and datasets are copyrighted by default, many carry specific license terms, and using them without correct attribution or permission is both an ethical failure and a legal risk.

The judgment problem is that third-party material comes with a patchwork of rules that the writer must actually investigate, not guess at. A Creative Commons image may require attribution, may prohibit commercial use, or may forbid alteration. A chart from a report may be copyrightable in its specific expression even if the underlying data is factual. A dataset may have terms of use that restrict redistribution. Fair use is a real defense but is narrower and more uncertain than writers assume. An agent that treats non-text material as freely available tends to commit quiet infringement, copying images and data without checking licenses or providing the required credit. The craft is treating every non-text element as a rights question, reading its license, and attributing it to the standard the license and basic honesty demand.

Use this skill when incorporating images, charts, datasets, code, or other third-party material, determining licensing or fair use, or writing attribution and credit lines for non-text elements. The goal is third-party material that is legally and ethically cleared and correctly credited.

## Core Rules

### Treat Every Third-Party Element As A Rights Question

The default assumption must be that any image, chart, dataset, or code you did not create is owned by someone else and governed by terms you need to find. "I found it online" is not a license. "It had no copyright notice" is not permission, since copyright exists by default in most jurisdictions. Before using any non-text material, identify its source, its license, and what that license permits.

For each element, determine:

- who created it and who holds the rights;
- what license, if any, applies;
- whether the license allows your intended use, commercial or not;
- whether attribution is required and in what form;
- whether modification or redistribution is permitted.

If you cannot establish the rights, do not use the material, or use it only where fair use clearly applies.

### Read And Honor The Specific License

Licenses are not interchangeable. A Creative Commons Attribution license lets you reuse with credit. A NonCommercial variant forbids commercial use. A NoDerivatives variant forbids alteration. A ShareAlike variant forces your derivative work to carry the same license. Public domain dedication means no restrictions. Misreading or ignoring these distinctions turns a permitted use into infringement. Read the actual license terms, not a summary you assume.

Honor license specifics:

- provide attribution in the form the license specifies;
- respect NonCommercial limits even on loosely commercial projects;
- do not alter NoDerivatives material;
- apply ShareAlike obligations where triggered;
- note public domain status correctly and do not imply copyright where none exists.

When terms conflict with your needs, find different material or seek permission.

### Attribute Non-Text Material To The License Standard

Attribution for images and data is not optional politeness; for licensed material it is usually a legal condition of use. A bare "source: website" rarely satisfies a Creative Commons requirement, which typically asks for the creator, title, source, license, and a link. Build attribution lines that meet the actual standard the license sets, and place them where readers can see them, usually in a caption or credits section.

A solid attribution line includes:

- the creator or rights holder;
- the title or description of the work;
- the source URL or publication;
- the specific license, with a link to its terms;
- any modifications you made, where required.

Vague or buried attribution fails both the license and the reader who wants to trace the material.

### Understand What Fair Use Covers And Does Not

Fair use permits limited use of copyrighted material without permission, but it is a narrow, fact-specific defense, not a blanket exception. It depends on the purpose of the use, the nature of the work, the amount used, and the effect on the market for the original. Comment, criticism, news reporting, and education lean toward fair use; wholesale reproduction for decoration or substitution leans against it. Writers routinely overestimate fair use, treating it as a license to republish anything for noncommercial purposes.

Apply fair use cautiously:

- use only what is necessary for the commentary or criticism;
- ensure your use transforms or comments on the original, not merely reproduces it;
- avoid uses that substitute for the original in its market;
- weigh the amount and importance of what you take;
- when the use is borderline, seek permission or find an alternative.

Fair use is a defense you may have to argue, not a permission slip.

### Respect Data Rights And Terms Of Use

Data sits in a confusing rights space. Raw facts are generally not copyrightable, but a specific compilation, selection, or arrangement of data can be, as can the particular expression of a chart or table. Many datasets come with terms of use that restrict redistribution, require attribution, or limit commercial application. Treating all data as free because facts are free leads to violations of compilation rights and license terms.

Check for data:

- whether the dataset has a license or terms of use;
- whether the compilation or arrangement is copyrightable;
- whether redistribution or modification is permitted;
- what attribution the provider requires;
- whether your use falls within the stated terms.

Attribute datasets and compilations to their providers even when the underlying facts are free.

### Credit Creators Of Charts And Visualizations

A chart or diagram is a creative expression, not just the data it displays, and its creator deserves credit. Reproducing a chart wholesale requires permission or fair use and attribution. Recreating a chart with the same design may still borrow protectable expression. When you adapt a chart, credit the original and note your changes. When you build a new chart from someone else's data, credit the data source.

Distinguish:

- reproducing a chart, which needs rights and attribution;
- adapting a chart, which needs attribution and possibly permission;
- building a new chart from sourced data, which needs data attribution;
- using your own data in a standard format, which needs no external credit.

### Handle Code And Software Material Correctly

Code carries its own licensing world. Open-source licenses range from permissive, requiring little beyond attribution, to copyleft, requiring derivative code to adopt the same license. Using code snippets without honoring their license can contaminate a project or violate terms. Even short snippets deserve attribution to their source, and substantial reuse requires compliance with the license.

For code material:

- identify the license of any code you reuse;
- comply with attribution and license-propagation requirements;
- credit the source of snippets even where license does not strictly require it;
- do not assume code is free because it was posted publicly.

### Verify Provenance Before Trusting A Source

Material circulates online with missing or wrong attribution. An image labeled "public domain" may not be; a chart's stated source may have itself copied it. Before relying on a license or credit line you found, trace the material to a primary source where feasible, and prefer authoritative repositories over random reposts. Using material from an unreliable intermediary inherits its errors.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Online Means Free

The most common error is treating anything findable on the web as free to reuse. Most images, charts, and datasets are copyrighted or licensed, and finding them is not permission. Always check rights before use.

### Vague Or Buried Attribution

"Source: Google," "image from internet," or a credit hidden in tiny print at the page bottom satisfies neither licenses nor readers. Attribution must name the creator and license and be visible where the material appears.

### Overestimating Fair Use

Writers invoke fair use to justify republishing entire images or long excerpts for illustration. Fair use is narrower than assumed and favors transformative, limited use. When in doubt, seek permission.

### Ignoring License Variants

Treating "Creative Commons" as a single permission ignores that its variants restrict commercial use, modification, and redistribution. Using a NonCommercial image commercially or a NoDerivatives image altered is infringement.

### Confusing Facts With Compilations

Because raw facts are not copyrightable, writers assume any data is free. But compilations, selections, and specific chart expressions can be protected, and dataset terms of use may bind regardless. Check the actual terms.

### Inheriting Wrong Attribution From Intermediaries

Reposting material with the attribution you found attached inherits any errors in that chain. A mislabeled "public domain" image is still infringing when you use it. Trace to the primary source.

### Altering Material That Forbids Alteration

Cropping, recoloring, or annotating a NoDerivatives-licensed image violates its terms, even with attribution. Respect modification restrictions or choose material that allows them.

## Self-Check

Before treating third-party material as cleared and credited, verify:

- The source, creator, and rights holder of each non-text element have been identified.
- The specific license has been read and its terms, including NonCommercial, NoDerivatives, and ShareAlike, are respected.
- Attribution is provided in the form the license requires, including creator, title, source, license, and link.
- Attribution is visible where the material appears, not buried or vague.
- Any reliance on fair use is limited, transformative, and defensible, not a blanket assumption.
- Datasets and compilations are attributed to their providers, and terms of use are honored.
- Charts and visualizations credit their creators, and adaptations note changes.
- Reused code complies with its license, including attribution and copyleft obligations; material has been traced to a primary source rather than trusted from an intermediary
- No element is used commercially when its license forbids commercial use; no element forbidding alteration has been modified
- Public domain material is correctly labeled and not implied to be copyrighted; you could defend the rights and attribution of every third-party element if challenged
