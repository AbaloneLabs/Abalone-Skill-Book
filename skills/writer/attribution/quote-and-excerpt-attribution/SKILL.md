# Quote And Excerpt Attribution

A quotation carries special weight. Readers treat quoted words as evidence, as the actual voice of the speaker, and as a guarantee that the source said precisely that. This is why quotation is powerful, and also why it is dangerous. A misattributed quote, a quote stripped of its context, a silently edited quote, or a quote presented as someone's view when it was sarcasm or hypothesis can distort meaning while hiding behind the apparent authority of verbatim text. The quotation marks signal fidelity, and when that fidelity is broken, even subtly, the reader is misled in a way that is hard to detect.

The judgment problem is that faithful quotation is not just about copying words exactly. It is about preserving the meaning the words carried in their original setting. A writer can transcribe every word perfectly and still mislead, by choosing a fragment that reverses the speaker's intent, by omitting the sentence that qualified it, by presenting a hypothetical as a stated belief, or by silently smoothing a rough quote until it says something smoother than the original. An agent that treats quotation as a copy-paste operation tends to produce quotes that are textually exact but semantically false. The craft is quoting accurately, attributing clearly, editing transparently, and supplying the context the reader needs to understand the quote as the source meant it.

Use this skill when incorporating direct quotations or excerpts, attributing speakers and sources, condensing or editing quotes, or checking that quoted material is faithful to its original meaning. The goal is quotations that earn the reader's trust.

## Core Rules

### Attribute The Right Source Clearly

Every quotation must be tied to the person or document that actually produced the words. Misattribution, whether to the wrong person, a vague "officials said," or a secondary source when the primary is available, undermines the quote's value and can defame or embarrass the wrong party. Attribution should be specific enough that a reader could verify the quote and judge the source.

Attribute precisely:

- name the speaker or author, not just the organization, where the individual matters;
- identify the venue, document, date, or occasion where the words appeared;
- distinguish the originator from the reporter or relay;
- when quoting a translation, name the translator or note the translation;
- when the source is indirect, signal it honestly ("said, according to").

A reader should always know whose words these are and where they came from.

### Quote Exactly, Or Mark Every Change

The default rule of quotation is verbatim fidelity: the words inside the quotation marks must be exactly what the source said or wrote. Any deviation must be marked. Silent editing, changing a word for flow, fixing grammar, or smoothing dialect, breaks the contract that the quote is the source's actual language. When changes are necessary, use accepted conventions to signal them.

Mark changes transparently:

- use brackets for inserted or substituted words;
- use ellipses for omissions, and ensure they do not distort meaning;
- use sic to signal that an error is in the original, not introduced;
- note significant paraphrases within brackets where needed for sense;
- indicate added emphasis, and note any emphasis in the original.

The reader should be able to trust that unmarked text is verbatim.

### Preserve The Meaning, Not Just The Words

Exact words can still mislead if the surrounding context is stripped away. A quote that was sarcastic, hypothetical, or immediately qualified can be presented verbatim yet convey the opposite of the speaker's intent. Faithful quotation preserves the meaning the words carried, which often means supplying the context that gives the words their sense.

Check that each quote reflects the source's meaning:

- does the surrounding text reverse or qualify the quoted words?
- was the statement sincere, ironic, hypothetical, or reported speech?
- does the quote omit a condition that changes its force?
- would the speaker recognize their meaning in how you have used the quote?

When context changes meaning, provide it. A technically exact quote that misleads is still a misuse.

### Handle Condensation And Editing Transparently

Writers often need to shorten or clean up quotes for length and readability. This is permissible, but only when done transparently and without altering meaning. Condensing a quote by removing redundant words is usually safe if marked; condensing it by removing the qualifier that changed its meaning is not. The line is whether the edited quote still represents what the source actually said.

Practices for honest condensation:

- use ellipses to show omissions, and check the result still reads as the source intended;
- use brackets to add words needed for grammar or clarity, kept minimal;
- avoid combining non-adjacent sentences in a way that implies continuity;
- never insert words that change the meaning, even in brackets;
- when a quote is too rough to use cleanly, paraphrase and attribute instead.

When in doubt between a heavily edited quote and a paraphrase, paraphrase.

### Provide Context For Every Quotation

A quote dropped into prose without context can be misread. The reader needs to know who the speaker is, why their words matter, what occasion produced them, and how they relate to the argument. Context is not padding; it is what lets the reader evaluate the quote rather than merely absorb it.

Supply at minimum:

- who is speaking and what stake or authority they have;
- the setting or document the quote comes from;
- what question or situation prompted the words;
- how the quote fits the point you are making.

A bare quote presented as self-evident invites misreading and often signals that the writer has not done the work of interpretation.

### Do Not Manufacture Or Reconstruct Quotes

A quote must be words the source actually said or wrote, recorded or recoverable. Reconstructing a quote from memory, assembling a composite from fragments the source did not say together, or inventing a quote that captures the "gist" are all forms of fabrication. Even when the invented words feel faithful, presenting them as verbatim is dishonest.

If you do not have an exact record:

- paraphrase and attribute, rather than quote;
- mark reconstructions clearly as paraphrase, not quotation;
- avoid presenting a summary as if it were the source's words;
- when relying on notes, distinguish what was recorded verbatim from what was reconstructed.

The quotation marks are a claim of exactness; do not make that claim unless you can support it.

### Distinguish Quotation From Paraphrase From Summary

These three forms of using a source carry different fidelity obligations, and blurring them confuses the reader about what is verbatim and what is interpreted. Quotation reproduces exact words. Paraphrase rewrites a specific passage in new words while preserving its meaning. Summary condenses a larger stretch of material in the writer's own words. Each requires attribution, but only quotation uses the source's language.

Keep the boundaries clear:

- quotation marks only around verbatim text;
- paraphrase attributed but not in quotes;
- summary attributed to the source of the ideas;
- no mixing that lets verbatim phrasing appear without quotes.

### Respect Fair Use And Proportion

Quoting at length, or quoting the heart of a short work, can exceed fair use and infringe copyright, especially with creative or commercial material. Quotation should be proportionate: enough to serve the purpose, not so much that it substitutes for the original. Long excerpts may need permission. The ethical and legal standard is to quote what is necessary and no more.

## Common Traps

### The Context-Free Quote

A verbatim quote presented without the qualifier, condition, or setting that gave it meaning misleads even though every word matches. Stripping context is the most common form of quote misuse because it is invisible to anyone who does not check the source.

### Silent Editing For Flow

Changing a word, fixing grammar, or smoothing dialect inside quotation marks, without brackets, makes the quote read better while breaking the promise of verbatim fidelity. If the source's words need changing, mark it or paraphrase.

### Selective Ellipses That Reverse Meaning

Removing a clause with an ellipsis can flip a quote's meaning entirely, especially when the omitted words qualified or negated the kept words. An ellipsis must never change what the source was saying.

### Misattributing To The Convenient Source

Quoting a secondary report while attributing the words to the primary speaker, or naming the wrong official, sends readers to the wrong place and can misrepresent who said what. Attribute to the actual source of the words.

### Paraphrase Disguised As Quotation

Putting quotation marks around words that are actually your paraphrase, even a close one, presents your interpretation as the source's exact language. If it is not verbatim, do not quote it.

### Irony Or Hypothetical Presented As Belief

A quote that was sarcastic, a thought experiment, or reported speech, presented as the speaker's sincere position, distorts meaning while looking exact. Always check what kind of statement the quote was.

### Over-Quoting To Pad Or Borrow Authority

Stringing together long quotes can substitute the source's voice for the writer's analysis, or borrow authority the writer has not earned. Quote to support your argument, not to avoid making one.

## Self-Check

Before treating quotations as faithful and properly attributed, verify:

- Each quote is attributed to the specific person or document that produced the words.
- The source is identified clearly enough that a reader could locate and verify the quote.
- Words inside quotation marks are verbatim, with every change marked.
- Brackets, ellipses, and sic are used correctly and do not alter meaning.
- No ellipsis omits words that qualify or reverse the quoted passage.
- Each quote retains the meaning it carried in its original context.
- Irony, hypotheticals, and reported speech are not presented as sincere belief; context is supplied so the reader can evaluate who, why, and where
- Condensed or edited quotes still represent what the source actually said; no quote is reconstructed, composited, or invented from memory or gist
- Paraphrase and summary are not presented inside quotation marks; long or central excerpts fall within fair use or have permission
- Quotation supports the argument rather than substituting for the writer's analysis; a reader checking the source would find the quote faithful in words and meaning
