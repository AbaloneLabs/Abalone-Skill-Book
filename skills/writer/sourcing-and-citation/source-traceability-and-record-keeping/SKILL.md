# Source Traceability And Record Keeping

Sources vanish. Web pages change, links rot, articles get edited, datasets get updated, paywalls move, and the precise wording a writer quoted three months ago quietly disappears. A draft that was fully sourced at the time of writing can become unverifiable by the time of publication, and the writer discovers this only when a reader, editor, or fact-checker follows a citation and finds something different, or nothing at all. The harm is not hypothetical; it is the most common way a careful piece becomes indefensible after the fact.

Traceability is a judgment problem because the record must be built at the moment of gathering, when it is cheap, rather than reconstructed later, when it is often impossible. An agent that treats sources as stable and self-evident tends to capture only the link, losing the date, the version, the access conditions, and the exact location of the claim. Later, when the source has shifted, the writer cannot prove what it said when they read it, and the citation becomes a liability. The craft is building a durable trail for every source, so that each claim can be traced back to exactly what was there, when it was there.

Use this skill when capturing source records during research, when building or maintaining a citation trail, when handling web and ephemeral sources, or when diagnosing why sources have become hard to verify. The goal is a record that survives the gap between research and publication, and beyond, so no claim is left hanging on a source that can no longer be checked.

## Core Rules

### Capture More Than The Link

A URL alone is not a source record. It tells you where something was, not what it said, when you read it, what version it was, or how to find it if the link changes. A durable record captures everything needed to relocate the source and reconstruct its content at the time of use. Build the full record at the moment of gathering, because the details that seem obvious now will be unrecoverable later.

A durable source record includes:

- the full citation, in the style the piece will use;
- the date you accessed the source, especially for web material;
- the specific location of the claim, such as page, section, timestamp, or paragraph;
- the exact wording for anything quoted, preserved verbatim;
- the version or edition, where the source has versions;
- a note on access conditions, such as paywall, login, or regional restriction.

The link is the least stable part of the record. Treat it as one element among many, and never as the whole record. If the link is all you have, you do not have a source; you have a pointer that may stop pointing.

### Record Access Dates For Anything That Can Change

Web sources are not fixed artifacts; they are live documents that can be edited without notice. A claim sourced to a website today may not match that website next month, and without an access date the citation becomes ambiguous: did the source say this when you read it, or did it change, or did you misread it? The access date anchors the citation to a specific moment and protects against later discrepancy.

Record access dates by:

- noting the exact date you read or retrieved each web source;
- treating any source without a fixed publication date as potentially volatile;
- re-checking volatile sources close to publication to confirm they still say what you cited;
- updating the access date if you re-verify, so the record reflects the last check.

Access dates matter most for news, institutional pages, social media, wikis, and any source under active revision. For stable sources like published books or archived journal articles, the publication date carries the weight; for everything else, the access date is what makes the citation honest.

### Preserve The Exact Wording And Location

Memory and paraphrase are not reliable records of what a source said. A quote recalled later is often subtly wrong; a statistic remembered without its context is often misapplied. Preserve the exact wording and the exact location for anything you might cite, so the citation can be verified word for word and the claim can be traced to its precise origin.

Preserve by:

- copying exact wording for any quotation, with quotation marks, at the moment of reading;
- recording the page, section, or timestamp for every specific claim or figure;
- capturing surrounding context, so a quote cannot be mistaken for something it was not;
- distinguishing in your record between direct quote, paraphrase, and your own reaction.

A source record that preserves exact wording and location lets you cite with confidence and revise without re-researching. A record that preserves only the gist forces reconstruction under deadline pressure, which is when misquotation and fabrication creep in.

### Archive Ephemeral And Load-Bearing Sources

Some sources are especially likely to disappear or change: social media posts, news articles behind moving paywalls, draft documents, internal pages, and material that the author might edit or delete. For load-bearing claims resting on such sources, archiving at the moment of capture is not optional. An archived snapshot preserves the source as it was when you read it, regardless of later changes.

Archive by:

- using a web archive service to capture a permanent snapshot of the page;
- saving a local copy, such as a PDF or screenshot, for material that may be removed;
- recording the archive link alongside the original URL in your source record;
- archiving social media posts individually, since they are frequently deleted or edited.

Archiving is cheap insurance for high-stakes citations. A load-bearing claim that depends on a tweet or a press release should never rest on a live link alone. If the source matters to the argument, preserve it in a form that cannot be silently altered.

### Track Versions And Editions

Many sources exist in multiple versions: editions of a book, drafts of a report, versions of a dataset, updates of a webpage. Citing without specifying the version creates ambiguity about what you actually read, and later versions may say something different. Track the version so the citation points to the specific artifact you used.

Track versions by:

- noting the edition, printing, or revision date for books and reports;
- recording the version or release for datasets and software;
- flagging when a source is a preprint, draft, or working paper rather than a final version;
- re-checking near publication whether a newer version has changed the claim you relied on.

A claim sourced to "the report" is weaker than one sourced to "the 2023 edition of the report." Version tracking is especially important for data, where updated releases can revise figures you cited, and for preprints, where the published version may differ from the draft you read.

### Maintain A Central, Searchable Record

Source records scattered across browser bookmarks, scattered documents, and memory are functionally lost when drafting begins. A central, searchable record lets you find any source by claim, author, or topic in seconds, which is what makes the record usable rather than merely stored. The system can be simple, but it must be unified and consistently maintained.

Build the record so it supports retrieval by:

- keeping all source records in one searchable location rather than scattered;
- tagging or labeling entries by topic, claim, or section of the piece;
- including a brief note on how each source relates to your central question;
- cross-referencing related sources so one leads to others;
- marking which sources are load-bearing versus peripheral.

A consistent plain-text system that you actually maintain beats a sophisticated system used inconsistently. The test is retrieval: when you need a specific source during drafting, can you find it and its full record immediately? If not, the system is failing its purpose.

### Verify The Trail Before Publication

The citation trail is only as good as its last verification. Between research and publication, sources can move, change, or vanish, and a citation that was honest at capture can become broken by publication. Build a verification pass into the final stage, checking that every citation still resolves to what you claim it says.

Verify by:

- following every link to confirm it resolves to the claimed content;
- re-checking volatile web sources against your captured record for drift;
- confirming page numbers and locations still match in any source that may have been reissued;
- replacing or archiving any source that has disappeared since capture.

A verification pass catches the decay that occurs during the gap between research and publication. It is the last chance to fix a broken trail before a reader finds it, and it is far cheaper than a post-publication correction.

## Common Traps

### The Link As The Whole Record

Capturing only a URL loses the date, version, wording, and location that make a source verifiable. Build the full record at the moment of gathering, not just the link.

### Missing Access Dates For Volatile Sources

Web sources can change without notice, and without an access date the citation becomes ambiguous. Record access dates for anything that is not a fixed publication.

### Relying On Memory For Wording And Figures

Recalled quotes and statistics drift under deadline pressure. Preserve exact wording and location at the moment of reading.

### No Archive For Load-Bearing Ephemera

A tweet, press release, or draft that carries weight can disappear or be edited. Archive load-bearing ephemeral sources at capture.

### Ignoring Versions And Editions

Citing without specifying the version creates ambiguity about what you read. Track editions, releases, and drafts, and re-check for updates near publication.

### Scattered Records That Defeat Retrieval

Source records spread across bookmarks and memory are lost when drafting begins. Keep a central, searchable, consistently maintained record.

### No Verification Pass Before Publication

Citations honest at capture can decay before publication. Verify that every citation still resolves before the piece ships.

## Self-Check

Before treating source traceability and record keeping as complete, verify:

- Each source record contains the full citation, not just a link.
- Access dates are recorded for all web and potentially volatile sources.
- Exact wording and specific locations are preserved for every quotation and key claim.
- Load-bearing ephemeral sources have been archived in a permanent form.
- Versions, editions, and drafts are tracked so the citation points to what you actually read.
- Source records are kept in a central, searchable, consistently maintained location.
- Entries are tagged or labeled so any source can be retrieved quickly during drafting.
- Load-bearing sources are marked and prioritized for verification.
- The record distinguishes direct quotes, paraphrases, and your own reactions; a verification pass has confirmed every link resolves to the claimed content
- Volatile sources have been re-checked near publication for drift or disappearance; a reader following any citation could find the source as it was when you read it
