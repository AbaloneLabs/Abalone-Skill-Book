# Source Evaluation And Credibility

Not all sources are equal, and treating them as if they were is how arguments collapse. A claim is only as strong as the source behind it, and a writer who cannot tell a peer-reviewed study from a press release, a primary document from a secondary interpretation, or an independent expert from an interested party will eventually build an argument on something that cannot bear the weight. The harm is usually invisible until a reader who knows the source landscape dismantles the piece, by which point the credibility damage is done.

Source evaluation is a judgment problem because credibility is contextual, not absolute. The same source can be authoritative for one claim and unreliable for another; a primary source can be indispensable for what happened and misleading for what it meant; an interested party can be the best source for their own position and the worst source for an neutral assessment. An agent that applies a single rule, such as "academic is better" or "official is better," tends to misjudge sources systematically, overtrusting familiar formats and undertrusting less conventional ones. The craft is matching each source to the specific claim it is being asked to support.

Use this skill when selecting sources for a piece, when judging whether a source can carry a particular claim, when distinguishing source types, or when a draft relies on sources whose credibility has not been examined. The goal is to know, for every load-bearing claim, whether the source behind it is strong enough and honest enough to survive scrutiny.

## Core Rules

### Match Source Strength To Claim Weight

Not every claim needs the strongest possible source, but every load-bearing claim does. The credibility required scales with the stakes: a passing illustration can rest on a modest source, while the central claim of the piece must rest on the strongest available evidence. Evaluate sources relative to the weight they carry in your argument, and concentrate verification where the load is heaviest.

Match by asking:

- is this claim load-bearing, such that if the source is wrong the argument falls;
- is the claim contested, requiring stronger evidence than an uncontested fact;
- is the claim surprising or counterintuitive, requiring more authority to be believed;
- would a skeptical reader demand a better source than the one offered.

A peripheral claim can survive a weak source; a foundational claim cannot. Identify which sources are carrying weight and hold them to a higher standard than sources used for color or context.

### Distinguish Primary, Secondary, And Tertiary Sources

Source type determines what a source is good for, and confusing the types leads to misweighting. A primary source is closest to the event or phenomenon: the original document, dataset, interview, artifact, or direct observation. A secondary source interprets, analyzes, or reports on primary material. A tertiary source compiles and summarizes, such as an encyclopedia, textbook, or review article. Each has a distinct role and distinct limits.

Use each type for what it does best:

- primary sources for what actually happened or was said, with the caveat that primary does not mean unbiased;
- secondary sources for interpretation, synthesis, and expert analysis of primary material;
- tertiary sources for orientation, background, and established consensus, never as the authority on a contested point.

A tertiary source is a starting point, not an endpoint. Citing an encyclopedia for a contested claim invites dismissal. Conversely, insisting on primary sources for settled background is wasteful. Match the source type to the claim's needs and the field's conventions.

### Evaluate Authority Relevant To The Claim

Authority is not a general quality; it is specific to a domain. A Nobel laureate in physics is not an authority on nutrition; a celebrated CEO is not an authority on macroeconomic policy; a famous doctor is not an authority on every medical question. Evaluate whether the source's expertise is relevant to the specific claim being made, not merely whether the source is eminent.

Assess relevant authority by:

- the source's training, credentials, or sustained work in the specific field of the claim;
- their direct experience with the specific phenomenon, case, or data;
- their standing among peers who work on the same problem;
- whether their authority is recognized across the field or only within a faction.

Beware of borrowed authority: a source famous in one domain cited as if their eminence transfers to another. The relevant question is not "is this person an authority" but "is this person an authority on this."

### Detect Bias, Stake, And Conflict Of Interest

Every source has a perspective, and pretending otherwise makes evaluation weaker, not stronger. Bias does not automatically discredit a source, but undisclosed or unexamined bias does. The goal is to identify what might shape a source's claims, weigh it, and disclose it to the reader where it matters. A source with a stake can still be the best source for some claims and the worst for others.

Detect stake by asking:

- does the source have a financial, political, institutional, or personal interest in the claim's outcome;
- is the source an advocate, employee, or beneficiary of a position;
- does the source's funding or affiliation create pressure toward a conclusion;
- would disclosure of the stake change how a reader weighs the claim.

An interested source is not forbidden; it is flagged. Citing an industry study for industry data may be appropriate; citing it to settle a question about industry harm is not. When stake is relevant, name it, and prefer independent corroboration for claims where the stake creates risk.

### Prefer Independent Corroboration For High-Stakes Claims

A single source, however strong, is more vulnerable than two independent sources converging on the same point. For high-stakes, contested, or surprising claims, seek corroboration from sources that do not derive from each other. Independence matters: two articles citing the same press release are one source, not two.

Corroborate by:

- finding sources with separate origination of the evidence, not shared dependence;
- checking whether independent methods reach the same conclusion;
- looking for confirmation across different stake positions, which is especially strong;
- distinguishing a consensus of independent experts from a repeated single claim.

When independent sources converge, the claim gains strength beyond any one. When sources diverge, the divergence itself becomes part of the story you tell. Do not present a single source as settled when independent sources disagree.

### Recognize Editorial And Methodological Quality

Credibility depends not only on who produced a source but on the process behind it. A peer-reviewed journal article, an investigative report with editorial oversight, a dataset with documented methodology, and a government statistic with a known collection process each carry quality signals that a blog post, a press release, or an unreviewed preprint do not. Evaluate the process, not just the product.

Quality signals include:

- editorial review, peer review, or fact-checking before publication;
- documented and reproducible methodology for data and research;
- transparency about sources, funding, and limitations;
- a track record of corrections and retractions when wrong;
- independence from the interests the source covers.

A source that documents its method and corrects its errors is more trustworthy than one that asserts without showing its work. Prefer sources whose process you could inspect over those whose authority you must take on faith.

### Treat Anonymity And Aggregation With Care

Anonymous sources and aggregated claims, such as "experts say" or "studies show," carry less weight than attributed, specific evidence, because the reader cannot evaluate what they cannot see. Anonymity is sometimes necessary, especially in investigative work, but it shifts the burden of credibility onto the writer's judgment and the publication's reputation. Use anonymity and aggregation deliberately, not as a shortcut.

Handle them by:

- explaining why a source is anonymous when anonymity is used;
- providing as much context as possible, such as role and stake, without exposing identity;
- avoiding vague aggregation like "many experts" unless you can show the breadth;
- treating aggregated claims as illustration, not proof, unless the aggregation is itself documented.

"Studies show" without citation is a red flag. If studies show it, name them. If experts say it, identify enough about them for the reader to judge. Vague authority is weaker than specific authority and often signals that the specific authority would not hold up.

## Common Traps

### Treating All Sources As Equal

Applying a single credibility rule regardless of claim weight or source type leads to systematic misweighting. Match source strength to the specific claim and its stakes.

### Borrowed Authority Across Domains

Eminence in one field cited as authority in another overweights the source. Evaluate authority relevant to the specific claim, not general fame.

### Undisclosed Stake Disguised As Neutrality

Failing to identify a source's interest hides what might shape the claim. Detect and disclose stake where it affects how the reader should weigh the evidence.

### Tertiary Sources For Contested Claims

Citing encyclopedias or summaries for points under dispute invites dismissal. Use tertiary sources for orientation and primary or analytical sources for contested claims.

### Shared-Origin "Corroboration"

Two sources repeating the same underlying claim are one source, not two. Seek independent origination for genuine corroboration.

### Vague Aggregation As Authority

"Experts say" and "studies show" without specifics carry little weight and often conceal weak evidence. Name the experts or the studies.

## Self-Check

Before treating source evaluation as complete, verify:

- Each load-bearing claim rests on a source strong enough to carry its weight.
- Source types (primary, secondary, tertiary) are matched to the claims they support.
- Authority is evaluated as relevant to the specific claim, not as general eminence.
- Bias, stake, and conflicts of interest are identified and disclosed where they matter.
- High-stakes or contested claims are corroborated by independent sources.
- Editorial and methodological quality has been considered, not just the source's identity.
- Anonymity and aggregation are used deliberately, with context provided where possible.
- No contested claim relies on a tertiary source as its final authority.
- "Corroboration" reflects independent origination, not shared dependence on one source; sources with stakes are flagged rather than presented as neutral
- The reader could assess each major source's credibility from what you provide; vague appeals to authority have been replaced with specific, attributable evidence
