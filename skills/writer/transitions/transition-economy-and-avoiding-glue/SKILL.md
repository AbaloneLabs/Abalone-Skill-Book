# Transition Economy And Avoiding Glue

Less is more with transitions, but only when the less is the right kind. Economical writing does not mean stripping out every connective word; it means using transitions deliberately, so that each one does real work and none are ornamental. The failure mode this skill addresses is glue: connective words and signposting phrases sprinkled through prose to create the appearance of flow, when in fact they add no information and often mask the absence of genuine logical connection. Glue makes prose feel busy without making it feel connected.

The judgment problem is that agents tend to over-transition defensively. Uncertain whether the reader will follow, they load every junction with "furthermore," "in addition," "moving on," "as mentioned earlier," and "it is important to note that," hoping volume will substitute for clarity. But excessive glue has the opposite effect: it numbs the reader, buries the real relationships under labels, and signals insecurity. Worse, when transitions are hard and the writer papers over the difficulty with more glue, the underlying structural problem goes unaddressed. Recognizing glue, cutting it, and reading the resulting hard transitions as structural diagnostics is the discipline this skill develops.

Use this skill when revising any drafted prose for tightness and flow: essays, reports, articles, memos, guides, and long-form posts. The goal is to prevent the agent from shipping prose whose transitions are ornamental filler rather than load-bearing logic.

## Core Rules

### Cut Every Transition That Adds No Information

The first pass of transition economy is deletion. Read each connective word and signposting phrase and ask: if I removed this, would the reader lose any information about how the ideas relate? If the answer is no, cut it. Many transitions are pure decoration: "it is important to note that," "as we have seen," "moving forward," "needless to say." They signal nothing and slow everything.

After cutting, check whether the relationship is still clear. Often it is, because the ideas themselves carry the connection. If it is not, you have found a place where a real transition is needed, and you can now write one that does work rather than leaving the glue in place.

### Distinguish Load-Bearing Transitions From Ornament

Not all transitions should be cut. A load-bearing transition signals a relationship the reader genuinely needs: a contrast that reverses direction, a consequence that changes stakes, a shift in level or mode, a pivot the reader would otherwise miss. These earn their place. Ornament transitions merely fill space or hedge against the writer's anxiety that the reader might not follow.

Test each remaining transition: does it change how the reader holds the next idea compared to holding it without the signal? If yes, keep it. If the idea reads the same with or without the word, the word is ornament. Keep only what changes the reader's frame.

### Recognize Glue As A Symptom, Not Just A Flaw

Excessive glue is often a symptom of a deeper problem. When a writer feels the need to smother a junction in connectives, it is usually because the two ideas do not actually connect well: they are in the wrong order, one does not belong, or a needed intermediate idea is missing. The glue is the writer's unconscious signal that the structure is strained.

When you find a glue-heavy junction, do not just cut the words and move on. Ask why the writer felt the need for them. Investigate the structure. Often the right fix is reordering, adding the missing link, or cutting one of the ideas, after which the transition writes itself cleanly and the glue is no longer tempting.

### Prefer Structural Connection To Lexical Glue

The strongest connective structure is structural, not lexical. Parallel construction, repeated key terms, sentence rhythm, and the old-to-new information pattern carry relationships without connective words. When the structure carries the connection, transitions become emphasis rather than scaffolding, and the prose feels both tight and clear.

Reach for structural connection first. Add a lexical transition only when the structure cannot carry the relationship on its own, which is usually at genuine contrasts, pivots, and level shifts. Most accumulation, continuation, and example relationships need no lexical transition at all if the structure is sound.

### Avoid Signposting That Tells Instead Of Shows

Signposting phrases ("first, I will discuss X; then I will turn to Y") tell the reader about the structure instead of embodying it. A little signposting at major junctures can help, but habitual signposting makes the reader feel they are reading a tour guide rather than the thing itself. It also adds words without adding ideas.

Prefer showing the structure through the prose's own movement. If the reader can feel the progression from the content, the signpost is redundant. Reserve explicit signposting for genuinely complex structures where the reader benefits from a map, and keep it brief.

### Calibrate Transition Density To Junction Difficulty

Not every junction needs the same treatment. A simple continuation between closely related sentences may need no transition at all. A contrast that reverses the reader's expectations needs a clear signal. A shift between major sections may need a brief orienting bridge. Calibrate the transition's presence and weight to how much help the reader needs at that specific junction.

Flat transition density, where every junction gets the same connective treatment, is a sign the writer is not thinking about each junction. Vary the treatment: nothing where nothing is needed, a word where a word helps, a sentence where a sentence is warranted.

### Treat The Hard Transition As A Diagnostic

When a junction resists clean transition even after cutting glue and trying structural connection, treat it as a diagnostic signal. The difficulty is telling you something: the ideas may not belong adjacent, the order may be wrong, or a needed idea is absent. Do not solve it by piling on more glue.

Investigate the structural cause. Reorder, insert, or cut until the transition becomes natural. The moment a previously hard junction flows without forced connectives is the moment the structure is right.

### Know When No Transition Is The Right Transition

Sometimes the best transition is none. Two ideas placed in deliberate juxtaposition, with nothing between them, can create a relationship the reader feels more powerfully than any word could signal. Juxtaposition is a transition technique, not the absence of one. Use it when the relationship is one the reader should infer and feel, such as a shocking contrast or an ironic pairing.

Decide consciously for silence. The absence of a transition is a choice when it serves the effect; it is a flaw when it leaves the reader stranded. Know the difference.

## Common Traps

### Decorative Glue

Connective words and signposting phrases that add no information fill prose with noise and signal insecurity. Cut them.

### Signposting Instead Of Structuring

Telling the reader about the structure rather than embodying it makes the prose feel like a tour and adds words without ideas.

### Flat Transition Density

Treating every junction the same, regardless of difficulty, shows the writer is not thinking about each connection. Calibrate to need.

### Glue Masking Structural Problems

Piling connectives on a hard junction hides rather than fixes the underlying issue. The hard transition is a signal to fix the structure.

### Over-Reliance On Lexical Transitions

Using words for every connection, never structure, produces mechanical prose. Structural connection should carry most of the load.

### Cutting Too Aggressively

Economy is not amputation. Cutting load-bearing transitions that the reader genuinely needs produces stranded, choppy prose. Cut ornament, keep what does work.

### Missing The Power Of Deliberate Silence

Filling every junction with a word prevents the use of juxtaposition, which can signal a relationship more powerfully than any connective.

## Self-Check

Before treating the transition economy as complete, verify:

- Every connective word and signposting phrase was tested for whether it adds information; ornament was cut.
- Remaining transitions are load-bearing, changing how the reader holds the next idea.
- Glue-heavy junctions were investigated as symptoms of structural problems, not just cleaned up.
- Structural connection, not lexical glue, carries most of the connective load.
- Signposting is minimal and reserved for genuinely complex structures.
- Transition density is calibrated to junction difficulty, not applied uniformly.
- Hard transitions prompted structural fixes rather than forced connectives.
- No load-bearing transition was cut in the name of economy.
- Deliberate silence and juxtaposition are used where the effect serves the relationship; the prose feels both tight and clear, neither glue-heavy nor stranded
- Each remaining transition would be missed if removed, confirming it earns its place; a reader feels the connections through the ideas, not through a layer of labels
