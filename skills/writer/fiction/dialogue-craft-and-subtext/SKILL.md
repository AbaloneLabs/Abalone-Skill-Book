# Dialogue Craft And Subtext

Good dialogue is not recorded speech. It is compressed, shaped speech that does more than one thing at once. Real conversation is full of circling, filler, and non sequitur; fiction dialogue keeps the texture of real talk while cutting everything that does not advance character, conflict, or stakes. The craft is making speech sound natural without being natural, because natural speech on the page is usually boring.

The deeper layer is subtext: what the conversation is actually about beneath the words people say. People rarely say exactly what they mean, especially when the stakes are emotional. They deflect, test, threaten, plead, flirt, and punish through indirection. A scene where characters state their feelings directly is a scene with no tension. A scene where the real subject is never named but is felt by the reader in every line is a scene doing the work of fiction. Agents tend to miss this because the surface, the literal words, looks complete. But dialogue without subtext reads as flat, on-the-nose, and emotionally unconvincing, no matter how clever the individual lines.

Use this skill when drafting or revising dialogue, when speech feels flat or expository, when characters sound alike, or when conversation fails to carry conflict. The agent has latitude in voice, rhythm, and form. The obligations are that dialogue operates on multiple levels, that each voice is distinct and consistent, and that speech dramatizes rather than reports.

## Core Rules

### Make Dialogue Do At Least Two Things

Strong dialogue multitasks. A line that only conveys information is exposition in costume. A line that only shows character may stall the plot. Aim for each exchange to carry at least two of: advance the plot, reveal character, build or shift the relationship, raise or change the stakes, create conflict, or establish subtext.

Before keeping a passage of dialogue, ask what each beat is doing. If a line only delivers data the reader needs, find a way to make it also reveal who is speaking or strain the relationship. If a line only shows personality, make sure it also moves the scene.

### Build The Scene Around Subtext

Subtext is the meaning beneath the literal speech, created by the gap between what a character says and what they want, feel, or fear. That gap is produced by stakes: when something matters and cannot be said directly, characters talk around it.

To engineer subtext:

- give each character a different objective in the scene;
- ensure at least one character cannot or will not say the real thing (due to pride, fear, shame, strategy, or love);
- let the real subject emerge through avoidance, emphasis, contradiction, and what is refused;
- let silence, interruption, and topic change carry meaning;
- trust the reader to infer; do not narrate the subtext the dialogue already shows.

A useful test: can you state what the scene is really about, and is that subject different from what the characters are literally discussing? If they are identical, the dialogue is probably too direct.

### Differentiate Voices Consistently

Characters should be identifiable by how they speak, not only by tags. Voice differentiation comes from stable, character-specific patterns.

Differentiate through:

- vocabulary range and register;
- sentence length and rhythm;
- use of abstraction versus concreteness;
- tendency to ask, assert, deflect, or accuse;
- relationship to silence and to interruption;
- verbal tics used sparingly, never as the whole voice;
- what each character avoids saying.

A character's voice should stay consistent across scenes unless a deliberate shift dramatizes change. If two characters' lines could be swapped without the reader noticing, the voices are not yet distinct.

### Handle Dialect And Idiom With Care

Dialect, accent, and idiom can vivify a voice or demean it. Phonetic spelling and heavy dialect spelling slow the reader, risk caricature, and can read as condescending toward the group represented. Prefer evoking voice through word choice, syntax, rhythm, and idiom rather than through distorted spelling. Use nonstandard grammar consistently and deliberately, and make sure it reflects a real speech community rather than a stereotype.

Ask whether the dialect serves the character and the story or whether it signals otherness for the reader's benefit. If the latter, reconsider.

### Use Tags And Action Beats Deliberately

Dialogue tags and action beats are tools, not decoration. The default "said" is often strongest because it disappears, letting the voice carry the weight. Reserve expressive tags for moments where the manner of speaking matters and "said" cannot convey it.

Use action beats to:

- anchor the speaker without a tag;
- reveal emotion the speech hides;
- control pacing and insert pause;
- show power dynamics through physical behavior;
- break a long speech into readable units.

Avoid overusing beats that merely gesture, such as constant smiling, nodding, or sighing. Each beat should earn its place by adding information the words lack.

### Let Conflict Drive The Exchange

Dialogue without conflict is people taking turns talking. Conflict does not require shouting; it requires opposed objectives. Two characters who both want different things from the same conversation will generate tension even in calm speech. Identify what each character wants in the scene and why they cannot both get it. The conversation becomes a negotiation, a contest, or a seduction rather than a briefing.

Even agreement can carry conflict if one character is concealing doubt, maneuvering for advantage, or protecting someone.

### Deliver Exposition Through Pressure, Not Lecture

Exposition through dialogue is necessary but dangerous. Characters telling each other things they already know, purely for the reader's benefit, produces the "as you know" effect and breaks belief.

To deliver background naturally:

- put the information where it is under pressure, where one character has reason to demand or resist it;
- let it emerge through argument, interrogation, or misunderstanding;
- let characters lie, misremember, or be wrong, so exposition carries unreliability;
- give the reader only what the scene needs now, withholding the rest;
- prefer showing the consequence of past events to narrating the events themselves.

If information must be conveyed and no character would naturally say it, consider whether dialogue is the right vehicle at all.

### Choose Dramatize Versus Summarize

Not every conversation deserves to be rendered line by line. Dramatize dialogue when the exchange itself is the event: when conflict, character, or stakes live in how things are said. Summarize dialogue when only the outcome matters, when the content is routine, or when full rendering would slow the story without earning its space.

The decision is about attention. Spend rendered dialogue on the scenes the reader needs to experience, and compress the rest into summary that keeps the story moving.

### Control Rhythm And Pace

Conversation has music. Vary line length, use interruption and trailing-off, insert beats and silence, and let some exchanges come fast while others stretch. Rhythm signals emotion: short clipped lines suggest tension or urgency; long meandering ones suggest evasion, intimacy, or control. Read dialogue aloud to find where it stumbles or where the beat feels wrong.

## Common Traps

### On-The-Nose Speech

When characters say exactly what they feel or think, the scene loses tension and the reader feels spoon-fed. Real emotional stakes produce indirection. If the most important thing is being said outright, lower the scene's honesty or raise its risk.

### As-You-Know Exposition

Characters explaining shared knowledge to each other solely for the reader signals artifice. If both speakers already know it, they would not say it. Find a pressured reason for the information to surface, or move it out of dialogue.

### Homogeneous Voices

When every character uses the same syntax and vocabulary, the reader cannot tell who is speaking without tags. This usually means the writer's voice has overridden the characters'. Build each voice from distinct patterns and test by masking the tags.

### Over-Tagging And Adverb Tags

Piling on "he said angrily," "she exclaimed," "he retorted sharply" usually means the words are not carrying the emotion. Strong dialogue makes the manner clear; tags should recede. When adverbs do the work, the dialogue is too thin.

### Everyone Is Witty

If every character speaks in polished epigrams, wit becomes the writer's voice, not the characters', and the cast blurs into one cleverness. Let some characters be plain, halting, or blunt. Wit is most effective when it is not uniform.

### Dialect As Caricature

Heavy phonetic spelling and stereotyped idiom reduce characters to markers of class, region, or race. Prefer syntax and word choice that evoke speech without distorting it, and ensure the portrayal respects the speech community.

### Dialogue That Does Not Move

Long exchanges where nothing changes, no objective shifts, and no new pressure arrives are talking that the story does not need. Every scene should leave the situation altered, even slightly.

### Narrating The Subtext

If the dialogue already shows the tension, do not add narration explaining what the characters really mean. Trust the reader. Explaining subtext after creating it insults the inference you invited.

## Self-Check

Before treating dialogue as complete, verify:

- Each passage of dialogue is doing at least two things at once, not merely conveying information.
- The scene has identifiable subtext: the real subject differs from the literal subject, and it is conveyed through indirection rather than narration.
- Each character's voice is distinguishable by syntax, vocabulary, and rhythm, and remains consistent across scenes unless a shift is deliberate.
- Dialect and idiom, if used, evoke speech through word choice and syntax rather than caricature or demeaning phonetic spelling.
- Dialogue tags and action beats are used deliberately, with "said" as the default and expressive tags reserved for where manner matters.
- The scene is built on opposed objectives, so conflict drives the exchange rather than characters merely taking turns.
- Exposition is delivered under pressure, with no "as you know" moments where characters state shared knowledge for the reader's benefit.
- The choice to dramatize versus summarize each conversation is deliberate, based on whether the exchange itself is the event.
- Rhythm varies through line length, interruption, silence, and beats, and reads naturally aloud.
- No narration explains the subtext the dialogue has already shown; the reader is trusted to infer.
