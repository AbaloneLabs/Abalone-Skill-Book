# Scene Goal And Conflict Construction

A scene is the smallest unit of change in a story. Its job is not to show time passing or to deliver information; its job is to move something from one state to another through pressure. A well-built scene has a character who wants something concrete in this moment, an obstacle that resists that want, and a result that leaves the situation different from how it began. Without these, a scene is description, exposition, or filler wearing the costume of drama.

The judgment problem is that agents tend to write scenes as occasions rather than as machines. They place two characters in a room because the plot needs them to talk, or they describe a location because the setting is next on the outline, without asking what each person wants right now, what stands in the way, and what is different when the scene ends. The result is the most common failure in narrative drafts: scenes that are well-written line by line but accumulate into nothing, because nothing in them was ever at stake and nothing ever changed.

Use this skill when drafting or revising any scene in fiction, memoir, narrative nonfiction, scripts, or interactive story. The goal is to make every scene a unit of change with clear goal, conflict, and consequence. The agent has freedom to write quiet scenes, but should understand that quiet does not mean inert.

## Core Rules

### Give The Viewpoint Character A Scene Goal

Every scene needs a viewpoint character who wants something specific and immediate, something that could plausibly be achieved or denied within the scene. "Wants to be happy" is not a scene goal. "Wants to get the landlord to fix the heater before the inspection tomorrow" is a scene goal. The goal should be concrete enough that the reader can tell whether the character got it, lost it, or traded it away by the scene's end.

Clarify the goal early. The reader should understand what the character is trying to accomplish before they can care whether they succeed. A scene where the goal only becomes clear in retrospect forces the reader to reread, and usually loses them first.

Distinguish the scene goal from the larger story want. A character may want, overall, to win a custody battle; in this scene, their goal is to get through a parent-teacher meeting without losing their temper. Scene goals are steps toward, or obstacles to, the larger want.

### Make The Conflict Real And Present

Conflict is the force that resists the goal. It can come from another character with an opposing want, from the environment, from the character's own limits, or from a moral tension inside them. Whatever its source, it must be genuinely capable of preventing the goal, not merely inconvenient.

Test the conflict:

- Does the opposing force have its own understandable motivation, not just arbitrary obstruction?
- Is the resistance present and active in the scene, or only referenced?
- Could the viewpoint character plausibly lose?
- Does the conflict escalate within the scene rather than sitting flat?

A scene where everyone agrees, or where the obstacle is nominal, has no engine. Even a quiet scene needs tension: an unspoken thing, a risk of saying the wrong word, a choice with a cost.

### Raise Scene-Level Stakes

Stakes are what the character loses, suffers, or forfeits if the scene goal fails. Without stakes, the reader has no reason to care about the outcome. Stakes can be external (a job, a relationship, safety, money) or internal (pride, self-image, a secret, a chance at love), and the strongest scenes braid both.

Make the stakes specific and personal. "It would be bad" is not a stake. "If she admits the mistake here, she loses the promotion she has sacrificed three years for" is a stake. The reader cares in proportion to how much the character cares and how clearly the cost is shown.

### Ensure The Scene Ends In Change

A scene must leave the story in a different state than it found it. The classic formulation is that a scene ends in one of four ways: yes (the goal achieved), no (the goal denied), yes-but (the goal achieved at an unexpected cost), or no-and (the goal denied and things got worse). The last two are usually the most useful, because pure yes tends to deflate tension and pure no can stall momentum, while complication keeps the story moving.

The change can be:

- a new piece of information revealed;
- a relationship shifted;
- a plan made or ruined;
- a decision forced;
- a secret exposed or buried;
- an option closed off;
- the character's understanding of their situation altered.

If you cannot state, in one sentence, what is different at the end of the scene, the scene probably has not earned its place.

### Link The Scene To The Larger Arc

A scene that changes something internally but connects to nothing outside it can feel self-contained and decorative. Each scene should push the larger story forward: advance the plot, deepen character, raise the stakes of the central conflict, or set up a future turn. Ask how this scene's outcome changes what the character can or must do next.

Avoid scenes that exist only to convey backstory or exposition. If information must be delivered, build a real goal and conflict around it so the delivery is dramatized rather than reported.

### Let Conflict Reveal Character

Because a scene forces a character to act under pressure, it is one of the best engines for revealing who they are. The choices a character makes when blocked, the methods they resort to, what they refuse to do, and how they treat the person in their way all show character more powerfully than description can. Design the conflict so that the character's response exposes something the reader did not know, or confirms something with new weight.

### Vary Scene Intensity And Type

A story made only of high-intensity confrontation exhausts the reader; a story made only of quiet conversations drifts. Vary the kind and scale of conflict across scenes: an overt argument, a tense negotiation, a solitary struggle with a decision, a scene where the conflict is entirely internal and almost nothing is said. Variation lets the loud scenes feel loud and the quiet scenes carry weight.

### Cut Or Merge Scenes That Do No Work

If a scene has no clear goal, no real conflict, and produces no change, it should be cut, merged into another scene, or rebuilt. Holding onto a scene because it contains a line you like, or because it was in the outline, is how drafts accumulate dead weight. Be willing to salvage the good material and discard the empty container.

## Common Traps

### The Talking-Heads Scene With No Stakes

Two characters discuss a topic, exchange information, and part. Nothing was wanted, nothing was risked, nothing changed. This reads as exposition in dialogue form. Give at least one of them a goal the other can obstruct.

### The Goal That Is Never In Doubt

If the viewpoint character achieves the scene goal easily and predictably, the scene has no tension. The reader needs to feel, at points, that failure is genuinely possible. Let the conflict push back hard enough to make success uncertain.

### Conflict Without Understandable Opposition

An antagonist who blocks the goal for no reason, or purely to be difficult, feels like a prop. Give opposing forces their own coherent wants, so the conflict is between two real desires rather than between a hero and a cardboard wall.

### The Scene That Changes Nothing

A beautifully written scene that leaves the situation exactly as it began is a luxury the story may not be able to afford. If nothing is different afterward, ask whether the scene belongs in the work at all, or whether its function can be folded into another scene that does change something.

### Starting Too Early And Ending Too Late

Scenes often bloat because they begin before the pressure starts (arrivals, greetings, weather) and end after the change has landed (lingering reactions, exits, summaries). Begin near the moment the goal meets its obstacle and end soon after the change, letting the next scene pick up the consequence.

### Confusing Activity With Conflict

Characters who are busy, moving, and talking are not necessarily in conflict. Activity without resistance produces motion without tension. Ensure something in the scene genuinely pushes back against the goal.

## Self-Check

Before treating a scene as complete, verify:

- The viewpoint character has a specific, immediate, achievable-or-deniable goal stated or implied early.
- The scene goal is distinct from the larger story want but connected to it.
- A real conflict, with understandable opposition, is present and active in the scene.
- The character could plausibly fail to achieve the goal.
- Stakes are specific and personal, both external and internal where possible.
- The conflict escalates within the scene rather than sitting at one level.
- The scene ends in clear change: yes, no, yes-but, or no-and.
- You can state in one sentence what is different at the end of the scene; the scene's outcome pushes the larger story forward or sets up a future turn
- The conflict forces choices that reveal character; scene intensity and type vary across the work rather than all matching one register
- No scene has been kept solely for a liked line or outline fidelity when it does no work; the scene begins near pressure and ends soon after the change, without bloated setup or trailing reaction
