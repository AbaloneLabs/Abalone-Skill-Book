# Email And Professional Correspondence

Professional email is read in a crowded inbox by someone who owes you nothing. Its success is decided in the first two seconds: the subject line, the sender, and the opening sentence determine whether the message is opened, read fully, or dismissed. A well-written email gets a response; a poorly written one gets ignored, misread, or resent. The stakes are usually low per message but high in aggregate, because email is where most professional relationships are maintained or eroded.

Agents tend to miss this because email feels casual. The instinct is to write the way one speaks, to include context out of politeness, to hedge requests so as not to seem demanding. But every hedge costs clarity, and every unnecessary paragraph costs attention. Professional correspondence is a craft of compression and tone: say what you need, make the path forward obvious, and leave the reader feeling respected rather than burdened.

You have latitude in warmth and formality depending on the relationship, but the obligations are fixed: the subject must promise what the body delivers, the purpose must be visible immediately, and the reader must know exactly what you want them to do.

## Core Rules

### Write The Subject Line Last, And Make It A Promise

The subject line is the most read part of any email. It should tell the reader what the message is about and, where possible, what it needs from them. A vague subject like "Quick question" or "Following up" forces the reader to open the message to learn what it is, and competes with every other vague subject in their inbox.

Write the body first, then write a subject that faithfully summarizes it. Prefer specifics: "Approval needed by Thu: Q3 budget revision" is far better than "Budget." If the email requires action, signal it in the subject. If it is informational, signal that too, so the reader can triage. Never let the subject oversell or mislead; a subject that does not match the body trains the reader to distrust your messages.

### Lead With The Purpose, Not The Pleasantry

Open with the reason for the email in the first sentence or two. A brief greeting is fine, but do not bury the point under paragraphs of context, rapport, or apology. The reader should know within two sentences why you are writing and what you want.

If you need a decision, state it. If you are giving information, state what and why it matters. If you are requesting a meeting, say so and propose times. The opening is not the place to narrate how you came to write the email; it is the place to make the email's purpose unmistakable.

### Make The Ask Singular And Specific

The most common reason emails go unanswered is that the reader cannot tell what is being asked, or is being asked too many things at once. If you need something, ask for one clear thing. If there are multiple requests, separate them visually and prioritize, so the reader knows which matters most.

Be specific about what "yes" looks like: the decision, the file, the introduction, the date. Vague requests like "thoughts?" or "let me know" offload the work of defining the task onto the reader. A specific request is easier to answer and harder to defer.

### Calibrate Tone To Relationship And Stakes

Tone in email is not decoration; it is the signal of the relationship and the seriousness of the message. A note to a close colleague can be warm and brief. A message to a senior executive, a client in a tense situation, or a wider audience needs more care. Match the register to the context, and adjust for culture, seniority, and the emotional state of the recipient.

Avoid two opposite failures. Do not be so casual that you seem to underestimate the stakes, and do not be so formal that you sound cold or bureaucratic. When the message is sensitive, a bad outcome, a refusal, or a correction, slow down and reread for how it will land. Sarcasm, irony, and dry humor routinely misfire in text and across cultures; default to clarity over wit.

### Keep It Short Enough To Be Read

Length is a tax on the reader. Most professional emails should be readable in under a minute. If a message must be longer, use structure, headings, or bullets so the reader can scan. A wall of text signals that the writer did not respect the reader's time enough to edit.

Cut anything that does not serve the purpose. Pleasantries beyond a line, redundant context, throat-clearing openings, and CYA disclaimers that no one reads all add cost. The discipline of revision is to ask, of each sentence, whether removing it would change what the reader does. If not, remove it.

### Handle Sensitive And Negative Messages Deliberately

Bad news, refusals, corrections, and escalations require more care, not more words. State the message directly but humanely. Do not bury bad news in optimism or surround it with so much softening that the reader misses it. Do not deliver criticism in a way that humiliates. Aim for the reader to understand the message clearly and feel respected by how it was delivered.

For high-stakes or emotionally charged messages, consider whether email is the right medium at all. Some conversations belong in a call or in person. When you must write, reread the message imagining the reader's worst-case emotional interpretation, and revise until that interpretation is no longer plausible.

### Reply And Forward With Context

When replying, quote enough of the original that the reader knows what you are responding to, but not so much that your answer is buried. When forwarding, add a short note explaining why you are forwarding and what, if anything, you want the new recipient to do. A forwarded thread with no framing forces the reader to reconstruct your intent from a chain they did not live through.

Be deliberate about who is on the To, Cc, and Bcc lines. To means action is expected. Cc means FYI. Misusing these lines creates confusion about who owns the next step. Add people to a thread only when they need to be there, and consider whether a long thread should be summarized into a fresh message.

### Proofread For The Errors That Erode Credibility

Email is fast, but the wrong kind of error damages trust. The reader does not expect perfection, but they do notice when you get their name wrong, reference the wrong project, leave a placeholder unfilled, or send a message clearly meant for someone else. These errors signal carelessness more than typos do.

Before sending, check the recipient line, the subject, any names or specifics, and that every attachment is actually attached. If the message matters, read it once aloud in your head. The cost is seconds; the benefit is not having to send a correction.

## Common Traps

### The Subject That Says Nothing

"Hi" or "Question" gives the reader no reason to prioritize the message and guarantees it competes with every other vague subject. Specific subjects get read.

### Burying The Ask Under Context

Writers often build up to the request out of politeness. The reader gives up before reaching it. Lead with the purpose; provide context after.

### Asking Too Many Things At Once

A laundry-list email gets partially answered or ignored. One clear request per message, or clearly prioritized requests, gets action.

### Tone That Misfires Across Distance

Warmth reads as informality; brevity reads as coldness; humor reads as hostility. Without tone of voice, every choice is amplified. Reread for the worst plausible reading.

### The Email That Should Have Been A Meeting

Some things are too complex, too sensitive, or too likely to be misread for email. Choosing the wrong medium creates more problems than it solves.

### Forwarding Without Framing

A forwarded thread with no note forces the recipient to guess why they received it and what to do. A sentence of framing turns a burden into a task.

### Sending In Haste Or Anger

Email written in frustration almost always reads worse than intended and cannot be unsent. Draft it, step away, and reread before sending.

## Self-Check

Before treating the email as ready to send, verify:

- The subject line specifically reflects the content and signals any action required.
- The purpose of the email is clear within the first two sentences.
- The ask is singular, specific, and tells the reader exactly what "yes" looks like.
- Tone matches the relationship, stakes, and likely emotional state of the recipient.
- Length is justified; the message can be read quickly, with structure if it is long.
- Nothing in the message would land as cold, sarcastic, or hostile in the worst plausible reading.
- Sensitive or negative content is delivered directly but humanely, not buried or over-softened.
- The medium is appropriate; the message would not be better as a call or meeting; recipient lines are correct: To for action, Cc for awareness, and no one unnecessary
- Names, project references, and specifics are accurate; placeholders are filled; attachments are actually attached, and any quoted or forwarded context is framed
- A reader skimming only the subject and first sentence would grasp the essence; the message respects the reader's time more than it showcases the writer's effort
