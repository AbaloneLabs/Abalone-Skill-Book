# Memo And Report Writing

Memos and internal reports exist to move decisions and align understanding inside an organization. They are not essays, and they are not judged by elegance. They are judged by whether the right people, often busy and reading on a phone between meetings, can grasp what matters, what is at stake, and what is being asked of them, fast enough to act. A memo that is thorough but buries the decision point has failed even if every sentence is correct.

Agents tend to miss this because the instinct is to build a case the way an academic argument builds: context, then evidence, then conclusion. In executive and operational communication that order is backwards. The reader needs the conclusion and the ask first, then the reasoning, then the detail. The discipline of bottom-line-up-front is not a stylistic preference; it is a structural obligation shaped by how decision-makers actually read.

You have latitude in length and format, but the obligation is fixed: every internal document should make the decision-relevant content reachable in the first few seconds, and the supporting material should be organized so a reader can go as deep as they need and stop.

## Core Rules

### Lead With The Bottom Line

Open with the single most important thing the reader needs to know: the recommendation, the decision required, the status, or the risk. A reader who stops after the first paragraph should still have the essence. State the bottom line in plain terms, not as a tease. "I recommend we delay the launch by two weeks to fix the payment bug" is a bottom line. "There are several factors affecting our launch timeline" is not.

If the document requires a decision, name the decision and the recommended option in the opening. If it is informational, state the key finding. If it is a warning, state the risk and the proposed mitigation. The reader should never have to hunt for the point.

### Make The Ask Explicit And Actionable

Many memos fail because the ask is implied rather than stated. "We should consider reviewing the policy" is not an ask. "Approve the revised policy by Friday so Legal can publish Monday" is an ask. If you need approval, funding, a decision, a review, or an introduction, say exactly what you need, from whom, and by when.

Distinguish the decision ask from informational context. A reader who cannot tell whether they are being asked to do something will either ignore the memo or act on the wrong assumption. Put the ask where it cannot be missed, typically in the opening and again at the close.

### Structure For Skimming And Depth

Internal readers skim first, then decide whether to read more. Build the document in layers so each layer serves a different reading depth. A subject line and opening paragraph carry the essence. Headings carry the structure. The first sentence of each section carries the section's point. Body paragraphs and appendices carry the detail.

Use consistent, predictable sections. Common patterns include bottom line, background, analysis, options, recommendation, risks, next steps. Not every memo needs every section, but the sections you do use should appear in a familiar order so the reader knows where to look for each kind of information.

### Frame Around Decisions And Tradeoffs

Decision-makers read to choose, not to be educated. Frame the content around the decisions the reader faces. When presenting options, give each a name, state what it entails, and compare them on the criteria that actually govern the choice: cost, risk, timing, dependencies, reversibility. Then make a recommendation and justify it against those criteria.

Avoid presenting options without a recommendation unless the document's explicit purpose is to surface choices for someone else to decide. A memo that lays out three paths and says "we should discuss" offloads the thinking the reader expected you to do. Do the analysis; offer the judgment; let the reader override it.

### Quantify Stakes And Confidence

Vague stakes produce vague decisions. Where possible, attach numbers to the consequences: revenue at risk, cost of the option, number of customers affected, hours of effort, probability of the outcome. Even rough estimates with stated assumptions are more useful than unquantified assertions, because they let the reader calibrate.

Separate what is known from what is assumed from what is unknown. State your confidence level and the evidence behind it. A reader who knows a number is an estimate with wide error bars can reason about it; a reader who treats an estimate as a fact will be blindsided. Honesty about uncertainty builds the trust that makes a recommendation credible.

### Respect The Reader's Time And Attention

Length is a cost the reader pays. Every section, paragraph, and appendix should earn its place. Cut anything that does not help the reader decide, understand, or act. Background that the reader already has does not belong in the body; if it is needed at all, it belongs in an appendix the reader can skip.

Prefer short sentences and plain words. Jargon is acceptable only when it is the shared vocabulary of the audience and speeds understanding; otherwise it excludes and obscures. A memo is not improved by sounding formal. It is improved by being clear.

### Match Format To Function And Audience

Different documents serve different functions, and the format should follow the function. A one-page decision memo differs from a weekly status update, which differs from a quarterly business review, which differs from an incident report. Match length, section structure, and level of detail to what the reader needs from that specific document.

Consider who else will see it. Memos get forwarded, screenshot, and quoted out of context. Write so that the document survives being read by someone outside its original audience without losing its meaning or embarrassing the author. Avoid references that only make sense to people in the room.

### Close With Next Steps And Ownership

End by translating the document into motion. State what happens next, who owns each next step, and by when. A memo that ends on analysis without next steps leaves the reader to infer the action, and inference is where execution drifts. Even an informational memo should close with what the reader should now do or watch for.

If the document requires a decision, restate the ask and the deadline at the close. If it requires follow-up from others, name them. Ownership and timing are not details; they are what turn a document into progress.

## Common Traps

### Burying The Recommendation

The most common memo failure. Building context for two pages before stating the recommendation guarantees that busy readers miss it or skim past it. The bottom line belongs at the top.

### The Ask That Is Not An Ask

Phrasing a request as a suggestion or observation leaves the reader unsure whether action is expected. If you need something, ask for it directly and specifically.

### Options Without A Recommendation

Presenting alternatives without committing to a judgment forces the reader to do the analysis you were asked to do. Offer the choice, but also offer the recommendation.

### Background That Drowns The Point

Writers often front-load everything they know. Readers usually need only enough context to interpret the recommendation. Move deep background to an appendix.

### Unquantified Stakes

"This is a significant risk" tells the reader nothing they can act on. "This risks losing roughly 8% of monthly revenue, with high confidence" gives them something to weigh.

### Treating The Memo As A Showcase

A memo is not the place to demonstrate thoroughness or intellect. Padding a document to look comprehensive wastes the reader's time and signals insecurity. Say what is needed and stop.

### Ignoring Forwarding Risk

Internal documents travel. A candid aside or an unexplained reference can create confusion or damage when read by an unintended audience. Write as if the document may be shared.

## Self-Check

Before treating the memo or report as complete, verify:

- The bottom line, including any recommendation or decision, appears in the opening, not buried later.
- The ask is explicit: what is needed, from whom, and by when.
- The structure supports skimming, with headings and first sentences that carry the point.
- Options, if presented, are compared on the criteria that govern the decision and accompanied by a recommendation.
- Stakes and consequences are quantified where possible, with assumptions stated.
- Confidence levels are distinguished: known facts, estimates, and unknowns are separated.
- Background is limited to what the reader needs, with deeper context moved to appendices; length is justified; every section earns its place against the reader's time
- Jargon is used only where it is the audience's shared vocabulary and aids clarity; the format matches the document's function and the audience's expectations
- Next steps, owners, and deadlines are stated at the close; the document would survive being forwarded to an unintended reader without losing meaning
- A reader who stops after the first paragraph still has the essence; nothing in the document is there to impress rather than to inform or decide
