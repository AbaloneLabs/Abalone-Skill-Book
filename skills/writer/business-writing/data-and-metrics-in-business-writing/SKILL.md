# Data And Metrics In Business Writing

Data is the most powerful and most easily abused tool in business writing. A well-chosen metric can settle an argument, justify a decision, and build credibility in a way that prose alone cannot. A poorly chosen or misleading metric can do the opposite: it can obscure a weak case behind an impressive-looking number, cherry-pick a favorable period while hiding an unfavorable trend, or present a precise figure so stripped of context that it misleads even as it looks authoritative. The central problem is that data feels objective, and that feeling of objectivity is exactly what makes data dangerous when it is selected, framed, or presented carelessly or dishonestly. Readers trust numbers more than they trust prose, and that trust is easily betrayed.

The common failure is to treat data as self-evident, as if a number speaks for itself. Numbers never speak for themselves; they are always selected, bounded, and framed by the writer's choices, and those choices determine whether the number illuminates or misleads. A second failure is to use data as decoration, dropping in metrics to look rigorous without asking whether they actually support the argument. The result is business writing that appears data-driven but is actually data-draped: the numbers create an impression of rigor while doing no real work, or worse, doing misleading work. The skill is in choosing metrics that genuinely bear on the decision, presenting them honestly even in persuasive contexts, and contextualizing them so the reader understands what they mean and what they do not.

Use this skill when incorporating data or metrics into business writing, when choosing which figures to cite, when presenting data in persuasive contexts, when contextualizing numbers, when deciding between visual and textual presentation, or when judging whether data strengthens or obscures the message. The agent has freedom in which metrics to use, but the obligation to present data honestly and to let it do real work is not optional.

## Core Rules

### Choose Metrics That Bear On The Actual Decision

The first question is not which metrics are available but which metrics actually matter to the decision the document supports. A document full of impressive metrics that do not bear on the decision is data-draped, not data-driven. Before citing any number, ask what decision the reader is making and which metrics genuinely inform it. A revenue growth figure matters to an investment decision; a page-view count probably does not. Selecting metrics by relevance to the decision, rather than by availability or impressiveness, is what makes data serve the argument rather than decorate it.

To choose relevant metrics:

- identify the decision the reader must make;
- determine which outcomes or risks that decision turns on;
- select metrics that measure those outcomes or risks directly;
- exclude metrics that look good but do not bear on the decision, even if they are flattering;
- prefer a few decision-relevant metrics over many tangential ones.

### Present Data Honestly Even In Persuasive Contexts

Business writing is often persuasive, and the temptation in persuasive contexts is to present data in the light most favorable to the case. This is where data abuse is most common and most damaging. Cherry-picking a favorable period, comparing to a weak baseline, citing a metric without its denominator, or framing a decline as a slower rate of growth, all of these make the case look stronger while misleading the reader. Honest data presentation does not mean abandoning persuasion; it means persuading with data that withstands scrutiny, because a reader who catches a misleading presentation distrusts the entire document.

Honest presentation practices:

- show the relevant time period, not just the favorable slice;
- use appropriate baselines and comparisons, not ones chosen to flatter;
- include denominators and context so absolute numbers are interpretable;
- present unfavorable data alongside favorable, especially when it bears on the decision;
- distinguish correlation from causation, and do not imply causation the data does not support;
- avoid presenting a slowing decline as growth, or a partial metric as a complete picture.

A case built on honest data persuades readers who check it. A case built on misleading data persuades only until the first check, then collapses along with the writer's credibility.

### Contextualize Every Number

A number without context is meaningless or misleading. "Revenue grew 20 percent" tells the reader nothing useful without knowing the baseline, the period, the comparison, and whether the growth is good relative to expectations, the market, or the cost of achieving it. Contextualizing a number means giving the reader the frame to interpret it: what it is being compared to, whether it is good or bad, what it includes and excludes, and what the relevant benchmark is. Without context, the reader projects their own interpretation, which may be quite different from what the writer intends.

Context to provide:

- the baseline or comparison the number is measured against;
- the time period and whether it is typical, favorable, or unfavorable;
- whether the number meets, exceeds, or falls short of expectations or benchmarks;
- what the number includes and excludes, such as one-time items or specific segments;
- the denominator or rate basis, so absolute figures are interpretable;
- any caveats that affect interpretation, such as a change in methodology.

### Avoid Cherry-Picking And Misleading Selection

Cherry-picking is the selection of data that supports the case while omitting data that undermines it. It is the most common form of data dishonesty in business writing, and it is often semi-conscious: the writer includes the metrics that look good and forgets the ones that do not. The remedy is to ask, for every metric included, whether there is a relevant metric that tells a different story, and to include that metric too if it bears on the decision. A document that presents only favorable data may persuade in the moment, but it fails when the reader encounters the fuller picture elsewhere.

To avoid cherry-picking:

- ask, for each metric included, what an honest critic would want to see alongside it;
- include the metrics that complicate the case when they bear on the decision;
- present trends, not just endpoints, so the reader sees the full shape;
- avoid selecting a time window because it flatters, and disclose the window chosen;
- distinguish a genuinely strong result from a carefully framed weak one.

### Distinguish Precise From Accurate, And Meaningful From Impressive

A number can be precise without being accurate, and accurate without being meaningful. Precision, many decimal places, creates an impression of rigor that may be false if the underlying measurement is uncertain. Accuracy, correctness, matters more than precision, and meaningfulness, relevance to the decision, matters more than either. Business writers often reach for precise or impressive numbers when meaningful ones would serve better. A rounded figure that captures the real trend is more useful than a precise figure that misleads.

Watch for:

- false precision, reporting many decimal places the measurement does not support;
- accuracy without meaningfulness, a correct number that does not bear on the decision;
- impressive without relevant, a large or growing metric that does not inform the choice;
- vanity metrics, numbers that look good but measure nothing decision-relevant;
- metrics chosen for their effect rather than their truth.

### Choose Visual Versus Textual Presentation By The Data's Job

Data can be presented as prose, as a table, or as a chart, and the choice should be driven by what the data needs to do in the document. A trend is often best shown as a chart, because the eye grasps shape faster than prose conveys it. A precise lookup is best shown as a table. A single key figure may be best stated in prose. The wrong format obscures the data: a table where a chart would reveal a trend, or a chart where a precise value is needed. Choose the format that lets the reader extract the insight with the least effort.

Format guidance:

- use charts to show trends, comparisons, and relationships that the eye grasps quickly;
- use tables for precise values the reader must read or compare exactly;
- use prose for single key figures, context, and interpretation;
- avoid charts that distort, through truncated axes, misleading scales, or irrelevant decoration;
- ensure every visual is labeled, sourced, and interpretable without the surrounding prose.

### Let Data Strengthen The Message, Not Obscure It

Data should serve the message, not bury it. A common failure is to load a document with so many metrics, tables, and charts that the core argument disappears, and the reader is left to extract a conclusion from a pile of numbers. Data strengthens the message when it is selected for relevance, presented clearly, and tied explicitly to the argument. It obscures the message when it is excessive, poorly integrated, or left to speak for itself. The test is whether the data makes the argument clearer or whether it replaces the argument with noise.

To ensure data strengthens rather than obscures:

- tie each metric explicitly to the point it supports, in the surrounding prose;
- cut metrics that do not bear on the argument, however impressive;
- lead with the conclusion the data supports, then present the data, not the reverse;
- avoid overwhelming the reader with volume; a few well-chosen figures beat many tangential ones;
- ensure the reader could state the argument after reading, rather than only recalling numbers.

## Common Traps

### Data As Decoration

Dropping in metrics to look rigorous without asking whether they support the argument. Use only metrics that bear on the decision.

### Cherry-Picking Favorable Data

Selecting periods, baselines, or metrics that flatter while omitting those that complicate. Include the data an honest critic would demand.

### Numbers Without Context

A figure without baseline, period, or benchmark is meaningless or misleading. Contextualize every number.

### False Precision

Many decimal places on an uncertain measurement create false rigor. Prefer accuracy and meaningfulness over precision.

### Vanity Metrics

Numbers that look good but measure nothing decision-relevant. Choose metrics by relevance to the decision.

### Implying Causation From Correlation

Presenting correlated data as if one caused the other, without support. Distinguish correlation from causation explicitly.

### Data Burying The Message

So many metrics that the argument disappears. Tie each metric to its point and cut what does not serve.

### Distorting Visuals

Truncated axes or misleading scales that exaggerate a trend. Present visuals honestly and label them fully.

## Self-Check

Before treating the use of data as sound, verify:

- The metrics chosen bear directly on the decision the document supports, rather than being selected for availability or impressiveness.
- Data is presented honestly in persuasive contexts, with favorable and unfavorable relevant data shown together.
- Every number is contextualized with baseline, period, benchmark, and caveats so the reader can interpret it.
- Cherry-picking was avoided; metrics an honest critic would demand are included when they bear on the decision.
- Precision is supported by the measurement; meaningfulness is prioritized over impressive-looking figures.
- Visual versus textual presentation was chosen by what the data needs to do, with visuals labeled and non-distorting.
- Each metric is tied explicitly to the point it supports, so data strengthens rather than obscures the message.
- No vanity metrics are used in place of decision-relevant ones.
- Causation is not implied from correlation without support.
- A reader could state the document's argument after reading, rather than recalling only a pile of numbers.
