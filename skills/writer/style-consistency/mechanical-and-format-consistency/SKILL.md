# Mechanical And Format Consistency

Mechanical consistency is the unglamorous layer of craft that readers notice only when it fails. Capitalization, numbers, dates, hyphenation, list punctuation, heading levels, and spacing are individually trivial. But they are frequent, and their inconsistencies accumulate. A reader may not register any single switch between "ten" and "10," or between "decision-making" and "decision making," but they feel the aggregate as carelessness. And carelessness, once felt, erodes trust in the substance. A document that cannot hold its own formatting steady invites the question of what else it cannot hold steady.

The common failure is to treat mechanical choices as too small to matter, or to make them ad hoc as the draft grows. The writer capitalizes a term in one section and lowercases it in another, formats three lists three different ways, and switches between date styles without noticing. Each choice felt right in the moment. Together they produce a document that reads as assembled rather than authored. The remedy is to understand that mechanical consistency is a system, decide the system early, and enforce it in a dedicated pass rather than trusting moment-to-moment instinct.

Use this skill when enforcing mechanical and formatting consistency, when deciding among competing conventions, or when a document feels sloppy despite accurate content. The agent has freedom to choose any consistent system, but the system must actually be consistent, and the choices must serve the reader rather than the writer's convenience.

## Core Rules

### Treat Mechanical Consistency As A System

Mechanical choices are not isolated; they form a system. The way numbers are handled, the way headings are capitalized, the way lists are punctuated, the way dates are formatted, these interact to create the document's mechanical identity. Decide the system as a whole, ideally before drafting, and apply it uniformly. A document with a coherent mechanical system reads as professionally produced; one with ad hoc choices reads as improvised.

Define the system across:

- numbers and numerals;
- capitalization of terms and headings;
- dates and times;
- hyphenation of compounds;
- list punctuation and structure;
- heading levels and hierarchy;
- spacing and emphasis conventions.

### Decide Number Style By Defensible Rule

Number style is a frequent source of inconsistency. The rule should be clear and applied uniformly: spell out one through ten and use numerals above, or use numerals for all measurements, or follow a specific guide. The exact rule matters less than that one exists and is held. Mixed number style, "five" then "12" then "twenty," reads as careless even when each instance was individually reasonable.

Decide and hold:

- the threshold for spelling out versus using numerals;
- treatment of numbers at sentence beginnings;
- treatment of large numbers, ranges, and ordinals;
- units, measurements, and percentages;
- consistency between prose and tables or figures.

### Hold Capitalization Stable For Defined Terms

Terms that function as defined concepts should be capitalized, or not, consistently throughout. Random capitalization, where a term is capped in one paragraph and lowercased in the next, signals drift. Decide whether a term is a proper noun or defined concept requiring capitalization, then hold that decision. The same applies to headings: choose a capitalization style, title case or sentence case, and apply it to every heading at that level.

Check:

- defined terms are capitalized or lowercased consistently;
- headings within a level share one capitalization style;
- job titles, departments, and product names hold stable;
- capitalization does not shift between sections without reason.

### Standardize Date And Time Formats

Dates and times are surprisingly disruptive when inconsistent. Mixing "January 5, 2024," "5 January 2024," and "1/5/24" within one document forces the reader to parse format as well as content. Choose one date format and one time format, apply them everywhere, and watch for ambiguity, especially regional date order, that could confuse an international reader.

Standardize:

- date format, including month, day, and year order;
- time format, including time zone where relevant;
- ranges and spans;
- consistency between prose, tables, and headers;
- avoidance of ambiguous formats like 03/04/24.

### Resolve Hyphenation Deliberately

Compound terms are a chronic source of inconsistency. "Decision-making," "decision making," and "decisionmaking" may all appear unless a rule is set. Hyphenation often follows convention, the compound is hyphenated as an adjective but open as a noun, but the convention must be applied consistently. Build a term list for compounds that recur, and hold their forms.

Watch:

- adjective versus noun use of compounds;
- prefixes and suffixes, anti-, pre-, -like;
- terms that recur across the document;
- agreement between prose and any glossary or index;
- evolution of compounds over a long document.

### Format Lists With One Logic

Lists should follow one internal logic: the same introductory pattern, the same capitalization of items, the same terminal punctuation, the same grammatical form. A list that mixes full sentences with fragments, or capped items with lowercased ones, or items ending in periods with items ending in nothing, reads as disorderly. Decide the list style by the list's function and apply it to every list.

Standardize:

- whether list items are full sentences or fragments;
- capitalization of the first word of each item;
- terminal punctuation;
- grammatical parallelism across items;
- introductory colon or dash usage.

### Check Mechanical Consistency In A Dedicated Pass

Mechanical consistency cannot be trusted to emerge from line-by-line drafting. It must be enforced in a dedicated pass that checks each category across the whole document. Read once for numbers, once for capitalization, once for dates, once for hyphenation, once for lists. Category-by-category checking catches inconsistencies that sentence-by-sentence reading misses. This pass is where professionalism is made or lost.

## Common Traps

### Treating Mechanical Choices As Too Small To Matter

Individually small, mechanically inconsistent choices accumulate into a felt sense of carelessness. Treat the system as a whole.

### Ad Hoc Choices That Drift Across A Long Document

Making each choice in the moment, without a governing system, guarantees drift. Decide the system early and enforce it.

### Inconsistent Number Style

Mixing spelled-out numbers and numerals without a rule reads as sloppy. Hold one defensible threshold.

### Random Capitalization Of Defined Terms

Capping a term in one place and lowercasing it in another signals drift. Decide and hold capitalization for each defined term.

### Mixed Date And Time Formats

Inconsistent or ambiguous dates force the reader to parse format. Standardize one format and apply it everywhere.

### List Formatting Without Internal Logic

Lists that mix sentence types, capitalization, and punctuation read as disorderly. Apply one logic to every list.

### Trusting Line-By-Line Editing To Catch Inconsistency

Sentence-level reading misses category-wide drift. Run a dedicated mechanical consistency pass for each category.

## Self-Check

Before treating mechanical and format consistency as complete, verify:

- A coherent mechanical system was decided before or early in drafting.
- Number style follows one defensible rule applied uniformly.
- Defined terms and headings hold stable capitalization throughout.
- Dates and times use one consistent, unambiguous format.
- Hyphenation of recurring compounds is resolved and held.
- All lists follow one internal logic of grammar, capitalization, and punctuation.
- Heading levels and hierarchy are applied consistently.
- Spacing and emphasis conventions do not drift across sections.
- A dedicated mechanical consistency pass checked each category across the whole document; no term, number, date, or list shifts form without a defensible reason
- Prose, tables, and figures agree on mechanical conventions; a reader would not stumble over a formatting surprise anywhere in the document
