# Character Want Need And Flaw

A character becomes dramatic through the architecture of their desires. What they want drives them forward; what they need pulls them toward change; and the flaw, the wound, or the misunderstanding that separates the two is where the real story lives. Without this architecture, a character is a collection of traits and biographical facts, interesting perhaps, but inert. With it, every scene becomes a test of who they are and who they might become.

The judgment problem is that agents tend to design characters from the outside in. They assemble appearance, backstory, quirks, and a role in the plot, and then try to generate motivation from that surface. But motivation does not come from biography; it comes from desire under pressure. The other common failure is to make want and need identical, so that getting what the character wants automatically gives them what they need, which removes the central tension that makes a character arc matter. The richest characters want something they are wrong about, and the story is the process by which the wrongness is exposed.

Use this skill when designing or revising central characters in fiction, memoir, scripts, and narrative nonfiction. The goal is to build want, need, and flaw into a coherent engine that generates choices, conflict, and change. The agent has freedom to write characters who do not change or who change for the worse, but should understand what each choice costs and signals.

## Core Rules

### Define The Want As Concrete And External

The want is what the character is actively pursuing in the story, the goal they believe will make their life right. It should be concrete enough to act on and specific enough to fail at. "Wants to be loved" is too abstract to drive scenes; "wants to win back her ex by the reunion" is a want that generates action, obstacle, and consequence.

A strong want has:

- a clear object or outcome the character can pursue;
- a reason it matters to this particular character now;
- a believable connection to their history, wound, or identity;
- stakes that make failure genuinely costly;
- the capacity to put the character in conflict with others and with themselves.

The want is the engine. If you cannot say what the character wants in one concrete sentence, the story has no forward pull.

### Define The Need As Internal And Transformative

The need is what the character actually requires to become whole, healthy, or fulfilled, and it is usually something they do not recognize, resist, or actively flee. Where the want is external and conscious, the need is internal and often unconscious. The need is the real subject of the arc: the change the character must undergo, the wound they must face, the belief they must surrender.

A need might be: to forgive a parent, to accept that they cannot control everything, to learn that vulnerability is not weakness, to stop defining themselves through others' approval. The need should be connected to the flaw, because the flaw is usually the character's misguided attempt to cope with the unmet need.

### Build The Gap Between Want And Need

The central tension of most character arcs lives in the gap between what the character wants and what they need. When the want and the need are different, even opposed, every step toward the want can be a step away from the need, and the story becomes the pressure that forces the character to choose or to discover the difference. This gap is what makes a character arc dramatic rather than merely eventful.

Ask: does getting what the character wants actually give them what they need? If the answer is yes, there is no real arc, only a quest. If the answer is no, or not without a cost, you have the material for transformation. The strongest stories make the character achieve the want only to find it empty, or sacrifice the want to claim the need, or refuse the need and suffer the consequences.

### Design The Flaw As A Lived Coping Mechanism

The flaw is not a random negative trait tacked onto the character. The most believable flaws are coping mechanisms, defenses, or mistaken beliefs that once protected the character and now harm them. A person who cannot trust was probably betrayed. A person who controls everything was probably left helpless. A person who performs charm learned that love was conditional on being pleasing.

Design the flaw so that:

- it has a believable origin in the character's history or wound;
- it actively obstructs the need;
- it is connected to the want (the character pursues the want in a flawed way);
- it is something the reader can understand and even sympathize with;
- it can be tested, exposed, and forced to change through the story's pressure.

A flaw that is merely mean, stupid, or weak makes a character hard to care about. A flaw that is a human defense makes a character recognizable.

### Let The Arc Emerge Through Choice Under Pressure

Character change does not happen through introspection alone. It happens through choices the character makes under pressure, especially choices where the want and the need collide. Each major choice should test the flaw: does the character repeat the old pattern, or do they begin to break it? The accumulation of these choices is the arc.

Design key turning points where the character must choose between the want and the need, between the old defense and the new risk. Let the reader see the cost of each choice. A character who changes without paying anything for it has not really changed.

### Distinguish Change, Growth, And Stasis Arcs

Not every character changes for the better. Be deliberate about the kind of arc you are building:

- **Positive change arc:** the character moves from flaw toward need, surrendering the false want or transforming it.
- **Growth arc:** the character does not transform but expands, gaining understanding or capability.
- **Flat or stasis arc:** the character holds firm and changes the world around them, often embodying a truth others come to see.
- **Negative or corruption arc:** the character moves away from the need, doubling down on the flaw, often with tragic results.

Each arc type places different demands on the story. Know which you are writing and structure the choices accordingly.

### Make The Want, Need, And Flaw Visible In Behavior

Readers do not experience a character's interior architecture through explanation; they experience it through what the character does. Show the want through pursuit, the flaw through the self-defeating choices it produces, and the need through the moments when the character almost reaches for it and pulls back. Let the architecture be inferred from behavior, contradiction, and consequence, the way we read real people.

### Give Secondary Characters Their Own Wants And Needs

The protagonist is not the only character who deserves an inner architecture. Major secondary characters become three-dimensional when they have their own wants, needs, and flaws that intersect, support, or collide with the protagonist's. Characters who exist only to serve the protagonist's arc feel like furniture; characters with their own arcs feel like people.

## Common Traps

### The Want And Need Are Identical

When getting the want automatically satisfies the need, the arc has no tension. The character simply has to succeed. Build a real gap, even a contradiction, between the two.

### The Flaw As Decorative Trait

A flaw listed in a character sheet but never shown shaping choices is not a flaw, it is a label. Let the flaw actively obstruct the need and distort the pursuit of the want.

### Change Without Cost

A character who transforms because the plot requires it, without difficult choices or sacrifice, feels unearned. Change should cost something: a relationship, an identity, a belief, a comfort.

### The Passive Protagonist

A character to whom things happen, who is pushed through the plot without pursuing a want of their own, feels like a witness rather than a protagonist. Give them a goal they actively chase, even if they chase it badly.

### Backstory Mistaken For Motivation

A detailed history does not generate drama unless it produces a present want, need, and flaw. Do not assume that knowing a character's past makes them motivated; connect the past to what they want and fear now.

### The Perfectly Self-Aware Character

A character who fully understands their own flaw and need has nowhere to go. The flaw works partly because the character does not see it clearly. Preserve some blindness in the character so the story can be the process of revelation.

### Flat Secondary Characters

When everyone except the protagonist lacks inner architecture, the world feels thin and the protagonist's relationships feel instrumental. Give major secondary characters their own stakes.

## Self-Check

Before treating the character design as complete, verify:

- The character has a concrete, external want that can be pursued and failed at.
- The character has an internal need that is distinct from, and possibly opposed to, the want.
- There is a real gap between want and need that generates dramatic tension.
- The flaw is a believable coping mechanism with an origin, not a random negative trait.
- The flaw actively obstructs the need and distorts the pursuit of the want.
- The arc emerges through choices under pressure, especially choices where want and need collide.
- You know which arc type you are writing (change, growth, flat, or negative) and have structured choices to match.
- Change, where it occurs, costs the character something real; the protagonist actively pursues a goal rather than passively receiving the plot
- Backstory is connected to present want, need, and flaw rather than existing as biography alone; the character retains enough blindness about their own flaw for the story to dramatize revelation
- Major secondary characters have their own wants, needs, and stakes; the want, need, and flaw are visible in behavior and choice rather than only stated in description
