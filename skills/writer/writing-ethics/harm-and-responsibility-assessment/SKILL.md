# Harm And Responsibility Assessment

Publishing is an act with consequences. The moment writing reaches readers it can expose a person, damage a reputation, retraumatize a survivor, enable harassment, leak private information, incite panic, or legitimize a dangerous idea. The writer is not a neutral conduit through which information harmlessly passes; the writer chooses what to publish, how to frame it, what to name, what to omit, and how much weight to give collateral damage. Treating publication as a neutral delivery of facts is how careful writers cause harm they did not intend and did not think to weigh.

This is a judgment problem because the obligation to inform and the obligation to minimize harm often pull against each other, and neither can be absolute. A piece that avoids all possible harm publishes nothing of consequence; a piece that pursues truth without any regard for consequence becomes reckless. The line is not found by applying a single rule but by asking, for each specific piece, who could be harmed, how seriously, whether the harm is necessary to the public value, and whether the same value could be achieved with less damage. An agent that skips this assessment, or treats it as a formality cleared by good intentions, tends to default to publishing whatever is most compelling, which is not the same as what is most justified. The craft is publishing what is worth the harm it causes and no more.

Use this skill when preparing to publish reporting, profiles, opinion, personal essays, true-crime or trauma narratives, exposes, or any piece whose publication could affect identifiable people or vulnerable groups. The goal is responsible publication, not suppression.

## Core Rules

### Identify Who Could Be Harmed And How

Before publishing, map the people and groups the piece could affect, beyond the obvious subject. Harm radiates outward from a piece: to the person named, to their family, to people who share their characteristics, to sources, to communities depicted, and to readers who may be triggered or misled. Naming the full set of potentially affected parties is the first step in taking responsibility for them.

Ask:

- Who is identifiable in the piece, directly or by implication?
- What could happen to each of them if the piece is published as written?
- Are there vulnerable parties, such as minors, survivors, undocumented people, or those unable to respond?
- Could the piece enable harassment, doxxing, or violence?
- Does naming a group expose its members to collective harm?

A harm you have not named is a harm you cannot weigh.

### Weigh Public Value Against Potential Harm

Not all harm justifies suppression, and not all public value justifies harm. The decision to publish rests on a proportionality judgment: does the public value of the information outweigh the harm it will cause, and is there no less harmful way to deliver that value? A piece that exposes serious wrongdoing may justify real harm to a powerful wrongdoer; a piece that satisfies curiosity at the expense of a private individual usually does not.

Assess proportionality by:

- the seriousness and likelihood of the harm;
- the importance and uniqueness of the public value;
- whether the subject is a public figure with power or a private person with none;
- whether the information is already public or would be newly exposed;
- whether the harm falls on the person responsible for the wrong or on bystanders.

When the value is modest and the harm is serious, the case for publication is weak.

### Distinguish Necessary From Gratuitous Detail

Much harm in publishing comes not from the decision to cover a topic but from the details chosen. The fact that a person died by suicide may be newsworthy; the method described in detail may be gratuitous and dangerous. The fact that someone was victimized may matter; graphic specifics may retraumatize the survivor and titillate readers without adding public value.

For each difficult detail, ask whether it is necessary to the reader's understanding or whether it serves spectacle. Cut or generalize what does not earn its place. The same story can usually be told with less specific harm.

### Protect The Vulnerable And The Unconsenting

People have different capacities to consent to and survive publication. A public figure who sought attention and holds power can absorb scrutiny a private citizen cannot. A source who agreed to be quoted may not have understood the reach of the piece. A minor cannot meaningfully consent. A survivor may have agreed to share their story without foreseeing how it would feel to see it published and circulated.

Adjust the standard by vulnerability:

- raise the bar for naming private individuals, especially minors;
- confirm that sources understood the scope and permanence of publication;
- offer survivors meaningful control over their own stories where ethically possible;
- avoid identifying people by implication when direct naming would be wrong.

Consent obtained under information asymmetry is not full consent.

### Consider Collateral And Collective Harm

A piece about one person can harm many. Depicting a member of a marginalized group in a stereotyped way can reinforce prejudice against the whole group. Exposing one individual's private life can tar their family or community. Publishing a method or location can create copycat harm. The writer is responsible not only for the direct subject but for the ripples.

Ask whether the piece, taken as a whole, would make life harder for people who share the subject's characteristics, and whether that collective effect is justified by the specific value of the piece.

### Choose Framing That Minimizes Avoidable Harm

How a story is framed changes the harm it does. Sensationalist headlines, dehumanizing language, and framing that centers the perpetrator over the victim all amplify damage without adding information. The same facts can be reported with framing that respects subjects and readers while losing nothing of public value.

Prefer framing that:

- avoids exploitative or titillating language around suffering;
- centers the humanity of those harmed rather than reducing them to their victimization;
- does not gratuitously repeat stigmatizing identifiers;
- distinguishes allegation from established fact;
- avoids language that could incite or normalize harm.

### Know When Not To Publish

Some pieces should not be published at all, or not yet, or not in the form drafted. The discipline to withhold is part of responsible publishing, not a failure of nerve. Withholding may be temporary, pending verification, consent, or changed circumstances, or permanent when the harm decisively outweighs the value.

Conditions that weigh against publication include: the central claims are unverified and the subject cannot respond; the only value is private curiosity; the harm falls on people who did not choose exposure; the piece would enable serious wrongdoing; or the same understanding could be conveyed without identifying the vulnerable party.

## Common Traps

### Public Interest Confused With Public Curiosity

"Interest" in the sense of attention is not the same as "interest" in the sense of public value. A scandal involving a private person may attract enormous curiosity while serving no public function. Publishing to satisfy curiosity, dressed up as public interest, causes harm without justification.

### The Thrill Of Exposure

Writers can become invested in the dramatic payoff of revealing something, which biases them toward publishing and toward more sensational framing. The satisfaction of the reveal is the writer's, not the reader's or the public's, and it is a poor basis for a publication decision.

### Treating Consent As A Blank Check

A subject's agreement to be featured does not absolve the writer of responsibility for harm. People consent to things they do not fully understand, under pressure, or out of trust that may be misplaced. Consent is relevant, but it does not by itself make harmful publication ethical.

### Ignoring The Ripple Effects

Focusing only on the main subject, writers often miss harm to family, sources, communities, or readers who share characteristics with those depicted. A piece judged only by its effect on the named subject routinely causes larger collateral damage that was never weighed.

### Graphic Detail Mistaken For Honesty

Including every harrowing particular can feel like courageous truth-telling, but graphic detail often serves the reader's appetite for sensation rather than understanding. Honesty about suffering does not require maximal specificity, and restraint is not the same as evasion.

### Publishing First, Weighing Later

Under deadline or competitive pressure, writers sometimes publish and then discover the harm, at which point mitigation is far harder. Harm assessment must happen before publication, when choices about naming, detail, and framing can still prevent damage rather than only react to it.

## Self-Check

Before treating the piece as responsible to publish, verify:

- All people and groups who could be harmed, directly or by implication, have been identified.
- The public value of the piece has been weighed against the seriousness and likelihood of harm.
- The subject's position, power, and vulnerability have been factored into the decision.
- No graphic or identifying detail is included that does not earn its place.
- Vulnerable or unconsenting parties, including minors and survivors, have been given appropriate protection.
- Collective and collateral harm to people sharing the subject's characteristics has been considered.
- Framing, language, and headlines minimize avoidable harm without losing public value.
- The piece distinguishes allegation from fact and does not prejudge the unproven.
- Consent, where relied on, was informed and not obtained under pressure or asymmetry; the option of not publishing, or publishing later or in altered form, has been genuinely considered
- The same public value could not be achieved with materially less harm; the decision would be defensible to the people most affected by the piece
