# Avoiding Stereotype And Bias

Examples and framing carry assumptions, and assumptions carry bias. Every time a writer reaches for an example, names a character, assigns a role, or illustrates a point, they make choices that either reflect the full range of human experience or reproduce a narrow default. Most bias in writing is not deliberate; it is the momentum of unexamined defaults. The writer reaches for the example that comes easiest, and the example that comes easiest is shaped by stereotypes absorbed from a lifetime of media, culture, and habit. The result is prose that feels neutral to its author and exclusionary or reductive to the reader who is flattened by it.

The judgment problem is that bias hides in the easy choice. The first example a writer thinks of is often the stereotypical one: the male CEO, the female assistant, the older person confused by technology, the person of a certain race in a certain role. Choosing differently requires noticing the default and deciding whether it serves the piece or merely reproduces a pattern. The harm is cumulative: repeated stereotyped examples teach readers who belongs where, and readers who match the stereotype feel reduced while readers who do not feel erased. This skill prevents the rework of revising biased examples and prevents the quiet harm of exclusionary framing.

Use this skill when choosing examples, characters, names, and scenarios; when framing arguments that touch on identity; or when auditing a draft for unexamined bias. The agent has freedom to depict any person in any role, but must make those choices deliberately rather than by default, and must avoid framing that flattens or excludes.

## Core Rules

### Notice The Default Before Accepting It

The first example that comes to mind is rarely neutral; it is shaped by stereotype and habit. Before accepting the default, pause and ask where it came from. The goal is not to second-guess every choice but to make the easy choices conscious, so that stereotypes are chosen rather than stumbled into.

Ask of each example:

- Why did this example come to mind first?
- Does it rely on a stereotype about identity, role, or trait?
- Would the example work as well with the identity dimensions changed?
- Am I using this person as a person, or as a type?

Noticing the default is the precondition for choosing differently. Defaults that survive scrutiny can stay; defaults that exist only by momentum should be replaced.

### Vary Examples Across Identity Dimensions

Homogeneous examples signal a narrow imagination and exclude readers who do not appear in them. Varying examples across gender, race, age, class, disability, and other dimensions reflects the actual world and invites a wider range of readers to see themselves. The variation should be woven through the writing as ordinary, not flagged as gesture.

Vary by:

- distributing names, roles, and scenarios across identity dimensions;
- avoiding the pattern where only the default identity appears in lead roles;
- letting diversity appear without commentary, as normal;
- ensuring variety is accurate and not itself stereotyped;
- reflecting the actual composition of the context you are describing.

Variety as habit is invisible and effective; variety as gesture is visible and often counterproductive.

### Avoid Pairing Identity With Stereotyped Traits

Stereotyping happens when an identity is consistently paired with specific traits, roles, or outcomes. The male expert and the female note-taker, the older person baffled by technology, the person of color in a service role: each pairing reinforces an association the writing did not have to make. Break the pairing by assigning traits and roles independent of identity.

Check pairings:

- Are expertise, authority, and competence distributed across identities?
- Are support, service, and vulnerability also distributed?
- Does any identity appear only in stereotyped roles?
- Would swapping identities change the reader's assumptions in a telling way?

If swapping identities would surprise the reader, the original pairing was carrying stereotype.

### Examine Framing For Who Is Centered

Bias lives not only in examples but in framing: whose perspective organizes the piece, who is treated as the default subject, who is the object of concern. Framing that centers one identity as the norm and others as deviations reproduces hierarchy even when the content is sympathetic. Examine whose viewpoint structures the argument and whose experience is treated as universal.

Examine framing by asking:

- Whose perspective is the default?
- Who is the subject, and who is the object of description or concern?
- Are some groups defined by their difference from a norm?
- Does the framing assume one identity's experience as universal?

Re-centering may mean starting from a different perspective, treating multiple experiences as equally normal, or refusing to make any group the deviation.

### Avoid Singular Representative Examples

A single example from a marginalized group can carry the weight of representing the whole group, which flattens both the example and the group. If the only woman in a piece is also the only person of color, she stands for too much. Avoid making any single character or example bear the representative burden for an entire identity.

Avoid singular representation by:

- including multiple people from any group you depict;
- not requiring one example to carry the whole group;
- letting members of the same group differ from one another;
- avoiding the "one of each" token structure;
- ensuring no identity appears only once and only as its stereotype.

Real groups contain variety; writing should reflect that rather than compress it.

### Audit Names, Scenarios, And Settings

Bias often hides in the specifics: the names chosen, the scenarios selected, the settings assumed. A piece set only in corporate offices, using only Anglo names, assuming only certain family structures, signals a narrow world even when no individual example is stereotyped. Audit the specifics to see whether they reflect a wider reality.

Audit:

- Do the names reflect a range of cultures, or only the default?
- Do scenarios assume one class, region, or family structure?
- Do settings exclude environments where many readers live?
- Are the examples drawn from a narrow slice of experience?

Widening the specifics widens the readers who feel addressed.

### Check For Bias In Comparisons And Contrasts

Comparisons and contrasts can encode hierarchy. When a piece contrasts a "normal" reader with an "other," or uses one group as the baseline against which others are measured, the contrast itself carries bias. Even sympathetic contrasts can other the group being contrasted. Examine the structure of comparisons to ensure they do not rank identities or treat one as the standard.

Watch for:

- contrasts that make one identity the norm and others the variation;
- comparisons that rank groups on a scale of competence, virtue, or normality;
- framings that measure marginalized groups against a dominant default;
- "even" constructions that imply surprise ("even women can...").

Comparisons should illuminate difference without establishing hierarchy.

### Test With Readers From Depicted Groups

Writers are poor judges of their own bias because their defaults feel natural to them. Readers from the groups depicted see what the writer misses. Testing with representative readers, or at minimum role-playing their perspective honestly, reveals bias that author judgment cannot.

Test by:

- asking readers from depicted groups how the examples land;
- watching for patterns of feedback across different readers;
- taking criticism without defensiveness;
- revising based on what the test reveals;
- treating the feedback as intelligence, not attack.

Where formal testing is not possible, role-play the perspective of the depicted reader honestly, and remain open to correction after publication.

## Common Traps

### Accepting The First Example Without Question

The default example is often the stereotypical one. Pause before accepting it; choose deliberately.

### Pairing Identity With Stereotyped Roles

Consistently casting identities in stereotyped roles reinforces the associations inclusive writing resists. Distribute traits and roles independently.

### Token Representation

A single example from a marginalized group carrying the representative burden flattens both the example and the group. Include variety.

### Centering One Identity As The Norm

Framing that treats one identity as universal and others as deviations reproduces hierarchy. Examine who is centered.

### Bias Hidden In Comparisons

Contrasts that rank groups or measure them against a default encode hierarchy. Illuminate difference without ranking.

### Homogeneous Names And Settings

Using only default-culture names and narrow settings signals a small world. Widen the specifics.

### Assuming The Writer Can See Their Own Bias

Defaults feel natural to the person who holds them. Test with readers from the groups depicted.

## Self-Check

Before treating the examples and framing as unbiased, verify:

- The default example in each case was noticed and chosen, not accepted by momentum.
- Examples vary across identity dimensions as a habit, not as a gesture.
- No identity is consistently paired with stereotyped traits or roles.
- The framing does not center one identity as the norm against which others are measured.
- No single example bears the representative burden for an entire group.
- Names, scenarios, and settings reflect a wider range than the writer's default.
- Comparisons and contrasts illuminate difference without establishing hierarchy.
- The piece has been tested, or honestly role-played, against readers from depicted groups; members of depicted groups would feel addressed rather than flattened or erased
- Swapping identities in examples would not reveal telling assumptions; no group appears only once and only in a stereotyped role
- The examples reflect the actual composition and variety of the world being described; bias that remains is acknowledged and addressed, not defended
