# Person First And Identity Language

How a person is described shapes how they are seen. "A person with autism" and "an autistic person" refer to the same human being, but they make different claims about the relationship between the person and the trait. Person-first language puts the person before the condition ("person with a disability"); identity-first language puts the identity forward ("disabled person"). Neither is universally correct. Different communities have different, strongly held preferences, and using the wrong one can signal distance, disrespect, or a failure to listen, even when the writer meant well.

The judgment problem is that there is no single rule that satisfies every community. Some disability communities strongly prefer person-first language; others strongly prefer identity-first; some are split internally. A writer who memorizes one convention and applies it everywhere will get it wrong for someone. The deeper skill is understanding why each convention exists, what each signals, and how to find out what a given community currently prefers. This skill prevents the harm of misdescribing people and the rework of revising language after members of the community object.

Use this skill when writing about disability, identity, or any community with its own language preferences; when choosing between person-first and identity-first constructions; or when handling terminology for groups whose preferences vary. The agent has freedom to follow either convention, but must follow the one the community in question actually prefers.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Each Convention Signals

Person-first and identity-first language are not stylistic variants; they carry meaning. Person-first language emerged to resist the reduction of people to their conditions, asserting the personhood that dehumanizing language had denied. Identity-first language emerged from communities who experience their identity as integral, not separable, and who reject the implication that the identity is something to be distanced from. Understanding both histories lets you choose with awareness rather than habit.

Person-first signals:

- the person is not defined by the condition;
- distance between the person and the trait;
- often preferred in contexts that emphasize dignity and individuality.

Identity-first signals:

- the identity is integral, not detachable;
- pride and acceptance rather than distance;
- often preferred by communities that have reclaimed the identity.

Neither is inherently more respectful; respect is measured by whether the choice matches the community's preference.

### Follow The Community's Stated Preference

The governing rule is community preference. The community being described is the authority on how it wishes to be described. A writer's personal preference, an editor's habit, or an outdated style guide does not override what the community itself asks for. Finding and following the community's preference is the core practice.

Find preference by:

- consulting writing and advocacy by members of the community;
- checking current style guidance from disability-led organizations;
- noting whether the community speaks with a clear consensus or is split;
- when split, following the preference of those most affected by the term;
- updating as preferences evolve, since they do change over time.

When you cannot find a clear preference, choose the convention most commonly used by the community itself, not by outsiders writing about it.

### Recognize That Preference Varies Across Communities

There is no universal answer because communities differ. Many person-first preferences are strong in some disability communities (for example, "person with an intellectual disability"), while identity-first preferences are strong in others (for example, "disabled person," "autistic person," "Deaf person"). Treating one convention as correct for all disabilities guarantees errors elsewhere.

General patterns, to be verified not assumed:

- some communities prefer person-first ("person with a disability" in some contexts);
- some communities prefer identity-first ("disabled," "autistic," "Deaf");
- Deaf culture often treats Deaf as a cultural identity, capitalized;
- preferences sometimes differ within a single community across regions or generations.

Treat every community as its own case. Verify rather than generalize.

### Avoid Reducing People To A Single Trait

Both conventions can fail if they reduce a person to one dimension. Person-first language can still reduce if every mention foregrounds the condition; identity-first language can reduce if the identity becomes the only thing said about the person. The deeper principle is that a person is more than any one trait, and language should not make the trait the whole of the description.

Avoid reduction by:

- mentioning the identity only when it is relevant;
- describing people with full humanity, not only their condition;
- varying description so the trait is not always the lead;
- not defining people by diagnosis, disability, or identity alone;
- giving people agency, voice, and context in the writing.

The convention matters; so does the surrounding framing. A correct label in a reducing frame still fails.

### Use Accurate, Non-Stigmatizing Terms

Beyond the person-first or identity-first choice, specific terms carry stigma, pity, or medicalization that harms. Terms that infantilize, that frame disability as tragedy, or that use outdated clinical or slang labels signal disrespect regardless of convention. Choose terms that are accurate and that the community accepts.

Avoid:

- outdated clinical terms that have become slurs;
- language that frames disability as suffering or tragedy ("suffers from," "afflicted with");
- infantilizing terms ("special needs," euphemisms that avoid saying "disability");
- metaphors that stigmatize ("wheelchair-bound," "confined to a wheelchair");
- inspirational framing that uses disabled people as vehicles for others' uplift.

Prefer accurate, neutral, community-accepted terms, and let the person's experience be described in their own terms where possible.

### Capitalize And Format According To Community Norm

Some identities carry formatting conventions that signal respect. "Deaf" is often capitalized when referring to the cultural community; identity labels may have specific capitalization or form that the community uses. Following these conventions signals attention; ignoring them signals carelessness.

Observe:

- capitalizing Deaf when referring to Deaf culture;
- following the community's spelling or capitalization of identity labels;
- using the form the community uses, not a standardized outsider form;
- noting when a term is capitalized as a marker of pride or political identity.

Small formatting choices carry real meaning to the people they describe.

### Distinguish Description From Diagnosis

Writing about disability often confuses describing a person with reciting a diagnosis. A person is not their medical chart. Descriptive, identity-respecting language focuses on the person's experience and self-understanding, while diagnostic language reduces them to clinical categories. The distinction matters especially in journalism, where the impulse to specify diagnosis can override relevance.

Distinguish by:

- including diagnosis only when it is relevant to the story;
- using the person's own language for their experience;
- not leading with diagnosis as if it explains the person;
- avoiding speculative or armchair diagnosis;
- treating medical information as private unless the person has shared it.

Description respects the person; diagnosis can reduce them. Know which you are doing.

### Handle Internal Disagreement With Humility

Some communities are internally divided about language. Within a single disability community, some members may prefer person-first while others prefer identity-first, and both may have legitimate reasons. When preference is split, do not pretend a consensus exists, and do not impose one. Follow the preference most strongly held by those most affected, and remain humble about the choice.

When preference is split:

- acknowledge that the community is not unanimous if it matters to the context;
- follow the preference of those most affected by the term in question;
- do not use internal disagreement as an excuse to ignore preference entirely;
- be consistent within a single piece once you have chosen;
- remain open to correction from members of the community.

Humility is not paralysis; it is choosing with awareness that the choice may not satisfy everyone.

## Common Traps

### Applying One Convention Universally

Memorizing person-first or identity-first and applying it to every community guarantees errors. Preference varies; verify each case.

### Outsiders Overriding Community Preference

A writer's or editor's preference does not outweigh the community's stated wish. The community is the authority on its own language.

### Reducing People To Their Condition

Even correct convention fails if the trait becomes the whole description. Mention identity only when relevant, and describe people fully.

### Using Stigmatizing Or Pitying Language

"Suffers from," "afflicted with," and euphemisms like "special needs" carry stigma or pity. Use accurate, neutral, accepted terms.

### Treating Diagnosis As Description

Reciting a diagnosis is not the same as describing a person. Distinguish medical information from identity and experience.

### Ignoring Formatting Norms

Capitalization and form carry meaning. Ignoring Deaf culture's capitalization or a community's chosen form signals carelessness.

### Using Internal Disagreement As An Excuse

When a community is split, follow the preference of those most affected. Do not use disagreement to justify ignoring preference.

## Self-Check

Before treating the identity language as complete, verify:

- You understand what person-first and identity-first language each signal, not just their surface form.
- The convention chosen matches the community's stated preference, verified from community sources.
- You have not applied one convention universally across communities that differ.
- People are described with full humanity, not reduced to a single trait or diagnosis.
- Terms are accurate, non-stigmatizing, and free of pity or infantilizing euphemism.
- Capitalization and formatting follow the community's norms.
- Diagnosis is included only when relevant, and not treated as the explanation of the person.
- Where the community is internally divided, you followed those most affected and remained humble; the language was checked against current community guidance, not outdated memory
- No term reduces the person to a medical category or a symbol for others' uplift; the person's own language for their experience has been respected where available
- You have been consistent within the piece once a convention was chosen; a member of the community would recognize the language as attentive rather than careless
