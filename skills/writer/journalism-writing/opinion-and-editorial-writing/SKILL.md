# Opinion And Editorial Writing

Opinion writing is the form where a writer takes a public stance and argues for it. Its job is not to report neutrally but to persuade, to clarify, to challenge, or to bear witness, from a clearly identified point of view. The difficulty is doing this responsibly: arguing from evidence rather than assertion, engaging opposing views rather than ignoring them, and maintaining the line between informed opinion and mere assertion of preference. A strong opinion piece changes how the reader thinks; a weak one merely vents.

Agents tend to miss what makes opinion writing hard because taking a stance looks like the whole task. The temptation is to assert loudly, to stack agreeable points, to preach to those already convinced, or to perform outrage without doing the work of argument. But persuasive opinion writing is disciplined: it makes a defensible claim, supports it with evidence and reasoning, anticipates and answers objections, and earns the reader's consideration even when the reader disagrees. The voice is distinct, but the argument must be sound.

You have latitude in voice, length, and approach depending on the outlet and form. The obligations are fixed: the stance must be clear, the argument must be supported, opposing views must be engaged honestly, and the piece must remain distinguishable from reported fact.

## Core Rules

### Make A Clear, Defensible Claim

An opinion piece needs a clear thesis: the stance the writer is taking, stated explicitly enough that the reader knows what is being argued. Vague gestures toward a topic, or a collection of related complaints without a governing claim, leave the reader unsure what case is being made. State the claim early and let it govern the piece.

The claim must be defensible, meaning supportable by evidence and reasoning within the scope of the piece. Avoid claims so sweeping they cannot be argued in the available space, and avoid claims so narrow they do not merit an opinion piece. Calibrate the claim to what you can actually establish. "This policy will fail" is a large claim requiring substantial support; "This policy carries three specific risks that have gone undiscussed" is a more defensible claim that can be argued fully.

### Argue From Evidence, Not Assertion

The difference between opinion and mere opinion is evidence. Every significant claim should be supported by something the reader can weigh: data, documented events, expert reasoning, historical comparison, or transparent logic. Assertion repeated with emphasis is not argument; it is volume. Show the reader why a reasonable person should hold this view.

Be honest about the strength of your evidence. Distinguish what is well-established from what is likely from what is your judgment call. Overstating the certainty of evidence undermines the argument, because a reader who catches the overstatement distrusts the rest. Understating where the evidence is thin, and relying on reasoning where evidence is incomplete, builds credibility. The reader need not agree with your conclusion to respect how you reached it.

### Engage The Strongest Opposing View

A persuasive opinion piece engages the best version of the opposing argument, not a caricature of it. Steelman the other side: state its strongest case fairly, acknowledge what is true in it, then explain why your position nonetheless holds. This demonstrates that you have understood the question fully and that your stance survives the real challenge, not just a weak one.

Avoid strawman arguments, which misrepresent the opposing view to knock it down easily. Readers who hold the opposing view recognize the misrepresentation instantly and stop trusting the piece, while readers who are undecided sense the evasion. Engaging the strongest opposing view is harder, but it is what separates argument that persuades from argument that only flatters the already convinced.

### Distinguish Opinion From Reported Fact

Opinion writing must be clearly identifiable as opinion, not disguised as neutral reporting. This means distinguishing the writer's judgments and interpretations from established facts, and being transparent about what is reported versus what is argued. A fact cited in an opinion piece should be accurate and attributed; the opinion built on it should be marked as the writer's own.

Beware the slippage where interpretation is presented as fact. "The mayor's policy failed" may be opinion; "the mayor's policy reduced housing starts by 12 percent, according to city data, and I argue this failure warrants a new approach" separates the fact from the judgment. Maintaining this distinction protects the reader's ability to weigh the argument and protects the credibility of the broader publication, where opinion and news must remain distinguishable.

### Use A Distinct Voice That Earns Its Authority

Opinion writing allows, and benefits from, a distinct voice. The reader comes to an op-ed partly for the writer's perspective and personality, not only for the argument. A strong voice can make a position vivid and memorable in ways plain exposition cannot. But voice must serve the argument, not substitute for it.

Voice earns its authority through the quality of thought behind it. A distinctive voice attached to shallow reasoning reads as performance. A distinctive voice attached to rigorous argument, honest engagement, and genuine expertise reads as worth reading. Let the voice express a real perspective, but ensure the substance can stand on its own. Wit, anger, and elegance are tools; the argument is the foundation.

### Calibrate Certainty To The Case

Opinion writers are tempted to certainty beyond what the case supports, because certainty sounds authoritative. But overclaimed certainty is a vulnerability: a reader who notices the overreach discounts the whole argument. Calibrate your confidence to the evidence. Where you are sure, say so and show why. Where the case is probable but not certain, say that. Where you are calling for judgment in the face of uncertainty, be explicit that this is what you are doing.

Intellectual honesty about uncertainty is itself persuasive, because it signals the writer can be trusted. A piece that admits the limits of its case while still making the case effectively demonstrates rigor. A piece that claims more than it can deliver signals that the writer values victory over truth, and the reader tunes out.

### Bear Witness When Argument Is Not The Point

Not all opinion writing is argument. Some of the most powerful opinion pieces bear witness: they bring attention to something overlooked, give voice to an experience, or testify to a truth the writer has lived. Witness is a legitimate and important mode, but it carries its own obligations: honesty about the writer's perspective, care not to generalize one experience into a universal claim without warrant, and respect for the reader's freedom to weigh the testimony.

Distinguish witness from argument in the piece. If you are testifying to experience, do not also claim to be proving a policy conclusion unless you do the work of argument. If you are arguing, do not dress assertion up as personal witness to shield it from scrutiny. Each mode has its standards; meet the standards of the mode you are using.

### Take Responsibility For The Stance

Opinion writing puts the writer's name and judgment behind a public claim, and that carries responsibility. Consider the consequences of the stance: who is affected, who might be harmed, whether the argument is responsible given what it asks of others. Strong opinion writing is courageous, but courage is not license for recklessness. A stance that targets the vulnerable, distorts for effect, or argues in bad faith may attract attention but corrodes the writer's and the outlet's credibility.

Take ownership of the argument, including its implications. Do not hedge to the point of saying nothing, but do not disclaim responsibility for the consequences of what you argue. The strongest opinion writers stand behind their stances fully, revise when they are wrong, and treat the reader as an adult capable of disagreement. Responsibility is not timidity; it is the discipline that makes persuasion honorable.

## Common Traps

### Assertion Instead Of Argument

Repeating a claim with emphasis is not making a case. Support significant claims with evidence and reasoning.

### Strawman Opposition

Misrepresenting the opposing view to defeat it easily signals evasion and alienates readers who hold that view. Engage the strongest case.

### Disguising Opinion As Fact

Presenting interpretation as established fact blurs the line between opinion and reporting and undermines credibility. Separate the two.

### Preaching To The Convicted

Stacking points that only persuade those already agreeing changes no minds. Engage readers who do not yet agree.

### Certainty Beyond The Evidence

Overclaiming authority the case cannot support is a vulnerability readers notice. Calibrate confidence to the evidence.

### Voice Without Substance

A distinctive style attached to shallow reasoning reads as performance. The argument must stand on its own beneath the voice.

### Reckless Stance

A position that targets the vulnerable or argues in bad faith may draw attention but corrodes credibility over time.

## Self-Check

Before treating the opinion piece as complete, verify:

- The piece makes a clear, defensible claim that the reader can identify and that the space can support.
- Significant claims are argued from evidence and reasoning, not merely asserted.
- The strongest opposing view is engaged fairly, not caricatured or ignored.
- Opinion and interpretation are clearly distinguished from reported fact.
- Facts cited are accurate and attributed; the judgment built on them is marked as the writer's own.
- The voice serves the argument rather than substituting for substance.
- Certainty is calibrated to the strength of the evidence and reasoning; if the piece bears witness rather than argues, it meets the standards of witness without overgeneralizing
- The stance has been considered for its consequences and taken responsibly; the piece engages readers who do not yet agree, not only the already convinced
- Nothing in the piece distorts evidence or quotation to strengthen the case dishonestly; the writer's perspective and potential conflicts are transparent where relevant
- The argument could change a reasonable reader's thinking, not merely affirm it; the piece remains clearly identifiable as opinion, distinct from neutral reporting
