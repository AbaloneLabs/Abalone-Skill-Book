# News And Lede Writing

News writing exists to deliver verified information to a reader who needs to understand what happened and why it matters, quickly and without distortion. Its organizing logic is the inverted pyramid: the most important information first, supporting detail in descending order of importance, and the least essential last. This structure is not a stylistic preference; it reflects how news is read, often under urgency, often truncated by editing or attention, and it ensures that the reader gets the essential facts even if they stop after the first paragraph.

Agents tend to miss what makes news writing hard because the prose looks plain. The temptation is to build toward the point the way other writing does, to add color, to dramatize, to delay. But news writing inverts the usual logic: the conclusion comes first, the lede carries the news, and every subsequent paragraph must be less essential than the one before. The harder discipline, however, is accuracy. News that is vivid but wrong, or vivid but unverified, is a failure regardless of how well it reads.

You have latitude in length and some voice depending on the outlet and format. The obligations are fixed: the lede must carry the news, the structure must prioritize by importance, and every fact must be verified and attributed.

## Core Rules

### Write The Lede As The News In One Breath

The lede is the most important sentence in a news story. It must convey the news, who, what, when, where, and the essential why, in a single, clear statement that a reader can grasp immediately. A reader who reads only the lede should know the core of the story. Write it to be read in one breath, with no throat-clearing, no scene-setting, and no delay.

Decide what the news actually is before writing the lede. The news is the newest, most consequential, most relevant fact for the reader, not the background or the process. A common failure is lede that describes the situation instead of the development. "The city has long struggled with flooding" is not news; "The city council voted Tuesday to spend 40 million on flood infrastructure after the third major flood this year" is news.

### Apply The Inverted Pyramid Deliberately

Order information by importance, not by chronology. After the lede, the next paragraph should carry the next most important information, and each subsequent paragraph should be less essential than the one before. This allows readers to stop at any point and still have the most important facts, and it allows editors to cut from the bottom without losing the core.

The inverted pyramid requires a clear sense of what matters most. Rank the facts: what is the news, what is the context needed to understand it, what is the supporting detail, what is the background, what is the peripheral color. Then write them in that order. If you cannot rank them, you do not yet understand the story well enough to write it.

### Answer The 5 Ws Early And Explicitly

The reader of news needs the basic facts answered promptly: who, what, when, where, and why. These should appear in the lede or the first few paragraphs. Do not make the reader hunt for the date, the location, or the people involved. Answering the 5 Ws is not formulaic padding; it is the minimum contract of a news story.

Distinguish which W is the news. In some stories the who is the news, a prominent figure resigns. In others the what is the news, a new law passes. In others the where or when is the news, a first-of-its-kind event. Lead with the W that carries the news, and supply the others in support. The why often takes longer to establish and may unfold across the story, but the reader should be given the available why early, with attribution.

### Verify Every Fact And Attribute Every Claim

Accuracy is the first obligation of news, above style, above speed, above narrative satisfaction. Every fact in a news story should be verified, and every claim that is not directly observed should be attributed to a source the reader can weigh. "The company laid off 200 workers, according to a spokesperson" tells the reader where the information comes from; "The company laid off 200 workers" stated unattributed implies the reporter verified it independently.

Distinguish what you know from what you were told from what is alleged. Use precise attribution language: "according to," "said," "confirmed," "alleged," "claimed," each carries a different weight. Do not present a source's assertion as established fact. If information is contested, say so. If you could not verify something, either verify it or keep it out of the story. The temptation to include a vivid but unconfirmed detail must be resisted; one unverifiable claim puts the whole story's credibility at risk.

### Separate Fact From Interpretation

News writing reports what happened and what was said, with attribution. It does not interpret, opine, or draw conclusions unless the story is explicitly analysis, and even then the analysis must be labeled and grounded in reported fact. Keep the reporter's judgment out of the news account; if judgment is needed, it belongs in attribution to sources or in a clearly separated analysis.

Beware the slanted word that smuggles interpretation into a news report. "Admitted" implies guilt; "said" is neutral. "Slammed" dramatizes; "criticized" reports. "Controversial" inserts the reporter's assessment; describe the dispute and let the reader judge. The discipline of neutral language is not blandness; it is the protection of the reader's ability to form their own judgment from verified facts.

### Write Tight, Cut Hard

News writing rewards economy. Every word competes for limited space and limited attention. Prefer short, clear sentences. Prefer active voice, which is shorter and clearer than passive. Cut adjectives and adverbs that do not add verified information. Cut the background paragraph that the reader does not need to understand the news. Revision in news is largely subtraction.

Tight writing also serves accuracy. The more words, the more chances for error, ambiguity, and unintended connotation. A lean sentence that says exactly what was verified is safer and clearer than an elaborate one that drifts. Write to convey the verified facts with the fewest, clearest words, and cut everything else.

### Handle Breaking News Under Uncertainty Honestly

Breaking news is written under time pressure and incomplete information, and the temptation to fill gaps with assumption is strong. Resist it. Report what is verified, attribute clearly, and be explicit about what is not yet known. "The cause is under investigation" is honest; "The cause appears to be" is speculation dressed as reporting.

Update rather than overwrite. As information develops, correct the record visibly. If an earlier version was wrong, correct it transparently rather than silently changing it. The credibility of news depends on the reader's ability to trust that what they read is the best current verified account, and that errors are acknowledged, not hidden. Speed is valuable; accuracy is essential; when they conflict, accuracy wins.

### Match The Format To The Story And Outlet

News takes many formats: a brief, a straight news report, a roundup, a live update, a wire story. Each has conventions of length, structure, and voice. Match the format to the story's weight and the outlet's norms. A breaking alert is a sentence or two; a full report develops the context; a roundup weaves multiple developments.

Within any format, the inverted pyramid and the accuracy obligations hold. Format changes presentation, not principle. Whether the story is 100 words or 1,000, the lede carries the news, the structure prioritizes by importance, and every fact is verified and attributed.

## Common Traps

### The Buried Lede

Building toward the news rather than leading with it loses readers who stop early and buries the most important fact. Lead with the news.

### Chronology Instead Of Importance

Telling the story in time order rather than importance order violates the inverted pyramid and lets less essential material push the essential down.

### Unattributed Claims

Presenting a source's assertion as established fact overstates the reporting and risks the story's credibility. Attribute every non-observed claim.

### Slanted Language

Words that smuggle interpretation, "admitted," "slammed," "controversial," insert the reporter's judgment into a news account. Use neutral language.

### Vivid But Unverified Detail

Including a dramatic detail that has not been verified puts the whole story's accuracy at risk. Verify or cut.

### Speculation Under Deadline

Filling gaps in breaking news with assumption trades accuracy for completeness. Report what is verified; acknowledge what is not.

### Silent Corrections

Changing an error without acknowledging it erodes trust. Correct visibly and transparently.

## Self-Check

Before treating the news story as complete, verify:

- The lede conveys the news in one clear statement a reader could grasp alone.
- The structure follows the inverted pyramid, with information ordered by importance.
- The 5 Ws are answered early, with the most newsworthy W leading.
- Every fact is verified, and every non-observed claim is attributed to a weighable source.
- Attribution language is precise, distinguishing confirmed from alleged from claimed.
- Fact is separated from interpretation, with the reporter's judgment kept out of the account.
- Language is neutral, free of slanted words that smuggle assessment into reporting; the writing is tight, with every word earning its place and passive voice avoided
- In breaking news, what is unknown is acknowledged rather than filled with speculation; any error in an earlier version is corrected visibly and transparently
- The format matches the story's weight and the outlet's conventions; no vivid but unverified detail has been included for narrative effect
- A reader who stops after the lede still has the core of the story; the story could withstand scrutiny from a skeptical editor or subject
