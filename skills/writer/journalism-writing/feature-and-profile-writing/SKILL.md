# Feature And Profile Writing

Feature and profile writing is reported journalism that uses the tools of story, scene, character, and narrative, to make a true subject vivid and meaningful. Where news delivers the facts fast, a feature lives inside the facts, letting the reader spend time with a person, a place, or a situation until they understand it from the inside. The difficulty is doing this without bending the truth to serve the story: the scenes must be reported, the character must be fair, and the meaning must arise from what was actually found, not imposed to make a tidier piece.

Agents tend to miss what makes features hard because the form rewards drama. The temptation is to heighten, to make a subject more vivid or a narrative cleaner than the reporting supports, to cast a person as hero or villain to serve the arc. But a feature's authority depends on the reader trusting that the vividness is real. The craft is finding the story that genuinely lives in the reporting, characterizing the subject in full rather than in service of a thesis, and using scene to let the reader see rather than to dramatize beyond what was observed.

You have latitude in structure, voice, and length. The obligations are fixed: the reporting must support every scene and characterization, the subject must be portrayed fairly, and the piece must find a genuine story rather than impose one.

## Core Rules

### Find The Story In The Reporting

A feature is not a topic; it is a story found inside a topic. Before writing, identify what makes this subject a story: a conflict, a transformation, a question, a contradiction, a character worth understanding, a situation that reveals something larger. The story is what gives the piece forward motion and what makes a reader care.

Resist the survey approach, the feature that tells the reader everything about a subject but has no story. A profile that recounts a person's biography from birth has no engine; a profile that pursues a question, what drives this person, what cost have they paid, what do they reveal about their field, has momentum. Find the question or conflict the reporting uncovered, and build the piece around it.

### Build The Profile Around A Controlling Question

A profile is not a comprehensive biography; it is a portrait organized around a controlling question about the subject. What does this person's career reveal about their industry? What drives someone to devote their life to this? How does someone become the person who could do this thing? The controlling question selects which details matter and gives the profile a center of gravity.

The controlling question should be one the reader would find worth asking, and one the reporting can genuinely address. Avoid the hagiographic profile that exists to celebrate and the hit piece that exists to diminish; both impose a conclusion rather than pursuing a question. The strongest profiles hold the subject in honest tension, admirable and flawed, remarkable and recognizable, and let the reader see a whole person.

### Render Character Through Observed Specificity

In a profile or feature, character emerges through observed specifics: what the person does, what they say, how they inhabit a space, the objects around them, the way others react to them. Do not summarize a person's character; show the details that let the reader infer it. The brand of coffee, the way they answer a hard question, the photo on the desk, the pause before a reply, these render a person more vividly than adjectives.

Fairness requires showing the whole subject, including details that complicate the portrait. A profile that includes only flattering detail is public relations; one that includes only damaging detail is an attack. Select specifics that reveal the person truthfully, including the inconvenient, and let the reader hold the complexity. Quote the subject in their own words, with enough context that the words are not distorted.

### Use Scene That Is Reported, Not Invented

Scene is the feature's signature tool, placing the reader inside a moment so they can see and feel it. But every scene must be reported: the writer was there, or reconstructed it from sources specific enough to support the detail. Dialogue must come from someone present or from the record. Sensory detail must be observed or sourced, not invented to enrich the scene.

Calibrate the vividness of the prose to the strength of the reporting. If you were in the room, you can render what you saw and heard. If you reconstructed from interviews, attribute the basis and keep the detail within what sources support. Inventing a detail to make a scene land is the cardinal violation of the form; one invented particular puts every reported particular in doubt. When the reporting is thin, use summary and reflection rather than fabricated scene.

### Weave Scene, Context, And Reflection

A feature moves between scene, which dramatizes; context, which supplies the background and stakes; and reflection, which makes meaning. Pure scene without context confuses the reader who lacks the background; pure context without scene is a report, not a feature; pure reflection without grounding is essay, not journalism. The craft is integrating all three.

Place context near the moment it illuminates, rather than in a block at the top. Let a character's situation supply the stakes for a statistic; let a historical fact land at the moment it explains a present action. Reflection should arise from the reported material, the writer thinking alongside the reader rather than pronouncing from above. The integration is what lets a feature be both vivid and substantive.

### Be Fair To Subjects And Sources

Feature writing involves real people who did not ask to be characters. The writer has power to shape how they are seen, and that power carries an obligation of fairness. Portray subjects in their complexity, not reduced to serve a thesis. Seek their side of disputes. Distinguish what they said from what you infer. Do not distort through selective quotation or loaded description.

Consider the consequences for the subject. A profile can affect a person's reputation, career, or safety. Weigh the public interest against the potential harm, and include identifying or damaging detail only when it is necessary and accurate. Fairness does not mean softness; a hard truth reported accurately is fair. It means the subject is portrayed as a full person, not used as material.

### Find The Right Structure For The Story

Features use a range of structures: a scene opening that drops the reader in, then circles back; a chronological narrative; a braided structure that interweaves the subject's story with context; a question-driven structure that pursues an answer. Choose the structure that serves the story and the controlling question, not the structure that feels literary for its own sake.

Open with something that earns the reader's commitment: a scene, a tension, a striking detail, a question. Avoid the generic opening that introduces the subject with background the reader does not yet care about. Close with resonance rather than summary; the ending should leave the reader holding the meaning the piece has built, not restate it.

### Distinguish What You Observed From What You Were Told

Transparency about method builds the reader's trust. Distinguish what the writer witnessed directly from what was reconstructed from interviews, documents, or other sources. Where a scene is reconstructed, signal its basis. Where a quote comes from an interview, the reader understands it as the subject's words; where it comes from another source's recollection, that should be clear.

This transparency is especially important for inner states and motivations. A feature can report what a person did and said; it can attribute motives based on what the person or others stated; it cannot know a person's hidden thoughts unless they were expressed. Present inference as inference, and let the reader weigh it. Honesty about what is known versus inferred is what separates reported feature from invented narrative.

## Common Traps

### The Topic Survey Without A Story

Recounting everything about a subject without a controlling question or conflict produces a reference piece, not a feature. Find the story.

### Inventing Scene Detail

Adding sensory detail or dialogue the reporting does not support contaminates the whole piece. Calibrate vividness to sourcing.

### Hagiography Or Hit Piece

A profile that only celebrates or only attacks imposes a conclusion rather than portraying a person. Hold the subject in honest complexity.

### Context Dumped At The Top

A block of background before the reader cares stalls the piece. Weave context in where it illuminates.

### Distortion Through Selective Quotation

Using quotes out of context or loaded description to make a subject appear a certain way violates fairness. Quote in context.

### Claiming To Know The Unknowable

Presenting inferred motives or hidden thoughts as fact overreaches the reporting. Attribute or mark as inference.

### The Generic Opening

Introducing the subject with background the reader does not yet care about fails to earn commitment. Open with scene, tension, or question.

## Self-Check

Before treating the feature or profile as complete, verify:

- The piece is built around a genuine story or controlling question found in the reporting, not a topic survey.
- The profile holds the subject in honest complexity, neither hagiography nor hit piece.
- Character is rendered through observed specifics, not summary adjectives.
- Every scene is reported, with vividness calibrated to the strength of sourcing.
- No sensory detail or dialogue has been invented to enrich a scene.
- Scene, context, and reflection are integrated rather than separated into blocks.
- Context is placed near the moments it illuminates, not dumped at the top; the subject is portrayed fairly, with their side of disputes sought and complexity shown
- Quotes are used in context, without distortion through selection or framing; what the writer observed is distinguished from what was reconstructed or inferred
- Inner states and motives are attributed or marked as inference, not presented as fact; the structure serves the story and controlling question, not literary display
- The opening earns the reader's commitment rather than offering generic background; the closing leaves resonance rather than restating the piece's points
