# Rollback And Recovery From Bad Revisions

Not every revision improves a draft. Sometimes a round of editing, a structural rewrite, or a batch of accepted changes leaves the piece worse: the voice has flattened, the argument has drifted, a cut removed something load-bearing, or the accumulated changes have broken what was working. The writer's task at that moment is to recognize that the revision failed, decide whether to roll back, and recover an earlier state without losing whatever good work came with the bad.

Agents tend to mishandle bad revisions in two directions. Some refuse to acknowledge the damage and keep polishing a worsened draft, sinking more effort into a version that should be abandoned. Others panic and revert wholesale, discarding the good changes along with the bad and losing recent work. Use this skill when a revision made things worse, when deciding whether to roll back, or when restoring an earlier state safely. The goal is to recover deliberately, preserving what is worth keeping while escaping what harmed the piece.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That A Revision Made Things Worse

The first step is honest diagnosis. Writers often resist admitting a revision failed because of sunk effort, because the changes came from a respected editor, or because the damage was gradual. But shipping a worsened draft does more harm than rolling back.

Signs a revision went wrong:

- the voice has flattened or shifted away from the piece's stance;
- the argument has become muddied or self-contradictory;
- a load-bearing passage was cut or weakened;
- the opening and ending no longer align;
- the piece now reads as a compromise rather than a written work;
- quality you could feel before is now absent and hard to locate.

Name the damage honestly before deciding what to do about it.

### Diagnose The Source Of The Damage Before Acting

Before rolling back, understand what went wrong. A rollback that does not address the cause invites the same failure next time.

Investigate:

- was it a single bad change or an accumulation;
- was it a structural revision that broke the logic;
- was it feedback incorporation that eroded voice;
- was it a rewrite that lost the original's center;
- was it time pressure that produced sloppy changes.

Diagnosis tells you whether to revert everything, revert selectively, or rebuild from an earlier state with the lessons learned.

### Decide Between Full Rollback, Selective Rollback, And Rebuild

Recovery is not always all-or-nothing. The right approach depends on how much good work is mixed with the bad.

Options:

- full rollback, restore an earlier clean version and discard the failed revision entirely;
- selective rollback, keep the good changes and revert only the damaging ones;
- rebuild, start from an earlier version and re-apply the worthwhile changes carefully.

Full rollback is right when the revision failed comprehensively. Selective rollback works when most changes were fine but a few caused the damage. Rebuild suits cases where the revision introduced both real improvements and serious problems.

### Preserve Good Work Before Rolling Back

A wholesale revert can destroy good changes that came with the bad. Before rolling back, identify and preserve anything worth keeping.

Protect by:

- copying the current version before any rollback;
- noting which specific changes were improvements;
- saving any new material, examples, or fixes worth retaining;
- keeping the failed version recoverable in case you need something from it.

Recovery should lose only what deserves to be lost, not everything recent.

### Restore From A Known Good State

Rollback is only safe if you have a known good state to return to. This is where versioning discipline pays off. Restore from a version you have verified, not from memory or a guess.

Ensure:

- the target version is intact and recoverable;
- you have confirmed it is the state you want to return to;
- the restoration is clean, not a partial merge that reintroduces problems;
- the restored version is immediately designated as the new current version.

If no clean earlier state exists, recovery becomes a rebuild, which is harder and more error-prone.

### Re-Apply Worthwhile Changes Carefully After Rollback

After a rollback, the improvements that were mixed into the failed revision can be re-applied, but one at a time and with evaluation. This is where selective recovery earns its value.

Re-apply by:

- taking each worthwhile change in isolation;
- evaluating it against the restored version;
- applying it only if it improves the piece without reintroducing damage;
- checking the surrounding text still works after each application.

This turns a failed revision into a source of useful material rather than a total loss.

### Re-Establish The Reader Promise After Recovery

A bad revision often drifts the piece away from its original promise. After recovery, confirm the restored or rebuilt version still delivers what the piece is for.

Check:

- does the recovered version still fulfill the reader promise;
- has the purpose drifted during the failed revision and back;
- do the opening and ending align again;
- has voice and stance been restored, not just text.

Recovery is not complete until the piece reads as itself again, not merely as an earlier file restored.

### Document What Went Wrong To Avoid Repeating It

A bad revision is a lesson. Recording what happened helps prevent the same failure in future work and explains the rollback to collaborators.

Record:

- what the revision attempted;
- why it damaged the piece;
- which specific changes caused the harm;
- what the recovery approach was;
- what to watch for next time.

Documentation turns a costly mistake into improved practice.

### Communicate Rollbacks To Collaborators

If editors, co-writers, or stakeholders were involved in or aware of the revision, a rollback affects them. Silent rollback breeds confusion and distrust.

Communicate:

- that the revision did not work and is being rolled back;
- the reasoning, tied to the piece's quality;
- what is being preserved from the failed revision;
- the plan going forward.

Collaborators who understand a rollback is in service of the work accept it; those surprised by one do not.

## Common Traps

### Refusing To Admit The Revision Failed

Sunk effort and respect for the editor lead writers to keep polishing a worsened draft. Honest diagnosis comes first.

### Wholesale Revert That Loses Good Work

Reverting everything discards improvements mixed in with the damage. Preserve the worthwhile changes first.

### Rolling Back Without Diagnosing The Cause

Reverting without understanding why it failed invites the same mistake next time. Diagnose before acting.

### Restoring From An Uncertain State

Recovery is only safe from a verified good version. Guessing at the target reintroduces risk.

### Re-Applying All Changes At Once After Rollback

Bulk re-application reintroduces the damage. Re-apply worthwhile changes one at a time with evaluation.

### Skipping The Reader-Promise Check After Recovery

Restoring text is not enough. Confirm the recovered piece still fulfills its purpose and voice.

### Silent Rollback That Surprises Collaborators

Unexplained rollbacks erode trust. Communicate the reasoning to anyone affected.

## Self-Check

Before treating recovery from a bad revision as complete, verify:

- You honestly diagnosed that the revision made the piece worse.
- You investigated the source of the damage before acting.
- You chose full rollback, selective rollback, or rebuild based on how much good work was mixed in.
- Good changes were preserved before any rollback.
- The rollback restored from a verified known-good state.
- Worthwhile changes were re-applied one at a time with evaluation.
- The recovered version still fulfills the reader promise and purpose.
- Voice, stance, and the alignment of opening and ending were restored; the failed version was kept recoverable in case material is needed from it
- What went wrong was documented to prevent repetition; collaborators were informed of the rollback and its reasoning
- The recovered piece reads as itself, not merely as a restored file; the recovery lost only what deserved to be lost, not all recent work
