# Cultural Sensitivity And Representation

Representation is never neutral. Every time a piece of writing depicts a community, a culture, a religion, or an identity, it either reinforces the reader's existing picture of that group or revises it. Writers who have not examined their own position tend to reproduce the dominant culture's defaults: they reach for stock characters, shorthand traits, and familiar framings that feel efficient but flatten real people into types. The harm is cumulative and concrete: repeated shallow representation shapes how readers see whole groups, and members of those groups read the piece and recognize themselves reduced.

The judgment problem is that good intentions do not prevent harm. A writer can mean well and still exoticize, tokenize, stereotype, or appropriate. The failures usually come from deficit: writing about a group the writer does not know well enough, using them as instruments for a point that is not theirs, or assuming the writer's frame is the natural one. This skill prevents the rework, the public harm, and the loss of trust that come from careless representation, and it prevents the opposite failure of avoiding all depiction of difference, which leaves writing homogeneous and erases the people who deserve presence.

Use this skill when depicting cultures or identities other than your own, choosing examples that involve marginalized groups, writing fiction with characters across difference, or handling race, ethnicity, religion, or indigeneity in any genre. The agent has freedom to write across difference, but must do so with earned knowledge, humility, and accountability to the people depicted.

## Core Rules

### Examine Your Position Before Depicting Others

Representation begins with knowing where you stand. A writer depicting a culture or identity they do not share brings assumptions they cannot fully see. The first move is to locate your own position: your culture, your privileges, your distance from the group you are depicting, and the power asymmetry between writer and subject. Locating position is not confession; it is accuracy.

Ask:

- What is my relationship to the group I am depicting?
- What power do I hold that they do not, in this context?
- Where did my knowledge of them come from, and is it sufficient?
- What am I likely to get wrong because I am outside?

Honest answers change what you attempt. Sometimes the right move is to narrow the claim, hand the subject to someone better positioned, or collaborate with members of the group.

### Earn The Right Through Sustained Knowledge

Responsible depiction requires knowledge that cannot be faked. A few impressions, a documentary, or stereotypes absorbed from media are not enough. Earned knowledge comes from sustained engagement: primary sources, work by members of the community, direct relationships where possible, and humility about what you still do not know.

Sources of earned knowledge:

- writing and scholarship by members of the community;
- direct testimony and interviews, with consent;
- cultural materials produced within the community;
- consultation and sensitivity reading by people who belong to the group;
- honest acknowledgment of the limits of your knowledge.

If you cannot access earned knowledge, narrow the depiction until it matches what you can stand behind. Vague depiction is better than confident misrepresentation.

### Depict People As Individuals, Not Types

Stereotyping happens when a character or example stands in for an entire group, carrying traits assigned to the category rather than to the person. The defense is specificity: depict people as individuals with interior lives, contradictions, and contexts, not as representatives of a type. A member of a group can have any trait; the harm is when the trait is presented as the group's essence.

Practices:

- give characters from any group their own wants, flaws, and contexts;
- avoid using a single character to represent an entire people;
- do not assign traits to a group that the plot treats as explanatory;
- let members of the group differ from one another;
- resist the shorthand of "the representative figure."

Specificity is the antidote to flattening. A real person is more than their category.

### Avoid Appropriation Of Voice, Story, And Sacred Material

Appropriation is taking what belongs to a community, especially one with less power, and using it for your own benefit without permission, relationship, or accountability. It is most acute with stories, voices, sacred or ceremonial material, and cultural practices that have meaning the outsider cannot carry. The test is not whether you can depict the material, but whether you should, and who benefits.

Watch for appropriation when:

- you are telling a story that belongs to a community's lived experience;
- you are using sacred, ceremonial, or restricted knowledge;
- you are profiting from a culture's art, symbols, or practices;
- you are speaking for a group that can and should speak for itself;
- your depiction displaces work by members of that group.

When appropriation is a risk, the right moves are collaboration, attribution, deferral, or choosing a different subject.

### Do Not Tokenize Or Insert Representation As Decoration

Tokenism is including a member of a marginalized group to signal inclusiveness without giving them real presence. The token character exists to mark the piece as diverse but has no interiority, no stakes, and no function beyond their identity. Readers from the group recognize this instantly, and it often harms more than omission.

Avoid tokenism by:

- giving any character real stakes, agency, and context, not only identity;
- not requiring a single character to carry the whole group;
- not inserting diverse presence only to claim inclusiveness;
- ensuring representation is integrated into the world, not pasted on.

Representation that is real serves the story or argument; representation that is decorative serves the writer's image.

### Check Framing For Who Is Centered And Who Is Othered

Framing decides who is the subject and who is the object. Many well-meaning pieces center the dominant group as the norm and present other groups as deviations, problems, or subjects of concern. The frame itself can other people even when the content is sympathetic. Examine whose perspective organizes the piece and who is looked at rather than looking.

Check:

- Whose viewpoint structures the narrative?
- Who is the default, normal subject?
- Are members of the group agents or objects?
- Does the piece define them by their difference from the dominant norm?
- Whose interests does the framing serve?

Re-centering may mean starting from the group's own perspective, using their terms for themselves, or letting their stakes govern the structure rather than the outsider's curiosity.

### Use The Group's Own Terms And Respect Self-Naming

Names carry power. The terms a community uses for itself often differ from the terms imposed on them, and imposed terms can carry histories of harm. Using a group's self-chosen name and terminology is a basic form of respect; using outdated or imposed terms signals distance or disrespect.

Practices:

- use the names and terms the community currently uses for itself;
- recognize that preferences vary and evolve; check current usage;
- distinguish insider self-naming from outsider labels;
- when a group has internal disagreement about terms, follow the preference of those most affected;
- avoid terms that originate in derogation unless the community has reclaimed them and you are part of that community.

Terminology is not a minor detail. It is a signal of whether you have listened.

### Be Accountable And Willing To Revise

Responsible representation is not a one-time achievement. Even careful writers make mistakes, and communities are the authority on whether a depiction has harmed them. Accountability means being open to correction, taking criticism seriously, and being willing to revise or withdraw work that has caused harm.

Accountability practices:

- seek feedback from members of the depicted group before publication where possible;
- listen to criticism without defensiveness;
- distinguish good-faith critique from bad-faith attack, and learn from the former;
- be willing to correct, revise, or apologize;
- do not treat being called out as the primary harm.

Accountability is not punishment; it is the ongoing relationship that responsible representation requires.

## Common Traps

### Confusing Good Intentions With Responsible Depiction

Meaning well does not prevent harm. Depiction is judged by its effect on the people represented, not by the writer's feelings about it.

### Using A Single Character To Stand For A Group

One character carrying the weight of an entire people flattens both the character and the group. Specificity, not representation-by-proxy, is the standard.

### Appropriating Sacred Or Restricted Material

Taking ceremonial, sacred, or restricted knowledge for artistic use, especially from a marginalized community, is harm dressed as appreciation. Some material is not yours to use.

### Centering The Outsider's Perspective

Framing that makes the dominant group the norm and others the subject of curiosity reproduces the very hierarchy the writer may be trying to resist.

### Tokenism Disguised As Inclusion

Including diverse presence without interiority or stakes signals diversity without practicing it. Tokens harm the groups they supposedly represent.

### Relying On Outdated Or Imposed Terminology

Terms carry histories. Using labels a community has rejected or never chose signals that you have not listened.

### Avoiding All Depiction Of Difference Out Of Fear

Refusing to write across difference at all produces homogeneous work that erases marginalized presence. The answer is earned, humble depiction, not avoidance.

## Self-Check

Before treating the representation as complete, verify:

- You have examined your own position and the power asymmetry between you and the group depicted.
- Your knowledge is earned through within-community sources and direct engagement, not impressions.
- Characters or examples from any group are depicted as individuals with interiority, not as types.
- No sacred, ceremonial, or restricted material has been used without permission and relationship.
- No character is tokenized; every depicted person has real stakes and agency.
- The framing centers the group's own perspective rather than treating them as objects of curiosity.
- Terminology follows the community's current self-naming.
- The piece has been checked, where possible, by members of the group or a sensitivity reader; you are accountable and willing to revise if the depiction has caused harm
- The representation serves the people depicted, not only the writer's image of inclusiveness; you have not confused good intentions with responsible effect
- Where you lacked earned knowledge, you narrowed the claim rather than overreaching; a member of the depicted group would recognize themselves as subjects, not as material
