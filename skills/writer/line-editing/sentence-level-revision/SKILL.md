# Sentence Level Revision

Sentence-level revision is the pass that happens after structure is settled and before word-level polish and proofreading. Its job is to make each sentence carry its meaning with clarity, the right rhythm, and the right emphasis. This is where a draft that is structurally sound becomes prose that is actually pleasant and efficient to read, where muddy constructions sharpen, where monotonous rhythm varies, and where the important words land in the positions of power.

Agents tend to under-invest in sentence-level revision because the draft already "works" structurally, or to misdirect the effort into word-swapping that does not address the sentence's actual problems. The harm of skipping or shallow line revision is prose that is comprehensible but tiring: sentences that bury their point, rhythms that drone, emphasis that falls on the wrong word, and constructions that make the reader work harder than necessary. The reader may not name the problem, but they feel the drag.

This skill covers the judgment for line-by-line revision: how to read a sentence for clarity, rhythm, and emphasis; how to diagnose what makes a sentence weak; how to revise without overworking into stiffness; and how to keep the line pass focused on sentences rather than drifting into structure or proofreading. The agent has freedom in revision choices, but must read every sentence deliberately during this pass.

## Core Rules

### Line Edit Only After Structure Is Settled

Sentence-level revision presumes the larger decisions are made. If structure, purpose, and section jobs are still in flux, line editing is premature, because sentences will be cut or rewritten when the structure changes. Confirm the structure is settled before investing in the line pass.

The line pass is high-effort per sentence. Spending it on sentences that will not survive structural revision is waste. Sequence: developmental and structural revision first, then the line pass, then word-level polish and proofreading.

### Read Each Sentence For Clarity First

The first question of a sentence is whether the reader can understand it on one reading. Clarity is the foundation; rhythm and emphasis matter only once the meaning is clear. A sentence that is clear but plain is better than one that is rhythmic but confusing.

Test clarity by asking:

- what is the single main point of this sentence;
- does the sentence say that point directly, or does it bury it;
- are the subject and verb clear and close together;
- does the reader know what each pronoun refers to;
- is there a subordinate clause or insertion that breaks the main thread.

If the reader could misread the sentence or has to backtrack, fix clarity before anything else. Often the fix is to bring subject and verb together and to move interruptions out of the main clause.

### Diagnose Before You Rewrite

A weak sentence is weak for a specific reason. Diagnosing the reason prevents aimless rewriting. Common sentence-level weaknesses include buried verbs, nominalizations, passive constructions that hide the actor, stacked modifiers, interrupted main clauses, and end-weight violations.

Diagnose by asking which of these applies:

- is the real action hidden in a noun (make a decision instead of decide);
- is the actor hidden or absent (it was decided);
- do modifiers pile up before the subject;
- does an insertion break the subject-verb-object spine;
- does the sentence end on a weak word or a minor point;
- are two independent ideas jammed into one sentence.

Match the rewrite to the diagnosis. A sentence suffering from buried verbs needs active verbs, not a new metaphor.

### Control Emphasis Through Position

In English, the ends and beginnings of sentences carry the most emphasis; the middle carries the least. A sentence that ends on a weak word or a minor qualification buries its point. Revision often means moving the important material to the end and the orienting material to the beginning.

Emphasis principles:

- put old or orienting information at the start;
- put the new, important, or surprising information at the end;
- avoid ending on a weak word like prepositions or filler unless intentional;
- use the start to connect to the previous sentence and the end to land the point.

If a sentence's emphasis feels off, check whether the important word is buried mid-sentence or trailing at the end after a weak qualifier. Moving it often fixes the sentence without changing its words much.

### Vary Sentence Length Intentionally

Monotonous rhythm exhausts the reader. A passage of all long sentences feels exhausting; a passage of all short sentences feels choppy. Variation creates music and controls pace. Long sentences accumulate, complicate, or build momentum; short sentences clarify, punch, or stop.

Revise rhythm by checking:

- do three or more sentences in a row share roughly the same length;
- is there a short sentence where a punch is needed;
- is there a long sentence where complexity or accumulation is needed;
- does the rhythm match the content, fast for urgency, slow for reflection.

Read the passage aloud to feel the rhythm. Where it drones, vary the length. Where it feels choppy, join related sentences.

### Keep Subject And Verb Close

The spine of an English sentence is subject-verb-object. When the subject and verb are separated by long insertions, the reader loses the thread and has to work to reconnect them. Keeping the spine tight is the single most reliable clarity fix.

Watch for:

- long modifiers or clauses between subject and verb;
- long modifiers or clauses between verb and object;
- parentheticals that interrupt the main action;
- stacked noun phrases that delay the verb.

When you find a separated spine, either move the interruption to the beginning or end of the sentence, or split the sentence. The reader should meet the subject, then the verb, with minimal delay.

### Prefer Active Voice Unless Passive Earns Its Place

Active voice (the committee decided) is usually clearer and stronger than passive (a decision was made by the committee) because it names the actor and uses a stronger verb. But passive is not always wrong; it earns its place when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately hidden, or when the receiver of the action is the sentence's true topic.

Use passive deliberately when:

- the actor is unknown or unimportant;
- the focus belongs on the receiver of the action;
- you want to avoid assigning blame directly;
- the active version would be awkward or misleading.

Do not use passive out of habit or to sound formal. Default to active; switch to passive only with a reason.

### Revise Without Overworking

Line revision can over-polish a sentence until it becomes stiff, uniform, or lifeless. The goal is clarity, rhythm, and emphasis, not perfection. A sentence that is clear and has some texture is better than one that has been sanded smooth until it loses character.

Guard against overworking by:

- stopping when the sentence does its job, not when it is flawless;
- preserving the writer's voice and texture where it serves the reader;
- avoiding the urge to rewrite every sentence in the same polished mold;
- reading the revised passage in flow to confirm it still sounds alive.

### Keep The Line Pass Focused On Sentences

The line pass is for sentence-level problems. Resist drifting into structural revision (which belongs to an earlier pass) or proofreading (which belongs to a later one). If you notice a structural problem during the line pass, note it and handle it separately; if you notice a typo, note it for the proofreading pass. Mixing layers dilutes the line pass's effectiveness.

## Common Traps

### Line Editing Before Structure Is Settled

Polishing sentences that will be cut or moved is wasted effort. Confirm structure first; the line pass comes after.

### Rewriting Without Diagnosing

Aimlessly rephrasing a weak sentence without knowing why it is weak produces a different weak sentence. Diagnose the specific weakness, then revise to fix it.

### Burying The Point At The End

Ending a sentence on a weak qualifier or minor detail buries the emphasis. Move the important material to the end where it lands.

### Monotonous Sentence Length

A run of same-length sentences drones. Vary length intentionally to create rhythm and control pace.

### Separating Subject And Verb

Long insertions between subject and verb break the sentence's spine and force the reader to backtrack. Keep the spine tight.

### Passive Voice Out Of Habit

Default passive produces weak, actor-less prose. Use active voice unless the passive earns its place for a specific reason.

### Over-Polishing Into Stiffness

Endless line revision can sand the life out of prose. Stop when the sentence does its job; preserve texture and voice.

## Self-Check

Before treating the sentence-level pass as complete, verify:

- The line pass was done only after structure, purpose, and section jobs were settled.
- Every sentence was read once specifically for clarity on a single reading.
- Each revised sentence was diagnosed for its specific weakness before rewriting.
- Subject and verb are close together in most sentences, with minimal interruption.
- Important information sits at sentence ends; weak qualifiers do not bury the point.
- Sentence length varies intentionally to create rhythm rather than monotony.
- Active voice is the default, with passive used only where it earns its place.
- The pass stayed focused on sentences and did not drift into structural revision or proofreading.
- No sentence was over-polished into stiffness; texture and voice were preserved; the revised passage reads aloud with rhythm and momentum
- No sentence forces the reader to backtrack to understand it; the line pass improved clarity, rhythm, and emphasis without changing the structure or meaning
