# Plain Language For Accessibility

Plain language is an accessibility practice, not merely a style preference. For many readers, complex sentences, rare words, and dense structure are not a mild inconvenience but a wall. A reader with cognitive load, a reader with low literacy, a reader reading in a second language, a reader who is fatigued or in pain, and a reader using assistive technology all pay a higher cost for the same prose than a rested fluent native speaker does. When writing ignores this, it excludes people who have the right to understand it, especially when the writing carries instructions, rights, benefits, health, legal, or safety information.

The judgment problem is that accessibility-oriented plain language is not the same as general clarity. General clarity aims at the typical reader; accessibility plain language aims at the reader who faces the highest barrier. The two overlap, but the accessibility frame changes the standard: a sentence that is clear enough for most readers may still exclude the readers plain language is meant to serve. Writers also resist accessibility plain language because they fear it dumbs down the content or insults fluent readers. Neither fear is justified when the practice is done well. This skill prevents the harm of exclusion and the rework of rewriting public-facing content after it has failed the readers who needed it most.

Use this skill when writing public-facing, instructional, legal, health, financial, or government content; when applying WCAG readability guidance; or when revising prose to serve readers with cognitive, literacy, or language barriers. The agent has freedom to use complex language for expert audiences, but should never let default complexity exclude readers who must understand the content.

## Core Rules

### Treat Readability As An Access Requirement

When content carries rights, obligations, health, safety, or money, comprehension is an access requirement, not a nicety. A reader who cannot understand their lease, their medical instructions, their benefits letter, or their safety procedure is materially harmed. In these contexts, plain language is part of the duty to make the content usable, parallel to providing physical access.

Treat readability as required when the reader:

- must act on the text to receive a benefit or avoid a penalty;
- is making a decision about health, money, rights, or safety;
- has no alternative source for the information;
- may be reading under stress, fatigue, or time pressure;
- includes people with cognitive disabilities, low literacy, or limited language proficiency.

In these cases, the standard is not "clear enough for most" but "understandable by the readers who face the highest barriers."

### Write To The Highest Barrier, Not The Average Reader

General clarity targets the median reader. Accessibility plain language targets the reader who will struggle most. Designing for the highest barrier tends to improve the piece for everyone, because removing unnecessary complexity helps all readers, while designing only for the average leaves the struggling reader behind.

Design for readers who may be:

- reading in a second or additional language;
- managing cognitive load, fatigue, or pain;
- reading with low literacy or limited formal education;
- reading under time pressure or distress;
- using screen readers or other assistive technology.

Ask whether the reader facing the highest barrier could understand and act on the text. If not, simplify until they can, without sacrificing accuracy.

### Control Sentence Length And Complexity

Long sentences are the most common barrier in otherwise clear writing. Each clause, condition, and parenthetical adds load. Readers with cognitive or language barriers often lose the thread in sentences that fluent readers parse easily. Accessibility plain language favors shorter sentences with one main idea each.

Reduce sentence complexity by:

- aiming for an average sentence length appropriate to the audience and content;
- breaking sentences that carry more than one main idea;
- keeping subject and verb close together;
- limiting embedded clauses, parentheticals, and stacked conditions;
- putting conditions before actions ("If X, then Y") so the reader knows the gate first.

Length targets are guides, not laws; some ideas need longer sentences. But default to shorter, and add length only when the content requires it.

### Choose Common Words And Define Necessary Terms

Rare words slow every reader and stop many. Readers with low literacy or limited language proficiency often do not have the rare word in their vocabulary and cannot infer it from context. Common words are recognized faster and with less effort, freeing attention for meaning.

Choose vocabulary by:

- preferring the common word when it carries the meaning ("use" over "utilize");
- defining any necessary technical or legal term on first use, in plain words;
- avoiding jargon, legalese, and bureaucratic phrasing that adds no precision;
- checking that definitions use vocabulary the reader already has;
- using the same term consistently rather than varying for elegance.

When a rare word is necessary, define it. When it is not necessary, replace it.

### Structure For Scanning And Stepwise Reading

Many readers, especially those with cognitive load or limited time, do not read linearly. They scan for the part that applies to them. Structure that supports scanning turns a wall of text into navigable content. Headings, short paragraphs, numbered steps, and lists let readers find what they need and act on it.

Use structure to aid access:

- front-load the answer or the action before the explanation;
- use descriptive headings that name the section's content;
- break procedures into numbered steps in the order they are performed;
- use bullet lists for parallel items, conditions, or options;
- separate "what to do" from "why" so action is not buried in context.

Structure is accessibility infrastructure. A well-structured plain piece is easier to use than a beautifully written dense one.

### Make The Action And The Stakes Explicit

In instructional and transactional content, the reader needs to know what to do and why it matters. Buried actions and unstated stakes cause errors, missed deadlines, and harm. Make the action explicit and put it where the reader will find it, and make the consequence of acting or not acting clear.

Ensure:

- the action is stated as a direct instruction, not implied;
- the action appears early and is repeated where relevant;
- deadlines, amounts, and requirements are concrete and prominent;
- the consequence of inaction is stated plainly;
- the reader knows what to do if they cannot complete the action.

Implicit instructions fail the readers who most need clarity. State the action.

### Respect WCAG And Established Readability Guidance

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines and related readability standards codify practices that improve access for disabled readers. Treating these as compliance checkboxes misses the point, but ignoring them removes concrete, tested guidance. Use the standards as a floor, not a ceiling.

Relevant practices include:

- writing so the meaning is clear on first reading;
- providing definitions or expansions for unusual terms and abbreviations;
- avoiding content that requires a reading ability higher than necessary;
- supplementing text with structure, headings, and summaries for longer content;
- ensuring content works with assistive technology, including consistent structure.

Standards evolve; check current guidance rather than relying on memory. The goal is access, and the standards are tools toward it.

### Test With Readers Who Face Barriers

Writers are poor judges of accessibility because they already understand the content. The only reliable test is whether the intended readers, including those who face barriers, can understand and act on the text. Testing with representative readers reveals what readability formulas and author judgment cannot.

Test by:

- asking readers with relevant barriers to paraphrase the key points;
- watching where readers slow down, reread, or give up;
- checking whether readers can complete the intended action after reading;
- including readers with cognitive disabilities, low literacy, and non-native proficiency;
- revising based on what the test reveals, not defending the original.

Where formal testing is not possible, role-play the highest-barrier reader honestly. Better still, advocate for real testing when the stakes are high.

### Preserve Accuracy While Reducing Complexity

A persistent fear is that plain language distorts meaning. Done well, it does not. The discipline is to reduce complexity without removing necessary precision. A plain version that loses accuracy has failed; a complex version that excludes readers has also failed. The goal is the simplest expression that preserves the meaning.

Balance by:

- simplifying syntax and vocabulary before simplifying content;
- keeping necessary distinctions, even if they require a bit more length;
- using examples to carry precision that abstract phrasing would mangle;
- testing whether the plain version still conveys the original meaning;
- accepting more length where accuracy requires it.

Accuracy and access are allies, not opponents, when the writer refuses to sacrifice either.

## Common Traps

### Designing Only For The Average Reader

Clarity aimed at the median reader leaves the highest-barrier readers behind. Accessibility plain language targets the readers who struggle most.

### Equating Plain Language With Dumbing Down

Plain language carries the same meaning in a more accessible form. It does not remove necessary complexity; it removes unnecessary difficulty.

### Letting Readability Formulas Override Judgment

Formulas measure proxies, not comprehension. Use them as signals, but verify with real readers and real meaning.

### Burying The Action In Context

Long explanations before the instruction cause readers to miss the instruction. Front-load what the reader must do.

### Using Jargon To Sound Authoritative

Rare words signal expertise to the writer but exclude the reader. Authority comes from being understood, not from being opaque.

### Assuming The Writer Can Judge Accessibility

Writers already understand their own content, so it feels accessible to them. Only representative readers can confirm access.

### Sacrificing Accuracy For Simplicity

Simplifying past the point of accuracy produces a readable but wrong text. Preserve necessary distinctions even at the cost of length.

## Self-Check

Before treating the plain language work as complete, verify:

- The content's purpose and stakes have been assessed, and readability treated as required where access matters.
- The piece is designed for the highest-barrier reader, not only the average.
- Sentences are short, carry one main idea, and keep subject and verb close.
- Vocabulary uses common words, with necessary terms defined on first use in plain language.
- Structure supports scanning: headings, steps, lists, and front-loaded answers.
- The action and its stakes are explicit and placed where the reader will find them.
- Established readability and accessibility guidance has been consulted and applied.
- The text has been tested, or honestly role-played, against readers facing barriers; accuracy has been preserved; simplification removed difficulty, not meaning
- Necessary distinctions remain even where they add length; a reader with cognitive load, low literacy, or non-native proficiency could understand and act on the text
- No action, deadline, or consequence is left implicit; the piece reads as respectful, not condescending, to the readers it serves
