# Triaging And Evaluating Feedback

Feedback is not a set of instructions. It is a body of evidence about how the draft lands on readers, mixed with preferences, misunderstandings, projections, and occasional mistakes. The writer's job is not to implement every comment but to triage: to decide what the feedback reveals, what is worth acting on, what should be declined, and what should be set aside without action. Treating all feedback as equally valid produces compromised, overworked drafts; treating all feedback as an attack produces defensive, unimproved ones.

Agents tend to fail at triage in predictable ways. They either accept everything to seem responsive, or they accept the feedback that is easiest to implement rather than the feedback that matters most. They confuse a reviewer's confidence with correctness, and they fail to notice when a piece of feedback is right about a problem but wrong about the solution. Use this skill when sorting through comments on a draft, deciding what to act on, weighing feedback from different sources, or resisting the urge to comply with everything. The goal is to extract the genuine signal from feedback while preserving the writer's authority over the piece.

## Core Rules

### Read All Feedback Once Before Acting On Any Of It

The first impulse when receiving feedback is to start fixing. Resist it. Read every comment through, without editing, to see the whole picture before responding to any part.

A complete first read lets you notice:

- patterns, where multiple reviewers flag the same passage;
- contradictions, where reviewers disagree about the same section;
- the overall distribution of concerns versus isolated nitpicks;
- which comments reveal real problems versus personal preference;
- where your own assumptions about the draft were wrong.

Acting comment by comment causes you to miss patterns and to over-weight whichever comment you read most recently.

### Separate The Reaction From The Proposed Fix

Most feedback bundles two things: a reaction to the text and a suggested remedy. The reaction is often valuable even when the remedy is wrong. Learn to split them.

For each substantive comment, identify:

- what the reader experienced, such as confusion, boredom, disbelief, or offense;
- where in the text that reaction occurred;
- what the reviewer thinks should be done about it;
- whether the proposed fix actually addresses the reaction.

A comment that says "this example doesn't work, replace it with X" may be right that the example fails but wrong about the replacement. Fix the underlying problem with your own judgment.

### Weigh Feedback By Source And Relevance

Not all feedback carries the same authority. A comment's value depends on who gave it and how well they represent the target reader.

Consider:

- is this reviewer close to the intended reader;
- does the reviewer have relevant expertise for this claim;
- is the reviewer speaking within their lane or outside it;
- does the reviewer have a stake or bias that shapes their view;
- has this reviewer been reliable on this kind of judgment before.

A subject-matter expert's correction of a fact outweighs their opinion on rhythm. A representative reader's confusion outweighs a non-reader's aesthetic preference. Calibrate weight deliberately.

### Look For The Underlying Problem Behind Each Comment

Even wrong feedback usually points at something. A reviewer who misreads a passage may be revealing that the passage is genuinely ambiguous. A reviewer who wants more emotion may be sensing that the stakes are unclear.

Translate each comment into a question:

- what in the text could have produced this reaction;
- is there a real weakness here even if the reviewer misdiagnosed it;
- would the target reader likely have the same experience;
- is this a content problem, a clarity problem, or a tone problem.

When you find a real underlying problem, fix it on your own terms even if you reject the specific suggestion.

### Distinguish Useful Critique From Wrong Feedback

Some feedback is simply wrong. It misreads the piece, asks for a different piece than the one you are writing, or pushes changes that would harm the reader. You are allowed to decline it.

Feedback is likely useful when it:

- identifies confusion a real reader would share;
- catches a factual error or logical gap;
- reveals a broken promise between opening and body;
- exposes unintended tone or implication;
- points to missing evidence for a load-bearing claim.

Feedback is likely wrong or low-value when it:

- asks the piece to serve a different audience or purpose;
- imposes the reviewer's stylistic preferences universally;
- contradicts established facts without evidence;
- requests hedging that weakens true and defensible claims;
- reflects a misreading the target reader would not make.

Decline wrong feedback clearly and without defensiveness.

### Cluster Feedback To Find Structural Issues

Individual comments often point to a single structural weakness. Clustering reveals what isolated reading misses.

Group comments by:

- section or passage;
- type of concern, such as clarity, evidence, tone, or pacing;
- whether they cluster around a particular claim or transition;
- whether multiple reviewers independently raised the same issue.

A cluster of unrelated comments on the same section usually means that section has a structural problem worth solving once, rather than patching comment by comment.

### Decide Explicitly What To Act On, Decline, And Defer

Triaging means producing a clear set of decisions, not a vague sense of agreement. After reading and clustering, make an explicit list.

For each comment, assign one of:

- act on it, with a specific planned change;
- act on the underlying problem in your own way;
- decline it, with a brief reason;
- defer it to a later version or a different piece.

Writing the decisions down prevents drift during revision and gives you a record if a reviewer asks why their comment was not taken.

### Preserve The Writer's Authority Over The Piece

Feedback informs the writer's judgment; it does not replace it. The writer remains responsible for the final piece and is the one who answers for it.

Keep authority by:

- making the final call on every change, even accepted ones;
- refusing to implement changes you cannot defend;
- not outsourcing structural decisions to a committee;
- being willing to disappoint a reviewer when the reader is better served;
- remembering that you, not the reviewers, will be credited or blamed for the result.

A piece revised by consensus tends to satisfy reviewers and bore readers.

### Close The Loop With Reviewers Whose Input You Declined

Declining feedback respectfully preserves relationships and often improves future feedback. Reviewers who understand their input was considered give better notes next time.

When declining:

- acknowledge the specific reaction they had;
- explain briefly why you did not make the change;
- reference the piece's purpose or reader when possible;
- thank them for the time and care.

Silent declines breed resentment and lower the quality of future feedback.

## Common Traps

### Implementing The Easiest Comments First

Tackling trivial fixes first feels productive but crowds out the structural issues that actually matter. Address the load-bearing feedback early.

### Equating Confidence With Correctness

A reviewer who states a preference forcefully is not necessarily right. Weight by relevance and evidence, not volume.

### Acting On Feedback That Asks For A Different Piece

Some comments want a different audience, purpose, or genre. Declining them is correct, not stubborn.

### Fixing The Symptom The Reviewer Named

Implementing the reviewer's exact suggestion can leave the real problem in place. Fix the underlying weakness.

### Accepting Everything To Appear Responsive

Overcompliance produces a draft that satisfies reviewers and loses readers. Responsiveness is not the same as good judgment.

### Treating All Reviewers As Equal

A fact correction from an expert and a tone preference from a casual reader do not carry the same weight. Calibrate.

### Losing Track Of Decisions Across Rounds

Without a written triage, the same declined comment returns in the next round and the draft never converges.

## Self-Check

Before treating feedback triage as complete, verify:

- You read all feedback once before acting on any of it.
- Each substantive comment was split into reaction and proposed fix.
- Feedback was weighted by source, expertise, and relevance to the target reader.
- You looked for the underlying problem behind each comment, not just the surface request.
- Wrong or off-scope feedback was identified and declined with reason.
- Comments were clustered to reveal structural issues.
- Each comment has an explicit decision: act, act differently, decline, or defer.
- Accepted changes serve the reader, not just the reviewer; the writer's authority over the final piece was preserved
- Declines were communicated respectfully to preserve the relationship; a written record of decisions exists to prevent reopening settled issues
- The triaged set of changes strengthens the piece's purpose rather than diluting it; no load-bearing feedback was skipped because it was hard to implement
