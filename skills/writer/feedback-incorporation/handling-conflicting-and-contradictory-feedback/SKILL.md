# Handling Conflicting And Contradictory Feedback

When two or more reviewers give opposite advice about the same passage, the writer faces a problem that compliance cannot solve. One reviewer wants the piece shorter; another wants more depth. One finds the tone too casual; another finds it too stiff. One wants a claim softened; another wants it stated more boldly. If the writer tries to satisfy both, the result is a hedge that resolves nothing. If the writer defers to whichever reviewer spoke last or loudest, the piece becomes arbitrary. The only way through is for the writer to make the call, openly and on the merits.

Agents tend to mishandle contradictory feedback by either splitting the difference or escalating the conflict upward. Both abdicate the writer's actual job, which is to hold the whole piece in mind and decide what serves the reader. Use this skill when reviewers disagree, when feedback contradicts itself, or when you must choose between opposing suggestions. The goal is to resolve conflicts deliberately, with reasoning the writer can defend, rather than letting the draft become a negotiated settlement that pleases no one.

## Core Rules

### Recognize That Contradiction Is Information, Not A Problem To Smooth Over

When reviewers disagree, the instinct is often to find a middle wording that offends neither. But contradiction is itself data. It tells you the passage is doing something contested, or that the reviewers are reading from different assumptions about the piece.

Before resolving, diagnose the conflict:

- are the reviewers disagreeing about facts, about tone, about scope, or about purpose;
- are they imagining different readers;
- is one reviewer closer to the target audience than the other;
- is the passage genuinely ambiguous in a way that needs clarifying;
- does the conflict reveal that the piece's purpose itself is unclear.

Smoothing over a conflict without diagnosing it leaves the underlying tension in the piece.

### Return To Purpose And Reader To Break The Tie

When reviewers disagree, the piece's purpose and reader are the tiebreakers. The question is never which reviewer is more senior or more insistent, but which choice better serves what the piece is for.

Ask:

- which option moves the reader toward the promised outcome;
- which option keeps the piece's primary purpose intact;
- which option fits the genre and its obligations;
- which option the target reader would actually prefer;
- which option preserves the stakes the piece depends on.

If the purpose and reader are clear, most conflicts resolve themselves. If they do not, the conflict may be exposing an unresolved planning question that should be settled first.

### Diagnose Whether The Conflict Is About Substance Or Preference

Not all contradictions are equal. Some are substantive disputes about what is true or effective; others are pure preference clashes where either choice would work.

Substantive conflicts involve:

- factual claims and their support;
- logical structure and whether an argument holds;
- whether evidence is sufficient for a load-bearing claim;
- whether a framing is fair or misleading.

Preference conflicts involve:

- word choice between two adequate options;
- sentence rhythm or paragraph length;
- level of formality within an acceptable range;
- which of two valid examples to use.

Resolve substantive conflicts by appeal to evidence and reader effect. Resolve preference conflicts by choosing consistently with the piece's voice and moving on.

### Check Whether The Reviewers Represent The Target Reader

A common source of contradiction is that reviewers are not the target reader. An expert may want more technical depth than the audience can use; a generalist may want more hand-holding than the audience needs. Their disagreement may simply reflect that neither is the reader.

Assess each reviewer's proximity:

- how close is this reviewer to the intended audience;
- does this reviewer share the audience's knowledge, stakes, and constraints;
- is the reviewer projecting their own preferences onto a different reader;
- would the actual reader experience the passage as this reviewer did.

Weight the input of the reviewer closest to the target reader, and discount input from reviewers reading outside their lane.

### Avoid The Compromise That Satisfies No One

The tempting move with contradictory feedback is to find wording that nods toward both sides. This usually produces a vague, hedged sentence that neither reviewer likes and that confuses the reader.

Compromise fails when it:

- hedges a claim so it no longer says anything definite;
- keeps contradictory elements that pull in different directions;
- satisfies the letter of each suggestion while defeating its spirit;
- removes the clarity or force both reviewers actually wanted.

Prefer a clean decision in one direction over a muddy split. A piece that takes a clear position is easier to defend than one that hedges.

### Make The Decision Explicit And Document The Reasoning

When you resolve a conflict, record what you decided and why. This protects you if the dispute returns and helps reviewers understand the call.

For each resolved conflict, note:

- the conflicting inputs and who gave them;
- the decision you made;
- the reasoning, tied to purpose, reader, or evidence;
- whether either reviewer was notified of the resolution.

Documented decisions prevent the same argument from recurring in later rounds and demonstrate that the writer is exercising judgment rather than ignoring input.

### Communicate Resolutions To The Reviewers Involved

When reviewers disagree and you choose a side, tell them. Silent resolution breeds suspicion that input was disregarded; open resolution builds trust even when a reviewer's suggestion was not taken.

In communication:

- acknowledge both perspectives briefly;
- state the decision and the reasoning;
- reference the piece's purpose or reader as the basis;
- invite continued input if new evidence emerges.

Reviewers who see their conflict taken seriously give better feedback in the future, even when they lose the specific dispute.

### Escalate Only When Authority Or Risk Genuinely Requires It

Some conflicts do involve real authority or risk and should be escalated rather than decided unilaterally. Knowing when to escalate is part of handling contradiction well.

Escalate when:

- the conflict involves legal, compliance, or safety risk;
- two parties with genuine decision rights disagree;
- the dispute is about strategic direction beyond the writer's mandate;
- resolution would require changing the agreed scope or brief.

Do not escalate ordinary craft disputes. Escalating everything makes the writer appear unable to exercise judgment and slows the work to a halt.

### Re-Read The Resolved Passage As A Reader

After resolving a conflict, read the passage fresh, as the target reader would, without the context of the dispute. The resolution is only correct if it works for someone who never saw the feedback.

Check:

- does the passage now read clearly and intentionally;
- has the resolution introduced new confusion or hedging;
- does the passage fit the surrounding text and the piece's voice;
- would the target reader experience it as effective.

If the passage reads as a compromise to the writer's eye, it will read as one to the reader too.

## Common Traps

### Splitting The Difference On Every Conflict

Compromise wording usually satisfies neither reviewer and confuses the reader. Prefer a clean decision.

### Deferring To The Loudest Or Last Reviewer

Volume and recency are not authority. Weight input by relevance to the reader, not by insistence.

### Smoothing Over Conflict Without Diagnosing It

Hiding a contradiction under vague wording leaves the underlying tension in the piece and often resurfaces later.

### Escalating Ordinary Craft Disputes

Pushing every disagreement upward signals an inability to judge and stalls the work. Escalate only genuine authority or risk conflicts.

### Treating All Reviewers As Equally Close To The Reader

Reviewers reading outside the target audience produce conflicting input that does not reflect the reader's actual experience.

### Failing To Document Resolved Decisions

Without a record, the same dispute returns in the next round and the draft never converges.

### Letting Conflict Paralyze The Revision

Endless weighing of opposing inputs stalls progress. Make the call, document it, and move forward.

## Self-Check

Before treating conflict resolution as complete, verify:

- Each contradiction was diagnosed as substantive or preference before resolving.
- Resolutions were tied to the piece's purpose and target reader, not to reviewer seniority.
- The proximity of each reviewer to the target audience was considered when weighting input.
- No conflict was resolved by a hedge that satisfies neither side.
- Each resolution was made explicitly and documented with reasoning.
- Reviewers involved in a conflict were informed of the decision and its basis.
- Escalation was reserved for genuine authority, risk, or scope conflicts.
- The resolved passage was re-read fresh as the target reader would experience it; the resolution did not introduce new confusion or weakened claims
- The piece's voice and reader promise survived the resolutions; documented decisions prevent the same disputes from recurring
- The writer exercised judgment rather than deferring to the loudest voice; the draft moved forward rather than stalling under unresolved contradiction
