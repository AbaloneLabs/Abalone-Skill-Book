# Genre Identification And Reader Contract

Genre is not a label applied after writing; it is a contract made before the reader finishes the first paragraph. When a reader recognizes a piece as a how-to, a memoir, a case study, an op-ed, or a research brief, they instantly calibrate their expectations for evidence, voice, structure, pacing, and payoff. Writers who ignore genre produce pieces that fight their own form: a guide without steps, a story without movement, an argument without stakes. The reader feels the wrongness without being able to name it, and abandons the piece.

Genre identification is a judgment problem because the same material can be shaped into several genres, each with different obligations and different failure modes. A failure story can become a postmortem, a narrative, a warning, or an analytical essay, and each choice changes what the reader expects and what the piece must deliver. An agent that treats genre as a neutral container tends to borrow a genre's surface without honoring its contract, producing hybrids that satisfy no reader.

Use this skill when starting a piece, when a draft feels formless despite good material, when choosing among possible treatments of the same subject, or when diagnosing why readers do not engage. The goal is to identify the genre deliberately and then meet the obligations that genre creates.

## Core Rules

### Identify The Genre Before Drafting, Not After

Genre decides structure, evidence type, voice, length, and ending before a sentence is written. Identifying it late means rewriting, because the first draft was shaped for the wrong contract. Name the genre as an explicit planning decision.

To identify genre, ask:

- what is the primary reader outcome (decide, understand, feel, act, enjoy);
- what evidence does this outcome require (data, story, steps, analysis, testimony);
- what structure does this evidence imply (sequence, argument, investigation, scene);
- what voice does the reader expect (authoritative, intimate, neutral, dramatic);
- what length and depth does the platform and reader tolerate.

The answers point to a genre family. A reader who must decide needs a briefing or recommendation; a reader who must understand needs an explainer; a reader who must feel needs narrative or memoir. Match the genre to the outcome, not the writer's habit.

### Treat Genre As A Contract With Specific Obligations

Each genre carries obligations the reader expects to be met. Breaking them is allowed only when done deliberately and signaled; breaking them by accident reads as incompetence. Know the obligations of the genre you have chosen.

Common genres and their core obligations:

- how-to or guide: usable, ordered steps the reader can follow and that actually work;
- explainer: accurate orientation that builds understanding from the reader's starting point;
- argument or op-ed: a contestable claim supported by evidence and engaged with objections;
- case study: a real case with transferable insight, not voyeurism or anecdote;
- narrative or memoir: movement, stakes, and change, not only description;
- review: a judgment grounded in explicit criteria the reader can evaluate;
- report or briefing: decision-relevant information, scoped and prioritized for the decider;
- profile: a person revealed through specificity, not summary of accomplishments.

A guide without workable steps has broken its contract. A case study without transferable insight has broken its. Identify the obligation and meet it, or choose a different genre.

### Match Genre To Purpose And Reader, Not To Habit

Writers often default to the genre they are comfortable with regardless of whether it serves the piece. A narrative writer turns everything into story; an analyst turns everything into argument. But the right genre is the one that serves the reader's outcome, not the writer's reflex.

Match by asking:

- does this genre deliver the outcome the reader came for;
- does the reader trust the evidence type this genre uses;
- does the platform expect this genre, or will it feel miscast;
- does the writer have the material and authority this genre demands.

If the reader needs a decision and the writer produces a reflection, the genre mismatch dooms the piece even if the reflection is beautiful. Choose the genre that fits the job, and develop the skill to write in genres outside your comfort zone.

### Understand That Genre Sets Reader Calibration

Genre is how the reader calibrates attention, skepticism, and patience. A reader entering a memoir lowers their factual skepticism and raises their emotional attention. A reader entering a research brief raises their factual skepticism and lowers their patience for flourish. Mislabeling the genre miscalibrates the reader for the entire piece.

Calibration effects:

- narrative lowers fact-checking instinct and raises emotional investment;
- analysis raises evidentiary standards and lowers tolerance for sentiment;
- instructional lowers skepticism and raises impatience for theory;
- opinion raises counterargument instinct and lowers tolerance for hedging;
- investigative raises curiosity and lowers tolerance for premature conclusions.

A piece that signals one genre but delivers another confuses the reader's calibration. A memoir-styled piece that turns out to be an argument leaves the reader unsure whether to feel or to refute. Keep the signal and the substance aligned.

### Honor The Genre's Structural Conventions

Each genre has structural conventions the reader expects, even unconsciously. Violating them without reason produces disorientation. Honoring them gives the reader a map they can follow, freeing attention for the content.

Structural conventions by genre:

- guide: problem framing, prerequisites, ordered steps, troubleshooting, summary;
- explainer: orient, define, build, complicate, conclude;
- argument: claim, evidence, counterargument, refinement, implication;
- case study: context, situation, action, outcome, transferable lesson;
- narrative: scene, pressure, escalation, turn, resolution;
- review: subject, criteria, evaluation, comparison, judgment.

Conventions are scaffolding, not prison. You may rearrange them when the rearrangement serves the reader, but do so deliberately and signal the deviation so the reader can follow.

### Know When A Genre Is The Wrong Choice

Sometimes the material cannot honestly support the genre the writer wants. A story without stakes cannot be a narrative; a topic without a contestable claim cannot be an argument; a process that cannot be reduced to steps cannot be a guide. Forcing the material into an ill-fitting genre produces a piece that fails its own contract.

Recognize mismatches:

- the material is informational but the writer wants narrative drama it does not have;
- the material is experiential but the writer wants analytical rigor it cannot bear;
- the material is a single anecdote stretched into a universal claim;
- the material lacks the evidence the chosen genre's readers will demand.

When the genre fights the material, change the genre or change the material. Do not force a contract the piece cannot honor.

### Signal Genre Clearly So The Reader Can Commit

The reader needs to recognize the genre early to commit the right kind of attention. Vague or mixed signals leave the reader hedging, unsure how to read what follows. Signal the genre through title, opening, structure, and voice.

Signaling moves:

- titles that name the form ("A Guide to," "A Case Study of," "Why");
- openings that establish the form in the first paragraph;
- structural cues like numbered steps, section headers, or scene breaks;
- voice that matches the genre's expected register;
- framing that tells the reader what they will get and how to read it.

Clear signaling is not limiting; it is respectful. A reader who knows the genre can give the piece the right kind of attention and judge it by the right standard.

## Common Traps

### Borrowing A Genre's Surface Without Its Obligations

Calling something a "case study" without transferable insight, or a "guide" without usable steps, borrows credibility without delivering the substance. The reader feels the emptiness.

### Defaulting To A Comfortable Genre

Writers who always reach for the same genre produce mismatched pieces when the material or reader needs a different form. Genre should follow purpose, not habit.

### Mixed Signals That Miscalibrate The Reader

Opening like a story and continuing like a report, or titling like an argument and delivering a survey, leaves the reader unsure how to read the piece and lowers trust.

### Forcing Material Into An Ill-Fitting Genre

When the material cannot support the genre's demands, the piece fails its contract. Change the genre or develop the material rather than forcing the fit.

### Ignoring Platform Genre Expectations

A platform's readers expect certain genres and reject others. Writing a long analytical essay for a feed built on quick takes, or vice versa, fights the medium regardless of quality.

### Treating Conventions As Optional Decoration

Structural conventions exist to help the reader. Discarding them without reason produces disorientation, not innovation. Deviate deliberately and signal it.

## Self-Check

Before treating the genre choice as settled, verify:

- The genre is identified explicitly before drafting, not discovered after.
- You can name the specific obligations the chosen genre creates and confirm the piece meets them.
- The genre matches the reader's primary outcome, not merely the writer's comfort.
- The reader's calibration (skepticism, patience, emotional investment) fits the genre signaled.
- Structural conventions of the genre are honored or deliberately, signaled deviations.
- The material can honestly support the genre's demands without forcing.
- The genre is signaled clearly through title, opening, structure, and voice; no competing genre is silently running alongside the primary one
- The platform's genre expectations were considered and either met or knowingly challenged; a reader who recognizes the genre would find the piece honors its contract
- The chosen genre is the best fit among the available options, not just the first considered; the evidence type used matches what the genre's readers will trust
- The ending fulfills the specific payoff the genre promises; the piece does not borrow a genre's credibility while dodging its obligations
