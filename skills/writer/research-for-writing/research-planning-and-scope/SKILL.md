# Research Planning And Scope

Research without a plan becomes hoarding. The writer gathers source after source, each promising to be the missing piece, until the investigation swallows the deadline and the actual writing never begins, or begins from a pile too large to synthesize. The opposite failure is just as common: a writer skips planning, grabs the first three search results, and produces a piece whose claims outrun its evidence. Good research is not measured by volume gathered but by whether the right questions were asked and the right evidence was sought to answer them.

Research planning is a judgment problem because the scope of investigation depends on the piece's claim, the reader's skepticism, and the available time, and none of these is fixed. Research too narrow and the piece is indefensible; research too broad and the piece never gets written. An agent that treats research as gathering rather than questioning tends to produce drafts that are over-sourced but under-argued, full of citations that do not actually support the claims being made.

Use this skill before beginning research for any piece, when forming research questions, when deciding how much evidence is enough, or when diagnosing why a research phase has stalled or sprawled. The goal is a bounded investigation that serves the piece rather than displacing it.

## Core Rules

### Start From Research Questions, Not Topics

A topic tells you what to gather; a research question tells you what to look for. Research driven by topic collects everything related and drowns in it; research driven by questions targets specific gaps and stops when they are filled. Form questions before opening a single source.

Strong research questions:

- are specific enough that an answer would change the piece;
- are narrow enough that evidence can actually address them;
- distinguish what you need to know from what would be nice to know;
- include the questions the skeptical reader will ask;
- include the questions the claim's strength depends on.

Convert a topic into questions: from "remote work productivity" to "what does the evidence actually show about output changes, and where do the studies disagree?" The questions define the boundary of useful research.

### Match Evidence Type To Claim Type

Different claims require different evidence, and gathering the wrong type wastes effort. A causal claim needs different support than a descriptive one; a normative claim needs different support than a predictive one. Plan research around the claim type the piece will make.

Match by claim:

- descriptive claims need observation, data, or testimony about what is;
- causal claims need evidence of mechanism, correlation, or experiment;
- predictive claims need trend data, models, or expert forecasting;
- normative claims need values, precedents, or consequences;
- comparative claims need parallel cases or benchmarks.

If the piece will make a causal claim but you can only gather descriptive evidence, either change the claim or change the research plan. Do not let weak evidence silently upgrade into a strong claim during drafting.

### Scope The Investigation Against The Piece's Needs

Research scope should follow the piece, not the field. A 1500-word essay cannot carry the evidence a white paper can, and over-researching a short piece wastes time and tempts padding. Scope the investigation to what the piece can actually use.

Scoping factors:

- the strength of the central claim and the evidence it requires;
- the skepticism level of the target reader;
- the length and depth the piece can hold;
- the deadline and the time research can reasonably take;
- the accessibility of the sources needed.

Set a research budget in advance: how many hours, how many sources, how deep into each. A budget forces decisions about what matters and prevents the investigation from expanding to fill all available time.

### Identify The Load-Bearing Evidence Early

Not all evidence in a piece carries equal weight. Some sources or facts are load-bearing: if they fall, the argument falls. Identifying these early lets you focus verification effort where it matters most, rather than treating every source as equally important.

Find the load-bearing evidence by asking:

- which facts, if wrong, would collapse the central claim;
- which sources provide the foundation other sources depend on;
- which statistics or quotes are repeated or built upon;
- which claims would, if challenged, leave the piece defenseless.

Concentrate verification on the load-bearing evidence. A piece can survive a weak peripheral source; it cannot survive a wrong foundational one. Know which is which before drafting.

### Plan For The Reader's Skepticism

Research is not only about supporting your claim; it is about anticipating and addressing the reader's objections. A piece that ignores the strongest counter-evidence reads as one-sided and loses the skeptical reader. Plan research to engage the opposition, not avoid it.

Include in the plan:

- the strongest objection to the central claim and the evidence behind it;
- the cases where the claim does not hold;
- the credible voices who disagree and why;
- the limitations of the supporting evidence.

Engaging counter-evidence strengthens the piece when handled fairly. Research that only seeks confirmation produces advocacy that informed readers dismiss. Plan to research against yourself.

### Know When To Stop Researching

Research can expand indefinitely, and the failure mode of perfectionism is never starting to write. Knowing when to stop is a planning decision, not a feeling. Define stopping conditions in advance so the transition to drafting is deliberate.

Stop researching when:

- the core research questions have credible answers;
- the load-bearing evidence is verified or its limits are known;
- additional sources are repeating what you already have;
- the strongest counter-evidence has been found and engaged;
- the research budget is exhausted;
- further research is avoiding the harder work of synthesis and writing.

Diminishing returns signal the stop. If the tenth source adds nothing the first nine did not, the investigation is complete. Move to synthesis before the pile becomes unmanageable.

### Distinguish Research That Serves The Piece From Research That Displaces It

Research can become procrastination, especially for writers who find gathering more comfortable than synthesizing. The displacement is often unconscious: the writer believes one more source will finally make the argument clear, when the real problem is that synthesis has not begun. Recognize displacement and intervene.

Signs of displacement:

- research continues past the point of diminishing returns;
- the writer feels unable to start drafting despite ample material;
- each new source postpones the writing rather than enabling it;
- the investigation keeps expanding to new questions beyond the original scope.

When displacement appears, stop researching and start synthesizing, even if the material feels incomplete. A draft from incomplete research reveals the real gaps far better than more gathering does.

### Build A Research Plan That Can Be Revised

First research plans are often wrong, like first drafts. As evidence arrives, the questions may need refining, the scope may need widening or narrowing, and new sub-questions may emerge. Build the plan to accommodate revision rather than treating it as fixed.

Revision triggers:

- evidence contradicts the assumed claim and shifts the question;
- a source reveals the original question was the wrong one;
- the accessible evidence cannot support the planned claim strength;
- a stronger angle emerges from the material itself.

Revise the plan openly: update the questions, the scope, and the budget together so the investigation stays coherent. A research plan that cannot change is a constraint, not a guide.

## Common Traps

### Researching The Topic Instead Of The Question

Topic-driven research gathers everything and synthesizes nothing. Convert the topic to specific questions before researching, and let the questions bound the work.

### Over-Researching To Avoid Writing

Gathering can feel like progress while actually being procrastination. Set stopping conditions and honor them; start synthesizing even when the material feels incomplete.

### Confirmatory Research That Ignores Counter-Evidence

Seeking only support produces advocacy that informed readers reject. Plan to research the strongest objections, not just the supporting case.

### Weak Evidence Quietly Upgraded To Strong Claims

Descriptive evidence cannot support causal claims, no matter how many sources repeat the assertion. Match evidence type to claim type from the start.

### Treating All Sources As Equally Important

Spreading verification evenly wastes effort on peripheral sources while load-bearing evidence goes unchecked. Identify and prioritize the evidence the argument actually depends on.

### No Stopping Condition

Without defined stopping conditions, research expands to fill all available time and the piece is never written. Decide in advance when enough is enough.

## Self-Check

Before treating the research plan as ready or the research phase as complete, verify:

- Research is driven by specific questions, not by the topic alone.
- Evidence type is matched to claim type, and weak evidence is not silently upgraded.
- The investigation is scoped to what the piece can actually use, given length and deadline.
- Load-bearing evidence is identified and prioritized for verification.
- The plan includes researching the strongest counter-evidence, not just confirmation.
- Stopping conditions are defined and recognized when reached.
- Research has not become displacement for the harder work of synthesis; the plan can be revised as evidence arrives without losing coherence
- The research budget is set and respected, with deliberate decisions about tradeoffs; peripheral sources are not treated as equal in importance to foundational ones
- The questions the skeptical reader will ask are part of the research plan; further research is producing diminishing returns rather than new insight
- The transition to synthesis is planned, not postponed indefinitely; the scope of research matches the strength of claim the piece intends to make
