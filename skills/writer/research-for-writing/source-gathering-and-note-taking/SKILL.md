# Source Gathering And Note Taking

Gathering sources is easy; gathering them so they are actually usable is hard. Most research fails not at the finding stage but at the capture stage. A writer reads something valuable, fails to record it in a form that survives the gap between research and drafting, and later either cannot find it, cannot remember what it said, or cannot cite it honestly. The result is a draft built on fading memory and vague paraphrase, supported by sources the writer can no longer locate, which is another way of saying unsupported.

Source gathering and note-taking are judgment problems because the goal is not comprehensiveness but usability. A system that captures everything but retrieves nothing is hoarding; a system that retrieves instantly but captures too little starves the draft. The craft is capturing the right material in a form that travels cleanly into writing, with enough context and provenance that the notes can be trusted and cited without re-doing the research. An agent that treats note-taking as passive transcription tends to produce piles that paralyze rather than enable.

Use this skill when building or using a research system, when gathering sources for a draft, when organizing material so drafting is not blocked, or when diagnosing why collected research is not making it into the writing. The goal is a capture process that serves synthesis rather than displacing it.

## Core Rules

### Gather With The Question In Hand

Source gathering without a question becomes browsing, and browsing accumulates material that feels relevant but serves no specific need. Enter each search with the research question you are trying to answer, so you can recognize when a source actually helps and when it merely interests.

Gather deliberately by:

- stating the question the source is meant to answer before reading deeply;
- noting whether the source answers, partially answers, or fails to answer it;
- recording why this source matters relative to the others already gathered;
- stopping when the question is answered rather than reading exhaustively.

A source that does not answer a current question may still be worth a quick capture for later, but do not let it expand the investigation. Question-driven gathering keeps the pile bounded and purposeful.

### Capture In A Form That Survives The Gap To Drafting

Notes taken for the moment of reading rarely survive to the moment of writing. A highlight, a mental note, or a vague paraphrase decays quickly and becomes unusable. Capture in a form that will still make sense weeks later, when the context of reading has faded.

Durable capture includes:

- the specific claim, quote, or fact in the source's words or your precise paraphrase;
- the page, section, timestamp, or location for exact retrieval;
- enough context that the note makes sense without the surrounding source;
- your immediate reaction or use, marked as distinct from the source's content;
- the bibliographic detail needed to cite honestly.

Separate what the source says from what you think about it. Conflating the two is how paraphrase drifts into invention and citation becomes unreliable. Mark the boundary clearly in every note.

### Record Provenance And Credibility As You Gather

A source's value depends on where it came from, and provenance is far easier to record at the moment of gathering than to reconstruct later. Capture credibility signals now so you can weigh sources during synthesis without re-investigating each one.

Record for each source:

- the author and their relevant expertise or stake;
- the publication, platform, or institution and its editorial standards;
- the date and whether the information is current;
- the evidence type (primary, secondary, expert opinion, anecdote, data);
- any conflict of interest or bias the reader would want to know.

Provenance recorded later is often wrong or missing. Recording it during capture makes later evaluation honest and citation defensible. A perfectly quoted claim from an unreliable source is still an unreliable claim.

### Build A System That Retrieves, Not Just Stores

A note system's value is measured at retrieval, not at storage. A pile of highlights you cannot search or sort is functionally absent during drafting. Design the system so that when you need a specific fact, quote, or counter-argument, you can find it in seconds.

Retrieval-supporting practices:

- consistent tagging or labeling by theme, claim, or question;
- a single searchable repository rather than scattered files and bookmarks;
- summaries or abstracts that let you scan many sources quickly;
- cross-references between related notes so one leads to others;
- a way to mark load-bearing versus peripheral material.

The system can be simple, but it must be consistent. A consistent plain-text system beats a sophisticated system used inconsistently. Choose a level of structure you will actually maintain.

### Take Notes That Are Already Partly Synthesized

Raw notes force full synthesis to happen during drafting, when time pressure and fatigue produce weak connections. Notes that include a layer of initial synthesis lighten the drafting load and surface insights while the material is fresh.

Add synthesis in capture:

- write one sentence on how this source relates to your central question;
- note agreements and contradictions with other sources as you encounter them;
- record the question or objection this source raises that you had not considered;
- mark the strongest quote or fact for likely use in the draft.

These synthesis annotations are cheap at capture time and expensive to reconstruct later. They turn a passive pile into an active network of relationships that drafting can draw on directly.

### Avoid Hoarding By Applying Disposal Discipline

Hoarding is the failure mode where every source feels potentially useful and nothing is discarded, until the pile is too large to use. Disposal discipline is the judgment of recognizing what will not serve the piece and letting it go, so the usable material is not buried.

Apply disposal by:

- deleting or archiving sources that do not answer any current question;
- consolidating redundant sources into the single strongest one;
- marking sources as out-of-scope rather than keeping them in the active pile;
- accepting that some gathered material will not be used, and that is correct.

The goal is a working set that fits in the mind, not a comprehensive archive. A smaller, well-curated set produces better drafts than a vast, unsorted one.

### Maintain Citation Integrity From The First Note

Citation problems are almost always capture problems that surface late. A quote used without a source, a paraphrase that drifted, a statistic whose origin was lost, these are failures of the note-taking system, not of the drafting stage. Build citation integrity into capture so drafting never has to guess.

Integrity practices:

- capture exact wording for anything you might quote, with quotation marks;
- distinguish direct quote, paraphrase, and your own thought visually;
- record the source for every claim, even if you are unsure you will use it;
- never let a note's origin become ambiguous through later editing;
- when in doubt, over-cite provenance rather than under-cite.

A draft built on notes with clean provenance can be cited confidently and revised without fear. A draft built on ambiguous notes forces re-research under deadline pressure, which is when errors and fabrication creep in.

## Common Traps

### Highlighting Instead Of Capturing

A highlight in a source decays without context and is often unretrievable later. Capture the meaning and provenance in your system, not just a mark on the original.

### Hoarding Sources Out Of FOMO

Keeping every potentially relevant source buries the usable material. Apply disposal discipline so the working set stays navigable.

### Conflating Source Content With Your Reaction

Mixing what the source says with what you think makes later citation unreliable and invites accidental invention. Mark the boundary in every note.

### Inconsistent Systems That Defeat Retrieval

A system used inconsistently cannot be searched reliably. Choose a level of structure you will maintain and apply it uniformly.

### Missing Provenance Reconstructed Late

Provenance guessed at during drafting is often wrong. Record credibility and citation detail at the moment of gathering.

### Raw Notes With No Synthesis Layer

Pure transcription forces all synthesis into the drafting stage under pressure. Add initial synthesis annotations during capture to surface connections while fresh.

## Self-Check

Before treating the gathering and note system as ready for drafting, verify:

- Each source was gathered with a specific research question in hand, not by browsing.
- Notes are captured in a form durable enough to make sense weeks later.
- Provenance and credibility signals are recorded at the moment of gathering.
- The system supports retrieval through consistent tagging and a searchable repository.
- Notes include a layer of initial synthesis, not only raw transcription.
- Disposal discipline has kept the working set navigable rather than hoarded.
- Citation integrity is built into capture, with quotes, paraphrases, and thoughts distinguished; the boundary between source content and your reaction is clear in every note
- Load-bearing material is marked so it is not buried among peripheral sources; redundant sources have been consolidated into the strongest representative
- The system is consistent enough that you can actually find what you need during drafting; no source in the active pile lacks a reason for being there
- Out-of-scope material has been archived or removed rather than left to clutter; a draft built from these notes could be cited honestly without re-research
