# Blueprints And Coverage

An exam is a sample. It cannot test everything taught, so what it chooses to test sends a powerful signal about what the course actually values. Without a blueprint, exams drift toward whatever was easy to write, whatever appeared in the textbook's test bank, or whatever the instructor remembered to include. The result is an exam that may be internally consistent but externally misaligned: it does not represent the course's stated outcomes, it over-weights some units and ignores others, and it fails to distinguish students who learned deeply from those who guessed well. A blueprint makes the sampling decision explicit and defensible.

Use this skill before building an exam, weighting content and cognitive levels, ensuring coverage, or auditing whether an exam represents the course. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing exams that are well-written question by question but incoherent as a whole, sampling the wrong material at the wrong cognitive levels.

## Core Rules

### Build A Blueprint Before Writing Questions

A blueprint is a table that specifies what the exam will cover before any question is written. It forces a deliberate sampling decision rather than an accidental one.

A blueprint typically records, per question or section:

- the learning outcome measured;
- the topic or unit;
- the cognitive level, remember through create;
- the question format;
- the point value;
- the estimated time;
- whether the outcome was taught and practiced.

Building the blueprint first prevents the common pattern of writing questions as they come to mind and ending with an unbalanced exam.

### Weight Content Proportional To Course Emphasis

The points allocated to a topic should reflect its importance in the course, not its ease of testing. If a unit carried three weeks and a major assignment, it should carry proportional exam weight.

Sources of weighting:

- time spent in class;
- reading and assignment load;
- outcome priority;
- prerequisite role for later courses;
- program or accreditation emphasis.

A unit that got one class meeting should not dominate the exam because its questions were easy to write. Weighting should be defensible to a student who asks why the exam emphasized what it did.

### Distribute Cognitive Levels Deliberately

An exam's cognitive profile should match the course's stated outcomes. If outcomes emphasize analysis and evaluation, the exam should not be dominated by recall.

Plan cognitive distribution by:

- listing the cognitive level of each outcome;
- allocating points so the highest-level outcomes get meaningful weight;
- ensuring that the cognitive ceiling of the exam matches the cognitive ceiling of the course;
- avoiding the drift toward recall that happens when high-level questions are hard to write.

If the course promised analysis but the exam caps at application, students who prepared for analysis are penalized and the exam signals that recall was sufficient.

### Ensure Every Major Outcome Is Assessed

Every outcome that carries weight in the course should have at least one assessment point on the exam, unless it is assessed elsewhere. Outcomes with no exam presence are effectively optional.

Build the blueprint by:

- listing all course outcomes;
- assigning each to at least one exam question or section;
- noting where an outcome is assessed by a project or paper instead;
- flagging outcomes that are under-represented relative to their importance.

An outcome with no assessment is not an outcome; it is a wish.

### Sample Across Units, Not Just The Final Weeks

Exams often over-weight recent material because it is fresh in the instructor's mind and easy to write about. A cumulative exam should represent the term, not the last three weeks.

Practices:

- allocate points across units proportional to their weight;
- include integration questions that cross units;
- avoid letting the final unit crowd out foundational material;
- decide explicitly whether the exam is cumulative and sample accordingly.

If the exam is not cumulative by design, say so, and ensure students know which units are covered.

### Balance Breadth And Depth

An exam that samples every topic shallowly measures breadth without depth. An exam that goes deep on one topic measures depth without representativeness. Decide deliberately.

Consider:

- a few deep questions that reveal reasoning;
- broader coverage questions that confirm foundational learning;
- integration questions that require connecting units;
- the time budget that constrains how much depth is feasible.

A good exam usually mixes a breadth component with a smaller number of depth questions, weighted to reflect outcome priority.

### Calibrate Time Against The Exam Window

A blueprint that fits the points may not fit the clock. Estimate the time each section requires for a prepared student and compare to the exam window.

Time factors:

- reading load for essay and problem questions;
- computation time for quantitative work;
- decision time for multiple choice;
- review and revision time;
- buffer for students who process more slowly, within accommodation limits.

An exam that cannot be finished in the window by a prepared student measures speed, not learning. Cut content or extend time rather than racing students.

### Review For Fairness And Construct Relevance

Each question should measure the intended construct, not incidental skills. Review for:

- reading load that measures literacy rather than the concept;
- cultural references that measure background rather than learning;
- trick wording that measures test-taking rather than knowledge;
- prerequisite skills that some students may lack;
- format demands that disadvantage some students.

A question that measures something other than the intended outcome adds noise and bias to the score.

### Document The Blueprint For Future Iteration

Keep the blueprint with the exam. It lets you:

- compare planned versus actual coverage;
- identify items that performed poorly for revision;
- maintain consistency across offerings of the course;
- hand off the course to a colleague with rationale intact;
- respond to student questions about why the exam was weighted as it was.

A blueprint is also evidence if a grade is challenged. It shows the exam was designed, not arbitrary.

### Reconcile The Blueprint With Other Assessments

The exam is one part of the assessment system. Reconcile it with:

- assignments and projects that assess outcomes the exam does not;
- formative checks that prepared students for the exam's cognitive level;
- the cumulative grade weighting so no outcome is over- or under-assessed overall.

An exam that duplicates what a project already measured wastes opportunity; an exam that ignores outcomes no other assessment covers leaves them unassessed.

## Common Traps

### Writing Questions Before Planning Coverage

Letting questions emerge ad hoc produces an unbalanced exam. Build the blueprint first.

### Weighting By Ease Of Writing

Over-weighting topics with easy questions misrepresents the course. Weight by importance.

### Recall Dominance By Default

Defaulting to recall because high-level questions are hard to write signals the wrong priorities. Plan cognitive distribution.

### Over-Weighting Recent Material

Letting the final unit dominate because it is fresh under-represents the term. Sample across units.

### Breadth Without Depth

Sampling everything shallowly fails to reveal reasoning. Mix breadth with depth.

### Time Miscalibration

An exam that cannot be finished measures speed. Calibrate time against the window.

### Construct-Irrelevant Difficulty

Reading load, cultural references, and trick wording measure the wrong thing. Review for construct relevance.

### No Documentation

Discarding the blueprint after the exam loses the chance to improve. Document and retain it.

## Self-Check

- Is a blueprint built before questions are written, mapping outcome, topic, cognitive level, format, points, and time?
- Is content weighted proportional to course emphasis, defensible to a student who asks?
- Does the cognitive distribution match the course's stated outcomes rather than defaulting to recall?
- Is every major outcome assessed on the exam or explicitly assessed elsewhere?
- Does the exam sample across units rather than over-weighting recent material?
- Is there a deliberate balance of breadth and depth questions?
- Has the time required for a prepared student been estimated against the exam window?
- Has each question been reviewed for construct relevance and absence of bias?
- Is the blueprint documented with the exam for future iteration and challenge response?
- Is the exam reconciled with other assessments so the overall system neither duplicates nor gaps coverage?
- Could you explain to a student why the exam was weighted and structured as it was?
