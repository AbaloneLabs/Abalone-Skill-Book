# Fairness And Validity

An exam produces a score, but a score is only meaningful if the exam is fair and valid. Fairness means that the exam measures learning rather than circumstance, identity, or test-taking skill. Validity means that the score supports the inferences being drawn from it, such as whether a student mastered the material, is ready for the next course, or meets a program standard. Instructors often treat fairness and validity as abstract concerns, but they are concrete: a biased or invalid exam produces wrong grades, blocks qualified students, advances unqualified ones, and exposes the institution to challenge.

Use this skill before finalizing an exam, reviewing for bias and accessibility, handling accommodations, interpreting results, or defending grading decisions. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing exams that feel rigorous but measure the wrong thing or measure it unequally across students.

## Core Rules

### Establish What Inferences The Score Must Support

Validity is about the inferences drawn from a score. Before finalizing an exam, identify what decisions the score will inform:

- course grade and progression;
- readiness for a subsequent course;
- program completion or certification;
- identification of students needing support;
- comparison across sections or instructors;
- accreditation evidence.

Different inferences require different evidence. A score adequate for grading within a course may not support claims about program readiness. Match the exam's design to the strongest inference it must support.

### Ensure Construct Validity

The exam should measure the intended construct, the knowledge or skill the course taught, and not incidental abilities. Threats to construct validity include:

- reading load that measures literacy rather than the concept;
- cultural references that measure background rather than learning;
- testwiseness that measures test-taking skill;
- speed that measures processing pace rather than mastery;
- prerequisite skills some students lack;
- format demands that disadvantage some students.

Review each question for whether a student who knows the construct but lacks the incidental skill could answer it. If not, the question measures the wrong thing.

### Check Reliability Across Questions And Graders

Reliability is the consistency of the score. A reliable exam produces similar scores across equivalent questions and across graders.

Threats to reliability include:

- too few questions to sample stably;
- ambiguous questions that different students interpret differently;
- essay grading without rubrics that varies by grader;
- inconsistent application of partial credit;
- questions that depend on a single ambiguous term.

For high-stakes exams, use rubrics, calibrate graders on sample papers, and review score distributions for anomalies. For multiple choice, review item statistics for questions that do not discriminate.

### Audit For Bias Across Student Groups

Bias means the exam measures the construct differently for different groups. Audit for:

- gender, racial, or ethnic performance gaps not explained by preparation;
- language-loaded questions that disadvantage multilingual students;
- culturally specific examples irrelevant to the concept;
- names, scenarios, and contexts that consistently represent one group;
- assumptions about resources or experiences not all students share.

Track item-level performance across groups. Items with large group disparities that are not explained by overall performance may be biased and should be revised or retired. Bias is often invisible to the instructor; data and colleague review help surface it.

### Design For Accessibility From The Start

Accessibility is easier when designed in, not retrofitted after an accommodation letter. Build in:

- accessible document structure and readable formats;
- clear typography and adequate white space;
- alternatives to color-only distinctions in diagrams;
- captioned or transcribed media;
- adequate time calibrated to the task, not to speed;
- multiple ways to demonstrate the same learning where feasible;
- compatibility with screen readers and assistive technology.

Accommodations adjust the delivery for individual students, but many barriers are removed by universal design that benefits everyone.

### Handle Accommodations Through The Official Process

Accommodations are determined by disability services, not by the instructor. Route requests through the official process and implement approved accommodations faithfully.

Practices:

- reference the accommodation process in the syllabus;
- implement approved accommodations without requiring students to re-disclose disability;
- coordinate exam logistics, separate room, extended time, assistive technology, in advance;
- protect student privacy about accommodations;
- do not invent your own accommodations or deny approved ones.

Deviating from the official process creates legal risk and harms students.

### Make Grading Transparent And Defensible

Grading should be transparent enough that a student can understand how their score was produced and defensible enough to withstand challenge.

Practices:

- publish grading criteria or rubrics in advance;
- grade anonymously where feasible to reduce bias;
- use multiple graders with calibration for large exams;
- document partial credit logic;
- return exams with feedback, not just scores;
- have a regrade process that is fair and consistent.

Opaque grading invites disputes and bias. Transparent grading builds trust and improves learning.

### Interpret Results Before Assigning Final Grades

Before converting scores to grades, examine the distribution. A result where everyone scores below 60 may indicate a problem with the exam, not the students.

Consider:

- the distribution shape and whether it discriminates;
- whether specific questions were universally missed, suggesting a wording or teaching problem;
- whether the mean is consistent with prior offerings;
- whether to curve, and if so, on what defensible basis;
- whether any group performed disproportionately poorly, suggesting bias.

Curving is a legitimate tool but should be principled, not a fix for a poorly designed exam. Examine causes before adjusting scores.

### Protect Integrity Without Undermining Fairness

Integrity measures protect the validity of scores but can themselves introduce unfairness if applied unevenly. Balance:

- clear rules about permitted materials and collaboration;
- proctoring that does not assume guilt or target groups;
- honor code references and institutional process for violations;
- question security that does not create anxiety;
- avoiding formats that make cheating easy without making the exam unfair.

Integrity measures that target particular students or assume dishonesty erode trust and can themselves be biased.

### Document Decisions For Challenge Response

For high-stakes exams, document the design rationale, blueprint, grading process, and any adjustments. This documentation supports responses to grade challenges, accreditation reviews, and student questions. It also helps future iterations of the course.

## Common Traps

### Treating Validity As Abstract

Validity is about whether the score supports the inferences drawn. Ignoring it produces exams that measure the wrong thing.

### Construct-Irrelevant Difficulty

Reading load, cultural references, and speed measure the wrong construct. Review for construct relevance.

### Unreliable Essay Grading

Grading without rubrics varies by grader and invites disputes. Use rubrics and calibrate.

### Invisible Bias

Bias is often invisible to the instructor. Use data and colleague review to surface it.

### Retrofitting Accessibility

Waiting for accommodation letters to address barriers delays access. Design accessibility from the start.

### Inventing Accommodations

Instructors cannot approve their own accommodations. Route through the official process.

### Opaque Grading

Grading without published criteria invites disputes and bias. Make grading transparent.

### Curving To Fix Bad Design

Curving a poorly designed exam hides the real problem. Examine causes before adjusting scores.

### Targeted Integrity Measures

Proctoring that targets groups erodes trust and introduces bias. Apply integrity measures evenly.

## Self-Check

- Are the inferences the score must support, grading, progression, certification, explicitly identified?
- Has each question been reviewed for construct validity, ensuring it measures the intended learning?
- Is reliability addressed through adequate sampling, clear wording, rubrics, and grader calibration?
- Has the exam been audited for bias across student groups using item-level data where available?
- Is accessibility designed in from the start rather than retrofitted after accommodation requests?
- Are accommodations routed through the official disability services process and implemented faithfully?
- Is grading transparent, with published criteria, anonymous grading where feasible, and a fair regrade process?
- Are results interpreted, distribution examined and anomalous items reviewed, before final grades are assigned?
- Are integrity measures applied evenly without targeting particular students or groups?
- Is the exam design, blueprint, grading process, and any adjustments documented for challenge response and future iteration?
- Could you defend the exam's fairness and validity to a student, a colleague, or an accreditation reviewer?
