# Scoring And Grade Decisions

Writing a fair exam is only half the work. The other half is translating a set of raw scores into grades that mean what they claim to mean, and doing so defensibly. This is where many exams fail, not in the questions but in the scoring logic. An exam can be well-written and still produce grades that are arbitrary, because the cutoffs were chosen by feel, the curve was applied to hide a bad distribution, partial credit was inconsistent, or a single ambiguous question shifted every student's grade. The decisions made after students put down their pens, how partial credit is awarded, how raw points become letter grades, whether and how to adjust scores, are the decisions students most often challenge, and they are the decisions most often made hastily at the end of a tired grading week.

Use this skill before finalizing exam scores, setting grade cutoffs, deciding on a curve or adjustment, interpreting item analysis, or responding to a grade appeal. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing grade decisions that are arbitrary, that reward or punish students for exam flaws, or that cannot be defended when challenged.

## Core Rules

### Separate Question Scoring From Grade Assignment

Conflating these two stages corrupts both. First, score each question as accurately as possible, awarding partial credit by a consistent scheme, so that the raw score reflects what the student actually demonstrated. Only after all raw scores exist should you consider how they map to grades. If you adjust the mapping because the raw scores look wrong, you are using the curve to fix a problem that may lie in the questions, and you will hide the real cause. Score honestly first, then decide what the scores mean.

### Define Partial Credit Logic Before Grading

For constructed-response questions, decide in advance how partial credit will be awarded. Will you give credit for correct setup even if the arithmetic fails? For a correct final answer reached by a flawed method? For a partially correct argument that reaches the right conclusion? Write the logic down and apply it consistently. Inconsistent partial credit is one of the largest sources of grade unfairness, because two students with equivalent understanding receive different scores depending on which grader read which question. For multi-grader exams, calibrate on sample responses so that the partial-credit scheme is applied the same way by everyone.

### Decide What The Raw Score Represents Before Choosing Cutoffs

Before setting grade boundaries, decide what a raw score of, say, 72 out of 100 actually means. Is it the percentage of points earned, a measure of mastery, or a rank relative to peers? These are different philosophies and they produce different cutoffs. Criterion-referenced grading sets cutoffs based on demonstrated competence, so that 80 percent means "met the standard" regardless of how many students reached it. Norm-referenced grading sets cutoffs based on the distribution, so that grades reflect relative standing. Most course grading is a mix, and the instructor should be conscious of which philosophy is driving each cutoff rather than drifting between them unconsciously.

### Set Cut Scores Deliberately, Not At Round Numbers

The most common cutoff error is defaulting to round-number boundaries, 90/80/70/60, as if they were natural. They are not; they are conveniences that often bear no relationship to what students actually demonstrated. A score of 79 and a score of 80 may be statistically indistinguishable, yet one receives a different grade. Examine the distribution for natural breaks, where gaps between scores suggest meaningful differences, and consider whether cutoffs should fall there. Where you must use predetermined percentage cutoffs, be aware that they assume the exam's difficulty was calibrated so that the percentages correspond to competence, which is rarely verified.

### Use The Angoff Or Modified Angoff Approach For High-Stakes Cutoffs

For exams with serious consequences, certification, licensure, progression gates, the cutoff should be set by a defensible standard-setting method rather than by instructor judgment alone. The Angoff method asks subject-matter experts to estimate, for each question, the probability that a minimally competent candidate would answer correctly, and aggregates these to a recommended cut score. Modified versions add review of actual performance data. These methods are not perfect, but they produce a documented rationale that can withstand challenge, unlike a cutoff chosen because it felt right.

### Interpret Item Statistics Before Trusting The Total Score

Before converting raw scores to grades, examine the items. Item analysis reveals questions that do not discriminate, that everyone missed, or that correlate negatively with total score, meaning stronger students did worse on them. Such items are likely flawed, and including them in the total distorts every student's grade. Common signals:

- a question that every student missed may be misworded or mistaught, not a fair measure of learning;
- a question with near-zero discrimination, where the top and bottom students answered equally well or poorly, contributes no information;
- a negative discrimination item, where stronger students did worse, is almost certainly flawed and should be reviewed or dropped.

Decide in advance how you will handle flawed items: rescore with the item removed, give everyone credit, or revise the key. Apply the rule consistently rather than choosing the option that happens to raise the mean.

### Examine The Distribution Before Curving

Curving is legitimate but dangerous. Before applying any adjustment, look at the distribution shape. Is it reasonably normal, bimodal, skewed, or compressed against the ceiling or floor? A distribution compressed at the bottom may indicate an exam that was too hard or mistimed, not students who failed to learn. A distribution with a long tail of very low scores may include students who never engaged, and curving to rescue them inflates grades dishonestly. Diagnose the cause before choosing the adjustment. A curve applied to hide a flawed exam harms the next cohort, because the flaw is never corrected.

### Choose A Defensible Curving Method

If adjustment is warranted, choose a method you can explain. Common approaches include:

- adding a fixed number of points to every score, justified when an item was flawed;
- setting the highest score to 100 and scaling the rest, justified when the exam was hard but the top students demonstrated mastery;
- mapping to a target distribution, justified only for explicitly norm-referenced grading;
- dropping or rescorcing flawed items, justified by item analysis.

Avoid methods chosen because they produce a desired mean. The mean is a result, not a target. Any adjustment should have a stated rationale tied to evidence about the exam, not to a preferred grade distribution.

### Document The Rationale For Every Adjustment

For each grade decision, record why it was made: the item analysis that led to dropping a question, the standard-setting method that produced the cutoff, the rationale for the curve. This documentation is what makes a grade defensible. When a student appeals, a documented rationale converts a confrontation into an explanation. When you teach the course again, the documentation tells you what to fix.

### Apply Adjustments Consistently And Transparently

Once an adjustment rule is set, apply it to all students identically. Do not make individual exceptions that undermine the consistency of the scheme, and do not apply hidden adjustments that students cannot see. Transparency about how raw scores became grades builds trust and reduces appeals, even when students dislike the result.

## Common Traps

### Curving To Hit A Desired Mean

Choosing an adjustment because it produces a target average hides exam flaws and has no defensible basis. The mean should be a result, not a goal.

### Round-Number Cutoffs By Default

Using 90/80/70/60 as if natural ignores the actual distribution and the meaning of scores. Examine where meaningful breaks occur.

### Inconsistent Partial Credit

Awarding partial credit by feel, varying by grader or by question, produces grades that depend on who read the work. Define and calibrate the scheme.

### Trusting Flawed Items In The Total

Including items that everyone missed or that discriminate negatively distorts every grade. Review item statistics before finalizing.

### Conflating Scoring And Grading

Adjusting the score-to-grade mapping to fix a problem that lies in the questions hides the real cause. Score honestly first, then interpret.

### Hidden Adjustments

Applying curves or dropped items invisibly erodes trust and invites appeals. Make the logic transparent.

### Individual Exceptions

Granting ad hoc adjustments to some students but not others undermines consistency and fairness. Apply rules uniformly.

### Undocumented Decisions

Grade decisions made without a recorded rationale cannot be defended on appeal and cannot inform future exam design. Document every adjustment.

## Self-Check

- Are question scoring and grade assignment handled as separate stages, with honest raw scoring preceding any cutoff decision?
- Is partial credit logic defined before grading and applied consistently, with calibration across multiple graders?
- Have you decided whether grading is criterion-referenced, norm-referenced, or mixed, and is that philosophy consciously driving the cutoffs?
- Are cut scores set by examining the distribution and natural breaks, or by a standard-setting method, rather than defaulting to round numbers?
- For high-stakes exams, is the cutoff set by a defensible method such as Angoff with a documented rationale?
- Have item statistics been reviewed, with flawed items identified and a consistent rescoring rule applied?
- Has the distribution been diagnosed for cause before any curve was applied, distinguishing exam flaws from student disengagement?
- Is the curving method, if used, one you can explain and justify with evidence rather than a desired mean?
- Is the rationale for every adjustment documented so the grade can be defended on appeal and used to improve the next exam?
- Are adjustments applied consistently and transparently to all students, with no hidden or ad hoc exceptions?
