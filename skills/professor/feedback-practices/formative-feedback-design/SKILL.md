# Formative Feedback Design

Feedback is the single most powerful influence on learning, when it is done right. Done wrong, it is ignored. The difference is design. Feedback that arrives after the grade, that justifies a score rather than guides revision, or that students cannot act on because the work is over, teaches students to look at the grade and discard the comments. Effective formative feedback arrives while students can still use it, targets specific and actionable improvements, and creates structures that require students to act on it. The goal of feedback is not to judge work but to change future work.

Use this skill before designing feedback systems, choosing timing and frequency, deciding comment-versus-grade balance, or diagnosing why feedback is not improving student work. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing feedback that is extensive but inert, or that ranks students rather than develops them.

## Core Rules

### Time Feedback So Students Can Act On It

Feedback is formative only if students can use it to improve. Feedback on a final paper, returned after the course ends, cannot be acted on and is effectively summative. Design feedback to arrive while revision is still possible.

Timing principles:

- return feedback before the next related assignment, not after;
- build in revision opportunities after feedback;
- use drafts and milestones so feedback informs a revision;
- for end-of-term work, connect feedback to a future course or portfolio.

If students cannot act on feedback, it is evaluation, not development.

### Separate Feedback From Grades Where Possible

When comments accompany a grade, students tend to read the grade and ignore the comments, because the grade has already settled the matter. Strategies to make feedback functional:

- give comments before the grade, on drafts;
- require a response to feedback before revealing the grade;
- use grade-less formative checkpoints;
- frame comments as guidance for the next work, not justification for the score.

The cognitive sequence matters: feedback processed before the grade is more likely to be used.

### Make Feedback Specific And Actionable

Generic feedback ("be more analytical," "unclear") tells students there is a problem but not how to fix it. Specific, actionable feedback names what to do differently.

Actionable feedback:

- identifies a specific instance in the work;
- explains why it falls short relative to a criterion;
- suggests a concrete next step or strategy;
- references a model or exemplar where useful;
- prioritizes one or two key improvements over exhaustive correction.

Students cannot revise based on "improve." They can revise based on "state your claim before presenting evidence, as in the model on page three."

### Prioritize Feedback To Avoid Overload

Exhaustive feedback, every error marked, overwhelms students and consumes instructor time. Prioritize the few changes that would most improve the work, and let smaller issues go or address them through resources.

Prioritization:

- focus on criteria tied to the learning outcome;
- address higher-order issues (argument, structure) before lower-order (mechanics);
- limit to two or three key points for the student to work on;
- use rubrics or guides so students can self-check smaller issues.

Two actionable comments beat fifty red marks.

### Target The Task, The Process, And Self-Regulation

Feedback can address three levels: the task (was this answer correct), the process (is the reasoning sound), and self-regulation (how is the student monitoring and adjusting their learning). Task feedback is necessary but insufficient. Process and self-regulation feedback develop transferable skill.

- task: this calculation is incorrect because of a sign error;
- process: your method assumes linearity where the system is nonlinear;
- self-regulation: you checked your work against the rubric before submitting; what would help you catch this kind of error next time.

Self-regulation feedback builds independent learners who no longer need your comments.

### Close The Feedback Loop

Feedback that is given but not used is wasted. Build structures that close the loop, requiring students to act on feedback:

- require a revision incorporating prior feedback;
- have students write a brief response to feedback before the next submission;
- use cover memos where students describe how they used prior feedback;
- build feedback portfolios that track recurring issues.

Closing the loop teaches students that feedback is for using, not just receiving.

### Use Multiple Sources Of Feedback

Instructor feedback is not the only source. Use peer feedback, self-assessment, automated feedback, and exemplars to expand feedback without overloading the instructor. Each source teaches different skills: peer feedback develops evaluation skill, self-assessment develops metacognition, exemplars calibrate quality.

Multiple sources also increase feedback frequency, which matters more than feedback volume.

### Balance Praise, Critique, And Guidance

Feedback that is all critique demoralizes; feedback that is all praise misleads. Balance:

- identify what is working, specifically, so it can be repeated;
- identify what needs improvement, specifically, so it can be changed;
- provide guidance on how to improve, concretely.

Empty praise ("good job") is not feedback because it does not guide. Specific recognition of what works is feedback because it shows the student what to keep doing.

### Make Criteria Visible So Feedback Makes Sense

Feedback lands when students understand the criteria it references. If the criteria are invisible, comments seem arbitrary. Use rubrics, exemplars, and explicit criteria so feedback connects to shared standards. Students who can see the target can use feedback to move toward it.

### Use Technology To Increase Feedback Efficiency

Technology can expand feedback capacity: comment banks for common issues, audio or video feedback for tone and efficiency, automated checks for mechanics, and learning analytics for patterns. Use technology to free instructor time for the high-value feedback that machines cannot give, not to replace human judgment.

## Common Traps

### Feedback After The Grade

Comments accompanying a final grade are read for the score, not used. Time feedback for action.

### Generic Comments

"Be clearer" cannot be acted on. Be specific and actionable.

### Exhaustive Correction

Marking every error overwhelms and wastes time. Prioritize.

### Task-Only Feedback

Correcting answers without addressing process or self-regulation limits transfer. Target all three levels.

### Open Loop

Feedback given but not required to be used is wasted. Close the loop.

### Instructor As Sole Source

Relying only on instructor feedback limits frequency and skill development. Use multiple sources.

### Empty Praise

"Good job" guides nothing. Recognize specifically what works.

### Invisible Criteria

Feedback without shared criteria seems arbitrary. Make criteria visible.

### Feedback Without Revision Opportunity

Comments on work that cannot be revised are evaluation. Build revision in.

### Volume Over Frequency

One massive comment dump matters less than frequent small feedback. Increase frequency.

## Self-Check

- Is feedback timed so students can act on it before the work is finalized?
- Is feedback separated from or sequenced before grades so students process the comments?
- Is feedback specific and actionable, naming what to do differently rather than only what is wrong?
- Is feedback prioritized to a few key improvements rather than exhaustively correcting every error?
- Does feedback address task, process, and self-regulation, not only correctness?
- Is the feedback loop closed through revision, response, or cover memos that require acting on feedback?
- Are multiple feedback sources used, including peer, self, automated, and exemplars?
- Is praise specific about what works, critique specific about what to change, and guidance concrete?
- Are criteria and rubrics visible so feedback connects to shared standards?
- Is technology used to increase feedback efficiency and frequency without replacing human judgment?
