# Peer And Self Assessment

Peer and self-assessment expand feedback beyond what an instructor alone can give, and they develop skills that instructor feedback cannot: the ability to evaluate work against criteria, to give and receive critique, and to monitor one's own learning. But these methods fail predictably when implemented naively. Peer review without training produces vague, unhelpful, or harmful comments. Self-assessment without structure produces inflated or defensive ratings. The value lies not in the activity itself but in the training, structure, and calibration that make student feedback useful.

Use this skill before designing peer review or self-assessment, training students to give feedback, or diagnosing why peer feedback is superficial. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing peer or self-assessment that wastes class time, produces unreliable feedback, or teaches students bad habits of evaluation.

## Core Rules

### Treat Peer And Self-Assessment As Skills To Be Taught

Students are not born able to give useful feedback or assess their own work accurately. Both are skills that must be taught, modeled, and practiced. Do not assume that assigning peer review produces good peer review.

Teach explicitly:

- what criteria define quality in this kind of work;
- how to read work against criteria rather than preference;
- how to give specific, actionable, respectful feedback;
- how to receive feedback without defensiveness;
- how to self-assess honestly rather than to justify.

Training is the difference between useful peer feedback and noise.

### Use Structured Protocols And Prompts

Unstructured peer review, "read your partner's paper and give feedback," produces vague comments. Structure the review with specific prompts and protocols that guide attention.

Structured approaches:

- provide a rubric or criteria checklist for reviewers to apply;
- use guiding questions tied to specific criteria;
- use protocols like "describe, evaluate, suggest" that separate observation from judgment;
- have reviewers locate specific passages rather than comment generally;
- require reviewers to prioritize two or three key points.

Structure converts vague goodwill into useful feedback.

### Calibrate Peer Evaluation Against Expert Standards

Students often misjudge quality, especially early. Calibrate peer evaluation by having students assess the same work the instructor assesses, then compare and discuss discrepancies. Calibration teaches students what quality looks like and improves the reliability of subsequent peer feedback.

Calibration practices:

- use anonymous sample papers of varying quality;
- have students apply the rubric, then reveal expert scores;
- discuss why judgments diverged;
- repeat periodically to maintain calibration.

Calibrated peers give feedback closer to what an expert would give.

### Use Self-Assessment To Develop Metacognition

Self-assessment's deepest value is metacognitive: it teaches students to monitor their own learning, a skill that transfers beyond any course. Design self-assessment to develop this skill, not just to collect a self-rating.

Metacognitive self-assessment:

- have students apply the rubric to their own work before submitting;
- ask what they would do differently and why;
- have them identify where they are confident and unsure;
- connect self-assessment to revision and goal-setting;
- track self-assessment accuracy over time.

Accurate self-assessment is a sign of mature learning; inflated or deflated self-assessment signals work to do.

### Protect Against Bias And Collusion In Peer Review

Peer review is vulnerable to bias: friends rate friends kindly, strangers harshly, and groups may collude to inflate each other. Mitigate:

- use anonymous review where feasible;
- randomize pairings rather than letting friends review each other;
- use multiple reviewers to average out individual bias;
- spot-check peer feedback quality and address patterns;
- weight peer feedback appropriately, not as the sole determinant.

Bias and collusion undermine fairness and teach students that feedback is gameable.

### Make Peer Feedback Matter To The Recipient

Students invest in peer review only if it matters. If peer feedback is collected and ignored, students learn to do it perfunctorily. Make it matter:

- require revision incorporating peer feedback;
- have recipients respond to feedback, noting what they used and why;
- include peer feedback quality as a small grade component;
- showcase how peer feedback improved specific work.

When peer feedback visibly changes work, students give better feedback.

### Grade The Quality Of Feedback, Not Just The Work

Peer review is itself a learning activity. Grade the quality of the feedback students give, not only the work they receive feedback on. This signals that giving good feedback is a skill worth developing and deters perfunctory reviews.

Feedback quality criteria:

- specific reference to criteria and passages;
- actionable suggestions for improvement;
- respectful and constructive tone;
- prioritized rather than exhaustive;
- accurate relative to standards.

### Use Self-And-Peer Together For Richer Feedback

Combining self-assessment and peer assessment creates richer feedback than either alone. Have students self-assess, then receive peer feedback, then compare the two. Discrepancies between self and peer ratings are instructive: they reveal blind spots and calibrate self-perception.

### Scaffold Complexity Across The Term

Peer and self-assessment skills develop over time. Start with structured, low-stakes activities early in the term, and increase complexity and stakes as students build competence. Early peer review might use a simple checklist; later review might involve substantive critique of argument and evidence.

### Use Technology To Manage Peer Review Logistics

Peer review logistics, assigning partners, collecting feedback, managing anonymity, can consume time. Use LMS peer-review tools or dedicated platforms to handle logistics so instructor and student time goes to the feedback itself.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Students Can Give Good Feedback

Peer review without training produces noise. Teach the skill.

### Unstructured Review

"Give feedback" produces vague comments. Use structured prompts and rubrics.

### No Calibration

Uncalibrated peers misjudge quality. Calibrate against expert standards.

### Self-Assessment As Justification

Self-rating to defend one's grade teaches nothing. Use it for metacognition.

### Bias And Collusion

Friends and groups distort peer ratings. Anonymize and use multiple reviewers.

### Feedback That Does Not Matter

Collected-and-ignored feedback teaches perfunctory review. Make it count.

### Grading Only The Work

Not grading feedback quality signals it does not matter. Grade the feedback.

### Self Without Peer Or Vice Versa

Either alone is weaker than the combination. Use both.

### Full Complexity Immediately

Demanding expert-level peer review from novices fails. Scaffold complexity.

### Logistics Consuming Time

Manual peer assignment wastes effort. Use technology.

## Self-Check

- Are peer and self-assessment treated as skills that must be taught, modeled, and practiced?
- Is review structured with rubrics, guiding questions, and protocols rather than left open-ended?
- Is peer evaluation calibrated against expert standards to improve reliability?
- Does self-assessment develop metacognition, not just collect a self-rating?
- Are bias and collusion mitigated through anonymity, randomization, and multiple reviewers?
- Does peer feedback visibly matter to recipients through revision and response requirements?
- Is the quality of feedback itself graded, not only the work being reviewed?
- Are self-assessment and peer assessment combined so discrepancies reveal blind spots?
- Is complexity scaffolded from low-stakes structured activities to more demanding review over the term?
- Is technology used to manage logistics so time goes to feedback rather than administration?
