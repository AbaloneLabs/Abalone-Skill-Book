# Research Program Planning

A research career is built not by single projects but by a coherent program: a sequence of connected projects that accumulate into a distinctive contribution to a field. Early-career researchers often pursue projects opportunistically, chasing what is fundable, publishable, or interesting in the moment, and end with a scattered record that does not add up to a recognizable profile. Mid-career researchers often find themselves spread across too many directions, unable to go deep in any. Research program planning is the deliberate work of choosing which projects to pursue, how they connect, what to decline, and how the whole trajectory builds toward a scholarly identity and impact.

Use this skill before planning a research program, sequencing projects, deciding what to pursue or decline, or diagnosing why a research record feels scattered. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a list of projects that are individually fine but collectively incoherent, or a plan so rigid it cannot adapt to findings and opportunities.

## Core Rules

### Define The Central Question Or Problem That Unifies The Program

A coherent program is organized around a central question, problem, or phenomenon that the individual projects illuminate from different angles. Identify:

- the big question your work addresses over years;
- the phenomena or mechanisms you study;
- the contribution you aim to make to the field;
- what would be true if your program succeeded.

The central question need not be fixed forever, but without one, projects do not accumulate. A program where each project starts from scratch produces breadth without depth.

### Sequence Projects So Earlier Work Enables Later Work

Programs build momentum when projects feed each other. Plan sequencing so that:

- early projects establish methods, data, or credibility;
- middle projects tackle the central question with increasing ambition;
- later projects synthesize, generalize, or transfer findings;
- each project produces assets, data, code, measures, relationships, used by later ones;
- dead ends inform rather than derail the next step.

A program where every project requires new infrastructure from scratch is inefficient. A program where projects compound is generative.

### Balance Depth And Breadth Deliberately

Depth establishes expertise and credibility; breadth prevents insularity and opens new opportunities. Decide deliberately:

- how much of the program stays in a core area to build depth;
- how much reaches into adjacent areas for breadth;
- when to consolidate depth versus when to expand;
- how breadth projects connect back to the core.

Defaulting entirely to depth produces a narrow profile vulnerable to field shifts; defaulting entirely to breadth produces a scattered record with no signature contribution.

### Align The Program With Career Stage And Constraints

A program must fit the researcher's stage, resources, and timeline. Consider:

- early career: establish a distinctive contribution, build a track record, position for tenure;
- mid-career: deepen, lead larger projects, mentor, and expand influence;
- resources: funding, time, collaborators, students, infrastructure;
- timeline: tenure clock, grant cycles, sabbatical opportunities;
- risk tolerance: how much to invest in high-risk projects versus reliable ones.

A program designed without regard to stage produces either overreach that cannot be completed or caution that never distinguishes the researcher.

### Decide What Not To Do

Program coherence depends as much on declining as on pursuing. Practices:

- define criteria for what fits the program;
- decline projects that do not connect to the central question;
- avoid being driven entirely by funding availability;
- resist the pull of fashionable topics that do not advance the agenda;
- protect time for the core by saying no to attractive distractions.

A researcher who pursues every opportunity ends with no signature. Coherence requires selectivity.

### Build In Methodological And Theoretical Coherence

Programs cohere not only by topic but by method and theory. Decide:

- whether the program will develop or apply a distinctive method;
- whether it will advance a theoretical position across projects;
- how methodological and theoretical choices connect projects;
- where to invest in new methods versus leverage existing ones.

A program with a distinctive method or theoretical lens is more recognizable and influential than one defined only by topic.

### Plan For Outputs Beyond Single Papers

A mature program produces more than individual papers. Plan for:

- synthetic works that integrate the program's findings;
- books or monographs where appropriate;
- datasets, tools, or measures that others can use;
- public-facing work that translates findings;
- edited volumes or special issues that shape a conversation;
- training the next generation of researchers in the area.

Outputs that compound, datasets and tools that others build on, extend the program's influence beyond citation counts.

### Anticipate How The Field Will Evolve

Programs exist in moving fields. Anticipate:

- which directions the field is moving toward;
- which questions are likely to become central;
- which methods are emerging that you should adopt or develop;
- where your contribution will remain distinctive as the field shifts;
- when to lead a new direction versus join an existing one.

A program that ignores field evolution can find its contribution overtaken; one that chases every trend loses coherence. Position deliberately.

### Build Capacity Through People And Infrastructure

A program scales through people. Plan for:

- recruiting students and postdocs whose work advances the program;
- building collaborations that extend the program's reach;
- developing infrastructure, labs, datasets, codebases, that persist;
- training successors who carry the work forward.

A program that depends entirely on the principal investigator does not scale. One that builds capacity outlasts individual projects.

### Allow For Pivot Without Losing Coherence

Research produces surprises. Build in capacity to pivot:

- identify findings that would change the program's direction;
- maintain enough flexibility to follow important surprises;
- distinguish a productive pivot from a loss of focus;
- restate the central question when it evolves.

A program so rigid it cannot incorporate findings becomes brittle. One so flexible it has no center becomes scattered. Aim for coherence with adaptability.

## Common Traps

### Opportunistic Project Pursuit

Chasing fundable or fashionable projects without connection produces a scattered record. Organize around a central question.

### Starting From Scratch Each Project

Projects that share no infrastructure are inefficient. Sequence so work compounds.

### Defaulting To Breadth

Pursuing many areas prevents a signature contribution. Balance depth and breadth deliberately.

### Ignoring Career Stage

A program that does not fit tenure clocks or resources cannot be executed. Align with stage.

### Inability To Decline

Pursuing every opportunity produces no coherence. Decide what not to do.

### Topic-Only Coherence

A program defined only by topic, without method or theory, is less distinctive. Build methodological and theoretical coherence.

### Single-Paper Outputs

A program measured only by individual papers misses compounding outputs. Plan datasets, tools, and syntheses.

### Ignoring Field Evolution

A program that does not anticipate field shifts can be overtaken. Position deliberately.

### Solo Dependence

A program that depends only on the PI does not scale. Build capacity through people.

### Rigidity Or Drift

A program too rigid to pivot becomes brittle; one too flexible loses coherence. Balance both.

## Self-Check

- Is there a central question or problem that unifies the program across projects?
- Are projects sequenced so earlier work enables later work through shared assets and findings?
- Is the balance of depth and breadth chosen deliberately rather than by default?
- Does the program fit the researcher's career stage, resources, timeline, and risk tolerance?
- Are there clear criteria for what to decline to protect program coherence?
- Does the program have methodological or theoretical coherence, not only topical coherence?
- Are there planned outputs beyond single papers, syntheses, tools, datasets, public work?
- Has the likely evolution of the field been considered in positioning the program?
- Does the program build capacity through students, collaborators, and infrastructure that persist?
- Is there capacity to pivot on important findings without losing the central coherence?
- In five years, would a colleague be able to describe your distinctive contribution to the field?
