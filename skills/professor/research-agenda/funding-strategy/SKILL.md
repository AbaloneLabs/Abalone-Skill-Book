# Funding Strategy

Research funding is both enabling and distorting. The right funding at the right time accelerates a research program, supports students and infrastructure, and provides validation that opens further opportunities. The wrong funding strategy, chasing every grant, over-promising to funders, letting fundable questions displace important ones, or failing to plan for gaps, distorts the research agenda, burns time on unsuccessful applications, and creates dependency that compromises scholarly independence. A sustainable funding strategy aligns funding pursuits with the research program, sequences applications realistically, and maintains work that funders will not support but the field needs.

Use this skill before planning funding pursuits, choosing among grant opportunities, sequencing applications, managing a funding gap, or diagnosing why funding efforts are not producing a sustainable trajectory. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a funding strategy that is busy but incoherent, or that subordinates the research agenda to funder priorities.

## Core Rules

### Align Funding Pursuits With The Research Program

Funding should serve the research program, not define it. Practices:

- pursue funding for projects that advance the central question;
- decline funding that would pull the program off course, even if available;
- distinguish core work that needs funding from work that can proceed without it;
- ensure funded projects connect to the program's trajectory;
- avoid letting funder priorities set the research agenda.

A program shaped entirely by what is fundable loses its scholarly coherence. Align funding to the program, and where the program cannot be funded, find other ways to sustain it.

### Diversify Funding Sources To Reduce Dependency

Dependency on a single funder creates vulnerability and can compromise independence. Diversify across:

- federal or national research councils;
- foundations and private funders;
- institutional internal grants;
- industry partnerships where appropriate and conflict-managed;
- collaborative and consortium funding;
- fellowships and career awards.

Diversification protects against the loss of any single source and reduces pressure to conform to one funder's priorities. It also hedges against shifting political and budgetary environments.

### Sequence Applications Realistically

Grant applications are time-intensive and compete with research and teaching. Sequence them:

- do not apply for everything available;
- prioritize applications by fit, likelihood, and strategic value;
- stagger deadlines to avoid simultaneous heavy loads;
- build in time to revise and resubmit, since first submissions often fail;
- balance high-stakes applications with smaller, more likely ones;
- protect research time during application periods.

Over-application produces many weak submissions and exhausted researchers. Strategic sequencing produces fewer, stronger applications.

### Build A Pipeline Rather Than Reacting To Deadlines

Sustainable funding comes from a pipeline of applications at different stages, not from reacting to each deadline as it arrives. Build a pipeline by:

- maintaining a rolling list of target funders and opportunities;
- drafting applications ahead of deadlines;
- planning resubmissions for declined proposals;
- cultivating funder relationships between applications;
- tracking success rates and adjusting strategy.

A reactive strategy produces rushed, weak applications. A pipeline strategy produces prepared, competitive ones.

### Manage The Funding Gap Proactively

Most researchers face funding gaps between grants. Plan for them:

- maintain bridge funding or institutional support where possible;
- sequence grant end dates to avoid simultaneous gaps;
- identify work that can proceed without external funding;
- plan for reduced capacity during gaps rather than pretending they will not happen;
- communicate with students and staff about funding timelines honestly.

A gap that surprises everyone produces crises, layoffs, and stalled projects. A gap that is planned for produces a managed contraction.

### Balance Fundable And Unfundable Work

Some important work is not fundable by traditional sources: theoretical work, critical scholarship, replication, negative results, work in unfashionable areas. Sustain it by:

- protecting time for unfundable work alongside funded projects;
- using smaller grants, fellowships, or institutional support for it;
- collaborating to share the load;
- advocating for the value of such work in evaluations;
- not letting fundable work crowd out everything else.

A program that does only fundable work may be financially sustainable but scholarly impoverished. Deliberately sustain the unfundable work that matters.

### Understand Funder Priorities And Fit

Each funder has priorities, review cultures, and success patterns. Understand them:

- read funded proposals and successful awards where available;
- attend funder informational events;
- talk to program officers, who often provide valuable guidance;
- assess whether your work fits the funder's mission before applying;
- tailor the proposal to the funder's language and priorities without distorting the work.

Applying without understanding fit wastes effort. Fit assessment before application improves success and saves time.

### Protect Scholarly Independence From Funder Pressure

Funding can come with explicit or implicit pressure that compromises independence. Protect it by:

- reading sponsorship and grant terms before accepting;
- resisting restrictions on publication, data sharing, or topic choice;
- disclosing and managing conflicts of interest;
- declining funding whose terms compromise integrity;
- maintaining work the funder does not control.

Independence lost for funding is hard to recover. The short-term gain rarely justifies long-term constraint, especially where integrity is at stake.

### Use Funding To Build Capacity, Not Just Outputs

Funding's long-term value is in capacity: students trained, infrastructure built, collaborations formed. Plan funding to:

- support students and postdocs whose work advances the program;
- build datasets, tools, and methods that persist beyond the grant;
- establish collaborations that outlast the funding;
- create infrastructure that future projects leverage.

Funding spent only on immediate outputs produces no compounding return. Funding spent on capacity extends the program's reach.

### Evaluate And Adjust The Strategy Over Time

Track the funding strategy's performance:

- success rates by funder and project type;
- time spent on applications versus funded work;
- whether funded projects advanced the program;
- whether the strategy is sustainable across cycles;
- whether unfundable work is being sustained.

Adjust based on evidence. A strategy that produces low returns or distorts the program should be revised, not repeated out of habit.

## Common Traps

### Chasing Every Grant

Applying for everything available produces weak submissions and exhaustion. Prioritize by fit and strategy.

### Single-Funder Dependency

Relying on one funder creates vulnerability and conformity pressure. Diversify sources.

### Reactive Deadline Chasing

Rushing applications at deadlines produces rushed work. Build a pipeline.

### Ignoring Funding Gaps

Pretending gaps will not happen produces crises. Plan for them proactively.

### Fundable Work Crowding Out Important Work

Doing only what is fundable impoverishes the program. Sustain unfundable work deliberately.

### Applying Without Fit Assessment

Applying where the work does not fit wastes effort. Understand funder priorities first.

### Accepting Compromising Terms

Funding whose terms restrict independence or integrity costs more than it gives. Protect independence.

### Outputs Over Capacity

Funding spent only on immediate papers produces no compounding return. Build capacity.

### Static Strategy

Repeating a low-return strategy out of habit wastes effort. Evaluate and adjust.

### Over-Promising To Funders

Promising more than can be delivered to win funding creates later credibility problems. Promise realistically.

## Self-Check

- Do funding pursuits serve the research program rather than define it?
- Are funding sources diversified to reduce dependency on any single funder?
- Are applications sequenced realistically to avoid overload and allow revision?
- Is there a pipeline of applications at different stages rather than reactive deadline-chasing?
- Are funding gaps planned for with bridge support and honest communication?
- Is unfundable but important work deliberately sustained alongside funded projects?
- Is funder fit assessed before application, with program officer contact where useful?
- Are funding terms reviewed to protect scholarly independence and integrity?
- Does funding build capacity, students, infrastructure, collaborations, not just immediate outputs?
- Is the funding strategy evaluated by success rates and program alignment and adjusted over time?
- Could you sustain the core of your research program if your largest funder disappeared?
