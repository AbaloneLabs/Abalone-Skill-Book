# Dissemination Plan

Research that is not disseminated does not contribute to the field, and research that is disseminated poorly, to the wrong audience, at the wrong time, or without the framing that makes it intelligible, fails to have the impact it could. Dissemination is often treated as the final mechanical step after the research is done, but it is a strategic activity that shapes which conversations the work enters, who builds on it, how the researcher's profile develops, and whether the findings reach the audiences who need them. A dissemination plan made deliberately aligns outputs with the research program, sequences publications to build a trajectory, and balances specialist depth with broader reach.

Use this skill before planning dissemination, choosing venues, sequencing publications, or diagnosing why strong work is not having impact. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a dissemination approach that is haphazard, that targets the wrong audiences, or that fails to connect individual outputs into a recognizable program.

## Core Rules

### Align Dissemination With The Research Program

Each output should serve the program's trajectory, not just exist as a line on a CV. Practices:

- identify which conversations the work should enter;
- choose venues that reach the audience that matters for that conversation;
- sequence outputs so earlier work positions later work;
- frame each output to connect to the program's central question;
- avoid scattering outputs across unrelated venues with no thread.

A dissemination record where each paper appears in a different subfield with no connecting logic produces breadth without recognition. Aligned dissemination builds a recognizable profile.

### Match Venue To Audience And Purpose

Different venues serve different purposes. Choose deliberately:

- top field journals reach specialists and signal quality;
- generalist or interdisciplinary journals reach across fields;
- conferences reach active researchers and build networks, especially early career;
- edited volumes position work within a specific conversation;
- public-facing venues reach practitioners, policymakers, or the public;
- datasets, tools, and preprints reach builders and accelerate uptake.

Submitting everything to the highest-prestige journal regardless of fit wastes time and misroutes the work. Match venue to the audience whose engagement you want.

### Sequence Publications To Build A Trajectory

The order of publication shapes how the work is received. Plan sequencing:

- lead with a foundational or methodological paper that establishes the approach;
- follow with empirical papers that build on it;
- synthesize with integrative work that connects the findings;
- position review or theoretical papers at inflection points;
- avoid burying the program's signature contribution in an obscure venue.

A trajectory where the strongest work appears first and synthesis follows builds recognition. A trajectory where key papers are scattered or delayed fails to accumulate.

### Balance Specialist Depth With Broader Reach

Specialist depth establishes credibility; broader reach extends influence. Balance both:

- maintain a core of specialist outputs for the home field;
- periodically produce cross-field or generalist work to extend reach;
- translate findings for adjacent fields where they are relevant;
- use public-facing work to reach practitioners and publics where appropriate;
- avoid sacrificing depth for visibility or visibility for depth.

A program with only specialist depth remains insular; one with only broad reach lacks credibility. Deliberately cultivate both.

### Plan For Timing And Currency

Timing affects impact. Consider:

- whether the field is ready for the finding;
- whether related work is emerging that you should engage;
- whether delaying risks being scooped or becoming irrelevant;
- whether conference presentation before journal submission is strategic;
- whether preprints accelerate uptake or compromise review.

Some work benefits from being early; some from being timely alongside a wave. Match timing to the work's role in the conversation.

### Use Multiple Formats To Reach Different Audiences

A single finding can be disseminated in multiple formats to reach different audiences:

- the full research paper for specialists;
- a methods or dataset paper for builders;
- a conference talk for active researchers;
- a review or perspective piece for the broader field;
- a policy brief or op-ed for practitioners and publics;
- a blog or social media post for rapid engagement.

Multiple formats are not self-plagiarism if framed and disclosed properly; they are strategic translation to different audiences.

### Build Visibility Through Engagement, Not Just Publication

Visibility comes from engagement, not only from appearing in print. Build it by:

- presenting at conferences and inviting discussion;
- engaging with citing and related work;
- participating in the conversations your work enters;
- responding to critiques constructively;
- collaborating with groups that extend reach;
- mentoring students who carry the work forward.

A paper published and ignored has less impact than one published and actively placed into conversation. Engagement multiplies dissemination.

### Manage Open Access And Sharing Strategically

Open access and data sharing affect reach and integrity. Decide deliberately:

- whether open access fits the work and the audience;
- how to comply with funder and institutional open access policies;
- whether to share data, code, and materials, and under what license;
- how preprints interact with journal policies;
- how to make work discoverable to those who need it.

Open practices can dramatically extend reach, especially for work relevant to lower-resourced institutions and countries. They also support integrity and reproducibility.

### Plan For Impact Beyond Citations

Citations are one measure of impact but not the only one. Plan for and track:

- adoption of methods, datasets, or tools;
- influence on policy or practice;
- uptake in teaching and curricula;
- engagement by publics and media;
- contributions to the field's direction.

A dissemination plan optimized only for citations may miss higher-impact paths. Define impact broadly and pursue the forms that matter for the work.

### Adapt The Plan To Findings And Field Response

Dissemination plans should adapt. If a finding provokes strong response, plan follow-ups. If a venue rejects work, reconsider audience fit. If the field shifts, reposition. A rigid plan misses opportunities; an adaptive plan capitalizes on how the work is received.

## Common Traps

### Dissemination As Afterthought

Treating dissemination as the mechanical final step wastes the work's potential. Plan it strategically.

### Venue Chasing Prestige

Submitting only to the highest-prestige venue regardless of fit misroutes work. Match venue to audience.

### Scattered Outputs

Papers across unrelated venues with no thread produce no recognition. Align dissemination with the program.

### Depth Or Reach Only

Specialist-only work stays insular; reach-only work lacks credibility. Balance both.

### Ignoring Timing

Publishing too early or too late relative to the field's readiness reduces impact. Time deliberately.

### Single-Format Dissemination

Using only the research paper misses audiences that need other formats. Translate across formats.

### Publish And Ignore

Failing to engage after publication limits impact. Build visibility through engagement.

### Closed Access By Default

Restricting access without reason limits reach, especially to lower-resourced audiences. Manage openness strategically.

### Citation-Only Impact

Optimizing only for citations misses higher-impact paths. Define impact broadly.

### Rigid Planning

A plan that cannot adapt to response misses opportunities. Adapt to findings and field reception.

## Self-Check

- Does each output serve the research program's trajectory and central question?
- Is the venue chosen for audience and purpose rather than prestige alone?
- Are publications sequenced to build a trajectory rather than scattered?
- Is there a deliberate balance of specialist depth and broader reach?
- Has timing been considered relative to field readiness and emerging work?
- Are multiple formats used to reach different audiences for the same finding?
- Is visibility built through engagement, not just publication?
- Are open access and sharing decisions made strategically to extend reach?
- Is impact defined beyond citations to include adoption, policy, teaching, and public engagement?
- Does the plan adapt to findings, field response, and emerging opportunities?
- Would the right audiences know about and be able to build on your work?
