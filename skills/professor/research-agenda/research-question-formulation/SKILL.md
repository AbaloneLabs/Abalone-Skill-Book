# Research Question Formulation

The research question is the single most consequential decision in any scholarly project, and it is the one researchers most often get wrong. A vague question produces vague work; a question already answered produces redundant work; a question that cannot be investigated with available methods produces stalled work; a question chosen to seem impressive rather than to sustain interest produces abandoned work. Yet researchers routinely begin projects from topics, methods, datasets, or funding opportunities, and only belatedly discover that they never formulated a question that could carry the work. Good question formulation is a discipline of moving from diffuse interest through specific puzzle to a researchable claim, testing the question against novelty, significance, and feasibility before committing years to it. The quality of everything downstream, design, method, analysis, and contribution, is bounded by the quality of the question.

Use this skill before formulating or revising a research question, evaluating whether a question is well-posed, converting a topic into a question, assessing novelty and significance, or diagnosing why a project is producing thin or derivative work. The goal is to prevent the agent from endorsing questions that are too broad, too narrow, already answered, unfalsifiable, or chosen for the wrong reasons, and from treating the question as a formality rather than the foundation of the project.

## Core Rules

### Move Deliberately From Topic To Question To Claim

Most researchers begin with a topic, a broad area of interest, and mistake it for a question. A topic is necessary but insufficient. Move through the arc deliberately:

- topic: a broad area, such as "inequality and education";
- question: a specific puzzle within the topic, such as "how does school funding policy affect within-school tracking decisions";
- claim: a tentative answer the research will test or develop, such as "decentralized funding shifts tracking decisions from policy to teacher discretion";
- contribution: what the answer adds to existing scholarship.

A project that stalls at the topic stage produces a survey rather than a contribution. A project that reaches a claim and contribution is researchable.

### Test Whether The Question Is Genuinely Researchable

A fascinating question that cannot be investigated is not yet a viable research question. Test researchability:

- can the necessary evidence be obtained, observed, measured, or accessed;
- is there a method that can address the question;
- are the key terms defined well enough to operationalize;
- can the question be addressed within the available time and resources;
- are there ethical or access barriers that prevent investigation.

A question that fails these tests may be philosophically interesting but is not yet researchable. Reformulate until it can be investigated, or set it aside.

### Ensure The Question Implies A Defensible Method

A well-formed question carries within it the shape of the method that can answer it. Examine the inferential logic the question demands:

- causal questions require designs that can support causal inference;
- interpretive questions require designs that can support interpretation;
- comparative questions require comparable cases or data;
- descriptive questions require representative observation;
- normative questions require philosophical or ethical analysis.

A question whose logic mismatches the available method produces work that cannot bear the claim. Align question and method, and if they cannot align, revise one or the other.

### Assess Novelty Against The Actual Literature

A question is novel only relative to what has already been answered. Assess novelty honestly:

- conduct a real literature search, not a casual one;
- identify the closest existing works and how the proposed question differs;
- confirm the gap is genuine, not already filled;
- distinguish genuine novelty from incremental reshuffling;
- recognize that replication, extension, and methodological transfer are legitimate if framed honestly.

Assuming novelty without searching produces derivative work. Position the question against the real literature.

### Distinguish Significant From Merely Interesting

A question can be novel and interesting without being significant. Significance asks what changes if the question is answered:

- does it resolve a live debate in the field;
- does it open new directions for research;
- does it challenge existing assumptions;
- does it matter beyond the immediate case;
- does it connect to larger theoretical or practical problems.

A question whose answer changes nothing is low-significance regardless of its cleverness. Weight significance alongside novelty.

### Avoid Questions That Predetermine The Answer

Some questions are constructed so that only one answer is possible, which makes them unfalsifiable and uninteresting. Watch for:

- leading formulations that embed the desired conclusion;
- questions so narrow that the answer is trivially determined;
- questions that cannot be disconfirmed by any evidence;
- questions whose terms are defined to guarantee the result.

A question worth asking is one whose answer could surprise you. If you already know the answer, it is a demonstration, not research.

### Test Whether The Question Can Sustain The Work

A research question must carry a project through years of work. Test its sustaining power:

- does it remain interesting after the initial enthusiasm fades;
- does it generate sub-questions and follow-ups;
- does it connect to a larger research agenda;
- can it survive the inevitable pivots and surprises;
- would you still want to work on it in difficult conditions.

A question that cannot sustain interest through the difficult middle phase of research will be abandoned. Choose questions with depth.

### Calibrate Scope To The Project's Constraints

Scope failures, too broad or too narrow, are the most common formulation problem. Calibrate:

- too broad: a question that would require a book series or a multi-decade project;
- too narrow: a question whose answer is trivial or of interest to almost no one;
- the right scope is answerable within the project's constraints and significant enough to matter.

A useful test: can the contribution be stated in one or two sentences? If it requires a paragraph of qualifications, the scope is likely still off.

### Examine The Motives Behind The Question

Questions are chosen for reasons, and the reasons shape whether the work succeeds. Examine:

- is the question chosen for intrinsic interest or instrumental advantage;
- is it chosen to seem impressive, to please an advisor, or to fit a funding opportunity;
- is it chosen because the data is convenient rather than because the question matters;
- does the question connect to what the researcher genuinely cares about.

Questions chosen for the wrong reasons often collapse when the work gets hard. Choose questions that can sustain genuine commitment.

### Refine The Question Iteratively As The Work Develops

A research question is rarely final at the start. Refine it iteratively:

- revisit the question after the literature review;
- revise it after pilot work reveals what is actually investigable;
- sharpen it as the method and data clarify what claim is supportable;
- restate it when findings surprise and the original framing no longer fits.

Treating the question as fixed prevents the work from improving; treating it as perpetually open prevents progress. Refine deliberately.

## Common Traps

### Topic Mistaken For Question

Beginning from a broad area without formulating a specific question produces a survey, not research. Move to a question and claim.

### Unresearchable Question

A fascinating question that cannot be investigated with available evidence or methods is not viable. Test researchability.

### Method-Question Mismatch

A question whose inferential logic the available method cannot support produces indefensible claims. Align question and method.

### Assumed Novelty

Assuming a question is novel without searching the literature produces derivative work. Position against real literature.

### Clever But Insignificant

A novel, interesting question whose answer changes nothing is low-value. Weight significance.

### Predetermined Answer

A question constructed so only one answer is possible is unfalsifiable and uninteresting. Allow surprise.

### Unsustainable Question

A question that cannot sustain interest through difficulty will be abandoned. Choose depth.

### Scope Miscalibration

A question too broad cannot be completed; one too narrow is trivial. Calibrate scope.

### Wrong Motives

A question chosen to impress, please, or fit funding rather than for intrinsic significance collapses under pressure. Examine motives.

### Fixed Question

Treating the question as immutable prevents the work from improving. Refine iteratively.

## Self-Check

- Has the project moved from a broad topic to a specific question with a tentative claim and contribution?
- Is the question genuinely researchable, with obtainable evidence and a viable method within constraints?
- Does the question's inferential logic match a method that can support the claim it implies?
- Has novelty been assessed against a real literature search, with the closest works identified?
- Is the question significant, such that its answer would matter to the field, and not merely interesting?
- Is the question constructed so that evidence could disconfirm it, rather than predetermining the answer?
- Can the question sustain interest and generate follow-ups across the project's full duration?
- Is the scope calibrated, answerable within constraints and significant enough to matter, with a one-to-two-sentence contribution?
- Is the question chosen for intrinsic significance rather than instrumental advantage or convenience?
- Is the question treated as refinable through literature review, pilot work, and findings rather than fixed at the start?
