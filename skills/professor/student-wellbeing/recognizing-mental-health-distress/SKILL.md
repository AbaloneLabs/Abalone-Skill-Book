# Recognizing Mental Health Distress

Students experience high rates of anxiety, depression, burnout, and more serious mental health conditions, and these often manifest first in the academic setting — as missed deadlines, withdrawal, deteriorating work, or changed demeanor. Professors are not clinicians and should not diagnose, but they are often the first to notice that something is wrong, and their response can be the difference between a student getting help and a crisis deepening. The failure mode is interpreting every sign of struggle as a character or motivation problem: the student who stops coming to class is "lazy," the student whose work deteriorates is "not trying," the student who seems flat is "disengaged." These readings miss distress that is visible if looked for, and they compound the student's suffering with shame and isolation. Recognizing distress is not therapy; it is noticing, naming, and referring with care.

Use this skill when a student's behavior or performance changes, when deciding whether to reach out, when managing a conversation about wellbeing, and when determining the right referral. The goal is to prevent the agent from misreading distress as disengagement or from oversteering into a clinical role the professor is not qualified to fill.

## Core Rules

### Treat Sudden Changes In Behavior Or Performance As A Signal

Distress rarely announces itself; it shows up as change. A student whose work was strong and suddenly deteriorates, who stops attending, who becomes uncharacteristically withdrawn or agitated, who misses multiple deadlines after being reliable, or whose writing takes on a dark or hopeless tone is signaling. The signal is the *change*, not any single behavior. A student who has always been quiet is not necessarily in distress; a student who has become suddenly isolated may be.

### Know The Common Presentations Of Distress

Distress manifests differently across people and conditions. Common presentations include:

- academic: missed deadlines, deteriorating quality, stopped attending, inability to concentrate;
- social: withdrawal from peers, dropping activities, isolation;
- emotional: flatness, tearfulness, irritability, hopelessness, expressions of being a burden;
- physical: visible exhaustion, weight change, neglect of self-care;
- direct: statements of despair, wanting to disappear, or suicidal ideation — always take these seriously.

You are looking for patterns and changes, not diagnosing conditions.

### Reach Out Early And Privately

When you notice a change, reach out — early, privately, and without accusation. A brief, caring message naming what you've observed ("I've noticed you've missed the last two deadlines and haven't been in class; I wanted to check in") opens a door without forcing it. Frame concern, not judgment. Many students in distress feel invisible; being noticed by someone who matters can be the first step toward help.

### Listen Without Trying To Fix Or Diagnose

When a student responds, listen more than you speak. Resist the impulse to solve the problem, minimize it ("everyone gets stressed"), or diagnose it. Your role is to convey that you hear them, that you care, and that help exists. Reflective listening — "That sounds really hard; thank you for telling me" — is more useful than advice. Most students do not need you to be their therapist; they need you to be a caring professor who points them to one.

### Know Your Referral Pathways Cold

You cannot refer well to resources you don't know. Know your institution's counseling center, urgent mental health services, dean of students office, crisis line, and after-hours options. Know what is confidential, what is not, and what the wait times typically are. When you refer, be specific and warm: "The counseling center is at X; they have same-day urgent appointments. Would it help if I walked you over, or if we looked at the website together?" A vague "you should get help" rarely results in help.

### Distinguish Distress From Disability And From Ordinary Stress

Not every struggle is a mental health crisis. Ordinary academic stress, grief, and life transitions are real but not clinical. Disabilities — including mental health disabilities — may require accommodations through the disability services office rather than (or in addition to) counseling. Suicidal ideation, psychosis, severe depression, and trauma responses are clinical and require professional care. Calibrate your response to what you're seeing, and when in doubt, consult the counseling center or dean of students about how to proceed.

### Take Any Mention Of Suicide Or Self-Harm Seriously

Any reference to suicide, self-harm, or wanting to disappear — even framed as a joke or offhand — must be taken seriously. Do not keep this confidential out of respect for the student's privacy; the obligation to safety overrides. Connect the student to crisis resources immediately, and follow your institution's protocol, which typically involves the counseling center, dean of students, or campus emergency services. It is always better to err toward action; a student may be angry in the short term and alive in the long term.

### Follow Up And Sustain Care

A single conversation is rarely enough. Follow up with the student — a brief check-in a week or two later signals that your concern was real and that they are not alone. Continue to hold normal academic expectations where possible, while accommodating documented needs. Sustained, caring attention from even one stable adult can be a significant protective factor.

## Common Traps

- **Reading distress as character failure.** The most common and damaging error: interpreting the symptoms of a mental health crisis as laziness, disengagement, or incompetence.
- **Waiting for certainty before reaching out.** You do not need a diagnosis to express care. Waiting until you're "sure" often means waiting until a crisis.
- **Minimizing to be comforting.** "Everyone gets stressed" or "it'll pass" dismisses real distress and shuts down disclosure.
- **Trying to be the therapist.** Professors who attempt clinical roles overstep their competence, blur boundaries, and can harm the student. Refer.
- **Not knowing the resources.** A referral to "the counseling center, somewhere on campus" usually fails. Know the specifics.
- **Keeping suicide risk confidential.** The instinct to respect privacy can be lethal when self-harm is involved. Safety overrides confidentiality.
- **One-and-done.** A single conversation without follow-up leaves the student feeling briefly noticed and then abandoned. Sustain the attention.
- **Ignoring your own limits.** Supporting students in distress is taxing. Know your own boundaries and use your institution's resources and colleagues for support.

## Self-Check

- When a student's behavior or performance changes suddenly, do I treat the change as a possible signal of distress rather than a character judgment?
- Am I familiar with the common presentations of distress — academic, social, emotional, physical, and direct — and looking for patterns, not single behaviors?
- Do I reach out early and privately, framing concern rather than accusation?
- When a student discloses, do I listen and reflect rather than minimize, fix, or diagnose?
- Do I know my institution's counseling, crisis, and dean-of-students resources specifically enough to make a warm, concrete referral?
- Can I distinguish ordinary stress, disability, and clinical distress, and route each appropriately?
- If a student mentions suicide or self-harm, do I act immediately and follow institutional protocol rather than keeping it confidential?
- Do I follow up after the initial conversation to sustain the student's sense of being seen and supported?
