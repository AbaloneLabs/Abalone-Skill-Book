# Supporting Students In Crisis And Referral

A student in acute crisis — suicidal ideation, a mental health emergency, an assault disclosure, a sudden loss, a psychotic break, or a threat to self or others — is a high-stakes moment where the professor's response matters enormously and where the wrong instinct can cause harm. The failure modes are two: some professors freeze or minimize, hoping the crisis resolves itself or someone else handles it; others overstep, attempting to manage a clinical or legal situation they are not equipped for. The professor's role in crisis is narrow but vital: ensure immediate safety, connect the student to the people and systems equipped to help, and sustain a caring presence without becoming the responder. Doing this well requires knowing the protocols before the crisis arrives, because in the moment there is no time to look them up.

Use this skill when a student is in or near crisis, when deciding how to respond and whom to involve, when managing confidentiality and safety, and when reviewing whether a crisis response was adequate. The goal is to prevent the agent from freezing, overstepping, or mishandling a moment where the right action is time-sensitive and consequential.

## Core Rules

### Know The Crisis Protocol Before You Need It

In a crisis, you will not have time to research. Before any crisis, know:

- the 24/7 crisis line for your campus and community;
- how to reach the counseling center's urgent services and after-hours line;
- the dean of students office and its emergency contact;
- campus security or emergency response numbers;
- the Title IX office (for assault and harassment disclosures);
- the basic institutional protocol for a student who may harm themselves or others.

Keep these accessible. A professor who fumbles for resources in a crisis loses critical minutes.

### Assess Immediate Safety First

The first question is always safety. Is the student safe right now? Are they with someone? Do they have the means and intent to harm themselves or others? If there is any indication of imminent risk, do not leave the student alone; contact crisis services or emergency response immediately. Academic concerns — the missed exam, the failing grade — are irrelevant until safety is established. Calibrate the urgency of your response to the immediacy of the risk.

### Make Warm, Active Referrals

A referral is only as good as the handoff. "You should call the counseling center" rarely results in a call. A warm referral — sitting with the student while they call, walking them to the office, offering to make the first contact together, or in acute cases calling crisis services with them present — dramatically increases the chance the student actually receives help. In the moment, lower every barrier you can.

### Understand The Limits Of Confidentiality

Students often disclose in confidence and ask you to keep it there. Confidentiality is important but not absolute. It does not extend to situations involving risk of harm to the student or others. Be honest about this: "I care about what you've told me, and I want to respect your privacy. Because I'm worried about your safety, I need to involve people who can help — let's talk about how to do that in a way that feels as safe as possible." Betraying a confidence to protect a life is the right call; doing so deceptively damages trust.

### Use The Dean Of Students As Your Partner

The dean of students office exists to coordinate student support across academic and non-academic domains. They can follow up with a student you're worried about, connect them to resources, coordinate with faculty across courses, and manage institutional responses. You do not have to carry a worried-about-a-student situation alone; a referral to the dean of students is often the right move, especially when the concern is ongoing rather than acute.

### Handle Disclosures Of Assault Or Harassment Correctly

If a student discloses sexual assault, harassment, or related misconduct, your response has legal and institutional dimensions. Know whether you are a mandatory reporter under your institution's Title IX policy (most employees are). If you are, tell the student this before they disclose, so they can choose what to share. Listen without investigation, believe the student, and connect them to the Title IX office and supportive resources. Do not attempt to resolve the matter yourself.

### Do Not Attempt Clinical Or Investigative Roles

You are not the student's therapist, investigator, or emergency clinician. Attempting these roles oversteps your competence, blurs boundaries, and can harm the student and expose you and the institution to risk. Your role is to notice, care, ensure safety, and refer. When you find yourself wanting to do more, that signal usually means you should involve someone whose role it is to do more.

### Document And Report Per Institutional Requirements

For significant concerns, follow your institution's documentation and reporting expectations. This may involve a care or concern report to the dean of students, a Title IX report, or a counseling center referral. Documentation protects the student (by ensuring continuity of care) and protects you. Keep records factual and non-speculative.

### Take Care Of Yourself Afterward

Supporting a student in crisis is emotionally taxing, and secondary trauma is real. After a crisis, debrief with a trusted colleague, use your institution's employee assistance resources, and recognize your own limits. A professor who absorbs crisis after crisis without support will eventually burn out or become unable to respond well.

## Common Traps

- **Freezing or minimizing.** Hoping the situation resolves itself or "isn't that serious" delays action when minutes matter.
- **Overstepping into clinical or investigative roles.** Professors who try to be therapists or investigators harm students and expose themselves and the institution to risk.
- **Cold referrals.** "Call this number" without active handoff rarely results in the student receiving help. Make the referral warm.
- **Promising confidentiality you can't keep.** Agreeing to keep a disclosure secret, then facing a safety obligation, damages trust and can endanger the student.
- **Not knowing the protocol.** Fumbling for resources during a crisis loses time. Know the protocol before you need it.
- **Going it alone.** Carrying a worried-about-a-student situation without involving the dean of students leaves you without support and the student without coordinated care.
- **Mishandling assault disclosures.** Investigating, minimizing, or failing to report (when required) harms the student and violates policy and law.
- **Ignoring your own wellbeing.** Absorbing crisis after crisis without support leads to burnout and impaired future responses.

## Self-Check

- Do I know — and have accessible — the crisis line, counseling urgent services, dean of students contact, security, and Title IX office for my institution?
- In a crisis, do I assess immediate safety first, before any academic consideration?
- Do I make warm, active referrals — sitting with the student, walking them over, calling together — rather than handing out a phone number?
- Am I honest with students about the limits of confidentiality, especially when safety is at stake?
- Do I use the dean of students as a partner for ongoing concern, rather than carrying it alone?
- For disclosures of assault or harassment, do I know my reporting obligations and connect the student to Title IX resources without investigating myself?
- Am I staying within my role — noticing, caring, ensuring safety, referring — rather than attempting clinical or investigative work?
- Do I document per institutional requirements and take care of my own wellbeing after a crisis?
