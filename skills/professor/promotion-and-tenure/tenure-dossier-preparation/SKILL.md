# Tenure Dossier Preparation

The tenure and promotion dossier is the central document in the most consequential personnel decision of an academic career. It assembles evidence of research, teaching, and service into a record that external reviewers, departmental committees, college committees, and administrators use to judge whether a candidate meets the standards for tenure or promotion. Dossiers fail not usually because the candidate is unqualified, but because the case is poorly made: evidence is missing or buried, the narrative is defensive or vague, external letters are mismanaged, and the record does not speak to the specific criteria and standards the institution applies. A well-prepared dossier makes a strong case visible and easy to evaluate; a poorly prepared one obscures a strong record and creates risk.

Use this skill before preparing a dossier, writing a candidate statement, assembling evidence, managing external review, or supporting a colleague's case. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a dossier that under-presents a strong record, that mishandles external letters or evidence, or that fails to speak to the institution's actual criteria.

## Core Rules

### Know The Criteria, Standards, And Process

The dossier must speak to the institution's actual standards. Understand:

- the formal criteria for tenure or promotion in the unit and institution;
- the relative weight of research, teaching, and service;
- the expected trajectory and level of achievement at the decision point;
- the timeline, milestones, and required components;
- the bodies that review and their distinct concerns;
- the role of external review and how letters are used.

A dossier written without knowing the criteria is written blind. Start by studying the standards and process.

### Build The Case Around The Criteria

Organize the dossier so the case maps to the criteria. Practices:

- structure the narrative around the review categories, research, teaching, service;
- within each, address the specific criteria the unit applies;
- make the trajectory and level of achievement explicit;
- connect evidence to claims rather than asserting them;
- anticipate the questions reviewers will ask.

Reviewers evaluating many dossiers look for the case to be made, not inferred. Build it around the criteria explicitly.

### Assemble Comprehensive And Well-Organized Evidence

Evidence is the foundation. Assemble it comprehensively:

- research: publications, grants, presentations, creative work, with context on venue quality and contribution;
- teaching: course evaluations, peer reviews, syllabi, teaching materials, student outcomes, advising;
- service: committee work, leadership, professional contributions, with substance not just membership;
- contextualize evidence where reviewers need it, e.g., acceptance rates, journal rankings, course loads;
- avoid burying key evidence in volume.

Reviewers cannot evaluate what they cannot find or understand. Curate and contextualize evidence.

### Write A Strong Candidate Statement

The candidate statement is where the case is made in the candidate's voice. Write it to:

- present an integrated scholarly narrative, not a list of accomplishments;
- explain the research agenda, its significance, trajectory, and future direction;
- articulate a teaching philosophy and evidence of effectiveness;
- describe service contributions and their impact;
- address challenges honestly and frame them constructively;
- connect research, teaching, and service where they genuinely integrate.

A statement that is a list is a missed opportunity; a statement that weaves a compelling narrative of contribution and trajectory makes the case memorable and credible.

### Present Research With Significance And Trajectory

Research cases succeed when significance and trajectory are clear. Present:

- the central questions and why they matter;
- the methods and contributions of the work;
- the quality and impact of venues, with context reviewers need;
- the trajectory from dissertation to current work to future direction;
- external recognition, citations, invitations, awards;
- collaboration and independence, especially relative to the field's norms.

Reviewers, especially external, judge significance and trajectory. Make these explicit rather than assuming they are evident.

### Present Teaching With Evidence And Development

Teaching cases succeed when effectiveness and development are evidenced. Present:

- quantitative and qualitative evidence of student learning and satisfaction;
- peer review and teaching awards;
- course development and curricular innovation;
- range across course levels and formats;
- mentoring and advising outcomes;
- a trajectory of improvement and investment in teaching.

Teaching presented without evidence reads as perfunctory. Build the teaching case with the same seriousness as research.

### Present Service With Substance And Impact

Service cases succeed when contributions are substantive, not titular. Present:

- the work done, not just the committees joined;
- leadership and impact where present;
- external professional service, reviewing, editing, leadership;
- the relevance of service to the institution and profession;
- a trajectory of increasing responsibility.

Service presented as a list of memberships reads as thin. Describe substance and impact.

### Manage External Review Strategically

External letters are often decisive. Manage them strategically:

- identify reviewers who are senior, credible, and able to evaluate the work;
- provide a complete and well-organized packet;
- frame the questions to elicit evaluation against standards;
- avoid arm's-length violations while ensuring credibility;
- anticipate and contextualize the letters in the case.

Poorly chosen reviewers or poorly prepared packets produce letters that are weak, hostile, or unhelpful. Manage external review as a strategic part of the case.

### Address Weaknesses Honestly

Every record has weaknesses. Address them:

- explain gaps or slow periods with context;
- frame shifts in direction as development, not drift;
- acknowledge where standards are still being met;
- avoid defensiveness or blame;
- show how weaknesses are being addressed.

Weaknesses left unaddressed are noticed and filled in unfavorably by reviewers. Honest, constructive framing builds credibility.

### Follow Format And Procedural Requirements

Dossiers have strict format and procedural requirements. Follow them:

- required sections and their order;
- page limits and document limits;
- formatting and submission systems;
- deadlines at each stage;
- confidentiality and disclosure rules.

Procedural failures delay or derail cases. Follow requirements exactly.

### Support Colleagues Ethically

When supporting a colleague's case, do so ethically:

- write letters that are honest and specific;
- evaluate against criteria, not friendship;
- disclose relationships and conflicts;
- respect confidentiality of the process;
- avoid advocacy that overstates or misrepresents.

Letters that overstate lose credibility and damage the writer's standing. Honest, specific support is most valuable.

### Prepare For Each Review Level

The dossier is reviewed at multiple levels. Prepare for each:

- department committee knows the field and the candidate;
- college committee may be less field-specific;
- university committee and administration apply institutional standards;
- external reviewers apply disciplinary standards.

A case that works only at the department level may fail higher. Ensure the case speaks to each audience.

## Common Traps

### Ignorance Of Criteria

Writing without knowing standards produces a blind case. Study criteria first.

### Asserting Rather Than Evidencing

Claims without evidence do not persuade. Connect claims to evidence.

### Buried Evidence

Volume that hides key achievements weakens the case. Curate and contextualize.

### List-Style Statement

A statement that is an accomplishment list misses the narrative opportunity. Weave a compelling case.

### Uncontextualized Research

Research without significance, venue quality, or trajectory context is hard to evaluate. Make these explicit.

### Thin Teaching Case

Teaching without evidence reads as perfunctory. Build it with evidence and development.

### Titular Service

Service as membership lists reads as thin. Describe substance and impact.

### Mismanaged External Review

Poor reviewer choice or packet preparation produces weak letters. Manage strategically.

### Hidden Weaknesses

Weaknesses left unaddressed are filled in unfavorably. Address honestly.

### Procedural Failure

Format or deadline violations delay or derail cases. Follow requirements exactly.

### Overstated Support Letters

Letters that overstate damage credibility. Write honest, specific support.

### Single-Audience Case

A case that works only at department level may fail higher. Speak to each review level.

## Self-Check

- Do you know the institution's criteria, standards, weights, timeline, and process for the decision?
- Is the dossier organized to map explicitly to the review criteria?
- Is evidence comprehensive, well-organized, and contextualized for reviewers who need it?
- Does the candidate statement present an integrated narrative of research, teaching, and service?
- Is research presented with significance, venue quality, trajectory, and external recognition explicit?
- Is teaching presented with quantitative and qualitative evidence, development, and range?
- Is service presented with substance and impact rather than titular lists?
- Is external review managed with credible reviewers and well-prepared packets?
- Are weaknesses addressed honestly and constructively rather than hidden or defensive?
- Are format, page limits, deadlines, and procedural requirements followed exactly?
- When supporting colleagues, are letters honest, specific, and free of overstatement?
- Does the case speak to department, college, university, and external audiences?
- Could a skeptical reviewer find the case clear, evidenced, and addressing the criteria at each level?
