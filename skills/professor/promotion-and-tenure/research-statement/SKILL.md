# Research Statement

The research statement articulates a faculty member's research agenda for audiences who must judge its significance, feasibility, and trajectory. It is central to job applications, tenure and promotion, grants, and awards, and it is the document where a record of work becomes a coherent program with a future. Weak statements are lists of papers or projects with no integrating vision, no articulation of why the work matters, and no credible future direction, leaving reviewers unable to see the agenda as a whole or to invest in it. A strong statement presents a compelling research vision, grounds it in past contribution, and projects a feasible and significant future, positioning the work for the audience and decision at hand.

Use this skill before writing a research statement, articulating a research agenda, describing past and future work, or positioning research for review. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a statement that is a publication list rather than a vision, that overclaims or underclaims, or that fails to speak to the audience and decision.

## Core Rules

### Articulate A Coherent Research Vision

The statement's core is a vision of the research agenda. Articulate it:

- the central problem or question that drives your work;
- why it matters, to the field and beyond;
- the approach or perspective that distinguishes your contribution;
- how your work connects into a coherent program, not discrete projects;
- the significance and potential impact of the agenda.

A vision integrates discrete projects into a program reviewers can understand and invest in. Without it, the statement is a list.

### Ground The Vision In Past Contribution

The future vision must be credible based on the past. Ground it:

- summarize prior work as the foundation for the agenda;
- highlight key contributions and their impact;
- show the trajectory from dissertation to present;
- connect past work to future direction logically;
- provide evidence of productivity and recognition.

Reviewers invest in futures built on demonstrated capability. Show that the vision is grounded in a track record.

### Project A Feasible And Significant Future

The statement must look forward credibly. Project:

- specific research questions or projects for the near and medium term;
- why these are the right next steps given the agenda;
- the methods and resources required;
- the expected contributions and impact;
- how the future builds on and extends the past.

Vague futures, "I will continue to do important research," are unconvincing. Specific, feasible, significant futures show direction and capability.

### Make Significance Explicit

Reviewers outside your subfield need significance made explicit. Practices:

- explain why the problem matters, not assume it is self-evident;
- describe the contribution relative to the state of the field;
- use accessible language for non-specialist reviewers;
- provide context on the field's direction and your place in it;
- connect to broader scientific, societal, or disciplinary significance.

Significance assumed is significance missed. Make it explicit for the audiences who will read the statement.

### Tailor To The Audience And Purpose

Statements serve different purposes. Tailor:

- job applications emphasize vision, fit, and potential;
- tenure review emphasizes achievement and trajectory;
- grants emphasize the specific project and feasibility;
- awards emphasize distinction and impact.

A statement that serves all purposes serves none well. Adapt emphasis, length, and framing to the decision at hand.

### Show Fit Where Relevant

For job applications and some grants, fit matters. Show it:

- connect your agenda to the institution's strengths and priorities;
- identify potential collaborators and synergies;
- show how you complement rather than duplicate existing work;
- address resources and environment the institution offers;
- articulate what you bring that is distinctive.

Fit shown generically is forgettable; fit shown specifically demonstrates research and genuine engagement.

### Present Methods And Feasibility Credibly

Reviewers judge whether the work can be done. Present:

- the methods and approaches you use and why;
- evidence of capability, prior results, preliminary work;
- resources required and access to them;
- awareness of challenges and how you address them;
- a realistic scope and timeline.

Overclaiming feasibility or under-specifying methods raises doubts. Credible presentation builds confidence.

### Address Independence And Collaboration Appropriately

The statement should convey your role in the work. Address:

- your distinctive contribution relative to collaborators;
- independence, especially for early-career scholars, from advisors;
- the role of collaboration in advancing the agenda;
- leadership of projects and teams where relevant;
- the balance appropriate to your field and stage.

Fields differ in collaboration norms; present your role in a way that is accurate to the field and compelling to reviewers.

### Use Accessible And Precise Language

Statements are read by specialists and non-specialists. Balance:

- precise enough to demonstrate expertise;
- accessible enough for reviewers outside the subfield;
- free of unnecessary jargon and acronyms;
- well-structured with clear flow;
- appropriately concise.

Statements that are too technical lose non-specialists; those too general lose specialists. Calibrate for the mixed audience.

### Connect Research To Teaching And Service Where Genuine

Where the connection is genuine, articulate it:

- how research informs teaching;
- how students are involved in research;
- how the agenda connects to institutional or societal goals;
- service that advances the research community.

Forced connections read as contrived; genuine connections show integration and broaden the case.

### Reflect And Revise

Strong statements are revised. Practices:

- seek feedback from mentors and peers;
- revise for clarity, significance, and feasibility;
- update as the agenda evolves;
- tailor for each application or review;
- ensure the statement is current and accurate.

A statement is a living document. Treat it as an argument that is refined over time.

### Be Honest About Scope And Limits

Credible statements acknowledge limits. Practices:

- avoid overclaiming impact or certainty;
- acknowledge challenges and open questions;
- present preliminary results as preliminary;
- scope the future realistically;
- avoid presenting every project as transformative.

Overclaiming strains credibility; honest scoping builds trust and makes genuine significance stand out.

## Common Traps

### List Of Papers

A statement that is a publication list has no integrating vision. Articulate the agenda.

### No Future Direction

A statement only about the past has no agenda to invest in. Project a feasible future.

### Significance Assumed

Assuming reviewers see significance leaves it unsaid. Make it explicit.

### Generic Across Purposes

One statement for all purposes serves none. Tailor to the decision.

### Generic Fit

Generic fit claims are forgettable. Show specific synergies.

### Overclaimed Feasibility

Claims without methods, evidence, or resources raise doubts. Present credibility.

### Unclear Role

Failing to distinguish your contribution from collaborators is ambiguous. Clarify your role.

### Inaccessible Or Over-Technical

Too technical loses non-specialists; too general loses specialists. Calibrate language.

### Forced Connections

Contrived links to teaching or service read as inauthentic. Connect only where genuine.

### Static Statement

A statement never revised decays and misses the current agenda. Reflect and revise.

### Overclaiming

Presenting everything as transformative strains credibility. Scope honestly.

### Ignoring The Field's Direction

Failing to position work relative to the field's trajectory misses significance. Contextualize.

## Self-Check

- Does the statement articulate a coherent research vision that integrates your projects into a program?
- Is the vision grounded in past contribution with evidence of productivity and recognition?
- Does it project a specific, feasible, and significant future direction?
- Is significance made explicit for reviewers, including those outside the subfield?
- Is the statement tailored to its purpose, job, tenure, grant, award?
- Where relevant, is fit with the institution shown specifically rather than generically?
- Are methods, feasibility, resources, and challenges presented credibly?
- Is your distinctive role and independence clear relative to collaborators?
- Is the language calibrated for a mixed audience of specialists and non-specialists?
- Are connections to teaching and service genuine rather than forced?
- Has the statement been revised with feedback and kept current?
- Is the scope honest, avoiding overclaiming while conveying genuine significance?
- Could a reviewer describe your agenda, its significance, and its future after reading this statement?
