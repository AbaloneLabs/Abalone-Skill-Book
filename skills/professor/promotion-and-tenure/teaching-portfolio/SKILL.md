# Teaching Portfolio

The teaching portfolio documents and makes the case for a faculty member's teaching effectiveness and contribution. It is used in tenure and promotion, job applications, teaching awards, and program review, and it is the primary way teaching, often undervalued relative to research, is made visible and evaluable. Weak portfolios are collections of evaluations and syllabi with no narrative, no evidence of learning, and no development, which leaves reviewers unable to distinguish strong teaching from perfunctory performance. A strong portfolio presents a coherent teaching philosophy, evidence of student learning, development over time, and impact on courses and curricula, making the case that teaching is a serious, scholarly contribution.

Use this skill before building a teaching portfolio, writing a teaching philosophy, documenting teaching effectiveness, or preparing teaching evidence for review. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing a portfolio that is a document dump rather than a case, that relies on evaluations alone, or that fails to demonstrate learning and development.

## Core Rules

### Build The Portfolio As A Case, Not A Collection

A portfolio is an argument that teaching is effective and consequential. Build it as a case:

- a teaching philosophy that frames the approach;
- evidence of student learning and engagement;
- documentation of course and curriculum development;
- evidence of teaching improvement over time;
- impact on students, courses, and the program;
- reflection that shows scholarly engagement with teaching.

A collection of documents asserts teaching happened; a case demonstrates it was effective and developed.

### Write A Teaching Philosophy That Is Specific And Operational

The teaching philosophy is the portfolio's spine. Write it to:

- articulate your beliefs about how students learn in your field;
- connect beliefs to specific practices you use;
- show how philosophy evolves with experience and evidence;
- avoid generic statements that could belong to anyone;
- ground claims in your actual courses and students.

Generic philosophies, "I believe in student-centered learning," are forgettable and indistinguishable. Specific, operational philosophies show thoughtfulness and connect to evidence.

### Use Multiple Forms Of Evidence

Teaching effectiveness is multi-dimensional; use multiple evidence forms:

- student evaluations, quantitative and qualitative, with context;
- peer reviews of teaching, formative and summative;
- direct measures of student learning, work samples, pre-post measures, capstone performance;
- teaching materials that show design, syllabi, assignments, activities;
- evidence of innovation and experimentation;
- awards, grants, and recognition for teaching.

No single measure, especially evaluations alone, is sufficient. Triangulate across forms to build a credible case.

### Contextualize Student Evaluations

Evaluations are widely used but widely misinterpreted. Contextualize them:

- report trends over time, not single points;
- compare to course, department, and institution norms;
- note course characteristics that affect evaluations, level, size, required vs elective;
- attend to qualitative comments for specific strengths and weaknesses;
- acknowledge known biases in evaluations by student characteristics and field.

Presenting raw evaluations without context invites misinterpretation. Contextualized evaluations are more credible and honest.

### Document Student Learning Directly

The strongest evidence is direct measures of learning. Document:

- examples of student work showing development;
- pre-post or longitudinal evidence of capability gains;
- performance on capstone or integrative assessments;
- outcomes, where students go after the course;
- how you assess and use learning evidence to improve.

Learning evidence moves the case from "students liked the course" to "students learned," which is the actual goal of teaching.

### Show Course And Curriculum Development

Teaching contribution includes what you build. Document:

- new courses designed and their rationale;
- revision of existing courses and why;
- curriculum development at the program level;
- materials and methods you created and shared;
- the adoption of your work by colleagues.

Development shows teaching as scholarly contribution, not just delivery. It is often undervalued and under-documented.

### Demonstrate Improvement Over Time

A trajectory of improvement is compelling. Demonstrate it:

- show how courses evolved in response to evidence;
- document professional development in teaching;
- show response to peer review and feedback;
- reflect on what did not work and how you changed it;
- connect improvement to outcomes.

Static teaching, even good teaching, is less compelling than teaching that visibly develops. Improvement shows investment and reflection.

### Address Range And Diversity Of Teaching

Teaching across contexts demonstrates breadth. Document:

- range of course levels, introductory to advanced;
- range of formats, lecture, seminar, lab, online;
- range of student populations and needs;
- advising and mentoring as part of teaching;
- teaching for majors, non-majors, and graduate students.

A portfolio showing only one course type undersells the contribution. Show range where it exists.

### Include Reflection And Scholarship Of Teaching

Reflection elevates a portfolio. Include:

- reflection on what works and why;
- engagement with the scholarship of teaching and learning;
- experimentation and its results;
- presentations and publications on teaching where relevant;
- connection of teaching to disciplinary and institutional goals.

Reflection shows teaching as intellectual work, not just performance. It distinguishes scholarly teaching from routine delivery.

### Curate For The Audience And Purpose

Portfolios serve different purposes. Curate accordingly:

- tenure and promotion emphasize evidence and impact;
- job applications emphasize philosophy and potential;
- teaching awards emphasize distinction and innovation;
- program review emphasizes contribution to the program.

A single generic portfolio underserves each purpose. Tailor emphasis while maintaining the core evidence.

### Keep The Portfolio Current

A portfolio built once decays. Maintain it:

- update evidence each term;
- add new courses and developments;
- refresh the philosophy as you evolve;
- keep materials organized for quick assembly.

A current portfolio is ready when needed and captures development that is otherwise lost. Build the habit of ongoing documentation.

### Be Honest About Challenges

Honest portfolios are more credible. Address challenges:

- acknowledge courses or terms that went less well;
- describe how you diagnosed and addressed problems;
- show that you take critical feedback seriously;
- avoid presenting a flawless record that strains belief.

A portfolio that admits no weakness reads as defensive or inflated. Honest engagement with challenges builds credibility.

## Common Traps

### Document Dump

A collection without narrative asserts teaching happened but not that it was effective. Build a case.

### Generic Philosophy

Philosophy that could belong to anyone is forgettable. Be specific and operational.

### Evaluations Alone

Relying on evaluations alone is insufficient and misinterpretable. Use multiple evidence forms.

### Uncontextualized Evaluations

Raw evaluations invite misinterpretation. Contextualize with trends, norms, and course characteristics.

### No Learning Evidence

Without direct learning measures, the case rests on satisfaction. Document learning.

### Development Undocumented

Course and curriculum contribution is often invisible if not documented. Show what you built.

### Static Presentation

Teaching shown as unchanging undersells investment. Demonstrate improvement.

### Narrow Range

Showing only one course type undersells contribution. Document range.

### No Reflection

A portfolio without reflection reads as routine delivery. Include scholarly engagement.

### Generic Across Purposes

One portfolio for all purposes underserves each. Curate for audience and purpose.

### Stale Portfolio

A portfolio built once decays and misses development. Keep it current.

### Inflated Record

A flawless presentation strains credibility. Be honest about challenges.

## Self-Check

- Is the portfolio built as a case with a narrative, or a collection of documents?
- Is the teaching philosophy specific, operational, and grounded in your actual practice?
- Does the portfolio use multiple forms of evidence beyond student evaluations?
- Are student evaluations contextualized with trends, norms, and course characteristics?
- Is there direct evidence of student learning, not just satisfaction?
- Is course and curriculum development documented with rationale and impact?
- Does the portfolio demonstrate improvement and development over time?
- Does it show range across course levels, formats, and student populations?
- Does it include reflection and engagement with the scholarship of teaching?
- Is the portfolio curated for its specific purpose, review, application, award?
- Is the portfolio current, with ongoing documentation rather than one-time assembly?
- Is the record honest about challenges and responsive to feedback?
- Could a reviewer distinguish your teaching as effective, developed, and scholarly from this portfolio?
