# Peer Review Ethics And Confidentiality

Peer review is a position of trust. A reviewer sees unpublished work before the rest of the field, and that access carries obligations that are easy to violate through carelessness rather than malice. Manuscripts are confidential; ideas belong to authors until published; and a reviewer's first duty is to the integrity of the record, not to personal advantage. The failure mode is treating review as a routine favor or a line on a CV while quietly absorbing the author's ideas, sharing the manuscript for "help," or steering decisions toward friends and rivals. These failures are rarely dramatic — they accumulate as small conveniences that erode the system.

Use this skill before accepting a review, while reading the manuscript, when conflicts arise, and when deciding what to tell editors. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing reviews that are technically competent but ethically compromised, or from declining reviews that should have been declined to protect the process.

## Core Rules

### Treat The Manuscript As Strictly Confidential

An unpublished manuscript is privileged information. The author has shared it with you, through the editor, for one purpose: evaluation. You may not use it, circulate it, or discuss it beyond that purpose.

- Do not share the manuscript with students, postdocs, collaborators, or colleagues, even to "check a calculation," unless the journal explicitly permits it and you disclose it to the editor.
- Do not use the author's ideas, data, methods, or findings in your own work before publication.
- Do not discuss the work at conferences, in lab meetings, or on social media in ways that could identify it.
- Store the manuscript securely; delete it after the review is complete unless retention is required.
- If you need expert input on a specific point, ask the editor first, not a colleague directly.

### Declare Conflicts Of Interest Before Accepting

A conflict of interest is any relationship or interest that could bias your judgment or create the appearance of bias. The standard is not whether you *feel* biased but whether a reasonable observer would question your impartiality. Declare proactively; let the editor decide.

Common conflicts include:

- recent collaboration or co-authorship with any author (typically within 3-5 years);
- direct supervision or mentorship relationships;
- financial interests in the work's success or a competitor's failure;
- personal or family relationships;
- ongoing disputes or adversarial interactions;
- intellectual or financial competition where the manuscript's outcome affects you.

When in doubt, declare. An over-declaration costs nothing; a hidden conflict that surfaces later damages your reputation and the record.

### Review What Was Submitted, Not What You Would Have Done

A common ethical drift is reviewing the manuscript the author *should* have written rather than the one submitted. This produces reviews that demand a different study, reject methodological choices that are defensible, or penalize scope decisions. Evaluate the work on its own aims and stated scope. You may note that a different design would have been stronger, but the core judgment must be whether the submitted work meets the standard for its claims.

### Protect Author Identity And Dignity

Even in double-blind or single-blind review, you may infer authorship. Do not act on that inference. Do not write reviews that are personally dismissive, sarcastic, or cruel. Critique the work, not the people. A review that humiliates authors is an ethical failure regardless of its technical accuracy, and it disproportionately harms early-career and underrepresented scholars who have less insulation against hostile review.

### Be Honest With Editors, Especially In Decline

If you cannot review fairly, cannot meet the deadline, or lack the expertise for part of the work, tell the editor immediately. Declining late, accepting and then ghosting, or reviewing outside your competence harms the author and the journal. Editors prefer an early, honest decline to a late or compromised review.

## Common Traps

- **The "I'll just show one colleague" shortcut.** Confidentiality is not scalable by good intentions. One shared manuscript can cascade; the colleague may share further. If you need help, route it through the editor.
- **Using the idea before publication.** Reviewers sometimes internalize an unpublished method or framing and use it, genuinely believing they thought of it. Exposure creates prior art in your mind. Keep rigorous separation and document your own independent development if a conflict arises.
- **The friendly review.** Reviewing friends' work leniently, or declining to appear harsh, corrupts the record. If you cannot be rigorous with a friend, decline.
- **The hostile competitor review.** The mirror failure: using review to delay or damage a competitor. This is research misconduct, not sharp practice.
- **Inferring authorship and adjusting accordingly.** Recognizing a lab's style and then softening or harshening the review is bias, even when unconscious. Review the manuscript as written.
- **Reviewing outside expertise and hiding it.** Reviewers sometimes accept to appear knowledgeable, then give shallow reviews on the parts they don't understand. Editors cannot calibrate the review if they don't know the limits.
- **Venting frustration in the review.** Reviewing is unpaid labor, and frustration can leak into tone. Hostile reviews are an ethical problem, not just a style problem.
- **Retaining manuscripts indefinitely.** Keeping manuscripts "just in case" extends privilege beyond the review and risks inadvertent use.

## Self-Check

- Have I stored the manuscript securely and avoided sharing it with anyone not authorized by the editor?
- Did I declare every conflict of interest — collaborative, financial, personal, competitive — before or upon accepting, and let the editor decide?
- Am I reviewing the submitted work on its own aims, rather than the study I wish the authors had conducted?
- Is my tone professional and focused on the work, with no personally dismissive or humiliating language?
- Did I tell the editor honestly about deadline risk, expertise gaps, or inability to review fairly, rather than accepting and underperforming?
- Have I avoided using the author's ideas, data, or framing in my own work before publication?
- Will I delete or securely archive the manuscript per journal policy once the review is complete?
- If I inferred authorship, did I consciously set that inference aside and judge the manuscript as written?
