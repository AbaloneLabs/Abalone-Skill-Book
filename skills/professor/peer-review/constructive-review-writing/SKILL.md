# Constructive Review Writing

A peer review is a piece of professional writing with real consequences. A vague or poorly structured review wastes the editor's time, frustrates the author, and can delay or kill work that deserved better. A precise, well-organized review helps the editor decide, helps the author improve, and strengthens the record — even when the recommendation is rejection. The failure mode is writing a review that is a stream of impressions: a few scattered criticisms, a recommendation pulled from intuition, and no structure that lets the editor act. Good reviewing is a craft, and like any craft it has a shape.

Use this skill before and while drafting a review, when deciding on a recommendation, and when revising in response to author rebuttals. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing reviews that are technically competent in content but structurally useless to the people who rely on them.

## Core Rules

### Separate Major From Minor, And Be Explicit About Which Is Which

Editors triage reviews by severity. A review that mixes fatal flaws with typo corrections forces the editor to guess which matters. Organize your review so the editor and author can immediately see what is decisive.

A strong structure:

1. A brief summary of the paper's contribution in your own words (2-4 sentences). This proves you read it and helps the editor confirm scope.
2. An overall assessment: the significance, soundness, and presentation, with your recommendation stated clearly.
3. Major comments — the issues that must be addressed for the work to be sound or publishable, numbered for easy reference.
4. Minor comments — clarifications, references, typos — grouped separately and clearly labeled.

### Tie Every Major Critique To A Specific Consequence

"Unclear," "unconvincing," and "weak" are useless without explanation. For each major point, state what the problem is, why it matters, and what would resolve it. A useful test: could the author fix this point without guessing? If not, the comment is not actionable.

- Bad: "The methodology is unconvincing."
- Better: "The sample size of n=12 is underpowered to detect the effect size claimed in the abstract (you would need roughly n=45 for 80% power at alpha=0.05). Either justify the smaller sample with a power analysis, temper the claim, or run a larger study."

### Calibrate The Recommendation To The Journal's Standard

Recommendations mean different things at different venues. "Major revision" at a selective journal may mean "this is promising, prove it"; at a less selective venue it may mean "fix these or it's out." Read recent issues to calibrate what the journal actually publishes, and recommend accordingly. When uncertain, err toward the more conservative recommendation and explain your reasoning to the editor.

### Distinguish Between Fixable And Fatal Flaws

Some problems can be addressed in revision; others cannot. Be clear about which category each major issue falls into. A fatal flaw — data that cannot support the claim, a methodological error that invalidates the results — should be stated plainly, with your reasoning, so the editor can act. A fixable flaw should be framed as a required revision, not a reason to reject.

### Provide A Confidential Note To The Editor When It Matters

The editor-only comment is where you flag conflicts, concerns about authorship or integrity, doubts about your own expertise on part of the work, or a recommendation that differs from what you told the author. Do not use it to vent. Use it to give the editor information they need to make a decision.

### Be Specific About What You Do Not Know

If part of the work falls outside your expertise, say so in the editor note and, where relevant, in the review itself. Editors can recruit additional reviewers; authors can calibrate your comments. Pretending to expertise you lack produces confident-sounding reviews that are wrong.

## Common Traps

- **The impression dump.** A review that lists feelings without structure forces everyone to reconstruct your reasoning. Editors deprioritize such reviews.
- **Demanding novelty you would not require of yourself.** Reviewers often hold others to a higher originality bar than they hold their own work. Check whether your "not novel enough" standard is one you actually apply in your own submissions.
- **The moving goalpost.** Adding new required experiments or analyses in each round of revision. State the bar in round one; don't invent new bars in round two unless the author's response genuinely opened a new issue.
- **Confusing preference with requirement.** "I would have used method B" is not a requirement unless method A is flawed. Distinguish between your taste and the work's validity.
- **Overstating certainty.** Writing "this is clearly wrong" when you mean "I'm not sure this holds." Overclaiming in reviews damages your credibility with editors over time.
- **The terse rejection.** A one-paragraph rejection with no specifics helps no one and signals you did not engage. If you recommend rejection, explain why with the same rigor you would for a revision.
- **Retaliatory or lenient calibration based on author identity.** Softening for friends, harshening for competitors. Editors notice patterns.
- **Ignoring the rebuttal.** In revision rounds, authors often address comments thoughtfully; refusing to accept a good rebuttal because you "still feel the same way" is not reviewing, it is vetoing.

## Self-Check

- Does my review separate a brief summary, overall assessment, major comments, and minor comments, with the recommendation clearly stated?
- Is every major critique tied to a specific consequence and a specific fix, so the author could address it without guessing?
- Have I calibrated my recommendation to what this journal actually publishes, not to an abstract ideal?
- Did I distinguish fatal flaws (which justify rejection) from fixable flaws (which justify revision)?
- Did I provide a confidential editor note for conflicts, integrity concerns, or recommendation nuances, rather than venting?
- Did I disclose the limits of my expertise rather than reviewing confidently outside my competence?
- Have I avoided demanding more novelty, scope, or method than I would require of my own work?
- In a revision round, am I evaluating the author's rebuttal on its merits rather than mechanically repeating my original objections?
