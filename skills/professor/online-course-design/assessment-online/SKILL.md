# Assessment Online

Online assessment multiplies the challenges of classroom assessment. Integrity is harder to assure when students are remote and resources are accessible; validity is harder when proctoring and conditions vary; equity is harder when bandwidth, devices, and environments differ; and the temptation to replicate in-person exams online often produces assessments that are neither secure nor valid nor fair. Effective online assessment rethinks what is assessed and how, leaning toward authentic and frequent assessment, designing for integrity rather than surveillance, and ensuring that the assessment measures learning rather than privilege, bandwidth, or test-taking under remote stress.

Use this skill before designing online assessment, addressing online integrity, building authentic assessment, or evaluating the fairness and security of online exams. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing online assessment that is insecure, invalid, inequitable, or that relies on surveillance in ways that damage trust and learning.

## Core Rules

### Reconsider What And How You Assess Online

Do not simply move in-person exams online. Reconsider:

- what capabilities actually need to be assessed;
- whether a timed exam is the right tool or whether authentic assessment fits better;
- how the online environment changes what is feasible and valid;
- whether the assessment measures learning or measures online test-taking;
- the balance of formative and summative assessment.

Online is an opportunity to assess better, not just remotely. Reconsider the assessment design, not only its delivery.

### Favor Authentic And Applied Assessment

Authentic assessment is both more valid and more integrity-resistant online. Favor:

- applied projects and performances over recall exams;
- portfolios showing development over time;
- open-ended tasks that require original work;
- authentic audiences and contexts where possible;
- assessment of process and reasoning, not just final product.

Authentic assessment measures higher-order capabilities and is harder to outsource or copy, addressing integrity through design rather than surveillance.

### Design For Integrity Rather Than Surveillance

Integrity is best designed in, not surveilled. Practices:

- write questions that require application and analysis, not searchable recall;
- use personalized or randomized variants where feasible;
- allow open resources and assess their use rather than prohibiting them;
- use oral follow-ups or defense of work for high-stakes assessment;
- build in multiple low-stakes assessments rather than a few high-stakes ones.

Surveillance-based proctoring, webcam monitoring, lockdown browsers, signals distrust, raises equity and privacy concerns, and often fails to ensure integrity. Design integrity in.

### Ensure Validity Under Online Conditions

Validity asks whether the assessment measures what it claims. Ensure it online:

- timed exams may measure speed and bandwidth as much as learning;
- technical failures invalidate results for affected students;
- remote conditions vary and affect performance;
- proctoring stress affects performance;
- align the assessment conditions with what you intend to measure.

An assessment that is valid in a classroom may be invalid online if conditions contaminate the measurement. Check validity under actual online conditions.

### Address Equity Explicitly

Online assessment affects students unequally. Address equity:

- bandwidth, device, and environment differences affect performance;
- timed assessments penalize students with slower connections or distractions;
- proctoring tools have documented biases and accessibility problems;
- synchronous exams exclude students in other time zones or with work;
- provide alternatives and accommodations proactively.

Equity is not an add-on. Assessment that ignores it measures privilege alongside learning.

### Build In Frequent Low-Stakes Assessment

Frequent low-stakes assessment serves multiple goals online. Build it in:

- reduces the pressure and stakes of any single assessment;
- provides early warning of disengagement and difficulty;
- supports learning through feedback rather than only measuring it;
- distributes integrity risk across many small assessments;
- accommodates varied schedules and conditions.

A few high-stakes exams online concentrate risk, integrity and equity. Frequent low-stakes assessment spreads and reduces it.

### Use Rubrics And Clear Criteria

Clear criteria support fairness and learning online. Use rubrics:

- make expectations and criteria explicit before the assessment;
- use rubrics for consistent and transparent grading;
- provide rubrics to students to guide and self-assess work;
- calibrate grading across sections and graders;
- use criteria to focus feedback.

Rubrics are especially valuable online where students work more independently and need clarity to guide effort.

### Provide Timely And Actionable Feedback

Online students depend on feedback more than classroom students. Provide it:

- return work promptly while it is still relevant;
- make feedback actionable, connected to future work;
- balance automated feedback for frequency with human feedback for depth;
- create feedback loops that students must use;
- prioritize feedback on high-stakes and authentic work.

Delayed or generic feedback squanders the learning value of assessment. Build timely, actionable feedback.

### Plan For Technical Failure And Contingency

Online assessment will encounter technical failure. Plan for it:

- have alternatives for students who experience failures;
- build in grace periods and flexibility;
- avoid single-point-of-failure high-stakes assessments;
- communicate contingency plans in advance;
- treat reported failures credibly rather than suspiciously.

Rigid online assessment that assumes perfect technology fails real students. Plan for failure.

### Ensure Accessibility Of Assessment

Assessment must be accessible to all students. Ensure:

- assessment platforms are accessible with assistive technology;
- timed assessments accommodate disabilities appropriately;
- media and instructions are accessible, captioned, described;
- multiple means of response where possible;
- accommodations are integrated, not bolted on.

Inaccessible assessment excludes students and may violate obligations. Build accessibility in.

### Protect Privacy And Data

Online assessment tools collect data. Protect students:

- evaluate proctoring and assessment tools for privacy and data practices;
- minimize data collection to what is necessary;
- be transparent with students about what is collected and why;
- avoid tools with problematic data practices;
- balance integrity concerns against privacy rights.

Surveillance tools can compromise student privacy and trust. Evaluate and minimize their use.

### Communicate Expectations And Rationale

Online assessment works best when expectations are clear. Communicate:

- why assessments are designed as they are;
- what integrity expectations are and how they are enforced;
- how to get help and accommodations;
- how feedback will be provided and used;
- the consequences of academic dishonesty.

Students who understand the rationale and expectations are more likely to engage honestly and effectively.

## Common Traps

### Replicated In-Person Exams

Moving classroom exams online unchanged produces insecure and invalid assessment. Reconsider the design.

### Recall-Heavy Assessment

Searchable recall questions invite dishonesty and measure little. Assess application and analysis.

### Surveillance Reliance

Proctoring and lockdown tools signal distrust and raise equity and privacy concerns. Design integrity in.

### Contaminated Validity

Timed or bandwidth-dependent assessment measures conditions, not learning. Check validity.

### Equity Ignored

Assessment that ignores bandwidth, device, time zone, and proctoring bias measures privilege. Address equity.

### High-Stakes Concentration

A few high-stakes exams concentrate integrity and equity risk. Use frequent low-stakes assessment.

### Vague Criteria

Unclear criteria produce inconsistent grading and confused effort. Use rubrics.

### Delayed Feedback

Late or generic feedback wastes learning value. Provide timely, actionable feedback.

### No Contingency Plan

Rigid assessment that assumes perfect technology fails real students. Plan for failure.

### Inaccessible Assessment

Assessment that excludes students with disabilities violates obligations. Build accessibility in.

### Privacy Neglect

Surveillance tools can compromise privacy and trust. Evaluate and minimize data collection.

### Unexplained Assessment

Assessment without communicated rationale breeds confusion and dishonesty. Communicate expectations and why.

## Self-Check

- Have you reconsidered what and how to assess online rather than replicating in-person exams?
- Does the assessment favor authentic and applied work over recall where appropriate?
- Is integrity designed in through question design and personalization rather than surveillance?
- Is the assessment valid under actual online conditions, uncontaminated by speed, bandwidth, or stress?
- Is equity addressed for bandwidth, devices, time zones, environments, and proctoring bias?
- Is there frequent low-stakes assessment rather than a few high-stakes exams?
- Are rubrics and clear criteria used for transparency and consistency?
- Is feedback timely, actionable, and connected to future work?
- Is there a contingency plan for technical failure that treats students credibly?
- Is the assessment accessible to students with disabilities and varied needs?
- Are privacy and data practices of assessment tools evaluated and minimized?
- Are expectations and rationale communicated clearly to students?
- Could you defend this assessment as measuring learning rather than privilege, bandwidth, or test-taking under remote stress?
