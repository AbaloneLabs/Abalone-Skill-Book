# Institutional Change And Advisory Leadership

Universities are conservative institutions, and changing them — whether to improve teaching, reform a program, address inequity, or adapt to new conditions — is slow, political work that requires more than a good idea. The failure mode is approaching change as a matter of proposing the right solution and expecting rational adoption, then being surprised and embittered when good proposals stall, get diluted, or generate backlash. Effective change leadership understands that institutions move through relationships, coalitions, timing, and sustained engagement, and that durable change requires building ownership across the people who will implement it. This is not cynicism about institutions; it is realism about how they actually work. A professor who understands institutional change can accomplish far more than one with better ideas but weaker change craft.

Use this skill when leading or participating in a change initiative, when serving in advisory or leadership roles, and when diagnosing why an initiative is stalling or failing. The goal is to prevent the agent from mistaking a good proposal for a successful change, or from burning out on change work through avoidable strategic errors.

## Core Rules

### Build The Case With Evidence And Narrative

Change requires both evidence and narrative. Evidence — data on the problem, examples from peer institutions, evidence on the proposed solution — gives the change rational force. Narrative — why this matters, what is at stake, what we could be — gives it motivational force. Most failed change initiatives have one without the other: a data-rich case no one cares about, or an inspiring vision with no grounding. Build both, and connect them to the institution's stated mission and values, which is the language in which institutional change is legitimately argued.

### Build Coalitions Before Proposing

Change proposed by a lone advocate is easily dismissed; change backed by a coalition is hard to ignore. Build support before the formal proposal: talk to colleagues individually, understand their interests and concerns, recruit allies, and bring credible endorsements to the table. The coalition should include the people who will implement the change and those whose endorsement carries weight. A proposal that arrives with broad, visible support has a fundamentally different reception than one that arrives alone.

### Understand And Address The Interests Of Stakeholders

People resist change for reasons, and those reasons are often legitimate: increased workload, threat to autonomy, concern about quality, fear of loss, or genuine disagreement about merits. Understand the actual interests and concerns of the stakeholders — faculty, staff, administration, students — and address them in the design and the advocacy. Change that ignores stakeholders' real concerns generates resistance that good arguments won't overcome. Where concerns can't be addressed, acknowledge them honestly rather than dismissing them.

### Sequence Change And Pick Battles

Not all change is possible at once, and attempting too much simultaneously produces overload and backlash. Sequence change: start with what is achievable and builds momentum, demonstrate success, and use that credibility for harder changes. Pick battles deliberately — some fights are worth the cost, others aren't — and conserve political capital for the changes that matter most. A change leader who fights every battle wins none of them.

### Work With, Not Just Against, Administration

Durable change usually requires alignment between faculty and administration, not confrontation alone. Identify administrators whose interests align with the change and work with them; understand the constraints they operate under (budget, legal, political); and build proposals that work within those constraints where possible. Pure opposition to administration may feel righteous but rarely produces durable change. That said, maintain independence: alignment is not capitulation, and some changes require faculty pressure on administration.

### Anticipate And Plan For Resistance And Backlash

Resistance to change is normal, not a sign of failure. Anticipate who will resist and why, prepare responses to likely objections, and don't be surprised or demoralized when resistance emerges. Backlash often comes after a change is implemented, as people experience its reality; plan for this and have the sustained engagement to defend and adjust the change through the inevitable pushback. Changes that survive backlash become the new normal; changes abandoned at the first resistance are reversed.

### Design For Sustainability Beyond The Champion

Institutional changes often depend on a single champion and reverse when that person leaves, burns out, or loses interest. Design changes for sustainability beyond any individual: build them into structures, policies, and roles; distribute ownership across multiple people; document the rationale and the implementation; and ensure the change is resourced to continue. A change that requires its original champion to persist is not yet an institutional change.

### Evaluate, Adjust, And Celebrate

Build evaluation into the change from the start, and be willing to adjust based on what you learn. Sustained change is iterative: implement, evaluate, adjust, re-implement. And celebrate successes along the way — institutional change is hard, often thankless work, and recognizing progress sustains the coalition and the energy for the next effort.

## Common Traps

- **The rationalist fallacy.** Believing a good proposal will be adopted on its merits. Institutions move through politics and relationships, not just reason.
- **Proposing without a coalition.** Lone proposals are easily dismissed. Build support before the formal moment.
- **Ignoring stakeholders' real concerns.** Resistance driven by legitimate concerns isn't overcome by better arguments. Address the concerns.
- **Attempting too much at once.** Overload produces backlash. Sequence change and pick battles.
- **Pure opposition to administration.** Confrontation alone rarely produces durable change. Build alignment where possible.
- **Surprise at resistance.** Resistance is normal. Anticipate it, prepare for it, and don't be demoralized by it.
- **Champion-dependent change.** Changes that reverse when the champion leaves aren't yet institutional. Build them into structure and distribute ownership.
- **No evaluation or celebration.** Change without evaluation drifts; change without recognition of progress loses its coalition.

## Self-Check

- Have I built both the evidence and the narrative for the change, connected to the institution's mission and values?
- Have I built a coalition — including implementers and credible endorsers — before the formal proposal?
- Do I understand the real interests and concerns of stakeholders, and have I addressed them in design and advocacy?
- Am I sequencing change — starting with achievable wins, picking battles, conserving capital — rather than attempting everything at once?
- Am I working with aligned administrators and within their constraints, while maintaining faculty independence?
- Have I anticipated resistance and backlash, prepared responses, and planned for sustained engagement through the pushback?
- Is the change designed to survive beyond me — built into structure, distributed across people, documented, resourced?
- Is there a plan to evaluate, adjust, and celebrate progress, sustaining the coalition for the long effort?
