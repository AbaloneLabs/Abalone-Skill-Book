# Shared Governance And Faculty Voice

Shared governance — the division of authority between faculty and administration over academic matters — is the structure that distinguishes a university from a corporation. Faculty hold primary authority over the academic enterprise: curriculum, degrees, standards, promotion, and the educational mission. Administrators hold authority over resources, operations, and legal compliance. The failure mode is letting this structure erode unnoticed: faculty disengage from governance as tedious or pointless, administration accumulates decisions that should be faculty-led, and the university drifts toward a model where faculty are employees who execute and administrators decide. This drift is rarely the result of a single power grab; it is the accumulation of disengagement, abdication, and the gradual normalization of administrative authority over academic matters. Exercising faculty voice is not optional service; it is the defense of the academic mission and the institution's character.

Use this skill when participating in governance, when deciding whether and how to engage on an issue, and when diagnosing why faculty governance has become weak or performative. The goal is to prevent the agent from disengaging from governance or from mistaking administrative consultation for faculty authority.

## Core Rules

### Understand The Actual Governance Structure And Authority

Effective participation requires knowing how authority is actually distributed at your institution: what the faculty senate or council decides, what departments control, what is administrative discretion, and what is governed by statute, regulation, or board policy. Read the faculty handbook, the governance documents, and the bylaws. Much faculty disempowerment comes from not knowing what authority faculty actually hold and therefore ceding it by default. Know the structure before you engage it.

### Distinguish Faculty Authority From Administrative Authority

Some decisions are legitimately faculty-led (curriculum, degree requirements, academic standards, promotion criteria), some are legitimately administrative (budget, facilities, legal compliance), and many are shared. When an administrator proposes a change to a faculty-led area — a new program, a changed requirement, a revised promotion standard — recognize that this requires faculty governance, not just administrative decision, and insist on the proper process. Conversely, respect administrative authority in its proper domain. Shared governance is a division, not a contest for total control.

### Show Up And Participate Substantively

Governance runs on the people who show up. Faculty who attend meetings, read documents, serve on committees, and vote shape decisions; faculty who don't, cede decisions to those who do — and to administrators who fill the vacuum. Participation is not performative; it requires reading the materials, forming positions, and engaging substantively in discussion. The faculty who govern well are the faculty who prepare and participate, not merely the faculty who attend.

### Insist On Genuine Consultation, Not Performative Notice

A common erosion pattern is administration "consulting" faculty after a decision is effectively made, or providing notice rather than seeking input. Genuine consultation happens early enough to shape the decision, includes the relevant faculty governance bodies, and treats faculty input as consequential. When consultation is performative — a fait accompli presented for ratification — name it and insist on real engagement. The faculty handbook and governance documents often specify what genuine consultation requires; hold the institution to its own rules.

### Engage On Substance, Not Just Process Or Politics

Faculty governance is most legitimate and effective when it engages the substance of academic decisions: the educational merits of a program change, the academic implications of a policy, the standards implied by a promotion criterion. Governance that devolves into pure process wrangling or political maneuvering loses moral authority and effectiveness. Bring substance to the table — the educational, scholarly, and mission-level implications — and the faculty voice carries weight.

### Build Coalitions And Sustain Engagement

Governance influence is rarely won by a single voice; it is won by coalitions of faculty who share a position and sustain engagement over time. Build relationships across the department, college, and senate. Coordinate with colleagues who share your analysis. Sustained engagement — across multiple issues and years — is what builds the credibility and relationships that let faculty voice actually shape decisions. One-off outrage rarely changes anything; consistent participation does.

### Document And Use Institutional Memory

Governance decisions have histories, and administrations and contexts change. Maintain records of governance decisions, rationales, and commitments — in department files, senate archives, and your own notes. Institutional memory is what prevents a settled decision from being reopened by a new administrator who doesn't know the history, and what lets you hold the institution to commitments over time.

### Know When To Escalate

When normal governance channels are bypassed or ignored, escalation may be necessary: to the full faculty, to the senate, to higher administration, to the board, to the faculty union if applicable, or to external accrediting bodies. Escalation is a last resort and should be used judiciously, but faculty who never escalate have no recourse when governance is genuinely violated. Know the escalation paths before you need them.

## Common Traps

- **Disengaging as pointless.** The belief that governance doesn't matter becomes self-fulfilling; the faculty who don't participate cede decisions to those who do and to administration.
- **Not knowing the structure.** Much disempowerment comes from not knowing what authority faculty hold. Read the governance documents.
- **Ceding faculty-led decisions by default.** Letting administration decide matters that are properly faculty-led, because engagement is hard or slow.
- **Accepting performative consultation.** Treating notice or post-decision input as genuine consultation. Insist on early, consequential engagement.
- **Substance-free governance.** Governance that devolves into process or politics loses legitimacy. Bring educational and scholarly substance.
- **One-off outrage.** Single episodes of protest rarely change decisions. Sustained coalition engagement does.
- **Losing institutional memory.** Without records, settled decisions get reopened and commitments get forgotten. Document.
- **Never escalating.** Faculty who never escalate have no recourse when governance is genuinely violated. Know the paths.

## Self-Check

- Do I know my institution's actual governance structure and what authority faculty hold, from the handbook and governance documents?
- Can I distinguish properly faculty-led decisions from administrative ones, and do I insist on faculty process for the former?
- Do I show up, prepare, and participate substantively in governance, rather than treating attendance as sufficient?
- When consulted, do I distinguish genuine consultation from performative notice, and insist on early, consequential engagement?
- Do I bring educational and scholarly substance to governance, rather than letting it devolve into process or politics?
- Am I building coalitions and sustaining engagement over time, rather than relying on one-off protest?
- Do I maintain and use institutional memory — records of decisions, rationales, commitments — to hold the institution to its word?
- Do I know the escalation paths and use them judiciously when normal governance channels are bypassed?
