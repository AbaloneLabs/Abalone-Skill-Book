# Authorship Decisions

Authorship is the currency of academic careers. It determines credit, hiring, promotion, funding, and reputation, and disputes over authorship are among the most common and damaging conflicts in research collaboration. Unlike many professional decisions, authorship norms are field-specific, often implicit, and frequently misunderstood, which means that well-intentioned researchers can produce arrangements that feel fair to them but violate the expectations of collaborators, institutions, or journals. Authorship decisions made late or vaguely almost always produce conflict; decisions made early, explicitly, and against defensible criteria produce arrangements that hold up under scrutiny.

Use this skill before deciding authorship, establishing norms with a new collaboration, resolving a dispute, or advising a trainee on authorship. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing authorship arrangements that are ambiguous, inequitable, or ethically indefensible, any of which can damage careers and relationships.

## Core Rules

### Agree On Authorship Norms Before Research Begins

The single most effective practice is to discuss authorship before work starts, not after. Early discussion:

- establishes expectations while relationships are good;
- surfaces differing assumptions before they become conflicts;
- clarifies what contributions will earn authorship;
- sets a framework for order that can be revisited as contributions evolve.

Revisit the agreement at milestones, since actual contributions often diverge from plans. An early conversation is not a contract; it is a shared understanding that prevents later surprise.

### Use Defensible Authorship Criteria

Authorship should reflect contribution, not hierarchy, favor, or custom. Most fields converge on criteria that require:

- substantial contribution to conception or design, or to data acquisition, analysis, or interpretation;
- drafting or critically revising the work for important intellectual content;
- final approval of the version to be published;
- accountability for all aspects of the work.

Contributions that do not meet these thresholds, such as general supervision, providing funding, or routine technical assistance, may warrant acknowledgment rather than authorship. Know your field's specific norms, which vary, but anchor decisions in contribution.

### Match Authorship Practice To Field Norms

Authorship conventions differ sharply across fields, and applying the wrong convention creates conflict. Clarify:

- whether order indicates contribution, with first author as lead, or is alphabetical;
- whether the last position denotes seniority or supervision, common in lab sciences;
- whether co-first or co-last authorship is recognized and how it is marked;
- whether corresponding authorship carries specific meaning;
- how large collaborations and consortia handle authorship.

Assuming your field's norms apply to a cross-field collaboration is a common source of dispute. State the convention explicitly.

### Define What Earns Authorship Versus Acknowledgment

Ambiguity about the threshold for authorship produces both unfair exclusion and unfair inclusion. Draw the line:

- authorship: substantial intellectual contribution to conception, design, analysis, interpretation, or writing;
- acknowledgment: technical assistance, editorial help, general advising, provision of space or funds, data sharing without intellectual contribution.

Gift authorship, adding someone who did not contribute to maintain relationships, and ghost authorship, omitting someone who did contribute, are both ethical violations in most frameworks. Both distort credit and accountability.

### Determine Order Through Contribution, Not Politics

Authorship order should reflect relative contribution. Practices:

- estimate contributions honestly against the criteria;
- discuss order openly with all contributors;
- revisit as contributions shift during the project;
- use co-first or co-last designations when contributions are genuinely equal;
- document the rationale for the final order.

Order determined by seniority, politics, or who pushed hardest erodes trust and misrepresents the work. Order determined by contribution is defensible.

### Handle Trainee Authorship With Care

Trainees are vulnerable to unfair authorship arrangements. Protect them by:

- ensuring trainees who lead projects are first author;
- clarifying whether the mentor's role warrants co-authorship or last authorship per field norms;
- not using trainee work to advance the mentor's agenda disproportionately;
- teaching trainees authorship norms so they can advocate for themselves;
- documenting contributions to support the trainee's case in disputes.

A mentor who routinely takes first authorship on trainee-led work, or who adds themselves where contribution does not warrant, exploits the power imbalance.

### Address Cross-Institutional And Consortium Authorship Explicitly

Multi-site and consortium projects have complex authorship. Address:

- whether the consortium is named as author with a group letter, or individuals are listed;
- how contribution within the consortium is recognized;
- governance of who is included and in what order;
- the role of the steering committee versus individual contributors;
- journal requirements for group authorship.

Consortium authorship disputes are common and bitter because the stakes and the number of parties are large. Establish governance early.

### Document Contributions To Support Decisions

Keep records of who contributed what, at what stage. Contribution documentation:

- supports authorship decisions with evidence;
- helps resolve disputes when memories diverge;
- provides material for the contributions statement many journals require;
- protects contributors whose work might otherwise be invisible;
- creates accountability for claimed contributions.

A contributions statement, using a taxonomy like CRediT where adopted, makes authorship decisions transparent and defensible.

### Resolve Disputes Through Process, Not Force

When authorship disputes arise, resolve them through process:

- revisit the original agreement and the actual contributions;
- apply the field's authorship criteria;
- consult the journal's policies if relevant;
- involve a neutral third party, department chair or ombud, if needed;
- avoid retaliatory or punitive changes to authorship.

Disputes resolved by the most powerful party's preference produce resentment and reputational damage. Disputes resolved by criteria and process are more durable.

### Plan For Authorship Changes During The Project

Contributions evolve. A contributor planned for first author may do less; a contributor planned for acknowledgment may do more. Build in:

- periodic review of contributions against planned authorship;
- willingness to revise order or inclusion as work develops;
- early communication of changes rather than surprise at submission;
- a process for adding or removing contributors against criteria.

Treating the initial agreement as immutable when contributions have changed produces arrangements that no longer reflect the work.

## Common Traps

### Late Authorship Discussions

Deciding authorship after the work is done guarantees conflict. Discuss before and revisit at milestones.

### Criteria-Free Decisions

Authorship by hierarchy or favor is indefensible. Use contribution criteria.

### Wrong Field Norms

Applying one field's conventions to a cross-field collaboration causes disputes. State the convention.

### Gift Or Ghost Authorship

Adding non-contributors or omitting contributors are both ethical violations. Match inclusion to contribution.

### Order By Politics

Order determined by seniority or pressure misrepresents the work. Order by contribution.

### Exploiting Trainees

Taking disproportionate credit for trainee-led work exploits power imbalance. Protect trainee authorship.

### Undocumented Contributions

Relying on memory for who did what invites disputes. Document contributions.

### Power-Based Dispute Resolution

Resolving disputes by the strongest party's will damages relationships. Use criteria and process.

### Immutable Initial Agreements

Refusing to revise authorship when contributions change produces inaccurate arrangements. Plan for changes.

### Ignoring Journal Requirements

Journals have specific authorship policies and contribution statements. Know and meet them.

## Self-Check

- Was authorship discussed and agreed before research began, with revisits at milestones?
- Are authorship decisions based on defensible contribution criteria rather than hierarchy or favor?
- Has the relevant field convention on order, first, last, corresponding, co-first, been clarified explicitly?
- Is the line between authorship and acknowledgment clearly drawn, avoiding both gift and ghost authorship?
- Does authorship order reflect relative contribution, with co-designations where contributions are equal?
- Are trainees who lead projects given appropriate first authorship, with mentor roles per field norms?
- Are cross-institutional and consortium authorship arrangements governed explicitly?
- Are contributions documented to support decisions and provide material for contribution statements?
- Are disputes resolved through criteria and neutral process rather than the preference of the powerful party?
- Is there a process for revising authorship as contributions evolve during the project?
- Could each author's inclusion and position be defended to a colleague or a journal editor on the basis of contribution?
