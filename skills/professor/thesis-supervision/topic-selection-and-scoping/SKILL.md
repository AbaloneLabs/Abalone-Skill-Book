# Topic Selection And Scoping

Choosing a thesis or dissertation topic is one of the highest-stakes decisions a graduate student makes. The topic determines the years of work that follow, the student's entry into a scholarly community, the feasibility of completion, and often the trajectory of an early career. Students typically arrive with topics that are too broad, too vague, derivative of a favorite reading, or chosen to please an adviser rather than to sustain the student's own interest. Good supervision at this stage is not about imposing a topic; it is about helping the student find a question that is genuinely original, demonstrably feasible, intellectually generative, and completable within the constraints of time, resources, and skill.

Use this skill before advising a student on topic selection, scoping a research question, evaluating feasibility, or redirecting a student whose topic is unlikely to succeed. The goal is to prevent the agent from endorsing topics that sound impressive but cannot be completed, or that are so safe they produce no contribution.

## Core Rules

### Help The Student Find A Question They Can Sustain

A multi-year project requires genuine interest; students who choose topics to please an adviser or to seem impressive often stall. Probe what the student actually cares about:

- which problems do they return to voluntarily;
- which readings provoked strong reactions;
- which methods do they enjoy or want to learn;
- what would they still want to work on in three years;
- what connects to their longer-term goals.

A topic the student finds intrinsically interesting survives the difficult middle phase of research. A topic chosen instrumentally often collapses there.

### Move From A Topic To A Question

Students arrive with topics, broad areas of interest. The supervisor's job is to help convert a topic into a research question with a defensible claim, a method, and a contribution.

The arc:

- topic, a broad area such as "migration and labor";
- question, a specific puzzle such as "how do temporary visa workers negotiate wage bargaining across employers";
- claim, a tentative answer such as "workers on tied visas accept lower wages because exit is constrained";
- contribution, what is added to existing scholarship such as "extends bargaining theory to legally constrained labor markets";
- method, how the claim will be tested or developed.

A topic that cannot be pushed to a question with a method and a contribution is not yet a thesis.

### Test Feasibility Ruthlessly

A beautiful question that cannot be answered within the student's constraints is not a viable topic. Assess feasibility across:

- data or source access, can the student actually obtain what they need;
- ethics and approvals, will IRB or equivalent review be feasible and timely;
- methods, does the student have or can they acquire the needed skills;
- time, can the work be completed within the degree timeline;
- funding, are there resources for fieldwork, equipment, travel, or participant compensation;
- language and cultural access, if relevant;
- institutional and political constraints in the field site.

Feasibility failures are the most common reason projects stall. Better to confront them at the topic stage than in year three.

### Scope To A Contribution, Not A Field

A dissertation makes a specific contribution, not a survey of a field. Students often propose topics that would require a book series. Help them narrow to a contribution that is:

- specific enough to complete;
- original enough to matter;
- connected enough to existing literature to be intelligible;
- generative enough to support future work.

A useful test: can the contribution be stated in one sentence? If it requires a paragraph of qualifications, the scope is likely still too broad.

### Assess Originality And Positioning

A topic must add something to existing scholarship. Help the student position the work:

- conduct a real literature search, not a casual one;
- identify the closest existing works and how the proposed work differs;
- confirm the gap is genuine, not already filled;
- identify the conversation the work will enter;
- clarify what would count as a contribution to that conversation.

Originality does not require novelty for its own sake. Replication, extension, methodological transfer, and application to a new case are all legitimate contributions if framed honestly.

### Match Topic To Career Goals Where It Matters

For some careers, the dissertation topic is the foundation of a research agenda; for others, it is a credential. Help the student think about:

- academic job market expectations in the field;
- industry or policy career relevance;
- whether the topic supports a coherent publication trajectory;
- whether the methods are transferable to post-degree work.

The topic need not be optimized for the job market, but the student should make an informed choice rather than discover constraints later.

### Sequence The Topic Decision With Coursework And Methods

Topic selection is not a single moment. It unfolds through coursework, methods training, pilot work, and reading. Help the student sequence:

- coursework that builds relevant knowledge;
- methods training aligned with the likely approach;
- a pilot or exploratory study to test feasibility;
- a literature review that sharpens the question;
- a proposal that commits to a scoped version.

Treating the topic as fixed too early forecloses learning; treating it as perpetually open prevents progress.

### Document The Evolving Question

Encourage the student to keep a living document of the question, its rationale, its positioning, and its method. Revise it as the work develops. This document becomes the basis for the proposal, the defense, and eventually the introduction, and it forces the student to articulate the contribution repeatedly.

### Plan For The Inevitable Pivot

Most dissertations pivot. Data does not arrive, a method fails, a key source is inaccessible, or the question evolves. Build scoping that anticipates this:

- identify a primary question and a feasible fallback;
- design data collection or analysis to support more than one question where possible;
- normalize revision as part of research, not failure;
- agree on checkpoints where scope is revisited.

A topic scoped with no room to pivot becomes a trap when reality intervenes.

## Common Traps

### Endorsing A Topic To Please

Accepting a topic because the student is enthusiastic, when you see feasibility problems, sets up a future crisis. Raise concerns honestly.

### Topic Without Question

A broad area is not a thesis. Push to a question, claim, method, and contribution.

### Ignoring Feasibility

A beautiful question with no data access is not viable. Assess feasibility early.

### Scope Of A Field Survey

A topic that aims to cover a field cannot be completed. Narrow to a contribution.

### Skipping The Literature Search

Assuming a gap exists without searching produces derivative work. Position against real literature.

### Premature Commitment

Locking the topic before coursework and pilot work forecloses learning. Sequence the decision.

### No Pivot Plan

A topic with no fallback becomes a trap when data or methods fail. Plan for revision.

### Optimizing For Novelty Alone

A topic chosen for novelty without feasibility or interest stalls. Balance originality with sustainability.

## Self-Check

- Has the student identified a question they can sustain over years, not just one that seems impressive?
- Has the topic been pushed from a broad area to a specific question, claim, contribution, and method?
- Has feasibility been tested across data access, ethics, methods, time, funding, language, and field constraints?
- Is the scope narrow enough to complete and broad enough to matter, with a one-sentence contribution statement?
- Has a real literature search confirmed the originality and positioned the work in an existing conversation?
- Does the topic align with the student's career goals in an informed way, whether academic or otherwise?
- Is the topic decision sequenced with coursework, methods training, and pilot work rather than fixed prematurely?
- Is there a living document of the evolving question, rationale, positioning, and method?
- Is there a primary question and a feasible fallback that allows pivot when reality intervenes?
- Could you defend this topic choice to a committee as feasible, original, and completable within the degree timeline?
