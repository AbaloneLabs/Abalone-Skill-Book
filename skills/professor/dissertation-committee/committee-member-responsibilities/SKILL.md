# Committee Member Responsibilities

Serving on a dissertation committee is a serious obligation that is often undertaken with too little reflection. Faculty agree to serve, then let the work slide to the bottom of the priority list, arriving at exams underprepared or returning chapters months late. Or they overstep, treating the student's project as their own or imposing demands that serve their agenda rather than the student's. Being a constructive committee member is a distinct skill from being a good researcher or teacher: it requires reliability, intellectual generosity, respect for the student's project and the chair's role, and the judgment to challenge productively without obstructing.

Use this skill before agreeing to serve on a committee, defining your role, reading student work, or participating in exams and defenses. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing committee service that is negligent, self-serving, or harmful to the student's progress and well-being.

## Core Rules

### Agree To Serve Only If You Can Commit

Committee service is a multi-year obligation. Before agreeing, assess honestly whether you can meet it:

- do you have the expertise to contribute meaningfully;
- can you return readings and attend meetings reliably over the timeline;
- are you genuinely interested in the project, or serving only out of obligation;
- do you have the time given your other commitments.

It is more professional to decline than to serve negligently. A non-responsive committee member delays the student's degree and erodes trust.

### Respect The Chair's Primary Role

The chair or advisor has the primary mentoring relationship and decision authority. Your role is to contribute expertise and independent judgment, not to compete with the chair or override their guidance. Respect the hierarchy:

- raise substantive concerns through discussion with the chair where appropriate;
- do not give the student directives that conflict with the chair's guidance without coordination;
- support the chair's synthesis of committee feedback;
- defer to the chair on process and timeline decisions.

Competing authority confuses the student and undermines the advising relationship.

### Give Feedback That Serves The Student's Project, Not Your Agenda

A common failure is the committee member who redirects the student's project toward their own interests, demanding the dissertation engage their preferred theorists, methods, or questions. Give feedback that serves the project the student and chair have defined:

- distinguish essential concerns from your preferences;
- offer suggestions as options, not mandates;
- respect the project's scope and the student's voice;
- avoid imposing your agenda under the guise of rigor.

Rigor and intellectual contribution do not require commandeering the project.

### Be Reliable And Timely

Students depend on committee feedback to progress. Delays cascade into degree delays. Commit to:

- returning readings within an agreed timeframe, typically two to four weeks;
- attending scheduled meetings and exams;
- responding to communications promptly;
- flagging anticipated delays rather than going silent.

Reliability is the baseline of constructive committee service. Chronic lateness is not a personality quirk; it is a failure of obligation that harms students.

### Read Substantively And Give Specific Feedback

Skimming a chapter and offering generic comments wastes everyone's time. Read substantively and give feedback specific enough to act on:

- engage the actual argument and evidence;
- identify both strengths to preserve and weaknesses to address;
- reference specific passages and pages;
- distinguish major issues from minor ones;
- frame feedback as questions and options where the student has discretion.

Substantive feedback advances the dissertation; generic feedback signals you did not engage.

### Distinguish Your Role At Different Stages

The committee member's role shifts across the dissertation process:

- proposal stage: help scope the project and identify feasibility issues;
- research stage: advise on method and approach as needed;
- writing stage: give feedback on drafts at agreed intervals;
- exam stage: assess readiness and identify gaps;
- defense stage: evaluate the completed work against the standard for the degree.

Calibrate your engagement to the stage rather than applying defense-level scrutiny to a proposal draft.

### Participate Constructively In Exams And Defenses

Qualifying exams, proposal defenses, and the final defense are high-stakes events for the student. Participate constructively:

- come prepared, having read the work;
- ask questions that probe understanding, not that humiliate;
- engage in scholarly exchange, not performance;
- offer substantive critique within professional norms;
- support the student in demonstrating their knowledge.

Defenses are examinations, not hazing. The goal is to assess and develop, not to perform superiority.

### Maintain Confidentiality And Professionalism

Committee deliberations, drafts, and exam content are confidential. Do not discuss the student's work or performance outside the committee, do not share drafts, and maintain professional boundaries. Breaches of confidentiality damage the student and the institution's trust.

### Advocate For The Student's Interests

A constructive committee member advocates for the student within the process: ensuring fair treatment, supporting needed resources, and speaking up if the student is being poorly served by the chair, the program, or the institution. Advocacy is part of the role, not an overstep, when the student's interests are at stake.

### Know When To Raise Concerns About The Chair Or Process

If the chair is not serving the student, through neglect, abuse of power, or incompetence, the committee member has a responsibility to act. This is delicate but important:

- raise concerns directly with the chair where possible;
- involve the graduate director or department chair if needed;
- prioritize the student's progress and well-being;
- document specific concerns.

Silence in the face of a dysfunctional advising relationship makes the committee complicit in the harm.

### Step Off The Committee If You Cannot Serve

If circumstances change and you can no longer serve reliably, step off the committee promptly and through proper process, so the student can replace you. Clinging to a committee seat you cannot serve delays the student and signals a failure of professional responsibility.

## Common Traps

### Agreeing Then Neglecting

Serving without committing to reliability delays the student. Decline if you cannot serve.

### Competing With The Chair

Undermining the chair's authority confuses the student. Respect the primary role.

### Imposing Your Agenda

Redirecting the project to your interests is self-serving. Serve the student's project.

### Chronic Lateness

Delayed feedback cascades into degree delays. Be timely.

### Generic Feedback

Vague comments signal disengagement. Read and respond substantively.

### Defense-Scrutiny On Drafts

Applying final standards to early work paralyzes progress. Calibrate to stage.

### Humiliating Questions In Exams

Performance-oriented questioning harms students. Probe constructively.

### Breaching Confidentiality

Sharing drafts or deliberations damages trust. Maintain confidentiality.

### Silence About Dysfunction

Failing to act on a harmful advising relationship makes you complicit. Raise concerns.

### Clinging To An Unserved Seat

Holding a committee role you cannot fulfill delays the student. Step off promptly.

## Self-Check

- Have you agreed to serve only with a genuine commitment to the multi-year obligation?
- Do you respect the chair's primary role and decision authority without competing?
- Does your feedback serve the student's project rather than your own agenda?
- Are you reliable and timely in returning readings and attending meetings and exams?
- Do you read substantively and give specific, actionable feedback?
- Is your role calibrated to the dissertation stage rather than applying defense-level scrutiny throughout?
- Do you participate in exams and defenses constructively, probing without humiliating?
- Do you maintain confidentiality and professional boundaries?
- Do you advocate for the student's interests within the process?
- Would you raise concerns and act if the chair or process were failing the student?
