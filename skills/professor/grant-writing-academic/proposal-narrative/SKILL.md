# Proposal Narrative

A grant proposal is a persuasion document judged by reviewers who read many proposals under time pressure. Unlike a research paper, which reports completed work to peers who chose to read it, a proposal asks reviewers to bet scarce funds on work that has not yet been done. This changes the writing task entirely: the proposal must establish that the question matters, that the approach will work, that the team can deliver, and that the funder's investment will produce returns. Strong proposals are not just well-written; they are strategically constructed to survive a competitive review where most proposals fail.

Use this skill before drafting or revising a grant proposal narrative, structuring the case for support, articulating significance, or preparing for resubmission. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing proposals that are research-sound but review-weak, where the science is credible but the case for funding is not made.

## Core Rules

### Write For The Reviewer, Not For Yourself

The proposal is read by reviewers who are not you, who are reading many proposals, and who are looking for reasons to rank yours lower to advance others. Write for them:

- assume the reviewer is smart but not in your exact subarea;
- front-load the question and why it matters;
- make the logic visible, do not make the reviewer infer it;
- use signposting and summaries so a skim captures the case;
- avoid jargon and insider references that exclude.

A proposal written for the author's own thinking is often impenetrable to reviewers. Write for the reviewer's constraints.

### Establish Significance Before Method

Reviewers fund questions that matter, not just methods that work. Establish significance before diving into method:

- what important problem or question does the work address;
- why now, what has changed that makes this tractable;
- what gap exists in current knowledge or capability;
- what would change if the work succeeds;
- who cares about the answer and why.

A proposal that opens with method before establishing why the question matters reads as technique in search of a problem. Establish the stakes first.

### Articulate A Clear And Feasible Set Of Aims

The aims are the spine of the proposal. They should be:

- few in number, typically two to four, so each is developed;
- distinct, not overlapping or dependent in ways that risk cascading failure;
- framed as questions or goals, not just tasks;
- feasible within the budget and timeline;
- collectively sufficient to address the central question.

A common failure is too many aims, each underdeveloped, or aims that depend on each other so that failure of one sinks all. Design aims that are distinct and individually valuable.

### Align Aims, Methods, And Outcomes

Each aim must have methods that address it and outcomes that demonstrate it was met. Build explicit alignment:

- for each aim, the methods that will achieve it;
- for each method, the expected outcomes and how they test the aim;
- for the set of aims, how outcomes combine to answer the central question;
- alternatives if the primary approach fails.

Misalignment, where aims and methods do not map, is a frequent reason for low scores. The reviewer should see the logic of how each aim will be accomplished.

### Demonstrate Feasibility With Preliminary Data And Track Record

Reviewers bet on proposals they believe can be delivered. Demonstrate feasibility:

- preliminary data showing the approach works;
- prior publications establishing the team's capability;
- access to required data, populations, equipment, or sites;
- methods that are established or for which readiness is shown;
- a timeline that is realistic, not aspirational.

A proposal with no preliminary evidence that the team can do the work is a high-risk bet. Feasibility evidence lowers the perceived risk.

### Address Innovation Without Overclaiming

Funders often weight innovation, but overclaiming novelty invites skepticism. Position innovation honestly:

- what is genuinely new, method, question, population, application;
- how it advances beyond the closest existing work;
- why the innovation matters, not just that it is new;
- avoid claiming novelty where the work is incremental.

Honest innovation positioning is more persuasive than inflated claims. Reviewers know the literature and discount overclaiming.

### Anticipate And Address Risks And Alternatives

Reviewers look for what could go wrong. Address risks proactively:

- identify the main risks to each aim;
- describe alternative approaches if the primary fails;
- show contingency thinking rather than assuming success;
- distinguish risks that are manageable from those that are fundamental.

A proposal with no acknowledgment of risk reads as naive. A proposal that names risks and shows mitigation reads as mature.

### Make The Team Visible As Capable

Reviewers fund teams, not just ideas. Show the team:

- the principal investigator's relevant track record;
- co-investigators and their specific roles and contributions;
- collaborators who bring required expertise;
- institutional support and resources;
- how the team composition matches the work's demands.

A strong idea with a team that lacks the needed expertise is a weak proposal. Match team to task and show the match.

### Fit The Funder's Mission And Review Criteria

Each funder has a mission and review criteria. Fit them:

- read the funder's stated priorities and strategic plans;
- use the funder's language and framing where honest;
- address each review criterion explicitly, significance, innovation, approach, team, environment;
- check the solicitation for specific requirements;
- talk to the program officer for guidance.

A scientifically excellent proposal that does not fit the funder's mission will not be funded. Fit is not distortion; it is intelligibility to the funder.

### Write And Revise Across Multiple Drafts

Strong proposals are revised, not drafted. Build in:

- an outline reviewed before full drafting;
- a complete draft well before the deadline;
- internal review by colleagues who simulate reviewers;
- revision based on feedback, especially on significance and clarity;
- a final polish pass for the reviewer's reading experience.

Last-minute proposals carry avoidable weaknesses. Multiple drafts with feedback produce competitive proposals.

### Plan For Resubmission

Most proposals are not funded on first submission. Plan for resubmission:

- read the reviews carefully for substance;
- address the concerns substantively, not defensively;
- use the resubmission to strengthen, not just defend;
- document changes clearly for the reviewer;
- consider whether the work fits a different funder if the fit was wrong.

Resubmission success rates are often higher than first submission, because the feedback improves the proposal. Treat first submission as the start, not the end.

## Common Traps

### Writing For Yourself

A proposal written for the author's thinking is impenetrable to reviewers. Write for the reviewer.

### Method Before Significance

Opening with technique before establishing stakes reads as method in search of a problem. Establish significance first.

### Too Many Aims

Numerous underdeveloped aims signal overreach. Keep aims few and developed.

### Aims-Methods Misalignment

Aims without matching methods invite low scores. Build explicit alignment.

### No Feasibility Evidence

A proposal with no preliminary data or track record is a high-risk bet. Demonstrate feasibility.

### Overclaiming Innovation

Inflated novelty claims invite skepticism. Position innovation honestly.

### Ignoring Risks

A proposal with no risk acknowledgment reads as naive. Address risks and alternatives.

### Invisible Team

A strong idea with an unproven team is weak. Show team capability and fit.

### Ignoring Funder Fit

An excellent proposal that does not fit the mission will not be funded. Fit the funder.

### Single-Draft Proposals

Last-minute work carries avoidable weaknesses. Revise across drafts with feedback.

### Treating Rejection As Failure

Discarding reviews after rejection wastes feedback. Plan and improve for resubmission.

## Self-Check

- Is the proposal written for a smart, time-pressed reviewer outside the exact subarea?
- Is significance established before method, so the stakes are clear?
- Are aims few, distinct, feasible, and collectively sufficient to answer the central question?
- Is there explicit alignment among aims, methods, and expected outcomes?
- Does preliminary data and track record demonstrate feasibility and lower perceived risk?
- Is innovation positioned honestly without overclaiming?
- Are risks identified with alternative approaches for the main ones?
- Is the team visible as capable, with roles and expertise matched to the work?
- Does the proposal fit the funder's mission and address each review criterion explicitly?
- Was the proposal drafted, internally reviewed, and revised before submission?
- If declined, will the reviews be used to strengthen a resubmission rather than discarded?
- Would a reviewer rank this proposal highly after reading many in a panel?
