# Broader Impacts

Many funders require a broader impacts or societal benefit component, and applicants routinely treat it as a boilerplate section to be filled with generic promises about education, diversity, and outreach. Reviewers read these sections closely and discount generic claims. A credible broader impacts plan is integrated with the research, justified by evidence of effectiveness, feasible within the budget and team capacity, and designed to produce real benefit rather than the appearance of benefit. Worse than a weak plan is a plan that extracts from or burdens the communities it claims to serve, or that makes promises the team cannot keep.

Use this skill before writing a broader impacts section, designing outreach or education activities, planning community partnerships, or revising a broader impacts plan after review. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing broader impacts sections that are generic, unfeasible, extractive, or disconnected from the research.

## Core Rules

### Connect Broader Impacts To The Specific Research

The strongest broader impacts plans derive from the research itself rather than being bolted on. Connect them:

- identify what the research produces that could benefit society, knowledge, tools, methods, data;
- design impacts that flow from those products;
- avoid generic activities unrelated to the specific work;
- let the research inform the impact and the impact inform the research where possible.

A plan to "promote STEM education" attached to a project on a unrelated topic reads as generic. A plan that translates the project's specific findings into educational materials reads as integrated.

### Design For Credible, Evidence-Based Impact

Reviewers discount activities with no evidence of effectiveness. Design for impact:

- base activities on what is known to work in education, outreach, or engagement;
- cite evidence for the chosen approach where it exists;
- partner with practitioners who know the audience;
- avoid inventing novel approaches when established ones are more reliable;
- build in evaluation to learn whether the activity worked.

A plan that promises transformative outcomes from unproven activities is not credible. A plan that uses established approaches with realistic outcomes is.

### Make Activities Feasible Within Budget And Capacity

Broader impacts activities require time, money, and expertise. Make them feasible:

- budget realistically for the activities proposed;
- ensure the team has or partners with the needed expertise;
- do not over-promise relative to the project's scale;
- phase activities so they do not overwhelm the research;
- use institutional resources and existing programs where possible.

A plan that promises national transformation on a small budget signals overreach. A plan that scales activities to capacity signals realism.

### Partner Authentically With Communities And Audiences

Where impacts involve communities, schools, practitioners, or publics, partner authentically:

- engage partners in designing the activity, not just receiving it;
- respect partners' time and expertise, often with compensation;
- ensure the activity serves the partner's needs, not only the researcher's;
- avoid extractive relationships that take without giving back;
- plan for sustainability beyond the grant.

Extractive partnerships, where researchers take community access and deliver little, damage relationships and reputations and are increasingly recognized as unethical. Authentic partnership is slower but durable.

### Address Diversity, Equity, And Inclusion Substantively

Many broader impacts sections address broadening participation. Do so substantively:

- identify specific barriers the project will address;
- use evidence-based approaches to broadening participation;
- partner with institutions and programs serving underrepresented groups;
- avoid tokenism and one-off activities that cannot produce change;
- build pathways, mentoring, and support that can persist.

Generic statements about valuing diversity without specific, evidence-based activities are discounted. Substantive plans that address specific barriers are taken seriously.

### Integrate Education And Training Where Appropriate

Research projects often produce education and training impacts through the people they support. Integrate:

- support students and postdocs whose training advances their careers;
- involve undergraduates, especially from underrepresented groups, in research;
- develop courses or modules based on the research;
- create open educational resources where feasible;
- connect to existing educational programs rather than duplicating.

Education and training grounded in the research are credible and often the most sustainable impact.

### Plan For Dissemination Beyond Academia

Research findings often have audiences beyond academia who could use them. Plan dissemination:

- identify the practitioners, policymakers, or publics who could benefit;
- translate findings into accessible formats, briefs, tools, media;
- partner with intermediaries who reach those audiences;
- time dissemination to when decisions are being made;
- avoid assuming publication alone reaches non-academic audiences.

Dissemination that stops at journal publication reaches only academics. Broader impacts often require deliberate translation.

### Build In Evaluation And Learning

Broader impacts activities should be evaluated to learn whether they worked. Build in:

- clear intended outcomes for each activity;
- measures appropriate to the activity, not just participation counts;
- feedback from participants and partners;
- reflection and revision based on what is learned;
- honest reporting of what worked and what did not.

Activities with no evaluation cannot improve and cannot demonstrate impact. Evaluation need not be elaborate but should be genuine.

### Ensure Ethical Engagement

Broader impacts activities have ethical dimensions. Ensure:

- informed consent and appropriate review for activities involving people;
- respect for community knowledge and self-determination;
- avoidance of harm, including from well-intentioned interventions;
- transparency about goals and relationships;
- data and privacy protection for participants.

Ethical failures in outreach and engagement can cause real harm and damage trust that took years to build. Treat broader impacts activities with the same ethical rigor as the research.

### Align With Institutional And Funder Priorities Without Distortion

Broader impacts plans should align with funder priorities but not distort the research or the community work. Balance:

- address the funder's stated broader impacts criteria;
- use the funder's language where honest;
- do not invent impacts to fit fashionable priorities;
- maintain integrity of the research and the partnerships.

Alignment that distorts the work or the relationships undermines credibility. Align where honest and decline where not.

## Common Traps

### Generic Boilerplate

"Promote STEM education and diversity" without specifics is discounted. Connect to the specific research.

### Unproven Activities Promising Transformation

Activities with no evidence base promising large outcomes are not credible. Use evidence-based approaches.

### Over-Promising On Small Budgets

National transformation on a small budget signals overreach. Scale to capacity.

### Extractive Partnerships

Taking community access without giving back damages relationships and reputation. Partner authentically.

### Token Diversity Activities

One-off activities that cannot produce change are discounted. Build substantive pathways.

### Dissemination Stopping At Publication

Assuming journals reach non-academic audiences misses impact. Translate deliberately.

### No Evaluation

Activities without evaluation cannot demonstrate or improve impact. Build in genuine evaluation.

### Ethical Neglect

Outreach without ethical review can cause harm. Apply the same rigor as the research.

### Distortion For Funder Fit

Inventing impacts to fit priorities undermines credibility. Align where honest.

### Impacts Disconnected From Research

Bolted-on activities unrelated to the work read as generic. Integrate with the research.

### Unsustainable Activities

Activities that end with the grant produce little lasting benefit. Plan for sustainability.

## Self-Check

- Do the broader impacts derive from the specific research rather than being bolted on?
- Are activities based on evidence of effectiveness rather than unproven promises?
- Are activities feasible within the budget, timeline, and team capacity?
- Where communities are involved, are partnerships authentic, mutually beneficial, and non-extractive?
- Are diversity, equity, and inclusion addressed substantively with specific, evidence-based activities?
- Are education and training integrated with the research where appropriate?
- Is dissemination planned beyond academic publication to reach practitioners, policymakers, or publics?
- Is there genuine evaluation to learn whether activities produced their intended outcomes?
- Are ethical dimensions, consent, community respect, harm avoidance, addressed?
- Does the plan align with funder priorities without distorting the research or the partnerships?
- Is there a plan for sustainability beyond the grant period?
- Would a reviewer and a community partner both find this plan credible and respectful?
