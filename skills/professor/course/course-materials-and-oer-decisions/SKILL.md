# Course Materials And OER Decisions

The choice of course materials is simultaneously a pedagogical decision, an equity decision, and a financial decision, and instructors too often weigh only the first. A textbook that costs two hundred dollars is not merely a reading requirement; it is a barrier that causes some students to skip buying it, share outdated editions, or drop the course, and those students are disproportionately the ones with the least financial margin. Open educational resources, OER, remove that barrier but introduce their own judgment problems: quality varies enormously, alignment to learning outcomes is not guaranteed, and adoption requires more than swapping one text for another. The judgment problem is selecting course materials that serve learning while being affordable, accessible, and aligned, and making the commercial-versus-open decision deliberately rather than defaulting to whatever textbook was used last year or whatever the publisher's representative recommended.

Use this skill when selecting textbooks or readings, when evaluating or adopting OER, when addressing student access and affordability, when authoring or adapting open materials, or when managing licensing and copyright for course content. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating materials selection as a trivial choice, from adopting OER without evaluating quality and alignment, and from ignoring how cost and access shape who actually learns.

## Core Rules

### Treat Materials Selection As A Learning And Equity Decision

The materials an instructor assigns determine what students read, how much they pay, and whether they can access the content at all. A decision made casually, reusing last year's text or accepting a publisher's bundled package, carries consequences students bear. Treat materials selection with the same seriousness as learning outcome design, because the materials are where the outcomes meet the students.

Treat it seriously by:

- evaluating materials against learning outcomes, not against familiarity or convenience;
- considering who can and cannot afford the assigned materials;
- checking whether required materials are actually used in the course, or are required in name only;
- recognizing that cost shapes access, and access shapes learning.

A material students cannot afford is a material most of them will not read, regardless of how well it aligns.

### Evaluate OER Quality Before Adopting

OER solves the cost problem but does not automatically solve the quality problem. Open materials range from excellent to poor, and some are abandoned drafts, ideologically slanted, or riddled with errors. Adopting OER because it is free, without evaluating it against the same standards applied to commercial texts, trades a cost problem for a quality problem.

Evaluate OER by:

- reviewing the material for accuracy, currency, and completeness, as you would a commercial text;
- checking editorial oversight, peer review, and adoption history at other institutions;
- assessing alignment to your learning outcomes and student level;
- examining whether the material reflects current scholarship and inclusive perspectives;
- testing usability, navigation, and accessibility before assigning.

Free is not a quality argument. Apply the same rigor to OER as to any adopted material.

### Assess Total Student Cost And Access Impact

The listed price of a textbook is not the full cost students bear. Bundled access codes, homework platforms, printing, and edition churn inflate the real cost, and access codes that expire turn a purchase into a rental. Assess the total cost and how it affects access, especially for students with financial aid delays or limited funds at term start.

Assess total cost by:

- calculating the full cost including access codes, platforms, and required supplements;
- checking whether materials are reusable across the course sequence or expire;
- identifying whether lower-cost options, used, rental, or older editions, are viable;
- considering when in the term students must pay, and whether that aligns with aid disbursement;
- recognizing that even moderate costs force choices among courses for students with limited funds.

An access code that costs one hundred dollars and expires is a hidden fee that excludes students who cannot pay on day one.

### Verify Accessibility Before Adoption

Course materials must be accessible to students with disabilities, and accessibility varies widely. Publisher platforms often have accessibility gaps, scanned PDFs of book chapters are frequently unreadable by screen readers, and some OER lack structure or alt text. Adopting inaccessible materials shifts the burden onto the student who needs accommodation and may be impossible to remediate mid-term.

Verify accessibility by:

- requesting accessibility information or VPATs from publishers before adopting;
- checking whether digital materials are screen-reader compatible and keyboard navigable;
- ensuring videos have captions and readings have proper structure and alt text;
- avoiding scanned image PDFs in favor of born-digital, structured text;
- testing materials with accessibility checkers before assigning.

Inaccessible materials are not just non-compliant; they exclude specific students from the course.

### Align Materials To Learning Outcomes, Not Coverage

A common trap is selecting materials for coverage, ensuring all topics are represented, rather than for alignment to what students should learn. Comprehensive textbooks encourage coverage-oriented teaching that rushes through content without depth. Select materials that support the learning outcomes, even if that means assigning selected chapters or shorter, focused readings.

Align by:

- mapping materials to specific learning outcomes, not to a topic list;
- being willing to assign chapters selectively rather than assigning a whole text for completeness;
- choosing depth over coverage where outcomes emphasize understanding over exposure;
- supplementing a core text with targeted readings where the text is weak.

Coverage is not learning. Materials should serve outcomes, not signal thoroughness.

### Manage Copyright And Licensing Deliberately

Course readings drawn from copyrighted sources require copyright management, through fair use, library licensing, or permissions. Defaulting to uploading PDFs without analysis risks copyright violation, while over-caution denies students legitimate access under fair use. Manage copyright deliberately, understanding the boundaries and using library resources such as e-reserves and licensed databases.

Manage copyright by:

- applying fair use analysis to copyrighted readings rather than assuming permission or prohibition;
- using library-licensed e-resources and e-reserves where available, which handle licensing;
- respecting license terms on OER, such as Creative Commons attribution and share-alike requirements;
- seeking permissions where fair use does not apply;
- documenting decisions so they are defensible and repeatable.

Fair use is a judgment, not a blank check, and ignoring it is not safer than using it carefully.

### Plan For Maintenance And Currency

Materials age. A textbook edition becomes outdated, an OER's links rot, a dataset becomes stale, and scholarship moves on. Adopting materials without a maintenance plan leads to courses taught from outdated content. Plan for updates as part of the adoption decision.

Plan for maintenance by:

- checking edition cycles for commercial texts and anticipating updates;
- reviewing OER periodically for broken links, outdated content, and scholarship currency;
- building in a review cycle so materials are revisited, not adopted permanently;
- preferring materials with active maintenance or community updates where possible.

A material adopted once and never reviewed teaches the past.

### Involve Students In Material Feedback

Students are the primary users of course materials, yet their experience is rarely solicited. They know which readings were useful, which were impenetrable, which platforms caused friction, and which they skipped. Gathering student feedback on materials improves future selection and surfaces access problems the instructor did not notice.

Involve students by:

- asking which readings and resources students found most and least useful;
- surveying access problems, cost burden, and platform usability;
- using feedback to revise the next iteration's material choices;
- treating students as informants about their own learning, not just recipients of assigned content.

Materials chosen without student input are chosen blind to how they are actually used.

## Common Traps

### Selecting Materials Casually

Reusing last year's text or accepting a publisher package without evaluation ignores pedagogy, cost, and access. Evaluate deliberately.

### Adopting OER For Cost Alone

Free does not mean good. Evaluate OER quality, alignment, and accessibility as rigorously as commercial materials.

### Ignoring Total Cost And Access Codes

Listed price hides the real burden of access codes, platforms, and expiration. Assess total cost and timing.

### Adopting Inaccessible Materials

Inaccessible platforms and scanned PDFs exclude students with disabilities. Verify accessibility before adoption.

### Selecting For Coverage Rather Than Outcomes

Comprehensive texts encourage coverage-oriented teaching. Align materials to learning outcomes, even selectively.

### Uploading Copyrighted Readings Without Analysis

Fair use requires judgment, not assumption. Use library licensing and apply fair use deliberately.

### Adopting Once And Never Reviewing

Materials age. Plan maintenance and currency reviews.

### Ignoring Student Feedback On Materials

Students know what worked and what failed. Solicit and use their feedback.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Materials are evaluated against learning outcomes, not selected for familiarity or coverage.
- [ ] OER, if adopted, has been evaluated for quality, accuracy, alignment, and accessibility, not just cost.
- [ ] Total student cost, including access codes and platforms, has been assessed and minimized where possible.
- [ ] All materials are accessible to students with disabilities, verified before adoption.
- [ ] Materials support depth aligned to outcomes rather than superficial coverage.
- [ ] Copyright is managed deliberately through fair use analysis, library licensing, or permissions.
- [ ] A maintenance and currency review plan exists for the adopted materials.
- [ ] Student feedback on materials has been solicited and used to inform future selection.
- [ ] No required material is assigned that is not actually used in the course.
- [ ] The materials decision considered equity and access, not only pedagogy.
