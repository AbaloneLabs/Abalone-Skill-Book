# Multi-Section And Large Course Coordination

When a course scales beyond a single instructor and a single room, the central challenge stops being pedagogy in the abstract and becomes coordination. A multi-section course with several instructors, or a large-enrollment course with teaching assistants and graders, can deliver wildly different experiences depending on which section a student lands in. One section gets rigorous, consistent grading; another gets lenient or erratic marks. One TA holds generous office hours; another is unreachable. One instructor covers the core methods; another drifts into personal interests. These inconsistencies are not minor irritations; they produce grade inequity, accreditation problems, integrity disputes, and a legitimate student sense that the course is unfair. Coordination is the deliberate work of making a multi-section or large course behave like a coherent course rather than a collection of independent fiefdoms.

Use this skill before coordinating a multi-section course, managing teaching assistants or graders, running a large-enrollment course, designing shared assessments, ensuring grading consistency, or scaling engagement and support. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating sections or TAs as independent operators and producing a course whose experience and standards vary unpredictably across students.

## Core Rules

### Decide What Must Be Shared And What May Vary

Not everything in a multi-section course should be identical, but the boundaries must be deliberate. Distinguish:

- shared elements: core learning outcomes, major assessments, grading standards, key policies, required materials, core topic coverage;
- locally flexible elements: teaching style, activity choice, examples, pacing within bounds, supplementary readings, discussion topics.

A course where nothing is shared is really several different courses under one number, producing inequity. A course where everything is identical straitjackets instructors and suppresses the strengths of diverse teachers. Define the shared core explicitly and protect it, while allowing principled local variation.

### Build A Shared Course Infrastructure

Coordination requires common infrastructure. Establish:

- a shared syllabus template with required and optional sections clearly marked;
- a common assessment set, or a common assessment bank with equivalent items;
- shared rubrics for all major assignments;
- a shared LMS shell or clearly mirrored structure;
- a common gradebook scheme and weighting;
- shared policies on attendance, integrity, late work, and accommodation.

When each section invents its own infrastructure, consistency becomes impossible to verify or enforce. Shared infrastructure makes the course legible to coordinators and fair to students.

### Calibrate Graders Before And During Grading

Grading inconsistency is the most common and damaging failure of multi-section and large courses. Calibrate deliberately:

- hold grading norming sessions before major assignments are scored;
- have all graders score the same sample papers and compare;
- discuss disagreements and refine the rubric until scoring converges;
- use double-scoring on a sample of work to monitor drift;
- designate a head grader or coordinator to adjudicate borderline cases;
- re-norm after the first major assignment, when real patterns emerge.

Without calibration, two students with identical work can receive very different grades depending on their section. Calibration is the single highest-value coordination activity.

### Define TA And Grader Roles Explicitly

Teaching assistants and graders need clear roles to avoid both exploitation and underuse. Specify:

- what each role is responsible for, grading, leading discussion, holding office hours, proctoring;
- expected hours per week and how to handle overflow;
- what decisions they can make independently versus what requires instructor sign-off;
- how they communicate with students and on whose behalf;
- how they handle integrity concerns, accommodations, and disputes;
- how their performance is evaluated and supported.

Ambiguous roles produce TAs who either overstep or disengage. Clear roles produce reliable support and a better student experience.

### Coordinate Content Coverage Across Sections

Students in different sections should leave with comparable preparation, especially when the course is a prerequisite. Coordinate:

- a shared list of required topics and skills;
- agreed sequencing so later courses can rely on prior coverage;
- common core problems, cases, or readings;
- a shared understanding of depth expected for each topic;
- regular check-ins on pacing so sections do not diverge widely.

A section that skips a core method because the instructor found it dull leaves its students unprepared for the next course. Coordinate coverage to protect the curriculum downstream.

### Manage Communication Across Instructors And TAs

Coordination fails when communication is ad hoc. Establish:

- regular meetings of the instructional team, weekly or biweekly;
- shared documentation of decisions, policies, and changes;
- a channel for urgent questions and consistent answers;
- a process for handling student issues that cross sections;
- a way to surface and resolve disagreements among instructors.

Ad hoc communication produces inconsistent answers to students and quiet drift between sections. Structured communication keeps the course coherent.

### Ensure Equity In Student Experience And Support

Students should not receive a worse education because of which section or TA they were assigned. Monitor:

- whether office hours, response times, and support are equitable across sections;
- whether grading outcomes differ systematically by section or grader;
- whether resources and materials reach all students equally;
- whether accommodations are implemented consistently;
- whether complaints cluster in particular sections, signaling a problem.

Systematic differences across sections are an equity issue, not just a quality issue. Track them and intervene.

### Scale Engagement For Large Enrollment

Large courses cannot rely on the intimacy of a seminar, but they need not be passive. Scale engagement deliberately:

- use structured active learning that works at scale, polling, think-pair-share, structured discussion;
- deploy TAs to facilitate small groups within the large room;
- use discussion sections or labs to create smaller communities;
- create online spaces for questions and peer support;
- use early-alert systems to identify struggling students;
- protect some instructor contact through office hours, targeted outreach, or staged access.

A large course that abandons engagement produces anonymity and disengagement. A large course that engineers engagement can still feel connected.

### Handle Integrity, Accommodation, And Disputes Consistently

These high-stakes issues must be handled the same way across sections. Ensure:

- integrity cases follow the same process and standards everywhere;
- accommodations are implemented consistently and respectfully;
- grade disputes and appeals follow a shared process;
- exceptions and extensions are granted on consistent criteria;
- documentation of decisions is kept for all sections.

Inconsistent handling of these issues is where multi-section courses generate formal complaints and legal risk. Consistency protects both students and the institution.

### Evaluate The Course As A Whole, Not Only By Section

At the end of the term, evaluate the multi-section course as a system. Examine:

- whether outcomes were attained comparably across sections;
- whether grading distributions differ in ways that signal inconsistency;
- whether student feedback reveals section-level problems;
- whether coverage and preparation were consistent enough for downstream courses;
- what coordination changes would improve the next offering.

Section-only evaluation misses systemic problems. Whole-course evaluation reveals whether coordination is actually producing a coherent course.

## Common Traps

### Treating Sections As Independent

Letting each instructor run their own course under one number produces inequity. Define and protect the shared core.

### Inventing Infrastructure Per Section

Separate syllabi, assessments, and rubrics make consistency unverifiable. Build shared infrastructure.

### Skipping Grader Calibration

Uncalibrated graders produce different grades for identical work. Calibrate before and during grading.

### Ambiguous TA Roles

Unclear roles lead to overstepping or disengagement. Define roles explicitly.

### Drifting Content Coverage

Sections that skip or rush core topics leave students unprepared downstream. Coordinate coverage.

### Ad Hoc Communication

Unstructured communication produces inconsistent answers and drift. Meet and document regularly.

### Ignoring Section-Level Equity

Systematic differences in support or grading across sections are equity failures. Monitor and intervene.

### Passive Large Courses

Abandoning engagement at scale produces anonymity. Engineer engagement deliberately.

### Inconsistent Integrity And Accommodation Handling

Different processes across sections generate complaints and risk. Standardize high-stakes handling.

### Section-Only Evaluation

Evaluating only by section misses systemic coordination problems. Evaluate the whole course.

## Self-Check

- Is there a clearly defined shared core of outcomes, assessments, rubrics, policies, and coverage, with principled room for local variation?
- Is shared infrastructure, syllabus template, LMS shell, assessment bank, gradebook scheme, in place across all sections?
- Are graders calibrated before and during grading, with norming sessions and drift monitoring?
- Are TA and grader roles, hours, authority, and evaluation defined explicitly?
- Is content coverage coordinated so students leave comparably prepared, especially as a prerequisite?
- Does the instructional team communicate through regular meetings and shared documentation rather than ad hoc?
- Is student experience, support, and grading monitored for equity across sections?
- Is engagement engineered for scale rather than abandoned in large enrollment?
- Are integrity, accommodation, and dispute processes consistent across all sections?
- Is the course evaluated as a whole at term end, not only section by section?
