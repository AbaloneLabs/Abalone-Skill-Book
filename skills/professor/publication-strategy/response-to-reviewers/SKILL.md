# Response To Reviewers

Peer review is the gatekeeper of scholarly publication, and how an author responds to review determines whether months of work reach publication or stall in revision cycles. A revision is not a negotiation where the author defends their work against attack, nor is it capitulation where the author makes every change requested. It is a reasoned engagement with critique in which the author decides which changes strengthen the work, makes them clearly, and defends the rest with substance. Authors who respond defensively, dismissively, or chaotically provoke rejection of work that could have been published; authors who respond with substance and clarity often convert skeptical reviewers into advocates.

Use this skill before responding to reviews, planning a revision, writing a response to reviewers, or handling a difficult review. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing responses that antagonize reviewers, concede too much, or fail to make the changes that would have secured acceptance.

## Core Rules

### Read The Reviews Carefully Before Responding

The first response to reviews should be reading, not writing. Reviews often contain more substance than a first emotional reading captures. Practices:

- read all reviews slowly and completely;
- separate the emotional reaction from the substantive content;
- identify the core concern behind each comment, even when poorly expressed;
- distinguish major issues that threaten the contribution from minor requests;
- look for the underlying standard the reviewer is applying.

A response written in reaction to tone rather than substance misses the chance to address the real concerns. Read for the argument beneath the wording.

### Categorize Comments By Type Of Response Needed

Not every comment requires the same response. Categorize:

- accept and revise: changes that strengthen the work, make them;
- partially accept: revise where the reviewer is right, defend where not;
- defend with substance: the reviewer is wrong, explain why with evidence;
- clarify: the reviewer misunderstood, revise the text to prevent the misunderstanding;
- out of scope: the request exceeds the paper's purpose, explain respectfully.

Categorizing prevents two failures: capitulating to comments that should be defended, and defending comments that should be accepted.

### Make A Revision Plan Before Writing The Response

Before drafting the response, plan the revision:

- list each comment and the planned response;
- identify which require textual changes and where;
- note where changes require new analysis or data;
- estimate the work and timeline;
- ensure the plan addresses every comment, including defenses.

A revision plan ensures no comment is missed and that the response is coherent rather than ad hoc. It also reveals whether the revision is feasible in the time allowed.

### Write A Response That Is Respectful, Specific, And Substantive

The response to reviewers is read as evidence of the author's scholarly maturity. Write it to:

- thank the reviewers for substantive engagement;
- address each comment specifically, by number or quote;
- state the change made and where in the manuscript;
- explain defenses with evidence and reasoning, not assertion;
- avoid sarcasm, defensiveness, or dismissing the reviewer.

Reviewers are often the same people who will decide the next cycle. A respectful, substantive response builds goodwill; a hostile one hardens opposition. Even when a reviewer is wrong, engage the argument seriously.

### Distinguish Reviewer Preferences From Requirements

Reviewers often express preferences as requirements. Distinguish:

- changes required for validity, accuracy, or to meet the contribution's claims;
- changes that would strengthen the work but are not required;
- preferences about style, framing, or scope that reflect the reviewer's taste.

Make required changes fully. Consider strengthening changes on their merits. Decline preference changes respectfully when they do not serve the work, explaining why.

### Defend With Evidence, Not Assertion

When you decline a change, defend with substance. Practices:

- cite specific evidence or literature that supports your position;
- explain the reasoning that leads to the current choice;
- acknowledge the reviewer's concern and why it does not change the conclusion;
- propose a clarification that addresses the underlying worry;
- avoid "we disagree" without substance.

A defense that rests on the author's authority alone fails. A defense that engages the reviewer's reasoning with evidence often succeeds, even when the reviewer initially disagreed.

### Revise The Manuscript To Address Underlying Concerns

Often the best response to a reviewer's specific comment is a manuscript revision that addresses the underlying concern more broadly. Practices:

- if a reviewer misunderstood a method, revise the method section for clarity;
- if a reviewer wants a limitation acknowledged, add a limitations discussion;
- if a reviewer questions generalizability, frame the scope explicitly;
- if a reviewer finds a claim overstated, soften and qualify it.

Addressing the underlying concern prevents the same comment from recurring and strengthens the work, rather than just satisfying one reviewer.

### Handle Contradictory Reviews Deliberately

When reviewers contradict each other, do not simply pick one. Practices:

- identify the underlying standards each is applying;
- consider whether both concerns can be addressed, perhaps by clarifying scope;
- explain to the editor how you navigated the contradiction;
- make the choice that best serves the work's contribution.

Contradictory reviews are common and are one reason the editor, not the reviewers, makes the decision. Show the editor you engaged both seriously.

### Address The Editor's Concerns Explicitly

The editor, not the reviewers, decides acceptance. Address the editor's concerns explicitly:

- identify what the editor flagged in the decision letter;
- respond to the editor's framing of the reviews;
- summarize how the revision addresses the editor's priorities;
- note where reviewer comments were balanced against each other.

An editor who sees their concerns addressed is more likely to accept, even if not every reviewer request was met.

### Manage The Timeline And Communicate

Revisions take time, and extensions are often available. Practices:

- assess realistically whether the revision can be done in the allowed time;
- request an extension promptly if needed, with a credible plan;
- communicate proactively rather than letting the deadline pass silently;
- submit a complete revision that addresses every comment.

Silent missed deadlines can lead to withdrawal and resubmission, losing the review history. Communicate.

### Use Rejection As Information

If the work is rejected, use the reviews as information. The concerns raised often reveal how the field will receive the work. Revise based on the substance before submitting elsewhere, and choose the next venue in light of what the reviews revealed. Rejection after one cycle is not failure; it is feedback that can strengthen the work for the right venue.

## Common Traps

### Defensive Or Hostile Response

Sarcasm and dismissal harden reviewer opposition. Respond respectfully and substantively.

### Capitulation To Every Request

Making every requested change can weaken the work and signals lack of judgment. Defend where right with evidence.

### Missing Comments

Failing to address every comment signals carelessness. Use a revision plan to cover all.

### Assertion Without Evidence

"We disagree" without substance fails. Defend with evidence and reasoning.

### Surface Fixes Only

Satisfying a specific comment without addressing the underlying concern invites recurrence. Revise broadly.

### Ignoring Contradictory Reviews

Picking one reviewer over another without explanation loses the editor's confidence. Engage both.

### Neglecting The Editor

Addressing only reviewers and not the editor misses the decision-maker. Address editor concerns explicitly.

### Silent Missed Deadlines

Letting deadlines pass without communication can lose the review history. Communicate proactively.

### Tone Matching Reviewer Tone

Responding in kind to a hostile review damages your standing. Maintain professional tone regardless.

### Treating Rejection As Endpoint

Discarding reviews after rejection wastes the feedback. Use it to strengthen the work for the next venue.

## Self-Check

- Have all reviews been read carefully for substance beneath tone before drafting a response?
- Has each comment been categorized as accept, partially accept, defend, clarify, or out of scope?
- Was a revision plan made before writing the response, covering every comment?
- Is the response respectful, specific, and substantive, citing changes made and where?
- Are reviewer preferences distinguished from requirements, with preferences declined respectfully when they do not serve the work?
- Are defenses supported by evidence and reasoning rather than assertion?
- Does the manuscript revision address underlying concerns, not just surface comments?
- Are contradictory reviews engaged seriously with an explanation to the editor?
- Are the editor's specific concerns and framing addressed explicitly?
- Is the timeline managed with proactive communication and extension requests if needed?
- If rejected, will the reviews be used to strengthen the work before the next submission?
- Would an editor reading this response conclude the author engaged the reviews seriously and improved the work?
