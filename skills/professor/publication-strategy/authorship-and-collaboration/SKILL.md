# Authorship And Collaboration

Authorship is the currency of academic credit, and because it is currency, it is also the most common source of conflict in research collaboration. Who is listed, in what order, and with what acknowledged role determines who gets hired, promoted, funded, and cited for years after the work is done. Yet authorship decisions are often made late, informally, and under assumptions that differ across fields, labs, generations, and cultures. A senior researcher may assume that supplying data or funding earns a name; a junior researcher may assume that only those who wrote the manuscript deserve credit; a collaborator from another discipline may expect alphabetical ordering. When these assumptions collide, the result is resentment, disputes that delay publication, and sometimes formal misconduct findings for gift, ghost, or coerced authorship. The work of authorship is not just deciding a byline; it is making the credit architecture of a collaboration explicit, fair, and defensible before it becomes a dispute.

Use this skill before forming a collaboration, deciding author order, writing a contribution statement, managing a team of contributors, or resolving an authorship disagreement. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing authorship arrangements that are ambiguous, inequitable, field-inappropriate, or vulnerable to challenge as unethical.

## Core Rules

### Agree On Authorship Expectations Early And In Writing

The single most effective practice is to discuss authorship at the start of a collaboration, not at the end. At the outset, teams should agree on who is likely to be an author, what the likely order is, and what would change it. This is not a binding contract, because the work will evolve, but it establishes shared expectations and surfaces divergent assumptions while they can still be discussed calmly. Revisit the agreement at major milestones, when a new contributor joins, when the scope shifts, or when a draft takes shape. Late, vague authorship discussions are where most disputes originate.

### Apply A Defensible Authorship Standard

Use a recognized standard for who qualifies as an author, and state which one you are using. The most widely cited is that authorship requires substantial contribution to the conception or design, or to analysis and interpretation, plus drafting or critically revising the work, plus final approval, plus accountability for the work. Mere data collection, routine technical help, provision of funding or materials, or general supervision does not by itself meet this bar. Such contributions belong in the acknowledgments, not the byline. Applying a standard consistently protects the team from both over-crediting, listing someone who did not contribute substantively, and under-crediting, omitting someone who did.

### Understand That Author Order Means Different Things In Different Fields

Author order conventions vary, and a collaboration across fields must reconcile them. In many lab and biomedical fields, the first author did the most work, the last author is the senior investigator who led and funded the project, and middle authors contributed substantially but less than the first. In some fields, co-first or co-last authorship marks equal contribution. In mathematics, theoretical physics, and some humanities fields, authors are often listed alphabetically, and order does not signal contribution. In some areas, reverse alphabetical or contribution-based ordering applies. Do not assume your field's convention is universal. In a cross-disciplinary team, decide the convention explicitly and explain it in the paper or cover letter if it could be misread.

### Distinguish Authorship From Acknowledgment

Not every helpful contribution earns authorship. Acknowledgments are the proper place for funding sources, infrastructure, technical assistance, editorial help, data provision by third parties, and general institutional support. The line is contribution to the intellectual substance of the work. Someone who ran assays but did not shape the question or interpretation is acknowledged; someone who shaped the analysis is an author. Be generous in acknowledgment but rigorous in authorship, because inflating the byline dilutes the credit of genuine contributors and can constitute misconduct.

### Avoid Gift, Ghost, And Coerced Authorship

Three practices are widely treated as unethical and can trigger misconduct findings. Gift authorship lists someone who did not contribute, out of generosity, obligation, or politics. Ghost authorship omits someone who did contribute substantially, often a paid writer or a contributor whose involvement is inconvenient to disclose. Coerced authorship demands credit for someone, often a senior figure, who did not earn it, leveraging power over junior researchers. All three corrupt the integrity of the byline. If a name does not reflect genuine contribution, it should not be there, regardless of seniority or expectation.

### Use Contribution Statements To Make Credit Transparent

Many journals now require a contribution statement specifying what each author did. Write it honestly and specifically, not as boilerplate. A clear statement, who conceived the study, who collected data, who analyzed, who wrote which sections, who secured funding, lets readers and evaluators understand the actual division of labor. It also disciplines the team to confront whether each listed author genuinely contributed, because the statement must be defensible. Treat the contribution statement as a record of truth, not a formality.

### Handle Disputes Through Process, Not Power

When authorship disagreements arise, resolve them through a defined process rather than by seniority. Revisit the contribution standard, review what each person actually did, and consider the field's conventions. Where the dispute cannot be resolved internally, consult the department chair, a research integrity officer, or the journal's editor before submission. Submitting a paper with an unresolved authorship dispute risks a formal complaint that can halt or retract the publication. It is better to delay and resolve than to submit and fight.

### Plan For Authorship Across A Project's Lifetime

A collaboration often produces multiple papers, and authorship should be planned across the project, not paper by paper. Agree on a principle for how credit will be distributed across the set of outputs, for example, that the lead role rotates, that each paper's first author is whoever led that specific study, or that a core team appears on all papers. Planning the arc prevents the situation where the same person is first author on everything by default and others feel their contributions were never recognized.

### Manage Credit For Junior Researchers And Students

Junior researchers, postdocs, and students are the most vulnerable to authorship inequity, because power asymmetries make it hard for them to claim credit they have earned. Be deliberate about ensuring their substantive contributions are recognized with appropriate authorship, often first authorship when they led the work. Mentoring includes teaching them how authorship works, advocating for their credit in collaborations, and not absorbing their work into a senior byline. Conversely, do not inflate student authorship beyond their contribution, which teaches the wrong lesson and dilutes the byline.

### Coordinate Corresponding Authorship And Ongoing Responsibilities

The corresponding author handles submission, communication with the journal, and often the integrity of the final manuscript. Decide who this is deliberately, because it carries ongoing responsibility through revision, publication, and post-publication queries, sometimes for years. The corresponding author is not necessarily the first or last author. Ensure the corresponding author has the full picture of the work and the authority to represent the team.

## Common Traps

### Late, Vague Authorship Discussions

Deciding the byline only when the draft is finished guarantees disputes. Agree on expectations early and revisit them.

### Assuming Your Field's Convention Is Universal

Author order means different things across disciplines. In cross-field teams, state the convention explicitly.

### Gift Authorship For Seniority Or Politics

Listing someone who did not contribute, however senior, is unethical and can trigger misconduct findings.

### Ghosting A Real Contributor

Omitting someone who contributed substantially, including paid writers, corrupts the byline and is misconduct.

### Coerced Authorship By Power

Demanding credit through leverage over junior researchers is unethical and damages the team.

### Inflating The Byline

Listing routine technical or funding help as authors dilutes the credit of genuine contributors. Use acknowledgments.

### Boilerplate Contribution Statements

Vague contribution statements hide the real division of labor and let unearned authorship pass. Write them specifically.

### Resolving Disputes By Seniority

Letting power rather than contribution decide authorship produces resentment and risks formal complaints. Use a process.

### Default First Authorship For Everything

One person leading every paper by default leaves others' contributions unrecognized across a project. Plan the arc of credit.

### Under-Crediting Juniors

Power asymmetries let junior contributions be absorbed into senior bylines. Advocate for the credit they earned.

## Self-Check

- Were authorship expectations discussed early in the collaboration and revisited at milestones, rather than only at submission?
- Is a recognized authorship standard being applied consistently, with non-substantive contributions placed in acknowledgments?
- Is the author-order convention understood and, where it could be misread across fields, explained in the paper or cover letter?
- Is the byline free of gift, ghost, and coerced authorship, with every name reflecting genuine intellectual contribution?
- Does the contribution statement specify each author's role honestly and specifically, rather than as boilerplate?
- If a dispute has arisen, is it being resolved through a defined process and consultation, not by seniority?
- Has authorship been planned across the project's lifetime, so credit is distributed fairly across multiple outputs?
- Are junior researchers and students receiving authorship that reflects their substantive contributions, with credit advocated for rather than absorbed?
- Has the corresponding author been chosen deliberately, with the standing to represent the team through and after publication?
- Could each author, if asked, defend their presence on the byline by pointing to specific contributions to the work?
