# Manuscript Preparation

A manuscript is the vehicle through which research reaches a field, and its quality determines whether strong research is recognized. Reviewers and readers cannot see the years of work behind a submission; they see only the pages in front of them, and a manuscript that buries the contribution in jargon, lacks a clear argument, presents methods unclearly, or ignores the venue's conventions will be rejected or ignored regardless of the underlying quality. Manuscript preparation is not formatting; it is the work of making research intelligible, persuasive, and usable to the audience that should build on it.

Use this skill before drafting or revising a manuscript for submission, structuring the paper, writing for a target venue, or finalizing a submission. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing manuscripts that are research-complete but reader-hostile, or that fail to make the contribution visible.

## Core Rules

### Make The Contribution Visible Early And Often

The most common manuscript failure is burying the contribution. Readers and reviewers decide quickly whether a paper is worth their time, so the contribution must be visible early and restated throughout.

Practices:

- state the contribution in the abstract, not just the topic;
- frame the introduction around the gap and the contribution that fills it;
- restate the contribution at the end of the introduction;
- connect each section back to the contribution;
- summarize the contribution in the conclusion with its significance.

A paper where the contribution is discoverable only on page twelve will be rejected before page twelve is read. Make it impossible to miss.

### Structure The Paper To Serve The Argument

Structure is not a template to fill; it is the architecture of the argument. Structure so that:

- the introduction establishes the question, gap, and contribution;
- the background or literature positions the work and establishes the gap;
- the methods establish credibility and reproducibility;
- the results present the evidence;
- the discussion interprets, connects to literature, and acknowledges limitations;
- the conclusion states the contribution and significance.

Adapt the structure to the field and venue conventions, but ensure each section advances the argument rather than merely reporting what was done.

### Write The Introduction As A Funnel To The Contribution

The introduction moves the reader from the broad importance of the area to the specific contribution. A strong introduction:

- establishes why the area matters;
- identifies the specific gap or problem;
- states how the paper addresses it;
- previews the contribution and approach;
- maps the paper for the reader.

A common failure is an introduction that reviews the field without converging on the gap, leaving the reader unsure what the paper claims. Converge.

### Position The Work Against Real Literature

Literature positioning is not a comprehensive review; it is a strategic placement of the work in a conversation. Position by:

- identifying the specific conversation the work enters;
- citing the closest existing work and showing how this differs;
- acknowledging the foundations the work builds on;
- avoiding both overclaiming novelty and underclaiming contribution;
- engaging genuinely with work that complicates the contribution.

A literature section that lists papers without engaging them signals the author has not positioned the work. Positioning requires argument, not citation count.

### Present Methods For Reproducibility And Credibility

Methods should let a qualified reader evaluate the work and, where relevant, reproduce it. Present:

- the design and its rationale;
- data sources, sampling, and access;
- measures, instruments, and their validation;
- analytical procedures and software;
- assumptions and how they were checked;
- ethical approvals and consent where relevant;
- enough detail to evaluate validity without overwhelming.

Methods too thin to evaluate invite rejection on credibility grounds. Methods so dense they are unreadable invite rejection on clarity. Aim for evaluability.

### Report Results Honestly And Clearly

Results should report what was found without spin. Practices:

- report effect sizes and uncertainty, not just significance;
- distinguish the planned analysis from exploratory or post hoc analyses;
- present results in an order that serves the argument;
- use tables and figures that are readable and self-contained;
- avoid selective reporting that overstates findings;
- report null and unexpected results honestly.

Results spun to imply more than they show damage credibility when readers look closely, and many readers look closely.

### Write The Discussion As Interpretation, Not Repetition

The discussion interprets results and connects them to the field. A strong discussion:

- restates the contribution in light of the findings;
- connects findings to the closest existing work;
- acknowledges limitations honestly;
- considers alternative explanations;
- identifies implications and future directions;
- avoids overclaiming what the study shows.

A discussion that merely repeats the results wastes the section's purpose. Use it to establish what the findings mean.

### Match The Venue's Conventions And Audience

Each venue has conventions for structure, length, terminology, and citation. Match them:

- read recent papers in the target venue for structure and style;
- use the venue's preferred terminology and citation format;
- respect word or page limits;
- follow the venue's reporting norms for the field;
- write for the venue's audience, specialist or generalist.

A manuscript that ignores venue conventions signals the author did not engage with the venue, which biases reviewers against it.

### Revise For Clarity At The Sentence And Paragraph Level

Clarity is not optional; it determines whether the work is understood. Revise for:

- one idea per sentence;
- topic sentences that signal the paragraph's point;
- active voice and concrete subjects where possible;
- defined terms used consistently;
- removal of hedging that obscures the claim;
- elimination of jargon that excludes the audience.

Clear writing is not dumbing down; it is respecting the reader's attention. Dense, jargon-laden prose loses readers regardless of the work's quality.

### Ensure Ethical And Reporting Standards Before Submission

Before submission, confirm:

- accurate and complete authorship and contributions;
- disclosure of conflicts of interest and funding;
- ethical approvals and consent documentation;
- responsible data reporting without fabrication or selective omission;
- compliance with reporting standards in the field;
- availability of data and materials where claimed.

Ethical failures discovered post-publication are far more damaging than pre-submission corrections. Check before, not after.

### Get Feedback Before Submission

Internal review catches problems that the author is blind to. Before submission:

- have co-authors read and revise;
- seek feedback from colleagues outside the immediate team;
- use a writing group or mentor;
- revise based on feedback, especially on the contribution's clarity;
- allow time between drafting and final revision for fresh eyes.

A manuscript submitted without internal review carries avoidable weaknesses. Feedback is part of preparation, not an admission of weakness.

## Common Traps

### Burying The Contribution

Hiding the contribution deep in the paper guarantees it is missed. State it early and often.

### Template Structure Without Argument

Filling sections without advancing the argument produces a report, not a paper. Structure to serve the argument.

### Introduction That Does Not Converge

A literature-heavy introduction that never reaches the gap loses the reader. Converge to the contribution.

### Citation Listing Without Positioning

Listing papers without engaging them signals poor positioning. Argue against the literature.

### Methods Too Thin Or Too Dense

Methods that cannot be evaluated or cannot be read both invite rejection. Aim for evaluability.

### Spun Results

Overstating findings damages credibility with careful readers. Report honestly.

### Discussion As Repetition

Repeating results in the discussion wastes the section. Interpret and connect.

### Ignoring Venue Conventions

Flouting the venue's format signals disengagement. Match conventions.

### Dense, Jargon-Laden Prose

Unclear writing loses readers regardless of quality. Revise for clarity.

### Skipping Internal Review

Submitting without feedback carries avoidable weaknesses. Get feedback first.

### Ethical Gaps Discovered Late

Ethical problems found post-publication are far worse than pre-submission fixes. Check before submitting.

## Self-Check

- Is the contribution stated in the abstract, introduction, and conclusion so it cannot be missed?
- Does the structure serve the argument rather than merely report what was done?
- Does the introduction funnel from the area's importance to the specific gap and contribution?
- Is the work positioned against real literature through argument, not citation listing?
- Are methods presented with enough detail to evaluate validity and, where relevant, reproduce?
- Are results reported honestly with effect sizes, uncertainty, and null findings?
- Does the discussion interpret findings, connect to literature, and acknowledge limitations?
- Does the manuscript match the target venue's conventions, audience, and format?
- Has the prose been revised for clarity at sentence and paragraph level?
- Have ethical standards, authorship, conflicts, approvals, and data availability been confirmed before submission?
- Has the manuscript received internal feedback and revision before submission?
- Would a busy reviewer understand the contribution within the first two pages?
