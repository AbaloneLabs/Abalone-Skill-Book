# Prerequisite And Sequencing Logic

Prerequisites and sequencing are the load-bearing structure of a curriculum. When they are well designed, each course receives students prepared for its content and delivers preparation for the next. When they are poorly designed, advanced courses spend weeks re-teaching foundations, students hit walls mid-course, and instructors blame either the students or the prior course. Most sequencing problems are not student deficits; they are design failures where the stated prerequisite does not actually deliver what the next course assumes.

Use this skill before setting prerequisites, ordering a course sequence, deciding topic sequence within a course, or diagnosing preparation gaps. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing sequences that look logical on a catalog page but break down in practice because the assumed preparation is not guaranteed.

## Core Rules

### Distinguish True Prerequisites From Recommended Background

A true prerequisite is knowledge or skill without which a student cannot succeed and which the prior course is guaranteed to deliver. Recommended background is helpful but not essential. Conflating the two pollutes enrollment data and blocks students unnecessarily.

For each prerequisite claim, ask:

- is this knowledge essential to begin the course, or merely helpful;
- does the named prior course actually teach it to the required level;
- could a student acquire it another way (placement exam, self-study, transfer);
- what happens if a student lacks it: failure, struggle, or mild disadvantage.

Reserve prerequisite status for true essentials. Use corequisites or recommended background for the rest.

### Verify That Prerequisites Actually Deliver The Assumed Preparation

The most common sequencing failure is the aspirational prerequisite: a course lists another as required, but the prior course does not reliably teach the needed content, or teaches it at a lower level than assumed. Verify by examining the prior course's actual outcomes, assessments, and student work.

Verification steps:

- list the specific knowledge and skills the advanced course assumes;
- map each to the prior course's outcomes and assessments;
- confirm the prior course teaches them to the required cognitive level;
- check that the prior course's assessments actually require demonstrating them.

If the chain breaks, either strengthen the prior course, lower the advanced course's assumptions, or add a bridge.

### Sequence Topics By Cognitive Dependency, Not Topic Affinity

Within and across courses, order topics so that each builds on what precedes it. Cognitive dependency means a concept or skill is needed to learn the next one. Topic affinity means concepts are related but not sequentially dependent.

Dependency-driven sequencing:

- teach methods before requiring their application;
- teach foundational concepts before theories that use them;
- teach critique skills before requiring independent analysis;
- teach safety and ethics before lab or field work that needs them.

Affinity-driven sequencing (grouping related topics) can feel coherent but leave students unable to do the work because a needed precursor came later.

### Identify And Address Bottleneck Courses

A bottleneck course is one that gates progression: many students cannot proceed until they pass it, and its failure rate is high. Bottlenecks may be inherent to the discipline or may be artifacts of poor sequencing, overloaded content, or misaligned prerequisites.

Diagnose bottlenecks:

- is the content genuinely difficult, or is preparation uneven;
- is the course overloaded with content that could be distributed;
- are prerequisites delivering what the course assumes;
- is the assessment measuring learning or gatekeeping.

Address bottlenecks by strengthening preparation, redistributing content, adding support, or redesigning assessment, not by lowering standards arbitrarily.

### Design For Spaced Practice Of Foundational Skills

Skills that students must retain for advanced work decay if taught once and never revisited. Deliberately space practice of foundational skills across the sequence so they are reinforced, not just introduced once.

Examples:

- quantitative reasoning revisited in multiple courses, not only stats;
- writing practiced across the major, not only in a first-year course;
- primary source analysis recurring with increasing complexity;
- lab techniques revisited in later courses with new applications.

Spaced practice converts fragile exposure into durable capability.

### Account For The Reality Of Course Availability

Sequencing logic that assumes ideal enrollment breaks when courses are not offered every term, when students take courses out of order due to scheduling, or when transfer credit substitutes for a course. Design sequences that are robust to real enrollment patterns.

Robustness practices:

- avoid long chains of strictly sequential required courses;
- offer critical courses frequently enough to prevent delays;
- build review or bridge content into courses that follow variable-preparation entry points;
- accept reasonable substitution pathways for transfer students.

### Use Placement And Diagnostic Tools To Surface Preparation Gaps

Listed prerequisites do not guarantee preparation, especially with transfer credit and time gaps. Use placement exams, diagnostic assessments in the first week, or prerequisite knowledge checks to identify students who need bridge support before they fall behind.

Early diagnosis lets you intervene with review modules, tutoring referrals, or permission to drop without penalty, rather than discovering the gap at the first exam.

### Coordinate With Colleagues Who Teach Adjacent Courses

Sequencing is a collective property of a curriculum, not a property of a single course. Instructors of adjacent courses must agree on what is delivered and assumed. Regular coordination prevents the silent drift where one instructor cuts a topic the next course depends on.

### Document The Sequencing Rationale

When a sequence is well designed, the rationale is invisible and can be lost when faculty turn over. Document why each prerequisite exists, what each course delivers to the next, and what the intended developmental arc is. This preserves design intent for future instructors and accreditors.

## Common Traps

### Aspirational Prerequisites

Listing a course as prerequisite that does not deliver the assumed preparation sets students up to fail. Verify delivery.

### Conflating Prerequisite With Recommended Background

Over-requiring prerequisites blocks students and pollutes data. Reserve for true essentials.

### Affinity Over Dependency Sequencing

Grouping related topics without checking cognitive dependency leaves students unable to do later work.

### The Bottleneck As Inevitable

High-failure gatekeeping courses are often fixable through preparation, content distribution, or assessment redesign. Diagnose before accepting.

### One-Shot Foundational Skills

Teaching a skill once and assuming retention ignores decay. Space practice across the sequence.

### Ideal-Enrollment Assumptions

Sequences that assume courses are always available in order break for transfer and part-time students. Design for real patterns.

### No Early Diagnosis

Discovering preparation gaps at the first exam is too late. Use early diagnostics.

### Silent Drift Between Courses

Instructors cutting content the next course depends on, without coordination, breaks sequences invisibly. Coordinate regularly.

### Undocumented Design Intent

A well-designed sequence whose rationale is lost becomes fragile at faculty turnover. Document the reasoning.

## Self-Check

- Is each listed prerequisite a true essential, verified to be delivered by the prior course at the required level?
- Have recommended backgrounds been distinguished from true prerequisites to avoid over-blocking students?
- Does topic sequencing follow cognitive dependency rather than mere topic affinity?
- Have bottleneck courses been diagnosed for fixable causes rather than accepted as inevitable?
- Are foundational skills spaced and revisited across the sequence rather than taught once?
- Is the sequence robust to real enrollment patterns including transfer, part-time, and scheduling gaps?
- Are placement or diagnostic tools used to surface preparation gaps early enough to intervene?
- Do instructors of adjacent courses coordinate on what is delivered and assumed?
- Is the sequencing rationale documented so design intent survives faculty turnover?
- Could a colleague reconstruct why each prerequisite and sequence decision was made from the documentation?
