# Hidden Curriculum And First-Gen Navigation

Higher education runs on a hidden curriculum: the unspoken norms, vocabulary, expectations, and pathways that are obvious to those who inherited college knowledge and invisible to those who didn't. First-generation students — those whose parents did not complete four-year degrees — are as capable as any other students, but they navigate an institution that assumes knowledge they don't have. The failure mode is treating the hidden curriculum as neutral background and interpreting first-gen students' confusion, missteps, or non-participation as deficits: the student who doesn't come to office hours is "disengaged," the student who doesn't know to ask for a recommendation is "not ambitious," the student who doesn't understand "office hours are by appointment" is "not taking initiative." These readings blame students for not knowing what we never told them. Making the hidden curriculum explicit is one of the highest-leverage things a professor can do for equity, because it converts invisible disadvantage into accessible knowledge without lowering any standard.

Use this skill when teaching, advising, or mentoring first-generation students, when designing syllabi and communications, and when diagnosing why capable first-gen students are underperforming or leaving. The goal is to prevent the agent from mistaking navigation gaps for ability gaps.

## Core Rules

### Recognize First-Gen Students As Capable, Not Deficient

First-generation students are not less prepared or less able; they are navigating an institution designed for people with inherited knowledge they don't have. The gap is information and navigation, not intelligence or work ethic. Approach first-gen support as removing barriers and providing access to knowledge, not as remediation of deficit. This framing shapes everything from how you interpret their behavior to how you communicate.

### Make The Hidden Curriculum Explicit

Much of what predicts success in college is never taught because it's assumed. Make it explicit:

- what office hours are for and how to use them (many first-gen students don't know office hours are free, optional, and welcome);
- what "syllabus," "credit hour," "prerequisite," "withdrawal," and "incomplete" actually mean;
- how to email a professor (subject line, tone, what to ask);
- how recommendations, research opportunities, and internships work and when to seek them;
- what academic integrity means in practice, not just in the abstract;
- how grading, GPA, and academic standing work;
- what resources exist and that using them is normal, not remedial.

State these things in the syllabus, in class, and in advising — repeatedly, because students who don't know they don't know won't ask.

### Don't Assume Family Financial And Logistical Knowledge

First-gen students often lack family knowledge about financial aid, work-study, textbook costs, housing, and the logistics of college life. They may be working substantial hours, sending money home, or navigating food or housing insecurity. They may not know that they can ask for help, that emergency funds exist, or that a professor can connect them to resources. Be aware that first-gen students' academic struggles may reflect financial and logistical pressures, and know how to connect them to financial aid, the dean of students, and emergency resources.

### Normalize Help-Seeking And Office Hours

First-gen students often avoid office hours and support services because they don't know what they're for, fear being seen as inadequate, or feel they are imposing. Counter this directly: explain what office hours are for, invite students by name, structure early low-stakes office-hours encounters (e.g., a brief meeting to discuss a draft), and frame help-seeking as what successful students do. The students who most need office hours are often the least likely to come; design to bring them in.

### Address Imposter Phenomenon And Belonging

First-gen students frequently experience imposter phenomenon — the sense that they don't belong and will be found out — at higher rates, because the institution constantly signals that it was designed for someone else. Name imposter phenomenon openly, signal that first-gen students belong and are expected, and build community among first-gen students where possible. Belonging is not soft; it predicts persistence.

### Provide Transparent Pathways To Opportunities

Opportunities — research, internships, scholarships, study abroad, graduate school — flow through networks and knowledge that first-gen students often lack. Make pathways explicit: announce opportunities in class, explain how to apply, write recommendations proactively, and don't wait for students to discover opportunities through osmosis. The students who hear about opportunities through family networks are not more deserving; they are better networked. Level the field by making pathways transparent.

### Avoid Singling Out Or Stereotyping First-Gen Students

Support first-gen students without marking them. Avoid calling on them as representatives, assuming financial struggle, or framing them in deficit terms. Some first-gen students don't identify with the label or don't want it known. Design for first-gen students by making knowledge explicit for everyone, which serves first-gen students without singling them out, and offer individualized support through advising relationships where disclosure is voluntary.

### Connect Students To First-Gen Programs And Community

Many institutions have first-gen programs, living-learning communities, and peer mentoring that provide the community and navigation support that makes a difference. Know these programs and connect students to them. A first-gen student connected to a community of peers and mentors persists at higher rates than one navigating alone.

## Common Traps

- **Mistaking navigation gaps for ability gaps.** Interpreting first-gen students' confusion or non-participation as deficit rather than as a signal of hidden curriculum.
- **Assuming knowledge that was never taught.** Syllabus jargon, office-hours norms, and opportunity pathways are obvious to some and invisible to others. Make them explicit.
- **Blaming students for not seeking help.** First-gen students often don't seek help because they don't know it's available or feel they shouldn't need it. Design to bring them in.
- **Ignoring financial and logistical pressures.** First-gen students are more likely to be working, supporting family, or facing insecurity. Academic struggles may reflect these pressures.
- **Leaving imposter phenomenon unaddressed.** First-gen students' belonging threats are real and predict departure. Name and counter them.
- **Waiting for students to discover opportunities.** Opportunities flow through networks first-gen students lack. Make pathways transparent and proactively invite.
- **Singling out or stereotyping.** Marking first-gen students or assuming their experiences erodes the belonging the support is meant to build.
- **Not connecting to first-gen community.** Isolated navigation is harder than supported navigation. Connect students to programs and peers.

## Self-Check

- Am I treating first-gen students as capable people navigating an institution designed for others, rather than as deficient?
- Have I made the hidden curriculum — office hours, syllabus jargon, recommendations, integrity, resources — explicit in the syllabus, in class, and in advising?
- Am I aware that first-gen students' academic struggles may reflect financial and logistical pressures, and do I know how to connect them to support?
- Have I normalized help-seeking and structured early, low-stakes encounters with office hours and support?
- Do I name imposter phenomenon and actively signal that first-gen students belong?
- Are pathways to opportunities — research, scholarships, graduate school — transparent and proactively offered, not left to networks?
- Am I supporting first-gen students without singling them out or stereotyping their experiences?
- Do I connect first-gen students to institutional programs and peer communities that support navigation?
