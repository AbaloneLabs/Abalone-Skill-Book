# Academic Integrity And AI In Assessment

Academic integrity is not a compliance overlay added to a finished assessment plan. It is a property of how the assessment is designed, what evidence it requires, and what behaviors it rewards. An assessment that can be passed by submitting purchased work, by pasting a prompt into a language model, or by copying a classmate is not measuring learning; it is measuring access and willingness to shortcut. The arrival of capable generative AI has not created the integrity problem, but it has exposed how much traditional assessment depended on tasks that machines can now perform, summarizing, paraphrasing, generating plausible prose, producing boilerplate code. The response cannot be purely surveillance, because surveillance erodes trust, targets some students over others, and chases an arms race the institution will lose. The durable response is to redesign assessment so that the evidence of learning is harder to outsource and the rules about tools are clear.

Use this skill before designing or revising assessments for integrity, deciding policy on generative AI tools, writing an integrity statement for a syllabus, or responding to a suspected violation. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing assessments that are trivially cheatable, policies that are unenforceable or ambiguous, or integrity processes that punish students without due process or that damage the learning environment.

## Core Rules

### Treat Integrity As A Design Problem Before A Policing Problem

The strongest integrity protection is an assessment that is not worth cheating on because it does not reward shortcut behavior. Before adding surveillance, ask whether the assessment itself invites dishonesty. Assessments are more vulnerable when they rely on generic prompts that can be reused, when the product can be produced without the process, when the work is unconnected to the specific course context, and when a single high-stakes submission determines the outcome. Assessments are more robust when they require process artifacts, personalized or course-specific tasks, in-person demonstration, oral defense, revision based on feedback, and work that builds on class-specific material a model or mill cannot know.

Redesign is more durable than detection. A take-home essay on a generic topic can be written by anyone or anything; a reflective analysis tied to a specific in-class discussion, with drafts and a defense, cannot.

### Decide An Explicit Policy On Generative AI Tools

Ambiguity about whether AI tools are permitted is itself a major source of integrity violations, because students reasonably disagree about what is allowed. State the policy explicitly, at the level of each assignment where it can vary. A useful framing distinguishes:

- prohibited use, where the tool may not be used at all;
- limited or acknowledged use, where the tool may be used for specific purposes such as brainstorming or grammar checking, with disclosure;
- unrestricted use, where any tool use is permitted and the assessment evaluates the result.

Different assessments in the same course can sit in different categories, and that is legitimate as long as each is labeled. Avoid implicit policies where students must guess the instructor's expectation. When tools are permitted, define what acknowledgment looks like, such as an AI-use statement describing what was generated and how it was used.

### Design For Process, Not Just Product

When the product can be generated by a tool or purchased from a service, the process becomes the evidence of learning. Build in visible process:

- drafts with feedback before the final version;
- annotated bibliographies or reading notes;
- in-class writing or problem-solving sessions;
- oral defenses or check-ins where students explain their own work;
- version history or incremental submissions;
- reflection on how the work was produced.

Process evidence does not eliminate dishonesty, but it raises the cost of faking an entire performance and it gives the instructor legitimate grounds to question work whose process cannot be explained.

### Make Tasks Specific, Personal, And Context-Bound

Generic tasks are the most outsourceable. A prompt like "discuss the causes of climate change" exists in a thousand essay-mill databases and can be generated in seconds. A prompt tied to a specific local case study, a particular dataset from the course, a personal reflection connected to a class activity, or a response to a guest lecture is far harder to outsource because the necessary context is not publicly available. Personalization also improves learning, because it asks students to connect concepts to their own experience rather than reproduce generic material.

### Avoid Reusing Published Or Public Questions

Reusing questions from a textbook test bank, a prior public exam, or an online repository makes the answers trivially available. Even slight modifications, changing names, numbers, or scenarios, are often insufficient because the underlying solution structure is searchable. For high-stakes assessment, generate fresh questions each term, maintain question security, and avoid posting full exams where they can be indexed. Where a question bank must be used, rotate items and avoid drawing a large fraction of an exam from any single public source.

### Calibrate Stakes To Reduce The Incentive To Cheat

Catastrophic high-stakes assessments create the strongest incentive to cheat, because the cost of failure is total and the reward for shortcut success is large. Distributing weight across multiple components, offering revision and recovery, and lowering the stakes of any single submission reduce both the pressure and the payoff of dishonesty. This is not lowering standards; it is reducing the conditions under which otherwise honest students rationalize a shortcut.

### Write A Clear Integrity Policy And Process

Students should know the rules, the consequences, and the process before any violation occurs. A sound policy states what counts as a violation, how suspected violations are handled, what due process students are entitled to, and what the range of consequences is. Avoid policies that are vague about what is prohibited or that concentrate unchecked discretion in the instructor. Route formal allegations through the institutional process rather than imposing ad hoc penalties, both for fairness to the student and for the instructor's protection.

### Investigate Suspected Violations Fairly

When you suspect a violation, avoid the twin failures of ignoring evidence and of acting on weak evidence. Gather the specific evidence, the submitted work, matching sources, AI-detection signals where relevant, discrepancies between the work and the student's known capability. Remember that AI detectors produce false positives, especially against multilingual writers, and that similarity scores require interpretation, not blind application. Confront the student with the evidence and allow them to respond before concluding guilt. A student whose work is unusually polished may have improved, used permitted help, or been falsely flagged; do not assume dishonesty from style alone.

### Protect The Learning Environment During And After A Case

An integrity case affects more than the accused student. Avoid public accusations, protect the student's privacy, and avoid creating a climate of suspicion that poisons the rest of the class. At the same time, do not let fear of conflict lead to ignoring clear violations, which signals to other students that dishonesty is tolerated. Consistent, private, process-driven handling preserves both fairness and trust.

### Teach Integrity Rather Than Only Enforce It

Many violations come from students who do not understand citation, collaboration boundaries, or what counts as their own work. Teach these explicitly, especially for first-generation and international students whose prior norms may differ. Provide examples, model proper attribution, and clarify where collaboration ends and individual work begins. Education reduces violations that stem from confusion and leaves enforcement for genuine dishonesty.

## Common Traps

### Policing Instead Of Designing

Adding surveillance to a cheatable assessment chases an arms race and erodes trust. Redesign the assessment so shortcut behavior is not rewarded.

### Ambiguous AI Policy

Leaving students to guess whether AI tools are permitted guarantees violations born of misunderstanding. State the policy per assignment.

### Generic, Outsourceable Prompts

Tasks that exist in public databases or that a model can answer without course context are trivially cheated. Make tasks specific and personal.

### Relying On AI Detectors As Proof

Detection tools produce false positives, especially against multilingual writers, and similarity scores need interpretation. Use them as signals, not verdicts.

### Reusing Public Questions

Drawing from test banks or posted exams makes answers searchable. Generate fresh items and maintain security.

### Catastrophic Stakes

A single high-stakes submission maximizes the incentive to cheat. Distribute weight and offer recovery.

### Ad Hoc Penalties

Imposing penalties without the institutional process risks unfairness and procedural challenge. Route formal allegations through official channels.

### Style-Based Accusations

Accusing a student because the work seems too polished ignores legitimate improvement and permitted help. Base accusations on specific evidence and allow a response.

### Ignoring Clear Violations

Avoiding confrontation when evidence is strong signals that dishonesty is tolerated and erodes trust among honest students. Act through proper process.

### Assuming Students Know The Rules

Many violations stem from confusion about citation and collaboration. Teach integrity explicitly rather than only enforcing it.

## Self-Check

- Does the assessment design reward genuine learning over shortcut behavior, through process evidence, personalization, or in-context tasks?
- Is there an explicit, per-assignment policy on generative AI use, distinguishing prohibited, acknowledged, and unrestricted use?
- Does the assessment require visible process, such as drafts, defenses, or incremental work, that cannot be faked wholesale?
- Are prompts specific and tied to course context rather than generic and publicly answerable?
- Are exam questions fresh and secure, avoiding reuse of public test-bank items?
- Are stakes distributed so that no single catastrophic submission maximizes the incentive to cheat?
- Does the integrity policy clearly state what is prohibited, the process, due process rights, and the range of consequences?
- Are suspected violations investigated with specific evidence, with AI detectors and similarity scores treated as signals rather than proof?
- Is the student given a chance to respond before guilt is concluded, and are formal allegations routed through the institutional process?
- Is integrity taught explicitly, with examples of proper citation and collaboration, rather than only enforced after violations?
