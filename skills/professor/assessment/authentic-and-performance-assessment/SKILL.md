# Authentic And Performance Assessment

Authentic and performance assessment asks students to demonstrate learning by producing work that resembles what practitioners, citizens, or scholars actually do, rather than by selecting answers on a timed test. A well-designed authentic task reveals whether a student can transfer concepts to realistic conditions, integrate skills under constraints, and produce a defensible artifact. A poorly designed one feels elaborate but measures effort, formatting, or theatrical performance rather than the underlying competence. Professors often adopt authentic assessment because it sounds engaging, then discover that it is hard to grade consistently, vulnerable to integrity issues, and demanding to scaffold. The work of designing authentic assessment is the work of making realism serve measurement rather than substitute for it.

Use this skill before designing performance tasks, portfolios, simulations, capstones, clinical or studio assessments, competency-based evaluations, or any assessment that asks students to apply learning to realistic contexts. The goal is to prevent the agent from producing tasks that look authentic but are ungraded reliably, misaligned with outcomes, or so open-ended that they measure persistence and formatting rather than the intended competence.

## Core Rules

### Define The Competence Before The Scenario

Authenticity is a delivery method, not a learning outcome. Before designing the scenario, define the competence the task must reveal:

- the specific skills or knowledge the task should elicit;
- the level of performance expected, novice, competent, proficient;
- the transfer the task is meant to test, near transfer to similar contexts or far transfer to novel ones;
- the decisions, judgments, or productions that constitute evidence of that competence.

A task built from a vivid scenario without a defined competence tends to measure engagement with the scenario rather than mastery of the outcome. Start from what the student should be able to do, then choose the realistic context that best elicits it.

### Make Realism Serve Measurement

Realistic contexts can motivate students and elicit transfer, but realism has costs: more ambiguity, more variable difficulty, more grading complexity. Use realism where it adds measurement value:

- when the outcome is itself a professional or disciplinary performance;
- when transfer to realistic conditions is part of the learning goal;
- when authentic constraints, audience, or purpose shape the quality of the work;
- when realistic data or artifacts reveal skills that abstract problems hide.

Avoid realism that obscures measurement:

- scenarios so elaborate that students spend effort on context rather than competence;
- ambiguity so high that strong students cannot tell what is being asked;
- constraints that introduce extraneous difficulty unrelated to the outcome.

The test is whether the realistic elements help reveal the competence or merely decorate the task.

### Scaffold Authentic Tasks Across The Term

Authentic tasks are rarely completable cold. They integrate many skills and require scaffolding:

- teach component skills before the integrative task;
- provide models or annotated examples of the target performance;
- build in milestones, proposal, draft, revision, with feedback at each;
- offer low-stakes practice with realistic constraints before the graded performance;
- teach the metacognitive skills the task requires, planning, monitoring, revising.

An unscaffolded authentic task measures who already had the integrated skill, not who learned it in the course. Scaffolding makes authentic assessment a learning instrument, not just a measurement one.

### Specify Audience, Purpose, And Constraints Explicitly

Authentic work is defined by its rhetorical situation. Make explicit:

- the audience the work addresses, a client, a review board, a public, a peer;
- the purpose the work must achieve, persuade, diagnose, design, decide;
- the constraints under which it is produced, time, resources, format, ethics;
- the criteria by which the audience would judge success.

Vague prompts like "write a policy brief on climate adaptation" produce wildly variable interpretations. A prompt that specifies audience, purpose, constraints, and success criteria produces comparable work and fairer grading.

### Design Rubrics That Capture Authentic Quality

Authentic work resists reduction to correctness, so rubrics must capture dimensions of quality. Build analytic rubrics that define:

- content accuracy and depth;
- disciplinary reasoning or method;
- application to the realistic context;
- communication quality for the specified audience;
- professional or ethical judgment;
- integration of multiple skills.

Distinguish dimensions that are part of the assessed competence from those that are incidental. If formatting or presentation is not part of the outcome, do not let it dominate the rubric; if it is part of professional competence, weight it deliberately.

### Ensure Fairness Across Realistic Variation

Authentic tasks often vary across students or groups, which creates fairness risks. Manage variation by:

- using common rubrics across all versions of the task;
- calibrating graders on sample work before scoring;
- ensuring equivalent difficulty across variants or topics;
- giving students equitable access to resources, data, or contexts;
- avoiding scenarios that assume cultural or experiential background not available to all.

Fairness in authentic assessment depends less on identical tasks than on equivalent opportunity to demonstrate the competence and consistent standards for judging it.

### Address Integrity Through Task Design

Authentic tasks can resist shortcut better than recall exams, but they are not immune to misuse, including AI-generated work, ghostwriting, or recycled artifacts. Design for integrity:

- make tasks specific to the course, the term, or local data;
- require process artifacts, drafts, reflections, data, code history;
- connect the task to in-class work or individualized elements;
- ask for metacognitive explanation of choices, not only the product;
- use viva or defense components for high-stakes performances;
- align permitted tool use, including AI, with what the task is meant to measure.

Integrity designed into the task is more effective than integrity policed after submission.

### Plan For Sustainable Grading

Authentic assessment is grading-intensive. Plan for sustainability:

- use rubrics to structure and speed scoring;
- consider calibrated peer review for some dimensions;
- stage grading across milestones rather than at the end;
- use collective feedback for common patterns and individual feedback for specifics;
- scale task complexity to the number of students and available graders.

A beautiful authentic task that cannot be graded sustainably will collapse into delayed, shallow feedback. Design grading capacity into the task.

### Connect Authentic Assessment To Professional And Civic Identity

Authentic tasks can do more than measure; they can help students see themselves as practitioners. Connect the task to:

- the professional or civic roles students may enter;
- the ethical responsibilities those roles carry;
- the standards of the discipline or field;
- portfolios or artifacts students can carry forward.

Used this way, authentic assessment becomes part of identity formation, not only evaluation.

### Evaluate Whether Authentic Assessment Is The Right Choice

Not every outcome needs authentic assessment. Use it when:

- the outcome is itself a performance or transfer task;
- realistic context adds measurement value;
- the course can sustain the scaffolding and grading cost;
- integrity and fairness can be managed.

Use more constrained assessment when the outcome is best measured by recall, structured problem solving, or explanation. Do not adopt authentic assessment as a fashion; adopt it where it serves the learning.

## Common Traps

### Scenario Without Competence

An elaborate realistic scenario that does not map to a defined competence measures engagement with the scenario, not mastery. Define the competence first.

### Realism That Obscures Measurement

Over-elaborate contexts, excessive ambiguity, or extraneous constraints introduce difficulty unrelated to the outcome. Keep realism in service of measurement.

### Unscaffolded Integrative Tasks

Asking students to integrate many skills without prior component practice measures who already could do it. Scaffold the integration.

### Vague Prompts

Prompts without specified audience, purpose, constraints, and criteria produce incomparable work and unfair grading. Specify the rhetorical situation.

### Rubrics That Reward Incidental Quality

Letting formatting, length, or presentation dominate a rubric when they are not part of the outcome measures the wrong thing. Weight dimensions deliberately.

### Unequal Difficulty Across Variants

Allowing students to choose topics of wildly different difficulty undermines fairness. Ensure equivalent opportunity to demonstrate competence.

### Assuming Authentic Means Integrity-Proof

Authentic tasks can still be outsourced, AI-generated, or recycled. Design integrity into the task with process artifacts and specific constraints.

### Unsustainable Grading Load

Complex authentic tasks across many students without rubric structure or staging collapse into delayed feedback. Plan grading capacity.

### Authentic Assessment As Fashion

Adopting performance tasks because they sound engaging, when constrained assessment would measure the outcome better, wastes effort. Match format to evidence.

## Self-Check

- Is the competence the task must reveal defined before the scenario is designed?
- Do the realistic elements help elicit the competence, or do they decorate and obscure it?
- Is the task scaffolded across the term so it measures learning rather than prior ability?
- Are audience, purpose, constraints, and success criteria specified explicitly in the prompt?
- Does the rubric capture dimensions of authentic quality relevant to the outcome, without letting incidental qualities dominate?
- Is difficulty equivalent across variants, topics, or groups, with common standards applied consistently?
- Does the task design address integrity through specificity, process artifacts, and aligned tool-use rules?
- Is grading capacity planned so feedback is timely and sustainable?
- Does the task connect to professional, civic, or disciplinary identity where that is part of the learning goal?
- Is authentic assessment chosen because it serves the outcome, rather than because it sounds engaging?
