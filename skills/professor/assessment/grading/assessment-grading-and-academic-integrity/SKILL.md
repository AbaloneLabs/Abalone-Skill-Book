# Assessment Grading And Academic Integrity

Assessment in higher education has two jobs: support learning and make defensible judgments. Grading fails when students cannot see what quality means, when assessments reward hidden advantages, when feedback arrives too late to matter, or when academic integrity rules are unclear. Academic integrity is not only catching misconduct; it is designing tasks, expectations, and support so students understand what counts as legitimate work.

Use this skill before creating exams, papers, projects, rubrics, grading policies, AI-use rules, peer review, feedback routines, or grade appeal guidance. The goal is to help the agent design assessments that are aligned, fair, transparent, accessible, and institutionally defensible.

## Core Rules

### Align Assessment With Learning Outcomes

Assess what the course claims to teach. If the outcome is critical analysis, do not rely only on recall. If the outcome is technical fluency, include authentic application. If the outcome is collaboration, define how collaboration will be observed and evaluated.

For each assessment, identify:

- outcome assessed;
- knowledge or skill required;
- evidence students must produce;
- criteria for quality;
- format;
- allowed resources;
- time constraints;
- feedback opportunity;
- accessibility considerations.

Avoid using assessment formats only because they are easy to grade. Easy grading can produce weak evidence.

### Make Criteria Transparent Before Submission

Students should know how work will be judged before they do it. Rubrics, checklists, annotated examples, model responses, and discussion of criteria can reduce confusion.

Clarify:

- task purpose;
- required components;
- performance levels;
- disciplinary conventions;
- citation expectations;
- collaboration boundaries;
- AI tool policy;
- late and revision policy;
- common errors;
- submission format.

Do not hide important criteria in verbal reminders that some students miss.

### Use Rubrics Carefully

Rubrics can improve fairness, but they can also oversimplify complex work. A rubric should reflect the actual judgment being made.

Check:

- criteria match outcomes;
- descriptors are observable;
- levels are meaningfully distinct;
- weights reflect importance;
- criteria do not double count the same issue;
- language is understandable to students;
- room exists for holistic judgment where appropriate;
- rubric works across expected topic choices.

Test the rubric against sample work before relying on it. Revise if two graders would interpret it differently.

### Design For Academic Integrity

Integrity rules should be explicit and task-specific. Students need to know what help is allowed, what tools are allowed, what must be cited, and what counts as collaboration.

State:

- individual versus group work rules;
- permitted sources;
- prohibited assistance;
- citation requirements;
- AI tool use allowed or disallowed;
- disclosure requirements;
- data or code sharing rules;
- reuse of prior work;
- exam materials allowed;
- consequences and process under institutional policy.

When using AI-related restrictions, distinguish brainstorming, editing, translation, code generation, data analysis, and final authorship. Vague "do not use AI" or "AI allowed" language creates avoidable disputes.

### Reduce Unnecessary Barriers

An assessment may unintentionally measure speed, tool access, English fluency, test anxiety, disability barriers, or prior hidden knowledge instead of the intended outcome.

Review:

- reading load unrelated to objective;
- time pressure;
- inaccessible file format;
- unclear directions;
- technology requirements;
- cost of materials;
- group scheduling burden;
- cultural assumptions;
- disability accommodations;
- language support;
- alternative formats when appropriate.

Keep rigor in the intended construct, not in avoidable friction.

### Plan Feedback That Can Be Used

Feedback is most useful while students can still improve. If the only feedback is a final grade, the course loses a learning opportunity.

Build:

- proposal feedback;
- draft feedback;
- problem-set review;
- exam wrapper;
- office hour feedback;
- peer review with structure;
- audio or written comments;
- group feedback memo;
- revision opportunity;
- targeted practice after errors.

Feedback should identify what worked, what did not meet criteria, and what to do next. Avoid writing more comments than students can process.

### Maintain Grading Consistency And Records

Grades should be explainable. Instructors need records that support decisions without exposing private information unnecessarily.

Maintain:

- rubric version;
- answer key;
- grading notes;
- gradebook entries;
- late approvals;
- extension approvals;
- accommodation adjustments;
- regrade requests;
- academic integrity referrals;
- communication records;
- grade changes.

Protect student privacy under FERPA or applicable local policy. Do not discuss grades in public channels or with unauthorized people.

### Handle Appeals And Regrades Through Process

Grade disputes should follow institutional policy. The professor should be fair, consistent, and documented.

For regrade requests, define:

- deadline;
- format;
- whether whole assignment may be regraded;
- required student rationale;
- who reviews;
- timeline;
- finality;
- escalation path.

Do not negotiate grades based on pressure, sympathy, or end-of-term urgency. Consider documented errors and criteria-based arguments.

### Interpret Assessment Data For Teaching

Assessment results should inform instruction, not only produce grades.

Analyze:

- item-level performance;
- rubric criteria patterns;
- common misconceptions;
- disparities by section or modality;
- assignment directions that caused confusion;
- pacing issues;
- academic integrity patterns;
- student feedback;
- missing work patterns.

If many students miss the same concept, consider reteaching or revising instruction rather than treating the issue as only student failure.

## Common Traps

### Grading Hidden Expectations

Students cannot meet standards they were never taught or shown. Make disciplinary norms explicit.

### Overweighting High-Stakes Exams

High-stakes assessments can be appropriate, but they amplify anxiety, inequity, and single-day disruption. Use multiple evidence points when possible.

### Treating Academic Integrity As Only Punishment

Clear expectations, assignment design, support, citation practice, and transparent AI rules reduce problems before enforcement is needed.

### Using Participation Grades As Personality Grades

Define participation behavior. Avoid rewarding only frequent speaking.

### Giving Feedback Too Late

Feedback on a final product after the course ends does not support improvement unless students can use it later.

### Ignoring Accommodation Logistics

Assessment design must work with approved accommodations. Coordinate early with the disability office or institutional process.

### Discussing Grades In Unsafe Channels

Grades and academic records are private. Use approved systems and institutional rules.

## Self-Check

- Does each assessment align with stated course outcomes?
- Are criteria, rubrics, examples, allowed resources, collaboration rules, and AI-use expectations clear before submission?
- Has the rubric been checked for observable criteria, meaningful levels, and consistency?
- Does the assessment measure the intended learning rather than avoidable barriers?
- Are academic integrity expectations task-specific and tied to institutional process?
- Is feedback timely, actionable, and connected to revision or future performance?
- Are grading records, exceptions, accommodations, regrades, and grade changes documented?
- Are student privacy and grade communication rules followed?
- Is there a fair regrade or appeal process that avoids ad hoc negotiation?
- Are assessment results used to improve teaching, pacing, directions, and support?
