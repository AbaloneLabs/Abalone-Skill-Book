# Academic Integrity And Assessment Design

Academic integrity is not primarily an enforcement problem; it is a design problem. Assessments that are easy to cheat on, generic essay prompts answerable by AI, take-home tests with no authentication, recycled questions from a test bank, invite dishonesty and then punish students for accepting the invitation. The current technology environment, with generative AI capable of producing passable essays and code, and contract cheating services offering to complete any assignment, has made traditional assessment designs newly vulnerable. The response cannot be only more surveillance and harsher penalties, which are expensive, invasive, and often ineffective. The more durable response is designing assessments that are hard to cheat on because they are personalized, process-oriented, and tied to specific classroom context, while also teaching integrity norms explicitly and handling violations fairly. The judgment problem is designing assessment that reduces the incentive and opportunity for dishonesty without turning the course into a surveillance regime, and responding to integrity questions in a way that is educational rather than purely punitive.

Use this skill when designing assessments to resist cheating, when addressing AI-generated or contract-cheated submissions, when writing integrity expectations, when deciding on proctoring or authentication, when teaching citation and integrity norms, or when handling suspected violations. The goal is to prevent the agent from relying on surveillance as the primary integrity strategy, from designing assessments that invite cheating and then punishing it, and from treating integrity violations as moral failures rather than design and pedagogy signals.

## Core Rules

### Treat Integrity Primarily As A Design Problem

The most effective integrity strategy is designing assessments that are not worth cheating on. When assessments are personalized, process-oriented, and tied to classroom context, the effort and risk of cheating exceed the effort of honest work. Design should be the first line of defense, with enforcement as backup.

Design for integrity by:

- personalizing assignments to the specific student, class, or context so generic answers do not fit;
- assessing process, drafts, reflections, and in-class components, not only final products;
- tying assessment to specific course content, discussions, or data that outside sources do not have;
- rotating prompts and questions so recycled answers do not work across terms.

Design reduces the opportunity for dishonesty; surveillance only catches it after the fact.

### Adapt To The Generative AI Environment

Generative AI has changed what assessments are vulnerable. Generic essay prompts, standard code exercises, and summary tasks can now be completed by AI competently. Assessments that rely on these formats invite AI-assisted dishonesty and are also of questionable value when AI can do the work. Adapt assessment to the reality that AI exists, deciding deliberately where AI use is permitted, where it is not, and how to assess what AI cannot do.

Adapt by:

- deciding explicitly where AI use is allowed, encouraged, or prohibited, rather than leaving it ambiguous;
- assessing skills AI does poorly, such as original analysis, personal reflection, and synthesis of class-specific material;
- designing tasks that require integrating specific course content AI does not have access to;
- where AI is permitted, assessing students' ability to use it critically rather than pretending it does not exist.

Pretending AI does not exist, or banning it without enforcement capacity, is not a viable strategy.

### Assess Process, Not Only Product

A final product alone cannot distinguish honest work from dishonest. A polished essay might be the student's best effort or a purchased paper; correct code might be genuine learning or copied. Assessing the process, drafts, version history, in-class work, and reflection, makes dishonesty far harder because the process must be faked in addition to the product.

Assess process by:

- requiring drafts, outlines, or proposals that precede the final product;
- incorporating in-class writing, problem-solving, or discussion that informs the grade;
- asking students to explain their work, through reflection, presentation, or conference;
- using version history or staged submission to show the work developing over time.

A student who did the work can explain it; one who did not cannot, and process assessment reveals the difference.

### Teach Integrity Norms Explicitly

Many integrity violations, especially around citation, paraphrase, and collaboration, stem from genuine confusion about the norms rather than deliberate deception. Students come from different educational backgrounds with different conventions, and assumptions that they know the discipline's integrity norms are often wrong. Teach the norms explicitly rather than punishing violations of unstated rules.

Teach norms by:

- explaining citation, paraphrase, and collaboration expectations for the specific discipline and assignment;
- providing examples of acceptable and unacceptable practice, including AI use boundaries;
- teaching integrity as a disciplinary practice, not just a rule set;
- distinguishing developmental errors in citation from deliberate deception in response.

A student who mis-cites because they never learned better is not equivalent to one who buys a paper.

### Use Proportionate And Educational Responses To Violations

When violations occur, the response should be proportionate to the violation and, where appropriate, educational rather than purely punitive. A first citation error is a teaching opportunity; deliberate contract cheating is a serious offense. Conflating these erodes trust and turns integrity into a trap. Match the response to the nature and intent of the violation.

Respond proportionately by:

- distinguishing developmental error, confusion, and deliberate deception;
- using educational responses, rewriting or integrity modules, for developmental issues;
- applying serious consequences, through institutional processes, for deliberate dishonesty;
- avoiding the temptation to make examples of students, which undermines fairness.

A response system that treats every violation identically is neither fair nor effective.

### Be Cautious With Surveillance And Proctoring

Online proctoring, plagiarism detection, and AI detectors are marketed as integrity solutions, but they have significant limitations and costs. Proctoring is invasive and can disadvantage students with disabilities, anxiety, or non-standard environments. Plagiarism detectors produce false positives and can be circumvented. AI detectors are notoriously unreliable, especially against non-native English writing. Use surveillance cautiously, understanding its limits.

Be cautious by:

- weighing the intrusiveness and false positive risk of proctoring against the assessment stakes;
- treating plagiarism and AI detector results as signals for investigation, not proof;
- recognizing that detectors disproportionately flag non-native English and neurodivergent writing;
- preferring design-based integrity strategies over surveillance where possible.

Surveillance that produces false accusations harms innocent students and does not reliably catch the guilty.

### Design Collaborative Assessment Honestly

Group work is common, but integrity around collaboration is often ambiguous. Are students allowed to discuss approaches? To share resources? To review each other's work? Vague collaboration policies invite violations and disputes. Define collaboration expectations explicitly and design group assessment with individual accountability.

Design collaboration honestly by:

- stating explicitly what collaboration is allowed and what is individual work;
- building individual accountability into group projects, so contributions are visible;
- assessing both group product and individual contribution;
- teaching responsible collaboration as a skill rather than assuming students know the boundaries.

Ambiguous collaboration rules produce violations that are really policy failures.

### Maintain Fairness And Due Process

Integrity processes must be fair, with due process for accused students. An instructor who acts as accuser, judge, and jury without process risks unfair outcomes and institutional liability. Follow institutional integrity processes, present evidence clearly, and allow students to respond to accusations. Fairness protects both honest students, from false accusation, and the integrity system's legitimacy.

Maintain fairness by:

- following institutional academic integrity processes rather than acting unilaterally;
- presenting evidence and allowing the student to respond before any consequence;
- documenting the evidence and the process;
- recognizing the serious consequences of an integrity violation for students and proceeding carefully.

A integrity system perceived as unfair loses legitimacy and cooperation from students and faculty alike.

## Common Traps

### Treating Integrity As Enforcement Rather Than Design

Surveillance catches some cheating; design reduces the incentive. Prioritize assessment design.

### Ignoring The Generative AI Environment

Generic prompts are now AI-answerable. Adapt assessment deliberately to the AI reality.

### Assessing Only Final Products

Products alone cannot distinguish honest from dishonest work. Assess process.

### Punishing Violations Of Unstated Norms

Many violations reflect confusion, not deception. Teach integrity norms explicitly.

### Treating All Violations Identically

Developmental errors and deliberate deception differ. Match response to nature and intent.

### Over-Reliance On Unreliable Detectors

AI and plagiarism detectors produce false positives and flag non-native writing. Use as signals, not proof.

### Ambiguous Collaboration Policies

Vague collaboration rules invite disputes. Define expectations explicitly with individual accountability.

### Acting As Accuser And Judge Without Process

Integrity processes require due process. Follow institutional procedures and allow student response.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Assessments are designed to reduce the incentive and opportunity for dishonesty, not only to catch it.
- [ ] The assessment design accounts for generative AI, with explicit decisions about where it is permitted.
- [ ] Process, not only final product, is assessed through drafts, in-class work, or reflection.
- [ ] Integrity norms, including citation and collaboration, are taught explicitly for the discipline.
- [ ] Responses to violations distinguish developmental error from deliberate deception and are proportionate.
- [ ] Proctoring and detectors are used cautiously, with awareness of false positives and disparate impact.
- [ ] Collaboration expectations are stated explicitly with individual accountability in group work.
- [ ] Institutional integrity processes and due process are followed for any formal violation.
- [ ] No assessment relies on formats, like generic essays, that are now trivially AI-answerable.
- [ ] Integrity is framed as a disciplinary practice and shared norm, not only a rule set to enforce.
