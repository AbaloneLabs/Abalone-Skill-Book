# Authentic And Applied Assessment

Traditional assessments, multiple-choice tests, short-answer recall, and decontextualized problem sets, measure whether students can recognize or reproduce information under artificial conditions. They are easy to grade and reliable to score, but they often measure a thin slice of what students should actually be able to do. Authentic assessment asks students to perform the work of the discipline: to write as a historian writes, to diagnose as a clinician diagnoses, to design as an engineer designs, to argue as a lawyer argues. This kind of assessment measures transferable competence rather than recall, and it motivates students by connecting learning to real purposes. But authenticity introduces its own judgment problems: authentic tasks are harder to grade reliably, harder to design well, and harder to align with limited time and large classes. The judgment problem is designing assessments that are authentic enough to measure real competence while remaining feasible, fair, and aligned to the learning outcomes, rather than defaulting to recall tests because they are easy or adopting elaborate projects that cannot be graded consistently.

Use this skill when designing authentic or performance assessments, when moving from recall to applied tasks, when creating portfolios or project-based assessments, when balancing authenticity with grading feasibility, or when deciding whether a traditional or authentic approach better fits the outcomes. The goal is to prevent the agent from mistaking novelty for authenticity, from creating assessments that are engaging but ungradeable, and from abandoning authenticity because traditional tests are easier.

## Core Rules

### Define What Authenticity Means For The Discipline

Authenticity is not generic. What counts as authentic work differs by discipline: a lab report is authentic in science, a case brief in law, a design portfolio in engineering, a close reading in literature. Authentic assessment should mirror the actual intellectual work practitioners in the field do, not just be a creative variation on a test. Define authenticity concretely for the discipline before designing the task.

Define authenticity by:

- identifying what competent practitioners in the field actually produce;
- designing assessment tasks that mirror that production, with realistic audience and purpose;
- avoiding generic "creative" tasks that are novel but not disciplinary;
- grounding the task in the real problems, genres, or processes of the field.

A poster presentation is not authentic for every discipline just because it is not a test.

### Align Authentic Tasks To Specific Learning Outcomes

Authenticity is a means, not an end. The end is measuring specific learning outcomes. An authentic task that is engaging but does not align to the outcomes measures the wrong thing. Start from the outcomes and design an authentic task that elicits evidence of those outcomes, rather than starting from an interesting task and hoping it maps.

Align by:

- listing the outcomes the assessment must measure;
- designing the task so that completing it requires demonstrating those outcomes;
- checking that no major outcome is unassessed and no task component is unaligned;
- resisting the temptation to add engaging elements that do not serve outcome measurement.

An authentic task that does not measure the outcomes is theater, not assessment.

### Design For Transfer, Not Just Application

Authentic assessment should measure transfer, the ability to apply learning to new contexts, not just application within the context taught. A task that exactly mirrors a practiced problem measures reproduction; a task that requires applying the underlying competence to a new situation measures transfer. Design tasks that are realistic but not identical to examples students have seen.

Design for transfer by:

- using novel contexts, cases, or data that students have not seen before;
- requiring students to select and apply concepts rather than follow a practiced procedure;
- avoiding tasks that reward pattern-matching to known examples;
- including open-ended elements where students must frame the problem, not just solve it.

If the task can be completed by reproducing a practiced template, it measures reproduction, not transfer.

### Build In Realistic Audience And Purpose

Authentic work is done for a real audience and purpose, not just for the instructor's grade. A report written for a hypothetical client, a memo to a decision-maker, a presentation to peers, or a product for a user all create the motivation and constraints of real disciplinary work. Building in audience and purpose transforms the task from exercise to performance.

Build in audience and purpose by:

- specifying who the audience for the work is, beyond the instructor;
- defining the purpose the work is meant to achieve, to inform, persuade, decide, design;
- using realistic constraints, such as length, format, or available information;
- where possible, involving real audiences, such as peer review, community partners, or public presentation.

A task with a real audience and purpose engages students in ways a graded exercise cannot.

### Balance Authenticity With Grading Feasibility

The central tension in authentic assessment is that rich, realistic tasks are hard to grade reliably, especially in large classes. A complex project may be authentic but produce grades that vary by grader and are slow to return. Ignoring this tension produces assessments that are engaging but unfair or unmanageable. Balance authenticity with feasibility from the design stage.

Balance by:

- using rubrics that define criteria clearly so grading is consistent across complex tasks;
- scaffolding large authentic tasks into graded components so feedback is timely;
- considering class size when designing; highly authentic tasks may need adaptation for scale;
- combining authentic tasks with more structured assessment where reliability is critical.

Authenticity that makes grading impossible undermines the assessment's fairness and timeliness.

### Use Scaffolding To Make Complex Tasks Learnable

Authentic tasks are often complex, and assigning them without scaffolding sets students up to fail. Scaffolding breaks the task into learnable components with feedback at each stage, so students build toward the authentic performance rather than attempting it cold. Scaffolding preserves authenticity of the final product while making the learning path manageable.

Scaffold by:

- breaking complex tasks into stages, each with feedback before the next;
- providing models of strong and weak performance so students see the target;
- offering practice on components before requiring integrated performance;
- reducing scaffolding as students gain competence, moving toward independent performance.

An authentic task assigned without scaffolding measures prior preparation more than course learning.

### Ensure Fairness Across Diverse Students

Authentic tasks can introduce inequities that traditional tests avoid. Project-based work may advantage students with more time, resources, or prior experience with the genre. Group work may allow some students to free-ride while others carry the load. Real-world tasks may assume cultural or resource context not all students share. Design for fairness within the authentic format.

Ensure fairness by:

- providing necessary resources and time so the task does not advantage those with more of either;
- structuring group work with individual accountability components;
- avoiding tasks that assume specific cultural, economic, or technological context;
- checking that the task format does not systematically advantage or disadvantage particular students.

Authenticity is not an excuse to reproduce advantage through a different format.

### Combine Authentic And Traditional Assessment Deliberately

Authentic assessment does not require abandoning traditional assessment. Recall of foundational knowledge, structured problem-solving, and efficient measurement of broad content still have roles. The strongest assessment programs combine authentic tasks, for transfer and competence, with traditional tasks, for coverage and efficiency, chosen deliberately for what each measures best.

Combine deliberately by:

- using traditional assessment for foundational knowledge and broad coverage;
- using authentic assessment for transfer, integration, and disciplinary competence;
- being explicit about what each component measures and why it is included;
- avoiding redundancy where multiple assessments measure the same outcome.

The choice between authentic and traditional should follow the outcome, not ideology.

## Common Traps

### Mistaking Novelty For Authenticity

A creative task that does not mirror disciplinary work is novel, not authentic. Ground tasks in real practice.

### Authentic Tasks Unaligned To Outcomes

An engaging task that does not measure the outcomes is theater. Start from outcomes.

### Tasks That Measure Reproduction, Not Transfer

If the task mirrors practiced examples, it measures pattern-matching. Use novel contexts for transfer.

### Ignoring Grading Feasibility

Rich tasks that cannot be graded reliably or timely are unfair. Balance authenticity with feasibility.

### Complex Tasks Without Scaffolding

Assigning complex authentic tasks cold sets students up to fail. Scaffold toward performance.

### Authentic Tasks That Introduce Inequity

Projects and group work can advantage students with more resources or prior experience. Design for fairness.

### Ideological Rejection Of Traditional Assessment

Recall and structured problems still have roles. Combine formats based on what each measures.

### Audience-Free Performance

Work done only for the instructor's grade lacks the motivation and constraints of real disciplinary work. Build in audience and purpose.

## Self-Check

- [ ] Authenticity is defined concretely for the discipline, grounding tasks in real disciplinary work.
- [ ] The authentic task is aligned to specific learning outcomes, not chosen for engagement alone.
- [ ] The task measures transfer through novel contexts, not reproduction of practiced examples.
- [ ] Realistic audience and purpose are built into the task beyond the instructor's grade.
- [ ] Grading feasibility has been considered, with rubrics and scaffolding to ensure reliability and timeliness.
- [ ] Complex tasks are scaffolded into learnable components with feedback at each stage.
- [ ] The task has been examined for fairness, avoiding advantages based on resources, time, or background.
- [ ] Authentic and traditional assessment are combined deliberately, each measuring what it does best.
- [ ] Group work, if used, includes individual accountability.
- [ ] The task would be recognized as meaningful work by a practitioner in the discipline.
