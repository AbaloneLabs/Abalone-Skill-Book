# Mentoring Relationship And Expectations

A mentoring relationship is not a friendship, a contract, or a service transaction, though it shares features with each. It is a developmental partnership in which a more experienced person supports the growth of a less experienced one over time. Most mentoring relationships that fail do not fail because of a dramatic conflict; they fail slowly, through accumulated mismatches in expectation, unclear roles, inconsistent communication, and unspoken assumptions about what each party owes the other. Professors often reproduce the mentoring they received, good or bad, without examining whether it fits the student in front of them. The work of establishing a mentoring relationship well is the work of making the implicit explicit: naming what each party wants, what each will do, how often they will meet, how feedback will work, and what happens when expectations diverge.

Use this skill before beginning a mentoring relationship, drafting a mentoring agreement, setting expectations with a new mentee, renegotiating a relationship that has drifted, or diagnosing why a mentoring relationship feels stuck or strained. The goal is to prevent the agent from treating mentoring as an unstructured goodwill arrangement and producing relationships that disappoint both parties through avoidable misalignment.

## Core Rules

### Establish The Purpose And Scope Of The Relationship Early

Mentoring relationships serve different purposes, and confusion about purpose is the most common source of failure. Clarify early:

- what kind of mentoring this is, academic, research, career, personal-development, or some combination;
- what the mentee hopes to gain;
- what the mentor is able and willing to offer;
- the time horizon, a single project, a degree, a career stage, or open-ended;
- whether the relationship is formal, with institutional roles, or informal.

A mentee who expects career coaching from a mentor who thinks they are providing research supervision will be disappointed, and neither party will understand why. Name the purpose and check that both share it.

### Make A Working Agreement, Not Just Good Intentions

Goodwill is not a plan. Convert intentions into an explicit working agreement that covers:

- meeting frequency and duration;
- how meetings are scheduled and who initiates;
- what the mentee brings to each meeting, agenda, progress, questions;
- what the mentor commits to, feedback turnaround, review of work, advocacy;
- communication channels and response-time expectations;
- how the mentee's progress will be tracked;
- how either party can raise concerns or renegotiate.

A written or clearly stated agreement is not bureaucratic; it is protective. It gives both parties a reference point when memory fades or stress rises, and it makes renegotiation possible rather than emotional.

### Align Expectations In Both Directions

Expectations flow both ways. The mentee has expectations of the mentor, and the mentor has expectations of the mentee. Surface both:

- what the mentee expects: availability, feedback quality, advocacy, sponsorship, emotional support, network access;
- what the mentor expects: preparation, initiative, honesty about progress, meeting commitments, professional conduct;
- where these expectations are realistic and where they need adjustment;
- what happens when an expectation cannot be met.

Unspoken expectations become resentments. Spoken expectations become negotiable.

### Match Mentoring Style To The Mentee's Stage And Needs

Effective mentoring is not one-size-fit-all. A first-year student needs more direction and structure; an advanced doctoral candidate needs more autonomy and challenge; a junior colleague needs peer-like collaboration with strategic guidance. Adjust:

- how directive versus facilitative to be;
- how much structure versus openness to provide;
- how often to check in versus step back;
- how much to challenge versus support;
- how much to share your own experience versus elicit theirs.

A mentoring style that works for a dependent novice will stifle an emerging independent scholar. Match style to stage and revisit as the mentee develops.

### Define Boundaries And Roles Clearly

Mentoring relationships involve power and intimacy, which makes boundaries essential. Clarify:

- the mentor's institutional roles and duties, evaluator, recommender, supervisor;
- what the mentor can and cannot keep confidential;
- limits on the relationship, not a therapist, not a parent, not an employer of last resort;
- how to handle dual relationships, when the mentor is also teacher, examiner, or co-author;
- how personal and professional dimensions interact.

Blurry boundaries produce conflicts of interest, dependency, and harm. Clear boundaries protect both parties and make the relationship sustainable.

### Build Feedback Into The Relationship

Feedback is the engine of mentoring, but it must work in both directions. Establish:

- how the mentor gives feedback to the mentee, form, frequency, directness;
- how the mentee can give feedback to the mentor, including when something is not working;
- regular check-ins on the relationship itself, not only the work;
- a norm that raising concerns is welcome, not a betrayal.

Mentoring relationships without feedback channels drift; the mentee stops raising problems, the mentor stops hearing them, and small frictions accumulate into rupture. Build feedback in from the start.

### Plan For The Mentee's Growing Independence

The aim of mentoring is, in most cases, the mentee's eventual independence, not their permanent dependence. Plan for this:

- gradually shift decision authority to the mentee;
- reduce scaffolding as competence grows;
- support the mentee in building their own network beyond the mentor;
- celebrate and make visible the mentee's independent achievements;
- discuss what the relationship will look like as the mentee advances.

A mentor who keeps the mentee dependent has failed at a core task of mentoring. A mentor who withdraws support too early abandons them. Time the shift deliberately.

### Address Difference And Power Honestly

Mentoring relationships often cross differences of identity, background, seniority, and power. Acknowledge:

- how differences in gender, race, nationality, class, or background shape the relationship;
- how the mentor's power affects what the mentee feels able to say;
- how the mentor's assumptions about the mentee may be wrong;
- how the mentee's identity may affect their experience in the field and the institution.

Pretending difference does not exist does not make it disappear; it makes it harder to discuss. Honest acknowledgment builds trust.

### Renegotiate When Circumstances Change

Mentoring relationships are not static. Renegotiate when:

- the mentee's goals or direction change;
- the mentor's capacity changes, sabbatical, new role, increased load;
- the relationship hits a difficulty that earlier norms cannot absorb;
- the mentee reaches a stage that calls for a different style;
- external events, funding, health, family, reshape what is possible.

Treating the original agreement as fixed guarantees that change will produce strain. Treating it as renegotiable lets the relationship adapt.

### Recognize When The Relationship Should End Or Be Handed On

Not every mentoring relationship should continue indefinitely. Recognize when:

- the mentee has outgrown what this mentor can offer;
- the relationship has become unproductive or harmful;
- a different mentor would better serve the mentee's current needs;
- the mentee is ready for full independence;
- the relationship has run its natural course.

Ending or transitioning a mentoring relationship well, with honesty, closure, and appropriate handoff, is a sign of good mentoring, not failure. Clinging to a relationship past its usefulness serves neither party.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Shared Purpose

Assuming both parties want the same thing from mentoring without checking produces silent misalignment. Name the purpose.

### Goodwill Without Agreement

Relying on goodwill instead of a working agreement leaves no reference point when stress rises. Make expectations explicit.

### One-Way Expectations

Focusing only on what the mentee should do, while the mentor's commitments stay vague, breeds resentment. Align expectations both ways.

### Fixed Mentoring Style

Using the same directive style regardless of the mentee's stage stifles growth. Match style to stage.

### Blurry Boundaries

Letting personal and professional roles blur creates conflicts and harm. Define boundaries clearly.

### No Feedback Channel

Relationships without a way to raise concerns drift into silent dissatisfaction. Build feedback in.

### Keeping The Mentee Dependent

A mentor who never shifts authority fails the core aim of mentoring. Plan for independence.

### Ignoring Difference And Power

Pretending identity and power differences do not matter prevents honest conversation. Acknowledge them.

### Treating The Agreement As Fixed

Refusing to renegotiate when circumstances change strains the relationship. Renegotiate.

### Clinging Past Usefulness

Holding a relationship open when it should end or transition serves neither party. End or hand on well.

## Self-Check

- Is the purpose and scope of the mentoring relationship, its type, horizon, and formality, explicitly established and shared?
- Is there a working agreement covering meetings, preparation, commitments, communication, and progress tracking, rather than goodwill alone?
- Are expectations aligned in both directions, with the mentee's hopes and the mentor's commitments both made explicit?
- Is the mentoring style matched to the mentee's stage and developmental needs, and revisited as they grow?
- Are boundaries and roles, including institutional duties and confidentiality limits, clearly defined?
- Is there a feedback channel that works both ways, including a norm that raising concerns is welcome?
- Does the relationship plan for the mentee's growing independence rather than permanent dependence?
- Are differences of identity, background, and power acknowledged honestly rather than ignored?
- Is the agreement treated as renegotiable when goals, capacity, or circumstances change?
- Is there willingness to end or transition the relationship when it has served its purpose, with honesty and appropriate handoff?
