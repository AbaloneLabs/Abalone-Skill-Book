# Research Mentoring And Supervision

Research mentoring is not simply assigning tasks and giving comments on drafts. Professors shape how students and trainees learn research standards, data practices, authorship norms, safety expectations, collaboration habits, and professional identity. Poor supervision can create confusion, exploitation, authorship disputes, data loss, misconduct risk, and preventable stress. Good mentoring makes expectations explicit and checks primary work before problems become serious.

Use this skill before designing a research mentoring plan, advising a graduate student, running a lab or research group, setting authorship expectations, reviewing a thesis project, creating data-management norms, or responding to trainee concerns. The goal is to make the agent attend to research integrity, role clarity, evidence, power dynamics, and institutional process.

## Core Rules

### Define The Mentoring Relationship

Mentoring expectations differ for thesis advisor, dissertation chair, committee member, lab PI, course project supervisor, postdoctoral mentor, independent study supervisor, and informal mentor.

Clarify:

- role of professor;
- role of student or trainee;
- degree or program requirements;
- funding relationship;
- employment relationship;
- project ownership;
- expected deliverables;
- meeting cadence;
- decision authority;
- committee or co-mentor roles;
- institutional policies.

Do not leave students to infer norms from hidden department culture. Make expectations explicit.

### Establish Project Scope And Milestones

Research projects need scope discipline. Students can lose months when questions, methods, data, or feasibility remain vague.

Define:

- research question;
- contribution;
- literature base;
- method;
- data source;
- ethics or IRB requirements;
- equipment or software;
- analysis plan;
- writing milestones;
- funding constraints;
- conference or publication goals;
- realistic timeline;
- risk points.

For theses and dissertations, align milestones with program deadlines. For lab projects, align with funding, safety, and collaboration obligations.

### Teach Research Standards Explicitly

Responsible conduct of research should not be assumed. ORI materials emphasize that mentors help trainees learn research regulations, institutional policies, and standards.

Discuss:

- data acquisition;
- data management;
- data sharing;
- data ownership;
- authorship;
- plagiarism;
- image or figure handling;
- peer review confidentiality;
- conflicts of interest;
- human subjects;
- animal subjects;
- lab safety;
- grant charging;
- publication ethics;
- research misconduct reporting.

Point students to institutional policies and required training. If the field has specific standards, name them.

### Review Source Data And Work Products

Mentors should not only review polished outputs. Primary data, code, lab notebooks, transcripts, field notes, stimuli, survey instruments, and analysis logs reveal problems earlier.

Review:

- raw data existence;
- data provenance;
- consent or permission;
- data cleaning decisions;
- code reproducibility;
- version control;
- lab notebook completeness;
- sample inclusion and exclusion;
- statistical assumptions;
- figure generation;
- citation practice;
- backup process.

Do not wait until manuscript submission to discover that data cannot be explained.

### Set Authorship And Credit Expectations Early

Authorship disputes often arise because expectations were implicit. Discuss criteria and revisit as contributions change.

Clarify:

- expected contributions;
- author order norms;
- acknowledgement norms;
- data ownership;
- student thesis rights;
- PI role;
- collaborator role;
- criteria for manuscript authorship;
- conference presentation rights;
- use of shared datasets;
- process for resolving disputes.

Authorship standards vary by field and journal, so use relevant professional and institutional policies. Do not promise authorship based only on hierarchy or funding if field norms require substantive contribution.

### Manage Power And Well-Being Responsibly

Mentoring involves power. Students may depend on the professor for funding, grades, visas, references, graduation, employment, and future opportunities.

Watch for:

- unreasonable workload;
- unclear expectations;
- pressure to work unsafe hours;
- retaliation fear;
- discrimination or harassment;
- authorship coercion;
- delayed feedback;
- blocked graduation;
- financial dependency;
- conflict of interest;
- mental health concerns.

Use institutional support processes when concerns exceed the professor's role. Do not act as therapist, lawyer, or immigration adviser unless qualified; refer to appropriate offices.

### Create Communication Norms

Many mentoring breakdowns are communication breakdowns. Students need to know how feedback works and when silence means approval or delay.

Define:

- meeting frequency;
- agenda owner;
- document-sharing method;
- feedback turnaround;
- urgent communication channel;
- expected independence;
- decision log;
- task tracker;
- disagreement process;
- absence or leave plan.

After meetings, record decisions and next steps. This protects both mentor and trainee.

### Support Professional Development

Research mentoring includes career development, not only project completion.

Discuss:

- career goals;
- teaching experience;
- publication strategy;
- conference participation;
- grant or fellowship applications;
- networking;
- job market preparation;
- portfolio or CV;
- collaboration skills;
- peer review practice;
- public communication;
- nonacademic pathways.

Do not assume every trainee wants the professor's career path.

### Escalate Problems Through Institutional Channels

Some issues require formal process: misconduct allegations, harassment, safety violations, IRB noncompliance, authorship disputes, grade disputes, discrimination, funding misuse, or severe conflict.

Know:

- graduate program director;
- department chair;
- research integrity officer;
- IRB or IACUC;
- ombuds office;
- Title IX or civil rights office;
- disability office;
- HR;
- counseling or student support;
- safety office.

When a formal duty exists, do not handle it informally to avoid discomfort.

## Common Traps

### Assuming Students Know The Hidden Curriculum

Students may not know what counts as progress, authorship, rigor, independence, or professionalism. Explain norms.

### Reviewing Only Final Drafts

Final drafts hide data and method problems. Review source materials periodically.

### Delaying Feedback Until It Blocks Progress

Long feedback delays can derail graduation, funding, and publication. Set realistic turnaround norms.

### Treating Authorship As A Reward

Authorship should follow contribution and field standards, not favoritism, hierarchy, or morale management.

### Confusing Supervision With Control

Students need autonomy appropriate to level. Overcontrol can prevent learning; under-supervision can create risk.

### Ignoring Power Dynamics

Students may not feel free to say a workload is unreasonable or a plan is unclear. Invite structured feedback.

### Handling Misconduct Informally

Research misconduct and compliance issues require institutional process. Do not privately negotiate serious allegations.

## Self-Check

- Are mentor, trainee, committee, funding, employment, and decision roles explicit?
- Are research question, method, data, ethics review, milestones, and feasibility clear?
- Are responsible conduct topics and institutional policies named?
- Are source data, code, notes, analysis decisions, and reproducibility reviewed before final output?
- Are authorship, author order, acknowledgement, data ownership, and dispute process discussed early?
- Are power dynamics, workload, well-being, discrimination, harassment, and retaliation risks considered?
- Are communication cadence, feedback turnaround, decision logs, and urgent channels defined?
- Does mentoring support career goals beyond the immediate project?
- Are formal issues escalated to research integrity, IRB, ombuds, Title IX, HR, or other institutional channels when required?
- Does the plan reduce ambiguity rather than relying on hidden departmental norms?
