# Mentoring Struggling And At-Risk Students

Most mentors will encounter students who struggle: a promising student whose work suddenly deteriorates, a doctoral candidate who stops responding, an undergraduate who disappears midway through a project, a mentee whose motivation collapses and whose confidence follows. The temptation in these moments is to respond at one of two extremes, either to rescue the student by absorbing their problems and lowering expectations, or to withdraw, treating the struggle as the student's private failure. Both responses abandon the student. Effective mentoring of struggling students requires the mentor to diagnose what kind of struggle is happening, respond at the right level, connect the student to appropriate support, maintain appropriate expectations, and know when the situation exceeds the mentoring role and requires escalation. It also requires the mentor to distinguish between a student who is temporarily overwhelmed and one whose path or fit has genuinely changed.

Use this skill before intervening with a student who is struggling, falling behind, losing motivation, in crisis, or at risk of failing or withdrawing. The goal is to prevent the agent from either over-functioning in ways that create dependency and exceed the mentor's role, or under-functioning in ways that abandon the student to a spiral they could have been helped out of.

## Core Rules

### Diagnose The Kind Of Struggle Before Intervening

Struggle has many causes, and the right response depends on the cause. Before acting, assess which of these, or which combination, is operating:

- academic difficulty, the work exceeds current preparation or skill;
- skill gap, a specific missing competency that can be built;
- time or workload overload, too many demands relative to capacity;
- motivation or purpose loss, the student no longer sees why they are doing this;
- personal crisis, illness, loss, family emergency, financial shock;
- mental health, depression, anxiety, trauma, burnout;
- relational or climate harm, bias, harassment, isolation, advisor conflict;
- fit or direction change, the path no longer fits the student's goals or abilities.

A rescue that ignores the cause will not help. Diagnose first, then match the response to what is actually wrong.

### Intervene Early Rather Than Waiting For Crisis

Most academic spirals are visible long before they become crises. Warning signs include:

- missed meetings or deadlines;
- deteriorating quality of work;
- reduced communication or withdrawal;
- sudden drop in engagement or attendance;
- expressions of hopelessness or self-doubt;
- visible stress, exhaustion, or changed demeanor.

Early, low-stakes intervention, a check-in, a conversation, a revised plan, is far more effective than waiting until failure is imminent. Mentors who wait for the student to ask often wait too long.

### Have A Direct, Non-Judgmental Conversation

When you notice struggle, initiate a direct conversation. Practices:

- name what you have observed without accusation, "I've noticed your work has shifted";
- express care and curiosity, not disappointment;
- ask open questions and listen more than you talk;
- avoid jumping to solutions before understanding the situation;
- separate the student's worth from their current performance;
- make it safe to tell the truth about what is happening.

Students who are struggling often feel shame, which makes them hide. A non-judgmental conversation lowers the shame enough for honesty.

### Match Support To The Level Of Need

Different struggles call for different levels of support. Calibrate:

- light support: a revised timeline, clearer expectations, a check-in cadence;
- moderate support: skill-building resources, tutoring, reduced load, a structured recovery plan;
- intensive support: leave, medical or mental health care, formal accommodation, programmatic intervention;
- redirection: honest conversation about whether the current path is right.

Matching support to need avoids both under-supporting a student in real trouble and over-supporting a student who needs a nudge rather than a rescue.

### Connect To Institutional Resources Rather Than Solo Problem-Solving

Mentors are not therapists, financial aid officers, disability coordinators, or counselors, and trying to play those roles fails the student. Know and refer to:

- academic support, tutoring, writing centers, quantitative help;
- disability and accommodation services;
- counseling and mental health services;
- financial aid and basic needs support;
- advising and student affairs for broader intervention;
- institutional channels for bias, harassment, or safety concerns.

Referral is part of good mentoring, not a substitute for it. Stay involved while connecting the student to specialized help.

### Maintain Appropriate Expectations And Accountability

Support does not mean abandoning expectations. A student who is struggling still benefits from clear, achievable expectations and accountability. Practices:

- co-create a realistic recovery plan with specific next steps;
- maintain the academic standards that the work requires;
- distinguish accommodating a barrier from lowering the standard;
- follow up on commitments rather than letting them slide silently;
- hold the student accountable while holding them in care.

Lowering expectations silently teaches the student they cannot meet them. Maintaining expectations with support teaches them they can.

### Address Motivation And Purpose, Not Only Performance

When a student's motivation collapses, addressing only performance misses the core issue. Explore:

- whether the work still connects to what the student cares about;
- whether the student has lost sight of why they started;
- whether burnout has drained the capacity to care;
- whether the path still fits the student's evolving goals;
- whether the student needs a break, a pivot, or a renewed sense of purpose.

Sometimes the most supportive thing a mentor can do is help a student reconsider their direction honestly rather than pushing them through a path that no longer fits.

### Recognize And Respect The Student's Agency

Even a struggling student is the agent of their own life. Respect that:

- the student may choose a path the mentor would not choose;
- the student may need to pause, leave, or change direction;
- the mentor's role is to inform and support, not to decide;
- pushing a student toward the mentor's preferred outcome overrides their agency;
- a student's decision to step back may be the right one.

Mentoring supports agency; it does not replace it. The student lives with the consequences.

### Know When To Escalate

Some situations exceed the mentoring role and require escalation. Escalate when there is:

- risk of harm to self or others;
- disclosure of harassment, assault, or discrimination that triggers reporting duties;
- suspected academic misconduct;
- severe mental health crisis;
- safety concerns in a lab, field, or clinical setting;
- institutional or legal obligations that cannot be met informally.

Know your mandatory reporting duties and the relevant offices. Escalation is not betrayal; it is how serious situations get the response they require.

### Follow Up And Sustain Support Over Time

A single intervention rarely resolves a struggle. Sustain support:

- check in regularly after the initial conversation;
- monitor whether the recovery plan is working;
- adjust support as the situation evolves;
- celebrate progress, not only absence of crisis;
- remain available even after the acute phase passes.

Students who feel abandoned after the crisis passes learn that support is performative. Sustained follow-up makes support real.

## Common Traps

### Waiting For The Student To Ask

Waiting until the student asks for help often means waiting until failure is imminent. Intervene early on warning signs.

### Rescuing And Over-Functioning

Absorbing the student's problems, doing their work, or lowering standards creates dependency and exceeds the mentor's role. Match support to need.

### Diagnosing Before Understanding

Jumping to solutions before understanding the cause of struggle wastes effort and can harm. Diagnose first.

### Withdrawing From Discomfort

Pulling back because the situation is uncomfortable abandons the student to a spiral. Stay engaged.

### Playing Therapist Or Counselor

Trying to provide mental health, financial, or legal support solo fails the student. Refer to specialists.

### Silently Lowering Expectations

Dropping expectations without discussion teaches the student they cannot meet them. Maintain expectations with support.

### Ignoring Motivation And Purpose

Addressing only performance when motivation has collapsed misses the core issue. Explore purpose.

### Overriding The Student's Agency

Pushing the student toward the mentor's preferred outcome disrespects their agency. Inform and support their choice.

### Failing To Escalate When Required

Handling serious situations informally to avoid discomfort can violate duties and endanger the student. Escalate when required.

### One-Shot Intervention

Treating a single conversation as resolution leaves the student unsupported after the acute phase. Follow up over time.

## Self-Check

- Have you diagnosed the kind of struggle, academic, motivational, personal, mental health, climate, fit, before intervening?
- Did you intervene early on warning signs rather than waiting for crisis or for the student to ask?
- Was the initial conversation direct, non-judgmental, and oriented to understanding rather than accusation?
- Is the level of support matched to the level of need, neither under-supporting nor over-functioning?
- Have you connected the student to appropriate institutional resources rather than trying to solve everything solo?
- Are expectations maintained with support, rather than silently lowered or rigidly imposed?
- Has motivation and purpose been explored, not only performance?
- Is the student's agency respected, including the possibility that stepping back or changing direction is the right choice?
- Do you know your escalation duties and the relevant offices for risk, misconduct, crisis, and safety?
- Is support sustained through follow-up rather than ending after a single intervention?
