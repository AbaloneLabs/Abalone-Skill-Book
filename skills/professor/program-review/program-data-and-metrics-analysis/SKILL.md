# Program Data And Metrics Analysis

Program review and planning run on data — enrollment, retention, graduation, student outcomes, faculty productivity, finances — and the quality of decisions depends on the quality of data interpretation. The failure mode is making high-stakes decisions on metrics that are misleading, incomplete, or chosen to support a predetermined conclusion: citing raw enrollment without context, celebrating a graduation rate that hides disparities, or cutting a program based on a single year's data. Data does not speak for itself; it must be interpreted with awareness of what it measures, what it obscures, and what context it requires. Professors who can read program data critically — who know which metrics matter, which mislead, and what context they need — make better review and planning contributions and resist decisions driven by vanity metrics or political convenience.

Use this skill when analyzing program data, when interpreting metrics for review or planning, and when diagnosing why decisions are being made on weak or misleading evidence. The goal is to prevent the agent from misreading data or from letting vanity metrics drive consequential decisions.

## Core Rules

### Understand What Each Metric Actually Measures

Every metric has a definition, and the definition determines what it tells you. "Enrollment" may mean headcount, credit hours, majors, or seats filled, and these tell different stories. "Graduation rate" may be four-year, six-year, first-time-full-time, or all-comers, and the choice dramatically changes the number. Before interpreting any metric, understand exactly what it measures, what population it includes and excludes, and what it does and doesn't capture. Metrics interpreted without understanding their definitions mislead reliably.

### Contextualize Metrics Against Trends And Peers

A single number tells little; trends and comparisons tell more. A graduation rate of 60% means one thing if it's rising from 45% and another if it's falling from 75%. An enrollment of 50 majors means one thing at a program's peer institutions averaging 30 and another if they average 200. Always contextualize metrics against the program's own trend (multi-year, not single-year) and against relevant peers. Decisions made on single-year or uncontextualized numbers are decisions made on noise.

### Disaggregate To Reveal Disparities

Aggregate metrics conceal disparities that matter ethically and practically. A program with a strong overall graduation rate may have a much weaker rate for first-generation students, students of color, or transfer students. Disaggregate key metrics by student characteristics — race, gender, first-generation status, transfer status, Pell status — to reveal whether the program is serving all students or only some. Decisions made on aggregates that hide disparities perpetuate the disparities.

### Distinguish Leading From Lagging Indicators

Some metrics are leading indicators (they predict future outcomes) and some are lagging (they report past outcomes). First-year retention predicts eventual graduation; current graduation rates reflect decisions made four to six years ago. Leading indicators let you act before outcomes are locked in; lagging indicators tell you whether past actions worked. Use both, and don't confuse a lagging indicator for a real-time signal. A program celebrating a strong graduation rate while its first-year retention is collapsing is celebrating the past while losing the future.

### Avoid Vanity Metrics And Misleading Indicators

Vanity metrics — numbers that look good but don't reflect health — drive poor decisions. Examples: total applications (irrelevant if yield is collapsing), gross enrollment (irrelevant if students aren't progressing), or selective citation of favorable years. Equally misleading are metrics used out of context to support a predetermined conclusion, such as citing one year of declining enrollment to justify cutting a program with a strong long-term trend. Recognize vanity and weaponized metrics, and insist on meaningful indicators.

### Use Multiple Measures To Triangulate

No single metric captures program health. Triangulate using multiple measures: enrollment trends, progression and retention, graduation, learning outcomes assessment, post-graduation outcomes, faculty productivity, and financial contribution. A program strong on one metric and weak on others tells a complex story that requires judgment, not a single number. Triangulation protects against decisions driven by whichever metric happens to favor a preferred conclusion.

### Connect Metrics To Causes, Not Just Outcomes

Data shows outcomes; understanding causes requires investigation. If retention is falling, why — advising, curriculum, financial aid, climate, competing programs? If a demographic group is underperforming, what barriers are they encountering? Resist the temptation to treat a metric as a self-explanatory verdict; use it as a prompt to investigate causes. Decisions based on outcomes without understanding causes treat symptoms and miss leverage points.

### Be Honest About Data Limitations

Program data is often incomplete, lagging, or collected for purposes other than the question at hand. Be honest about what the data can and cannot tell you, and avoid overclaiming from weak evidence. A decision honestly acknowledging data limitations and seeking better evidence is stronger than one overclaiming from inadequate data. Intellectual honesty about evidence is itself a form of rigor that improves decisions over time.

## Common Traps

- **Interpreting metrics without understanding definitions.** "Enrollment" and "graduation rate" mean different things under different definitions. Know what you're measuring.
- **Single-year or uncontextualized numbers.** Decisions made on noise rather than trends. Always contextualize.
- **Aggregate metrics that hide disparities.** Strong overall numbers can conceal failure to serve specific student groups. Disaggregate.
- **Confusing lagging for leading indicators.** Celebrating past outcomes while current indicators collapse. Watch the leading indicators.
- **Vanity and weaponized metrics.** Numbers chosen to look good or support a predetermined conclusion. Insist on meaningful indicators.
- **Single-metric decisions.** No one number captures program health. Triangulate.
- **Outcomes without causes.** Treating a metric as a verdict rather than a prompt to investigate. Understand causes.
- **Overclaiming from weak data.** Drawing strong conclusions from incomplete or lagging evidence. Be honest about limitations.

## Self-Check

- Before interpreting any metric, do I understand its exact definition — what it measures, and what it includes and excludes?
- Have I contextualized the metric against multi-year trends and relevant peer comparisons, rather than reading a single number?
- Have I disaggregated key metrics by student characteristics to reveal whether the program serves all students or only some?
- Am I distinguishing leading indicators (which predict) from lagging ones (which report), and watching the leading indicators?
- Am I recognizing and rejecting vanity metrics and metrics used out of context to support predetermined conclusions?
- Am I triangulating across multiple measures rather than letting a single metric drive the decision?
- Am I using metrics as prompts to investigate causes, rather than treating them as self-explanatory verdicts?
- Am I being honest about the data's limitations rather than overclaiming from incomplete or lagging evidence?
