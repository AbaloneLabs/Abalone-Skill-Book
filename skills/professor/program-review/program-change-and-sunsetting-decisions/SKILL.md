# Program Change And Sunsetting Decisions

Programs are not static: fields evolve, student demand shifts, resources constrain, and institutions must sometimes grow new programs, restructure existing ones, merge overlapping units, or sunset programs that no longer serve their purpose. These decisions are among the hardest in academic governance because they involve real people — students mid-degree, faculty whose careers are invested, staff whose jobs are at stake — and because they are easily driven by short-term financial pressure or political convenience rather than by careful educational judgment. The failure mode is twofold: avoiding necessary change until crisis forces rushed, harmful decisions; or making change hastily and punitively, without due process, transition planning, or care for the people affected. Good program change decisions are timely, evidence-based, humane, and protective of the students and faculty whose lives they disrupt. They are also, when done well, how institutions stay vital.

Use this skill when participating in decisions to grow, restructure, merge, or sunset programs, and when diagnosing why such decisions are being avoided or mishandled. The goal is to prevent the agent from letting necessary change become a crisis or from making change in ways that harm people and erode trust.

## Core Rules

### Base Decisions On Evidence And Educational Rationale

Program change decisions should be grounded in evidence — enrollment trends, outcomes, financial contribution, alignment with mission, field evolution — and in clear educational rationale connected to the institution's mission. Decisions driven by single-year financial pressure, by a dean's preference, or by political convenience, without educational rationale, are likely to be wrong and to damage the institution. Insist on multi-year evidence, honest assessment, and explicit rationale before deciding.

### Act Before Crisis Forces Rushed Decisions

The worst program changes happen under crisis, when declining enrollment or budget pressure forces rapid cuts that harm students and faculty and that could have been managed with earlier, more deliberate action. Watch for the leading indicators — sustained enrollment decline, thin faculty pipelines, weak outcomes, mission drift — and act while options remain. Early, deliberate change preserves choices; crisis-driven change eliminates them. The discomfort of early action is far less than the harm of crisis action.

### Protect Students In Transition

When a program is restructured or sunset, students currently in the program must be protected: they must be able to complete their degree through teach-out plans, with the courses, advising, and support they need. Design teach-out carefully, communicate clearly and early, and honor the institution's commitment to students who enrolled in good faith. Students harmed by a poorly managed sunset become a lasting reputational cost; students supported through a transition become evidence the institution can be trusted.

### Treat Faculty And Staff With Due Process And Care

Program changes affect faculty and staff careers, and these people must be treated with due process, transparency, and care. Follow the personnel policies and any collective bargaining agreements; provide adequate notice; offer transitions, reassignments, or support where possible; and avoid punitive or humiliating treatment. The way an institution treats faculty and staff during difficult changes shapes its culture and its ability to recruit and retain talent for decades. Harsh treatment in cuts is remembered and resented.

### Consider Restructuring And Merging Before Sunsetting

Sunsetting is the most disruptive option and should generally be considered after less disruptive alternatives: restructuring the program to address weaknesses, merging with an adjacent program to share resources and broaden scope, or revising the curriculum to better serve demand. These alternatives may preserve the people and much of the program while addressing the underlying problem. Jumping to sunset without considering restructuring or merging is often a failure of imagination and care.

### Engage Faculty Governance Authentically

Program change is a faculty governance matter, not a purely administrative decision. Engage the faculty — in the program and in the broader unit — authentically and early, not as a ratification of a decision already made. Faculty who participate in the analysis and the decision are more likely to support the outcome and to contribute the on-the-ground knowledge that makes the decision better. Bypassing faculty governance produces resistance, poor decisions, and damage to shared governance.

### Communicate Honestly And Throughout

Program change generates anxiety and rumor, and poor communication makes both worse. Communicate honestly and throughout the process: what is being considered, what the timeline is, what the criteria are, and what the implications are for students, faculty, and staff. Honest communication is harder in the short term (it surfaces conflict) but far better in the long term than secrecy or spin, which destroy trust when the decision arrives.

### Connect Change To Strategic Direction

Program changes should fit a coherent strategic direction for the institution, not be ad hoc reactions to immediate pressure. A program sunset that fits a clear strategy — investing in strengths, responding to field evolution, reallocating to growth areas — is defensible and often wise; a program sunset that is an isolated cut without strategic context looks arbitrary and erodes confidence. Connect change decisions to the institution's stated strategy and mission.

## Common Traps

- **Crisis-driven change.** Avoiding decisions until crisis forces rushed, harmful cuts. Act early on leading indicators.
- **Finance- or politics-driven change.** Cutting or merging based on single-year pressure or preference, without educational rationale and multi-year evidence.
- **Neglecting teach-out for students.** Stranding students mid-degree destroys trust and reputation. Design and resource teach-out carefully.
- **Punitive treatment of faculty and staff.** Harsh cuts damage culture and recruitment for decades. Follow due process and treat people with care.
- **Jumping to sunset.** Sunsetting without considering restructuring or merging forecloses less disruptive options.
- **Bypassing faculty governance.** Administrative decisions imposed on faculty produce resistance and poor outcomes. Engage faculty authentically.
- **Secrecy and spin.** Poor communication breeds anxiety, rumor, and mistrust. Communicate honestly throughout.
- **Change without strategic context.** Isolated cuts look arbitrary. Connect decisions to a coherent strategic direction.

## Self-Check

- Is the decision grounded in multi-year evidence and clear educational rationale connected to mission, not single-year pressure or preference?
- Are we acting on leading indicators before crisis forces rushed decisions?
- Is there a careful, resourced teach-out plan that protects students currently in the program?
- Are faculty and staff being treated with due process, transparency, and care, per policy and any collective bargaining agreements?
- Have we considered restructuring and merging before deciding to sunset?
- Is faculty governance engaged authentically and early, not merely asked to ratify?
- Are we communicating honestly throughout, rather than relying on secrecy or spin?
- Does the change fit a coherent strategic direction for the institution, rather than appearing as an arbitrary isolated cut?
