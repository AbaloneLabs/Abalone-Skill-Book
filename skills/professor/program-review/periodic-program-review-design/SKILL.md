# Periodic Program Review Design

Periodic program review — the comprehensive evaluation of an academic program on a multi-year cycle — is, alongside accreditation, one of the main mechanisms by which institutions assess and improve their academic offerings. Done well, it is a genuine opportunity for a program to take stock, identify strengths and weaknesses, and chart a course for improvement with institutional awareness and support. The failure mode is treating program review as a compliance ritual: producing a self-study that defends the status quo, recruiting friendly external reviewers who will affirm whatever they're told, and filing the report without consequence. Review done this way consumes enormous effort and changes nothing, breeding cynicism about the process. Review done well is honest, evidence-based, externally informed, and consequential — it produces real evaluation and real improvement, and it earns the program credibility and resources.

Use this skill when designing or participating in program review, when preparing a self-study, and when diagnosing why a review produced no genuine evaluation. The goal is to prevent the agent from running program review as a defense of the status quo or as an empty compliance exercise.

## Core Rules

### Define Clear, Meaningful Review Criteria

Program review should evaluate the program against meaningful criteria: academic quality, currency of curriculum, student learning outcomes, demand and enrollment, faculty productivity and composition, resources, and alignment with institutional mission. Define the criteria clearly at the outset, and ensure they are the criteria that actually matter for the program's health — not generic checkboxes. Criteria that are vague or that avoid the hard questions (is this program viable? is it excellent? is it serving students?) produce reviews that answer nothing.

### Build An Honest, Evidence-Based Self-Study

The self-study is the program's own assessment, and it sets the tone for the review. An honest self-study presents the program accurately — strengths and weaknesses — backed by evidence (enrollment trends, graduation rates, assessment results, placement data, faculty productivity, peer comparisons). A self-study that defends the status quo and conceals weaknesses signals to reviewers and administration that the program is not engaging honestly, and it invites external reviewers to find what the program wouldn't acknowledge. Honest self-assessment, paired with improvement plans, is far more credible and useful.

### Engage Credible External Reviewers

External reviewers provide the perspective that internal self-assessment cannot, and their credibility determines the review's value. Recruit reviewers who are genuine experts in the field, who are independent (not collaborators or friends of the program's faculty), and who will speak honestly rather than affirm reflexively. Brief them thoroughly, give them access to real evidence and real people, and invite them to identify both strengths and concerns. Friendly reviewers who rubber-stamp the program waste the review; credible, honest reviewers make it valuable.

### Use Real Benchmarking Against Peer Programs

Program review should situate the program against relevant peers: comparable programs at peer institutions, in terms of size, scope, mission, and outcomes. Real benchmarking — using comparable data on enrollment, graduation, placement, faculty productivity, and resources — reveals whether the program is strong, adequate, or struggling relative to where it should be. Benchmarking that cherry-picks favorable comparisons or avoids honest peer assessment produces a self-flattering review that informs no one.

### Connect Review Findings To Action And Resources

A program review that produces findings but no action is a wasted exercise. Connect the review to a concrete action plan with owners, timelines, and resource implications. Findings that require resources — faculty lines, advising, space, curriculum revision — should be routed into the budget and planning processes that can provide them. A review whose findings are filed without action breeds cynicism; a review whose findings drive change justifies the effort.

### Engage Faculty Broadly In The Review

A program review conducted by the chair and a few faculty, unrecognized by the rest, produces a self-study that doesn't reflect the program and findings that no one owns. Engage the faculty broadly in the self-study, in interpreting the external review, and in planning the response. Broad engagement produces an accurate review and builds the ownership needed to act on findings. A program that has reviewed itself collectively is positioned to improve; a program that has been reviewed by a few and ignored by the rest is not.

### Include Honest Assessment Of Viability

Some program reviews should confront the question of viability: whether a program with declining enrollment, thin faculty, weak outcomes, or misalignment with mission can or should continue. This is painful, and the instinct is to avoid it, but avoiding it serves no one — not the students in a struggling program, not the faculty whose careers are stalling, not the institution allocating scarce resources. Honest viability assessment, conducted with care and due process, is part of responsible program review, even when the answer is difficult.

### Use Review To Inform Strategic Direction

Beyond evaluating current health, program review should inform strategic direction: where the field is going, what the program should become, what new initiatives or sunsetting of old ones the review suggests. A review that evaluates the present without considering the future misses the chance to shape the program's trajectory. Use the review to ask not only "how are we doing?" but "what should we become?"

## Common Traps

- **Compliance-ritual review.** Self-studies that defend the status quo and friendly reviewers who rubber-stamp produce reviews that change nothing.
- **Vague criteria that avoid hard questions.** Criteria that don't ask about quality, viability, or currency answer nothing meaningful.
- **Self-flattering self-study.** Concealing weaknesses invites external reviewers and administration to find them, damaging credibility.
- **Friendly external reviewers.** Reviewers who affirm reflexively waste the review. Recruit credible, independent experts.
- **Cherry-picked benchmarking.** Favorable comparisons that avoid honest peer assessment produce a self-flattering review.
- **Findings without action.** Reviews filed without consequence breed cynicism. Connect findings to action and resources.
- **Narrow faculty engagement.** Reviews conducted by a few and unrecognized by the rest can't drive improvement.
- **Avoiding viability questions.** Protecting struggling programs from honest assessment harms students, faculty, and the institution.

## Self-Check

- Are the review criteria clear, meaningful, and willing to ask the hard questions about quality, currency, and viability?
- Is the self-study honest and evidence-based, presenting weaknesses alongside strengths with improvement plans?
- Are external reviewers credible, independent experts who will speak honestly, not friendly affirmers?
- Is the benchmarking real — using comparable peer data on meaningful metrics — rather than cherry-picked?
- Are findings connected to a concrete action plan with owners, timelines, and resource implications?
- Is the faculty broadly engaged in the self-study and the response, so the review reflects and is owned by the program?
- Where viability is a concern, is it assessed honestly with due process, rather than avoided?
- Does the review inform strategic direction — what the program should become — not just evaluate present health?
